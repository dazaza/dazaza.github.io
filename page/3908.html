It is commonly used in radio frequency (RF) and telecommunications to express absolute power values. The formula is: \[ \text{Power (dBm)} = 10 \times \log_{10}\left(\frac{\text{Power (mW)}}{1\ \text{mW}}\right) \] 2. **dBi (Decibels isotropic)**: dBi measures the gain of an antenna compared to an ideal isotropic radiator, which emits energy uniformly in all directions. It indicates how much stronger the signal is in a particular direction compared to an isotropic source. 3. **dBd (Decibels dipole)**: dBd measures the gain of an antenna relative to a half-wave dipole antenna. The relationship between dBd and dBi is: \[ 0\ \text{dBd} = 2.15\ \text{dBi} \] So, an antenna with 0 dBd gain has 2.15 dBi gain. 4. **dB (Decibels)**: dB is a logarithmic unit used to express ratios between two values, such as power, voltage, or intensity. It does not have an absolute reference point unless specified (e.g., dBm, dBi). 5. **dBc (Decibels relative to carrier)**: dBc is used to measure the power level of a signal relative to the power of the carrier frequency. It is often used in communications systems to describe the level of interference or spurious signals compared to the main carrier signal.
