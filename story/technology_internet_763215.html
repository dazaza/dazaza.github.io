Apple adjusts App Store ranking algorithm, blacklist companies reach out to users

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 17:13:33

The iTunes account theft incident has actually existed for a long time, but it has always been an individual phenomenon. However, recently more and more netizens have complained on Weibo and various APP game application forums that when they check the purchased items in the App Store, they found that besides the applications they truly downloaded, there were also a large number of applications that they had never downloaded before. These applications are mostly domestic mobile games, long martial arts novels and other suspected ranking-brushing applications. Investigations revealed that hackers had already entered the APP ranking-brushing industry in an organized manner and openly sold stolen accounts.

From the complaints of netizens, it was found that those repeatedly exposed games or applications were exactly the games that had been consistently ranked high in the Apple China region rankings. But these games were not actively downloaded by netizens, let alone opening accounts to try them out. Thus, netizens naturally associated themselves with being "ranking-brushed" due to account theft!

Ranking-brushing companies openly sell ranking-brushing services

Apple's AppStore has always ranked all APPs based on their download volumes; the higher the download volume, the higher the APP ranks, and the exposure rate is also the highest. In other words, the higher the APP rank, the more opportunities the application has to gain benefits. High-quality applications can naturally get official recommendations, but for those average-quality applications, they generally buy their way onto the list through paid methods to make the application listed. Thus, the interest chain of ranking-brushing was created. Some insiders speculated that APPs entering the top 10 of AppStore could achieve natural downloads of 30,000 to 50,000 per day. APPs entering the top three of categorized rankings could achieve around 10,000 natural downloads. The conventional operation method of ranking-brushing companies is: registering a large number of iTunes accounts and controlling these accounts through programs to download designated applications, thus allowing the promoted applications to gain a large number of downloads in a short period of time, instantly improving their ranking. By using the "exposure effect" of the rankings, users are induced to believe this is a "popular game," thereby obtaining real downloads and activations. Some media learned that a ranking-brushing company named iTunesRank claimed in its promotional materials, "Ranking and download volume have a direct relationship; the higher the download volume, the higher the ranking. Currently, we can generate 150,000 downloads per day, which can push your APP to the top of the free rankings on the same day." This company quoted prices, stating that during a certain period, brushing to the top five of the iPhone store's overall China rankings would cost 25,000 yuan; top ten would cost 19,900 yuan; top twenty would cost 15,000 yuan.

When journalists searched for "ranking-brushing" on Taobao, they discovered that many Taobao sellers were openly selling ranking-brushing services. However, unlike regular sellers who describe their products, ranking-brushing sellers typically list a price of 1,000 yuan for AppStore ranking-brushing. Through communication via Taobao Wangwang, some sellers claimed that about 1,500 yuan could get you into the top 200 rankings, and if you needed a higher ranking, it would require more fees. The higher the ranking, the higher the price, but the specific price depends on the required time frame.

Apple changes rules, ranking-brushing fails, transition to account theft

The Apple Store rankings are determined by a complex calculation formula. In addition to daily app launches and program usage time statistics, download volume is a decisive factor affecting the rankings, and ranking-brushing companies focus on manipulating this aspect.

However, it is rumored that Apple recently adjusted its algorithm, making account behavior recognition stricter. The automatically registered accounts used for ranking-brushing lack real user behaviors (including complete downloads, activations, uninstalls, etc.), so their influence on rankings during ranking-brushing is less significant compared to accounts with real user behaviors. Fully simulating real user behavior is still quite challenging with current ranking-brushing technology, hence account theft became a path.

Stolen accounts used for ranking-brushing may lead to app removal, severe cases involve legal responsibility

Using stolen accounts to brush up an app will result in Apple sending a large number of such emails to the victims. After receiving the email, users who discover their accounts have been compromised can file a complaint with Apple (by calling Apple's customer service hotline at 4006272273 or contacting iTunes Store online customer service). If Apple continues to receive such complaints, they might send warning letters to developers or even directly remove the app from the store. In more serious cases, if the theft and use of stolen accounts for ranking-brushing cause significant economic losses to users, the illegal behavior becomes more severe, leading to possible legal sanctions. In severe cases, imprisonment could be imposed!

Tips

Common account theft methods

1

Need to input AppleID when using jailbroken product applications

Some users like to jailbreak to get more free apps. Some iOS assistant software can help you install apps while in a jailbroken state, similar to 91 Assistant, etc. Non-jailbreak assistants like i-Tools require users to fill in their AppleID, which is the iTunes account, along with the password. This data can become a target for hackers to steal, and most accounts are stolen under such circumstances.

2

Hackers steal accounts via email servers and filter iTunes accounts

This is a common account theft method where hackers write a program to attempt logging into iTunes one by one using these accounts, filtering out usable accounts. A single operation like this could match hundreds of thousands of iTunes accounts.

3

Gaming or registration leads to phishing scams

Some games and app registrations require filling out email registrations. If you use your iTunes account for registration, and coincidentally, the source of bad app ranking-brushing is also the game developer, they can obtain your AppleID through phishing methods.

Solutions

Currently, Apple has not provided a very clear solution, but from the experience of fruit fans, as long as you don't use jailbroken Apple products for downloading and follow Apple's instructions step-by-step for downloading, the chance of being hacked is minimal.
