I. Overview:

Acne is a common chronic, inflammatory, and disfiguring skin disease among young men and women, also known as "pimples" or "teenage zits." It mainly occurs on the face, chest, and back. Common symptoms include pimples, small rashes, pustules, nodules, cysts, and scars. Acne is a common skin condition during adolescence, affecting approximately 90% of teenagers to varying degrees, usually subsiding gradually after the age of 25. Recurrent pimples, papules, pustules, or even cysts and scars on the face can severely affect appearance and mental health. The occurrence of acne is related to various factors, and treatment must consider all aspects of the disease, choosing the optimal plan to achieve the goal of cure and beauty.

II. Pathogenesis:

Acne can be divided into several types: common acne, conglobate acne, scar acne, blood rash acne, necrotic acne, premenstrual acne, and juvenile acne. The mechanism involves excessive keratinization at the follicular opening, leading to blockage and accumulation of keratin and sebum forming comedones (sebaceous plugs). Inflammation around the comedones leads to papules, pustules, nodules, and deeper damage results in scarring.

(1) Main Factors: Endocrine factors such as increased androgens causing hyperfunction of sebaceous glands resulting in more sebum secretion, abnormal keratinization of the pilosebaceous duct, narrowing of the duct diameter. When the epithelial cells shed from the follicle wall mix with sebum, they clog the follicle opening, forming comedones. The role of microorganisms within the follicle, such as Propionibacterium acnes, Malassezia ovale, and Staphylococcus aureus (Demodex mites are not the primary cause of acne but act as opportunistic pathogens).

(2) Related Factors: Genetics, diet (high fat, sugar, cocoa, etc.), improper cosmetics and topical hormone medications, emotional stress, and certain chemical factors such as mineral oil, iodine, bromine, lithium, and some drugs like isoniazid can trigger and worsen acne.

III. Clinical Characteristics:

(1) Common Acne: Lesions primarily consist of papules, blackheads, pustules, and dark red nodules. The damage is polymorphic and can both trigger and worsen acne.

(2) Conglobate Acne: Besides having comedones, papules, and pustules, it also features nodules, cysts, sinuses, and scars, making it one of the most important types of acne. If improperly handled, these types of acne can leave punctate atrophic scars.

IV. Treatment:

Principle: Actively prevent, combine internal and external treatments, and comprehensively treat.

(1) General Therapy:

1. Diet: Avoid eating fragrant, sweet, spicy, and greasy foods and drinking alcohol. Drink more water, eat more fresh vegetables and fruits, and maintain regular bowel movements (as constipation can exacerbate acne).

2. Cleansing: Wash your face twice daily with warm water (about 40°C) or weakly acidic soap. Sulfur medicinal soap is better. This helps dissolve and reduce sebum secretion, clear excess oils from the skin surface, remove pore-blocking substances, and facilitate the external flow of sebum.

3. Cosmetics: Avoid using oily cosmetics (oil-in-water emulsions), avoid long-term use of corticosteroid drugs, and choose gel-based, water-soluble cosmetics containing cooling agents.

4. Adjust emotions, live a regular life, and avoid excessive stress in work and study.

5. Avoid squeezing comedones and papules with your hands.

(2) Internal Therapy:

Adopt anti-inflammatory, bactericidal measures, counteract androgens, and correct follicular hyperkeratosis. For example, drugs that inhibit microorganisms within the follicle, such as clindamycin, sulfonamides, dapsone, etc., have shown efficacy in treating acne. A significant issue with antibiotic therapy is the emergence and increasing number of drug-resistant bacterial strains. Another issue is the lengthy course of treatment, generally requiring 3-6 months. Shorter courses may affect therapeutic outcomes. Counteracting androgens such as spironolactone, cimetidine, diethylstilbestrol, etc., can easily lead to endocrine disorders, feminization in males, irregular menstruation in females, etc., and should not be used routinely but only under medical supervision.
