998-yuan knock-off phone passes itself off as satellite phone, claiming that it is free to make a call

by a9107857 on 2009-11-22 22:33:57

Since late September, multiple satellite TV channels have aired a shopping advertisement for the "OBEE (Chinese name 'Oubi') satellite phone." The ad claims that this phone uses the latest American aerospace communication technology and offers free calls and video calls for life. Yesterday, CCTV aired a report titled "Unveiling the Lies of OBEE Satellite Phone," revealing the true face of this so-called "most incredible phone." Although reporters in Nanjing did not find this "satellite phone" or its mold in some large mobile phone wholesale markets yesterday, they learned during interviews that the Nanjing Consumers' Association had indeed received related complaints. Thus, the Consumers' Association and merchants urge consumers to "open their eyes" and not easily believe such direct-response television sales.

The "Most Incredible Phone" in History

Receive satellite signals, make calls for free?

Since late September, many provincial satellite TVs across the country have broadcast an advertisement for the "OBEE satellite phone." Yesterday, the reporter found this video online. Two hosts introduced this phone, named "Oubi" in Chinese, with extremely exaggerated tones. Allegedly, this phone uses the latest American aerospace communication technology, directly transmitting signals via communication satellites in space. Both regular calls and video calls can enjoy lifetime free calls, available for only 998 yuan per unit, claiming to provide only one hundred units per day. Is there really such a magical phone? Recently, CCTV Channel 2's program "Consumer Advocacy" aired a report, through two consumers who purchased the "OBEE163" model phone, step by step uncovering the false face of the "satellite phone": It turns out that the claim of "making calls for free" is impossible to achieve. As for the video call function demonstrated by the two hosts, it turned out to be astonishingly expensive.

Mr. Zhang and Mr. Li both ordered due to the promise of "free calls." After Mr. Zhang received the phone and opened the manual, he found no mention of "American satellite communication." Upon turning on the phone, he was even more confused— the phone prompted him to insert a SIM card. Since it's a satellite phone, why does it need an operator's card? The staff explained that the SIM card is used for identity recognition and must be inserted to receive satellite signals. After checking his balance, Mr. Zhang then used the video call. Clicking into the video call menu, the screen only showed options like "online chat buddies," all filled with strangers' numbers. Mr. Zhang couldn't dial any number, and when he randomly clicked one for a video chat, he found there was no image of himself— it turned out the phone lacked a front-facing camera, meaning video calls could only be made using the rear camera. This meant video calls were essentially unachievable. Mr. Zhang checked his bill and found increased GPRS data usage.

Mr. Li was also frustrated; he made a long-distance call lasting over twenty minutes, but upon checking his bill, he found he lost over ten yuan. Mr. Zhang encountered the same issue. They realized then that the claim of "free calls" was all a scam. Not only were the calls not free, but Mr. Zhang's bill even doubled. Originally, the phone contained many traps: within the SMS menu were options like jokes and pictures. Accidentally pressing them would result in receiving numerous charged messages, typically costing one or two yuan each.

Could a satellite phone really cost 998 yuan?

Some satellite phone dealers claimed that a genuine satellite phone usually costs between five thousand to ten thousand yuan, with very high call rates. The lowest rate equates to 1.7 yuan per minute in RMB. For 998 yuan, it's impossible to buy a satellite phone, and the idea of using a China Mobile or China Unicom SIM card to receive satellite signals is completely absurd.

What kind of phone carries so many lies? A CCTV reporter checked on the Telecommunication Equipment Network Management website and found that this phone is a GSM dual-band GPRS digital mobile phone, with absolutely no mention of "satellite phone." The CCTV reporter then located the producer of this phone, Guizhou Zhenhua Oubi Communication Co., Ltd. Manager Wu was baffled by the description of this "powerful" phone, stating it was done by national distributors without their knowledge. The reporter then approached Shenzhen Hengtaitech Co., Ltd., the national distributor, but they denied everything, telling the reporter to contact Zhenhua again. At this point, Manager Wu left after expressing "strangeness," while an engineer surnamed Chen complained that Zhenhua was "an unknowing victim," stating their "OBEE163" phone wasn't even produced yet, and the phone in the advertisement was counterfeit, done by a certain distributor, whom they planned to sue but refused to disclose any information about.

If it has nothing to do with Zhenhua, then why does the complaint hotline on the phone packaging match Zhenhua's? Moreover, when Mr. Li called to inquire, the service personnel claimed it was their product and provided a dedicated repair number for the phone. Simultaneously, the reporter noticed that the application entity listed on the Telecommunication Equipment Network Management website was China Zhenhua (Group) Science and Technology Co., Ltd.

Reporter Investigation

Nanjing Market Has Not Seen Such a "Satellite Phone"

Yesterday afternoon, the reporter visited several large mobile phone wholesale markets in Nanjing. Chao时空 Communication Plaza on Danfeng Street is reputedly "Jiangsu's largest mobile phone wholesale market," but after circling around the mall, the reporter didn't see this phone, and many merchants even said, "I've never heard of this brand." When asked if there are phones that use satellites to achieve "free calls," the merchants all surprisingly replied, "Impossible!" Some joked that if it were true, no one would buy SIM cards, just phones—"If that happened, how would operators survive?" Many citizens also said they hadn't heard of it and wouldn't buy such a phone.

In Zhuhai Road Mobile Phone City, two or three merchants displayed several models of OBEE phones. Miss Li told the reporter that she sells OBEE9518, a model from last year, "I only sell Zhenhua's this model." The reporter saw that this phone's packaging lists functions like "3 million pixels, extra-large screen, dual-SIM dual-standby," with no mention of "satellite." She said, "Zhenhua only produces ordinary phones, with no special functions." Angrily, she added, "Those TV ads are all scams! Zhenhua hasn't produced such a phone, and I've never heard the manufacturer mention it." She said Zhenhua is a legitimate factory producing "five-code machines," referring to phones that pass national telecommunications network access permits, allowing them to be sold in malls with good after-sales services.

Merchants Cry Out Against Deceptive TV Sales

"If there really was such a phone, I'd definitely buy a hundred units!" A merchant selling phones laughed, believing TV shopping exaggerates. "TV shopping these days is truly harmful to consumers!" Ms. Wang nearby said, a customer of hers fell for a counterfeit Apple phone advertisement, "It was 'spend 399 yuan and get a 399 yuan big gift package,' but when they bought it, it turned out to be even worse than a counterfeit!" That customer said they would never trust TV or online advertisements again.

Several merchants said they are experienced and can tell right away that the phones shown on TV shopping are fake. "Some features are simply impossible to achieve, like the satellite reception feature you mentioned—ordinary phones can't do that." Additionally, the prices on TV are too cheap—if the phone isn't available on the market, then the likelihood of the phone being false increases.

They said that those usually deceived are middle-aged and elderly people or those greedy for small benefits, most people remain relatively rational. Nevertheless, they hope citizens don't easily believe TV shopping advertisements. On the other hand, they hope TV stations reform and ensure the authenticity of the advertisements aired. "After all, earning money is hard for common people!" One merchant lamented.

Online Sales Still Exist, Business is Good

The reporter searched Taobao, Baidu Youa, and other shopping websites, finding quite a few stores still selling OBEE "satellite phones." A store called "Fengyun Digital Mall" made a commitment of "truthful descriptions," yet it also proclaimed, "OBEE satellite phone, no need for operators, using American aviation technology (space communication technology) to transmit signals via communication satellites, making calls anywhere in the world costs nothing. Video chats and calls cost nothing. 24-hour domestic and international long-distance calls cost nothing. Can freely enter any video chat room and chat with handsome men and beautiful women from all over the country."

Of course, some stores used evasive tactics, calling the phone a "satellite phone" but not introducing any performance parameters on the webpage, leaving consumers clueless when shopping. Only a few stores adopted relatively honest methods, using TV advertising slogans while adding disclaimers: "The satellite phone function is purely fictional. Measured tests show that the free call function requires GPRS support to achieve internet phone functionality, needing operator support and generating GPRS data charges." They also noted that the measured pixel count was less than 5 million.

The reporter noticed the sales records of many stores, showing significant sales volume for this phone, almost every store having shipments, with one store selling six units in two days. With a large number of stores selling OBEE phones online, the total sales volume suggests a considerable number of deceived consumers.

■ Reminder

Relevant departments have received multiple complaints

Yesterday afternoon, the reporter consulted with the staff of the Nanjing Administration for Industry and Commerce Consumer Complaint Reporting Hotline 12315. Another complaint about OBEE phones was heard. Over a month ago, citizen Mr. Li saw an OBEE phone advertisement aired on TV at night. Initially, he didn't believe there could be such a "free call" miracle. But after watching it for a few days, he was convinced by the host's persuasive promotion, "It sounds so good, and several provincial TV stations are broadcasting this ad, maybe it's really free?" He decided to call and inquire. A female staff member answered his call and informed him that free calls were only possible between phones of the same model. Thus, Mr. Li ordered two units, but upon receiving the phones, he discovered the situation wasn't as described in the advertisement. Later, Mr. Li repeatedly called the original shopping hotline, but they pushed back, saying they were only responsible for sales, not after-sales service, and the problem remained unresolved.

Subsequently, the reporter learned from the Nanjing Consumers' Association Complaint Handling Center that they had already received multiple complaints about OBEE "satellite phones." A staff member surnamed Yang told the reporter that all complaining consumers believed the TV advertisements and purchased through telephone shopping. After receiving the phones and making payments, they realized they had been deceived. "This type of phone merely replaces ordinary call fees with GPRS data fees. If it's video calls, the GPRS data consumption is huge, and it's actually quite expensive!" He admitted that since the manufacturer is not in Nanjing and sales are conducted via telephone direct sales, with payment upon delivery handled by courier companies, the Consumers' Association finds it difficult to regulate such fraudulent behavior.

The staff of the Consumers' Association frankly stated that telephone direct sales currently have many problems. Consumers should be especially cautious when shopping via this method. Upon delivery, don't rush to pay. Instead, first open the packaging to confirm the product has the advertised features before paying. "If they don't allow you to inspect it, nine times out of ten, there's something fishy, and you shouldn't accept or pay for the goods." At the same time, he emphasized that advertisements like "free calls" should not be trusted, as there's no such thing as a free lunch.

Currently, relevant departments have begun investigating this issue.
