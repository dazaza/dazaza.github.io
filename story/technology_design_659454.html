It is worth noting that not all patients with type 1 diabetes have obvious clinical symptoms, meaning that those without clinical symptoms of type 1 diabetes are not necessarily free from the disease. Some people are unaware of type 1 diabetes and, despite having its clinical symptoms, fail to recognize them. For example, they might consider increased thirst and hunger as signs of good health ("able to eat and drink well means good health"), or attribute weight loss to the belief that "wealth cannot buy old-age slimness." These situations can easily lead to missed diagnoses or even delayed treatment. Foreign studies have found that some patients with type 1 diabetes may experience a gap of 7 to 10 years between onset and definitive diagnosis. In other words, many patients with type 1 diabetes unknowingly suffer from the disease for years before receiving a clear diagnosis, a situation particularly common among patients with type 2 diabetes, which warrants extra caution.

When your pancreas completely stops producing insulin or produces insufficient amounts to meet your body's needs, you develop type 1 diabetes. The result of insulin deficiency is reduced glucose uptake by cells, preventing them from obtaining the energy they need, and also reducing the liver's absorption and storage of glucose. Another consequence is abnormally elevated levels of glucose in your blood, which severely often leads to acid-base imbalance.

The typical clinical symptoms of late-stage type 1 diabetes include the "three more and one less" - increased thirst, increased appetite, increased urination, and weight reduction (weight loss or emaciation), along with fatigue and weakness. Men may experience sexual dysfunction, and women may experience amenorrhea. In the late stages of severe cases, dehydration, malnutrition, secondary infections, and complications such as cardiovascular, neurological, renal, and ocular issues may occur, leading to various physical signs. The liver may swell.

Not all patients with type 1 diabetes exhibit clinical symptoms at the beginning, meaning that not all early cases of type 1 diabetes present with clinical symptoms, a situation frequently seen in patients with type 2 diabetes.

What other clinical symptoms are there in late-stage type 1 diabetes? Type 1 diabetes is a common disease, a chronic genetic tendency endocrine metabolic disorder. Its basic pathological physiology involves absolute or relative insulin secretion insufficiency, leading to disorders in sugar, fat, and protein metabolism.

In the first phase of elderly type 1 diabetes: characterized by glomerular hyperfiltration and mild kidney enlargement. It manifests as an increase in GFR, increased plasma flow in glomerular capillaries, and elevated capillary pressure among hemodynamic changes. GFR increases by 25% to 40%, reaching up to 150ml/min, and kidney volume increases by about 25%, but there are no significant histopathological changes or any clinical symptoms. Current general clinical diagnostic methods cannot detect this, making it the early stage of type 1 diabetes.

In the third phase of elderly type 1 diabetes: it manifests as persistent increased urinary albumin excretion (UAE) (20-200g/min), highly selective proteinuria, known as early nephropathy or early DN. In this phase, GFR gradually returns to approximately normal levels, and blood pressure may slightly increase but does not reach hypertensive levels. In early DN, the increase in GFR and plasma flow has some connection to the state of blood sugar control, and both can decrease after blood sugar control. Some have found that a high-protein diet can increase GFR, but limiting protein intake can lower GFR.
