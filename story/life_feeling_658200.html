The fallen flowers surround a lone man, while swallows fly in pairs in the drizzle.

by zhouzhou56 on 2012-02-02 22:35:12

A lonely man amidst fallen flowers, swallows flying in pairs in a drizzle

After several days of scorching weather with temperatures reaching 30 degrees, Guiyang finally saw some rain. Undoubtedly, this brought a cooling respite to the stifling heat, refreshing the air and invigorating one's spirit. Before dawn, birds were already chirping incessantly outside my window. Opening the window, a fresh scent of soil mixed with fallen flowers greeted me. Beneath the gardenia tree, petals scattered on the ground after being beaten by the rain. Suddenly, a line from Yan Xiaoshan's "Lín Jiāng Xiān" came to mind: "A lonely man amidst fallen flowers, swallows flying in pairs in a drizzle." I have always been fond of this line. Yan Xiaoshan was the seventh son of Yan Shu, a renowned Ci poet of the Northern Song Dynasty who held the position of Chancellor. Known as Little Yan, he, like his father Yan Shu, was a staunch advocate of Xiaoling (short Ci poems). After Yan Xiaoshan, the art of Xiaoling gradually disappeared.

An Yiru has already provided a clear portrayal of Yan Xiaoshan. For such a talented individual, capable of mastering Xiaoling to such an exquisite degree, it is truly enviable. Yan Xiaoshan experienced significant ups and downs in life. From a young age, he was accustomed to luxury and wealth amidst silk and powder. His father, Yan Shu, held the official title of Academician of the Jixian Hall and passed away at the age of 65 in the second year of Emperor Renzong's Zhihe era. He was also a famous Ci poet. Following his father's death, his family fortunes began to decline. As they say, once a person leaves, their tea grows cold. The former bustling household was now left with only embers. Yan Xiaoshan fell from a life of comfort and warmth into the harsh realities of human warmth and indifference.

However, Yan Xiaoshan was a man of deep emotions. His refusal to rely on the powerful was his most prominent characteristic. His writings also "refused to use the language of new scholars," and he had no interest in managing finances, spending millions while leaving his family cold and hungry. Moreover, he suffered unkindly returns for his kindness, "people betrayed him a hundred times without resentment, trusting others completely and never doubting their deceit." This is a perfect depiction of Yan Xiaoshan. Despite his tumultuous life, he maintained an aristocratic demeanor, being both infatuated and proud. In an era obsessed with fame and fortune, Yan Xiaoshan believed that integrity and dignity were more important than anything else, representing the final line of defense he could hold onto.

After waking from a dream, the high tower’s locked gates are seen, the curtains hang low after the wine wears off. Last year's spring regrets return just as before. A lonely man amidst fallen flowers, swallows flying in pairs in a drizzle.

I remember the first time I met Xiao Ping, wearing a double-hearted character embroidered robe. She expressed her longing through the strings of the lute. The bright moon of that time still shines today, having once illuminated her graceful figure returning like a cloud.

The "fallen flowers" corresponding to the "drizzle" paints a picture of beauty tinged with sorrow. On the surface, the poem describes the scenery, but in reality, it conveys the feelings of parting and reluctance. Throughout the entire poem, not a single word uses the characters for "emotion" or "sorrow," yet it perfectly captures the melancholy, helplessness, pain, and farewell. This is the essence and soul of the poem. One can imagine the situation at the time: Yan Xiaoshan, in his destitute state, woke up from a drunken stupor on a dreamlike balcony, whereupon the madam mercilessly urged him to leave. What a poignant farewell it must have been! He stepped out of the pavilion and stood on the flower-lined path, gazing far into the distance at Xiao Ping who was reluctant to part. How inconsiderate the swallows were, flying in pairs through the fine rain curtain, passing overhead. Even the swallows could be in pairs, but what about people?

Through the strings of the lute, she expresses her longing; the bright moon of that time still shines today, having once illuminated her graceful figure returning like a cloud. It vividly portrays Yan Xiaoshan's desolate and solitary appearance. In his memory, the moment they first met, their hearts were already intertwined, leaving a lifelong impression. This is Yan Xiaoshan, immersing himself in poetry and wine, possessing a deeply sincere and passionate awareness of love. This was his way of resisting the obsession with fame and fortune, and those tender, heart-wrenching Xiaoling poems became the sole solace for his soul...
