A New Era of Smartphone Navigation: Windshields Turned into Displays

by anonymous on 2013-09-12 12:02:44

Projecting navigation onto the windshield can greatly reduce the accident rate.

Introduction: The September 10th printed edition of The Wall Street Journal published an article titled "Smartphone Navigation Without Looking Down," which reviewed that despite some imperfections, Garmin's Head-Up Display (HUD) system, which projects navigation information directly onto the windshield, can significantly lower the probability of traffic accidents occurring.

The following is the full text of the article:

Overall Evaluation

With smartphone navigation apps like Google Maps, we no longer feel lost when arriving at unfamiliar places. However, while driving, even glancing down at the phone screen for a second may lead to safety hazards.

To reduce this risk, GPS manufacturer Garmin has introduced its HUD system. This dashboard accessory connects directly via Bluetooth with iPhone, Android, or Windows Phone devices and projects navigation information from Garmin StreetPilot or Navigon smartphone applications onto the windshield.

Garmin believes that since this system displays information within the driver’s line of sight, it is safer than using a smartphone for navigation.

After using it for a week, I found this system indeed does not easily distract attention. Although I usually rely on the voice navigation feature of Google Maps, I often personally click on my iPhone to confirm the next turning point. With Garmin's HUD system, this information is immediately displayed right in front of me, so I check my phone less often, allowing me to focus more on the road. Garmin's app also provides precise navigation information.

Nevertheless, there are still some shortcomings with this system. First, fixing the reflective film on the windshield is troublesome, and since Garmin's HUD system can only use the vehicle's power supply, an unsightly power cable must be placed under the dashboard.

This accessory costs $150 and requires one of Garmin's navigation apps, each of which costs an additional $50. Even so, it is cheaper than many head-up systems equipped in high-end vehicles today. However, this is still an extra expense, requiring the purchase of another device for your car. Nonetheless, if you prioritize safety, Garmin's HUD system is certainly worth considering.

Installation Process

Garmin's HUD system is about the size of a small GPS device, resembling a large BP pager with a rectangular screen, which can be mounted directly above the dashboard via an adjustable bracket based on the shape of the dashboard. I placed the Garmin HUD system at the bottom of the car's windshield, almost just to the right of the steering wheel. Since it is not permanently fixed, it can be used in different cars.

I dislike that Garmin's system can only use the vehicle's power source because careful placement is required to avoid the power cord blocking other control buttons on the dashboard. A "wireless" design through charging would have been better. Additionally, the adapter comes with a USB port to charge smartphones.

Garmin's HUD system offers two projection options. One involves attaching a transparent film to the windshield, while the other uses a reflective lens attached to the device. Attaching the transparent film is more troublesome; first, water needs to be sprayed on the inside of the windshield, then the film must be fixed, and finally, air bubbles should be removed using a credit card. If the transparent block is moved, it cannot be reused. The company's website sells these films separately for $10 each.

I chose the reflective lens because it was more convenient. I simply attached it above the device to use it. The only downside is that due to the angle, the position where Garmin's HUD system can be fixed on the dashboard might be limited depending on the dashboard's shape.

It is important to note that Garmin's HUD system itself is not a GPS device; it needs to connect with a smartphone and display information from Garmin or Navigon navigation apps. Unfortunately, it is incompatible with Google Maps, Apple Maps, or third-party apps.

Usage Process

I downloaded the Garmin StreetPilot app on my iPhone 5, which includes maps of North America, and connected it via Bluetooth to Garmin's HUD system in less than a minute. Afterward, I could play the voice navigation instructions of this app through my car's audio system using the auxiliary earplugs.

I tested Garmin's HUD system in many areas of San Francisco and Santa Cruz. After entering the address, the system would indicate the initial direction with a large arrow, showing the distance to the next turn, estimated arrival time, speed limit, and my current speed. If you exceed the speed limit, it will display a red warning symbol.

When driving on highways, Garmin's HUD system displays small arrows at the bottom of the projection area to indicate the number of lanes and reminds you which lane to use before the next turn. Garmin's HUD system automatically adjusts the brightness of the projection, so whether it is day or night, the content projected is clearly visible.

I think its voice navigation is not as natural as Google Maps and is a bit slow.

However, overall, I really like Garmin's HUD system, which displays all the information needed to reach your destination and makes it easier to concentrate compared to using just a smartphone, thereby reducing the likelihood of accidents.
