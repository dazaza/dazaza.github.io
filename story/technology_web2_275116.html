Couple Builds Public Welfare Website to Help Abducted Children; 14,000 Volunteers Registered

by mingdaolin on 2009-11-24 11:05:07

On October 10, 2009, in Fuzhou, parents from Guizhou looking for their child posted the child's photo on the back of their clothing.

Reporter Tian Guolei

In the morning of May 19, 2009, a little girl suddenly knelt in front of her, holding a large iron basin and stretching it out to her. On her back was a tightly tied rope, the other end of which was attached to a wooden cart where a young man with one leg sat and sang.

This was the scene that appeared before Song Ting that day.

Song Ting is a mother of a two-year-old child and also a member of the "Baby Come Home" volunteer organization in Fuzhou.

Song Ting said the face of despair of the little girl often flashes in her mind. "I feel very guilty, that little girl is likely to have been trafficked and then controlled to beg. I should have called the police at that time."

The head of the Fujian "Baby Come Home" volunteer organization, Xue Jian, told the reporter from China Youth Daily that there are currently more than 200 volunteers like Song Ting across Fujian province. They use their spare time to discover and collect information about wandering or trafficked children and send the information to the local public security bureau and the database established by the "Baby Come Home" reunion website.

The founder of the "Baby Come Home" volunteer organization, Zhang Baoyan, recently introduced to the reporter from China Youth Daily that currently there are over 14,000 registered volunteers on the "Baby Come Home" platform, spread across most provinces in the country.

The only two compromises

Zhang Baoyan's son was once "trafficked" when he was five years old, after which she began paying attention to trafficked children.

One day in 1992, Zhang Baoyan's mother took her child to the market and while engrossed in shopping, she turned around and found the child missing. Her mother hurriedly asked others to help search in the market but could not find him.

"When my mom ran to my workplace and told me the child was gone, I was completely stunned, feeling as if the sky was falling," Zhang Baoyan said.

Fortunately, Zhang Baoyan found her son that night. Zhang Baoyan, who thoroughly understood the anxious, painful, and desperate feelings of parents who lost their children, started paying attention to children getting lost or being trafficked and provided assistance to parents searching for their children.

Initially, the help provided by Zhang Baoyan mainly involved mobilizing her relatives and friends to inquire about relevant information about trafficked children, helping post missing person notices, or reporting to the police station. Later, she realized this way was too primitive, not only wasting manpower and financial resources, but also achieving unsatisfactory results.

Thus, she began contemplating a "faster and more convenient way to search."

By chance, Zhang Baoyan discovered some websites specifically posting missing person information online. This discovery made Zhang Baoyan feel extremely delighted. By displaying missing person information through the network and comparing and verifying them, the efficiency of finding children can be greatly improved.

However, she found that almost all the missing person websites charged fees for posting information at that time. "Many parents have been searching for their children for years, and their economic situation has already become quite difficult. Even making them pay five or six hundred yuan is not easy at this time."

She discussed with her husband, who worked in the network technology department of Tongliao Normal College, and decided to build a non-profit website for searching for children.

On April 30, 2007, the non-profit website "Baby Come Home" was born.

At first, the idea of the website was very simple, just providing a place for parents of trafficked children to post photos, feature descriptions, etc., of their children online. Gradually, some parents who frequently traveled across the country searching for their children voluntarily posted other information about trafficked children they obtained during their search process on the website for sharing.

Later, some ordinary netizens who browsed the "Baby Come Home" website began actively posting information about abandoned, wandering, and begging children they encountered at welfare institutions or on the streets.

On January 18, 2008, the "Baby Come Home" volunteer organization was registered and officially established at the Civil Affairs Bureau of Tonghua City, Jilin Province, Zhang Baoyan's hometown. Zhang Baoyan now became a full-time volunteer. Every day, she logs online early in the morning around six or seven o'clock, maintaining the website content and managing nearly 70 volunteer groups across the country, collecting news. When she discovers information that needs help, she immediately contacts the other party, and in case of emergency, she will immediately post it in various groups across the country.

Zhang Baoyan has always adhered to the non-profit nature of the website. So far, there have been only two commercial links on the "Baby Come Home" website: one is a certain commercial website that opened a hotline for "Baby Come Home", allowing parents and volunteers from all over the country to contact Zhang Baoyan through this hotline without paying long-distance call fees; the other is the website of a certain biological food company in Shandong, which produced hundreds of thousands of small cards printed with portraits and information of missing children and stuck them on the bottles of beverages produced by the company.

Zhang Baoyan said, these were her only two compromises.

"Cart Party", "Sleeping Party", "Help-seeking Party"

Mentioning the volunteer "Zaizi", most volunteers in Fujian would give him a thumbs up.

"Zaizi" is the online name of volunteer Mr. Cao in "Baby Come Home". "Zaizi" is a young man from Sichuan who once assisted the Ministry of Public Security and the State Administration for Industry and Commerce in dismantling a major pyramid selling group. Now, he is a volunteer for "Baby Come Home". In August 2009, after searching for clues about a trafficked child online, he went alone to Zhangzhou to assist the local police in rescuing the child. At that time, he told a local media in Fujian, "This is the 41st trafficked child I have participated in rescuing."

In fact, most volunteers are not "lone heroes" like "Zaizi", but rather urban white-collar workers like Song Ting. Due to limited time and energy, they mainly focus their efforts on assisting street-wandering and begging children.

"Rescuing trafficked children, volunteers face ruthless human traffickers, requiring strong technical skills and posing great dangers, whereas assisting street-wandering and begging children is relatively easier." Zhang Baoyan told reporters from China Youth Daily that the latter is currently the action focus of "Baby Come Home" volunteers.

Street-wandering and begging children come in various forms. Through long-term observation, volunteers in Fuzhou categorized the images of street-wandering and begging children into three types:

First is the "Cart Party": A child dragging a cart with a large speaker bound on it and a "disabled" adult sitting on it singing. The adult is the supervisor, coercing and instructing the child pulling the cart to beg for money.

Second is the "Sleeping Party": In bustling areas of the city, an adult holds a sleeping child to evoke sympathy from passersby and beg for alms.

Third is the "Help-seeking Party": An adult kneels on the street with several children, directing the children to continuously kowtow and cry to beg for alms.

When encountering such situations, volunteers generally observe nearby first, checking if there are any surveillance cameras, and tailing. Then, they tentatively ask the begging adults some questions, such as where their home is, the names and ages of the children, etc.

More volunteers, however, gather and compare various online information to help children return home. On September 11, "Little Fairy" (using her online name per her request), a Beijing "Baby Come Home" volunteer, saw the missing child Shi Xiaoyi's family seeking information on the forum of the "Baby Come Home" website. The information showed that Shi Xiaoyi got separated from his family in Beijing in 2005.

Subsequently, "Little Fairy" reposted the information to the Beijing "Baby Come Home" volunteer QQ group. That day, a volunteer in the group responded, saying that the Beijing Minor Protection Center had posted a missing child notice for a child named "Zhang Xiaoyi" who got separated from his family in Beijing on the "Baby Come Home" forum.

"Little Fairy" mobilized volunteers to find "Zhang Xiaoyi's" post among the vast number of posts while contacting the Beijing Minor Protection Center to report the situation and sending the photo of "Zhang Xiaoyi" obtained from the center and the photo in the missing child notice of "Shi Xiaoyi" to the "Baby Come Home" volunteer information comparison group. The feedback from the comparison group was: It is highly likely to be the same person.

On September 14, "Little Fairy" finally contacted the seeker. "I immediately recognized this 'Zhang Xiaoyi' as my son," said Shi Xinwang, a Henan man who begged on the streets and slept rough in Beijing to look for his son and once climbed onto a bridge in front of Beijing Railway Station to contemplate ending his life. He was excited.

At 11 a.m. on September 16, Shi Xinwang met his son, whom he had been separated from for four years, at the Beijing Minor Protection Center. "As soon as he saw me, he ran over and called me Dad. After four years apart, under the care of the center, he has grown taller, and I am very grateful to them," Shi Xinwang said.

Shi Xinwang said he still has one regret: He has never met the volunteers who helped him find his child, and he does not even know the real name of the volunteer "Little Fairy." "They only let me call them 'volunteers,' they are kind-hearted people," Shi Xinwang said.

"Our value is not reflected by doing something earth-shattering. If there are no wandering and begging children on the streets, we would rather 'retire' from the volunteer organization," said Jin Jianhui, a volunteer from Fuzhou.

Volunteer actions encounter difficulties

In Zhang Baoyan's view, the solid foundation for bringing more trafficked children home lies in the volunteers collecting sufficient and reliable information. However, sufficient and reliable information means that volunteers face more danger, misunderstanding, and criticism during their actions.

In June 2009, Huang Youming, a "Baby Come Home" volunteer from Xiamen, together with more than ten local volunteers, searched for the traces of the "cart parties" within the city. After tracking for a period, they found that every night at around 11 p.m., tricycles would transport the scattered "cart parties" across Xiamen to a parking lot in Malong. Two trucks with Anhui license plates, one large and one small, were parked in the parking lot. At night, the "cart parties" controlling more than ten children rested inside the truck cabins. In the early hours of the morning, the children would again be transported by tricycle to various bustling streets in Xiamen.

"The relationship between the adults on the carts and the children pulling the carts is highly suspicious. No parent would be so heartless as to make their own child pull a heavy cart in the scorching sun and rain to beg," said Huang Youming. After investigating the regular gathering and dispersal of the "cart parties," Huang Youming believed that these people were "organized, mobile professional beggars, and the children were likely under control."

The tracking process constantly faced danger. One day, a volunteer found a truck often parked at the Malong parking lot near the Shigu Mountain Overpass. When the volunteer approached to lift the rear curtain of the truck, several big men suddenly stood up inside the truck and approached the volunteer.

To facilitate communication among volunteers, the Fujian "Baby Come Home" volunteers created a QQ group, but joining this QQ group required very strict verification and checks. "We must ensure that everyone in the group is a volunteer so that our exchanged information won't be leaked," said Xue Jian. During operations, volunteers only referred to each other by their online names. Sometimes, they would disguise themselves as couples or strangers to monitor and track the begging children.

Besides unpredictable dangers, volunteers would also suffer misunderstandings and criticisms due to their inability to provide conclusive evidence.

On the afternoon of May 24, 2009, Jin Jianhui discovered a "sleeping party" in front of Dabai Department Store in Fuzhou: two boys lying on a blanket, asleep, with a middle-aged woman kneeling to beg.

After observing for a while, Jin Jianhui dialed 110. About 10 minutes later, officers from Dongjie Police Station in Gulou District arrived at the scene and took the woman and the children to the station.

At Dongjie Police Station, the officer questioned the woman about where the children came from and what their names were. The woman only replied that she was "from Guizhou" and shook her head, indicating that she didn't understand or couldn't speak Mandarin.

Jin Jianhui invited a Guizhou native working in Fuzhou via a live radio broadcast to act as a translator, but still made no progress.

At 10 p.m., the chief of Dongjie Police Station personally came to question the situation and "began to show displeasure." The on-duty officer started accusing Jin Jianhui of reporting without concrete evidence, blindly suspecting the woman of being a trafficker, and abusing police resources.

An hour later, Dongjie Police Station decided to release the woman and demanded that Jin Jianhui apologize to the woman.

"How could a woman who doesn't speak or understand Mandarin come from Guizhou to Fuzhou?" Jin Jianhui still believed that the woman was suspicious. He said that later, he saw the woman begging with two children at a crossroads on Guangda Road, and "those two children were not the ones brought to the police station last time."

"If I encounter such a situation again, I will still report it. Finding one case is saving one child's happiness," said Jin Jianhui.
