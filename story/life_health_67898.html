WHO: Tests show EV71 virus unchanged, no increased toxicity

by kk2040 on 2008-05-10 15:04:20

Dr. Hans Troedsson, WHO Representative in China, revealed at a joint press conference held by the Ministry of Health and WHO office in China that all laboratory tests have proven that "the EV71 enterovirus which caused the outbreak in Fuyang, Anhui has not mutated at all, and its genotype is still the same as previous years." This means that the virus's toxicity has also not increased.

Patients with typical symptoms do not need to be hospitalized

The other day, Dr. He Jianfeng, Deputy Director of the Epidemic Prevention Institute of Guangdong Provincial Center for Disease Control and Prevention pointed out that "parents should differentiate between common hand-foot-mouth disease and hand-foot-mouth disease caused by EV71". Currently, the vast majority of patients in Guangdong are suffering from common hand-foot-mouth disease, mild cases that are not difficult to treat. Patients can be admitted to hospitals above the second level, and generally do not need to be hospitalized for observation. Academician Zhong Nanshan recently also pointed out that hand-foot-mouth disease does not equal EV71; other 20 types of enteroviruses can also cause hand-foot-mouth disease. For those who compare EV71 with SARS, Academician Zhong emphasized that they are completely different. The pathogen of "SARS" was unclear when it first appeared, while the hand-foot-mouth disease caused by EV71 virus has always been present with a clear pathogen. The Ministry of Health has also developed detailed treatment guidelines. He stated that although there is no special drug for hand-foot-mouth disease so far, the vast majority of cases can be cured.

Which situations require visiting secondary medical institutions or pediatric departments of general hospitals? What symptoms should lead to immediate medical attention or referral to larger hospitals for mild patients? Dr. He Jianfeng said that most patients with typical symptoms of hand-foot-mouth disease are mild cases. Typical symptoms include acute onset, fever, maculopapular rashes and vesicles on palms or soles, and rashes may also appear on buttocks or knees. The rashes are surrounded by inflammatory redness with less fluid in the vesicles; scattered vesicles appear on oral mucosa with obvious pain. Some children may also experience coughing, runny nose, loss of appetite, nausea, vomiting, and headaches. If a child is diagnosed with mild hand-foot-mouth disease, hospitalization is generally not required. "Parents must closely monitor whether their children show signs of worsening conditions," when symptoms such as drowsiness, lethargy, rapid heartbeat (central nervous system symptoms), persistent fever, convulsions of hands and feet, vomiting blood, or coffee-colored foam occur, medical attention must be sought immediately. As long as early detection and early treatment are carried out, most severe cases can recover.

EV71: A cunning "old" virus

EV71 is not a new virus that has just emerged. As early as the mid-1970s, Bulgaria and other places successively experienced outbreaks of EV71 viruses mainly characterized by central nervous system infections. Currently, ribavirin, a commonly used drug for viral diseases, is mainly used against the EV71 virus.
