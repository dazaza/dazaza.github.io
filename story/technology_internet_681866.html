On the Implications of Androgyny Theory for Gender Role Education in China

by lsxzcty on 2012-02-28 15:56:57

**Keywords**: Gender education, Androgyny, Gender roles, Gender stereotypes

**Abstract**: In traditional Chinese gender role education, the stereotypical notions about male and female gender roles inevitably affect children's growth, seriously hindering their development. As an optimal psychological health model, the currently popular androgynous education in the United States can offer many insights for China’s gender education. According to relevant studies, there are far fewer Chinese college students with androgynous personalities compared to those in the United States. Since androgynous personality is gradually formed in a specific social environment, strengthening androgynous education requires the joint efforts of the entire society.

In past gender role education for children, the educational pitfall of gender stereotyping often appears. Gender stereotyping, also known as gender role stereotypes, refers to the motives, values, behaviors, and personality traits that members of society universally recognize as suitable for men or women. This reflects the expectations of culture or subculture regarding the appropriateness of behavior for different genders. When these expectations become rigid and unchanging views, they turn into gender stereotypes. Both sexes jointly construct human society, and society expects men and women to play different roles and have different behavioral patterns, forming relatively fixed views on the psychological characteristics and activities that men and women should possess. However, gender stereotypes influence children from a young age, prompting them to establish dogmas related to gender-appropriate behavior. As a widespread social psychological phenomenon, gender stereotypes permeate all aspects of social life. In China, the reasons for their formation are very complex. [1](p61-63) The outdated norms regarding male and female gender roles inevitably affect children's growth and severely hinder their development. Studies show that neither men nor women benefit from gender stereotyping. With the development of society, traditional gender role concepts should give way to the notion of gender equality.

**Part One**

The currently popular educational philosophy in American gender education— androgynous education—offers us many insights. This educational ideology believes that overly strict and absolute gender stereotypes will limit the intellectual and holistic personal development of children and adolescents. Boys who are too masculine and girls who are too feminine generally develop their intelligence, physical strength, and personalities in a one-sided manner, with lower IQs and EQs. Specifically, this manifests as unsatisfactory academic performance (especially severe bias in certain subjects), lack of imagination and creativity, either lacking independent opinions when facing problems or being overly stubborn, and difficulty in flexibly adapting to the environment. Conversely, boys who possess gentle and meticulous qualities, and girls who possess strong and brave qualities, tend to develop their intelligence, physical strength, and personalities comprehensively. They perform well in both arts and sciences and are often liked by teachers and classmates. After growing up, men and women who embody "the strengths of both genders" hold more advantageous positions in modern competitive society. [2](p55)

This ideal gender role model— androgynous personality—has its English word *androgyny* originating from the Greek words *andro* (man) and *gyne* (woman). In psychology, androgyny refers to an individual who clearly possesses both pronounced male personality traits and pronounced female personality traits, combining toughness and gentleness, decisiveness and meticulousness, and expressing them differently according to the situation. [3](p300) Compared with other single-gender normative trainings, this is an ideal human example that transcends traditional gender classifications and has more positive potential. It allows its possessors to better enjoy equal educational resources during socialization and fully develop their potential. Therefore, it has been valued by experts both domestically and internationally. In the process of pursuing ideal gender-conscious personality models, its connotations continue to evolve and have become a subject of attention and discussion among many scholars in modern times. It provides practical and feasible theoretical basis for promoting educational equity and gender justice.
