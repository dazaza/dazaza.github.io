"We've found the sunny side of life in professional football. It's our responsibility to give back to society and make others a little happier." Bayern Munich president Uli Hoeness places great emphasis on the club's contributions to society. Assisting other professional clubs is just a small part of this; they focus more on their own contributions.

The official name of Bayern Munich includes "e.V.", which stands for a membership-based club that does not aim for profit. However, the stock company controlled by Bayern Munich is a massive economy with over 300 million euros in revenue, which sometimes causes confusion. The German Ministry of Finance has even questioned whether the club is using its "e.V." status to avoid taxes. In reality, Bayern Munich uses the membership fees paid by over 135,000 members and the profits from its holding company to support non-professional projects, which is the basic social responsibility of the club.

Several foundations operate under Bayern Munich's umbrella. They help disabled and seriously ill children find purpose in life and realize their self-worth. The "National Team Disabled Fans Club," established in 2006, has helped over 100 disabled fans attend national team matches. The "Hassanberger Dawn Fund" takes care of more than 80 underprivileged youths, ensuring they are not discriminated against by society. A project caring for children with cancer, founded in 1985, helps sick children receive treatment in Munich and also supports the patients' families. The "Munich Lunch Club" aims to raise essential living supplies for 1700 people.

In this kind of atmosphere within the club, the players have also established their own foundations. Former national goalkeeper Oliver Kahn created a foundation named after himself to guide young people to focus more on their lives. The most famous is Philipp Lahm's foundation, which primarily operates in Africa, inspired by his visit to South Africa before the World Cup.

Before 2010, Lahm visited South Africa with several teammates. He was shocked by the living conditions of local families when he visited a children's village built by the United Nations. The stark contrast between rich and poor left a deep impression on Lahm. When he learned about the prevalence of AIDS in the area, Lahm, who had been warmly welcomed, decided to give something back.

Lahm's foundation provides running shoes to Ethiopian athletes known for producing long-distance runners, establishes a football center in Johannesburg, South Africa, where children can play, study, and gather. Their project in Cape Town attracts children away from drugs, teaching them to play football and reducing the local crime rate. Lahm even established a football team there that entered the South African first division. In Mozambique, Lahm's foundation collaborates with the Bavarian Football Association to build a youth football system. With extensive experience, Lahm's foundation returned to Germany to launch new projects encouraging more girls to play football and helping immigrant families integrate into local society.
