(1) Lotus in the Breeze at Crooked Courtyard

Lotus in the Breeze at Crooked Courtyard

Lotus in the Breeze, with the theme of viewing lotus flowers in summer, is the second of the Ten Scenes of West Lake after Spring Dawn at Su Causeway. "Crooked Courtyard" was originally a brewery opened by the Southern Song Dynasty court, located near today's Lingyin Road Hongchun Bridge, adjacent to the then shore of the West Lake. The nearby lake surface was used for cultivating lotus flowers. Every summer, with the gentle breeze blowing, the fragrance of lotus and alcohol drifted everywhere, intoxicating people even without drinking. Southern Song poet Wang Wei wrote a poem praising it: "People seeking refuge from the heat return from Cold Spring, clouds gather on the wharf under the cool evening sky. I love how the scent follows people far away, only after crossing the high bridge do they buy boats." Later, the Crooked Courtyard gradually declined and fell into disrepair. After Emperor Kangxi of the Qing Dynasty selected the Ten Scenes of West Lake, he built the Lotus in the Breeze Scenic Stele Pavilion beside the Su Causeway Cross Bridge. What remains now is merely a small courtyard with a small patch of lotus flowers in front.

The most eye-catching feature of Lotus in the Breeze remains the summer appreciation of lotus flowers. Within the park, large and small lotus ponds cultivate hundreds of varieties of lotus flowers, among which the most charming are those in the Wind Lotus scenic area. Here, the water surface dominates, distributed with famous varieties of lotus such as red lotus, white lotus, double-decked lotus, speckled gold lotus, and twin-lotus, with lush lotus leaves and graceful lotus blossoms. Bridges of various shapes are constructed over the water surface. People walking across the bridges feel as if they are walking among the lotus flowers, with people leaning against the flower posture and flowers reflecting human faces, creating an enchanting relationship between flowers and people.

Autumn Moon on Calm Lake

(2) Autumn Moon on Calm Lake

The scenic area is located at the west end of Bai Causeway, at the southern foot of Solitary Hill, bordering Outer West Lake. In fact, as one of the Ten Scenes of West Lake, there was no fixed scenic spot for Autumn Moon on Calm Lake during the Southern Song Dynasty. This can be seen from the poems written by literati of that time as well as during the Yuan and Ming Dynasties, which mostly express admiration for the scenery from the perspective of returning home by boat at night and appreciating the moon on the boat. For example, Sun Rui's poem from the Southern Song Dynasty contains the lines "The cold moon causes the spring to stop flowing, where does the oar song come from while boating?" During the Ming Dynasty, Hong Zhanzu wrote in his poem: "In autumn, people board boats to admire the ripples, fairy mountain pavilions appear like dust in the mirror." In the famous woodblock prints of the Ten Scenes of West Lake from the Wanli period of the Ming Dynasty, the picture of "Autumn Moon on Calm Lake" still features tourists raising their heads to look at the moon from the lake boats as the main subject.

The current location of the Autumn Moon on Calm Lake scenic spot was actually determined after the 38th year of the Kangxi era. At that time, the existing Dragon King Hall was converted into an imperial book tower. A stone platform protruding into the lake was built in front of the tower, surrounded by railings, with waterside pavilions constructed beside it, hanging the Kangxi imperial inscription "Autumn Moon on Calm Lake" plaque. Later, people also built a scenic stele pavilion out of stone nearby.

After the founding of the People's Republic of China, through re-planning and annual renovations, expansions, and new constructions, this area has formed a narrow strip of lakeside gardens. Planted with seasonal flowers and trees, dotted with artificial mountains and rock formations, pavilions and towers are scattered throughout, making it more suitable for moon viewing, tea tasting, and leisure activities.

The West Lake autumn moonlit nights have been considered beautiful times since ancient times, full of poetic sentiment and picturesque beauty. The high tower of Autumn Moon on Calm Lake overlooks the waves, its elegant windows face the water, the platform is wide, and the view is open. On autumn nights, looking far into the distance from here, you see the bright moon in the sky, the lake and sky as one blue expanse, the golden wind bringing coolness, and the water and moon blending together, making it unclear what night it is. In fact, the beautiful scenery is not limited to autumn or moonlit nights. Qing dynasty scholar Luo Chengxiang once wrote a couplet: "Summer breezes come through the window in winter days, rolling up the curtains reveals the bright moon on the front mountain and the back mountain."

Despite the fact that the Autumn Moon on Calm Lake scenic area may be the smallest among the Ten Scenes of West Lake, people can still explore and experience historical relics and cultural connotations here. As early as the Tang Dynasty, the Lookout Pavilion was built here. During the Southern Song Dynasty, with the construction of the royal Taoist temple Four Saints Extending Auspiciousness Temple on Solitary Hill, the Moon Viewing Pavilion was also built here. These two "lookouts" - "lake lookout" and "moon lookout" - laid the groundwork for the eventual location of Autumn Moon on Calm Lake here. During the Wanli period of the Ming Dynasty, when Chief Eunuch Sun Long spent a huge amount of money to rebuild the old scenes of West Lake, he made major repairs to the Lookout Pavilion.

Literary figure Zhang Dai recorded in "Dream Seeking in West Lake": "The pavilion was beautifully renovated, with an added terrace that could enjoy both the wind and the moon, and also set up seats for music, singing, and drama performances every day. Now it has been changed into the Dragon King Hall."

The Hitian Yibi Building located in the western part of the scenic area was originally a relic from the private villa "Luoyuan" of Jewish tycoon and "adventurer" Hahong at the end of the Qing Dynasty and beginning of the Republic of China. It later became the cradle of China's modern emerging woodcut movement - the Bayi Society. Nowadays, it has been transformed into the Xiling Academy of Painting and Calligraphy, adding a scholarly atmosphere to the scenic beauty of the lake and mountains.
