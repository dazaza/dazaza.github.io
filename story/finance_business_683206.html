Ten Misalignments in College Students' Career Planning

1. Mistaking Ideals for Goals

What is an ideal? In the eyes of college students, this is a very unfamiliar term. As they grow older, under the impact of real-world interests, they appear increasingly mature and have already relegated ideals to the realm of childhood and distanced them from their minds. Even if some people have ideals, they mistakenly equate them with goals.

An ideal should be the ultimate manifestation of the result we are pursuing. In terms of career, professional ideals are more often represented by specific positions, such as if your professional ideal is to become a Human Resources Director, then the positions of HR Specialist and Personnel Manager would be the career goals that help you achieve your professional ideal. However, many college students only focus on their professional ideals without realizing the various stages of career goals, making it impossible to achieve their professional ideals. This is like wanting to build a ten-story building but not wanting to construct the first nine floors when it comes to actual execution. You think it's a waste of time, but while dreaming of the tenth floor, your dream remains unfulfilled.

Therefore, among college students, there are various kinds of professional ideals, such as hoping to become a star, a scientist, the richest person in the world, or even the president. These seemingly difficult-to-achieve ideals are not impossible. After all, no one is born as the richest person in the world or the president (except for a few rare cases, such as in the UK, where inheritance plays a role, but their predecessors started from scratch, and their descendants are just lucky). Therefore, in professional development, the size of your heart determines the size of your stage. However, college students treat professional ideals merely as ideals, and even many people convert ideals into goals. Goals are what we can achieve, which are the phase divisions in the process of achieving our professional ideals. Only by converting grand professional ideals into numerous achievable goals can we ultimately realize our professional ideals. Unfortunately, while many college students shout about wanting to achieve their professional ideals, they fail to convert their ideals into career goals, let alone achieve these converted career goals, resulting in grand professional ideals turning into mere fantasies.

2. Mistaking Means for Ends

We may have had such experiences: We decide to dine at a restaurant to entertain friends visiting us. So, you painstakingly think about what to eat with your friends. You think and calculate, rejecting one idea after another, investing a lot of thought, but you forget the purpose of entertaining your friends. Initially, wasn't it just to foster friendship? Dining is merely a means to express friendship, but you spend much time and energy on the means, which is highly counterproductive!

College students also make this seemingly simple yet very real mistake.

After converting professional ideals into career goals, we need to look at the means available to achieve those goals. But in this process, we mistake the means to achieve our goals as the purpose of doing things. When we choose different operational methods with the mindset of achieving goals, many people end up choosing operational methods just for the sake of choosing them. They forget why we chose the operational methods in the first place, leading to situations where work is done without achieving the goal or even working just for the sake of working. For example, we can bridge the gap in our professional knowledge through learning, but often, many college students during the learning process are simply focused on learning or pondering what, how, and when to learn, putting in great effort. Constantly motivating themselves to study instead of playing has already deviated from the original goal of learning. Considering these unrelated factors has already shifted away from the purpose of learning.

3. Mistaking Pathways for Results

There are many pathways to achieve career goals. These pathways represent the career routes to achieve professional ideals. There are multiple career routes to achieve professional ideals, each composed of different career factors. Although all these routes can lead to professional ideals, they differ in terms of time, timing, and difficulty. It's like knowing how many roads lead to professional ideals is knowledge; but choosing a shortcut to move towards professional ideals is wisdom.

Therefore, once we determine our professional ideals, it's important to analyze our situation comprehensively and summarize different paths. Combining career environment and available resources, we can make the optimal choice of career routes. We must combine our comprehensive factors to choose the most suitable path for ourselves. Choosing the best path is to achieve our career goals faster and ultimately realize our professional ideals.

Only by realizing our professional ideals do we achieve the final results we need. As long as this ultimate result has not been achieved, we cannot slack off. For instance, a student's professional ideal might be to become a sales director. He chooses this promotion trajectory: sales representative, salesperson -> sales supervisor -> regional sales manager -> sales department manager -> sales director. Each stage of this path serves to achieve the result of becoming a sales director. However, after becoming a sales supervisor, some college students lose the awareness and drive to advance to the position of regional sales manager. Consequently, being a sales supervisor becomes their pursued career result, leaving their dream of becoming a sales director unfulfilled.

Although "all roads lead to Rome," we need to find the shortest route to Rome!

4. Mistaking Industry for Position

We often see many college students' resumes stating job intentions like architectural design institutes, construction companies, municipal engineering companies, or companies related to architecture. In fact, they are targeting the architecture industry.

But can they really perform all jobs within the architecture industry? Of course not. Even they themselves don't know what they can do or what they are best at. The final hiring decision depends on specific companies and specific positions. Not having a corporate target is acceptable, but lacking a position target, what can they do, and how well can they perform? Companies hire employees who can handle specific positions. If you don't meet the qualification requirements for the position, how can the company hire you? When college students mistake the industry for the position they want to apply for, it reveals more about their lack of core competitiveness and awareness of forming core competitiveness. Therefore, in job hunting, they place more hope on "casting a wide net to catch big fish," thinking it will increase opportunities. In reality, others won't know what they can do and thus won't give them chances.

From such experiences, we can discover that they fundamentally lack understanding of professional knowledge, even common sense about the professional world. An industry and a position are different concepts. An industry represents the largest national economic factor, while a position refers to the specific role college students aim to serve. In essence, college students' employment is about specific positions. That is to say, if college students understand the content of a specific position and meet its requirements, they can solve the problem of employment. Of course, if you want greater development in this field, you need to better understand the dynamics of the industry and the top companies within it, allowing you to develop stably within the circle.

5. Mistaking Employment for Career Choice

The widely propagated slogan in campuses "employment first, career choice later" misleads college students' career perspectives to a large extent. We all know that a person's career time is limited. We cannot spend too much time choosing careers and delay the time for striving in a suitable career. Simply put, if we summarize the success of a person's career as choosing a suitable career and achieving something in it, then the sum of these two factors equals the total duration of a person's career. As for college students, the issue is that they haven't placed the choice of a suitable career more in university years but transferred it to after graduation. University years can and should completely address this career choice issue, which inadvertently extends the time for college students to achieve career success.

In our limited life journey, realizing professional ideals isn't so much about what you do but more about what you don't do. Only by focusing all your energy, time, and resources on professional ideals can they possibly be realized. In other words, in professional development, knowing when not to act leads to knowing when to act!

After determining professional ideals, college students will inevitably involve specific industries and professions. Our development is more about starting from specific professions. Thus, the unavoidable question is whether to prioritize employment or career choice when graduating in the fourth year. From the perspective of realizing professional ideals, the work we do must be closely related to career goals. Otherwise, the work done will not support professional ideals, making their realization indefinitely delayed.

Therefore, we need to choose positions related to career goals. However, a significant number of college students mistake employment for career choice, assuming that any job will teach them something. In reality, at the level of professional development, choosing is more important than effort! If the direction is wrong, walking further only moves you farther from the goal.

6. Mistaking Career Choice for Major

When college students seek employment, there is a misconception: choosing a career is confined to the major studied. In terms of major relevance, choosing a career related to the major indeed greatly supports and aids career prospects. However, the issue arises when the initial choice of major isn't based on personal interest or internal adaptability. In other words, the major studied during university might not be the most suitable for the individual. If the first choice was incorrect, there's no reason to persist with the second choice, otherwise, it compounds the error.

Actually, only when our professional ideals and the corresponding career goals derived from them are highly related to our majors does the major become a key factor affecting our career choices. Otherwise, there's no need to be constrained by the major studied. We have no reason to continue down the wrong path.

In terms of professional ideals, the referred "major" is the knowledge required for the future profession. It could be the current major studied, a preferred major, or a major changed to pursue, etc. In short, it's the basic knowledge needed to achieve professional ideals. Therefore, if our professional ideals aren't aligned with our major, we shouldn't be bound by it. Remember, from the perspective of professional ideals, what we do is what we wish for, and what we wish for is what we like. Whether the major is relevant only needs consideration when linked to professional ideals, not based on the career prospects of the major studied.

If we chose the wrong major due to lack of self-awareness and understanding of rules during the college entrance exam, then choosing a career upon graduation is our second chance to rediscover ourselves. Therefore, if the major studied doesn't satisfy our ideals and pursuits, we can choose again upon graduation to align closer with our ideals.

7. Mistaking Major for Ability

When college students job hunt, their resumes may show such situations: listing senior economist qualifications and writing down the major and courses studied under abilities or specialties. This clearly reflects a misunderstanding—they equate the major studied with personal ability.

In fact, the major studied is merely the preference filled out during the college entrance exam, containing our aspirations and interests, but more often blindness and helplessness. A major is just knowledge—a systematic integration of theoretical knowledge and methods in a specific social division area—but it is not ability, nor does it represent anything. Moreover, outdated textbooks and rigid teaching methods further discount the minimal practical capabilities gained. Therefore, when college students create resumes, they shouldn't list what their major teaches or claim what they truly learned in university. Instead, they should first examine whether the major studied is liked and whether it is relevant to their future career.

Ability is the wisdom to solve practical problems. However, possessing extensive professional knowledge does not mean one has the ability to solve practical problems. Understanding theoretical knowledge serves more as a storage function, but how much can be retrieved and applied depends on one's ability. To illustrate, no one knows more than an encyclopedia, but you never see it solving specific problems!

If the major studied is not the one intended for the future, no matter how well the professional knowledge is mastered, it is not an ability because it does not reduce the job gap. Don't expect the major to build the core competitiveness of your professional ideal. Therefore, when writing down the courses studied in the major on the resume, one must be cautious because not everything studied in the major supports any job application. Hence, don't generalize the major studied as a job-hunting advantage, especially since the understanding of the major is not yet proficient.

8. Mistaking Knowledge for Skills

This misconception lies in college students mistaking foreign language and computer theory levels for operational skills or confusing the distinction between the two.

In reality, possessing foreign language level four or computer level two only indicates reaching some basic theoretical requirements, especially under the education system with Chinese characteristics, which requires certain distinctions. Companies focus more on practical skills rather than certificates obtained, although without certain skill levels, one wouldn't pass exams to get certificates. Here, we emphasize that in resumes, it's better to explain theoretical certifications with actual numbers or examples, such as describing foreign language proficiency as translating foreign articles or conversing with foreign tourists, or converting computer proficiency to typing 80 words per minute, capable of operating and creating office documents, which are more convincing and straightforward expressions.

Therefore, when applying for target positions, one should not equate theoretical knowledge with required operational skills. Any job requires not only necessary theoretical knowledge but also corresponding operational skills. Only when knowledge is transformed into skills can one secure a livelihood and seek greater development.

Knowledge more reflects what you know and understand, whereas skills reflect what you can do and how well you can do it. Having a specialized skill allows survival, but possessing knowledge alone without action, unless using knowledge as leverage for academic development, is insufficient. Currently, many college students lack the necessary operational skills required for positions and are unaware of this issue. Therefore, when companies ask about skills, they respond with theoretical knowledge, inevitably leading to elimination.

9. Mistaking Interests for Work

In daily life, we have ways to entertain and relax, but some college students mistake these interests as crucial factors in choosing careers or are swayed by them, refusing jobs unrelated to their interests or using it as an excuse to avoid undesirable work. This misconception profoundly affects college students' career views.

In reality, interests are ways to enjoy life. In interests, we find balance between career, love, and leisure, preventing dissatisfaction in life. Once interests become work, the triangular balance in life is broken, leading to frustration. Typically, when interests are positioned as work, it's arduous because leisure has turned into work. Without leisure, one quickly becomes tired of the job. In the world, perhaps only three types of people truly turn interests into work: scientists, artists, and prostitutes. This shows how few and difficult it is to turn interests into professions.

In fact, interests and hobbies are not necessarily suitable careers. In professional development, we must recognize that liking something is one thing, being competent is another, and choosing is yet another. Jobs chosen based on interests significantly enhance development, but it's not an absolute influencing factor. Therefore, what influences our choice of careers and competence isn't necessarily interests, hobbies, or personality. These intrinsic factors at most prevent us from performing better but cannot decide whether we can or succeed. One just needs to invest more effort. But if one combines interests with work, one must endure more loneliness and solitude and find another way to balance, otherwise psychological issues may arise.

10. Mistaking Experience for Ability

Some college students indeed participate in social practices, such as tutoring, promoting, or serving, and write these experiences as their abilities when graduating. In reality, these practical experiences provide more opportunities for college students to interact with and understand society, but these experiences do not necessarily equate to abilities. In professional development, experience does not necessarily represent ability.

Experience only represents the past, summarizing the activities and events one has participated in over time. Ability, however, is the unique core advantage formed from experience, summarizing how effectively tasks are completed and what results are achieved. Converting experience into ability requires transformation. Experience merely provides a platform for growth, but whether it transforms into ability is uncertain.

Everyone has past experiences, but not everyone forms abilities from them. Reasons include either not focusing on specific tasks, leading to scattered efforts that hinder specialization in one area, or failing to cultivate core competencies in the tasks performed. In summary, experience does not necessarily equate to ability. Therefore, a lesson for college students is that blindly engaging in unrelated social practices without direction cannot form the expected abilities.
