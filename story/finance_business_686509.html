Are art stocks a "sucker's game"?

by anonymous on 2012-03-02 09:57:23

Are art stocks a "game of fools"?

Due to the modification of trading rules, a lawsuit is approaching the Tianjin Culture Exchange. On September 28th, Wu Shaohui, the director of Beijing Bangdao Law Firm, informed reporters that the case of investors suing the exchange for "unfair trading rules" had been mediated, but the result was not satisfactory. After the National Day holiday, they would formally file a lawsuit against the Tianjin Culture Exchange.

An investor who entered with 1.3 million yuan now has only 22 yuan left, leading to litigation over trading rules.

"The complaint has already been written, and after the National Day holiday, we will formally file a lawsuit against the Tianjin Culture Exchange," Wu Shaohui, director of the Beijing Bangdao Law Firm, told this reporter on September 28th. He plans to sue the Tianjin Cultural Art Exchange (Weibo) on the grounds that the risks in the form contract were not adequately warned.

Previously, Wu Shaohui revealed that the "Contract Law" is a fundamental law in economic activities. The Tianjin Cultural Art Exchange has violated the provisions of the Contract Law regarding standard contracts. While providing standard contracts to investors entering, it did not sufficiently warn about the potential risks in share transactions. Wu Shaohui said that currently, 15 lawyers are involved in upholding rights against the Tianjin Cultural Art Exchange. There are seven or eight affected investors from Shandong, Shanghai, and Anhui provinces. In addition, the Zhengzhou Cultural Art Exchange is also included in the lawyers' rights protection list.

It is reported that these customers believe there are problems with the trading rules of the Tianjin Cultural Art Exchange, causing a significant reduction in their assets. It is claimed that some customers invested 1.3 million yuan and lost hundreds of thousands; others invested 200,000 yuan and ended up with only tens of thousands; even worse, one customer invested 1.3 million yuan and now has only 22 yuan left. Some insiders mentioned that an investor from Huzhou, Zhejiang, put all his family's savings into purchasing share products from the Tianjin Cultural Art Exchange, borrowed some high-interest loans, opened five or six accounts to buy, totaling more than 1.2 million yuan. Now, the purchased products have severely depreciated, leaving only more than 600,000 yuan. Some netizens jokingly referred to it as "winning the lottery like getting stabbed."

It is reported that this lawsuit does not target any specific cultural exchange or any individual, but rather focuses solely on the trading rules. While upholding the rights of investors, it hopes that the cultural exchange industry can develop healthily, truly achieving openness, fairness, and regularity.

The number of cultural exchanges may exceed 50, necessitating a review of trading loopholes.

The Tianjin Cultural Art Exchange was the first to try out fractionalized trading of artworks and gained certain benefits in the process.

Previously, Wei Pengju, the executive dean of the Central University of Finance and Economics Institute of Cultural Creativity, said in an interview with the media: "The rush to establish cultural exchanges across the country is directly related to the popularity of the Tianjin Cultural Art Exchange. Later development models will emulate the Tianjin Cultural Art Exchange, so this case will serve as a warning to other cultural exchanges, making them pay more attention to the perfection and stability of the rules."

According to statistics, by the end of June this year, the number of domestic cultural exchanges had exceeded 20. By July 31st, the total number of operational and preparatory cultural exchanges nationwide reached 36, among which 21 had officially opened. After August, the Han Tang Art Exchange, Fujian Strait Cultural Art Exchange, Jiangxi Cultural Art Exchange, and others successively opened or established. Due to hasty establishment, some cultural exchanges were found to still be under renovation for basic office premises. According to insiders, many cultural exchanges are still in preparation across the country. It is estimated that by the end of 2011, the total number of national cultural exchanges will reach or exceed 50.

Since the proliferation of cultural exchanges, there have been constant disputes and continuous questioning. Although the amount involved in this case is negligible compared to the total amount of fractionalized art trading at national cultural exchanges, it is enough to cool down fractionalized art trading, prompting cultural exchanges across the country to re-examine the loopholes in their cultural art trading.

Who decides the valuation of artworks?

Data shows that the first batch of art stocks at the Tianjin Exchange once increased in price by 17 times in just two or three months, with a market value breaking through 100 million yuan.

When the art stocks of the Tianjin Cultural Art Exchange were extremely popular, they also brought about market madness. However, doubts from various parties continued to arise. These doubts mainly focused on art appraisal and evaluation.

According to records from Artron Art Network, among Bai Gengyan's 656 total auction items, except for one preview item, 327 items were successfully auctioned, while 328 items failed to sell, resulting in a failure rate of 50%, with a total auction amount of 20.198 million yuan. If evaluated based on the price per square foot, actually, before 2010, the auction price of Bai Gengyan's works mostly hovered below 20,000 yuan per square foot, with the lowest price being less than 200 yuan per square foot.

According to Mr. Che Haifeng from Shenzhen University's School of Art and Design, at the 2010 Jia De autumn auction, Zhang Daqian's work created in 1947, "Tai Yi Guan Quan Tu," widely recognized as a peak creation of Zhang Daqian's ancient imitation, finally sold for 40.32 million yuan; Qi Baishi's work "Flower and Fruit Four Screens," highly sought after by collectors, sold for 47.6 million yuan. However, "Huang He Pao Xiao" was priced at 6 million yuan, clearly not in line with market rules. It is reported that due to the lack of authoritative art appraisal and evaluation institutions domestically, the valuation of artworks became an issue.

Bai Gengyan's "Huang He Pao Xiao" and "Yan Sai Qiu" once separately reached sensational prices of 18.7 yuan and 18.5 yuan, with the "shares" values of these two works reaching as high as 112.2 million yuan and 92.5 million yuan respectively! Such prices could buy some fine works by masters like Qi Baishi and Zhang Daqian.

Even a rotten stone can be hyped up to the sky

Why did Bai Gengyan's paintings increase in price dozens of times within a year? What force made the two paintings rise nearly 14 times in just a month and a half?

The reason for the large deviation from market prices in artworks listed on the Tianjin Cultural Art Exchange, someone helplessly commented, could only be attributed to "everyone believing in its enormous future valuation potential." "The only reason for the explosive price hike is because everyone in circulation is passing the hot potato, purely speculative trading," Liu Jipeng, Director of the Capital Research Center of China University of Political Science and Law (Weibo), vividly analogized in Weibo: "For example, if there's a stone on the ground, you can auction it and say it's worth how much money. I consider whether there will be someone buying it later, so I can buy it today. No longer considering its intrinsic security attributes and value issues. As long as someone is dumber than me, I can be that fool."

Car Haifeng, a teacher at Shenzhen University's School of Art and Design, familiar with the art market, said that the most important aspect of art investment is still based on the historical and cultural value, artistic value contained in the artwork. However, many people investing in "art stocks" do not care about the artistic value and true market value of the painting, and have never even seen what the painting looks like, blindly following trends into the market, posing significant risks.

Risk and benefit coexist; there are many failed cases

Journalists discovered that after the initial crazy speculation, according to data provided by the Tianjin Cultural Art Exchange website, by September 23rd, the first batch of art shares, once very glorious, had fallen from a high of around 18 yuan to more than 3 yuan.

According to the "China Art Market White Paper" statistics for the first half of 2011, this spring auction turnover has already broken through 50 billion RMB, with a total turnover reaching 50.8 billion RMB. This autumn auction turnover is expected to be around 55 billion RMB. That is to say, the annual turnover of China's art auction market will break through a scale of approximately 100 billion RMB. In recent years, despite the impact of the financial crisis, China's art market has remained uniquely prosperous, with no decline in the physical prices of all kinds of artworks. From antiques and calligraphy and paintings to jade and porcelain, prices have been rising without interruption.

However, risk and benefit always coexist. In the history of foreign art fund development, there have been several cases where investments ended in failure. In the late 1980s to early 1990s, the Paris National Bank invested 22 million USD to purchase 16 French and Italian paintings and sketches. Seven years later, the bank sold the set of artworks in its investment portfolio, but regrettably, the final total sale price was 8 million USD less than when they were purchased.

In addition, New York Chase Bank issued an art fund worth 300 million USD, Morgan Rich Investment Group released an art investment fund worth 25 million USD, and a Japanese foundation invested 520 million USD to purchase 7300 Western modernist and impressionist paintings. However, the investment funds of these three companies all suffered severe losses.
