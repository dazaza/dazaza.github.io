by zhihui009 on 2011-01-18 17:39:20

Japan was a great beneficiary of Western technological civilization at the beginning of its modernization. They placed the ultimate goal of intellectual property protection in promoting the development of national industries. For this purpose, the Japanese went to great lengths to help Japanese companies actively absorb advanced foreign technologies.

In 1885, Japan announced the "Patent Monopoly Ordinance" (Patent Monopoly Ordinance), which became Japan's initially implemented patent law. The Japanese government also designated the day of announcement - April 18th - as Japan's "Invention Memorial Day" until today.

In 1888, after re-examining the patent systems and operations of European and American patent offices and combining them with the implementation of the domestic "Patent Monopoly Ordinance", the Japanese announced the "Patent Ordinance" (Patent Ordinance). This ordinance improved patent administrative management. It is worth noting that this ordinance (and the previous "Patent Monopoly Ordinance") excluded foodstuffs, addictive substances, and medical adjustment methods from the scope of patent protection, a policy that continued in Japan's subsequent patent legislation. At the same time, it stipulated that foreigners could not enjoy Japanese patent rights. This caused great controversy at the time. It was not until 1899 when Japan joined the Paris Convention for the Protection of Industrial Property, revised the "Patent Law", "Design Law", and "Trademark Law", that foreigners' industrial property rights were legally recognized.

Afterwards, Japan made multiple revisions to its patent law. However, from an overall perspective, Japan basically adopted a weak patent protection strategy during the initial stages of its economic development: restricting the scope of patent protection, abandoning the U.S.'s "first to invent" principle in favor of the "first to file" principle in patent examination, and establishing a series of systems such as compulsory licensing and information disclosure. These systems helped Japanese companies calmly absorb foreign technology and then catch up.

As a late-developing country, it was a wise move for Japan to adopt a weak intellectual property protection strategy while actively modernizing its intellectual property legal system. However, this inevitably brought pressure from Western countries. Under international pressure, in 1975, Japan extended the scope of its patent protection to chemical and pharmaceutical products. Objectively speaking, these industries had already developed considerably within Japan and possessed the ability to compete with their foreign counterparts, making this adjustment timely and appropriate.
