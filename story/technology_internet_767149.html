Why are tablet magazine apps so lackluster?

by anonymous on 2013-10-08 18:38:39

When tablets became popular, magazine apps were frequently mentioned because the size of a tablet is similar to that of a traditional printed magazine. Compared to smartphones, which have smaller screens, we can enjoy a better magazine reading experience on tablets. This assumption makes perfect sense: people have the motivation to read, and tablets offer a size advantage. However, years have passed, so why does the tablet magazine market still remain lukewarm?

This isn't a question of whether tablets are suitable for reading. So where do we usually get articles from on our tablets? It's apps like Zite and Flipboard, right? Even Twitter or Facebook are decent sources. These apps generally don’t produce content; they just manage what’s already available.

On the other hand, dedicated magazine apps are “deserted.” As Jon Lund, the chairman of the Danish Online News Association (DANA) and founder of knowledge-sharing site Memit, experienced, even if I paid for these magazine apps, I still couldn’t be bothered to open them. For this reason, he analyzed why this situation might occur.

8 Apps per Day

Last year, Nielsen survey data showed that on average each smartphone had 41 apps installed. Not long ago, App Annie Index statistics showed that an average iPhone had 80 apps installed. Flurry data from April this year pointed out that smartphone users only open 8 apps daily, with the most commonly used being Facebook, YouTube, and some gaming apps. Localytics highlighted an even harsher reality: about 22% of apps are opened only once.

Although there aren't specific data points regarding tablets, considering the usage habits and time spent on mobile devices, there isn’t much space left for magazine apps on either smartphones or tablets. Faced with the general public, magazines can only survive within specific reader groups and cannot enter the tablets of most users, nor can they occupy much of their usage time.

Hermit in the Information Stream

Magazine apps themselves cannot compete with those super apps, and the content of magazine apps is in a hidden state within the network information stream.

When magazine content appears within the app, it isn’t a webpage; this article cannot be indexed, nor can it be searched online. Even if it could, clicking often leads to the relevant app store rather than the article itself. This isolated state prevents magazines from gaining greater influence.

This situation also exists on social media. We cannot use Twitter or Facebook to link to articles in magazine apps, and Zite and Flipboard also cannot include such article content.

Laggards of the Times

When we open a magazine app, it feels like holding a beautiful but outdated media product. Although these products have visually appealing effects, interactive interfaces, embedded videos, and carefully designed navigation tools, the elaborate layout would look stunning even if printed. However, for internet users, fragmented, open things are easier to accept.

Many magazine apps also perform poorly in terms of socialization. Many magazine apps often attract users to like and comment. However, readers can only complete these conversations within the app. The orderly and relatively closed magazine experience goes against the sharing and openness of social networks.

Paid Magazines Don't Sell

This year, tablet sales may surpass desktops, yet success stories of magazine apps on this platform are hard to find.

According to data from the Alliance for Audited Media, Jon Lund compiled the proportion of paid digital magazine subscriptions compared to total paid subscriptions, selecting data from 25 relatively best-selling magazine apps. On average, digital subscriptions accounted for 12% of total subscriptions. Most percentages remained in single digits. Taking Reader's Digest and The New Yorker as examples, the proportions were only 6% and 7%, respectively. The highest was Game Informer, closely related to games and the web, at 38%. This includes premium factors because subscribers receive discounts on games.

Taking another famous magazine, Wired, as an example, they launched a tablet subscription version in May 2010. By the end of last year, they had only 100,000 digital subscribers, while the total number of paid subscribers reached 850,000. These two figures pale in comparison to another statistic: Wired's website has 20 million monthly unique users.

Additionally, even though magazine apps are priced lower, they still fail to attract a large number of users. Take The Daily as an example, the iPad subscription was only $0.99 per week, but only 100,000 people subscribed to the digital version.

Of course, Jon Lund believes that the unpopularity of magazine apps is relative to the magazines themselves. E-reading is a trend, but magazine apps develop slowly. Relative to the total circulation of magazines, the share occupied by magazine apps is small, which is undeniable. However, if magazine apps are positioned as supplements, then the current situation is acceptable. After all, magazine apps mostly replicate the content of physical magazines, positioning themselves as appendages or filling gaps in the electronic platform.

In terms of publishing, physical reading remains the mainstream. However, the current predicament of magazine apps is worth pondering: how exactly should they occupy new channels of distribution?
