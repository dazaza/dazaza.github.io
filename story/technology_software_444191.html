Any single dot or line, when reaching the middle part of its execution (stopping, lifting the brush), will generally produce a few types of external contours. These external contours are determined by different starting and stroke techniques. There are normal contours and there are abnormal ones. Different starting angles and directions can also result in different brush hair movement methods. The differences in starting techniques and mid-stroke contours lead to different turning points and ending contours.

(I)

Breaking the superstition of brush techniques.

Brush techniques are actually very complex, and talking about them can be quite tedious. My brother Xiao Wenfei from "Oriental Art.Calligraphy" once asked me to discuss brush techniques, but I didn't have time and didn't want to make it into a textbook-like list, so I kept delaying. Nowadays, many short-term training classes don't focus much on specific brush techniques. Teachers often look at students' work and say things like: this stroke isn't strong enough, that one isn't round and powerful enough, etc., mostly focusing on the results while omitting the process. This clearly involves brush techniques, yet teachers often don't explain, whether it's because they don't know, can't express it, or simply choose not to. A friend of mine once attended a lecture by Liu Yanhu, where he presented his work and asked Mr. Liu if the brushwork was correct. Mr. Liu shook his head and said two words: "Not correct," then stopped. Stopped, but where was it incorrect? Mr. Liu didn't want to elaborate further, and my friend didn't dare to ask more. Thus, this single "not correct" became a mystery, something mysterious.

When summarizing brush techniques, I emphasized these terms: "smooth/reverse" and "physics." Without blushing or hesitating, I say that these four words encompass most of the content of brush techniques and reveal their core issues.

First, let's look at "smooth/reverse." Any thing has two sides, this dialectical relationship is known to all, and it applies equally well to brush techniques. For example, in running script, take a very simple example - writing the vertical hook in the character "yong" - there are at least two ways to write it: smooth writing and reverse writing. That is, following the vertical line, smoothly releasing the tip at the hook (similar to central tip), which is called "smooth"; or when the vertical line is written, noticing that the brush hairs are already slightly tilted (the flattened hairs, with one side lower left and the other higher right), at the hook crossing the tip (brush hairs intersecting), which is called "reverse." Like this, writing any single stroke has at least these two methods: either smooth or reverse, with no single standard. Understanding this point is quite important. Whoever says a particular stroke must be done in a certain way is confining oneself unnecessarily. Of course, if referring to a specific stroke in a particular copybook, there is a clear, single way to write it.

Beyond smooth and reverse, there are many other "eccentric paths," for example, broken strokes - when I write the vertical stroke in the character "yong," instead of continuing to write and release the tip, I stop, lift the brush, and then move on to write other strokes. This move probably cannot be restrained by the "Two Wangs" brush techniques, requiring a set of techniques from Yan Zhenqing, Kang Youwei, He Shaoji, Lin Sanzhi, etc., to "explain" it.

This is like people, who have at least two kinds: men and women. And another kind, transgenders.

Connected strokes have smooth and reverse, adjacent strokes also have smooth and reverse. For example, after I finish writing the hook in the character "yong," what about the next stroke, the "picking"? It's simple, continue with smooth or reverse. If smooth, the brush may basically return to center (like central tip); if reverse, the brush changes its face. Continue, and repeat the same logic. Thus, the brush hairs cycle between changing faces, returning to center, and repeating this process endlessly. What is referred to as "eight-sided sharp edges" is exactly this phenomenon. True masters, with one stroke, continuously change faces, writing fifteen to twenty characters, or even thirty to forty characters or more, without any problem.

Adjacent strokes also involve a trajectory issue. This trajectory also divides into smooth and reverse. For example, writing a hand-raising radical, after finishing the horizontal stroke, how does the vertical stroke connect? At least two methods: smooth connection, directly connecting the pen and going down; reverse connection, circling from the upper left, upper right, or below, either actually or virtually making a circle. This circle is also called an "8-character circle," based on the yin-yang principle, allowing the brush hairs to adjust and return to center.

This brush trajectory, combined with the intertwining movements up, down, left, and right, forms a kind of energy flow, producing momentum - brush momentum, body momentum. Momentum is built on the basic strokes, then through your own emotional expression, it appears. Even if you write the basic strokes accurately, if you don't infuse your emotions and unique feelings, it remains merely a form, lacking momentum. This is the big issue of "technique surpassing the path," similar to how people need to solve not only meal problems but also emotional and intellectual issues, which is quite important. Momentum also doesn't leave out smooth and reverse, known as following or opposing momentum. How exactly is momentum implemented? Here, I'll quote Brother Kongtang's words: "My stomach is hungry now, and there's a movie to see tonight."

Are brush techniques really this simple? Yes, they are this simple. This is the basic principle. The complexity lies in the details of operation and the mobilization of momentum, etc. For example, within one stroke, specifically how to follow? How to oppose? What should the brush hairs generally be like at the time? Etc., this is the norm. Beyond the norm, there are abnormalities (complexities ongoing). The scope expands to writing states, tools, materials, holding the brush, etc. If you hold a wolf-hair brush and write on glossy paper, knowing how to follow and oppose makes everything easy, a piece of cake (if using a wolf-hair brush on raw paper, it's a bit self-inflicted trouble. As for writing the Two Wangs on raw paper, you're either a genius or an idiot). But if you're holding a long goat-hair brush, constantly thinking about how to do it, it's quite troublesome. Those soft, lazy, unsupported mud-like bristles don't listen to you, you can only naturally, spontaneously, and "vaguely" lead the next stroke based on the state of the previous one. Note, "vaguely," in double quotes, the brush is vague, but you shouldn't be. Lin Sanshi indeed benefited from the long goat-hair brush, with blurry, watery effects, but he himself was clear and clean.

Following the natural generation, regardless of wolf or goat hair brushes, they all follow this principle, with some distinctions. This is actually the "physical principle." Physical principle is equivalent to a reflex response. What is a reflex response? Baidu explains it as: "When the organism is subjected to various harmful stimuli, the level of adrenocorticotropic hormone and glucocorticoid in the blood increases, causing a series of systemic reactions to resist harmful stimuli." It's like when I punch you, you instinctively shrink back and kick me. In brush techniques, it's: the brush is pressed and rebounds. That's all.

Understanding smooth/reverse and physics allows for random adaptation and continuous growth. These condensed four words can expand infinitely, from one to two, two to three, endlessly.

I'm reminded of the silent Liu Yanhu. To say he doesn't understand or can't express it shows a lack of understanding of the vastness of heaven and earth. I can only guess according to my thoughts, and the word I come up with is: tiring. Don't think that just a little bit wrong, when truly explained, can be clarified in a few words. Worse still, after explaining, you might still be completely confused.

Speaking from brush techniques (Part II)

From the previous short article, it seems not many people clearly understand smooth/reverse and physics. Some people, upon seeing the three words "wild fox zen," immediately roll up their sleeves, as if hearing "rat" triggers an automatic search for a stick or practice yelling, without considering that the person might be from Disney. Those who understand, I not only thank them but seek opportunities to learn from them, as they see the essence at a glance, indicating they stand higher than me. Those who roll up their sleeves without understanding, although I'm a bit afraid, I am very grateful, as they generate plenty of clicks for me, playing the role of a star's (a bit dizzy) operational company or paparazzi behind the scenes. Therefore, I'm not afraid of being laughed at for lacking eloquence, and I seize a popular term from Zhongshu.com to express my sincerity: respect.

Others find it interesting, but I feel incomplete, so I consider adding a few more words.

Smooth/reverse and physics, I mentioned earlier, "encompass most of the content of brush techniques and reveal their core issues." In fact, however, this is just a better summary and explanation of brush techniques found during my practice and teaching process, a bit of experience, not a theory. I can't and don't want to write like theorists who cover every detail, I only record my understanding and insights. Rather than reciting ancient theories fluently while writing poorly, I'd rather talk nonsense. Chapter 39 of "Dream of the Red Chamber" says: The old woman talks nonsense, but the sentimentalist searches for roots. Both the old woman and the sentimentalist are my idols.

For example, during the writing process, the brush hairs generally appear in central tip, side tip, twisted tip, etc. Twisted tip means the brush hairs are tangled together, which can be considered reverse. Relatively speaking, central tip and side tip are naturally both smooth.

For example, during the writing process, a brush generally uses movements such as parallel motion, swinging, rotating, pressing/lifting, and pausing.

Parallel motion refers to small amplitude movements of the brush handle or wrist, such as three-finger grip, like writing in the style of the Two Wangs, encountering relatively less resistance during movement, thus it can be called smooth.

Swinging (swaying the brush) compared to parallel motion, involves larger amplitude movements of the brush handle, wrist, etc., such as Zhang Xu whose writing looks like rolling on the ground (without finding teeth), Mi Fu who claims "hanging hand" (suspended elbow), Huang Tingjian who holds the brush high, etc., easily creating a sense of unity in the strokes. Swinging actually includes reverse top actions, with obvious reverse strokes, greater resistance, thus it can be called reverse.

Rotating also comes in two basic types: smooth-tip rotation (such as Huaisu), reverse-tip rotation (such as Zhu Yunming). Smooth-tip rotation keeps the brush hairs in a basically central tip state. Reverse-tip rotation, if maintaining curvature, is actually twisting, with the brush hairs twisting at the turn; if forming an angle, the brush hairs clearly flip to the other side, becoming flipping.

Lifting can also be divided into smooth lifting and reverse lifting. For example, when writing the dot on the left side of the grass radical, smooth lifting means pointing down and then smoothly lifting to the upper right, reverse lifting means pointing down, then moving clockwise around a circle before lifting. Pressing can also be divided into smooth pressing and reverse pressing, such as when writing a horizontal fold, smooth pressing means smoothly pressing the brush down, reverse pressing (with minimal or no action) means changing direction near flipping. Regarding pausing, from the perspective of the brush hairs' state, it is smooth.

Other terms, such as scraping and breaking, can also be explained accordingly:

Scraping involves suddenly changing the brush tip direction (which is actually flipping or twisting), or tilting the brush handle first, then quickly standing it up during movement, with the belly of the brush scraping across the paper surface, creating a large reverse effect. All count as reverse.

Evenly connected strokes that usually break at the turns are considered broken strokes. Broken strokes also have two methods of connection: central tip connection (such as Yu Rensun), flipping connection (such as Zhu Yunming). Lin Sanshi's broken strokes often suddenly break within a single line, then naturally connect with smooth tip, which is the smooth connection method. He also uses the reverse connection method, where the next stroke doesn't follow the current momentum but deliberately breaks and changes the brush face, such as when he writes the character "chang," after the horizontal stroke ends, he switches to the knife edge side of the brush hairs to cut (slash) out; or when writing the "mu" radical, after the horizontal stroke ends, a vertical follows similarly; or when writing the character "er," the last stroke continues in this manner. The lines produced by cutting out are generally thin and round, deviating from the Two Wangs' style.

The above are simple examples (no images provided for entertainment reasons), showing that the state of the brush hairs, the movement of the brush, and the terminology of brush techniques all contain the principles of smooth/reverse. Knowing smooth/reverse means knowing adaptability. Further understanding reveals that "eight-sided sharp edges" is actually nothing more than this. As for the physical principles, that's like Buddha Sakyamuni, all idle gods and wild ghosts are fully "under control."

(III)

Pen techniques have always been mysterious. The brush touches the paper, and it's over in an instant, making retrospective analysis prone to omissions. Usually, explanations are brief and vague, eventually making it seem mysterious.

People often compare the use of chopsticks to the use of a brush, and they indeed have many similarities. To pick up a piece of fatty meat, one instinctively assumes a posture, without paying attention to how the posture is formed, quickly stuffing it into a drooling mouth; to achieve a certain effect in writing, one consciously changes the brush hairs or posture, though instantaneous, absolutely precise. From this, we can see that using chopsticks is more casual, while using a brush is slightly more deliberate. Exaggeratedly speaking, using a brush is more like a fashion show, where each step follows a set of rules, leaving no room for carelessness.

Those who believe in the chopstick analogy use the brush entirely "naturally," often obscuring and confusing what they should know, leaving them unaware of why things happen. One must be like a fashion show, occasionally subconsciously showing off, thereby understanding the principles, knowing the limits, and gaining freedom. Only after showing off can one truly understand nature and understand "chopsticks."

Thus, combining randomness and deliberation, using a brush at least includes three major contents: brush principles, brush techniques, and brush intent. Hongzhai Suibi records Dongpo's poetry "not conforming to others," which I admire but cannot reach. Fortunately, discussing brush usage is not like composing poetry, emphasizing experience rather than "innovation," so where I borrow ideas from others, it precisely indicates our shared understanding and correctness of speech.

Casually picking up a brush and writing freely, the shapes of the dots and strokes appear randomly, and the direction of the brush hairs presents itself randomly—this is the basic brush principle, or the principle of using chopsticks, the principle of nature. On this basis, each person deliberately controls and dispatches, and later generations summarize various skills, which can be called artificial principles. Natural principles are like grains in their shell, artificial principles are like rice after the shell is removed—wherever there's a need for something to be rounder, squarer, thicker, finer, drier, or moister, it cannot be achieved without artifice. Artificial principles are built on natural ones, representing a high degree of "nature."

Regardless of whether it's natural or artificial, brush principles are closely related to tools and materials. Wolf hair, sheep hair, raw paper, processed paper—all have different natures, leading to different appearances, which need not be elaborated further.

Combining natural and artificial principles, various techniques are summarized, giving rise to officially named "brush techniques." Techniques are varied and countless. What about twisting, smoothing, cutting, swaying, retreating, flipping, rushing, squatting, brushing, stopping, pressing, lifting, adjusting, reversing, turning, folding, twisting, central tip, side tip, etc.? The names are numerous, and since every stroke can be written in multiple ways, it's unnecessary to go into detail here. I'm interested in two other points, which can gather fragments into a whole, organizing and varying basic techniques.

As the examples above show, the principle of smooth and reverse almost covers the variation rules of brush usage. This is not "those who follow thrive, those who oppose perish," nor is it either/or. Instead, it's pushing the boat with the water or rowing against the wind, combining the straightforward with the perilous extremes. Smooth and reverse themselves have primary and secondary relationships. From the perspective of friction between the brush and paper, reverse is predominant, and smooth is auxiliary. From the perspective of rounded or angular turns, smooth is predominant, and reverse is auxiliary. From the application of central tip and side tip, smooth is predominant, and reverse is auxiliary. Records in the "Records of the Three Kingdoms・Shu Book・Biography of the First Emperor" cite "Nine Provinces Spring and Autumn": Pang Tong said to Liu Bei, "Jingzhou is desolate, its people exhausted. To the east, there is Sun Wu, to the north, Cao. With a tripod strategy, it is difficult to achieve ambition. Now Yizhou is wealthy and strong, with a population of a million. Its four armies are well-equipped, treasures abundant without seeking from outside. We can temporarily borrow it to settle great matters." Liu Bei replied, "Now, my adversary is Cao Cao. Cao is urgent, I am lenient; Cao is brutal, I am benevolent; Cao is deceitful, I am loyal. I act contrary to Cao, and only then can matters succeed. To lose credibility with the world for trivial reasons is something I will not do." Pang Tong said, "In times of strategic change, it is certainly not possible to define everything with a single method. Attacking the weak and exploiting confusion is the business of the Five Hegemons. Taking by force and guarding with righteousness, rewarding with virtue, after achieving stability, granting a large fiefdom, how does it betray trust? Not taking today will ultimately benefit others." Liu Bei then proceeded. There is a phrase in the text, "taking by force and guarding with righteousness." Taking by force is a temporary measure; guarding with righteousness is covering up the loss of trust by bestowing favors and winning hearts. Here too, smooth is predominant, and reverse is auxiliary, consistent with the relationship between central tip and side tip in brush usage. Side tip is akin to a daring reconnaissance team, while central tip is the main force in battle. Figures like Zhang Ruitu and Yang Weizhen, who used reverse techniques extensively, historically played supporting roles. On the other hand, figures like the Two Wangs, Zhang Xu, Yan Zhenqing, Huaisu, Mi Fu, and Wang Duo, who favored circular techniques, could take the lead.
