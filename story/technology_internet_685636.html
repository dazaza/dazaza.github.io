Experts from the Yangzhou Armed Police Hospital's Anorectal Center point out that what medicine refers to as hemorrhoids includes internal hemorrhoids, external hemorrhoids, and mixed hemorrhoids. This is a chronic disease where the venous plexus at the bottom of the anal rectum and the anal mucosa become dilated, forming one or more soft venous masses. So, what are the common causes that induce hemorrhoid diseases?

Common causes that induce hemorrhoid diseases include:

1. Occupational relations: People standing or sitting for long periods, carrying heavy loads over long distances, can affect venous return, causing slow blood flow in the pelvic cavity and congestion of the abdominal organs. This can lead to excessive engorgement of the hemorrhoidal veins, decreased venous wall tension, and easy blood stasis and expansion of the vessels. Additionally, due to insufficient exercise, reduced intestinal peristalsis, delayed stool passage, or habitual constipation, pressure and stimulation on the veins can cause local engorgement and obstructed blood return, leading to increased pressure within the hemorrhoidal veins and reduced venous wall resistance.

2. Local stimulation and dietary irregularities: Cold, heat exposure, constipation, diarrhea, excessive alcohol consumption, and eating spicy foods can all stimulate the anus and rectum, causing engorgement of the hemorrhoidal venous plexus, affecting venous blood return, and resulting in decreased venous wall resistance.

3. Anatomical reasons: When people stand or sit, the anus and rectum are located lower down. Due to gravity and organ compression, venous return upwards is significantly hindered. The rectal veins and their branches lack venous valves, making it difficult for blood to return, easily causing blood stasis. The special arrangement of these vessels makes them prone to fecal blockage, affecting blood return. The veins pass through loose submucosal tissues without adequate support, making them susceptible to dilation and curvature.

4. Genetic factors: Inherent weakness of the venous walls reduces their resistance, unable to withstand vascular pressure, leading to gradual expansion.

5. Increased intra-abdominal pressure: Factors such as intra-abdominal tumors, uterine tumors, ovarian tumors, enlarged prostate, pregnancy, overeating, or prolonged squatting on the toilet can increase intra-abdominal pressure, hindering venous blood return.

6. Elevated anal venous pressure: Conditions like liver cirrhosis, liver congestion, and cardiac insufficiency can cause anal vein engorgement, increased pressure, and affect the blood return of the rectal veins.

Experts from the Yangzhou Armed Police Hospital's Anorectal Center remind patients that although hemorrhoids are not serious illnesses, their complications can be highly harmful to health and should not be left untreated.
