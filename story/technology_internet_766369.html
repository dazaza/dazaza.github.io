A Detailed Explanation of the Dynamic Focus Design Concept in iOS 7

by anonymous on 2013-09-11 14:36:34

This article was written by Yi Zhang, Creative Director of eico design. From the perspective of professional designers, it interprets the dynamic focus design philosophy in iOS 7, which was officially released recently. The motion designers at eico design have also conducted high-speed simulations and breakdowns of iOS 7's new dynamic design (see the video below), analyzing and organizing the rate curves in the Parallax spatial dynamics to help designers and product personnel better understand its design philosophy and changes.

Just as we easily lose our way in most identical office buildings, but can easily identify our location and destination in many newly designed shopping malls or ancient courtyards, excellent architectural interior design not only guides people through signage but also resonates spatially with individuals through structural space and unique "rhythms."

Similarly, in product interface design, as the number of functions and interfaces increases exponentially, whether users can understand their current "position" and clearly judge how to proceed at the next "crossroads" is an important criterion for evaluating the usability design of a product. In other words, interfaces that easily cause users to "get lost" are significantly flawed.

As an important part of interface experience, the dynamic design of product interfaces is not merely used to increase visual sensory stimulation but more importantly influences users' perception and understanding of the product. Since the release of the first-generation iPhone, the dynamic design of the iOS platform has demonstrated the spatial hierarchy relationship between the X, Y, and Z axes through its dynamic performance. Through the dynamic expressions of the iOS platform, users can easily understand the "world" within the screen and their own position coordinates within it. Good dynamic design and performance were also one of the reasons why users and the world were amazed by the birth of the iPhone in 2007.

After the release of the iOS 7 beta version, we began to deconstruct and study the new version. As the most watched operating system platform in the world, the influence of iOS 7 on future product design goes without saying, so such research and learning are undoubtedly beneficial for product design.

In terms of dynamic design, I worked with eico motion designer Yefei Bai to break down and simulate the multiple beta versions that have been released. Today, we can see a new dynamic design mindset integrated into the iOS 7 platform, from beta version to official release, mainly including two aspects: one is the dynamic switching centered around the operational focus, and the second is the spatial rate change based on the "Parallax" structure.

Operation Focus Centered

In the desktop system and built-in applications of iOS 7, the interface transition effect known as UI Collection View Transition Layout is officially adopted. Previously, the interface transitions between different levels in the iOS system were almost all performed through a single sliding box method.

In iOS 7, however, the collection view transitions mostly adopt this kind of motion mode centered around the user’s operational focus. Its characteristics are that the reference point or line of the collection view interface movement is no longer solely the past screen center or edge, but rather the user's operation point or axis serves as the reference point or line for the interface transition movement. The movement path has also shifted from past fixed-path motions to dynamic modes that move according to the operation point and axis. The significance lies in further connecting the user's operation with the dynamic interface transition, guiding users to shift their visual focus during different interface transitions. In most previous interface transition dynamics, users had to reposition their visual focus after each interface transition was completed.

Firstly, it can be seen that in the desktop level, whether it is the launch dynamic of clicking an icon to open an APP or the expansion effect of opening an APP folder, both are centered around the user's operation point as the magnification center. However, the effect rate after each region click is also different, and this rate rule runs throughout all similar dynamics, which will be detailed later in the text.

The same rule applies to other built-in applications in iOS 7. In the redesigned calendar, the switch dynamic from the year view to the month view is dynamically magnified centered around the specific date clicked by the user. The process from the month view to the day view involves a vertical enhancement along the row of the clicked date on the Y-axis.

In the new album interface, the switch dynamics from annual -> highlights -> moments involve the enlargement effects of photo thumbnails in different sizes until full-screen viewing, with their dynamic motion trajectories depending on the user's touch point on the screen.

For the new design of Safari, the design thought of operation focus guidance is mainly reflected in the switching method of multiple window selections. Compared to the old version, the new Safari adopts a vertically stacked browsing window design method, using a stereoscopic perspective stacking effect to better utilize vertical screen space. Meanwhile, during the click-to-select-window-to-magnify-switching effect, the clicked position serves as the baseline for stereoscopic perspective motion.

Actually, the dynamic motion based on the operation focus is not a fresh product that appeared with iOS 7; similar design methods have already been adopted in apps like Clear, Evernote, and some designs in iOS 5. But for iOS, comprehensively introducing such a design concept at the system level can be considered a significant improvement since its inception six years ago.

"Parallax" Spatial Rate

The above-discussed switching mode based on the operational focus is decomposed from the perspective of motion direction trajectory. For dynamic design, however, motion rate is another dimension affecting user perception. It's like the same musical score, played with different tempo speeds, could result in completely different outcomes, such as lullabies versus electronic dance music.

In the application launch dynamic design of iOS 7, the motion time is not only 80% longer than iOS 6, but there is also a significant difference in the rate curve. In the graph below, the X-axis represents the duration of dynamic motion, while the Y-axis represents the motion rate. It can be seen that the acceleration and deceleration processes of the dynamic motion in iOS 6 are relatively even from start to finish. In iOS 7, however, the application launch dynamics begin with higher acceleration, reach top speed, and then decelerate very smoothly until the end. This motion rate gives users a more relaxed and comfortable feeling, but for iOS system users over the past six years, it represents a challenge to their perception habits.

But apart from changes in time and acceleration curves, the dynamic rate of iOS 7 also includes a new dimension: the spatial rate corresponding to its new "Parallax" structure. At WWDC 2013, Apple introduced the "Parallax" interface parallax space structure and added many targeted design elements throughout the system. In dynamic design, it is embodied through a special calculation mode of motion rate.

In the simulation video, it can be seen that although most of the collection view dynamic rate curves in iOS 7 are similar, there are subtle differences in the rate curves of the interface dynamics triggered by different operation points. The rule is that under the same interface dynamic duration, with the screen center point as the origin, the closer the operation focus is to the screen edge, the higher the starting speed of the interface dynamic process.

This differentiated dynamic rate approach, relative to fully consistent dynamic effects, creates a feeling that the content at the center point is closer to the operator, while the screen edges feel farther away. Through dynamic rates, it builds a rate space in the user's perceptual subconscious. Such a design approach is common in some vertical shooting games but is less frequently seen in interface product design.

Conclusion

Through further analysis and understanding, we can see that the iOS 7 design team has begun to think about interface product design from more new dimensions and depths. The new iOS system product will bring varying degrees of influence on users, products, and industries in the coming years. Many of these changes are exciting, especially in the many new transformations that have turned previously unachievable design solutions into reality. For future products, while bringing new challenges, it actually also provides a whole new space for imagination and execution.
