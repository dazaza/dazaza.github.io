How Tim Cook's Apple Will Move Forward

by szjqd510 on 2011-11-16 10:02:10

Where is Tim Cook's Apple heading? As a long-time employee of Apple, Cook became the Chief Operating Officer in 2005 and temporarily took over the company during Jobs' multiple medical leaves. Cook is low-key in his actions and has established his own management style at Apple; this is clearly different from the passionate management style of co-founder Jobs. In August 2011, when Tim Cook took over from Steve Jobs as the head of the company, he stated that Apple Inc. would not change. The so-called "no change" mainly refers to the momentum of Apple's rapid development. Few people truly believe this will be a predetermined fact about the future, and most are cautiously observing. The global Apple fans' frenzied purchase of the last iPhone 4S led by Jobs shows their attitude towards Apple's future.

Cook is a long-term employee of Apple, becoming the Chief Operating Officer in 2005, and took charge of the company during Jobs' multiple sick leaves. Cook acts low-key and has established his own management style within Apple; this is obviously different from the passionate management style of co-founder Jobs. With Jobs gone, Cook needs to continue maintaining Apple's strong momentum: according to informed sources, in recent weeks, Cook has already started managing administrative affairs such as employee promotions and business reporting structures, things that Jobs was never interested in. Compared to Jobs, the 50-year-old Cook has increased communication with employees, frequently sending emails to all staff, referring to them as "team". To put it bluntly: this is not something a leader should do, meaning what Tim Cook does is at most the role of an executive director. If Jobs were still alive, Cook's approach would be blameless. Now, what Cook needs to do is to keep Apple moving forward aggressively, but such practices are likely to mire Apple in the quagmire of collective management.

In addition, Cook recently announced a charity plan, promising that the company will match each employee's donation to non-profit organizations up to a limit of $10,000 per person annually, starting first in the U.S. This practice is hard for anyone to say is bad, but it is indeed a form of corporate management distraction. On one hand, it benefits society, on the other hand, it benefits the brand, but it is not beneficial to corporate management. A manager of a company should not be just an ordinary member of the team; he should be the leader of the enterprise team, not just a group leader. This is different from Jobs: reportedly, at a retreat meeting in 2010, Jobs opposed corporate donations.

Technically speaking, Jobs' insight into the consumer's inner desires is something that Tim Cook cannot perceive or compare to. Moreover, Jobs would never tell others his inner thoughts or teach others. That is to say, even though Cook and Jobs have worked together for many years, it is difficult for Cook to know where Jobs gets his grasp on social consumer demand and technological orientation. Perhaps only Jobs himself can perceive it, and even Jobs might not be able to explain how his subconscious unique function formed. Tim Cook can only establish his own style to take Apple to a new level, but whether he can succeed remains uncertain. Because compared to Cook, although Jobs left behind a magnificent Apple building, if Cook wants to further elevate it and establish his own style, he must inject his own spirit. In this way, what Jobs left him might just be considered an unfinished building.

The measures taken by Cook since officially assuming the position of CEO on August 24th have provided some clues as to how he will run Apple in the coming years: Apple's future development will be more planned, rather than being dominated by Jobs' personal intuition as in the Jobs era. This statement by Cook should be regarded as his guiding principle for continuing to lead Apple forward. This guideline basically determines that Cook cannot become Jobs, because Cook will apparently make great efforts in establishing a team, meaning Apple's future will depend on the team led by Cook. This is obviously completely different from the previous Apple management team. Jobs' self-reliant management style forms a team method and Apple management team management method almost entirely opposite. Unless Cook removes most of the old employees from the management or fires them and rebuilds a new team.

It is said that in recent weeks, Cook reorganized Apple's large education and training department, which had long been outside the overall organizational structure of the company. Apple's education and training department has always been relatively independent. Cook split this department into sales and marketing functions and incorporated them into the corresponding company-level business departments. This move is about Apple's future training plans, using indoctrination to continue Apple's cause is like a dream for fools. Jobs never bothered to patiently instruct employees but instead scolded and utilized them. It may seem that Jobs is unsympathetic, but the excellent products created show that the customer is God, and showing sympathy to customers is real human sentiment.

For Cook to become another excellent Jobs from being an excellent CEO is almost impossible. Apple will once again fall into the trap of "discussion" in collective management, and Apple products' extremely "thin", extremely "beautiful", extremely "perfect" styles come from Jobs' unfeeling management style. Now Cook is introducing team management into Apple, which means that Apple's future products will popularize this "sharp" style; product popularization means that Apple's innovative traits will be destroyed in the masses, and that irresistible and stimulating innovation attraction will disappear. Apple will disappear, and Tim Cook will become the stray dog of Apple's acquisition.
