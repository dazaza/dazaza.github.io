Red bean paste green? Eye protection is not so simple

by ucibv623 on 2012-03-06 21:03:38

Rumor: In order to protect your eyesight, please make the following settings on your computer: Desktop-> Right Click-> Properties-> Appearance-> Advanced-> Item Selection (Window), Color1 (L) Selection (Other), change the hue to: 85. Saturation: 123. Brightness: 205 -> Add to Custom Colors-> Select OK in Custom Colors-> OK. This tone is configured by ophthalmologists and will effectively relieve eye fatigue over long periods of use while protecting your eyes.

Truth: Since we were young, we have been taught that after writing or reading for a period of time, we should look at green plants in the distance to protect our vision. This practice does indeed have some effect. Later, merchants based on this "common sense" promoted notebooks made from green writing paper, claiming they could prevent nearsightedness. The rumor about "bean paste green protecting eyesight" can be considered the modern version of green notebooks.

So are these methods really effective? Before discussing these issues, let's first define "green." Green is a common color in nature, one of the primary colors, with a wavelength between 520-570nm. In the RGB color model, the RGB value for pure green is (0, 255, 0), and any color with the second highest value in its RGB combination belongs to the green family. The bean paste green set as described in the beginning of the rumor has an RGB value of (199, 237, 204), which is similar to celadon green.

Let's search online for articles about green writing paper. Almost all vaguely state that green paper can effectively relieve visual fatigue. Indeed, green light is located at the center of the visible spectrum (http://www.chensite.com) and falls within the range of the most sensitive frequencies for human eyes [1]. Additionally, color psychology suggests that green can make people feel comfortable and calm [2]. However, none of this is sufficient to prove that green can protect vision, nor is there any relevant research supporting this claim.

From the perspective of contrast recognition [3], green paper does not have any special positive impact on vision. There are studies showing that under low saturation conditions, green paper and white paper do not significantly differ in their effects on near vision (referring to close-range eye resolution) [4]. On the contrary, if the paper color saturation is too high, it may reduce the contrast between text and background, making the text harder to distinguish, which could cause more eye strain. After searching, no reliable literature or experiments support the idea that using green paper books for extended periods can protect vision.

Looking at distant green plants to relax strained eyes is an effective way to prevent myopia. However, it's not so much the green color of the plants that relaxes the eyes but rather the fact that plants are much farther away than books. Focusing on distant objects puts the ciliary muscle in a relaxed state, giving it rest. Believing that green notebooks can effectively protect eyesight is less reliable than simply looking out the window more often.

As previously mentioned, green's ability to protect eyesight is not dependable. Setting the computer background to bean paste green might only make the screen brightness less glaring. For problems such as dry eyes caused by reduced blinking, or visual fatigue and upper body pain due to posture and screen distance, "bean paste green" has no effect.

Prolonged computer use can indeed lead to visual problems. The American Optometric Association (AOA) defines this as Computer Vision Syndrome (CVS) [6], also known as VDT syndrome. Its main symptoms include visual fatigue, blurred vision, dry eyes, headaches, neck pain, and shoulder discomfort. The main causes of these problems include overly bright or dim screens, inappropriate distance between eyes and the screen, improper posture, reduced blinking during computer use, and pre-existing vision problems in users.

Conclusion: There is no research supporting "green protects eyes," and this topic is rarely discussed on foreign websites, suggesting it is a local rumor possibly derived from the correct practice of "looking at distant green plants to protect eyesight." While setting the computer background color to bean paste green does no harm, relying on this operation to protect eyesight is unreliable.
