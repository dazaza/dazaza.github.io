Beijing adds 10 outlets for vehicle purchase tax

by aochun8383 on 2012-03-01 15:09:07

Recently, the Beijing State Taxation Bureau announced that 10 new collection sites for vehicle purchase tax would be added. Starting from May 10th, when buying a vehicle, taxpayers can choose to pay the vehicle purchase tax at any of the existing collection sites in Chongwen, Chaoyang, Haidian, Fengtai, and Changping districts or the newly added collection sites in Mentougou, Tongzhou, Shunyi, Daxing, Fangshan, Huairou, Miyun, Pinggu, Yanqing, and Development Zone. It is understood that the business scope of the newly added vehicle purchase tax collection sites includes: accepting domestic vehicle tax payment applications via credit card swipe as well as subsequent services such as tax refunds, replacement of lost tax payment certificates, change of ownership, transfer registration, etc., for vehicles that have completed tax payments. However, they do not accept domestic vehicle tax payment applications via cash or bank transfer; nor do they accept tax payment applications for imported vehicles or exempted vehicles. The above-mentioned vehicles include cars, motorcycles, electric vehicles, trailers, and farm vehicles. In addition, the business scope of the existing vehicle purchase tax collection sites in Chongwen, Chaoyang, Haidian, Fengtai, and Changping Districts remains unchanged.

On the morning of June 10th, the Standing Committee of the Beijing Municipal Committee of the Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference passed "Several Suggestions on Compiling the Twelfth Five-Year Plan for National Economic and Social Development of Beijing Municipality." Focusing on the compilation of the Twelfth Five-Year Plan, various specialized committees of the Beijing Municipal Committee of the Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference conducted research lasting more than half a year. The research report proposed suggestions on major issues related to the development of the capital during the Twelfth Five-Year Plan period, covering 22 specific contents including accelerating the transformation of economic development patterns and adjusting economic structures, urban construction and integrated urban-rural development, coordinated regional economic development, ecological environment construction, livelihood improvement, and social construction.

The increase in vehicle purchase tax and congestion fees are included in the "Research Report on Issues of Total Vehicle Control and Demand Management" conducted by the Urban Construction and Environmental Protection Committee. According to the report, as of February this year, Beijing had 4.132 million motor vehicles, among which there were 3.291 million privately owned small cars, with over 80% concentrated within the Sixth Ring Road. The average private car ownership per household in the four urban districts was 1.8 times that of comparable areas in Paris and 2.3 times that of New York. The survey showed that the annual average mileage of privately owned small cars in Beijing was 15,000 kilometers, more than twice that of Tokyo. The average annual cost of use was 10,681 yuan, with over 40% of trips being less than five kilometers. There was a clear phenomenon of excessive use of privately owned small cars. It took 48 years for Beijing’s motor vehicle ownership to reach one million vehicles, six and a half years to reach two million, three years and nine months to reach three million, and only two years and seven months to reach four million. Tokyo took 12 years to achieve this change.

According to the current intensity of private car use in Beijing, every net increase of one million private cars requires an additional 28.2 million kilometers of road network capacity, equivalent to the entire road network capacity within the Third Ring Road, and an additional parking area of 30 square kilometers, equivalent to half the area within the Second Ring Road.

Compared with the motorization development process of world-class cities, the development of motor vehicles in Beijing has the characteristics of "three highs": high-speed growth, high-intensity use, and high-density aggregation. The reasons for the "three highs" include "four lows": low threshold for purchasing vehicles, low cost of using small cars, low public awareness of green travel, and low service level of alternative modes of transportation.

The research report believes that currently, the annual growth rate of motor vehicles in Beijing has exceeded 500,000 units. Controlling the growth rate of motor vehicles at its root is the most effective, direct, and urgent measure. The government should quickly organize relevant departments to carry out total control work on motor vehicles, study and propose annual control indicators for automobile growth based on the progress of public transportation construction, increase the vehicle purchase tax, increase the cost of first-time car purchases, strengthen the management of vehicle registration, and strictly implement the method of managing vehicle registration according to parking space certificates. Increase parking fee standards, implement differentiated parking management fee policies, apply different parking rates in different areas and at different times, further expand the geographical scope of implementing differentiated parking prices, and increase the cost of using motor vehicles and suppress demand through measures such as charging congestion fees, pollution fees, and environmental taxes.

While suppressing motor vehicle demand, efforts should also be made to densify the urban rail transit network and promote the construction of transfer and connection supporting facilities. When planning new lines, facilities for transferring between ground buses, private cars, and bicycles should be built simultaneously. Efforts should also be made to increase the demarcation of bus-only lanes, especially in congested sections where the pace of marking bus-only lanes should be accelerated.

Strengthen the management of behaviors that encroach upon pedestrian walkways and bicycle paths, return the sidewalks to pedestrians, and return the bicycle paths to bicycles. Launch the "Half-Hour Green Travel" campaign to advocate walking and cycling within half an hour throughout the city.

How feasible is it to register vehicles based on parking spaces?

When it comes to the "implementation of vehicle registration management according to parking space certificates," many veteran drivers are familiar with this measure. A veteran driver recalled that starting January 1, 1998, the "Beijing Motor Vehicle and Driver Management Measures" came into effect. The Beijing Public Security Traffic Management Bureau stipulated that starting February 20, 1998, all car buyers must hold proof of parking space to complete vehicle registration procedures.

"At that time, you had to hold a three-part parking space certificate to handle registration procedures," one car owner recalled. The so-called "three-part parking space certificate" meant that when new car owners went to their district traffic team to fill out the "parking space certificate," one copy was kept by the owner, one by the traffic team, and another was sent by the traffic team to the Beijing Vehicle Management Office. It was learned that the initial purpose of introducing the parking space certificate was to strengthen the management of motor vehicles in the city, gradually standardize the parking order of motor vehicles in the city, and alleviate the current situation of urban traffic congestion.

However, things did not go as smoothly as expected. After this measure was introduced, it sparked heated discussions among many people. A veteran car owner recalled that he bought his car in 2002. "We all felt that the parking space certificate we paid a lot of money for was just a certificate, a necessary tool for registering a license plate and passing the annual inspection, but not a real parking space," he said. In November 2001, a report in the China Youth Daily stated that insiders believed that the parking space certificate did not achieve the desired results of the relevant authorities, but instead led to increased arbitrary charges and became a new means for parking lots to generate revenue. This was because the management of issuing parking space certificates seemed unclear and irregular. At that time, even advertisements and some cigarette vendors could offer "proxy services for parking space certificates." It was easier and cheaper to obtain these certificates through such channels.

A reporter found that when calling the consultation hotline of the Beijing Public Security Traffic Management Bureau's Vehicle Management Office, the consultants all claimed they had "never heard of" the "parking space certificate." A veteran driver recalled that this measure had been abandoned for four or five years.

Members of the Beijing Municipal Committee of the Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference once again proposed the implementation of vehicle registration management according to parking space certificates. But does this measure have practical significance? Wu Zhonghua, editor-in-chief of the Interactive Department of Yiche.com, said that this measure is theoretically feasible, but it might be difficult to implement in practice. "It sounds good, but there were reasons why it wasn't implemented before."

Wu Zhonghua believed that this measure might lead to a sharp rise in parking fees, and the private trading of "parking space certificates" could resurface. "If implemented, stricter supervision will be needed."
