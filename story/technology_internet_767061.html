The Story of a National Geographic Photographer and the Lumia 1020

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 17:33:09

Photographer Chase Jarvis once said: "The best camera is the one that’s with you."

In recent years, this statement has been regarded as a golden rule by the rising group of mobile phone enthusiasts. Mobile phones have never competed with professional cameras in terms of image quality, but their convenience in capturing daily life moments due to their always-available nature provides an advantage in sourcing material.

But does this saying hold true for a professional photographer?

Recently, Nokia attempted a bold project, some details of which were disclosed by Digital Trends.

Stephen Alvarez, a full-time photographer for National Geographic, usually works with mountains, seas, and other natural landscapes.

To complete his assignments, Alvarez typically carries at least three full-frame cameras along with several lenses. Not to mention the laptop, external hard drives, and a plethora of other photography equipment.

One day, Nokia invited him to take on a special collaboration: to go out and capture a series of landscape photographs, but with the condition of only carrying an unreleased smartphone. Alvarez found this requirement intriguing and readily accepted the challenge.

However, as more details of the collaboration unfolded, Alvarez began to feel uneasy. It turned out that one of the photos he was to take for Nokia would be used as a three-page spread inside the 125th-anniversary issue of National Geographic, which seemed to be beyond his usual scope?

With these doubts in mind, Alvarez received Nokia's secret product early — what later became known as the Lumia 1020.

At the time, the Lumia 1020 had not yet been released. They had three pre-production test units, making them among the first people in the world to use the phone. Nokia staff secretly demonstrated the device to Alvarez and his assistant in a hotel room in New York, giving them a week before departure to familiarize themselves with the product. As a professional photographer, Alvarez was astonished by the imaging quality of this 41-megapixel camera. At least now, he was willing to give it a try.

In June of this year, Alvarez chose to travel with his assistant to the southwestern United States, home to the Colorado Grand Canyon, because he was curious about how this camera would perform in front of grand and rugged natural scenery. They embarked on a ten-day photography journey. Like many companies do to maintain secrecy for unreleased products, the Lumia 1020 was housed in a metal casing, making it look quite unique. When they appeared at popular tourist spots like the Grand Canyon, no one noticed that they were using a Nokia phone.

During these ten days of work, Alvarez insisted on completing all tasks solely with the Lumia 1020, and the results did not disappoint him. With the help of its high pixel count, the intricate details of the canyon's rock formations were preserved in the photographs. Additionally, thanks to its large sensor, the camera performed admirably in complex lighting conditions and low-light situations.

Alvarez, who had never previously produced final images with any mobile phone, printed some of his works into 22-inch photographs and was astounded by the results. Even his colleagues at National Geographic couldn't believe these images were taken with a phone.

What comforted Alvarez most was that this "single-phone" light gear greatly reduced the burden of this mission: "In the Grand Canyon area, temperatures often reach 50 degrees Celsius, and adding another 20 kilograms of photographic equipment would be quite uncomfortable," he remarked.

So, in the end, would Alvarez abandon his DSLR camera?

The answer is clear: No! "Mobile phones can never replace professional cameras; they cannot switch lenses or set up complex flash systems," Alvarez added, "but if (phones) are to replace compact cameras, there is absolutely no problem with that."
