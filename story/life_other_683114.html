Why does the "school bus standard" continue to attract doubts?

Since the Gansu school bus accident in November 2011, the formulation of school bus safety standards has been placed on the agenda. On December 27, 2011, the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology announced that it was seeking public opinions on the draft of four national standards newly formulated by the National Automotive Standardization Technical Committee Passenger Car Sub-committee, including the "School Bus Safety Technical Conditions" (hereinafter referred to as the "Draft for Comments"), immediately sparking strong doubts from bus manufacturers, because although the "Draft for Comments" was organized and formulated by the National Automotive Standardization Technical Committee Passenger Car Sub-committee, it was largely drawn up by Zhengzhou Yutong Automobile, leading to the "Draft for Comments" adopting a large number of existing school bus standards from Yutong as industry standards.

In order to quell the doubts, on January 5 and January 11, 2012, consecutive discussion meetings were held with the participation of domestic mainstream bus manufacturers, whose opinions were largely adopted and made substantial revisions to the initial draft. On January 12, the "School Bus Safety Technical Conditions" and three other national standards were discussed and reviewed by the National Automotive Standardization Technical Committee, reducing them to two mandatory national standards: the "Special School Bus Safety Technical Conditions" (for review) and the "Strength of Special School Bus Seat Systems and Their Vehicle Fixtures" (for review).

After the four standards were reduced to two, there was no longer a clear specification for the position of the school bus engine, and the previous fierce debate over "long-nosed" and "flat-nosed" vehicles was settled, with the three-year transition period being shortened to one year. The thickness of the bumper and the height of the floor previously specified for special school buses were no longer clearly required, but the requirement for bus rollover tests was added, and the lateral tilt stability angle was increased from 32 degrees to 35 degrees.

However, the doubts did not stop there. Many university experts who did not participate in the formulation of the standards believed that the new standards did not specify the values and test methods of specific technical standards, which would affect subsequent implementation and enforcement. Professor Zhang Jinhuang, director of Tsinghua University's Collision Laboratory, believed that although the final draft proposed requirements for rollover protection of school buses, it lacked specific quantifiable indicators.

A billion-dollar market leads to more types of domestically produced school buses than in the US

Don't doubt this subheading. At the first China School Bus Exhibition held last week, even the staff of Blue Bird, the American school bus company present at the scene, marveled at the richness and diversity of models on display at the domestic school bus exhibition. The emergence of such a rich and varied range of school buses is not unrelated to the temporary lack of standards. Faced with the upcoming massive school bus market in the country, all companies have skills surpassing those of the Eight Immortals.

Commercial vehicle companies such as JAC Motors and Youngman did not participate in the exhibition, yet this school bus exhibition could still be considered a collective show of domestic school bus products. From small and medium-sized school buses with about 20 seats to large school buses with 60 seats, from "long-nose" school buses to school buses similar to flat-headed buses, they seem to have appeared overnight before everyone's eyes, collectively showcasing their talents. At the scene, reporters saw that most school buses are still modified based on existing buses, with no difference in appearance, engine layout, body, or chassis compared to existing buses. Many car factories spared no effort to bring out more school bus products, converting their existing buses into school buses by changing the outfit and seats for display. Vehicles specifically developed for school use became the focus of exhibitions by major automobile manufacturers. With engines positioned at the front and a protruding engine compartment, these products, which look closer to American school buses, attracted more professional audiences. Some car companies emphasized school bus management, believing that good management of school buses and drivers is more important than an incredibly sturdy small car.

Since the standards have not yet been issued, why have bus companies taken collective action without hesitation? The reason is simple: the scale of the market, worth hundreds of billions, is simply too tempting, and no one wants to lose the opportunity while waiting. According to estimates by She Zhenqing, deputy secretary general of the Bus Branch of the China Highway Society, China has 2862 county-level units, each roughly needing 300 to 500 school buses, with a total demand exceeding one million. Calculating at 200,000-300,000 yuan per school bus, the procurement cost alone will reach 200-300 billion yuan.

Foreign brands rush into the school bus market

The bustling Chinese school bus market also attracted sensitive foreign brands. Early school bus manufacturer Blue Bird, Navistar, the largest school bus manufacturer in the United States, and Allison Transmission, among other well-known international companies, are all prominently listed. It is understood that Navistar is a giant in the production of commercial vehicles and engines in North America, a globally renowned truck and car manufacturer, and ranked among the world's top 500. Currently, Navistar's comprehensive market share in North America is approximately 21%, with a 50% share in the school bus market. Navistar specializes in producing long-nosed vehicles, i.e., the typical "long-nosed" American school buses. In 2011, Navistar completed its cooperation negotiations with JAC Motors and will establish a joint venture factory in China. School buses are undoubtedly the product they want to bring to China the most. Relevant personnel from Navistar stated that they hope to quickly introduce North American school bus products and technology to China and gradually achieve localized production.

But in the absence of school bus standards, the Chinese school bus market has actually been dominated by Chinese bus companies, like Yutong, King Long, FAW, and Jinbei, whose buses have already been used by many schools. Although foreign brands have a long history of school bus manufacturing, they face difficulties entering the Chinese market on a large scale due to issues such as cost and understanding of local conditions in China.

Can children in remote areas ride school buses?

At the school bus exhibition, reporters learned that the pre-sale price of various domestically produced school buses ranges from 200,000 to 500,000 yuan, which is equivalent to an astronomical figure for some schools or school bus companies currently using mini-buses as school buses, let alone those in remote areas. And it is often in relatively remote areas where school buses are urgently needed, and school bus problems frequently occur. Children living in counties or rural areas have relatively longer distances to travel to school, especially under the policy of centralized enrollment, where many schools are moved to the center of the county, making the journey to school long and arduous for children in remote villages, thus requiring school buses. Without school bus standards, various vehicles can carry children to school, but once school bus standards are introduced, remote areas will be required to use nationally certified school buses. If schools and students cannot afford the cost of school buses, many children may face the situation of having no bus to ride.
