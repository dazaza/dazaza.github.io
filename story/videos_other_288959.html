Global Times reported on December 1 that the November 18th issue of the British Jane's Defence Weekly published a special article titled "Japan's New Dawn" under the "Country Briefings" section. The article first mentioned that with China's rise, Japan's new government has adjusted its policies towards the US after coming to power, paying more attention to participating in regional affairs, which has caused concern in the United States. The article then provides a comprehensive summary and interpretation of the current land, sea, and air capabilities of Japan's Self-Defense Forces. When discussing Japan's Air Self-Defense Force, it mentions that Japan's independently developed new stealth fighter aircraft, the "X-2 Shinshin," will conduct its first flight in 2013.

The article begins by saying that the new Japanese government led by Prime Minister Yukio Hatoyama is conducting a comprehensive review of the U.S.-Japan alliance relationship, which is bound to cause further concern in Washington. The center-left Democratic Party of Japan (DPJ), led by Yukio Hatoyama, defeated the Liberal Democratic Party (LDP) in the parliamentary election on August 30, ending the LDP's almost uninterrupted rule since its establishment in 1955 (the LDP was briefly out of power only in 1993 for half a century). The LDP seemed to have completed its historical mission in the post-Cold War era — supporting Japan to play a military role in helping the United States "counter the communist bastion" (China and the Soviet Union). Meanwhile, the U.S.'s "nuclear umbrella" protected Japan from potential enemies such as China, North Korea, and the Soviet Union; this move also reassured other countries in the region that had once been colonized or occupied by Japan that Tokyo would not return to the path of militarism.

Jane's mentioned that Junichiro Koizumi served as Japan's prime minister from 2001 to 2006, during which time Japan sought to strengthen its bilateral military and security relations with the United States. Japan's motivation for doing so was reinforced by China's military buildup, North Korea's nuclear demands, and the global threat of terrorism. Under the actions of then-U.S. President George W. Bush and Japanese Prime Minister Junichiro Koizumi, the U.S.-Japan security alliance relationship during this period was often referred to as the "golden age." Koizumi also dispatched the Japan Ground Self-Defense Force (JGSDF) to Iraq to support U.S. military operations.

The Jane's article then discusses how China's economic rise is changing the economic and industrial structures both globally and within the region. Japan's new Prime Minister, Yukio Hatoyama, is enthusiastic about the East Asian Community and aims to develop it into an Asian version of the European Union, advocating for a common Asian currency to adapt to the rapidly developing regional economic situation. The article then describes Japan's tendency to distance itself from the U.S. and prioritize closer ties with Asian countries, especially regarding issues concerning the status of U.S. troops stationed in Japan and specific base issues. The new Japanese government has made some different moves compared to previous prime ministers, whether it be the relocation of the Futenma (which houses the U.S. Marine Corps) base in Okinawa or the merger issues between the Futenma and Kadena bases, where there have been disagreements between the new Japanese government and the U.S. military side. Additionally, on October 16, the Japanese government decided to extend the deadline for formulating the new National Defense Program Outline (NDPO), originally planned to be completed by the end of the year, by one year until the end of 2010. These signs indicate that Japan's policy towards the U.S. may undergo significant adjustments.

Next, the article by Jane's enters the key section, using "Land forces", "Naval forces", and "Air forces" as subheadings respectively, providing a comprehensive summary and interpretation of the current land, sea, and air capabilities of Japan's Self-Defense Forces.
