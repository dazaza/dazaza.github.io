Relay to car decoder On April 2nd, a worker moved around near a cable shaft where cracks were discovered. According to comprehensive reports by Xinhua News Agency, in order to prevent highly radioactive water from flowing into the sea from the cable shaft near the water intake of Unit 2 at the Fukushima Daiichi nuclear power plant, Tokyo Electric Power Company (TEPCO) injected concrete and water-absorbing swelling materials to stop the discharge of contaminated water, but it was later found that the amount of water flowing into the sea did not decrease.

A 20-centimeter crack was found on the side wall of the cable shaft near the water intake of Unit 2 at the Fukushima Daiichi nuclear power plant on the 2nd day. Highly radioactive water flowed directly into the sea through this crack. On that day, TEPCO poured concrete into the shaft twice in an attempt to seal the crack, but failed, with contaminated water continuing to flow into the sea.

Detection data showed that the concentration of radioactive iodine-131 in the wastewater within the shaft was more than 10,000 times higher than normal levels in a reactor during operation. Japanese authorities believe that the contamination of seawater near the Fukushima Daiichi nuclear power plant is related to the wastewater leaking from the aforementioned cable shaft.

This shaft is made of concrete, about 2 meters deep, and workers can enter underground horizontal tunnels to check cables via the shaft.

The polymer swells 50 times after absorbing water. On the 3rd day, TEPCO drilled holes above the concrete underground tunnel next to the shaft and then injected these "high molecular polymers". This material, similar to the material used in baby "diapers", swells 50 times its original volume after absorbing water.

Workers simultaneously threw wood chips, paper, and cement into the shaft in hopes of stopping or delaying the leakage of accumulated "dirty water" from the shaft.

However, according to AP reports, as of the evening of the 3rd day, the latest leak-stopping method failed to stop the "dirty water" from leaking. But the staff on-site have not given up, and the final results may not be known until the 4th day.

Methods are being sought to quickly stop the leakage. Japanese Prime Minister's aide Hosono Goshi said at a press conference on the 3rd day that it would still take "several months" for the Fukushima Daiichi nuclear power plant to stop leaking radioactive substances. "After this phase (radioactive substance leakage stops), the next goal is to stabilize the nuclear power plant and install a perfect cooling device for the reactor."

Cabinet Secretary Edano Yukio said in an interview with the media that the government and TEPCO are looking for "other possible solutions to shorten the time needed to stop the nuclear leak."

Nuclear and Industrial Safety Agency spokesman Nishiyama said that TEPCO plans to start injecting nitrogen into the containment vessel of Unit 1 at the Fukushima Daiichi nuclear power plant as early as the 5th day to prevent hydrogen explosions.

According to Xinhua News Agency, TEPCO announced on the 3rd day that the bodies of two staff members who went missing after the March 11th great earthquake were found in the basement of the turbine building of Unit 4 at the Fukushima Daiichi nuclear power plant. This is the first time TEPCO has confirmed the death of Fukushima Daiichi nuclear power plant staff.

TEPCO said that the bodies of the two staff members were found on March 30th. They both had multiple external injuries, and it was speculated that they might have died from hemorrhagic shock around 16:00 on the day of the March 11th great earthquake. The victims were 24-year-old Kojo Kazuhiko and 21-year-old Terashima Takaki, who worked in the First Operation Management Department of the First Nuclear Power Plant before their death.

Witnesses at 02011808.com Promoting agricultural products in Tokyo

With unsold agricultural products due to restrictions, basic fruits and vegetables from Fukushima have been removed from shelves in Tokyo supermarkets.

On the morning of the 3rd day, at the transportation hall near Yurakucho Station in Tokyo, a promotional event for Fukushima agricultural and livestock products attracted a large number of Tokyo residents. Under the organization of the Japanese Agricultural Cooperatives, vegetable farmers, fruit farmers, and livestock farmers from Shirakawa City, Fukushima Prefecture, came to promote their products.

In the temporary exhibition area, there were plenty of cucumbers, tomatoes, strawberries, rice, beef, and other products from Fukushima. Among them, cucumbers sold the fastest. A farmer introduced that in just one morning, 2,000 cucumbers were sold out.

A Tokyo housewife named herself Matsumoto and bought bags of Fukushima-produced rice, vegetables, and beef slices. She said she believed these foods were not contaminated by radioactive substances. Fukushima is her hometown, and during such difficult times, she wanted to contribute to her hometown through her actions.

To alleviate consumer concerns, the exhibition area also provided radiation detectors. "Currently, what Fukushima farmers worry most about are various reports regarding radioactive contamination of agricultural products," said Suzuki Akio, president of the East-West Shirakawa Agricultural Cooperative of Fukushima Prefecture, hoping that this promotional event could reduce consumers' unease.

Since the nuclear leakage occurred at the Fukushima Daiichi nuclear power plant, surrounding areas' agricultural and livestock products have been found to exceed radioactive substance standards. On March 23rd, the Japanese government implemented "consumption restriction" measures, issuing a "halt shipment" order for all leafy vegetables, including spinach and cabbage, as well as broccoli and cauliflower produced in Fukushima Prefecture.

However, agricultural products from Fukushima Prefecture not covered by the order also suffered collateral damage. Some out-of-town wholesalers refused to accept agricultural products from Fukushima.

In a large supermarket in Tokyo, vegetables and fruits produced in Fukushima have basically disappeared from the shelves, and vegetables from other regions in Kanto have also decreased significantly compared to usual days. Additionally, imported vegetables and fruits have noticeably increased. Many customers repeatedly confirm the origin of the vegetables before placing them in their shopping carts.
