Should we legislate to ban school teachers from paid tutoring

by alexsgxu on 2009-10-27 17:22:49

Should there be legislation banning teachers from giving paid private tutoring?

By Xu Shuguang

Illustration by Tang Chuncheng

The "Draft Regulation on Compulsory Education in Shandong Province" (hereinafter referred to as the "Regulation") is currently open for public comment. The most controversial point of this legislative draft lies in its explicit stipulation that teachers currently employed may not engage in paid private tutoring or part-time work, thus sparking heated debates among educators, students, and parents. (China Youth Daily, October 17)

Teachers currently employed giving paid private tutoring has always been a focal point of discussion across various sectors of society. Despite much controversy, "paid private tutoring" continues to flourish nationwide like a spreading fire, with constant criticism directed at it. In some regions, the issue has even reached the level of legislative consideration. Recently, Zhejiang province proposed conditional legalization of paid private tutoring, while Shandong aims to legislatively ban teachers currently employed from engaging in paid private tutoring and part-time activities. These two places' starkly different legislative measures have once again pushed this sharp topic to the forefront.

So, should the matter of teachers giving paid private tutoring be banned or legalized? If we merely discuss whether it should be banned or legalized, I feel this itself represents significant progress. At least, we all acknowledge the importance of legal means, and discussions on this issue should take place within the framework of a rule-of-law society. What needs to be discussed is simply the wording of the law itself. Therefore, I believe the law should be established, and how to ban it should be clearly stated.

Several years ago, laws were already enacted to prohibit teachers currently employed from engaging in paid private tutoring, but this phenomenon persists and even worsens. Upon investigation, apart from the lack of enforcement strength in the law, the key reason is the continuous social demand. A "one-size-fits-all" approach obviously cannot fundamentally solve the problem. Rice plants in a field grow unevenly; for some students who cannot well absorb knowledge in class, they must be given "special attention" to keep up with the pace. Besides regular classroom teaching, extracurricular tutoring is necessary. Providing individual guidance tailored to each student's situation also aligns with the principle of education according to individual aptitude. For these tutorials beyond normal teaching, giving teachers appropriate compensation is fair and reasonable.

However, critics and opponents of paid private tutoring are not without valid points. Taxpayers and parents have already paid their children's tuition fees. If there are other temptations outside the classroom, it is difficult for teachers to ensure they do not act solely out of self-interest, withholding knowledge in class and reserving truly valuable content for paid private tutoring after school hours. In this way, students wanting to learn effectively would have to pay extra money, which precisely fosters unethical practices among teachers. Withholding knowledge in class and only revealing it during "private tutoring" for profit transforms the pure teacher-student relationship into a naked monetary transaction, a situation that deserves condemnation and severe crackdown; however, it cannot be denied that the vast majority of our teachers are conscientious and exemplary, not greedy for money.

Therefore, it seems that simple prohibition or legalization are both incorrect. My suggestion is that paid private tutoring can be conducted, but not for one's own students. Teaching one's own students well is a duty. If teachers seek part-time work or have spare capacity, they can contribute to other children in need of help. However, the prerequisite is that they must first perform their primary duties well. This requires specific schools to strengthen evaluations of teachers' work, ensuring from both workload and teaching quality that teachers indeed fulfill their responsibilities.
