**Hong Kong Company FAQs**

**Q: What conditions are required to set up a limited company in Hong Kong?**

A: At least one shareholder aged 18 or above is required. Mainland citizens or overseas individuals with a passport or ID card are eligible. A registered office address and a statutory secretary for the limited company (usually provided by us) are also needed.

**Q: How do I proceed with the procedures to set up a Hong Kong company?**

A: You just need to fill out a power of attorney form. The form includes three aspects:

1. Confirm the Chinese and English names of the company.

2. Determine the amount of registered capital, which has a minimum of HKD 10,000 (usually divided into 10,000 shares). The capital does not have to be fully paid, but can be increased according to actual needs.

3. Submit the shareholder's information and ID copy. If there are more than two shareholders, not every shareholder must become a director, but at least one director is required. After receiving your information on these three aspects, we will prepare all the necessary legal documents for you to sign within the next day. The process usually takes about 10 working days after signing the documents.

**Q: How long does it take to register a new company? How long does it take to buy an existing shell company? Can I change the name of an existing company? How long does it take to change the name?**

A: Registering a new Hong Kong company takes about 10 working days from the date of signing all legal documents; buying an existing company takes 1-2 working days (express service available on the same day); setting up a branch company takes 3 working days. After purchasing an existing company, you can change its name, which requires 15 working days.

**Q: What is the difference between a shareholder and a director?**

A: A shareholder is an investor in a joint-stock company, while a director is elected by shareholders and is responsible for managing the company. The chairman of the board is the head of the board of directors. Often, shareholders are also directors.

**Q: How is naming a Hong Kong company different from that of mainland China?**

A: Naming a Hong Kong company is relatively free. Companies that are difficult to register on the mainland may be registered in Hong Kong as long as the name is not duplicated. Regardless of the size of the registered capital, Hong Kong allows company names to include terms such as "international," "group," "holdings," "industry," "investment," "college," "association," "center," "foundation," and "institute." A preferred location can also be added before the company name, such as France, USA, Italy, Japan, Hong Kong, Shanghai, etc., or even personal names and hometowns, reflecting personalization.

**Q: Are there any restrictions on the business scope of a Hong Kong limited company?**

A: Hong Kong is one of the freest commercial trading ports in the world, and there are no significant restrictions on the business scope. Companies can engage in any type of business, such as finance and investment, healthcare, shipping, import-export trade, real estate, construction, interior decoration, information networks, clothing textiles, tourism, cultural publishing, etc. Many small and medium-sized enterprise owners do not specify their business scope to avoid limiting their operations.

**Q: Where is the registered capital of a Hong Kong company reflected? Is it necessary to pay the full amount?**

A: The minimum registered capital for a Hong Kong limited liability company is HKD 10,000, and there is no requirement to pay it upfront (no capital verification). The registered capital is only reflected in the company's articles of association. If clients want to prove their registered capital, they can simply show the articles of association, or our accountants can issue a credit certificate (CERTIFIED TRUE COPIES).

**Q: What services are included when setting up a Hong Kong limited company?**

A: The services include:

1. Government certificate fees (company registration certificate, business registration certificate)

2. Printed materials (18 copies of the articles of association, 1 share register, 1 set of application forms)

3. Seal costs (1 signature stamp, 1 steel seal, 1 alteration seal)

4. One year of registered office address fees

5. One year of statutory secretary service fees

6. Miscellaneous fees (document fees, courier fees, travel expenses)

7. Professional accounting service fees

**Q: How should we make the payment after confirming the establishment of an offshore company?**

A: Payment methods: You can pay in cash, transfer, wire transfer, check, or draft to our designated Hong Kong or mainland account. Cash payments can be made in Hong Kong, LA, KUALA LUMPUR, Shenzhen, Guangzhou, Chengdu, Yiwu, etc. Overseas individuals can remit funds to the Hong Kong account; mainland residents can remit funds to the Shenzhen account. Account details are available upon request.

Payment procedure: After filling out the power of attorney, customers must prepay 70% of the fees, with the remaining 30% paid upon completion of the company setup. The full stamp duty on registered capital (1/1000 of the registered capital) must be prepaid. Our agency and clients follow the same payment method.

**Q: After establishing a Hong Kong company, what annual fees are required?**

A: There are two main annual fees: annual review and financial reporting, as well as accounting and auditing.

1. Annual review and company registration fees must be paid each year. Prices fluctuate based on government adjustments, typically ranging between HKD 3,000-5,000. These fees must be paid even if the company has no business activities.

2. Accounting and auditing are required if the company operates and generates business. If the company has no business activities, then accounting and auditing are not required, but tax filing is still mandatory.

**Q: Who is responsible for the annual accounting and auditing of a Hong Kong company? If handled by you, what documents are required? Specific charges? How long does it take?**

A: Accounting can be done by the company itself or entrusted to a Hong Kong accounting firm. Auditing must be handled by certified accountants or auditors. If we handle it, you must provide all business records: ledgers, invoices, receipts, payment vouchers, bank statements, accounts receivable/payable. Generally, bookkeeping takes about one month, and auditing takes 1-2 months.

**Q: What is the difference between statutory secretary services and business secretary services?**

A: According to government regulations, a limited company must have a statutory secretary, who can be either a local individual or corporation in Hong Kong. We can help arrange the statutory secretary for you. A statutory secretary is a formal secretary required by the Hong Kong government, facilitating communication with the government and handling official documents.

Business secretary services are tailored to client needs, offering practical business support, including answering calls, relaying messages, sending/receiving mail, letters of credit, logistics, etc. The advantage of business secretary services is that it helps establish your corporate image, equivalent to having your own employees, office space, and a complete set of corporate resources in Hong Kong, making business operations smoother.

**Q: If our company has no office or staff in Hong Kong, how should we manage and operate after establishing the Hong Kong company?**

A: Our business secretary services are specifically designed for this purpose. For more details, please refer to the business secretary service details.

**Q: What is the taxation situation for Hong Kong enterprises?**

A: Hong Kong is a low-tax free port. The only tax companies must pay annually is profits tax, which is calculated at 17.5% of net profit. For example, if a company's annual revenue is HKD 10 million, gross profit is HKD 2 million, and net profit is HKD 1 million, the profits tax payable would be HKD 100 * 17.5% = HKD 175,000. If the company does not make a profit, it does not need to pay taxes.

**Q: Are products entering or leaving Hong Kong taxed?**

A: Except for tobacco and alcohol, goods generally entering or leaving Hong Kong are not taxed. However, all imports and exports must be declared. Customs duties are charged at 0.5% (for goods valued up to HKD 470,000) and 0.3% (for goods valued over HKD 470,000).

**Q: Are there any taxes to be paid when setting up a company?**

A: Yes. When setting up a Hong Kong company, you must pay the stamp duty on registered capital. This is charged at 1/1000 of the registered capital. For example, if the registered capital is HKD 1 million, the stamp duty would be HKD 1,000, which is paid once to the government during registration and does not need to be paid again.

**Q: Which department issues invoices and receipts for Hong Kong companies?**

A: Invoices and receipts for Hong Kong companies are not issued by the tax authority but are printed and issued by the company's board of directors based on the nature of the business. Invoices, receipts, and other documents become effective once stamped with the company seal and signed by the responsible person.

**Q: Can all shareholders of my Hong Kong company be mainland residents if I plan to use it to set up a foreign joint venture or wholly-owned enterprise in the mainland?**

A: Generally, a Hong Kong company can consist entirely of mainland shareholders. However, if you want to set up a foreign joint venture or wholly-owned enterprise, the Ministry of Foreign Trade and Economic Cooperation (MOFTEC) usually invites at least one overseas shareholder to sign the relevant documents. Therefore, arranging for an overseas shareholder to join the company is advisable.

**Q: Can our newly established Hong Kong company directly set up a foreign joint venture or wholly-owned enterprise in mainland China?**

A: Generally, foreign investors coming to China must have been in operation for over a year. Specific requirements depend on local policies.

**Q: Can a company account be set up in a foreign bank within China if registered in Hong Kong? For example, through a Hong Kong bank’s Shenzhen branch?**

A: Yes. After your company is established, the directors can open an offshore account with a foreign bank in China using their mainland ID cards. However, the directors must personally visit the bank to open the account.

**Q: How are various daily tasks like tax filing handled for Hong Kong companies? Can we entrust you with these tasks? What are the annual fees?**

A: In Hong Kong, tax filing is done once a year. The specific fees depend on your company's actual situation. If you choose our business secretary service, tax filing is included without additional charges. However, accounting and auditing require separate fees, which are determined by the turnover and the volume of documents. If the company has no business activities, there are no additional fees.

**Q: How is the tax rate for Hong Kong companies calculated? What is the specific tax filing deadline?**

A: The tax rate in Hong Kong is calculated at 17.5% of actual profits. Hong Kong companies typically use March 31st as the fiscal year-end, and you can file taxes up to one year later.

**Q: What do the terms CORP and BODY CORPORATE mean on the business registration certificate?**

A: The term CORP under the business nature section means there is no restriction on a particular business type; any business allowed under the Companies Ordinance can be conducted. Alternatively, you can specify a business type such as electronics, chemicals, or trade. BODY CORPORATE refers to the legal status of a corporate entity.

**Q: If I need to rent an office, how should I proceed?**

A: Depending on your needs, we offer services such as company incubation, desk rental, office rental, or entire floor rental. Just let us know your requirements, and we will promptly find suitable office space for you.

**Q: Can I check the background information of a Hong Kong limited company online?**

A: Currently, this is not possible. Anyone with questions about a company can visit the Companies Registry in Hong Kong's Admiralty district for inquiries.

**Q: After setting up a publishing house, how can I legally publish books?**

A: After setting up a publishing house, you are qualified to apply for publishing. However, two steps remain: applying for an International Standard Book Number (ISBN) or International Standard Serial Number (ISSN), and registering as a publisher before publishing. Once these steps are completed, you can legally publish books. If you wish to distribute in mainland China, you can apply to the relevant authorities as a Hong Kong publisher.

**Q: What are ISBN and ISSN? What is the difference between them?**

A: ISBN stands for International Standard Book Number, and ISSN stands for International Standard Serial Number. Having these numbers indicates that your publications meet international standards and are registered with the ISBN and ISSN international organizations. These numbers allow you to query publication background information anytime. The fundamental difference lies in the target audience: ISBN is issued to book publishers, while ISSN is issued to magazine publishers.

**Q: If business is poor, how much must I pay annually if I apply to suspend the company?**

A: A Hong Kong company can apply for temporary suspension at any time. Apart from paying the annual report fee to the Companies Registry and the annual review fee to the Inland Revenue Department, no other fees are required. If market conditions improve, the company can resume operations immediately, saving many costs.

**Q: Can my Hong Kong company be dissolved?**

A: Yes. If your company is no longer in use and you do not cancel it, the Hong Kong government will assume the company continues to exist and engages in illegal operations. The government may impose cumulative fines on illegally operating companies, and serious cases could result in imprisonment for the board. Since dissolving a company also incurs fees, we recommend selling the company as the best option.
