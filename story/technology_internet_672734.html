Wine Knowledge (Part 6): How to Identify Imported Wines Through Labels

by allenbough222 on 2012-02-23 10:10:37

Nowadays, there are many slogans in the market such as original bottle import, original equipment import, and origin country winery filling etc. If you do not have certain knowledge of identifying imported wines, it is really difficult to distinguish between true and false. Here I will teach you a few basic identification methods:

First, for all imported products, China Customs requires them to paste obvious Chinese labels. Therefore, the back of the original imported wine will have an obvious Chinese label. If there is no regular Chinese label, it indicates that its import channel is unclear.

Second, you can judge whether it is imported wine according to the barcode. The first few digits of the barcode represent a country: mainland China 690-692, South Africa 600-601, Spain 84, France 300-379, Portugal 560, Australia 930-939, Austria 900-919, Chile 780, New Zealand 94, Romania 594, Italy 80-83, Hong Kong 489, Argentina 779, Greece 520, Germany 400-440, USA Canada 00-09, Singapore 888, UK 50, South Korea 880, India 890. If the barcode on the Chinese label starts with [69], but it is still sold as imported wine, then it means that this so-called original imported wine is definitely not true. It is very likely to be made from imported grape juice filled domestically.

Third, imported wines have a special feature: there is a wine belly under the bottle! If you observe carefully, you will find that sommeliers generally put their thumbs under the bottle when pouring wine. That is because the thumb is the coldest part of the human body and has the least effect on the original taste of the wine.

But to choose high-cost-performance imported wines, you need to look at the following four points. We will take French wine as an example.

1. Look at the wine grade on the label. The French wine law divides wine into four levels: AOC is the highest level of French wine. When we get a bottle of wine, we can identify whether it belongs to AOC level from the label of the wine bottle: the label is marked as Appellation + region name + Controlee. Generally speaking, the first and last words of this mark do not change, while the middle word changes according to different regions. If both are AOC-level wines, they can be further divided into many levels. In general, the smaller the place name in the middle, the better the wine.

2. Look at the year of the wine. Compared with wines from other countries, French wines are more complex and it is not necessarily good wine just because it is AOC level. Good wine must come from a good year. Because the quality of wine depends on the quality of the grapes, and the quality of the grapes is determined by the origin and the climate and management of the grapes in that year. Since 2000, the good years have been 2000, 2003, 2004, and 2005. So when you are confused by the French wine label, choosing wine by year may be a good idea. The year on the label indicates that the raw material grapes were harvested in that year. And some people who know a little about wine overly pursue the year, thinking that the older the year, the better the wine. Of course, this is not wrong, but such wine is mostly used for investment and collection rather than drinking. In the French wine industry, 90% of the products cannot withstand long-term storage and should be consumed within a few years to ten years after leaving the factory. And these wines are also good wines.

3. Identify original imported wine and domestic bottled wine, bottle appearance. Is the printing on the bottle label clear? Is it counterfeit or reprinted? Is there anything unusual about the bottle cap? Are there any signs of being opened? Does the international barcode on the back label start with 3? Domestically bottled ones start with 6. Is there a Chinese label on the back label? According to Chinese law, all imported food must have a Chinese back label. If there is no Chinese back label, it may be smuggled import and the quality cannot be guaranteed. Is the color of the wine unnatural? Are there any unknown floating objects in the wine? (Note: a small amount of sediment at the bottom of the bottle is normal crystallization) The wine turns bad when the color becomes turbid. Open the bottle and see if the text on the cork is the same as the text on the bottle label. In France, bottles and corks are dedicated. Smell the smell of the wine. If the wine has a strong smell like nail polish, it has gone bad. Taste the taste of the wine. Drink the first sip of wine. The wine is smooth when passing through the throat, and problematic wine has a stimulating feeling. After swallowing the wine, if the remaining odor in the mouth has a chemical smell or a foul smell, it is abnormal.

Introduced by a friend, I got in touch with a French red wine called Ai Ling Castle. After drinking it, I felt it was quite good. The taste was richer, and even if I drank a lot, it didn't make me dizzy. The taste was different from some domestic wines. Next time, I will recommend it to my friends.
