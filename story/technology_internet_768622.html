WeChat chases and chases, and there's still WhatsApp ahead.

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 20:14:39

Tencent released its third-quarter financial report yesterday, disclosing that the combined monthly active user base of WeChat and WeChat International (Weixin) had reached 271.9 million. This figure represents an increase of 77 million users compared to when it was first disclosed in May. Within just six months, WeChat has achieved such a scale of new users, indicating that it is still growing rapidly.

In terms of monthly active users (MAU), only WhatsApp from the U.S. stands ahead of WeChat. (Facebook and Twitter are not instant messaging tools, but in fact, WeChat's MAU has already surpassed Twitter's 232 million MAU.) The latter announced at last month’s Nokia World conference in Dubai that its monthly active users were 350 million. Competitors like LINE and KakaoTalk only disclose registered user numbers and do not publicly share their MAUs. In terms of registered users, WeChat has long surpassed LINE, KakaoTalk, or Kik Messenger.

WhatsApp Becomes WeChat's Biggest Obstacle

Tencent has disclosed WeChat's MAU data three times so far, all accompanying financial reports. WeChat's biggest competitor, WhatsApp, has disclosed its MAU data four times. It is difficult to say whether one influenced the other to reveal more user data — both WeChat and WhatsApp were very low-key before 2013, rarely disclosing registered users or active user numbers, which contrasts sharply with LINE and KakaoTalk — but as more data emerges, the public gains a clearer understanding of their development status, much like how Apple and Samsung's lawsuits provide deeper insights into their operations.

The following is my compilation of the monthly active user data publicly disclosed by WeChat and WhatsApp. The green bar on the left is unofficial data provided during the "WeChat fee" storm in March this year, based on competitive analysis conducted by China Mobile. It is for reference only. The rest are official data from WeChat and WhatsApp.

In the chart above, WhatsApp's 200 million MAU is an approximate number, announced by its CEO Jan Koum at an AllThingsD conference this year. He simply stated that WhatsApp's monthly active users had exceeded Twitter's, which reached 200 million active users on December 18 of last year (currently at 232 million). Therefore, during the charting process, April 16 was "considered" as the time when WhatsApp reached 200 million MAU, though the actual date might be earlier.

From the chart, we can see that both WhatsApp and WeChat are progressing according to their own momentum. WeChat/Weixin's monthly active users increased by 77 million over six months. WhatsApp's monthly active users increased by 150 million over the same period, double that of WeChat. Although WeChat has made remarkable progress, WhatsApp, leveraging its inertia advantage, still grew faster than WeChat and has become WeChat's largest competitor in the messaging app category.

A Quiet Giant More Low-Key Than WeChat

However, WhatsApp remains a very low-profile "silent giant." The four instances of MAU data shown in the chart were all announced by its CEO either through media or third-party platforms (such as Nokia World or AllThingsD conferences). Their official Twitter account is only used to announce another set of data: the number of messages users send daily.

October 31, 2011: 1 billion messages sent daily

August 23, 2012: 10 billion messages sent daily

December 31, 2012: 18 billion messages sent daily

June 13, 2013: 27 billion messages sent daily

August 6, 2013: 31 billion messages sent daily (announced by CEO, official account not updated)

After facing competition pressure from latecomers like WeChat, WhatsApp has started making more appearances recently. They have updated their monthly active user count four times this year. However, WhatsApp has never disclosed its registered user numbers. According to The Next Web's estimate, with 300 million active users, the registered user count should be between 500 million and 1 billion. In contrast, WeChat has also stopped updating its total registered user count after reaching 300 million in January this year. Both parties seem to officially recognize each other as competitors.

Nevertheless, WhatsApp still holds a first-mover advantage. It launched as a messaging app in 2009, making it the pioneer of such products, two years ahead of WeChat. Born in the U.S., it quickly gained users in Europe and expanded to Asia. WhatsApp's first-mover advantage is a barrier that latecomers find hard to overcome, especially among mature European and American users who are less likely to switch platforms after building social connections. — By comparison, WeChat, LINE, and KakaoTalk have achieved rapid growth outside Europe and America, particularly in Asia.

In a June statistic from Onavo (acquired by Facebook in October this year), WhatsApp's first-mover advantage is clearly marked.

WhatsApp's Crisis

WhatsApp has remained relatively focused, even appearing stubborn and outdated as similar competitors emerged. It wasn't until August this year—four years after its founding—that WhatsApp officially launched voice message functionality. Meanwhile, WeChat and LINE had developed similar features early on and had already introduced video calling. Especially, WeChat, LINE, and KakaoTalk have become platforms for distributing digital content, while WhatsApp insists on being only an "instant messaging tool."

This positioning is closely related to the founder and CEO Jan Koum. At an AllThingsD event in April this year, he expressed strong disdain for advertisements. During an ATD interview in August, he reiterated his thoughts: "We polish everything repeatedly," he said. "We don't plan to launch a big gaming platform. Every product detail is so important; I spent six months making sure that the voice message feature runs smoothly on all platforms."

Focusing on utility applications is WhatsApp's clear brand label and the greatest affirmation from its existing users. However, on the other hand, it could be WhatsApp's biggest potential risk, mainly manifested in two trends.

Firstly, smartphones are gradually completing the revolution against traditional feature phones. According to a survey by Ericsson, in the third quarter of this year, global smartphone shipments accounted for 55% of total phone shipments and 25-30% of existing phone users. By 2019, smartphones will account for 60% of the overall mobile user base—not just new users (as shown in the graph below).

The further popularization of smartphones—IDC data shows that the average selling price (ASP) of smartphones dropped by 12.5% in the third quarter this year—the decrease in ASP will accelerate smartphone adoption—will weaken WhatsApp's traditional advantages. WhatsApp is renowned for its unified cross-platform experience. At the Nokia World conference in October, Koum mentioned that there were 250,000 new users on the Asha platform, meaning 7.5 million new Symbian users per month. WhatsApp also has many users on the BlackBerry platform, with its users scattered across various platforms. However, the current trend in smartphone development is towards iOS, Android, and Windows Phone, with other platforms gradually being marginalized. When these edge platforms switch to smartphones, they may be attracted by other products. — Of course, the social relationships accumulated by WhatsApp in its early stages can mitigate this impact to some extent.

Secondly, the increase in younger user demographics brings more diversified demands for smartphones. WhatsApp launched in 2009, and its early users were not typical smartphone users but rather SMS users of traditional phones. This group tends to be older, more conventional in thinking, and less accepting of new things compared to younger generations. They prefer "pure" communication tools, even if they have to pay an annual fee of $0.99.

But young people who have grown up with the Android wave since 2011 have inherently more diverse demands for smartphones, not just satisfied with "utility applications." Building a digital content platform to distribute games, music, videos, or text content better meets their needs. This is why WeChat, LINE, and KakaoTalk have quickly grown in the Asian market—China, Japan, and South Korea are the fastest-growing smartphone markets under the Android wave. Meanwhile, the rapid smartification changes in Southeast Asian and East Asian countries have been seized by WeChat and LINE. In the U.S. market, the rise of emerging social tools like SnapChat and Vine is also diverting young users. Data from Onavo shows that the user activity of WhatsApp on the iOS platform dropped from 9.25% in May to 7.97% in August.

The trend of the smartphone wave and the youth-oriented IT consumption will be the crisis that WhatsApp faces. In fact, WhatsApp is changing. In July this year, the company announced that users could download WhatsApp for free, with the annual fee due only after one year. In August, it launched the voice message function. However, despite claiming product supremacy, WhatsApp's changes are still too slow. There have been almost no major functional changes in the past four years, and now it has become a follower in product innovation—although its CEO dislikes advertisements and gaming platforms, product functions should not lag behind.

If WhatsApp moves left, sticking to its tool nature; then WeChat moves right, reinforcing its platform attributes. In yesterday's earnings call, Tencent President Martin Lau emphasized that the focus would shift to "ensuring users interact more with the WeChat platform," both domestically and internationally, strengthening the platform attributes of WeChat/WeChat International and building an ecosystem that retains users.

Thus, WeChat seems unwilling to be another Genghis Khan, capturing and defending every territory. As for WhatsApp, leading the pack, what does it have to defend its kingdom?—Especially when it faces competition not just from WeChat alone.
