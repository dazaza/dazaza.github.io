The posture and demeanor of the photographed person

1. Standing posture portraits: Standing posture is one of the most common postures in portrait photography. It can fully display the beauty of the human body line. There is a large degree of freedom and many poses. When posing for standing portraits, note the following points:

A. One leg should be straight while the other is free to cross or slightly lift. The straight leg should not bend, and the lifted leg should maintain body balance. Adjusting legs and feet is the basis for adjusting the upper part of the body.

B. The head, chest, and hips should not normally be in a straight line. Let the main body line form a curve or S-line.

C. The planes formed by the head, chest, and hips should generally not be on the same plane. For example, if the chest block faces forward, the face should turn to the side. If the chest turns sideways, the buttocks should turn forward.

D. Normally, posing starts from the feet.

2. Sitting posture portraits: Compared to standing portraits, sitting portraits have more limitations but can form beautiful curves and help ease the tension of the subject. Sitting portraits are divided into three types based on the angle to the camera: oblique side sitting posture, back sitting posture, and side-back sitting posture. Based on the angle formed by the upper and lower body, they can be further divided into right-angle sitting posture, obtuse-angle sitting posture, and acute-angle sitting posture. Based on the crossing style of the legs, they can be further divided into thigh-crossed and calf-crossed styles. When shooting sitting portraits, note the following points:

A. Sitting portraits are suitable for expressing static emotions. The expression of the subject should be reserved and contained.

B. Handling the joints is crucial. Front-facing sitting postures are rarely used as knees pointing towards the lens make thighs appear short and clumsy.

C. Handling the spine is very important. Emotions are written on the back, especially in side-back and back sitting postures. When emotions are high, the spine is straight. When emotions are low, the spine curls significantly. In normal conditions, the spine has a slight curve. When sitting, photographers should remind subjects not to sit too firmly and keep their backs off the chair back. Support the upper body with the spine.

D. Sitting portraits do not easily show body shape, so loose and baggy clothing is generally not chosen. To better show body curves and limb lines, tight-fitting clothing is recommended.

E. When posing sitting postures, first find the most appropriate body proportion in the lens. Only when proportions are normal will the visual sensation be comfortable. Smooth and complete lines are an important issue in handling sitting postures. Sitting postures often interrupt extending body curves, making lines appear fragmented and disordered. However, sitting postures can more calmly display the advantages of the upper body shape, using the outer contour features of the upper body to show body shape combined with harmonious cooperation of limbs to form relatively complete and smooth body curves. Be careful not to show twisted lines caused by rolling and squeezing in exposed parts such as arm bends, waist abdomen, and thighs.

3. Double-person posing: Double-person group photos are often encountered in wedding photography. Compared to single-person photos, double-person photos have more diverse beautiful pose designs. Not only does it include changes in the individual blocks, lines, and expressions of each person, but also the interaction between people. When designing double-person beautiful poses, do not forget the response and change in spirit and form between the two. When shooting double-person wedding photos, note the following aspects:

A. When designing double-person beautiful poses, attention should be paid to the changes in body blocks between each other. From the perspective of human structure, the human body is divided into three major body blocks: head, chest, and hips. When studying double-person beautiful poses, we divide the two people's body blocks into two groups. Generally, there are six basic structural combinations relative to the camera: woman facing forward and man sideways, woman half-sideways matching man half-sideways, woman half-sideways against man half-sideways, man sideways and woman sideways, man facing forward and woman facing forward, man backside and woman backside. On the basis of these six basic combinations, further fine-tuning can produce countless beautiful pose designs.

B. When designing double-person beautiful poses, attention should be paid to the changes in the lines of the picture. Lines in the picture have different meanings. For example, straight lines give the feeling of being straightforward and majestic, suitable for representing male beauty. Curves give a gentle and peaceful charm, suitable for representing delicate environments or gentle female beauty. Slanting lines have a sense of motion. If slanting lines occupy a small amount in the picture, the sense of motion is less. If slanting lines increase, the entire picture's sense of motion becomes stronger. When the tilt level of slanting lines is not too large, the sense of motion also becomes relaxed.

Four, Beautiful posing should be consistent with the character expression and environmental atmosphere.

In portrait photography, the posture and expression of the photographed person and the artistic space where the person is located are interrelated. Different environments where the photographed person is located result in different beautiful pose designs. For example, when showing the photographed person in a warm environment, the person's posture design should be softer. When the photographed person is in a lively environment, the beautiful pose design should be more unrestrained.

In portrait photography, the posture design of the photographed person and other elements in the picture form a complete whole. Any change in any element in the picture will affect the beautiful pose design of the photographed person. Photographers must not study beautiful pose designs without considering the actual situation of the photographed person and their environment.
