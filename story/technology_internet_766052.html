Google Agonistes: The Aftermath of Brin's Affair

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 14:41:02

"Hey, have you heard? Our company's founder is having an extramarital affair!" "Haha, of course, it's big news!" "Haha!" "It seems to be a complicated story. The sad male lead decided to join a Chinese company, did you see the follow-up?" "Yes, Xiaomi. Because he was betrayed by the other woman." "So will they get a divorce?" "Maybe."

"What about that sad male lead? Is he important to Google? I mean: will his departure be a big deal for Google?"

"I think not. I mean: people leave Google every day, there are a lot of smart guys here. Things will pass quickly, our stocks performed well today, no drop, so the market is saying 'who cares'..."

This conversation took place at around 2 AM last Friday with an excited American employee at Google. To be honest, writing something like this feels a bit like Gossip Girl and makes me uncomfortable. After all, this is a very personal and sorrowful piece of news, but it indeed hit the entire Silicon Valley in a flash.

Since AllthingsD under The Wall Street Journal first disclosed the marital issues of Google co-founder Sergey Brin at 3 PM on August 28th U.S. time, CBS News from the CBS television and radio network followed up quickly with a huge report titled "Silicon Valley is being rocked by a reported love affair".

In addition, MailOnline, one of the world's most visited newspaper websites, presented Brin’s marital issues as if it were an episode of the American TV drama Gossip Girl set in the west: large photos and concise text. After reading through all the information, the basic facts can be summarized as follows: Brin married his wife Anne Wojcicki in 2007, they have two children, and both are 40 years old this year. Brin's current net worth is $22.8 billion, while Wojcicki is the founder and CEO of the personal genomics and biotechnology company "23andMe".

A few months ago, the high-profile couple in Silicon Valley began living separately, but it wasn't known to the public until last week when Brin was exposed to be dating Amanda Rosenberg, an employee at Google. Meanwhile, there was no indication whether Brin's marital issues had any direct relation to Rosenberg. Soon after, Hugo Barra, Rosenberg's ex-boyfriend, announced that he would be heading to Beijing in October to work for the Chinese smartphone manufacturer Xiaomi. Barra was the Vice President of Product Management for Android at Google and led the launch of the Google Nexus tablet.

Prior to this, Rosenberg's name was always associated with Barra. Rosenberg, 26 years old, identifies as Asian-British and joined Google London in the summer of 2008. Two years later, she became the manager responsible for the development of mobile business in Europe, the Middle East, and Africa. Last January, Rosenberg moved from London to work at Google's headquarters in Silicon Valley and was promoted to the marketing manager of Google Glass within seven months.

In her personal blog, Rosenberg described her initial thought of working in the U.S.: “Hello new boss, hello new team… immediately liking me (all caps).” Over these two years, Rosenberg has been described as playing a key role in the development of Google Glass, such as coming up with the catchphrase “OK Glass” for the new product.

Barra, who announced he was leaving for China, said that he decided to start a new chapter in his career and praised his colleagues at Google as “the most amazing group of people he has ever worked with.”

Due to Brin and Anne Wojcicki signing a "pre-nuptial agreement" before their marriage, Brin's marital issues are considered to have little impact on Google's development. Barra's departure is seen as the only result (though there are also claims that these two events are unrelated), and despite Susan Wojcicki, Anne's sister, being a senior executive at Google as the Senior Vice President of Google's Advertising and Commerce departments, considering the Americans' matter-of-fact style, the subsequent impact of this story on Google should be minimal.

However, quite a number of people expressed disapproval of Brin's second spring. This isn't based on morality but rather criticizes Brin's behavior for increasing potential conflicts of interest within Google, harming employee morale, and violating company relationship rules.

According to Google's code of conduct, romantic relationships between employees are neither prohibited nor encouraged. "Romantic relationships between senior managers and junior employees can create countless problems," a report on Yahoo Finance quoted an opinion in the U.S. tech community, stating, "from the company's perspective, dating subordinates is usually a bad idea because it could potentially damage employee morale and violate confidentiality agreements."

"Why are there usually policies discouraging such relationships?" a former IBM executive and current director of the Applied Ethics Center at Santa Clara University in Silicon Valley said: "This is because such relationships often produce different levels of power, which may lead to undue pressure and conflicts of interest."

Sometimes, these relationships can explode and cause significant harm, such as former HP CEO Mark Hurd allegedly having an inappropriate relationship with a woman who worked for him, leading to his resignation. The woman claimed that Hurd pressured her into having sex with him, and although HP's board found no evidence of any violations of the company's sexual harassment policy, Hurd was held accountable for other violations and eventually settled with the woman.

"However, unless those startling 'fee stories' (financial risks caused by emotional disputes) emerge, Brin's new relationship might not be so serious," pointed out a professor specializing in leadership and psychology at George Washington University, "unless something else with major impact hits Google, my guess is: this isn't really a problem and will be forgotten in the next news cycle."

Notably, Brin's new relationship with Rosenberg may also be related to the timing and deepening affection during the development of Google Glass, which Google is vigorously promoting. Google Glass is regarded by the U.S. tech community as an expansion of Google's business model, meaning: Google is beginning to make profits from consumers instead of advertisers, shifting from a free search engine service for users to collecting money from consumers like Apple does.

In other words, Google Glass represents a new way for Google to utilize its core advantages to reach users: on one hand, Google Glass signifies that Google already possesses advanced consumer products; on the other hand, behind Google Glass lies Big Data, and all internet companies' core advantage is the massive amount of data in their backend systems.

The shift towards electronic consumer goods is probably a trend currently shown in the U.S. tech industry. In July last year, during a "Android developer meetup" event in Silicon Valley, one topic we discussed in our free time was how "Google, Apple, Amazon, and others, after completely transforming the communications industry, have all begun gradually transitioning into Consumer Electronics companies." Of course, it's still too early to say this about Facebook, and Apple has always been an exception that's hard to categorize—it has always been an electronic consumer goods company.
