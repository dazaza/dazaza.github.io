How to treat pelvic inflammatory disease

by abc12344d8u on 2012-03-06 18:16:28

How is pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) treated? PID is often a mixed infection caused by more than one pathogen. Although pathogens can spread through the blood or lymph, sometimes they directly spread from surrounding tissues or organs. However, the vast majority of PID cases are caused by pathogens in the vagina traveling along the mucosal surface to reach the pelvic organs. Inflammation of the reproductive organs and surrounding tissues is often not isolated but rather mutually influential, inflaming simultaneously.

The main causes of PID include:

1. Persistent abnormal vaginal discharge: due to untreated infections of trichomonas, fungi, gonococcus, mycoplasma, chlamydia, and genital warts in the reproductive tract. In recent years, the rate of such infections has gradually increased.

2. Poor menstrual hygiene: such as sexual intercourse during menstruation, using unclean sanitary products, and contact with contaminated toilet seats.

3. Post-procedural infections in the uterine cavity: such as blind curettage, tubal flushing, hysterosalpingography, intrauterine device insertion/removal, and hysteroscopy causing pelvic infections.

4. Postpartum or post-abortion infections: pre-existing inflammation that was not controlled before childbirth or abortion, and sexual intercourse within one month after childbirth or abortion, allowing bacteria to travel upward through the unclosed cervix which still secretes blood.

5. Adjacent infections: direct spread from neighboring organ infections, such as appendiceal abscesses or acute peritonitis.

Key Misunderstandings in Treating Pelvic Inflammatory Disease

The pelvis is where female internal reproductive organs are concentrated, making it highly susceptible to bacterial infections leading to PID. Due to non-obvious symptoms, PID is often ignored, leading to worsening conditions. In actual clinical practice, insufficient understanding and improper medical procedures have led to several misconceptions in treating PID, including:

Misunderstanding 1: Improper cleaning: Contact with items carrying pathogens or lack of daily cleanliness may lead to PID. However, excessive vaginal douching or prolonged soaking baths can easily alter the vaginal pH, providing an environment for bacterial proliferation.

Misunderstanding 2: Interrupted treatment: The treatment of PID should follow the principle of early treatment for early effectiveness. Treatment should be conducted in professional hospitals by qualified doctors. Do not relax when the condition improves, stopping treatment halfway. This not only delays the best treatment opportunity but also lays the groundwork for potential serious consequences.

Misunderstanding 3: Arbitrary medication: Avoid falling into the trap of incorrect treatments for gynecological infectious diseases, which could worsen the condition and delay proper treatment, turning minor issues into major ones. Sometimes, surface symptoms might be controlled while the underlying condition remains hidden, potentially leading to lifelong regret—infertility—for women.

Clinical symptoms of PID often include lower abdominal pain, general discomfort, high fever, headache, increased vaginal discharge, menstrual irregularities, and various other uncomfortable symptoms.
