The Meaning and Characteristics of Financial Globalization

Examining recent developments in international economic life, the prominent trend and characteristic is the globalization of the economy. In the last 20 years of the 20th century, the trend of economic globalization became unmistakably evident to the world. As an expression of the globalization wave at the economic level, economic globalization is primarily characterized by large-scale cross-border flows of goods, services, technology, and capital, as well as the global allocation and reorganization of various production factors. Economic globalization is the extension and inevitable requirement of economic marketization and internationalization, reflecting the deepening interdependence among economies worldwide. From a content perspective, economic globalization can be divided into production globalization, trade globalization, investment globalization, and financial globalization. Financial globalization is the core content and advanced stage of economic globalization, determined by another feature of recent world economic development - the financialization of the economy. The so-called financialization of the economy refers to the replacement of the real economy by a financial economy, with the degree of financial assetization of social assets continuously deepening. The economic relationships between nations are increasingly manifested as international financial relationships, such as international creditor-debtor relationships, international equity-dividend relationships, international principal-agent relationships, and international risk-insurance relationships. Finance, due to its suitability for [digitization] and [ultimate markets], has become a core, dominant, and strategic element in economic life, with its penetration and influence on all areas and levels of the social economy constantly increasing. As the degree of economic financialization deepens, the globalization of the economy prominently manifests as the globalization of finance.

Financial globalization refers to the trend of countries or regions crossing borders in terms of financial business, financial markets, financial policies, and laws, leading to mutual dependence, influence, and gradual integration. It is manifested in the globalization of elements such as monetary systems, capital flows, financial markets, financial information flows, financial institutions, as well as the globalization of financial policies and legal systems. On one hand, financial globalization constitutes an inherent part of economic globalization, being the inevitable requirement and natural outcome of the development of production globalization, trade globalization, and investment globalization, interacting and influencing each other with them. On the other hand, financial globalization, due to the nature and development status of finance, has its own unique content and operational rules.

Firstly, from the historical process of financial globalization, financial globalization is not a newly emerged phenomenon but a continuous developmental process, representing the continuous expansion and deepening of financial integration on a global scale. This process does not unfold uniformly or occur overnight but rather exhibits characteristics of temporal stages, spatial regionalism, and structural imbalance. The trend of financial globalization was initially visible in the 1960s and 70s, rapidly advanced in the 80s, and since the 90s, has entered a climax stage due to the fuller unfolding of its diverse contents. Spatially, financial globalization can manifest as regionalization, and to a certain extent, financial globalization is the result of the promotion and function of financial regionalization. The interaction between financial regionalization and globalization, which both integrate and repel each other, has become a hallmark of recent global financial development. Another significant characteristic of the development of financial globalization is the continuous expansion of the range of involved subjects, gradually extending from developed countries to developing countries. However, this process is uneven, with different countries and regions participating in financial globalization to varying degrees, enjoying different benefits, and experiencing differing rates of financial development. Developed countries and their multinational financial institutions occupy a dominant position in the process of financial globalization, serving as the rule-makers and primary beneficiaries of financial globalization. Developing countries, especially the least developed ones, often find themselves in a passive and constrained position in the process of financial globalization, facing threats of "marginalization" and "fourth-world-ization," generally sharing fewer benefits of financial globalization while being more impacted by its waves.
