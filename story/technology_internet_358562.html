Chapter One: Labor Law

The concept of labor law: It refers to the legal regulation of labor relations and other social relations closely related to labor relations.

Characteristics: First, labor law is a legal department that emerges at a certain stage of capitalist development; Second, labor law has a close relationship with civil law and is a legal department that has separated from civil law. It also has a close relationship with factory legislation and has gradually developed from factory legislation. Third, it is an independent legal department that regulates labor relations and relations closely linked to labor relations.

Features of labor relations regulated by labor law: First, the reason for the occurrence of labor relations is the process of realizing labor, i.e., workers directly participate in the production of goods or provision of services. Second, labor relations occur only after employers hire workers and connect them with the labor process. Third, labor relations arise from paid professional labor, and non-professional, unpaid, or obligatory labor relationships are not regulated by labor law.

Other relations regulated by labor law: Labor relations constitute the main and most basic type of relation regulated by labor law, but they are not the only ones. Besides labor relations, labor law also regulates other social relations closely linked to labor relations. These social relations may be necessary prerequisites for labor relations, direct consequences of labor relations, or relations that arise as a result of labor relations.

Content of labor law: (1) Employment promotion system (2) Collective bargaining and collective contract system (3) Labor standards system (4) Vocational skills development system (5) Social insurance system (6) Labor dispute resolution system (7) Labor supervision and inspection system

Role of labor law: (1) Protecting the legitimate rights and interests of workers (2) A necessary means to prevent and resolve labor disputes (3) An indispensable role in the operation of the labor market: manifested as follows: first, confirming workers as owners of labor power, placing workers in the legal position of suppliers in the labor market. Second, establishing labor relations through employment contracts, providing legal conditions for mutual selection between labor supply and demand and the free flow of labor as a factor of production in the market. Third, implementing unified labor standards and social insurance systems to ensure basic protection during the use and mobility of labor, while also ensuring the continuity of labor resources in the labor market.

Basic principles of China's labor law: (1) The principle that labor is both a right and an obligation: Labor is the right of citizens; it means enjoying the labor rights including the right to employment and the right to choose employment. Labor is the obligation of citizens. (2) The principle of protecting the legitimate rights and interests of workers: (a) Preferential protection and priority protection: Preferential protection means that labor law protects both parties in labor relations while tilting towards protecting workers who are relatively weaker in fact. Priority protection means that in specific circumstances, when there is a conflict between the interests of workers and employers, labor law should prioritize the protection of workers' interests. (b) Equal protection: All workers' legitimate rights and interests are equally protected by labor law. (c) Comprehensive protection: Workers' legitimate rights and interests include property rights and personal rights, statutory rights and agreed-upon rights, regardless of whether their content involves economics, politics, culture, etc., or whether they exist before, after, or after the termination of labor relations. (d) Basic protection: This is the minimum protection for workers, which is the protection of workers' basic rights. (3) Principle of rational allocation of labor resources: (a) Dual value orientation: Labor relations as the social relationship of the combination of labor force and means of production, is also the social form of labor resource allocation. (b) Macro-allocation of labor resources: The allocation of social labor among various employers across society. (c) Micro-allocation of labor resources: Arrangement of workers' job positions, working hours, and workload within the employer, i.e., division of labor and collaboration in the labor process.

Elements of labor legal relations: (1) Subjects of labor legal relations: Participants in labor legal relations, bearers of labor rights and obligations. (2) Objects of labor legal relations: Targets of labor rights and obligations, namely labor power. (3) Labor administrative legal relations: The subjects include labor administrative entities and labor administrative counterparts. Labor administrative counterparts are one of the parties in labor administrative legal relations who are in a managed position, mainly workers and employers. The object is the common target of the rights and obligations of labor administrative entities and labor administrative counterparts.

Procedures and effectiveness of internal labor rules in enterprises: Procedures: First, employee participation in the formulation of internal labor rules. Second, submission for review and filing. Third, formal publication. Effectiveness: Although enterprise internal labor rules are called factory regulations and discipline, they are not laws. Their effectiveness comes from the empowerment of the law. Employers should establish and improve rules and regulations according to the law, and employees should abide by labor discipline. Manifested as: First, internal labor rules must be fully implemented within the scope of the unit, all kinds of labor behaviors and employment behaviors in the labor process must be constrained by labor rules, the rights and obligations of all employees and employers should be based on internal labor rules. Second, disputes between employees and employers due to the execution of internal labor rules shall be handled according to the legally prescribed labor dispute resolution procedures. Third, internal labor rules can serve as appendices to labor contracts.

Chapter Two: Human Resource Management

Characteristics of human resources: (1) Human resources are "living" resources, having initiative, cyclicity, and wear-and-tear. Material resources only create value through the processing and creation of human resources. (2) Human resources are the main source of profit creation, especially in the new economy, where the innovative ability of human resources is the greatest wealth of enterprises. (3) Human resources are a strategic resource. (4) Human resources are infinitely developable resources. Currently, the degree of potential development of people does not correspond well with the actual potential of human resources.

Definition of modern human resource management: It is a process of acquiring, integrating, maintaining, motivating, controlling, adjusting, and developing human resources. Simply put, modern human resource management mainly includes seeking talent, utilizing talent, nurturing talent, motivating talent, and retaining talent.

Differences between modern human resource management and traditional personnel management: (1) In terms of management content, traditional personnel management centers on tasks, while modern human resource management centers on people. (2) In terms of management form, traditional personnel management belongs to static management, whereas modern human resource management belongs to dynamic management. (3) In terms of management methods, traditional personnel management mainly adopts institutional control and material incentive measures, while modern human resource management adopts humanized management. (4) In terms of management strategies, traditional personnel management focuses on short-term or current personnel work, while modern human resource management not only pays attention to solving short-term or current issues but also emphasizes the overall development, prediction, and planning of human resources. (5) In terms of management technology, traditional personnel management follows rules mechanically, while modern human resource management pursues scientificness and artistry. (6) In terms of management systems, traditional personnel management is mostly passive and reactive, while modern human resource management is mostly proactive and developmental. (7) In terms of management tools, traditional personnel management uses single methods mainly relying on manual operations, while modern human resource management generates results automatically through computers. (8) In terms of management levels, traditional personnel departments are often just executive departments to higher-ups, rarely participating in decision-making, while modern human resource management departments are at the decision-making level.

Five elements of job evaluation: Labor responsibility, labor skills, labor psychology, labor intensity, labor environment.

Labor Contract: A labor contract is an agreement between a worker and an employer to determine the labor relationship and clarify the rights and obligations of both parties. Establishing a labor relationship requires signing a labor contract.

Principles for concluding labor contracts: Concluding and amending labor contracts should follow the principles of equality, voluntariness, and mutual agreement.

Invalid Labor Contracts: Labor contracts that violate laws, administrative regulations, or are concluded through fraud or coercion belong to invalid labor contracts. From the time of conclusion, they have no legal binding force and are confirmed by the Labor Dispute Arbitration Committee or the People's Court.

Probation Period: Refers to a period not exceeding six months for mutual understanding and selection between the employer and the employee.

Form and Content of Labor Contracts: (1) Term of the labor contract (2) Job content (3) Labor protection and working conditions (4) Labor remuneration (5) Labor discipline (6) Conditions for termination of the labor contract (7) Liability for breach of the labor contract. In addition to these essential clauses, parties can negotiate additional contents such as confidentiality of trade secrets.

Term of Labor Contracts: The term of labor contracts is divided into fixed terms, indefinite terms, and terms determined by the completion of a specific task. Indefinite-term labor contracts refer to contracts without an agreed termination date. Contracts determined by the completion of a specific task refer to contracts whose termination date is the end of the task.

Amendment of Labor Contracts: During the performance of the labor contract, if circumstances change, both parties can amend some clauses of the labor contract through mutual agreement. The unamended part of the labor contract remains valid.

Termination of Labor Contracts: Upon expiration of the labor contract or fulfillment of the agreed conditions by the parties, the labor contract terminates naturally. If the employee is within the medical treatment period, pregnancy, childbirth, or breastfeeding period, the term of the labor contract should automatically extend until the end of these periods.

Renewal of Labor Contracts: Upon expiration of the labor contract, both parties can renew the labor contract through mutual agreement.

Termination of Labor Contracts: Termination of labor contracts refers to the legal act of interrupting labor management before the labor contract is fully performed due to certain reasons. Termination of labor contracts can be divided into statutory termination and agreed termination. According to the provisions of the Labor Law of the People's Republic of China, labor contracts can be terminated either by one party according to the law or by mutual agreement between both parties.

Economic Compensation: Employers should pay economic compensation to employees according to the provisions of the Labor Law and the Measures for Economic Compensation for Violations and Termination of Labor Contracts issued by the Ministry of Labor (Lao Bu Fa [1994] No. 481).

Labor Disputes: Refers to disputes arising between the parties of a labor relationship due to the realization of labor rights and the fulfillment of labor obligations.

Scope of Labor Disputes: (1) Disputes arising from dismissals, expulsions, layoffs of employees, and resignations or voluntary departures of employees. (2) Disputes arising from the implementation of national regulations concerning wages, social insurance, welfare, training, and labor protection. (3) Disputes arising from the performance of labor contracts. (4) Disputes arising between employees of state organs, public institutions, social organizations, and individual businesses and their assistants or apprentices. (5) Other labor disputes stipulated by laws and regulations that are handled according to the Enterprise Labor Dispute Handling Regulations.

Labor Dispute Resolution Institutions: (1) Labor Dispute Mediation Committees: Specialized organizations for handling labor disputes between the unit and its employees. Composition: (a) Employee representatives (b) Employer representatives (c) Union representatives of the employer. (2) Labor Dispute Arbitration Committees: Specialized institutions for handling labor disputes. (3) People's Courts: Judicial organs, responsible for hearing cases where parties are dissatisfied with the arbitration committee's ruling.

Chapter Three: Labor Economics

Characteristics of Labor Elements: (1) Motivation: Also known as subject-driven. (2) Self-selection: Labor elements as subject elements have the autonomy to decide and preferentially choose whether to provide labor supply and the quantity and direction of labor supply. (3) Individual Differences: Different labor element individuals differ in their knowledge and skill conditions, labor participation rate tendencies, labor supply directions, and societal labor positions demanding and selecting them. (4) Non-economic: People as producers of labor elements consider more than just "high income" economic benefits; they also consider non-economic aspects.

Stock and Increment of Labor Demand: Labor demand can be divided into existing demand and additional demand. The stock part of labor demand is generally met by existing labor supply, with a small amount of original labor supply changing. The increment in labor demand arises from expanded production by enterprises, establishment of new industries, particularly the large-scale development of emerging sectors and consumer service sectors.

Types of Unemployment: (1) Aggregate unemployment (2) Structural unemployment (3) Frictional unemployment (4) Skill-based unemployment (5) Technological unemployment (6) Selective unemployment

Division of the Labor Market: (1) From the perspective of market stratification: Divided into general markets and talent markets, with general markets further including skilled worker markets and unskilled worker markets. (2) From the perspective of market content: Divided into various specialized and occupational markets, such as electronic engineer markets, computer software personnel markets, construction worker markets, and nanny markets. (3) From the perspective of market form: Divided into institutional markets, temporary concentrated markets (such as labor supply-demand meetings or talent recruitment fairs), and scattered markets (like carpenters waiting at intersections). (4) From the perspective of market scope: Divided into internal employer markets, sectoral industry markets, regional markets, national markets, and international markets. (5) From the perspective of social recognition: Divided into organized markets and non-organized or spontaneous markets, legal markets and illegal markets. (6) From the perspective of market environment: Divided into free markets, monopolistic markets, and government-intervention markets. (7) From the perspective of market supply and demand: Divided into non-equilibrium markets and equilibrium markets. Non-equilibrium markets include both supply shortages and oversupply. Even in equilibrium markets, structural imbalances remain.

Government Administration of the Labor Market: (1) Efficient, open, comprehensive (2) Combination of unified leadership and decentralized management (3) Balancing economic benefits and social benefits

Methods of Statistical Investigation: (1) Census (2) Sampling survey (3) Key investigation (4) Typical investigation (5) Statistical reporting system

Chapter Five: Human Resource Planning

Types of Organizational Structures:

(1) Line Structure: The simplest centralized organizational structure form, also known as the military-style structure. Leadership relationships are established vertically without dedicated functional departments.

Advantages: Simple structure, clear and unified command system, explicit responsibilities and authorities, few horizontal contacts, easy coordination, rapid information communication, timely problem-solving, high management efficiency.

Disadvantages: Lack of specialized management divisions, making it difficult to focus on major enterprise management issues.

Applicability: Suitable only for small-scale or simple, stable businesses.

(2) Line and Staff Structure: Based on the line structure, corresponding functional departments are set up under the leadership of the plant manager (general manager), forming an organizational structure combining the general manager's unified command with the advisory and guiding roles of functional departments.

Advantages: Combines centralization and decentralization, implements unified command, introduces professional management practices, and leverages the advisory and guiding functions of functional departments.

Disadvantages: Insufficient attention to major corporate issues, leading to increased decentralization in organizational reform when setting up management committees or improving coordination systems fails to solve problems.

Applicability: A form conducive to improving management efficiency, widely applicable in modern enterprises.

(3) Divisional Structure: A decentralized structure following the principle of "centralized decision-making, decentralized operation."

Advantages: 1. Delegation of authority 2. Enhanced initiative and creativity, improving the company's adaptability 3. Achieving high specialization 4. Clear responsibilities and permissions tied to material interests and business performance.

Disadvantages: Overlapping organizational structures, bloated management personnel, neglect of overall corporate interests.

Applicability: Suitable for companies with diversified operations, significant differences in market environments, and strong adaptability.

(4) Matrix Structure: Functional department series formed by project teams established to complete a temporary task.

Characteristic: Possesses a dual command system.

Advantages: Facilitates cooperation and coordination between departments, communicates situations, utilizes personnel without increasing numbers, resolves contradictions between stable organizational structures and variable management tasks, provides an appropriate organizational structure form for combining comprehensive management with specialized management.

Disadvantages: Relatively complex organizational relationships.

Subsidiaries and Branches

Characteristics: Subsidiaries are not branches and have independent legal status; branches are branches with no legal or economic independence and are not independent legal entities.

Factors Influencing and Constraining Organizational Structure: Information communication, technical features, business strategy, management system, enterprise scale, and environmental changes.

Different Models of Departmental Structure: (1) Designing departmental structures centered on work and tasks, resulting in line structures, line and staff structures, and matrix structures. (2) Designing departmental structures centered on outcomes, typically manifesting as divisional structures and simulated decentralized structures. (3) System structures formed by designing organizations centered on relationships, usually for multinational corporations.

Informal Organizations: Systems composed of multiple factors unconsciously systematized by two or more individuals.
