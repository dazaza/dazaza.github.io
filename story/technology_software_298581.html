Indian Garment Industry May Witness Massive Layoffs Next Year

by mttasok10 on 2009-12-08 01:33:47

The Apparel Export Promotion Council (AEPC) of India said that the government must investigate the reason for the 50% jump in fabric prices over the past two months. Many garment exporters have been forced to cut production or fulfill orders at a loss.

Rakesh Vaid, chairman of the Indian Apparel Export Promotion Council, said: "Aside from price fluctuations, fabric supply has become a serious issue. Textile mills are not delivering according to agreements." He said that the increase in fiber and yarn prices is being passed on to garment manufacturers. However, overseas buyers, with fixed selling prices, cannot absorb such significant price increases. Using the cost of cotton camisoles as an example, the cost increased from 164 rupees/piece in July to 199 rupees/piece now, a rise of 24%.

Mr. Vaid said that thin cotton cloth (92x80) traded at 20 rupees per meter in July, and it has now risen by 55%, to 31 rupees per meter. Thin cotton cloth 82 x 72 rose by 57%, from 18.50 rupees/meter to 29 rupees. Similarly, fine thin cloth 60s 92 x 104 rose by 50%, from 24 rupees to the current 30 rupees. The chairman of the Indian Apparel Export Promotion Council said that in April, the cost of 2x60 yarn was 740 rupees per piece, and it has now risen to 1030 rupees per piece.

The global economic recession has led to a decline in retail sales in all importing countries. The new financial crisis in the Middle East also raises doubts about whether the economy is about to recover. Therefore, the operating conditions of India's garment industry are either at a loss or with very low profit margins, only 1-2% profit. Mr. Vaid said that by January next year, there may be unprecedented large-scale unemployment, leading to social problems in national garment manufacturing clusters.

In the first half of this fiscal year (April-September 2009-10), the export price of Indian garments decreased by 7.32% compared to the previous year. In October 2009, India's garment exports were severely affected, decreasing by 17.2% year-on-year to $603 million.

From January to September this year, India's garment exports to the United States, its largest market in the world, fell by 6.46% to $2.27 billion, compared to $3.07 billion during the same period last year. However, China's exports increased by 1.95% to $17.23 billion, and Bangladesh's exports increased by 2.35% to $2.66 billion.

Vaid said: "The government must implement fiscal relief measures to save Indian garment exports." He said he is concerned about collateral damage to the industry. "The economic stimulus packages and other measures announced so far have had little impact on the Indian garment industry. The measures announced either do not benefit the industry or revoke previous benefits."

The president of the Indian Apparel Export Promotion Council requested that the rebate rate be increased to 13.25% to mitigate the growing cost disadvantage. He asked for a 2% tax-free treatment under the Market Linked Focus Product Scheme for exports to the US and EU, cancellation of countervailing duties on textile machinery, extension of service tax exemptions to all export-related services, and replacement of rebates before service tax collection with direct reductions.

The Indian Apparel Export Promotion Council represents 8,000 small, medium, and large garment exporters. Last year, India exported $11 billion worth of garments, making it the fifth largest exporting country in the world.
