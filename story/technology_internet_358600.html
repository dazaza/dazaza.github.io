The World Bank continues to have a positive outlook on China's economic growth prospects.

by ditan360 on 2010-06-21 10:46:06

Louis Kuijs, Senior Economist at the World Bank and lead author of the Quarterly Report

Scott Rozelle, World Bank's Chief Economist for China

Low-carbon Network News: The World Bank's latest "China Economic Quarterly," released on the 18th, suggests that China's economy continues to grow strongly but has slowed slightly in the recent period.

The "China Economic Quarterly," which regularly evaluates the Chinese economy, notes that following last year’s large-scale stimulus policies, government-led investment growth has slowed this year. However, this has been somewhat offset by the robust growth in real estate investment. Under the influence of favorable labor market conditions, household consumption has maintained good growth. Key indicators and industrial production statistics show that although the growth rate remains high in the second quarter, there has been a certain degree of mild slowing down.

After exports fell into a trough early in 2009, they rebounded quickly. Nevertheless, due to a surge in imports and worsening trade terms, China's trade surplus has further decreased. Inflation is showing signs of picking up, but core inflation remains low. However, skyrocketing housing prices have prompted the government to adopt strict measures to regulate the real estate market, including tightening mortgage access requirements.

The Quarterly Report argues that despite lingering concerns about fiscal risks in some high-income countries, the global growth outlook continues to improve. This is largely thanks to the dynamism of emerging markets. Nonetheless, significant risks remain in the global economic outlook. Following a rapid start in 2010, China's growth may gradually slow down, mainly due to partial normalization of macroeconomic policy stance and regulatory measures taken in the real estate market in April.

World Bank's Chief Economist for China, Scott Rozelle, said: "We expect full-year GDP growth of 9.5% in 2010 and 8.5% in 2011. This year's growth should be less dependent on investment and more benefited from improved external trade conditions. Consumption will continue to be supported by a strong labor market." External surpluses are expected to continue declining this year. With no pricing pressures globally and little likelihood of a wage-price spiral, inflation will remain contained this year.

Louis Kuijs, Senior Economist at the World Bank and lead author of the Quarterly Report, said: "Given the strong growth prospects, it makes sense to further normalize the entire macroeconomic position to contain major macroeconomic risks. The considerable uncertainties surrounding the positive economic outlook require maintaining policy flexibility rather than blindly continuing with economic stimulus." It is appropriate for the central government to focus on strengthening loan management for local government investment platforms, but interest rates remain very low. China could allow interest rates to play a greater role in monetary policy, which would be beneficial.

Looking further ahead, policymaking needs to consider several characteristics of the medium-term outlook. Based on forecasts for key determinants, economic growth is expected to trend downward during the 2010-2020 period but will still remain at a fairly high level. When setting growth targets for the next decade, it is necessary to acknowledge that potential growth rates may slow down. The anticipated slowdown in potential growth highlights the importance of policies that help sustain productivity improvements, including promoting labor redistribution, enhancing human capital, and encouraging innovation.

In addition, further reforms are needed to ensure that economic growth is sustainable in social, energy, and environmental aspects. Fiscal policy reforms in some areas are key to achieving this goal. Further progress in social security and labor market reforms is important for both boosting productivity and improving social benefits.

The government's intention to eliminate environmental barriers and strengthen the role of private enterprises in the economy is welcome. In this regard, it would be beneficial to clarify what role the government envisions state-owned enterprises playing in China's economy.
