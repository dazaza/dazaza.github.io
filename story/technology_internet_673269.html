Geotextiles, geomembranes, geocomposites and special geosynthetic materials primarily use various high polymer substances such as polyethylene, polypropylene, and polyvinyl chloride as their raw materials. In recent years, geotextiles, geomembranes, and geocomposites have been widely used in water conservancy projects. Geotextiles are divided into two types: woven and non-woven geotextiles. Among them, needle-punched non-woven geotextiles, which feature high porosity, large permeability, and good drainage performance, are commonly used as drainage and filter facilities in dam engineering. Geomembranes have good impermeability with a vertical permeability coefficient less than 110-11cm/s and are cost-effective; however, they have relatively low CBR puncture strength, making them unsuitable for areas of the project with high impermeability requirements. They are generally used for vertical impermeability at the dam foundation. Geocomposite materials are products that combine two or more types of geosynthetics. There are numerous varieties with different functions. Among them, composite geomembranes serve dual roles of impermeability and drainage and are widely applied in dam engineering.

### Construction Technology Requirements for Composite HDPE Geomembrane Impermeable Body

① **Process Flow**

For the Batou Reservoir dam's composite geomembrane impermeability, a section cyclic operation involving simultaneous excavation, laying, tamping, and protection is adopted. The construction process flow is shown in Figure 5.5.3.2.

**Figure: Geomembrane Impermeability Construction Process Flow Chart**

② **Base Surface Cleaning**

The base surface must be cleaned according to design requirements; this is critical to ensuring impermeability. Sharp stones, tree roots, and other debris must be thoroughly removed. The base surface should not have local unevenness. The cleaned base surface should be compacted with a rammer or tamp plate to ensure it is dense and flat.

③ **Composite Geomembrane Laying**

When laying the membrane, it must be laid from top to bottom. The membrane-to-membrane joints and the membrane-to-base surface must be pressed flat and tightly adhered. However, the membrane should not be stretched too tightly; it should be slightly loose without leaving air bubbles under the membrane. Since geomembranes are thin and light, after laying, before the protective layer is properly placed, they can easily be blown by the wind. Therefore, the area for one-time membrane laying should not be too large, and it is best to lay the membrane while simultaneously covering it with protective layer material.

Based on the manufacturer’s data, the width of the composite geomembrane is 1.0~1.9m. For this project, a 1.9m-wide geomembrane was selected to reduce joint material usage. Joint methods include heat bonding, glue bonding, and lapping. This project design adopts glue bonding with a joint width of 50mm.

If any punctures or tears are found in the composite geomembrane, they must be repaired using a piece of composite geomembrane three times the size of the damaged area glued over the damaged part.

④ **Protective Layer and Facing**

The 10cm-thick protective layer soil material above the geomembrane must be screened. It should not contain particles larger than 6mm, otherwise, it may puncture the geomembrane. The protective layer soil material must be compacted to ensure a dry density greater than 1.5 and samples should be taken regularly for inspection. When backfilling the protective layer and building the facing stone wall, care must be taken to gently place the materials to avoid puncturing the geomembrane. Below the facing stone wall, a 100mm-thick crushed stone or gravel cushion layer should be laid to prevent erosion of the protective layer soil due to changes in water level and wind waves. Dry-laid facing stones should be manually hung and laid with the stones closely embedded. The individual weight of the facing stones should be greater than 48kg, and all gaps should be filled with smaller stones.

⑤ **Boundary Treatment Around the Periphery**

The requirement for boundary treatment around the periphery is to tightly connect the composite geomembrane with the surrounding soil body, block seepage entrances, cut off lateral seepage paths, and prevent seepage water from entering under the geomembrane to form water blisters, which could burst the geomembrane when the reservoir water level drops. Therefore, a water cutoff trench must be excavated around the periphery, and the geomembrane must be buried in the trench. Specific arrangements can be seen in the design drawings.
