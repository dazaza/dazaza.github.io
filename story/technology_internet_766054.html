Meizu's Transformation Part I: Whose Phone to Make

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 14:41:22

For fans who spend their time on forums and watching live streams, as well as those present at the launch event, yesterday was a memorable day. It wasn’t because the Meizu MX3 was particularly great or its price especially friendly, but because Meizu began to listen more attentively to their voices.

These users love to pontificate about everything under the sun, often posting on forums to instruct J.Wong (Meizu's founder) on how to design phones. They also criticize Meizu’s marketing strategies and point out flaws in their products due to their deep affection. Simultaneously, they are extremely sensitive—arguing fiercely with fans of other brands outside, but criticizing harshly among themselves behind closed doors. If ranked by loyalty, this is China's most loyal, most "mindless" fan group.

But their pain points are frequent: J.Wong doesn't like the nine-grid input method; hundreds of pages of forum discussions still fail to bring about change—it doesn't take much code to implement, but simply because J.Wong doesn't like it. FM radio, a trivial chip-related feature, has been debated since the M8 era but remains absent because J.Wong deems it unnecessary for an "internet" phone.

In summary, the cries of hundreds or even thousands of users pale in comparison to J.Wong's authority. Batch after batch of people have tried to challenge J.Wong's dominance, all ending in failure. I once summarized that Meizu used to be a company making phones solely for J.Wong. Many inside Meizu agree with this sentiment.

However, today's launch event surprised me—the intention to downplay J.Wong was very clear, and I believe this was J.Wong's own decision.

At the previous launch, Flyme 2.0 was presented as "the most J.Wong-style interaction," running through Li Nan's keynote theme. I attributed this to a lack of confidence, needing to pull out "J.Wong" to help establish a new style and use it as a selling point.

In last night's launch, I counted that apart from during the introduction of the MX3's tactile refinement process when Meizu CEO Bai Yongxiang mentioned J.Wong five times consecutively, the remaining mentions were scattered throughout Li Nan's introduction of Flyme 3.0—adding up to no more than 15 times across the entire event.

This does not mean J.Wong has lost control of the company or fully retreated, but rather that when a company founder is overly dominant, the risks are also evident—would you still trust Hammer without Luo Yonghao? How many IT commentators and users criticize Apple without Jobs, portraying Tim Cook as an ignorant wasteful CEO? Only by gradually reducing one's influence during peak times and bringing more suitable people to the forefront can one avoid associating Meizu entirely with J.Wong.

Additionally, two changes made the coal oil (Meizu fans) cheer, albeit with a tinge of bitterness.

Firstly, Flyme 3.0 includes a large number of transition animations. During the Flyme 2.0 era, the "wabi-sabi" style absolutely rejected such flashy methods. This forced many users to root their phones and install custom ROMs with built-in transition animations. When announcing this, Li Nan himself joked, "I know you all want it."

Secondly, the Meizu browser can finally be moved. Long-time coal oil should be shedding tears at this point—this issue was hotly debated between forum users and J.Wong, with J.Wong ultimately winning—"my most commonly used app is placed in the center, which is reasonable." When announcing this change, Li Nan quipped, "this browser can finally be moved," drawing laughter from the entire audience. This was previously unimaginable due to J.Wong's control and a certain degree of OCD.

The underlying implication of these seemingly minor changes is that J.Wong is actively aligning with the needs of the "coal oil." In other words, he is aligning with the needs of potential consumers. Because the proportion of new users for the MX2 is high, transition animations were a sore point when comparing Flyme 2.0 with MIUI. As for the browser, it's purely about accommodating new users unfamiliar with J.Wong's "dark history," avoiding posts in the forum titled—“Why did I buy a phone where the default browser icon cannot be moved?”

Whether it's diminishing J.Wong's influence or actively catering to consumers, both are good things for Meizu. This company, located in Zhuhai with fewer than 20 people in its Beijing office, is shedding its "take it or leave it" shell and beginning to integrate into mainstream consumer habits.

If the previous Meizu was an icy beauty from a small town who could silence anyone with a word, now she is at least slightly adorned and striving to smooth out her edges, retaining her prideful yet principled personality while gradually integrating into the grand stage of Beijing and attempting to guide the game. In short, when Meizu begins to make phones not solely for J.Wong, other domestic smartphone players, who have fallen behind in craftsmanship and design, should be more cautious.
