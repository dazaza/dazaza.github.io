Etiquette at banquets

1. Share happiness together, avoid private conversations.

Most banquets have many guests, so try to bring up topics that most people can participate in and gain agreement. Avoid monopolizing the conversation with personal interests or running off-topic, neglecting others. Especially avoid whispering privately, which can create jealousy and affect the atmosphere.

2. Focus on the host and guest of honor, grasp the overall situation.

Most banquets have a theme or purpose. When attending, observe everyone's expressions to distinguish between main and secondary roles. Don't simply drink for the sake of drinking and miss out on networking opportunities. Also, don't let overly boisterous drunks disrupt the host's intentions.

3. Appropriate language, humorous wit.

The banquet table can showcase one's talent, knowledge, cultivation, and social grace. Sometimes, a witty remark leaves a deep impression and fosters goodwill. Knowing when to speak appropriately, being humorous and elegant is crucial.

4. Moderate persuasion, avoid forcing.

At the table, some may try to turn the drinking session into a battlefield, persuading others to drink more. Thinking "alcohol defines a hero" might work for heavy drinkers, but it puts light drinkers in a difficult position. Excessive persuasion can ruin existing friendships.

5. Toasting order, clear hierarchy.

Toasting is also a skill. Generally, the order follows age, position, and host-guest identity. Before toasting, consider the sequence carefully. Even with unfamiliar people, inquire about their status or observe how others address them to avoid awkward or hurtful situations. When someone important is present, show extra respect, but if there are higher-ranking or older individuals, toast them first to avoid making everyone uncomfortable.

6. Read people's expressions, understand their hearts.

To gain praise at the table, learn to read people's expressions. In social interactions, understanding others' thoughts helps play your role well.

7. Gradual exposure, steady as a rock.

At the banquet, assess the situation and your capabilities accurately. Don't get too excited, preserve some sobriety and conversational restraint. Reveal your strengths gradually to maintain a stable presence, avoiding the impression of limited ability, making others underestimate your potential.

Attention to details

1 - Seating arrangement

Generally speaking, seating arrangements follow the principle of "left is honored, east is respected" and "facing the door is honored." For round tables, the main guest faces the door, and positions near the main guest are more honorable, with closer proximity being more prestigious. On square tables, if there's a seat facing the door, the right seat facing the door is for the main guest. If not facing the door, the right seat facing east is the chief seat. For large banquets, the chief table is front and center, with the left side following the order of 2, 4, 6, and the right side following 3, 5, 7, according to the identity and relationship of the main and guest.

If you are the host, arrive early and wait near the door to guide guests to their seats. If you are a guest, follow the host's arrangements.

2 - Ordering dishes

If time allows, wait for most guests to arrive before passing around the menu and letting them order. As a business banquet, budget concerns are important, so doing homework beforehand and choosing an appropriate venue is crucial. Guests usually understand your budget. Moreover, if you're paying, guests won't feel comfortable ordering, leaving it to you. If your boss is present, don't let them order unless they insist. Otherwise, it appears disrespectful.

As a guest, you shouldn't be too proactive in ordering. Let the host take the lead. If encouraged, you can order a non-expensive dish that no one dislikes. Ask others for their preferences, like "Is there anything anyone doesn't eat?" or "What would you prefer?" After ordering, consult others: "I've ordered, does it suit everyone?" or "Should we add anything else?"

When ordering, be confident. Follow these three principles:

1. Consider the number of people. Generally, one dish per person is a common rule. Add more if there are more men.

2. Balance the dishes. A good meal includes meat and vegetables, cold and hot dishes. If there are more men, add more meat; if more women, add lighter vegetables.

3. Importance of the banquet. For regular business banquets, dishes averaging $50-80 are acceptable. For key figures, include premium dishes like lobster, knife fish, shad, or even abalone and shark fin.

Don't ask the server about prices or haggle, as it makes your company appear stingy and makes clients uncomfortable.

Attachment: Chinese dining ordering guide - Three priorities and four taboos

A standard Chinese feast typically starts with appetizers, followed by hot dishes, main courses, dim sum, and soup. If feeling heavy, dessert can be added, ending with fruit. Ensure the menu covers all these categories.

Priority dishes:

1. Dishes with Chinese characteristics. When hosting foreigners, this is especially important. Dishes like spring rolls, glutinous rice balls, dumplings, lion's head, Kung Pao chicken aren't necessarily gourmet but are favored by many foreigners due to their distinct Chinese features.

2. Local specialties. Dishes like Xi'an's lamb soup, Mao's red-braised pork, Shanghai's red-braised lion's head, and Beijing's hot pot are better received than generic seafood when entertaining local guests.

3. Restaurant specialties. Featuring the restaurant's signature dishes shows thoughtfulness and respect for the guests.

When planning the menu, consider the guests' dietary restrictions, especially the main guest's. These restrictions mainly include four aspects:

1. Religious dietary restrictions must be strictly observed. For example, Muslims generally avoid pork and alcohol. Chinese Buddhists avoid meat and pungent foods like garlic, onions, leeks, and mustard. Some Buddhist followers abstain from beef specifically, especially when hosting overseas Chinese guests.

2. Health-related restrictions. Certain foods are unsuitable for specific health conditions. For example, people with heart disease, cerebrovascular disease, arteriosclerosis, hypertension, or stroke aftermath should avoid dog meat. Hepatitis patients should avoid mutton and soft-shelled turtles. Gastroenteritis or gastric ulcer sufferers should avoid soft-shelled turtles. Hypertension and high cholesterol patients should limit chicken soup.

3. Regional preferences vary. Cater to these differences when planning the menu. For instance, people from Hunan province generally prefer spicy food and dislike sweets. People from English-speaking countries usually avoid pet meat, rare animals, animal organs, heads, and claws. Additionally, avoid serving tough-to-eat dishes to foreign guests, as they may feel uncomfortable spitting out food they can't chew.

4. Occupational restrictions. Some professions have unique dietary prohibitions. For example, government officials cannot accept meals during official duties, cannot overindulge during official banquets, cannot exceed prescribed standards, and cannot consume strong alcohol. Similarly, drivers cannot drink alcohol while working. Ignoring these rules could cause embarrassment.

3 - Eating manners

Chinese people generally value eating etiquette. As workplace etiquette becomes more important, dining manners also require attention. The following tips apply to Chinese dining:

At the beginning of a Chinese banquet, the first wet towel provided is for wiping hands, not the face. When serving lobster, chicken, or fruit, a small water basin with lemon slices or rose petals is provided for washing hands. To wash, dip fingers alternately, gently swish, and dry with a small towel.

Observe cultural politeness. Avoid repeatedly urging foreign guests to eat. Instead, introduce Chinese cuisine's characteristics and let them decide. Some people enjoy urging others to eat or even serving food for them. Foreigners don't have this habit, and excessive insistence may offend them: "I said I'm not eating, why force me?" Conversely, when attending foreign-hosted banquets, don't expect the host to repeatedly offer you food. If you wait for others to serve you, you might go hungry.

Eat elegantly. Wait for the host's invitation before starting. Don't rush ahead of the host. Use chopsticks civilly, waiting for the dish to reach you before picking. Don't scoop ahead of neighbors, and don't overload your plate. Chew slowly for digestion and proper dining etiquette. Avoid stuffing large bites into your mouth, which looks greedy. Don't be picky, don't focus only on preferred dishes, and don't pile favorites onto your plate. Maintain graceful dining movements—don't bump neighbors, don't spill food onto the table, and don't splash soup. Avoid unnecessary noises like slurping soup or smacking lips, which are considered rude. Avoid talking with food in your mouth. Don't spit bones or fish bones onto the table; instead, cover your mouth with a napkin and use chopsticks to place them on a plate. Food dropped onto the table shouldn't be eaten. Don't fiddle with utensils or point chopsticks at others. Avoid scratching yourself openly. When using toothpicks, cover your mouth with a hand or napkin. Prevent utensils from making noise. After the meal, use a napkin, tissue, or provided towel to gently wipe your mouth. Avoid wiping your neck or chest excessively. Refrain from loud belching or sighing after eating. Don't leave prematurely unless the host indicates it's time to end.

Regarding toasting:

1. Host toasts the main guest.

2. Supporting guests toast the main guest.

3. Main guest returns the toast.

4. Supporting guests toast each other.

Remember: As a guest, never overshadow the host by indiscriminately toasting others, which is impolite and disrespectful to the host.

4 - Serving tea

This tea-serving knowledge applies both to client visits in the office and business dining settings.

First, ensure clean tea ware. When guests arrive, offer them a seat before preparing tea. Before brewing, thoroughly wash the tea ware, especially if it hasn't been used in a while, to remove dust and dirt. Rinse the teapot and cups with boiling water before serving tea. This ensures hygiene and demonstrates politeness. Serving tea in dirty utensils is impolite and unappetizing.

Nowadays, most companies use disposable cups. Before serving tea, ensure the disposable cups have saucers to prevent burns and allow guests to comfortably hold their cups.

Second, serve the appropriate amount of tea. Start with the right amount of tea leaves—not too much or too little. Too many leaves make the tea too strong; too few make it tasteless. Adjust based on the guest's preference for strong or weak tea. When pouring tea, whether in large or small cups, avoid filling them too full, which could spill and wet the table, chairs, or floor, or even scald oneself or guests, causing embarrassment. Also, avoid filling the cup too little, which might seem insincere.

Third, serve tea properly. Traditionally, both hands are used to serve tea unless physically impaired. For cups with handles, one hand holds the handle while the other supports the bottom. For handle-less cups, avoid grabbing the rim with fingers, which looks unrefined and unhygienic. Imagine guests licking finger marks from the rim—it's unpleasant.

Refilling tea: If your superior's or client's cup needs refilling, take the initiative. You can signal the server to refill or, better yet, refill it yourself. This provides a convenient way to fill silence. Always refill the superior's and client's cups first, then your own.

5 - Leaving the table

Banquets and tea parties usually last over two hours. After mingling and meeting some people, you might want to leave early. Here are some techniques for leaving gracefully:

Avoid causing a mass exodus when the party is lively. Instead of saying goodbye to everyone in a group conversation, quietly bid farewell to two or three nearby people and slip away.

When leaving mid-event, inform the host and apologize. Don't just disappear.

After greeting the host, leave promptly. Avoid engaging the host in long conversations at the entrance, as they have many responsibilities and other guests to attend to. Taking too much of their time could embarrass them in front of others.

Some people, when leaving a banquet or tea party, ask everyone they know if they want to leave together. This can prematurely end a lively event, annoying the host. A graceful person avoids this mistake.

Preparation before drinking

Alcohol harms the liver, a fact known to all. To minimize damage to the stomach and liver and reduce the risk of fatty liver, preparation is crucial. Before attending a banquet, eat something at home to line your stomach. What should you eat? High-protein foods are recommended, such as eggs, milk, or soy milk, as these can bind with alcohol in the stomach, reacting and reducing alcohol absorption. Biscuits or pastries also work, as an empty stomach absorbs alcohol quickly, leading to intoxication. Avoid pairing alcohol with salted fish, sausage, or cured meats, as these contain harmful pigments and nitrosamines that react with alcohol, damaging the liver and oral and esophageal mucosa, potentially causing cancer.
