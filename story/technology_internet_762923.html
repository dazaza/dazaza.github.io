"The Inevitable 'Death of Yougou': Belle's E-commerce Has Many Fatal Flaws"

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 18:06:13

Recently, Yougou.com, which has been suffering from a severe loss of executives, received another blow. On July 17th, it was confirmed by an insider at Yougou.com that COO Zhang Xiaojun is set to leave and is currently processing his resignation. As one of the founding members of Yougou.com, Zhang Xiaojun's departure holds significant implications, indicating that Yougou.com is indeed facing serious crises.

Let us first review the e-commerce journey of Belle International, the parent company of Yougou.com:

A few years ago, Belle cautiously ventured into e-commerce through Taobao distribution, Tmall, and B2C distribution (B2C distribution completed via Taixiu.com), led by veteran group member Xie Yunli with a not-so-luxurious e-commerce team. They initially sold products under Belle’s own brands before gradually introducing exclusive online models and eventually launching new online brands. This development path was considered a more solid case of traditional brand companies successfully entering e-commerce compared to Li-Ning prior to 2011.

However, following the over $100 million financing and rapid growth of Letao.com and Hao Le Mai in 2010, Belle Group felt compelled to act. On one hand, there was concern about being overly reliant on distribution channels; on the other hand, they assessed their significant advantages in the e-commerce domain, including:

One, numerous brands: In addition to Belle's several major women's shoe brands, the agency-managed international sports brands like Adidas and Nike experienced astonishing growth in online sales;

Two, easier category expansion: For Belle Group, known for its women's shoes and agency-managed famous sports brands, how to achieve absolute market advantage and status through multi-brand and multi-category strategies became a key consideration. Expanding offline stores for brand and category diversification was obviously too slow, while ongoing e-commerce operations could easily expand brands but struggled to quickly increase categories;

Three, financial advantage: During collaborations with e-commerce enterprises, Belle realized that e-commerce is capital-intensive. Belle Group's financial strength allowed them to develop without relying on external capital, enabling them to outlast many vertical e-commerce companies dependent on capital;

Four, talent advantage: Through years of e-commerce practice, they had cultivated a highly professional and execution-oriented e-commerce team capable of handling large-scale e-commerce business or beyond;

Five, supply chain advantage: This excited Belle internally and was the core reason attracting high-level executives from brand e-commerce or platform e-commerce. After all, for e-commerce, the supply chain always captivates attention as it seems to be the critical factor determining survival aside from funding. The supply chain includes product research and development, production, warehouse management, delivery, after-sales service, and a series of "business" content. In traditional enterprises emphasizing "business" over "e-commerce," this is perceived as the greatest competitive advantage over e-commerce companies.

On the basis of these advantages, Belle e-commerce decided to make a big move. First, they purchased the double-pinyin domain yougou.com,

abandoning the somewhat copycat “topshoes.cn” Taixiu.com directly for redirection, stopping supplies to all distribution websites, while maintaining direct third-party online stores like Taobao and Tmall but reducing investment and focus. They recruited JD.com VP and Vancl VP at high salaries to serve as CMO and COO respectively, and dispatched senior veterans to take up the CEO role, forming a golden triangle. They aimed to build a full-category vertical B2C mall, implementing multi-brand strategies on one hand and expanding into apparel and other categories on the other, realizing the dream of a department store empire unattainable offline!

Everything sounded perfect, but every dream must face the harsh reality. The realities were:

One, higher technical requirements for independent B2C: Based in Shenzhen headquarters, Belle couldn’t recruit more excellent technical talents, and outsourcing technical services companies were scarce in Shenzhen. However, technology determines user experience, backend management efficiency, and reliable data. Independent B2C belongs to dynamic technology, differing from relatively static technologies like ERP. Continuous changes remain a hurdle that many traditional enterprises cannot overcome;

Two, increased management difficulty with category expansion: Someone once said that even excelling in women's shoes doesn't guarantee success in men's shoes, something Belle likely experienced deeply when expanding into men's footwear. Expanding into women's clothing and accessories within a year inevitably brought challenges to the backend. For traditional brand enterprises accustomed to meticulous calculations, this rapid approach worked for Vancl during special times and was understood within Vancl and JD.com systems. However, whether Belle, despite employing Vancl's COO and JD.com's CMO, could understand and execute with "exaggerated costs" remains questionable;

Three, system issues: Many people outside e-commerce and traditional enterprises struggle to grasp this concept. Simply put, it involves department settings (organizational structure), approvals, finance and budget, reporting methods, corporate culture, communication styles, execution methods, risk control, processes, and regulations, collectively forming an enterprise's so-called "mechanism." This conflict results in e-commerce trying to persuade traditional sectors to accept its "system," while traditional teams attempt but fail to fully understand e-commerce's "system," leading to inefficiencies and instability in the execution team. Eventually, the chairman or board might say, "E-commerce and traditional enterprises each have strengths and weaknesses; the future is integration, so you must integrate in these areas..." If such "integration without harmony" occurs, it leads to so-called management innovation, and even the "CEO rotation system" appeared in a company like Belle!

It was originally thought that Yougou.com, part of Belle's e-commerce division, was far ahead, with top executives waiting for spin-off listings to enjoy fame, fortune, and countless examples of traditional enterprises succeeding in the e-commerce era. Instead, they are now entangled and distressed by these hard-hitting issues.

After all, whether it's the veterans who grew alongside Belle or the poached e-commerce VPs, it's evident they aren't leaving due to incompetence. When the VPs from JD.com return, they continue to excel; when those from Vancl have the courage to start businesses, what are the hard-hitting issues? Are they the three points mentioned above, or are these not only Belle's problems but also the common issues faced by many traditional enterprises attempting to enter e-commerce?
