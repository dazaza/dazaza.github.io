http://www.niugu8.com My home is by the Han River. In my hometown, there is a fairly famous small river that flows through. When it reaches our place, it has just taken a turn, so although the riverbed is shallow, the river surface is quite wide. Our small city is built along the water and can be considered a rare hydrophilic city. It's said that before liberation, this place was known as "Little Hankou." Imagine in the old days when transportation was not well-developed, this little river stretched north to Shaanxi and south to Hankou, with boats shuttling back and forth, bustling with activity! Although the former prosperity is no longer, it has brought today's peace, with clear river water, clean air, distinct seasons, willow trees shading the riverbank, and pedestrians like paintings... This small river is the Han River.

Children living by the river almost always play in the water from a young age, whether they have learned how to swim or not. Everyone has an especially close relationship with the river water. Children usually start learning to swim formally from their parents around the ages of seven or eight. For us, the river water is a paradise full of beauty but also a devil that worries the parents: what if the child mischievously falls into the river, or encounters the flood season? These dangers are enough to make one’s heart race, so learning to swim naturally became a necessary skill. The methods parents use to teach their children to swim vary: some find a shallow place and slowly teach with a rope; others simply grab the child, regardless of anything, and throw them into deep water, and it's done. Practice has proven that the latter method far surpasses the former in terms of speed.

I belong to the slower type, and my father chose the more humane method of teaching me to swim, so I learned even slower. It took me about one or two years to gradually get the hang of it. Later, my dad saw that the gentle approach wasn't working and the progress was too slow, so he resorted to toughness. He took me to the middle of the river with a life ring, took away the life ring, and left me alone. Just as I thought I was about to "run out of gas," the survival instinct made me muster up the courage to swim back to the shore. What a long swim it was! I swam for over an hour. When I finally climbed ashore, I was completely exhausted. I remember lying motionless on the shore like a dead pig, yet I was incredibly happy inside. Ha ha, I finally learned how to swim!

After learning how to swim, playing in the river became much freer. I often competed with my buddies to see who could swim faster, farther, and with more styles. I particularly admired those who could stand on the embankment 1-2 meters above the river surface and dive in. Their graceful postures made me feel more excited than watching diving programs on sports channels! Some "diving" buddies could stay underwater for a long time after jumping from the shore without surfacing. The buddy who could stay underwater the longest had the greatest prestige among his peers. There is a funny story about "diving" that I still remember vividly: That summer, the day before, everyone went swimming, and coincidentally, the water level by the riverbank was pretty deep. The kids took turns standing by the river and jumped in like dumplings. Everyone enjoyed it and agreed to meet the next day to play "diving" again. The next day, as soon as everyone gathered, a very mischievous child stood in the same spot and dived in first to claim the top spot, but then we heard his scream. It turned out the water level was much shallower than yesterday, so he hit a rock when he dived in...

And thus, we grew up slowly playing in the Han River water. We gained a lot of knowledge, learned many techniques for surviving in the water, and had many beautiful memories.

Since I am a girl, my father no longer allows me to dive or "jump into the water." He thinks it's ungraceful for girls, so I lost a lot of joy. I could only watch younger children play freely or swim alone to the center of the river far from the crowd to experience that tranquility. Whenever the river flows past me, it is soft and light, yet has a strong attraction, making me dance with it. If I want to break free from its control, I often need a lot of strength, but I am always indulged in its embrace and cannot extricate myself. I put on a swimming ring, let it take me wherever it wants to go, and the feeling is like flying.

As the weather gradually gets colder, fewer people swim. After winter sets in, only the older uncles continue to brave the rapids in the Han River. As a child, I always dreamed of growing up to be like these uncles and grandfathers who swim in the winter, but when I grew up, I realized that due to physiological reasons and other aspects, there are few female winter swimmers. Moreover, once you decide to swim in winter, you must persevere and not fish for three days and dry your nets for two. It's best to swim from summer all the way through to winter; otherwise, it's hard to adapt to the changing weather.
