Schmidt's claim that Android is more secure than iPhone draws laughter

by anonymous on 2013-10-10 18:39:01

Google Chairman Eric Schmidt is well-known in the industry for his distinctive facial expressions during speeches. During a recent interview at the Gartner Symposium/ITxpo, Schmidt once again made a startling claim: "Android has a security advantage over Apple's iPhone."

It was reported that Schmidt made this statement while conversing with Gartner analyst David Willis at the event. Willis asked, "If you were to conduct a random survey of the audience here, you would find that most of them do not consider Android as their primary platform, mainly because they perceive Android as insecure." Schmidt immediately rebutted, explaining, "Insecure? Android is at least more secure than the iPhone."

After Schmidt made this statement, it was noted by journalists that there was a burst of laughter from the audience. However, Schmidt did not elaborate further on his point, only mentioning that "the billions of Android devices activated on the market go through very stringent security tests."

In addition to this, Schmidt also addressed the long-criticized issue of Android fragmentation during the conference. He stated, "Google is addressing this problem by creating a more unified Play Store. We have convinced all major Android device manufacturers to maintain compatibility with the Play Store on their devices, which is a significant breakthrough for Android."

It should be noted that according to a recent U.S. government survey, many Android devices are more susceptible to malware due to the lack of available upgrade channels. Nevertheless, Schmidt insisted that the key aspect of Android is "ensuring that the Android phones users purchase can support a wide range of applications."

Towards the end of the conference, Schmidt reiterated Android's excellent performance in terms of security and also discussed topics such as the future of email, consumer expectations regarding privacy, and the possibility of integrating Google Now into enterprise services.

"You can accomplish many tasks you wouldn't expect using Gmail, Chrome, and Android, and this simultaneously enhances the user experience," Schmidt concluded.

New Book Publication Imminent

According to Business Plus under Hachette Book Group, Schmidt is currently writing a new book about how he led Google and advised on company decisions over the past decade. This book is expected to hit shelves next fall. Previously, Schmidt co-authored a book titled 'The New Digital Age' with Google Creative Director Jared Cohen, which was published in April this year.

The title of Schmidt's new book is 'No Adult Supervision Required: How To Build Successful 21st Century Companies.' The book will include insider stories from Schmidt's tenure at Google and other companies, aiming to provide executives and entrepreneurs seeking better corporate management methods and experiences with an alternative approach.

Google Senior Vice President Jonathan Rosenberg will co-author the book with Schmidt. From the book's title, it is clear that the main values conveyed in the content come from Schmidt himself. In fact, as early as January 20, 2011, Schmidt expressed his intention to write books of this kind. After announcing he would no longer serve as Google's CEO and instead take on the role of Executive Chairman of the Board, Schmidt posted a controversial tweet: "(Google) has outgrown the need for day-to-day adult supervision!"

At the time, The Wall Street Journal analyzed that Schmidt's tweet might have been a response to a viewpoint expressed by Google co-founder Sergey Brin in 2001. Back then, Brin revealed in an interview, "To be honest, we hired Schmidt as CEO to get 'parental supervision.'" It is worth noting that unlike 'The New Digital Age,' which has a broader audience, 'No Adult Supervision Required: How To Build Successful 21st Century Companies' primarily targets entrepreneurs looking to learn from Schmidt's successful business philosophy.

"To create products that meet market demands, one must first attract and manage a group of new employees with strong technical foundations. This book will provide readers with practical and simple methods to achieve this goal," Schmidt said in a statement.

Of course, for those readers hoping to read more about Schmidt's behind-the-scenes stories from his time at Google, they may have to wait for Schmidt's next book.
