Baby's Breath

by songsdp82pm on 2012-02-29 10:37:13

The sound of firecrackers bidding farewell to the old year, and every household replacing the peach wood charms for a new spring. Time truly cannot withstand折腾 (tossing and turning). In the rush and bustle, 365 days and nights have slipped by. Sighing at the ruthlessness of time and lamenting the fleeting nature of youth, whether good or bad, people must cross the threshold of the "year barrier." Now, saying "year barrier" might not be appropriate, but during my childhood, this once-a-year grand festival wasn't easy to get through either. Of course, I only gradually understood the bitterness of the adults with the word "year barrier" as I grew older.

I was born in the year of the Tangshan earthquake. 1976 was a year of frequent major events in China, with several leaders passing away one after another. Following that was the rectification of chaos, and "practice is the sole criterion for testing truth." After struggling under political gloom, the common people finally saw the long-awaited sun again. However, the proposal of high-level theories and the actual improvement of the common people's lives were not something that could happen overnight. The grassroots cared about their "vegetable baskets" and the "money in their pockets." After ten years of turmoil and upheaval, the remnants of the ideology of "preferring socialist grass to capitalist seedlings" still shackled people's actions. Life was bitter for those who lived in that era. There wasn't enough food to eat, no extra money to spend, and even if they wanted to earn money, there was nowhere to do so. My grandmother's feet got phlebitis, and she only took some "painkillers" when the pain became unbearable. But even this small amount of medicine money made my mother feel stretched thin. Seeing my grandmother's suffering, my mother had no choice but to plant spinach in the yard. Once it grew, she would pack it into baskets and secretly walk more than twenty miles to sell it in the city. When selling, she had to be on edge, like a thief, selling while dodging, running from the west end of the city to the east end, fearing being caught by the "urban management" personnel. My mother was not someone who liked scheming; she was just an ordinary, honest Chinese woman. But for the sake of the elderly, she had to grow vegetables and tobacco leaves to exchange for some pocket money. Now when I tell this to my children, they don’t believe me, but it’s indeed true.

"A good year is easy to pass, but a difficult spring is hard to endure." This was what my mother often reminisced about and frequently told us, but as a child back then, how could I understand? Even in the early eighties, people's side dishes weren't as abundant as they are now, and we couldn't eat “return order” vegetables. Some families, when harvesting tomatoes, would steam them, cut them into strips, and put them into infusion bottles for use during banquets in the first month. In those days, eating a dish of "tomato scrambled eggs" in winter was a very prestigious thing. My mother managed our household meticulously, and the melon seeds and peanuts prepared before the New Year were never allowed to be touched by us. We were only allowed to eat them after all the relatives and friends visiting had been entertained. Some fish and meat that hadn’t been touched would be constantly served and taken off the table, allowing us to eat through the first lunar month. In some households, by the time the New Year arrived, all the peanuts and melon seeds had already been eaten by the adults and children; before the first lunar month ended, the main courses like fish and meat used to entertain guests were all gone, requiring borrowing here and there, and perhaps not repaying until the end of the year.

Children don’t understand the pain of adults; they love celebrating the big New Year and look forward to it. Girls look forward to wearing new clothes, boys look forward to carrying red lanterns and setting off fireworks, and even more so, they look forward to eating the New Year’s Eve dinner and having a dumpling meal. "Who doesn’t eat dumplings during the New Year?" People say this less now, and hear it less too, but when I was little, eating dumplings was rare because our family was poor! The pitiful amount of rice and flour saved up was reserved for entertaining guests and improving the life of the elders who had suffered all their lives. Buying fireworks for the New Year wasn’t like today where people buy them by the box; we only bought one or two strings of "small firecrackers," and even these "sounds" weren’t dared to be lit all at once with a bang. Instead, they were scattered and lit one by one. For some firecrackers with loose fuses that didn’t explode, we would break them open from the side, gather the gunpowder together, and light it with incense—this was called "zī lǎo guānzi" (spurting old man). Wealthier people would buy large fireworks and set them off in the middle of the street, with many children watching, and I was among them. Why were they all children? At the time, I didn’t think about it, but as I grew older, I came to understand the reasons behind it.

What do families do on New Year's Eve now? They watch the Spring Festival Gala. However, the live broadcast of the Spring Festival Gala that the public considers the first was in 1983, and before that, there was no such grand event. On that night, the happiest thing for children was carrying lanterns. Little friends, dressed in new clothes, each holding differently shaped flower lamps, gathered in groups of three or five, running around in the alleys. The dark night appeared warm and harmonious under the beautiful candlelight.

Perhaps our childhood seems dull and barren compared to today's children, but in my memories, it was the most beautiful.

Memories are as splendid as fireworks, and this brilliance can only be deeply buried in the heart, embodying people's aspirations for a better life. How can one know sweetness without understanding bitterness? Look at our current lives—so rich and colorful, free from worries about food and clothing. Generations from the previous era couldn’t match today's wealth even during festivals. Families gather together, watching the Spring Festival Gala while summarizing the harvests of the year and planning for the next year. Reunion, happiness, and harmony flow and rise between the sumptuous New Year’s Eve dinners.

"Setting off fireworks!" With the fifty-shot fireworks ascending in sequence and exploding, lighting up the night sky like stars, beautifying the night sky.

Under the starlight, my mother's face glowed red, and even her wrinkles were filled with happiness and completeness.

I dedicate this article to the older generation, like my parents, who experienced those tough times, wishing them good health and the ability to enjoy this happy life every day; also wishing my peers to fully embrace this auspicious era, using our youth and wisdom to create an even more beautiful spring!
