How to Handle Burns, Scalds, and Bruises

[Overview]

Wounds can be categorized into abrasions, bruises, puncture wounds, cuts, bites/stings, burns/scalds, and sprains.

1. Abrasions are the most common type of superficial skin injury, usually mild.

2. Bruises result in purple or black marks due to broken blood vessels under the skin.

3. Cuts are soft tissue injuries caused by sharp objects.

4. Puncture wounds are skin injuries caused by sharp objects such as needles, scissors, bamboo skewers, or nails. These wounds may appear small on the surface but can cause deep tissue damage, leading to infections like abscesses or tetanus if drainage is inadequate.

5. Bites/Stings mainly include cat/dog bites and bee stings. Cat/dog bites often involve cutting and tearing of tissues, resulting in varying degrees of contusion and laceration. Bee stings occur when the bee's stinger injects venom into the skin.

6. Burns/Scalds are tissue damages caused by flames, hot liquids (water, soup, oil, etc.), or steam.

7. Sprains commonly occur in ankles, wrists, and lower backs. The affected limb experiences pain and limited mobility.

[Cause]

Abrasions, bruises, punctures, cuts, burns/scalds, and sprains are mostly accidental injuries that occur due to carelessness in daily life or labor. Bites/stings often happen in summer when animals become more agitated due to the heat.

[Consequences]

For superficial wounds with minor tissue damage, patients can handle them independently. However, for deeper wounds with significant local damage and heavy bleeding, immediate medical attention is required.

If a wound shows signs of redness, swelling, warmth, and pain, it indicates bacterial infection. In such cases, self-medication should be avoided, and medical help should be sought promptly. For diabetic patients, especially those with poor blood sugar control, even small wounds can lead to non-healing ulcers and potentially severe consequences like amputation.

[Treatment Methods]

Minor external injuries vary, and treatments differ based on the condition:

1. Abrasions

- Mild: Apply red antiseptic solution several times daily, keeping the wound dry and open unless it’s prone to frequent contact or friction, in which case a bandage can be used. Change the bandage frequently. Disinfect daily with hydrogen peroxide or iodine until the wound heals.

- Deep: If the dermis is damaged and there’s seepage of blood and lymph fluid, first disinfect with 75% alcohol swabs, moving from the edge toward the surrounding area, repeating 2-3 times. Then clean with saline. If the wound is dirty, use 3% hydrogen peroxide. Afterward, apply topical anti-inflammatory medication like red antiseptic solution, purple antiseptic solution, or Yunnan Baiyao, and cover with gauze. Antibiotics can also be taken orally, and the wound will heal in 10-15 days.

2. Bruises

- When bruised, avoid rubbing. Instead, rinse the area with cold water and apply a cold compress. If no complications arise, the bruise will fade within a few days.

3. Cuts

- Mild: Disinfect the surrounding skin with iodine, 3% hydrogen peroxide, or alcohol. Apply anti-inflammatory powder or Yunnan Baiyao on the wound and cover with gauze. On the third day, check the wound; if there’s no sign of infection, re-disinfect with iodine and re-bandage.

- Deep: For larger wounds with heavy bleeding, apply pressure to stop the bleeding for about 5-15 minutes before properly bandaging the wound.

- Glass cuts: Carefully inspect the wound. If you suspect foreign objects, rinse repeatedly with water. If objects are embedded, seek medical attention immediately. Removing them blindly could lead to excessive bleeding. Stop the bleeding first, then seek medical treatment promptly.

By following these guidelines, individuals can effectively manage minor injuries at home while recognizing when professional medical care is necessary.
