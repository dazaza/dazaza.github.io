Can French foie gras be made in China?

by 5tem573o6b on 2009-12-03 15:34:41

In ten years, Europe will ban the production of foie gras. This expensive world-class delicacy will create the largest market gap in its homeland of France. Meanwhile, on the other end of the world, China has become the largest goose-raising country.

The Beijing Maxim restaurant prepares foie gras. Photographed by Gaopeng, a reporter for The China Weekly. Workers at a foie gras company in Shandong force-feed geese. Photographed by Fengxiang, a reporter for The China Weekly.

In another ten years, Europe will ban the production of foie gras. This expensive world-class delicacy will create the largest market gap in its homeland of France. Meanwhile, on the other end of the world, China has become the largest goose-raising country.

France has now become the most desirable place for Chinese foie gras manufacturers.

Reported by Fengxiang of The China Weekly from Beijing and Shandong: Jiang Wen, Gong Li, Zhang Ziyi, are all regulars at the Maxim restaurant in Chongwenmen, Beijing. A dish that these celebrities must order is: Bordeaux wine foie gras terrine, priced at 208 yuan.

"Completely the same taste as the one made at the Maxim restaurant in Paris," said Chef Chunwei Shan of Maxim restaurant with great confidence. However, unlike the 1980s when foie gras was air-freighted from France, now, the foie gras comes from a company in Yanqing, Beijing.

Cruel Foie Gras

Bordeaux wine foie gras terrine, this dish name might be confusing, but it's actually quite simple to explain: during the marinating process, Bordeaux red wine is added; the foie gras is cut into trapezoidal shapes, resembling bricks used for making brick walls.

In 1983, Pierre Cardin decided to open an authentic Western restaurant in Beijing, and naturally, foie gras was indispensable as the signature dish of French cuisine. Besides the Bordeaux wine foie gras terrine, another popular way to eat foie gras is - seared foie gras.

"The tender fatty goose liver, seared until golden brown and crispy on the surface with a slight hint of char just right, when sliced, the inside is perfectly soft and fragrant, its smooth texture glides gently over the tongue, like a long passionate kiss that intoxicates both mind and spirit." An article discussing French cuisine described the seared foie gras thusly.

As an indispensable signature dish, the restaurant's supply of foie gras must be guaranteed. In 1983, Maxim restaurant had to rely on imports from Hong Kong for most of its ingredients except salt, eggs, and carrots, and foie gras had to be directly purchased from France. Now, the foie gras used in the restaurant is procured from a company in Yanqing County, a suburb of Beijing.

"Nowadays, not only can China produce its own foie gras, but France also doesn't have enough foie gras to export." One of the reasons mentioned by He Guangyin, the manager of Maxim restaurant, was the widely discussed news among European and American countries not long ago —— the "2019 Ban": By 2019, the EU will prohibit all its member states from producing foie gras.

The reason being: the method of producing this delicacy is too cruel.

A squawking goose is grabbed by the neck, a metal tube is inserted into its esophagus, connected to a funnel on the other end. Workers use a spoon to pour handfuls of corn kernels down, then pinch its neck to make it fall directly into its stomach without the ability to regurgitate.

This process repeats every six hours. Each time, the goose eats four times its normal amount within 40 seconds, leading to an enlarged fatty liver —— a normal goose liver weighs about 150 grams, while a force-fed goose liver weighs at least 650 grams or more. This fatty liver is what humans crave as a delicacy, scientifically named "foie gras."

From hatching, a young goose first enjoys three months of happy free-range life —— swimming in lakes, eating grass; then living together for a month, and finally being confined in a small cage for 20 days of force-feeding before being slaughtered for its liver.

During these 20 days, it will be in a painful state of neither able to live nor die. Always on the verge of being bloated to death, it struggles to breathe in the iron cage and cannot move. Due to the abnormal swelling of the liver, its body becomes extremely fragile. Many geese die halfway through.

This process is similar to the method used for making Peking duck, but even more cruel. Force-feeding ducks requires only two meals a day, while force-feeding geese needs four meals. As a goose farmer put it, it's a "non-natural natural growth process."

"You claim to be the country that respects human rights and animal welfare the most in the world." Professor Zhang Boyang, a retired professor from China Agricultural University who has been studying force-feeding since the 1980s, once told the French, "In fact, the cruelest thing in the world is done by you."

If geese were capable of expressing themselves, they would surely grab a goose feather pen and write in beautiful elegant French round handwriting the famous quote by Zola: "I protest!"

Gentlemen Keep Away from the Kitchen

For this reason, in the West, many countries condemn the method of producing foie gras and gradually ban its production.

In 1998, under pressure from animal protection organizations, the EU officially condemned a series of countries producing foie gras. Among them, Poland, Denmark, Germany, Austria, the UK, and Norway quickly responded, successively banning the production of this food in their countries. Previously, a foundation named after the famous French actress Brigitte Bardot had been protesting against this practice for many years.

In 2006, Israel also joined this group. Its Supreme Court acknowledged in its ruling that "the ruling will severely affect the interests of goose farmers and their freedom of profession and property." Despite this, the two goose farms that had been operating for more than forty years were closed.

An even stricter measure came from "Terminator" Schwarzenegger. In 2004, he signed a law banning not only the production of foie gras in California but also its sale.

Now, the only two countries in the EU still producing foie gras are France and Hungary. The so-called "2019 ban" is their final ultimatum.

As the birthplace of foie gras, France remains the largest consumer market in the world, with an annual consumption of more than a thousand tons. The French, who have been accustomed to eating foie gras for several centuries, naturally don't want to give it up. On October 18, 2005, the French parliament passed a bill by voting, stating that foie gras is "a cultural and culinary heritage of France that should be vigorously protected."

However, it also cares about its reputation as a country respecting human rights and animal welfare, forcing it to close most of its goose farms and instead import from Hungary. "Our French counterparts tell us: they now use foie gras imported from Hungary." Said Chef Chunwei Shan of Maxim restaurant.

This is also the approach taken by the vast majority of countries that have banned the production of foie gras. Europeans and Americans are also "gentlemen keep away from the kitchen", wanting to eat it but finding it cruel. Hungary has thus become the world's largest exporter of foie gras, exporting more than 1500 tons of foie gras annually, with more than 30,000 goose farmers making a living from it. However, they are uneasy under the "2019 ban".

Now, is it the Chinese people's turn to take the stage? (Source: The China Weekly)
