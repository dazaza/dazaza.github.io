Introduction to Pony Ma

Pony Ma (born October 29, 1971), male, from Chaoyang, Guangdong Province, is the Executive Director, Chairman of the Board, and Chief Executive Officer (CEO) of Tencent Company. He is responsible for the overall strategy planning, positioning, and management of the group. As one of the main founders, he has been employed by the company since 1999. Before his current role, he was in charge of the research and development of Internet paging systems at Shenzhen Runxun Communications Development Co., Ltd., a supplier of telecommunications services and products.

In December 2009, the British Sunday Times published a list of "The World's Ten Most Recognizable Chinese in the Next Decade," which included Pony Ma, CEO of Tencent. The paper stated that these Chinese individuals would bring about a different world and were likely to become as well-known as American IT giants Bill Gates and Warren Buffett in the next decade.

Pony Ma - Professional Experience

Early Entrepreneurship

In November 1998, Ma co-founded Shenzhen Tencent Computer System Co., Ltd. In June 2000, Tencent became profitable. In 2001, Ma licensed the Q-Gen brand to Guangzhou Dongliliheng for operation, and by 2003, they had over 100 specialty stores. At the end of 2003, Ma began operating an online game. He successfully developed Tencent from a small company into one of China's leading Internet service providers. Ma has always pragmatically led Tencent's development and guided the innovation of its products and services. He previously worked at Runxun Communications, responsible for the R&D of Internet paging systems. Ma graduated from Shenzhen University's computer science program in 1992. Back then, he wandered between some small and medium-sized companies, barely considered part of Shenzhen's elite circle. By chance, Ma came across ICQ, an instant messaging tool developed by an Israeli. Ma thought ICQ was great—how great?

Ma believed it could be used as a PC-based pager. At that time, mobile phones were symbols of great wealth, and having a pager was quite prestigious too—you'd definitely impress any girlfriend. Ma thought ICQ provided instant messaging on computers but had a significant drawback—it lacked a Chinese version, making it inconvenient to use. Thus, Ma wanted to create a Chinese version of ICQ, gathering a few friends to establish a company and develop a Chinese ICQ, showcasing the sharp insight and natural business acumen of people from Chaozhou.

Initially, Tencent faced many difficulties. Many times, QQ almost used underhanded methods to steal space and bandwidth because there wasn't enough money to buy servers. However, the Chinese version of ICQ, OICQ, was very popular, with many downloads. In 2000, it was OICQ's winter and Tencent's winter too. The first internet bubble swept through all of China's internet sector, and Tencent was no exception. Someone from Tencent's upper management approached Sina and Chinanet, offering to sell the company for 1 million yuan, which seemed astronomical at the time. They felt they could abandon this uncertain Chinese version of ICQ. However, both Sina and Chinanet's executives politely declined, saying, "Could you please close the door on your way out?" Now we're grateful that neither Sina nor Chinanet used 1 million yuan to eliminate this QQ that connects us infinitely. At that time, they thought it was too expensive—a mere chat embed—and there were serious concerns about infringing on the rights of the Israeli-developed ICQ, so they refused to buy it for 1 million yuan! They decided they could create their own, and later, Sina's UC proved this point.

After the dream of being a beautiful bride faded, QQ realized everything had to rely on itself. Ma invested almost everything he had, but QQ didn't know where its profit points were. Was it only possible to charge users a meager registration fee for applying for QQ numbers? (For a period, QQ charged for number applications, a testament to its difficult times.)

Fortunately, venture capitalists saw potential in Tencent at this point, investing money to help Tencent list in Hong Kong. At that time, QQ lacked cash capital; if anything, its capital was the hundreds of millions of QQ users like you and me. From that moment on, Tencent entered a new era—the capital era, a dream Ma had even in his sleep. After listing, Ma's personal net worth quickly soared to 1.7 billion Hong Kong dollars! To emphasize, this was when Tencent just listed—not now. As for how much Ma is worth now, I suggest asking Ma personally.

According to reliable sources, Tencent's current market value is 3.3 billion USD, approximately 27 billion RMB.

Through imitation, Ma inadvertently built a vast QQ empire, changing the way Chinese people communicate. Mobile phones, telephones, emails, and QQ have become essential tools for most Chinese people. Indeed, many younger generations can do without mobile phones or emails but cannot live without QQ, where they find countless people to chat with and enjoy numerous games. Starting from a certain period, Tencent began diversifying its strategy, owning its portal site qq.com, quickly becoming China's fourth-largest portal site. Alibaba's Taobao runs very well, so Tencent launched paipai.com based on the promising C2C model in China. Chen Tianqiao's Shanda earns a lot from online gaming, so Tencent introduced QQ Fantasy and QQ Hall. Google and Baidu excel in search, and the search market is already the second-largest battleground on the internet, so Tencent created its own search engine soso.com. Tencent not only expands online like a network octopus endlessly expanding the Tencent Empire but also offline, as evidenced by its successful promotional activities with Coca-Cola. I advise everyone that, in the near future, seeing QQ Towers or QQ Malls in Shenzhen, Guangzhou, Shanghai, and Beijing will be a step in the QQ development plan under the very capable yet low-profile guidance of Ma.

Ma, a mysterious and low-key person, doesn't like to perform like some corporate presidents. New QQ products often appear quietly before our eyes. In fact, every expansion of QQ effectively seals the throat of competitors. For instance, the launch of QQ Games ended Hao Fang's once-invincible glory, burying that brilliance in yesterday forever! We don't know how many such stories will play out in QQ's future expansion.

Difficulties and Opportunities

Like other startups in the internet industry, funding and technology were Tencent's biggest issues. "First, we lacked funds; when we got them, the software couldn't keep up."

In February 1999, Tencent developed its first "Chinese-flavored" ICQ, known as Tencent "QQ," which was welcomed by users, with registered users growing rapidly to tens of thousands in a short time. With the increase in users, servers needed constant upgrades, but even the server hosting fees of a couple of thousand yuan were unbearable for the company. "We could only use others' servers wherever we could, starting with just an ordinary PC placed in a broadband-equipped room, then secretly placing the program on others' servers."

"At that time, we thought if we couldn't afford it, let's sell it," Ma recalls with relief: "But selling QQ brought us trouble. I talked to many ICSPs (Internet Content Providers), and they all wanted exclusive buyouts." This made Ma, who originally planned to make money by selling QQ software to multiple companies, very hesitant. The most nerve-wracking negotiation was with Shenzhen Telecom Data Bureau. They were prepared to offer 600,000 yuan, but Ma insisted on 1 million yuan, and they couldn't reach an agreement, leading to the deal falling through.

Unable to sell the software, but with rapid user growth, the investment required to operate QQ grew larger, forcing Ma to raise funds everywhere. Banks said they hadn't heard of loans secured by "registered user numbers"; domestic investors were mostly concerned with how many computers and other fixed assets Tencent had. In the second half of 1999, inspired by former internet friend Ding Lei's overseas financing, Ma started seeking foreign venture capital with a commercial plan book revised six times and over 20 pages long, eventually meeting IDG and Pacific Century CyberWorks. "They invested 2.2 million USD, each taking 20% of the company's shares. When QQ reached 20 million users, the money hadn't run out."

With this funding, the company purchased IBM servers with 200,000 megabytes capacity. "When they were placed on the table, my heart was filled with joy," Ma recalls, still delighted.

Pony Ma: Innovation Needed for the Rise of China's Internet Enterprises

Ma introduced that the internet industry is characterized by high knowledge density, high added value, and high industrial driving ability. Moreover, it has the features of low energy consumption and low environmental pollution—"two lows." Therefore, the internet service industry is not only a high-end service industry but also an environmentally friendly and green service industry.

In his speech, Ma believed that the internet industry is very new, changes very fast, and has a relatively short history.

Ma believed that the rise of local enterprises in China's internet could mainly be attributed to local innovation. Local companies can fully understand the characteristics and development patterns of Chinese users, developing applications and services truly suitable for Chinese internet users. This is the fundamental factor. In the future, international giants can also do well, such as Google recruiting many top talents, but localization is still necessary. In the future, we will see more high-end service industries opening up and entering the capital market. In terms of enterprise management mechanisms, talent attraction, and incentive systems, reforms must be carried out to compete with international and more professional service-oriented companies.

Building an Open Platform

On June 16, 2011, during Tencent's first-ever partners conference in its 12-year history, Ma looked relaxed and said, "This was not a tough decision at all." He announced that apart from fully opening up eight major platforms, the total scale of Tencent's Industry Win-Win Fund for investments would double to 10 billion yuan. Tencent would also allocate 20 billion yuan, equivalent to its annual revenue at that stage, to various third-party partners on the open platform, aiming to "recreate another Tencent." Since announcing a six-month strategic transformation preparation period on December 5 of the previous year, all eight major platforms of Tencent—Tencent Friends, QQ Space, Tencent Weibo, Tenpay, e-commerce, SouSuo, CaiBei, and QQ—were fully opened. Tencent's hundreds of millions of users were also gradually opened to third-party partners, and a 5 billion yuan Tencent Industry Win-Win Fund was established to invest in companies in the industry. To date, the Tencent Industry Win-Win Fund has already invested over 2 billion yuan, and Tencent's open platform has attracted nearly 20,000 partners. Ma expressed hope that the number of partners would exceed 100,000 in the future.

When talking about two weeks earlier when Zhou Hong, chairman of Qihoo 360, accused Tencent of unwillingness to cooperate with Qihoo 360 and being "fake open," Ma responded: "We must adhere to our commitment, which is that we will never cooperate with 360. Moreover, 360 is not a developer, so the issue of whether to connect with us doesn't exist. Simply selling ads cannot claim to be an open platform. If an open platform lacks experience and capability in application development, how can it allow partners to make money?"

Overview of Tencent

Tencent was founded in Shenzhen in November 1998 and is one of China's earliest developers of internet instant messaging software. In February 1999, Tencent officially launched its first instant messaging software—"Tencent QQ"—and went public on the main board of the Hong Kong Stock Exchange on June 16, 2004 (stock code 700). Currently, Tencent has developed into one of China's largest providers of internet application services and mobile value-added services. In the future, Tencent will continue to uphold the spirit of innovation and excellence, with the mission of enhancing human life quality through internet services, gradually realizing the vision of becoming "the most respected internet company." As of September 21, 2007, Tencent became the first company in China's internet history to have a market value exceeding 10 billion USD, reaching 10,621,330,245.51 USD.
