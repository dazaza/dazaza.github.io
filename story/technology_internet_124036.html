I. Expand the shopping model

The female shopping model is a very advanced behavioral pattern. Girls' shopping behavior is highly rational, and we can roughly divide their actions into four stages:

The first stage - wandering, watching, and comparing. This process is equivalent to an information-gathering phase, similar to our market research, examining styles, prices, and store conditions.

Secondly, girls tend to shop in groups rather than alone, engaging in team-based operations where they discuss various factors together.

Thirdly, after completing their discussions, they frequently negotiate and communicate with the shop owner (the bargaining process).

Fourthly, after purchasing items, they continue to evaluate, regretting if they find a better deal, which indicates a strong ability for reflection.

Throughout this entire behavior process, comparison, discussion, communication, and reflection are all core elements of rationalized behavior in philosophy and psychology. In contrast, men's shopping process usually involves going directly to a store, seeing what they want to buy, paying immediately, without prior information gathering, discussion, communication, or bargaining phases. They may not even have the ability to regret afterward, showing a high degree of irrationality.

However, in the workplace, men's performance is exactly the opposite of women's. When doing business, they will comprehensively analyze competitors, discuss strategies to defeat opponents, and summarize experiences after success. This process is similar to girls' shopping processes, being highly rational. Women's workplace behavior resembles men's shopping behavior patterns, which is a more simplified process. Women actually possess this highly rationalized capability but need self-awareness and adjustment to fully apply the rationality of their shopping behavior to their workplace actions, thereby increasing their chances of success in the workplace.
