The Seven Biggest Mistakes Bill Gates Made at Microsoft

by anonymous on 2013-10-11 19:21:51

Recently, in an interview during a fundraising event at Harvard University, Bill Gates frankly admitted that requiring early Windows users to use the complex Control·Alt·Delete key combination to log into early PCs was a mistake. At the time, IBM keyboard designers were unwilling to provide Microsoft with the "single key" it requested, and Gates tacitly approved of their approach. "It was a mistake," said Gates. But was this really the biggest mistake made by Gates, Microsoft, and the PC industry? Perhaps not. Here are some more examples:

1. The Clippy Software Help System

Clippy was introduced to assist PC users in using Microsoft Office software. Despite its good intentions, it was far from being liked. "It seems like you're writing a letter, would you like some help?" Undoubtedly, Clippy's trendy "opening line" drew the strongest backlash from Microsoft Office users.

Starting in 1997, Clippy would automatically appear in Microsoft Office software. By 2001, Clippy was no longer a default feature, and Microsoft even began mocking Clippy in advertisements for its new products to emphasize the ease of use of the new Office software. This photo was taken in 2001, showing Gates presenting a T-shirt to Mr. Clippy as a retirement gift. To be honest, it was somewhat touching.

In the 2007 update, Microsoft Office completely removed the Clippy help system.

2. Blue Screen of Death

You're happily browsing files on your PC when suddenly your screen turns blue, displaying lines of error codes, followed by a reboot. Unfortunately, what you've encountered is the infamous blue screen of death.

Every time a blue screen occurs, Microsoft creates a file that stores crash information on your hard drive. Your old computer may have had many such files. The blue screen phenomenon is primarily caused by hardware issues.

In recent years, Microsoft claims to have been working hard to reduce or even eliminate blue screens in the latest versions of Windows.

3. Windows Vista Operating System

"For an operating system meticulously crafted over five years, Windows Vista lost its reputation surprisingly quickly," wrote David Pogue, tech columnist for The New York Times in 2009, "One day, its users might sign a petition demanding access to older versions of its flagship product, but not every company survives long enough to see that day."

His prediction proved accurate. Windows Vista, the successor to Microsoft's Windows XP, quickly received almost universal criticism from users. Its most criticized aspects included slower computing speeds, shorter battery life, and a confusing interface.

"I have to say, Vista is one of the products I regret launching the most," said Steve Ballmer, then-CEO of Microsoft, shortly before his retirement in August of this year.

4. Zune Music Player

Launched in 2006, Zune was Microsoft's answer to the iPod. "Zune is our big move," said Gates while promoting Zune. "Over the next few years, it will lead our progress."

However, Microsoft entered the market too late. Neither Zune nor the Zune HD launched in 2009 could rival Apple's well-established mp3 player, the iPod. By 2012, Microsoft quietly phased out Zune, redirecting Zune users to Xbox Music, a music service on Xbox 360.

"Zune was a highly competitive product, but it was full of contradictions in its design philosophy," said Nate Anderson of Ars Technica, a prominent American tech blog. "This device was meticulously detailed, showing much effort, but its basic functions were terrible."

5. Internet Explorer Browser

Before becoming a laughingstock, Internet Explorer was practically everyone's go-to web browser. In 2003, it reached its peak, capturing 95% of the market share.

But then it plummeted.

Internet Explorer gradually became just a tool used to download other browsers that people truly wanted. Over the past few years, a wave of faster and more personalized browsers emerged, such as Safari (2003), Mozilla Firefox (2004), and Google Chrome (2008). Despite still holding a relatively stable market share and ranking 10th among browsers in 2012, Internet Explorer has faced significant challenges.

6. Comic Sans Font

In 1993, typographer Vincent Connare invented this font. While working at Microsoft, Connare was asked to use this font for the Microsoft Bob interface and virtual assistant. Therefore, he decided to create a cartoonish font for a talking dog featured in the project.

This font first appeared in Windows 95 and quickly spread to every computer worldwide. Eventually, it became the "most hated" font.

7. Steve Ballmer

Yes, Gates should apologize to Steve Ballmer. Ballmer, Gates' college classmate and long-serving CEO of Microsoft, took the helm of the company starting in 2000.

However, you may have noticed that all the major mistakes mentioned above occurred during Ballmer's tenure as CEO of Microsoft. In that sense, we should thank Gates instead.
