Xiaomi TV R&D内幕: Skyworth President once considered investment, but was preempted by LeTV

by anonymous on 2013-09-18 21:42:55

The smart TV market, which has been disrupted by video websites such as Letv and iQIYI, has become even more heated due to Xiaomi's entry.

Five days after the launch of Xiaomi's TV, it faced a strong backlash from home appliance companies. Veteran TV giant Skyworth not only launched a 1999 yuan smart TV in collaboration with Alibaba but also openly criticized Xiaomi TV, stating that compared to professional home appliances, Xiaomi TV was like "the Stone Age encountering industrial civilization."

Skyworth publicly compared Xiaomi's 47-inch TV with its own 42-inch Coolpad TV: the former costs 2999 yuan, the latter 1999 yuan; the former uses V13 panels, the latter higher-end V14 panels; the former is 8.4mm thick, the latter 5.9mm. Skyworth aimed to declare its advantages in smart TV hardware, hence showing no fear of Xiaomi.

Moreover, Skyworth did not just compete fiercely with Xiaomi in the smart TV sector.

According to recent revelations by iQIYI CEO Gong Yu at a smart TV forum, the internet box product jointly launched by Skyworth and iQIYI is expected to be on the market within 1-2 months. The iQIYI and Skyworth box products will adopt a model where hardware is charged but content is free, with Xiaomi Box clearly being one of the main target competitors.

After their traditional territory was "invaded," other home appliance companies also emphasized that today's surviving TV companies had emerged from past TV battles, and Xiaomi’s price strategy was bad luck, picking a group of tough opponents. A senior executive of a TV company told Tencent Technology, “We can't afford to lose in terms of momentum and public opinion to Xiaomi.”

Undoubtedly, the smart TV market will enter an intense phase of offensive and defensive battles. What is less known is that Skyworth, which now strongly opposes Xiaomi, once had ties with Xiaomi TV. According to Xiaomi co-founder and head of Xiaomi TV, Wang Chuan, Skyworth President Yang Dongwen once intended to invest in Xiaomi TV and engaged in multiple exchanges with Wang Chuan, but the cooperation fell through due to differing philosophies.

Skyworth Had Intended to Invest in Xiaomi TV

Xiaomi TV began its project last year, led by DuoKan. Lei Jun admitted that Xiaomi acquired DuoKan to recruit key figures — Wang Chuan. Before joining Xiaomi, Wang Chuan had already participated in founding Xiaomi, interviewing several co-founders early on, so he is considered a co-founder of Xiaomi.

In fact, Lei Jun and Wang Chuan have deep connections beyond this project. When Kingsoft Software founder Qiu Bojun retired, Wang Chuan once acted as CEO of Kingsoft Software on behalf of Lei Jun until the current CEO Zhang Hongjiang took over and completed the handover. It was during this time as acting CEO of Kingsoft that Wang Chuan met Yang Dongwen, President of Skyworth.

In early 2012, Yang Dongwen led a team to visit Zhang Hongjiang to discuss cooperation between Skyworth TVs and Kingsoft Cloud, aiming to integrate Kingsoft Cloud into Skyworth's cloud TVs. Wang Chuan happened to be conducting work handovers with Zhang Hongjiang at the time, so Zhang invited Wang to meet Yang Dongwen. During their exchange, Wang discovered that Yang Dongwen was very interested in Xiaomi's model and introduced him to how Xiaomi could be used to make TVs.

Interestingly, Wang found that Yang Dongwen's understanding of Xiaomi did not come from news reports but was influenced by his son. Yang Dongwen's son is a "Mi fan" who told him many stories about Xiaomi, giving Yang Dongwen a clear understanding of Xiaomi's model. During their conversation, Yang Dongwen proactively proposed that Skyworth would fully invest while Wang Chuan would develop smart TVs.

Yang Dongwen offered favorable conditions: Skyworth would hold a small stake. To show sincerity, after leaving Kingsoft headquarters, Yang Dongwen sent people multiple times to communicate with Wang Chuan, hoping for him to develop smart TVs.

However, Xiaomi TV and Xiaomi Phone follow the same principle: initially selling near cost price, later offsetting costs through scale, earning money through value-added services and derivative income. As Lei Jun admitted, if smart TVs fail, Xiaomi might suffer heavy losses.

This was the biggest disagreement between Wang Chuan and Yang Dongwen. If they were to use Xiaomi's model, they would have to sell at cost price. In Yang Dongwen's view, Skyworth could not accept making TVs that not only didn’t make money but could also incur losses, leading them to abandon the idea of cooperating with Wang Chuan.

Despite this, Yang Dongwen did not give up on the idea of developing smart TVs. He recently openly admitted that Skyworth had been planning smart TVs for three years, believing that TVs, compared to PCs, phones, and tablets, have advantages in screen size, clarity, and color gamut range. Moving forward, the development of smart TVs mainly lies in open platforms and human-computer interaction, which determines the strategic transformation of TV companies.

Skyworth also intensified cooperation with internet companies, launching three models of smart TVs: 42-inch 2D Youth Edition, 3D Dream Edition, and 55-inch 3D Edition. The 42-inch Youth Edition is priced as low as 1999 yuan. Coolpad has become the second major brand under Skyworth TVs, differentiated in product positioning, R&D, and sales, but sharing logistics, warehouses, delivery, and services.

Now, Skyworth continues to fire shots at Xiaomi TV, whereas Xiaomi does not want to directly confront Skyworth, emphasizing instead the use of internet methods to make TVs. Xiaomi Technology Chairman Lei Jun openly pointed out that Xiaomi TV is equivalent to a large-screen computer, with TV being just one function. Xiaomi only promotes in areas where it can implement logistics, with limited initial releases of smart TVs.

Xiaomi Boxes Hit Policy Barriers

After unsuccessful communication with Skyworth, Wang Chuan did not abandon the idea of making smart TVs. At this point, Lei Jun also wanted to make smart TVs, and they quickly agreed. However, since Xiaomi had no prior experience in making smart TVs, they adopted a gradual approach for Xiaomi TV. Wang believed that boxes are an important subset of smart TVs, and starting with Xiaomi Boxes would allow them to test market reactions and see if there was real demand.

Wang's validation proved successful, with good word-of-mouth and high activity around Xiaomi Boxes, sparking continuous discussions about the living room wars. Official Xiaomi data shows that Xiaomi Boxes have a daily active rate of 40% and a weekly active rate of 70-80%.

Xiaomi Boxes did not start smoothly, as they touched upon the No. 181 document of the National Radio and Television Administration, facing the danger of being terminated right after birth. Although Xiaomi Boxes eventually "revived," they still underwent significant cuts, with some functions removed. Even after collaborating with CNTV, Xiaomi Boxes could only be sold in pilot regions such as Shanghai, Hangzhou, and Changsha.

For Xiaomi, which had experienced smooth sailing and dominated the smartphone market, this was undoubtedly a setback. Reflecting on this experience, Wang Chuan frankly admitted to Tencent Technology that Xiaomi had no prior experience with boxes and couldn't predict everything, so they could only do their best and leave the rest to fate.

Wang also pointed out that Xiaomi Boxes held high internal prestige, despite limited sales. Xiaomi Boxes had paved the way for Xiaomi TV, conducting many tests regarding MIUI compatibility with Xiaomi TVs. Now, Xiaomi TV integrates all the functions of Xiaomi Boxes.

Data shows that Xiaomi TV uses a 47-inch LG/Samsung 1080p HD screen, equipped with Qualcomm's 8064 quad-core 1.7G processing chip, claiming to meet users' needs for live TV programs, online videos, and interactive gaming. The Xiaomi TV app store integrates various games and applications, allowing users to install and delete freely. Like Xiaomi phones, it focuses on high cost-performance.

Relatively speaking, Xiaomi appears late to the smart TV market, lagging behind collaborations like iQIYI with TCL and another video website, LeEco, which entered earlier than Xiaomi's disruptive entry into the smartphone market.

Xiaomi TV currently hasn't unveiled its actual product yet. Besides experimenting with boxes, why has it been delayed? A former home appliance industry insider frankly told Tencent Technology that they hadn't seen Xiaomi TV but had obtained Xiaomi and LeEco set-top boxes. LeEco's set-top box was released six months earlier than Xiaomi's. From the set-top box perspective, whether in UI design or technical realization, LeEco lags significantly behind Xiaomi.

"I guess LeEco and Xiaomi spent different amounts of effort on their products, with Xiaomi having higher requirements, thus taking longer. Another explanation is that LeEco is clever, while Xiaomi is too slow." The person added, "I wouldn't say LeEco's product is bad, but it should be worse than Xiaomi's, which is a common consensus in the industry. Compared to LeEco, Xiaomi's set-top box embodies the industrial spirit of traditional Chinese TV manufacturers and set-top box manufacturers."

Xiaomi personnel also told Tencent Technology that Xiaomi's preparation time for smart TVs wasn't shorter than LeEco's. LeEco just released its product first, while Xiaomi announced only after ensuring product quality. In fact, it was precisely after Xiaomi refused Foxconn as its contract manufacturer that LeEco found Foxconn.

Xiaomi's thinking in the smart TV field learns from Apple. iPads were developed internally by Apple long ago but were ordered back to the drawing board several times by Jobs because they didn't meet his standards. Wang Chuan said that Apple wasn't the first to make smartphones nor the first to make MP3 players, but Apple's greatest advantage is exceeding user expectations, which is Xiaomi's goal for smart TVs.

Why Skyworth Targets Xiaomi Instead of Criticizing iQIYI

The development pattern of the smartphone industry serves as a cautionary tale for emerging devices like smart TVs.

Former giants Nokia, Motorola, and BlackBerry have been severely impacted, even acquired by tech giants. Xiaomi, however, has surged ahead in the smartphone sector, with its latest round of financing valuing it at 10 billion US dollars, and it harbors ambitions to reach annual sales of hundreds of billions within a few years.

Lei Jun stated in interviews that the low activation rate of smart TVs and the inability to make money stems from the lack of unified standards among models, making adaptation difficult. This is akin to iPhone using a unified platform requiring adaptation for only three to five models, whereas Android requires adaptation for hundreds of models. If in the future Xiaomi standardizes mass production to reach tens of millions of units,

If Xiaomi's modular operation method succeeds, it would undoubtedly be a nightmare for traditional home appliance companies. Yang Dongwen openly admitted that internet companies entering the hardware market pose a great impact on traditional manufacturing: firstly, significantly lowering price barriers, as internet companies offer very low hardware prices; secondly, TV manufacturers previously relied solely on hardware for profits, whereas internet companies earn more from content, advertisements, etc.; and traditional manufacturing enterprises are still unfamiliar with the internet, needing to learn internet operational experiences, such as user management and experience.

This reflects the genuine fear of most home appliance companies. Tencent Technology learned that last year, senior executives of home appliance companies visited Lei Jun multiple times to learn from his successful "new media marketing + online sales + cost control" model in the smartphone sector.

Interestingly, why do home appliance manufacturers direct their criticism towards Xiaomi and LeEco, especially Xiaomi, while sparing companies like iQIYI that also venture into the smart TV field? Even iQIYI Vice President and Head of Internet TV Division, Duan Youqiao, was invited to attend the launch of Skyworth's collaboration with Alibaba?

An informed source revealed to Tencent Technology that Duan Youqiao and iQIYI CEO Gong Yu are university classmates, and Duan joined iQIYI at the beginning of 2012. After Duan joined, iQIYI formed its internet TV department. Before joining iQIYI, Duan worked at Skyworth for many years.

However, this isn't the fundamental reason why iQIYI receives preferential treatment from Skyworth and other home appliance companies. iQIYI's collaboration with TCL isn't aimed at making money from hardware; hardware revenue still goes to TCL, and conversely, TCL can also receive a share from software services. iQIYI's aggressive promotion of internet TVs is actually aimed at embedding its own mobile client.

Duan Youqiao disclosed to Tencent Technology that in January 2012, mobile video accounted for less than 5% of iQIYI's video views. They predicted that by the end of 2013, mobile video would rival PC video, but by the last day of August this year, iQIYI statistics showed that although over 90% of iQIYI's revenue still comes from the PC end, mobile video volume has surpassed PC volume, showing enormous potential.

This means that in the future, iQIYI may not only collaborate with TCL but also with other manufacturers like Skyworth. Duan Youqiao pointed out that besides TCL, iQIYI's collaboration with another home appliance manufacturer on smart TVs may soon be launched. An internal person at iQIYI stated that iQIYI's content section could also collaborate with Xiaomi TV, though currently, home appliance companies' technology seems more mature.

Of course, the relationship between traditional color TV manufacturers and internet companies is quite delicate, with cautious cooperation. They attempt to build a "firewall" between trial products and existing businesses—brand, model, channel differentiation—even maintaining vigilance towards partners.

According to media present at Skyworth's launch event, the last paragraph of a press release on September 10th used the title "Crossing Boundaries Without Overstepping," emphasizing that "collaborating companies must maintain their own bottom lines and focus on what they're good at, not crossing boundaries for the sake of gaining the upper hand." This clearly indicates that Skyworth does not wish to lose control of the collaboration.
