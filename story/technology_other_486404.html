Marine monitoring for what

by wujizhui on 2011-09-07 16:58:09

With the country paying increasing attention to the ocean, the quality of marine environmental and protection of the marine environment have also become an indispensable part of marine development. Marine surveillance stations have been established in almost all coastal cities across the country to conduct routine monitoring of nearshore areas. This has provided huge data support for China's marine environmental protection and laid a solid foundation for the development of marine environmental protection. However, in any one of China’s industries, there exists to varying degrees a phenomenon of shortsightedness which is becoming increasingly obvious in marine affairs, including co-built marine stations where staffing disparities are quite large. But the monitoring work is becoming increasingly burdensome and technical, and I think that there are too many deficiencies in many aspects of marine stations at prefecture and county levels. Technical personnel cannot keep up with the development of technology and cannot conduct monitoring with ease. It is foreseeable that the data may be unreliable. Especially key data, ordinary chemical data actually cannot represent the basic conditions of a sea area. In addition, marine monitoring stations generally have low benefits, and the work is mostly routine monitoring, which prevents highly skilled talents from entering. Therefore, the monitoring capabilities of ordinary monitoring stations fundamentally cannot improve, resulting in disconnection phenomena. This further hinders the update and popularization of monitoring technology. This also has a lot to do with the inaction of superior departments. A small monitoring station now seems dispensable, completely going against the normal track of technological development. Technologically powerful sub-bureaus and central stations make up the main part, leading to a situation of everyone governing their own territory regardless of the life or death of subordinate departments. This has a lot to do with the current system of modern Chinese society. As for monitoring stations at the prefecture level, they might as well be abolished. Ordinary monitoring stations with low technical thresholds further prevent highly skilled talents from entering. This is detrimental to the development of marine monitoring undertakings. These are just some complaints of mine when I suddenly woke up from sleep.
