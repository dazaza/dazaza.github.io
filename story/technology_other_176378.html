How to do IT outsourcing governance well?

by jiji_20009 on 2009-07-14 11:27:19

In the process of enterprise informatization, more and more projects are being outsourced, bringing with them an increasing number of risks. Where should quality control for IT outsourcing begin?

In 1776, the famous British economist Adam Smith published "The Wealth of Nations," in which Smith concentrated on discussing the role of "social division" in promoting production development. The subject we are going to discuss today — IT outsourcing — is an extension of the "social division" concept into IT construction and IT services.

As the number of outsourced projects gradually increases, so do the risks: how to evaluate service providers, contract terms, quality control, and pricing are all potential "traps." Any issue at any stage can affect the progress and quality of the entire project.

How to conduct quality control for IT outsourcing projects? We invited Tang Jun, Manager of the Information Technology Department of China Storage and Transportation Development Co., Ltd. (hereinafter referred to as "China Storage and Transportation"), Xie Guangjun, Director of the Information Center of Beijing Yanjing Brewery Group Co., Ltd. (hereinafter referred to as "Yanjing Beer"), and Zhang Hong, Director of the Computer Center of the Sichuan Rural Credit Cooperatives (hereinafter referred to as "Sichuan Rural Credit Union"). After breaking down the various stages of IT outsourcing projects, we asked these three individuals to introduce how they ensure quality control in their IT outsourcing projects.

Under normal circumstances, in the entire process of an IT outsourcing project, which stage is most prone to issues, and how do such issues affect the final quality acceptance of the project?

Tang Jun: Let me first give a brief introduction about our company: China Storage and Transportation is a traditional logistics company mainly focused on warehousing and is one of the larger logistics suppliers in the country. IT is not our strong suit, so almost all of our IT projects are carried out through IT outsourcing. From my past experience, I believe that selecting an outsourcing service provider is a very important step; if this selection is poorly made, it is easy for problems to arise. Not long ago, during the course of one of our projects, the service provider suspended the project due to its own strategic transformation. This not only affected the progress and quality of the project but also impacted subsequent secondary development, upgrades, etc. Looking back now, this unsuccessful outsourcing project was actually a failed example.

Zhang Hong: Let me also introduce the background of the Sichuan Rural Credit Union: The Sichuan Rural Credit Union is a local small-to-medium-sized financial institution headquartered in Chengdu. We currently have over 5,000 business outlets, with deposits and loans amounting to approximately 3 billion yuan, and daily transaction volume reaching 3 million transactions. In terms of data scale, we can rank among the top ten largest data centers nationwide.

The rural credit cooperatives separated from the Agricultural Bank in 1996. Before 2006, the electronic level of the Sichuan Rural Credit Union had hardly improved and remained at the same level as when it separated from the Agricultural Bank in 1996. In 2006, the computer center was established. At that time, we were under tremendous pressure because we had to develop all the systems that other banks had already implemented, including core business, office automation, credit management, query analysis, etc. They had been building their information systems for more than ten or even twenty years, while we just started in 2006. Our computer center currently has more than 70 employees, which is incomparable to the scale of other banks with 2,000 to 3,000 people. Therefore, for us, all projects must be outsourced; in order to catch up with competitors' IT development within a short period, we will certainly choose outsourcing.

After more than two years of IT construction, which essentially means dealing with IT outsourcing companies for more than two years, I feel that the easiest stage where problems occur is what General Tang mentioned earlier — the selection of service providers, and another is how to achieve goals in phases during the project rather than trying to accomplish everything at once.

Xie Guangjun: There's no need for much introduction about Yanjing Beer. We are a company engaged in the production and sales of beer and beverages, and our informatization construction began in the 1990s, with many projects being carried out through outsourcing. Regarding the point raised by Director Zhang about "how to achieve goals in phases," I fully agree. Our company is constantly encountering such situations because the initial demand research time for most projects is insufficient. As the system develops, user demands increase, at which point the information department or CIO needs to control customer demands, defining which ones are primary and which can be postponed. However, this is often difficult to grasp and communicate, making this stage very prone to problems.

Two CIOs have mentioned the critical step of "selecting outsourcing service providers." So, I would like to ask the three of you if there are any good methods for selecting outsourcing service providers that you could share with us from your work experiences?

Zhang Hong: In selecting outsourcing service providers, I believe accurate evaluation is crucial. You need to provide very clear prompts to the bidding companies in your tender documents; otherwise, there will be significant consequences later on.

For example, with our reporting system, many companies came to bid when we were developing this system. Some of these companies may have only worked on projects for city commercial banks with hundreds of outlets or relatively smaller-scale rural credit unions. They might think their systems could be directly applied here and thus quoted a relatively low price, eventually winning the bid.

However, during the development process, they suffered greatly because the data volume here was on a completely different scale compared to what they had previously handled. On our side, the data was massive, and all their previous programs had to be rewritten, including table index design, table relationships, etc., all needing changes. It was equivalent to rewriting a new system, far from their initial expectations. Their low price led to losses, and in some cases, disputes ending up in court. Currently, we have a supplier facing such a crisis with us.

Regarding how to control your outsourcing service provider, we have a small method. As General Tang mentioned earlier, the extent of the outsourcing service provider's investment in your project is very important. Outsourcing service providers often have businesses nationwide, working on many projects simultaneously. How much they invest in your project is something we financial institutions are particularly concerned about when constructing IT systems.

To address this, our approach is quite clear: we require companies cooperating on our core business systems to establish branches in Chengdu. Moreover, key positions such as software project managers and staff at the branch must meet our requirements for qualifications. These people must be technical backbone members, and the company must ensure a certain number of such personnel to lay the foundation for subsequent services.

Tang Jun: When we initially selected outsourcing vendors, we always wanted to find IT service providers who had experience with similar projects, but such ideal candidates are hard to come by. Finding an IT outsourcing vendor completely familiar with your business is very challenging.

Later, we turned to those who had worked on somewhat similar projects. Of course, there are also indicators like technical strength and development capabilities, but these are common indicators. Another aspect is understanding through preliminary communication whether the outsourcing vendor has long-term development plans and determination in the field of warehouse logistics informatization. If so, we would adopt a strategic partnership cooperation model, establishing a "win-win" mode.

Xie Guangjun: Both Director Zhang and General Tang introduced some methods earlier, and our methods are also similar. If we select IT outsourcing service providers, we mainly consider the following factors:

First, their experience in the industry. If they lack prior experience in this industry, then a completely new industry and a completely new project would be very challenging for any developer;

Another factor, as Director Zhang mentioned, is quality and cost issues. During our bidding process, it's not necessarily better with lower prices; we need to provide quantitative prompts and constraints regarding quality and cost.
