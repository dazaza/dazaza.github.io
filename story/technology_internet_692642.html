Li Diantui Talks About Nourishing the Stomach

by anonymous on 2012-03-06 10:50:45

Li Dian-gui, a teacher of academic experience inheritance for famous old traditional Chinese medicine nationwide. He is currently the president of Hebei Province Hospital of Traditional Chinese Medicine and vice president of Hebei Medical University. He has his own unique insights and therapies in treating spleen-stomach diseases and liver-gallbladder diseases. As the saying goes: “Stomach disease needs three parts treatment and seven parts conditioning. If we want to cure chronic stomach disease that troubles us, we need to put more efforts on conditioning.” Once mentioned about conditioning, professor Li Dian-gui who is over 60 years old but very healthy, talked about his specialty. In his view, many people's stomach diseases are caused by their neglect. For example, some people often feel decreased appetite, nausea, belching, fullness or dull pain in the upper abdomen, but they always think it’s just because they didn’t eat well, and that their stomachs are a little uncomfortable. They either take some medicine themselves or endure it until it passes. In fact, it’s precisely these kinds of neglect that lead to the continuous development of the disease, and when it gets serious, there might even be a risk of cancer. From the perspective of traditional Chinese medicine, stomach disease is not solely caused by problems with the stomach. Therefore, taking care of the stomach should also consider the spleen and liver. Professor Li pointed out that besides quitting smoking and alcohol, paying attention to diet is the key. Strengthening the spleen and stomach: According to traditional Chinese medicine, the diet at the beginning of spring should mainly consist of pungent-warm and light food. This is because pungent-warm can dispel cold, and light can remove dampness, thus enabling the body to resist the invasion of wind-cold and rheumatism evils. For example, you can frequently cook porridge with Job's tears, yam, jujube, Poria cocos, and water chestnut to strengthen the spleen and replenish qi. Greasy, raw-cold, sour-astringent, sticky-hard foods, as well as extremely spicy-hot foods, will generate heat and fire, harming the spleen and stomach, so they should be avoided; vegetables and fruits can prevent "getting heaty", so they should be eaten more. Nourishing the spleen and liver: In spring, the liver is vigorous, making it easy to overpower the spleen and cause spleen-stomach diseases. Since sour taste is the inherent flavor of the liver, at this time we should reduce sour taste and increase sweet taste. The famous Tang dynasty doctor Sun Simiao once said: "In spring, we should reduce sour and increase sweet to nourish the spleen." It is recommended to consume more jujubes and yams in spring. Besides, excessive rising of liver qi in spring can cause recurrence of old spleen-stomach diseases. And the function of the liver is closely related to one's mental state, so we must learn to self-regulate our emotions, vent any unpleasant feelings in time, to avoid harming the liver.
