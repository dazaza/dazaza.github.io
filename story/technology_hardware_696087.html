Audi future of lighting technology: LED lanterns, matrix light

by ledlamp8 on 2012-03-07 20:24:51

Audi's LED headlight technology is significantly ahead of the competition. This technology was first introduced in 2008 in the energy-saving lamps of the Audi R8 supercar. The color temperature of LED lighting is 5,500 Kelvin, similar to daylight, which reduces visual fatigue. The maintenance-free service life of LEDs is longer than that of the vehicle itself. Lower power consumption is also one of the key advantages of efficient LEDs. For instance, the energy consumption of LED low beam lights is only about 40 watts per unit, even lower than the already highly efficient xenon headlights. The innovation in LED headlight technology now features a new design. In the Audi A8, for example, the recent light contains 10 separate lens modules, forming a unique arc across the headlight assembly. Just below this arc are another 22 white LEDs and 22 yellow LEDs, functioning as daytime running lights and turn signals. Homogeneous thick-walled technology enables them to display a more uniform and coherent band of light. Powered by two powerful four-chip LEDs and a free-form reflector system, the high beam provides superior highway lighting and turn signals, with a separate auxiliary function responsible for switching between high beam and low beam. LEDs do not generate particularly high temperatures; the maximum temperature of red LEDs can reach about 120 degrees Celsius, while white LEDs reach 150 degrees Celsius, far below the 400 degrees Celsius of halogen headlights. Therefore, Audi designers have specifically designed the headlight glass to ensure that the LED heat can effectively transfer to the headlight glass, ensuring no snow or fog accumulates during winter.

Matrix lighting is the name of Audi's headlight technology. The principle involves dividing the LED into many different light sources. Independent LEDs with lenses or mirrors provide superior illumination in any situation, without rotating authorities, simply following the lighting needs to open, close, or dim. Matrix beam headlights use cameras, navigation systems, or additional sensors to obtain necessary information. If the camera detects other vehicles or lights in front of built-up areas, this innovative technology will automatically adjust the high beam, gradually fading it out. The technology can also identify road topography and adjust lighting according to weather conditions such as rain or fog.
