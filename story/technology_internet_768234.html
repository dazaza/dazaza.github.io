Ex-Amazon Employee: Over-Investment Leads to Overall Company Losses

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 19:18:53

Recently, Eugene Wei, a former Amazon employee, conducted a comprehensive analysis of Amazon's business model and the misconceptions people have about the company. In his analysis, Eugene revealed that Amazon's current retail business is profitable; however, due to excessive and rapid investments, the company as a whole is experiencing losses.

Last week, Amazon reported its second consecutive quarterly loss. However, this did not negatively impact the company’s stock price. On the contrary, investors increased their investment in Amazon's stock, which pushed the share price to a record high and prompted industry analysts to set a high target price of $400 per share.

This phenomenon seems strange—Amazon isn't making money, yet it has been rewarded by the market. Some jokingly commented on Twitter that Amazon is a charity, while others questioned the company's profitability. Eugene argued that these jokes and doubts are incorrect. He stated that Amazon's retail operations are actually profitable, and the company's losses are mainly due to its heavy and rapid investments aimed at expanding global business and increasing sales volume. To further explain this, Eugene provided a more detailed description of Amazon's business model.

Eugene said: "In my view, an unprofitable business model would be if you spent $2 producing a cup of lemonade and sold it for $1 in the store. But if you sell it for $2 and it only costs you $1 to produce, then you're definitely making a profit. Therefore, you might decide to open lemonade stands on every street globally to expand your market, but this expansion and investment will require reinvesting all your profits to buy locations and materials for selling lemonade, ultimately aiming to establish lemonade stands on every street worldwide. In my opinion, this business model sounds profitable rather than being a charity."

Overall, Amazon has a profitable business and has already mastered the path to profitability. Currently, the company is heavily investing in warehouses and other facilities to build a more profitable global business.

Eugene's analysis of Amazon's business model is worth reading, and its main points are as follows:

Explanation of Amazon's business model: "Amazon is a typical fixed-cost business model that uses the internet to maximize the use of the company's fixed assets. Once it achieves sufficient sales volume, and the profits from those sales exceed the base of fixed assets, Amazon will become profitable. In fact, Amazon has already overcome this obstacle in the past."

"Only a few of Amazon's retail businesses are losing money. Whenever a retail business incurs a loss, Amazon strives to identify the reasons and make adjustments. Indeed, there are some retail businesses that lose money, but most are profitable."

"As a platform, Amazon is highly profitable. Many merchants and companies use Amazon's platform to sell products, and these businesses incur very low costs for Amazon."

"The reason Amazon is losing money is mainly because the company is heavily investing in warehouses and other expensive facilities to gain a competitive edge. However, Amazon has also discovered that fast and affordable delivery can boost sales, so the company is investing in these areas as well."

Many in the industry believe that if Amazon gains a monopoly in e-commerce and starts raising prices, it will eventually become profitable. In response, Eugene countered: "These claims are wrong. If Amazon were to stop investing now, it would quickly become profitable. Therefore, solving Amazon's profitability issue mainly depends on stopping its investments."
