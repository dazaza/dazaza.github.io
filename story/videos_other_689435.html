The starting point of the Uniqlo business

by wgyuvs8a on 2012-03-04 07:28:26

It all began on the morning of June 2, 1984. On that day, in a small alley off one of the main commercial streets in the central district of Hiroshima, in a place called Fukutcho, a casual wear retail store named "UNICLO CLOTHING WAREHOUSE" (literally meaning "unique clothing warehouse") opened its doors.

This store was unlike any other. It targeted teenagers and only sold affordable, high-quality casual wear. More importantly, it adopted the American campus warehouse CD sales model, selling clothes in a warehouse-style retail environment with self-service shopping. Because of the low prices and wide selection, "customers could shop for clothes as easily as browsing magazines at a bookstall," enjoying an undisturbed, convenient shopping experience. During the rainy season, shopping here became a novelty in the Japanese town, even drawing out the long-absent sun to make an appearance.

The unique positioning and exciting advertising slogans drew long queues outside the store early in the morning. As time passed, the overwhelming crowds instilled fear. To prevent chaos inside the store, staff had to restrict the number of customers entering multiple times. This frenzied state continued into the next day. Many years later, Tadashi Yanai recalled: "Local radio reported on the crowded conditions on the first day of opening. Perhaps due to nervousness, I said something unusual into the microphone during an interview: 'We sincerely apologize, but customers currently in line might not be able to enter the store. Please don't come anymore.' I think this statement was unprecedented."

This marked the beginning of the "Uniqlo" venture.

Tadashi Yanai said that his father made this starting point possible. "I always thought I wasn't suited for this kind of life. But somehow, I inherited my father's suit store and expanded it into today's Uniqlo. Looking back, I feel there is an inexplicable connection between my life path and my father's." Yanai wrote in his autobiography, "Nine Losses, One Win."

Born in 1949 in Yamaguchi Prefecture into a "clothing family," Yanai's father was an old-fashioned merchant, known for his sincerity and loyalty. With an older sister and two younger sisters, this meant that as the only son, Yanai had to take over his father's business from an early age. In Yanai's memory, his father seemed to always be scolding him.

This led to Yanai's rebellious nature. From 1967 to 1984, over a span of 17 years, Yanai was a notorious "bad kid." Like his peers in the "baby boomer" generation, he was swept up in Western hip-hop, the Beatles, and other rock-and-roll pop cultures, indulging daily in movies, pinball parlors, and mahjong shops. Approaching graduation, what he thought about most was "not having to work would be best." However, during this period, under his father's "coercion," he worked various jobs, such as reluctantly joining the Jusco supermarket recommended by his father and being transferred to work as a clerk in a clothing mall — all done half-heartedly.

Since his father was busy with his construction company, he decided to hand over the men's clothing store to Yanai.

At that time, "Kojun Shoji" had grown from an individual store in 1963 to a company with two stores selling men's clothing and VAN leisurewear, registered as "Kojun Shoji Co., Ltd." with capital of 6 million yen, and annual sales reaching 100 million yen.

This decision of trust made Yanai start rethinking his own life. He began learning like other clerks, arranging merchandise, managing inventory, and keeping accounts. Like all beginners, he encountered various problems. As he learned more about the company, he discovered operational issues such as inefficiency and slow capital turnover. When Yanai decided to reform these chronic problems, an issue arose: five out of six employees resigned.

"Even when the store manager, an old employee, resigned, my father didn't blame me a single word, Uniqlo. Instead, one day, my father handed over the important account books and seal of the company to me." Yanai said that from then on, he understood that the baton of the company had been passed to him, and he must do his utmost to run the company well.

The entrepreneurial journey is always tortuous and difficult, and Yanai was no exception.

In the 1980s, Japan's clothing retail industry was undergoing a transformation, with suburban stores springing up like mushrooms after rain. Some specialty stores selling imported goods appeared in Japan's suburbs. Sensing this trend, Yanai began trying to open branches in places like Shimonoseki, Kokura, Konodai, and Hiroshima, even attempting to open several women's clothing specialty stores.

However, the results were not good. Many stores opened and closed repeatedly. The benefit of failure was that Yanai slowly realized that although the stores struggled, casual wear sold well, and self-service might make consumers more comfortable. Yanai thought, why not open a self-service clothing store offering affordable casual wear? Soon, Yanai believed the timing was ripe.

As mentioned at the beginning of this section, the success of the first store finally allowed Yanai to emerge from the shadows, and he found his groove. Uniqlo quickly rose, opening 22 stores in the first three years of expansion. In 1991, the company name changed from Kojun Shoji to "Fast Retailing," translated in Chinese as "Xun Xiao" (meaning "fast sales"). By July 1994, when the company was listed on the Hiroshima Stock Exchange, Uniqlo already had 100 stores.

In 1999, on Yanai's 50th birthday, his father passed away. At the funeral, he told relatives and friends: "My father was the biggest competitor of my life." For the first time, he burst into tears in front of everyone, crying like a child.
