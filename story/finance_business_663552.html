The two different class blogs of the professor are here in this Tianya community where knowledgeable people gather.

by cfgsdftre77 on 2012-02-11 23:08:26

When it comes to professors, the first thing that comes to my mind is not "beast" or "animal," but rather pigs. Comparing professors to beasts and animals is a great disrespect to them, while comparing them to pigs is the utmost respect, mainly due to the admiration for Mr. Zhu Bajie (Pigsy from Journey to the West). Many people like Bajie, even including some monkeys. If even monkeys like something, how could humans possibly dislike it? Therefore, comparing professors to people's favorite animal reflects the affection for professors. This affection is heartfelt, making it as sincere as love.

People often eat pork and can hardly do without it daily, but many people's understanding of pigs is limited to the various slices and ribs on their plates. They know nothing about life in the pigsty, let alone the social structure of pigs. In reality, pig society is also highly stratified, roughly divided into two types based on job nature: one type engages in grassroots work, dedicated to the dining table, affectionately referred to by experts as "working pigs," or what ordinary people call "meat pigs." The other type has a different job nature, responsible for continuing the lineage, specifically breeding boars and sows for reproduction, abbreviated as "breeding pigs." Breeding pigs belong to a fortunate class; they possess the maximum amount of resources, never lack food or water, don't have to endure cold winds, and can live a married life every day, potentially living long lives. This noble yet corrupt lifestyle almost rivals that of human emperors. The primary function of emperors is essentially mating, which is very similar to the role of breeding pigs. Since breeding pigs can be compared to human emperors, one can imagine how enviable their status is to the meat pigs.

In contrast, meat pigs are not so lucky. Meat pigs are born to undergo a surgery shortly after birth—castration or ovariectomy. This surgery is akin to the procedure of removing certain parts in the palace, but it is even more cruel. Eunuchs still have desires after losing certain parts and can play with concubines or maids using other body parts such as fingers or tongues, whereas meat pigs grow small parts but have no desires at all. Looking at the opposite sex evokes only familial feelings, not romantic ones. Female pigs are even more pitiful; they have never even experienced menstruation. Their life is incredibly monotonous, yet they cannot avoid working. Their job is to grow as much meat as possible. Once they've grown tall, long, and fat, they will immediately be slaughtered to feed people. This is the entirety of a meat pig's life. Compared to breeding pigs, this fate is too tragic, making it difficult to enjoy pork upon reflection. However, pigpens are tightly controlled, and their fate is in the hands of those who feed them. Thus, meat pigs are doomed to a fate worse than dogs from birth. Such a world is even less fair than the human world.

Comparing professors to pigs is because professors can also be divided into two classes like pigs. Some professors resemble breeding pigs, whether male or female, they are the elite among professors. These individuals, like breeding pigs, possess the maximum amount of resources—food, tools, money—all available. They do not lack status either. The main work of these professors is not teaching, doing research, or pursuing knowledge but sleeping, eating, then sleeping again, and endlessly engaging with women. They are considered elite because they have countless servants at their command, writing articles, conducting research, and even handling finances for them. They lead carefree lives without worrying about survival or work. Just like each breeding pig, their main job besides wasting food is to use themselves as mating tools—not with their spouses but with students. In this sense, such professors are undoubtedly breeding pigs. This comparison may seem derogatory towards professors, and I apologize sincerely. What I actually want to talk about is love, which is the most important work and pursuit in a professor's life. As intellectuals and upper-class individuals, these professors understand love the most deeply. Their love often resembles that of ancient emperors—stunning and lasting for years, creating rare and valuable legacies, just like the legacy produced by Professor Yang Zhenning and his student Mrs. Wong Fan. Overall, the lives of these professors are truly admirable. Professors admire them, many others admire them, and I admire them too. Unfortunately, not every farmer-like person can become a professor like a breeding pig. This is mainly due to genetic differences and bloodline distinctions. For example, Professor Kong Qingdong from Peking University carries the unique genes from Confucius in every cell, making him part of the rarest and noblest bloodline in the world. If such professors don't act like breeding pigs, the entire human race would be in trouble—should we let dogs take over the role of breeding pigs?

Then there are the professors akin to meat pigs, representing a lowly class within the professoriate—the "meat pigs" among professors. Although these professors are also respectfully called professors, they live at the bottom of campus life, practically equivalent to gatekeepers or cleaning ladies. Unlike the privileged "breeding pig" professors who live carefree and successful lives, these professors engage in basic work, teaching large classes and smaller seminars, and the best-performing ones might become counselors. Beyond that, they have no other prospects. Engaging in such primitive work, without projects, funds, or the ability to write SCI papers, they receive meager rewards. Their income is pitiful. I always think these professors are pitiful, and they themselves often feel the same way. Just like the pigs destined for slaughter in the pigpen, they live in a "pigpen," with their fates firmly controlled by others. How can they choose their own destinies and paths?

Professors being divided into two classes like in pig society is because there indeed exist two completely different groups of professors today. Viewing professors as a whole, their society resembles that of pigs. Meat pigs are born unable to decide their own fates, and although breeding pigs are elites in pig society, they cannot become true nobility. Nobles can determine their own fates, but no matter how excellent the breeding pigs are, they cannot escape the reality of being confined and eventually being killed—not enjoying family happiness until old age. So pigs will always remain pigs, forever subject to being slaughtered. Now looking at our respected professors, even the top professors, can they really decide their own fates? In human society, pigs will always be pigs, and professors live lives like pigs.
