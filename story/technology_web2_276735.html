Black car owners in Lechang, Guangdong besieged a passenger bus and were detained

by 1dwd181q7w on 2009-11-25 10:49:30

Core tip: Starting from 2006, Guangdong Lechang launched a comprehensive crackdown on unlicensed minibuses. This action brought a strong survival crisis to the owners of these vehicles. In April 2006, more than 200 unlicensed minibuses were lined up in front of the Lechang government square, seeking means of livelihood from government officials. Owner Deng was administratively detained for 15 days in October 2009 for "gathering and obstructing public transportation." In mid-October, in front of the Yunyan Town Government of Lechang City, some unlicensed minibus owners attempted to stop rural passenger bus operations, with local police on high alert.

Southern Rural Newspaper reported on November 24 that Pingshi Town, Lechang City, Guangdong Province, is the first station of the Beijing-Guangzhou Railway entering Guangdong. A huge rural passenger transport market has attracted numerous unlicensed minibuses to this bustling northern Guangdong town. During the peak period of 2005 and 2006, over 700 unlicensed minibuses crowded the passenger transport market in the Pingshi area so tightly that it was described as "impervious to wind or rain."

Since 2006, in order to rectify the traffic order in the Pingshi area, relevant departments of Lechang City have launched several campaigns against unlicensed minibuses. From a complete ban, to incorporation into companies, and finally converting all to rural passenger transport, the official governance approach has gradually become clear during adjustments.

However, "veteran" unlicensed minibus owner Cheng Deng (a pseudonym) would never have thought that at the age of forty, he would end up in the Lechang City Detention Center during the final stage of the Pingshi area traffic rectification.

An administrative detention notice shows that Cheng Deng was sentenced to 15 days of administrative detention on October 14, 2009, for "gathering and obstructing public transportation." On October 19, Deng was released early.

At the same time, due to the inability to obtain access qualifications for the passenger transport market in the Pingshi area from the Lechang City Transportation Bureau, the medium-sized bus jointly purchased by Cheng Deng and other 117 people had been immobile in the parking lot for more than two months.

Cheng Deng often envisioned the beautiful moment when he could transform from an unlicensed minibus owner to a legal operator. However, after being thrown into the detention center, he completely became an opponent of the transportation department.

Comprehensive Ban: 200 Unlicensed Minibuses Demonstrate in Front of the Municipal Government

In Cheng Deng's view, since the first unlicensed minibus appeared in Pingshi Town, a historical drama of "wits versus wits" between unlicensed minibus owners and traffic management departments began. The current deadlock is the most exciting part before the curtain falls.

As early as 1993, Cheng Deng drove a microcar shuttling on the rural roads between Pingshi and Shaping, becoming the first generation of unlicensed minibus owners in the Pingshi area. "At that time, each town had at most 3 or 4 cars running, and the traffic management department was very relaxed in its management." Talking about the good old days of free galloping and making big money, Cheng Deng still feels nostalgic.

The prosperity of the market brought high profits. According to Cheng Deng's introduction, under normal circumstances, each unlicensed minibus could earn a pure income of 3000 yuan per month. "For those who worked hard, they could recover the cost of purchasing a car in one year."

In the following more than ten years, the number of unlicensed minibuses in the Pingshi area exploded. By around 2004, the average number had exceeded 500.

The turning point occurred in 2004. That year, a businessman from Dongguan came to Lechang, contracted the rural passenger transport lines in the Pingshi area, and opened passenger transport lines from Pingshi to Huangpu, Lao Pingshi, and Meihua. His investment of 3.5 million yuan was considered a major achievement in introducing funds from outside the city to Lechang.

The arrival of the regular army made the unlicensed minibuses feel the survival crisis for the first time, and they also began to find ways to respond. The emergence of the "queue for passengers" form made the competition among unlicensed minibuses gradually shift from disorderly to orderly. In the past, unlicensed minibuses would not hesitate to fight to grab passengers, but now they started to queue up according to a fixed order, "the next vehicle can only board passengers after the previous one is full."

In 2006, in the "siege" of unlicensed minibuses, due to the lack of price advantage and flexibility, the passenger transport lines operated by the businessman from Dongguan were basically in a semi-paralyzed state. Therefore, he had no choice but to seek help from the Lechang municipal government. A large-scale crackdown on unlicensed minibuses followed.

The storm-like ban brought a strong survival crisis to the unlicensed minibus owners. On a Thursday in April 2006, more than 200 unlicensed minibuses from the Pingshi area collectively moved south, lining up in front of the municipal government square in Lechang, seeking means of livelihood from government officials.

The "impressive" lineup of minibuses stood in front of the municipal government for six days before gradually dispersing. In the eyes of local officials, the unlicensed minibus owners had reached the "arrogant" level at that time. However, just as Lechang City was preparing to take further action against unlicensed minibuses, an unprecedented flood disaster forced this process to be interrupted.

Subsequently, the relevant departments of Lechang City also reflected on the direct ban of unlicensed minibuses. As a result, a "transition" plan emerged, which involved incorporating unlicensed minibuses into company management and then switching to medium-sized buses for operation.

However, the planners did not expect that this "recruitment" strategy, which currently benefited only part of the people, did not make all unlicensed minibus owners stand on the side of the government.

Corporate Reform: A Few Owners Sign Contracts Late at Night

According to the logic of the responsible person from the Lechang City Transportation Bureau, only those incorporated into corporate management in 2007 could obtain the "entry ticket" for subsequent conversion to rural passenger transport medium-sized bus operations. However, based on multiple sources of information, many key details of the corporate reform in 2007, which is now seen as a pivotal link, remain unclear.

According to the information provided by the Lechang City Transportation Bureau, starting from August 15, 2007, the work of rectifying the traffic order in the Pingshi area was restarted after being suspended for more than a year. All unlicensed minibus owners were required to register with the local town governments before September 10, 2007, and sign operating contracts with the only local passenger transport enterprise, Yongtong Passenger Transport Company of Ping Shi Yongtong Group (hereinafter referred to as "Yongtong Company"), from September 27 to 28. Those who failed to do so within the deadline would be deemed to have abandoned corporate management and would no longer be allowed to engage in passenger transport.

Records show that the progress of incorporating unlicensed minibuses in the Pingshi area into corporate management was not optimistic. Taking the three towns of Meihua, Shaping, and Yunyan as examples, among the 157 unlicensed minibus owners, only 39 signed operating agreements with Yongtong Company.

According to some people, the number 39 does not reflect the real enthusiasm of all owners for corporatization. A Mr. Liao, an owner, introduced to reporters that although incorporating into corporate operation meant an annual increase of nearly ten thousand yuan in expenses in exchange for the security guarantee provided by Yongtong Company, facing the rare opportunity to gain legal status, many people were tempted after some hesitation.

However, the opportunity quietly slipped away while many owners were asleep. Contrary to the official claim that the policy had been widely notified through various channels, multiple owners recalled that information about the corporate operation reform was known by most people only on September 28. At 8 p.m. that day, the owners received an urgent notice from the town government, requiring them to attend a meeting at Yongtong Company to receive notifications regarding matters related to incorporation into corporate management.

However, the corporate management contract was not signed on the spot during the meeting that night. And the next day, when many owners again requested to sign the contract, they were told that "the contract signing was already completed."

Subsequent reports indicated that after the meeting ended on the evening of September 28, a small part of the owners who had "close relations" with the Meihua Town government were notified to sign contracts with Yongtong Company around midnight, while many owners were already asleep; few owners from Shaping and Yunyan Towns received the "special notification."

The corporate reform in 2007 marked the beginning of the differentiation of the group of unlicensed minibus owners in the Pingshi area. Although they still did not obtain operating permits, any unlicensed minibus that signed an agreement with Yongtong Company could brazenly pick up passengers in Pingshi Town thereafter. Other unlicensed minibuses excluded from the corporate reform could no longer touch the "golden routes" originating from Pingshi and leading to other towns.

Rural Passenger Transport: Unlicensed Minibuses Fail to Seek "Recruitment"

In 2009, the related rectification work entered its final stage. Seeing that the unlicensed minibus owners from Huangpu and other towns had all transformed into rural passenger transport line operators through joint stock purchases, the 118 owners from Shaping and Yunyan Towns also began to plan their future, including Cheng Deng.

In September, Cheng Deng and others raised nearly 500,000 yuan, purchasing three medium-sized buses in succession, preparing to operate on the Pingshi-Shaping and Pingshi-Yunyan routes. However, significant risks were also hidden in this 500,000 yuan business deal. Before deciding to buy the cars, these people had not yet obtained the rural passenger transport "birth permit" issued by the Lechang City Transportation Bureau.

In Cheng Deng's view, this was not a reckless move, "We were drawing lessons from successful cases."

In May of the same year, without obtaining approval from the Lechang City Transportation Bureau, 33 owners from Meihua Town pooled funds to purchase 10 medium-sized buses and began operating on the Pingshi-Meihua route that month. The operation of this route has been normal until now, undoubtedly giving Cheng Deng and his group a sense of reassurance.

However, what the 118 owners did not expect was that on September 1, their application to open the Pingshi-Shaping route was rejected by the Lechang City Transportation Bureau. On September 13, when the two buses they initially purchased prepared to run the "first class" of the "Pingshi-Shaping" route, the traffic management department arrived promptly and firmly turned on the red light, confiscating the relevant vehicles. On September 30, another attempt to run the Pingshi-Yunyan route was also shelved due to the strong intervention of the traffic management department.

Another piece of news that followed tightened the brows of Cheng Deng and others. The Lechang City Transportation Bureau granted the rural passenger transport operation rights of the Pingshi-Shaping and Pingshi-Yunyan routes coveted by Cheng Deng et al. to the Meihua Passenger Transport Team composed of the 33 owners currently operating the Pingshi-Meihua route.

Regarding the act of the Lechang City Transportation Bureau "privately granting" the operation rights of the new passenger transport routes to the Meihua team, Cheng Deng believed that according to the "Road Transport Regulations", "if there are more than three applicants for the same route, a bidding form can be used to make licensing decisions," and the Lechang City Transportation Bureau directly designated operators without bidding, given the presence of competitors, clearly showing inappropriate administrative licensing.

From the perspective of the Lechang City Transportation Department, the 118 owners from Shaping and Yunyan Towns did not possess competitive qualifications. A relevant person in charge of the Lechang City Transportation Bureau introduced to Southern Rural Newspaper that the wild chicken cars in the Pingshi area eligible to convert to medium-sized bus operations must be the vehicles that had already signed contracts with Yongtong Company in 2007. Among the so-called 118 owners, the vast majority did not enter Yongtong Company's operations in 2007, and some were even engaged in freight or were social individuals without minibuses, thus not qualifying for transition to medium-sized bus operations, "which belongs to unreasonable requests." The person in charge stated that transitioning to medium-sized bus operations is part of a predetermined rectification plan, different from the usual concept of new routes; besides, rural passenger transport routes yield meager profits, and not many are interested, so there is no need for bidding.

The official statement did not convince Cheng Deng and others. On October 13, conflicts arose again. That day, the Pingshi-Shaping and Pingshi-Yunyan routes were officially opened by the Meihua team. Feeling discriminated against by the authorities, the owners from Shaping and Yunyan areas mobilized more than ten minibuses, surrounding the medium-sized buses and preventing their operation. Afterwards, nine car-blocking owners, including Cheng Deng, were subjected to "administrative measures" by the authorities.

(Original source: Southern Rural Newspaper)
