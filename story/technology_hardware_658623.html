Antiques are not necessarily more valuable the older they are.

by fbfshtwk0423 on 2012-02-04 10:56:53

Antiques are not necessarily more valuable the older they are. In the book "A Guide to Antiques" by Zhao Ruzhen from the Republic of China era, it is stated: "Some people mistakenly believe that the value of antiques lies solely in their age, and that all ancient objects are precious, with the older ones being more valuable. In fact, this is not true." It further explains: "The essence of antiques is their exquisite quality and fine craftsmanship, which cannot be replicated by later generations." Rarity makes things valuable; in antique auctions, rarity determines value. Some antiques were already precious at the time, such as the five great official kilns of the Song Dynasty—Guanyao, Ruyao, Ge Yao, Junyao, and Dingyao ceramics—which have the saying, "I would rather have one piece of Guan or Jun than an entire estate." During the Ming and Qing Dynasties, imperial kilns were supervised by court officials, with the emperor personally approving the clay, pigments, and designs before production could begin. Any surplus items were destroyed to prevent them from entering civilian circulation, and no expense was spared in terms of time, labor, materials, or cost. However, many ancient Han Dynasty pottery items used in daily life, despite their age, remain relatively worthless today and are less valuable than a single piece of Qing Dynasty official kiln porcelain.

If you wish to use antiques as investments or to benefit future generations, you must first understand some basic knowledge about antiques and calligraphy, avoiding blind following of trends. The media should not only hype antiques but also popularize knowledge among antique enthusiasts and collectors so that unscrupulous antique dealers' ever-changing scams cannot prevail.

These salvaged porcelain pieces, though soaked in seawater for years and some even covered with shells, still retain the beauty and elegance of blue-and-white porcelain, their captivating charm undiminished. This reminds me of what Mr. Shen Congwen once said: Good ceramics are a fusion of clay and life.

In the early 1990s, I helped a friend select over 20 pieces of Kangxi blue-and-white porcelain from a shop on an antique street in a Southeast Asian country. The porcelain included teapots, plum vases, yuhuchun bottles, brush holders, and plates, decorated with patterns of winding lotus flowers, chrysanthemums, morning glories, the Eight Immortals crossing the sea, warriors on horseback, and calligraphic texts from the front and back sections of Su Shi's "Chibi Fu."

For various reasons, my friend has been unable to bring these blue-and-white porcelain pieces back to China for nearly 20 years.

In Hainan, China, there have been discoveries of Kangxi blue-and-white porcelain, but recently, there have been reports of unscrupulous antique dealers sinking replicas into the sea to "plant" antiques as a scam.

There are even more examples of fake antiques harming future generations. I've heard of a story where two brothers divided their inheritance; the elder wanted the house and land, while the younger took a piece of antique, supposedly a Song Dynasty Jun porcelain. Passed down from great-grandfather to grandfather, when it came time for the grandson to inherit, the family had fallen into poverty. When the grandson needed money for university tuition and hoped to sell the antique, he discovered it was a late Qing Dynasty replica...

Today, porcelain is mass-produced, with shapes and designs customized and printed in bulk, cost-calculated, industrially manufactured, and produced on assembly lines, making them ubiquitous. In other words, the producers didn't treat them as artworks, so how can you collect them as art? Burying them underground or throwing them into the sea to "plant" them for a hundred years will only result in them being used as bowls and plates in the future, without significant appreciation in value. A piece of brass buried for ten thousand years won't turn into bronze!

"Planting antiques?" I've never heard of it. There might be two types of people involved in "planting antiques": one is illegal antique dealers who "age" items to deceive buyers, which is a very malicious form of forgery; the other consists of those who misunderstand the value of antiques, believing that the older an item is, the more valuable it becomes, thus burying items for future generations. The latter cannot be called forgery; it stems from a lack of knowledge about antiques. Such actions will not leave wealth for future generations but may instead harm them.

After purchasing, we learned that although these ancient porcelain pieces are Chinese cultural relics, according to the laws of that country, cultural relics are prohibited from being taken out of the country. Through an intermediary, my friend temporarily stored the purchased porcelain at the home of a local tour manager.

A few years ago, during a revisit to the old place, I accidentally found these porcelain pieces in an antique shop. The tour manager had photographed and modeled these 20 pieces of Kangxi blue-and-white porcelain, then commissioned skilled forgers in Jingdezhen, China, to replicate countless sets of Kangxi blue-and-white porcelain. Afterward, these replicas were returned, immersed in the sea, and the surface luster removed by seawater. Food that shellfish like to eat was placed inside the ceramics, allowing shellfish to gather and reproduce, resulting in many shells adhering to the ceramics, giving them an appearance of antiquity. Using a bait-and-switch tactic, customers saw the real items, but what was shipped out were the fakes. This manager repeatedly succeeded, deceiving many tourists. The genuine Kangxi blue-and-white porcelain pieces have remained untouched in the store for nearly 20 years.

A friend seriously told me, since you love antiques, why don't you 'plant' some for future generations? It would be beneficial! He mentioned that bronze artifacts can be buried underground to grow rust shells; porcelain can be submerged in seawater to allow marine organisms to breed and age them; even Taihu stones can be submerged in lakes to acquire patina!
