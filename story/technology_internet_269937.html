Seven "Don't Buy" Rules for Stock Trading

One, resolutely do not buy stocks after an abnormal volume surge. A surge in trading volume is generally a signal that the market's major players are beginning to exit. If investors are indeed interested in stocks with increased trading volume, they should wait and observe. A straight line can be drawn at the closing price of the day when the volume surged. If the stock price touches this line later, it may be worth considering buying.

Two, resolutely do not buy stocks after a sharp rise. If the stock price shoots up like a chopstick straight into the sky, it indicates that the main player has already exited. Entering at this point will likely result in being trapped. Sharp rises are driven by large funds. When a stock increases by 300% or even more, the original market major player withdraws, and a new major player usually does not form quickly. There typically won't be a large buyer stepping in immediately, making it hard for the stock price to rise in the short term.

Three, resolutely do not buy stocks after a significant rights issue. Rights issues present another opportunity for market major players to escape. For example, if a stock rises from 3 yuan to 20 yuan, few people would step in. However, after a 10-for-10 rights issue, the price becomes only 10 yuan, creating an illusion of being cheaper. People tend to prefer buying cheaply, but once retail investors enter, the market major players escape completely. If there is also a high trading volume after the rights issue, it is absolutely something to avoid.

Four, resolutely do not buy stocks with major problems. Do not enter stocks that are under serious management investigation; it's better to observe first. For instance, Yinguangxia and Kelon Electric. If problem stocks experience a sharp rise, it can be concluded that institutions are manipulating them. However, these stocks may face regulation or could experience a broken capital chain, presenting significant risks. Retail investors cannot handle such stocks and taking the risk is not worth it.

Five, resolutely do not buy stocks that have been in long-term consolidation. These stocks generally indicate that the main player has left, but has not yet taken away all the shares. When observing individual stocks, one should look at their continuous trends. Stocks that remain in long-term consolidation with little fluctuation should not be bought.

Six, resolutely do not buy stocks with publicized positive news. This is a common misconception among current investors. Even if the performance is excellent, everyone in China can see it and buy it, who can make money? Moreover, performance can be artificially manipulated. There is a famous saying in the market: "Good news known by everyone is definitely not good news; bad news known by everyone is definitely not bad news; when bad news is exhausted, it turns into good news; when good news is released, it might lead to a big fall."

Seven, resolutely do not buy stocks heavily held by funds. Fund accounts cannot be concealed and are disclosed once per quarter. Funds do not act as major players; they leave once they make a profit. Of course, this theory has a time factor and is most evident during bear markets. In bull markets, this can generally be ignored as stocks usually rise universally, and funds will not sell. During bear markets, funds flee, leaving retail investors trapped. Therefore, during bear markets, stocks heavily held by funds should not be bought.
