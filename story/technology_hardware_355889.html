What is a model

by dycymodel on 2010-05-26 10:59:00

What is the model at http://www.dycymodel.com? What is an analytical model? Asking this question now may seem a bit out of place, but I always feel that this term appears too frequently in everyday life without much consensus. It's just treated as a noun. People often think that building a model can solve everything, as if it were omnipotent. Below, unless otherwise specified, "model" refers to "analytical model."

Yesterday, I had to write a report, which was essentially a summary of the project. As usual, I needed to describe the "model," and this task has become increasingly headache-inducing because I don't really know what a "model" is. Gradually, I began to think that we shouldn't always use some kind of mining model to solve problems. In many situations, we just need to look at data statistics and make decisions by thinking a little. But doing so doesn't show the workload, and data statistics are too monotonous. Models appear more sophisticated. Thus, in an analytical application project, there must be something called a "model" to serve as a facade.

I struggled and finally managed to turn a series of analyses and statistics into two named models. After all the effort, looking at the PowerPoint slides, I started to ponder what exactly a model is.

It should have the following characteristics:

1. **Abstract**: Some people refer to mining models when they talk about models, while others might mean empirical models. Regardless, one feature is universal: it must be general, abstracted from many things and phenomena. Of course, some models only apply under specific conditions. If it only applies to individuals and not to the masses, it's not called a model. For example, a person who always goes to the toilet immediately after eating—it’s a personal habit, not a model. However, if you find that a group of people all go to the toilet immediately after eating, and 80% of them have short intestines, then you can abstract something like—people with short intestines tend to defecate right after eating. This is a model.

2. **Has a conclusion**: Data statistics are not models because they lack conclusions. This feature is closely related to the previous one because the result of abstraction is a conclusion, a rule. The expression of the rule could be in a simple "if-then" form or a more complex one, such as "x+y-z=a." Clearly, this conclusion applies to a specific group. The larger the group, the more universally applicable the conclusion. For example, "all humans will die eventually" is a very universal model applicable to humanity. Of course, it can be even more universal, like those scientists such as Einstein discovered, e=mc², which is even more impressive.

For now, these are the two features I could think of. I initially considered adding a "simplicity" feature, but realized that this is a feature of a "good model" rather than a necessary feature of a model.

With this understanding, I reviewed the so-called models from the past to see if they fit these criteria.

First, let's talk about mining models. Suppose we suggest a predictive model to predict when someone will die. This model needs to abstract from several people and might conclude something like—under normal circumstances, people die around the age of 80; drinking adds points, smoking subtracts points, excessive indulgence adds points, serious illness subtracts points... Unfortunately, many of our so-called mining models meet the first characteristic but not the second. Not that they lack the second characteristic, but they lack further interpretation. A KPI prediction can use a "model" to predict next quarter's value, but why does it get that value? Nobody cares. This is quite a sad situation.

Now let's talk about empirical models. Who says making decisions based on intuition isn't scientific? It depends on who's doing the intuiting. If you're experienced, your intuition leads to an empirical model. Here, experience itself contains a large number of samples, and summarizing experience is abstraction. A person, from ten previous experiences of heartbreak, summarizes and finds that to avoid heartbreak, one needs to say sweet nothings frequently, give gifts occasionally, and showcase one's abilities regularly. This can also be considered a model. If he could experience heartbreak a hundred times, undoubtedly, he would become an authoritative expert in love. From then on, he could dominate the field of love using his heartbreak model, and data mining wouldn't stand a chance against him.

Besides analytical models, there are many other types of models: data models, business models, conceptual models, airplane models, human body models... I tried applying the above two characteristics to these, but found that they generally only meet the first characteristic—they are abstract. Whether or not they lead to conclusions is debatable. Perhaps it depends on the perspective.

A model is an abstract simulation of real-world things.

An analytical model is an abstract simulation of patterns. A data model is an abstract simulation of relational entities, and a business model is an abstract simulation of business elements and processes. So-called models are simulations expressed through an abstraction process and represented in some form. Therefore, the so-called conclusion is merely the expression form of the pattern. The expression form of a data model is data structure, and a human body model is the puppet that looks like a person.

Thus, a model of models emerges—it is abstract and expressed through some form of representation.
