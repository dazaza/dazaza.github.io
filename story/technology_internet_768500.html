Apple's Remarkable Performance in Japan: Sales Growth Last Fiscal Year Far Exceeded China's

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 18:56:50

In the eyes of many companies, Japan is not an ideal growth market. Over the past two decades, the country's economy has been plagued by sluggish growth, accompanied by issues such as a declining birthrate and an aging population. However, Apple has achieved enviable results in the Japanese market in recent years.

Over the past two years, Japan has become one of Apple’s fastest-growing markets, with its growth rate far exceeding that of the domestic U.S. market, as well as Greater China and other emerging regions in Asia. Data shows that in the last fiscal year ending September 28, Apple’s sales in Japan amounted to $13.5 billion, representing a 27% increase. Meanwhile, the year-on-year growth rates in the Chinese market and other Asia-Pacific markets were 12.8% and 4.1%, respectively. Additionally, Japan is Apple’s most profitable market and the only region where operating profits increased in the company’s last fiscal year.

There is no doubt that the iPhone has been a crucial driving force behind Apple's success in Japan. Thanks to substantial marketing efforts and generous subsidies provided by local carriers, the iPhone has become a highly sought-after item in Japan, reminiscent of the days when Japanese consumers scrambled to buy Louis Vuitton handbags and Burberry scarves.

In September this year, NTT DoCoMo, Japan's largest mobile carrier, began selling the iPhone for the first time, undoubtedly providing new impetus for Apple's growth in Japan. Prior to this, Apple had already become the best-selling smartphone in Japan. According to data from Tokyo-based MM Research Institute, over the past six months ending in September, the iPhone accounted for nearly 40% (37%) of the Japanese smartphone market share. Moreover, in the fiscal year ending March 31, 2013, Apple's iPad dominated half of Japan's tablet market, holding more than 50% market share.

As the last major Japanese carrier to offer the iPhone, DoCoMo significantly reduced the price of the latest Apple iPhone model to attract other consumers and also introduced incentives to encourage existing users to upgrade their phones. In response, Japan's other two major carriers, KDDI and SoftBank, similarly launched iPhones with astonishing discounts.

Analyst Timothy Arcuri of investment firm Cowen & Co predicted that Apple would sell between 11 million and 12 million iPhones in Japan in 2013, with sales expected to increase to 20 million next year.

In the view of some professionals, the success of the iPhone in Japan can be attributed mainly to two factors. First, the Japanese mobile phone market shares similarities with the U.S., where phones are subsidized by carriers and come with multi-year contracts. In contrast, the subsidy system in emerging markets like China is less developed, making the high cost of purchasing an iPhone unaffordable for many consumers.

Second, Samsung's relative weakness in Japan is also one of the reasons for the iPhone's success. Globally, Samsung is the largest smartphone manufacturer, but in Japan, it ranks fourth after Apple, Sony, and Sharp, partly due to Japanese consumers' bias against Korean brands. Furthermore, the withdrawal of Japanese domestic mobile phone brands like NEC from the smartphone market has objectively created favorable conditions for Apple's development.
