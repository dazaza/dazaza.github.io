dnfwg First, the situation of weak external demand will continue for a period of time.

by dnfmeimei on 2009-12-06 01:06:31

The situation of financial regulation next year will be more complex: First, the issue of sustainability of recovery has not been resolved; second, the pressure of price increase is rising; third, there are structural problems in credit; fourth, there are significant uncertainties in the international finance sector. Many uncertain factors pose new challenges to financial regulation.

According to the Shanghai Securities News report: Compared with 2009 when efforts were made to "ensure growth," the tasks and situation of China's financial regulation in 2010 will be more complex.

First, the issue of sustainability of economic recovery has not been resolved. Under the strong stimulus of a package of government policies, China's economy basically established a recovery trend in 2009. However, China's economic recovery has not been smooth sailing and still faces two major difficulties: weak external demand and the switch of domestic growth momentum.

One aspect is that the situation of weak external demand will continue for some time, making it difficult to completely reverse China's export situation. Since the first quarter of this year, with the continuous introduction and effectiveness of various governments' measures to "stabilize finance and ensure economic growth," the real economies of major countries such as the United States, Japan, and Europe have begun to show signs of warming up. The improvement of the peripheral economy has also improved China's exports, showing a stabilization and recovery trend. However, we cannot be blindly optimistic about the improvement of China's export situation.

On one hand, whether China's exports can continue to improve and become a driving force for economic recovery still depends on the fundamental improvement and complete recovery of demand in countries such as Europe, America, and Japan. Currently, the economies of these countries are improving, but the recovery process is slow, and whether it can continue is still unpredictable, especially regarding employment conditions which are still deteriorating. The slump in employment will inevitably affect the recovery of residents' income and consumption, thus hindering their economic recovery.

On the other hand, the international trade frictions faced by China are becoming increasingly prominent, and the trade environment is deteriorating. In the first three quarters of this year, 19 countries initiated 88 trade remedy investigations against Chinese products, involving a total amount of $10.2 billion, increasing by 29% and 125% respectively compared to the same period last year. Especially recently, the United States has frequently taken trade protection actions against Chinese products. From January to September, the United States initiated 14 trade remedy investigations against China, decreasing by 6% in quantity compared to the same period last year, but the total amount involved reached $5.84 billion, increasing by 639% year-on-year.

Secondly, the current stabilization and recovery of China's economy mainly relies on government policy-driven, and the endogenous power of domestic demand growth is not optimistic. In 2009, the tapping of domestic demand was quite effective. From January to August, the cumulative actual growth rate of the total retail sales of consumer goods was 17.3%, the highest since 1985. In the first three quarters, the actual year-on-year growth rate of total fixed asset investment in society was as high as 37.4%, the highest level in nearly ten years.

However, there are many uncertainties about whether domestic demand can continue to expand on the basis of the high growth in 2009. Especially considering that the current housing and automobile consumption hotspots are already quite strong, the expansion consumption policies introduced in 2009, such as rural electrification, tax preferential policies for car and housing purchases, etc., will see diminishing effects in 2010, and the government's strengthening of economic structure adjustment and overcapacity management may limit the upward space of investment growth. Therefore, it is challenging for future domestic demand growth to exceed the level of 2009.

Second, the pressure of price increase is growing, and mild inflation will reappear. Since the second half of 2009, the year-on-year decline in the CPI and PPI has continued to narrow. Among them, the CPI decline contracted from 1.7% in June to 0.5% in October, while the PPI decline narrowed from 8.2% to 5.8% in October. The changes in CPI and PPI indicate that the deflationary pressures in the circulation and production stages are continuously weakening. Judging from the factors affecting the trend of prices, the year-on-year changes in CPI and PPI in 2010 will turn from negative to positive, ending deflation.

Firstly, calculated according to the monthly change rate of the CPI in 2009, the carry-over factor of the CPI in 2010 is around 1%, and the carry-over factor of the PPI is around 3%.

Secondly, social inflation expectations have formed. Since the outbreak of the international financial crisis, governments of various countries have successively launched unconventional financial and fiscal stimulus packages. Currently, the global range of stimulus policies has sparked inflation expectations. The activity in China's stock market and real estate market, the steepening of the bond yield curve, all reflect the formation of social inflation expectations. According to the central bank's household survey, in the third quarter of 2009, 44.6% of residents believed that the current prices are "high and hard to accept," an increase of 18.5 percentage points compared to the first quarter.

Thirdly, since March 2009, global oil, non-ferrous metals, and other commodity prices have continued to rise. The CRB Index (US Commodity Research Bureau Futures Price Index), which comprehensively reflects the trend of international bulk basic commodity prices, has continued to rise, increasing by 18% at the end of September compared to the end of March. By October, the international crude oil futures price had returned to near $80. If the US dollar continues to weaken in 2010, the world economy continues to improve, and demand for commodities increases, it will further stimulate the rise in international commodity prices, thereby exerting obvious imported inflationary pressure on China.

Finally, the high growth in money supply increases future inflation risks. According to the monetarist view, "inflation is a monetary phenomenon." Experience shows that there indeed exists a leading relationship between China's M2 and CPI changes. Overall, the CPI in 2010 will turn from negative to positive. Although prices are recovering, due to the severe overcapacity in China, most industrial products are oversupplied, so the transmission of China's price mechanism remains unsmooth. Prices will still exhibit structural characteristics, and the price increase will remain relatively mild, estimated to be within 3%.

Third, the rapid increase in credit has brought certain financial risks. The rapid increase in credit in 2009 has brought certain financial risks, mainly concentrated in three aspects:

One, part of the credit flowed into the stock market and real estate market, bringing the risk of asset price bubble expansion. In the first three quarters of 2009, the ratio of newly added loans to GDP was as high as 0.4, while this ratio remained stable between 0.1 and 0.2 in previous years. This indicates that the current real economic growth did not absorb such a large amount of credit funds, and part of the credit funds flowed into the asset market. Since the beginning of 2009, the "volume-price increase" performance of the real estate and stock markets indirectly explained this point.

Two, excessive credit expansion brings the risk of non-performing assets. In the credit issuance of 2009, commercial banks competed fiercely for credit resources, showing a certain degree of irrationality and excessive competition. In the first quarter of 2009, the proportion of newly added loans of state-owned commercial banks accounted for 50.5% of the total newly added loans of all financial institutions, an increase of 12.7 percentage points compared to the fourth quarter of the previous year, becoming the "leader" in credit issuance. However, after the second quarter, the share of newly added loans of the four major state-owned commercial banks dropped to less than 40%, and the loan growth of medium and small-sized banks exceeded that of state-owned banks, becoming the main force in credit issuance. The behavior of each commercial bank chasing each other in the loan competition showed a certain degree of blind following and irrationality. Moreover, the herd effect of commercial banks in some fields has caused excessive competition. Whether commercial banks can conduct prudent risk assessments under intense competition is worrying.

Three, there is a tendency for credit issuance to be medium-to-long-term, centralized, and project-oriented, with excessively high credit concentration. The credit issuance behaviors of banks are highly similar, increasing credit risks. In the credit expansion of 2009, most of the bank loans were issued to infrastructure construction projects dominated by the government, namely the so-called "railway, highway, and base" projects. Many loans were issued through local government financing platforms. The financial status of local government financing platforms is not transparent, making it difficult for banks to assess their credit ratings. Many projects lack sufficient risk assessments, posing higher inherent risks. In addition, the tendency towards long-term loans will exacerbate the term mismatch risk.

Fourth, the international financial situation is uncertain and may affect China's financial operations. Entering the post-crisis era, there are three major uncertainties in the international financial situation in 2010: First, will the ultra-loose monetary policy led by the Federal Reserve exit? Will the world enter a cycle of interest rate hikes? Second, will the US dollar continue to depreciate or will it turn from weak to strong? Third, will there be changes in the flow of international capital? Among these three uncertainties, the monetary policy shift led by the Federal Reserve will be the most critical.

1. If the Federal Reserve chooses to raise interest rates, it may lead to a stronger dollar and the return of international capital to the United States. After the outbreak of the international financial crisis, the dollar once strengthened, with the dollar index, which comprehensively reflects the trend of the dollar, reaching close to 90. But since March 2009, the dollar has depreciated again, and by the end of October, the dollar index had returned to around 75.

Summarizing the reasons proposed by various parties for the depreciation of the dollar, they mainly include: First, the global range of stimulus policies promoted the recovery of the world economy, also causing inflation expectations to intensify, raising the risk preferences of international investors, and weakening the "safe haven function" of the dollar. Second, the Federal Reserve started a zero-interest-rate and quantitative easing monetary policy in March, releasing unprecedented amounts of cheap dollars, leading to the rise of dollar carry trade. Third, moderate depreciation of the dollar is beneficial to the United States, and the US government has the motive to allow the dollar to depreciate. Dollar depreciation helps stimulate US exports, reduce imports, and improve the imbalance in the US current account. The "valuation effect" caused by dollar depreciation can also lower the US's real foreign debt denominated in dollars. Fourth, countries accelerate the diversification of their foreign exchange reserves, weakening the international status of the dollar.

Among these reasons, in terms of timing and influence, the launch of the Federal Reserve's quantitative easing policy has had the most significant impact on the dollar exchange rate. Just as the Federal Reserve's quantitative easing policy pushed the dollar down, its exit from this unconventional policy would also cause the dollar exchange rate to strengthen temporarily. And the Federal Reserve's interest rate hike and dollar strengthening would attract international capital back to the United States.

2. The possibility of the Federal Reserve raising interest rates before the third quarter of 2010 is small. Given the inflation expectations and inflationary pressures brought by the global super-loose monetary policy, with the good news from the real economy coming one after another, the financial markets stabilize and become active, some countries' governments and central banks have started to study the exit strategies for crisis response measures including quantitative easing monetary policy.

Federal Reserve Chairman Ben Bernanke publicly stated on July 21 that the Federal Reserve has considered issues related to the exit strategy. On October 6, the Australian central bank unexpectedly announced that it would raise the benchmark interest rate from 3% to 3.25%, becoming the first country among G20 members to tighten monetary policy. Australia was less affected by the international financial crisis, and the central bank raised interest rates out of concern for potential inflation. Its behavior does not have universal significance and is unlikely to trigger a global wave of interest rate hikes.

The interest rate hike by the Australian central bank is just a case. Only the Federal Reserve can truly lead the global wave of interest rate hikes and guide the large-scale exit of ultra-loose monetary policies in various countries. Due to the unclear prospects of economic recovery, the global exit from ultra-loose monetary policies led by the Federal Reserve will be exploratory and gradual. Analysts generally believe that the possibility of the Federal Reserve raising interest rates before the third quarter of 2010 is not significant.

3. The adjustment of the Federal Reserve's monetary policy has certain spillover effects, posing challenges to China's exchange rate policy and monetary policy. The adjustment of the Federal Reserve's interest rate directly affects the trend of the dollar exchange rate and the direction of international capital flows. If the Federal Reserve persists with zero interest rates and quantitative easing policies, the dollar will continue to weaken, not only pushing up the prices of international bulk commodities, aggravating China's input-type inflationary pressure, but also increasing the pressure on RMB appreciation, prompting hot money to further flow into China. If the Federal Reserve chooses to raise interest rates, then the dollar may emerge from weakness, possibly triggering the return of international capital to the United States, and the withdrawal of "hot money" may pose a threat to China's financial stability. (Author's unit: National Information Center, Li Ruoyu)
