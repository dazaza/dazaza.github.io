In the rapidly developing, highly competitive era of high-tech information, the theft or potential theft (potential theft) of trade secrets has increasingly drawn attention. Employees are more likely than ever to possess professional secrets and proprietary information, and they change jobs more frequently. When employees who [jump ship] use the trade secrets they know to give their new employers a competitive edge, the former employer faces significant commercial threats. Therefore, the protection of trade secrets in labor (employment) relationships has become a focal point of legislative concern.

1. Introduction of the Issue

A trade secret is an intangible property and, as part of intellectual property, is primarily protected by anti-unfair competition laws and tort law, such as the "Uniform Trade Secrets Act" of the United States, the "Uniform Trade Secrets Act" of Canada, Japan's "Act Against Unjust Competition", China's "Anti-Unfair Competition Law", as well as parts of the Restatement of Unfair Competition Law and the Restatement of Torts Law in the United States that pertain to trade secrets. However, anti-unfair competition laws and tort law exhibit severe lagging effects and cannot fundamentally and effectively curb infringing acts or fully protect corporate trade secrets. First, from the perspective of litigation subjects, most countries’ competition laws define the relationship between companies and external competitors but lack provisions regarding the relationship between companies and employees. Employees who know trade secrets resigning and then using them to work for competitors becomes an important route for infringement. Second, both belong to post-facto remedies. Since trade secrets have a fleeting nature, once lost, they are difficult to recover, necessitating prior prevention. Regarding relief methods, generally, only damages can be claimed, and injunctions have little significance because once a trade secret is disclosed, it no longer retains its trade secret nature.

Non-compete agreements (Noncompete Agreement or Covenant not to compete) are preventive measures that emerged in response to the shortcomings of anti-unfair competition law and tort law protection methods. It refers to the agreement signed between the employer and the employee when establishing an employment relationship, stipulating that within a certain period after leaving the company, the employee shall not work for another enterprise producing similar products that are in competition. Non-compete agreements were initially criticized abroad because employers used their economic advantage to directly restrict workers' rights to equal employment opportunities, freedom to choose their careers, and even jeopardized their right to make a living, conflicting with public policies promoting trade freedom. However, they were recognized in judicial practice and became a popular method for protecting trade secrets abroad. The National Science and Technology Commission of China first regulated non-compete agreements in Article 7 of "Several Opinions on Strengthening the Management of Technical Secrets in the Flow of Scientific and Technological Personnel," authorizing companies to sign non-compete agreements with employees. Other local regulations have subsequently made similar provisions. At the same time, legislation and case law strictly limit the validity of non-compete agreements. Legislation requires that the parties to the agreement (usually limited to employees who actually come into contact with the company's trade secrets), the duration, geographical scope, types of professions or activities, and economic compensation must comply with regulations; otherwise, the agreement is invalid. Foreign case law mainly comprehensively considers the above factors to determine whether the agreement is reasonable, and defendants often defend themselves by arguing that the agreement is unreasonable.
