The Love of Stones

In the second year of college, "Corn" fell in love with "Soybean." He was Corn's teacher, an oil painting instructor who liked to paint fruits and women. He had a buzz cut, a small mustache, sometimes wore a fisherman's hat, rarely smiled, and even seemed a little cold. But all of this, to Corn, was a kind of charm. Corn, who considered herself aloof, finally knocked on his door one moonlit night: "Teacher, I'm so cold..."

Without saying a word, Soybean embraced her and took her inside, then gently closed the door. There was a ten-year age difference between them, but that wasn't the problem. The real issue was that in the late 1980s, "teacher-student romance" was not allowed. Therefore, their relationship could only be conducted secretly, late at night, reminiscent of the meetings between ghost maidens and scholars in "Strange Stories from a Chinese Studio." It was exciting; Corn enjoyed this clandestine romance. Choosing a boyfriend is actually choosing a lifestyle, which ultimately reflects your personal taste. Corn was always self-assured. In her view, although she wasn't the class beauty, she was undoubtedly the most unique flower. So, in class and in the art studio, she didn't miss any opportunity to have an "electric eye contact" with Soybean. Indeed, her figure and lively,热情 personality were unmatched. Soybean was an introverted man who liked fiery girls like gunpowder. He usually paid special attention to this female student. The eyes of classmates were sharp; everyone knew he liked Corn. What made Corn grit her teeth in hatred was that this cold-blooded man never directly confessed or gave her even a hint of an unusual look. At most, he would linger behind Corn for a few more minutes, intently watching her paint. Every time this happened, Corn would feel a faint fragrance emanating from her body, and the teacher's breathing behind her would become increasingly coarse. This was a tempting yet alarming signal. After receiving it multiple times, Corn finally made the extreme move of knocking on his door that moonlit night. Thinking back to that night, Corn still feels terrified now. She realized she could be as brave as Liu Hulan, the female hero, but instead of facing death, she faced the killer of love, a male teacher named "Soybean."

Their midnight romance was incredibly beautiful. Soybean gradually relaxed, and the wildness he had hidden within him finally erupted. Corn's boundless tenderness also emerged under his influence, drifting like clouds and mist.

Just then, a fierce knock on the door shattered their dream. Leaders from the Art Department and school security officers stood solemnly outside Soybean's door, each holding a flashlight like monuments. The next day, Corn was publicly criticized, and Teacher Soybean was asked to write a self-examination and reflect deeply, or he might be expelled.

Corn was extremely frightened because her father had been beaten to death during a previous political movement. At the same time, she felt wronged. She and Soybean had never done anything too out of line. They believed that the "final line" should be crossed on their wedding night. Their love was only in the "prelude" phase, where they were still innocently exploring the first page of love. They sketched each other's nudes and used each other as models, but they both firmly held onto their inner passion. However, the relevant leaders did not believe them. If a young man and woman were locked in the same room at midnight without something happening, even ghosts wouldn't believe it.

This was a "miscarriage of justice," concerning a pure midnight love. At this moment, Corn decided to drop out of school. Getting into university was very difficult at the time, but Corn wanted to protect this love and Soybean's career. By dropping out, she would no longer be a student, more precisely, she would no longer be Soybean's student. Thus, they would no longer be in a teacher-student relationship and could openly love each other. Changing roles, they could still happily share various breaths. Unexpectedly, the relevant authorities readily agreed to Corn's request. Soybean opposed Corn making a "meaningless sacrifice," but he couldn't stop her. Eventually, they were together again. Corn rented a bungalow outside and took on some tutoring jobs. Life had to go on. She wanted to continue pretending to be a student in that city because she couldn't tell her mother about being expelled.

This incident was seemingly resolved satisfactorily. However, their love soon faced a crisis. For Soybean, public love had lost its fighting spirit. He no longer held Corn and talked about his vast knowledge. Instead, every time Corn excitedly came to see him, he would light a cigarette and sit silently, almost speechless. Each time, Corn had to ask him questions first, and he would lazily respond. Initially, Corn thought he was disheartened by the "incident," but half a month later, Corn keenly sensed an ominous sign. He no longer loved her, and he no longer kissed her. Even when he did open his mouth, it was icy cold. Unable to bear it anymore, that night, Corn grabbed his shoulders and looked into his eyes, asking, "Tell me, don't lie. Have you lost feelings for me?" Soybean nodded. Corn slowly let go, tears streaming down her face. One year and six months of love sprinting ended just like that, as if it were a dream. The first half was a beautiful dream, the second half a nightmare. Upon waking, she realized she had nothing left.

Corn walked through the cold wind until dawn, heartbroken, then blaming herself. Why had she sacrificed so much? In the end, the person she loved had quickly become a thief, a thief of emotions who stole her heart and then carelessly discarded it.

The best medicine for healing this wound could only be time.

Corn understood that she couldn't fall apart; she had to stand up. So, she worked tirelessly, filling every day with activities—tutoring, advertising design, drawing portraits for passersby, designing T-shirts, even knitting sweaters... Once, while selling T-shirts, a female college student brought a stone and asked Corn to add a few strokes with a paintbrush to make it a small gift. Since her boyfriend was born under the zodiac sign of the Tiger, she hoped Corn could paint the stone to look "tigerish."

This event greatly moved Corn. Why not open a gift shop specializing in couples in love? Each gift would be unique, with raw materials mostly being small stones. Then, using her painting skills, she could add lines and colors to create fresh and distinctive love gifts. Placing them in gift boxes, each stone could truly sing!

Beautiful stones singing songs of love. Say do and act. After preparing for a month, Corn's "Love of Stones" boutique finally opened. Business was good, and so was her mood. Although the scale was small, it was heartfelt. Corn used this beautiful work to heal her old wounds and earn money, beautify her life, and utilize her artistic talents. This was excellent.

Corn is now 35 years old and still single. She has created tens of thousands of love gifts, given temperature and spirit to tens of thousands of cold stones, and sent blessings to tens of thousands of people. Each stone she personally picked from the riverbed in the wild was natural, unpolluted, and unique. If she painted a heart on these silent stones, they became precious, like looking at countless love stories, making her smile unconsciously.

Frequent smiles are Corn's current life. In her store, there is a signature shelf composed of "female" and "son" forming the character "good." She said, if men and women are always "good" together, how wonderful that would be!

If fate allows, she does not exclude love returning, including from the painter named Soybean. Even at 100 years old, Corn will not reject love, provided her teeth haven't fallen out because she likes to bite to test whether the ring her boyfriend gave her is the purest gold.
