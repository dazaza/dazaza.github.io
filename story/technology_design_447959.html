In the PC industry, Acer has always been a star that catches people's attention. It abandoned the lower end of the smile curve - production, and instead focused on R&D and brand operations, becoming a lightweight company with only 6,000 employees globally. Its extremely fast product innovation speed, powerful global management capabilities, proficient multi-brand operation methods, and unique marketing model that combines international brand image with local marketing resources are all focal points of concern and discussion in the industry.

Through the global promotion of its successful European experience, Fortune Global 500 company Acer is now the world's second-largest personal computer and notebook brand. However, inconsistent with its global market position, Acer ranks only fifth in China's PC market share.

Compared to last year, Acer’s PC sales in China have doubled in 2010, and its transformation from domestic to international operations is beginning to bear fruit. Acer, which started with a European marketing model, is now undergoing a Chinese-style reform campaign, quickly adjusting and strengthening its products, channels, and brands according to local conditions.

"Good enough" versus "only the best"

In Acer's revenue, 50% comes from Europe, the Middle East, and Africa, 30% from Asia, while the huge PC user base in China contributes only 5% of the market share. This significant difference in market share has led Acer to overlook the enormous differences in product needs between the Chinese and foreign markets in the past.

Take laptop graphics card configurations as an example: In the mature markets of Europe and America, 80% of laptops use integrated graphics cards, whereas in China, independent graphics cards are almost considered standard by ordinary consumers. In fact, this reflects a less rational consumer psychology: For most users, integrated graphics cards are already sufficient to meet their daily needs. Blindly pursuing equipment with independent graphics cards not only increases costs but also leads to increased thickness, higher operating temperatures, and greater power consumption, actually damaging the basic portability of laptops.

Overall, European users prefer 15-inch and 16-inch laptops with integrated graphics cards. They may own multiple computers and require more specific configurations for each one. In contrast, Chinese consumers often expect one computer to handle more functions, requiring not only portability for work and travel but also the ability to play large games and watch high-definition movies.

There is a significant difference in the maturity of consumer behavior between China and overseas markets. "Overseas consumers tend towards rational consumption," said Lin Rongfeng, Director of Marketing at Acer, to *Successful Marketing*. "When making a purchase decision, most overseas consumers believe that 'good enough' is fine, while Chinese consumers who spend money always hope to buy the best thing. But we respect this difference."

In the past, Acer did not do enough to improve its products specifically for the mainland market. In fact, before 2009, Acer was still selling large-screen laptops of 14 inches or more that suited the tastes of European and American consumers in China. Many consumers viewed Acer as reliable in quality but lacking in sleek design.

Price first to brand first

What kind of channel model should be chosen? This is another hurdle Acer must overcome in the mainland market, a problem it has yet to solve.

The reason Acer could quickly grow into the top brand in the European PC market is mainly due to its strong R&D and supply chain management capabilities working together with local core channels to achieve local dominance and profit sharing. However, in China, Acer faces a much larger user base, a more scattered market, and a more complex channel system. Factors influencing Chinese user purchases go beyond just channels.

Abroad, there are only a few core channel partners. If you enter Best Buy, you will find products from various brands arranged by price range, and the salespeople are not exclusive to any particular brand. You can easily compare the appearance, configuration, and performance of different brands objectively within your desired price range and make a purchasing decision. Lin Rongfeng introduced to the reporter that the procurement and management systems of large channel partners also raise market entry barriers and reduce the existence of counterfeit brands. Such a market environment greatly benefits Acer, Lin Rongfeng told the reporter.

China's model is from general distributors to regional distributors to retail stores, resulting in complex channels. After entering the mall, consumers face countless brand stores, where prices for the same brand's products vary across different stores, and salespeople rarely collaborate, greatly increasing the difficulty of making a purchasing decision on-site. Lin Rongfeng said.

This means that the PC purchasing process for Chinese consumers is entirely different from that of European and American consumers. Domestic consumers often need to conduct extensive information searches beforehand, select three to five brands, look at rough models, and then compare and filter in the store to determine final pricing. In Europe, after considering general performance requirements and price ranges, you can easily make a decision on-site in a big store. They don't have to worry about haggling, price traps, or product tampering issues.

Different channels, different purchasing mechanisms, which determined that to break through in China, Acer had to change its primarily distributor-focused European model and shift its market promotion focus directly to actions affecting individual consumers.

Influencing distributors to influencing consumers

Lin Rongfeng introduced that since mid-2009, Acer conducted a large-sample brand survey targeting potential consumer groups in cities from tier one to tier five nationwide. The survey thoroughly examined several aspects including consumer PC brand awareness, Acer brand awareness, and purchasing behavior habits. The results were not optimistic: On average, out of every four respondents, one did not know what Acer was, and among those who had heard of Acer, very few truly understood it as the world's second-largest PC brand, a fully internationalized brand, and a successful multi-brand operator.

Besides the issue of brand recognition, there was one important finding: Even among different segmented populations, the common factor influencing purchasing decisions was trust in the brand, rather than certain features of the product.

Thus, unlike the usual appeals of youth, fashion, vitality, and color used by competitors, Acer re-launched its brand positioning as a trustworthy computer product, proposing the brand strategy of "I Trust, I Choose, Acer Computers."

Acer's brand reshaping is based on a fundamental principle: In China, the core of a brand lies in its ability to directly influence consumers, not just channels—consumers do not decide when they reach the store; they already have a preferred brand or product in mind before shopping.

Additionally, the psychological characteristic of Chinese consumers being easily influenced by advertisements cannot be overlooked.

In the European and American markets, due to differences in integrity factors, consumers generally believe that products available in large stores have already been screened by sellers, and all listed items are reliable goods. In China, however, no matter how good the product is, without advertising and promotion, many people worry that it is a small brand that cannot even afford advertising. Lin Rongfeng said.

Acer completely changed its former low-profile style, formulating a massive three-year market promotion plan to provide effective sales support for dealers and promote consumer brand recognition of Acer.

The 2012 Olympics will be held in London, and at that time, Acer will carry out large-scale global brand promotions as a TOP sponsor of the IOC. To maximize sponsorship effects, Olympic-themed promotional activities have already begun over the next two years. For example, in February 2010, Acer sponsored the Vancouver Winter Olympics and provided necessary equipment to build computer infrastructure for the first Singapore Youth Olympic Games held on August 14, 2010.

Starting in February this year, Acer purchased CCTV Spring Festival packages and increased its advertising efforts on CCTV. Meanwhile, Acer's brand appeared on outdoor billboards, subways, taxis, buildings, websites, and print media in many cities across the country.

This year marks the pioneering year of the promotion plan, achieving strategic transformation with the goal of establishing a trustworthy Acer brand image, which serves as the foundational platform for Acer's success. Lin Rongfeng said.
