Did Jobs resign due to health issues, as he has been plagued by illness up until now? - Love Life - Love Grapefruit

by luweimy on 2011-08-26 16:24:42

On August 25, 2011 Beijing time, Steve Jobs resigned from his position as CEO of Apple and proposed to the board that he hoped to serve as chairman of the board. In a letter to the directors of Apple's board and the Apple group, Jobs wrote: "I have always said that if there ever came a day when I could no longer fulfill my duties and meet expectations as Apple's CEO, I would be the first to let you know. Unfortunately, that day has come." (Quoted from NetEase News), this poignant declaration has made many worry about Jobs' current health condition. So, what illness has plagued Jobs all along? An old article by Xiao Ji will analyze it in detail.

Recently, Steve Jobs' pancreas has been a concern for many people in the IT industry. In October 2003, Fortune magazine reported that Jobs had been diagnosed with a "rare form of pancreatic cancer." He then underwent "pancreatic surgery" in July 2004. After the surgery, Jobs claimed that the disease had been "cured," and quickly returned to work. However, by April 2009, Jobs underwent another liver transplant surgery, reportedly related to his "pancreatic cancer." By January of this year, Jobs took leave again due to illness...

This has sparked much discussion among the public regarding Jobs' health status. It is well known that pancreatic cancer has the title of "king of cancers." For patients who have lost the opportunity for surgery, the median survival period from diagnosis to death is only 7-9 months; even for those who have the chance to undergo surgery, the median survival period is only 15-19 months, with a 5-year survival rate of 20%. If Jobs really had pancreatic cancer, it would be highly unlikely that he could have survived from 2003 until now. So, was Jobs fortunate enough to survive a major calamity, or is there another story behind this?

In an email sent to Apple employees, Jobs revealed the truth (3): he did not suffer from the "king of cancers," but rather another rare disease called an insulinoma. Among all pancreatic tumors, insulinomas account for only 1%, with over 40,000 new cases of pancreatic cancer diagnosed annually in the U.S., and approximately 3,000 new cases of insulinomas. Therefore, it is understandable why reports referred to Jobs' condition as a "rare form of pancreatic cancer." Strictly speaking, insulinomas are not considered pancreatic cancer. The pancreas has exocrine functions responsible for secreting digestive enzymes, as well as endocrine functions regulating human metabolism (especially sugar metabolism). The so-called "king of cancers" refers to malignant transformations of cells responsible for the pancreas' exocrine function, while insulinomas originate from cells responsible for the pancreas' endocrine function, specifically the islet cells, and belong to a type of pancreatic neuroendocrine tumor (Neuroendocrine Tumor).

Even within insulinomas, there are many subtypes. Based on whether they secrete endocrine hormones, they can be divided into functional insulinomas that secrete hormones and non-functional ones that do not. Depending on the hormone secreted, they can further be categorized into insulinomas, gastrinomas (Zollinger-Ellison Syndrome), glucagonomas, somatostatinomas, vasoactive intestinal peptide tumors (VIPomas), and pancreatic polypeptide tumors. Each different type can secrete different endocrine hormones, causing various symptoms. For instance, gastrinomas secrete gastrin, leading to Zollinger-Ellison syndrome, which primarily manifests as recurrent, difficult-to-cure peptic ulcers, diarrhea, abdominal pain, and gastroesophageal reflux; VIPomas, which secrete vasoactive intestinal peptides, mainly present with large amounts of watery diarrhea, with daily diarrhea volume reaching up to 6-8 liters, severe diarrhea leading to water-electrolyte imbalance, weight loss, etc.; insulinomas secrete insulin, causing recurrent, difficult-to-correct hypoglycemia; glucagonomas, which secrete glucagon, conversely cause refractory hyperglycemia, etc.

Regardless of the type, Jobs has suffered greatly. Unless the tumors with secretory functions are surgically removed, these symptoms cannot be effectively cured through medication or other conservative treatments. However, perhaps Jobs should be grateful that his tumors bring him various endocrine disorders, as having secretory functions usually indicates higher differentiation and lower malignancy, thus resulting in higher survival rates (4).

Besides whether they cause endocrine disorders, factors such as age, stage of the lesion, and whether metastasis has occurred also affect the patient's chances of survival. Jobs is only 56 years old, which is a favorable factor for him, but if the reason for his liver transplant surgery was liver metastasis of the tumor, the situation would not be optimistic. However, for patients with insulinomas, even if liver metastasis occurs, the chances of survival remain significant. The 5-year survival rate for patients with metastatic VIPomas can reach 60% (5), and for patients with metastatic gastrinomas, the 10-year survival rate is 30% (6), both far better than even pancreatic cancer patients without distant metastasis. If Jobs were a gastrinoma patient without liver metastasis, his 20-year survival rate could potentially reach as high as 100%.

Even if Jobs survives smoothly, whether he can return to work remains questionable. Different types of secretory insulinomas can cause different symptoms, but each type often severely affects the patient's quality of life and working ability. Even with Jobs' strong willpower, it is hard to imagine how he could complete his work under conditions of tens, or even dozens, of episodes of diarrhea per day. Typically, medications can partially alleviate symptoms caused by the tumor, but their efficacy is often unsatisfactory, and surgical removal of the tumor is the only effective way to completely relieve the symptoms.

However, not every patient has the opportunity for surgery. One of the biggest difficulties is locating the lesion before surgery. Insulinomas most commonly occur in the pancreas, but some patients may develop tumors outside the pancreas, such as in the gastrointestinal tract, liver, mediastinum, etc. Sometimes the tumor is very small, making it difficult to locate using many diagnostic methods, yet such a small lesion can still secrete large amounts of hormones, causing severe symptoms. In such cases, doctors have no choice but to allow the patient to endure the pain for some time, waiting for the tumor to grow larger, clarifying its location, before proceeding with surgery. On this point, Jobs was lucky. Before the surgery, he underwent a test called "endoscopic ultrasound-guided fine needle aspiration (EUS-FNA)."

EUS (endoscopic ultrasound) is an extension of ordinary gastrointestinal endoscopy technology, where an endoscope with an ultrasound probe is inserted into the patient's stomach, and the ultrasound probe examines the pancreas through the stomach wall. Since the pancreas is tightly adjacent to the posterior wall of the stomach, EUS allows the ultrasound probe to check the pancreas at the closest possible distance. This examination technique offers great advantages in diagnosing pancreatic tumors, not only because close-range ultrasound can easily detect small pancreatic tumors, but also because under ultrasound guidance, the doctor can insert a fine needle (FNA) through the endoscope, piercing through the stomach wall and the pancreas, directly reaching the lesion to extract cells, and examining the cell morphology under a microscope to confirm the nature of the lesion. Thanks to EUS-FNA, Jobs was able to clearly understand the nature of the tumor he suffered from, as well as the exact location of the mass, ultimately obtaining the opportunity for surgical removal of the lesion.

Regarding the type of surgery, speculation suggests that the most likely option is the Whipple procedure, which involves removing part of the pancreas, part of the stomach, part of the jejunum, duodenum, gallbladder, and common bile duct. This operation covers a large area, is highly complex, and carries significant risks, making it one of the most challenging surgeries in abdominal surgery (7). Besides the Whipple procedure, Jobs might have undergone other types of surgeries. Roughly speaking, the pancreas can be divided into three parts: head, body, and tail. The anatomical position of the pancreatic head is complex, intertwined with structures like the duodenum, gallbladder, and common bile duct, making them inseparable. Therefore, if Jobs' tumor was located in the pancreatic head, he would have had to undergo the extensive Whipple procedure, or the modified Child procedure with a slightly reduced resection range. However, if the tumor was located in the pancreatic body or tail, since this part of the pancreas is only entangled with blood vessels supplying the spleen, Jobs would only need to undergo a distal pancreatectomy with splenectomy, which removes the pancreatic body and tail along with the spleen. This surgery causes less damage, has fewer complications, and has a lesser impact on digestive and endocrine functions compared to the Whipple procedure. Regardless of the type of surgery, Jobs had the potential to achieve complete cure.

Unfortunately, it seems that Jobs might not have been completely cured after all. The reason for his liver transplant surgery was likely due to liver metastasis of the tumor. Even if the Whipple procedure or distal pancreatectomy went smoothly, and all visible lesions were removed during the operation, there might still be residual tumor cells in Jobs' body. These cells could enter the liver through various means, colonize, proliferate, and form metastases. Regrettably, we cannot determine the benign or malignant nature of insulinomas based solely on whether they metastasize—both benign and malignant insulinomas can metastasize. This means that even if liver metastasis occurs, Jobs still has a very good chance of long-term survival. Patients who undergo liver transplants due to unresectable pancreatic neuroendocrine tumor liver metastases have a median survival period of 55 months (8).

Although overall, if all the above speculations hold true, Jobs still has a good chance of survival. However, simply surviving is not enough to shoulder the responsibility of leading Apple forward. If residual tumor cells remain in Jobs' body after surgery, these "escaped" cells can continue to secrete hormones and produce symptoms; furthermore, after liver transplantation, Jobs needs to take immunosuppressants long-term, which weakens his resistance to diseases, making him more susceptible to infections or tumors. These risks may force Jobs to temporarily or permanently abandon his work. From another perspective, if the liver transplant surgery goes smoothly, no rejection occurs, the tumor cells are completely eliminated, and no serious infections occur post-surgery, Jobs still has the opportunity to engage in light to moderate physical labor.

No matter what, the public's discussions about Jobs' condition are rampant, and this article by the author merely adds fuel to the fire regarding various rumors. Whether Jobs' fans or Apple investors, all that can be done now is to pray for this IT superstar and optimistically hope for his rapid recovery.
