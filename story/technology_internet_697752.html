Chinese Concept Stocks Collectively Go to US for IPO: 5 Companies Raise Over $8 Billion

by bingo123 on 2012-03-08 17:26:01

By Luo Xiaowei, Beijing Report

The long-quiet Chinese Concept IPO (Initial Public Offering) is coming again!

In recent days, news of companies such as Shanda Literature, E-Media, Vipshop, CAR Inc., and Xinshengyuan going public in the U.S. has been circulating. “The ‘window’ has temporarily opened to allow some breathing room, but it won’t last too long, and the ‘wind’ outside won’t be too strong,” said Wang Ran, CEO of China Everbright Capital.

IPO Wave Resurges

“Some investment banks have already invested a large amount of capital in China’s internet industry, and very few of these enterprises meet the conditions for domestic listing, so the American capital market cannot indefinitely close the listing channel for Chinese internet companies,” Qin Hefang, a researcher at Tsinghua University’s School of Economics and Management Leadership Research Center, analyzed the recent situation of Chinese companies going public in the U.S. on February 29.

On February 25, Shanda Literature submitted an F-1 amendment document to the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC), restarting its IPO application with the stock code "READ," planning to list on the New York Stock Exchange and raise no more than $200 million. Last July, Shanda Literature had announced the suspension of its U.S. IPO due to poor conditions in the American capital market.

A week before Shanda Literature submitted its IPO application, on February 18, E-Media and Vipshop respectively submitted their IPO application documents to the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission. The former plans to raise up to $100 million, with the stock code ADCN, intending to list on the Nasdaq Stock Exchange. The latter plans to raise up to $125 million, with the stock code "VIPS," intending to list on the New York Stock Exchange.

E-Media positions itself as a bottom-to-top, end-to-end digital advertising platform, including technology platforms and media platforms. According to company data, net revenue was $10.79 million in 2009, $27.27 million in 2010, and $50.67 million in 2011, with a three-year compound annual growth rate of 117%. Its online advertising network attracted 486 million unique visitors by December 2011.

Vipshop positions itself as a B2C brand discount website in China, offering goods such as fashion, skincare products, luggage, leather goods, accessories, and perfumes at significant discounts compared to retail prices.

Ahead of Shanda Literature, E-Media, and Vipshop, Lenovo Holdings' subsidiary CAR Inc. took the lead on January 19 by submitting its prospectus to the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission, initiating the IPO process, planning to raise $300 million in the U.S. Meanwhile, recently, Shanghai pharmaceutical enterprise Xinshengyuan Group (shortened as Xinshengyuan) also applied for an IPO with the SEC, planning to raise $80 million.

Accordingly, there are now five companies—Shanda Literature, E-Media, Vipshop, CAR Inc., and Xinshengyuan—that have applied for IPOs with the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission, collectively raising approximately $805 million. Among them, Shanda Literature, E-Media, and Vipshop are IT and Internet companies, collectively raising $425 million, accounting for 52.8% of the total funds raised, and representing 60% of the number of companies, clearly showing that network technology remains the main force.

"The Window Won't Stay Open Longer Than Three Months"

“The ‘window’ has temporarily opened to allow some breathing room, but it won’t last too long, and the ‘wind’ outside won’t be too strong.” Regarding the recent start of Chinese companies' IPO actions in the U.S., Wang Ran told the Huaxia Times on February 29, “The window will not stay open longer than three months. For most of the year, it might remain slightly ajar, where one could force through, but it certainly wouldn't be the golden opportunity; generally speaking, most companies would not soar high.”

Since last year, especially in the second half of last year, Chinese concept stocks have been neglected or even shorted in the American capital market, resulting in many websites encountering setbacks in their U.S. IPOs, leading to cautious investments in Chinese internet companies. According to data from Qingke, which studies domestic venture capital situations, venture capital investment in China's e-commerce sector significantly decreased in the second half of 2011 compared to the first half.

Some e-commerce companies, particularly luxury e-commerce firms, experienced business contraction or even bankruptcy. Previously, former employees of Shangpin.com revealed that the company underwent massive layoffs around the Spring Festival due to its inability to secure venture capital funding. At the beginning of 2012, Zouxiu.com transferred its entire market and data management department in Beijing to its Shenzhen headquarters, allowing existing employees to either relocate to Shenzhen or choose voluntary resignation. In early December 2011, Huhaha.com was exposed for "owing salaries" and having 90% of its employees resign, with the site's founder Lian Tingkai being expelled by the board.

Meanwhile, group-buying websites, considered to have excessive bubbles, struggled due to tight funds.

“Compared to other industries, China’s internet industry has always had a relatively strong dependence on risk capital, forming a fixed model since the first wave of internet companies. Going public and cashing out is a necessary stage in this development model,” Qin Hefang told the Huaxia Times. “If the U.S. capital market closes the IPO path for Chinese companies, it may completely disrupt the development model of China's internet industry.”

Regarding the recent IPO applications of Shanda Literature, E-Media, Vipshop, CAR Inc., and other Chinese companies in the U.S., Qi Jianzhe, an analyst at Analysys International, expressed caution in an interview with the Huaxia Times on February 29. He believed that “this does not mean that the U.S. capital market has reopened its window for Chinese internet companies; we still need to see the results during the roadshows of these companies.”

He believed there were two main reasons why Chinese companies continue to submit IPO applications: First, the U.S. stock market has performed strongly recently, with both the NYSE and NASDAQ setting new highs. Second, recently listed Chinese concept stocks have performed well, indicating from a certain angle that investors have regained some confidence in Chinese concept stocks.

“In general, the U.S. market will not be particularly friendly to Chinese companies in 2012,” Wang Ran believes. “For companies solely focused on rushing abroad, instead of constantly assuming a starting position and possibly mistaking the starting gun, it would be better to use this year to focus on transformation when needed, layout when required, and profitability where possible.”
