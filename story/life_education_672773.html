Over-education may not be worth the cost.

by hunanshida on 2012-02-23 10:33:30

Teachers from Hunan Normal University's self-study examination program have discovered that every parent hopes their child will excel in studies, work, and life in the future. Driven by this wish, parents are becoming increasingly eager to have their children learn various cultural knowledge in advance so that they can perform better and more easily when they enter primary school, paving a smoother path for their future. Educators refer to this phenomenon as "advanced education."

Specifically, teachers from Hunan Normal University's self-study examination program point out the "harms" of advanced education for young children.

What adverse effects does advanced education in early childhood have on the physical and mental development of children?

Adverse Effect One: Imposing knowledge and skills that exceed a child's developmental stage onto a child who has not fully prepared in terms of ability will not only make parents feel frustrated but more seriously, it can affect the child's future development.

If Little Swallow's mother forces her to fly out of the nest before her feathers have grown, the result can only be watching helplessly as the young bird falls to the ground, and possibly losing her courage to fly forever. Similarly, scientists have found that many abilities in children appear during a certain period of growth. Parents' eagerness to instill traditional knowledge into their children's minds may instead create obstacles for them to absorb knowledge in the future. Imposing knowledge that children do not yet possess the ability to understand will only cause more confusion and fear. Especially when they face adults who go to great lengths to demand they complete tasks, they may feel afraid of failing to complete these tasks, doubt their own abilities, and the negative consequences of such lack of confidence will affect the child for life.

Adverse Effect Two: Many young parents, in their haste to see results earlier, neglect the accumulation of their child's abilities and only seek to quickly impart knowledge. However, they don't realize that doing so could likely limit the development of the child's abilities.

The energy of adults is limited, and so is that of children. When parents focus solely on how many nursery rhymes their child learns each day, how many words they can write, or how many math problems they solve, they overlook whether the child plays harmoniously with peers, or if they fall and get back up during play. Gradually, the child also only cares about the tasks arranged by adults, losing interest in exploration, and losing the drive to seek questions and answers. These things that both parents and children gradually distance themselves from and ignore hold greater significance for growth than knowledge itself. It's like building a house; in order to quickly construct skyscrapers, without laying solid foundations underground, serious adverse consequences are inevitable.

Adverse Effect Three: Some advanced education seems to have some effect on the child at present, but due to receiving irregular teaching methods in early childhood, bad habits are formed, making it difficult for them to accept correct teaching in the future, affecting their future development.

Teachers from Hunan Normal University's self-study examination website believe that nature wants children to remain childlike until they become adults. If we disrupt this order, it will cause some fruits to ripen prematurely; they grow neither full nor sweet, and rot quickly. In other words, we will cultivate young doctors and "old-before-their-time" children.
