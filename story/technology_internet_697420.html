What are the harms of mycoplasma infection in males?

by ukjl on 2012-03-08 15:23:09

What are the hazards of male mycoplasma infection? Mycoplasma infection is one of the common sexually transmitted diseases in men. There are sixteen kinds of mycoplasma in the human body, and three of them are pathogenic to humans. Among them, Ureaplasma urealyticum is a common pathogen of the human urinary and genital tracts, and is associated with many urinary and genital infections, perinatal infections, and infertility. It is also one of the pathogens of sexually transmitted diseases. So, what are the hazards of male mycoplasma infection? Here is a detailed introduction by experts: After mycoplasma infects through the urethra, patients may develop symptoms of urethritis, which can lead to chronic prostatitis. During prostate fluid examination, active and motile microbial colonies can be observed. Mycoplasma can further infect the seminal ducts, seminal vesicles, and testes, affecting the quality of sperm and semen, leading to infertility. Observations have found that mycoplasma can cause infertility through the following stages.

One, Ureaplasma urealyticum increases the rate of sperm deformities: Mycoplasma infection leads to an increased rate of sperm deformities, which is another characteristic causing infertility. According to clinical observations, the rate of sperm deformities in these infertile patients can sometimes reach as high as 80%.

Two, Ureaplasma urealyticum destroys spermatogenic cells: There are large numbers of spermatogenic cells in the seminiferous tubules of the testes, which develop and multiply to form sperm. When mycoplasma enters the seminiferous tubules from the urethra, prostate, etc., it destroys the spermatogenic cells, reducing the quality and quantity of sperm, thereby leading to infertility.

Three, Ureaplasma urealyticum interferes with sperm motility: Sperm motility is an important function of healthy sperm and is a key indicator of whether sperm can fertilize. Moreover, sperm movement must have a certain speed and frequency. After sperm is infected by mycoplasma, mycoplasma often attaches to the head and tail of the sperm, covering the entire sperm with varying sizes of attachments, making the sperm unable to swim effectively, entangling with each other, and leading to infertility.
