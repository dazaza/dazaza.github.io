Classification of crushing stations and specific analysis of each

by hx515317 on 2012-02-13 15:52:03

At present, domestic and foreign open-pit mines are continuously progressing in process optimization and reducing mining costs, especially the application of various types of crushing stations, each with its own characteristics. However, many documents or materials have inconsistent statements when discussing crushing stations. For example, there is no unified definition for semi-mobile and semi-fixed crushing stations. For a specific device, some materials refer to it as semi-fixed while others call it semi-mobile. Many mining workers are unclear about this in their articles. The purpose of this article is to define the concept of the form of crushing stations and introduce typical applications in domestic and foreign mines to facilitate effective communication in the mining community in the future.

1 Classification of Crushing Stations

Open-pit mine surface crushing stations are classified according to the degree of fixation of the crushing equipment and foundation: mobile, semi-mobile, semi-fixed, and fixed.

1.1 Mobile Crushing Station

Generally integrating feeding (which may include breaking large pieces at the feed opening), crushing, and conveying, it uses crawler or tire-type (or stepping type) movement and can adjust its position as the working face moves in the quarry. For example, Metso's Nordberg LT series mobile crushing station. Its advantages are: replacing dump trucks, reducing procurement costs; reducing transportation costs by 50% to 70%; reducing spare parts costs; reducing labor costs; reducing quarry road maintenance; and having safety and environmental protection features. Since it is a three-in-one device, and the continuous transport equipment connected to it is a belt conveyor, crawler-type mobile crushing stations are more suitable for use in large open-pit mines and are applicable to large-scale, continuous mining processes. Domestic mobile crushing stations are generally tire-type, with short wheelbase and towed travel to reduce turning radius, featuring mobility and flexibility. The maximum hourly capacity can reach 600 tons, with feed size up to 900mm, generally suitable for small mines or stone processing plants. Recently, Hongxing Machinery has developed a more flexible PF300 fully mobile crushing station for working face mining operations. This equipment can be equipped with a double-toothed roller screening crusher, double-toothed roller crusher, or hybrid type crusher to meet different user needs.

1.2 Semi-Mobile Crushing Station

The semi-mobile crushing station places the body on an appropriate working level within the open-pit quarry. As the working bench advances and deepens, track-mounted transport vehicles or other towing (pulling) equipment moves the entire (or partial) crushing unit. Such as those used in Qidashan Iron Mine and Shougang Water Plant Iron Mine. A semi-mobile crushing station generally consists of three parts (also known as three modules): feeding equipment, crushing equipment, and unloading equipment. Feeding equipment is further divided into hoppers (or bins) and feeding belts (or vibrating ore discharge machines or vibrating screening feeding machines). If a belt feeder is used, it can be either climbing or parallel. The type of crushing equipment varies widely, including gyratory, cone, jaw, hammer, roll, and impact crushers. Unloading equipment is mostly a belt, and usually climbing. Semi-mobile crushing stations are generally movable, which depending on the weight and height of the entire machine, can be either whole-unit movable or component movable. The most typical feature of a semi-mobile crushing station is that it has no concrete foundation connection to the ground. Its service life ranges from several months to several years, and the time required for one relocation typically does not exceed 48 hours. The distinction between semi-mobile and mobile crushing stations, apart from relocation time, is also important in terms of equipment layout. Semi-mobile crushing stations are usually located on the working side of the quarry, with rock haul distances less than 3 km. When the quarry steps extend beyond the reasonable haul distance and climb height for trucks, the next relocation occurs. It should be particularly noted that because of the heavy and high equipment in semi-mobile crushing stations, to achieve uniform feeding, the feeding part is usually located above the crushing equipment. For instance, the feeding platform at Shougang Water Plant Iron Mine is set above the crusher, with the feeding equipment connected to the rock mass via a trestle bridge to meet the load-bearing requirements. The feeding equipment should not be considered as part of a fixed crushing station just because it is fixed to the rock mass, since the core component is the crusher, which is relocatable.

1.3 Semi-Fixed Crushing Station

A semi-fixed crushing station is a transitional method from a semi-mobile to a fixed crushing station. In terms of connection to the ground, the crusher frame has a strong connection to the ground (anchor piles can be used at bedrock locations, and reinforced concrete foundations at loose areas). In terms of placement, it is generally fixed on the fixed side of the quarry, minimizing relocation frequency and increasing usage cycles, especially important for rock crushing and transportation. When relocating a semi-fixed crushing station, only the equipment body that is bolted to the foundation can be disassembled, moved, and reused, while the foundation buried below the surface is abandoned. There are examples in China's coal system where steel structures are pre-buried in the soil and excavated again for use during relocation, but due to the difficulty of strength verification for steel supports and the large workload of anti-corrosion treatment, this method is rarely used. Because a semi-fixed crushing station has a solid foundation, it can be larger in scale during selection, suitable for larger handling capacity mines. Also, because of the high foundation investment, it should be placed in a relatively fixed position as much as possible. For open-pit mines, this means the fixed side or non-working side, which is an important difference in application technology compared to semi-mobile crushing stations.

1.4 Fixed Crushing Station

Fixed crushing stations are usually arranged outside the quarry boundary, unaffected by the advancement and deepening of the quarry steps, with a strong connection to the ground. The feeding method is diverse, allowing direct unloading by truck or through a buffer hopper fed by a discharge device. Fixed crushing stations are generally found in the crushing workshop of a mineral processing plant, with the same lifespan as the mine. The biggest disadvantage is the long haul distance for ore, and it cannot be reasonably adjusted. Due to the large handling capacity and large feed size, fixed crushing stations often use gyratory or jaw crushers.
