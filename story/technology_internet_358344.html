Save Qinghai Lake, Save Our Home

by ditan360 on 2010-06-18 13:08:35

Low Carbon Network News: Qinghai Lake, known as the most beautiful lake in China, holds an important ecological position. This means that Qinghai Lake not only belongs to Qinghai and China, but also to the world. As a key water body ensuring the ecological security of the Tibetan Plateau and a treasure house of biodiversity, Qinghai Lake plays an indispensable role in the national ecological network system. Moreover, its unique geographical location and environmental characteristics make it a significant "model" for climate change in other regions around the world.

Qinghai Lake's highland lake ecological tourism and bird-watching ecological tourism have been listed as important destinations for eco-tourism in China. Qinghai Lake has become the top choice for many domestic and international tourists visiting Qinghai. Tourist routes centered around Qinghai Lake have become premium routes heavily promoted by many travel agencies both inside and outside the province.

Qinghai Lake is also in danger

The formation process of Qinghai Lake is quite interesting. About 200,000-2,000,000 years ago, it was a vast freshwater lake connected to the Yellow River system, with water flowing into the Yellow River through the inverted river in the southeast. Therefore, it was once an "outflow lake." Around 130,000 years ago, due to new tectonic movements, the surrounding mountains rose. At the end of the Pliocene, the Riyue Mountain and Wild Buffalo Mountain to the east of the lake rapidly ascended, blocking the original flow of the inverted river into the Yellow River and forcing it to flow westward into Qinghai Lake. Due to the blockage of the external drainage channel, Qinghai Lake evolved into an inland lake. Subsequently, due to evaporation, it gradually changed from a freshwater lake to a saltwater lake.

In 2000, through remote sensing satellite data analysis, the area of Qinghai Lake was over 4,256 square kilometers, with an estimated water volume of 7.39 billion cubic meters, the longest distance about 104 kilometers, the widest part about 62 kilometers, and the maximum depth 38 meters.

According to historical records, in the 95 years since 1908, the water level of Qinghai Lake dropped by about 13 meters, and the surface area shrank by more than 700 square kilometers. According to measurement data from Qinghai’s water conservancy department, since 1959, the average water level of Qinghai Lake has dropped by 12 centimeters annually, and the lake surface has lowered by 3.5 meters. The fastest drop in water level occurred in 2000, when it dropped by 21 centimeters in one year. At this rate, Qinghai Lake loses an average of 436 million cubic meters of water per year, splitting from a single plateau large lake into a cluster of "one large and several small" lakes. The main factors causing this situation include global warming, intensified human activities, and reduced rainfall, especially blind reclamation around Qinghai Lake, which has destroyed the water sources of inflow rivers. Statistics show that due to the significant reduction in water-supplying rivers, compared to the 1980s, the inflow water volume has decreased by 60%.

Lakes will eventually "die"

Researcher Yu Ge from the Nanjing Institute of Geography and Limnology at the Chinese Academy of Sciences stated that lakes, like humans, go through a "birth, aging, illness, and death" process. The shrinkage of lakes is a normal phenomenon, and all lakes are destined to face the fate of "death."

There are mainly three reasons for the "death" of lakes. First is the biological cause: Lakes will grow many plants and animals. If the water volume remains constant, the increase in plants and animals that require water will definitely reduce the water volume. Meanwhile, the dead bodies of plants and animals will cause sedimentation at the bottom of the lake. Plants like mangroves can also adsorb silt from rivers, leading to the transformation of lakes into swamps and eventual disappearance; Second is the mechanical cause: Silt carried by upstream rivers will gradually deposit at the bottom of the lake, filling it up; Third is the chemical cause: Water flows dissolve salts from rocks it passes through. If the inflow water volume (discharge) is less than the evaporation water volume, the concentration of salt in the lake water will continue to increase, ultimately drying up the lake water and turning the lake area into a large salt crust.

The birth and death of lakes often happen very quickly. For example, the melting of glaciers can rapidly form glacial lakes; the surge of seawater can form lagoons on land; what we remember deeply is the "dam lake" formed by landslides blocking rivers, which appeared multiple times after the Wenchuan earthquake. These lakes often evaporate quickly, or the blockages disappear and the lake water drains away, or they are washed away by floods without a trace.
