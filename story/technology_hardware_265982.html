This time, a survey conducted by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention found that of the 552 adults who participated, those who played videogames reported higher body-mass indexes and "a greater number of poor mental health days". One of the study’s co-authors, Dr. James B. Weaver III of the CDC’s National Center for Health Marketing, said that "Video-game players also reported lower extraversion, consistent with research on adolescents that linked video-game playing to a sedentary lifestyle and overweight status, and to mental-health concerns." The study goes on to say that videogames may be a form of "digital self-medication," allowing players to take their minds off of their problems. It’s a pretty safe bet that if you spend most of your time indoors playing videogames, you’re not going to be spending as much time being physically active, or at social events, which will naturally lead to a higher body-mass and possible psychological effects. But these results aren’t unique to videogames in any way. You could become just as depressed, and just as overweight watching TV, sewing, drawing, or playing guitar. It’s worth noting that this study was published in the American Journal of Preventive Medicine and written by doctors, for doctors. In terms of American health-concerns like heart-disease and psychological impairment, videogames surely contribute their fair share.
