"Taobao Time Machine" Evokes Consumer Memories: A Decade of Online Shopping in China

by geekzhang on 2013-05-06 22:17:46

In 1999, an internet survival experiment was staged in Beijing. The participants were locked in a transparent house, attempting to rely on online shopping to prove they could survive.Nowadays, what was once considered performance art has become a part of everyday life. For your health, you should go out, mothers say while complaining about how immersed you are in the internet, and then accepting packages delivered by couriers.The Taobao Decade Time Machine leads each user through a review of the changes that have occurred. This interactive online product is officially released today. When you open it, you can see your consumption status from 2003, when Taobao was founded, until 2013. During this period, societal changes have influenced everyone's consumer perspective.This small time machine allows us to carefully appreciate the details of China's macroeconomy. We see that over the past decade, Chinese people have started paying for their sense of security. Parents spend an average of 8,343 yuan per parent on imported milk powder; we see that post-80s and post-90s generations have become the main force in toy purchases, perhaps because they don't want to grow up; we also see that more than 100 million Chinese have purchased Korean wave products, exploring their own sense of fashion.Product managers say: Every purchase is your expression, and Taobao quietly records it for you.Dear, our shared decade.The beginning of the homebody era: Shanghai, Beijing, Hangzhou are capitals of online shopping: Online gaming is the national first sport.We are gradually moving all aspects of our lives online, thus marking the start of the homebody era.We get food and water, books and spiritual pleasure, and even exercise from the internet. Do you know what the favorite sport of Chinese internet users is? It's not table tennis or basketball; according to Taobao data, it's online gaming, with over 38 million gamers purchasing related items. In traditional sports, swimming ranks first, with the most people purchasing related items.And what is the favorite board game of Chinese people? Young people might say "Sanguosha," but data shows that the most purchased board game over the past decade is Mahjong, followed by cards and Sanguosha, indicating that new trends still need time to catch up with traditions.In the past decade, developed eastern cities have led the trend of online shopping and have been pioneers in fully integrating life with the internet. Ranked by the proportion of online shopping expenditure to total spending, Shanghai is undoubtedly China's capital of online shopping, closely followed by Beijing, Hangzhou, and Shenzhen, with Wuhan and Chengdu also making it into the top ten.And if ranked by province, Guangdong and Zhejiang rank first and second respectively.With the popularity of online shopping, perhaps a more virtual urban landscape will gradually emerge. Science fiction writer Liu Cixin commented that one day, people's lives in such cyberspace will be more real, more free, and more colorful than in the real city, and virtual space will replace the real city to become the main living place for future humans.After the tainted milk powder incident: 1.96 million anxious mothers only buy foreign milk powder: Shanghai has the most anxious mothers.Milk powder, complementary foods, baby bottles, tableware are all American, diapers are Japanese, toys are American, and skincare is also American. New mother Panpan counts the items she just bought for her baby while lamenting the money she spent.According to the time machine, this era of safety-conscious consumption began in the autumn of 2008. That autumn, the shocking nationwide Sanlu tainted milk powder incident occurred. Before this, most mothers bought domestic milk powder. After the tainted milk powder incident erupted, domestic milk powder sales were severely impacted, with a 41% drop in volume. Although nearly six years have passed, the unease of the Chinese public regarding the quality of domestic milk powder continues to ferment.To date, 1.96 million mothers like Panpan only buy foreign milk powder, and this number continues to grow. If you want to know where these 1.96 million anxious mothers are mainly located, the two high-pressure cities of Shanghai and Beijing are the most concentrated. Among them, the well-developed online shopping city of Shanghai accounts for as high as 9.5%, making it the region that pays the most for safety-conscious consumption.People's consumption for safety has reached unprecedented heights. Taking milk powder as an example, according to Taobao data statistics, from September 2008 to now, parents have spent a total of 16.3 billion yuan on foreign milk powder, averaging 8,343 yuan per parent.And purchasing for safety does not only stay on milk powder and other child-related necessities. The tainted milk powder incident merely opened the curtain on the unease of the Chinese public. Subsequent various disasters have made many people prepare for crises: after the May 12 earthquake, 245,000 people purchased emergency kits; the heavy rain in Beijing that claimed 37 lives made 2.15 million safety hammers subsequently fly continuously to all over the country; the heated PM2.5 at the end of 2012 made masks in Beijing run out of stock, and so far, 46 million masks have been consumed; the outbreak of avian influenza made 450,000 boxes of Isatis Root enter everyone's medicine cabinet. This indicates that we have already entered the era of safety-conscious consumption.Post-80s and Post-90s refuse to grow up: 16.88 million overgrown children pay for toys; one-third of plush toys are bought by post-90s.Drawers stuffed with hundreds of old game cartridges, televisions surrounded by vintage game consoles from different eras, a dedicated room for models during home renovations, friends gathering to play board games—this image seems far removed from the traditional impression of adults, yet it has clearly become a characteristic of the post-80s and post-90s generation.Kidult originates from the English word "kidult," referring to adults who, despite being grown-ups, still possess the mindset, personality, and interests of children. As the post-90s generation enters the late marriage cohort, the ranks of kidults continue to expand. Data from Taobao shows that over the past decade, a total of 8.09 million consumers aged 30-39 have purchased toys online, while even more toys have been bought by 16.88 million consumers aged 18-29, surpassing those over 30 who mostly have children, becoming the main consumer force for board games, video games, and model toys. Those over 30 have become the primary consumers of educational toys.Relevant personnel believe that this may be related to the sudden increase in life pressure after young people enter society, leading to a strong nostalgia for childhood.Adults' love for toys exceeds that of children, even in terms of infantile plush toys. More than one-third of plush toys are bought by people aged 18-24, surprisingly, male buyers of plush toys exceed females. Is it that men can only think of giving plush toys to their girlfriends, or are adult men increasingly fond of plush toys?A New Generation's Fashion Perspective: Coexistence of Korean Wave, Chinese Style, and Star Chasing; At Least 100 Million People Have Followed the Korean WaveIf we talk about the fashion hits of the 1980s in China, it was carrying a dual cassette recorder on your shoulder, wearing bell-bottom pants, and sporting sunglasses. But by 2013, when most people had become more fashion-conscious, it was hard to define what fashion was anymore because the uniform standards of fashion no longer existed.In 2005, Super Girl became an overnight sensation, and since then, there have been more pure men who don't wear skirts and effeminate men who favor androgynous styles. According to Taobao data, from 2005 to present, Taobao has sold 1.6 billion pieces of androgynous-style clothing, including 6.27 million pure men and 3.29 million effeminate men. Later boyfriend style inherited the androgynous style, characterized by loose casual tops and simple skirts or denim outfits for these girls.During the same period, sweet and elegant Korean-style women's clothing became popular along with hit Korean dramas like "Romantic Full House," "My Name is Kim Sam Soon," and "Dae Jang Geum." According to Taobao data, from 2005 to present, 118 million consumers have purchased Korean-style goods, meaning that one out of every five Chinese internet users has followed the Korean Wave.Today, Korean-style remains an important style in Taobao women's clothing. On April 24th, searching for Korean-style items on Taobao yielded 42.14 million results.While Korean drama fans wipe tears and chase the styles in the shows, Chinese style and domestic goods fever have quietly gained momentum. This trend can be traced back to between 2006-2007 when classic domestic goods, Flying Pigeon sneakers, were re-packaged by a foreign designer and worn by Orlando Bloom, the elf prince. This quickly prompted a reevaluation of domestic goods by the Chinese public and ignited enthusiasm among the youth. Meanwhile, this retro trend of domestic goods coincided with the international retro trend, becoming a new retro fashion for the Chinese. Statistics show that from 2011 to present, 2.3 billion pieces of cheongsam, Tang suit, Hanfu, and embroidery have been purchased.Besides, there is another group of star chasers who pursue what stars wear. Searching for "same outfit" on Taobao yields 2.45 million results. Can eating make you a foodie? No! A true foodie dares to face an enlarged stomach and a depleted wallet but cannot resist a picture of delicious food. A documentary in 2012 touched the most sensitive nerves of these foodies, pushing their pursuit of food to a climax.This also led to the emergence of another branch of foodies from that time - nighttime grocery hunters. Watching food introductions from various places at night while drooling and shopping on Taobao has become a habitual behavior for them. How powerful is this behavior?
