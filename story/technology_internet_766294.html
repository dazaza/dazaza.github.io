Apple said to be ready to hand over a cheaper iPhone to China Mobile

by anonymous on 2013-09-11 13:27:02

According to informed sources, Apple is preparing for iPhone shipments targeted at China Mobile, indicating that a long-anticipated distribution agreement between the two parties has likely been reached.

If this information from The Wall Street Journal proves accurate, it would mark the end of years-long negotiations between the two companies. China Mobile boasts 700 million users, making it the largest wireless carrier in China with a user base equivalent to seven times that of Verizon Wireless, the largest operator in the U.S. For Apple, Greater China represents its third-largest market by sales after the U.S. and Europe. It remains unclear whether Apple and China Mobile have signed an official sales agreement, but given Apple's plans to ship units, the deal is likely finalized.

According to insiders familiar with Apple's plans, the company will unveil two new iPhone models next Tuesday, set for release starting in September, including a high-end model and a cheaper version. One source stated that Apple has instructed its long-time manufacturing partner Foxconn to add China Mobile to the list of carriers to receive the budget iPhone, though the exact delivery schedule remains unknown.

A Pivotal Moment for Apple

Globally, only a few major wireless carriers, including China Mobile, have yet to become iPhone distributors. Apple’s decision to sign a distribution agreement with China Mobile comes at a critical time. While the iPhone remains Apple's best-selling product, it faces increasing competition for market share from rivals like Samsung Electronics. Simultaneously, Apple confronts growing doubts about its ability to innovate.

In the third fiscal quarter ended June 29, Apple reported $4.6 billion in revenue from Greater China, down 14% year-over-year. According to research firm Canalys, Apple's market share in China has dropped to 5%, placing it seventh in the market—behind leaders such as Samsung with 18%, as well as local manufacturers Lenovo Group and Huawei.

Apple CEO Tim Cook emphasized in April that China presents a significant growth opportunity for Apple, highlighting the large number of potential first-time smartphone buyers in the country. Apple also committed to doubling its retail chain in China from the current 11 stores within less than two years.

China Leading the Way

Canalys forecasts that total smartphone shipments in China will reach 352 million units this year, representing an 84% increase over last year—more than double the expected volume in the U.S. By 2015, sales are projected to further rise to 421 million units. Last year, China surpassed the U.S. to become the world's largest smartphone market, making it increasingly important for Apple to expand its share in the region.

One insider noted that Apple was initially reluctant to bring the iPhone to China Mobile's relatively unstable third-generation network due to concerns about slower download speeds and higher production costs. Meanwhile, China Mobile was frustrated by Apple's insistence on guaranteed sales volumes.

Another factor hindering Apple's success in China has been pricing. In China, the cost of iPhones is prohibitively high for many consumers; especially when compared to locally produced devices offering similar technical specifications at much lower prices. Additionally, while U.S. carriers subsidize upfront iPhone costs, Chinese carriers offer refunds that reduce monthly bills but do little to lower initial purchase costs for new phones.

Mutual Necessity

Now, both companies find themselves in a position where they need each other. Apple's sales in China have declined, while China Mobile continues to lose customers to competitors China Telecom and China Unicom, both of which distribute the iPhone.

The current version of the iPhone 5 sells for approximately RMB 5000 (around $815) without a contract, whereas most smartphones sold in China during Q2 were priced under $200. Although Apple has not disclosed any details about the budget iPhone, analysts predict its price could be around $400, placing it among mid-priced smartphones in China.

"A budget iPhone will definitely change the game," said an analyst specializing in Chinese smartphones at IDC. "For Apple, this will be a turning point."

Morgan Stanley analyst Katy Huberty forecasted in an August report that a $400 budget iPhone could increase Apple's market share in China by 13 percentage points, potentially reaching sales of 20 million units. She pointed out that the budget iPhone might erode Samsung's, HTC's, and Lenovo's market shares in China. If Apple reaches a distribution agreement with China Mobile, sales could reach 32 million units, accounting for roughly one-quarter of last year's global sales of 125 million units.

Growth Opportunities

Currently, Apple's users in China are mainly urbanites from major cities like Beijing and Shanghai, but the real growth opportunities lie in smaller towns. China Mobile dominates these areas, where users are beginning to replace feature phones with smartphones. Furthermore, the sales networks in these regions are less developed compared to major cities, making them more reliant on carrier influence.

"If Apple wants to succeed with a budget iPhone, partnering with China Mobile is crucial," said Nicole Peng, Research Director at Canalys China.

In Shijiazhuang, Guo Jie, 22, exemplifies the type of customer Apple is targeting. This young man, browsing in an electronics mall, expressed his desire to upgrade his Samsung feature phone to a simple and affordable smartphone. However, if spending a bit more could get him an iPhone, he'd consider it. "If it's RMB 3000 (approximately $390), I would buy it," he said.

Ma Tao, a vendor in the same electronics mall selling various products including smartphones, laptops, and electric shavers, noted that many customers considering the older iPhone 4 opt against it because they perceive it as outdated despite its lower price compared to the iPhone 5. If Apple releases a budget iPhone, "it will definitely sell well," he commented.

Pricing Pressure

Pricing pressure on Apple is mounting as leading Chinese manufacturers—besides those already mentioned, including ZTE, YuLong Computer Telecommunication Technology (Shenzhen) Co., Ltd., and Xiaomi—are willing to sacrifice short-term profits for market share. These companies believe today's entry-level consumers will form the foundation of tomorrow's market.

The popularity of low-cost phones indicates consumer demand for better value at lower prices. Huawei unveiled its flagship smartphone, Ascend P6, in June, which is thinner than the iPhone and features a 4.7-inch high-resolution display. Despite being Huawei's most expensive model, it is priced at only RMB 2688 (about $440), roughly half the price of the iPhone 5.

Shao Yang, Chief Marketing Officer of Huawei's device business unit, remarked that consumers will now have to choose between Huawei's premium smartphone and a budget iPhone. "People who buy iPhones often like to take them out of their bags and place them on the table so others can see," he said. "But with a budget iPhone, putting it on the table may not feel as easy."
