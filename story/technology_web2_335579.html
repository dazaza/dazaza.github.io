20 Big Losers of the Year: Google China and NetEase Make the List

by ezqirg8jg on 2010-03-29 12:52:20

Cover of the magazine "The First Financial Weekly"

"The essence of competition is not about who is stronger, faster or smarter, but rather who makes fewer mistakes." The First Financial Weekly has evaluated and listed large companies that performed poorly in the Chinese mainland market in 2009. What difficulties did they encounter?

Picking Faults with Large Companies

How did Adidas waste RMB 1.3 billion in Olympic sponsorship fees? Toyota replaced General Motors as the world's largest car company, but why was it slow to act in the Chinese market? What caused Coca-Cola to face a bribery scandal in China? Why did Lenovo incur losses after becoming a global company? In 2009, these companies faced tough times.

On May 24, 1999, John Richardson, CEO of Motorola's Iridium company, confidently told The Washington Post: "We provided an MBA classic case. First, we created a technical miracle, then we can think about how to make money with it."

Three months later on a Friday, two days after defaulting on a $1.5 billion bank loan, Iridium filed for bankruptcy protection.

Iridium indeed provided an MBA classic case, but ultimately became a classic failure case.

Why didn't this revolutionary technology bring Motorola the anticipated profits and market share, instead leaving it with billions of dollars in debt? Why didn't technological innovation and critical strategic decisions by management lead the company out of trouble, but instead into the abyss?

Tragedies similar to Iridium's have not ended. For ten years, such mistakes have continued to happen around us. There are no invincible generals in competition, nor are there any foolproof strategies. As American management consultant Merrill R. Chapman wrote in "IT Big Failures," the essence of competition is not about who is stronger, faster, or smarter, but rather who makes fewer mistakes.

What we focus on are large companies that performed poorly in China over the past year. This is the first time The First Financial Weekly has released a list of companies that had poor performances in the Chinese mainland market in the previous year.

You might be surprised to find that this list includes many superstar companies - multinational corporations like Google, Coca-Cola, Adidas, Toyota, and legendary domestic companies like Lenovo Group, Gome Electrical Appliances, and NetEase.

The reason we create such a list is to "pick faults" with these large companies.

After the crisis, superstar companies and traditional business rules were challenged and questioned. Giants fell one after another, leaders watched their power gradually disappear. Large companies were still shaken, many not yet recovered from the impact of the financial crisis. Bloomberg's statistics show that Microsoft, Cisco, and some computer companies saw order reductions, resulting in a $4.3 billion loss for global tech companies in 2009; the loss for the top 100 large financial companies worldwide reached as high as $660 billion due to the financial crisis.

For managers of these superstar companies included, they all hope their companies can maintain good operational status and create excellent market performance in the long term. To achieve this, they spare no expense, sometimes even blindly.

What we want to express is simple: just as there are no perfect people, superstar companies also have moments of confusion in the market and can make mistakes.

We hope to uncover some problems and errors at the moment when the tide recedes. We also hope to see these great companies reflect and improve, further move towards excellence, and ultimately present readers with a true business world.

In the list of underperforming companies in 2009, some made mistakes in strategy and market tactics (Adidas, Toyota, Lenovo Group); some former leaders lost market share (Sony Ericsson, Kodak, Hitachi); other companies missed optimal market opportunities (Coca-Cola, Chinalco).

It is worth mentioning that in the unique market environment of China, three companies on the list suffered operational impacts due to their founders' involvement in gray areas between politics and business (Gome Electrical Appliances, Hopsun Holdings, Maoye International). Interestingly, all three companies are involved in real estate as their main business. It should also be noted that the immature market economy environment in China led some companies to suffer severe blows due to asymmetric regulation and other irresistible forces (Danone, Google China, NetEase), making them the most tragic entries on the list.

Nothing remains unchanged forever. Today's leaders may be forgotten by consumers and the market tomorrow, while the underperformers may strive upward and create miracles in the future. We wish the underperformers of 2009 good luck next year.

20 Most Disappointing Companies of the Year

-Google China: The Pain of Being Unable to Perform

The departure of the Greater China President, government content rectification, tarnished brand image—Google China suffered heavy blows in 2009.

-Toyota Lost the Best Market

The world's largest automobile company lost its direction in the Chinese market, with its sales growth rate less than half of the overall increase rate of the Chinese automobile market.

-Adidas Inventory Crisis

Misjudgment of the Olympic effect and post-Olympic market resulted in the substantial investment of RMB 1.3 billion in Olympic sponsorship being somewhat wasted.

-Danone: Loss of Cash Cow

Lost the Wahaha brand, acquisition-based market share strategy declared a failure.

-Unlucky Morgan Stanley

Insider trading, suspicious real estate cases, hedge contract lawsuits—Morgan Stanley encountered multiple misfortunes in 2009.

-Coca-Cola: Missed Huayuan, Tangled in Scandals

Missed the non-carbonated beverage market opportunity with Huayuan, embroiled in bribery scandals, damaged the century-old store's image.

-Sony Ericsson: Lost Confidence

Strategic errors in both low-end and high-end smartphone markets caused this once market-leading technical pioneer to gradually lose its market share.

-Home Depot: Paying for Expansion

Closing loss-making stores and reforming remaining stores announced the failure of Home Depot's original rapid expansion strategy.

-Hitachi: Plasma Dead End

The financial crisis and plasma dead end caused Hitachi to report the second-largest corporate loss in human history.

-Kodak: Digital Aftermath

The gradually shrinking traditional business over the past decade has always been a significant source of profit for Kodak. Kodak's plan to reform 7,000 stores in China was considered merely changing the soup without altering the medicine.

-Mengniu: Control Ownership Drifted Away

Suffering double blows from the melamine incident and OMP incident, Mengniu helplessly returned to state ownership.

-Lenovo Group: Expensive Global Company

Full-year loss of $226 million in the 2008/09 fiscal year, the first full-year loss since 1997, and the biggest loss in its 25-year history.

-China Southern Airlines: Debt Snowballing

Too much reliance on domestically operated routes with slim profits, and also impacted by high-speed rail, causing Southern Airlines' debt to snowball.

-Hopsun Holdings: Back to Entrepreneurial Years

Financing difficulties, Hopsun Holdings failed to regain vitality in a booming real estate market.

-NetEase Trapped in Warcraft Shadow

The agency rights for the most popular online game in China, "World of Warcraft," did not bring NetEase good fortune, turning into a thorny big problem due to uncontrollable regulatory factors.

-Gome Electrical Appliances: Huang Guangyu Shock

Tight capital chain, crude expansion model blocked, Gome's position as industry leader is about to be lost.

-China Unicom: iPhone Cold Reception

Staggering prices scared away consumers, initial marketing efforts bore little fruit, indicating insufficient preparation in market strategy for this star product.

-Citic Pacific: Fate Seems to Be Mocking

Chairman Rong Zhijian resigned in disgrace, lost HKD 15.5 billion in Australian dollar financial derivatives investments, signaling the end of the Rong era at Citic Pacific.

-Chinalco: Lost Pricing Power

Acquisition of Rio Tinto obstructed, international pricing power dream shattered, domestic pricing power threatened by private aluminum industries.

-Maoye International: Huang Maoru Involved

Rumors of Chairman Huang Maoru being investigated sent Maoye-related listed companies on a roller coaster ride in the capital market.

Google China

The Pain of Being Unable to Perform

Google China had a rough year in 2009.

In January and April, Google China was publicly exposed twice by the Internet Illegal Information Reporting Center for containing pornographic obscene content and vulgar links on its website. In June, relevant national authorities suspended the website's overseas search services and related word search services for the same reasons, ordering rectification. In September, Li Kaifu, who brought Google into the Chinese market, announced his resignation to start his own venture. Subsequently, several localized services of Google stopped, and some employees left the company.

Previously, Google China's development was consistently burdened by conflicts between Google headquarters and China's industry regulatory policies. Google headquarters maintained a firm principle—no manual modification of search results—but Google China had to comply with relevant departmental policies and regulations.

Moreover, Baidu seemed like an insurmountable competitor. Despite continuous improvements in localized search quality and innovative localized R&D by Google China, the increase in market share remained limited.

For Google headquarters, compromising on principled issues for such a small increase in market share did not meet expectations, leading to more stringent criticism of Google China.

Now, Google China plans to erase the traces of localization during the Li Kaifu era and re-examine existing products and structures. This may be a "safe" survival strategy for Google China, but not necessarily the best one.

Facing the vast Chinese market and special regulatory policies, "abandonment" or "perseverance" poses a question for Google headquarters. They need to consider strategic issues in China, how to set and adapt to the game rules. Of course, for core regulatory departments, we hope to choose openness between "policy" and law. Only in such an environment can we possibly create great entrepreneurs and great companies.

Toyota

Lost Direction in the Best Market

Toyota Motor Corporation has occupied the number one spot globally for some time now. In October and November of the past 2009, this Japanese automaker achieved consecutive monthly increases in the U.S. market, which it values greatly and was severely hit by the financial crisis. However, in the world's best automobile market—the Chinese market—it seems a bit lost.

In the first eleven months of 2009, Toyota sold a total of 622,000 new cars in China. Although this figure increased by 20.7% compared to the same period in 2008, this growth rate is less than half of the overall 42% growth rate of the Chinese automobile market.

Toyota's disorientation began at the end of 2008. Affected by the financial crisis, Toyota suffered heavy blows globally and incurred huge losses. When assessing the Chinese market, despite cautiously setting a growth target exceeding 16%, it clearly underestimated the power of China's automobile revitalization plan.

Under the strong stimulus of the policy reducing purchase tax for models with engine displacements of 1.6L and below, China's automobile market surged. Although Toyota noticed this trend early in the year, due to the lack of models with engine displacements of 1.6L and below in its product lineup, this Japanese automaker, which had been smooth sailing in China for the past five years and excelled in marketing, experienced for the first time the frustration of "watching opportunities slip away."

Toyota China, which was depressed for half a year, began hurriedly launching the 1.6L Corolla in June 2009, increasing the promotion intensity of entry-level compact cars Yaris with engine displacements below 1.6L, and introducing the luxury car Lexus ES240 with a 2.4L displacement.

These passive changes allowed Toyota to return to the growth track in China. However, five large-scale recalls due to quality issues within a year also damaged Toyota's brand image in China.

Before the outbreak of the financial crisis, Toyota expanded globally and successfully replaced General Motors as the world's largest automobile company. However, as the financial crisis deepened, the automotive industry suffered severe blows, and Toyota, the global model of the automobile industry, was no exception, experiencing a sharp drop in sales and huge losses.

This blow made Toyota cautious when formulating its China strategy, seriously underestimating the benefits brought to the car market by China's economic stimulus policies. The step-by-step product launch and marketing strategies constrained Toyota's progress speed. Moreover, Toyota invested too much energy into rescuing the lifeless U.S. market, showing insufficient preparation when facing the vibrant largest automobile market in the world—China.

Now, it is time for Toyota to abandon arrogance and take the Chinese market seriously.
