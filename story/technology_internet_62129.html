Telecommunications - I don't have a monopoly

by 127702 on 2008-04-11 18:02:28

Not long ago, Wang Ruixiang, member of the National Committee of the Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference and vice chairman of the State-owned Assets Supervision and Administration Commission, said that there was no monopoly in the telecommunications industry. Wang Ruixiang said: "We should scientifically define so-called monopolies. For example, in the telecommunications industry, fixed-line business and mobile business are competitive. How can they be monopolistic enterprises? Of course not." This statement is really unexpected! For a long time, telecommunications monopoly has been an industry chronic problem that consumers have unanimously condemned and deeply abhorred, and the academic community has also been exploring ways to break the telecommunications monopoly. Is there a monopoly in China's telecommunications industry? It is necessary to further discuss this issue.

What is a monopoly? A monopoly means restricting competition, which generally refers to the behavior of excluding or limiting market competition by the actor. Generally speaking, there are three types of monopolies: economic monopoly, administrative monopoly, and industry monopoly. Economic monopoly refers to monopolistic behaviors spontaneously implemented by operators in market competition. Administrative monopoly refers to the act of government and its departments abusing administrative power to restrict competition. Industry monopoly refers to public enterprises (including transportation, water supply, power supply, telecommunications, postal services, etc.) and other operators with legally monopolistic positions (electricity, insurance, railways, postal services, etc.) abusing their advantages and implementing restrictive competitive behaviors.

It can be seen from the classification of monopolies that both administrative monopoly and industry monopoly exist in China's telecommunications market. The rigidity of telecommunications authorities in adjusting communication prices and the dominant position of telecommunications operators in their respective fields illustrate this point.

Indeed, in recent years, the telecommunications market has introduced a competitive mechanism, but it is mainly reflected in several major splits in the telecommunications market, forming the current major telecommunications groups. However, due to the failure to break through institutional restrictions, the competition promoted by this split remains incomplete. Just look at the financial reports of the current operators, and you will find that it is not that there is no monopoly in China's telecommunications market, but that the monopoly is too severe, and China Mobile is the most obvious example. Currently, China Mobile's profit margin exceeds 20%, controlling nearly 70% of the domestic mobile users. Did China Mobile implement the notice issued by the telecommunications regulatory authorities for the adjustment of roaming fees? Especially in the Beijing market, it is like an unbreakable piece of ice.

As for fixed-line operators without mobile services, their charges are also quite "arbitrary"! They have always claimed to align with international standards, yet the broadband rates they charge are quite high. According to statistics on internet costs in various countries in 2007 (based on the monthly average per 100Kb/s bandwidth standard), China's cost is $10.8 (approximately 80 RMB), while the UK’s is $0.63, the US’s is $0.49, and Japan and South Korea’s are $0.07 and $0.08 respectively.

Why don't the notices from the competent authorities get implemented? Why don't the service charges come down? It's because there is no competition, and all the telecommunications resources are in their hands! Isn't this a monopoly?

Is there a monopoly in the telecommunications industry? Another official's words may be more convincing. Wang Xueqing, director of the Institute of Economic Development and Reform of the National Development and Reform Commission, said: "The competition in the current telecommunications sector is still insufficient. The excessive profits shown in the financial statements of telecommunications operators indicate that their mutual competition is not excessive but far from enough." Such a judgment is closer to the truth.

"There is no monopoly in the telecommunications industry," senior officials who control the lifeline of the national economy made such remarks, and this joke that is not a joke deserves deep reflection!
