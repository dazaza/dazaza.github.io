Synopsis:

A man surnamed Kim (played by Jeong Jae-young) who originally intended to end his life in the Han River was fatefully drifted to an unknown deserted island. Since even dying isn't easy, he decides to try surviving on this peaceful little island. With that thought, Kim settles down on the beach of the uninhabited island, subsisting on wild food. One day, while sick, his distress signal is received by a woman (played by Jung Ryeo-won), who rescues him when he's at death's door. The woman has created her own secluded world within her room, managing her personal web page. Her unique lifestyle strongly arouses Kim's curiosity. Over time, love blossoms between them. However, the man realizes he cannot be truly happy living on this isolated Han River island and decides to return to his long-lost hometown. The woman, who hasn't stepped out of her house for three years, resolves to leave with him.

Their drifts have just begun...

Behind the Scenes:

Produced by Kang Te-gyu, directed by young director Lee Hae-joon, known for directing "Mighty Baby," this film tells the romantic story of a man who jumps into the Han River intending to commit suicide but ends up drifting to an unnamed small island where he encounters a reclusive woman. This peculiar plot earned it a spot as the eighth most anticipated movie of 2009. Lee Hae-joon is known as a "mischievous child" in the Korean film industry. His debut work "Mighty Baby" explores the coexistence of different kinds of people through the story of a little chubby boy striving to achieve his dreams despite numerous setbacks. In this new work, the lens shifts to adults, featuring a Korean version of Robinson Crusoe and a super homebody who lives entirely according to her own rhythm and desires. How fascinating and magical will their love be?

From August to December 2008, the director revised the script while shooting. CJ Entertainment, the producer, despite the controversy surrounding its 2008 release "The Divine Bow," achieved undeniable box office success. "Kim's Drift" utilized multiple locations previously used for "The Host." Unlike "The Host," this film aims to highlight the characters' unique personalities and stories using makeup and lighting technology. The scenes involving the two characters drifting together downstream of the Han River are enhanced with 3G computer graphics. According to the production team, the film will present a completely new visual feast, offering audiences a Korean adaptation of "Robinson Crusoe" that is both strange and fun yet filled with innovative creativity. When asked about the theme expressed through borrowing from a classic masterpiece, Lee Hae-joon frankly stated: "The busyness of modern society makes many people appear blindly indifferent on the surface, but deep down, they all desperately desire communication. The desire for interaction is a fundamental condition for long-term human happiness."

Fun Facts:

Chung Ri-won’s Messy Look:

Chung Ri-won's performance in her first film “Double Agent” was well-received. Even though her next project with Song Seung-heon, “Melrose,” was delayed, she still made waves in the Jongno area in 2009. In her new historical drama “Princess Deokhye, the Last Princess,” the production team aimed to transform her into a shining star akin to “Yu Ga-liong” of Korea. However, upon the release of the film stills, there was quite a stir. Her character suffers from autism, never leaving her home, with dark panda-like circles under her eyes, a pale complexion lacking vitality, a vacant gaze, and a thin frame—truly an ugly duckling. Originally, one might imagine her living on a paradise-like island with a fairy-like appearance. Unexpectedly, Lee Hae-joon didn’t spare her any “pity” and transformed her into a primitive Zhong Wuyan, not only unrefined but also slovenly. It’s no wonder that Chung Ri-won herself was startled when she first looked in the mirror. In the film, she plays a young woman who hasn’t left her house for three years, maintaining her spiritual life by managing her personal webpage: breakfast is corn, lunch is instant noodles, and dinner is skipped for dieting purposes. She replaces meal times with exercise and occasionally takes beautiful moonlit photos. Chung Ri-won personally contributed her thoughts to details such as the hairstyle design for her character.

Handmade Princess:

In the final stages of filming, Chung Ri-won, who usually has a knack for fashion design, handcrafted over 80 T-shirts for the crew members, putting considerable effort into both the colors and designs. It was natural for her colleagues to be deeply moved by her dedication. It is understood that the front of the shirts featured the movie title, while the back displayed the English initials of each person’s name. To emphasize a fashionable touch, Chung Ri-won specially used liquid oxygen pens for the lettering. Such a massive project required her to secretly investigate everyone's clothing sizes and purchase small items needed for the crafting process, which took nearly two days alone. After completion, she personally delivered each shirt to everyone. Interestingly, to boast about his special “intimacy” with Chung Ri-won, Jeong Jae-young mentioned during an interview: “My T-shirt has a particularly unique design, with differently shaped letters.” Due to this unprecedented “feat,” everyone jokingly referred to Chung Ri-won as the “handmade princess.”

Jeong Jae-young: Not Easy Being Robinson:

Starting as a supporting actor, Jeong Jae-young gradually became one of the leading male actors on Jongno Street. For this film, he kept his nails long for quite some time, reaching up to three centimeters in length. Even in the cold winter, he wore flip-flops barefoot to appear naturally relaxed. If actress Jung Ryeo-won's sacrifice was merely related to her appearance and ragged clothing, then he was thoroughly dedicated to "offering himself to dear cinematic art." Nevertheless, his naturally cheerful disposition allowed him to enjoy the experience: “The best part is that for the first time since birth, I let my hair grow freely without restraint.” In the morning, he no longer spent a lot of time grooming his hair. At the end-of-production ceremony in late 2008, when asked what he wanted to do first after completing filming, he immediately replied that he could finally cut his long fingernails and toenails. Director Lee Hae-joon joked, “Yes, that’s certainly true; even I found it unbearable and wanted to help him cut them!” In fact, these unusually long nails caused Jeong Jae-young significant inconvenience in daily life. Only after finishing the poster shoot did the conscientious actor stop being in this "semi-wild man" state.

5-hour High-altitude Suicide Shooting: Longest NG in Personal Acting History:

Even sitting on a zip line is something Jeong Jae-young fears due to his acrophobia. Yet, the second day of filming "Kim's Drift" involved the protagonist jumping from a high place by the Han River. This was one of the most challenging experiences in Jeong Jae-young's acting career. Despite his fear, he gathered his courage and stood on the high point, taking nearly six hours to complete a less than ten-second shot. To preserve his "tough guy image," Jeong Jae-young humorously invited Lee Hae-joon to vouch for the difficulty of the high-altitude shot, who solemnly told the media: “This particular shot indeed belonged to high-difficulty aerial photography.” Regardless, this heightened the audience's curiosity to witness this thrilling scene.

To avoid competing against "Thirst" and "7th Grade Civil Servant," the film's release date was postponed to May 14.
