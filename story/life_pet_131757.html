How to Train a Rottweiler to Bow

First, you must keep the little Rottweiler hungry because only food is the most irresistible temptation for it. Other means such as praise and cheering are just self-entertainment for humans.

Second, the Rottweiler in training must wear a collar. During training, one hand uses food to tempt the Rottweiler while the other hand holds the collar, making the dog stand on its hind legs.

Third, feeding should be accurate and timely. "Feeding" is a term used in animal training, meaning giving food. Once the Rottweiler performs an action correctly, immediately give it some food as encouragement.

Fourth, use consistent commands. Don't say "stand" at one time and "don't get up" at another. The Rottweiler's interest is all about eating; it doesn't have the energy to distinguish your varied commands.

Fifth, after learning to stand, you can teach it to turn around and bow. To teach turning around, hold the food above the Rottweiler's head and draw a circle. To teach bowing, guide the dog's front paw with your hand and manually teach it the bowing motion. Regardless of what action you're teaching the dog, constantly repeat the command aloud.

Note the following points:

1. The best age for training a Rottweiler is between half a year to one year old. Before one year, the puppy's intelligence has not fully developed, and after one year, the Rottweiler may have already formed habits and personalities that make training more difficult.

2. Dongguan Weijing Canine Industry recommends the best training time is every noon or afternoon, when its appetite is usually wide open. Two hours of training per day is sufficient. During training, be sure not to feed too much food. Once it's full, it might stop listening. Rest during training is also important.

3. Although different breeds have varying comprehension abilities, dogs are like humans—some aspects they are smart, others they are not so clever. Running-type dogs like Dalmatians are highly trainable but standing and bowing can be quite challenging for them. On the contrary, although Pekingese may seem "dumb," they are suitable for practicing these actions. Generally, standing can be learned in a day or two, and intelligent Rottweilers can learn bowing within a week.

4. The owner must be patient (after all, it's practicing to bring you face!). Don't rush or expect quick results. Use both coaxing and "enticement." However, when the Rottweiler slacks off or tries to avoid training, don't let it get away with it. You need to look stern, slap its bottom, or tap its head.

5. Once the Rottweiler learns these actions, it will never forget them in its lifetime. It knows that performing this action will result in tasty treats!

Wish your Rottweiler success in becoming well-trained soon!

Note: The "bowing ritual" is believed to have originated before the Zhou Dynasty, dating back at least 3000 years. Its basic posture involves holding fists together and raising them forward. After Jiang Taigong assisted King Wu in his successful revolution, the bowing ritual became widely practiced across the land.
