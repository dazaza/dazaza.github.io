I run a development team at Google.

by geekzhang on 2013-05-13 10:13:04

The person in the picture above is the author of this article: Matt WelshOriginal English Title: Running a Software Team at GoogleSince I left Harvard, many people have asked me what it's like now that I'm working at Google. I suspect that some people think there is a huge identity gap between being a tenured professor and a software engineer. But beyond the title, I am very happy with my work, and in this new role, I am more productive than at any time during my eight years as a professor at Harvard, even though being a professor and managing a development team have many similarities.I lead a development team at Google's Seattle office, responsible for a set of technical engineering projects related to mobile website performance. (You can learn more about the background of my team from my previous blog posts.) One of our more complex projects was the data compression proxy support in the recently released mobile Google browser. We are also responsible for developing the PageSpeed series of technologies, focusing on optimizing mobile web performance, as well as some other cool technologies that I cannot mention here.My official title is just a Software Engineer, which is the most common but coveted role at Google. I say coveted because all important decisions at Google are made by software engineers. Privately, people call me Tech Lead Manager, meaning that I am responsible not only for technical guidance but also for personnel management. At Google, the position of Tech Lead Manager is not very common; most teams have separate personnel dedicated to technical work and management. I hold both roles partly because the office is in Seattle, and if the team had to report to a full-time manager based at headquarters, it would create a lot of inconvenience. Moreover, I love holding both roles and enjoy this diversity.My job has four main aspects: (1) setting daily technical tasks for the team and ensuring they complete their tasks smoothly; (2) writing code myself; (3) acting as a liaison between the team and other project groups within Google, and finally (4) doing some personnel management for the team, including recruitment, performance evaluations, promotions, and rewards.People who have worked in academia will immediately see the differences between this and being a professor. Leading an academic research team in a university, the professor acts as a mentor, setting the technical scope for the research group and guiding graduate students. At Google, the biggest difference is that I cannot treat the people in my team as apprentices in the way a mentor treats graduate students. In fact, most of the people on my team are stronger technically than I am, and I completely rely on their hard work to develop robust and stable software. My job is to protect these engineers from distractions, support them in various ways, and help them complete their tasks successfully.It is clear that this differs significantly from life in academia. Unlike professors, I do not need to raise funds to keep research projects going. I rarely get distracted by committee work, various reviews, writing recommendation letters, or meaningless meetings. Of course, I don't have to teach students either. (I enjoy teaching, but getting good results requires a lot of work.) Most importantly, the success or failure of my team is not determined by arbitrary or often subjective personal review processes as in academia where almost everything depends on such reviews. Here is what I like most: if we do well, deliver products, and attract users, then we win. There's no longer a need to carefully consider what kind of font should be used in academic papers to please a few cantankerous old men on review committees. I digressed a bit.I probably spend about 50% of my time coding. I really need some fixed time each day to write code, which helps me calm down and clear my mind. Unlike others on the team, it's difficult for me to find long periods of uninterrupted time, so I mainly work on simpler tasks, such as writing MapReduce code to analyze service logs and generate performance reports. I really enjoy doing this kind of work; these tasks allow me to access massive amounts of data and break them down and summarize them in various interesting ways. Since I don't need to demonstrate superior programming skills to gain promotion opportunities, the more eye-catching new features are left to those on the team who are better than me.I have significant influence on the overall direction of software development in the team, including design and architecture. This is largely because I have more experience in system design compared to the younger members of the team, although I need to listen to the opinions of the actual coders on certain details I am not familiar with. A large part of my job is setting priorities, making decisions when choosing among several less-than-ideal solutions to solve a particular problem. (This also means that if the decision is wrong, I take responsibility.)I estimate that the personnel management work I do is not much different from what others in the industry do: writing periodic performance evaluation reports, formulating salary adjustment plans, recruiting new employees for the team, and advocating for promotions for team members. I regularly communicate with each of them, helping them understand the key points of their work, addressing their concerns, and guiding their career development.One of the biggest changes in my work is representing the team in contact with other teams at Google to ensure smooth communication and cooperation. My team is just a part of the larger Chrome project team, and we are in contact with development teams across various technical departments globally at Google. I frequently attend meetings to determine how to collaborate with other teams within the company. So, my work never gets boring. Moreover, our meetings are very efficient (most can be done within half an hour), although my meeting volume is nearly half of what it was when I worked at the university. (But compared to the university, only about 10% of meetings there yield substantive results.)Despite the busy workload, my working hours at Google are basically nine-to-five. I rarely work overtime in the evenings or on weekends unless there is something I am particularly eager to do, and no one emails me outside of work hours. (Recently, I spent a few hours in the middle of the night fixing a product bug due to a scheduling program in the development team.) I feel this is a relief from the continuous pressure of working as a professor. I feel that I am producing more work with less time, thanks to fewer distractions and the ability to maintain focus. Currently, our work method is: if we need to complete more work in a week than usual, we hire more people. Fortunately, it seems we haven't encountered such a situation yet.
