The biggest problem for Windows Phone is not the lack of applications.

by anonymous on 2013-09-18 21:52:04

After the release of the new iPhone, Apple's innovation capability became a focal point of discussion, and the debate between Android and iOS resurfaced.

Given that Android and iOS together hold over 90% of the smartphone market share, it is understandable that they dominate the headlines in the mobile industry. As BlackBerry continues to decline, Microsoft’s Windows Phone remains virtually the only hope for disrupting the current mobile landscape, despite being a distant third place behind the leaders.

However, significant changes are happening within Microsoft: Steve Ballmer will retire within the next year, and the company has finally taken the step of acquiring Nokia's Devices & Services division.

Nevertheless, one major issue facing Windows Phone is its apps—or rather, the "lack of apps." This problem seems to be an eternal obstacle as it strives to catch up with iOS and Android.

In fact, few people question the hardware of Nokia's Windows Phone 8 phones—their flagship Lumia devices are beautifully designed and robust, with possibly unparalleled camera functionality among smartphones. The Lumia 1020 is often referred to as a phone that can make calls. Additionally, Windows Phone 8, with its "Live Tiles," offers a refreshing change compared to the icon interfaces of Android and iOS.

There is no doubt that Windows Phone itself is quite appealing; the problem lies in the apps, right?

Researchers have recently conducted a detailed examination of some of the apps available on Android and iOS, exploring which ones Windows Phone has and which ones it lacks.

Apps Missing from Windows Phone

Windows Phone does not have Google Maps, but it does offer its own mapping service and Here Maps; there is no official Gmail app, though there are some decent third-party services, and users can also set up Google Sync locally on Windows Phone; the system lacks Chrome, but there is a counterfeit Chrome app that redirects to IE.

The system does not yet have an Instagram app, but third-party alternatives are fairly good, and there is an official Hipstamatic app. YouTube was once available on the platform thanks to the efforts of Microsoft developers, but due to a series of disputes, Google has currently disabled the video app.

These apps may be coming soon, but popular services like Vine, Path, and Flipboard have yet to appear on Windows Phone. Cloud storage services such as Dropbox, SoundCloud, RunKeeper, and Yahoo Mail have also not yet landed on the platform, although third parties offer applications similar to these services. Add Firefox, Airbnb, Snapchat, Uber, Wikipedia, Pinterest, and it becomes clear that many popular service providers have clearly not embraced Windows Phone.

It is evident that Windows Phone still lacks a large number of apps. However, from another perspective, the number of third-party native apps on the system exceeds expectations, and Microsoft has the financial resources to foster more of these apps.

Apps Already Available on Windows Phone

Skype, Twitter, BBC iPlayer (UK), Amazon, Amazon Kindle, Angry Birds, TuneIn Radio, Shazam, Spotify, Netflix, Evernote, PayPal, LinkedIn, Tumblr, Foursquare, LivingSocial, Runtastic, and many others already have Windows Phone versions. eBay is also available, though with some functional deficiencies. IMDb has launched an app for Windows Phone, but it hasn't been updated for a while. Rdio lacks some basic functions present in the iOS and Android versions. However, the platform does have a fully functional version of the word game Words With Friends, which should please potential lost users.

There is no Facebook app? Actually, there is—one developed by Microsoft itself, since Mark Zuckerberg and his company don't have time to develop it for them. As for the Groupon app, Nokia helped create one.

There are also many small tools available in the Windows Phone store, from calculators to flight search engines. Considering that many people truly need their smartphones just for internet access and a mobile browser, you might be puzzled as to why Windows Phone phones haven't attracted large numbers of users.

So, what exactly is the reason?

Self-Fulfilling Prophecy

In the eyes of many, the lack of so many native apps is certainly a problem. However, it should be noted that many people do not care about a large portion of the apps in the app stores.

They can fully accept Windows Phone, as the apps available meet their needs. Many people do not use Vine, Uber, or Snapchat on their iPhones or Androids. Tech professionals—whether programmers, marketers, or journalists—actually do not see the real demand for smartphone functionalities.

Thus, they repeatedly emphasize, "The biggest problem with Windows Phone is the lack of apps." This statement has appeared again and again, to the point that it has become a self-fulfilling prophecy.

For many, an excellent browser, Facebook, Twitter, Kindle, YouTube, Spotify, email, maps, navigation, photography, or some other services are actually enough. But in reality, people have not abandoned Android or iOS in favor of Windows Phone, leading to the belief that the low adoption rate of Microsoft's mobile system isn’t entirely due to the lack of native apps—it's at least not the direct cause.

Word-of-mouth plays a significant role in influencing electronics purchases outside the tech circle, and Microsoft has not benefited from this among the broader smartphone-buying public because the 'informed' individuals (developers, engineers, journalists, etc.) convey the message that Android and iOS are the only worthwhile mobile platforms—because of their vast app offerings. Thus, through this indirect route, the lack of apps has indeed become one of the biggest problems for Windows Phone.

It is understandable that developers do not invest a great deal of effort and resources into Windows Phone—its user base doesn't justify it. But even if Microsoft were to acquire all the missing apps mentioned above, its situation wouldn't change much—it needs to penetrate the hearts of the general consumer population, and the key is to simplify things.

Simplifying Product Lines

Even though Android is the most dominant mobile operating system, from an app standpoint, it still lags behind iOS.

It is rare for apps to launch Android versions before iOS versions; some apps take a long time to reach Android. Vine arrived on Android six months after its iPhone debut, and Instagram took over a year. Nike+ Fuelband? The iOS version has been out for 18 months, and there is still no Android version. There are also many excellent independent non-major brand apps that seem to have no plans to launch Android versions. Google is far less strict in its app quality review process than Apple.

Yes, Android also has app issues, mainly due to device fragmentation, whereas iOS only has two devices: iPhone and iPad. So why is Android thriving? The answer is simple—Google.

This search giant has become a market-dominant internet company, while Microsoft still relies on Office and Windows. With Google Docs, Maps, Google Earth, Search, and Gmail, Google is in an advantageous position to embrace the cloud. It also saw the future of mobile early on—acquiring Android in 2005. By fusing all these products into a "big melting pot," making Android open source so all OEM manufacturers could use it, and adding Google+, a powerful mobile giant emerged.

Microsoft possesses these elements—it brought Office to the cloud, moved Hotmail to Outlook, launched Bing Maps and Search, and owns Windows Phone. But it feels like it’s too late—Google has captured a large portion of the attention share in the mobile internet space, establishing an unshakable market position. On Apple's side, its hardware quality has been superior from the start, and it has spurred the current app ecosystem.

With the acquisition of Nokia's phone business, Microsoft must begin simplifying its product lines. Nokia has launched multiple phone models this year, some running Windows Phone and some not.

After Microsoft takes over Nokia's phone business, it should gradually phase out several of these devices—at least from a marketing promotion perspective. It should also abandon the S40 operating system. It needs to simplify matters, focusing on creating a few phones to cover the low, mid, and high-end markets. If it can strike a balance and introduce three to four attractive devices, avoiding both the single-product strategy of Apple and the fragmentation of Android devices, it could simplify product promotion and potentially achieve some growth. This might even mean limiting Windows Phone phones to Nokia devices, allowing HTC, Huawei, and Samsung to put all their eggs in the Android basket.

Additionally, Microsoft, with its substantial financial resources, can partner with more major retailers for its select flagship phones, ensuring prominent placement in stores.

Windows Phone does need more high-quality native apps, but this is not the root of the problem. It is merely a symptom of a key issue—the notion that everyone needs a multitude of apps on their smartphones. If all those who would be satisfied with a few trendy apps, IE, and an excellent camera were to embrace Windows Phone, then when developers realize it is a vibrant platform, native apps would naturally follow.

There is one thing Microsoft should not do. It should not frequently mock its competitors and their products with foolish tactics, as this gives the impression that it is desperate and willing to do anything, negatively impacting its brand image. Does it really think that disparaging Apple will attract people to switch to Windows Phone? This is very crazy, and whoever takes over from Ballmer should stay grounded and act quickly.
