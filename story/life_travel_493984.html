Top Ten Must-Visit Places for Shopping in Hong Kong

by zhayandehudie on 2011-09-23 15:39:00

"Buying things, eating things" refers not only to Hong Kong, but also expresses many people's preference for doing what they like without constraints or purpose. Therefore, when those who love food and entertainment come to Hong Kong, which is known as the paradise of delicious food and shopping, there is only one outcome: continuously buying things and eating. However, for first-time visitors to Hong Kong, it's inevitable to wonder where to buy things and where to eat delicious food. Here, we provide you with 10 good suggestions:

Mong Kok: Popular Shopping Area

Mong Kok is a famous "cheap shopping" area in Hong Kong. Besides having countless stores with numerous shopping options, the well-known "Ladies' Street" is located here. The actual street name of Ladies' Street is Tong Choi Street, which is a must-visit introductory course to understanding Hong Kong as a shopping paradise. Known as Ladies' Street, it goes without saying that this night market sells many women's clothing and accessories. When visiting Ladies' Street, don't be too particular about quality; just enjoy the fun of bargaining and buying cheap goods amidst the crowd.

Tsim Sha Tsui: Endless Surprises

Tsim Sha Tsui is another thriving gem of sales in the fragrant harbor. Famous clothing brands such as Esprit and Levis have their discount warehouses here. If you start from Tsim Sha Tsui and walk along Nathan Road, you can all the way reach Mong Kok, which is four MTR stations away. Along the way, the variety of shops and department stores will surely leave you dazzled, forgetting the soreness in your feet. Additionally, near the harbor, Tsim Sha Tsui's coastal area has built the Harbour City, which specializes in high-end products. This upscale mall connects the shopping areas of three hotels and leads directly to the Star Ferry Pier port. Not only is its scale impressive, but more importantly, it gathers many brand-name boutiques specializing in luxury goods. On the top floor of Harbour City, there is a "City'Super" supermarket specializing in Japanese products. You can find various unique and novel Japanese foods and items here.

Sham Shui Po: Wholesale Haven

Strictly speaking, Sham Shui Po is not a shopping area but rather a clothing wholesale hub. The numerous clothing wholesale stores in this district form their own unique style. Although most wholesale stores do not sell their clothing to the public, some still do retail business, allowing you the opportunity to buy some cheap and stylish clothes. After checking out the wholesale stores, you can then visit the retail stores. In Sham Shui Po, there is a quite prominent West Kowloon Centre. The 9-story shopping mall offers a diverse range of shopping choices. Additionally, there is a unique indoor roller coaster on the top floor, the first of its kind in Hong Kong.

New Territories Sha Tin: Mega Mall

Don't assume that the remote New Territories is an uninhabited agricultural area. Over the years, the New Territories has been developed into a residential area with towering apartment buildings, and naturally, neighborhood shopping centers have emerged to serve the residents. The New Town Plaza in Sha Tin is one of the best among them, as its size far surpasses that of ordinary neighborhood malls. Moreover, this shopping center is connected to other nearby malls, providing shoppers with endless excitement.

Victoria Peak: Night View

Shopping on the streets every day lacks novelty? Then go shopping on the mountain. Not climbing the mountain, but shifting your location to the mall on Victoria Peak to continue shopping. The mall on the mountain may not be large, but being able to shop away from the hustle and bustle in a serene environment is a nice alternative experience. As night falls, have dinner at an elegant Western restaurant overlooking the night view of the fragrant harbor, allowing the fatigue of a day of shopping to gradually fade away in a relaxed atmosphere...

Central Escalator: Hidden Alley Surprises

The continuous Central Escalator takes passengers from the ground level of Central all the way to the Peak, gliding through the alleys in the air. Riding the escalator is not just for getting around the city, but also for capturing hidden small paradises in the city. These small markets are hidden in the alleys of the financial district, but always emit a cheerful atmosphere under the cover of darkness. Seeing these simple markets squeezed between two gleaming modern skyscrapers, struggling to survive, you can't help but want to squeeze in and catch the infinite warmth in that small space.

Coppermine Bay: Typical Shopping City

Coppermine Bay on Hong Kong Island is like Orchard Road in Singapore, bustling and prosperous, filled with various luxurious malls such as Japan's崇光 (SOGO), 西武 (Seibu), 三越 (Mitsukoshi), and Hong Kong's 先施 (Sincere). In addition, the Hong Kong version of Times Square, named after the Times Squares in New York, USA, and Tokyo, Japan, is another shopping hotspot. A typical multi-story Seibu-type mall allows high-spending groups to spend extravagantly. When hungry, a 7-story building in the corner of the mall is filled with restaurants and banquet halls, ensuring a satisfying meal.

Tai Yuen Street, Wan Chai: Toy Paradise

Tai Yuen Street in Wan Chai is absolutely a paradise for toy enthusiasts. This inconspicuous little street actually hides many talents. The entire street is lined with rows of toy shops selling all kinds of toys. Whether they are rare editions, collectible editions, or the latest versions, almost all can be found on this toy street, and the prices are cheaper than in regular department stores. Additionally, another advantage here is that you can try to bargain. There's no guarantee of success, but at least you can enjoy the thrill of haggling. If you succeed, holding the toy in your hand will make you feel an extraordinary sense of accomplishment.

Stanley Market: By-the-Sea Market

In the southernmost part of Hong Kong Island, Stanley exudes an elegant European charm facing the sea. Walking through the open-air Stanley Market, selecting a wide variety of clothing, jewelry, and fine goods feels like being in a Sunday flea market in Europe or America. It feels very pleasant and leisurely. Compared to large department stores, it is a completely different shopping experience. As the sun sets, the row of seaside restaurants not far from the market will emit a touch of romance in the fading nightfall. Sitting in the restaurant, enjoying a glass of wine with the beautiful scenery, it's an unforgettable experience.
