How to Design Promotional Schemes Under Different Objectives

Author: Source: Internet Update Time: October 21, 2010

Large supermarkets hold a crucial monopolistic position in the retail sector. For many companies, entering large supermarkets is a fast track to enhance brand awareness and expand market share. Driven by competitive thinking and operational models focused on terminal sales, numerous enterprises aspire fervently to enter these large supermarkets. However, not every product or brand that enters a large supermarket can smoothly survive and grow.

One significant reason for this is the lack of effective promotional activities. Promotion is an essential part of a company's marketing process. Thomas J. Watson, the founder of IBM, once said: "Science gives business its momentum, but promotion gives it wings." How to use promotions to increase product sales and enhance brand awareness is a very important topic for companies.

Promotions in large supermarkets have always been one of the key focuses for companies, with significant human and material resources invested. However, the reality is that many promotional activities do not yield satisfactory results. In most cases, failed promotional activities are due to unclear objectives, lack of unified promotional plans, and inadequate planning schemes. Therefore, defining the purpose and goals of promotional activities can provide an overall concept for the entire promotional campaign and offer a set of standards and references for subsequent creative ideas, implementation, control, and evaluation of promotional effects. Without clear purposes and goals, promotional activities cannot be targeted effectively, and companies may find themselves at a loss when choosing promotional schemes.

Generally speaking, promotions mainly have the following objectives:

1. Launching new products

2. Enhancing brand awareness

3. Clearing inventory

New Product Promotions - Attracting Consumers and Increasing Purchase Desire

New products are often the best means for businesses to conquer the market. Properly executed promotions can quickly capture the market and enhance brand awareness; if improperly done, they can lead to the rapid failure of new products. Therefore, companies must be very cautious about promoting new products. However, the success of new products does not happen overnight. Like any new thing, people need time to recognize, understand, and accept them.

In the early stages of launching a new product, which involves cultivating customers' initial recognition, it is unwise to focus too much on price reductions as this might give customers the impression of low price, poor quality, and a less favorable brand image. During this period, what should be done is to strengthen customer goodwill and trust in the product. The best way to achieve this is through free sampling, gift offerings, trials, and live demonstrations at prominent locations in the store, such as main aisles or near cash registers. This approach allows consumers to quickly understand the product's features and significantly boosts their purchasing desire. On average, 70% of purchase decisions are made in-store, and consumers tend to buy products that catch their attention. Therefore, new product promotions should pay attention to the location and form of display.

After the initial cultivation, when consumers have gained some basic understanding of the product, promotions like gifts with purchase and outdoor roadshows can be carried out. Outdoor roadshows, due to their larger participation and influence compared to other on-site activities like special sales and samplings, are often used as a prelude to new product launches, mainly to attract crowds and create consumer pull. However, not all new products are suitable for such outdoor roadshows; it depends on the product's characteristics and the target consumer group. For instance, products aimed at college students would benefit from sponsoring events like soccer matches, summer camps, or campus concerts within universities. This approach helps manufacturers save on advertising costs while greatly enhancing the trust of their primary consumer base. An example is Uni-President's mini-marathon event to promote its new green tea product, which attracted a large number of consumers, leading to a sharp rise in sales performance and surpassing the sales of its mature product, Uni-President Fresh Orange, in a short period.

Brand Building - Specialized Thematic Promotions

Promotions are not just for selling products; they also play a role in brand building. A promotional activity must have a theme, which is the soul of the entire promotion, aiming to improve brand reputation. Digging deep into the psychology of target consumers, the most compelling promotional theme should be identified and used as the core of the entire promotion campaign. By integrating various marketing elements, an interactive atmosphere should be created between the brand and consumers at the point of sale, minimizing the psychological distance between consumers and the product/company, attracting a stable group of loyal consumers, and most effectively driving continuous growth in product sales.

The promotional theme must align with the product's brand proposition and positioning to avoid confusing or misleading target consumers. It must be determined based on the company's overall brand strategy. The promotional theme is the key to touching consumers, so it must closely relate to the interests of target consumers and address their concerns rather than being designed for the boss's approval. The theme should be concise, prominent, creative, and catchy, reflecting the core idea of the promotional activity. It should also leverage timely hot topics such as Spring Festival, Mother’s Day, Olympics, etc., having some news value and potentially attracting social media attention.

Thematic promotional activities mainly fall into three categories:

- Promotions centered around the product

- Promotions centered around seasonal characteristics

- Promotions combined with specific holidays

It's important to note that thematic promotional activities are not merely simple gift-with-purchase, discount, or roadshow formats but are centered around a series of themes that reflect the brand's propositions, positioning, and benefits to consumers.

Clearing Inventory - Choosing the Right Time and Place

When certain products have poor shelf life or excessive stock due to large purchases, companies often resort to promotions by concentrating on price reductions in large supermarkets to quickly clear inventory and recover funds. While this approach indeed alleviates financial and inventory pressure in the short term, it causes considerable damage to the product and brand image in the long run. Many large supermarket customers are relatively stable, influenced by foot traffic and purchasing power. Unless prices are extremely low, it is difficult to generate a strong buying impulse, making the clearance process time-consuming. Moreover, prolonged discounted sales can drastically reduce customer trust in the product, requiring a long recovery period for normal sales.

To mitigate these drawbacks, it is recommended to adopt opportunity sales by conducting temporary promotional carts outside large malls or supermarkets with high foot traffic. The advantage of this method is that these places typically have high customer turnover and stronger purchasing power, generating strong sales momentum and relatively shorter handling times. More importantly, because it emphasizes opportunity sales, it has minimal impact on regular sales. Most well-known brands, such as fashion brands like Clover, Lady's House, and Eiger, frequently use this approach. In most cases, sales performance from this type of sales method exceeds regular sales by more than tenfold.

Even Hershey's chocolate distributors found that during a week in November when they offered a 70% discount to clear inventory, their sales exceeded the total sales from September to November by 25%.
