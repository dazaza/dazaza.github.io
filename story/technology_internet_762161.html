Why Tencent and Chery are fighting for the QQ trademark: neither can afford to lose

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 14:05:55

Does Tencent produce cars? The answer is no, but as early as 2005 the company applied to register the "QQ" trademark for goods such as cars in class 12, and was approved in 2008. However, this move angered Chery Automobile Co., Ltd., a car manufacturer. In 2009, Chery filed a request to the Trademark Review and Adjudication Board (TRAB) to revoke Tencent's car-related trademarks on grounds of identical or similar trademarks for similar goods, and received support. After its trademark was revoked, Tencent sued the TRAB of the State Administration for Industry and Commerce. Yesterday, the Beijing No. 1 Intermediate People’s Court heard the case, with Tencent as the plaintiff, TRAB as the defendant, and Chery as the third party appearing in court.

As is well known, in China the companies associated with "QQ" are Tencent Technology and Chery Automobile, which belong to different fields. Tencent has a place in the Internet sector, while Chery's QQ car sales have cumulatively reached over a million units. From the sequence of use of "QQ", Tencent launched the instant messaging software QQ in 1999, while Chery Automobile launched the microcar Chery QQ in 2003. Regarding the question of who "QQ" belongs to, these two companies have been entangled for nearly 10 years.

On May 19, 2005, Tencent applied to register the "QQ" trademark for goods such as cars in class 12, and was approved on March 7, 2008. On November 26, 2009, Chery filed a request to TRAB to revoke the contested trademark on grounds that it violated the Trademark Law by copying and imitating famous trademarks, being identical or similar trademarks for similar goods, and infringing on prior rights of others. After review, TRAB believed that the registration of the contested trademark constituted the situation stipulated in Article 31 of the Trademark Law of preemptive registration of trademarks already used by others and having certain influence, and thus revoked the registered trademark of Tencent.

Tencent argued that the controversial ruling made by TRAB contained obvious errors and omissions, and that the contested trademark did not violate legal provisions, and should be maintained. Its agent introduced that on August 31, 2001, Tencent had applied to register the "QQ" trademark for services such as "information transmission, computer terminal communications" in class 38. After extensive promotion, the number of registered users of QQ communication services grew from 5 million in 2000 to over 200 million in 2003. Therefore, before Chery Automobile used the "QQ" trademark, Tencent's "QQ" trademark had already become a well-known trademark. In 2009, Tencent had obtained a certificate of well-known trademark qualification from relevant national departments, and any other company using the "QQ" trademark would constitute an infringement.

Chery Automobile, on the other hand, believed that Tencent's registration of the "QQ" trademark in the automobile field was malicious. As the third party in this case, Chery's agent stated that the trademark registered by Tencent in 2001 was not the "QQ" letters but a graphic. In contrast, Chery Automobile registered the "QQ" letter trademark in the automobile field in 2003. Therefore, the two enterprises used their respective trademarks in different fields, with Chery's "QQ" trademark in the automobile field preceding Tencent's. Moreover, the cumulative sales volume of Chery QQ has exceeded one million units, and advertising and promotional expenses amounted to more than 200 million yuan, which should be protected.

In addition, Tencent believed that its registration of the "QQ" trademark in multiple fields was protective registration for "QQ". However, Chery Automobile's planning of the car name and later promotional activities were all based on the already well-known "QQ" trademark and penguin mascot to achieve promotional effects, which constituted an infringement and could not generate legitimate prior rights.

The verdict of this case will be announced on another day. For both Tencent and Chery, having operated the "QQ" trademark for nearly 10 years, neither can afford to lose this lawsuit.

Is there a third way in the QQ dispute?

Tencent QQ is the first domestic instant messaging software launched by Tencent in 1999, and Chery QQ is a microcar launched by Chery in July 2003. Both "QQ"s enjoy high recognition in their respective fields.

Journalists found that as early as 2003 when the "QQ" car was introduced, Tencent had raised a trademark infringement controversy with Chery. Tencent said at the time they were busy with their IPO, so they did not file a public lawsuit. Later, Tencent sent a lawyer's letter to Chery, but Chery never gave Tencent a substantive response.

At the end of 2005, news about the lawsuit between the two parties over the QQ trademark appeared again in the media, but there was no further update on this lawsuit, with some media even calling it a publicity stunt.

This time, the trademark dispute between the two parties has once again been brought to court with Tencent as the plaintiff, TRAB as the defendant, and Chery as the third party. A trademark is the sign of a company in the market. For both Tencent and Chery, who have cultivated their brands for around ten years, neither can afford to lose. Some people believe that in this lawsuit, whoever is determined to be infringing will face enormous compensation. Is there only a win-lose situation in this trademark dispute, or is there a third way? Intellectual property expert Tang Guangliang from the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences told reporters that Tencent and Chery can completely collaborate to achieve a win-win situation.

Tang Guangliang said that the role of a trademark is to attract consumers' attention, acting as a link between merchants and consumers, and also as the image of merchants in consumers' minds. A trademark itself is a means rather than an end, and how to use this means is something each merchant must consider. In recent years, China's trademark protection has become increasingly mature. Whether there is infringement does not depend solely on the similarity of two marks; the key is whether consumers will be confused. If there is no confusion, then the use by two or more merchants can expand the influence of the trademark, which is beneficial to the users of the trademark.

"For example, if 'consumers buy Chery QQ cars because they think the car is produced by Tencent', or 'consumers use QQ chat tools because they like Chery QQ cars', then confusion can be said to exist," Tang Guangliang said. If the above situations do not occur, then the two sides do not need to fight over it. Because regarding trademarks, it is not necessarily owned by whichever merchant claims it, and even if it is owned, seeing one and eliminating another is not necessary. As long as the trademark is not maliciously defamed or the market is not occupied, it might even have a promoting effect, and the two sides can completely coexist peacefully and share interests.

In addition, Tang Guangliang said that from a rational market perspective, Tencent and Chery have no conflict. Chatting tools are mainly for the young market, and QQ cars are also widely loved by the youth. The two sides can completely collaborate, operate together, share the market, and achieve a win-win situation without unnecessary disputes over trademark ownership when there is no damage.
