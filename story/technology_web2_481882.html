The Possibility and Pathways of China's Military Presence in Africa

by akou065wgw on 2011-08-26 13:24:18

The continuous development of China's economy has already become a fact, and the rise of China as a major power will also be an irreversible trend. From the perspective of China's population, economic characteristics, and interests, the vision of future Chinese decision-makers must return from unrealistic space to reality. They must be able to see that China's economic existence is conditional. China is a global country, and Chinese products must be sold worldwide. China must also be able to obtain energy and other resources from all over the world. We believe that these are basic conditions. Without these conditions, China would not just cease to exist, but it would lose its meaning and value. For example, in the 1960s, China was mired in the chaos of the Cultural Revolution. The state still existed, but in the mainstream lives of people around the world, there was no need to consider China. The existence of this national entity was actually meaningless.

We study China's military presence in Africa, and its fundamental reason lies in our own needs, because we need it for our development and survival as a country.

Economic presence in Africa requires corresponding military presence

According to a World Bank report, from 2001-2006, China's infrastructure investment in sub-Saharan Africa surged from less than $1 billion annually to $7 billion. Nearly two-thirds of China's infrastructure investment in Africa is concentrated in hydropower and railway projects. By the end of 2007, China had provided financing for a total of 10 hydropower projects in sub-Saharan Africa, which are expected to increase electricity generation in the region by 30% when completed. In transportation, China invested $4 billion to repair 1,350 kilometers of railway and build 1,600 kilometers of new railway in Africa. Currently, about half of the projects (around 35) committed by the Chinese government in Africa are relatively small, with investments less than $50 million. In the future, the number of large-scale projects in Africa with investments close to or exceeding $1 billion is beginning to increase.

From a geographical distribution perspective, China's infrastructure investment in Africa is relatively concentrated, with over 70% of the investment distributed among Nigeria, Angola, Sudan, and Ethiopia. Among them, Angola and Sudan contribute crude oil to China on par with the Daqing Oilfield. According to World Bank statistics, in 2006, China's import of natural resources from sub-Saharan Africa reached $22 billion, 80% of which was crude oil imports. Currently, 30% of China's crude oil imports, 80% of cobalt imports, and 40% of manganese imports depend on Africa. During the process of investing in infrastructure in Africa, China increasingly uses the "Angola model," where the Export-Import Bank of China allows borrowing countries to use natural resources as a means of loan repayment.

It is worth noting that besides traditional oil and mineral resources, China's strategic investments in Africa also include developing overseas agriculture, such as grain cultivation and oilseed crops. Developing overseas agriculture not only helps solve China's food security issues but also addresses the increasingly pressing issue of insufficient arable land resources in the future. It should be known that in an underdeveloped African country, obtaining millions of acres of farmland is not difficult if political and diplomatic problems are resolved. According to relevant statistics, from 2000 to 2007, trade between China and Africa increased from $10 billion to nearly $70 billion, reaching $100 billion by 2010. It is estimated that there are now about 750,000 Chinese people in Africa, equivalent to the total number of other immigrants.

However, China is far from being without competitors in Africa. Based on our understanding, in the context of a global economic slowdown, funds from all over the world are flowing into Africa. In many economically backward cities in Africa, the hotel industry is exceptionally developed, and many of the visitors are government or company personnel with strategic intentions, as well as various merchants seeking opportunities in Africa. Whether economists, investors, or entrepreneurs, almost all agree that among continents inhabited by humans globally, Africa is the last continent with large-scale development prospects. Another factor is that time waits for no one. Many leaders of the younger generation in quite a few African countries have returned after studying abroad in developed countries and are highly educated individuals. Their understanding of market economies and resource values differs from that of their predecessors. If investments in Africa are not expedited, opportunities may vanish in an instant.

Taking strategic interests into account comprehensively, the Chinese government should be willing to make significant investments in Africa's strategic resources, dare to take appropriate risks, and invest more financial, technical, political, and diplomatic resources. In a gathering involving African resources where the whole world will participate, China clearly cannot be absent. However, to ensure China's long-term strategic interests in Africa, some form of military presence is necessary to deepen Sino-African relations. In fact, if China does not do so, Western countries will.

Military presence and strategy of Western countries in Africa

Several developed countries - the United States, France, the United Kingdom, etc. - have long-standing military presences and political-economic interests in Africa.

United States: The U.S.'s military presence in Africa can be traced back to 1903-1904, a history spanning 110 years. At that time, American Marines were ordered to protect the U.S. Consul General negotiating in Africa. Currently, the U.S.'s military affairs in Africa are jointly managed by the European Command, Central Command, and Pacific Command. In 2004, the U.S. Congress authorized the establishment of an African Expert Advisory Group and proposed a new policy framework towards Africa. This framework suggests that five factors over the past decade have increased America's attention on Africa: HIV/AIDS, oil, global trade, armed conflicts, and terrorism. These factors led to a "conceptual shift," prompting the U.S. to "strategically prioritize Africa." The National Security Strategy released by the White House in 2006 further identified Africa as "one of the top priorities of this administration." On February 6, 2008, President Bush approved the establishment of the U.S. Africa Command, showing a clear strengthening of the U.S.'s strategy towards Africa.

Currently, the U.S.'s focus on African countries is divided into three main regions: First, Somalia, referred to as the "Horn of Africa" in East Africa. The U.S. believes that changes in local regimes significantly impact the size of Al-Qaeda forces. Second, the Sahel region south of the Sahara Desert. The U.S. is concerned about cooperation between religious extremist groups like "Salafi" and Al-Qaeda within this region. In 2006, the U.S. allocated $500 million for the "Trans-Sahara Counterterrorism Initiative," providing military and development assistance to nine countries surrounding the Sahara Desert. In both East and North Africa, the U.S. military maintains a low profile, primarily using elite special forces to train and equip African national armies, engaging in joint operations with local militaries. Third, the Gulf of Guinea along West Africa, a region rich in oil production. Ensuring the security of America's oil and other natural resources supply in Africa should be the long-term strategic interest of the U.S.

Due to the U.S.'s excessive reliance on oil from the Gulf region and the unstable political situation in the Middle East, the U.S. government has decided to reduce oil imports from this area and increase oil acquisition from Africa. Officials from the U.S. Department of Energy estimate that by 2010, oil produced in Africa will meet a quarter of America's crude oil demand, surpassing Saudi Arabia's supply at that time. The U.S. also plans to "redirect 75% of current oil imports from the Middle East to Africa by 2025." Senior U.S. Navy officials have admitted that the U.S. Navy will strengthen its deployment along the West African coast to combat terrorism, illegal immigration, and drug smuggling, enhancing protection of U.S. oil interests. One of the key purposes of establishing the U.S. Africa Command is to ensure oil supplies from Africa, with the planned headquarters likely to be located in oil-rich West Africa.

France: Africa was once part of France's traditional sphere of influence. Among the 60+ member states of the global Francophone community, more than half are African Francophone countries. Economically, African countries seek aid from France, while politically, France needs support from African countries.

In terms of economics, France maintains ties with 14 African countries using the CFA franc (Benin, Burkina Faso, Ivory Coast, Mali, Niger, Senegal, Togo, Cameroon, Central African Republic, Chad, Congo Republic, Equatorial Guinea, Gabon, and Comoros). France guarantees the CFA franc's exchange rate with its financial credibility and foreign exchange reserves, setting it equal to 1/100th of a French franc and making it an internationally convertible currency. This unique monetary mechanism benefits these African countries immensely; in turn, they become common export markets for French goods. When the euro officially launched in early 1999, France persuaded its partners to peg the CFA franc to the euro.

Beyond political and economic dialogue, France maintains military cooperation with African countries, stationing approximately 7,000 troops in East Africa (Djibouti), Central Africa (Gabon, Chad, Central African Republic), and West Africa. These French soldiers have dual missions: maintaining regional peace and stability, and protecting the 120,000 French expatriates dispersed across African countries. France is the Western country with the largest military presence and most direct military interventions in Africa. France has conducted multiple direct military interventions in African countries, once earning the nickname "African Gendarmerie." Under the new policies established in 1998, France reduced unilateral military interventions in African conflict zones, scaled down its troop deployments in Africa, and increasingly participated in limited military engagements within the framework of the United Nations or through African regional organizations.

United Kingdom: The UK's military presence in Africa is tied to its long colonial history, where every instance of overseas colonization invariably followed the "army + trade" model. The British occupied Egypt in 1882, acquired Kenya in 1886, and seized Sudan in 1898. The UK was once one of the most influential countries in Africa, with its colonial territories covering more than half of the African continent.

For years, the UK has consolidated and expanded its traditional influence in Africa through the Commonwealth, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa, including influential countries like South Africa, Zimbabwe, Kenya, Uganda, Zambia, and Nigeria. Although the Commonwealth is an independent, loose multilateral organization, the UK uses it as a tool for implementing its foreign policy, maintaining close ties between the UK and Africa.

After World War II, with changes in the international landscape and the decline of UK's national strength, it became incapable of maintaining its rule over colonies like Kenya, where there were large numbers of European settlers, leading to a gradual reduction in its influence in Africa. The UK adjusted its policies, negotiating peaceful independence terms for these colonies to keep them within the Commonwealth and develop existing economic ties. This aligned with the needs of British monopoly capital.

Notably, in recent years, Germany, Russia, Japan, and other countries have also strengthened their political, economic, and military attention towards Africa, investing significantly in African countries. Linked with this attention, Russia and other countries are also attempting to dispatch military forces to Africa to showcase their military presence.

Rules and practices of international military forces' involvement in Africa

In today's international climate, where "neocolonialism" is highly vigilant, what model can a country adopt to secure its economic interests in African nations and legally display its military presence? This is a practical issue concerning strategic choices. Effectively utilizing existing international rules or practices could provide valuable references for China to enhance the protection of its interests in Africa in the future.

Summarizing the "military practical experiences" of international organizations and other countries in African nations, these can be roughly categorized as follows:

1. Anti-terrorism

Following the September 11 terrorist attacks, anti-terrorism has become the most compelling reason for international military actions and the best excuse for seeking international cooperation. Many of the U.S.'s recent military actions in the Americas have been justified under the guise of anti-terrorism. U.S. officials discussing anti-terrorism efforts in Somalia stated that Somalia has been exploited by terrorists, threatening the stability of the entire "Horn of Africa." The "Horn of Africa" refers to the protruding eastern part of the African continent, including Somalia and southeastern Ethiopia, facing the Arabian Peninsula across the Red Sea, holding strategic importance.

In 2003, U.S. President George W. Bush visited Africa, pledging $100 million to help East African and "Horn of Africa" countries enhance their counter-terrorism capabilities. That same year, the U.S. sent approximately 1,800 special forces to its base in Djibouti and conducted joint military exercises with the armies of Senegal and Gambia. In July 2005, 300 U.S. Army soldiers joined with eight North African and West African countries—Algeria, Mali, Morocco, Tunisia, and others—for a two-week large-scale live-action counter-terrorism military exercise. Based on this, the U.S. Department of Defense plans to establish over ten "forward operating bases or facilities" in North Africa. In March 2006, the head of the U.S. Central Command pointed out that terrorists are infiltrating the "Horn of Africa," necessitating U.S. measures. Months later, the U.S. pledged $500 million to launch the "Trans-Sahara Counterterrorism Initiative."

Currently, the U.S.'s military presence in Africa exhibits two clear trends: anti-terrorism needs in North Africa and the "Horn of Africa" region, and petroleum security needs in West Africa. Strategically, the U.S.'s Africa policy has combined anti-terrorism, oil, and troop deployment.

2. Combating Piracy

Combating piracy and protecting trade is another legitimate way for international military forces to intervene in Africa. This mainly focuses on the "Horn of Africa" region, especially Somali waters. According to a report titled "Threatening Global Trade, Supporting Local Wars" published by the Royal Institute of International Affairs on October 2nd, the total ransom paid in Somali coastal pirate activities in 2008 could range from $18 million to $30 million. The rising ransoms make piracy a lucrative activity, threatening global trade.

In recent years, numerous ship hijackings have occurred in Somali waters, including incidents involving Chinese crew members. On September 25th of this year, the Ukrainian cargo ship "Faina," carrying arms, was hijacked near Somali waters. The ship carried 21 crew members, 33 T-72 main battle tanks, grenade launchers, and other weapons and ammunition. The pirates demanded a $20 million ransom. Currently, U.S., EU, and Russian warships have surrounded the vessel, forming a standoff with the hijacked ship.

The hijacking of the "Faina" might lead to the creation of an international force. Some EU member states proposed on October 1st to establish a new force responsible for securing shipping lanes off the coast of Somalia. French Defense Minister Hervé Morin said that EU defense ministers have approved an international anti-piracy action plan, calling for cooperation with NATO. Eight EU member states, including Belgium, Cyprus, France, Germany, Lithuania, Netherlands, Spain, and Sweden, have indicated their willingness to provide ships needed to form the new force, with the UK possibly participating as well.

Russia aims to display its strength independently. Admiral Vladimir Vysotsky, Chief of the Russian Navy, recently announced that given the repeated kidnappings of Russian citizens by Somali pirates, Russia will soon join international efforts to combat piracy in Somali waters. However, "Russian warships will not participate in international actions but will complete this task independently."

Notably, regarding international agreements against piracy, there are two forms: multilateral agreements, such as the UN Security Council resolution passed in June authorizing foreign forces to enter Somali waters to combat piracy with the consent of the Somali transitional government; and bilateral agreements, such as those signed between foreign governments and the Somali government. For instance, Russia and Somalia will soon sign a specific agreement authorizing Russian warships to enter Somali territorial waters and hold pirates criminally liable.

After the "Faina" hijacking incident, the Somali government explicitly requested Russian intervention: "We invite all countries that can help Somalia combat piracy, especially Russia. The Somali government and president allow Russian warships to enter our waters, and if necessary, they can operate both at sea and on land."

3. Peacekeeping Operations

Peacekeeping operations are military actions conducted under the authorization of the UN or other international organizations under the name of peacekeeping.

On September 25, 2007, the UN Security Council passed Resolution 1778, deciding to deploy a UN mission composed of police and civilians in eastern Chad and northeastern Central African Republic, while authorizing the EU to send a force to the region. On January 11, 2008, the EU held a military conference in Brussels, indicating that the EU would deploy an EU peacekeeping force in Chad and the Central African Republic in February. This peacekeeping force consisted of over 3,700 soldiers from 14 member states. The EU approved a budget of 1.2 billion euros for this purpose.

The primary purpose of peacekeeping operations is to maintain peace in troubled areas, but another aim is to demonstrate military strength and presence, reflecting shifts in national policy. For example, the deployment of troops from multiple EU countries outside Europe represents an "unprecedented major operation," marking a shift in EU foreign policy.

4. Protection of Expatriates and Humanitarian Assistance

Protecting expatriates involves military or quasi-military actions taken abroad by countries to safeguard their nationals during overseas emergencies. For example, this year, French forces twice used force against Somali pirates, successfully rescuing French hostages. Meanwhile, U.S. forces have frequently employed military power to evacuate its citizens and third-country nationals from conflict zones in Africa. Additionally, overseas military operations often involve humanitarian assistance.

Factors driving China's military presence in Africa

Given the current developments, our research indicates that China's military presence in Africa is driven by the following five factors:

1. Sino-African Cooperation. There are extensive cooperative projects between China and African countries, which can include military-related projects. In fact, China has a long history and deep relationships in training military personnel for African countries. Therefore, substantial progress can be made in this area, expanding from military industries to army training, from instructors and combat units to security forces, forming a larger scale of military presence.

2. Investment Growth. Africa's economy has made tremendous progress in recent years, with young Africans always needing substantial funding. China, facing severe liquidity surplus issues, can target Africa to promote outward investment. As investments in Africa increase, substantial assets exist overseas, requiring a certain degree of military protection.

3. Energy Demand. China has irreplaceable needs in the energy sector in Africa and has already made massive investments and numerous large-scale projects. Due to the inherent fragility of energy production, these projects are easily targeted by bandits for extortion and terrorist attacks, necessitating comprehensive protection and involvement by military forces.

4. Joint Counter-Terrorism. Currently, the definition of terrorism is controlled by the United States, but China can also define it independently. With the courage to do so, China will have more operational rights in Africa. Within possible limits, China can also cooperate with Western countries in counter-terrorism efforts.

5. Combatting Pirates. Piracy and other banditry in Africa are rampant, involving killings, kidnappings, and extortions. In some countries, it has become commonplace. Notably, in Africa, kidnapping for ransom has become a profitable business, leading to the transformation of many tribal militias into specialized kidnap-for-ransom operations. In this regard, China can, based on international reasons, severely crack down through the presence of armed forces.

6. Protecting Citizens. Historically, China's protection of overseas residents has been inadequate, as evidenced by the repeated victimization of Indonesian Chinese. Currently, 750,000 Chinese citizens reside in Africa, and ensuring their human rights and interests is a natural responsibility of the Chinese government.
