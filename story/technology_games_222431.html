Before playing GGwan's "Dandan Tang", prepare a calculator on your desk. First, it can be used as a marker. After firing one shot, when others are taking their turns, adjust the position accurately and input the angle on the calculator. This is to prevent being hit off-angle by others or forgetting to readjust the angle after the next shot.

Firstly, at the start of the game, you need to analyze the enemy situation. The enemies in front of you must be eliminated first if possible. Besides that, you also need to find a suitable and safe spot for a parabolic attack. Count the distance to the enemy from this spot, then input the numbers on the calculator and set the angle. Pay attention to the wind direction. When others are shooting, remember whether the wind should be added or subtracted. Don't make the mistake of calculating the angle correctly but adding the wrong direction when it's your turn. Now it's your turn to shoot. If there is an enemy in front of you, quickly lower the angle and take him out. If there is no enemy in front, go directly to the previously calculated angle position, recalculate the wind direction, adjust the angle accordingly, and immediately launch the high cannon to take out the distant opponent.

When practicing wind correction angles, it may seem a bit difficult to calculate at first. With more practice, you will get the feel for it. Whole number winds are easy to calculate, even a first-grader can do it. For decimal point winds, for example, if you are on the left attacking the right and there is a →0.5 wind, that's 1 angle; with a →1.6 wind, it's 3 angles; with a →2.7 wind, it's two twos make four +1 angle equals 5 angles; with a →3.8 wind, it's two threes make six +1 equals 7 angles; with a →3.9 wind, it's two fours make eight angles. In other words, except for 0.9 winds which we calculate as 1*2, all other decimals should be adjusted towards the wind direction. If you are on the left attacking the right with a → wind, you need to raise the angle, while with a ← wind, you reduce the angle (mentally remind yourself: going away raises, coming back lowers). Similarly, we compensate for the remaining decimal winds, mentally reminding ourselves: "going away pulls back, coming back pushes forward." This is what I silently remind myself while playing.

If you're on the left attacking the right with a →1.6 wind, our angle is one two makes two +1 equals three. Add 3 angles to the original angle, and when shooting, remember the phrase "going away pulls back." That is, if you use a fixed power of 80, slightly shoot at 79 power. Actually, a difference of 0.1 wind doesn't matter much. However, for 0.3 wind, you must adjust, and for 0.4 wind, you can consider it as 1 degree. Shooting with 80 power is fine, resulting in a difference of 0.1, which is negligible. Of course, if you can precisely adjust for 0.2 wind, that's best. If you can't adjust precisely, don't exceed it. Exceeding 80 will definitely be inaccurate. Another point is to observe the cannon marks. If you were shooting at 78 degrees with 80 power and missed just a little, the next shot should still be at 78 degrees but increase the power to 82. This will be very accurate. Don't decrease the angle to 77, as it will still be inaccurate. Even if you hit, it will only be one shot, and he will be flipped up, making the second shot impossible. Similarly, if the cannon mark overshoots slightly, we still keep the angle unchanged and pull the power back by 2~3.

Another thing to note is, when attacking the opponent, try to aim for his neck, collar, sleeves, preferably the waistband, and never at his heels. In simple terms, aim forward, not backward. Forward allows continuous attacks, while backward will flip him up, making the second or third shots useless for polishing his shoes. We can still control this on the power bar.

If your original position is hit off-center or there is danger forcing you to move forward a bit, and it's your turn to shoot again without time to recalculate the positional difference, the angle remains unchanged. Simply pulling back a bit on the power is enough. As the saying goes, practice makes perfect. These might seem complicated for beginners, but with more practice, they become subconscious. All that remains in your mind is simple addition and subtraction. You can quickly complete the high-parabolic shot.
