05__Etiology and Pathogenesis

F99 Etiology and Pathogenesis: In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), it is believed that there is a dynamic balance maintained between the various organs and tissues within the body, as well as between the human body and the external environment. This balance ensures normal physiological activities. When this dynamic balance is disrupted due to certain reasons and cannot be immediately self-regulated back to normal, disease occurs. The etiology refers to the factors that disrupt the relative balance of the human body and cause disease.

In ancient TCM etiology, pathogenic factors were categorized into three types: external causes (such as wind, cold, etc.), internal causes (such as emotional disturbances), and non-internal/non-external causes (including improper diet, fatigue, trauma, etc.). These are pathological products formed in the disease process after the body is affected by certain pathogenic factors. They can directly or indirectly affect certain organs or tissues in the body, leading to multiple symptoms, and thus also belong to pathogenic factors.

In fact, what TCM refers to as "non-internal/non-external causes" sometimes equates to external causes, such as trauma; while others mainly involve internal causes but often combine with external factors to cause disease, such as improper diet, overwork or underwork, etc., which fall into this category. There is no single pathogenic factor that neither belongs to internal nor external causes. At most, some pathogenic factors may result from the combined action of internal and external causes. Strictly speaking, the causative factors recognized in TCM are divided into two major categories: internal and external causes.

Disease and health are relative. Normal physiological activities of the internal organs and meridians, along with the balance and coordination of qi, blood, yin, and yang, maintain the state of "yin-yang harmony." When the body is affected by certain pathogenic factors, abnormal physiological activities occur, disrupting the balance and coordination of qi, blood, yin, and yang, resulting in "yin-yang imbalance," leading to various symptoms and the occurrence of disease. TCM believes that the occurrence and development of diseases generally relate to two aspects: the righteous qi (vital energy) of the human body itself and the evil qi (pathogenic factors).

Parasites: TCM has long recognized that parasites can cause disease, referring to them as "worm accumulation." This condition often results from improper diet, excessive consumption of raw and cold fruits, and unclean food, leading to the generation of dampness-heat inside the body, which then fosters the growth of worms and eventually forms accumulations. Worm accumulation commonly presents with symptoms such as abdominal pain, poor appetite, yellowish complexion, and weight loss. Severe cases may also exhibit symptoms like syncope, abdominal distension, vomiting, and even the formation of gu syndrome. Various types of parasites can parasitize the human body, including roundworms, pinworms, tapeworms, schistosomes, cysticerci, etc., each with distinct characteristics. For example, roundworms residing in the intestines can cause intermittent abdominal pain; hookworm disease often manifests as yellowish complexion, muscle wasting, and craving for unusual substances; pinworm disease patients often complain of anal and perineal itching, and white thin thread-like pinworms can be found directly in these areas; tapeworm disease symptoms are relatively mild, and patients usually seek medical attention due to finding white tape-like segments or worm pieces in their stools; schistosomiasis leads to liver and spleen enlargement, impaired blood circulation, and fluid retention forming 'gu distension.'

Overwork and Fatigue Damage: Overwork includes both excessive physical labor and excessive rest. Normal work and physical exercise help promote the flow of qi and blood, enhancing physical fitness. Necessary rest helps eliminate fatigue, restoring physical and mental strength without causing illness. Only prolonged overwork, including physical labor, mental work, and excessive sexual activity, or complete lack of movement, can become pathogenic factors leading to disease.

Excessive labor refers to overworking in three aspects: physical labor, mental strain, and excessive sexual activity. Excessive physical labor refers to prolonged overexertion leading to accumulated fatigue and illness. Overworking depletes qi, eventually leading to shortness of breath, weakness, and emaciation. As stated in *Suwen - Theory of Pain*, "Labor depletes qi," and in *Suwen - Discussion on Five Qi*, "Prolonged standing harms bones, prolonged walking harms qi."

Excessive mental strain refers to excessive thinking affecting the heart and spleen. *Suwen - Great Treatise on the Correspondence between Yin-Yang and Images* states, "The spleen's function is associated with thought," and the heart governs blood and stores spirit. Excessive mental strain depletes heart blood and damages spleen qi, leading to symptoms such as palpitations, forgetfulness, insomnia, vivid dreams, poor digestion, abdominal distension, and loose stools.

Excessive sexual activity refers to lack of moderation in sexual life, leading to excessive depletion of kidney essence. The kidneys store essence and govern storage. Kidney essence should not be excessively depleted; frequent sexual activity depletes kidney essence, clinically manifesting as lower back and knee soreness, dizziness, tinnitus, lethargy, spermatorrhea, premature ejaculation, or even impotence in men, and irregular menstruation, dysmenorrhea, amenorrhea in women.

Excessive rest refers to complete lack of physical activity or exercise. Daily appropriate movement is necessary for smooth flow of qi and blood. Long-term lack of exercise weakens the functions of qi and blood and reduces spleen and stomach functions, leading to reduced appetite, weakness, lethargy, limb softness, obesity, palpitations, shortness of breath, sweating during activity, or secondary diseases. As stated in *Suwen - Discussion on Five Qi*, "Prolonged lying harms tendons."

Epidemic Qi: Epidemic qi, also known as pestilential qi, refers to a class of highly contagious pathogenic factors. In TCM literature, it is also referred to as "epidemic," "pestilential poison," "harmful qi," "abnormal qi," "toxic qi," "deviant qi," etc.

Epidemic qi-induced diseases have characteristics such as sudden onset, severe conditions, similar symptoms, strong contagiousness, and tendency to spread easily. Examples include large-head epidemic, frog epidemic, epidemic dysentery, diphtheria, scarlet fever, smallpox, cholera, etc. As stated in *Suwen - Supplemental Section - Needle Therapy Theory*, "When the five epidemics arrive, they infect each other regardless of age, and the symptoms are similar." Also in *Discussion on the Origins of Diseases - Volume Ten*, "When people are infected by deviant qi and fall ill, the disease spreads from person to person, potentially wiping out entire families." Here, the ancients not only pointed out the contagious nature of epidemic qi but also emphasized its serious harm to humans. *Treatise on Pestilential Fevers - Original Disease* states, "Epidemics arise from infection with heavenly epidemic qi... Regardless of age or strength, anyone who comes into contact with it will fall ill, entering through the mouth and nose." Here, it clearly points out that epidemic qi can be transmitted through the air, often entering the body through the mouth and nose.

The occurrence and spread of epidemics are often related to the following factors:

1. Climate factors: Abnormal changes in natural climate, such as prolonged drought, extreme heat, humidity, and foggy miasma.

2. Environmental and dietary factors: Such as pollution of air, water sources, and food.

3. Lack of timely preventive isolation measures.

4. Social factors: The prevalence of epidemics is related to the economic and cultural status of society. Generally, economically and culturally backward countries and regions are more prone to epidemics; economically and culturally advanced countries and regions experience fewer epidemics.

Internal Injury Due to Seven Emotions: The seven emotions refer to joy, anger, worry, pensiveness, sadness, fear, and fright—seven types of emotional changes reflecting one's mental state. In normal circumstances, the seven emotions generally do not cause disease. However, sudden, intense, or prolonged emotional stimulation exceeding the normal range of physiological activities can disrupt the flow of qi and lead to disharmony in the organs' yin, yang, qi, and blood, resulting in disease. Since it is one of the main pathogenic factors causing internal injury, it is also called "internal injury due to the seven emotions."

1.7.4.1 Relationship Between the Seven Emotions and Internal Organs' Qi and Blood: TCM believes that mental activities are closely related to the internal organs. As stated in *Suwen - Great Treatise on the Correspondence between Yin-Yang and Images*, "Humans have five zang-organs transforming into five qi, giving rise to joy, anger, thought, worry, and fear." Emotional activities must be based on the essence and qi of the five zang-organs. It further states that the heart is associated with joy, the liver with anger, the spleen with thought, the lungs with sorrow, and the kidneys with fear. Joy, anger, thought, sorrow, and fear are abbreviated as the "five emotions." Different emotional changes have different effects on the organs, and changes in the organs' qi and blood also influence emotional changes. For example, *Suwen - Discussion on Regulation of Channels* says, "Excess blood leads to anger, deficiency leads to fear." *Lingshu - Original Spirit* also states, "Deficiency of liver qi leads to fear, excess leads to anger. Deficiency of heart qi leads to sadness, excess leads to uncontrollable laughter." Thus, the seven emotions and the internal organs' qi and blood are closely related.

1.7.4.2 Characteristics of Disease Caused by the Seven Emotions: Unlike the six exogenous pathogens, which invade the body through the skin or mouth and nose, initially presenting with surface symptoms, the seven emotions internally injure corresponding organs, disrupting the flow of qi and blood, leading to various diseases.

1.7.4.2.1 Direct Injury to Internal Organs: *Suwen - Great Treatise on the Correspondence between Yin-Yang and Images* states, "Anger injures the liver," "Joy injures the heart," "Thought injures the spleen," "Sorrow injures the lungs," "Fear injures the kidneys." Clinically, different emotional stimuli can have varying effects on the organs. However, this is not absolute because the human body is an organic whole. As stated in *Lingshu - Mouth Inquiry*, "The heart is the master of the five zang and six fu organs... Therefore, sorrow, grief, and worry disturb the heart, and when the heart is disturbed, all the five zang and six fu organs are affected." This indicates that all emotional stimuli are related to the heart, which is the great master of the five zang and six fu organs. Damage to the heart spirit can affect other organs. For example, anger injuring the liver can lead to liver qi rebellion, affecting the spleen and stomach, resulting in symptoms like liver-spleen disharmony and liver-stomach disharmony.

The heart governs blood and stores spirit, the liver stores blood and regulates qi, and the spleen transforms and transports while being the hub of qi ascending and descending and the source of qi and blood production. Therefore, diseases caused by emotional injury often manifest as disharmony of qi and blood in the heart, liver, and spleen. For example, excessive thinking and mental strain often damage the heart and spleen, leading to dual deficiency of heart and spleen qi and blood, resulting in mental abnormalities and dysfunction of the spleen. Anger injuring the liver leads to upward movement of qi and blood, causing symptoms like chest and flank distension, sighing, stagnation of liver qi, or blood stasis leading to flank pain, menstrual disorders, amenorrhea, masses, etc. Moreover, emotional injury can transform into fire ("five emotions transforming into fire"), leading to symptoms like yin deficiency and hyperactivity of fire, or causing dampness, food, and phlegm stagnation.

1.7.4.2.2 Affecting Organ Qi Flow: *Suwen - Theory of Pain* states, "Anger causes qi to rise, joy causes qi to relax, sorrow causes qi to dissipate, fear causes qi to descend, shock causes qi to scatter, and thought causes qi to stagnate."

Anger causes qi to rise: Excessive anger can cause liver qi to rebel upwards, blood follows qi upwards. Clinically, this can manifest as qi reversal, red face and eyes, vomiting of blood, or even fainting.

Joy causes qi to relax: This includes relieving tension and dispersing heart qi. Normally, joy can relieve tension, making the nutritive and defensive qi flow smoothly, creating a pleasant mood. *Suwen - Theory of Pain* states, "Joy makes qi harmonize first, allowing nutritive and defensive qi to flow smoothly, hence relaxing qi." However, excessive joy can disperse heart qi, causing the spirit to wander, leading to inability to concentrate, or even madness and confusion. *Lingshu - Original Spirit* states, "Joy and happiness disperse the spirit and prevent it from storing."

Sorrow causes qi to dissipate: Excessive sorrow and worry can depress lung qi, causing depression and depletion of lung qi.

Fear causes qi to descend: Excessive fear can make kidney qi unstable, causing qi to descend, leading to urinary and fecal incontinence, or if fear persists, it can damage essence, leading to bone pain, flaccidity, and seminal emission.

Shock causes qi to scatter: Sudden shock can make the heart lose support, the spirit lose direction, and the mind lose focus, leading to panic.

Thought causes qi to stagnate: Excessive thought injures the spirit and spleen, leading to stagnation of qi. Ancient texts believe that thought originates in the spleen and matures in the heart. Excessive thought not only depletes heart spirit but also affects spleen qi. Excessive thought injures the heart and spleen, depleting yin blood, leading to palpitations, forgetfulness, insomnia, vivid dreams. Stagnation of qi obstructs spleen transportation and stomach reception, leading to poor appetite, fullness and distension of the abdomen, loose stools, etc.

1.7.4.2.3 Abnormal Fluctuations in Emotions Can Worsen or Rapidly Deteriorate Illness: Based on clinical observations, in many diseases, if the patient experiences intense emotional fluctuations, it often worsens the condition or rapidly deteriorates. For example, in patients with a history of hypertension, if they encounter anger, liver yang rises suddenly, causing a rapid increase in blood pressure, leading to dizziness, sudden fainting, or paralysis, facial deviation, etc.

Phlegm and Blood Stasis:

1.7.7 Phlegm: Phlegm is a pathological product formed due to dysfunction of the zang-fu organs and disruption of fluid metabolism, where fluids coagulate due to imbalance. It can be caused by various factors such as external pathogens invading the lungs, spleen, or kidneys, disrupting water distribution and excretion, or obstructing the triple burner's water pathways, affecting normal fluid metabolism. Accumulation of water-dampness leads to the formation of phlegm. Emotional stress can cause liver qi stagnation, transforming into fire that boils down body fluids into phlegm. Consuming rich and greasy foods or alcohol can also lead to dampness accumulation and phlegm formation. Generally, thick and turbid is considered phlegm, while clear and thin is considered fluid retention, and they are often used together.

Once phlegm is formed, it can circulate throughout the body, accumulating in various places, causing multiple diseases. Accumulating in the lungs, it causes wheezing, coughing, chest tightness, and expectoration; obscuring the heart, it leads to chest tightness, palpitations, insomnia, mental confusion, or even mania; accumulating in the stomach, it causes epigastric fullness, nausea, vomiting, and poor appetite; flowing into the meridians and muscles, it causes limb numbness, hemiplegia, or phlegm nodules, lymph nodes, abscesses, and metastatic swellings; phlegm rising upwards can cause dizziness and coma; phlegm stagnating in the throat leads to discomfort, often feeling as if something is stuck in the throat; fluid retention in the chest and ribs causes chest and rib distension, coughing-induced pain; if retained in the intestines, it causes intestinal rumbling and even diarrhea. Hence, there is a saying, "Most diseases are due to phlegm."

1.7.8 Blood Stasis: Blood stasis refers to the pathological condition where blood circulation throughout the body is impeded or locally stagnant, or where extravasated blood remains unabsorbed within the body. It is generally caused by qi deficiency, qi stagnation, blood coldness, trauma, etc.

Qi is the commander of blood, and qi flow promotes blood flow. Qi deficiency weakens blood flow, leading to easy stagnation and the formation of blood stasis; qi stagnation causes blood coagulation, forming stasis; blood coldness causes qi stagnation, impairing blood flow and leading to easy coagulation and stasis; trauma causes blood leakage outside the vessels, forming stasis.

After blood stasis forms, it not only affects blood circulation but also leads to organ dysfunction, causing various symptoms. For example, blood stasis blocking the heart channels causes chest tightness, heart pain, cyanosis of lips, and irregular pulse; blood stasis blocking the lung channels causes chest pain and hemoptysis; blood stasis in the liver causes flank pain and masses; blood stasis in the stomach causes hematemesis, gastric pain, black stool; blood stasis in the uterus causes lower abdominal pain, menstrual disorders, amenorrhea, dysmenorrhea, or even uterine bleeding; blood stasis in the limbs causes local swelling and pain or cyanosis, severely affecting mobility.

Although blood stasis-related diseases are numerous, clinical manifestations often share common features: pain is often sharp and fixed, tender on palpation; bleeding is dark purple, not fresh, or mixed with clots; locally, there may be ecchymosis, petechiae, and over time, palpable lumps that do not move on palpation.

Trauma: Trauma includes gunshot wounds, knife cuts, contusions, heavy lifting injuries, burns, frostbite, and insect or animal bites.

Gunshot wounds, knife cuts, contusions, and heavy lifting injuries can cause skin and muscle bruising, swelling, and pain, bleeding, or tendon and bone injuries, dislocations. Severe cases can damage internal organs or cause excessive bleeding, leading to serious conditions such as coma, convulsions, and collapse of yang qi.

Burns are mostly caused by high-temperature objects, boiling water, hot oil, or scalding. Mild cases damage the skin, causing redness, swelling, heat, pain, dry skin, or blisters and severe pain at the injured site; severe burns can damage muscles and bones, causing loss of sensation, leather-like wounds, or wax-white, charred yellow, or carbonized tissue; severe burns, with extensive damage, besides local symptoms, often lead to restlessness, fever, thirst, and reduced urination due to intense pain, toxic invasion, fluid evaporation or exudation, and even death.

Frostbite refers to systemic or localized injuries caused by low temperature exposure. Systemic frostbite, as cold is a yin pathogen that easily damages yang qi, excessive cold damages yang qi, losing warmth and promoting blood circulation, leading to shivering, gradual decrease in body temperature, pale complexion, blue lips, blue nails, numbness, fatigue, or drowsiness, weakened breathing, and slow pulse. If not treated promptly, death can occur. Local frostbite commonly occurs on hands, feet, earlobes, nose tips, and cheeks. Initially, the affected area becomes pale due to cold-induced constriction of the meridians, blood and qi stagnation, affecting warmth and nourishment, leading to pale skin, cold numbness, followed by swelling and cyanosis, itching, burning pain, or blisters of varying sizes, which often get infected when broken.

Insect or animal bites include venomous snake bites, wild animal attacks, rabid dog bites, or scorpion and bee stings. Mild cases cause local damage, swelling, and bleeding; severe cases damage internal organs or cause excessive bleeding leading to death. Venomous snake bites cause systemic poisoning symptoms, often leading to poisoning death if not treated promptly. Rabid dog bites initially present with local pain and bleeding, and after wound healing, following a latent period, symptoms such as restlessness, anxiety, jaw locking, convulsions, hydrophobia, and aerophobia may appear.
