Cancan is one year old now. Since I got pregnant, I've been a full-time mom at home for nearly two years. During these two years, I've gained a lot, and of course, there have been many annoying things too. Let me share some of my child-rearing experiences with you all. Of course, I'm just a new mom who's only 25 years old, so there are still many shortcomings that need to be pointed out by other moms.

After staying in the hospital for four days, we went home.

On the seventh day, Cancan started getting eczema on his skin.

Now looking back, adults often say during the confinement period: no ventilation! So it caused poor air circulation in the room. Plus, the weather in Huangshan in November started to get cooler, Grandma and Mom were both afraid the baby would catch a cold, so they dressed him in a lot of clothes, each time dressing him up like a little dumpling (though Cancan didn't actually catch a cold before he was half a year old). I think it was this lack of ventilation and too many clothes that led to Cancan starting to develop eczema, or more accurately, heat rash. At the time, I considered many possible allergens and even tried switching from cow's milk to goat's milk, but it was all in vain. During this time, I also tried many skincare products and small remedies, which I won't go into detail about here because they were ineffective. 2. About eating

Cancan's biggest problem is completely revealed in this area. From the time we started adding complementary foods after four months, he never ate a meal quietly sitting down. I think a big part of the responsibility lies with his grandmother. When he first started eating rice porridge, maybe due to unfamiliarity with new food or not yet being accustomed to swallowing, he was very reluctant to eat complementary foods. At the time, we were still staying at Grandma's house. My mom, in order to make the baby eat properly, kept shaking a rattle to attract his attention and then stuffed food into his mouth while he was distracted. After that, almost every meal for Cancan required toys or something else to divert his attention.

Here's a lesson for all moms: once bad habits are formed, they're hard to break.

Now, I just try my best not to give him anything to play with in his hands, and almost always eat in a fixed place, letting him gradually understand that it's time to eat. Now he can eat without playing with toys, but he still needs to watch cartoons to eat...

I will persist; give me some time, slowly, I'll make you fall in love with eating.

3. About fever

Cancan's first illness occurred at six months, completely due to my and his father's negligence. That day, we drove downtown to play. It was hot, but the weather in May could suddenly turn cool. On the way back, we had the air conditioning on low, and Cancan fell asleep in the car. At the time, I didn't even think to cover him with a little blanket, thinking it would be uncomfortable to suffocate in such hot weather. But I didn't realize that people are most likely to catch a chill when they're sleeping, especially when the car air conditioning was set so low. As a result, after we got home, he had diarrhea for a week, and started having a fever on the third day. He became listless, fussy. When we went to the community hospital, his temperature was 39 degrees Celsius. Looking at him made me cry, as I had no idea what to do facing a sick child for the first time. But after calming down, I remembered the physical cooling methods mentioned by many mothers and doctor friends of my husband, so I immediately went to the pharmacy and bought cooling patches, alcohol swabs, and physiological saline commonly used for reducing fevers.

On the first day, I gave him baths three times—morning, noon, and evening—with water slightly cooler than usual bath water. After washing, I wiped his neck, armpits, and wrists with alcohol swabs (many mothers say alcohol swabs are irritating and should not be used on babies, but this refers to high-concentration medical alcohol. Alcohol swabs with a concentration below 50% can be used on babies. Most alcohol swabs sold in pharmacies are 75%, so you can dilute them with water before use), then applied a cooling patch to his forehead. Clothes should be worn less than usual to allow body heat to dissipate. During this period, the baby should drink more water. Many elderly people say that when a baby has a fever, they should be wrapped up, sweat it out, and then they'll be fine, but this is an unscientific approach based on outdated ideas. By the end of the first day, his temperature dropped to 38.3 degrees Celsius the next day. Using the same method, if you have a thermometer at home, monitor the baby's temperature frequently. If it exceeds 39 degrees Celsius and doesn't subside, medication may be necessary, but intravenous drips should not be the first choice! On the third day, his temperature was around 37 degrees Celsius, and by the fourth day, Cancan's fever had completely subsided.

Actually, parents are bound to be anxious when their baby has a fever. Many mother friends also asked me if I wasn't nervous. How could I not be nervous or worried? At the time, seeing him so uncomfortable, I couldn't stop crying, but I told myself to stay calm. No one doesn't get sick. Don't rush to seek medical help unnecessarily. Babies are small, but they have a certain resistance, and their bodies are quite strong. So mothers should remain calm when faced with such issues and not panic. If you can handle it yourself, do it. You know, taking medicine is better than getting an IV drip, and not taking medicine is better than taking medicine. However, I'm referring to common fevers here. For fevers over 39 degrees Celsius, you can take antipyretics like Meilin, but for persistent high fevers or febrile seizures, you should go to the hospital. After all, we're not doctors ourselves, and we can only deal with what we're capable of. Don't gamble with your child's life.

4. About diarrhea

Cancan's diarrhea at six months was caused by alternating cold and heat leading to stomach discomfort, so it improved after the fever subsided. We used Smecta and Mama Ai at the time.

Recently, Cancan has started having diarrhea again. We used Smecta and Mama Ai, but it didn't improve, so we heard that cooked apple water has good effects against diarrhea. Indeed, one apple a day for two days, using only two apples, his stool returned to normal. Here's how to make apple water, which is actually very simple.

Wash the apple, cut it into pieces, put it in a pot, add an appropriate amount of boiling water, and cook for 10-15 minutes. Give the cooked apple water to the baby to drink when it's lukewarm. The leftover apple can be mashed into a puree and mixed with rice porridge for the baby to eat, or if the baby is Cancan's age and doesn't eat rice porridge anymore, you can give it to him directly. Of course, initially, the apple should be cut into small pieces to prevent choking, as even though children have teeth, their chewing ability is still poor, and almost all food is swallowed directly.

Speaking of apples, they are truly wonderful fruits. Eat cooked apples for diarrhea, and raw apples for constipation.

Carrots also have anti-diarrheal effects, but they must be cooked. Raw carrots have no nutrition; only when cooked does the beta-carotene become absorbable.

Autumn is the season when diarrhea occurs most frequently. If a stool test shows intestinal infection, treatment is needed; otherwise, eating cooked apples will suffice.

5. About coughing and bronchitis

Last month, Cancan had a cough for nearly half a month, with phlegm in his throat, making him very uncomfortable. Many times, the food he ate was completely vomited up due to coughing, so after a few days, his face turned sallow. We took him to see a pediatric expert in the city. After listening with a stethoscope, the diagnosis was fine bronchitis. I was scared again, wondering why it was bronchitis again. Later, I understood that so-called baby coughs generally fall under this disease category. It's like when adults catch a cold and cough; in children, doctors call it bronchitis. Some cephalosporin granules and cough-relieving oral liquids were prescribed. After three days, there was no improvement, so we went back to the expert for another listen to the lungs. The doctor suggested going to the hospital the next day for an IV drip, saying that if the IV drip wasn't administered, pneumonia might develop. Once again, I stood dumbfounded, wondering why it had to be pneumonia. In a daze, we spent that day, originally intending to go to the hospital for nebulization treatment. Thinking that the IV drip would cause the child pain and I wouldn't bear to see it, but after the doctor listened this time, the condition was good, nebulization wasn't necessary, and we were instructed to continue taking medicine at home. Coughs don't get better quickly, taking at least a week. Feeling relieved, Cancan was forced by me to take medicine for more than a week, but the results were not as expected, still not improving, coughing every night. Coughing so much that he vomited milk everywhere, my husband had to get up in the middle of the night to wash the sheets. Later, during a random phone call home, my father mentioned mugwort leaves. I remembered that my mother-in-law had left a lot of dried mugwort stored at home last Dragon Boat Festival, and I hadn't thought of when to use them until now. This time, I decided to try giving Cancan a mugwort leaf bath, and miraculously, the next day, Cancan almost stopped coughing. After five consecutive days of bathing, he was completely cured. Reflecting on forcing him to take medicine for more than a week, it was truly a sin. Such a good remedy that I had forgotten this time. Below, I will explain the method of treating common cold-induced coughs with mugwort leaves:

Cook mugwort leaves in a pot or directly soak them in the bathtub with boiling water. Finally, let the water cool and bathe the baby. If the baby has phlegm when coughing, here I recommend a relatively effective medicine: Mucosolvan. Its cough-relieving and expectorant effects are really good, recommended by other mothers. Mugwort baths combined with Mucosolvan, for general coughs, washing for three consecutive days usually leads to recovery. Fortunately, Cancan avoided the IV drip this time, escaping a major ordeal. Truly, it's heartbreaking to see such a young child get needles in the head or feet.

Some small details: Babies generally cough more when sleeping at night, so babies sleep poorly, and mothers don't sleep well either. Therefore, the last feeding before bedtime should be skipped, or given earlier. For example, if the baby eats at 9 PM and goes to bed, feed him at 8 PM, rest for an hour, and then sleep. Also, raise the pillow while sleeping, which makes breathing smoother and prevents vomiting milk after eating and then coughing.

6. About differences in child-rearing between two generations

First, not talking about mother-in-law and daughter-in-law relationships, let's talk about me and my mom. There were many conflicts in raising children, such as her always fearing the baby would catch a cold, so she always dressed the baby in too many clothes. Thinking about last winter, Cancan was still very small at that time, just twisting his head when held, with little movement. So he was bundled up like a dumpling throughout the entire winter. At the time, I didn't think about anything else, just feeling that it would indeed prevent catching a cold. But now, Cancan is different. His daily activity level is the highest among everyone in the family. Wearing too many clothes makes him sweat easily, and once he sweats, it stays trapped inside, and when he rests, as his body temperature drops, he catches a cold more easily. So now Cancan wears about the same amount of clothing as me, sometimes even less than me, because his activity level is much higher than mine. And when he sweats, I change his sweaty underwear immediately. Sometimes in one day, I change several sets of underwear. Here, I recommend a good item: gauze sweat towels. I bought several, inserting them into the baby's underwear, making sure they are flat, otherwise he will feel uncomfortable. After playing, if he sweats, just take it out and replace it with a clean one, which is much more convenient than changing clothes. But if he sweats too much and not just the back is wet, then the underwear needs to be changed.

Here, not dressing the baby in too many clothes doesn't mean wearing very little either. Catching a cold is a troublesome matter, so clothing should be adjusted according to the temperature. My mother-in-law is someone who fears heat, so she thinks Cancan is always dressed too warmly. My mother is someone who fears cold, so in her eyes, I always dress Cancan too lightly. Actually, whether to dress a child more or less depends on the person who takes care of the child daily, which is me. This is the habit of having one person fixedly take care of the child. Constantly changing caregivers leads to different habits and less understanding of the child, which can cause many inexplicable illnesses.

7. About crawling

My mother said I didn't learn to crawl as a child, I went straight to walking. My mother-in-law said the same about my husband. After having Cancan, I realized that we weren't unable to crawl, it's just that our environment back then probably didn't provide a place for us to learn to crawl. There's a saying: "Sit at seven months, crawl at eight months, grow teeth at nine months." So starting at eight months, I encouraged him to learn to crawl. After a month of practice, he still hadn't learned. At the time, I thought maybe he really couldn't learn. Later, for convenience, I bought a crawling mat. Wherever we went, I spread it out, letting him sit and play with toys on it, and within less than half a month, he was crawling very well.

In summary, I previously chose the wrong environment. For that month, I kept putting him on the bed to practice, but the soft mattress made him happily flip around or scoot a bit and then rest. Later, on the crawling mat on the floor, it wasn't as soft as the bed, so when he crawled, he wouldn't be pulled back by the sheet, and he moved forward. While practicing, I placed a toy he liked in front of him to entice him, gradually strengthening his arms and legs, allowing him to crawl more smoothly. Now, everyone who sees Cancan crawl is amazed at how fast he is. Haha, the initial laziness led to him learning to crawl, which made me very happy.

Later, I read a lot of parenting experts' discussions on crawling... Generally speaking, they covered the following points:

Crawling is the most basic ability in human evolution; it's the most important layer in the construction of the human brain; it's the most active participation of various parts of the infant's body in exercise; it's the form of exercise that activates the most areas of the infant's brain; it's the form of exercise that brings the most excitement throughout a person's life; it's the most efficient form of exercise to lay a comprehensive foundation for physiology, psychology, intelligence, and abilities throughout life.

Experts from the Pediatric Research Institute in Philadelphia conducted extensive research and concluded that encouraging babies to crawl early and often significantly helps their brain development, strengthens the neural motor regulation of hands, feet, and eyes, and inspires and expands the intellectual potential of babies.

Babies who crawl, start crawling early, and crawl often tend to be more agile, have better coordination, understand things faster, and have a strong thirst for knowledge; whereas babies who dislike crawling often appear dull, slow, emotionally low, and unwilling to meet new people or try new things.

At the same time, babies who crawl well and enjoy crawling have stronger cognitive abilities, better picture recognition and word pronunciation skills, as well as more stable walking and running compared to babies who crawl late or crawl less.

It might not be as exaggerated as saying that babies who don't crawl are stupid or those who do are smart. But it's a process of growth and learning, and it's best to let babies gradually learn rather than easily skipping this stage.

8. About diaper rash

Before Cancan was born, Grandma made dozens of cloth diapers, all made of pure cotton. At the time, I didn't think much about it, just preparing them. Of course, we also stocked up on disposable diapers, as how could we possibly potty train such a small newborn? We used disposable diapers for a long time, and one day Grandma took out a cloth diaper suggesting we use it instead. Unfortunately, we didn't notice, and the pants and blankets got completely soaked. From that point on, Cancan never used a cloth diaper again—it was simply too much trouble. Many mothers have asked me if Cancan has ever had a red bottom. Very fortunately, Cancan has never had a red bottom.

I summarized a few reasons:

Firstly, the quality of the diapers plays a significant role. Don't buy cheap, low-quality diapers, as they can greatly affect the baby's skin. Initially, Cancan used Japanese Merries, which I found to be the best—very soft, thin, and breathable. However, after the nuclear radiation incident in Japan, although people said it wouldn't affect us, I still switched brands. Later, I used premium Huggies, which were a bit stiff, and after urinating, the baby's bottom remained damp, indicating breathability, but they were not cheap. Later, I heard many babies were using Mabao, and I had always looked down on domestic products, but I decided to give it a try. After using a small pack, it turned out to be pretty good, averaging around 2 RMB per piece. This saved me some money on diapers. I only use diapers at night when sleeping. If Cancan sleeps through the night, one diaper will suffice. If he wakes up for nighttime feeding, I will definitely change his diaper afterward, otherwise, he wouldn't sleep comfortably. During the day, I switch to cloth pads because they are easy to change—just pull them out and throw them away, making it convenient to potty train. I've tried many brands, and the best one is Beishule's Thin Comfort, which is very thin, soft, and breathable, costing about 1.2 RMB per piece. In winter, Cancan uses about six pieces a day. Some mothers recommended Anshui Baby, so I bought four packs to try, and they were also pretty good, cheaper than Beishule, costing about 1 RMB per piece.

Secondly, diapers should be changed frequently, which is the key to preventing diaper rash. Don't think that just one pee means the diaper can hold more, trying to save on usage. A baby's skin is very delicate, and even the best diapers trap urine against the skin, so prolonged exposure to urine-soaked skin can easily lead to diaper rash.
