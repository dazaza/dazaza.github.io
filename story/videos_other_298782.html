Now let's look at how the nasal bones can be brought closer together? Using a small bone chisel from nasal surgery, under the skin covering the nose, cut and break the bone to separate it from the craniofacial bones, then press the separated small bone pieces to the appropriate position. In the figure above (Figure 2), the edges of the nasal bones are marked with red lines. We can compare the relationship between the edges of the nasal bones and the normal bone sutures and notches in the local area (Figure 1 above). A conclusion can be drawn: the nasal bones are not the main reason for the widening of the nasal base. Neither are they the main reason for the widening of the nasal root due to excessive contact with the supraorbital ridge (frontal bone). On the contrary, the cheekbones near the nasal bones are the main reason for the widening of the bony nasal triangle's base. Therefore, during surgery, we need to handle these small bone pieces, bringing them closer together, narrowing the nasal bones, which are not actually the anatomical "nasal bones." In the figure, the blue line marks the area of the cheekbone, which is the primary bone causing the widening of the bony nasal triangle's base. This area includes part of the nasal bone and part of the nearby cheekbone. In rhinoplasty, narrowing the nasal bone involves separating the bones within the blue line area from the craniofacial bones and pushing them to a new position to meet the requirements of rhinoplasty. This bone fragment is very thin, usually only 1 millimeter thick, so osteotomy only requires a very fine bone chisel. We typically perform two osteotomy lines. The first osteotomy line, as shown in the figure, follows the direction of the black arrow for one osteotomy. During osteotomy, we need to use a retractor to lift all the skin covering the surface of the nose, place the fine bone chisel at the blue point (in the figure), and then gently tap multiple times with a hammer, advancing the osteotomy line along the black arrow. Afterwards, we reposition the bone chisel at the edge of the piriform aperture of the nostril, preparing to perform the second osteotomy line along the green arrow. While advancing the osteotomy along the green arrow, gently push the nasal bone inward using the bone chisel. This force causes the bone fragment to break at the position marked by the red jagged line in the figure, sometimes producing a sound. When the red jagged line breaks, the bone fragment becomes completely free and can be fixed to a new position towards the midline.
