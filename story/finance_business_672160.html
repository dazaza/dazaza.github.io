50% of workplace people have procrastination!

by fvinli320 on 2012-02-22 16:00:08

Do you find yourself in such situations? You've decided to start exercising this year, but haven't started yet for various reasons; you know today is the deadline for a submission, yet you find endless excuses to do anything but put down a single word; that urgent task which needed immediate attention has been delayed due to trivial matters or just a small laziness. Psychologically, this is a condition known as "procrastination."

Industry experts from career forums introduce that "procrastination" often manifests in minor tasks, but over time, it significantly impacts the development of professionals. You know what needs to be done, but can never quite make up your mind to start.

According to investigations by Southern Television journalists, in recent years, paying attention to the psychological dynamics of professionals and the latest social phenomena has become a widely discussed topic across all social strata. A recent survey conducted by Baidu's recruitment website Bai Bai revealed that nearly 90% of professionals suffer from "procrastination." According to Bai Bai's findings, 86% of professionals admit they have "procrastination," while only 4% clearly state they do not have "procrastination."

It is evident that "procrastination," a psychological term that has existed abroad for 20 years, has also become a label many white-collar workers in China attach to themselves, turning into a common "occupational disease" in the workplace.

A TVS editorial survey shows that half of the professionals tend to procrastinate until the last moment, and more than 70% of them procrastinate frequently or consistently. Bai Bai’s investigation pointed out that 50% of professionals “won’t start working unless it’s the last minute,” another 19% “only delay for a short while,” 17% delay for “about a day,” and even 13% “won’t finish unless their superior reminds them again.”

Additionally, 43% of professionals experience "procrastination" frequently, 31% claim they "have always loved to drag things," 8% indicate "recently there are more instances of procrastination," and only 18% say "rarely, occasionally" do they procrastinate. Clearly, procrastination has become the norm in work for most professionals. Advisors from Bai Bai's Data Research Center believe this requires high vigilance and attention from both professionals and employers to avoid excessive procrastination causing losses and risks to normal work.

It is reported that over half of the professionals may procrastinate on any matter; the internet and a lazy mindset are the main culprits. According to Bai Bai's data, 54% of professionals “may procrastinate on big or small issues,” 35% usually procrastinate on “daily life trivia,” 24% procrastinate on “minor tasks like routine administrative affairs,” and notably, 10% even explicitly state they procrastinate on “major tasks like important reports or product designs.”

More than 80% of professionals with "procrastination" have less than five years of work experience, indicating that shorter work tenure and lower professionalization lead to the prevalence of "procrastination" among this group.

Bai Bai's Data Research Center believes that the harm of "procrastination" is enormous—it delays work or studies, affects emotions, disrupts team collaboration and interpersonal relationships, and can even harm physical health. It is suggested that professionals should understand the deeper reasons behind their procrastination, fully realize that their tardiness is completely unnecessary, list out the reasons for their procrastination one by one, overcome these reasons step by step, start doing things, and ultimately conquer "procrastination."
