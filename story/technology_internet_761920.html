How Facebook Uses Science to Design Emoji

by geekzhang on 2013-05-16 16:58:19

Facebook is designing more emotionalized emoticonsMay 15, Beijing time, foreign media recently published an article stating that Facebook is turning some expressions described by Charles Darwin in the 19th century into a set of emoticons, hoping to better capture a wide range of human expressions through these new emoticons.The following is the full text of the article:In 1872, Charles Darwin (CharlesDarwin) published "The Expression of the Emotions in Man and Animals", which cataloged human emotional expressions and their connections with the animal world. In this book, Darwin described more than 50 universal expressions.To date, with the help of psychologists studying facial expressions and an illustrator from Pixar Animation Studios, Facebook is transforming some expressions described by Darwin in the 19th century into a set of emoticons. Facebook aims to create new emoticons to better capture a broad spectrum of human expressions.We started this project because we were continuously paying attention to various issues people reported to Facebook, said Arturo Bejar, an engineer at Facebook. The problems related to these reports are not things Facebook needs to take action on, but they are issues people should address: What should I do when something you say makes me feel uneasy or when you post a picture I don't like?At that time, Bejar met Dacher Keltner, a psychologist at the University of California, Berkeley, who studies facial expressions and social interactions. In early 2012, Bejar invited Keltner to serve as a scientific partner for Facebook; according to Bejal, the purpose of the collaboration was to make people more kind and polite, thereby promoting more empathetic communication.They first began focusing on how research on empathy could help Facebook resolve conflicts between people; such conflicts were emerging in the reports received by Facebook. Facebook found that when people asked friends to remove photos they didn't like, if people included more emoticons in their messages, their friends were more likely to respond or comply rather than ignore the request.Therefore, Facebook began considering how to incorporate more expression information into its messaging services. When you talk face-to-face with someone, all kinds of communication happen—you see them nodding, you see them smiling, etc., but this does not occur during electronic communication," Bejal explained. One question we're trying to answer is: wouldn't it be great if we had better, scientifically-based emoticons?Over the past few months, they have been working on this, designing large cartoon emoticons called Facebook Stickers. Among the Facebook Stickers, a set of emoticons inspired by Darwin's "The Expression of the Emotions in Man and Animals" and based on empathy-related research is named Finch. This set of emoticons represents an attractive cartoon character who thinks, expresses emotions, and communicates. Matt Jones, a designer at Pixar Animation Studios, stated in a press release that he collaborated with Keltner to present the most accurate emotion-expressing emoticons.A portion of the Finch emoticon sketches drawn by Matt JonesTraditional emoticons can add a bit of emotional atmosphere to conversations conducted solely through text, but they do not provide much subtlety. For example, it is difficult to determine whether the meaning of this emoticon is disappointment or indifference, and it is also hard to judge whether $ means saying the wrong thing or spitting money out of your mouth.We may never be able to replicate the emotional atmosphere of face-to-face conversations using symbols. A study report published in 2007 about the use of emoticons in online chat activities warned that although the purpose of emoticons is to insert non-verbal expressions into text-only conversations, the meanings conveyed by emoticons may differ from real-life expressions because we must intentionally use them, unlike real-life expressions that occur spontaneously.The report pointed out: Although over time, emoticons may become habitual and less intentional when used, it remains unclear how emoticons (in computer-mediated communication) will be interpreted: as unconscious non-verbal facial expressions or as intentionally encoded elements for communicative purposes. Compared to real-life expressions, the applicability of emoticons is limited.However, Keltner believes that by integrating certain principles from Darwin's "The Expression of the Emotions in Man and Animals" with emoticons, he can add a bit more richness to existing emoticons. I have very naive ideas about emoticons because I've never sent any in my life, but I've always been watching how much important things are missing in our emotional lives," Keltner said.For instance, it is quite challenging to truly express the emotion of empathy using traditional emoticons. In Western culture, it is an underappreciated emotion," Keltner explained. We now know what that expression looks and sounds like due to scientific advancements.Facebook-designed expressions of awkwardness and empathyKeltner also hopes to accurately express the expression of awkwardness. Research has shown that blushing plays a crucial role in the emotion of awkwardness, indicating regret for crossing any social boundaries just made. When you show an awkward expression to anyone, it's like saying: sorry. This expression triggers feelings of forgiveness and trust," Keltner explained.To include more emotional data in a graphic, it means the graphic must be dynamic, just like human facial expressions. According to Lisa Feldman Barrettt, a psychology professor at Northeastern University, research findings on emotional perception indicate that people can better identify expressions on dynamic human faces compared to static ones. Additionally, the movement of the human face before and after an expression occurs can also help identify the expression.But Barrettt warned that without a broader conversation to serve as contextual reference, simply seeing an emoticon and trying to understand what emotion it conveys would be very difficult.Facebook-designed expression of gratitudeI am skeptical of the idea that people can see an expression in isolation and know what emotion it conveys, she said. Regarding expressions themselves, I think some expressions are not so clear in expressing emotions, and facial movements alone are insufficient to identify an expression.Facebook may eventually release more Finch emoticons: Keltner and Jones have designed a total of 50 emoticons, and so far Facebook has only used 16 of them.Bejal said that although Finch is a more scientific emoticon, other Sticker emoticons currently provided by Facebook can also express emotions to varying degrees. Bejal is eager to see how people will respond to these expressions, he said: I want to see people use these emoticons to communicate with others. Someday, Facebook might even launch voice functionality for the Finch emoticons.
