Illustration: A volunteer inside the tent. In the few days after I returned from Dujiangyan to Chengdu, I didn't want to do anything. Occasionally, I surfed the internet or wandered around. But I had no interest in going online. After being away from the city for more than ten days, when I suddenly turned on my computer, information came rushing at me, making me feel at a loss. When I went out for a walk, I had just come back from Shiren Park, originally wanting to find someone to chat with. There were still clusters of tents made of colorful stripes, but there was no one inside. Various ropes crisscrossed and filled the space. Occasionally, I saw one or two teacups placed nearby. Beside the park, old men and women played cards and drank tea. I stayed at a friend's house. During the day, he went to work, and I sat in the courtyard on the 8th floor looking around. It was a rare good day, the sky was as blue as washed. I listened to the noisy sounds around me, pigeons flew by, mahjong clattered, and children screamed loudly. Several families in this area have moved away. There were occasional aftershocks, and the buildings shook noticeably. In the afternoon, it was unbearably hot, and the bed was warm. I lay in an empty room, eyes closed. One by one, the photos I took, and the scenes they presented, jumped into my mind. I thought these things would be buried, but they pressed on me so urgently that I struggled to breathe. For several days after returning to Chengdu, I read martial arts novels, not wanting to sleep, eat, or talk, sitting quietly in a daze. This way of balancing has been broken. Earthquake, disaster, Wenchuan, ruins, death, crying, despair, helplessness, sadness, elderly people, children. These keywords appeared one by one, along with the faces of those people. Although I never saw the dead, the cries of the living pierced me again and again. When I chatted with them at the time, their tears flowed helplessly as they poured out their stories. I had already restrained myself at the time. But now in the city, these tears quietly welled up again. How should I narrate? I have always despised grand narratives and official rhetoric. Any premeditated propaganda always casts typical figures, which are shaped by circumstances. Every person is an individual, not a pawn to be used by the system. People are the most powerful elements in all disasters and news events. This power will not be ignored even after the disaster is over. Since I encountered many similar questions, some asked: "Why didn't you come earlier? Why didn't you go to Wenchuan and Beichuan?" Some even asked: "The disaster is over, why bother shooting them?" No explanation is needed. Just bringing a camera and ears is enough. Live among them rather than with a curious attitude. Integrate into them, get close and listen to them, including volunteers, urban residents, mountainous area victims, soldiers, etc. Where there are people, there are stories. You don't need to ask questions when chatting with people; they will tell you everything they have endured. They need to vent too much, often unable to control themselves. They are individuals with sensitive and honest emotions. Their emotions are laid bare in the face of disasters. On May 21st, I took the train from Beijing to Chengdu, and on the 23rd, I arrived in Dujiangyan, where I transported goods at the Dujiangyan material distribution center. In the following days, I went to Juyuan Middle School, the urban area of Dujiangyan, Xiang'e, Yingxiu, Hongkou, and Longchi. On the last day, I went to Mount Qingcheng, originally intending to chat with a few Taoist priests, but failed. I returned to Chengdu on June 1st. Based on my personal experiences, I will separately narrate about volunteers, logistics coordination, and what I saw and heard in various regions. These are based on my own truth.
