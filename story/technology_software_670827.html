Which symptoms during pregnancy require medical attention? SeekMedica.net

by lokes350 on 2012-02-21 11:48:41

1. Frequent vomiting: Most pregnant women experience vomiting in the early stages of pregnancy, which usually cures itself after a few weeks and is a normal physiological phenomenon. However, if there's frequent and severe vomiting where everything consumed is thrown up and not even a drop of water can be kept down, it’s important to seek medical attention early to prevent water and electrolyte imbalance that could endanger the health of both mother and child.

2. Excessive showing: If the size of the fetus doesn't match the month of pregnancy, such as appearing to be five or six months pregnant when only three or four months have passed, this often indicates twins or a possible molar pregnancy. It’s necessary to see a doctor promptly without delay.

3. Vaginal bleeding: Any occurrence of vaginal bleeding during pregnancy is abnormal. If accompanied by abdominal pain, it may indicate miscarriage, ectopic pregnancy, placental abruption, or premature birth. Early medical attention is essential.

4. Dizziness and blurred vision: If dizziness and blurred vision occur during pregnancy along with swelling, increased blood pressure, etc., it could be a sign of pregnancy-induced hypertension syndrome, requiring timely examination and treatment.

5. Severe swelling: During mid-to-late pregnancy, mild swelling in the lower limbs is common and considered normal unless accompanied by other discomforts. However, if severe swelling occurs alongside symptoms like reduced urination, dizziness, palpitations, shortness of breath, and protein in urine, immediate hospital treatment is necessary.

6. Palpitations and shortness of breath: In the later stages of pregnancy, due to the increase in fetal size, palpitations and shortness of breath may occur during strenuous physical activities, which is normal. However, if these symptoms are evident even during light activity or at rest, it might suggest the possibility of concurrent heart disease, necessitating prompt examination.

7. Full-body jaundice: If yellowing of the skin and sclera, dark tea-colored urine, nausea, vomiting, loss of appetite for greasy foods, and fatigue are noticed during pregnancy, viral hepatitis should be suspected, and early medical attention sought to prevent worsening of the condition.

8. Rubella infection or use of teratogenic drugs: If a pregnant woman is diagnosed with rubella or has had close contact with a rubella patient within the first four months of pregnancy, a full medical check-up is recommended as rubella can severely harm the fetus, leading to malformations in 30% to 50% of cases. Remedial measures such as abortion may need to be considered. Those who have used drugs like streptomycin or kanamycin in the first three months should also seek medical examination promptly.

9. Vaginal discharge of water: If vaginal discharge of water occurs before the expected delivery date, it may indicate premature rupture of membranes. Immediate hospitalization is required to prevent difficult labor and reduce threats to the fetus.
