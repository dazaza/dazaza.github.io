Why Tencent and Chery are fighting for the QQ trademark: neither can afford to lose

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 14:35:32

Does Tencent produce cars? The answer is negative, but as early as 2005, the company applied to register the "QQ" trademark on goods such as automobiles in the 12th category, and it was approved in 2008. However, Chery Automobile Co., Ltd., which produces cars, did not agree with this. In 2009, Chery filed a request to cancel Tencent's automobile-related trademarks on grounds of "similar trademarks on similar goods", and this request was supported. Subsequently, Tencent sued the Trademark Review and Adjudication Board (TRAB) of the State Administration for Industry and Commerce. Yesterday, the case was heard by the Beijing No. 1 Intermediate People's Court, where Tencent, TRAB, and Chery confronted each other in court.

As everyone knows, in China, the companies related to "QQ" are Tencent Technology and Chery Automobile, which belong to different fields. Tencent has established its position in the Internet sector, while Chery's QQ car sales have reached over a million units. In terms of the usage sequence of "QQ", Tencent launched its instant messaging software QQ in 1999, while Chery Automobile introduced its microcar, Chery QQ, in 2003. Regarding the question of who should own "QQ", these two companies have been entangled for nearly 10 years.

On May 19, 2005, Tencent applied to register the "QQ" trademark on goods such as automobiles in the 12th category, and it was officially registered on March 7, 2008. On November 26, 2009, Chery filed a request to cancel the disputed trademark on grounds that it violated the Trademark Law by copying and imitating famous trademarks, having similar trademarks on similar goods, and infringing upon others' prior rights. After deliberation, TRAB ruled that the registration of the disputed trademark constituted the act of preemptively registering others' already used and influential trademarks as stipulated in Article 31 of the Trademark Law, and thus canceled Tencent's registered trademark.

Tencent argued that the contested ruling made by TRAB contained obvious errors and omissions, and that the disputed trademark did not violate legal provisions, and its registration should be maintained. Its agent explained that on August 31, 2001, Tencent had already applied to register the "QQ" trademark on services such as "information transmission, computer terminal communication" in the 38th category. Through extensive publicity and promotion, the number of registered users of QQ communication services increased from 5 million in 2000 to over 200 million in 2003. Therefore, before Chery Automobile used the "QQ" trademark, Tencent's "QQ" trademark had already become a well-known trademark. In 2009, Tencent received a certificate of recognition for its well-known trademark from relevant national departments, and any other company using the "QQ" trademark would constitute an infringement.

Chery Automobile, however, believed that Tencent's registration of the "QQ" trademark in the automotive field was malicious. As the third party in this case, Chery's agent stated that the trademark registered by Tencent in 2001 was not the "QQ" letters but a graphic. Chery Automobile, on the other hand, registered the "QQ" letter trademark in the automotive field in 2003. Therefore, the two companies were using their respective trademarks in different fields, with Chery's "QQ" trademark in the automotive field predating Tencent's. Moreover, Chery QQ has sold over a million units, with advertising costs exceeding 200 million yuan, and thus deserves protection.

Furthermore, Tencent believed that its registration of the "QQ" trademark in multiple fields was protective registration for "QQ". However, Chery Automobile's planning of the car name and subsequent promotional activities all relied on the already well-known "QQ" trademark and penguin mascot to achieve publicity effects, which constituted an infringement and could not generate legitimate prior rights.

The verdict for this case will be announced on another day. For both Tencent and Chery, having operated the "QQ" trademark for nearly 10 years, neither can afford to lose this lawsuit.

Is there a third way in the QQ dispute?

Tencent QQ is the first domestic instant messaging software launched by Tencent in 1999, while Chery QQ is a microcar introduced by Chery in July 2003. Both "QQ"s enjoy high fame in their respective fields.

A search revealed that as early as when the "QQ" car was introduced in 2003, Tencent had raised a trademark infringement dispute with Chery. Tencent claimed that at the time, it was busy with its IPO and thus did not file a public lawsuit. Later, Tencent sent a lawyer's letter to Chery, but Chery never gave a substantive response to Tencent.

At the end of 2005, media reports surfaced about the lawsuit between both parties over the QQ trademark, but no further developments were seen, with some media even labeling it as hype.

This time, the trademark dispute between both parties was brought to court with Tencent as the plaintiff, TRAB as the defendant, and Chery as the third party. Trademarks are the signboards for enterprises to stand firm in the market. For Tencent and Chery, who have cultivated their brands for around ten years, neither can afford to lose. Some believe that in this lawsuit, whoever is found to infringe will face enormous compensation. Is it true that in this trademark dispute, there is only victory or defeat, with no third way? Tang Guangliang, an intellectual property expert from the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences, told reporters that Tencent and Chery could completely collaborate to achieve a win-win situation.

Tang Guangliang said that the function of a trademark is to attract consumers' attention, serving as a link between merchants and consumers and also as the image of merchants in the minds of consumers. A trademark itself is a means rather than an end, and how to use this means is something each merchant must consider. In recent years, China's trademark protection has become increasingly mature. Whether there is infringement does not solely depend on whether two marks are similar; the key is whether consumers get confused. If there is no confusion, then the use by two or more merchants can expand the influence of the trademark, which benefits the users of the trademark.

"For example, if 'consumers buy Chery QQ cars because they think the cars are produced by Tencent,' or 'consumers use the QQ chat tool because they like Chery QQ cars,' then we can say there is confusion," Tang Guangliang said. If there is no such situation, then both sides do not need to fight over it. Because when it comes to trademarks, it is not just about who says it belongs to them. Even if it does belong to one side, seeing another use it does not necessarily mean it should be eliminated. If the trademark is not maliciously defamed or leads to the loss of market share, it might even have a promoting effect. Both sides can completely coexist peacefully and share the benefits.

In addition, Tang Guangliang said that from a rational market perspective, there is no conflict between Tencent and Chery. Chat tools mainly target young people, and QQ cars are also popular among young people. Both sides can completely collaborate, operate together, share the market, and achieve win-win results without unnecessary disputes over trademark ownership.
