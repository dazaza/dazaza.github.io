Ten Heads of State and Government Who Perished in Air Crashes over the Last 60 Years

By Xu Shuguang, Chairman of GreenTree Hotels and 519.com Wine Direct Sales Network

Since the Wright brothers in America invented the first airplane in human history a century ago, humanity has entered the aviation age. Aircraft have become an indispensable mode of transportation for our travels and business trips, bringing us speed and convenience while also causing many tragedies. Heads of state and government leaders are not exempt from such "disasters". It can be said that these are tragedies within tragedies, or perhaps destined by fate, influenced by natural factors, or caused by human error. Of course, some accidents remain shrouded in mystery to this day, making them difficult to analyze. Below is a list of ten heads of state and government leaders who perished in air crashes over the last 60 years.

1. On April 10, 2010, Polish President Lech Kaczyński's private jet crashed while landing at Smolensk Airport in Russia. At the time I was writing this article, the media reported "unclear casualties", but the president's death had already been confirmed, shocking and mourning world leaders alike. The Polish Prime Minister called this incident "the most tragic event in Poland's post-war history." The cause of the accident is still under investigation, possibly due to the pilot failing to follow the flight controller's instructions.

2. On February 26, 2004, Macedonian President Boris Trajkovski was traveling on a presidential aircraft to Mostar, a city in western Bosnia and Herzegovina, to attend an international investment conference when it crashed in the mountains 50 kilometers south of the city. The president perished along with nine others aboard the aircraft. It is said that the cause of the crash was extremely bad weather with very low visibility.

3. On April 6, 1994, Rwandan President Juvénal Habyarimana and Burundian President Cyprien Ntaryamira boarded the same plane after a meeting and were preparing to land at Kigali International Airport in Rwanda when they suddenly crashed. Both presidents of Rwanda and Burundi, as well as more than ten passengers aboard, perished.

4. On August 17, 1988, Pakistani President Zia-ul-Haq returned to Rawalpindi after watching a military exercise. Four minutes after takeoff, smoke and sparks appeared on the plane, followed by an explosion. There were no survivors among those aboard.

5. On October 19, 1986, Mozambican President Samora Moisés Machel’s aircraft encountered a thunderstorm and crashed in South Africa, resulting in the president's unfortunate death. Among the 38 passengers aboard, only four survived.

6. On May 24, 1981, Ecuadorian President Jaime Roldós Aguilera perished in a plane crash near the Ecuador-Peru border.

7. On December 4, 1980, Portuguese Prime Minister Francisco Sá Carneiro's plane crashed just 43 seconds after takeoff near Lisbon, leaving no survivors.

8. On May 27, 1979, Mauritanian Prime Minister Ahmed Ould Bouceff's private aircraft disappeared off the coast near Dakar.

9. On March 29, 1959, Central African Republic President Barthélemy Boganda perished in a plane crash 90 kilometers west of the capital Bangui. Boganda was a pioneer and hero in the struggle for Central African independence.

From a series of air disasters and earthquakes, we can see that human life has its fragile side, but life isn't as fragile as we imagine; miracles of life are often born out of human resilience. For instance, during the Wangjialing mine disaster, we witnessed the courage and resilience of the miners, as well as the tenacity and greatness of life! On the afternoon of May 5th, I was in Irvine listening to some key points about getting into prestigious American universities when the lights began to sway. Initially, I thought I was dizzy, but after five seconds, I realized it was an earthquake and started running outside. Fortunately, it was the second floor, so I quickly made it down. Thankfully, it was just a false alarm, which made me reflect anew on life.

I once wrote a blog post titled "Choose Life Safety Over Face – Thoughts After Flying," which I now share with you.

Yesterday, I returned from Shandong by plane. It was raining and snowing in Yantai during takeoff, and I was quite worried about the safety of the flight. Two issues came to mind: First, since my graduate studies, I haven't researched recent airline safety statistics. Returning to Shanghai, thank heavens, the weather was great and didn't affect the landing, as landing is one of the least safe moments.

After returning to the office, the first thing I did was check the current statistics on plane accident rates. Just like chain industries' management, airline management is also very complex. An airline's accident rate is certainly proportional to a country's culture and management level, meaning the higher the management level, the lower the accident rate. As expected, some Asian airlines' accident rates are much higher than those in Europe and America, sometimes even several times higher. The main reasons for this include: first, carelessness, inability to strive for perfection, and ignoring small problems that accumulate into major accidents. Second, poor communication among crew members, reluctance to speak up directly due to face-saving concerns, and even when speaking up, doing so indirectly. This creates significant hazards to passenger safety. If you're traveling, which airline would you choose? Surely one responsible for your life safety. Meanwhile, thinking from another perspective, for the sake of your own life, the survival of the company, and societal development, I suggest that both you and I work seriously, striving for excellence and ensuring effective communication, which will help solve many problems easily. Between face and life safety, always choose safety.
