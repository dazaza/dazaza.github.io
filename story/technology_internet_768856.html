Google's self-driving car project: It all started with pizza delivery

by anonymous on 2013-11-25 11:35:53

After conducting 500,000 miles of driving tests, the Google self-driving car team this week gave New Yorker reporter Burkhard Bilger a rare opportunity to delve deeply into and report on the project. The adventures of Google's self-driving cars include delivering pizzas, treasure hunting, and avoiding deer.

It is still unknown whether the grand concept of self-driving cars will work — the most optimistic estimate is that they will enter the market in 5 to 10 years.

However, self-driving cars are part of Google's product roadmap, as it aims to turn science fiction into reality. Therefore, the evolution of such products is worth looking forward to.

The main character of Bilger's report is 33-year-old engineer Anthony Levandowski. He joined Google after developing an autonomous motorcycle for the DARPA Grand Challenge, an unmanned vehicle competition hosted by the U.S. Department of Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency. After working on the Google Street View project for a long time, Levandowski and the more famous inventor and professor Sebastian Thrun received permission from Google co-founders Larry Page and Sergey Brin to start building self-driving cars.

Trial Process

Thrun and others initially believed that self-driving cars could not navigate surface streets because there were too many variables. So what prompted them to change their minds and begin testing? It was a strange idea from a TV producer.

Bilger reported that "In February 2008, Levandowski received a call from the producer of Discovery Channel's series 'Prototype This.' The producer asked if he would be interested in developing an unmanned pizza delivery car. He and a group of graduates from UC Berkeley and other engineers successfully transformed a Prius into such a car within five weeks. They integrated a guidance system into the car and convinced the California Highway Patrol to allow the car to cross the Bay Bridge — from San Francisco to Treasure Island."

According to Thrun, successfully crossing the Bay Bridge prompted Page and Brin to allow them to continue testing for the next few months.

At the time, the two co-founders of Google "were like kids playing a treasure hunt game," giving the self-driving car team 10,100 miles of travel. "The roads meandered through every part of the Bay Area, from the lush streets of Menlo Park to the zigzagging Lombard Street. If the driver touched the steering wheel or the brakes even once, the trip would be canceled." The team completed all the trips within a year and a half.

Next Stop?

Bilger also reported more detailed content, but the real question lies in where the next stop for self-driving cars is? After Google's treasure-hunting phase ended, self-driving cars still face challenges on multiple levels.

1. Overcoming legal obstacles.

2. Figuring out how to bring those cars to market, as car manufacturers are highly resistant to the term "self-driving" (although they can all accept small, refined changes in their products that help drivers with machine intelligence technology).

3. Achieving further technological leaps in sensors and machine learning. Although statistically self-driving cars are safer, any mistake could negatively impact the entire effort.

As Bilger reported, "For example, the car has trouble in rainy weather; laser devices reflect off shiny surfaces. (Raindrops prompt a small cloud icon to appear on the screen, accompanied by a voice warning that it is about to exit autonomous mode.) It cannot distinguish between plastic concrete and hardened concrete, nor between soft asphalt and dry asphalt. It cannot hear the whistle of traffic police or follow their hand signals."

In the meantime, Google's self-driving cars are typically smarter than humans. For example, it can brake earlier based on traffic data regarding deceleration of vehicles ahead, and at night, it can sense deer crossing the road on wooded highways, significantly reducing speed.

Bilger said that improvements to the car are coming. "At a tech conference I attended, Levandowski showed the team Google's new laser scanner to be put into use within a year. Its detection range is several times that of the old model — increasing from 260 feet to 1,100 feet, and its resolution has improved by 30 times. It can identify metal plates less than two inches thick from 300 feet away. He told me that the size of the laser scanner will be roughly that of a coffee cup, and the cost will be about $10,000 — $7,000 less than the current model."
