Record 7.4 million Lumia sales still bad news for Nokia?

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 12:06:46

According to the latest financial report, Nokia sold 7.4 million Lumia smartphones in the second quarter of this year, a significant increase compared to 5.6 million units in Q1 and 4.4 million in Q4 of last year. This improvement might allow CEO Stephen Elop to breathe a sigh of relief.

However, a closer look at the data reveals that only 500,000 Lumia phones were sold in North America, an increase of 100,000 units from Q1's 400,000 but still representing a 17% year-over-year decline. Additionally, the average selling price of Lumia phones dropped by 20% compared to the previous quarter. Forbes editor Mark Rogowsky believes Nokia has become a low-end smartphone manufacturer. Furthermore, he raised the following points:

Currently, Windows Phone (WP) market share is largely supported by inexpensive smartphones. If Apple releases a low-cost iPhone, WP devices will face even tougher competition. More critically, WP is disappearing from the U.S. and parts of Western Europe, where most mobile app developers are concentrated. Developers often choose platforms based on the smartphone market share in their regions. The continued prosperity of third-party app development for Apple is due to iOS maintaining a 40% market share in the U.S., despite Android dominating globally. This means the situation for third-party apps on WP could worsen.

CNET editor Roger Cheng also expressed concerns about the future of Lumia phones:

The low sales of Lumia in North America indicate its brand influence remains weak in the region. However, Nokia’s decision to launch the Lumia 1020 in New York shows the company still values the North American market. It is puzzling why AT&T was chosen as the exclusive carrier for the Lumia 1020, despite repeated explanations from Elop. Popular devices like the iPhone 5, Galaxy S4, and HTC One are sold through multiple carriers. Moreover, when the best device is locked with AT&T, how motivated would Verizon and T-Mobile be to sell their Lumia phones?

I am optimistic about the future of Lumia phones, and believe the mid-to-low-end market could be the entry point for Nokia to re-enter the top tier of smartphone manufacturers:

There is a huge demand for mid-to-low-end smartphones in emerging markets. Not only Nokia, but also Samsung and other manufacturers are shifting focus from the high-end to the mid-to-low-end segments. For instance, the Lumia 520, priced at $150, had a WP market share of only 4.4% in May, which increased to 8.4% in June, and now stands at 12%, making it the most popular WP phone. Nokia's ability to achieve 7.4 million Lumia sales owes much to its mid-to-low-end models. Mid-to-low-end Lumia phones have lower purchase barriers, making it easier to expand market share. Expanding smartphone market share can enhance the brand influence of Lumia, potentially boosting sales of high-end Lumia phones. Nokia's primary task is to reduce losses. Although profits in the mid-to-low-end market are relatively lower, they add up over time. Attacking the mid-to-low-end market aggressively outside the fiercely competitive high-end segment is a sound strategy to compete with companies like Samsung and Apple.
