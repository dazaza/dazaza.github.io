My classmate who became a mistress

Rongrong was my classmate in junior high school. I knew very early on that she was being kept by someone as a mistress, so I wasn't particularly close with her. However, since we lived in the same city and were from the same village, we still occasionally interacted. If it weren't for the year I was so destitute that I had to borrow money from her, perhaps we would still be in touch. But during that time, she deeply hurt me.

At that time, I had spent all my money and couldn't find a job. Hungry and in a terrible mood, I went to Rongrong's place and mustered the courage to ask her for help. Rongrong didn't refuse, and I gratefully took the hundred yuan she gave me. Not knowing what to say, Rongrong suggested we go buy groceries together. Her place was much better than mine; there was a market right downstairs. At a meat stall, Rongrong bought twenty yuan worth of pork. When it came time to pay, I thought she would handle it, but instead, she gestured for me to pay. So, I handed over the hundred yuan I had just borrowed.

Not long after, I finally found a job that paid three hundred yuan a month. As soon as I received my salary, I quickly returned the money to Rongrong. I gave her the hundred yuan, expecting her to decline politely, but she readily accepted it without hesitation. From then on, I rarely contacted her again.

Time flew by, and before I knew it, more than ten years had passed. I had married, had children, and through my own efforts, owned a car and a house. One day, when I returned to my hometown, I met one of Rongrong's neighbors, who told me about Rongrong's past.

When Rongrong's father passed away, her youngest brother, who was born after years of avoiding family planning policies, was only one year old. Her oldest sister was twelve, and there were two other sisters aged ten and seven. The slim Rongrong became the pillar of the family. Her father suddenly passed away in April, and during the wheat harvest in May, Rongrong and her mother worked hard all day to finish cutting the wheat. By the time they loaded the cart, it was already late at night. Other families had long finished their work and gone home. Despite their best efforts, the mother and daughter couldn't pull the heavy cart out of the wheat field. In despair, they cried together in the wheat field. Neighbors, seeing how difficult their lives were, arranged a marriage for the good-looking Rongrong during the busy wheat season, helping her family get through this exhausting period.

Having a partner meant less worry about physical labor, but as summer was coming to an end, all three of her sisters needed money for school. The man could help with some work, but he didn't have the means to cover the costs of schooling for all her sisters. Out of necessity, Rongrong decided to work. In those days, finding work wasn't easy. Soon, Rongrong fell into bad company. After working in a dance hall for a year, she met a man in his thirties or forties whose child was already in elementary school. He was ugly, less than 160 cm tall, and his legs didn’t even reach the ground when riding a motorcycle. However, he had some money—not a lot, but he seemed reliable and not too slick. Rongrong decided to depend on him, leaving the dance hall behind. Thanks to him, her siblings' tuition and her family's living expenses were taken care of, and she no longer had to resort to disreputable means to make a living. At the time, it seemed like a reasonable choice.

Rongrong’s older sister, only two years younger, had graduated and soon also started working. Under Rongrong’s care, her path went smoothly. She quickly found a boyfriend who worked for the government, had a stable income, and was a good person. They got married after a year and lived happily ever after. However, once married, Rongrong’s sister stopped contributing any money to the family. Meanwhile, Rongrong continued to bear the burden of supporting the family. Their mother urged Rongrong’s sister to contribute, but it was useless—she always claimed she had no money. Despite this, Rongrong never complained. In fact, when her sister needed money to buy a house, Rongrong even gave her some.

During one of Rongrong's visits home, her grandmother, who was relatively well-off and skilled in small business, actually asked Rongrong to repay a debt. She said that Rongrong’s father had borrowed five hundred yuan from her while he was alive, and now that he was gone, it was up to his daughter to repay it. This shocked Rongrong to the point where she almost fainted.

Listening to Rongrong's neighbor recount these stories changed my perception of her. Indeed, her stinginess was something several of us fellow villagers had noticed. Now, I realized that her stinginess was directed towards outsiders, but when it came to her family, she was absolutely selfless and forgiving.

After returning to the city, I drove with my daughter to see Rongrong, whom I hadn't seen for more than ten years. Surprisingly, in this rapidly changing city, Rongrong was still renting the same apartment. What used to seem spacious and beautiful to me now appeared old and cramped. The man she depended on was still the short man. She had given birth to a boy for him, who was now seven years old, but she still hadn't gained any official status. His wife was still in her original position, though wrinkles had begun to appear on her forehead. Rongrong remained a third party, a person without title, and in others’ eyes, a morally corrupt mistress. But I no longer looked down on her. I know she doesn't like that man either. I know she wants to find a handsome man she likes and live a good life. But for some people with disadvantaged backgrounds, walking the path they desire is incredibly difficult. To outsiders, I may seem to have it much better than Rongrong, but is doing business really my wish? No. From childhood, I loved painting. But if I made a living from painting instead of making money through business, my father would have to endure pain and bow his head to pull a cart on muddy rural roads, and my mother would forever struggle in poverty. My siblings would never have a chance to rise above their circumstances. So, even though I didn't want to, I had to do it.

Maslow said: human survival is divided into five levels—the first level is survival, the second is safety, the third is social interaction, the fourth is respect, and the fifth is self-actualization. The leap from the first level to the fifth is enormous. Given the current stage of social development, for us, living according to our own wishes is unrealistic.
