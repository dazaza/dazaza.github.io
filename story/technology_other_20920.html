Can "children's checks" bear the responsibility of children's integrity?

by chengshangdao on 2007-05-31 13:38:42

“I'll write you a check!” These words did not come from an adult, but from an elementary school student. The children's "generosity and magnanimity" is not about money, but about promises. Nowadays, the way children make promises to each other is no longer "hooking pinkies and jumping, never changing for a hundred years," but rather writing "promise checks" from the "bank."

"Children's Checks" Bestsellers

A reporter at a certain bookstore's children's literature section in Yulin saw that these "children's checks" are actually small booklets the size of a passbook, with cartoon patterns printed on the cover. Based on the content of the agreement and the recipient, they are divided into series such as "citizen checks," "school checks," "friend checks," and "family checks." These respectively use things that students, friends, and family members should do, such as "I promise to get up on time tomorrow" or "I promise to give my seat to those in need on the bus," as the basis for making some "checks" for children to fill out. Each page of the check is divided into a stub and a ticket, listing items such as the recipient, validity period, payment content, and issuer's signature. The bookstore staff told the reporter: "This kind of book encourages children to keep their promises through the form of paying checks, and it is quite popular among children. Overall, the 'children's checks' sell pretty well, and there are many versions."

"Children's Checks" Test Children's Integrity

The reason why "children's checks" have become popular is because they cater to the playful and curious nature of elementary school students. In the classroom, the contents of the "check" promises are varied: "I promise not to forget you after graduation," "I promise not to fool around with you," etc. Although "children's checks" are just a game, they can test the integrity of students.

Mrs. Liu, who once bought "children's checks" for her child, told the reporter that not long ago her son gave her a "check," promising to raise his math score above 90 points. As her son's grades gradually improved, she began to realize that this "children's check" could cultivate her son's sense of keeping promises, achieving what pure preaching could not. To make learning fun, Mrs. Liu also started "writing checks" with her son, fulfilling all the promises she made to him, such as going on weekend outings. Influenced by his mother, her son was happy to take the "check" as a commitment letter: getting along well with neighbors, spending no more than one hour online every day, not throwing clothes around everywhere. Mrs. Liu told the reporter: "One 'check' after another has prompted him to develop good habits."

When Integrity Becomes a "Game"

Can a little "checkbook" really bear the responsibility of cultivating a child's integrity? While "children's checks" have won the approval of some parents, they have also faced a lot of doubts.

In Guding Primary School, when the reporter interviewed the parents of students, most parents believed that "children's checks" are just a disguised version of a pledge, "changing the soup but not the medicine," without any substantial effect. Some parents also believe that relying on "writing checks" to keep one's word has the suspicion of turning integrity into checks and money, and they worry that children will casually "write checks" to classmates and friends, making promises here and there, but only writing them down carelessly without actually doing anything. Once such "promises" happen too often, integrity becomes a game.

Mr. Huang helplessly recounted some incidents: for a while, he found that his daughter was staying up late doing homework. After asking, he learned that his daughter had broken a classmate's pen and thus promised to copy the text for that classmate in the form of a "check." When Mr. Huang mentioned this incident to his colleagues, surprisingly, several parents said they had similar experiences. One colleague even discovered that his son's notebook contained a note written by a girl saying, "To thank you for giving me candy, I promise to be your girlfriend." Some children's promises were quite novel, such as "Tonight we will compete online until death," "Whoever dares to bother you, I will stand up for you," etc. Many parents worried that under the guise of "fulfilling promises," children would make mistakes or go too far, thinking that they were being "honest" and feeling proud of themselves, ultimately becoming unable to distinguish right from wrong.

Some parents said that as a game, playing with "children's checks" moderately does no harm, but thinking that this alone can cultivate a child's sense of integrity is like looking for a sword by marking the boat. For parents and schools, the important thing is to teach children to understand the true meaning of a promise; as for the carrier of the promise, it is not the key.

Positive Guidance, "Teaching Through Fun"

Relevant experts from the Yulin Education Bureau believe that although "children's checks" are not a bad way to help children establish a sense of integrity, "integrity" as a character trait cannot be cultivated overnight. It is understandable for elementary school students to play this game, but attention needs to be paid to guidance. Integrity itself is a basic quality, and children should not be misled into thinking that integrity requires an intermediary to supervise. As elders, we should teach children according to their aptitude, positively guide them, and "teach through fun." Parents should set an example, create an atmosphere of integrity within the family, and serve as role models with good behavior, subtly cultivating the child's sense of integrity. In addition, parents can also guide children to learn financial management knowledge during games, letting them understand the relationship between money and life.
