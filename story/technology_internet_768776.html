"Hatching Twitter" Chapter One: The Unlikely Start of Friendship Among the Four Founders

by anonymous on 2013-11-22 11:50:26

The Chinese version of the new book "Hatching Twitter" has been published by Zhanlu Culture.

On the first day of Twitter's IPO, as the co-founder and largest shareholder of the company, 41-year-old Evan Williams had a net worth exceeding $3.2 billion.

Few people know that at the age of 25, this boy who grew up on a farm was actually destitute, with his head full of idealism.

In the new book "Hatching Twitter" by Nick Bilton, a technology journalist for The New York Times, Evan is the first character to appear. However, the scene at that time was embarrassing, "Evan grabbed the black wastebasket in the corner of the office and trembled uncontrollably, his palms sweating cold sweat."

Such descriptions let this book, which records the growth history of Twitter, abandon the beautiful illusion that a high market value tech company might bring from the very first chapter.

In the subtitle of the book, the author's writing intention is clear and explicit: telling a true story about money, power, friendship, and betrayal.

This story happened among the four founders of Twitter. A farm boy who became famous for creating and selling Blogger, an entrepreneurial youth who yearned to change the world with coding, a tattooed hacker who was shy and full of strange ideas, and a diplomat who loved to joke around and make friends laugh.

They are Evan Williams, Noah Glass, Jack Dorsey, and Biz Stone. Four big boys with different backgrounds met each other through various accidental encounters and gradually entered a startup company called Odeo.

Evan was the investor and CEO of the company, Noah was the founder, Jack was a hired programmer, and Biz gave up Google stocks worth $2 million to join this dirty startup as an ordinary programmer.

In their respective youthful times, what kind of personalities did these four people have, and how did they meet? The first chapter of "Hatching Twitter" tells the beginning of this wild era. Below is an excerpt from the book:

@Evan, click the button and make it public to the world

Evan Williams, a farm boy who became famous for creating and selling Blogger

It was 1997, right in the middle of the contemporary gold rush and the tech boom. At that time, Evan was already 25 years old, penniless, and full of idealism. He only found a job writing marketing materials for a media company named O'Reilly.

He was very shy, awkward, and difficult to adapt socially. In contrast, he preferred spending hours alone in thought.

Evan always had peculiar entrepreneurial plans, especially when he was still in Nebraska, although most of them were never implemented.

After teaching himself to write code, he began working at Intel and HP. Until he met a lively programmer, a girl named Meg, and together they started a company called Pyra Labs.

To help Pyra Lab's staff track their work progress, Evan and an employee built a simple internal diary website.

During one week in the summer of 1999, while Meg was on vacation, Evan released the diary website to the world. He called it Blogger — using a word that didn't exist at the time. He believed it would allow people without any computer programming knowledge to create weblogs or blogs.

Later, the five-person team of Pyra Labs disbanded, leaving Evan alone to run the company set up outside his living room.

He had a good vision for Blogger. He believed that in the future, everyone would have their own blog — equivalent to having their own online newspaper.

By 2002, Blogger had nearly 1 million users worldwide and close to 90 million blogs — a quite impressive number at the time.

@Noah, longing to change the world with coding

Noah Glass, an entrepreneurial youth longing to change the world with coding

Noah first saw Evan in Forbes magazine. He saw Evan smiling in front of a computer with a bright yellow Blogger label stuck in the lower corner of the screen. In the distance, behind the smiling Evan, there was a kitchen visible through a window. And Noah was sitting right there in that kitchen.

"Wow wow wow, my goodness!" Noah exclaimed loudly, a radiant smile on his face. He stood there for a moment, dazed between the photo and reality.

He quickly opened the back door of the kitchen and rushed out onto the balcony. "Hey, Blogger!" Noah shouted. Evan turned around, slightly startled by the sudden shout. "Are you Evan Williams from Blogger?" Noah asked, enthusiastically introducing himself, "I'm Noah, Noah Glass." It turned out they had been neighbors all along.

Noah's hometown was Santa Cruz, Northern California, where he was born in a small, dilapidated house next to an even more decrepit barn — a place where hippies used to gather. His mother and other neighbors made ends meet by handcrafting candles and other small trinkets.

Although Evan was reserved and quiet, he was attracted by Noah's lively and outgoing personality, and they became good friends.

As their friendship deepened, Evan confided in Noah that Google had approached him wanting to buy Blogger, potentially for millions of dollars.

Noah was proud and excited about the news. This meant that Evan, who was often so poor that he couldn't afford enough food, would soon become wealthy and no longer have to worry about basic sustenance.

Then, on February 15, 2003, Evan received the call. Evan Williams had succeeded! Countless dollars existed in the form of ones and zeros.

And Noah began planning his own new venture, a company called "Odeo." It was a voice blogging service similar to music, allowing anyone to easily create and share content, and download it to an Apple iPod player.

Noah even convinced Evan, who already had plenty of money, to invest in the company. Later, to secure more investment, Noah agreed to Evan's condition — that Evan must serve as the company's CEO.

Noah clasped his hands together, feeling heavy-hearted. He had no choice but to agree to trade the position of CEO of Odeo for $200,000 in investment and the keys to the old apartment he had seen in Forbes magazine.

@Jack, living "behind the scenes"

Jack Dorsey, a shy and imaginative tattooed hacker

Few people noticed this 28-year-old man who sat day after day by the window of a café on the corner.

To many, he was invisible. He was born with a speech impediment. When people asked his name, he said "Jah" instead of "Jack Dorsey." Although he overcame his speech problem through therapy, it left an indelible mark on his communication skills.

His speech impediment also helped him find a friend: an IBM low-end personal computer that entered his home when he was eight years old. He quickly fell in love with the monochrome display of the computer and learned to speak to it in code.

Jack had a silver nose ring in his nostril, and he liked it. Later, he left a company because the boss wouldn't let him work with the nose ring.

One afternoon in 2005, as he sat there gazing at the sparsely populated park, he felt particularly down. His life in San Francisco wasn't what he truly wanted. He desperately hoped to work for a real emerging company, but he didn't have that kind of luck.

As he sat calculating how he could escape this dead-end job, he noticed a familiar face walking past the window. That person was Evan, about whom there were many stories on the internet regarding his sale of a multi-million-dollar company.

Jack quickly put together a resume and sent it to Evan, informing him that he had just seen him at the coffee shop. Subsequently, Jack got the opportunity to interview at Odeo. The interviewers were Evan and Noah. It went smoothly, and Jack joined Odeo.

Jack almost immediately became a freelancer and fit perfectly into Odeo's culture. He was a computer enthusiast without a diploma but passionate about programming. He established solid professional principles for himself, ensuring speed and accuracy for every task he undertook.

@Biz, leaving Google for Odeo

Biz Stone, a diplomat who loved to joke around and make friends laugh

Early October 2005, Biz Stone sat in a small meeting room with the bosses of Google.

"Biz, you need to realize that if you leave now, you'll be giving up all your preemptive rights, worth over $2 million," Biz grinned broadly and said, "I'm leaving."

Biz wasn't rich. His upbringing: a poor child growing up among the rich.

However, such experiences did not make him a reclusive man but rather a "clown" who brought joy to others. Throughout his life, he entertained his mother and sister with jokes to make them feel better after his father's drunken rages.

Biz's sense of humor helped both his career and social activities. Regardless, jokes can also be used to avoid conflicts, especially in the workplace.

In the summer of 2003, Evan had already worked at Google for a few months, trying to establish himself in this large company. Biz learned about Evan's "one-click publishing" idea and wanted to help him spread the concept of blogging. Eventually, Evan helped him secure a job at Google.

In 2004, after Evan left Google, Biz was deeply saddened because his new bosses didn't trust or respect him. So, in 2005, he believed he had accumulated enough and wanted to follow Evan into a new project.

Because Biz and Evan's friendship had grown so close over the past two years, he ultimately handed in his white Google ID card, gave up a large sum of money, just to exchange it for the freedom to start a new life and came to Odeo.

Amidst chaos and grime, Biz began his new work alongside his former boss and good friend, along with a group of homeless individuals.
