Kneeling bunker shot training method, avoid taking too much sand... Common problems beginners often make:

1. If you frequently hit the ground hard behind the ball, lifting up a large patch of grass, if at the driving range, you'll hear a dull thud followed by soreness and numbness in your arms, this indicates that your weight is too far back, meaning during the downswing your weight remains on your right foot (opposite for left-handers) rather than shifting to your left foot. Weight transfer is fundamental to ensuring a smooth swing.

2. If your shots are inconsistent, sometimes good sometimes bad (usually more bad), then your head movement is too exaggerated, either looking up too early or deliberately lowering your head or moving it sideways during the downswing. Excessive head movement causes you to miss the sweet spot. Keep both eyes on the ball throughout the swing, chin pointed toward the ball, don't rush to see where the ball has gone; only lift your head during the follow-through – this is what's commonly referred to as "never look up."

3. If your ball consistently flies straight to the right or left, this means your clubface was pointing in that direction at impact. Check if your grip is correct, whether the clubface is parallel to the target line at address, and if you're pulling back instead of following through correctly during the downswing. Fix any issues, which should be relatively easy.

4. Slices or hooks are more complicated. If the ball doesn't fly straight but turns midway, this indicates your swing path is incorrect. Start checking from your setup and aim. If you have a coach, it's best to consult them, as it's usually difficult to grasp the essence of swing path just from written instructions.

5. If the ball never gets airborne, either missing the ball entirely or topping it causing it to roll along the ground, the problem lies in lifting your left arm during the downswing or looking up too early. Another reason could be an unstable lower body, standing up before making contact. We should maintain our height, with knees still bent.

6. Another point worth mentioning is that many beginners tend to hit the ball with excessive force. Golf is a leisurely sport, not requiring intense physical exertion, and it doesn't need brute strength. Hitting too forcefully disrupts rhythm, preventing proper timing and losing the true essence of golf. Try to slow down your movements; you play your best when you feel relaxed hitting the ball. This is the often-heard phrase "never force it."
