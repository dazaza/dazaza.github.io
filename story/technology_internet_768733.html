Who can be the fourth mobile operating system?

by anonymous on 2013-11-20 11:56:20

When it comes to smartphones, the first things that come to mind are definitely Apple's iPhone or Google's Android, and perhaps Microsoft’s Windows Phone. To date, 80% of the market share has been captured by Android products, with iOS holding around 13%, Windows Phone occupying only about 3.6%, and BlackBerry, the fallen aristocrat, retaining a mere 1.7% of the market share. Based on the current market situation, there is no doubt that Android's dominance and iOS's lead will continue in the next two years. Windows Phone may see a slight increase, while BlackBerry could continue to decline.

Clearly, in the mobile intelligent system domain, there is never a shortage of stories where "newcomers replace old ones." From the decline of Symbian OS and Palm OS to the rise of Android and iOS, the landscape has been completely overturned in just a few short years. So, what emerging operating systems are currently available in the market? What is their development and future outlook like? Below, we will take a look at these four fringe systems: Sailfish, Firefox OS, Tizen, and Ubuntu Touch, to see which one has the potential to capture the remaining 2% or more of the market share and become the fourth largest mobile operating system.

1. Sailfish

Representative model: Jolla

Market prospects: ★

Sailfish's background is somewhat tragic. Its development team consists of former MeeGo developers from Finland who were abandoned by Nokia and Intel, now known as Jolla. This operating system is essentially an improvement upon MeeGo, continuing the card-based multitasking management and multi-gesture operation style. Additionally, it incorporates a flat design into its interface, making its overall style more modern.

Currently, the hardware support for Sailfish is limited to Jolla itself, with the sole product being the Jolla smartphone. The design of the Jolla phone resembles the previous Nokia N9, with a square and simple appearance typical of Nordic manufacturers. In terms of hardware specifications, it features a 4.5-inch qHD (540*960 pixels) screen, a dual-core Qualcomm processor, LTE network support, 16GB storage space, and an 8-megapixel camera. It is priced at $399 (approximately RMB 2431) and will officially launch in Finland on November 27th this year. For now, only the Finnish operator DNA has partnered with them.

The market competitiveness of the Jolla phone is average.

Evidently, judging solely from the hardware specifications, the Jolla phone is positioned in the mid-to-low-end range but is priced relatively high. Compared to low-end Android models, it has no significant advantages. Furthermore, Sailfish has not attracted any third-party smartphone manufacturers, and it only has one Nordic operator as a partner, which means the sole Jolla phone will remain a niche product. Lastly, the software resources for Sailfish are also concerning. The limited number of installations is clearly insufficient to attract software developers, so how many users would choose a smartphone solely for its aesthetically pleasing interface? This awkward positioning makes the future of the Jolla phone extremely uncertain. Perhaps Sailfish, like MeeGo, will never become a mainstream operating system.

2. Firefox OS

Representative models: ZTE Open, Alcatel One Touch Fire

Market prospects: ★★★★

Firefox OS, like Android, is an open-source mobile operating system based on the Linux kernel. However, Firefox OS uses Open Web technology, functioning as a browser-based web operating system with Gecko as its core browser engine and developed using HTML5.

Firefox OS phones have already hit the market at very low prices.

Currently, hardware manufacturers such as ZTE, Huawei, Alcatel, and Geeksphone have joined the Firefox OS camp. The models they have launched are already available in Spain, priced at around RMB 550, selling out within hours. Whether it's the ZTE or Geeksphone brand, the hardware configurations of the Firefox OS phones are quite basic, typically featuring a 3.5-inch HVGA resolution screen, a 1GHz single-core Qualcomm processor, 4GB storage space, and a 3-megapixel camera, hence the extremely low price.

The advantage of Firefox OS phones lies in their clear positioning, primarily targeting emerging markets. Additionally, its web-based nature imposes minimal hardware requirements, allowing users to enjoy smooth internet applications at a low cost. Moreover, Firefox OS has attracted some well-known software and hardware vendors to develop hardware and software for it. Overall, accurate positioning and abundant resources should enable Firefox OS to go further.

3. Tizen

Representative model: None yet

Market prospects: ★★★

Tizen is jointly developed by Intel and Samsung and is a hybrid of MeeGo and LiMO. It can be considered a product born out of the challenges faced by Intel and Samsung in their own mobile systems. Tizen is also an open-source standardized platform based on Linux, capable of supporting various devices such as smartphones, tablets, smart TVs, and in-car intelligent terminals. Due to the collaboration of these two giants, it has garnered much anticipation from both the industry and consumers.

However, since its release in 2011, Tizen remains in a state of "much ado about nothing," despite multiple prototype phone demonstrations by Samsung. To date, only a Tizen tablet has been released in the Japanese market, and it is exclusively targeted at developers.

Of course, Tizen still has some advantages. For instance, Samsung's gradual "de-googlization" on its Galaxy series flagship smartphones lays the groundwork for Tizen. Even if Android is replaced by Tizen in the future, most users will still focus on "Galaxy" rather than "Android." Meanwhile, Intel will leverage its chip leader's resource advantages by offering favorable conditions to attract hardware manufacturers. Nevertheless, at present, Tizen urgently needs to achieve real mass production and make a strong debut with a couple of star devices to gain more user attention.

4. Ubuntu Touch

Representative model: None (can be installed on Nexus devices)

Market prospects: ★★

Unlike the previous operating systems, although Ubuntu Touch is also a fully open-source system based on Linux, it seems to lack comprehensive commercial operations. For example, there are no exclusive models yet, and it can only be installed on existing devices like the Nexus 4 and Nexus 7 for developers to experience.

Canonical, the open-source system company known for running the free Ubuntu desktop system, has yet to turn a profit, and the Ubuntu desktop OS has not disrupted Microsoft Windows as expected, casting a shadow over the development of Ubuntu Touch. The advantage of the Ubuntu Touch system lies in its integration with the desktop system. Connecting it to a display allows you to use the desktop version of the OS, achieving the "one phone + one OS" replacement for the existing "mobile + desktop" mode. However, the current completion rate of the system is low, and Canonical's crowdfunding project for the Ubuntu phone ultimately failed, indicating that the appeal of both its system and hardware is quite limited.

Of course, Canonical hasn't given up. Despite the lack of third-party manufacturer support, the company still plans to launch two Ubuntu Touch phones next year and states that it is negotiating carrier cooperation. If everything goes smoothly, Ubuntu Touch phones will officially hit the market in the first half of 2014. However, whether from the mobile end or the desktop end, the appeal of the Ubuntu OS is relatively limited, and its high-end positioning overlaps with Android and iOS. Therefore, it will be challenging to achieve good market results in the short term.

Summary

So, what factors does a successful smartphone operating system need? Through the success of Apple and Google, it might boil down to the following two points:

1. A dominant hardware manufacturer

The best example is Apple's iOS, which has grown and developed due to the strength of innovative hardware products like the iPhone and iPad.

2. A dominant platform provider

It is evident that Android has grown in this way. Google leveraged its powerful resource integration and influence, incorporating various services and attracting renowned hardware manufacturers, leading to Android's current success.

Among the four fringe mobile systems, Intel and Samsung's Tizen appears to have the most star quality, relatively possessing the most resources, though not an absolute advantage, requiring careful management. While Firefox OS may not be a platform with absolute appeal, it wins through clever positioning and could potentially capture some markets beyond the reach of Apple and Google. On the other hand, Sailfish and Ubuntu Touch seem to be following outdated paths, lacking appeal in both platform and hardware. Without truly groundbreaking features to attract users, they will likely remain fleeting phenomena.
