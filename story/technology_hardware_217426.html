I. According to everyone's brief one or two sentence demands, it is really difficult for me to determine what exactly you need. Therefore, it is also hard for me to configure a computer suitable for each friend.

II. Netizens who directly ask about configurations are generally newbies in building computers, and most of them have basically zero knowledge about DIY computer building. Everyone should know that the current DIY market is chaotic with unscrupulous merchants running rampant. Without some time spent learning, and without a certain foundation in building computers, it's very easy to get scammed during the process of building a computer.

III. DIY itself is something that emphasizes personalization. The person who knows you best is yourself, so only the computer you build yourself will truly suit your needs.

Considering the above points, I offer the following suggestions to those new to building computers:

I. If you really want to build your own computer, then you should spend at least one to two weeks learning some basic knowledge about building computers. If you're sure you don't have the time to learn, then I suggest you choose a branded computer. Because if you're a complete novice, the chance of getting scammed while building a computer is almost 100%; even if you find an acquaintance to help you build it, the chance of being overcharged is at least 90%.

II. How to learn the basics of building a computer is actually quite simple. You can start from the following points:

1. Spend more time browsing forums where enthusiasts share their build posts and component reviews. Soon, you'll have a vague concept of various aspects of building a computer.

2. Find a configuration that suits you; after all, you understand your needs the best.

3. Post the configuration you think suits you on the forum, explain your needs, and let the experts there evaluate it for you. Learn from their feedback, and soon you'll have a configuration that's right for you.

4. Through the above learning process, before actually building your computer, make sure to carefully read posts on the forum about how to avoid being scammed (there are actually many such posts).

By following these steps, building a computer that satisfies you shouldn't be a big problem.

Finally, I would like to mention several aspects to consider when asking about your computer building needs on the forum:

1. What is the purpose of your computer? Is it for general office work and internet surfing, playing poker and solitaire, or playing large games? What kind of games do you play? What other games do you plan to play in the future? If you watch movies, do you watch regular videos, compressed TV series, or high-definition movies? How long do you keep the computer on each day? Do you use BT overnight?

2. What is your budget for building the computer? I suggest students without income act within their means.

3. What type of monitor do you want? CRT? LCD? Standard screen or wide screen? What size? I suggest CRT or standard screen LCDs for gaming, and wide screen LCDs for watching movies, especially high-definition wide-screen movies.

4. Do you prefer Intel or AMD platforms? If you want both, you must clearly state your reasons; usually, people won't configure both.

5. Do you like overclocking? Overclocking has some wear on hardware, uses different electricity, and requires comprehensive consideration.

6. If you're upgrading an old machine, please report the detailed configuration of the old machine, the purpose of the upgrade, the budget, and which old components will continue to be used.

7. If you're using it for design work, please specify whether it's for 3D design or graphic design, which programs you usually use, how large the images are, whether it's professional or just for fun, or if you're just starting out.

Please read the above carefully and think it through before asking questions!

Also, please don't use words like "kneeling and begging"! Just come out and ask...

For friends who are new to computers and want to build their own, the most important thing to guard against is traditional thinking inertia and the deception of unscrupulous merchants. So how do we develop the ability to resist such deception? Let me summarize the "Three 'Taboos' and Eight Precautions" of building a computer.

First Taboo: Having a "Friend" Assemble Your Computer

Perhaps in the minds of those who haven't bought a computer yet, there's this ingrained notion: Since I don't understand computers, I should find someone trustworthy to help me assemble one. This way, I won't get counterfeit parts, and I'll get the lowest price. But are you sure the "friend" you've found is really familiar with computers?

I have two examples: One of my buddies wanted to build a computer. He wasn't very knowledgeable about computers, so he consulted his neighbor next door. His neighbor recommended a computer shop run by a relative. The next day, I went with my friend to this so-called "friend's" computer shop. The person there first asked about our budget, then gave us the lowest priced components. As expected, the price list and component prices were obviously not cost-effective, so we left with an excuse.

Another buddy wanted to build a computer. Before building, he contacted his brother-in-law's friend and brought me along to meet him. On the way, he told me that his brother-in-law's friend should be reliable since he ran his own computer shop (this was precisely what I was worried about...). When we arrived at the market, I was completely flabbergasted — yes! This counter was absolutely his own, and he was definitely the boss! The reason for my shock — XXX specialized in buying and selling second-hand computers...

Alright, since we were already there, let's stay calm. He said he could assemble a new computer, so we had no choice but to listen to his spiel. We handed him a pre-written configuration list for him to quote a price. He ran downstairs to the assembly plaza, then came back up to tell us: "You won't be able to get the brands listed in your configuration at this price. I can switch them out for you, and the price will match." After a round of switching, all the first and second-line brands were replaced with "eleventh or twelfth-line" brands...

Clearly, in the situation described above, because my friend and the "friend" he sought out to build the computer had never met, naturally, such a "friend" would mercilessly rip him off. These kinds of computer assemblers love "friends" like you because they know full well: since you've come for my reputation as a "friend," no matter what parts I give you or what price I quote, you won't say anything, after all, we're "friends," aren't we?

Summary: Look rationally at such "friends." I firmly believe in this saying: "If I were the owner of a computer shop, and a close friend came to build a computer, I'd rather not make any profit and give him the bottom price. But for ordinary friends... hmmmm... only you and I know, and heaven and earth know."

Second Taboo: Only Recognizing "First-Line" Brands

"Buying a motherboard, you must use ASUS! Buying a graphics card, you must use XFX! As for others, I only recognize Samsung!"

This isn't me deliberately defaming the aforementioned brands, and indeed ASUS, XFX, and Samsung products are really good. However, some friends blindly pursue these brands due to hearsay, which is incorrect. For example, if you want to buy a cost-effective motherboard, choosing a second-tier motherboard at the same price level will definitely be much more cost-effective. XFX also has many cost-effective products. If you're willing to spend more time online researching relevant materials, you'll find that there are actually many more brands to choose from. As for Samsung, a first-tier brand, its strength cannot be denied, and it does produce many excellent products. However, it's not necessarily true that everything made by Samsung is the best. For instance, I've encountered friends whose Samsung optical drives, after prolonged use, have experienced issues with not ejecting discs, which is quite troubling...(hopefully, these non-ejecting issues have only happened to me...)

Summary: Various brands compete with each other, and ultimately, consumers benefit. Looking rationally at and choosing every brand, you'll find that there are actually more products available to choose from.

Third Taboo: The Latest is the Best

Whenever manufacturers release new products, friends who half-understand specifications should take note. Just because a new hardware standard is released doesn't mean its performance is necessarily better. New products just hitting the market will inevitably have some bugs. Furthermore, can you say that HD3450's performance is definitely better than 2600XT? Can you say that because it supports DX10, the 8500GT DDR2 version card's performance can significantly surpass the DDR3 version of the 7300GT? I doubt anyone would sacrifice overall performance to play those slideshow-like games just to see the so-called special effects...

Summary: Hardware will continuously evolve, but newer generations don't necessarily perform better than the previous generation.

Alright, now that we've covered the three taboos, let's discuss the eight precautions against JS (unscrupulous merchants).

PS: Here, JS refers to JXS (dealers), abbreviated as JS...(Let's be understanding; after all, they need to support their families and make a living... It depends on how everyone interprets the meaning of JS, hehe...)

Precaution One: Beware of Arrogant JS

Under the drive of profit, this type of JS will typically belittle the configuration you hold in your hand. For example, if you've already selected a configuration and asked him to write it up, he will often start from his own standpoint: "Look at your configuration, it's completely unreasonable. How can you choose INTEL's U? Nowadays, everyone builds computers with AMD's U. INTEL's U is expensive and not that great in performance; AMD's U is cheap, has low heat output, performs well, and is perfect for gaming. Also, your graphics card, I highly do not recommend ATI. Who uses ATI cards nowadays? ATI cards perform poorly in games, have bad drivers, and tend to crash frequently. You should choose NVIDIA cards!" Additionally, regarding the motherboard you chose, he might say, "That's not P35, it's P31, frankly speaking. Nowadays, it's CH8 (P35 carries ICH9, sweat, doesn't even know how to pronounce ICH and starts misleading...) Look at this XX-brand motherboard, it's equipped with CH7 northbridge (sweat again... I get it! Southbridge is called MCH!) It's not a P35 motherboard at all. Instead of this, why don't you choose XX-brand P31..."

* Such JS might make you laugh and cry simultaneously. Friends with short tempers might feel an urge to beat him up.

Precaution Two: Beware of Opportunistic JS

Their secret weapon is to grasp the customer's psychology. For example, if you go to a computer shop to build a computer, you notice that the model number of the component you want is quoted tens or hundreds of yuan higher. Naturally, you would question why they quote 9XX yuan while another place quotes 8XX yuan. Aren't they charging too much? At this point, the JS feigns confusion, hesitates for a moment, and then gives a very definite answer: "We can't do that price! Others might be able to give you that price, but we definitely can't! We are the agent of XX brand, and we know this price inside out!" After the JS hesitates again, as if waking up from a dream, he says, "Oh, I know now! The reason they can give such a low price is probably because it's gray market goods! Or refurbished goods! There's no guarantee on quality assurance! We provide three years, maybe they only provide one year! If you want, we can directly transfer the goods from their place for you. Would you like that?"

* Walk around more, don't hang on one tree. They claim to be agents of XX brand, but is this agent the main agent?

Precaution Three: Beware of Discriminatory JS

JS usually judge how much you know about computers from your speech, and then treat you accordingly. Most friends focus their energy on the prices of the three major components, often overlooking the case and power supply, which are usually the most profitable for them. This type of JS may intentionally lower the price of the three major components or offer even lower prices, of course, no one does business to lose money, and the difference? Of course, they'll make it up from the components you don't pay attention to...

* When assembling a computer, don't ignore the prices of cases, power supplies, and peripheral consumables...

Precaution Four: Beware of Fabricating Facts JS

This type of JS is quite ruthless. They quote prices very smoothly, everything you want is "in stock", all components are quoted at flat prices, and while writing the order slip, they compliment your astuteness: "To be honest, you seem to know a lot about computers. This configuration of yours is really good..." After gaining your trust through flattery and signing the order for preparation, they say, "Oops, unfortunately, the XX brand you want is out of stock today, what should we do? Let me call and check if other places have it." After pretending, they regretfully tell you, "I'm really sorry, this brand sells well (or doesn't sell well, merchants don't stock it) and there's no stock. What should we do? Why don't we choose another brand instead, they're the same to use." Their sweet talk and smile service have gained your trust, at this point... Usually, you would accept their suggestion... If you don't accept it... Well, you've already paid the deposit~ Hmmmmm~~~

* Don't sign the contract and pay the deposit hastily. Wait until all the goods are prepared and inspected before signing the contract and paying the deposit.

Precaution Five: Beware of Manipulative JS

Businessmen making money also talk about losing money, whoever says they're making money is surely losing it! Not just selling computers, even clothing and grocery store merchants are the same, it can be said that this is a common trick used by any type of merchant: This component's price is really the lowest we can give you! If we make it any lower, we'll lose money! We're not making any profit anyway! If you don't believe us, go ask others! Anyway, there's more than one computer shop to build a computer! If you're someone who doesn't like trouble, you might say, "Give me a little discount and I won't bother anymore, it's troublesome, just give me the lowest price." "We really can't go any lower! I'm not making money, I can't afford to lose money either! This price, I see you understand, here's the deal, we'll give you XX yuan cheaper, it's already the lowest, if you can take it, take it, if not, look elsewhere..."

* Does he say one, it's one? Does he say two, it's two? You might as well walk a few more steps, you might save enough for a few taxi rides...

Precaution Six: Beware of Vague JS

Some JS, when quoting, assembling computer friends usually notice: CPU - Dual Core XXX Motherboard - P35 Graphics Card - 8500GT Memory - 1G ... Total Price XXXX Yuan. At first glance, similar configurations seem much cheaper than other shops... This is one of their commonly used tricks - Vague Excellence##! Using the same model numbers and lower prices to catch your eye. As for the specific brand of the motherboard... As for the specific brand or model of the graphics card or memory...

* Before assembling, you must first understand the specific model parameters and local market conditions of the hardware!!

Precaution Seven: Beware of Flattering JS

This type of person has relatively low technical content, basically relying on a smooth-talking mouth. Whatever you say is good, he'll follow and say how good it is. Of course, you should also know this principle: you get what you pay for! Everyone says it's good, so the price will surely be quite good~ As long as you recognize this good product, then just pay up obediently...

* Of course, JS isn't affirming that the product you want is good, but affirming that your action of taking out your wallet is good!

Precaution Eight: Beware of Deceptive JS

Actually, talking about deception, the responsibility shouldn't be entirely attributed to JS, because sometimes they are innocent (whether truly innocent or pretending to be innocent, this is unknown). Things happen for a reason, after all, they are not the producers of the products.

For example, once I bought an XX-brand graphics card, the official specification was to use ☆☆☆1.4N memory chips, but I was careless and didn't carefully check the specifications before taking the goods home. Upon returning home, I discovered that the memory had been downgraded by the manufacturer to HY2N chips. When I confronted them, they showed no intention of accepting returns and acted very innocently. After several rounds of arguments, they finally agreed to return my goods. Here, it should be noted that regardless of the type of product provided by suppliers, dealers should inform consumers of detailed specifications. Even if they are unaware, if the consumer hasn't received a satisfactory product and the product hasn't been damaged, immediate returns should be offered.

* Still, carefully inspect the goods beforehand. Even if they can return the goods, it's still troublesome going back and forth multiple times.
