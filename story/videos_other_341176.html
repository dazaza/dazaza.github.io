The first military reformer Newton discovered the law of universal gravitation when he saw an apple fall to the ground, Archimedes discovered buoyancy in a bathtub, and Watt invented the steam engine after seeing a kettle spout steam. These are all stories of accidental discoveries of truth that people have heard many times. Of course, things were not so simple. However, it was not uncommon for some parties to not take much notice at first, but later on, these events turned out to be epoch-making. In the fourth year of King Jing of Zhou (541 BC), in a place called Dayuan, there was a small war between the army of Jin State and the Bai Di tribe. This unintentionally became a watershed event in the history of Chinese military - the transformation from chariot warfare to infantry warfare. This is how it was recorded in "Zuo Zhuan? Chapter Zhao Yuan Year 1": "In Dayuan, General Xun Wu (Mu Zi) of Jin State defeated the Wuzhong and various Di tribes by transforming chariots into infantry formations." Before the battle, Wei Shu said: "They are on foot while we are in chariots, and the terrain we encounter is narrow. If we use ten men per chariot, we will surely win. If we trap them in a narrow pass, we will win again. Let us all become infantry, starting with me." He then dismantled the chariots to form infantry lines, with five chariots forming three groups. A favorite noble warrior of the chief commander refused to lose his status and declined to get off the chariot and become a soldier, so he was executed as a warning. Wei Shu lured the enemy with a tightly organized infantry formation. The Di soldiers had never seen such an arrangement before, mocked the Jin army's disorganization, and rushed forward without waiting for the formation to be completed. As a result, they were routed immediately. In this war, General Xun Wu (Mu Zi) led the troops to attack Wuzhong, and met the Bai Di tribe in Dayuan. Given the narrow battlefield terrain where chariots could not maneuver easily and would find it hard to engage with the Di infantry, Wei Shu suggested "transforming chariots into infantry," meaning converting the armored warriors on chariots into foot soldiers, changing chariot warfare into infantry warfare, and leading by example. A noble warrior favored by the chief commander was unwilling to lose his identity and refused to get off the chariot and become a soldier, so he was killed to set an example. Wei Shu lured the enemy with a tightly organized infantry formation. The Di soldiers had never seen such an arrangement before, mocked the Jin army's disorganization, and rushed forward without waiting for the formation to be completed. As a result, they were routed immediately. Transforming chariots into infantry marked the first realization of the shift from chariot warfare to infantry warfare, and the first appearance of the infantry formation in combat, which was its historical significance. Wei Shu thus became the first recorded military reformer in Chinese history. Such a great event of its time came about so simply and easily, without any forewarning or psychological preparation, not at all spectacularly. Chariots began to appear during the Shang Dynasty and were promoted and popularized with the implementation of the well-field system in the Western Zhou Dynasty. During large-scale battles between states like Jin and Chu, up to four or five thousand chariots were used, and medium-sized states had over a thousand chariots. At the time, each chariot was driven by horses and manned by three warriors, accompanied by several foot soldiers behind. There was a class difference between the armored warriors on the chariots and the foot soldiers on the ground. "Chariot warfare in the Spring and Autumn period was a kind of aristocratic war. Sometimes it was viewed as a competition, with certain procedures for arraying troops and recognized principles for engaging in battle, still constrained by 'rituals'" (Huang Renyu, "Talking About Chinese History Along the Heron River"). But by the late Spring and Autumn period, when feudalism was collapsing and royal authority was waning, the Zhou rituals were declining and swords were clashing, and the well-field system was being abandoned in favor of private fields. The roads in the fields were no longer neatly shaped as they were during the public field period, making it impossible for chariots to run freely. The original master-servant relationship between armored warriors and foot soldiers began to reverse, and the internal relationships within the army urgently needed adjustment. All of this meant that reforming the chariot system was inevitable, it just depended on who had the insight and the courage to step forward and break through this barrier. Regardless of whether Wei Shu himself intended it or not, history pushed him into the position of a military reformer. "Transforming chariots into infantry" also accompanied the birth of the infantry formation, another significant stroke in the history of military reform. Wei Shu formed five-five style infantry formations based on the unit of "wu". Fifteen men from five chariots were compiled into three "wu", and five "wu" formed a small square formation. The entire army was divided into five square formations, one in front (vanguard), two in the middle (left and right wings), and two at the back (front guard and rear guard). They could advance to attack and retreat to defend. The orderliness of chariot warfare combined with the flexibility of infantry warfare, and chariot warfare was henceforth replaced by infantry warfare, introducing the concept of battle arrays on the battlefield. In the era of cold weapons (and even in the early stages of the era of firearms), in the history of world wars, this method of warfare has always been the most advantageous form of infantry combat, and traces of it can be found in the phalanxes of Sparta and ancient Rome. Wei Shu's ancestors were all capable generals, but his own debut was rather pitiful. In the eighth year of Duke Ping of Jin (550 BC), the Luans family, exiled in the struggle among the nobles, attempted to make a comeback. Luan Ying relied on old friendships to persuade Wei Shu to act as an inside man, while he himself led the family troops to launch a night raid on the capital city of Jin, Jiangcheng. In the emergency, Fan Yang, a young general of the Fans family, rushed to Wei's residence and captured Wei Shu, who was preparing to get into a carriage to welcome Luan Ying. Held hostage, Wei Shu had no choice but to comply, and Luan Ying, having lost his inside man, was defeated and fled in disgrace (Zuo Zhuan, Chapter Xiang Gong Year 23). Later, Wei Shu led troops in a few minor battles. After the Battle of Dayuan, he became the central general of Jin. In the twelfth year of Duke Qing of Jin (514 BC), Han Xuanzi, the prime minister, stepped down due to old age, handing over his position to Wei Shu. At that time, Jin was swaying between alliances and conflicts among several powerful families. From the start of his administration, Wei Shu divided the fiefs of the Qis and Yangshes, which had been destroyed, into several counties and appointed a group of officials from different families who were diligent and capable of maintaining their heritage to manage them. This move received praise from later generations. For instance, after appointing his clansman Wei Wu as the doctor of Gengyang, Wei Shu specifically sought others' opinions, asking if anyone would consider them as partisans. Others told him about Wei Wu's strengths and cited the example of King Wu of Zhou sealing his brothers after defeating the Shang dynasty, saying: "There is no other consideration in appointments, only virtue, regardless of intimacy or distance," indicating that this move was close to literary virtue, putting him at ease. Wei Wu did not fail in his duties upon taking office. Once, before making a judgment, a wealthy family in Gengyang bribed Wei Shu. Upon learning this, Wei Wu hurried to Wei Shu's residence and persuaded him to return the female musicians offered by the wealthy family in Gengyang through sighing three times during a meal. Jia Xin, appointed as the doctor of Qi, was advised by Wei Shu before taking office, saying, "I heard about your deeds from Shu Xiang, knowing that you have contributed to the royal family, so I specially recommended you. You must continue to work hard." When Confucius learned of Wei Shu's recommendation of virtuous talents, he praised it, saying: "Close ones are not neglected, distant ones are not overlooked, this can be called righteousness" (Zuo Zhuan, Chapter Zhao Gong Year 28). Moreover, Wei Shu was a studious person who was not ashamed to seek advice from others and could humbly listen to others' opinions. In Zuo Zhuan, Chapter Zhao Gong Year 29, there is a record of Wei Shu seeking advice from others about the nine virtues and knowledge about dragons. In the third year of Duke Ding of Jin (509 BC), Wei Shu represented Jin and convened the feudal lords at Di Quan, and fell ill and died on the way back. He served as prime minister for six years, and his posthumous title was Xian Zi. Among the nobles of Jin, the Wei family was not originally a major clan, but under Wei Shu's generation, they gradually consolidated their family's status and rose to the ranks of the senior ministers. In the struggles among the nobles, the Wei family always benefited. After Wei Shu took office, the power struggle in Jin intensified. First, Zhao Xuanzi cast the criminal tripod, followed by the destruction of the Fans and Zhongs families. Then Wei joined forces with Han and Zhao to destroy the Zhi family. By the twenty-third year of King Weilie of Zhou (403 BC), the once dominant state of Jin was finally dismembered by the three families of Wei, Zhao, and Han, marking the end of the Spring and Autumn period and the beginning of the Warring States period in Chinese history. Social development requires momentum, and reform is an important driving force for social development, but reform mainly relies on social forces rather than individual strength. Military reform is the inevitable result of historical development reaching a certain stage, naturally a social behavior rather than an individual one. Every aspect of military reform is deeply imprinted with the marks of the times, with its prerequisites and opportunities that promote its maturity; it cannot pre-create a great man solely for the sake of reform. When the ship of history sails to a certain moment, requiring someone to step forward to make an important decision and adjust its course, there will inevitably be someone who stands up to complete this historic reform task. However, the opportunity for reform does not visit everyone, nor can everyone seize the opportunity when it comes. The first monkey to come down from the tree onto the ground, the first primitive human to stand up, whether intentional or unintentional, is unknown, but who could deny that these actions were not epoch-making in the history of human evolution? This may be the inevitability and contingency of history. Dismantling chariots and becoming infantry announced the end of hundreds of years of chariot warfare history and established the infantry system. Its historical achievements, like King Wuling of Zhao's creation of the cavalry system, are great turning points in the history of Chinese military affairs. Thus, crowning Wei Shu with the title of military reformer is indeed justified. However, Wei Shu's fame is far inferior to that of King Wuling of Zhao. In the Records of the Grand Historian: Wei Family Chronicle and the Records of the Grand Historian: Jin Family Chronicle, he passes by quickly, leaving few traces, let alone the event of dismantling chariots and becoming infantry, which should be recorded in history. It seems that history can bury talents too. Ask those who read history, how many know Wei Shu? Society needs reform, and therefore needs people like Wei Shu. Whether they are intentional or unintentional, clever or rigid, regardless of whether history's selection is hasty or careful, the crown of reformers will naturally fall on their heads. Their greatness lies in their accomplishment of epoch-making events. One might say they are ordinary because of the favor of opportunity. Therefore, whether a person can achieve something indeed depends half on opportunity and half on oneself.
