Zhiseng Education Network [http://www.zsjykj.com/] reports: Finance major is a popular major in the self-study examination, and has been favored by candidates for its practicality and ease of employment. As a bachelor's degree graduate of the self-study finance major, I would like to share with all candidates the skills I have learned in studying finance over the past few years, as well as how to handle the relationship between public courses and specialized courses.

First, study public courses, then specialized courses

I majored in finance both at the associate and bachelor levels. Here, I would first like to remind candidates whether they have already decided to study finance. Because finance courses are highly specialized, whether at the associate or bachelor level, there are at least five courses that are incompatible with other majors. If you pass these courses and then decide to switch majors, it will be regrettable.

After selecting your major, I suggest candidates start with public courses, mainly to prevent them from switching majors during their studies, because the majority of public courses are universal and there is basically no situation of "wasted effort." After passing the public courses, you can choose the unified examination courses for economics-related majors, laying a foundation for studying specialized courses. For example, the "Introduction to Economic Law" in the associate segment and "Marketing" in the bachelor segment of the finance major. Next, you can focus on preparing for the study of specialized courses.

First, read the textbook thoroughly, then understand the content

Whether it's a public course or a specialized course, when studying self-examination courses, the textbook is the most important learning tool, and all the exam content is in the textbook. After getting the textbook, candidates should gain a preliminary understanding of the course by flipping through the table of contents, outline, etc. As mentioned earlier, due to the strong professionalism of finance courses, and since most candidates do not work in financial institutions, it is difficult to digest the textbook content by linking theory with practice. This requires candidates to have a thorough understanding of the textbook in order to understand the content of each knowledge point. Therefore, being familiar with the textbook is very important.

In fact, candidates can use media, internet, and other channels to understand and consolidate the content they are studying. For example, in textbooks such as "Monetary Banking," there are two important concepts: M1 and M2, which refer to broad and narrow money supply. During exams, question setters usually assess candidates' understanding and differentiation of these two concepts through multiple-choice questions. Most candidates choose to memorize the textbook, but I remembered them by browsing the People's Bank of China website and reading news. The People's Bank regularly releases relevant statistical reports on its website, including M1 and M2. In this way, turning dead knowledge into living knowledge, and having data as support, makes understanding and memorization easier.

First, list the structure, then find the key points

Through thoroughly reading the textbook, we can discover that there are patterns in the writing of the textbook, meaning that the course can be introduced in a modular way. For example, "Financial Market Studies" runs through the entire textbook with aspects such as an overview, market, risk, and regulation. After fully familiarizing themselves with the textbook, candidates can try to list the structure of the textbook, then enrich it, which is what we usually call "reading the textbook thinly." The next step is to find the key points. Candidates can refer to past exam questions, exam outlines, and teaching aids to summarize key content and review targetedly.

Most specialized courses in the examination plan for the finance major are only arranged once a year, so candidates must prepare carefully, take it seriously, and spend most of their study time on the textbook to lay a solid foundation.
