LinkedIn Founder Debunks Seven Big Venture Capital Myths

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 16:49:44

Reid Hoffman, the founder of American professional social network LinkedIn and partner at venture capital firm Greylock, last week published an article on his personal homepage addressing seven common misconceptions entrepreneurs have when attracting venture capital.

Below is the full text of Hoffman's article:

At Greylock, my partners and I are committed to a mission: to help entrepreneurs whenever possible—whether they're part of our portfolio, whether we’re considering investing in them, or even if it’s our first meeting.

Entrepreneurs often ask me to help them refine their pitch decks for fundraising. Due to Greylock's strong emphasis on integrity and confidentiality, we never share these pitch decks with others. However, there is something I can disclose: the materials I used in 2004 when raising LinkedIn's Series B funding from Greylock.

This May marked LinkedIn's tenth anniversary. Reflecting on our early days as a startup, I realized that there seemed to be no public access to the pitch decks of successful companies from their early stages. So, I thought: why not make these plans available to everyone, not just the entrepreneurs supported by Greylock, to help more people who are starting businesses?

Today, I’ve posted LinkedIn’s Series B pitch deck on my personal website, which covers three main topics:

1. How entrepreneurs should approach fundraising presentations.

2. The development journey of LinkedIn.

3. A comparison between the consumer internet landscape in 2004 and today.

To help better understand the fundraising process, here are seven widely circulated misconceptions about it:

1. Misconception: Startups raise money solely for the capital.

Reality: Successful fundraising also involves establishing partnerships, which can be more valuable than the money itself.

A successful round of fundraising clearly helps your company raise capital, but there are other key results you should focus on. For example, top-tier investors can significantly expand your network, helping you recruit employees and attract customers. They can also serve as a source of intelligence, aiding you in navigating potential challenges and opportunities.

In other words, the ideal investor is like a co-founder who provides financial support. This is why some already wealthy entrepreneurs still seek funding from experienced investors for their next ventures—they know that partnering with angel investors or venture capitalists brings more than just money.

Unfortunately, many investors bring negative value ("dumb money"), where they not only fail to add value but also interfere with normal operations. Ideally, you can find investors who bring positive added value ("smart money").

How do you determine if an investor can add value? Observe during targeted outreach and the fundraising process if they provide constructive feedback. Do they understand your market? Are their questions aligned with your own concerns? Can you learn from their feedback? Are they passionate about the problem you're trying to solve?

2. Misconception: If your team is strong, showcase it at the beginning of your slides.

Reality: The start of your presentation should highlight the investment thesis.

The first 60 seconds of your presentation are crucial, so the opening matters. Most entrepreneurs begin by listing their team. While the team is indeed important, it shouldn't be the opening focus. Instead, you should directly present the investment thesis—the core things that potential shareholders must believe in to invest in your company.

Your first slide should clearly outline the investment thesis in 3 to 8 bullet points, then provide additional content to support these points and build trust with investors—including background information about the team. Clearly stating the investment thesis allows investors to give targeted feedback, helping you improve and eventually reach alignment.

This advice applies to seed-stage fundraising as well. Yes, seed investors understand there are many unknowns and the idea may change significantly, so they carefully assess the team's capabilities. But even at this stage, using the investment thesis to capture investor attention first, followed by showcasing the team, is equally important.

3. Misconception: All pitch decks follow the same structure.

Reality: You should decide based on your situation whether to focus on data or concept.

Your investment thesis can either be data-driven or concept-driven. Which one should you choose?

In a data-driven pitch, the emphasis on data is because current figures are highly favorable to you. Investors will evaluate your company based on the data. When LinkedIn went public, we used data to promote ourselves to public market investors, presenting them with a series of multi-year data points.

In a concept-driven pitch, while data is used, its primary role is to support an emerging concept. A concept-driven pitch showcases your vision of the future and how you intend to create it, aiming to align investors with your ideas. Therefore, it uses more predictive future data rather than historical data.

4. Misconception: Avoid discussing any risks associated with your business to prevent undermining investor confidence.

Reality: Clearly identify risk factors and demonstrate control over them.

Experienced investors know that risks are everywhere. If they ask you about risks and you don’t have an answer, they will lose trust in you—either because they think you're dishonest or because they think you're incompetent.

You might be seen as dishonest because they may feel you know the risks but chose not to disclose them, showing a lack of sincerity in cooperation. Or they might see you as incompetent because they may feel you don't have the ability to understand that all projects carry risks—including your own. Therefore, you should clearly state 1 to 3 major risks that could impede your success and explain how you plan to mitigate them.

5. Misconception: Claiming you have no potential competitors is a significant advantage.

Reality: Acknowledge various forms of competition and articulate your competitive edge.

Entrepreneurs often claim they have no competitors, thinking it’s an easy way to leave a lasting impression. But if you really say this, it either shows you believe the market is completely inefficient or that others consider your market to have no value. Both ideas are foolish.

Markets are always efficient—once valuable opportunities emerge, they will be discovered by others. To win investor trust, you need to show investors that you understand competitive risks and explain why you will succeed.

Express your competitive advantage by answering: Why will you stand out in a particular business? What is your competitive edge? If you aren’t clear and decisive, investors won’t believe you have a winning advantage.

6. Misconception: Avoid comparing yourself to other companies to emphasize your uniqueness.

Reality: Use analogies to promote yourself.

Every great consumer internet company grows into a unique organization. But in the early stages, you should use analogies to describe future success and potential. Given the limited time for presentations, this helps investors reference cases they already understand.

I once heard one of the best pitches for the Hollywood movie "Man’s Best Friend." They told investors that if a film like "Jaws" could be hugely successful, designing a similar scenario on land and replacing the shark with a dog could also result in big box office earnings. Although the movie turned out poorly, the pitch was excellent.

It’s important to note that using analogies in promotion doesn’t mean you should rely on analogical reasoning when developing your specific strategy. Using analogical reasoning in strategic planning can be dangerous because startups encounter many pitfalls, so details matter. Thus, when planning strategies, you should be cautious about using analogies. However, during high-level promotional speeches, analogies can be very effective.

7. Misconception: Focus solely on the current fundraising pitch, and future rounds will naturally fall into place.

Reality: Beyond this round of funding, think about the next one as well.

Whenever you raise funds, you should think about the next round of financing. Assuming you successfully complete this round, how will you raise money in the future? How will you pitch to future investors? What will they worry about? What should you address next?

B-round investors will certainly want to see the slides you used in your A-round, C-round investors will want to see the B-round slides, and so on. After creating the A-round pitch deck, I showcased a growth curve that helped us secure the investment, but I was also confident that we would surpass this growth trend in the future. I hoped to say in the B-round pitch: “Here was my original prediction, and here is how I achieved it.” Since we exceeded the expectations outlined in the A-round, investors would trust us and believe we could also exceed our revenue projections in the B-round.
