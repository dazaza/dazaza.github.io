Open registration for .cn domain names: form over substance

by xwkj0983 on 2012-02-23 14:57:51

Previously, the domain name registration company IP Mirror based in Singapore released a message stating that starting from 9:00 AM on February 12, 2012, individuals or organizations from the UK, Hong Kong, Singapore, Malaysia, and Australia would be able to directly register Chinese domain names. Would this be an injection of a stimulant for China's consistently underperforming domain names? In my view, this small step towards internationalizing .cn domains holds more significance as a declaration of attitude, falling into formalism.

It is understood that the domains opened up for partial overseas regions include ".cn", ".中国", ".公司", and ".网络". According to CNNIC's approval process, the Server Hold status of these domains will be lifted, allowing direct resolution to websites and activation of email services. Would this have the effect of clearing the gloom for .cn domains, whose total number of registered domains has been stagnant? Some industry insiders estimate that this internationalization initiative might trigger the largest .cn registration wave in over two years.

Do international audiences need .cn?

Is it really so? Actually, no. For a country's top-level domain, its main target audience is the internet users of that country. Of course, in some international exchange environments, the .cn domain can also serve as a representation of national image. Experts from renowned domestic domain management experts China Nuowang nuo.cn analyzed that examples include the Beijing Olympics (beijing2008.cn), the Shanghai Expo (expo2010.cn), and the Guangzhou Asian Games (gz2010.cn). These are all international events where the official websites use their country's top-level domain, which not only reflects national honor but also plays a better role in promotion and publicity. Statistics show that during the Beijing Olympics, the official website received 200 million visits per day. However, in daily exchanges, stepping out of the country, using another country's top-level domain seems like a rootless floating weed, losing direction.

For a local enterprise, there is almost no need to use another country's top-level domain on its official website. Whether from cultural integration, commercial promotion, or customer acceptance, there is no suitable entry point; for multinational corporations, if their business has extended to other countries or regions, they can apply for a corresponding country-specific domain as a sub-site of the official website. But this is unnecessary to apply for abroad unless it becomes redundant.

Why push .cn out?

In 2008, the number of registered Chinese domain names, including .cn, once surpassed Germany’s .de with 12.36 million registrations, ranking first globally in terms of country top-level domain totals. The reason for such a rocket-like increase can be attributed to two main factors. First, from 2006 to 2007, coinciding with the rebound of the internet after a low period, and along with the recovery of the domestic economy, the development of numerous small and medium-sized enterprises drove the informatization process, leading to increasing demand for website construction; secondly, facing gradually improving economic conditions, to further promote the development of China's internet industry, CNNIC launched a "1 yuan/year for .cn domain" promotional discount in March 2007. As a significant inducement, the number of .cn domain registrations skyrocketed immediately.
