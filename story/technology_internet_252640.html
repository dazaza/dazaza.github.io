Teaching children to juggle balls can quickly improve IQ

Ball juggling is probably the most basic of all circus acts: three balls are deftly tossed and caught between the juggler's two hands, sometimes while riding a unicycle. Whether you've ever tried or successfully learned this fundamental circus skill or not, according to an American medical journal, teaching children to juggle can significantly help in developing their attention, coordination, and patience.

As adults, few may actually master this performance. Many parents might think it’s too difficult for children and give up on the idea. However, the key point isn't how difficult the act is, but rather teaching both the child and ourselves that without repeated practice and patience, no one can learn anything. This aligns with the Chinese ancient fable "The Oil Seller" where dexterity comes with practice.

Starting to practice juggling is hard, and children will certainly experience many failures, feel disappointed, and even consider giving up. At this moment, we need to let them know that everyone feels discouraged at times, and they can take a brief adjustment, but only through constant practice can they achieve perfection. Not easily giving up and persisting will lead them to success.

Now, I will introduce some simple beginner methods:

1. Start with one ball. Toss one ball from your left hand and catch it with your right hand, repeating this process. It may seem simple, but any complex movement or discipline is built upon simplicity. The size of the ball should be suitable for the child's grip; too large and they won’t be able to hold it, too small and they might drop it. A soft ball with little bounce is best, as it reduces the risk of injury and won’t roll away far if dropped.

2. Two-ball practice (one). Hold two balls, gently toss one into the air. If you're right-handed, start with the ball in your right hand, and if you're left-handed, start with the left. When the first ball falls in front of you, transfer the second ball to the hand that just threw the first one, then catch the falling ball. Repeat this process.

3. Two-ball practice (two). Once comfortable with the previous step, move on to practicing tossing with both hands. Begin by throwing the ball from your right hand (or left), count silently 1, 2, then throw the ball from the other hand. Your right hand catches the ball thrown from the left, and vice versa. Remember, the throwing motion should be gentle.

4. Three-ball practice. Once the child has mastered the two-ball toss and catch, they can advance to three balls. Though it will be very challenging and likely result in failure, it's crucial to encourage the child to keep trying. Use slightly smaller balls this time. Hold two balls in your dominant hand (the one you write with) and one in the other hand. Throw the ball from your dominant hand, wait until it descends within reach of the other hand, then throw the ball from the non-dominant hand while simultaneously throwing the second ball from the dominant hand.
