Memories of Youth - Our Junior High School English Textbook

by ofree on 2007-09-22 15:36:12

Note: This article is reprinted from - Everyone Loves English Textbooks. Internet users are also welcome to reprint it, but please note...

People born in the 80s mostly used the same set of junior high school English textbooks. If they were asked to create a dialogue based on what they learned, nine out of ten would blurt out something like this:

"What’s your name?"

"My name is Li Lei."

"How are you?"

"Fine, thank you. And you?"

"I’m fine too. How do you do."

"How do you do."

This classic textbook was published by Longman and edited by Alexander for the People's Education Press.

Anyone who attended junior high school classes and listened to the lessons would be familiar with the content and character relationships in the book – a phenomenon that puzzles me greatly. Moreover, everyone seems to have thought along the same lines, and when discussing these topics online, it feels like reciting slogans. Of course, because I often slept on my desk during class back then, I didn't delve deeply enough into the textbook, so many places weren't as profound to me as they were to others. Now, taking advantage of people's discussions, I can organize and promote a spirit of collaboration. The following content includes some ideas from others and some of my own thoughts, all mixed together.

First, let's introduce the characters.

There are two main protagonists: Li Lei and Jim. In the English textbook, they represent China and the West respectively.

Li Lei, translated as "Lei Li," is a Chinese boy with a short, flat haircut who likes wearing light-colored T-shirts. He appears to be a typical well-behaved Chinese student. However, he isn't a dull bookworm; he enjoys playing. I remember one lesson where he went out to play with a ball while Jim was grounded at home for not finishing his homework. This dynamic personality makes him popular wherever he goes, able to become close friends with both studious Lin Tao and active Jim.

Jim Green, whose full name is James Allen Green, translated as "Jim Green (James Allen Green)," is a British child living in China. He has brown curly hair and likes wearing dark-colored T-shirts, giving the impression of being energetic, smart, but somewhat careless, making frequent small mistakes.

Next, let's talk about the female lead and other important characters.

Han Meimei

If there is any debate over who the first male protagonist is, Han Meimei is undoubtedly the first female protagonist. This Chinese girl with shoulder-length hair is elegant, gentle, wise, and kind, embodying almost all the virtues of women. In the book, she acts more like an older sister, helping her classmates solve problems.

Some people say Han Meimei looks like a woman cadre, very left-leaning...

Kate

Kate Green, Jim's sister. Like her brother, Kate is a nickname; her full name is Catherine (Kathleen). She also has brown curly hair (for some reason, Jim and Kate always remind me of Ron and Ginny in Harry Potter, while Han Meimei reminds me of Hermione).

Kate is lively and cute, and her role grows increasingly significant later on. I wonder why she doesn't play with kids her own age and instead plays with Jim's friends, who are several years older.

Lucy and Lily

The adorable twin sisters come from the United States. They look exactly alike (the illustrator was lazy, ha). At first, they always appeared together, but later the editor deliberately expanded the differences between them, and they often appear alone.

Ann

Ann is Canadian, with long golden hair, representing standard Western lineage. She is not in the same class as Jim and Li Lei, but she is good friends with Han Meimei. Additionally, she has another Chinese friend named Chen Hua (a girl). Ann's most important appearance in the book is her birthday party, where almost all the main characters attend. It was here that I learned about fried chicken and fish and chips. However, I didn't understand the concept until KFC appeared in our hometown...

Lin Tao

As a character appearing in the color pages, Lin Tao is still quite important. However, for a long time, he doesn't appear, and he is squeezed into the second group. As the only bespectacled boy (adults don't count), Lin Tao has the appearance of a wise intellectual. But don't think he is weak; in the third year of junior high school, he participated in the relay race and became a champion.

Sam and Bill

These two are Jim's best friends and benefit greatly from their association. Together, they raise the sports level of Class 1 by a notch. No doubt, they are mischievous troublemakers.

Wei Hua

Han Meimei's good friend. I noticed that L, H, J, and A, the four major figures, each have their own best friends. Wei Hua is another warm and virtuous Chinese girl, though she seems less mature than Han Meimei.

Ling Feng

Compared to other Chinese children, Ling Feng appears more mature and independent. He shows up frequently in the later stages, but what I remember most vividly is the first time he appeared when Jim's frisbee hit him on the head...

Bruce

An Australian boy who was delighted to see a snowy Christmas in China. Afterward, he made multiple appearances, the most interesting of which was a trip to Monkey Island with a large group of people.

Mary

She appeared much later, another Western-style long-haired beauty. I seem to recall her surname as Smith.

Other characters...

Besides the aforementioned 15 individuals, there are some other memorable classmates. For example, Ma Lili appeared in the first and second years of junior high school and was always the one shouting "Stand up." It seems she was Student No. 1 in Class 1. There were also Sun Huimin, Li Ming, Liu Ming, Fang Xiaoling, Bob White... etc.

Then, the complex relationships among the characters

In fact, it's a touching love story. Back then, I never even thought about this part of the content. Those who could pair people up must be precursors to fan fiction writers. But looking back, even young me seemed to notice that Li Lei always had ulterior motives, and Jim seemed to harbor resentment towards Li Lei for losing out to him. Additionally, Uncle Wang was very powerful, similar to Dr. Tea...

But I couldn't imagine how twisted the backstory really was!

Interpretation Version One:

Han Meimei is a beautiful, generous, intelligent, and pretty girl, while Li Lei and Jim, one Chinese and one foreigner; one handsome and lively, the other carefree and unrestrained. Adolescent emotions are hard to fathom. According to my analysis, little Han might have secretly fallen for Li Lei, while Jim, on the other hand, is deeply infatuated with Han Meimei, unable to extricate himself.

There is evidence to support this: once, during an extracurricular activity picking apples, Han Meimei and the cool guy under the tree exchanged glances and got carried away. At that moment, Jim, who had been ignored, shouted loudly: "Be careful!" From this, his concern can be seen clearly. Han Meimei merely replied with a simple "Thank you" before continuing her conversation with the cool guy...

A dramatic twist occurred in the second semester of the first year when two adorable Canadian twins, Lucy and Lily, transferred to the class. From then on, Li Lei fell in love with someone else and left Han Meimei. Throughout the second semester, dialogues in the textbook indeed featured Li Lei and the twins as the main characters. It was at this point that Han Meimei realized that Jim had silently watched over her for an entire semester. From then on, the two walked hand in hand toward a happy future...

Isn't this the plot of a classic Japanese drama?

Interpretation Version Two:

Jim likes Han Meimei, and Li Lei also likes Han Meimei, but Lucy likes Li Lei. My evidence is from a particular lesson that I remember clearly, something like "Can I borrow your ruler?" Han Meimei and Li Lei were sitting in the front row, and Han Meimei borrowed a ruler from Li Lei. Jim was watching them from behind with a very strange look, filled with envy and malice... Next to him sat Lucy, looking down. At the time, I thought it was because Lucy didn't want to witness this scene. As a girl, she couldn't express her feelings outwardly like Jim... So she bowed her head in sadness...

I always felt that Miss Gao and Jim Green's father had a thing going on, and when Li Lei first visited Jim's house and met his sister Kate Green, he had ulterior motives... Anyway, I've always particularly disliked Li Lei, finding him despicable and hypocritical. Also, Jim's family seemed super cool and wealthy, flying to the UK for Christmas, with his father even asking the principal for leave, who seemed to give him special treatment. They even had an apple tree at home...

Another vivid memory was when Li Lei and Jim played frisbee, and Jim hit Li Lei on the head. At the time, I thought Jim did it on purpose, especially since it happened not long after the ruler incident.

Isn't this the plot of a brain-dead Korean drama?

Interpretation Version Three:

Back then, I thought Tom's dad was the handsomest, followed by Jim's dad. Jim had a cute sister named Kate, while Lucy and Lily's dad was the ugliest! At the time, I wondered how such an ugly man could have two beautiful and adorable daughters? I believe Lucy and Lily were actually the children of their mom and Tom's dad.

Interpretation Version Four:

There's a shocking buried truth deeper within. Jim had a conversation with his sister:

Jim: What’s the time now?

Kate: It’s ten o’clock.

Jim: Let’s go to bed.

Kate: We go to bed together at ten.

Do you remember these?

1. The Green family had two pets, a parrot named Polly and a kitten named Mimi. Polly appeared so many times that it was astonishing, and it could even leave behind classic phrases, like "No news is good news!"

"Polly, can you spell its name?"

"Yes, I can. P-O-L-L-Y."

Since then, anything called Polly has been a parrot, and all parrots are called Polly.

2. There was a lesson asking Mrs. Sue Read's age, and she answered mysteriously: "Ah... it's a secret."

Listening to the recording, the whole class burst into laughter.

3. How to distinguish between the twins Lily and Lucy? Despite the illustrator's laziness, painting them identically, someone noticed that Lily was prettier, even though Lucy had more screen time. In the lesson about eating mooncakes, it was discovered that Lucy liked nuts, while Lily preferred egg yolk.

4. Miss Gao had long wavy hair, exuding femininity...

5. A woman seemed to have stomach pain and went to see a doctor. After pressing on her stomach repeatedly, the woman finally cried out "Ouch," and the doctor diagnosed her with having eaten raw fruit. Evil-minded people naturally saw evil things in this scene... But speaking of "ouch," ever since then, people used this pronunciation when laughing and playing in the corridor.

6. An unforgettable phone number, 6098724, six o nine eight seven two four.

Looking back at this set of textbooks, it was actually quite successful. From the dialogues to the plot settings to the lyrics, it was very suitable for beginners learning English. Through constant repetition, it left a deep impression, ensuring that at least self-introductions and helping people find their way were well understood. Some might call it formulaic, but at least it served its purpose (even if only minimally), unlike many later textbooks where students learn but don't know how to apply what they've learned.

The last lesson takes place at Ayers Rock in Australia, with just one sentence: "Goodbye everyone, Good luck!" Truly heartwarming, this is youth!
