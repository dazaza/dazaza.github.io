Studying abroad in Japan is highly appropriate right now.

by bridge2009 on 2009-12-04 14:20:42

There have been quite a few favorable news about studying in Japan since the beginning of this year. Starting from a few months ago, the duration of two types of visas for going to Japan, namely "study" and "attend school", has been extended by 3 months respectively. When graduates apply for work visas, their employment is no longer limited to being related to their major. Policy analysis: multiple benefits. Shen Jie from the study-abroad intermediary agency believes that after Japan launched the "300,000 international students acceptance plan" last year, the proportion of Chinese students among all international students in Japan will continue to grow. Zhang Zhe, who is currently studying international politics in Japan, is more interested in the employment channels provided by the Japanese government for international students to work in Japan after graduation. In the past, it was quite difficult for Chinese students to find jobs in Japan that were relevant to their majors, but now the scope of job hunting for international students has been broadened.

According to statistics, from 2009 to 2020, the Japanese government needs to achieve a 150% growth rate to meet the goal of accepting 300,000 international students. In order to achieve such a huge growth, both the government and schools will introduce various favorable policies in the near future to attract enough international students to study in Japan. At that time, whether it is the entrance examination, admission procedures, or immigration channels, international students will be able to feel the acceptance of Japanese universities and society towards them.

"Actually, studying in Japan does not necessarily mean staying there to work, but rather valuing the experience and background of studying abroad." Kevin, a current college graduate preparing application materials, said that currently in China's job market, the starting salary for Japanese language talents has ranked 9th, and the employment ratio has reached 7:1 (that is, one person chooses 7 positions). "Although the working pressure in Japanese companies is relatively high, the employee benefits are quite good." Kevin said that studying in Japan does not require spending too much time adapting to the intense cultural conflicts, and this experience may help him develop better in top Japanese companies in the future domestically.

In addition, for a large number of students studying in Japan to learn the language, the formal approval of the "integration of study and schooling" bill within Japan is also good news. This bill allows students going to Japan next year to study the language to directly obtain a student visa and enjoy the same welfare treatment as formal international students.

Language schools: Pathway to study abroad

Currently, most of the direct recruitment in China is from ordinary or private institutions in Japan, while it is very difficult for Chinese international students to apply to national public universities. They can participate in the training provided by language schools and various guidance for further education. After students have acquired certain abilities, they can then take exams such as the "International Students Unified Examination" or the independent entrance examination held by each university to enter their ideal institution. "National public universities are highly competitive locally in Japan, they do not lack applicants, nor will they make great efforts to expand overseas markets. Therefore, it is quite difficult for international students to apply directly from overseas." A study-abroad consultant said that after the introduction of favorable policies for studying in Japan, Chinese students may find it easier to apply for training at language schools and directly enter national public universities in Japan.

For a long time, Chinese students have had some misconceptions about Japanese language schools, thinking that language schools are just places to learn Japanese. In fact, Japanese language schools also guide students to pass the "Japanese International Student Test," which will affect the success or failure of their study applications. The "Japanese International Student Test" is a national unified exam taken by international students to enter universities, equivalent to China's "Gaokao." The test content not only includes Japanese, but also mathematics and comprehensive subjects (liberal arts examine geography, history, etc., while science examines physics, chemistry, etc.). However, some special programs of some special institutions do not require students to provide scores from this exam but instead assess their English ability.

The "Japanese International Student Test" exam has no testing sites in China, and the relevant specialized textbooks are also difficult to buy in China. That is to say, even if Chinese students have a high level of Japanese, they may still fail to pass this exam smoothly due to the lack of corresponding tutoring available domestically. Excellent Japanese language schools will provide tutoring related to the exam and use the specialized textbooks for the exam, guiding students on how to choose schools and majors, and even arrange pre-entrance exam training, including Japanese writing and interview tutoring.
