The Status of Microberlinia in the Global Timber Industry

It is precisely the long-standing rosewood sentiment of the Chinese nation that has naturally made Hainan Huanghuali and Small-Leaf Ebony the "Dragon and Phoenix" of rosewood. However, on this side of the vast ocean in Europe and America, the "King of Wood" belongs to another type — what the Europeans call "Imperial Wood," which we now commonly know as Microberlinia. Some merchants use sports arenas to vividly explain the difference: "If Hainan Huanghuali and Small-Leaf Ebony are champions of the Asian Games, then Microberlinia is the champion of the Olympics." From this statement, we can clearly understand the status of Microberlinia in the global timber industry. The dominant position of Microberlinia as a new generation of rosewood is gradually being established. Let us wait and see how Microberlinia leads the Chinese rosewood market to new heights!

Microberlinia wooden products are popular in the European and American markets.

Microberlinia possesses both the hard wood characteristics of Dalbergia cochinchinensis and the color and warmth of Dalbergia odorifera, and the finished furniture it produces surpasses that of Dalbergia cochinchinensis. It can be described as "naturally revealing elegance, with beauty still existing in its light," and it features naturally beautiful and varied deep black stripes, like clouds, like mountains, magical and ever-changing.

Wandering through craft stores, music instrument stores, and high-end coffee utensil stores in Europe and America, you will be pleasantly surprised to find that in the most prominent places on the counters, Microberlinia often occupies a place. Vividly carved Microberlinia wooden crafts; brightly colored, elegant-sounding Microberlinia guitars; smooth, naturally crafted Microberlinia golf clubs, etc., all become products that European and American consumers dream about. Various woodworking products made from Microberlinia have already swept through Europe and America, making European and American consumers flock towards them, as it has become a symbol of taste and status.

The noble origins of Microberlinia

The status of Microberlinia in Europe and America is akin to that of Hainan Huanghuali in China, but due to Microberlinia mainly being produced in Central America far from China, it entered the Chinese market relatively late. In 1996, Microberlinia began entering China, but its commercial use did not start until after 2000. By 2008, there were still relatively few companies using this material to produce furniture, so people's awareness of this wood was low.

When Microberlinia first entered the market, many consumers doubted whether Microberlinia was red sandalwood. Some even thought Microberlinia was fake red sandalwood, a name arbitrarily given by timber merchants to attract consumers. However, Yang Jiaju, the first drafter of the Chinese Redwood National Standard, had already "verified its identity." Microberlinia belongs to the family Fabaceae genus Dalbergia and is classified under the red sandalwood category in the "Redwood" national standard (GB/T 18107—2000 "Redwood"). Therefore, Microberlinia indeed comes from a "noble lineage" and is genuine redwood without a doubt.

The emerging dominance of Microberlinia as the new generation of redwood

Currently, it is difficult to find new Hainan Huanghuali, and Small-Leaf Ebony is countable on one hand. Dalbergia cochinchinensis is also nearing its "end phase." According to merchants, just Xianyou in Fujian consumes more than 6,000 tons of Dalbergia cochinchinensis per month, and conservatively estimated, the national annual consumption exceeds 100,000 tons. However, the raw materials of Dalbergia cochinchinensis from Vietnam and Laos are becoming increasingly scarce, and prices are gradually rising to levels that furniture manufacturers cannot afford.

In this situation, there must be new wood to "stabilize the situation," and the emerging dominance of Microberlinia as the new generation of redwood is evident.

The import volume of Microberlinia in China doubles every year. Nowadays, in regions such as Dayong in Zhongshan, Shanghai, and Jiangsu-Zhejiang, a large number of furniture factories are already using this wood to produce furniture. In the Dayong area alone, around 150 companies use Microberlinia to make classical furniture. Moreover, these merchants buy in bulk, purchasing dozens or even hundreds of tons at a time. Consumers are also gradually recognizing the furniture made from Microberlinia.

Microberlinia vs Dalbergia cochinchinensis

Both types of wood belong to the red sandalwood category, with similar materials, but Microberlinia surpasses Dalbergia cochinchinensis in density, grain, and oiliness. In terms of price, we can see the enormous appreciation potential of Microberlinia. When it first entered China, Microberlinia cost only a few thousand yuan per ton, but now it has risen to tens of thousands of yuan per ton. Its appreciation ability is undeniable.

Additionally, the most advantageous factor of Microberlinia is its relatively abundant resources. Dalbergia cochinchinensis is already nearing its "end phase," and currently, specifications thicker than 30 cm are already hard to find in the market. On the other hand, Microberlinia, which is gradually becoming well-known, has relatively rich resources, a boundary that Dalbergia cochinchinensis finds difficult to overcome compared to Microberlinia.
