What are the differences between axial flow fans and centrifugal fans?

by abcbubde on 2012-03-06 12:46:19

What are the differences between axial flow fans [http://www.bjsffj.com/] and centrifugal fans?

When a centrifugal fan operates, the power machine (mainly an electric motor) drives the impeller to rotate inside a volute casing. Air is drawn in through the inlet at the center of the impeller. Due to the dynamic effect of the blades on the gas, the pressure and speed of the gas are increased, and under the action of centrifugal force, the gas is thrown along the blade path towards the casing and discharged from the exhaust port. Since the flow of gas within the impeller mainly occurs in the radial plane, it is also called a radial flow fan.

In comparison, for the same power rating, an axial flow fan [http://www.bjsffj.com/] has a larger airflow but lower pressure, whereas a centrifugal fan has a smaller airflow but higher pressure.

Relatively speaking, the pressure of a centrifugal fan is greater than that of an axial flow fan, and in pipeline installation environments, centrifugal fans are generally selected.

Axial flow fans are usually installed at the rear end of the motor, on the non-shaft extension side. The airflow of an axial flow fan follows the axis of the motor, hence the name "axial flow fan."

When an axial flow fan operates, the power machine drives the impeller to rotate inside a cylindrical casing. Gas enters through the collector, gains energy as it passes through the impeller, increasing its pressure and speed, and then discharges axially. Axial flow fans can be arranged in three ways: vertically, horizontally, or inclined. The diameter of small impellers is only about 100 millimeters, while large ones can exceed 20 meters.

Small low-pressure axial flow fans [http://www.bjsffj.com/] consist of an impeller, casing, and collector, and are typically installed on the walls or ceilings of buildings; large high-pressure axial flow fans consist of a collector, impeller, streamline body, casing, diffuser cylinder, and transmission components. The blades are evenly distributed on the hub, with the number usually ranging from 2 to 24. The more blades there are, the higher the wind pressure; the installation angle of the blades is generally between 10 and 45 degrees, and the larger the installation angle, the greater the air volume and pressure. The main parts of an axial flow fan are mostly made by welding or riveting steel plates.
