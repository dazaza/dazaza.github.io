Maturing as a Programmer – How to Supercharge Your Career

One thing that has been clear since I was 11 years old is that I wanted to become a programmer. That year, I got my hands on a Commodore 64 machine and started writing BASIC programs on it. I loved programming so much that soon the time I spent coding surpassed the time I spent playing games. There’s something beautiful and pure about programming that nothing in real life can replace. Even with bugs, deadlines, and the frustration of failure, programming remains magical.

I went to engineering school, earned a degree in computer science, and then deepened my understanding of computer courses at the local University of Bergen over the next few years. Eventually, I grew tired of being a student and decided to look for a job.

In my career journey, most of my time has been spent as a programmer. In the first two years, I worked at a web design company where I handled everything from developing their publishing system on the server side (in Perl) to providing customer support.

After that, I co-founded a company with a few friends to develop a massive multiplayer online game. We spent nine years creating Darkfall, learning what it takes to build a large-scale game from scratch. It was hard work, but I learned a lot.

Four years ago, I joined a fairly large local consulting firm as a consultant, and last year, I seized the opportunity to start a new IT consulting company (Sonat Consulting AS) with some good friends and colleagues.

So, where am I now? I’m about to enter the next 40 years of my life. I’m reflecting on what I’ve accomplished so far and thinking about where I want to go from here.

Most programmers reach the peak of their careers around the age of 30. At this point, you still have the drive to learn, and you've accumulated enough experience to intuitively solve problems at work. This becomes your safety net for the rest of your career—if you stay in the field.

Some programmers continue coding into their forties, either because they are passionate about it or because they have enough free time to do so.

However, at some point, almost all programmers will hit their peak, lose motivation, and start looking for other opportunities. What are the possible directions for further development as a programmer? The traditional thought is that programmers can transition into project management or architecture. I believe there's a third path that offers the best career advancement.

This third path involves developing Business Process Improvement (BPI) skills.

As a programmer, you implement new systems and upgrade existing ones. Some of this work requires interacting with business experts, product customers, and potential clients. As you gain more experience, you’ll notice changes in yourself—you'll find better solutions more easily. You'll discover how understanding the broader business context can significantly aid problem-solving.

My advice is to broaden your perspective and take a step back to see the bigger picture. You need to recognize that the systems you're currently working on are just part of a larger business process—a supporting system. By using BPI methods like Lean or Six Sigma, you can analyze business processes, identify ways to optimize them, and then develop systems that integrate better with the business.

Many companies are willing to hire a BPI expert to suggest improvements to their production and overall processes, and then bring in some programmers to develop or enhance their systems to support these optimized processes. However, it’s rare to find someone who combines both roles.

Adding BPI skills to your repertoire isn't a new or extreme idea, but it’s often not considered as a career development option. Simply having these skills makes your boss or client happier. Based on my experience, we should recognize this as a viable career direction—a set of skills that various companies and organizations are actively seeking.

An IT consultant who knows how to program and understands business processes is highly sought after by any company and typically receives better compensation. Just developing the habit of looking at a system from different angles makes you a better programmer, let alone whether you directly or indirectly apply BPI techniques.

By mastering methods like Lean, Kanban, and Six Sigma, along with a holistic perspective and persistent efforts to improve systems and processes, you can earn trust and respect. If done correctly, your input in project planning meetings will be highly valued, and you'll have greater positive influence on the company's development and changes, rather than just being a purely technical resource.

Additionally, such technical expertise is generally not outsourced because these talents are usually used to oversee outsourced projects.

You might think: "Why should I learn these business process skills? I just want to code!" That's fine. But when I gained more BPI knowledge, your programming career reached its peak, then started to decline and became obsolete, eventually being outsourced. ;-)
