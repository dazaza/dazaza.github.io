You've grown up under my eyes, some stars worth noting in this fall's new films

by 303729213 on 2010-09-23 11:38:16

The girl who was just a moment ago a sexy little thing that could easily get lost in the crowd in Hollywood, is now clearly a wild lily blooming in the spring.

Gemma Arterton: The Spring of Wild Lilies

This year at Cannes Film Festival, Stephen Frears' new film "Tamara Drewe" was incredibly popular, with queues stretching to the beach for late-night screenings. Mike Leigh's "Another Year" was too somber, Woody Allen's "You Will Meet a Tall Dark Stranger" too slick, but "Tamara Drewe" was just right - lightly sarcastic, slightly bitter, clever without being overly so. The story follows Tamara, a beauty queen who returns to her hometown, loves impulsively, gets vain, loses herself, and finally leaves again with a clear direction, finding her honest self. The story goes through the cycle of summer, autumn, winter, and spring, stirring with anticipation in the season of restlessness, settling in the autumn harvest, lying dormant in the icy cold, and ending when all things begin anew, as does life and love. This film is like the warm spring breeze in England, clean, fresh, inviting. The vitality of the actress in the film is palpable, truly endearing. After watching this film, audiences don't remember the director, but feel that Gemma Arterton is the best discovery. A group of proper yet awkward British critics were moved to tears: this girl was just a moment ago a sexy little thing that could easily get lost in the crowd in Hollywood, and now she is clearly a wild lily blooming in the spring.

Unlike Rebecca Hall from an aristocratic family, Gemma, with her apple cheeks, freckles, and upturned nose, is a sparrow from the countryside, a Cinderella who has worked hard by the stove. Her family is working-class blue-collar; her father is a welder, her mother a cleaner. She was a somewhat insecure little girl because she was born with six fingers, though she had surgery at a young age. Her family didn't give her much "artistic" influence; before entering the Royal Academy of Dramatic Art, her "professional" training was mostly rehearsals with the middle school choir. Even after receiving a scholarship to the drama academy, the girl still felt somewhat insecure. She said that her classmates were well-read and articulate, but she lacked that scholarly air. This left her in a constant state of unease; while others "analyzed" and "interpreted" roles, she relied more on instinct. Her performance came from her body before her mind.

She wasn't the kind of girl who could rationally analyze everything perfectly. Watching Lars von Trier's films, she could confidently judge that the protagonist in "Breaking the Waves" was much better than Bjork's character in "Dancer in the Dark." This was an instinctive, intuitive preference, but this intuition itself was reliable. Many people wouldn't have thought that the carefree doll face we see on the red carpet was actually a very versatile student in school. She could play King Lear and Othello; the stage energy in this young body was a mystery waiting to be solved. She defined herself as a "character actor," but she also understood that "most people don't see it that way." Her young, beautiful body became a double-edged sword; three years ago, she was chosen by the 007 production team, and Hollywood only required her to be a sexy doll. If not in Britain, if she hadn't performed in plays in the West End of London, looking at the Hollywood films she acted in before "Tamara Drewe," it would be tragic: appearing for just a few minutes in "Quantum of Solace," having a brief romance with Mr. Bond before being killed, naked and covered in tar on Bond's bed; a dispensable flower vase in "Clash of the Titans;" or playing the princess saved by Jake Gyllenhaal in "Prince of Persia." In the past two years, she had contracts for 14 films, and thanks to a few Hollywood blockbusters, she became a sex fantasy object for many male viewers — ah, originally a piece of pure jade, the Americans were simply wasting talent.

Fortunately, she returned home, back to the stages of the West End, back to intellectual British cinema, like making a U-turn at the edge of a cliff. The sexy freckled girl turned into the biggest expectation of British cinema. Gemma was almost playing herself in "Tamara Dreve," or rather, reality shone through the movie: the once-insecure ugly duckling hoping to be attractive, wanting to be liked by men, men debating whether top writers are just pretentious talkers or grounded in reality, the girl realizing after experiences of love and loss that after the glitz and glamour, washed away makeup, the simple countryside is the most real. Returning isn't escaping, it's a better start. Tamara goes on the road again after returning home, just like Gemma coming back to the small world of British cinema, ahead lies a season full of spring sunshine — she is only 24, how young.

Talent ultimately isn't Superman; the sea of learning is boundless, fatigue is inevitable. Dave, James Franco's younger brother, said, "Every time we watch a movie, James falls asleep, and 'Inception' was no exception." Would he fall asleep watching 'Howl?'

James Franco: This Guy Really Has Talent

How can one describe James Franco? He was the morally ambiguous anti-hero in the Spider-Man trilogy, the young poet possessed by Allen Ginsberg's spirit in 'Howl,' and a recognized wunderkind in the movie industry—directing short films, writing novels, holding art exhibitions, and currently pursuing his Ph.D. in English Literature at Yale University. In an era where the terms 'wunderkind' and 'talented woman' have been devalued, the 32-year-old James Franco is one of the few young individuals who still deserves the adjective 'versatile.' And he truly makes one sincerely exclaim, 'This guy really has talent!'

Whether in mainstream commercial films like 'Spider-Man' and 'Rise of the Planet of the Apes,' or in independent art films like 'Howl,' James leaves the impression of someone embodying the archetype of 'fragile, sensitive, and unrestrained,' seemingly driven purely by intuition and talent. In fact, the true fuel behind these passionate performances is meticulous preparatory research. Perhaps due to reading extensively, James isn't the type who grabs a script and dives straight into the camera. He takes things seriously—so seriously that it's endearing. When a New York Times journalist approached him for an interview, James had done his homework—reading the journalist’s blog and Twitter posts, and even watching the short films about music and movies the journalist had previously made. It was as if he were preparing to interview the other person. His first words upon meeting were, “Our relationship is akin to you being the director and me the actor, together completing a conversation led by you. Each time I accept a script or meet a director, I first understand their previous works. But meeting you, I have no reference, so I can only read your blog and try to understand what you might be thinking.” As a fellow professional, I admire the New York Times journalist; interview subjects like James are rare among young actors today. He may have some old-fashioned rigidity and bookishness, but what's commendable is his earnest respect for people and work.

If preparation for interviews is already this thorough, one can imagine how much deskwork he must do before acting. Calling him a 'research-based actor' is entirely justified. Let's talk about his new film 'Howl.' This isn't a traditional biographical film but a recreation of an interview Allen Ginsberg gave in 1957, interwoven with the process of publishing 'Howl' and Ginsberg himself reciting 'Howl' in a smoky café. While the film was still raising funds, James had already immersed himself in Ginsberg's world, seeking out all kinds of visual materials related to Ginsberg, watching documentaries from those days, listening to interview recordings, reading biographies, consulting experts in American contemporary literature, and discussing the Beat Generation with them. Although he doesn’t have Ginsberg’s exaggerated Dumbo ears, he wore similarly exaggerated black-rimmed glasses—James not only studied him but tried to live in his world, searching for the sensitive and cruel youth, then letting his soul occupy his body across over 50 years. When 'Howl' was screened at the Berlin Film Festival, opinions on the film itself varied, but no one denied that James Franco was the most suitable to play Ginsberg—back then, Ginsberg fervently sought ways to connect with the world and find the most effective means of expression, discovering it through words; now, James continues Ginsberg’s quest, finding it on the silver screen. Across half a century, the two youths have reached the same destination, merging on the film reel. The director of 'Howl' said, "James was possessed by the soul of the Beat Generation: he works hard, is emotional, and urgently wants to know the best way to express himself."

Therefore, acting alone isn't enough. Hence, this big boy paints, writes novels, finishes his master's degree at NYU, and pursues his Ph.D. at Yale. He says that living in this world requires one to 'not be confused.' He doesn't want to chat about fame and gossip or scandals with others. For instance, when talking about 'Howl,' he would engage the journalist in discussions about Ginsberg rather than how he played Ginsberg. This recognized wunderkind is fundamentally a perpetually unsatisfied student.

Regardless of talent or diligence, wunderkinds are ultimately not supermen; the sea of knowledge is vast, and fatigue is inevitable. His younger brother Dave said, "Every time we watch a movie, James falls asleep, and 'Inception' was no exception." Hmm, I wonder if he would fall asleep watching 'Howl?'

After flying far and wide, she will return to London next, find a small house, and collaborate with her father to rehearse 'Twelfth Night'—London, theater, is her habitat.

Rebecca Hall: Queen of the Stage

'The Town' premiered at the Venice Film Festival. After watching the movie, the public expressed regretful sighs about Ben Affleck: average script, averagely directed film, and his own performance was unremarkable. Only the lead actress Rebecca Hall was stunningly impressive, leaving people unsure whether to praise or lament her performance—praising her as the sole brilliance in this mediocre film, and lamenting that the film was too bland to highlight her enduring grace and beauty.

My impression of her was her continuous performances of Shakespeare and Chekhov at the Old Vic Theatre in London. That summer, she performed from 'The Winter's Tale' to 'The Cherry Orchard,' sometimes as the wronged queen, other times as the eldest daughter silently sacrificing for her family. This tall, slender girl seemed calm and composed but held the audience's eyes like a magnet, becoming the absolute center of the stage, overshadowing Ethan Hawke who shared the stage with her. Born into a theatrical family, this girl naturally followed in her father's footsteps, seemingly unintentionally, yet fatefully destined. Her mother was an opera singer, her father a director. At nine, she made a cameo appearance in a mini-series directed by her father. The next year, her father brought ten-year-old her to an empty theater, held her on the stage, and asked her: Do you want to be a child star or an actress? The ten-year-old girl thought for a moment and said, "I want to be an actress." "Then," her father said, "you should enjoy your childhood fully and grow up like any ordinary girl."

For the next ten years, she indeed kept a distance from the entertainment circle, received no acting training, joined no performing groups, and even went to Cambridge University to study English Literature. It was Woody Allen's assistant director who found this Cambridge girl after searching high and low, insisting she play the lead role Vicky in 'Vicky Cristina Barcelona.' "I saw her for the first time when she was 21, beautiful enough to make me dizzy. Her beauty doesn't belong to red carpets or magazine covers; those are too distant and fantastical. She is different; her beauty carries a tangible realism that makes one feel refreshed and compelled to appreciate." Such a girl doesn't wear sunglasses to avoid cameras; instead, she might appear in a plain face in Chelsea district's small cafes on many unremarkable mornings, pleasing to the eye and completely natural. What moves people about her is that understated charm.

Just like in 'Vicky Cristina Barcelona,' Scarlett Johansson embodies a certain biological instinctual sensuality, Penelope Cruz a wild untamed passion, and Rebecca is the safest and most composed one. She is the kind of well-bred girl who occasionally indulges in whimsy but maintains control over her direction. Vicky's personality could easily come off as worldly and calculating, but luckily it's her who plays it, with a natural scholarly demeanor and ladylike quality, expressing a hundred twists of melancholy, which makes Scarlett seem less convincing in comparison.

It is this subtle balance that creates the girl's irreplaceable appeal. She dislikes gossip interviews, refuses to take magazine photos, and is tired of reporters probing into her relationship with director Sam Mendes, speculating whether she interfered in his marriage leading to his divorce from Kate Winslet. Despite disliking it all, she handles it with a smile and wit, saying, "Sometimes one must endure unpleasant things to gain the freedom to do what they like." See, she is such a clear-headed and measured girl; her life, personality, and performances all carry a sense of knowing when to advance and retreat. Filming 'The Town,' mischievous Americans intended to criticize her London accent, but the girl spoke perfect American English, surprising everyone. She casually explained, "My mom is American, and I often went to New York and Los Angeles when I was young, feeling equally at home in New York and London." Floating between America and Britain, New York and London, a nomadic life was something she grew accustomed to from a young age, especially in the last couple of years. 'The Winter's Tale' toured worldwide, followed by filming 'The Town' in Boston. Over the past twenty months, Rebecca has wandered with two suitcases, not even having her own place in London, storing all her belongings with a storage company. After flying for so long, the weary bird returns to its nest. Next, she will go back to London, find a small house, and collaborate with her father to rehearse 'Twelfth Night'—London, theater, is her haven.

Woody Allen, Sam Mendes, Ben Affleck—the renowned directors she has worked with, young and old—all consistently praised her: excellent acting, even better temperament. Adaptable, non-contentious, such composure comes from genuine confidence, and the bearing of a 28-year-old stage queen originates precisely from this.
