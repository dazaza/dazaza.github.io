How should stock investors view these central SOEs that are heavily involved in derivatives? And how should they deal with listed central SOE companies that engage in derivatives trading?

Stock Knowledge:

I. The Mission of Central SOEs

Central SOEs should shoulder the mission of national strategic economy rather than chasing high profits. Stock forums wonder why high-ranking officials of these SOEs have not noticed this. Or perhaps they do understand but have their own difficulties. What are these difficulties?

II. Unclear Rewards and Punishments

The high salaries, high benefits, and high bonuses of the heads of central SOEs make us ordinary people drool. All can be forgiven if their performance is good, but it doesn't make sense when they still receive high salaries and bonuses even when their performance is negative. Someone comes out to claim rewards when there are achievements, but when problems arise, responsibilities become unclear! So how were your bonuses distributed initially? Whoever benefits should bear the responsibility - such a simple matter, could the central SOEs really not understand this?

III. Absence of Supervision

From the coal mine explosion in Heilongjiang which occurred a week after the safety supervision department left, we can see that supervision in some areas and departments of our current society has become almost non-existent. The floating losses of 68 central SOEs are not accidental; events like the 1997 Zhuzhou Smelter zinc futures incident, the 2004 China Aviation Oil Singapore incident, and the 2005 Liu Qibing National Reserve Copper incident all reflect this. But where is the supervision? Should we also consider the inaction of supervisory bodies in the future?

IV. Habitual Losses

Almost all we hear about are the losses of central SOEs. Why are there so few central SOEs making money? Such coincidences have been happening for many years, but we hope it doesn't continue.

V. Irrelevant Appointments

Take last year as an example, a person who couldn't make it in football suddenly became the CEO of a state-owned listed company. I would die before believing that he could run the company well. But somehow, he became the CEO. Impressive, isn't it?

The process of buying and selling stocks is as follows: When buying or selling stocks, you must specify whether you're buying or selling, the name (or code) of the stock, the quantity, and the price. This order is only valid on the day it is placed. The content of the order includes the abbreviation (code) of the stock you want to buy or sell, the quantity, and the price at which you wish to buy or sell the stock. The abbreviation of a stock usually consists of three to four Chinese characters, and the stock code is six digits long. When placing an order, the stock code and abbreviation must match. Additionally, there are certain regulations regarding the quantity of stocks being traded: the quantity for buying stocks must be a whole multiple of 100, whereas the quantity for selling does not need to be a whole multiple of 100. There are four ways to place an order: counter submission, telephone automatic order, computer automatic order, and remote terminal order.
