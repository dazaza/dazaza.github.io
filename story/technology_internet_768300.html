Twitter Rejected Facebook's Acquisitions Twice, Want to Know the Reasons?

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 20:10:14

In his new book, "Hatching Twitter: A True Story of Money, Power, Friendship, and Betrayal," New York Times reporter Nick Bilton reveals that Facebook CEO Mark Zuckerberg made two official offers to purchase Twitter through its co-founder Jack Dorsey, rather than just one as previously reported by the media.

TechCrunch editor Matthew Panzarino, after getting an early read of the new book, discovered something particularly noteworthy mentioned within.

At the end of October 2008, shortly after Dorsey was ousted as CEO and reassigned to a powerless role as chairman of the board, the other two co-founders of Twitter, Ev Williams and Biz Stone, were invited to visit Facebook headquarters and hold a meeting with the company's CEO, Zuckerberg. What was Facebook's intention? Clearly, it was to acquire Twitter.

Bilton explained in the book that Zuckerberg had been working with Dorsey for months, attempting to arrange for Facebook to completely acquire Twitter. However, because Dorsey was removed from his position as CEO, Zuckerberg's previous plans came to nothing. In an email written by Zuckerberg to Dorsey, he listed out point by point why the combination of Facebook and Twitter would make sense.

Among these reasons, there was even a threat that Facebook would "develop products similar to Twitter in the future." This email from Zuckerberg essentially hinted subtly: either sell to us, or we will clone your product.

During the talks attended by Williams, Stone, and Zuckerberg, the two co-founders of Twitter valued their company at $500 million.

Zuckerberg was not shocked by this valuation, as his communications with Dorsey had already made him aware that this was the price that should be considered. But Facebook ultimately did not acquire Twitter. In an email written by Williams to the company's board of directors, the reasons for Twitter rejecting Facebook's acquisition offer were disclosed.

Bilton partially quoted the content of this email in the book, which wrote: "For me, there are three reasons to sell this company. First, the acquisition price is good enough, or the valuation reflects the company's future (we often say Twitter is a billion-dollar company. I think the value is many times more than that.) Second, there is an urgent, real threat from competitors. (Nothing can pose a war-like threat to Twitter) Third, you choose to work with other excellent people. (I never use Facebook. I have many concerns about the people at Facebook and how the company conducts business.)"

There are several interesting points in this section by Williams. First, by 2008, Twitter's board of directors already viewed the company as a billion-dollar enterprise, and Williams believed that Twitter's valuation far exceeded $1 billion.

At the time, Twitter only had over 11 million users. In Twitter's current prospectus, the company's valuation has reached $11.9 billion. That is to say, even when the fundamentals were average in 2008, Twitter's management already had great confidence in the company.

The reason Williams had such confidence in the company at the time was mainly due to the fact that there were no competitors for Twitter in the market, and no company could "pose a substantial threat to Twitter." Twitter's concept and its execution were very unique, making it difficult even for a company like Facebook to imitate and succeed.

Bilton also revealed in the book that Yahoo! once attempted to acquire Twitter for $12 million but was politely refused by the then newly established Twitter. At the time, Twitter only had 250,000 active users.

On the other hand, Dorsey harbored conflicting feelings about Facebook's acquisition offer. The book pointed out that Dorsey had expressed, "As long as the price is fair, whether acquired or not, it will be a successful story." At the time, Dorsey had just been ousted as CEO and had little chance to reclaim control of the company.

Looking back now, Dorsey's thoughts at the time were wrong, but they were supported by shareholder and venture capitalist Peter Fenton at the time. The reason was simple; he thought Facebook's offer was reasonable.

However, Twitter's board agreed with Williams' viewpoint and rejected Facebook's acquisition offer. Subsequently, Zuckerberg desperately tried to ingratiate himself with Dorsey but refused to offer him a title as a product manager. Ultimately, Dorsey did not join Facebook, Twitter went public, and Dorsey regained voting rights within the company.
