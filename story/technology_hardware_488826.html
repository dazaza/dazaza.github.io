I was in high school that year.

by tpvtaoirp on 2011-09-13 19:08:35

That year, I was in high school, and it was a season of great hardship for my family. As if our house wasn’t already leaking during the endless rain, we were originally poor, and then we suffered a major hailstorm that left all the crops in the field damaged beyond repair. This meant the year’s harvest was ruined. My father's hair turned gray overnight, not just because of his crops, but also due to the difficult decision of whether I should drop out of school.

No matter what, the child must not drop out of school halfway. This was my father's promise to me and himself. Because of our financial difficulties, I had to carefully manage every penny of my meager living expenses at school. I ate the cheapest food in the cafeteria and only half-filled my stomach with each meal. Even so, the little money I had would always run out before the month ended, leaving me powerless against the costs of life.

I never participated in any voluntary activities organized by my classmates. My stinginess and tight-fistedness were my Achilles' heel, often leaving me defenseless when they attacked me. But I had my own pride: my academic achievements were consistently top-notch, and my basketball skills were unmatched in the school. They allowed me to always stand tall with my head held high.

The school was going to host a basketball game, and as the main player of my class, I had to participate. However, a problem lay before me: what shoes should I wear for the match? I envied my classmates who wore dazzling white athletic shoes—Adidas, Peak—if I could run and play on the basketball court in such a pair of shoes, how free and heroic it would be!

But I only had two pairs of cloth shoes: the one I was wearing and the new one in my bag, both made by my mother herself. Although these shoes were stitched with care, I never felt comfortable in them. They were only suitable for the mountain paths of my hometown; once on the city roads marked with various signs, my feet felt like they were stepping on burning coals. People always stared at my shoes first, and I noticed their feet were clad in beautiful shoes. At that moment, I felt deflated. A pair of shoes revealed my hard-to-talk-about background: a poor country bumpkin. Once, when my father came to school, my classmates shouted: "Your dad is looking for you at the school gate." I asked them how they knew it was my father, and they said, "Because he wears shoes different from yours." Then they burst into uncontrollable laughter, mocking laughter that could tear a person apart. I looked down at my shoes, symbols of poverty and embarrassment, and wished I could kick them all the way to Antarctica, never to return to my feet again.

So I decided to ask my father for a pair of sneakers. Despite knowing how expensive they were, and despite being usually obedient and understanding towards my parents, those nights, I only dreamed of one thing: I was wearing blindingly white sneakers, flying across the basketball court. I kept dunking, dunking nonstop. It was as if I had grown wings, I was flying!

At that time, I didn't know our family had suffered a disaster. In the phone call, I still joked around with my father: "Dad, your son is in dire straits!" Father didn't mention the calamity at home, pretending to be relaxed, saying, "Don't worry, Dad will send you money tomorrow, and everything will be alright."

I didn't expect my father to personally deliver the money. Usually, he sent it through the post office. I complained about his lack of foresight, not knowing how to calculate costs, as the round-trip fare would cost much more than the remittance fee. But my father said he had come along with someone else and hadn't spent any money. What about the return trip? I was still nagging about my father's clumsiness, but he didn't get angry. He had never been angry in his entire life. With a simple smile, he said, "Isn't this a good chance to see you?"

Dreams are dreams, but reality brought me back to the truth. When I told my father I wanted a pair of sneakers, he seemed very troubled. He said he hadn't brought extra money, stuttering and apologizing. "If you play well, your classmates will cheer for you regardless of what shoes you wear," he said, though even he felt this consolation was weak, speaking softly as if muttering to himself.

I cried, right in front of my father. I could have foreseen the result—my parents had no spare money for such luxuries—but I still cried, feeling deeply wronged. My father stood there, continuously rubbing his hands, like a child who had done something wrong, appearing at a loss. Without saying goodbye to my father, I turned and went back to school.

Thus, the dream of sneakers was completely shattered. I thought I couldn't embarrass myself in front of the whole school, couldn't let everyone laugh at me because of my cloth shoes, so I decided to quit the basketball team. The teacher found me and asked for the reason I was quitting, and I stammered, saying I wanted to focus more on studying.

In fact, they had no idea how much I longed to run on the basketball court!

Just the day before the match, the gatekeeper called, saying someone was looking for me. At the school gate, I saw my father. In his hand was a brand-new pair of sneakers, dazzlingly white, making my eyes water. I thought I was still dreaming until my father urged me to try them on, confirming it was real. Though not a famous brand, they captivated me immediately. They were truly handsome—I called them "white angels." I couldn't help asking my father, how could he afford to buy them?

My father said that since the day he heard my wish, he couldn't resist going to the mall to inquire about the prices of those sneakers, preparing to go home and fetch the money to buy them for me. But the price of each pair made him gasp in shock. Standing in front of the counter, he stared at those ugly sneakers, actually imagining his son's wish. Just then, people were moving goods, finding my father in the way, so they kept pushing him aside. My father was used to working, disliking their childish manner of handling goods. He couldn't resist helping them move the goods, doing three times the work of one person. After finishing, the boss insisted on giving him some compensation, but he refused, saying he had only helped a little and shouldn't accept payment. But the boss insisted, and he pointed to the sneakers on the shelf, scratching his head and shyly telling the boss, "I'll work for a week for you, can I exchange it for that pair of sneakers?"

That week was an incredibly arduous trial for my father. Physical labor wasn't the problem; the key was the issue of eating and sleeping. Since he had very little money, my father could only eat one meal a day, and each meal consisted of just one steamed bun. Having nowhere to stay at night, my father had to sleep under a bridge, covered in mosquito bites...

And so, the shoes were finally obtained. My father proudly recounted the story, but I shed tears again. My father panicked: "What's wrong? Not satisfied with the style?" He could change it... I shook my head vigorously, saying I was satisfied. "You're already a big boy, don't cry all the time," my father patted my shoulder, saying he needed to rush home or wouldn't make it back by night. More than 100 miles away, my father insisted on walking back.

I got anxious, grabbing my father's hand, asking which mall it was so I could return the shoes and buy him a bus ticket home. My father refused stubbornly, and I hugged him, saying, "Dad, trust me, without these shoes, I can still walk with my head held high."

At that moment, I felt I had suddenly grown up, truly grown up.

In that game, I played in plain cloth shoes. I ran tirelessly, shot incessantly, and scored continuously, unstoppable, as if I had wings, flying. While running, I thought of my father; while shooting, I thought of my father. I wanted my father to know that I was his best son.

From then on, I earned a nickname at school, just like Jordan: "Flyer."

After that, I studied even harder. Finally, in the summer of the next year, I was admitted to the university I had always dreamed of. I became the golden phoenix that flew out of our mountain village, I really could fly, and it was my father who gave me strong and confident wings.

Father, my only wings are with you. Only you can make me soar.
