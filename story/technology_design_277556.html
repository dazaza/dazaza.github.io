No. 51 In 2002, Zhu Rongji made a trip to India aimed at promoting a new era in Sino-Indian cooperation – he made a special point of visiting the Bangalore headquarters of Infosys – one of India’s most successful software companies. "You are number one in software. We are number one in hardware. If we put these together, we are the world’s number one."

No. 52 This conjunction, if it occurs, will have major historical effects – India and China constitute the two fastest growing economies in the world and together are home to over a third of the planet’s population. While the first wave of Asia’s rise was powered by the small ‘Tiger Economies’ (Korea, Hong Kong, Singapore and Taiwan), it is increasingly clear that Asia’s giants will supply the engine that powers the next wave.

No. 53 For China and India to grow they need to start learning from each other. China has the hardware but still needs to concentrate on ‘software’. Criticism one often hears of Shanghai: India’s software sector hobbled by its faulty hardware. Bangalore, the country’s ‘silicon plateau’, is groaning under the weight of a crumbling infrastructure. India and China are weak in precisely those areas where the other is strong.

No. 54 Sino-Indian connections are already starting to occur. Most of the big players in India’s software industry – TCS, Infosys, Satyam, Wipro – have already set up in China.

No. 55 In 2005, relations were further enhanced when Wen Jiabao visited India in order to forge a strategic partnership aimed at settling the border dispute and enhancing political, military and trade relations.

No. 56 On his visit, Wen reiterated Zhu’s remark: “Cooperation is just like two pagodas (temples), one hardware and one software. Combined, we can take the leadership position in the world. When the particular day comes, it will signify the coming of the Asian century of the IT industry.”

No. 57 Indian prime minister made what is perhaps an even stronger statement: Together, India and China could reshape the world order… Columnist Thomas Friedman wrote in the New York Times that when historians look back, it is the simultaneous rise of China and India that will be the big story of the beginning of this millennium – Outsourcing of course has a crucial role in this story.

On the one hand – in the business sphere, supportive (mostly Western companies that are outsourcing work to Asia) – in the realm of politics, popular commentary growing fear of outsourcing – Western Backlash – this backlash can be seen nightly on one of the most popular shows on CNN – hosted by commentator Lou Dobbs and titled Exporting America – Export jobs overseas Political friction.. China and India are stealing our jobs – a popular refrain especially surrounding an election.

No. 62 The Future of China software offshore Outsourcing. Despite political pressures, tendencies towards short term protectionism (recent election in America bad for China) barring a major catastrophe, Outsourcing is unstoppable – what we are seeing now is only the tip of the iceberg.

No. 64 To understand why this is so, return to the question: Why outsource in the first place?

No. 65 As we said at the beginning, the best way to understand outsourcing is to look at its opposite – the vertically integrated firm – one of the most famous texts on this Ronald Coase – The nature of the firm.

No. 66 Firms exist because of transaction costs… it costs to get things on the open market – there are costs to contract out .. more economically efficient to hire and do things in-house.

No. 67 Internet, where information can travel free of cost, has dramatically lowered transaction and partnering costs around the globe.

No. 68 With the reduction and sometimes even elimination of transaction costs, companies are now increasingly turning to outsourcing – this allows them to enhance competitiveness shedding peripheral tasks to specialize in those areas that generate most value.

No. 69 Forrester research has shown that companies can save 50% on their IT bills if they outsource.

No. 70 Stats such as this make sense of research like McKinsey which estimate offshore outsourcing will increase by 30 to 40% in the next year.

Reasons include Risk mitigation (don’t put all your eggs in one basket) Low cost talent pool (India is running out of people) Access east asia..
