{Xiaoli Film Studio} "Mulan": The Pleasure of Being Covered in Dramatic Clichés

by nomyliu996 on 2009-11-29 16:23:25

A household topic is both an advantage and a disadvantage. While it removes the audience's acceptance barriers to characters and plot, it also puts forward higher requirements for creators, who need to see more unexpected surprises from this character. But in this aspect, "Mulan" is indeed unremarkable. Ma Chucheng is a director suitable for shooting films facing post-90s audiences. However, when he grasps the deep-intentioned topic with such an immature mentality, he can only cover his creative incompetence with melodrama. The scene of slaughtering prisoners of war should be the climax of this theme. The intention to make people cry is obvious, but here the plot design and scene scheduling are extremely ridiculous. Singing has already been very melodramatic, and letting a group of lambs waiting to be slaughtered find the camera with various expressions in the singing, I finally burst into laughter out of sadness, TV series. When Hu Jun played the Rouran King retreating troops at the end, a friend beside me said a sentence that made me laugh out loud: "I was driven away by your absurdity!" Ma Chucheng turned a tragic play into a comedy!

The more melodramatic plot comes from the love story between Zhao Wei and Chen Kun. In fact, this is not a play that tests acting skills. Mulan herself is dressed as a man, so distortion is natural. As for those grimacing expressions that involve many facial muscles, they have nothing to do with acting skills. So I am confused by what some critics said about Zhao Wei's breakthrough in acting skills before. What did they see? In fact, I really hate the cross-dressing drama in domestic film and television works. The roles that are easily distinguishable by gender with just normal intelligence are accepted by everyone as a clumsy gender swap. Look at Dustin Hoffman's "Tootsie," look at how he plays a woman, or then we can discuss acting.

Chen Kun's Wen Tai is an interesting character, not because the character itself is interesting, but because the embarrassment and boredom given to this character by the director makes me feel interested. First of all, the sacrifices he made to become "stronger" for Mulan seem very absurd to me. Later on, the opposite-sex performances between Chen Kun and Zhao Wei made me feel a strong sense of gender dislocation. Chen Kun's overly feminine demeanor, resentful eyes, pitiful appearance, and constant tears in front of Zhao Wei dressed as a man are simply too funny, making me reach the point of "I laugh every time I see you." Not to mention those brain-damaged lines like "I count stars every day," there is no poetry in their hearts, yet they still try to come up with poetic lines, resulting in losing even basic logic, leaving only a mouthful of ashes. It would have been better if Mulan's identity as a woman wasn't revealed so early and they had explored some homosexual themes instead.

However, where "Mulan" falls short compared to "Painted Skin" is that "Painted Skin" has a lot of small-scale emotional stories with little interpretative difficulty, while "Mulan" carries a grand anti-war theme. For directors like Ma Chucheng, such a theme is difficult to grasp, giving people the feeling of a small horse pulling a big cart. From Mulan's first participation in battle, she shows a strong psychological aversion to killing, and this becomes a psychological thread running throughout the film. Ma Chucheng may be afraid that the audience won't understand, so he constantly strengthens the protagonist's psychology through characters' actions and dialogues. And when all this is based on Mulan's numerous war achievements and countless enemy killings, this verbal anti-war declaration seems very pale and affected. Moreover, this thread is spread out flatly without repetition, and the character's growth only stays on the accumulation of casualties under the blade. If Mulan goes through a process from instinctively avoiding bloodshed to becoming a numb killing machine and then having her anti-war emotions awakened, the film would gain more layers and thematic elevation.

In terms of film genre, "Mulan" belongs to a war movie, but the war scenes are not grand. However, being used to the super-realistic martial arts style that defies gravity, the hard and realistic style here gives the first local battle an overwhelming momentum. The last luring-enemy battle, from the archers' sweep-shooting, shield soldiers' injuries to the infantry and cavalry's final attack, although it has the shadow of "Red Cliff", the cold weapon warfare mode is still quite qualified. But "Mulan" and "Painted Skin" have the same feature, that is, the limited war or action scenes only exist as gimmicks, and what truly supports the main storyline is the patched-up soap opera plots, making the film resemble a TV series.

When God closes a door, He always opens a window. When domestic comedies increasingly lose their humor, I find new joy in the bloody pseudo-blockbusters. I admit that "Mulan" brought me happiness, but this happiness is merely a byproduct of the creators' earnest sentimentality and deliberate nobility. Therefore, the more I see them seriously concocting these bloody plots, the more I indulge in the joy brought by this overflow of bad taste.
