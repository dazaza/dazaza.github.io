Children's teeth, have you neglected them?

by chongwuyin5r on 2012-03-07 06:13:24

Do you enjoy "kissing" your dogs? But as soon as they open their mouths, the unbearable bad breath makes you hesitant to get close. Many pet owners must have this problem. Just like humans, dogs can also develop bad breath if their mouths are not cleaned for a long time. This is especially serious for dogs that eat home-cooked meals. One of the main reasons for bad breath is the lack of proper and timely cleaning of the dog's teeth. Tartar and calculus accumulate on the teeth, causing them to emit an unpleasant odor. In severe cases, it can lead to gum inflammation and periodontal disease. Leftover food particles trapped in the gaps between the dog's teeth can also cause bad breath, which over time can lead to cavities. If a dog has poor dental health, its appetite will naturally be affected. Long-term issues can affect its digestive function and overall health.

Dental Care

Unclean teeth in dogs can lead to problems such as pus discharge from the gums and gingivitis. Once tartar forms (the brownish calculus at the junction of the tooth and root), it becomes difficult to clean. However, if left untreated, dogs may start losing teeth at a young age. Dental care should begin from puppyhood.

There are two methods for brushing:

Method One: Use a soft towel or sterilized gauze to wipe the dog's teeth clean. Then use cotton swabs dipped in garlic juice to rub the teeth and gums. If there are leftover food particles stuck in the gaps between the teeth, use small tweezers to remove them. The specific method is as follows: Take a piece of gauze soaked in saline water and wrap it around your index finger. Wipe the gums and gaps between the teeth in sequence, gently so as not to injure the gums. Once the dog gets used to it, use a dedicated pet toothbrush with some pet toothpaste to brush its teeth.

Method Two: Use a dog-specific toothbrush and toothpaste (available at pet stores) to clean the teeth and gums, but do not scrub back and forth. The specific brushing method is similar to how humans brush their teeth, cleaning each tooth and massaging the gums one by one along the tooth gaps. First, find a time when both you and your dog are relaxed. In the initial days, simply give it a quick brush as you normally would when holding it. Gently stroke its cheeks for one or two minutes, offering appropriate protection and praise. Place a little pet toothpaste on your fingers and let the dog taste it. After a few days, it will become accustomed to this method. Then consider using a toothbrush or finger brush. Apply a little toothpaste on the toothbrush, gently lift the upper lip, and slowly brush the teeth and gums in circular motions. Gradually increase the number of teeth brushed daily, but do it slowly, without brushing too far away. Increase the brushing time to about 30 seconds, ensuring every corner is reached. After brushing, give your dog some praise and rewards. If dog-specific toothbrushes are unavailable in your area, a child's soft-bristle toothbrush can be used as a substitute. However, never use human toothpaste for dogs, as it can cause digestive system discomfort. Dog-specific toothpaste is made with non-foaming transaminase components, making it safe even if swallowed. Pet toothpaste is designed with flavors that dogs like, such as chicken and beef, so while dogs might initially be unaccustomed to brushing, they won't completely reject it. Follow the instructions on the pet toothpaste label, using the appropriate amount of toothpaste to brush each tooth surface. In the beginning, dogs may try to escape due to unfamiliarity, so owners need to patiently help them adapt. Brushing your dog's teeth is a gradual process; don't rush or expect immediate perfection. Remember, dogs are stubborn animals. If they perceive something as frightening, they will continue to feel that way. Therefore, starting gently is important. Over time, brushing will become a habitual routine, and they will fully accept it.

Common Dental and Oral Diseases:

Dog oral and dental care often requires the attention of the owner. If you want your beloved dog to have strong teeth and good health, you should not neglect any health care measures. When you notice dental problems in your dog, it may already be quite severe. Below are some common dental and oral diseases in dogs:

1) Tartar and Calculus

Tartar mainly consists of food debris and bacteria, which are the culprits behind bad breath and periodontal disease. Once tartar forms on a dog's teeth, it becomes very difficult to remove.

Canine tartar refers to hardened mineral deposits usually formed near the base of the teeth and the gum line, potentially affecting areas above and below the gum line. It gradually spreads throughout the mouth, causing gingivitis or providing a rough surface for plaque to adhere. It is formed by the calcification of dental plaque, which consists of bacterial clusters attached to the tooth surface.

Calcium in a dog's saliva attaches to the tooth surface and combines with plaque, developing into tartar. Regular removal of plaque prevents tartar formation. Once tartar starts to accumulate, its surface attracts more plaque, which after calcifying deposits on the existing layer of tartar, increasing its volume. Hence, tartar is always covered by plaque. Bacterial reproduction releases toxins that gradually erode the inner side of the teeth, leading to dangerous toxin ingestion in dogs. Additionally, bacteria and their toxins irritate the periodontal tissue, causing gingivitis characterized by red, swollen gums and bad breath. Further progression can lead to periodontitis, characterized by inflamed periodontal tissues, receding gums, exposed roots, loose teeth, pain during chewing, or difficulty biting down. Therefore, removing tartar is the most basic method for preventing and treating periodontal disease.

This isn't the worst case; some dogs have embarrassing breath before they even open their mouths. Some even lose teeth at just 3 or 4 years old.

For occlusal and masticatory teeth, the effect of periodontal bacteria is relatively weak. Thus, mastering correct care knowledge and providing comprehensive oral hygiene, care, and prevention for your dog is crucial.

Treatment Method: Like plaque, tartar must be removed mechanically. Since tartar is harder and more difficult to remove, professional veterinarians must perform dental cleaning for significant results.

2) Gingivitis

Gingivitis is the precursor to periodontal disease. At the junction of the gums and teeth, known as the "gingival sulcus," because dogs do not know how to clean their own teeth, after countless meals, their gums accumulate a lot of food residue, leading to excessive bacterial growth. Once bacteria invade the gums, they cause inflammation and pain.

3) Periodontal Disease

Canine periodontitis involves acute or chronic inflammatory processes of the periodontium, including the gingival margin, periodontal pocket, periodontal ligament, and alveolar bone. Periodontal disease caused by bacterial activity includes adherent plaque and calculus on the tooth surface. The mechanical stimulation and release of bacterial toxins from plaque and calculus along the gingival margin cause soft tissue inflammation. If not promptly treated, the inflammation further develops, causing recession of the gingiva and alveolar bone, leading to gingival shrinkage, alveolar bone resorption, and loose teeth. Painful chewing or ineffective biting may occur. Additionally, damage to the gingival tissue, malocclusion, low calcium diet, or bacterial invasion of the gums during the disease process can easily lead to this condition.

1. Diagnosis

Symptoms include bad breath, drooling, red gums, swelling, softening, shrinking, exposed tooth roots, loose teeth, and visible purulent secretions or pus discharge upon squeezing the gums. Radiological imaging shows localized bone dissolution in the alveoli, indicating apical abscesses.

2. Prevention and Treatment

Under anesthesia, remove plaque, calculus, and food debris from the dog’s teeth. Pay attention to clearing plaque and calculus from under the gumline and on the tooth root surface (i.e., within the periodontal pocket). Polish all tooth surfaces as much as possible to prevent reaccumulation and adhesion of plaque and calculus. Extract loose teeth. For chronic, treatment-resistant periodontitis, relevant teeth should be extracted. If the gums are hypertrophic, excess tissue can be removed using electrocautery. Postoperatively, apply iodine glycerin to the dog’s gums. Systemically administer antibiotics, complex vitamin B, niacin, etc.

3. Preventive Measures

Regularly check the dog’s mouth and teeth, and clean tartar regularly with gauze. Feed solid food.

4) Cavities Besides humans, dogs can also suffer from cavities. The cause of cavities is food debris remaining in the mouth, where bacteria feed on the residues and multiply. During reproduction, they produce acidic substances that, upon contact with the teeth, dissolve the calcium in the enamel, forming cavities. Cavities can cause gum pain, tooth decay, and reduced appetite in dogs.

5) Malocclusion

The causes can be divided into two types: one is developmental issues in the dog's upper and lower jaws, resulting in improper opening and closing. The second occurs when permanent teeth grow improperly due to retained baby teeth, causing misalignment. Dogs with malocclusion experience difficulty eating as their teeth get stuck when closing their mouths.

6) Broken Teeth

Dogs naturally love to chew objects, sometimes even stones, which can wear down or break their teeth. Active dogs may also run into things and break their teeth. Aging can also contribute to this issue.

7) Sublingual Abscess

If the sublingual salivary gland becomes blocked, a large fluid-filled lump appears on the underside of the tongue, known as a sublingual abscess. If the owner notices this problem, they should immediately seek veterinary surgery to drain the fluid. Blockages can be caused by a calculus or even a grass seed.

8) Double Teeth

Puppies typically start teething at around two months old, and by 4-6 months, their baby teeth fall out and permanent teeth grow in. If there is a calcium deficiency at this time, it can cause retained baby teeth, slow permanent tooth growth, thin enamel layers, weak structures, and double rows of teeth or misaligned permanent teeth.

By 8-9 months old, all baby teeth should have fallen out and been replaced by permanent teeth. However, some puppies' teeth develop more slowly, and the permanent teeth fail to push out the baby teeth, resulting in double rows of teeth.

Simply put, double teeth are like protruding teeth in humans! Two teeth grow out of one socket! Breeds like Pomeranians, Chihuahuas, Yorkshire Terriers, and Schnauzers are more prone to this condition.

Dangerous "Double Teeth": Some pet owners might think that double teeth are not a big issue, only affecting aesthetics slightly. However, the harm is evident.

Harmful Factors of Double Teeth:

1. First, retained baby teeth can affect the normal development of permanent teeth, leading to malformed permanent teeth and malocclusion.

2. Double teeth easily accumulate dirt! Food residues easily get stuck between two adjacent teeth, which cannot be cleaned thoroughly with gauze or toothpicks. Over time, this leads to tartar buildup and bad breath—how foul it can get!

3. Bacteria proliferate in the mouth, causing gastrointestinal discomfort, eventually leading to periodontal disease, and ultimately resulting in feeding difficulties for the dog, thereby affecting its lifespan (by 1-2 years).

Solution:

1. Start giving your dog dry dog food from two months old and avoid soft foods. Ensure adequate calcium intake during the teething period! Additionally, if your dog primarily eats animal liver or meat, it's essential to supplement calcium appropriately. These foods contain very low levels of calcium and high concentrations of vitamin A, which inhibits calcium absorption. Therefore, it's best to stop feeding liver and promptly supplement calcium.

2. Parents should pay attention to changes in their dogs' teeth during the teething period, ensuring they can replace all their teeth normally. If not, timely assistance is necessary. Give your dog plenty of chew toys and play tug-of-war games with ropes, helping loosen baby teeth manually.

3. Baby teeth generally fall out before the dog turns one year old. Extraction before this age is not recommended, as it could destabilize the gum bed, affecting other teeth. Extraction should only be considered if the baby tooth is loose enough. Veterinarians generally recommend waiting until the baby tooth is loose enough to extract, as dogs may not tolerate the procedure well, and administering anesthesia poses risks. Even loose teeth should not be extracted all at once but done in stages.

4. If your dog still has double teeth after turning one year old, take it to the hospital immediately to have the baby teeth extracted.

Home Dental Care

To reduce dental problems in dogs, it is recommended that dog parents provide weekly dental care.

If your dog's teeth have accumulated a lot of tartar, it is best to take it to a pet hospital or grooming salon for professional handling by a veterinarian or expert. Attempting to handle it yourself may result in inexperienced handling, injuring the dog's gums and enamel, leading to oral inflammation or other oral diseases. Even dogs that receive regular daily dental care should ideally have professional dental cleaning or oral examinations once or twice a year. This can be done conveniently when taking your dog for vaccinations. Be prepared mentally, as if your dog's tartar is severe and it does not cooperate well, the veterinarian may need to administer general anesthesia, which carries certain risks. Therefore, it is best to regularly clean your dog's teeth to prevent tartar buildup to this extent.

Impact of Diet on Teeth

Daily dietary habits also affect a dog's teeth. Generally speaking, dry dog food is better than wet dog food or canned food. Dry food is harder, allowing dogs to scrape their teeth clean while chewing, whereas wet dog food lacks this function and easily leads to tartar buildup. Dogs' teeth need frequent exercise. Many dog owners, seeing their pets chew hard dry dog food, compassionately feed them soft meat or soaked dog food instead, which actually harms them. Some owners leave dog food out all day, allowing their dogs to eat whenever they want, which is not ideal. Each meal increases bacterial activity in the mouth. Feeding your dog twice a day at fixed times reduces bacterial activity to twice a day. Frequently giving your dog bones (preferably from pigs or cows), hard snacks, less sugar, or "dog chew toys" not only exercises their teeth but also gives them something to do, preventing destructive chewing. This is a win-win solution. Lastly, note that bad breath in dogs is not always due to dental issues; it could also be caused by indigestion or other diseases. If you notice weight loss, increased thirst and urination, vomiting, abdominal swelling, loss of appetite, or other abnormal symptoms alongside bad breath, it could indicate liver or kidney problems or diabetes. A full medical examination at the hospital is advised.

PS: Here are a few easy ways to remove dog tartar:

1. Brushing Teeth

Humans can feel the unpleasantness of their mouths after just one day without brushing. It's unimaginable what months, years, or even a lifetime without brushing would be like. Brushing your pet's teeth once daily (or at least twice a week) can maintain dental health and prevent bad breath to the greatest extent. Toothbrushes: Pet stores sell specially designed toothbrushes for pets. Alternatively, you can use a piece of gauze wrapped around your finger, though it's more troublesome. Toothpaste: Never use human toothpaste for pets, as the detergent components can harm their stomachs. While we may find beef or chicken-flavored toothpaste unappealing, it's highly attractive to pets. Human toothpaste containing fluoride, calcium, and mint is truly disgusting to them. Usage Method: Getting your pet to cooperate with brushing is almost impossible at first. If you follow the correct steps, everything will go smoothly after some time.

1. Start early. Initially, train them to form the habit of brushing without using toothpaste or a toothbrush. Simply massage their teeth lightly through the lips with your fingers. Keep the massage short and reward them with tasty treats afterward, making them associate brushing with happiness.

2. After some time, proceed to the next step: gently lift the lips, dip a gauze-wrapped finger in toothpaste, and massage a few teeth, starting with 2, then 3, 4... Soon, your pet will allow you to brush its teeth.

3. Once your pet matures, you can buy specialized toothbrushes and brush their teeth regularly.

2. Carrots

If your pet doesn't picky eat, occasionally chewing on small pieces of raw carrots can gently clean their teeth, scraping off tartar.

3. Coarse Fiber

Coarse fiber foods (such as corn, peanuts with shells...) promote intestinal peristalsis. When a pet's digestive function is healthy, oral diseases, especially bad breath, naturally improve. Good digestion means good teeth. This is a well-known fact.

4. Dry Feed

Compared to canned food, dry dog food and cat food more effectively remove tartar and eliminate bad breath. Therefore, it's best to separate dry and canned food during feeding.

5. Dog Chew Toys

Pet supply stores usually offer various colorful and diverse dog chew toys. These products generally clean teeth and remove tartar. Letting your dog chew on these items benefits health and passes time, achieving dual goals.

6. Knotted Ropes

Besides dog chew toys, you'll find knotted rope-like toys in pet stores. If your pet likes these, it's great because these toys function similarly to dental floss while being chewed.

7. Ox Tails

Ox tails contain many strong tendons. They not only act like knotted ropes as dental floss but also taste better. So next time you make ox tail soup, don't forget to save some for your pet to chew on.

8. Fragrance

Sometimes, pet stores sell pet-specific oral fragrances. These products are merely non-toxic perfumes for pets and do not truly solve bad breath problems. However, using perfume occasionally to mask odors is better than constant bad smells.

9. Rubber Toys

If your pet refuses to cooperate with brushing, you can try applying toothpaste to rubber toys. Pet-specific toothpaste usually tastes good, and rubber toys are easier to clean. This method is relatively acceptable for both humans and dogs.

10. Timed Feeding

If you leave a bowl of food on the ground all day, meaning your pet can eat anytime from morning till night, bacteria on the teeth will proliferate increasingly. The correct feeding method is to set fixed times and frequencies each day, limiting each meal to no more than 30 minutes. Regardless of whether the pet finishes eating, remove and wash the food bowl afterward. This not only reduces bacterial growth opportunities but also helps pets develop good eating habits, benefiting their digestive systems.
