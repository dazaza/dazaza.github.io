The film created a spectacular viewing scene of empty alleys at that time, and the main actor Jet Li became an overnight star because of it. In the just passed Year of the Tiger, Hong Kong director Stanley Tong gathered big-name stars such as Andy Lau, Jackie Chan, Nicholas Tse and others to create a powerful remake of the classic "The New Shaolin Temple", challenging the classic. Of course, it takes commendable courage to challenge a classic, but the birth of "The New Shaolin Temple" was fraught with difficulties, even could be said to be full of landmines, and any slight negligence could lead to all previous efforts being wasted. So, in the production of this movie, how did the creators navigate through these dangers and defuse these landmines step by step? Today, we will reveal these secrets one by one for you.

As the saying goes, "All martial arts under heaven originate from Shaolin," so shooting the legendary story of Shaolin Temple naturally becomes the best subject for martial arts movies. However, since the great success of "Shaolin Temple" in 1982, although many film directors have focused on this subject, it has been difficult to surpass this classic.

Especially in the last ten years, the Shaolin Temple theme has almost become a recognized minefield in films, with very few people daring to touch it. Therefore, this time taking on "The New Shaolin Temple" to challenge the old version of "Shaolin Temple" put immense pressure on director Stanley Tong.

Stanley Tong: I put a lot of pressure on myself. The biggest is to capture the spirit of Shaolin Temple, especially the Zen and martial arts aspects. I want to portray this realm, which is very difficult. Perhaps I need to find my own perspective on how to approach Jet Li's fighting style from before.

Despite bearing heavy pressure, Stanley Tong, as a representative director of current Hong Kong action films who has directed famous films such as "New Police Story", "Baby Plan", and "Keep Talking and Nobody Explodes", ultimately decided to bravely enter the minefield of Shaolin Temple-themed films. For this, he devised three strategies, using three major breakthroughs to turn this minefield into his lucky place.

Breakthrough One: Gathering Big-Name Stars to Counter Jet Li

Compared to the situation in the old version of "Shaolin Temple" where the kung fu king Jet Li stood alone, Stanley Tong made his first major breakthrough by gathering multiple big-name stars to join forces against Jet Li.

Stanley Tong: The first one must be Andy Lau, the rest are still undecided.

Stanley Tong's intention of recruiting talent is very clear. Although Jet Li is a martial arts all-around champion, if one or two stars can't beat him, then with so many big names together, even if their kung fu isn't as good as yours, won't their star power exceed yours?

Stanley Tong: There will definitely be comparisons with the old version of Shaolin Temple, but I have Andy Lau, and then Nicholas Tse. I know the chemistry between my actors.

Breakthrough Two: Shaolin Kung Fu vs Long Guns and Firearms

Stanley Tong's second major breakthrough was changing the ancient background of previous Shaolin Temple films, setting the film era in the warlord chaos period of the 1920s. This way, it broke the traditional constraints of Shaolin Temple films involving fist-to-fist combat, creating a new spectacle of Shaolin kung fu versus long guns and firearms.

Regarding the possible doubts about this spectacle, Stanley Tong was well-prepared.

Stanley Tong: Many people will ask, if you set the film in this era, shouldn't people just shoot and fight, making all kung fu useless? Yes, you can explain it like that. Did kung fu not exist back then? If you need to sacrifice, then do so as long as it has value.

Breakthrough Three: Promoting the Spirit of Zen and Martial Arts in a Unique Way

After finding enough commercial selling points for the film, Stanley Tong began working on the movie theme. Most previous Shaolin Temple films mainly focused on showcasing Shaolin kung fu. Stanley Tong's third major breakthrough was promoting the spirit of Shaolin "Zen Martial Arts" in a unique way, giving audiences a fresh feeling.

Stanley Tong: Choose a story, and also express the spirit of Shaolin Temple. I heard something called "Zen Martial Arts," which is also martial arts, and requires meditation. Then I thought using this (theme) to give Andy Lau, to express it.

"The New Shaolin Temple" battles classics, facing countless challenges akin to stepping on mines. Stanley Tong breaks through three major barriers, Andy Lau faces three dangerous situations to defuse bombs, Jackie Chan clears hidden dangers before the mines, Nicholas Tse ventures towards the mountain of mines. "China Movie Report" Spring Festival Special Program presents "The New Shaolin Temple Mine Clearance Record."

Stanley Tong used three major breakthroughs to create "The New Shaolin Temple," seemingly clearing the minefield, but in reality, this behind-the-scenes effort formed new dangerous mines. As he himself said, all these breakthrough pressures were concentrated on the leading actor Andy Lau. Can the idol king Andy Lau play a Shaolin monk whose martial arts can counter long guns and firearms, yet believes in cultivating Zen through martial arts? At least three bombs are tied to him.

Bomb One: Can the Idol King Play Like a Monk?

As a superstar spanning decades in the Chinese-language film industry, Andy Lau's idol charm and fashionable appeal have deeply taken root in people's hearts. Using this face to play an ordinary simple monk is like planting a bomb. Can Andy Lau playing a monk convince the audience? Director Stanley Tong couldn't help but worry about this.

Lan Hua Bio: Actually, when he (Stanley Tong) approached me, he was afraid I wouldn't shave my head, so he kept telling me that we didn't need me to shave my head, tricking me into playing this role.

Seeing Stanley Tong's worries, Brother Hua voluntarily stepped forward, making significant sacrifices to play like a monk.

Andy Lau: I said I must shave. Andy Lau cutting his hair in front of the camera makes everyone feel like he is making a huge sacrifice, but actually, it's not that bad.

In the film, it was precisely this segment where Andy Lau personally shaved his own head that made the audience forget Brother Hua's idol identity and accept his monk role.

Bomb Two: Can Someone Without Martial Arts Foundation Perform Authentic Moves?

After shaving his hair, Brother Hua already exudes the aura of a monk in appearance. However, he obviously has a much bigger hurdle to overcome. Previous actors who starred in Shaolin Temple films, represented by Jet Li, were truly skilled experts. Making someone without martial arts foundation like Brother Hua perform authentic Shaolin kung fu, without relying on special effects for authenticity, isn't that equivalent to tying a bomb on him?

Andy Lau: If you want to compare with Jet Li, I think I will definitely fall short.

To prevent this bomb from detonating, Andy Lau personally went to Shaolin Temple to practice authentic Shaolin boxing basics months before the filming started.

Andy Lau: I practiced for about 3 months, before the shooting.

Reporter: Which kind of boxing did you practice? Shaolin?

Andy Lau: Shaolin... taught me for a month, then another month was ... Master.

After three months of diligent practice, during the filming, Andy Lau finally had confidence in his Shaolin skills.

Andy Lau: All of Jet Li's performances are very beautiful. We hope this time I can meet everyone's expectations, and I believe I have the confidence to meet everyone's expectations.

Bomb Three: Can Someone Easygoing and Reserved Vividly Play a Ruthless Character?

In the film, Andy Lau plays Hou Jie, who after experiencing a great setback in life, realizes and becomes a calm Shaolin monk who cultivates Zen through martial arts. But initially, he was a typical ruthless and cruel villain.

Surprisingly, it was this villainous role that made Brother Hua feel a challenge he had never experienced before in his acting career. You might ask, hasn't Andy Lau played villains before? Wasn't his performance in "Infernal Affairs" and "Disciples" excellent? Shouldn't this be a piece of cake? When we threw this question to Brother Hua, he carefully explained the reasons to us.

Andy Lau: In "Disciples," because he needs to commit crimes, he doesn't want to, why does he want to open a grocery store? It's because he doesn't want people to know who he is, doesn't want them to notice who he is, so he is not showy, he is reserved. Then in "Infernal Affairs," the bad guy, because he is inside the police department, he cannot act like that, because every cop there is very good at spotting bad guys, so his acting is more appealing, even more reserved, he fundamentally doesn't let people notice who he is.

Originally, the villains Brother Hua played before were ruthless, but they were very steady and reserved characters, focusing on hiding their identities. But this time in "The New Shaolin Temple," the Hou Jie character he plays is very different. He is arrogant, looking down on everyone, domineering.

Andy Lau: He is a very domineering character, he feels like who else is there in this world? Those who follow me will prosper...

This kind of performance troubled Brother Hua, who has been gentle and low-key for many years. To overcome his own personality and vividly portray the domineering nature of the character, Brother Hua, burdened with the bomb, had to dig into his memory.

Andy Lau: Temper or that kind of arrogance, I think this is innate, I really had that kind of vibe when I was young. Now as an adult, I realize that getting angry is useless.

With this childhood experience, Brother Hua brilliantly portrayed a domineering character for the first time, forming a stark contrast with the character's later transformation, successfully showcasing the realm of stopping violence and cultivating Zen through martial arts that Director Stanley Tong wanted to express.

It seems that this time, to shape the characters well, Andy Lau indeed went through a lot of effort and successfully defused the three bombs tied to himself. But movies require group performances to complement each other, and the supporting actors' performances are equally crucial. Director Stanley Tong was well aware of this. Unexpectedly, compared to previously encountering difficulties and actively defusing mines, this time in selecting supporting actors, Stanley Tong began to proactively touch mines, and the first one he touched was a giant mine - he actually wanted to invite elder brother Jackie Chan to play a supporting role for Andy Lau! Touching Mine One: Inviting Jackie Chan to Play Supporting Role for Andy Lau

As an internationally renowned action superstar, except for guest appearances out of friendship, Jackie Chan has always been the leading man on screen. Even in Hollywood, he is undoubtedly the main actor.

Although in the movie "Doom Generation 2," Jackie Chan once played a supporting role, that was partly due to the fight scenes with Donnie Yen, and more importantly, to support the main character in the film, his beloved son Jaycee Chan.

Despite having collaborated on multiple films such as "Who Am I," "New Police Story," and "Baby Plan," Stanley Tong and Jackie Chan have had a good relationship for over a decade. However, asking elder brother Jackie Chan to expend time and effort to play a supporting role for equally talented Andy Lau, Stanley Tong himself knew this was a giant mine, which could explode and leave him shattered. Thus, Stanley Tong cleverly devised a series of strategies. First, he attracted Jackie Chan with the film's story and promised him a leading role.

Jackie Chan: When the director told me this story, I liked it quite a bit, and with Andy Lau, I said I haven't cooperated with Andy Lau for a long time, originally I was also one of the main actors.

Only after Jackie Chan agreed to take on the film did Stanley Tong tell him he wasn't the main actor, but by then, the promise-keeping elder brother Jackie Chan was too late to back out.

Jackie Chan: I've already committed to Mr. Yang (Joseph Chang) and the director, I can't not shoot it.

Since he couldn't be the main actor and already had other projects lined up, Jackie Chan naturally didn't want to spend too much energy on "The New Shaolin Temple." Seeing the situation turning sour, Stanley Tong came up with another plan. He told Jackie Chan that this time it was just a cameo appearance, and it wouldn't take much time.

Jackie Chan: Then I could only make a cameo appearance, I said I'll make a cameo.

Only upon arriving at the filming location did elder brother Jackie Chan realize he had been thoroughly tricked by his old friend Stanley Tong.

Jackie Chan: I saw it wasn't a cameo anymore, you took seven days to shoot what should have taken fourteen days, every day shooting me from morning till night, but it was quite fun.

Having come, he had no choice but to settle down and play a supporting role for Andy Lau, becoming a key figure in the transformation and enlightenment of Andy Lau's character in the film.

Jackie Chan: He wants to learn kung fu from me, I said I am not a dedicated person, studied for two years and didn't do well, now I'm in the kitchen. Originally, I knew kung fu, so I told my mistakes to Andy Lau, letting him improve, enlightening him, and eventually succeeded in enlightening him.

Meanwhile, Jackie Chan vaguely felt that he also carried a bomb on him, because playing a supporting role to Andy Lau wasn't easy, and any relaxation could overshadow his own brilliance. How could the prestigious elder brother Jackie Chan be content to remain buried gold in the soil? Thus, he tried every means to design highlights for his role.

Jackie Chan's first idea was to have the cook monk he played speak Henan dialect mixed with southern and northern accents, though not fluent, it was amusing, capturing the audience immediately.

Next, Jackie Chan completely reversed the usual real kung fu performances, focusing on the characteristic of the Shaolin cook monk who constantly revolved around pots and pans, turning kneading dough and stir-frying dishes into kung fu, creating another highlight for himself and the film.

As early as during the pre-production stage of "The New Shaolin Temple," golden partner Nicholas Tse, who had collaborated with Stanley Tong on four works, received an invitation from the production team, but at that time, Stanley Tong hadn't decided what kind of role he would play. It wasn't until the film was about to start shooting that Stanley Tong suddenly had an idea to have Nicholas Tse play the sinister and vicious main villain Cao Man. However, he might not have realized that this was another bomb he was about to touch.

Why is it considered a bomb for Stanley Tong to have Nicholas Tse play the villain? Originally, regardless of whether it was in the movie "The Promise" or "Storm Riders 2," the oddball villains portrayed by Nicholas Tse were heavily criticized.

If Stanley Tong didn't know this situation, it would be understandable, but what's puzzling is that as the party involved, Nicholas Tse happily accepted the call from Stanley Tong to play the villain again after receiving the phone call.

Nicholas Tse: One morning, the director called me and said, "How about you playing a big villain?" I was very excited, I thought it was a great opportunity, I told him, okay.

Originally, since "Bodyguards and Assassins," the transformed Nicholas Tse's acting skills soared rapidly, but the more he was in his prime, the more his past failures became a thorn in his side. Where he fell, he wanted to get up from there, and Stanley Tong's call gave him exactly that opportunity.

Nicholas Tse: From my debut to now, I've always stuck to one sentence, if you like Nicholas Tse just because of his popularity or his so-called looks, then don't like Nicholas Tse. If this time when you leave the cinema, you feel, in the movie, cursing Nicholas Tse, using dirty words to describe Nicholas Tse, then I think I've achieved my goal as an actor, because I'm playing a villain.

Learning from past failures, this time Nicholas Tse playing the villain Cao Man didn't just limit himself to the surface-level hysteria or oddness, but instead tried every means to portray the inner cruelty of the character.

Nicholas Tse: Because he is very extreme, if I want to kill you, I will use the cruelest, most exaggerated method to kill you. I won't just shoot you, I will come up with many methods that look quite cruel or will make people shiver while performing.

Hard work pays off, Nicholas Tse's efforts finally gained recognition from the audience, turning what was originally a landmine into a bomb that ignited the audience's enthusiasm for watching the film.

Looking back at the birth process of "The New Shaolin Temple," the main creators could be said to knowingly walk towards the mountain of mines! The film has already been released nationwide on January 19th, whether it can become a classic like the old version of "Shaolin Temple" still needs time to verify, but the pioneering and courageous spirit of the creators of "The New Shaolin Temple" will remain in our memories.
