How foreign countries carry out Internet regulation, we should learn - Nanjing SEO Bar

by maomaochaoren on 2010-04-08 09:20:57

How do foreign countries supervise the Internet and ease the contradictions within it? Let's take a look at what South Korea is doing.

On April 1st, Chairman Choi Si-chung of the Korean Communications Commission held a lunch discussion with the CEOs of South Korean Internet companies such as NHN and G Market.

Representatives from the Internet industry focused on raising issues of discrimination that have appeared in the Internet market following the advent of mobile networks. They argued that regulatory measures such as the real-name confirmation system, location information laws, and pre-game review systems have caused losses for South Korean companies compared to foreign enterprises.

The CEO of a portal company questioned the effectiveness of these regulations, pointing out that foreign companies can provide maps of various locations in South Korea with a resolution of 15cm, whereas South Korean companies, despite developing comparable technology, can only provide services at a 50cm level and must apply mosaic processing to major public facilities.

The CEO of an online mall stated that requiring three levels of approval - from merchants, credit companies, and electronic certification - when selling items online is rare worldwide. He emphasized that consumers could shop securely without being subjected to such significant inconvenience.

Chairman Choi mentioned that a special deregulation task force has been established regarding the real-name confirmation system and location-based information services. The task force aims to minimize harm caused by malicious comments and abuse of personal location information while ensuring that businesses are not obstructed in their operations. He also added that regarding pre-game review systems and the disclosure of public information, they will communicate the difficulties faced by enterprises to relevant departments and strive to ease related regulations.

What does this news illustrate? I believe there are two key points worth considering.

Firstly, South Korea’s regulatory policies are indeed comprehensive, forward-looking, and strict, which are precisely what China urgently needs. Often, much-needed regulatory measures are nowhere to be seen, while those visible are deemed unreasonable, indicating that China lags behind in Internet regulation and needs further improvement.

Secondly, a positive Internet environment is jointly built through good communication between enterprises and regulators. Only by establishing an equal communication mechanism can any Internet-related issue be effectively resolved.

However, in our situation, regulators often act in a high-handed manner, making it difficult to engage in dialogue with Internet companies, especially private enterprises and grassroots levels. Many regulatory measures are issued as administrative orders, and illegal behaviors often occur during enforcement. Many enforcers inherently believe that they are officials and you are citizens, implying that you can only obey them without any right to appeal.

I believe that an ideal Internet society must achieve equal communication, rational law enforcement, effective regulation, and a long-term development mindset. Otherwise, it will lead to a tense and highly adversarial relationship between the Internet industry and government regulators, resulting in complaints and harm to China's Internet industry rather than promotion.
