What are the characteristics of the regionality of national architecture?

by anonymous on 2012-02-22 21:37:58

Where there are humans, there is construction. The intermediate economic professional in construction always coexists with humans. In "Hanfeizi & middot; 5 Dù", it is written: "In ancient times, there were few people but many beasts. People could not win against the beasts, insects, and snakes. Then a wise man came. He built nests from wood to avoid various harms." From this, we can infer that nest-dwelling was probably a primitive method of habitation adopted by human society. With the discovery of fire, humans began to live around fires in caves, expanding the activity range of human society. It wasn't until the Yellow Emperor, Xuanyuan, taught everyone to build houses that people entered a new era of habitation, bidding farewell to their nomadic lifestyle. Since the Yanhuang period, Chinese civilization has been passed down for 4,680 years. During this time, culture has been inclusive and accommodating, and construction has been diverse, forming a unique style of Chinese architecture. Traditional dwellings undoubtedly best showcase this style.

In today's China, rapid economic growth has led to a surge in construction, presenting all sorts of buildings overnight to the modern public—European, Japanese, American, Singaporean, Hong Kong styles... Truly, it's like "a sudden spring breeze brings forth thousands of buildings in myriad forms." These varied architectural forms gathered together have prompted our reflection on contemporary Chinese architecture.

Building TV stations, making houses messy, constructing a bird's nest just to kick a ball, or building an egg-shaped structure just to listen to music—what kind of architectural form is this? What actions? What logic? These showmen probably fear being called artless, fearing honesty, insisting on making grotesque faces for others to see; ugly to begin with, they still heavily apply makeup, fearing they won't attract attention. Columns must be bent and slanted at all costs, exhausting people, always wanting to express their uniqueness through so-called "personalized design." They lack fundamental knowledge of architecture, thinking they are free from Earth's gravity. It's unclear from which day onward, but the small caps on residential buildings have once again become small cantilevered boards. Recently, residential roofs across the country have become increasingly varied, these cosmetic tricks having almost no relation to the architectural body. One can't help but ask, what kind of art is this?

The development of architecture in China has followed a different path than in the West. Chinese architecture, from the Shang Dynasty to present, spanning over three thousand years, has formed its own unique architectural system. Ancient Chinese architectural theory, from Laozi's interpretation of architecture to pre-Qin classics such as "Kaogongji," Han Dynasty's "Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art," Tang Dynasty Wang Xiaotong's "Jigu Suanjing," Song Dynasty Qin Jiushao's "Mathematical Treatise in Nine Sections" and Li Jie's "Yingzao Fashi," Ming Dynasty's "Yuanye," Qing Dynasty's "Gongduan Yingzao Lu" to the modern "Yingzao Fayuan," meticulously describe the methods and theories of ancient Chinese architecture, serving as important treasures in China's architectural field. However, the teaching of architectural knowledge in China has traditionally been passed down from master to apprentice, father to son, resulting in the loss of skills upon the death of individuals, hindering the development of architectural studies. It wasn't until the early 20th century that the title and teaching methods of modern architects were introduced from the West, changing this situation.

Due to the characteristics and limitations of China's architectural education model, combined with socio-economic factors affecting architectural creation, the research and development of architectural creation theory and methods lagged behind Western countries for a considerable period. The difficult environment for architectural creation, the isolation of architectural BBS, the decades-long stagnant structural system of construction departments, and the slow progress of housing commoditization have caused various mistakes in China's architectural creation over the past few decades. This is mainly reflected in the one-sided emphasis on traditional experience while neglecting methodological research; focusing on borrowing from historical materials while ignoring innovation in architectural creation thought and methods; emphasizing the spatial combination, proportion, scale, and other sensory factors of architecture while neglecting the scientific aspects of architecture's relationship with society, environment, culture, utilization, and technology, turning architecture into a vehicle for personal emotions pursuing artistic spirit and uniqueness. This phenomenon is particularly prominent in China's current "large-scale construction" era, where various foreign schools of thought flood in, and architects yearn for instant fame. No wonder some works that we consider quite good are incomprehensible to foreigners.

"What architecture needs to do is nothing more than to give residents a bit more warmth when they return home." Dutch architect Aldo van Eyck described home design thusly. Architecture is for better living; anything beyond this is merely auxiliary. Many new constructions we see today are putting the cart before the horse. For instance, many so-called green communities merely pursue an increase in green space ratio while neglecting deeper residential concepts in architecture. Some buildings use individual heating systems even though urban central heating is available, increasing the cost for owners and wasting energy. Overseas individual heating systems are used only when neighborhoods are far from urban areas, and using central urban heating would incur significant additional costs. Therefore, green architecture should focus more on saving energy.

Architecture has a strong sense of nationality. Some developers, in order to display aristocratic style, blindly copy Western architecture into China, causing cultural discord, leading to laughable situations. Since the 1990s urban real estate development boom, European-style grandeur has become increasingly popular, reaching its peak around 1996, evolving into a fashion actively or passively pursued by homeowners, local officials, even urban planners and architects. European-style grandeur ranges from simply applying Western architectural moldings or components to mimicking Western proportions and styles on facades, to seeking majestic classical compositions in architectural groups or urban planning. Taking Shanghai as an example, two-thirds of property advertisements in 1996 mentioned "European-style grandeur (emotion)" and "European artistic courtyard residences," gradually fading away by 2002. Foreign architecture on Chinese land may suit a few people with overseas backgrounds, but for someone who has never left China and grown up locally, facing Western aristocratic lifestyles might initially be intriguing, but over time becomes boring, akin to staying in a hotel abroad.

The uniqueness of architecture is based on its national character. Once disconnected from the supporting land, architecture becomes mere flashy shapes. Many of China's current constructions prioritize form over the essence of living. Italian architect Gregotti said: Contemporary architecture in Italy, France, Spain cannot be confused, each clearly retaining its own distinct features, but this cannot be seen in China. Is this the sorrow of Chinese architecture, or superficial criticism? Some say the "Ten Great Buildings" of the 1950s represent a peak in national-style architectural practice, while the 1980s "recapturing ancient capital charm" was a vulgar regression. Why does Beijing naturally evoke thoughts of siheyuans? Why do dwellings differ? Each building is connected to the local atmosphere—the sunlight, humidity, wind direction, vegetation—all these factors create the uniqueness of architecture. Houses in Hainan can face north, but not in Beijing; houses in Lingnan can connect inside and outside, but not in Northeast China; houses in Shanghai can be villas, but not in Lhasa. Just as southern coconut trees cannot grow in cold northern regions, beauty alone isn't enough—it must also be livable. Shenzhen's Window of the World can condense global silhouettes, but those are just shadows; copying Buckingham Palace verbatim in China would make it fake. The reason the Bund in Shanghai is famous is not because of the foreign-style houses built there, but because there was a group of foreigners who once lived there.

Chinese architecture must, on one hand, promote national culture, and on the other, seek new breakthroughs. We do not oppose returned students introducing new elements, but we oppose copying so-called avant-garde things. Good architecture should be a combination of Chinese culture and the essence of architecture. Materials and structures can be modern, but the created ambiance must be very "Chinese." We must bid farewell to blind imitation and let architecture return to its essence. Architects should study the environment, society, and the interaction between architecture and human activities with a normal and objective mindset, achieving a perfect state of harmony between architecture and nature in their creations.

As China steadily steps onto the world stage of modern architecture, the level of China's architectural creativity will increasingly attract global attention. Architects are no longer captivated by the poetic charm of ancient Chinese architecture but hope to stand out in the realm of modern architectural creation. Chinese architects should create works not only rooted in the ancient philosophy of "harmony between heaven and humanity" and reforming nature but also embracing today's research environment and sustainable development concepts of coexistence with nature.

This article reflects on the emergence, development, and evolution of creative design concepts in China's architectural planning since the reform and opening-up, emphasizing how to create architecture that fits Chinese culture and returns to the essence of architecture under the ideological background of contemporary architectural design trends.
