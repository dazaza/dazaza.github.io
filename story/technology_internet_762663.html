"The Inevitable 'Death of Yougou': Belle's E-commerce Has Many Fatal Flaws"

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 16:37:43

Recently, Yougou.com, which has suffered a severe loss of senior executives, received another blow. On July 17th, it was confirmed by an insider from Yougou.com that Zhang Xiaojun, the COO of Yougou.com, is leaving and is currently processing his resignation. As one of the founding members of Yougou.com, Zhang Xiaojun's departure holds significant implications, indicating that Yougou.com is indeed facing serious crises.

Let’s first review the e-commerce journey of Belle International, the parent company of Yougou.com:

A few years ago, Belle started its foray into e-commerce in a low-key manner, steadily developing through three channels: Taobao distribution, Tmall, and B2C distribution (B2C distribution completed via Taoxiu.com). Led by group veteran Xie Yunli, a relatively modest e-commerce team began by selling products under Belle's own brands, followed by exclusive online versions and eventually new online brands. This development path was considered before 2011 to be a more solid example of a traditional brand successfully entering the e-commerce sector compared to Li-Ning.

With the rise of Letao.com and Hao Le Mai in 2010, both of which received over $100 million in financing and rapidly developed, Belle Group became restless. On one hand, there was concern about being constrained by distribution channels again; on the other hand, after evaluation, they realized they had enormous potential advantages in the e-commerce field, including:

1. A wide range of brands: In addition to several of Belle's own women's shoe brands, sales of internationally renowned sports brands such as Adidas and Nike have grown at an astonishing rate online;

2. Easier category expansion: For Belle Group, known for its women's shoes and agency of well-known sports brands, how to gain absolute market advantage and status through multiple brands and categories has been the most thought-provoking issue in recent years. Expanding offline stores to expand brands and categories is clearly too slow, while ongoing e-commerce operations can easily expand brands but cannot quickly expand categories;

3. Financial advantage: During cooperation with e-commerce companies, Belle discovered that e-commerce is a capital-intensive business, and Belle Group's financial strength allows it to develop without relying on capital, thereby defeating more vertical e-commerce companies that must depend on capital, becoming the "survivor" who reigns supreme;

4. Talent advantage: Through several years of e-commerce practice, Belle has already cultivated a highly professional and strong execution-oriented e-commerce team, capable of handling large-scale e-commerce business or even more;

5. Supply chain advantage: This excites Belle internally and is also the core reason attracting high-level executives from brand e-commerce or platform e-commerce. After all, for e-commerce, supply chain management always fascinates because it seems to be the part determining life and death aside from capital. The supply chain includes product research and development, production, warehouse management, delivery and after-sales service—a series of "business" contents. In traditional enterprises emphasizing "commerce" over "e-commerce", this is regarded as the greatest competitive advantage over e-commerce companies.

Based on these advantages, Belle's e-commerce decided to make a big push, first purchasing the double-character domain name yougou.com,

Directly abandoning the somewhat counterfeit “topshoes.cn” Taoshou website for redirection, stopping supply to all distribution websites, while retaining direct third-party online stores like Taobao and Tmall Mall but reducing investment and attention. High salaries were offered to attract JD.com VP and Vancl VP to serve as CMO and COO respectively, and a group elder was dispatched to serve as CEO, forming a golden triangle. They aimed to build a full-category vertical B2C mall, implementing multi-brand strategies on one hand, and expanding to clothing and other categories on the other, realizing the dream of a department store empire impossible to achieve offline!

Everything sounded perfect, but every dream needs to face the hard reality, and the reality is:

1. Higher technical requirements for independent B2C: Belle headquartered in Shenzhen couldn't recruit more top technical talents, and outsourcing technical service companies are scarce in Shenzhen. However, technology determines user experience, backend management efficiency, and reliable data. Independent B2C belongs to dynamic technology, different from relatively static technologies like ERP. Continuous changes remain a hurdle many traditional enterprises still can't overcome;

2. Increased management difficulty due to category expansion: Someone once said doing well in women's shoes doesn't mean doing well in men's shoes either, something Belle probably deeply felt when expanding its men's shoe category. Expanding to women's wear and accessories within a year would undoubtedly cause difficulties in the backend. For traditional brand enterprises accustomed to meticulous calculations, this rapid approach worked during Vancl's special period and could be understood within Vancl and JD.com. Although Belle hired Vancl's COO and JD.com's CMO, whether they could be understood and execute with "exaggerated costs" remains questionable;

3. System: This is difficult for people not bridging e-commerce and traditional enterprises to understand. Simply put, it involves departmental setup (organizational structure), approval processes, finance and budgeting, reporting methods, corporate culture, communication styles, execution methods, risk control, process systems—these constitute what is called the "mechanism" of a company. The result of this conflict is that e-commerce attempts to persuade acceptance of its "system," while the traditional team tries but fails to fully understand e-commerce's "system," leading to inefficiency and instability in the execution team. Eventually, the chairman or board might say, "E-commerce and traditional enterprises each have their strengths and weaknesses, and in the future, they will certainly merge, so you must also integrate in these areas..." If this so-called "integration without harmony" appears, it leads to so-called management innovation, and thus the "CEO rotation system" actually appeared in a company like Belle!

It was originally thought that Yougou.com, the e-commerce arm of Belle, was far ahead, with several executives waiting for the spin-off IPO to enjoy success and wealth, setting examples of countless traditional enterprises succeeding in the e-commerce era. Instead, they found themselves entangled and distressed by these hard injuries.

After all, whether it's the veterans who grew up with Belle or the recruited e-commerce professionals, it can be seen that they didn’t leave because they lacked ability. When the VPs recruited from JD.com return to JD.com, they continue to perform admirably, and those from Vancl have the courage to start their own ventures. Are these hard injuries merely the three points mentioned above? Or are these not only Belle's hard injuries but also the hard injuries of many traditional enterprises attempting to enter e-commerce?
