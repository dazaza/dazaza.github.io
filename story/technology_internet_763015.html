Google, Don't Worry: Facebook Search Is No Threat

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 12:09:37

On the evening of July 19th, it was originally intended that Facebook’s “Graph Search” would become an intelligent search engine to help people explore the depths of social networks, find content forgotten due to its length, or uncover content in the News Feed that had been blurred.

In January, Facebook launched Graph Search with great fanfare and bestowed upon it the title of "the third pillar," sending it forth to its base of a billion users. Now, 30% of English-speaking users in the U.S. can use Graph Search, making it Facebook's most important product after News Feed and Timeline.

However, Graph Search has many issues. It is difficult to change user perceptions; in the past, Facebook users only used search to find friends and add them to their network. Now, Facebook hopes to change user intent.

It is worth noting that for nine years, users have never truly searched on Facebook. Why should they start now?

Facebook wants to change user habits, which is a daunting task. Before changing how people understand social networks and before altering the functions it provides, talk of Facebook eating into Google's search revenue is nothing more than idle chatter. Prior to Graph Search, Facebook processed a billion queries daily, but these searches (if you consider finding friends as searching) did not bring real financial returns to Facebook.

If you ask Facebook, "Why search?" Facebook will tell you: "Graph Search is a form of expression defined by the users of the social network themselves. Its content is free, not dictated by the company, unlike News Feed and Timeline, which are company-determined."

Theoretically, Graph Search opens up a new window where every piece of social network information can be accessed according to your own 'terminology.' You can use natural language instead of search terms to learn about the people you want to know, find the places you wish to visit, and collect movies and music you like.

But in reality, understanding Graph Search is much more complex, far more intricate than simply typing keywords into a search box. This is because the parameters of Graph Search are strange and open-ended, making its use akin to wandering into an unfamiliar country without a guide.

Altimeter Group digital advertising and media analyst Rebecca Lieb believes that, in fact, Facebook has previously restricted people from searching. People come to Facebook to focus on their friends or foes, not to conduct "complex searches," because Facebook initially did not allow such actions. When Facebook introduced a conceptual search experience, users were unprepared. Facebook search requires users to engage in "headache-inducing" thought processes to pose questions, only to receive useless information in return.

BIA/Kelsey technology analyst Jed Williams points out that Facebook faces some difficulties because people are gradually getting accustomed to it providing just one thing: the Feed. By introducing search, Facebook is fighting against what it originally established.

Facebook Graph Search product manager Loren Cheng admits that for its 1.2 billion users, search is a foreign concept, and Facebook needs to make users understand what it can do.

"What should I ask?" This question confuses many Facebook users when searching. To address this issue, Facebook continuously adjusts search prompts that appear when users click on the search bar.

Facebook is playing a guessing game to determine what users want to do with Graph Search, because it doesn't know. Loren Cheng says: "We observe different user groups and strive to figure out what Graph Search is useful for. We want to connect it with the users' natural intentions."

Google has also integrated social network content into its search. Two years later, Google+ claims to have 190 million users, though this figure is debatable.

The challenge Facebook search faces is that users have not changed their search habits and continue to use it to find people. Even Loren Cheng admits that most Graph Search queries are for finding people.
