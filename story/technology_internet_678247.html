**Color Cognition**

**I. Classification of Colors**

1. There are two methods for classifying colors. According to common practice, they can be divided into achromatic and chromatic color systems. On the color wheel, hues can also be categorized into cool and warm tones.

- (1) **Achromatic Color System**: Refers to black, white, and various shades of gray.

- (2) **Chromatic Color System**: Refers to red, orange, yellow, green, cyan, blue, purple, and all other colors derived from these hues.

2. **Classification of Colors on the Color Wheel**

- 1) **Cool Tones**: Colors such as blue and purple on the color wheel evoke a sense of coldness and are classified as cool colors.

- 2) **Warm Tones**: Colors such as red, orange, and yellow on the color wheel evoke warmth, hence they are called warm colors.

*Note*: The warmth or coolness of colors is not absolute but relative. Even within the same hue, there can be distinctions between warm and cool tones. For example, compared to blue, lemon yellow is warmer, but compared to medium yellow, it appears cooler.

---

**II. Primary Colors, Secondary Colors, and Tertiary Colors**

1. **Primary Colors**: Also known as first-order colors, primary colors are the fundamental colors that can be mixed to create all other colors. In pigments, the three primary colors are red, yellow, and blue.

2. **Secondary Colors**: Secondary colors, also called second-order colors, are created by mixing the primary colors in varying proportions. Examples include:

- Red + Yellow = Orange

- Yellow + Blue = Green

- Red + Blue = Purple

3. **Tertiary Colors**: These are formed by combining two secondary colors or a primary color with a secondary color. Compared to primary and secondary colors, tertiary colors have a significantly lower saturation level.

---

**III. Three Elements of Color (Hue, Value/Brightness, Saturation/Purity)**

1. **Hue**

Hue refers to the appearance or identity of a color, distinguishing one color from another. For example, red represents a specific category of hue. Adding white to pure red creates variations in brightness and purity, such as pink, but the original hue remains unchanged. Variations like vermilion, deep red, and rose red all belong to the red family but differ in warmth or coolness.

2. **Value/Brightness**

Value refers to the lightness or darkness of a color. Among the three elements of color, value has the strongest independence and can be represented separately using black, white, and gray (achromatic system). Adding more white increases the brightness, while adding more black decreases it.

3. **Saturation/Purity**

Saturation, also known as purity or vividness, refers to the intensity or clarity of a color. Mixing any color with white, black, or gray reduces its saturation. The more black, gray, or white added, the lower the saturation becomes.

---

**IV. Principles of Color Coordination**

1. **Clear Tone System**: Adding white to pure colors results in high brightness and low saturation, creating effects such as relaxation, vitality, youthfulness, and expansion. This is suitable for people with fair skin and petite body types.

2. **Muddy Tone System**: Adding a certain amount of black to pure colors lowers the brightness and creates an elegant and solemn effect.

3. **Analogous Colors**: Analogous colors are those obtained by adding black, white, or gray to any single color on the color wheel, forming a harmonious series of colors. In color coordination, analogous colors provide a stable and gentle combination. (The angular difference between analogous colors does not exceed 15 degrees, making it the weakest form of hue contrast.)

4. **Adjacent Colors**: Adjacent colors are those located between 30 to 60 degrees on the color wheel. They are next to each other and produce a harmonious and balanced effect when combined. (In the hue circle, the angular difference generally does not exceed 45 degrees.)

5. **Complementary Colors**: Complementary colors are those positioned directly opposite each other on the color wheel (approximately 180 degrees apart). They create the strongest contrast in color combinations, resulting in clear, vibrant, and striking visual effects. When complementary colors are mixed, the result is usually a shade of gray or black. (Examples: yellow vs. purple, orange vs. blue, red vs. green.)
