Japanese people can't figure out why Chinese are so obsessed with housing

by alexsgxu on 2009-11-30 23:55:44

By Xu Shuguang, Chairman of the格林豪泰Hotel and 519.com Wine Direct Sales Network

A survey on "the relationship between happiness and housing" published in the China Youth Daily on November 19 showed that 80% of Chinese respondents believed that "happiness is related to housing," with 69.9% believing that "housing is essential for a happy family." However, the Japanese do not agree with this view, as more than 90% of Japanese netizens think that happiness has nothing to do with housing, urging the Chinese to think twice before acting (Changjiang Daily, November 26).

Is happiness related to housing? In this survey, 80% of Chinese netizens believe it is relevant, while more than 90% of Japanese netizens do not think there is a connection between the two. At the same time, they express confusion over the Chinese obsession with housing. In today's era of skyrocketing housing prices where many people exclaim they cannot afford homes, owning a house has become an indicator of happiness. But when happiness becomes a byproduct of housing, how can we determine whether it is a "blessing" or a "curse"?

In traditional Chinese consciousness, having a home means having a house because without resolving the housing issue, personal household registration becomes a major problem. Moreover, individual welfare, benefits, medical insurance, and even the next generation becoming "black households" unable to attend school are all issues constantly troubling "mortgage slaves." How can they talk about happiness?

Nowadays, housing prices have soared to astronomical levels, making homeownership feel like chasing after mirages or moon reflections in water, leaving people to only lament their inability to own a home. Originally, striving for a house should be a very fulfilling endeavor. However, some people treat owning a house as the ultimate meaning of life, completely transforming themselves into "mortgage slaves," working their entire lives for a house or severely compromising their lifestyles to buy one. They wake up early and work late every day, never having time to enjoy life, exhausting themselves both physically and mentally. How can they talk about happiness?

Some may have bought houses but now worry about falling housing prices because a drop in price means their property will "devalue." People with such concerns cannot possibly be happy, especially since they aren't real estate investors—this is simply self-inflicted trouble. Perhaps these are precisely the aspects that confuse the Japanese. Additionally, we should recognize that in China, there are indeed many people who cannot afford houses. Does this mean they are not happy or content? Clearly, that is not the case.

The definition of happiness is diverse; it is a question of a thousand opinions from a thousand people. Some believe achieving good academic results and finding a good job is happiness, others think success and fame equal happiness, and still others believe staying at the Green Tree Inn Hotel is happiness... Recently, the TV series "Snail House," which revolves around the theme of housing, has been popular. It narrates the "blood and sweat history" of Shanghai white-collar workers striving for homes, delving into the emotions of mortgage slaves. It tells us that although having a house does not necessarily mean having a happy family, whether one owns a house or not indeed affects a family’s happiness. As for why 90% of Japanese netizens disagree with the idea that "houses are related to happiness," this is merely a matter of "different perspectives among different individuals." The national conditions differ, and so do people's definitions of happiness. Debate on this topic is quite normal.

In my opinion, achieving happiness should encompass both material and spiritual life. Basic material living conditions form the foundation of happiness, while spiritual life is an important component of happiness. Many people enjoy abundant material comforts but still do not feel happy. Why is that? Because material enjoyment is ultimately limited, whereas human desires are infinite. Overly pursuing material enjoyment can easily lead to hedonism. Only spiritual wealth represents true wealth, and spiritual happiness represents true happiness!

Life's happiness does not depend on whether we possess abundant material wealth or whether we achieve fame and success. Happiness is a state of mind, an inner feeling, experience, and satisfaction. Regardless of whether one owns a house, looks beautiful or ugly, lives rich or poor, holds high or low status, as long as the individual feels happy, then they are truly happy. Learning gratitude and learning to be content makes it easier for people to feel happiness.
