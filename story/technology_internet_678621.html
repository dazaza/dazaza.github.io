Rogers: US economic growth is expected to slow, prosperity is an illusion

by ikonfx123456 on 2012-02-27 15:49:37

DATONG FINANCIAL [http://www.ikonplc.com/]: On January 31, the U.S. Congressional Budget Office pointed out in its released [Budget and Economic Outlook] report that if tax increases and federal government spending cuts take effect as planned in 2013, then the U.S. economic growth will slow down significantly to 1.1%; well-known investor Jim Rogers even viewed the current prosperity of the U.S. economy as an [illusion] created by the Obama administration's stimulus measures under the backdrop of the election year.

The above forecast and the fact that eight out of ten Federal Reserve Board members do not support starting interest rate hikes before 2014 reflect that the recently improved U.S. economy has essentially not yet recovered its strength and is difficult to completely摆脱摆脱摆脱 shed its dependence on stimulus policies.

Rogers calls the boom an [illusion]

The U.S. Congressional Budget Committee predicted in the report that the U.S. Gross Domestic Product (GDP) growth in 2012 would reach 2%, slightly higher than the International Monetary Fund's (IMF) corresponding forecast of 1.8% growth made last week. However, considering that the preliminary value of the U.S. GDP growth in the fourth quarter of last year, announced last Friday, was as high as 2.8%, the content of the report clearly indicates that the U.S. economy will lose its growth [acceleration] in 2012.

In light of this, the report simulated an [Alternative Fiscal Scenario], assuming that under conditions where most tax cuts are extended and spending cuts are postponed, the U.S. economic growth rate in 2013 will rise to 2.75%.

In addition, the Congressional Budget Office expects that the U.S. economy will not fully realize its potential until 2018, and the institution also raised unemployment rate forecasts until 2021; among them, the national unemployment rate forecast for the next two years remains above 8%, and monthly unemployment rate data may even return to above the 9% level line before the end of 2013.

Regarding this, Rogers, who has always been [sharp-tongued], pointed out without reservation that historically, during every major election, the U.S. government tries its best to inject funds into the market to win the election, a tactic that has never failed; so, the recent situation is no exception, the Obama administration continuously activates the printing press to increase public spending, creating a [false appearance] of prosperity.

Policy space is very limited

Rogers did not explicitly state whether the Obama administration's actions to create a [false appearance] of prosperity will continue throughout 2012, but in fact, the U.S. federal government's policy space for stimulation before the presidential election is already very limited; and its $440 billion employment stimulus plan proposed last November was not "approved" by the U.S. Congress controlled by the Republicans, and currently only a few small-scale stimulus cases targeting students and teachers, totaling less than $50 billion, introduced in September and October last year, are still in effect.

American media pointed out that the U.S. Congress had passed Obama's request to raise the debt limit last Thursday, but the new debt space can only meet the basic operations of the U.S. federal government before the election. Once the Democratic government hopes to propose any expenditure plan with substantial stimulating effects, it will be difficult to smoothly pass through Congress. In addition, with the federal government budget deficit expected to exceed one trillion dollars for four consecutive years, the Obama administration has difficulty obtaining sufficient fiscal stimulus space.

Professor Bu Ruobai of the School of Economics and Management at Central University of Finance and Economics emphasized that the Federal Reserve's loose monetary policy tone may objectively play a role in stimulating the economy. But he pointed out that the Federal Reserve usually exercises caution in election years, avoiding being entangled in partisan interests; therefore, although the institution announced in its first monetary policy decision of the year that it would maintain ultra-low interest rates until the end of 2014, if no more easing plans are introduced, the effects of the policy signals at the beginning of the year will fundamentally fail to form sustained stimulation for the U.S. economy.
