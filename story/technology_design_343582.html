The Wa ethnic group's dietary culture primarily revolves around rice as their staple food. In the Ximeng region, the Wa people enjoy cooking vegetables, salt, and rice together in a pot to make a thicker, softer porridge-like dish. In other regions, they prefer steamed rice. During busy farming seasons, they eat three meals a day, while on regular days, they eat two meals. Chicken congee and tea flower porridge are considered premium everyday foods. Drought-resistant rice is often freshly pounded before eating, and all ages consume chili peppers. There is a saying among the locals: "Without chilies, one cannot feel full," emphasizing their love for spicy food.

The Wa people’s main source of meat comes from domesticated animals such as pigs, cattle, and chickens. Additionally, they have the habit of eating rodents and insects. In some areas, they also consume insect larvae, including over ten varieties like red caterpillars that live on plants, broom caterpillars, and gourd tree bugs. Typically, edible insects are cooked with rice to make porridge, adding vegetables, salt, and mixed with chili peppers, creating a savory and spicy flavor.

Beekeeping is relatively common among the Wa people, but their methods are unique. They use hollowed-out logs sealed at both ends, leaving small holes for wild bees to enter and exit. These logs are placed in forests or under eaves, allowing the bees to multiply and produce honey. The honey is harvested 2-3 times a year, and the bee larvae are eaten along with it.

The Wa people generally enjoy drinking alcohol and bitter tea. The alcohol they drink is homemade “soaked water wine,” which not only does no harm to the body but also benefits health. In recent decades, the Wa people have started consuming distilled white liquor. They especially love bitter tea, sometimes brewed so strong that it almost becomes tea paste. Although it tastes bitter, it leaves a refreshing sensation afterward, making it an excellent thirst quencher in hot climates.

Chewing betel nuts is a universal preference among Wa men, women, and children. Whether during labor breaks or leisurely conversations, they always chew betel nuts. The ones they chew are substitutes made by boiling oak leaves and lime, claimed to strengthen teeth.

Typical foods include: tea flower porridge, fire-roasted snake meat, green pea stir-fried ant eggs, and fried firewood worms.
