Dianping.com: The Bone That Giants Can't Chew, Living in the Zone of Discomfort

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 16:46:53

Tao Zhang, CEO of Dianping

Written by Yuan Yin, edited by Jing Yang, photographed by Nan Xiao

Tao Zhang, CEO of Dianping, has once again postponed the company's IPO.

In 2011, Dianping received a $100 million Series C funding round from Sequoia Capital and others, valuing the company at $1 billion. According to convention, it should have chosen an opportune time to go public within the following one to two years, but it didn't. Instead, in 2012, it raised another $60 million in its Series D round.

With Dianping's scale and development momentum, going public would not be difficult. However, Tao Zhang feels that going public now would be too costly. He breaks down the big internet (PC + mobile) into four elements: people, information, goods, and services. Each connection point offers opportunities for giants to emerge. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, and Alibaba connects people with goods. The space between people and services is where the fourth giant could arise. Since 2003, Dianping has been working hard to accumulate data for this purpose, finally waiting for the explosion of mobile internet, where phones connect "location" with "services." Following Zhang's logic, no company seems more fitting than Dianping for the imagination of the fourth giant.

But the outside world doesn't see it that way. In recent months, with frequent acquisitions by BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent), Dianping has become a presumed acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition target; whichever last name from BAT it adopts could help them achieve greatness sooner. On the other hand, Dianping's size pales in comparison to BAT, and it's still too early to declare it a future giant. Moreover, whether it's Tencent WeChat's public platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after investing in AutoNavi and Meituan, all are directly targeting Dianping's territory—local life services centered around food, drink, entertainment, and leisure.

Before proving the following two things, Dianping's IPO would equate to a "discounted sale." First, Zhang needs some time to prove that what Dianping is doing now, BAT cannot do. Second, Dianping is about to engage in the ultimate battle of group buying. If it secures the top position, its valuation could increase several times over.

2013 is Dianping's "decisive year." Regarding speculation and rumors, Zhang pays them no heed. He is very clear: "The internet has indeed entered a great era of integration, but Dianping will not be acquired."

Zhang categorizes companies that might be acquired into three types. The first type possesses platform attributes but is better suited to develop within the ecosystem of a giant, such as maps and email. These products can easily attract large numbers of users but struggle to find independent business models. Others, like browsers, while profitable, earn far less through navigation services than their strategic value to large companies. "Giants are willing to invest in a mail product because it helps with brand and user retention, but I've never heard of a standalone mail company succeeding." The second type involves industry consolidation, such as in the video industry, where acquisitions expand market share. The third type is when a company faces financial difficulties and sells itself to survive.

Now, Meituan has been brought under Alibaba's wing. In theory, Dianping should quickly seek a "backer" to avoid being defeated by rivals gaining capital and expanding rapidly. However, Zhang says, "Giants offer us limited help. We do need users, but execution is more important. Mergers and acquisitions would reduce our execution capabilities and could marginalize us within a large company." Furthermore, he must maintain the most precise "distance" between Dianping and the giants. Getting too close to any one of them would amount to "choosing sides," making Dianping an enemy to the others.

Thus, logically speaking, acquiring Dianping has reached a dead end. Zhang counters, "Why would we take their money? We're not short on cash; what we need are resources. If a giant were willing to take a small stake and provide us with many resources, I might consider it. But would they? Would a giant give you resources if they only held a small stake?"

Weapon One: Dirty and Hard Work

In the mobile internet space, opportunities for companies solely focused on pure internet products are becoming fewer. New opportunities lie in those that combine with traditional industries. Zhang believes that life services primarily revolve around food, drink, entertainment, and leisure. Other niche markets, such as ride-hailing, can form good business models, but it's unlikely for any of them to grow into giants.

For food, drink, entertainment, and leisure, user needs are simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is the core of life services, which is exactly where Dianping started. Whether in content, member accumulation, or brand, newcomers will find it hard to surpass Dianping.

The most representative product form of discounts is group buying. Group buying has now entered the championship battle, and Dianping will face off against Meituan in 2013. This is a winner-takes-all market; as long as there are two strong players, the industry's gross margin will be reduced, making stable profits impossible.

A visible future lies in reservations. Reservations can create a true online-to-offline loop, but this must be built on the foundation of Dianping having absolute advantages in both information and discounts. Otherwise, creating a closed loop would be difficult. In 2012, Dianping began experimenting with reservations. Zhang said, "Currently, feedback from users and merchants is positive, but if reservations were done alone, it would be hard to solve the chicken-or-egg problem."

Of course, life services present some even "heavier" opportunities. For example, food delivery is a rigid demand for users and helps merchants increase sales. In the past couple of years, food delivery companies like "To Home Gourmet Society" have flourished. However, the core of food delivery is delivery logistics—the food must be delivered within a short time, and issues like soups need to be addressed. The logistics requirements are higher than those of fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang said, "If executed well, food delivery presents opportunities, but it's harder dirty work. What Dianping is already doing is dirty and tiring enough."

If judged by age, Dianping is already an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has painstakingly accumulated merchant information and user reviews, which Zhang refers to as "dirty and hard work." This primitive growth model once limited its speed, but the past pain has become Dianping's core barrier today. It can be said that currently, apart from Meituan, Dianping has no real competitors in the life services sector.

However, he must remain vigilant against BAT's "clutches." Baidu's intervention is the most direct. Baidu Maps has over 100 million users, and users can use the map to search for nearby dining and other life services. Tencent, considered to have secured its ticket to mobile internet, has launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest goods trading platform, and expanding goods from physical items to services is logical, especially since it has incorporated Meituan and AutoNavi into its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there hasn't been an explosive growth in either reality or data," Zhang said. Brand positioning is crucial; the key is which product users choose first when they want to eat, drink, entertain, or relax.

Besides, in the vast field of life services, competition among giants will inevitably cross paths, and no one can completely monopolize the area. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained on Weibo and WeChat. Zhang feels that in this way, Dianping is actually safer. "In niche areas like car rentals and business travel, the massive user base redirected by giants could easily overwhelm existing companies."

As for Alibaba, which has invested in AutoNavi, wanting to invade Dianping's territory isn't so simple. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, which can always be categorized regardless of how peculiar they may seem. A user's experience of eating a meal depends on various factors such as the environment on that day, the waitstaff, and the chef. Managing merchants cannot rely on systems and tools like Alibaba does; it must establish a 'ground force' managed by humans.

"All three giants want to take this path, and there's room for imagination, but this is not a highway; it's a rugged mountain road," Zhang believes. First, providing life services requires ground teams deployed at various levels, something BAT lacks the genes and capability to manage offline. Second, they lack the willingness; life services require focused effort, not just sending out a small team, and making money is slow. "A company finds it hard to excel at two important tasks simultaneously, let alone needing to consolidate its advantage in its original field."

Weapon Two: Small Ships Are Easier to Maneuver

In 2011, when Dianping secured $100 million in financing, Zhang told the outside world that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, times change quickly, and before Dianping went public, his analogies fell from grace.

He wasn't particularly anxious about it. "They failed to become big companies because they lacked offline teams. Now, they all want to be America's Dianping."

The current path is one Zhang has gradually explored himself. When he felt that selling keyword ads and coupons was outdated, the emergence of group buying caught his eye. Initially, like the Americans, he viewed group buying as a marketing platform, one deal per day. But as orders increased, he realized that group buying had transactional properties. Thus, he envisioned Dianping's future as a life service trading platform. The two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were precisely used for Dianping's transformation from an information platform to a trading platform.

Without this "genetic modification," Zhang might not have had the confidence to compete for the fourth seat among the giants. But genetic modification sounds simple but is equivalent to Dianping's second entrepreneurship, an extremely painful process.

First, compared to selling ads separately, the gross margin of a trading platform decreases, requiring stricter cost control and efficiency. Second, a trading platform demands higher inter-departmental communication capabilities.

Previously, to maintain Dianping's impartiality and independence as an information platform, the content and sales teams were entirely separated. Zhang even deliberately established a "firewall" between departments—no matter how important the client, the information on the platform couldn't be modified for sales purposes, which once led to mutual hostility between content and sales teams. Now, as a trading platform, the editorial and sales teams need deep communication to create good marketing plans, which require not just planning but also editorial cooperation. He said, "Maintaining information quality remains important, but there's no need to reinforce animosity anymore. The firewall needs to come down."

Though editors can logically accept this, individual behaviors and values have inertia, and when combined across the entire company, the inertia becomes even greater. To address this, Zhang specifically held several meetings to encourage more communication between departments. Months later, an editor manager casually mentioned to a sales manager, "I used to think you were the enemy, but now I realize you're my colleague."

The sales department also needed genetic modification. To promote conversion, Zhang cleverly gave the offline team dual roles. First, for merchants, salespeople should transform into marketing consultants—not just selling products but helping merchants devise marketing strategies. Second, for the company, marketing consultants are the company's procurement agents. Dianping is like a supermarket; not only must the product quality be good, but the variety must be rich. Marketing consultants should aim to help users procure group-buying products. Of course, dual roles will inevitably lead to a series of complex issues. Frontline personnel will find it challenging to balance the scales themselves.

Substantially, Dianping's "dual role" is quite similar to Alibaba splitting into 25 business units. Decentralizing decision-making to frontline staff allows for faster response speeds but increases management difficulty. Similarly, compared to frontline employees, Dianping's management team suffers more during the genetic modification phase.

Every year, Zhang breaks down Dianping's company goals into various departments, which are then further broken down layer by layer by senior executives. However, breaking down these goals isn't simple arithmetic. Li Jing, co-founder of Dianping, responsible for the sales team, sometimes finds unresolved merchant demands due to sales issues affecting performance. Thus, Li Jing created a new position, setting up communication specialists who collect information daily from marketing consultants, submit it to other departments, and track feedback. He often tells his team, "Being adept at embracing change doesn't mean liking change; no one likes change. Everyone feels uncomfortable when faced with change—all employees must go through discomfort zones, including me."

The numbers reflect the "weight" of Dianping's execution team. Out of more than 3,000 employees, over 2,000 work offline. The ground force is responsible for communicating directly with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group buying, and combined marketing solutions.

For this core force that resists the clutches of BAT, Dianping has a unique management and evaluation system.

They aren't called "salespeople;" from the first day, their business cards read "Marketing Planning Consultant." Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the stage of watching news and chatting on QQ, lagging behind Taobao merchants by at least three to five years in terms of internet understanding. Marketing planning consultants must inform merchants of online users' needs and how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing said, "Even for a high-end restaurant, the marketing consultant wouldn't suggest a grouponsale for abalone or shark fin, possibly switching to other dishes, because online users and offline users have different needs."

An outstanding marketing consultant must be an all-rounder. They need to explain to restaurant owners or barbershop owners the fundamental differences between discounts and group buying and which discount aligns better with their positioning. If facing a hot pot restaurant owner, the marketing consultant should know whether it's the off-season or peak season, which products' costs rise during certain seasons, and which decrease, proposing a group-buying menu that both merchants and users prefer.

This places high demands on marketing consultants, requiring them not only to be internet marketing experts but also industry experts. Li Jing said, "I must admit, it's impossible for every consultant to be a double expert, so we set star ratings for consultants." Like hotels, marketing consultants range from one-star to five-star, with the best ones being called Super Stars.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline workers aren't tech-savvy graduates from prestigious universities, making it challenging for them to understand internet products. Dianping has established a knowledge base for marketing consultants, containing knowledge points and related cases that both new and experienced employees can browse. If encountering a new issue, employees can ask questions in the knowledge base, and thousands of consultants nationwide will help answer them.

Many knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees based on their practical experiences. Dianping has set up a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, with employees who help others the most each quarter receiving rewards. Every quarter, Dianping also conducts skill assessments for employees, with results directly tied to promotions.

Compared to internet knowledge, industry knowledge is harder to acquire. Some experiences are summarized by traditional merchants after years of industry practice. A Super Star marketing consultant told Li Jing that hair salons should avoid offering wash-cut-blow dry package deals because men lack motivation to buy them, and women mostly blow dry without washing and cutting together. Li Jing was astonished, "We men would never have thought of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing consultants are not only divided by cities but also by industries such as catering, hairdressing, and leisure. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is further subdivided into hotpot, Western cuisine, and buffets, with one marketing consultant responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing consultants can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, making merchants less resistant when selling products," he said.

Each marketing group holds its own case-sharing sessions: what difficulties a particular type of merchant encountered, what marketing needs they had, what solutions the company provided, and the outcomes. Outstanding cases are reported upward, and Dianping officially shares one case each week.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition called "Entrepreneurial Winner." Within the first three months of joining, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products but listening to merchants sharing their experiences. If they are responsible for hotpot restaurants, after three months, they must submit a business plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hotpot Restaurant Owner." Like graduation, employees present their defense, and industry supervisors evaluate the winners, rewarding them accordingly.

Strategically speaking, whether it's Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, their tasks are quite similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O are familiar to anyone with basic industry knowledge, and their spoken words are almost identical. "The era where storytelling and concept creation could lead to success is over. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model lies in management and execution, not strategy," Zhang said.
