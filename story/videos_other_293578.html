Market Judgment: Uptrend in the first quarter, retracement in the second quarter, stock forum, oscillating uptrend in the second half of the year. Liquidity: Policy effect decays, but the direction in the first quarter remains positive. Financial market liquidity is mainly driven by three aspects: Firstly, the rapid increase in commercial bank loans leading to a rise in the money multiplier; Secondly, foreign exchange reserves formed by trade or FDI surplus and the resulting issuance of base money; Thirdly, the enhancement of money circulation speed leading to the activation of savings. In 2010, liquidity gradually shifts from policy-driven to endogenous growth-driven, with the credit effect gradually weakening. RMB appreciation and savings activation are potential over-performing directions, so although the policy effect of liquidity is decaying, its direction in the first quarter remains neutral to positive.

Economic Growth: Certain recovery in the first quarter, sustainability constrained by structural factors. Economic growth is jointly driven by cyclical fluctuations and structural trends. From a cyclical perspective, economic growth largely depends on the improvement of real estate investment and external economies; from a structural perspective, the sustainability of economic growth relies on global rebalancing and domestic structural adjustments. Based on historical experience, the adjustment of global economic growth patterns should be based on institutional innovation or technological innovation. Although technologies in new energy, environmental protection industries, and information industries are gradually developing, they still cannot independently drive the economic cycle at present. It can be said that the global economic rebalancing in 2010 will be quantitative rather than qualitative. The future consumption engine of the world will switch to a dual-drive system involving developed countries and emerging markets, making it difficult for the global economy to return to the previous high-speed growth.

Based on the investment clock of liquidity, economic growth, and inflation cycles, when liquidity decreases but both the economy and inflation are in an upward phase, it is the best time for stock investments; the macroeconomic setting in the first quarter supports market strengthening.

Funds Supply and Demand & Valuation: In 2010, funds supply is less than demand, with a funds supply of 971 billion yuan and stock supply of 104.9 billion yuan. Combining progress on some projects, as well as the historical seasonal distribution of financing (often in the second half), and the need for the Issuance Review Committee to report annual results, funds supply is usually abundant in the first quarter. Bank financing may concentrate in the second and third quarters, with the second quarter facing the greatest funding pressure. The overall valuation of the stock market is near historical averages, with most industries exceeding historical averages. Therefore, from a historical dimension, valuations do not have a clear direction. Compared with peripheral markets and other asset returns, there is still room for valuation improvement.

Investment Themes: Focus on midstream manufacturing, with structural opportunities upstream and downstream.

Currently, cyclical industries face uncertainties such as a decrease in fixed asset investment growth, reduced liquidity, and policy exits, while downstream industries we recommended in the fourth quarter are expected to maintain a favorable fundamental situation in 2010. However, from a market valuation perspective, cyclical industry valuations are relatively low, while consumer goods valuations are relatively high. Whether logically deduced or empirically tested, optional consumption "high consolidation" will lead to excess returns being passed upstream. Upstream resources, though constrained by declining liquidity, benefit from the recovery in demand and "capacity reduction." The liquidity-driven price increases in commodities in 2009 were inconsistent with historical experiences due to simultaneous inventory and price increases. In 2010, liquidity-driven "price increases" will return to normal, and "volume increases" driven by economic recovery will begin. Our configuration primarily seeks industry "volume" points. We believe that the valuation increase in cyclical industries comes from the elimination of constraints such as policy regulation, financing pressure, and overcapacity, or from institutional changes like margin trading and short selling, and stock index futures, which enhance the collateral value of large-cap stocks. Valuation increases in the consumer sector come from earnings outperformance. Although financials are undervalued, their large market capitalization and financing pressures before the second quarter make it difficult for valuations to increase in the first quarter. Overall, we recommend over-allocating to the following industries in the first quarter of 2010: transportation, machinery, chemicals, steel, coal, food and beverage, commerce, agriculture, and pharmaceuticals.
