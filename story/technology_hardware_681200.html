Can atrophic gastritis become cancerous? After treatment, simple mild chronic atrophic gastritis has a good prognosis. Chronic atrophic gastritis was once considered a prelude to gastric cancer, i.e., a precancerous lesion. Nowadays, this understanding is somewhat biased. Experts indicate that there is indeed a certain relationship between atrophic gastritis and gastric cancer, based on the following:

1. For patients with atrophic gastritis, biopsies taken during endoscopy that show colonic-type intestinal metaplasia and atypical hyperplasia of the gastric mucosa may potentially develop into gastric cancer, which is an acknowledged fact.

2. Epidemiological surveys have shown that in populations from areas with high incidence of gastric cancer, the incidence of atrophic gastritis is also relatively high.

3. Pathological examinations reveal that around gastric cancer tissues, atrophic lesions of the mucosa are commonly found.

Chronic atrophic gastritis of moderate or severe degree, especially when accompanied by colonic-type epithelial metaplasia or atypical hyperplasia as shown in pathological examinations, is considered a precancerous lesion. If not treated actively and properly managed, it can easily lead to gastric cancer.

Therefore, patients with chronic atrophic gastritis need regular follow-up gastroscopy: general chronic atrophic gastritis should be reviewed every 3 years; those with incomplete colonic-type intestinal metaplasia and mild atypical hyperplasia should be reviewed annually; those with moderate atypical hyperplasia should be reviewed every 3 months; and those with severe atypical hyperplasia should be regarded as having transformed into cancer and may require surgical resection. Only through active treatment, proper lifestyle adjustments, and regular follow-ups can the condition be improved or stabilized, thereby preventing the occurrence of gastric cancer.

How to prevent proctitis and its complications? Gastrointestinal experts from Zhengzhou Xiehe Hospital indicate that proctitis commonly occurs in individuals with weak constitutions, low immunity, heart, lung, liver, gastrointestinal diseases, respiratory infections, infectious diseases, constipation, diarrhea, hemorrhoids, anal-rectal prolapse, anal fistula, polyps, anal-rectal stenosis, rectal tumors, rectal injury, foreign bodies, etc., all of which can cause inflammation of the rectum. Severe cases require immediate hospitalization for surgical treatment.

1. Preventive Measures

Avoiding anal intercourse can prevent the sexual transmission of proctitis; reducing intake of caffeine, dairy products, and high-fat foods. Artificial sweeteners may lower the incidence of proctitis. Studies have linked ulcerative colitis with frequent consumption of these foods; various stress-reducing methods, such as yoga, tai chi, and deep relaxation, can also reduce the likelihood of developing proctitis. Research shows that stress can cause inflammation of the intestinal wall, possibly leading to complications and inflammatory bowel disease.

2. Preventing Complications

Complications of proctitis can be caused by the formation of ulcers and result in severe bleeding. Proctitis caused by colonic ulcers may progress to involve a wider range of the colon and other parts of the gastrointestinal tract.

Mild proctitis or the use of common ointments or foams generally does not require long-term drug therapy. In more severe cases of proctitis, the efficacy of conventional treatments tends to be lower, with failure rates reaching up to 35% in some cases. Overall, most patients with proctitis who receive appropriate treatment and ongoing medical supervision at Zhengzhou Xiehe Hospital generally have a favorable prognosis.
