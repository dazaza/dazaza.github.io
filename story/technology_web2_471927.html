Fight Fire with Fire - Remote Control Plane

by diujiaskxc on 2011-08-06 14:41:32

My son went to the United States for further studies, and settled there after graduation. He even found me a foreign daughter-in-law named Susan. Now, my little grandson Toby is already 3 years old. This summer, my son applied for a family visit visa for me. I stayed in the U.S. for three months and was greatly surprised by the way my Western daughter-in-law Susan educated her child, such as remote control planes.

If you don't eat, then you stay hungry

Every morning when Toby wakes up, Susan puts breakfast on the table and goes about her own business. Toby climbs onto the stool himself, drinks milk, and eats bread slices. After he's full, he returns to his room, finds clothes and shoes in the wardrobe, and dresses himself. Since Toby is only 3 years old, he can't distinguish the front and back of clothes or the left and right of shoes. Once, Toby wore his pants inside out again, and I quickly wanted to help him change, but Susan stopped me. She said that if he felt uncomfortable, he would take them off and put them on correctly; if he didn't feel any discomfort, then let it be. That whole day, Toby ran around with his pants inside out, and Susan acted as if she didn't see it.

Another time, Toby went out to play with a neighbor's little friend and soon came running home panting, saying to Susan: Mommy, Lucy said my pants are inside out, really? Lucy is a little girl from the neighboring house, who is 5 years old this year. Susan smiled and said: Yes, do you want to change them back? Toby shook his head, took off his pants himself, carefully looked at them, and put them back on. From then on, Toby never wore his pants inside out again.

I couldn't help but think about my granddaughter who was five or six years old and didn't know how to use chopsticks, couldn't tie shoelaces in primary school. Now she is in boarding junior high school, and every weekend, she brings home a pile of dirty clothes.

One noon, Toby threw a tantrum and refused to eat. Susan scolded him a few words, and the angry little Toby pushed the plate to the ground, spilling the food all over the floor. Susan looked at Toby seriously and said: It seems like you really don't want to eat! Remember, from now until tomorrow morning, you can't eat anything. Toby nodded firmly and replied: Yes! I secretly laughed in my heart, these mother and son are both quite stubborn!

In the afternoon, Susan consulted with me, and decided that I would cook Chinese food for dinner that night. I understood immediately. Toby loves Chinese food, and it must be because Susan thought Toby didn't eat well at lunch and wanted him to eat more at dinner.

That evening, I showcased my culinary skills, making Toby's favorite sweet and sour pork ribs, oil-steamed large shrimp, and also made Chinese-style cold noodles with Italian pasta. Toby loved eating those cold noodles, and the little person could eat a whole big plate.

When it was time for dinner, Toby happily climbed onto the stool. However, Susan walked over, took away his plate and cutlery, and said: We've already agreed that today you can't eat, and you agreed yourself too. Toby looked at his serious mom and burst into tears, crying and saying: Mommy, I'm hungry, I want to eat. No, what's been said has to count. Susan showed no mercy.

I felt pity and wanted to plead for Toby, to say some good words, but I saw my son giving me a look. Recalling when I first arrived in the U.S., my son told me that when parents educate their children here, others should not interfere, even elders are no exception. Helpless, I had to remain silent.

Throughout that meal, poor little Toby sat in his toy car, helplessly watching us three adults eat voraciously. I finally understood Susan's real intention in asking me to make Chinese food. I believe that next time, when Toby wants to throw a tantrum and toss his bowl, he will definitely remember the experience of being hungry while watching his parents and grandmother enjoy delicious food. The feeling of hunger is unpleasant, especially when facing your favorite food.

Before bedtime, Susan and I went together to say goodnight to Toby. Toby boldly asked: Mommy, I'm very hungry, can I eat Chinese noodles now? Susan smiled and shook her head firmly: No! Toby sighed, and asked again: Then, can I eat after I wake up from sleeping? Of course, you can. Susan answered gently. Toby smiled sweetly.

Most of the time, Toby eats very actively. He doesn't want to miss out on food due to refusing to eat and endure hunger again. Every time I see Toby concentrating on eating big bites, with food sticking to his mouth and face, I think of my granddaughter. When she was Toby's age, several adults followed her around holding bowls of food just to coax her to eat, but she wouldn't comply, and even made demands: buy a toy after finishing one bowl, another toy after another bowl...

Repaying someone with their own method

One day, we took Toby to the park to play. Soon, Toby started playing kitchen games with two girls. Plastic small pots, shovels, plates, and bowls were scattered everywhere. Suddenly, the mischievous Toby picked up the small pot and hit one of the girls on the head hard, and the girl, startled, burst into tears. The other girl, younger, seeing this, was frightened and cried too. Perhaps Toby didn't expect such serious consequences, standing aside, stunned.

Susan walked over, clarified the cause and effect of the incident, and without a word, picked up the small pot and hit Toby on the head hard. Unprepared, Toby fell sitting on the grass and wailed. Susan asked Toby: Does it hurt? Will you do it again next time? Toby cried while shaking his head vigorously. I believe he won't do it again.
