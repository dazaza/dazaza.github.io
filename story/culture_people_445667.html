Recently, an article has been frequently shared on Facebook and Twitter titled "Nurse Reveals the Top 5 Regrets People Have on Their Deathbeds." The original article was written by a woman named Bronnie Ware. Bronnie Ware specializes in caring for those who are dying, so she has had the opportunity to hear many people express their deepest regrets as they approach the end of their lives. She summarized that there are five things which most people regret the most.

I am curious as to why so many people have shared this article. Perhaps it is because this is something you can never experience in advance. You do not often face the death of others, and even less often do you have the chance to hear someone on their deathbed tell you what they regret the most. And even if you do hear it, you may think that you still have plenty of time. We seem to never truly empathize; perhaps only when our own lives come to an end will we realize what we have missed and what we regret the most.

1. I wish I had the courage to live a life true to myself, not the life others expected of me.

This is the most common regret heard.

There is a psychological theory that states people regret more the things they did not do than the things they did. So when people look back at the end of their lives, they often realize that there were many dreams they should have pursued but did not. Our way of living, our jobs, our emotions, and our partners—how many of us live lives others expect of us rather than lives we truly want? Or possibly, all along, we have mistaken the life others expect of us for the life we want.

When you are plagued by illness, you realize that you could have and should have let go of many concerns to pursue the life you wanted—it seems a bit too late now.

2. I wish I hadn't worked so hard.

Ware says this is something every man she cared for would say. Because of work, they missed out on the joy of watching their children grow up and the warmth of being with their loved ones, which is their deepest regret and guilt. This will also become an issue for today's career women.

If you simplify your life a little, you might find that you are doing many things you think you need to do but actually don't need to. Freeing up the space these tasks occupy may make you happier.

3. I wish I had the courage to express my feelings.

Too many people suppress their feelings and thoughts just to maintain "peace," avoiding conflicts with others. Gradually, they become mediocre, unable to become the person they could have been. In fact, many diseases are related to the long-term suppression of anger and negative emotions.

Perhaps when you speak your mind, you may offend certain people. But maybe from then on, because of your honesty, you will form unexpected friendships; or maybe a confrontation will allow you to break free from exhausting relationships that require you to suppress your feelings to maintain. Either way, you win, right? — Of course, speaking your mind does have its limits.

4. I wish I had kept in touch with my friends.

We only realize the value of old friends when we need them.

How many people neglect their friends and once brilliant friendships due to their busy lives. Many people finally let go of money and power as they near death, yet they cannot let go of the emotions and attachments in their hearts. Whether friends or lovers, in the final days of life, they are our deepest concerns.

5. I wish I had let myself be happier.

This one might be surprising, but it is indeed among the top 5. Many people only realize at the end of their lives that "happiness is a choice."

They have been trapped in their established habits and lifestyles for too long, accustomed to concealing, pretending, and putting on a smiling face in front of others. As the Mayday song goes, "You're not truly happy; your smile is just the protective color you put on." They thought it was life that made them unhappy, but in reality, it was themselves who made themselves unhappy.

It is only when facing death that they realize, how does it matter what others think of you? Whether perceived as foolish or strange, having sincere laughter is worth more than anything.
