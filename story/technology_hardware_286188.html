Impression of the City South

by fkeiigtd on 2009-11-30 08:51:28

Writing the two characters "Chengnan" easily brings to mind "Old Events in the South of the City," as well as the song "Farewell," whose melody stirs deep within: Beyond the long pavilion, by the ancient road, green grass stretches endlessly to the sky. The evening wind brushes against the willow, the flute's sound lingers faintly, and beyond the setting sun lie the mountains.

There is a Chengnan Park in Shaoyang. The day before yesterday, while accompanying a colleague on business in Shaoyang, I managed to steal some leisure time amidst the tasks and took a stroll. It wasn't a weekend, yet the park was not deserted. Everywhere, couples embraced closely, whispering sweet nothings to each other without any regard for others' presence—by the riverside, in the winding corridors, under the shady trees, or on the benches beside the path. Also present were children around eight or nine years old, wearing roller skates, gliding past as lightly as swallows. Just like many fleeting moments of youth, they quickly disappeared from sight.

I also encountered numerous brides taking wedding photos here. Their rented dresses, even though inexpensive, were still somewhat faded in color. With heavy layers of foundation on their faces, their makeup was so vibrant that it almost obscured their natural features. Yet, they were still enviable. Regardless of how dirty these dresses might appear to outsiders, they continue to carry the purest fantasies and simplest wishes for happiness of one bride after another regarding marriage. Their hearts would undoubtedly be filled with happiness, despite the fact that these dresses were much cheaper than imagined (I once considered buying one for myself, thinking it would cost thousands, so I gave up. After marriage, I saw online that an evening gown could be purchased for 89 yuan, and the style was quite nice. Of course, good ones are still expensive). Because of this happiness, women eagerly bind themselves with the chains of marriage, work hard to raise a child who may not even bear their surname, diligently support their in-laws, take part of his meager salary either boldly or humbly, endure being scrutinized by him as if they were old furniture or a free nanny, and gradually realize through his repeated lies: men are naturally lustful, and no man is not fickle; husband, husband, just manage within the bounds of a hundred feet; as long as the red flag doesn't fall, let the colorful flags flutter...

Entering the cage of marriage, women find the truth too terrifying to reveal. In marriage, women have too much bitterness, and the little sweetness left to savor is merely the moment of wearing the wedding dress. Despite knowing that these wedding dresses were not bought by their husbands.

I love watching brides, although I wish them happiness but feel sorrow as a woman. This feeling is akin to knowing that flowers will wither after blooming, yet still admiring their full bloom.

The elderly are another kind of scenery in life. They sit in groups in the long pavilion, holding their songbooks seriously, singing challenging folk songs with less-than-professional skills. My colleague jokingly suggested I go over to teach them, but I shot him a glare. I haven't dared to sing for a long time. My voice has been ruined for a long time, perhaps I still sing better than these elders, but their passion for life and art is something I don't dare to desecrate. Perhaps I could joyfully join them in singing, but talking about teaching them would be a desecration. All sincere singing, regardless of whether the voice is sweet or lacks technique, is beautiful and moving. So I stayed still, not daring to move, only listening intently to their aged voices singing "Night on the Grassland" and "That's Me." In my heart, they are no less wonderful than Yu Junjian or Dai Yuqiang.

I greedily observe them, their faces etched with the ravages of time, their smiles resembling withered chrysanthemums. Among them, the uncle with the loudest voice casually rolls up one trouser leg and then lets it down again, but this does not at all detract from their beauty in my eyes and heart. A person, having endured the realities and trials of life, can still retain such optimism and openness, making them both adorable and respectable. I hope that when I grow old, I too will age gracefully, able to smile like a withered chrysanthemum but still retaining a lingering fragrance.

My colleague said, the city is different from the county, offering much richer cultural activities during leisure time. I nodded. Once, such a colorful and quality-filled life was my ideal, which is why I tried so hard to leave the county where I grew up. However, just as I finally managed to buy a house by the Xiangjiang River Scenic Belt, settle down, and was about to fulfill these childhood dreams, I hesitated and stopped in my tracks. Endless confusion and melancholy. I realized, ultimately, butterflies cannot fly across the sea.
