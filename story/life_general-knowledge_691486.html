How to prevent side effects of drugs?

There is a saying "all medicines are one third toxic", which simply but truly reveals the nature of drugs. Yes, drugs are something that "can be either toxic or curative". That is to say, no matter what kind of drug, it must contain some toxins.

After hearing this, you might feel scared. But this is the essence of medicine. In other words, if a substance cannot be toxic, then it cannot be called a "drug".

This phrase "all medicines are one third toxic" refers to the dual nature of drugs. Generally speaking, we call them "effects" and "side effects". For example, when a cold causes fever, we take antipyretics, but this medicine can cause quite a burden on the gastrointestinal tract and liver. At this point, we can say that the antipyretic reduces body temperature, which is an "effect", while the burden on the gastrointestinal tract and liver is a "side effect". The efficacy of a drug always involves both "effects" and "side effects".

When taking medication, we should not only think about the "effect" and ignore the "side effect". Otherwise, the worst situation would happen: the medicine prescribed for treating disease ends up harming the body.

To prevent regrets and suffering caused by side effects, the most important thing is to maintain good communication with the doctor when prescribing medicine. Accidents caused by side effects may result from insufficient professional knowledge of some doctors, insufficient explanation of the drugs, inadequate understanding of the patients themselves, and other complicated reasons.

When we see a doctor and he is about to prescribe medicine, we should first ask clearly why we need to take this medicine? What side effects will occur after taking it? Can our body tolerate it? And listen carefully to the doctor's instructions.

In recent years, the practice of "informed consent (doctors have the responsibility to inform patients of their condition and treatment methods)" has been fully implemented. Therefore, no matter what questions the patient has, the doctor needs to answer truthfully. If you encounter a doctor who does not explain in detail or thinks there is no need to tell the patient too much, I suggest changing to another hospital.

However, those who bear the "responsibility of informing" are not just doctors. Patients themselves should also clearly state their own conditions to the doctor so that the doctor can understand your physical constitution and medical history. Because some side effects incidents often occur due to insufficient information provided by the patients.

For example, if a patient tells the doctor "I seem to have a fever recently", the doctor actually cannot know whether the fever occurred in the past two or three days or started a month ago. Therefore, you should specify "since last weekend" or "since last autumn", which will allow the doctor to immediately understand your health status.

If you are allergic to certain types of drugs or are taking medications for other diseases at the same time, please make sure to inform the doctor in advance. Sometimes taking several kinds of medicine together can easily lead to accidents, so don't be careless about this.
