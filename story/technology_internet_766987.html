I want an iPhone, it has the appearance of 5c and a heart of 5s beating

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 10:58:58

　　Two iPhones Diverged…

　　The thing I'm about to say may surprise a lot of friends: After trying out the iPhone 5c and 5s for a week, I prefer the 5c. This result surprised me as well.

　　Like many others, I didn't pay much attention to the iPhone 5c; overall, it's just an iPhone 5 with a different shell. However, when I actually used it, I was deeply "attracted" to it.

　　Let me clarify: I still want to buy an iPhone 5s, but only because the iPhone is my most frequently used phone. A faster and more efficient iPhone is obviously better, which is the significance of the 5s upgrade.

　　But the iPhone 5c is all about "beauty," and I didn't expect to feel this way.

　　Apple gave me two devices for testing: a space gray iPhone 5s and a blue iPhone 5c. There are already many professional reviews of these two devices and iOS 7, so I won't repeat them here. Below, I'll share some thoughts I had while using these two devices:

　　iPhone 5c

　　A few weeks ago, when Apple announced the iPhone 5c/5s, I wrote on Tumblr that the iPhone 5c looks like it was specifically designed by Ive for iOS 7. After handling it for a while, I became even more convinced of this feeling.

　　The iPhone 5c and iOS 7 are a perfect match. The most obvious connection between the hardware and software is their color usage, but it doesn't feel too "superficial." This visual link is partly due to the colorful border around the screen. You see this every day, and you're far more likely to see it than the back cover.

　　The colored "ring" around the iPhone gives it personality. In previous iterations, Apple tried to place all the front elements behind the screen, keeping the screen at the forefront. But this time, Apple didn't do that.

　　This sense of resonance becomes even more apparent after turning on the device. Apple has pre-set wallpapers that correspond to the specific color of the iPhone 5c. This is another small but important detail, and these details give this iPhone an unprecedented "personality." (Of course, after turning it on, you can change the wallpaper.)

　　After the launch event, I also tried the iPhone 5c. At the time, I tweeted that the buttons on the frame were a bit "troublesome" to press. I was wrong. They are made of plastic, which naturally makes them cheaper to produce than those on the iPhone 5s, but they don't feel cheap. Pressing them feels solid, as if they are integrated with the body.

　　Unlike the 5s, all the buttons on the 5c, including the SIM card slot, speaker, power button, etc., match perfectly.

　　It turns out that the price of the iPhone 5c isn't exactly cheap either. Many people might wonder if the use of plastic material will make it look cheap. If you have the chance to handle it, you'll realize that this concern is unwarranted. Yes, it's made of plastic, but in terms of feel, it doesn't feel "plastic" at all. Just compare the plastic packaging of the iPhone 5c with the actual feel of the device. One feels cheap, while the other feels solid. When you hold it, the 5c feels like a polished stone.

　　Therefore, the 5c feels better in use than the 5s and its predecessors. It resembles the earlier iPhone 3GS or 3G, which also used plastic shells, except the back isn't curved. From this perspective, the 5c is more like a synthesis of past iPhone designs, an iPhone with smoother curves. In contrast, the iPhone 5s is a thorough industrial design piece.

　　Of course, some people will prefer the iPhone 5s, which comes down to personal preference between aluminum straight lines and plastic rounded curves. Interestingly, the iPod touch now seems like a combination of both: the aluminum 5s paired with the rounded curves of the 5c.

　　Before the release, Ben Thompson mentioned that we could finally welcome a more affordable iPhone, as the hardware of the iPhone 5 was already "good enough." Setting aside the issue of affordability, I agree with his view on the hardware— the hardware of the iPhone 5, which is now the core of the iPhone 5c, still feels fast.

　　After using all iterations of the iPhone, I can clearly see how much the performance of the iPhone has improved over time. In terms of speed enhancement, Apple has done significantly better than other manufacturers (I still believe that the upgrade from the 3G to the 3GS was the most noticeable). This update is one of the smallest changes in terms of sensory experience among all upgrades.

　　Of course, I know that from a data perspective, doubling the CPU is a significant improvement. It might be because almost all current apps haven't fully utilized the advantages of the new A7 and 64-bit processor, but when comparing and playing with the 5s and 5c, the difference in speed isn't very noticeable. The 5s loads apps and content slightly faster than the 5c, but these differences aren't something you'd overly notice.

　　This is one of the reasons why I prefer the iPhone 5c. In the past, once a new model came out, it was hard for me to go back to the slower older version. This time, it's different.

　　iPhone 5s

　　The standout feature of the iPhone 5s is undoubtedly Touch ID, and it works smoothly—you'll use it often. Clearly, this is a feature that other manufacturers will follow after Apple introduced it. After using the iPhone 5s, I would unconsciously try to trigger Touch ID on other iPhones, much like how you instinctively tap the screen when switching from an iPad to a MacBook after using the iPad for a long time.

　　Regarding the reports that hackers have successfully bypassed Touch ID, I want to say: if you want a smartphone that is 100% secure, then you shouldn't buy it at all. Despite this, Touch ID remains a remarkable advancement in security.

　　For me, what's more important is that Touch ID makes shopping and app updates on the iPhone faster and less painful. It's a great idea. After seeing countless cases in this field where efforts ultimately failed, I'm still amazed at how smoothly it works. But this is precisely Apple's approach: transforming mediocrity into greatness.

　　I hope Apple can come up with a safer solution before opening Touch ID to third-party apps after it has been hacked. And despite the likely increase in doubts about Touch ID, Apple will surely find a safer solution.

　　Another key upgrade is the camera on the 5s. Frankly, it's difficult to distinguish the new camera on a small screen. However, the difference becomes more apparent when importing photos into iPhoto, especially in low-light conditions.

　　The dual-tone LED flash is a significant upgrade, but I rarely use it. The "SIo-Mo" function is the best feature of the new camera. Slow-motion capture isn't new, but Apple has certainly implemented it well.

　　Cases

　　Another point regarding the iPhone 5s vs. 5c is their respective official cases. In my opinion, the case for the 5c is better than that of the 5s.

　　I received a brown leather case for the 5s and a pink case for the 5c. Comparatively, the 5c case is not only visually more appealing but also easier to install and remove. Removing the case from the 5s is not as easy, which reminded me of the Kindle cases Amazon released.

　　The right way to design a case for a device should be like the one for the 5c. It's easy to remove and adds value beyond just protecting the device. My only complaint is that the cutout extends all the way to the bottom, exposing not only the iPhone logo but also the FCC label, which seems a bit careless. I would prefer if the three rows of cutouts at the bottom were removed.

　　As for the 5s, although it does provide protection, it's not really my style. After using it for a short while, the corners got damaged. Overall, however, the cases for both devices serve functions beyond just protection and are another cash cow for Apple's high profits.

　　Divergence

　　An interesting thought came to mind after the new models were released: the iPhone 5c is aimed at regular consumers, while the iPhone 5s is a "Pro" version designed to meet higher-end user needs. Apple itself markets it as "forward-looking."

　　During my time using the 5s and 5c, this thought kept coming to mind. For me, the 5s is like the aluminum MacBook Pro, while the 5c is like the old white plastic "MacBook." The 5c is a bit more approachable. Whether this holds true will depend on actual sales figures. Reports indicate that the 5s is selling better than the 5c, but these numbers don't prove anything since the early buyers are mostly loyal users.

　　I was surprised by my own fondness for the 5c. The 5c doesn't feel like a cheap iPhone, and it fits remarkably well with iOS. If you don't care whether the camera is excellent or outstanding, I would unhesitatingly recommend the 5c to you. It's worth emphasizing again that the speed of the 5c is sufficient for your daily needs, at least for now, and probably even next year.

　　Finally, I realized what I truly want: an iPhone with the body of the 5c and the internals of the 5s. Fortunately, I've heard rumors that Apple will sell such a device next year. Unfortunately, there will also be an upgraded version of the 5s at that time. (I particularly dislike the concept of "high-end," especially when it's associated with "gold.")

　　And this, perhaps, is the fundamental reason why Apple's stock keeps falling.

The iPhone event will be held on September 10th.
