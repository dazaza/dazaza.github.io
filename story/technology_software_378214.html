Baidu Xiaosheng: The Android app I'm using

by xdash on 2011-01-14 17:06:35

(The author of this article, Xiaosheng, is currently a product manager at Baidu Mobile Department and a co-founder of Mobile Design China)

As professionals in the mobile internet industry, we need to research various popular Android/iOS applications, especially those similar to our own projects. We should understand them from the perspectives of visual design, interaction design, information architecture, and operations. However, as a Moto Milestone user, I also use various applications in daily life. Graduating from an industrial design background, I have a preference for simplicity, like MIJI, IKEA, 1984, and Douban. Therefore, I have higher requirements for the visual interface when choosing mobile applications, just like how programmers love cutting-edge technology. Professional backgrounds can even influence the way we choose products in everyday life.

Invited by XDash to talk about commonly used applications, there's no need to mention familiar ones like Gmail, Gtalk, and browsers. So I'll pick four less exposed "daily essentials."

Jorte

Jorte Notebook is like a simplified version of Google Calendar. Its main functions are simple and easy to use, with less frequently used features placed in the settings. Upon opening the program, the first page displays the calendar, allowing you to quickly check your schedule. You can choose different visual styles and arrangement methods, with dynamic view transitions and left-right swipe gestures for page flipping. It can sync with Jorte Calendar or Google Calendar, supports data export and import, and users who love synchronization will definitely enjoy it.

The functionality can quickly and efficiently meet most of the users' needs regarding calendars. It supports up to 16 different types of desktop widgets, more than any other application, which can satisfy any screen display space requirement.

When clicking on a date, a dialog box pops up for creating a new event, where you can also choose "previous day" and "next day." These two options are actually unnecessary. The Moto Milestone has a resolution of 480*854 pixels, even with a horizontal resolution of 320 pixels, each date’s clickable area can reach 45 pixels. After converting the precision, it already meets the thumb-clicking area for adult users, with little chance of accidental clicks.

Clicking on a single task allows you to view the task details, set the status, and edit. The underline style for "show details" is a web design standard. Personally, I'm quite against seeing such designs in client applications; it might be related to Android's own control design. Hiding some infrequently used "advanced" options within the page is very consistent with user operation habits.

Compared to Jorte, the design of the sixth-ranked 365 Calendar in the Android market is slightly more complex, with more information on the pages and more functions. 365 Calendar is a typical example of transplanting iOS design completely onto Android, with unresponsive physical back button operations, which I personally find awkward.

365 Calendar includes lunar terms, fitting the national context. The schedule and calendar are just different views, but there's a lot of repetition between the information in adjacent views. Initially using it, the default display was Beijing weather. I thought I could modify it in "more" or "settings," but after failing twice, I realized it was in the weather section under "tools," without support for automatic location. This is a common issue when having too many functions. In terms of desktop widgets, combining it with time makes its widget usability higher than Jorte's, occupying the user's main screen position.

Baidu Around Me

Just released in December, Baidu Around Me already has a WAP version and an Android version. The polar bear on the launch screen is pretty cute and looks very friendly. In my personal opinion, the previous client interface design of Dianping.com needed significant changes, and there was almost no improvement in half a year. New LBS products like Baidu Around Me, Shanda Qieke, and QQ Food are all more aesthetically pleasing. However, currently, Dianping.com still has a clear data advantage.

For LBS products, I personally don't care much about check-in features but rather what convenient information services they can provide. I asked several colleagues in the LBS industry about improvements to user check-ins, and the answers were that as long as users like to check in, it's fine—they just post their current location and activities. Personally, I think this idea is a bit naive. It would make the product very pure, but there are still many designs that can be done around check-ins or user geographical locations.

For Baidu Around Me, I personally focus on how to quickly discover nearby merchants, obtain information to help me determine action goals, and what I can do upon reaching my destination. Baidu Around Me still needs further consideration in terms of interface details; personally, seeing so many ">" on the homepage drives me crazy. What the main interface of a mobile product displays can reflect what the product design focuses on, such as the main page of Dianping.com showing commercial areas and channels, while the main page of Jiapang shows friends' check-in information.

If you often go to the movies, Mtime is indispensable. Last year, I worked on a mobile product for querying movies and selecting seats, so I paid more attention to Mtime, and I indeed have the need to check movie information.
