Buffer Mom holds her one-year-old daughter to make double eyelids, saying that she doesn't want her child to lose at the starting line.

by sznstejx3q3 on 2012-03-05 10:19:02

Nowadays, in major plastic surgery institutions, it is commonplace to see mothers bringing their children for plastic surgery consultations. But have you ever seen anything as shocking as this? Yesterday, at the Plastic Surgery Department of the City Emergency Center, a beauty-conscious mother brought her just-over-one-year-old daughter to the hospital for double eyelid surgery, fearing that her daughter would lose in the "starting line" of appearance.

The doctor firmly rejected and criticized the mother's behavior. Experts say that plastic surgery must conform to the objective laws of human physiology and generally should only be done after the body has fully developed. Moreover, plastic surgery is based on the individual's aesthetic cognition and willingness; even parents do not have the right to impose their own aesthetic views on their children.

At around 10 AM yesterday, Director Wu Yi of the Plastic Surgery Department had just finished a surgery when Xiao (a pseudonym) and her nanny walked into the consultation hall with her just-over-one-year-old baby. Initially, Wu thought Xiao was recommending someone familiar for surgery and greeted them warmly in the office.

“Director Wu, look, Niu Niu (a pseudonym) has single eyelids, which makes her eyes look small and unattractive. Can you help her with double eyelid surgery?” Without any preliminary conversation, Xiao handed her daughter over to Wu and requested he personally perform the surgery.

“Is it appropriate for such a young child to undergo surgery?” Wu initially thought Xiao was joking. “I’m serious,” Xiao explained earnestly. She believed her daughter’s single eyelids made her eyes appear smaller and were a significant flaw in her appearance. Therefore, she wanted to give Niu Niu double eyelid surgery now so that when her daughter grew up and developed her own aesthetic views, she wouldn't worry about her single eyelids and could become more beautiful and confident among her peers.

After listening to Xiao’s explanation, Wu immediately refused her request. To dissuade Xiao, Wu invited Head Nurse Fan Shimei to join him in educating Xiao half-jokingly and seriously. Wu explained to Xiao that from a medical perspective, double eyelid surgery requires cooperation between the patient and the doctor, but Niu Niu was too young to follow the doctor's instructions to blink repeatedly. Additionally, plastic surgery is generally recommended only after the body has fully developed. Since Niu Niu was only one year old, performing the surgery would violate medical principles and still might not achieve perfect results during future development.

As a fellow woman, Nurse Fan psychologically counseled Xiao, criticizing her for holding such a view. She advised Xiao to focus more on cultivating her child's abilities and inner cultivation rather than physical appearance, which would benefit her child's mental and physical health.

After nearly half an hour of discussion, Xiao abandoned the idea of surgery for her daughter and sheepishly admitted she had oversimplified the matter.

It turns out Xiao, 30 years old and living in a high-end neighborhood in Yubei, had participated in multiple beauty pageants and worked as a model for fashion magazines due to her striking beauty during university and afterward. After marrying two years ago, she became a full-time housewife.

According to Director Wu, Xiao had undergone several plastic surgeries herself, including rhinoplasty, cheekbone reduction, and liposuction. Her obsession with plastic surgery led her to consider giving her infant double eyelids.

“Haha, I really oversimplified things and sincerely accept the criticism from the doctors and nurses,” Xiao confessed in a phone interview with the Chongqing Evening News. She admitted her daughter was already very pretty, and the single eyelids were merely a minor flaw in her mind. She just wanted her daughter to be more beautiful and perfect from a young age.

Xiao also expressed that through this incident, she would pay more attention to her methods of educating her child and provide proper guidance.

Doctors warn that parents should not impose their aesthetic views on their children. Director Wu reminded parents that clinically, except for some scar surgeries, minors are not advised to undergo plastic surgery, especially major procedures like cheekbone reduction, breast augmentation, or jawline reshaping.

From a physiological standpoint, minors are still in their developmental peak, and their organs have not yet stabilized, making them unsuitable for plastic surgery. Ethically, plastic surgery is a personal aesthetic choice, and parents should not impose their own views on their children. If medical institutions inadvertently follow the parents' wishes and perform plastic surgery on children, it may cause greater psychological harm to the child.

Psychology professor Mei Qixia from Chongqing Medical University also stated that while parents’ desire for beauty is understandable, excessive pursuit can negatively impact the child's physical and mental health. For example, if Xiao’s "appearance-first" mindset persists, it will subtly influence her daughter, leading her to believe that being pretty is the most important thing and pursue superficial beauty.

Plastic surgery satisfies many people's desire for beauty, but it does not mean one can arbitrarily modify their body. Plastic surgery experts emphasized that these procedures must follow the objective laws of human physiology, and the timing and principles of different surgeries should be carefully considered.

Optimal ages for various plastic surgeries:

- Double Eyelid Surgery: Requires local anesthesia and the ability to open and close eyes repeatedly according to the doctor's instructions. It is recommended to wait until the individual has sufficient recognition ability and psychological maturity, typically around 16 years old (after puberty).

- Lower Eyelid Bags Correction: Common in middle-aged and elderly individuals over 40. It is recommended to perform the surgery after 45, possibly combined with an eye lift. Performing it too early can lead to premature aging of the skin around the eyes.

- Rhinoplasty: Children often have low nasal bridges and flat noses, but these usually develop naturally with age. Rhinoplasty is best performed after the body has fully developed, typically around 18 years old.

- Ear Reconstruction: To avoid affecting normal psychological development, the surgery can be performed around ages 5-6, when the ear size is similar to adults and growth is less affected.

- Cleft Lip/Palate Repair: Ideally completed within the first 2-3 months for cleft lips and by age 2 for cleft palates.

- Facial Wrinkle Removal: Suitable for individuals aged 40-60. If opting for injectable wrinkle removal, it is not recommended before 37 years old.

- Breast Augmentation: Typically performed after full development issues arise, such as underdeveloped breasts, post-breastfeeding shrinkage, or gender reassignment surgery. However, severe cases of macromastia may warrant surgery in adolescents.

- Liposuction: Generally suitable for individuals aged 20-50. Minors in their growth phase and those with serious coronary heart disease, hypertension, pulmonary dysfunction, diabetes, or coagulation disorders are not eligible.

Sixty percent of children's motivation for plastic surgery comes from their parents. According to reports from the emergency center and other hospitals, while Xiao's case of wanting plastic surgery for her one-year-old daughter is extreme, cases where parents dominate their children's decisions for plastic surgery are not uncommon. Many teenagers, especially during winter and summer breaks, visit hospitals with their parents for consultations regarding double eyelid, rhinoplasty, or chin augmentation surgeries. In routine consultations, over sixty percent of children are encouraged by their parents to undergo plastic surgery, particularly those preparing for university entrance exams, where parents hope to enhance their child's image and confidence through plastic surgery.
