Will Sony's and Microsoft's new game consoles triumph in the gaming industry?

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 19:26:01

Sony and Microsoft's new video game consoles are about to hit the shelves, and they will undoubtedly be one of the hottest gifts this Christmas. However, for the gaming industry, an uncertain factor remains: what will happen after Santa leaves?

For decades, consoles have been at the core of the video game industry. But Sony's new PlayStation 4 and Microsoft's Xbox One are now entering a redefined environment shaped by tablets, smartphones, and Facebook, all of which offer a vast number of games at low prices.

Moreover, there is evidence suggesting that mobile devices are eroding traditional console sales, which have been on a declining trend for years. Therefore, the entire gaming industry is closely watching whether the new generation of consoles from leading manufacturers Sony and Microsoft can maintain dominance in the evolving gaming landscape.

A hot Christmas gift

The majority in the gaming industry are optimistic about these new consoles, mainly due to the pent-up demand among hardcore gamers. These loyal fans will not hesitate to spend hundreds of dollars on the latest consoles. It has been seven years since Sony last released a console, and eight years for Microsoft, longer than the typical lifecycle for new consoles.

"I think initially there will be some hype, some great games, and then heavy marketing spending," said David Gardner, a venture capitalist at London Venture Partners and former executive at U.S.-based game company Electronic Arts (EA). "I just don't know if, three or four years down the line, traditional gaming will hold the same appeal as it does this Christmas."

The true test for the new consoles may come next holiday season when Sony and Microsoft's production lines are fully operational, and some cautious users start weighing the pros and cons of these new consoles.

To attract as many users as possible, both companies have added new entertainment features to their consoles, such as playing videos through Netflix applications. However, the issue is that cheaper devices from Apple, Roku, and other providers also offer these entertainment features, putting pressure on the consoles.

Consoles offering value for money

Despite being positioned as entertainment hubs, games remain the core. Both Sony and Microsoft have enhanced the processing power of their consoles to ensure higher image quality and more realistic effects in games. The difference between the two consoles lies in price: Xbox One costs $500; PlayStation 4 is priced at $400 and will launch in the U.S. and Canada on November 15. Microsoft's higher price is due to the inclusion of its motion-sensing peripheral, Kinect.

Xbox One's higher price has raised eyebrows for many, but Microsoft claims that the additional features of this console justify its cost. For example, Xbox One can automatically recognize the identity of people in front of the TV and present different games and TV shows based on family members' preferences. It can also play videos from traditional cable set-top boxes, provide stylish program guides, and allow users to switch channels with voice commands.

"We're trying to offer players a broader value proposition," said Yusuf Mehdi, Vice President of Strategy Marketing for Xbox. "We've created top-tier games and provided a one-stop entertainment system."

A challenge for the new consoles in attracting mainstream users is proving that their games are significantly better than previous ones. The release of the last generation of games coincided with the widespread adoption of HD TVs, which dramatically improved the visual experience and captured players' hearts. Games on the new consoles need to distinguish themselves from current offerings.

Gears of War game designer Cliff Bleszinski said that the new consoles might struggle to pass the "mom test," overcoming skepticism from wallet-holders, the parents. "The jump in picture quality in games is no longer significant; it can only improve incrementally," he remarked when discussing the new consoles.

Everyone could be a winner

According to consultancy PricewaterhouseCoopers' latest entertainment and media report, global sales of physical and digital console games reached $24.9 billion last year, accounting for approximately 39% of total game sales. In comparison, in 2008, this figure was $29.4 billion, representing 56% of total game sales.

Microsoft and Sony hope to avoid the rapid decline in sales seen previously. Both companies believe their consoles will sell well upon release.

In interviews, Mehdi stated that pre-orders for the Microsoft Xbox One have already sold out; as the console launches in the U.S. and other major markets on November 22, stores may implement limited supplies. "We will increase production as much as possible," he said.

Sony reported that pre-orders for the PlayStation 4 have exceeded one million units. Jack Tretton, President and CEO of Sony's American gaming division, believes this strong momentum will continue for several years. He noted that mobile devices have helped many people get into gaming. He expects that some of these individuals will enjoy the immersive large-screen gaming experience offered by consoles.

"In the past few years, many people have never played games; however, due to smartphones and tablets, they have now started enjoying them," Tretton said. John Riccitiello, former CEO of EA, mentioned that even if mobile gaming continues to erode the market share of console gaming, the new consoles will still sell well. "The gaming market is large, and everyone could potentially be a winner," he said.
