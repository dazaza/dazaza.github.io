The key to preventing cervical spondylosis is to slow down the degeneration process of the cervical intervertebral disc. Poor sleeping posture, improper working posture, and inappropriate physical exercise are all common causes of cervical osteoarthritis degeneration. Developmental cervical canal stenosis, congenital cervical malformation, etc., are potential factors for the onset of cervical spondylosis. Lack of blood supply can lead to necrosis. Cervical spinal canal stenosis is generally divided into two major categories: congenital developmental and acquired secondary spinal canal stenosis. According to the location of the stenosis, it can be divided into cervical spinal canal stenosis, lumbar spinal canal stenosis, and thoracic spinal canal stenosis. The most common one is lumbar spinal canal stenosis. Cervical spinal canal stenosis often falls under myelopathic cervical spondylosis, while thoracic spinal canal stenosis is less common. Cervical spinal canal stenosis is anatomically divided into central stenosis, lateral recess stenosis, and neural foraminal stenosis. When clinical symptoms appear due to various reasons causing cervical canal stenosis, it is called cervical spinal canal stenosis syndrome. Clinical symptoms vary according to the degree of compression on the cervical spinal cord. Generally, there are varying degrees of changes in sensation, movement, and sphincter function of the limbs. Severe cases may result in paralysis. In summary, through the above introduction, everyone should actively prevent the condition and seek medical attention immediately if symptoms occur to avoid delaying treatment. It's best to regularly undergo physical examinations to prevent potential issues. Cervical spondylosis refers to a clinical syndrome caused by degenerative changes in the cervical intervertebral discs, hypertrophy of the cervical vertebrae, neck injuries leading to bone hyperplasia in the cervical vertebrae, or herniation of intervertebral discs, thickening of ligaments, stimulating or compressing the cervical spinal cord, cervical nerves, and blood vessels, resulting in a series of symptoms. Mainly manifested as neck and shoulder pain, dizziness, headache, numbness in the upper limbs, muscle atrophy, severe cases with spasticity in both lower limbs, difficulty walking, and even numbness in the limbs, urinary and fecal incontinence, leading to paralysis. It is more common in middle-aged and elderly people, with a higher incidence rate in men than in women.
