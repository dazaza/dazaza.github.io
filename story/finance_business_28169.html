White Crow » The rhythm of start-ups

by huahuaniu on 2007-08-22 08:28:15

I don't know whether it's because I'm too busy or too lazy, or for some other reason. Although I still maintain a keen sense for anything, I rarely like to write about some design details anymore. Many articles were left unwritten after I started them.

Today, I'll start writing something related to startups, summarizing and reflecting on the issues in my advisory work...

Firstly, let's talk about the rhythm of startup companies:

1. The foundational construction phase. Plan the initial and long-term plans for the product, and realize the basic framework and functions of the product.

In the early stages of a startup, any product usually has grand goals. Everyone in the entrepreneurial team can "think of what they can do", they can have ten thousand ideas at any time. The hardest thing to do at this point is subtraction - planning the basic architecture of the product, and clarifying what is not the basic point of the product and can be skipped for now.

I've seen many startup teams that don't know what the focus is at the beginning, wasting a lot of manpower and resources doing everything they can think of. In the end, they struggle to support a large system and are unable to make further progress.

And, at this point, you must not get too bogged down in the details of functionality and experience.

You need to know that the unique advantage of making internet products is that "the internet is the largest testing ground". First, put out the perfected basic functions, and you will find that optimizing while using is much better than always working in isolation.

Of course, if you're just trying to show some results to investors or get VC funding, then that's a different story.

2. The accumulation and observation phase. Fully optimize the user experience of the existing architecture and functions through operations, observe, accumulate, statistics, think, and summarize "what users are doing", "why they do it this way", "what other needs might arise", and ultimately formulate the mid-term plan for the product;

The most frightening thing at this stage is: "treating the operation of the product as the service itself, with the entire product being led by operations."

For example, a photo album website launched an activity called "See which celebrity you look most like" and wanted to promote it heavily. So they incorrectly placed this feature in the main navigation of the product.

I've met many excellent entrepreneurs who consistently agree during our exchanges that "the hardest thing to do at this stage is 'not holding back'", discovering a "possible opportunity" at any time and wanting to expand the scope of services.

There are simply too many "choices" and "opportunities". When the company is still in its infancy and not yet mature, you must not expand easily.

A music website should not quickly switch to selling CDs just because users asked where they could buy a certain CD shortly after going online; a book website should not hastily switch to e-books just because users said "Why isn't there an electronic version?" after it went live.

Changing directions three times in a year is equivalent to having no direction.

At this stage, "making all possible mistakes" is more important than anything else, but it shouldn't be the mistake of "constantly changing direction".

3. The expansion phase. Determine the product direction and achieve rapid results through various means.

Products change according to market changes, especially in the internet field. Myspace, Youtube, including Google, their initial plans were not what they are now. But after they discovered that their model and route were mature, they expanded as quickly as possible.

So far, I haven't seen any internet project that completely followed its predetermined direction for the first five years. Even now very large internet companies are constantly updating their goals and directions. Companies like Yahoo that remain unchanged have already tasted the bitter fruit...

An appropriate rhythm is crucial for any startup. The foundational construction phase, the accumulation and observation phase, and the expansion phase should all have different rhythms. Only by mastering the rhythm can there be long-term development.
