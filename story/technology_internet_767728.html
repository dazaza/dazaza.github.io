Nine Major Technological Breakthroughs in the History of Mobile Phones: Touchscreen Phones Born in 1993

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 13:10:23

Since the debut of the first mobile phone in New York in 1973, mobile phones have been around for 40 years. The world's first commercially available mobile phone was launched by Motorola in 1984, named DynaTAC. This "giant" phone, which took over 10 years to develop and was priced at a staggering $4000 (equivalent to 9280 RMB based on the exchange rate at the time), measured 254 mm in height, weighed 790 grams, and offered only 30 minutes of talk time. After this, mobile phones began to develop rapidly and have now become an essential part of human life: according to the latest market report, out of the global population of 7.1 billion, 6.8 billion people own their own mobile phone.

1. Mobile network construction (1979)

Clearly, mobile communication is based on mobile networks; without the network, everything is just talk. In 1979, the first generation of mobile communication technology (1G) was officially built in Tokyo by Japan's NTT company and subsequently popularized in countries like the United States. Until 1991, 2G (GSM) technology was promoted globally by Finland, officially ushering mobile communication technology into the digital domain, supporting more mid-range and data services, and making short message functionality popular. By 2001, 3G networks started to appear and were gradually popularized. Currently, developed countries have gradually popularized 4G LTE networks, and this technology is expected to officially land in China's domestic market by the end of this year.

2. Short Message Service (1993)

Short message service (SMS) is a product of the 2G era, changing how people communicate. This 160-character short message was officially launched in 1992, and the next year Nokia introduced the first mobile phone that supported sending and receiving text messages. To some extent, text messages also influenced the design trends of mobile phones, such as T9 keyboards and QWERTY keyboards. Today, every second, 200,000 text messages are sent globally.

3. The first touch screen mobile phone (1993)

The IBM "Simon," launched in 1993, was likely the world's first smartphone, even though the term didn't exist then. It combined the functions of a mobile phone and a PDA and was the first to feature an integrated touch screen, although the feel of early touch screens was quite poor. Subsequently, mobile phone manufacturers such as Motorola, Sony Ericsson, Samsung, and HTC continuously sought truly suitable touch screen designs for mobile phones. However, it was Apple that, in 2007, launched the iPhone with high resolution and multi-touch capabilities, truly establishing the standard for touch screens. Nowadays, capacitive touch screens have become a standard feature of smartphones.

4. Internet function (1996)

The Nokia 9000 Communicator was the first mobile phone to offer internet access, but it was very expensive and required connection to a PC to go online, lacking wireless network functionality. Wireless Application Protocol (WAP) technology was introduced in 1997 and gradually established standards, after which WAP browsers became common features on mobile phones. Subsequently, advancements in network and terminal technologies enabled today’s smartphones to smoothly access full HTML web pages, becoming mainstream. According to relevant reports, 29% of global internet traffic in the second quarter of 2013 came from mobile devices.

5. Email function (1996)

1996 seems to have been a year of great leaps forward for mobile device data functions, with terminals like the Nokia 9000 and RIM 900 Inter@ctive Pager beginning to support email functions. Later, BlackBerry email services were officially launched in 1999. Currently, almost all smartphones can support email functions and have become the primary devices for people to read emails.

6. GPS function (1999)

The first commercially available mobile phone with built-in GPS functionality was launched by Benefon in 1999, designed specifically for outdoor sports enthusiasts. It had waterproof features and allowed users to send their location coordinates via text messages or emergency buttons. Subsequently, GPS functionality gradually appeared in mobile phones, but its true popularity came with the advent of A-GPS, which significantly improved the cold start speed of GPS chips and provided more accurate positioning results.

7. MP3 playback function (2000)

Samsung SPH-M100 was the world's first mobile phone with an integrated MP3 player. Subsequently, Sony also launched Walkman series phones, making mobile phone MP3 playback mainstream. Of course, what truly replaced dedicated MP3 players were smartphones, including iPhones and Android phones, which can play various music formats with excellent sound quality, eliminating the need to carry a dedicated music player.

8. Camera function (2000)

The world's first camera phone was launched by Sharp in the Japanese domestic market, featuring an 110,000-pixel camera. Sanyo then launched a camera phone in the U.S. market in 2002. Currently, the image quality of camera phones can rival compact digital cameras, with a trend toward replacing them. Phones like the iPhone 5s, Nokia Lumia 1020, and Sony Xperia Z1 all have powerful camera functions and outstanding results. The convenient sharing features of smartphones also make people prefer using their phones for photography rather than digital cameras in daily life.

9. Voice control function (2009)

Voice dialing functionality has been around for many years, but users did not use it frequently. True compound voice operation functionality appeared on the Android system in 2009. Then, in 2011, Apple launched Siri, capable of more complex voice operations and certain conversational functions. Subsequently, Motorola's MOTO X, launched this year, features active Google Now voice recognition, allowing you to operate your phone without touching it.

What will be the next breakthrough in mobile phone technology?

By looking back, we have already seen the development process of mobile phones from digitization, internetization, multimedia, to intelligence. So, what will be the next breakthrough technology we look forward to? From the current perspective, wearable technology or more flexible forms may be what we expect: perhaps flexible display phones that can change shape arbitrarily, or smart glasses or watches that can truly replace mobile phones. It is easy to imagine that the next ten years of smartphones will be filled with surprises.
