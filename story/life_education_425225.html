12 Key Points for Tackling SAT Math Grid-In Questions in Zhi Sheng Education

by thatseed123 on 2011-05-05 22:37:14

For SAT math grid-in questions, it is crucial to carefully read the question, calculate the answer, and then consider its reasonableness. The grid-in section has a space for you to write your answer, but this space is not read during scoring. Only the bubbles below are counted for scoring. Therefore, even if you write the correct answer in the space but bubble in the wrong one, you will still receive no points. Do not assume that the computer can interpret what you wrote or expect any leniency for incorrectly bubbled answers.

1) The symbols available in the grid are only the digits 0-9, a decimal point, and “/” (fraction symbol). Therefore, the answer for grid-in questions must be within the range [0, 9999]. If you calculate an answer outside of this closed interval, stop immediately, re-read the question, check if the data you substituted is correct, verify if the calculated result is what the question asks for, and ensure the units are consistent.

2) A number can be written in any position on the grid and still be scored correctly. For example, 35 can be written in the first and second boxes, the second and third boxes, or the third and fourth boxes. However, out of habit, it is recommended to use right-alignment when filling in the grid. Additionally, a number cannot be split apart; for instance, 35 cannot have the 3 written in the first box and the 5 written in the third box.

3) Do not round off your answers unnecessarily. For instance, if you get an infinite repeating decimal (.3535353535), both (.353) and (.354) would be scored as correct. However, there’s always a risk of making a mistake while rounding. So, whatever number your calculator displays, just fill in as much as fits without spending time rounding. Never reduce a three-digit answer to one or two digits. For example, if your answer is (.3535353535), do not round it to (.4) or (.35); if possible, fill all available spaces.

4) Do not add a leading zero before the decimal point. For example, if your answer is (0.35353), simply fill in (.353). Writing (0.35) will be less precise than (.353).

5) Be sure to pay attention to the placement of the decimal point on your calculator.

6) For complex fractions, do not simplify unless necessary; just fill them in directly. For example, (38/6) can be filled in directly and will still be scored as correct. For numbers like (26/35), which don’t fit directly into the four-grid box, use your calculator to divide the numerator by the denominator and fill in the result as a decimal.

7) When answering grid-in questions, never fill in mixed fractions directly. Convert them to improper fractions before filling in.

8) Both fractions and decimals are acceptable as long as they are correct. For example, (6/9) can also be filled in as (2/3), (4/6), (8/12), (.666), or (.667) and still be scored as correct.

9) Sometimes, questions may have multiple correct answers. Filling in any one of them will be considered correct. Based on experience, such questions usually don't have more than five possible answers, often just 2-3. If you come up with more than ten results, re-read the question.

10) When filling in fractions, be careful to distinguish between “/” and the digit 1. Write “/” at a slant and 1 straighter to avoid confusion. After completing each grid-in question, compare your calculated answer with the one you’ve filled in, double-checking each digit to ensure it was bubbled correctly. Avoid writing overly exaggerated digits that might obscure the bubbles below.

11) Although the written portion does not count toward your score, it is still worth taking the time to write it down, as this increases the accuracy of bubbling correctly.

12) Each grid-in question is worth one point, so don't get overly stuck if you can't solve one. The advantage is that there is no penalty for wrong answers, so if you're truly stuck, go ahead and guess. In the SAT I math test, the questions are simple but full of traps, such as unit conversions, place values, and the use of negation words. These specific points of attention will be noted after related knowledge points.
