Michael

Introduction

Michael, also known as the Archangel Michael, is an angelic figure in mythology. In Christian art and sculpture, Michael often appears as a youth with golden hair, holding a red cross (or a red cruciform sword) battling or standing triumphantly over a dragon, where the dragon represents Satan.

Heavenly Right Wing - Michael

Introduction

Michael (translated as 'Who Is Like God') is the chief warrior by God's side and the supreme commander of the angelic army. The Bible refers to him as "one of the Chief Princes," meaning one of the great princes. He is depicted as having unmatched courage and power, possessing a beautiful appearance, being very handsome. His character is brave and decisive; although he is warlike, he is full of compassion, absolutely rejecting and mercilessly destroying sinful acts. He is the embodiment of "absolute justice" without any trace of darkness.

Deity Authority

Michael may be the result of the incorporation of the Akkadian god Ningirsu into Christianity. In Babylonian mythology, Ningirsu was the eldest son of the chief god Enlil, the god of war and soldiers, capable of easily destroying enemy nations. Legend has it that the celestial guard - the bird spirit Zu stole the "Tablets" symbolizing the chief god's authority. The gods Anu, Adad, the great goddess Inanna, and her son Shara were all commanded to subdue Zu but failed. Ningirsu, the son of Enlil, volunteered and, with the help of other gods like Ea, defeated the terrifying Zu and retrieved his father’s authority. Whether from Ningirsu's identity or achievements, he could very possibly be the prototype of Michael (this is speculation, please use cautiously).

In canonical and apocryphal Christian texts, five angels are mentioned: Michael, Gabriel, Raphael, Uriel, and Jeremiel. He might be the same entity as the Islamic angel Mikail (Michal). His rank is described as Seraphim, Power Angel, or Archangel. In Christian civilization, his deeds include: annihilating 150,000 Assyrian troops invading Jerusalem in one night, stopping Abraham from sacrificing his son Isaac, calling Moses from the burning bush to lead the Hebrews out of Egypt, capturing and imprisoning the ancient serpent (dragon) Satan for a thousand years (as recorded in Revelation). Michael, as the executor of humanity's creation and opponent of Satan, possesses power equal to the Great Demon King. Ancient Celtic people had a concept similar to China's "dragon veins," building Michael's temples at the intersection points of dragon veins to suppress them. Jewish legend says that Jehovah made Michael from fire and Gabriel from water.

In the Dead Sea Scrolls' "Battle of the Sons of Light and Darkness," Michael appears as the Deputy Lord of Heaven and Lord of Light, leading the angelic army against the dark legion led by the Lord of Darkness Belial. They fought for forty years, with the first three battles won by the Lord of Light and the next three by Belial. In the seventh battle, God himself intervened and defeated Belial.

Another identity of Michael is as the angel who counts human souls during the final judgment, guiding the dead to "this shore" and judging their fate after death (in Jewish mysticism, he is also listed as one of the angels of death). In Christianity, it is said that Michael guards the soul of the Virgin Mary (Mary), preventing others from defiling it.

Michael corresponds to the Egyptian god Sirius, the star deity. Thus, Michael takes on the role of a guide to the underworld. In Greek mythology, the corresponding deity would be Hermes or Apollo, representing the Greek letters Alpha, which signify "beginning" and "rebirth." Therefore, Michael is also the guide of souls and the guardian of light among the archangels.

"Angel of Mercy," "Angel of Justice," "Prince of God," Michael's name appears among the four archangels and seven archangels. According to the Old Testament Book of Daniel, Michael is the guardian angel of Israel, the only one among the sixty-nine guardian angels of emerging nations who did not fall. He is also the angel who will surely appear to lead people when the world falls into chaos. In Islam, Michael is described as having "emerald wings, crimson hair, with a million faces and mouths, speaking a million dialects, seeking Allah's forgiveness." The Quran records that cherubim were born from Michael's tears over the sins of believers. Early Christian sects, such as the Ophites, portrayed Michael as a lion-headed statue.

The standard image of Michael is with a sword in his right hand and scales in his left (possibly influenced by the Greek goddess of law and justice, Themis). In 1950, the Roman Curia designated Michael as the patron saint of police. In the classic Waite Tarot deck, the card "Judgment" depicts Michael blowing the horn of the end times, resurrecting the dead.

It is unknown whether due to Michael's numerous deeds or human curiosity about whether he ever rests, he is also regarded as the angel of "insomnia."

The red cross was originally the emblem of St. George, the patron saint of England, but from the Middle Ages onwards, Michael gradually became associated with St. George, making Michael the patron saint of England. The flag of England is based on the red cross of Michael-St. George.

Scott describes Michael in "The Lay of the Last Minstrel": "The red cross shone in his hand, and the moonlight through the glass poured blood onto the ground." Thus, the red cross represents Michael's divine authority.

Currently, the most famous Michael worship site worldwide is the Michael Cathedral in Washington D.C.

Michaelmas: A Christian holiday commemorating the Archangel Michael. Western churches celebrate it on September 29th, while the Orthodox Church celebrates it on November 8th.
