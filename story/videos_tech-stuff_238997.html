【Satellite Communication System Concept】

A satellite communication system is essentially a form of microwave communication, using satellites as relay stations to forward microwave signals for communication between multiple ground stations. The primary purpose of satellite communication is to achieve "seamless" coverage of the Earth's surface. Since satellites operate in orbits hundreds, thousands, or even tens of thousands of kilometers above the Earth, their coverage area far exceeds that of typical mobile communication systems. However, satellite communication requires ground equipment with significant transmission power, making it less accessible for widespread use. A satellite communication system consists of three parts: the space segment, the ground segment, and the user segment. The space segment acts as a relay station in the air, receiving electromagnetic waves from ground stations, amplifying them, and then retransmitting them to another ground station. The satellite itself includes two major subsystems: onboard equipment and the satellite bus. Ground stations serve as interfaces between satellite systems and terrestrial public networks. Ground users can also establish links through ground stations to access satellite systems. Ground stations also include satellite control centers and tracking, telemetry, and command stations. The user segment refers to various user terminals.

In the microwave frequency band, the entire working frequency band of a communications satellite is approximately 500 MHz wide. To facilitate amplification and transmission and reduce intermodulation interference, several transponders are typically installed on the satellite. Each transponder is allocated a specific working frequency band. Current satellite communications mostly use Frequency Division Multiple Access (FDMA) technology, where different earth stations occupy different frequencies, i.e., different carriers, which is suitable for point-to-point high-capacity communication. In recent years, Time Division Multiple Access (TDMA) technology has also been increasingly applied in satellite communications. Multiple earth stations share the same frequency band but occupy different time slots. Compared to FDMA, TDMA does not produce intermodulation interference, does not require up/down frequency conversion to separate signals from different earth stations, is suitable for digital communication, and allows dynamic allocation of transmission bandwidth according to traffic changes, significantly increasing actual capacity. Another multiple access technology is Code Division Multiple Access (CDMA), where different earth stations share the same frequency and time but distinguish addresses by using different random codes to encode information. CDMA employs spread spectrum communication technology, offering strong resistance to interference, good confidentiality, and flexible scheduling of transmission resources, making it suitable for systems with small capacity, wide distribution, and certain confidentiality requirements.

【Classification of Satellite Communication Systems】

1. Based on the working orbit, satellite communication systems are generally divided into the following three categories:

(1) Low Earth Orbit (LEO) Satellite Communication System: Located at an altitude of 500-2000 km, with relatively low transmission delay and power consumption, but each satellite covers a relatively small area. Typical systems include Motorola’s Iridium system. Due to the low orbit, LEO systems have short signal propagation delays, supporting multi-hop communications; they also have lower link losses, allowing the use of micro/small satellites and handheld user terminals. However, these advantages come at a cost: due to the low orbit, each satellite covers a smaller area, so a global system requires dozens of satellites, such as 66 satellites in the Iridium system, 48 in Globalstar, and 288 in Teledisc. Additionally, because LEO satellites move quickly, the time a single satellite remains visible to a user is short, leading to frequent handovers between satellites or carriers. Therefore, LEO systems are complex to construct and control, pose technical risks, and have relatively high construction costs.

(2) Medium Earth Orbit (MEO) Satellite Communication System: Located at an altitude of 2000-20000 km, with greater transmission delay than LEO but larger coverage area. A typical system is the International Maritime Satellite Organization (INMARSAT) system. MEO satellite communication systems can be seen as a compromise between geostationary and LEO systems, combining the advantages of both while mitigating some of their shortcomings. MEO satellites have relatively low link loss and propagation delay, still allowing the use of simple, small satellites. If both MEO and LEO systems use inter-satellite links, when users conduct long-distance communication, the propagation delay of the MEO system via its inter-satellite link subnet will be lower than that of the LEO system. Moreover, since their orbit is much higher than that of LEO satellites, each MEO satellite can cover a much larger area. When the orbit height is 10000 km, each satellite can cover 23.5% of the Earth's surface, meaning just a few satellites can cover the globe. With over a dozen satellites, most areas of the world can be doubly covered, allowing the use of diversity reception to enhance system reliability while keeping system investment lower than LEO systems. Therefore, MEO systems may be a superior solution for establishing global or regional satellite mobile communication systems. Of course, if broadband services need to be provided to ground terminals, MEO systems may face certain difficulties, while using LEO satellites for high-speed multimedia satellite communication systems offers better performance than MEO systems.

(3) Geostationary Earth Orbit (GEO) Satellite Communication System: Located at an altitude of 35800 km, i.e., geostationary orbit. Theoretically, three GEO satellites can achieve global coverage. Traditional geostationary satellite communication systems have the most mature technology. Since geostationary satellites were first used for communication, building a global satellite communication system using geostationary satellites has become the traditional model for satellite communication systems. However, geostationary satellites have an insurmountable obstacle: long propagation delay and large link loss, severely affecting their application in certain communication fields, especially in satellite mobile communications. First, geostationary satellites have high orbits, resulting in large link losses and requiring high-performance user terminal receivers. This system struggles to support direct communication between handheld devices via satellites or requires onboard antennas larger than 12m (L-band), placing high demands on the satellite's communication payload and hindering the use of small satellite technology in mobile communications. Second, due to the long link distance and large propagation delay, the one-hop propagation delay reaches hundreds of milliseconds, and adding processing time from voice encoders further increases the delay. When mobile users communicate via satellite with double hops, the delay may reach the second level, which is difficult for users, especially voice communication users, to tolerate. To avoid this double-hop communication, onboard processing must be adopted to give satellites switching capabilities, which inevitably increases the complexity of the satellite, not only increasing system costs but also posing certain technical risks.

Currently, geostationary satellite communication systems are mainly used for VSAT systems, TV signal relays, etc., and are rarely used for personal communications.

2. Based on the communication range, satellite communication systems can be divided into international communication satellites, regional communication satellites, and domestic communication satellites.

3. Based on the purpose, satellite communication systems can be divided into integrated service communication satellites, military communication satellites, maritime communication satellites, direct broadcast satellites, etc.

4. Based on the forwarding capability, satellite communication systems can be divided into satellites without onboard processing capability and satellites with onboard processing capability.

【Characteristics of Satellite Communication Systems】

1. Downlink broadcasting, wide coverage: Insensitive to ground conditions such as mountains and oceans, suitable for providing extensive coverage in areas with sparse traffic, allowing communication at any point within the coverage area, with cost independent of distance;

2. Wide working frequency band: Available frequency bands range from 150 MHz to 30 GHz. Currently, development has begun in the Ka band (40-50 GHz), which can support data services up to 155 Mbps;

3. Good communication quality: Electromagnetic waves in satellite communication mainly propagate outside the atmosphere, resulting in very stable wave propagation. Although propagation within the atmosphere may be affected by weather, satellite communication is still a highly reliable communication system;

4. Fast network construction speed and low cost: Apart from building ground stations, no ground construction is required. Operation and maintenance costs are low;

5. Large signal transmission delay: The round-trip transmission delay for high-orbit satellites reaches the second level, causing noticeable interruptions in voice services;

6. Complex control: Since all links in satellite communication systems are wireless, and satellite positions may continuously change, the control system is also relatively complex. Control methods include inter-satellite negotiation and ground centralized control.

【Development Trends of Satellite Communication Systems】

The main development trends of future satellite communication systems are:

1. Geosynchronous orbit communication satellites develop towards multi-beam, high capacity, and intelligence;

2. Low-orbit satellite constellations combined with cellular communication technology to realize global personal communication;

3. Small satellite communication ground stations will be widely applied;

4. Digital video broadcasting (DVB) and digital audio broadcasting (DAB) via satellite communication systems;

5. Satellite communication systems will integrate with IP technology to provide multimedia communication and internet access, including backbone networks for international and domestic use, as well as user direct access;

6. Microsatellites and nanosatellites will be widely used in data storage and forwarding communication, as well as inter-satellite networking communication.

【Successful Cases of Satellite Mobile Communication Systems】

Any communication through moving satellites and fixed terminals, fixed satellites and moving terminals, or both moving is referred to as a satellite mobile communication system. Starting from the 1980s, many Western companies began to recognize the huge demand for future global, seamless communication aimed at individuals, known as the globalization of personal communication, i.e., 5W {Whoever (anyone), Wherever (anywhere), Whenever (anytime), Whomever (anyone), Whatever (in any way)}. Consequently, they developed satellite mobile communication systems using medium and low orbit satellite constellations as aerial relay platforms, conducting satellite mobile phone, satellite live broadcasting/satellite digital audio broadcasting, internet access, and high-speed, broadband multimedia access services. By the 1990s, the main systems that had been built and put into operation included the Iridium system, Globalstar system, ORBCONN system, Messenger system (Russia), etc. Below are some successful cases among them.

1. Iridium System

The Iridium system belongs to the low Earth orbit satellite mobile communication system, proposed and led by Motorola, consisting of 66 satellites distributed across six orbital planes, evenly spaced across six orbital planes at an orbital height of 780 km. It mainly provides global mobile communication services for individual users, adopting ground centralized control, with inter-satellite links, onboard processing, and onboard switching functions. In addition to providing telephone services, the Iridium system also provides fax, global positioning (GPS), radio positioning, and global paging services. Technically speaking, this system is extremely advanced, but commercially it was a failure, suffering from issues like unclear target users and high costs. Currently, the system has largely revived, with the new Iridium company replacing the old one, repositioning itself and once again leading the new era of satellite communications.

2. Globalstar System

The Globalstar system design is simple, without inter-satellite circuits, onboard processing, or onboard switching functions, merely serving as an extension of terrestrial cellular systems, thus expanding the coverage of terrestrial mobile communication systems, reducing system investment, and minimizing technical risks. The Globalstar system consists of 48 satellites evenly distributed across eight orbital planes at an orbital height of 1389 km. It has four main characteristics: First, the system design is simple, lowering satellite costs and communication expenses; second, mobile users can utilize dual-diversity reception through multipath and multiple satellites, improving reception quality; third, high spectral efficiency; fourth, a large number of ground gateway stations.

3. ICO Global Communication System

The ICO system uses large satellites operating at a medium orbit of 10390 km, consisting of 10 operational satellites and 2 backup satellites deployed across two orbital planes, with five working satellites and one backup satellite per orbital plane. The data transmission rate provided is 140 kbps, with the ability to increase to 384 kbps. It primarily targets high-speed data transmission for non-urban areas, such as internet access services and mobile phone services.

4. Ellips0 System

The Ellips0 system is a hybrid orbit constellation system. It achieves global coverage with only 17 satellites, significantly fewer than the Iridium and Globalstar systems. In this system, 10 satellites are deployed on two elliptical orbits with a perigee of 632 km and an apogee of 7604 km, while another 7 satellites are deployed on an equatorial orbit at an altitude of 8050 km. Initially, the system provided mobile phone services for equatorial regions and began offering global mobile phone services in 2002.

5. Orbcomm System

The Orbcomm system is a small satellite mobile communication system capable of achieving global data communication. It features low investment, short cycle, combined communication and positioning capabilities, lightweight satellites, mobile phone user terminals, high automation levels, and strong autonomous functions. The Orbcomm system consists of 36 small satellites and ground components (including ground gateway stations, network control centers, and ground terminal facilities). Among these, 28 satellites are in supplementary orbital planes: the first orbital plane has 2 satellites at an orbital height of 736/749 km; the second to fourth orbital planes each have 8 satellites at an orbital height of 775 km; the fifth orbital plane has 2 satellites at an orbital height of 700 km, mainly to enhance communication coverage in high-latitude regions; additionally, there are 8 backup satellites.

6. Teledesic System

The Teledesic system is a low Earth orbit satellite mobile communication system focused on broadband business development. It consists of 840 satellites evenly distributed across 21 orbital planes. With additional backup satellites in each orbital plane, totaling 84, the entire system has 924 satellites. After optimization, the actually implemented Teledesic system has reduced the number of satellites to 288. Each satellite in the Teledesic system can provide 100,000 16kbps voice channels, and the entire system can provide over 1 million simultaneous full-duplex E1 rate connections during peak load. Therefore, the system not only provides high-quality voice communication but also supports video conferencing, interactive multimedia communication, and real-time bidirectional high-speed data communication and other broadband communication services.
