Riding Diary: Fallen into the mortal world, who will pity you

by ditan360 on 2010-06-28 09:56:53

Low Carbon Network exclusive report: When I woke up, it was already a brilliant morning with rosy clouds. In the distance, at the horizon where water meets the sky, there were streaks of red clouds. I oiled the chains of every bike. For a long-distance ride, the quality of equipment directly affects the smoothness of the journey. Even though I had no appetite, I forced myself to eat a bowl of Qi flower noodles. Eating soup noodles with oil cakes wasn't really eating; it was more like mechanical swallowing.

At 8:00 AM, we set off on time. My iron steed once again charged towards the June morning of Qinghai Lake with full enthusiasm, deeply inhaling the sweet and moist air. Cycling by the side of Qinghai Lake felt like accidentally plunging into a huge painting, losing one's sense of balance and direction. In front of my eyes was a green meadow adorned with dewdrops, and on the meadow grew rows of nameless wildflowers. Not far away, it connected to an endless expanse of blue lake water.

The grass was so tenderly green, and the flowers were so vigorously yellow. The lake water appeared profoundly deep, its blueness pure, profound, and gentle yet elegant.

Just as I was in awe of the charm of this blue color but struggling to find an appropriate metaphor, I suddenly remembered the nickname given to Qinghai Lake by ethnic minorities. In Mongolian, it is called "Kukunor," and in Tibetan, it is called "Tso Ngön Po," both meaning "blue sea." This probably explains the old saying - "indigo extracted from woad surpasses the blue of woad."

My companion explained scientifically: Have you noticed? Lakes on the plateau are always as pure as mirrors. Because the oxygen content in the lake water on the plateau is low, there are fewer plankton, and the transparency is high. Therefore, the lake water appears more crystal clear.

We sped southward, and the scenery on both sides kept changing. To the right, at the farthest point visible on the edge of Qinghai Lake, where the water met the sky, there was a long stretch of unretreating dark clouds. They rolled like galloping horses, emitting dazzling light under the scorching sun. Looking up, the vast clear sky stretched behind me, the pale blue dome reaching down to the boundless emerald grassland. On the grassland stood undulating dark brown mountains, which were the Qilian Mountains, Riyue Mountains, and Wild Buffalo Mountains. Beneath my feet, the white road was like a sacred khata (a ceremonial scarf), winding and rising towards distant places.

And all of this was soaked in the vitality of the early morning, permeated by the nourishment of the morning rain, appearing so fresh and tranquil. Those sparkling raindrops faintly shimmered among the grass, petals, the surface of the lake, the peaks of the mountains, and the flowing air. As long as I took a light breath, the air tasted sweet. I shouted loudly towards the distance to express my wordless emotion.

We kept encountering cyclists on the road. Most of them were riding heavy mountain bikes, carrying luggage, laboriously pedaling forward, nodding to each other as we passed, which served as a greeting.

When we passed through a village, a large black Tibetan dog suddenly rushed out from the right embankment, barking furiously and chasing after me. I didn't dare to get on the bike or brake. The dog ran while trying to bite, forcing me to the left side of the road. I couldn't stop, so I continued to pedal hard. Although the dog was racing against me, it was ultimately no match for me. When others saw this, I expected them to help resolve the situation, but they slowed down, not daring to approach, watching from afar as the Tibetan dog intercepted me. I wished that the dog would bite fake policemen instead, but unfortunately, the Tibetan dog went after them.

There was a long downhill slope, and we glided all the way to the bridgehead of Heima River. I realized too late that I had left my companions far behind. I stopped and panted heavily, waiting for them by the roadside. We arrived at Heima River at 11:28 AM. We found a spacious patch of grass by the riverbank to have lunch. We had cycled 60 kilometers in the morning, and we estimated that we still had about the same distance to cover in the afternoon. Unfortunately, my old problem on the plateau recurred, leaving me with no appetite. No matter how fragrant or delicious the food was, it wouldn't arouse my desire. Eating was merely for basic survival, and I forced it down.

Sitting by the Heima River resting, I envied those who were sound asleep. However, I had no sleepiness, so I sat hugging my legs, watching the fish gulls catch naked carp in the river, finding it very interesting.

The relationship between the upstream migrating naked carp and their predator, the fish gull, has lasted for hundreds of thousands of years. If we continue to destroy the environment, this food chain will end within 200 years because by then, Qinghai Lake will have dried up. Without Qinghai Lake, naturally, the biological chain related to the lake will also be broken. Two hundred years later, this place will become a barren alkali flat covering more than 4,256.04 square kilometers, a desolate new Gobi desert. I could almost see such a scene vividly, and my heart filled with sadness. I sincerely hope that humans start paying attention to low-carbon protection. I hope Qinghai Lake can exist forever.
