The partnership stated that more and more tourists are looking for sustainable vacations, and more and more tourist destinations are striving to reduce the impact brought by increasing numbers of visitors. However, if tourists wish to leave only a light footprint at their destination, they will have to choose from among 300 sustainable tourism standards.    The alliance said that the unified standard provides a resource that will gain wide recognition, just like the Forest Stewardship Council's label for timber products or the Energy and Environmental Building Certification System (LEED) certification for green buildings by the U.S. Green Building Council, An Kang Hotel.    Tensie Whelan, executive director of the Rainforest Alliance, said: "Many people don't understand what sustainable tourism is. This partnership aims to tell people the answer and ensure it is practical," referring to the Rainforest Alliance which co-founded the partnership with the UN Foundation and other UN agencies.    In recent years, sustainable tourism has gained increasing attention, now estimated to account for 1% of the tourism industry. In early 2007, due to growing interest in sustainable tourism, some environmental organizations concerned with this issue and major tourism businesses came together to formulate these standards, including boutique hotels, Hyatt Hotels, the American travelocity website, expedia website, etc., A Ba Hotel.    The standards require that tourism development does not negatively impact the geographical environment, local communities, or cultural heritage of tourist destinations. The organizers of the Standard Alliance said that if the standards were widely adopted, it would further green the supply chain of hotels and vacation spots, reducing the impact on wildlife and local communities.    The alliance will propose specific indicators for sustainable tourism operations. For example, a hotel must reduce its greenhouse gas emissions by a certain percentage. However, unlike some standards such as the USDA's organic product label applied uniformly to all food, the sustainable tourism operation standard will vary based on each place's unique environmental and cultural issues.   Specific requirements will be established by local organizations or governments according to specific local or regional conditions.  , Whelan said: "This develops local standards - unlike the Forest Stewardship Council - it is a place-based financial system."    Although the standards vary worldwide, the standard-makers indicated that they do not want to set overly strict standards that discourage operators from compliance. Kate Dodson, Deputy Director of the Sustainable Development Department of the United Nations Foundation, said: "We don't want to set the bar too high so it becomes an obstacle to start."    In terms of the environment, the standards require operators to measure and reduce energy consumption, water use, waste accumulation, and greenhouse gas emissions. Regarding social impacts, activities of local organizations need to follow a "code of conduct" approved by local organizations. Members must ensure that business operations "respect" cultural heritage and wildlife.    Tourism directly or indirectly provides 8% of global jobs and generates trillions of dollars in revenue worldwide. The World Tourism Organization estimates that by 2010, the number of tourists will reach 1.1 billion, and by 2020, it will reach 1.6 billion. However, the rapid development of tourism has also led to increasing environmental problems, including geographical damage, excessive energy consumption, and environmental pollution. According to estimates by the United Nations Environment Programme, tourism accounts for 5% of global greenhouse gas emissions, Anshan Hotel.
