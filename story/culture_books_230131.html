"Those People in the Cell" The unwritten rules in prison

by jqrxass on 2009-08-28 18:13:44

The novel tells the story of a young man who, in the 1970s, was wrongfully imprisoned. It recounts the people and events he witnessed during his time in jail, as well as his experiences fleeing across the border to Vietnam to "fight against the Americans and aid the Vietnamese." After being captured, he met a Vietnamese female prisoner in a Vietnamese prison. The novel delves deeply into the lack of legal systems within prisons at that time and the unwritten survival rules. It reveals the degradation and dehumanization suffered by both spirit and body in daily life—eating, drinking, urinating, defecating, and sleeping—and shines a light on the persistence and brightness of human nature.

### People in the Cell - Related Sections

In a world dominated by power, the degree of freedom can be arbitrarily adjusted by those in authority. A detention center differs from a prison; within a prison, there are reflection cells, and within these cells, there are prisoners with and without shackles, with varying weights of leg irons. Additionally, there are propaganda teams, boiler rooms, gag cloths, and return ropes. The levels of freedom and restriction are quite diverse. The extent of freedom is closely related to the satisfaction of desires such as eating, drinking, urinating, defecating, and sleeping. People seek benefits, avoid harm, flatter authority, form cliques, compete with each other, and cooperate, creating a rich tapestry of stories.

### People in the Cell - Part One

After being detained for more than three months in the district branch detention center, I became as slim as a street dog that had been wandering for over six months due to the hunger inflicted by the "bitter remembrance meal" under the "proletarian dictatorship." My fellow inmates said I had "three fasts": my buttocks were sharper than a cone, my spine sharper than a blade, and I could lie down faster than I could get up. They also said my vertebrae protruded like a string of sugar-coated haws.
