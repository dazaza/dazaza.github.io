In fact, WeChat is the real Taobao killer.

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 13:19:43

How to defeat Taobao? In the first tier are JD.com, Dangdang, and YiXun, etc., while Mogu Street and Meilijie form the second tier in mobile commerce. Taobao has dominated the e-commerce industry for 10 years without seeing a fragmented market. However, WeChat, which was founded just 3 years ago, quietly commands 500 million users. If WeChat were to direct keywords from its information feed to e-commerce or its own YiXun platform—WeChat could become Taobao's killer.

Taobao killed the malls

Founded on May 10, 2003, Taobao quickly gained user approval by granting sellers the ability to build their own online stores and operate independently. Compared to traditional malls, Taobao broke the concept of space, allowing shopping from home, whereas malls still depend on users having enough time and finding specific shopping locations.

Taobao solved the problem of letting users shop without leaving their homes. eBay charged users for displaying products and services on its website in China, but Taobao did not charge, thus building up a large base of sellers and buyers. In 2003, Taobao launched AliWangWang, an instant messaging tool, to help sellers and buyers interact.

The mindset of the mall era was to find prime locations, advertise, and wait for customers to convert from browsers to shoppers. Taobao solved the issue of shopping space through a series of unconventional methods that traditional manufacturers couldn't comprehend, thereby killing off the malls.

Afterward, the Taobao platform became conventional, and browsing Taobao became a daily habit for ordinary users, but the time issue was still unresolved. We need ample time to shop online, and after the e-commerce market heated up, JD.com and other old-school companies responded with nothing more than holiday sales. Without solving the time issue, Taobao remains strong.

None of them could kill Taobao

Mobile shopping malls represented by Mogu Street began to emerge. Their app product images, prices, and details are directly sourced from Taobao, with some reorganization and arrangement added. Furthermore, their secondary guidance behavior is based on analysis of Taobao user data. These apps make money by directing traffic to Taobao store merchants and taking a commission.

These applications shortened our shopping time via mobile means, expanding our activity range, but they did not fundamentally solve the shopping time issue; they merely optimized web content and moved it to mobile devices.

Users still worry about buying a certain color, style, or brand, lacking a strong incentive to complete this purchase.

Have you ever thought, if WeChat highlighted product keywords appearing in friend chats and added shopping links? Whether importing to its own YiXun platform or directing traffic to other platforms, such in-feed purchasing can rival Taobao in a short time. As for user experience issues, Zhang Xiaolong will surely address them.

Why WeChat?

WeChat disruptively solved the time issue. The impulse to buy does not stem from rational purchasing needs but rather occurs during chats where friends mention a product or directly recommend it, prompting consumption.

Becoming Taobao's killer was probably not part of WeChat's plan. From previous actions against marketing accounts and folded public accounts, Zhang Xiaolong remains cautious in product planning. Before keyword redirection, the 6-yuan sticker store was more like an experiment.

Even if WeChat doesn’t promote this idea, we can take away the insight that shopping will become part of information consumption. We don’t engage in conversations with friends with the intention of shopping; we just end up making purchases during these interactions. Can we also solve watching movies or listening to music within WeChat?

How WeChat kills Taobao

Impulse buying solves the consumption time issue and actually changes the way traffic monetization works.

The traditional e-commerce mindset is that traffic leads to purchases, which is the basic business model for ads and traffic-driving apps. However, purchasing driven by information consumption bypasses this fixed mindset. Users no longer need to choose a specific brand of clothing or a particular shopping guide site, or even a fixed shopping platform. Shopping demands arising with information consumption do not rely on the previous "logical" purchasing approach; they are random, jumping, and impulsive.

This impulse to buy accompanying information consumption is based on friendships. The alliance between Sina, AutoNavi, and Alibaba hopes to let users see consumption reviews on Weibo at malls and complete purchases through Alipay, but WeChat friends have stronger connections than Weibo followers. WeChat users can even skip over the steps of choosing a consumption location and multiple evaluations; a single suggestion from a close friend is enough to drive multiple purchases.

The most critical aspect of this accompanying consumption is stimulating "confused" and "conservative" users. "Confused" users often struggle with decisions like which shampoo flavor to buy. If during a chat, a friend suggests strawberry flavor suits them better, clicking on the "strawberry flavor" keyword to be redirected to the purchase page, one-click payment completes the purchase.

For "conservative" users who lack action, unintentional clicks during several chats cleverly avoid their indecisive nature.

Can search engines mimic WeChat’s model? The answer is no. Although search engine technology is based on keyword indexing, its information-oriented mindset determines that search engines cannot meet users' impulsive purchasing needs. When users start using search engines, they already have a purpose, seeking known products or information amidst chaotic data—not all users have purchasing intentions. If search engines popped up with "You might need XX," it would undoubtedly be a disturbance for information-seeking users.

WeChat's accompanying consumption is like discovering Lingbo Step in the WuLiang Jade Cave, finding a way to outsmart Taobao. However, whether it chooses to use this as self-defense depends on Zhang Xiaolong.
