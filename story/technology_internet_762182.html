Dianping: A Bone the Giants Can't Chew, Living Outside the Comfort Zone

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 14:09:27

Tao Zhang, CEO of Dianping

Written by Yuan Yin, edited by Jing Yang, photographed by Nan Xiao

Tao Zhang, CEO of Dianping, has once again postponed the company's IPO.

In 2011, Dianping received a $100 million Series C funding round from Sequoia Capital and others, reaching a valuation of $1 billion. By convention, it should have chosen to go public within one to two years afterward, but it didn't. Instead, in 2012, it raised another $60 million in its Series D round.

Given Dianping’s scale and development trajectory, going public would not be difficult. However, Zhang feels that an IPO at this point would be too costly. He deconstructs the internet (PC + mobile) into four elements: people, information, goods, and services. Each connection point offers the opportunity for a giant to emerge. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with each other, and Alibaba connects people with goods. The space between people and services is where the fourth giant could sit. Since 2003, Dianping has been diligently accumulating data, finally waiting for the explosion of mobile internet, which brings together "location" and "services" through smartphones. Following Zhang's logic, no company seems more suited to occupy the imagination of the fourth giant than Dianping.

But the outside world doesn’t see it that way. In recent months, with BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent) making frequent acquisitions and strategic moves, Dianping has become a hypothetical acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition candidate; under any of the BAT brands, it could help them achieve great success earlier. On the other hand, Dianping's size pales in comparison to BAT, and it may still be premature to declare it a future giant. Moreover, whether it’s Tencent WeChat's public platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after acquiring Gaode and Meituan, all are directly targeting Dianping’s territory — local life services centered on dining, entertainment, and leisure.

Before proving these two things, a Dianping IPO would equal a "discounted sale." First, Zhang needs time to prove that what Dianping does now, BAT cannot do. Second, Dianping is about to enter the final phase of the group-buying war, and if it can secure the top spot, its valuation could increase multiple times over.

2013 is Dianping's "year of decisive battles." Regarding speculation and rumors, Zhang remains deaf to them. He is clear: "The internet has indeed entered an era of great convergence, but Dianping will not be acquired."

Zhang categorizes companies that might be acquired into three types. The first type has platform attributes but is better suited to develop within the ecosystem of a giant, such as maps and emails. These products can easily attract large numbers of users but struggle to find independent business models. Some, like browsers, can make money, but the revenue from navigation services is far less than their strategic value to large companies. "Giant companies are willing to spend money nurturing email products because they help with branding and user stickiness, but I've never heard of a standalone email company succeeding." The second type involves industry consolidation, like in the video industry, where acquisitions expand market share. The third type includes companies facing financial difficulties who sell themselves for survival.

Now Meituan has already joined Alibaba's ranks. In theory, Dianping should also quickly seek a "backer" to avoid being defeated due to competitors gaining capital and rapidly expanding. However, Zhang says, "The giants offer us limited help. We do need users, but execution is more important. Acquisitions would reduce our execution ability, and we might even be marginalized by a larger company." Furthermore, he aims to maintain the most precise "distance" between Dianping and the giants. If Dianping gets too close to any one of them, it would be equivalent to "taking sides," making it an enemy of the others.

Thus, logically, the acquisition of Dianping becomes a dead knot. Zhang counters, "Why should we take their money? We don't lack funds; what we need are resources. If a giant is willing to invest a small amount and give us many resources, I could consider it. But would they? Would a giant provide you with resources if they only hold a small stake?"

Weapon One: Dirty Work, Hard Work

In mobile internet, opportunities for companies doing purely internet-based products are becoming fewer. New opportunities come from those combining with traditional industries. Zhang believes that life services mainly revolve around eating, drinking, entertainment, and leisure. Other niche markets, such as ride-hailing, can form decent business models, but it's hard for them to grow into giants.

For dining and entertainment, user needs are simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is the core of life services, which is precisely where Dianping started. Whether in content, member accumulation, or brand, later entrants find it hard to surpass Dianping.

The most representative product form of discounts is group buying. Group buying has now entered the championship battle phase, and Dianping plans to face off against Meituan in 2013. This is a winner-takes-all market. As long as there are two strong players, the industry's gross margin will be lowered, preventing stable profitability.

Another foreseeable future is reservations. Reservations can create a true online-to-offline loop, but they must be based on Dianping having an absolute advantage in both information and discounts. Otherwise, building a closed loop is difficult. In 2012, Dianping began experimenting with reservations. Zhang said, "Both user and merchant feedback has been good so far, but if reservations were done separately, it would be hard to solve the chicken-and-egg problem."

Of course, life services present some heavier opportunities. For example, food delivery is a rigid demand for users, and merchants can increase sales. Over the past few years, food delivery companies like "To Home Gourmet" have thrived. However, the core of food delivery is delivery—getting meals delivered in a very short time while addressing issues like soup spills. The logistics requirements are even higher than those for fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang said, "If food delivery is executed well, there's still opportunity, but it's harder dirty work and labor-intensive. What Dianping is doing now is already dirty and labor-intensive enough."

If judged by age, Dianping is already an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has painstakingly accumulated merchant profiles and user reviews, which Zhang refers to as "dirty work, hard work." This primitive growth model once restricted its speed, but the pain of the past has become Dianping's core barrier today. It can be said that, except for Meituan, Dianping has no real rivals in the life services sector.

However, he still needs to remain vigilant against BAT's "clutches." Baidu's involvement is the most direct. Baidu Maps already has over 100 million users, allowing users to search for nearby dining and other life services. Tencent, considered to have secured its ticket to mobile internet, launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest goods trading platform, and expanding goods from physical items to services is natural. Moreover, it has incorporated Meituan and Gaode into its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there hasn't been an explosive growth trend in either reality or data," Zhang said. Brand positioning is crucial; the key is which product users think of first when they want to eat, drink, play, or relax.

Moreover, in the vast realm of life services, competition among giants will inevitably overlap, and no one can completely monopolize the field. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained on platforms like Weibo and WeChat. Zhang feels that this makes Dianping safer instead. "In niche areas like car rentals and business travel, the massive user base directed by giants could easily overwhelm existing companies."

As for Alibaba, which invested in Gaode, invading Dianping's territory isn't easy either. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, which can be categorized no matter how peculiar. A user's experience of a meal depends on various factors like the day's environment, waitstaff, and chef. Managing merchants can't be done using systems and tools like Alibaba does; it requires a "ground force" managed by people.

"All three giants want to follow this path, and there is room for imagination, but it's not a highway; it's a rugged mountain road," Zhang believes. First, providing life services requires ground teams deployed across various levels, something BAT lacks the genes and capability to manage offline. Second, they lack the willingness; life services require dedicated effort and aren't something a small team can accomplish. Making money is slow. "It's hard for a company to excel at two important things simultaneously, let alone while they must consolidate their advantages in their original fields."

Weapon Two: Small Boats Turn Easily

When Dianping secured a $100 million financing round in 2011, Zhang told the outside world that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, the world changes fast, and before Dianping went public, his analogies had fallen from grace.

He wasn't particularly anxious about this. "They didn't become big companies because they lacked offline teams; now they're trying to become America's Dianping."

The current path is one Zhang has gradually explored step by step. When he felt that selling keyword ads and coupons was outdated, the emergence of group buying caught his eye. Initially, like the Americans, he viewed group buying as a marketing platform, offering one deal per day. But as orders increased, he realized group buying had the characteristics of a trading platform. Thus, he saw the future of Dianping as a life services trading platform. The two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were precisely used to transition Dianping from an information platform to a trading platform.

Without this transformation, Zhang might not have had the confidence to compete for the fourth seat among the giants. But while the idea of transformation sounds simple, it was equivalent to Dianping starting over, a process that was extremely painful.

First, compared to selling ads alone, a trading platform lowers gross margins and demands stricter cost control and efficiency. Second, a trading platform requires higher cross-departmental communication capabilities.

To maintain Dianping's impartiality and independence as an information platform, the content team and sales team were completely separated. Zhang even deliberately erected a "firewall" between departments — even for important clients, sales couldn't alter information on the platform. This once led to mutual antagonism between the content and sales teams. Now, as a trading platform, the editorial and sales teams need deep communication to create effective marketing plans. Besides planning, editors' cooperation is required. He said, "Maintaining information quality is still important, but there's no need to reinforce hostility; the firewall must be dismantled."

Though editors logically accept this, individual behaviors and values have inertia, and when combined across the entire company, the inertia is even greater. Zhang specially held several meetings to encourage more communication between departments. After a few months, an editor manager casually told a sales manager, "I used to think you were my enemy, but now I realize you're my colleague."

The sales department also needed transformation. To promote conversion, Zhang cleverly gave the offline team a dual identity. First, for merchants, salespeople should become marketing consultants, not just selling products but helping merchants devise marketing strategies. Second, for the company, marketing consultants are procurement agents. Dianping is like a supermarket; not only must product quality be good, but the variety of goods must also be rich. Marketing consultants should aim to help users procure group-buying products. Of course, dual identities will inevitably lead to a series of complex problems. For frontline personnel, this is quite challenging, requiring them to balance the scales themselves.

Substantially, Dianping's "dual identity" is similar to Alibaba's division into 25 business units. Decentralizing decision-making to the front lines allows the company to respond faster but increases management difficulty. Similarly, compared to frontline employees, the management team suffers more after Dianping's transformation.

Every year, Zhang breaks down Dianping's corporate goals into various departments, then senior executives further break them down layer by layer, but breaking down isn't simple arithmetic. Joint founder Jing Li manages the sales team. Sometimes unresolved merchant needs stem from sales issues, but affect sales. So, Li broke out a new position, setting up communication specialists who daily gather information from marketing consultants and submit it to other departments while tracking feedback. He often tells his team, embracing change doesn't mean liking change; no one likes change, and everyone feels uncomfortable when encountering it. "All employees must go through discomfort zones, including me."

Numbers reflect the "heaviness" of Dianping's execution team. Currently, among more than 3,000 employees, over 2,000 work offline. The ground force is responsible for directly communicating with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group-buying products, and integrated marketing solutions.

Dianping has a unique management evaluation system for this core team that resists BAT's influence.

They aren't called "salespeople"; their business cards have always read "marketing planning consultant" since day one. Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the era of watching news and chatting on QQ, lagging behind Taobao merchants by at least three to five years in understanding the internet. Marketing planning consultants need to tell merchants what online users need and how to conduct internet marketing. Li said, "Even for a high-end restaurant, marketing advisors wouldn't recommend a groupons for abalone or shark fins, possibly switching to other dishes, because online and offline users have different demands."

An excellent marketing advisor must be a versatile player. They need to explain clearly to restaurant owners and hair salon owners the fundamental differences between discounts and group buying, and which discount suits their positioning best. If facing a hot pot restaurant owner, the marketing advisor should know whether it's the off-season or peak season, which products' costs rise during certain seasons and decrease in others, proposing a group-buying menu that both merchants and users love.

This places high demands on marketing advisors, requiring them to be not only internet marketing experts but also industry experts. Li said, "I must admit, it's impossible for every advisor to be a double expert, so we set star ratings for advisors." Like hotels, marketing advisors range from one-star to five-stars, with the best being superstars known as Super Stars.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline employees aren't tech-savvy graduates from prestigious schools, making it difficult for them to understand internet products. Dianping established a knowledge base for marketing advisors, containing knowledge points and related cases, accessible to both new and old employees. If encountering new problems, employees can ask questions in the knowledge base, and thousands of consultants nationwide will help answer.

A large number of knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees based on practical experience. Dianping established a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, rewarding employees who help others the most each quarter. Every quarter, Dianping also conducts skill assessments for employees, directly linking the results to promotions.

Industry knowledge is harder to obtain than internet knowledge. Some experiences are summarized by traditional merchants after decades of trial and error in the industry. A Super Star marketing advisor told Li that the hairdressing industry should avoid group-buying packages for washing, cutting, and blow-drying, because boys lack motivation to buy, and girls prefer blow-drying without washing or cutting. Li was astonished, "We men would never think of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing advisors are not only divided by city but also by industries such as catering, hairdressing, and leisure. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is even subdivided into hotpot, Western cuisine, and buffets, with one marketing advisor responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing advisors can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, making merchants less resistant when selling products," he said.

Each marketing group holds its own case-sharing sessions: what difficulties a certain type of merchant encounters, what marketing needs they have, which company products are used in the solution, and how effective it is. Outstanding cases are reported upward, and Dianping officially shares one case each week.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition called "Entrepreneur Winner." In the first three months after joining, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products but listening to merchants sharing experiences. If they are responsible for hotpot restaurants, after three months, they must submit a business plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hotpot Restaurant Owner." Like graduation, employees defend their presentations on-site, with industry supervisors evaluating the winners, and the company giving certain rewards.

Strategically speaking, whether it's Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, their tasks are quite similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O sound alike to anyone familiar with the industry. "The era where storytelling and concept creation could lead to success is over. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model lies in management and execution, not strategy," Zhang said.
