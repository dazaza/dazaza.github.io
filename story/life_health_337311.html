What is "Zhong Qi"? In Traditional Chinese Medicine, "Zhong Qi" refers to the energy of the spleen and stomach. The spleen and stomach are the foundation of postnatal health, the source of blood and qi transformation, and the pivot of the body's ascending and descending qi movements. Because they are located in the middle burner, they have this name. Unfortunately, many modern people suffer from insufficient Zhong Qi.

When Zhong Qi is insufficient, it leads to weakness in the functions of the spleen and stomach, abnormal transformation and transportation, and affects the digestion and absorption of food. Common symptoms include fatigue, shortness of breath, weakness, sweating, reduced appetite with abdominal distension, weak speech, low spirits. From a facial appearance perspective, one may appear overweight due to deficiency, or show tooth marks on the tongue. Severe cases can lead to sinking of Zhong Qi, causing conditions such as gastropexy, uterine prolapse, and prolapsed anus. On the contrary, those with sufficient Zhong Qi will have strong stomach function, normal digestion and absorption, and ample production of blood and qi for nourishment.

In daily life, there are mainly three types of people who are prone to insufficient Zhong Qi:

1. Those with congenital insufficiency or weak spleen and stomach. Although the spleen and stomach are the foundation of postnatal health, if one has a constitutionally weak body and an unstable base from birth, it can also lead to weakness in the spleen and stomach and insufficient Zhong Qi later in life.

2. Those who overwork physically or mentally for a long time. Excessive physical and mental labor can damage the spleen's qi. When the spleen's qi is deficient, there is no source for the production of blood and qi, leading to malnourished limbs and symptoms of insufficient Zhong Qi like fatigue and weakness.

3. Those who do not eat regularly or excessively. As the saying goes, "overeating harms the spleen and stomach," meaning that binge eating or excessive hunger damages the spleen and stomach, leading to abnormal transformation and transportation of blood and qi, and insufficient Zhong Qi.

So, what should be noted when Zhong Qi is insufficient? First, in terms of diet, one should consume more easily digestible foods (such as noodles, milk, soft rice), avoid raw, cold, hard, and stimulating foods; eat small meals frequently, and avoid binge eating. Secondly, overwork must be avoided, as "nothing harms the spleen more than fatigue." Thought is associated with the spleen, and excessive thinking can harm the spleen. Therefore, patients with insufficient Zhong Qi should avoid both physical and mental exhaustion.

The representative prescription for treating insufficient Zhong Qi in Traditional Chinese Medicine is "Bu Zhong Yi Qi Tang". For convenience, similar traditional Chinese patent medicines are available on the market, such as Bu Zhong Yi Qi Wan and Ren Shen Jian Pi Wan. These have the effects of tonifying the center, strengthening the spleen, replenishing qi, and raising yang. Huangqi in the formula is praised as the "king of all qi-supplementing herbs," having the effects of tonifying the center, replenishing qi, consolidating the exterior, and raising yang, which can improve symptoms caused by qi deficiency such as fatigue and loss of appetite. Dangshen, Bai Zhu, and Gan Cao can replenish qi and strengthen the spleen, Chen Pi can regulate qi and invigorate the spleen, while Sheng Ma and Chai Hu can raise yang.
