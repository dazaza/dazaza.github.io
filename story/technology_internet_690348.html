20-Year-Old Boy Sentenced to Life for Killing Step-Mother, Hopes to See Birth Mother During Trial

by uelu6c6b on 2012-03-05 09:48:11

At the age of 12, he left home alone to make his way in the world, harboring resentment. At 20, he killed his stepmother: [http://news.sina.com.cn/s/2012-02-20/125323961097.shtml]. Sentenced to life imprisonment, his final wish is:

"I hope my mom comes to see me."

On February 20th, at the Beijing No.1 Intermediate People's Court, the courtroom doors were tightly closed. Li Zhiyan, who had just been sentenced to life imprisonment, was huddled on a chair in his prisoner's uniform awaiting to sign the court record.

Due to killing his stepmother, the 20-year-old Li Zhiyan was sentenced to life imprisonment in the first trial. "Death sentence or life imprisonment, it's all the same to me anyway; living has no meaning," Li Zhiyan said as he aimlessly glanced around. Li Zhiyan no longer wanted to fight back; the experiences after his parents' divorce had made him despair again and again.

But at night, lying alone in bed, Li Zhiyan couldn't help but fantasize, "If one day time could flow backward, if my mother regretted her actions and came looking for me, and if my father loved me like before, that would be wonderful..." But that day never came.

The rift began when his parents faked their divorce to gain more property during a housing demolition in Niujie in 1997. Each household tried every possible method to get more square footage, and the Li family was no exception. "After calculating, my parents decided to fake their divorce to get another house," Li Zhiyan said. The plan was well laid out: once they got the house, they would remarry at the Civil Affairs Bureau. However, before they even received the new house, everything changed.

"It was at this time that she appeared," Li Zhiyan could never forget the first time he met his stepmother Zhao Qing. "She looked like an older sister, appearing almost ten years younger than my father." From then on, the relationship between his parents became increasingly strained.

Living together

Mother sold blood to support her son's education

In 1997, Li Zhiyan was six years old. His parents' conflicts officially reached the court. The court ruled that Li Zhiyan would live with his mother, and Li Dong would pay 150 yuan per month as child support. Not long after the verdict, Wang Shuzhi packed up and left the old house in Niujie with her son.

In the adult world, what "separation" meant was something young Li Zhiyan couldn't yet understand. As they left, he waved goodbye to his father, "At that time, I thought that one day our family would reunite; separation was only temporary." Years later, Li Zhiyan recalled with a bitter laugh, but his father never returned.

Li Zhiyan and his mother's new home was in Hongmen, a small room where the bed took up most of the space. Less than four months after moving out, there was no further news about Li Dong's child support.

Because she had to raise the family and take care of her child without a formal job, Wang Shuzhi started working tirelessly as a day laborer, but the money was still not enough. To pay for her son's tuition fees, with no other options, Wang Shuzhi began selling blood for money, losing over thirty pounds in less than two years.

"As long as dad comes back and sees how mom is, they will reunite, and mom won't have to sell blood anymore," Li Zhiyan naively thought. However, after two whole years passed, his father never visited Hongmen once. Two years later, Li Dong remarried, marrying Zhao Qing, a woman nearly ten years younger from Gansu.

When Li Zhiyan was in the third grade, in order to get his father to come back and visit, after thinking for several days, he finally mustered the courage to call his father, lying that his mother was going to sell a kidney to pay for his tuition. However, before he could finish speaking, his father angrily shouted, "Your mom can sell if she wants, dying has nothing to do with me," then hung up the phone.

He sat dumbfounded in the room for half a day. Li Zhiyan finally understood, "Dad is never coming back."

Abandoned

Real mother disappeared without a word

"I hate my dad, at the time I wanted to kill him, but later I stopped hating him. It doesn't matter that he doesn't want me, I still have my mom." But Li Zhiyan didn't expect that soon after, his mother also left.

"July 2000, summer vacation before fourth grade." Every detail of that day was etched clearly in Li Zhiyan's memory.

Early in the morning, his mother suddenly felt stomach discomfort and pulled him along to the office to ask for money for medical treatment. An aunt from the office led him outside into the yard and told him to wait.

Bored waiting alone, Li Zhiyan squatted on the grass watching insects. A line of ants marched across the grass, unnoticed until the sun rose high above.

When he returned inside, there was already no one there.

Li Zhiyan ran down the corridor shouting, pushing open each door, but he couldn't find his mother. "Maybe mom couldn't find me and has already gone home." Thinking this, he ran back along the original path, but when he opened the door, there was no one at home. "Maybe mom didn't get money at the office and went to work to earn money for treatment." Li Zhiyan consoled himself. He moved a small stool and sat by the door waiting for his mom. When he was hungry, he ran to the neighbor's house for food, and when he was tired, he lay down on the bed to sleep. However, every time he woke up, the room was still empty. This lasted for a week.

"I feel like I've been forgotten, and I don't know where to look for her," Li Zhiyan recalled. When he woke up again, he had already been sent to the hospital by neighbors due to carbon monoxide poisoning, but when he opened his eyes, his mother was still not by his side.

Sitting dazed on the hospital bed, Li Zhiyan finally hugged the pillow and burst into tears, "Even if she went out to earn money, she should have come back by now." Li Zhiyan had to tell himself, "Mom has left."

More than ten years later, standing expressionless in the courtroom, Li Zhiyan's eyes reddened when talking about his mother.

However, throughout, Li Zhiyan never hated his mother, "I know I'm a burden. It's better that she left, so she doesn't have to sell blood to fund my education anymore."

Homeless

Fifth-grade suicide attempt on the road

After his mother left, Li Zhiyan had nowhere to go, so he went to stay at his father's house.

However, living with his father again, Li Zhiyan felt his father had changed. "Before, he rarely hit me, but now whenever he's in a bad mood, he comes home and beats and scolds me, using anything he can grab—ashtrays, clothes hangers, whatever he gets his hands on," Li Zhiyan said.

When primary school tuition fees were due, Li Zhiyan asked his father for money, and his stepmother slapped his hand, "We don't have extra money to pay for his tuition." His father sat silently on the side, saying nothing.

His homeroom teacher knew Li Zhiyan didn't have money for meals, so secretly paid his meal fees.

Li Zhiyan didn't want to go home to face his stepmother and father, so after school, he slept in the school basement, wearing smelly uniforms during the day. His classmates all avoided him because he smelled bad, and older students cursed him behind his back as a "wild child." Gradually, Li Zhiyan's personality became increasingly isolated.

"I hate my stepmother; if it weren't for her, my dad wouldn't treat me this way." For the first time, Li Zhiyan thought of death.

In the winter of 2001, before the first snow fell, the air was dry and cold. One afternoon at five o'clock, after standing by the roadside for hours, Li Zhiyan suddenly ran into the middle of the road and crazily threw himself under an oncoming car. A large truck sharply swerved and narrowly avoided hitting him.

Minutes later, two policemen ran over and forcibly dragged him back to the police station.

Recalling the incident, Li Zhiyan remembered that when his father Li Dong received the call from the police station, he simply said, "Wants to commit suicide? Let him die, he's just a burden anyway."

Every word from his father was deeply engraved in Li Zhiyan's mind, "If you want me dead so much, why did you give birth to me in the first place!"

That year, Li Zhiyan was in fifth grade. That year, he realized that although his father was still around, he was essentially an orphan.

In the second semester of sixth grade, unable to bear living at home any longer, Li Zhiyan found the Beijing Minor Assistance and Protection Center and stayed there until he graduated from elementary school.

"After that, I passed the entrance exam for an ordinary middle school, but my dad didn't let me go; he forced me to attend a vocational school and even refused to pay the tuition fee." Recalling this, Li Zhiyan, at the age of twelve, ultimately decided to leave Beijing and leave this nominal home behind.

Running away

At 12, embarking on a journey alone

The public bus swayed back and forth, and in the last row, Li Zhiyan tightly hugged his schoolbag by the window, with 20 yuan given by a stranger—a policeman at the shelter—in his pocket, remaining silent.

At 12, while other children were happily preparing junior high school supplies with their parents, Li Zhiyan embarked on a solitary journey.

Leaving the shelter, Li Zhiyan randomly boarded a long-distance bus. The bus headed south, stopping at its final destination in Guyan County, Hebei Province.

To fill his belly, at 12, Li Zhiyan was introduced to work as a waiter in a KTV, staying there for three years. Later, he summarized the environment there with four words: "a mix of good and bad."

"But consumption in Guyan County was low, and regulations were lax; at 12, I could find work and eat full meals, which was unimaginable in Beijing," Li Zhiyan said. However, being young, Li Zhiyan was often bullied in the KTV.

Finally reaching 15, Li Zhiyan resigned from his job in Guyan County and left, but afterward, he never stayed in one place for more than three months.

Li Zhiyan lost count of how many places he had been since leaving Beijing—"Baoding, Xingtai, Baotou, Mudanjiang...". Once unable to stay in a place, he earned enough for bus fare and moved to the next location.

Being underage, many factories wouldn’t hire him, so Li Zhiyan helped distribute flyers, earning ten or eight yuan a day, barely enough to fill his stomach.

With no place to sleep at night, subways, bridges, and train stations became his resting spots; during the day, he wandered the streets, knocking on doors to seek job opportunities.

Jobs were hard to find, and without money, Li Zhiyan lay starving under a bridge. It was then he first thought of stealing. (Continued on A16)

Hatred erupted - Killed stepmother at home in the dead of night

Opening Li Zhiyan's file, starting from the end of 2006, he was detained three times for disrupting public order and four times for theft, receiving sentences or imprisonments, the longest being ten months, followed by one year and three months of re-education through labor.

Having a criminal record, no company was willing to hire him.

Cornered, Li Zhiyan returned to Beijing and found his father. Father Li Dong contacted a factory owner friend in Mudanjiang, arranging jobs for both Li Zhiyan and Zhao Qing’s two nephews.

However, within a month, Zhao Qing’s nephew was dismissed for fighting.

"My stepmother told my dad it was definitely me who corrupted their kids," Li Zhiyan said.

Enraged, Li Dong called his son and berated him, soon having him fired by his friend.

This action reignited Li Zhiyan's hatred towards his stepmother, "My dad said if he saw me again in Beijing, he'd kill me, but I have no money here, how am I supposed to survive?" Li Zhiyan resented his stepmother, "My dad is just that kind of person—easily influenced, believing hearsay. If it weren't for my stepmother spreading rumors, he wouldn't treat me like this!"

In July 2011, having spent all his money, Li Zhiyan bought a train ticket and returned to Beijing alone.

On the night of September 3, 2011, Li Zhiyan sneaked into his father's house, "I originally just wanted to steal a few hundred yuan, buy a ticket, and leave Beijing to find work elsewhere."

However, accidentally bumping the table scared him, and he ran to the kitchen, grabbing a knife off the table intending to intimidate his stepmother.

Seeing his sleeping stepmother, childhood memories suddenly replayed like a movie—his stepmother refusing to pay his tuition, forcing him to run away to Guyan County at 12; starving on the street, stealing bicycles; and finding a job opportunity only to lose it because of her...

Li Zhiyan grew angrier with each thought. In one swift motion, his stepmother woke up, staring at him with wide eyes, a gaping hole in her chest, her orange sleepwear soaked in blood.

Terrified, Li Zhiyan grabbed the laptop and 200 yuan from the table and fled.

It wasn’t until he was caught by the police in an internet cafe that he realized, "My stepmother was dead."

Years-long wish: "I hope my mom comes to see me"

In the empty courtroom, after signing the trial record, Li Zhiyan slumped back into his chair. Minutes later, he began absentmindedly playing with the handcuffs on his wrist.

Just moments before, the judge of the Beijing No.1 Intermediate People's Court read the first-instance verdict: Li Zhiyan was sentenced to life imprisonment for intentional assault and ordered to compensate the victim's family over 650,000 yuan for various economic losses.

"Actually, whether death penalty or life imprisonment makes no difference to me; living has no meaning anyway," Li Zhiyan said indifferently regarding his punishment. However, when asked why neither of his parents attended the sentencing, Li Zhiyan softly replied, "My mom is just lazy, doesn't like reading newspapers, probably didn't know."

Since his parents divorced, Li Zhiyan hasn't had a single happy day, "I feel like a worn-out ball, kicked around by everyone, unwanted by anyone."

Yet, those who treated him kindly along the way, Li Zhiyan remembered them all—the counselor named Ms. Chai who chatted with him and took him to see a doctor in juvenile detention; his elementary school homeroom teacher who paid his meal fees when he was homeless.

Even after more than ten years, Li Zhiyan still held onto a thread of hope for his parents.

At fifteen, Li Zhiyan quit his job in Guyan County, Hebei, returned to Beijing, and went back to the old house in Hongmen alone, only to be informed by a neighbor, "Just half a month ago, Wang Shuzhi came back once, seemed to be packing things."
