Twitter's new system architecture delivers a significant performance boost.

by anonymous on 2013-08-19 15:15:32

On August 3rd, the popular airing of "Castle in the Sky" in Japan set a Twitter peak record with 143,119 tweets per second, which is 25 times the average Twitter tweets per second (TPS) of 5,700.

Notably, during this unexpected "flood peak," Twitter's new system platform did not experience any delays or outages despite the massive influx of tweets.

A comparison of the performance between Twitter's old and new architectures

Just three years ago, during the 2010 World Cup, a penalty kick and a red card caused a "tweet storm" that led to temporary unresponsiveness in Twitter's service. The so-called "pulse of the planet" frequently suffered from "heart attacks." Over the past three years, Twitter engineers worked around the clock trying to improve the Twitter system through incremental fixes, but as Twitter rapidly developed, these methods proved to be short-lived.

Ultimately, Twitter made the difficult decision to re-architect its criticized IT system. After the new platform went online, it achieved revolutionary improvements in performance and reliability. Whether it was the popular airing of "Castle in the Sky" or the Super Bowl final, neither event slowed down Twitter. Moreover, the new architecture provided strong support for the launch of new features such as multimedia tweet cards and cross-device message synchronization.

Recently, Raffi Krikorian (@raffi), Vice President of Platform Engineering at Twitter, wrote an article on the official Twitter blog sharing the methods and experiences of Twitter's new architecture. Below is a summary:

The reasons for re-architecting and the root of the problems

After multiple outages during the 2010 World Cup, we re-examined our system and discovered the following points:

We were running the world's largest Ruby on Rails application, with 200 engineers responsible for developing and maintaining this system. However, with the rapid growth of user scale and service quantity, all database management, Memcache links, and public API codes belonged to the same codebase. This created enormous difficulties for engineers in learning, managing, and parallel development.

Our MySQL storage system had encountered performance bottlenecks. There were read/write hotspots throughout the entire database.

Adding hardware could no longer solve the fundamental system issues — our front-end Ruby servers were handling far fewer transactions per second than expected, and their performance did not match their hardware capabilities.

From a software perspective, we had fallen into the "optimization trap." We were sacrificing the readability and flexibility of the codebase for performance and efficiency.

Re-examining the system and setting three major goals/challenges

Firstly, the new architecture must excel in performance, efficiency, and reliability, reducing latency and significantly enhancing customer experience. At the same time, the number of servers should be reduced to one-tenth of the original; the new system should isolate hardware issues to prevent them from escalating into large-scale outages.

Secondly, address the various drawbacks of the single codebase by attempting a loosely coupled service-oriented model. Our goal is to encourage best practices in encapsulation and modularity, but this time at the system level rather than at the library, module, and package levels.

Thirdly, and most importantly, support the rapid release of new features. We hope that some small teams with sufficient authority can make autonomous decisions and independently release user functions.

Before starting, we developed some proof-of-concept models, ultimately determining the principles, tools, and architecture for reconstruction.

Key measures for system reconstruction

Firstly, Front-end services: Replace Ruby VM with JVM. By rewriting the codebase and migrating Ruby VM services to JVM, performance improved tenfold, now reaching 10-20k requests/second/host.

Secondly, Programming model: Structure the system by service type, establish a unified client-server library bound with load balancing and failover strategies, allowing engineers to focus more on applications and service interfaces.

Thirdly, Adopt SOA (Service-Oriented Architecture) to enable parallel development.

Fourthly, Distributed storage of tweets. Even after breaking down the monolithic application into different "services," storage remains a huge bottleneck. In the past, Twitter used a single MySQL master database for linear tweet writing. Twitter decided to adopt a new partitioning strategy for tweet storage, creating a fault-tolerant sharded distributed database using the Gizzard framework. However, this meant that MySQL's unique ID generation function could no longer be used. Twitter solved this problem with Snowflake.

Fifthly, Monitoring and statistics. Converting a single application into a complex SOA application requires matching tools to manage it effectively. Twitter launches services quickly and needs data-driven decision support. Twitter's Runtime system team developed two tools for engineers: Viz and Zipkin.
