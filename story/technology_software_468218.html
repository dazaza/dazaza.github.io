Graffiti Art: Is it a Defacement of Art or Worth Collecting?

by diawudao on 2011-07-30 15:55:45

From the 1960s to the 1980s, New York was full of anti-culture and anti-tradition. In the gradually marginalized ghettos, some young people were also influenced by the tides of anti-racial discrimination, anti-war, and anti-sexual repression.

Graffiti originated from such social fringes. Rif considers himself as part of the first generation of graffiti artists; he started learning graffiti at the age of 10, first on paper, then practicing on walls in alleys, and finally painting on subway cars. Within this small circle, only those who reached the highest level of skill could paint on moving subway cars. Speaking of today's graffiti, Rif shows much disdain: now many can only be called graffiti writers, they are far from being considered artists. Graffiti has obvious underground characteristics, and since spray paint cans are readily available, a large number of graffiti works spread wildly across New York starting from the early 1970s, from train cars to station platforms, from street corners to toilet walls, almost everywhere. The "World Encyclopedia" writes: graffiti is often written on public toilets, public building walls, or stones in parks; some words and phrases are not very healthy; sometimes it’s just people's names, some related to sex, and many are political slogans. A modern art theorist comments on graffiti: the 1970s and 1980s were the glorious period for graffiti art. Its rise was not only an expression of African American folk art but also a psychological release for the oppressed groups and classes of that era. Therefore, on the surface, it has strong rebellious colors, a tendency to vent, and a casual style, even with some anti-traditional and anti-society spirit.

Most of the early graffiti artists were neither professional artists nor art students; they were mostly street youths. In their view, graffiti itself was a form of rebellion against authority. But this behavior quickly caught the attention of the media, mainstream art, and even the entire society. As a result, graffiti words gradually decreased, replaced by large and intricate images. Academic artists were gradually attracted by this creative mode, and graffiti entered the halls of high art, slowly becoming one of the mainstream art forms.

Cultural Trend Booster

Since the day it was assimilated, graffiti became a promotional tool for mainstream art. The fashion industry reacted the fastest; from luxury brands, jeans to sneakers, almost all have had close contact with graffiti. During the years when cross-over trends were popular, many graffiti masters collaborated with big brands, designing and launching highly personalized products that were favored by the market.

Vivienne Westwood, the punk mother, boldly used graffiti patterns in her fashion series, pushing the brand's personality to the extreme; Marc Jacobs invited designers to design graffiti handbags for Louis Vuitton, which sold exceptionally well. High-priced luxury items become even more popular when combined with graffiti, creating a market miracle where the more expensive, the better it sells.

Under the drive of market forces, graffiti artists are sometimes elevated to star status. Robin Banks calls himself an art terrorist, but that doesn't stop him from becoming one of the most influential figures in the British art world. Low-key is Banks' biggest feature; reportedly, no one knows his true identity, not even his father knows Banks is his son. Banks' works cover a wide range of topics, and his low-profile mysterious nature quickly gained public favor, causing his works to skyrocket from £150 per piece to tens of thousands of pounds.

Outliers under the System

Every year, the city-owned graffiti cleaning organization in New York clears nearly 20 million square feet of graffiti at thousands of locations; and the New York Police Department arrests hundreds of graffiti artists annually.

Not only in culturally rich New York, but even in garden cities like Portland, the city government spends a budget of $2 million each year to clean up graffiti.

Graffiti has become an international controversial topic. In Europe, although many countries tacitly allow the creative behavior of graffiti artists, the economic losses caused by graffiti still burden governments heavily. The German Ministry of Interior once complained that graffiti artists cause billions of euros in losses to Germany each year, while their cost is merely spray paint cans worth a few dozen euros. To completely eliminate the spread of graffiti, the German government even employed high-tech means, equipping police with helicopters equipped with infrared cameras to make them more quickly and effectively chase graffiti artists at night.

Is it artistic creation or environmental damage? People's opinions on graffiti vary greatly. Although Germany explicitly prohibits random graffiti, the former Berlin Wall was the world's longest graffiti wall (3.5 meters high, 166 kilometers long). The development of the fashion industry is also changing the survival conditions of graffiti; after being imprinted with commercial marks, graffiti is no longer a sign of social freedom but has become a driving force for trend indicators.

Graffiti (graffiti), originally meaning scribble in Italian, later referred to images or drawings randomly painted on walls. Graffiti has been around for half a century; despite entering China relatively late, it quickly gained recognition among young people as part of popular street culture. However, as an art form, underground-born graffiti has never truly become part of mainstream culture; countries around the world spend substantial amounts annually to remove various graffiti, leading some to believe that so-called graffiti is nothing more than pollution to cities and vision. Chinese Graffiti

On the Road Seven is one of the earliest people in China to come into contact with graffiti. After graduation, due to working in Tianjin, he gathered a few enthusiasts and founded ABSCREW, which consists of key members from several well-known graffiti teams in the Bohai Rim region, making it a strong combination.

According to Seven, the three main members of the team had all done graffiti in Tianjin, and since all three were from Shijiazhuang, they decided to form a team. As one of the four elements of HIP-HOP culture (the others being rap, streetball, and breakdancing), graffiti was introduced to China the latest, entering with the HIP-HOP craze ten years ago. Despite a significant gap compared to the development of graffiti culture in Europe and America, the current development path of domestic graffiti is still relatively healthy, without gang relations. Most people doing graffiti domestically are art academy students and designers, so it can also be considered a new form of art.

Seven and his team members mostly became obsessed with graffiti during college, but back then there weren't many graffiti enthusiasts in China (even now, the total number of graffiti enthusiasts in China does not exceed a hundred). Many people grew by imitating foreign graffiti works bit by bit. Seven said that currently in China, it is still difficult to make a living through graffiti; either you go the commercial route or choose to stay underground. The advantage of the former is that you can earn some income through graffiti, but the prerequisite is that you must put aside your own creative philosophy; the latter allows you to stick to your own philosophy, but ultimately you cannot support yourself through graffiti alone.
