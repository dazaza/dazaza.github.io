Apple Responds to iPad Case Electric Leakage: Normal Phenomenon

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 11:06:57

Recently, there have been media reports that the iPad mini experiences severe electrical leakage while charging, with a voltage tester revealing a body voltage as high as 150 volts. This has caught the attention of many "Apple fans." However, this is not an isolated incident. Since the beginning of this year, Apple's iPhones have repeatedly been reported to leak electricity during charging. The most heartbreaking case was when a flight attendant from China Southern Airlines died from electric shock. In response to multiple incidents of electrical leakage, Apple has never directly responded, instead opting for a "low-cost replacement power adapter" campaign to "save face."

Apple products are embroiled in an "electrical leakage" scandal.

Reports indicate that a user from Liaoning Province, Mr. Wang, discovered that his iPad mini had an electrical leakage issue. Recalling the incident, Mr. Wang said, "It was raining outside, and I returned home after work with wet shoes. Seeing that my iPad mini was almost fully charged, I wanted to unplug it, but immediately felt a tingling sensation in my hand." At the time, Mr. Wang used a multimeter at home to test the voltage of the iPad mini's body, which astonishingly reached 150 volts. The iPad mini he used was from Hong Kong, and he consistently used the original charger. Such a high voltage undoubtedly poses a significant hazard to daily safety.

After this incident was exposed online, it sparked heated discussions among netizens, with many expressing surprise. Relevant technical experts explained that the situation described by Mr. Wang, where "a part of the device has particularly high voltage," could very likely be due to "water ingress at the tail plug, causing electrical leakage." "The position with high voltage is exactly at the bottom of the machine near the charger, possibly due to dampness or water ingress, leading to small parts overheating during charging." He believed that internal short circuits in the machine components were not commonly seen.

Subsequently, China News Service found through a Baidu search using the keyword "Apple electrical leakage" that many netizens have experienced similar situations to Mr. Wang, feeling a tingling sensation upon touching their devices while charging. These products include Macs, iPhones, and iPads. A netizen named "Kismet" complained on a forum: "My Mac Pro13, bought just over a month ago, leaks electricity every time it charges, and I feel a tingling sensation when I touch it." Another netizen, "Yumao V," mentioned that while watching a movie on her iPad with her iPhone charger, she felt a tingling sensation when she touched the back of the device, initially thinking it was an illusion, but confirming the same sensation upon re-touching it. Netizen "Suiyu Er'an 66697" stated that upon testing his Apple phone with a voltage tester while charging, he found electrical leakage. Testing several relatives' phones revealed the same issue. Many netizens attributed this phenomenon to the conductive properties of the metal body of Apple products, with few believing it to be a quality issue causing the leakage.

After-sales service claims shell voltage is normal; experts refute this as dodging responsibility.

In fact, the realization of possible electrical leakage issues became more prominent due to the widely publicized incident of a China Southern Airlines flight attendant who died from electric shock while charging her iPhone. Following this, Mr. Wu from Northeast China also suffered an electric shock while charging his iPhone 4. Regarding Mr. Wang's experience, China News Service called Apple's after-sales service hotline, where the representative claimed that such occurrences were rare. He explained that a leakage of 150 volts should involve alternating current, whereas computers, phones, and iPads use direct current, typically controlled within 35 volts. The motherboard voltage on iPads is generally around 11-12 volts, which does not pose a threat to humans. An electrical expert introduced that usually, a direct current voltage exceeding 36V passing through the human body can cause harm, and an output voltage below 5V would not harm people. Is there still some potential safety risk with 11-12 volts on the iPad?

Apple repair personnel further stated that since most Apple products have aluminum alloy shells, it is inevitable for users to feel a tingling sensation while charging. Typically, during charging, there will be some shell voltage, and a slight numbness is considered normal and not indicative of electrical leakage, staying within safe voltage limits. If the voltage is too high, the device usually cannot function properly. Additionally, static electricity on the human body is higher in autumn and winter, so it is suggested to touch metal objects first before handling charging devices to neutralize the static and avoid shocks. Apple repair personnel repeatedly emphasized that the tingling sensation is normal.

Regarding the frequent recent cases of fatal electrical leakage involving Apple iPhones, Apple officially pointed fingers at counterfeit chargers. Telecommunications expert Xiang Ligang indicated that besides product quality, the non-replaceable battery design, represented by Apple phones, might also carry safety hazards. He believes that replaceable batteries are often encapsulated in metal, offering higher safety, whereas Apple phone batteries are non-replaceable. Previously, he replaced an Apple battery and found it lacked a metal casing, only covered by a plastic-like material, and the battery had already swollen. Comparatively, if these non-replaceable batteries develop quality issues, the danger is greater.

Additionally, China News Service interviewed Hong Shibin, the executive president of the Marketing Committee of the China Home Appliance Commercial Association. Hong believed that Apple's several responses were aimed at evading responsibility. He stated that when users feel a tingling sensation, it indicates a high level of electrical charge, and the numerous netizen reports suggest it is not an isolated occurrence. Apple's responses are merely excuses to avoid addressing inherent product issues. The fundamental solution lies in effectively and reasonably employing technology to prevent problems from occurring. Hong Shibin also expressed that if Apple had chosen to use some insulating materials during the initial design phase, it would not have been overly difficult. He concluded that Apple should focus on solving technical issues rather than arguing with consumers.
