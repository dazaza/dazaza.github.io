Xiaomi 3 After-sales Investigation: Heating is Normal, No Warranty on Double 11

by anonymous on 2013-11-20 11:54:47

“Actually, our own Mi 3 phone has this issue too; it gets hot after a while of use.” This was a direct complaint voiced by a Xiaomi customer service representative in front of me. He also revealed that: “Mi 1 and Mi 2 are the same.”

When we arrived at the Xiaomi customer service center located in the western area of Jianwai SOHO, there weren't many customers waiting for repairs. However, during the 30-minute wait for inspection, several users came to report issues. The problems with the Mi 3 were mainly concentrated on: body overheating, screen yellowing, unclear photography, and no signal during standby. On a repair feedback registration form, we also saw that a certain number of Mi 3 phones indeed had these issues, though the descriptions of the fault problems on the form were all relatively simple. So, has the Mi 3, which has been on the market for only two months with sales under one million units, really encountered quality issues?

Continuous use for an hour, temperature rises by 20 degrees

We have a first-batch 16GB TD version of the Xiaomi Mi 3 phone, equipped with the Tegra 4 chip, which is claimed to be the "fastest Xiaomi phone in history." However, the cost is its immense heat generation, so we conducted tests on it.

In normal standby conditions, the temperature of the Mi 3 phone is around 30°C. Next, we played "Need for Speed" continuously without charging. After about 17 minutes, the temperature exceeded 40°C, and after one hour, it exceeded 50°C, making the phone obviously hot to the touch. The main heating area was concentrated at the top earpiece of the phone, and if you used it to make a call at this time, it would definitely cause extreme discomfort.

At the same time, power consumption was also very noticeable. After one hour of gaming with the screen brightness at 50% and fully charged, only 71% of the battery remained. Thus, more than three hours of gaming would be enough to deplete the Mi 3 phone's battery, clearly showing its high power consumption.

User feedback is widespread, overheating has become a keyword

In the official Xiaomi forum, posts reflecting this issue are everywhere. Netizen "Bingmomomo" commented: "I tried playing games continuously for an hour and found that the heat generated is not just ordinary high, and when the device runs at high temperature, the screen appears more yellowish than usual. Could the overheating affect the screen?"

Netizen "Mingjun123" also reported: "It heats up after less than an hour of play." Netizen "Yingfeitini" said: "The heat is extremely severe; it gets hot even with minimal use."

Meanwhile, entering keywords such as "Xiaomi 3 overheating" or "Xiaomi 3 serious overheating" on Baidu yields over 4 million results, most of which are reviews and trial results, indicating that this has become a relatively common phenomenon.

After-sales detection shows no problem, leaving customers helpless

Regarding this issue, we went to Xiaomi's after-sales repair center to test the product in our possession, but the result was completely normal. According to the staff, the detection method was checking for any cold solder joints or leakage on the motherboard, as well as testing the battery voltage. In these "hard indicators," our Mi 3 showed no problems, but in actual usage experience, the excessive heat was clearly uncomfortable, and this situation would be considered unacceptable by any user.

Seeing us question the detection results, a staff member explained: "Actually, our own Mi 3 phones have this issue too; they get hot after a while of use."

At the after-sales repair shop, many customers coming to repair their phones also reflected to us that there was indeed an abnormal overheating problem, especially during long calls, charging, gaming, or internet browsing.

Is the processor the main culprit?

For the abnormal overheating issue of the Mi 3, many netizens believe it is due to the adoption of NVIDIA's Tegra 4 processor. Comparing it with the ZTE U988S phone released at the same time (which also uses the Tegra 4 processor), both phones indeed generate significant heat. When running the same game or performing downloads of the same duration, the heat generated increases noticeably, but the overheating of the Mi 3 has reached an intolerable level. This indicates that besides the inherent reasons of the chip itself, Xiaomi has not optimized design or properly handled power consumption control and heat dissipation, thus we believe this issue deserves the attention of the manufacturer.

Noisy night shots, netizens collectively criticize

At the same time, we also discovered numerous user complaints in the forum stating that the photo quality of the Mi 3 is "like trash," "even worse than Mi 2," "completely unworthy of 13-megapixel quality." Regarding this issue, we also conducted corresponding comparative tests.

Firstly, in situations with sufficient light, it can be seen that the image quality of the Mi 3, with the same 13-megapixel resolution, is medium to upper-class. Aside from some slight distortion at the edges, compared to sample photos taken by other phones, there aren't many deficiencies. At the same time, the focusing speed during shooting is fast, and even burst shooting is decisive, which is undeniable.

However, when taking photos at night, the image quality of the Mi 3 deteriorates significantly, especially with a large amount of noise in the dark areas, producing unpleasant red and green noise points that cover the entire frame. Meanwhile, the algorithms and optimizations within the Mi 3 are insufficient, with heavy smoothing marks that have already affected clarity. We believe that it is precisely the poor performance in low-light environments that has caused dissatisfaction among many users. But regarding this widespread reflection, the only suggestion provided on Xiaomi's official forum is: upgrade to the latest system version, or wait for the next system update.

Software upgrades do not solve the problem

Subsequently, we took this phone to Xiaomi's after-sales repair center. After inspection, the staff informed us that there was no issue with the shooting aspect. Upon further inquiry, we learned that what the staff could ensure was merely that "the camera can function normally." As for the poor imaging quality we encountered during testing, as well as the slow focusing speed many netizens have reported, the staff believed that "this does not fall under quality issues and cannot be detected."

We believe that the widely reflected issue of poor photo quality cannot simply be resolved by upgrading software, especially since the current performance falls far short of what was demonstrated during the product launch. From a component perspective, although the camera module works fine, this does not mean that its performance is satisfactory. Therefore, we believe that the current detection methods cannot meet the needs of users and are unable to truly identify the underlying problems.

"Double 11" promotional phones not covered by warranty?

While waiting for the phone inspection, we also encountered other issues related to Xiaomi's after-sales service.

A Ms. Zhang, who managed to grab a Mi 2S phone during the "Double 11" event, came to have her phone checked due to a sudden loss of signal. However, the staff informed her that phones purchased during the "Double 11" event could "only be inspected, not repaired." With a look of astonishment, Ms. Zhang was told that for repairs, she would need to contact Tmall Mall, as "we don't handle it here."

Ms. Zhang was puzzled and even angry. Why couldn't she enjoy the proper after-sales guarantee despite purchasing the phone through legitimate channels with complete procedures? Was it solely because she bought a promotional product during "Double 11"? "But this is still a Xiaomi phone!" Ms. Zhang said helplessly, adding that the phone didn't actually save her much money during "Double 11" and instead caused her trouble.

For users, encountering quality issues with a newly purchased phone is already frustrating, and being turned away by after-sales service citing the "Double 11" event as an unfounded reason is even more disheartening.

Postscript

For Xiaomi Corporation, adopting "hunger marketing" for all products is not inherently blameworthy. Even under circumstances where the Mi 3 was launched and the Redmi was selling hotly, the Mi 2S still followed the official policy of limited-time, limited-quantity grabs, a strategy we find understandable despite grumbles from "Mi Fans," as it pertains to the company's sales tactics.

However, after rapid development and accumulating a certain reputation, we hope Xiaomi can strictly monitor product quality, design, and assembly processes. Please do not blindly pursue the "high-end configuration" in terms of specifications. While emphasizing words like fastest, strongest, first, and feverish, please also keep product quality and after-sales care in mind to not betray user trust.

From this perspective, it might be time for Xiaomi to slow down its rapid development pace.
