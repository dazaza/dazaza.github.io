Wang Xiaochuan: Baidu's acquisition of 91 Wireless aims to build a "second-stage rocket" for wireless search.

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 12:06:26

Editor's Note: This article is excerpted from a post by Wang Xiaochuan, CEO of Sogou, on Zhihu analyzing Baidu's $1.9 billion acquisition of 91 Wireless. It is published with authorization from Zhihu (click here to view the original). Wang Xiaochuan believes that the greatest significance of Baidu's acquisition of 91 Wireless lies in building a "second-stage rocket" for wireless search. Strategically speaking, this direction is sound.

Baidu got the direction right with this acquisition. Baidu's biggest challenge now is whether its wireless strategy will succeed. If it does, Baidu will return to being a $100 billion company; if it fails, Baidu will become another Yahoo! Therefore, if an acquisition can influence the probability of success for Baidu's wireless strategy, whether it costs 2% or 10% of Baidu's current market value is more of a philosophical question rather than a technical issue.

To analyze the value of 91 within the context of Baidu's wireless strategy, we first need to answer what "wireless search" is. I mentioned earlier that "wireless search is a big pit," which has two meanings:

1. The product form of wireless search is not equivalent to web search. Is it map search, which Baidu is pushing hard on? Is it voice assistants (like Siri’s voice Q&A, which both Baidu and Sogou are working on)? Or is it vertical services like Taobao and Dianping? Or general platform products like WeChat and Sogou Number Pass? The answers will emerge amidst a hundred flowers blooming, but sticking only to web search and map search would be falling into the bottom of the pit.

2. Search always lags behind other business models within the ecosystem. If other services aren't mature or profitable, search won't be either. Baidu went public in 2005, five years later than some other internet companies that went public in 2010. This isn't just about product and technology breakthroughs, but also about recognizing the inevitable trends in industry development.

To put it more plainly, the meaning of "wireless search being a big pit" is that both the product form and monetization capabilities of wireless search are still in their early stages. Under the pressure of capital markets and corporate expansion inertia, rushing things means killing them. Baidu can't wait for wireless search to mature, and at the same time, it faces fierce competition from Sogou and 360 in PC search, naturally creating immense pressure. In such a situation, unable to directly continue the glory of PC search, how can Baidu lay out plans for various possibilities of future wireless search while also having traffic distribution capabilities and a revenue model?

The answer is "mobile assistant" type products. Here's a meaningful comparison: mobile assistants are equivalent to PC browsers; the mobile market is akin to a URL navigation site. With this acquisition, Baidu is building a "second-stage rocket" for wireless search, and this is the value of 91.

Sogou and 360 will both continue to build "third-stage rockets" in the wireless field. 360 has taken the lead, and Sogou will catch up in the second half of this year (spoiler alert...). Currently, the user base of 360 Mobile Guard and Sogou Mobile Input Method rank second and third respectively among wireless products (the first place is held by Tencent's QQ + WeChat).
