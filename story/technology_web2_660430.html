Guoping Tan's Training - SEO Experience Sharing - Light Year Forum - Doing Scientific SEO & PPC

by kelawwn9 on 2012-02-07 18:40:30

It has been a while since I attended Guoping's training, and I'm just now getting around to publishing this article. Due to individual differences in SEO foundations and primary research directions, there are inevitably many subjective factors at play in what we observe. So I'll try my best to write these thoughts as objectively as possible.

Training Duration: Based on my understanding, many people worry that the training is too short and doesn't cover enough material. Let’s take a look at the announcement for the next round of training:

You can see that the time has been extended compared to before (as stated in the announcement), with a total of 18 hours over three days. Meanwhile, many other SEO trainings that last two or three months may only add up to 4-5 hours, and it’s unlikely they would exceed 10 hours.

And because Guoping is quite verbose, the actual training time should be even longer. I've tried asking some questions during class that were supposed to be answered in half a minute (yes or no questions), but he often elaborates for about ten minutes. While this might seem excessive, I have personally benefited from the additional information he provides.

On the third day, the focus was mainly on methods to immediately increase traffic. There was a lot of content, and I foolishly went to work after the morning session, expecting to implement what I learned in the afternoon. As a result, I ended up buying instant noodles at a convenience store at 1 PM and didn’t get home until 6:20 PM, which was later than usual.

Training Cost: (The price for the next training has already increased to 6180 yuan and may go up further.)

Is the time required to earn 6180 yuan < the time saved by attending the training for 6180 yuan? If a rough estimate gives a "yes" answer, then the training is worth it, and you don't need to worry about other irrelevant factors.

For example, if you're in a small company where the network administrator is tasked with handling SEO occasionally when they remember, then this training is probably not worth it. There's a 50% chance your boss will be happy and give you an extra 500 yuan per month, meaning it would take about two years to break even, which clearly isn't cost-effective.

However, if you're doing SEO for a medium to large B2B company, why would anyone hesitate? For 6180 yuan, even a single-digit percentage increase in traffic is easy to recoup. In fact, many people know that a senior SEOer from a large B2B company attended the training, and their website's traffic growth in Google Ad Planner showed an approximate 100% increase over several months.

Training Difficulty: Do you understand what keywords are and what long-tail keywords are? If so, there shouldn't be any issues with understanding the training, but that doesn't mean everything taught will be useful.

A lot of things require technical implementation. Even something relatively simple like recording data—daily monitoring dozens of websites (ideally competitors) across multiple dimensions—would be incredibly time-consuming if done manually. It could take an entire day just to record one day's worth of data... Other methods naturally become even more complex. Even if you manage to create a basic prototype, the level of refinement directly affects traffic.

I recommend attending the training if you meet any of the following conditions:

1. Have an excellent note-taking habit and aim to record the content as completely as possible. Once your technical skills catch up, you can gradually implement what you've learned.

2. Have a basic understanding of technology (in my opinion, regardless of programming language, at least understand 'if' statements and know what programs can do; at least know HTML; and have some experience managing virtual hosting for servers and databases). After the training, you’ll have a general idea of how to proceed, and if your company has dedicated technical staff, you can communicate with them to complete specific tasks.

3. Be familiar with certain technologies, primarily the programming language used by your website, and have the ability to independently implement solutions. Ideally, also possess some knowledge of graphical user interface development.

After the Training: After saying all of this objectively, I'd still like to share a bit of my subjective opinion. There are some aspects of the training that I didn't particularly enjoy. Some overarching concepts felt overly repetitive, such as the formula “traffic = inclusion * ranking * click-through rate.” These should be clear if summarized frequently. Additionally, some SEO methods I had seen before, and the cases I observed personally were even more numerous than those presented in the training.

So initially, I wasn't entirely satisfied with the training, but especially during the third session, I deeply appreciated Guoping's extensive experience. Similar ideas and methods were discussed, but the details he covered were things I hadn't considered. And because Guoping is so talkative, he mentions countless details. Moreover, it's evident that many topics couldn't be further refined due to time constraints, so if you encounter problems during actual operations, asking him directly would likely yield significant help. During the training, Guoping mentioned multiple times (again, talkative...) his hope that someone could exchange ideas rather than dwell on meaningless things like PR.

As previously mentioned, Guoping has a wealth of experience, nearly 10 years of SEO expertise. This is something even beginners like myself can appreciate—comprehensive capabilities cannot be achieved overnight and always require long-term accumulation. Therefore, hoping to become an expert through a single training session is completely unrealistic.

I once attended a training session with Fuyi, and most people praised it highly. The result was that so-called SEOers simply updated articles daily and posted external links. This kind of work can be easily learned by anyone who knows computers in just half a day. But many people stop progressing after attending one training session.

Similarly, Guoping is far superior to Fuyi, without question. However, if you're limited, it’s still the same thing—any programmer could listen for a dozen hours and achieve better results than many SEOers with poor foundations listening for the same amount of time. Thus, this training is a starting point, not the end. How do you form your own "expertise"? Don’t let anything limit you.

Finally, Guoping mentioned EDM (Email Direct Marketing) in a forum group (it’s worth noting that the quality of the VIP group [for participants] and the forum group [for professionals] can't be compared...). He said that although anti-spam measures for emails are strict and difficult to bypass, studying them from a technical perspective makes them manageable challenges. Some people might hear that EDM works well and blindly attempt it, only to fail against anti-spam measures. However, a small group of people will calmly analyze the situation, and ultimately succeed. When applied to the SEO field, it’s no different.
