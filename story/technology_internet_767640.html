Microsoft has released a Remote Desktop remote control app, but there is no Windows Phone version yet.

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 11:50:13

Launched alongside Windows 8.1, Microsoft's Remote Desktop app enables users to control their home PCs from other devices. Strangely, Microsoft released versions of the app for Android, iOS, and even Mac, but there was no sign of a version for their own platform, Windows Phone (which sounds somewhat baffling!). Today, Microsoft revealed that the Windows Phone version of the Remote app is under development, though the exact release date remains unknown.

As the name suggests, this app serves as a "remote control" for business professionals to remotely manage their home PCs. Users can easily open applications or files on their home computers through the app, with video and audio synchronized to display on mobile devices. As long as there is a PC at home running the Windows system, users can use smartphones, tablets, or even another Mac to view and launch desktop applications on the PC.

Placing a PC desktop on a smartphone screen inevitably results in a less-than-ideal experience due to screen size limitations. What Microsoft is really targeting are tablet devices. The Microsoft Remote Desktop app is compatible with iPads and various Android tablets, featuring a virtual keyboard and function buttons within its interface. Regarding the decision to "overlook" their own Windows Phone platform, Microsoft explained that "the use case for controlling a personal computer via a phone is still niche."

Indeed, third-party apps of a similar nature have existed for quite some time, and remote control apps are already available for Windows tablets. The absence of a Windows Phone version for this app doesn't seem like a major issue. However, Microsoft's reasoning appears contradictory— if it's a "niche" market, why invest resources into making Remote Desktop perfectly compatible with iPads and Android tablets (and even offer a Mac version), rather than releasing a Windows Phone version simultaneously? From both the perspective of platform promotion and practicality (at least covering WP users), Microsoft's conflicting approach leaves people scratching their heads.
