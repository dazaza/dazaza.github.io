May stock market offers hundreds of excellent combinations - seizing the good opportunity is the key.

Recently, some domestic and international macroeconomic data have shown signs of recovery. The economy seems to be showing "bright spots," but these "bright spots" mostly stem from demand brought by "inventory replenishment." From December 2008 to February 2009, there was a continuous 12-week rise in steel prices domestically, signaling a warming up of the domestic steel industry. However, from March onwards, due to the rapid recovery of production capacity while downstream demand remains weak, domestic steel companies have been forced to continuously lower steel prices, leading the steel industry into a new round of "de-stocking."

Experts from the National Development and Reform Commission (NDRC) recently wrote an article stating: 1) In the first quarter, the scale of newly added credit reached as high as 4.58 trillion yuan, and the extremely loose monetary conditions will prevent the overall economic decline; 2) GDP growth in the second and third quarters will continue to recover, but this is due to the counter-cyclical effects of policy rather than genuine market bottoming out; 3) This adjustment in China's economic growth has been substantial, but the adjustment period has not been long enough, so it is far from over; 4) Currently, China's economy is at the intersection of three adjustment cycles: a 30-year long cycle, a 10-year medium cycle, and a 3-5 year short cycle, with adjustment pressures concentrated in recent years; 5) This round of growth adjustment is a mid-term adjustment, and adjustments lasting only half a year are insufficient to solve various problems facing China's economy.

It is worth noting that, under the worsening employment situation and declining income growth, the growth of private consumer demand within domestic demand will continue to slow down. In the first two months of 2009, the total retail sales of consumer goods increased by 15.2% year-on-year, which is a decrease of 5.4 percentage points compared to the average monthly increase in the fourth quarter of 2008. Among them, urban consumption growth in the first two months was only 14.1%, a drop of 6.4 percentage points from the fourth quarter of 2008. The main reason is the significant rise in urban unemployment rates and the clear decline in income growth expectations, which will cause a sustained decline in consumption growth throughout 2009.

Historical data from public mutual funds shows that once the average position of equity-oriented funds approaches historical highs or sets new historical highs, the A-share market will soon enter a phase of adjustment. Once the average position of equity-oriented funds approaches historical lows, the A-share market will soon see a phase of rally. Fund reports for the first quarter show that the average position of equity-oriented funds is around 76%, with a noticeable increase. On April 17, the dynamic position measurement of equity funds approached 80%, and even individual stock funds had positions as high as 99%. Meanwhile, although the number of companies held by QFII did not differ much, the number of shares held decreased significantly on a quarterly basis, dropping by 17.15%. If historical patterns remain effective, even if the A-share market does not experience a major adjustment in May, it will still be the month with the largest volatility in recent months.

From Figure 1, it is easy to see that since the 1664-point level, the Shanghai Composite Index has been running in an upward channel, with the line connecting 1664 and 1814 as the lower boundary and the line connecting 2402 and 2549 as the upper boundary. On April 22, after breaking through the upper boundary and setting a new rebound high at 2579 points, the Shanghai Composite Index was pulled back into the ascending channel. As long as it does not break below the lower boundary of the ascending channel formed by 1664 and 1814, the medium-term rebound starting from 1664 has not ended. However, the current distance between the Shanghai Composite Index and the upper boundary of the ascending channel is too close. If the A-share market does not undergo a certain degree of adjustment in the short term, even if the Shanghai Composite Index sets a new rebound high in May, the upward space for the A-share market will be relatively limited.

In 2008, the net profit attributable to the parent company of all A-shares dropped by 16.88% compared to 2007, with a price-to-earnings ratio based on 2008 performance exceeding 24 times. In the first quarter report of 2009, the net profit attributable to the parent company of all A-shares fell by 25.15% year-on-year. Excluding banking stocks, the remaining A-shares saw their net profits attributable to the parent company fall by 38.02% year-on-year. I estimate that the net profit attributable to the parent company of all A-shares in 2009 will drop by about 10% compared to 2008, with a price-to-earnings ratio based on expected 2009 performance around 26.4 times. The valuation of the A-share market has already fully reflected, or even over-discounted, the national stimulus economic policies and the expectations of improved corporate performance.

Currently, many investors are concerned whether the scale of new credit in the second quarter will significantly decrease. I believe that the new credit scale in the second quarter will be less than in the first quarter, but the decrease will not be too large. Mainly because from July to August, China will face the peak period for college graduate employment, plus the unemployed migrant workers, the government will prioritize solving the "employment" pressure.

Under such circumstances, continuously injecting liquidity into the real economy is one of the means. Although the marginal effect will continue to decline, constant injection will always play a certain role. I believe that as long as the trend of recovery in China's economy is "unstable," the scale of new credit will not experience a sharp decline. Once the recovery trend of China's economy becomes stable, the government will then shift its focus to "addressing potential inflationary pressures." I predict that after the "May Day" holiday, new stimulus economic policies will be introduced, especially policies aimed at stimulating domestic consumption.

Additionally, the U.S. economy, which has begun to show signs of recovery, is now facing a major threat from the swine flu epidemic. If the contraction of the U.S. economy turns out to be slightly more severe than currently expected, it will have a negative impact on the recovery of China's economy.
