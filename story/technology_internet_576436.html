"Is Twitter a social network?" sparks debate

by diglog on 2011-11-21 21:50:13

American venture capital expert Bill Gurley recently published an article on his blog titled "You Don't Have To Tweet To Twitter." The main point of the article is that "to use Twitter, you don't necessarily have to tweet." Bill Gurley uses Facebook as a comparison to explain his concept of a "social network": Facebook is a many-to-many communication network used for sharing information and personal updates with friends. The emphasis is on interaction: you add me as a friend, I add you as a friend, and then we share information with each other.

Twitter is a one-to-many broadcast network. It's not about getting everyone to "tweet," but rather about getting everyone to follow. I don't need the people I follow to follow me back, and I can even access the information I want on Twitter without registering. Just like I don't need Shaquille O'Neal to add me as a friend to access the information he shares and learn about his updates.

On Facebook, the audience for your shared information is limited to your friends, which makes it symmetrical. Twitter has a high degree of asymmetry; the audience for your tweets includes all those who follow you—not just those who mutually follow you.

Twitter is an "information tool," a "discovery engine," and an "enhanced RSS." It is a platform for media and celebrities to release information, and for most people, it is primarily an important source for obtaining information. Many people only consume information and never tweet.

Therefore, he believes that Twitter is not a true social network. Its competitors should be other "information tools" or "discovery engines," not Facebook. Undoubtedly, Facebook is the first platform for sharing information with friends, and its competitors should be email, instant messaging tools, and other symmetric social networks like MySpace.

The conclusion of this article—"Twitter and Facebook do not belong to the same category of social sites"—has sparked widespread discussion. In the eyes of the mass media, Twitter and Facebook have always been regarded as leaders in the social networking field. At this moment, someone suddenly comes out and says, "Actually, Twitter is not a social network." Your reaction might be, "What? Are you kidding?"

The debate triggered

After reading this blog post, TechCrunch editor Robin Wauters had a similar reaction, so he wrote an article titled "Twitter, There Is Nothing Wrong With Being A Social Network" as a response, meaning "Twitter is undoubtedly a social network."
