A positive growth for the import and export of 1nJk is just a matter of time.

by bkabc011 on 2012-03-04 14:28:27

Yesterday, the central bank announced that in September RMB loans increased by 516.7 billion yuan. The media thought this figure was somewhat unexpected, because beforehand, the industry had generally expected that new credit in September might continue to decline. Foreign media had quoted Chairman of China Banking Regulatory Commission Liu Mingkang as saying that new loans in September would only be between 300 and 400 billion yuan. In fact, this so-called unexpected result is based on predictions made by some industry insiders and institutions. If we look at it from a normal logical perspective, the 516.7 billion yuan in September exceeds the 355.9 billion yuan in July and the 410.4 billion yuan in August, which is entirely normal. Every September is a quarterly settlement point, and it often surpasses the amount of new loans in July and August, so the 516.7 billion yuan in September exceeding the 410.4 billion yuan in August is not unexpected.

Thus far, the total new RMB loans in the first three quarters amounted to 8.67 trillion yuan, an increase of 5.19 trillion yuan compared to the same period last year. Of course, related data shows that by the end of September, the balance of broad money supply (M2) was 58.54 trillion yuan, up 29.31% year-on-year, 11.49 percentage points higher than at the end of last year, and 0.78 percentage points higher than at the end of last month; the balance of narrow money supply (M1) was 20.17 trillion yuan, up 29.51% year-on-year, 20.45 percentage points higher than at the end of last year, and 1.79 percentage points higher than at the end of last month; the balance of market currency circulation (M0) was 3.68 trillion yuan, up 15.96% year-on-year.

The growth rate of these indicators continues to reach new highs. If we still describe the 29.31% increase in M2 as moderately loose and define the possibility of new loans reaching 10 trillion yuan for the whole year, which is almost three times the growth compared to last year, then that would be typical self-deception. Now, we are completely using an extremely loose monetary policy. But everything has its limits. I have consistently believed recently that China has entered the countdown to exiting this extremely loose monetary policy.

This judgment is supported by countless data. The improvement in import and export data is one of them. Yesterday, customs statistics showed that in September alone, China's total foreign trade import and export value was 218.94 billion US dollars, down only 10.1% year-on-year, which is a significant change compared to the cumulative drop of 20.9% from January to September, with a monthly increase of 14.2%. Both imports and exports exceeded 10 billion US dollars. Among them, the export value was 115.93 billion US dollars, down 15.2% year-on-year but up 11.8% month-on-month; imports were 103.01 billion US dollars, down 3.5% year-on-year but up 17% month-on-month. I checked the monthly import and export data for 2008 and found that the September data in 2008, in a year that set a new record for import and export in the history of New China, was second only to July’s highest. This indirectly illustrates the quality of the September data, which only dropped by 10.1% year-on-year. If we trace back to 2007, the total import and export in September already started to grow by more than 10 percentage points, because the year-on-year growth rate of import and export in September 2008 was 21.4%. With the corresponding base being low in subsequent months, positive growth in imports and exports is just a matter of time.

Last night, Beijing time, the Dow Jones Index re-surpassed 10,000 points. This is an important indicator of global economic recovery. The key is, the various psychological implications brought about by such important indicators will accelerate economic recovery. Once the economy enters a new economic cycle and enters the real expansion stage, if the world continues to maintain this extremely loose monetary policy, it won't be stimulating the economy or ensuring growth anymore, but rather creating malignant inflation. The Dow Jones Index re-surpassing 10,000 points will inevitably accelerate China's exit from its loose monetary policy.

In fact, for China, although the GDP for the third quarter has not yet been announced, the outside world generally predicts it to be between 8.5% and 9%. Such figures are not bad even in other years. Ensuring an 8% growth for the whole year is no longer under pressure. In this situation, although the government is still emphasizing that the economic stability is not yet stable and there are many uncertainties, they will temporarily continue the current monetary policy and fiscal policy. We can fully foresee that the exit from loose policies is just a matter of time.

Actually, the Reserve Bank of Australia raised interest rates by 25 basis points on October 6, which marked the first shot of monetary policy starting to shift in the later stages of the economic crisis. Subsequent major economies will naturally adjust their monetary policies step by step according to their own national economic conditions. China is definitely included. It's just that following convention, the scale of new loans in the fourth quarter won't be very large, so the decision-making level can continue shouting slogans, allowing this formalistic monetary policy to continue. But behind the scenes, they have already started planning the timing of the exit. Moreover, due to the rapid increase in new loans in the previous few months, many joint-stock banks have already revealed issues with insufficient core capital ratios. Even if they want to add new loans, they will find it hard to sustain.

Don't be deceived by the unexpected increase in new loans in September. You must plan ahead and should not continue large-scale investment and borrowing when economic policy is about to reverse. Otherwise, the breaking of the funding chain and the appearance of half-finished projects may not be news. When the government really proposes to implement an appropriately tight monetary policy, it will be too late. We must fully recognize that in 2008, the transformation of China's economic policy from tightening to extreme looseness took only a few short months.
