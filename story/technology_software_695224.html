Lu Hemin, the Movie Collector: A Lifelong Devotion to Cinema

If you were to see an office, garage, hallway, or meeting room filled from floor to ceiling with over 1700 movies and tens of thousands of film reels, where would you guess this place might be? If someone owned six movie projectors and gave over 500 free screenings in a year for their county's residents, who would you guess they might be? Without knowing the details, no one would expect that this place, with a collection surpassing even provincial and municipal film companies, is the office building of Lu Hemin, the General Manager of Heming Co., Ltd. in Wengan County, Hebei Province. All these film reels are part of his personal collection.

Is collecting old movie reels similar to collecting antiques and paintings, both elegant and appreciating in value, a path to wealth? Many people think so, but it's not quite like that. The essential difference is this: the film itself has no intrinsic value; its value lies in the movie, which only comes alive when projected for people to enjoy. However, each screening causes wear and tear on the film. A 35mm copy can only be shown 800 times, while a 16mm copy can only handle 400 screenings. Therefore, Lu Hemin says he never thought about preservation or appreciation when purchasing and collecting old movie reels. His sole purpose is to allow more people to appreciate the artistic beauty of classic films and experience the era captured within them. For the older generation, it’s a chance to reminisce, and for the younger generation, it’s an opportunity to understand the development journey of Chinese cinema.

Since his collection of old movie reels is the largest in the nation, Lu Hemin has become close friends with CCTV host Cui Yongyuan. Cui treats this place as his personal archive and often spends days watching old films here whenever he has time. He told Lu Hemin, "You have more old films than I do. It's great to have such a passionate friend." In November last year, CCTV's Movie Channel aired a documentary titled "Lu Hemin - The Three-Faceted Rural Film Projectionist." What are Lu Hemin's three identities? First, he is an entrepreneur whose company pays millions in taxes annually; second, he is the Party Secretary of Gaocun Village in Daliuhe Town; third, he is a volunteer film projectionist.

How does Lu Hemin balance these "three-in-one" roles? People know little about action movies, but they know about the enterprise he founded. Beyond following national regulations to pay all required taxes and fees on time, his company also selflessly solves practical problems for the local community. He once invested 150,000 yuan to pave a cement road for the village and another 100,000 yuan to build a water tower, completely resolving the villagers' drinking water issues. Lonely elderly people and impoverished families in the village have also received much assistance from him. When he noticed that the local nursing home and schools were in disrepair, he contributed over 400,000 yuan to help renovate and reinforce them... People also know that despite being busy and tired, he still doesn't neglect his love for and collection of old films, nor does he delay showing them to others. Now, Lu Hemin's collection of old films has become the best content for hosting guests at both the county and township levels, and he enjoys providing this service voluntarily. He says, "The more people who share in the art of old films, the happier I am. Although each screening wears down the film, I'm willing to let people watch."

From Lu Hemin's sincere smile and demeanor, it's easy to feel that he speaks from the heart. Through interviews, we learned that this love, which sprouted in childhood, has accompanied him through life...

A Well-Known Little Movie Fan

At the age of 46, Lu Hemin was born in a poor village in Wengan County, Hebei Province. Back then, if there was a good harvest, the whole village could go a few fewer days hungry; if there was a drought or flood, every household had half a year of bran and vegetables for food. In such conditions, life seemed especially hard for the Lu family, with five "little bald heads" always opening their mouths and having stomachs like bottomless pits, never full, and constantly crying out for food. As the youngest, Lu Hemin's parents knew he couldn't compete with his older brothers for food, so they tried to favor the youngest. But they found that their youngest son rarely complained of hunger, especially on days when there was a movie to watch. After eating just a few bites, he would run off. "This child runs so fast without eating anything, it's like he's bewitched," his mother sighed as she watched his retreating back.

His mother was right; Lu Hemin was enchanted by movies. Whenever he heard of a village showing a movie, he forgot hunger, thirst, fatigue, and sleep. Even now, he marvels at how many movies were shown back then. Political education in rural areas was strict, and showing movies was part of that political education. Regulations stipulated that every village had to show movies, whether rich or poor. Some richer villages showed more, poorer ones less, but none could skip entirely. He remembers the township officials saying, "Whether to show more or less depends on conditions, but whether to show or not depends on awareness and position." Thus, even their poor village showed a few movies each year. Fortunately, neighboring villages and nearby farms showed more, allowing him to see several movies each month, making him feel like it was always New Year's Day.

For a young rural boy, traveling several kilometers to see a movie shows just how much it attracted him. Outdoor movies usually start after dark, whenever it gets dark, sometimes finishing after 10 PM. Many people left after waiting impatiently for an hour or two. Once, a neighboring village was showing "Heroes' Children" when the power went out at the beginning. The outage lasted for hours. "Uncle, will it continue?" "It will, but we don't know when the power will return. Go home." "I won't go home until it finishes." This small movie fan, known by all the projectionists, was asked, "It might not end until who knows what time. Won't your mom worry?" No matter how the uncles tried to persuade him, he wouldn't leave, waiting until late at night. That day, power returned at 2 AM, and the projectionist found over 20 people remaining, with only one small head held high, eyes glued to the screen.

Movies were like the sun of Lu Hemin's childhood, filling his young heart with warmth. Characters like Zhang Ga, Red Child, and Haiwa from the movies became recurring figures in his dreams. Sometimes they played with him in the village, other times he fought Japanese soldiers with them. "If I could be a projectionist, how wonderful it would be. I could make them appear on the screen whenever I wanted," he thought one morning upon waking from a dream. This idea suddenly appeared in his mind and never disappeared. From then on, Lu Hemin didn't watch movies as simply as before. Each time, he arrived earliest at the screening site, positioning himself closest to the projectionist to observe how they operated the projector. He said he envied and became increasingly fascinated with them, longing to grow up quickly and become a projectionist himself.

Through Hardships, Dreams Persist

In 1983, the sweeping tide of reform made the 20-year-old Lu Hemin restless at home. He told his family, "We're living in such a good era; we can't sit around doing nothing. I want to try my luck in Tianjin." "What can a villager do in Tianjin? It's so big, you'll be clueless and probably can't even support yourself." The elders didn't understand his thoughts, but couldn't stop him. They knew this young man looked gentle but was stubborn and bold since childhood. Let him go...

Though only over a hundred kilometers away, Tianjin was vastly different from Wengan County. Lu Hemin had been to Tianjin before, but those visits were to relatives, bringing only excitement and joy. This time was different. He aimed to strike gold in this city, but wasn't sure if he could succeed. Starting by working for others and gradually understanding the situation, he believed this city could bring him opportunities. Sure enough, after some time, he discovered that many Tianjin enterprises needed labor protection gloves, often in short supply. He told himself, "This is an opportunity." So, he carefully investigated the market and began organizing production. Not until he started did he realize why others were reluctant to do it — labor protection gloves had very low added value and minimal profit. "Still, I'll do it. Doing something is better than doing nothing. Opportunities don't fall from the sky waiting for you to catch them. Running a business is the same. High-profit products won't come looking for you. Even Li Ka-shing, the Hong Kong tycoon, started with buttons," he told himself and threw himself into the work.

How much was Lu Hemin's first month's profit? Many wouldn't believe it — only 450 yuan. But this still thrilled him immensely. He counted the money repeatedly, thinking, "No matter how much or little, it's earned by myself." After keeping the money "warm" in his pocket for two days, Lu Hemin headed to the department store's electronics section and pointed at a 12-inch black-and-white TV, saying, "Comrade, please turn it on for me to see." "What's there to see? There's no program now, just the 'Earth' clock." "I'm not interested in programs; I want to buy a TV." "Are you really buying?" The salesperson, hearing his accent, realized he wasn't from the city. Knowing rural areas often experienced power outages at night and broadcasted programs only from 7:30 PM with limited channels, he wondered if this rural youth wasn't wasting his money.

Lu Hemin felt a bit "burnt," but not by money, rather by his passion for movies. After the implementation of the land contract responsibility system in rural areas, collective activities like movie screenings ceased to exist. Without movies, he felt more uncomfortable than anything else. Luckily, a neighbor bought a television, saving him temporarily. Every day, he could go there to watch TV. To this day, Lu Hemin remains grateful. He said his fellow villagers were truly simple and kind. Back then, he would sit in their homes every day, regardless of whether they liked it or not, leaving only when "Goodbye" appeared on the screen. At that time, he thought, "Once I have money, I'll buy a TV first, no matter what. Watching movies on TV, though smaller, is better than nothing." On his way home, Lu Hemin tightly hugged the TV, feeling a sense of satisfaction for the first time in his life.

After ten years in Tianjin, by 1993, many Tianjin enterprises were restructuring, shrinking the labor protection product market. Unable to continue, Lu Hemin decided to return home and start anew. Honestly speaking, returning home took courage and confidence. As the saying goes, "A good horse doesn't eat turned grass." He had worked in Tianjin for ten years, enduring hardships without earning much. Would he become a laughingstock? But Lu Hemin seemed unfazed. He knew life isn't always smooth sailing. Though he hadn't achieved great success in Tianjin, the ten years of experience were invaluable. As long as he kept moving forward, the path would naturally open up beneath his feet.

Soon after returning home, Lu Hemin established a promising wood industry company, primarily producing medium-density panels for construction and decoration. However, this venture also faced setbacks. The company was small, with few employees and low-tech, low-value-added products, leading to consistently low economic benefits and eventually falling into difficulties. What to do? Lu Hemin sought expert advice and learned the problem lay in quality. Improving quality depended on technological innovation. Thus, he hired professional technicians at high salaries to oversee production and train workers, significantly improving the product quality, making it well-known far and wide and competitive in the domestic market.

After the company entered a stable development phase, the "dream" slumbering in Lu Hemin's heart became clearer day by day...

Undeterred Efforts to Find Reels

One day three years ago, while chatting with friends, the conversation turned to movies. One friend mentioned, "I remember something. I heard that the Wengan County cinema is about to be demolished and is worried about how to deal with its inventory of movie reels." "Really?" "Absolutely, check if you don't believe me." Hearing this, Lu Hemin immediately stood up, bid farewell hastily, and drove to the county seat.

Upon arriving at the cinema, he confirmed the news. Asking about the fate of the reels, he was told, "What use are they? No one wants them." "I do!" Lu Hemin blurted out, surprising the staff, who eyed him suspiciously, unsure of his intentions. The shrewd Lu Hemin chuckled and explained his lifelong love for movies and his special affection for cinemas. With the cinema soon to be demolished, he wanted to treat the staff to dinner. During the meal, Lu Hemin passionately shared his love for movies, moved the staff, who offered to give him all the reels, calling it "the best handling method." That night, Lu Hemin excitedly examined these reels, carefully wiping rust off the reel boxes, and the next day had them repainted.

These were Lu Hemin's first genuine movie reels, including classics like "Tunnel Warfare," "Mine Warfare," "South and North Battles," and "Railway Guerrillas." From then on, his enthusiasm grew uncontrollably. Chatting with friends, doing business elsewhere, or exchanging with clients, he instinctively linked it to collecting movies, such as information on which cinemas were selling reels or who had certain films. In short, he sought more information to quickly realize his "movie dream."

What is "obsession"? Lu Hemin's actions provide the best interpretation. Someone once said that if he heard about reels anywhere, no matter the time or what he was doing, he'd drive off immediately. Once, he received a message from Shenyang about someone wanting to sell a feature film from the 1970s. Learning this at 8 PM, he drove non-stop for eight hours to Shenyang, reaching just as the sun rose. Unexpectedly, upon meeting the seller, they refused to sell. Lu Hemin understood it was due to his haste, as the refusal was likely a price negotiation tactic. "I've come so far; just tell me your price." "Alright, seeing your sincerity, I can't bear to disappoint you. Fixed price: 40,000 yuan." Hearing this, Lu Hemin glanced at the seller, pulled out the money from his bag, and handed it over.

In 2006, someone in Chengdu, Sichuan, called asking if he wanted four editions of "Newsreel" from when Chairman Mao met the Red Guards. Lu Hemin said he'd go immediately. Knowing the reasonable price was around 8,000 yuan, but the seller demanded 20,000. Understanding these sellers weren't collectors but viewed the reels as commodities for profit, this time he played it smart, negotiating prices and hiding his true feelings, even pretending indifference. Eventually, the seller softened, asking him to name a price. Lu Hemin secretly rejoiced, settling on 10,000 yuan. Unbeknownst to Lu Hemin, a year later, the person called again, offering to buy back the set for 50,000 yuan. "No! I collect for enjoyment, not profit," Lu Hemin firmly declined without hesitation.

Lu Hemin said he was willing to spend money and endure hardship to collect more movie reels. Last year, a company in Xiangfan, Hubei, wanted to dispose of 900 reels used to settle debts with a film company. Upon hearing this, he excitedly flew there the same day, quickly reaching an agreement and paid 50,000 yuan to take them. To transport them back, he spent another 5,100 yuan hiring a vehicle to drive overnight back to Wengan. Returning home, his wife, seeing his panda-like dark circles, worriedly said, "I don't care how much you spend on collecting movie reels, but working this hard isn't healthy. What if you exhaust yourself?" "No worries, happiness makes any fatigue disappear."

There are moments of joy where fatigue disappears, but there are also times when he returns home exhausted. Every time this happens, his family knows why and tries not to upset him. Once, someone in Anshan called saying they had a "Red Guards" import reel for sale. Import reels not only have better clarity but are more durable than domestic ones. He scheduled a time and place, and as usual, left his ongoing renovation and drove to Anshan. It was freezing cold, and when he arrived at the agreed location, the seller wasn't there and didn't answer calls. Too cold to wait outside, Lu Hemin had to stay in his car for hours, but the seller never showed up, forcing him to return to Wengan. On the way back, he grew increasingly tired and, upon arriving home, collapsed onto the bed...

Selfless Projectionist

To date, Lu Hemin has invested 1.5 million yuan in collecting movie reels, covering most major domestic films. Additionally, he purchased two 35mm and three 16mm projectors along with other equipment. Moreover, he set up a dedicated screening room, equipped better than a mid-sized cinema. What does he plan to do? People initially couldn’t guess, finding it strange. "What's strange? Just to screen movies for everyone. Watching alone has no meaning," Lu Hemin smiled and explained, extending invitations. This made some people murmur more, thinking he must be obsessed. Why else would he spend so much money serving others? His brain must be malfunctioning.

Lu Hemin had no time to explain and didn't care what others thought. His only hope was that people would come to watch movies. Unexpectedly, even when offered for free, few came, leaving the screening venue mostly empty with only two or three scattered attendees, none of whom stayed till the end. He asked why, receiving polite excuses of being busy or blunt replies of boredom. Friends advised him, "Television has everything. Comedy, romance, couples embracing—your movies are too 'red'; watching them feels like attending a political class… Don't waste so much effort. Watch for yourself if you like, or sell them if you don't." "What's this talk of profit? Why would I go through all this trouble just for myself? Am I crazy?" Lu Hemin didn't believe people disliked watching movies. To test this, he conducted an "experiment" at home, inviting his twenty-something children to watch with him. Initially, they refused outright, but he paid them to "accompany" him. After a few sessions, his son and daughter reported positively, saying they hadn't expected these movies to be so interesting when watched attentively. This reassured Lu Hemin. With so many young people in his company, they would soon grow fond of them. Thus, every evening, he alternated screenings in the factory yard, personally operating the projector each time. Soon, he noticed more people coming to watch, including workers from nearby factories. Lu Hemin said, hearing laughter in front of the screen brought immense satisfaction.

Word of Lu Hemin's free movie screenings spread quickly. Not only companies invited him, but nearby towns and villages also booked screenings, treating him like a professional projectionist. With numerous requests, although happy, Lu Hemin struggled to keep up. In 2007, he organized a team of three full-time projectionists to screen movies according to reservations. For nursing homes, he arranged mainly operas favored by the elderly, such as "Flowers as Matchmakers," "Women Generals of the Yang Family," and "Dream of the Red Chamber." For schools, he chose films promoting patriotism, righteousness, and motivation, including both old classics like "Wildfire Spring Wind Fight in the Ancient City," "Heroic Sons and Daughters," "Shangganling," and "South and North Battles," and newer films like "Not One Less," "Qiu Ju Goes to Court," and "Mulan." For villages, he selected lighthearted films like "Sweet Business," "They and She," "Xu Mao and His Daughters," and "Laughter in Moon Bay."

In summary, Lu Hemin treated movie screenings as important work. He said rural cultural life can't compare to cities, where cinemas and theaters abound. In some rural areas, there aren't even cinemas, and many teenagers don't know what movies are. With his interest and ability, he's glad to contribute to his fellow villagers…

Yearly, he freely screens over 500 movies for local residents, benefiting over 100,000 people, continuing for three years. For this, Lu Hemin has become a "celebrity," honored as "Provincial Rural Movie Projection Worker" and "Advanced Individual Sending Movies to the Countryside in Hebei Province." Now in Wengan County, few know Lu Hemin as a general manager; everyone knows him as a movie projectionist.

Lu Hemin said, rural cultural life can't match the cities, where cinemas and theaters are everywhere. In our rural areas, some places don't even have cinemas, and many teenagers don't know what movies are. Having this interest and ability, he's glad to do something for his fellow villagers. (Reported by Zhou Liandi)
