South Korea Demands Radiation Levels Be Listed on Phone Boxes

by sheyingmyq on 2012-02-21 21:43:22

In South Korea, where the number of mobile phone users accounts for more than 90% of the total population, mobile phone radiation has long been not only a concern for a large number of users but also one of the technical challenges for mobile phone manufacturers. In recent years, South Korean mobile phone manufacturers have taken measures in two main areas to reduce radiation: one is to place the antenna that transmits radio waves at the bottom of the phone, avoiding an increase in human radiation absorption due to the antenna being located near the ear at the top of the phone; the second is to adjust the direction of radiation to the back of the mobile phone, minimizing its impact on the human body.

Although the conclusion given by IARC is still far from confirming that mobile phones can cause cancer and has had almost no influence on government agencies setting and revising safety standards for mobile phone radiation, David, senior scientific advisor of the EU Environment Agency's Policy and Response Department, still stated, "Since the scientific community does not have a unified view on whether mobile phones cause cancer, we recommend that people take a prevention-first approach." Based on this, the British government suggests that if adults use mobile phones, they should shorten their call time; children under the age of 16 should avoid using mobile phones for chatting as much as possible, and if they must make calls, they should shorten the duration and frequency as much as possible.

In May 2011, the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) released a report deciding to classify radiofrequency electromagnetic fields generated during mobile phone use as "possibly carcinogenic". Although this report showed potential carcinogenic risks through epidemiological survey results, the evidence to conclude that electromagnetic fields cause cancer was insufficient.

In June this year, according to the law, South Korean wireless communication equipment manufacturers and importers need to label the intensity of electromagnetic radiation and the absorption rate level on the outer packaging of products such as mobile phones and tablet computers so that consumers can directly know the true harm value of radiation. The issue of whether mobile phone radiation causes cancer has once again become a public focus, sparking heated discussions among scientists and consumers around the world.

(In our Brussels, Los Angeles, Seoul, February 20th edition, our overseas correspondents Zhang Jie, Chen Yiming, Ma Fei)

In recent years, many scientific experiments have not provided credible biological mechanisms to prove that there is a necessary connection between mobile phones and cancer. So far, dozens of studies have been conducted by scientists around the world, with the most famous being the "Walkie-Talkie" series projects mainly carried out in Europe. The conclusions of these projects show that, except for some cases under extreme exposure conditions, the results regarding whether mobile phones cause cancer are absolutely negative. Extreme conditions refer to people who have used mobile phones for over ten years, where it is only possible to observe an almost imperceptible increase in the incidence of two rare types of brain cancer - glioma and acoustic neuroma.

Since most people recognize that the level of mobile phone radiation is proportional to the distance between the mobile phone and the human body, journalists in the United States rarely see people directly holding their mobile phones to their ears for conversations, and even less frequently see people hanging their mobile phones around their necks. The use of Bluetooth headsets and wired headphones is quite common.
