Nanjing recruitment: I learned 15 things during my one-year internship at a well-known PR company

by happy2010 on 2010-04-10 16:34:42

Previously, I was an intern for 14 months in three prestigious international PR firms. During that time, I met different leaders, did various jobs, was taught by different people, and gained different insights about the workplace. Suddenly, I wanted to write about all the things I learned during my internship. Although at the time I didn't understand, even resented, cried, or felt sad, today I can see that my rapid growth is inseparable from these theories. It was the countless seniors over these 14 months who helped me quickly transition from school to the workplace, saving me a lot of time adapting to society, thus allowing me to more quickly embark on my career path.

1. Any work must be completed from start to finish. If I only ask you to do step 1, you should consider whether there are steps 2 and 3. If I don’t have time to teach you the entire process, please think more on your own. Regarding this issue, I hope you read a small story called "The Potato Buying Story."

2. If you encounter problems you cannot solve midway, you must ask me immediately. Don't wait for me to chase you down. The reason I entrust you with tasks is because I believe that a carefully selected university student can complete simple tasks like chasing a media contact to check if they received a package. If I have to ask you, please don't tell me that no one answered the phone or that they couldn’t be found. When problems arise, don't throw them back at me; find ways to solve them. The workplace is a place where results matter. You must have the ability to solve problems. When you start thinking and finding solutions to all problems, you will make progress in both thinking ability and work capability. Be a thinker, not just a worker. Have your own value. (This was the most important lesson I received before my confirmation, and it's also the key transformation from being a student to working in the workplace.)

3. You don’t need to know what your colleagues do every day, etc. Although many vocational schools now encourage you to quickly understand this, our work is not fixed. Every day, projects differ completely, such as the amount of coffee I drink depending on how busy I am, and knowing these things is useless. Whenever I receive emails from many students asking similar questions, I really hope you can go home and check if your resume flows smoothly.

4. To understand any industry, start by doing every small task well, which includes not only finishing but also considering all methods, communication, potential issues, and skills related to it. Only when you fully understand and master each small task can you move forward faster.

5. If you really have nothing to do today, then look at websites related to the business and learn from the experiences of your seniors. There’s always something deeper to learn, no matter how trivial the work seems. Do not gather with other interns chatting and laughing, as if you're having a party at a café. Do not chat online idly, post water content, or gossip. You can do those things comfortably in your dorm bed, not come all the way to the company to do so. (I used to love going to cafés.)

6. You must think about how to demonstrate your value, not just work hard. Students with internship experience can certainly notice that interns always feel their work is exhausting, while full-time employees seem more relaxed as they climb higher in rank. In fact, the struggles of upper management are beyond your imagination. Upper management knows how to manage their time and affairs better, unlike interns who often panic. What upper management shows is not relaxation, but order. (I think this is very important. It took me a year to realize this issue, and I hope you can understand it earlier.)

7. Please don’t ask me why I continue here despite the low salary, or why I don’t take exams for others or help others compile books. I would only say that growth is more important than success, and temporary wealth doesn’t mean success.

8. Please don’t express your passion for public relations and then ask me what public relations is and how it differs from advertising. Please google it yourself on Baidu. Ask deep questions if you want to ask anything. Don’t blame me if I ignore such shallow questions.

9. You can dress up flamboyantly or smile innocently, but please don’t act flirtatiously in the company.

10. Please don’t decide whether to stay based solely on the level of salary, unless the place really can't guarantee your basic needs. The first three years are for accumulation and sedimentation, not demanding returns. The deeper you go, the more you will realize you’re still a novice, inexperienced to the point where you might think you were mistakenly recruited. (I often feel this way.)

11. If you have any problems at work, whether too tired or overwhelmed with workload, you must tell your leader. Don’t bear it alone. Don’t curse the company as abnormal if you can't go home at night, when no one actually knows you can't finish your work.

12. Finish your work at the company. An intern doesn’t need to bring work back to the dormitory to show off, as if without you the company would collapse. Go back to school and read more professional courses. Spend holidays playing. Balance work and life well. Don’t let an internship disrupt your world. That’s a sign of incompetence.

13. Don’t think about the company's interests according to your own ideas. Don’t think that skipping work to watch a concert benefits your creativity, and then return and criticize the boss for being stuck in his ways. Work hours are your output time, not your accumulation time. What did you do during school? Companies don’t pay for your personal growth and learning. If you're smart, you should know how to grow and train yourself through work.

14. Read books, lots of books, all kinds of books, to grow as fast as possible. Don't require a mature face, but have a mature heart. Don’t ponder childish questions like why the company isn’t as ideal as you imagined after graduating with a master's or Ph.D.

15. Learning ability does not equal work ability. School tests IQ, society tests EQ. If your EQ is too low, go home and catch up. Don’t return to school cursing how bad the company is.

Special Reminder: Always maintain your individuality, this is very important. No matter when or where, there is only one you. If you really feel strongly against something, please at least remain “externally transformed but internally unchanged,” and hold onto some inner beliefs. Also, have a dream, a goal, so you have the motivation to escape the muddled and indulgent social life, and avoid becoming vulgar.
