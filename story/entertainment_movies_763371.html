Synopsis:

The story is set in the Middle Ages, involving a Crusader knight returning home with his squire. Along the way, he encounters the personification of Death. At the time, Europe was ravaged by the plague, leaving him physically exhausted and filled with profound questions that only time could answer: What is the meaning of life? What is the essence of death? He decides to play a game of chess with Death, agreeing to let Death take him if he loses. The game proceeds intermittently as the knight continues his journey. His experiences along the way gradually reveal to him that truth lies in earthly happiness and that salvation does not come from divine power but from humanity's ability to create life and love each other. However, at this point, Death catches up with him, demanding they finish the unfinished game. The knight ultimately loses. The next day, it seems the wanderer sees Death taking the knight away, dragging him into a "Dance of Death" circle.

Behind-the-scenes Production:

The title comes from the Bible's Book of Revelation—"When the Lamb opened the seventh seal, there was silence in heaven for about half an hour." The film is rich with symbolic imagery, whether it be the figure of Death or the black eagle circling overhead, all carrying deep meanings. These images have become cornerstones of cinematic language, often imitated by later filmmakers. However, viewers uninterested in religion may find the film difficult to appreciate due to its heavy tone.

[Bergman's God]

If, as many people say, Bergman's dark and desolate "The Seventh Seal" is a movie about death, then this might be a mistake. "The Seventh Seal" is not simply a movie about misanthropy. If you've watched it three times, each time you'll gain new understanding: in fact, it's a movie about faith. Bergman plays a small prank in this movie; you must remain alert, distinguishing between preconceived faith and true faith. God is everywhere in this movie—in the clowns and newborn babies, in the morning scene, in the songs—we can see God's presence. But if we cling to our preconceived notions, we cannot truly perceive God because we overly attribute societal expectations to God's essence. God, Bergman tells us, is much grander than those preconceptions.

Perhaps Bergman isn't questioning preconceived faith. Jöns, Brock's wise squire, rejects Christian faith and even refuses to die like a coward before Death, unless he experiences the victory of life in the last moment. Jöns contrasts the pessimistic Brock: both face harsh realities but have insights into life and faith. Brock clings to the last remnants of "faith," unwilling to fall into eternal damnation, while Jöns believes hell is on earth. If he believed in an afterlife, he couldn't ignore Brock's faith, for him, God exists only in enlightened minds, and he chooses to accept atheism and his painful existence.

[Photography]

Bergman shows little interest in moving cameras. In this movie, the extensive use of long shots and static shots adds a sense of desolation and solemnity to "The Seventh Seal," giving it a feel akin to medieval morality plays. Cinematographer Gunnar Fischer's use of black-and-white tones makes the movie even more terrifying. The striking black outfit of Death against a dim background enhances the expressionist atmosphere, a tendency shared by many Nordic artists.

[Medieval History]

The depiction of medieval Sweden in the movie is inaccurate. A knight returning from the Crusades would not encounter the Black Death, as the last Crusade ended in 1271, and the Black Death swept through Europe in 1348. Additionally, the flagellation movement was imported from abroad to Sweden, and large-scale persecution of witches occurred in the 15th century. The themes of life and death explored in the movie are more akin to existentialist philosophy popular in the 1950s. Nevertheless, some images in the movie do originate from medieval art, such as the chess game with Death, which Bergman mentioned was inspired by a church painting from the 1580s.

[Bergman Talks About "The Seventh Seal"]

"The Seventh Seal" became a road movie, freely roaming across time and space, fully responsible for such freedom. After I handed over the script to Svensk, reactions were extremely cold. Then "Smiles of a Summer Night" began shooting and premiered at Christmas in 1955, despite various criticisms, it was very successful. In May 1956, it was screened at the Cannes Film Festival and won awards. I borrowed money from Bibi Andersson in Malmö because she was the richest among our group at the time. Then I flew to Cannes to meet Dimning, the boss of Svensk, who was busily selling the rights of "Smiles of a Summer Night" to anyone interested in the hotel. This experience was new to him! The only thing that could surpass his self-satisfaction was his own naivety. I placed the completed script of "The Seventh Seal" in front of him and said: "Take it or leave it, this is your only chance, Carl!" He said: "Yes! But I need to read it first!" "You've already rejected it, how haven't you read it?" "Oh? Maybe I didn't read it carefully!" I was forced to promise to quickly wrap up the movie. Excluding travel time for location shooting, we only had thirty-six working days and had to tighten our budget...

The final scene where Death leads the wanderers in a dance was shot at Hovs Hallar. A storm was approaching, and we were packing up to leave. Suddenly, I saw a strange cloud, and Gunnar Fischer immediately set up the camera again. However, several actors had already returned to our accommodation, and a few crew members and tourists hastily joined in without knowing what was going on. That famous death dance under the black clouds was actually an impromptu creation completed within minutes. Life is so unpredictable. We managed to wrap up the movie in thirty-five days.

"The Seventh Seal" is one of the few movies that truly resonates with me deeply. I can't explain why. It's not a perfect work, a bit crazy, a bit foolish, and somewhat hastily put together. But I think it's not neurotic at all. Full of vitality and willpower, it passionately expresses its themes with fervent desire and passion. At the time, I was still deeply troubled by religious issues, caught between two thoughts, unable to advance or retreat. Both sides were talking past each other, and my childish piety and strict rationality were in a cease-fire state—there was no neurotic plot between the knight and his Holy Grail. The movie also carries a warm, family-style humor. The miracle is created by children: the eighth ball in the juggling act must stay suspended in mid-air for a fraction of a second—that breathtaking moment!

My recklessness was quite astonishing. There were things I dared to shoot then that I wouldn't dare to shoot now. After the knight finishes his morning prayer and is preparing to pack up his chess set, he turns around and sees Death standing there. "Who are you?" the knight asks. "I am Death." At the time, I still harbored the childish, residual piety from my childhood, naively believing in miraculous redemption. But I also conveyed what I believe now. Everyone has their own divinity, but it completely belongs to this world. We can't find non-secular explanations. Thus, in my movies, there exists a residual, but not neurotic, honest, and childlike piety, coexisting peacefully with strict, practical attitudes.

"The Seventh Seal" is definitely my last movie about faith. This concept was passed down to me by my father since childhood. When shooting "The Seventh Seal," praying for someone or something was the center of my life. Praying came naturally to me.

There's a scene in "The Seventh Seal" that I both love and fear, Raval dying behind a big tree in the black forest; he buries his head in the soil, screaming in terror. I originally wanted to use a close-up, but I realized that a wider distance could enhance the horror. So when Raval died, I deliberately let the camera continue the long shot. Just at that moment, on the mysterious valley stage-like setting, a pale sunlight suddenly appeared. The weather had been gloomy all day, and just at the moment Raval died, the sunlight appeared as if pre-rehearsed.

My fear of death is closely related to my religious beliefs. Once, I underwent a minor surgery and was unfortunately injected with too much anesthesia. Suddenly, I disconnected from reality. Where did time go? It was such a split-second matter. I suddenly realized: this is death! I always thought the transition from existence to non-existence was an incredible thing. For someone constantly fearing death, that experience was a great liberation. However, I also felt a little sad, thinking that after the soul leaves the body, there would be extraordinary experiences, but apparently, that's not the case. Existence is followed immediately by non-existence. This is indeed a comforting thought. What I once thought was beyond this world is so mysterious and terrifying. In fact, it doesn't exist. This world is everything, all truths exist and happen in our lives. And we continuously move through each other's lives, influencing each other.

For Svensk, "The Seventh Seal" suddenly transformed into a magnificent masterpiece showcased at the Swedish cinema golden age memorial exhibition. This was absolutely not the purpose of making this movie. Poor thing, it was forced to debut in a suffocating premiere gala: a group of elegantly dressed audiences, fanfares, plus Dimning's speech—terrible! I tried my best to prevent such a premiere method, but I was powerless. Humanity's malice born out of boredom mercilessly pollutes everything. Afterwards, "The Seventh Seal" swept the world like wildfire. I received enthusiastic responses from all over. Many people felt that this movie touched the contradictions and pains deep within their hearts. But I will never forgive its premiere ceremony. (Excerpt from "Bergman on Film", Guangxi Normal University Press)
