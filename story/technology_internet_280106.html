Commonly Used Home Appliances Service Life Standards

by longersing on 2009-11-27 12:08:56

With the full-scale implementation of the rural home appliances program and the old-for-new replacement initiative, many environmental protection topics have once again been brought up. The experts' announcement of the service life standards for common household appliances is aimed at enabling everyone to know the age of their products and whether it's worth updating them.

TV

Service life: about 8-10 years

The components inside a TV are all prone-to-damage elements. Exceeding a certain period or a certain number of power on/off cycles may lead to failure, decreased insulation performance, and even combustion. After prolonged operation, accumulated dust and dirt will reduce the overall insulation and flame retardant properties of the TV, easily causing combustion, electric shock, and other dangers. Moreover, an aging TV, once subjected to vibration, impact, or collision, may cause wire short circuits or local overheating, and in severe cases, even cathode ray tube explosion. As electrical components age, users watching TV may encounter faults such as unclear images and trembling pictures, with radiation gradually increasing. This not only affects viewing quality but also creates safety hazards.

Air Conditioner

Service life: about 8-10 years

An air conditioner beyond its service life is prone to issues like water leakage, poor cooling, no cooling, etc., and consumes a lot of electricity, truly deserving the nickname "electric tiger." After long-term use, the evaporator fins will undergo serious oxidation, accumulating a lot of dirt over time. Since air conditioners operate in enclosed spaces for long periods, they can easily pollute the indoor environment, triggering respiratory diseases and endangering human health.

Refrigerator

Service life: about 12-16 years

Refrigerators are high-frequency-use appliances. Once past their usage limit, their cooling effect will be greatly reduced, and their preservation and sterilization functions will degenerate, making food taste mixed and less fresh. An aged refrigerator compressor not only affects work efficiency but also produces significant noise. If the refrigerator's wiring ages, there's a higher risk of electric shock and spontaneous combustion, and leaked refrigerants can cause environmental pollution. Additionally, the energy consumption of "over-age" refrigerators is astonishing; after 10 years of use, the power consumption will double compared to when it was initially used.

Washing Machine

Service life: about 8 years

A washing machine that has been used for a long time is prone to problems like water leakage, circuit short-circuit, and body heating, especially under high-temperature weather. Long working hours can make the machine overheat, increasing the possibility of spontaneous combustion. Furthermore, the aging of internal components might lead to electric leakage, causing the washing machine casing or water inside the drum to become electrified, posing a great danger to users during operation.

Electric Water Heater

Service life: about 8 years

It is understood that factors such as component aging, scale buildup, and expanded corrosion area in electric water heaters increase the likelihood of electric leakage. For storage-type electric water heaters, long-term use can easily cause scale formation on the heating tube surface, preventing heat dissipation, leading to heating tube surface bursting and damage, creating electric shock hazards. For ceramic-lined electric water heaters, prolonged use of magnesium rods may also lead to electric leakage. In addition, during the process of electrification and heating, the scale formed on the surface of the magnesium rod worsens the heating effect, extends heating time, and causes a sharp rise in electricity consumption.

Microwave Oven

Service life: about 10 years

The most prone-to-aging and wearing parts in microwave ovens are the magnetron and transformer. After long-term use, the time required to heat food will also extend, not only reducing heating efficiency but also increasing power consumption. For digital control microwave ovens, electronic panel buttons may become unresponsive or fail, possibly leading to incorrect operational instructions. In addition, some microwave ovens may experience drooping doors, increasing the coefficient of microwave leakage, adversely affecting human health. It is evident that the harm caused by these "over-age" home appliances cannot be ignored. The government and enterprises should actively educate consumers about the knowledge related to "over-age" home appliances. Meanwhile, manufacturers should also cooperate to organize recycling subsidies for "over-age" home appliances, allowing consumers to conveniently replace old appliances.
