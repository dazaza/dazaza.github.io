Elderly man releases grandfather's diary of the Wuchang Uprising: persuaded the Russian consulate to remain neutral

by anonymous on 2012-02-17 10:48:46

Xinhua News Agency, Wuhan (reporter Wu Rui) - "Do not let history become dust!" Li Guangyao, an 82-year-old resident of Hankou, has preserved the Wuchang Uprising diary of his grandfather, Li Guoyong. Yesterday, he authorized our newspaper to publish the diary's content, revealing overlooked events from the past on the occasion of the 100th anniversary of the Xinhai Revolution.

Historical records indicate that Li Guangyao's grandfather, Li Guoyong (1863-1942), actively advised and strategized for the Hubei Military Government during the Wuchang Uprising, earning the trust of Li Yuanhong. He served as the commander of both land and naval forces.

Yesterday, the reporter saw the printed version of Li Guoyong’s diary and Li Yuanhong's preface titled "The Rise of Gong." The diary is arranged chronologically and written in semi-classical Chinese, documenting events from the night of the uprising on October 10, 1911 (the 19th day of the 8th lunar month in the year Xinhai) to July 20, 1912 (the 7th day of the 6th lunar month in the first year of the Republic). These entries detail Li Guoyong's personal experiences during this time.

The reporter discovered that this diary was essentially a "weekly journal." In the final paragraph, Li Guoyong briefly recorded: On the morning of June 7, 1912, while riding to the military government's finance office, his horse was startled, causing him serious injury. During his week-long convalescence, Li Guoyong recounted events related to the Wuchang Uprising with friends, compiling them into a single volume titled "Li Guoyong's Own Account."

Currently, Li Guangyao holds a document titled "A Brief Account of Li Guoyong's Revolutionary Deeds." The original handwritten diary is registered as the first item in the Hubei Revolutionary Realities Museum logbook. A receipt in Li Guangyao's possession indicates that on December 7, 1961, after submitting the diary materials to the Wuhan Municipal Committee of the Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference, he received 12 yuan as "manuscript fees."

In 2002, when Li Guangyao's cousin who had emigrated to the United States returned for a family visit, relatives visiting the Red Building noticed there were "no memorials to their grandfather in the museum." At that moment, Li Guangyao realized this piece of history might have been "overlooked" by later generations. Last year, Li Guangyao, who had moved to Canada, returned to settle in Wuhan, hoping to make public the historical facts of the Wuchang Uprising as experienced by Li Guoyong, providing primary source material for the centenary commemoration of the Xinhai Revolution.

Interpretation of Li Guoyong's Wuchang Uprising Diary:

**Persuading the Russian Consulate to Remain Neutral**

On the night of the 19th day of the 8th lunar month in the year Xinhai, the Wuchang Uprising broke out. The next day, Li Yuanhong was elected as the governor, and Li Guoyong visited him along with others, offering strategic advice. Li Guoyong emphasized that the revolution should be premised on diplomacy.

Li Guoyong's nephew, who had studied in Russia, had diplomatic experience. On the afternoon of the 23rd day of the 8th lunar month, Li Guoyong and his nephew crossed the river to the Hankou concession to meet with the Russian consul. The Russian consul stated that unless the revolutionary army achieved victory, its status as a belligerent group would not be recognized by various countries. Li Guoyong considered that if the military government's status as a belligerent group was not recognized by the international community, the revolution would fail. He hoped the Russian consulate would support the revolution.

On the afternoon of the 23rd day of the 8th lunar month, Li Guoyong again requested the Russian consul to send a telegram to the Russian embassy in Beijing to forward it to all countries, recognizing the Hubei Military Government as a belligerent group. After the Russian consul agreed, he promptly issued a notice declaring neutrality in the concessions.

**Advising Li Yuanhong to Repel the Qing Fleet**

After the Wuchang Uprising, the Qing government sent both land and naval forces to suppress the revolution. Five Qing warships were stationed on the Yangtze River near the Liujiatemple. Li Yuanhong appointed Li Guoyong as the wartime commander of both land and naval forces.

On the first day of the ninth lunar month, Li Guoyong learned that the Qing navy admiral Sa Zhenbing's warship was docked at Yangluo, so he immediately returned to Wuchang to report to Li Yuanhong, but Li Yuanhong had no plan of action.

In the following two days, Li Guoyong learned from the Russian consulate that the Qing army intended to launch a combined land and naval attack to reclaim the three towns and requested all personnel in the concession to evacuate the battlefield for three days. Li Guoyong suggested that Li Yuanhong set up artillery positions at Qingshan and Liangwang, instructing them to immediately attack any enemy ships heading upstream. Indeed, on the afternoon of the seventh day of the ninth lunar month at 3 PM, two Qing warships took advantage of the Army's recent victories to advance upstream along the Yangtze River. They were ambushed by revolutionary forces stationed at Qingshan and suffered heavy losses. From then on, the Qing warships "dared not lightly approach Wuchang," and on the thirteenth day, the entire Qing navy defected.

**Reading the Appointment Letter of Huang Xing**

On the eighth day of the ninth lunar month, amidst tense battle conditions, revolutionary leader Huang Xing arrived in Wuhan, volunteering to oversee the defense of Hankou. The morale of the revolutionary army greatly improved. On the twelfth day, Li Yuanhong held a ceremony at the Yema Field to appoint Huang Xing as the Commander-in-Chief. According to Li Guoyong's records, he read the appointment letter on that day.

The next day, Li Guoyong visited Huang Xing, who was discussing battle plans with his staff based on maps. Huang Xing ordered his adjutant to take Li Guoyong to various defensive positions to boost troop morale. That evening, Li Guoyong met with Li Yuanhong and expressed concerns that Huang Xing was merely planning on paper and only defending nearby areas. He feared that the Qing army might secretly cross the Yangtze River upstream and urged Li Yuanhong to order Huang Xing to set up artillery positions downstream of Caidian and Helshan to defend Hankou.

**Planning Li Yuanhong's Retreat to Gedian**

On the seventh day of the tenth lunar month, Hankou fell. Huang Xing advised Li Yuanhong to retreat to Jiujiang, and Li Yuanhong convened a meeting with Li Guoyong and others. According to Li Guoyong's records, Li Yuanhong indicated his willingness to defend Wuchang to the death, saying that if the city fell, he would perish with it. He asked Huang Xing to leave on his own. Following Li Guoyong's suggestion, Li Yuanhong urgently telegraphed the provinces that had risen up to quickly send reinforcements to Hubei.

The next day, Li Guoyong discussed with Li Yuanhong that since Hankou had fallen, Wuchang had become the front line, which was unfavorable for the military government. They decided to relocate elsewhere. After discussion, they agreed to hold out at Gedian. Li Guoyong analyzed that Gedian, located downstream of Wuchang, could connect with the Three Rivers' reinforcement troops. Moreover, the already-rebelled navy upstream provided protection. Once reinforcements from the provinces arrived, the revolutionary army could cross the river to directly attack the Qing army's rear, preventing the enemy from coordinating their forces front and back. Li Yuanhong adopted this suggestion and sent people to prepare everything in Gedian.

The Li Family Was Once Glorious, Having a Mansion at the Provincial Library

Xinhua News Agency, Wuhan (reporter Wu Rui) - All of Li Guoyong's children graduated from Peking University and Wuhan University. His nephew, Li Zhilong, was the captain of the Yongxing ship, and Li Guangyao himself joined the Expeditionary Force at the age of 15. Yesterday, Li Guangyao shared stories about Li Guoyong with the reporter and introduced the rise and fall of this illustrious family.

To the west side of the current Hubei Provincial Library, there used to be a large house commonly known as the Li Mansion. Li Guangyao said this was awarded to Li Guoyong by Li Yuanhong, and he himself once lived in this house.

Li Guangyao explained that what is now Wulu Road was originally called Xiong Tingbi Road, and the address of the Li Mansion was No. 43. Now, "No. 43" is the address of the Hubei Provincial Library.

After the outbreak of the Anti-Japanese War, the Li family nearly went bankrupt. Li Guoyong returned to Mianyang to grow vegetables and passed away at the age of 80 in his hometown. The former young master of a prominent family, Li Guangyao, fled with his parents to Sichuan and attended the National Sixth Middle School in Sichuan.

At the time, the government called for "knowledgeable youth to join the army and fight Japan." At the age of 15, Li Guangyao secretly joined the army without telling his family. Due to his small stature, he borrowed a pair of high heels and hid iron weights in his pockets to pass inspection. Li Guangyao bid farewell to his family, flew from Chengdu to Kunming, transferred to an American Flying Tigers plane to India to equip with American weapons, and then flew to Burma. In Burma, Li Guangyao became a member of the Youth Brigade of the Expeditionary Force. Here, Li Guangyao's main task was to train and study while cleaning up the battlefield.

After the victory in the Anti-Japanese War, Li Guangyao, who did not want to participate in the civil war, was transferred to Wuhan and became a student again, later retiring from the Yangtze River Water Conservancy Committee.

Li Guoyong, whose courtesy name was Yushan, was from Mianyang, Hubei. Since childhood, he engaged in commerce and wrote in his diary, "There was not a day when I did not seek to associate with wise and capable people from across the country." He traveled twice to Japan to investigate industry and education and upon returning home, helped establish schools. Politically, Li Guoyong participated in organizing the Hubei Education General Association, the Railway Association, the Constitutional Preparatory Meeting, and the Constitutional Comrades Association. In "The Rise of Gong," Li Yuanhong praised him as having made outstanding contributions to the founding of the Republic. Before the Wuchang Uprising, he was a capitalist and a constitutionalist, but after the uprising, he actively speculated in the revolution like doing business.

Huang Xing (1874-1916) was a modern Chinese democratic revolutionary. Born in Shanhua (now Changsha), Hunan Province, after the Wuchang Uprising, he was elected as the Commander-in-Chief of the revolutionary army, fighting against the Qing army in Hankou and Hankou.

Li Yuanhong (1864-1928) was born in Mulan Township, Huangpi County. Li Yuanhong was the Governor of the Wuchang Uprising of the Xinhai Revolution and also the only person in Chinese history to have served twice as President and three times as Vice President.
