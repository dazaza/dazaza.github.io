4.15 Emergency Order! Use offensive strategies to cope with interest rate hikes

by huaxun880 on 2010-04-15 13:19:23

"Who said anything about raising interest rates?" The statement by Zhou Xiaochuan, Governor of the People's Bank of China, did not dispel the market's increasingly intensifying expectations of an interest rate hike. This morning, the National Bureau of Statistics will announce the main economic data indicators for the first quarter of the national economy, with the CPI once again becoming a focal point of attention. From various forecasts, if the economic data shows signs of overheating in the economy, the "shoe" of an interest rate hike could drop.

Firstly, economists predict that the GDP growth rate for the first quarter could reach as high as 11.6%, meaning that China's economic growth rate in the first quarter has rebounded to the highest level since the outbreak of the global financial crisis. This would push up inflation and put further pressure on the government to withdraw from economic stimulus policies with greater force.

Secondly, according to surveys by Dow Jones Newswires and Reuters, China's consumer price index in March is expected to rise 2.6% year-on-year, slightly lower than the 2.7% increase in February. However, the figures for March will be among China's first monthly economic data this year not severely distorted by seasonal factors. Rising prices for refined oil and steel will further push up the CPI. Standard Chartered Bank estimates that China's overall inflation rate in May-June could exceed 7%, with the full-year consumer price increase reaching 6.5%. Super-expected economic growth and inflation rates have led the market to worry that the central bank's timing for raising interest rates will be significantly advanced.

In addition, typically after adjusting the reserve requirement ratio two or three times, there will be an adjustment to interest rates. In this round, the reserve requirement ratio has already been adjusted twice, so the expectation for an interest rate hike is quite strong.

In fact, there is no consensus yet on the timing of the interest rate hike, but "raising interest rates in the second quarter" has become a consensus in the industry. Once the interest rate hike begins, it will be a significant shock to all sectors. Statistical results show that the degree of reaction to interest rate hikes decreases sequentially from machinery manufacturing to agriculture. Sectors with more capital-intensive operations are affected to a greater extent than labor-intensive industries. As key sectors for national macroeconomic regulation, the impact on industries such as building materials and cement, real estate, etc., ranks at the top; industries supported by national policies such as agriculture, transportation, and water and electricity production are barely affected by interest rate hikes; the impact of interest rate hikes on machinery equipment manufacturing will be significant.

However, interest rate hikes are seen as positive news for banks and insurance companies. Once interest rates start to rise, they will increase the net interest margin and net interest spread of commercial banks. Bank stocks will benefit from the interest rate hike factor, thus leading to further valuation enhancement of the sector. However, banks are significantly impacted by real estate, so the impact of interest rate hikes on the banking sector is neutral. Choosing insurance stocks as an "offensive defense" investment target is a win-win situation. The "offense" aspect is manifested in the upcoming launch of stock index futures, where large-cap blue-chip stocks will have opportunities to perform well, and insurance stocks as weighted stocks are expected to rise collectively. Secondly, insurance stocks are the best defensive varieties against interest rate hikes.

In the short term, interest rate hikes directly affect bank deposit rates, and insurance companies' fixed-term deposits will effectively benefit from the rise in interest rates, with the size of the benefit depending on the proportion of floating-rate deposits and the proportion of deposits maturing in the short term. Both China Life and Ping An Insurance companies can promptly benefit from the rise in interest rates through their fixed-term deposit assets. Additionally, in the long term, bonds are the main investment assets of insurance companies, and their yield changes are more stable compared to the stock market. Therefore, an increase in bond investment yields is crucial for improving the overall yield of insurance funds. Thus, the long-term yield levels of insurance companies are on an upward trend.
