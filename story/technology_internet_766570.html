Google Buys Bump, Developer Of Mobile Contact-Info Sharing App

by anonymous on 2013-09-18 21:59:30

Google Acquires Mobile Contact Information Sharing App Developer Bump

After raising nearly $20 million in funding, the app developed by Bump Technologies has become one of the most downloaded apps, but the company failed to find a real revenue channel. Today, the company announced that it has been acquired by Google.

Bump Technologies' Bump app allows users to share contact information and other content by gently bumping two phones together. Although it may not be appropriate to congratulate them, there's no denying that after being acquired by Google, Bump should have a brighter future.

Bump co-founder and CEO David Lieb said: "We strive to create an incredible user experience through innovation in mathematics, data processing, and algorithms, so we are extremely happy to join Google." The 25-person team, including Lieb and another Bump co-founder Andy Huibers, will likely all join Google.

The Bump app and the group photo-sharing app Flock launched by the company last year "will continue to run as before for now, awaiting future updates." However, Lieb did not mention in today's blog post how the Bump Pay app will fare, which was developed by Bump based on the PayPal platform, allowing users to make mobile payments by bumping their phones.

Neither Bump nor Google disclosed the specific financial terms of this acquisition, so it's hard to know exactly whether Bump and its investors received adequate returns. Bump's investors include startup incubator Y Combinator, Sequoia Capital, super angel fund Felicis Venture, SV Angel, venture capital firm Andreessen Horowitz, and many other angel investors.

In the early days of Apple's App Store, Bump achieved great success and gathered significant popularity. Users of Bump don't need to manually enter new friends' or colleagues' contact information; instead, they just need both parties to open Bump and bump their phones together to share contact information, photos, audio, video, or other selected files. As of March, the number of photos shared via Bump had reached 1 billion, and its total downloads had reached 125 million, up 25% from 100 million last August.

However, over time, other quick methods of sharing contact information began to emerge, while Bump remained a free app without finding any significant revenue channels. Although it raised $16.5 million in November 2011 from a financing round led by Andreessen Horowitz, this amount of funding may not have been enough to pay the salaries of Bump's strong mobile engineering team.

Subsequently, Apple announced a new feature called AirDrop for its iOS 7 operating system, which would make file sharing between a phone and nearby devices a native function, undoubtedly adding insult to injury for Bump. Affected by this, Bump's originally stable growth momentum may have been disrupted, leading to a point where it had to surrender. From these factors, the benefits of this acquisition deal for Bump may be greater than a simple "acqui-hire" deal, but not enough to be considered a major victory.

In some sense, this sale of Bump can also be seen as a failure. If Bump could have found a way to use its large user base to make money, it might have made a lot of cash, but the final result was not finding such a method, forcing it to submit to a tech giant. On the other hand, this "soft landing" is good news for investors, allowing them to avoid losing money, while the Bump team gains an opportunity to expand its influence using Google's resources.

What likely interests Google is not Bump itself, but Flock. This app uses geolocation to determine which Facebook friends are near a user and then reminds the user to share photos taken with those friends. The idea behind this app is that your friends may not share all photos on social media, but you still want to see these photos. Flock's design philosophy is to reduce the process of photo sharing as much as possible, making it almost automatic.

Google may want to incorporate Flock into Google+, thereby competing with Facebook's photo-sharing service and Dropbox's photo-saving service. The Party Mode of Google+ was a pioneer in the field of group photo sharing, but this relatively late entrant social network hasn't yet gained significant user engagement. Recently, Facebook launched a shared album feature, making it increasingly difficult for Google to achieve deeper development in this area.

This acquisition also means that Google can use its series of mobile communication patents to help nearby devices synchronize. The company can use these patents to improve the Android operating system and provide richer options for Near Field Communication (NFC).
