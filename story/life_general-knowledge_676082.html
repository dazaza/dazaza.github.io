What to do if a baby spits up milk?

The human stomach has two openings. One is connected to the esophagus, called the cardiac orifice, which is the entrance of the stomach, and the other is connected to the intestine, called the pylorus, which is the exit of the stomach. In infants, the cardiac orifice is relatively loose and cannot close tightly, making it easy for food to push it open. When there is slightly too much food in the stomach, it will push open the cardiac orifice and reflux back into the esophagus. The pylorus closes more tightly and can easily be stimulated by food to cause spasms, making the exit resistance greater, slowing down the passage of food or even preventing its passage. This causes the food to reflux from the pylorus to the cardiac orifice, breaking through the barrier, and the child will spit up milk.

In addition, the infant's stomach is in a horizontal position, with a small capacity that holds little food, making it easier for the food to return to the cardiac orifice. Even under normal circumstances, such as overeating, lying down immediately after feeding, milk can easily push open the cardiac orifice, flow back up the esophagus to the mouth, causing the baby to spit up milk. If the child occasionally spits up milk but appears fine otherwise, there is no need to worry. However, if the child spits up milk during every feeding and the amount of spitting is large, there may be an issue with the gastrointestinal tract itself, or there may be problems in other systems, and you should take the child to the hospital for examination.

Spitting up milk is one of the most common issues in infants, especially those under six months old. This is because the muscles of the infant are not yet fully developed, and their contraction ability is insufficient, so the junction between the stomach and esophagus cannot close tightly. At the same time, the infant's stomach is not tilted but almost horizontal, making it easier for milk to flow both ways, especially when there is too much milk in the stomach or during intense activity, making it easier for milk to flow both ways and cause spitting up.

Although infants have these physiological characteristics, if we pay attention to the following points regularly, spitting up milk can be completely prevented.

The posture during feeding must be correct. During feeding, the baby’s head should be slightly elevated, and the thumb and index finger should pinch the nipple while the rest of the fingers support the breast below to prevent the milk from flowing out too quickly. When using a rubber nipple for feeding, the hole should not be too large; it is best to make two or three small holes so that the milk does not flow too fast.

Feeding should be timed and measured. If the feeding amount is too much or the interval between feedings is too short, the stomach will become overly expanded, making it easier for the baby to vomit.

After each feeding, gently lift the child and let them lie on your shoulder. Gently pat the child’s back to expel the air swallowed during feeding, preventing vomiting.

After feeding, do not play or move the child excessively; it is best to let the child sleep quietly. The sleeping position should be on the right side to avoid pressing on the stomach and causing vomiting. Do not press the child too heavily, and ensure that clothing and wraps are not too tight.

If the child is vomiting due to catching a cold or other diseases, consult a doctor promptly. Once the illness is cured, the vomiting will stop.

Strategies for Dealing with Baby Spit-Up

Some babies will spit up milk immediately after eating when changing positions or lying down. This condition is medically referred to as regurgitation, a physiological phenomenon. This occurs because the baby's stomach is in a horizontal position, and any change in position makes it impossible for the stomach to maintain a level position, leading to regurgitation. Please don't worry; this will naturally improve once the baby reaches six months of age. However, some babies, whether lying down or being held, will vomit about ten minutes after eating each time. This phenomenon is mostly caused by pyloric spasm. The upper end of the stomach is called the cardia, and the lower end is called the pylorus. Pyloric spasm prevents milk from smoothly entering the duodenum, leading to vomiting. There are two methods to control this situation:

First, give your baby rice porridge one or two times a day. Rice porridge has a higher concentration than milk and is semi-liquid, which can cause the pylorus to relax due to gravity, thereby reducing the occurrence of vomiting.

Second, give the baby one to two drops of 1:2000 atropine solution fifteen minutes before each feeding, then proceed with feeding. This method is simple and easy to implement, the medication is inexpensive, and it is readily available. Most children's hospitals use this method to control infant vomiting, achieving satisfactory results.
