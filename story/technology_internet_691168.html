The Bittersweet History of a Job-Hopping Enthusiast

by liwuwang2012 on 2012-03-05 14:38:22

The Haste of First Employment

In the summer of 2004, after four years of studying foreign trade English, I finally left my obscure third-rate school to start a new life. However, I believe that the hasty experience of my first employment determined my attitude towards work and job-hopping in the future.

Choosing my first job was an extremely awkward beginning. Due to average grades in my major courses, I could only intern at a small local trading company. As classmates signed contracts one by one and the school urged us to leave, I had no choice but to become an export order follower at the internship company. The company, including the boss, had only four people. After completing a project, there would usually be half a month or even longer with nothing to do, during which time I struggled financially. Fortunately, my boyfriend often helped me out by buying food and also urged me to find a new job quickly. Thus, I spent my days online applying for jobs and made excuses to go to interviews in the afternoon. Three months later, I successfully changed jobs and became an assistant in the strategic development department of a Japanese company.

Once I started working at the foreign company, I realized how many rules there were. My Japanese boss would often get furious if computers or photocopiers were left on after work. Printing required using [gray] [quick], paper had to be double-sided, and pens could only be exchanged with empty cartridges. On sunny days with sufficient sunlight, lights must not be turned on. Weekend overtime was as common as home-cooked meals. At that time, I thought the employees were very passionate and the team spirit was good. But I was unlucky; after being there for a month, the boss felt our work was too idle, so he laid off the entire department. Before I could prepare myself, I was unemployed again. By the end of the year, with fewer opportunities in the recruitment market, I eventually found a sales position at a Taiwanese exhibition company.

In five years, I changed jobs 21 times

After doing the job for not long, my family thought it was unreliable for me to work in sales and advised me to take the civil service exam. Having not touched books for half a year, I wasn't optimistic about this exam. To stop my family from worrying about my work, I resigned and began preparing for the exam. Subsequently, I failed the national exam's written test and stopped at the interview stage for the local exam. Five months passed, and many of my classmates who graduated together had already been promoted, with rising salaries. Meanwhile, I had changed jobs three times within a year, with nearly half a year of unemployment, all of which without exception became obstacles in my subsequent job hunting.

Many large companies politely rejected me due to my frequent job-hopping and career gaps. In confusion, I kept repeating the cycle of job hunting—resigning—job hunting again. In the following four years of my career, I worked in industries with significant differences such as advertising, clothing, training, kitchenware, futures, cosmetics, glasses, logistics, insurance, enterprise management, and real estate intermediaries. The positions I held varied, including export order following, sales, front desk, administration, training specialist, recruitment specialist, and department assistant, mostly roles that were easily replaceable. In five years, I changed jobs 21 times, working in private, state-owned, and foreign enterprises, with the shortest tenure being six days and the longest eight months.

Each time I changed jobs, apart from gaining some superficial work experiences, I rarely distilled high-value work experiences. I met many friends but didn’t have deep connections with any of them. I changed jobs repeatedly, but my salary remained stagnant, sometimes even decreasing. Each resignation brought inevitable depression, making me increasingly afraid of job hunting, even wanting to escape the workplace forever and avoid facing new environments.

Blind Job-Hopping Leads to Bitter Consequences

After seeing my current situation, my boyfriend strongly suggested that I seek professional career counseling. He said that because I lacked self-awareness and career positioning, I didn’t know what kind of work to look for, what I liked to do, or what was most suitable for me. After blindly trying and failing 21 times in five years, I still couldn’t solve this problem, ultimately leading to today’s significant troubles.

A career planner helped me sort through my past work experiences and analyzed my frequent job-hopping. The teacher told me that my experiences are common problems among most "job-hoppers" today: unclear positioning and unclear goals! In fact, changing jobs is just one way to approach personal career goals. If you can do career positioning before changing jobs, fully consider your internal career orientation and unique business value, understand the company's strength, environment, and cultural background, thoroughly research and fully understand the position you will be taking on, prepare mentally, and then apply, the new job will naturally become more stable.

Among current job changers, over 60% belong to blind job-hoppers like me. Without proper career planning, I hastily changed jobs, resulting in the rapid depletion of each jump's accumulated energy. These five years of job-hopping experiences feel like a blood-tear-filled history of the workplace, shocking to behold. If I had a clear career positioning when I first looked for a job after graduation, or if I had systematically organized and planned after failing the civil service exam, would I still need to endure today's bitter consequences of job hunting? Finally, I want to say: the path of the workplace, first define, then plan, scientifically and rationally develop; tortuous job hunting, tears streaming, heart palpitations, don't become a job-hopping maniac!

Tom is a 2009 graduate of Marketing from a certain university. Although he has been working for less than a year, he has already changed jobs five times: after graduating, he joined a private company but jumped ship right after the probation period due to dissatisfaction with the low salary. In the next few months, he successively changed jobs at an advertising company, an insurance company, a consulting company, and a pharmaceutical company, spanning a wide range of industries that was astonishing. Joy, who graduated a year earlier than Tom, changed jobs eleven times in two years. Just after the Spring Festival holiday ended, he began frequently appearing at various job fairs because at a class reunion during the holiday, he saw everyone doing better than him, causing him to feel unbalanced. According to data from the Sunward Career Management Research Center, nearly 60% of job changers change jobs four times or more within a year, and this type of shallow sampling job hopping is becoming increasingly common in the workplace.

Many college graduates believe that trying different jobs and accumulating work experience across various industries makes it easier to find a job in the future. However, according to Sunward Career's long-term research on the recruitment market, most employers do not agree with this view. "Work experience" is not judged by time and quantity; having too much of this shallow sampling work experience does not add any points to job hunting and instead makes companies feel insecure: "[Rather than hiring such 'experienced' individuals, it might be better to hire 'blank slate' fresh graduates who are easier to train]." Therefore, how to effectively prevent the intrusion of hidden killers is an urgent issue that current new entrants to the workplace, especially recent and former college students, need to pay attention to and resolve. Hong Xiangyang, Chief Career Planner at the authoritative career planning consultation agency Sunward Career, believes that wrong job changes are not incurable and solutions can be sought from three aspects:

1. Insist on Working for at Least Three Months to Determine Suitability of the New Position

Generally, after changing jobs and entering a new environment, it is easy to experience reality shock, meaning discovering that the nature of work or workload exceeds one's ability or estimation. There may also be conflicts with superiors or colleagues, or dissatisfaction with the environment, leading to regret. Due to unfamiliarity with the new environment, once something goes wrong, it is often compared with the original company and position, and the more comparisons, the more one feels that this job change was a mistake, leading to thoughts of changing jobs again. Sunward Career believes this is actually an avoidance behavior. Because every job change requires some time to adapt to the new environment. Environments do not adapt to people; people must adapt to the environment. Since you got this job, you should at least try to work for three months, treating it as a challenge to see if you can overcome these difficulties. Even if you find that the position is moving further away from your career goal, you might as well work for three months, considering it as paying tuition fees. Although the cost is high, it will at least reduce restlessness and allow you to gain a clearer understanding of yourself during this time.

2. Look Broadly Within the Company for Internal Transfer Opportunities

If you determine that you are not adapting well to your current job, you might as well tell your boss directly and seek other positions within the company. Remember, your boss hires new employees hoping they will bring greater commercial value to the company and doesn't want employee talents to be wasted. In many large group companies, internal transfers and job changes often bring unexpected opportunities. Find an appropriate opportunity to communicate openly with your boss! Let him understand what you are best at, what kind of work you can accumulate experience from, and whether internal transfer is possible, giving you a more suitable position to showcase your talents. With sincere attitudes and effective communication, the boss will likely value you more and seriously consider your opinions.

3. Evaluate Employment Opportunities Rationally Plan Before Changing Jobs Again

When you confirm that your job change was a mistake and cannot change the result through communication, you can only "ride the donkey while looking for another horse," making a second job change. However, this time you must learn from your mistakes and not make another blind decision. The basis for making a job change decision is reasonable career planning. Hong Xiangyang suggests clarifying two key issues before making a decision: Is this decision bringing me closer to my future career goals? Will I live and work happier because of this? Once you have some answers, ask further: Do I hope to engage in such work? What skills and professional knowledge does this job require? Do I possess them? Which industry do I want to enter? Am I familiar with it? Where can I collect the above information? If there are deficiencies in professional knowledge and skills, do I need further study? If you can clearly answer these questions, you will know what you want to do and what suits you. If you're not sure, spend more time thinking, and if necessary, seek help from a career planner. Only in this way can you avoid another failed job change.

Life is not just about using your strengths well but also knowing how to profit from losses, summarize improvements from failures, and gain inspiration and exercise in adversity. Hong Xiangyang pointed out that after realizing the failure of a job change, you should first analyze the new environment carefully, seeking new opportunities or changes internally rather than immediately considering another job change. After all, if fundamental issues such as direction and core competitiveness are not clarified, things will only get worse. If the new environment is indeed unsuitable, then it's time to adjust direction. Direction determines the future! Source: Etiquette Kingdom Gift-giving Network www.li63.com
