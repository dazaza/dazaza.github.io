"The Inevitable 'Death of Yougou': Belle's E-commerce Has Many Serious Issues"

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 14:10:07

Recently, Yougou.com, which has been suffering from a severe loss of executives, was hit again. On July 17th, news came that according to insiders from Yougou.com, the COO Zhang Xiaojun will be leaving and is currently processing his resignation. As one of the founding members of Yougou.com, Zhang Xiaojun’s departure is particularly significant, indicating that Yougou.com is indeed facing serious crises.

Let's first review the e-commerce journey of Belle International, the parent company of Yougou.com:

Belle had been quietly testing the waters of e-commerce for several years, developing steadily through three channels: Taobao distribution, Tmall, and B2C distribution (B2C distribution completed via Taoxiu.com). Led by veteran group member Xie Yunli with a not-so-luxurious e-commerce team, they initially sold products under Belle's own brands, then gradually launched exclusive online models and new internet brands. This development path was considered a more solid success case for traditional brand enterprises entering e-commerce before 2011 compared to Li-Ning.

Following the over $100 million financing and rapid growth of companies like Letao.com and HaoLeMai in 2010, Belle Group became restless as a supplier to these two companies. On one hand, there was concern about being constrained by distribution channels again; on the other hand, an evaluation revealed that they had enormous development advantages in the e-commerce field, including:

1. Numerous brands: In addition to its own major women's shoe brands, the agency sales of internationally renowned sports brands like Adidas and Nike were growing rapidly online;

2. Easier category expansion: For Belle Group, known for its women's shoes and agency of well-known sports brands, achieving absolute market advantage and status through multi-brand and multi-category strategies was their main focus. Expanding brands and categories through offline stores was obviously too slow, while the existing e-commerce business could easily expand brands but struggled to quickly add categories;

3. Financial advantage: During cooperation with e-commerce enterprises, Belle discovered that e-commerce is a capital-intensive business. Belle's financial strength allowed it to develop without relying on external capital, thus enabling it to outlast many vertical e-commerce platforms that depended heavily on capital, becoming the "survivor is king" player;

4. Talent advantage: Through years of e-commerce practice, they already possessed a highly professional and efficient e-commerce team. The cultivation of talent could support the scale-up or even further growth of e-commerce operations;

5. Supply chain advantage: This was what excited Belle internally the most and also the core reason for attracting high-level executives from brand e-commerce or platform e-commerce. After all, for e-commerce, supply chain issues are always fascinating because they seem to determine life and death beyond just funding. The supply chain includes product R&D, production, warehouse management, delivery after-sales service, and a series of "business" content. In traditional enterprises emphasizing "business" over "e-commerce," this is considered the greatest competitive advantage over e-commerce enterprises.

With these advantages, Belle's e-commerce decided to make a big move, starting with purchasing the double-pinyin domain yougou.com,

abandoning the slightly imitation-like “topshoes.cn” Taoxiu website for redirection, stopping supplies to all distribution sites, while retaining direct-operated third-party online stores like Taobao and Tmall but reducing investment and attention. They recruited JD.com VPs and Vancl VPs at high salaries for CMO and COO positions respectively, dispatched senior veterans to serve as CEO, forming a golden triangle. They aimed to create a full-category vertical B2C mall, implementing multi-brand strategies on one hand, and expanding into clothing and other categories on the other, realizing the dream of a department store empire impossible to achieve offline!

Everything sounded beautiful, but every dream must face the harsh reality. The actual situation was:

1. Higher technical requirements for independent B2C: Belle's headquarters in Shenzhen couldn't recruit more excellent technical talents, and technical outsourcing services were scarce in Shenzhen. However, technology determines user experience, backend management efficiency, and reliable data. Independent B2C belongs to dynamic technology, different from the relatively static ERP technology. Continuous changes remain a hurdle that many traditional enterprises can't overcome;

2. Increased management difficulty due to category expansion: Someone once said that doing women's shoes well and men's shoes well is hard, which Belle probably realized deeply when expanding into men's shoes. Expanding into women's clothing and accessories within a year would understandably pose difficulties on the backend. For traditional brand enterprises accustomed to meticulous planning, such a fast approach worked during special periods for Vancl, and the internal culture of Vancl and JD.com could accommodate it. Although Belle had hired Vancl's COO and JD.com's CMO, whether they could be understood and execute with "exaggerated costs" remains questionable!

3. System: Many people don't understand this issue unless they've crossed both e-commerce and traditional enterprises. Simply put, department settings (organizational structure), approvals, finance and budget, reporting methods, corporate culture, communication styles, execution methods, risk control, processes, and systems form a company's so-called "mechanism." This conflict results in e-commerce trying to persuade traditional businesses to accept the "system" of e-commerce, while the traditional team tries to understand but can never fully grasp the "system" of e-commerce, leading to low efficiency and instability in the execution team. Ultimately, the chairman or board may say: "E-commerce and traditional enterprises each have their strengths and weaknesses, and in the future, they must surely merge...". If this so-called "merging without harmony" appears, it's called management innovation, and "CEO rotation system" actually appeared in a company like Belle!

Originally, it was believed that Yougou.com from Belle's e-commerce had taken the lead, and the top executives were waiting for a spin-off IPO to enjoy success, wealth, and honor, countless examples of traditional enterprises succeeding in the e-commerce era. Instead, they were agonizing over these hard injuries.

After all, whether it's the veterans who grew up with Belle or the e-commerce professionals recruited, it can be seen that they didn't leave due to lack of ability. When the VP from JD.com returned, they still achieved remarkable results, and when the VP from Vancl had the courage to start a business. Are these hard injuries the three points mentioned above? Or are these not only Belle's hard injuries but also the hard injuries of many traditional enterprises attempting to enter e-commerce?
