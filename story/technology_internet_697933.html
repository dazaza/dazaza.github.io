Bike English: A unique method to learn English speaking through English songs

by ac73p77m73r6 on 2012-03-08 21:36:08

How do the teachers at Biek English use English songs to learn English written language, help students learn pronunciation, vocabulary, sentence patterns, grammar, history and culture, and exercise their language communication skills? Some songs have a certain historical background. Learning and singing these songs not only helps with learning the language but also provides some historical and cultural knowledge and social background information.

English songs contain a variety of grammatical structures. When students learn to sing in English, they won't feel like they're studying grammar. The tedious and hard-to-remember grammar presented in an artistic form makes a deep impression. If you can't remember it accurately, just sing the song once, and it will be correct without error. The future continuous tense is a difficult grammar point, but in Richard Marx's "Right Here Waiting," there are such lyrics: "I’ll be right there waiting for you." Isn't this a good example? As long as you can apply it to other situations, you'll have a thorough understanding of the future continuous tense!

This song is about the great fire of London in 1666. With just a few sentences, it vividly depicts the situation of people fighting the fire. The sentences are smooth and easy to read. By learning such lyrics, many pronunciation difficulties such as stress, emphasis, linking, and rhythm are practiced, and your pronunciation and intonation will improve significantly.

At Biek English ("Today a foreign teacher sang an English song for me"). Indeed, besides asking students to pay attention to accumulating and mastering vocabulary and grammar, cultivating reading abilities, reading more, and speaking more, one of the reasons why the Biek English classroom is relaxed and pleasant is that the teachers often use topics like English songs and English movies to attract students' interest in learning.

For example, learning to sing Joe Hill's song, "I Dream I Saw Joe Hill Last Night Alive as You and Me...", the song is filled with mourning for Joe Hill. Through such songs, we can understand that Joe Hill organized workers' strikes, wrote songs in prison, and finally sacrificed bravely. Our admiration for Joe Hill arises naturally.

Learning to sing songs is a good way to deepen memory. English songs have concise vocabulary, profound meanings, and many popular "trendy words" and authentic expressions that can expand vocabulary. For instance, the song "One Sweet Day" which won the Grammy Award in 1996 has such lyrics: "I know eventually we’ll be together…", and you will gradually understand that "eventually" is the adverb form of "eventual". Once you remember these lyrics, the word "eventually" might be unforgettable for life.

Lyrics are poetic language, simple yet meaningful, with a fast rhythm and strong force. Learning lyrics is the most effective voice practice. Singing English songs requires clear articulation and pronunciation, and involves phonetic knowledge such as elision and linking. This requires singers to pay special attention to pronunciation and tone. For example, the children's song "London’s Burning" has only four lines: "London’s burning, London’s burning. Fetch the engines. Fetch the engines. Fire! Fire! Fire! Fire! Pour on water. Pour on water."

Learning to sing songs can also help learn many sentence patterns, and such examples are countless. In the lyrics of "I Swear," there are such sentences: "I swear, like the shadow that’s by your." Another example: "You give me wings, making me fly. You touch my head and could touch the sky." There is also the "there be" sentence pattern: "There is a place in your heart."
