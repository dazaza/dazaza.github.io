Buddhist incense offerings generally focus on burning incense, including tower incense, loose incense, powdered incense, and stick incense. These are lit and offered to the Buddhas and Bodhisattvas as a form of repentance for past misdeeds and sincere prayer. (1) Preparations before offering incense: When visiting a temple to offer incense, one should dress appropriately in elegant and moderate attire, avoiding excessive exposure or eccentricity. Before purchasing incense, one should wash their hands and face, adjust their mindset, and sincerely prepare themselves.

(2) Selection of Buddhist incense and candles: At the incense shop beside the burning area, choose thin stick incense and small candles (paper money is not used for Buddha offerings). The quantity and size of incense and candles do not matter; sincerity does. Even without buying incense or candles, other offerings can be made to the Buddha. Stick incense that has not been dyed and has a light fragrance is preferred. Large incense sticks, often called grass incense, are mostly made from wood shavings and grasses with added artificial chemical fragrances, which can harm health. If the smell is sharp, it is due to chemical substances irritating the nasal mucosa, making it unsuitable for Buddha offerings. Incense should be held by hand, avoiding plastic bags, while keeping environmental cleanliness in mind.

(3) Offering incense and candles: At the incense and candle burning area, remove the outer packaging of the incense and candles, and dispose of the packaging materials into bamboo baskets. Light the small candles and place them on the candle stand. Light the small stick incense, then insert it into the incense burner.

(4) Making wishes before the Buddha: Queue up orderly to enter the Three Jewels Hall. Bow three times before the Buddha, make your wish, pray for the Buddha's protection, and bow three more times. Do not kneel for too long to avoid inconveniencing others. In front of the Buddha and Bodhisattvas, there are donation boxes where you can donate money voluntarily, known as practicing almsgiving. The temples have strict financial management systems for these donations, using them for temple construction, establishing Buddhist academies, spreading Buddhist culture, and engaging in Buddhist charity work, thus accumulating boundless merit.
