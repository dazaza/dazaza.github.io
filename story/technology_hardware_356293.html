Melasma, also known as butterfly rash, is the most common pigmentation skin disease in women. Because it appears on the face and covers a relatively large area, it causes much distress to many beauty-conscious women.

Dermatologists have found through long-term research that the occurrence of this condition is often related to endocrine dysfunction (such as pregnancy, menstrual disorders, ovarian or uterine diseases, etc.). Especially when there is a hormonal imbalance, this condition is most likely to occur, and it is most commonly seen during periods of reproductive activity such as 3-5 months of pregnancy or 1-20 months after taking oral contraceptives. Additionally, during menopause or with reproductive organ diseases, increased estrogen and progesterone inhibit tyrosinase by glutathione, leading to increased melanin production.

At the same time, strong sunlight exposure, stimulation from inferior cosmetics, liver disease, visceral tumors, chronic malnutrition, deficiencies in vitamins A, C, or niacin are all possible triggers for this condition. Furthermore, a long-term lack of glutathione in the diet can increase the activity of tyrosinase in the skin, causing tyrosine to form dopa under the action of tyrosinase, which then oxidizes into dopaquinone and forms melanin, thus inducing this condition.

After careful research, some domestic dermatologists and health experts suggest that melasma patients should consume more of the following foods to achieve a certain therapeutic effect.

1. Eat more foods rich in vitamin C, such as oranges, tomatoes, young peppers, and small radishes. Vitamin C can inhibit the oxidation of dopaquinone in the skin, making dark oxidized pigments lighter and thereby inhibiting melanin formation.

2. Regularly consume foods rich in vitamin E, such as fresh lettuce, egg yolks, sesame seeds, and whole grains. As age increases, peroxidized lipids gradually accumulate in the body, and this substance easily induces melanin deposition. However, vitamin E has an antioxidant effect and can effectively inhibit the production of peroxidized lipids.

In addition, patients should avoid strong direct sunlight, reduce consumption of soy sauce, coffee, and other foods with deep pigmentation, and avoid using hormone-based ointments and stimulating cosmetics to minimize the incidence of melasma and maintain facial whiteness and smoothness.
