Google Launches "Project Link" to Bring Fiber Optic Connectivity to Developing Regions

by anonymous on 2013-11-21 14:22:08

Google launches Project Link, will build fiber internet networks in developing areas

Today, Google has launched "Project Link," which aims to provide internet access to developing regions around the world. Through this project, Google will begin installing fiber-optic cables in these regions, connecting existing local networks with undersea cables. Google also stated that this initiative will assist local internet service providers and operators in expanding their services.

This project is currently in a commercial trial phase, during which Google will charge local operators a certain fee. A Google spokesperson said: "We hope this can become a sustainable model that ultimately enables everyone to access the internet."

The first pilot for Project Link is already underway in Kampala (the capital of Uganda) and is looking to expand. Although Google has not revealed the next city, it is clear from the introduction of Project Link that Africa is not the only region of focus for Google, so it is likely that Google's presence will be felt in other regions as well.

Last month, Google joined forces with other tech giants such as Microsoft, Facebook, Yahoo, and Intel to form the Alliance for Affordable Internet (A4AI). The goal of this alliance is to help the United Nations Broadband Commission achieve one of its major objectives: providing internet access to all residents of developing countries by 2015 at a cost not exceeding 5% of their monthly income.

Two months ago, Mark Zuckerberg announced the establishment of Internet.org, an organization dedicated to spreading the internet to every corner of the world. Its partners include many of the world's leading tech companies, with Facebook, Ericsson, Nokia, Qualcomm, and Samsung being its founding members. They all aim to bring the internet to the two-thirds of the world that currently lacks connectivity.

It seems that the major players are beginning to extend their business reach into developing regions, which will undoubtedly help these areas develop more quickly. We are now one step closer to a true global village.
