Four factors force Lenovo Mobile to carry out overseas acquisitions, with three major difficulties

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 12:30:04

Although Lenovo executives have given many ambiguous responses to the industry's heated discussions about the "smartphone acquisition" event, after carefully analyzing the situation, we find that overseas smartphone acquisitions are not just an added bonus for Lenovo, but rather a lifeline. It goes without saying that the "snow" refers to the European and American markets, while the "coal" refers to a patent defense partner that can help Lenovo establish a foothold in these regions.

Regarding Lenovo's potential overseas acquisition targets, names like BlackBerry and HTC have circulated in the market. In reality, this list might be even longer. Those familiar with Lenovo's acquisition of Brazil's PC business know that Lenovo likes to prepare multiple options rather than be subject to excessive price demands.

Below, let's discuss why Lenovo wants to acquire an overseas smartphone company:

1. Patent Defense. Additionally, there are various types of patent trolls abroad, who make a living by extorting patent royalties from others.

2. Channel Systems. There are many types of smartphone channel systems, such as carrier channels. Theoretically, everyone competes fairly, but looking at China's carriers and their partners, brands like ZTE, Huawei, Coolpad, and Lenovo have more opportunities compared to other brands. This implies that achieving maximum benefits in the channel system without years of investment is difficult unless you have products as stunning as Apple's.

3. Brand Enhancement. Lenovo PCs already have high recognition in the U.S., but this does not significantly benefit Lenovo smartphones. For example, although Gree air conditioners rank first in the Chinese market, Gree small appliances may not necessarily give people peace of mind. Overseas acquisitions can significantly boost Lenovo's brand image. Take ThinkPad as an example; some foreigners' understanding of Lenovo PCs comes from the globally renowned ThinkPad being a Lenovo brand.

4. Capital for Time. In the past, Acer's acquisition of Gateway caused Lenovo a lot of trouble, which was a typical case of trading capital for time and space. Quickly obtaining access to the European and American smartphone markets through acquisitions is much more cost-effective than paying tuition fees.

Below, let's discuss where Lenovo's difficulties lie:

1. Money is an issue, but not a major one. Reasons include: Lenovo has consistently maintained a good cash flow. Moreover, acquisitions do not necessarily rely on cash; they can involve stock exchanges, or even Lenovo could issue additional shares.

2. Acquisition Integration is an issue, but not a major one. Reasons include: Looking at Lenovo's series of acquisitions and integrations, including businesses in the U.S., Western Europe, Japan, and Brazil such as IBM ThinkPad and NEC, the chances of success are relatively high.

3. Regulation is a significant issue. This is beyond the individual capabilities of Lenovo Group. It pertains to whether the regulatory bodies of the countries where multinational corporations operate will agree to asset sales. Friends familiar with Lenovo's history may know that, originally, the acquisition of IBM's PC business almost fell through at the last moment. In today's era of integrated hardware and software, smartphones are not merely hardware products; their impacts extend far beyond commercial considerations.

Final Words:

Although Lenovo needs an overseas partner to leverage, looking at various acquisition cases in the business world, the entities being acquired often have more urgent reasons for selling, and these reasons can be multifaceted. For foreign companies, Lenovo might be a good buyer. This company has a relatively high success rate in acquisition integration, and for the acquired entities, it usually maintains independent brand operations instead of simply replacing them in a crude manner like HP did with Compaq.
