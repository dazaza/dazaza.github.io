What are the characteristics of the regionality of national architecture?

by anonymous on 2012-02-20 18:19:09

Where there are human beings, there is construction. The preparation for the intermediate level economist exam in construction always coexists with humanity. In "Han Feizi: Five Pests," it is written: "In ancient times, there were few people but many beasts. People could not win against wild animals, insects, and snakes, so a wise man came forth. He built nests from wood to avoid all kinds of harm." From this, we can infer that nest-dwelling was probably a primitive method of habitation adopted by human society. With the discovery of fire, humans began to live around fires in caves, expanding the range of activities in human society. It wasn't until the Yellow Emperor Xuanyuan taught everyone to build houses that people entered a new era of habitation, bidding farewell to wandering over mountains and rivers.

Starting from the Yanhuang period, Chinese civilization has been passed down for 4,680 years. During this time, culture absorbed various influences, resulting in diverse and splendid constructions, forming a unique atmosphere in Chinese architecture. Traditional dwellings undoubtedly best exemplify this atmosphere.

In contemporary China, rapid economic growth has led to a constant rise in construction, with all sorts of buildings suddenly appearing before the masses. European, Japanese, American, Singaporean, Hong Kong styles... truly "Suddenly, as if overnight spring breeze comes, thousands of buildings display their various forms." The gathering of these differently shaped constructions causes us to reflect on contemporary Chinese architecture.

Building TV towers, making houses messy, building soccer stadiums as bird nests, and music halls as bird eggs - what kind of architectural form is this? What actions, what logic? These performers fear being called unartistic, fearing honesty, insisting on making grotesque faces for others to see; originally ugly, they still heavily disguise themselves, fearing not to attract attention. Columns must be bent or slanted, tormenting people to death, always wanting to express individuality through so-called "personalized design." They lack the fundamentals of architectural science, thinking that there's no gravity under their feet. I don't know from which day it started, but the small caps on residential buildings have become small cantilevered boards. Recently, across the country, residential rooftops have become varied and flamboyant, these cosmetic tricks almost unrelated to the main body of architecture. Can't help but ask, what kind of art is this?

The development of architecture in China has undergone a process different from the West. Chinese architecture has formed its own unique system since the Shang Dynasty, lasting more than three thousand years. Ancient Chinese architectural theory, from Laozi's interpretation of architecture to pre-Qin classics like "Kao Gong Ji", Han Dynasty's "Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art", Tang Dynasty Wang Xiaotong's "Jigu Suanjing", Song Dynasty Qin Jiushao's "Mathematical Treatise in Nine Sections" and Li Jie's "Yingzao Fashi", Ming Dynasty's "Yuan Ye", Qing Dynasty's "Gongduan Yingzao Lu" to modern "Yingzao Fayuan", has provided detailed descriptions of ancient Chinese architectural theory and methods, serving as important treasures in our architectural field. However, the teaching of architectural knowledge has always been passed down from master to apprentice, father to son, leading to the extinction of skills upon the death of the master, hindering the development of architectural science. It wasn't until the early 20th century that the title and teaching methods of modern architects were introduced from the West, changing the above situation.

Due to the characteristics and limitations of China's architectural education model, along with social and economic factors affecting architectural creation, research and development of architectural creation theories and methods lagged behind Western countries for quite some time. The harsh environment for architectural creation, closed architectural BBSs, decades of unchanged structural systems in construction departments, and the slow commodification of housing have caused various mistakes in China's architectural creation over the past few decades. These mainly manifest as one-sided emphasis on traditional experience while neglecting methodological research; focusing on referencing historical materials while ignoring innovation in architectural creation thought and methods; emphasizing spatial composition, proportion, scale, and other sensory factors of architecture while neglecting the scientific aspects of architecture related to society, environment, culture, use, and technology, turning architecture into a vehicle for deliberately pursuing grandeur and uniqueness in personal emotions. This phenomenon is especially prominent in the current era of large-scale construction in China, where various foreign schools of thought flood in, and architects desperately want to become famous overnight. No wonder some works we consider very good are incomprehensible to foreigners.

"The task of architecture is nothing more than to give residents an extra sense of warmth when returning home." Dutch architect Aldo van Eyck described home design in this way. Architecture is for better living; anything else outside of this is merely auxiliary, yet much of the new architecture we see today is putting the cart before the horse, preparing for intermediate level economist exams in construction. For example, many so-called green communities only pursue an increase in green space ratio while neglecting deeper living concepts in architecture. Some buildings, despite having urban heating available, insist on imitating foreign individual heating systems, increasing costs for owners and wasting energy. Overseas neighborhoods adopt individual heating because they are far from urban residential areas, and using urban heating would incur significant additional costs. Therefore, green architecture should focus more on saving energy.

Architecture has a strong national character. Some developers, in order to demonstrate aristocratic style, transplant Western architecture directly into China, causing cultural disharmony, making people laugh or cry. Since the 1990s in urban real estate development, the trend of imitating European styles has become increasingly prevalent, reaching its peak around 1996, becoming a fashion actively or passively pursued by property owners, local officials, even urban planners and architects. Imitating European styles ranges from simply applying Western architectural ornaments or components to copying Western proportions and styles, and even seeking grand classical compositions in architectural clusters or urban planning. Taking Shanghai as an example, two-thirds of real estate advertisements in 1996 mentioned so-called "European-style charm" and "European artistic courtyard residences," gradually fading out by 2002. Foreign architecture on Chinese land may suit a minority with overseas backgrounds, but for a person who has never been abroad and is born and raised in China, facing Western noble lifestyles might initially seem curious, but over time will feel monotonous, like staying in a hotel abroad.

The uniqueness of architecture is based on its national character. Once disconnected from the land beneath, architecture becomes just a flashy shape. Many current Chinese architectures prioritize form over the essence of living. Italian architect Gregotti said: Contemporary architecture, whether Italian, French, or Spanish, cannot be confused, each has its distinct features, but this is not seen in China. Is this the sorrow of Chinese architecture, or superficial criticism? Some say the "Ten Great Buildings" of the 1950s represent a peak in national style architecture practice, while the 1980s "regaining the ancient capital's appearance" was a vulgar regression. Why do people naturally think of siheyuans when talking about Beijing? Why do residential houses differ? Each building is connected to the land's qi, local sunlight, sunshine, humidity, wind direction, vegetation... All these factors create the uniqueness of architecture. Houses in Hainan can face north, but not in Beijing; houses in Lingnan can connect inside and outside, but not in Northeast; houses in Shanghai can be built as villas, but not in Lhasa. Just like southern coconut trees cannot grow in cold northern climates, beauty must also mean survival. Shenzhen's Window of the World can condense global silhouettes, but they are just shadows; even if Buckingham Palace were copied exactly in China, it would still be fake. The reason why the Bund in Shanghai is famous is not because someone built a group of Western-style houses there, but because a group of foreigners once stayed there.

Chinese architecture must promote national culture on one hand and seek new breakthroughs on the other. We do not oppose returned students introducing new elements, but we oppose copying so-called avant-garde things. Truly excellent architecture should combine Chinese culture with the essence of architecture. Materials and structures can be modern, but the created atmosphere must be very "Chinese." Farewell to blind transplantation, let architecture return to its essence of living. Architects should study the environment, society, and the relationship between architecture and human activity with a normal and objective mindset, to achieve the perfect realm of harmony between architecture and nature in creation.

As China steadily steps onto the world stage of modern architecture, the level of Chinese architectural creation will increasingly attract global attention. Architects are no longer infatuated with the poetic charm of ancient Chinese architecture, but hope to stand out in the field of modern architectural creation. Chinese architects should create works that not only embody the philosophy of "harmony between heaven and humanity" since ancient times but also possess today's research environment and sustainable development creative concept and level that harmonizes with nature.

This article reflects on the emergence, development, and evolution of design concepts in China's architectural design circle since the reform and opening-up, emphasizing how to create architecture suitable for Chinese culture and returning to the essence of architecture under the ideological background of contemporary architectural design trends.
