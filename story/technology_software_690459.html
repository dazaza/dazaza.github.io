Two retired armed police soldiers robbed and killed six women and dumped the bodies

by xue94fwsh on 2012-03-05 10:22:08

During the rush hours, some young, pretty and fashionable women in Lanzhou took a taxi; however, they embarked on a one-way trip.

On December 15th at 10 o'clock, a beam of sunlight penetrated through the tightly closed windows and shone into the center of the No.1 Criminal Trial Chamber of the Intermediate People's Court of Lanzhou City, illuminating the hands of a young man dressed in orange prisoner clothes who was sitting in the defendant's seat, just like a spotlight on the stage.

The judge was reading out the verdict while this man kept rotating a steel ball on his cold handcuffs with his right thumb and index finger. The other prisoner standing next to him, who was more than half a head taller, remained motionless.

Their names were Cheng Dengpan and Wang Huijun, both born in 1984, former armed police officers, residing in Xichafa Town, Gaolan County. Cheng Dengpan contracted a taxi. They were charged with jointly murdering six female passengers, including police officers, businessmen, hotel attendants, toll collectors, and students.

Several people went missing.

At 2 PM on December 29, 2009, middle-aged female businessman Xu Aijun parted ways with her husband Wang Hongwei outside their home near Peili Square in Anning District and took a taxi to handle affairs in Chengguan District. Xu Aijun independently operated a plastic steel aluminum alloy door and window factory, was 44 years old, and had a 6-year-old son.

At 6 PM that day, Wang Hongwei received a call from the teacher at Gansu Normal University Kindergarten where his son attended, saying that the child's mother hadn't picked up the child and asking him to hurry and pick him up.

Wang Hongwei felt very strange: his wife was a loving mother who would always pick up her son from school on time regardless of circumstances. After hanging up the phone with the teacher, he tried to contact his wife but failed to get through. Thereafter, Xu Aijun did not return home all night.

The couple had lived together for 11 years, and such a situation had never occurred. Early the next morning, Wang Hongwei reported the case to the Peili Police Station. The police said that since the disappearance was less than 24 hours, they could not file a case.

Where had his wife gone? Wang Hongwei was puzzled. Several days later, as his wife still hadn't returned, he became anxious and searched everywhere with Xu Aijun's family, but found nothing.

Almost simultaneously, there were multiple cases of women going missing in Lanzhou city. In newspapers, on the streets, and on forums, numerous "Missing Person Notices" appeared:

At 6:40 PM on December 14, 2009, Zhao Xin, a 21-year-old female clerk at Xiaoxihu Police Station, parted ways with her sister at the entrance of Gansu Provincial People's Hospital, crossed the zebra crossing, and disappeared in the crowd;

In the afternoon of December 20, 2009, Hu Baoxia, a 27-year-old woman from Wen'an County, Langfang, Hebei, who was on a business trip in Lanzhou, disappeared after leaving the "Hao Xiaozai" hair salon on Gaolan Road;

At around 6 PM on December 28, 2009, Yang Xiuli, a 24-year-old female staff member at the Liushu Toll Station of Gansu Province Expressway, disappeared near Zhengmao Hailong Garden on Yanxi Road;

At around 9 PM on December 30, 2009, Gao Xiaochun, a 23-year-old waitress at the Ouya Seafood Hotel at Xiguan Crossroads in Lanzhou, disappeared after work;

At 5:30 PM on New Year's Day 2010, Li Jing, an 18-year-old student at Tourism School, bought a ticket at Lanzhou East Bus Station on Pingliang Road and then went to Xizhan to buy clothes; her mother could no longer get through to her by phone;

...

These missing person cases shared a common feature: they basically involved young women and mostly occurred during the daytime.

Solving the Case with the Help of a Dry Well

The successive disappearances of women alarmed the Lanzhou police, leading them to form a special task force to deal with the situation. Investigations began based on the times and locations of the disappearances.

When Li Jing went missing, she told her mother that the bus was too crowded, so she decided to take a taxi; Yang Xiuli mentioned to her friend before disappearing that she would arrive within 10 minutes, implying she might also have taken a taxi. The police decided to focus on taxis as a key suspect.

What broke the case was a segment of surveillance footage taken when Gao Xiaochun went missing. According to a close friend of Gao Xiaochun who informed the police: she saw Gao Xiaochun get into a taxi. Surveillance video from the area also captured Gao Xiaochun entering a taxi.

Although the license plate number in the surveillance footage was unclear, the police cross-referenced the footage with GPS location information from all the city's taxis and discovered that driver Cheng Dengpan, driving a light blue taxi with the license plate number "Gan A86664," was a major suspect.

Cheng Dengpan, originally from Tuancun Village, Xichafa Town, Gaolan County, Lanzhou City, was born in November 1984, completed junior high school, served in the Armed Police in Xinjiang, and since March 2009, had been operating a taxi leased from Weilong Taxi Company, driving either day or night shifts with his father Cheng Shineng.

At around 6 AM on January 29, 2010, the police arrested Cheng Dengpan in a residential compound in Xiaoxihu, Qilihe District, Lanzhou City. Cheng confessed to driving the vehicle and collaborating with Wang Huijun to rob and murder several female passengers. Wang Huijun was Cheng's fellow townsman and comrade-in-arms. At around 7 AM that day, Wang Huijun was also captured in his rented apartment near Huachen Building in Chengguan District.

That day, the police led the two suspects to a dry well located on the south side of Sanchagou Mountain Road, about 60 kilometers north of Lanzhou City, in Cheng's hometown of Tuancun Village. The two confessed that the murdered female passengers were thrown into the well.

The dry well was found. It was a round earthen structure, 1.7 meters in diameter and 30 meters deep. After observation by the police using a telephoto lens, a human foot was spotted. The police immediately requested assistance from the Special Operations Team of the Lanzhou Fire Brigade to retrieve the bodies.

Two soldiers descended into the bottom of the well and successively retrieved six female corpses. According to forensic identification, one of the victims died from traumatic and hemorrhagic shock, while the remaining five were strangled to death by others, resulting in mechanical asphyxiation.

Murder Plan

Why did public transportation become a killing ground? Why did taxi driver Cheng Dengpan commit murder? On June 30, the case was heard in the Intermediate People's Court of Lanzhou. According to Cheng Dengpan's confession, the reason he killed these unarmed women was due to poverty at home. His relationship with his girlfriend was opposed by her parents, and under great pressure, she eventually left him. He initially wanted to kill his girlfriend's parents but felt sorry for her and lacked the courage to act.

Subsequently, Cheng Dengpan decided to kill innocent women as a way to vent his frustration. He approached his comrade Wang Huijun, proposing collaboration under the guise of "getting rich through robbery." Wang Huijun, who was unemployed, agreed.

They devised a detailed plan: Cheng Dengpan would be responsible for driving and selecting targets, while Wang Huijun would primarily wield a knife to intimidate. Their targets were young, beautiful, and fashionably dressed women. Once the customers got into the car, Cheng would notify Wang via phone and find an opportunity to meet up under the pretext of "carpooling." At a suitable location, Cheng would play a specific song as a signal to prompt Wang to take action, thereby carrying out the robbery. Cheng had already planned to dispose of the bodies in a dry well near his hometown, believing that its small opening and depth made it impossible to discover.

After committing four robbery-murders, the duo spent 10,000 yuan to purchase a silver-white Fukan car with the license plate number "Gan AG4392" to facilitate nighttime disposal of the bodies.

During the trial, the families of the six victims filed a civil claim for over 5.17 million yuan, and simultaneously sued Weilong Taxi Company and the merged Benma Taxi Company along with the supervisory authority, the Lanzhou Transportation Management Office, demanding joint liability. During the trial, Weilong Taxi Company claimed no responsibility but offered, out of moral consideration, to voluntarily pay 300,000 yuan in consolation money to the families of the six deceased.

On December 15, after deliberation, the Intermediate People's Court of Lanzhou ruled that both Cheng Dengpan and Wang Huijun were guilty of intentional homicide and robbery, with extremely serious crimes and a despicable nature. Both were sentenced to death in the first trial, with lifelong deprivation of political rights. The two defendants were ordered to jointly compensate the six victims' losses amounting to over 1.6 million yuan.

Regarding the request for taxi companies and other units to bear joint liability, the court believed that although Cheng Dengpan was a driver for Weilong Taxi Company and the offenses occurred in an operational taxi, the company granted legal and proper operational permissions. The criminal acts exceeded the scope of duties authorized by the taxi company and were deemed personal actions, thus not bearing joint liability. Benma Company is another independent legal entity, unrelated to this case, and therefore does not bear joint liability. The management of the taxi industry by the supervisory authorities does not fall within the scope of civil litigation attached to criminal cases.

Since neither of the two perpetrators had compensation capabilities, the families of the six victims pinned their hopes for compensation on the joint compensation from the taxi company. The judgment by the Intermediate People's Court of Lanzhou that "the taxi company has no responsibility" enraged the husband of the first victim, Zhao Xin, who tore up the verdict as soon as he exited the courtroom.

Government Responsibility

The key to the successful commission of the six murders was the collaboration between the two. The taxi driver picked up additional passengers mid-journey, allowing his accomplice to board and hold the female passenger hostage with a knife at the backseat, leading to her murder.

Why were there so many instances of carpooling? Why didn't single women take precautions upon seeing unfamiliar men boarding?

A journalist from Lanzhou said that the local government bears significant responsibility in this matter.

In Lanzhou, there are approximately 7,000 taxis. However, what puzzles all outsiders is: here, taxis operate on odd-even license plate restrictions, whereas private cars are limited to only 1/5 daily (two numbers out of 0-9), causing severe shortages in transport capacity. A local taxi driver stated that this peculiar regulation restricting public transportation has been in effect for over ten years and continues to this day.

"Black cabs are rampant. Many citizens detest carpooling and oppose it, yet they have no choice but to accept the reality of carpooling." A journalist from Lanzhou media said, "If there had been no carpooling, perhaps these six lives wouldn't have been lost. But the government hasn't reflected on this."

What troubles the families of the victims most is that the local government required the media to remain silent, suppressing reports on the case. Official statements were issued without allowing coverage of the incident. "It's said that reporting it would harm local economic development. Over the past two years, which television station or newspaper has reported this major case?" Wang Hongwei said, "If the police had filed the case earlier and widely publicized it through the media, warning citizens to be cautious, I believe the subsequent cases might not have happened."

Hu Baosong, the younger brother of victim Hu Baoxia, said, "My sister was murdered on December 20th. We arrived in Lanzhou on the 25th to look for her but found no trace. When we reported the case to the police station, their response was: she's not dumb or crazy, she probably went somewhere to play. Go home and wait; we can't file a case." "Due to the late filing of the case by the police, many people died. If the police had filed and solved the case earlier, such massive casualties wouldn't have occurred. Who will bear the responsibility?"
