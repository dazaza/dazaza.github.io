The Impact of International Economic Development on China's Economy

by hcxstry on 2012-03-01 14:41:38

Abstract: International economic integration is a fundamental trend in the social and economic development of today's world. In the process of global economic globalization, China should accelerate domestic institutional reform and construction to make it more adaptable to global market rules; fully leverage comparative advantages to actively expand foreign trade; focus on technological development and human resource strategies; establish a globalization development strategy, actively cultivate multinational corporations, and further promote the development of international economic integration.

Keywords: International economy; Chinese economy; Impact

1 Opportunities brought by international economic integration to the development of China's foreign trade

1.1 Opportunities brought by international economic integration to China's economy

International economic integration has brought historical opportunities to China, specifically manifested as: (1) International economic integration helps China utilize capital exports, advanced technology and equipment, and management experience from developed countries. (2) Economic globalization prompts China to continuously adjust its industrial structure and export commodity structure, thereby accelerating economic development. (3) The development of economic globalization and the gradual increase in the proportion of international trade help China fully leverage its comparative advantages to actively engage in international trade for greater benefits. (4) Economic globalization aids in the development of China’s multinational corporations.

1.2 Opportunities brought by international economic integration to attracting investment in China's foreign trade sector

International economic integration has built a bridge for the development of foreign trade in developing countries like China. It clearly shows that it is beneficial for developing countries to utilize foreign investment and invest abroad. From the current trends in global investment development, China faces many favorable conditions for attracting investment: First, the trend toward global economic integration is strong, and multinational companies are becoming the main business model for large companies in various countries, leading to more frequent flows of capital between countries. Second, due to China’s currently good economic growth, a stable and rapid growth is expected, which will attract a significant amount of surplus capital internationally. Third, the Southeast Asian financial crisis caused a rapid decline in the solvency and confidence of many emerging countries, lowering their external credit ratings. In the coming years or even decades, foreign investment flowing into these countries will significantly decrease. The reduction in credit ratings for these countries will relatively increase China's competitiveness, giving it an increasingly advantageous position in absorbing Western investments.

1.3 Opportunities brought by international economic integration to the development of China's foreign trade

After international economic integration, China continuously opened up, thus expanding its foreign trade. After nearly 20 years of reform practice, foreign economic and trade activities have become an important part of China's national economy and the most important growth point for China's economy. In recent years, the annual growth rate of China's gross national product is approximately 7%-8%, with about two percentage points of GDP growth driven by foreign trade. That is to say, around one-quarter of China's annual economic growth is achieved through foreign trade. In this context, economic globalization has promoted the growth of China's international trade, and the trade structure has also changed. Economic globalization has accelerated the flow of capital in China and shown diversified trends. Due to the convenience of transferring and settling funds internationally, favorable conditions have been created for further development of China's trade. At the same time, it allows China to allocate its surplus and deficit funds globally, turning idle funds into profitable capital, allowing China's trade to not be completely constrained by domestic savings and capital accumulation, thereby enabling better development of foreign trade.

2 Impacts of international economic integration on the development of China's foreign trade

For China, economic integration is like a "double-edged sword," providing unprecedented opportunities while also bringing numerous problems. This poses serious challenges for China.

2.1 Impact of international economic integration on China's economy

First, economic globalization increases the likelihood of fluctuations in China's economy. Under the backdrop of economic globalization, international economic fluctuations such as inflation, deflation, and financial crises will affect the country through international economic transmission mechanisms. China's market development is not yet sufficient, and its economic structure is relatively fragile, making it more susceptible to external adverse factors. If domestic policies are inappropriate, there could be a relatively severe financial crisis or even an overall economic crisis. Second, economic globalization will cause certain impacts on domestic industries and markets. Economic globalization connects China's domestic market with the international market, making it increasingly impossible to protect domestic industries through closed markets. In the past, China established many industries without comparative advantages through strict trade protection. With the massive influx of foreign goods and direct investment, these industries will face significant competition from foreign competitors, and some industries are inevitably going to be phased out. Third, economic globalization may jeopardize China's economic security. Specifically, this is reflected in: (1) Economic globalization places China in an unprecedentedly intense international competition, gradually losing its original comparative advantages. (2) Global free trade rules open China's doors, significantly weakening its ability to protect its own industries and markets. (3) Important economic sectors in China may be manipulated by multinational enterprises from developed countries, placing its small and imperfect market and fragile national industry in difficult situations. (4) The instability of international financial capital flows, especially the existence of large amounts of speculative capital, makes China's small and imperfect financial market more vulnerable to shocks.
