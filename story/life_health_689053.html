What is the relationship between osteophyte formation and osteoporosis?

by po13929357028 on 2012-03-03 17:23:24

Clinically, we often encounter such problems: patients suffer from osteoporosis and doctors recommend calcium supplements. However, after taking calcium supplements, radiographic examinations reveal that the patients have developed bone hyperplasia. So, what is the relationship between bone hyperplasia and osteoporosis? The orthopedic experts of Kunming Kunlu Hospital answer as follows:

Bone hyperplasia and osteoporosis are manifestations of the aging of bones and joints in middle-aged and elderly people. However, bone hyperplasia is by no means caused by osteoporosis. Instead, it is due to the long-term wear and tear on the human body's bones and joints, especially those bearing heavy loads and with more activities, such as knees and spines. After years of wear, the articular cartilage loses its normal smoothness and becomes rough. At the same time, the joint capsule, ligaments, and tendons around the joint become damaged and bleed. The body repairs this chronic wear and tear, and the way of repair is through hyperplasia, i.e., bone spurs.

Is bone hyperplasia and osteoporosis exclusive to the elderly?

Although bone hyperplasia and osteoporosis are inevitable phenomena of bone aging in middle-aged and elderly people, they do not occur in all middle-aged and elderly individuals. They are related to different races, different social environments, social experiences, as well as personal lifestyles and dietary habits. For example, middle-aged and elderly people who frequently engage in physical labor, outdoor activities, or consume fresh vegetarian diets have fewer occurrences of bone hyperplasia and osteoporosis. On the other hand, those who smoke frequently, drink excessively, or remain bedridden for a long time or participate less in outdoor activities have a higher incidence rate.

The essence of osteoporosis

The essence of osteoporosis refers to the reduction of bone mass. It is a complex result influenced by genetic, hormonal, and nutritional factors. Calcium and vitamin D deficiency, hyperthyroidism, inappropriate use of glucocorticoids, smoking, excessive drinking, and prolonged bed rest can all cause osteoporosis. Therefore, osteoporosis is not a single disease but is caused by several different reasons. For instance, sex hormones are important factors in the synthesis of human bones. Hence, when women go through menopause, the decrease or cessation of female hormone secretion severely affects bone synthesis. In fact, the reduction or loss of human bone mass begins at the age of 30-40, and it happens faster in middle-aged and elderly people.

Calcium supplementation cannot treat bone hyperplasia

Bones within the body are living tissues that continue metabolic activities as long as life exists. Calcium and phosphorus are the main components of bones. Under normal circumstances, they are concentrated in the form of bone salts in bones and teeth, maintaining their hardness. A very small part of calcium and phosphorus exist in blood and muscles and other soft tissues, keeping an appropriate ratio to maintain bone health. For middle-aged and elderly people, since their organs, like bones and joints, also age, especially with reduced secretion of gastric acid, pepsin, and salivary glands, as well as decreased activity of digestive enzymes, their intake, digestion, and absorption of calcium are seriously affected. At this point, calcium in the bones will be released to maintain a positive calcium balance. Therefore, it can be said that the essence of osteoporosis refers to the release and reduction of calcium in bones. Thus, moderate calcium supplementation is beneficial for preventing and treating osteoporosis in middle-aged and elderly people, but calcium supplementation cannot treat bone hyperplasia.
