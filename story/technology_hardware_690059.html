However, it is important to note that many students overly focus on grammatical correctness during their conversational training, leading to situations where they have to think for a long time before saying a sentence. In reality, spoken English does not demand high grammatical accuracy, as even native English speakers sometimes make "slips of the tongue." The purpose of language is communication; grammar serves this purpose. Overemphasizing grammar can create psychological barriers, but completely ignoring grammar is also incorrect, as it is an important component of language communication skills. D. Hymes (1971) pointed out that being merely grammatically correct is insufficient; one must also consider whether the language use is socially appropriate. Hymes' viewpoint has received relatively widespread agreement in the field of foreign/second language teaching. Canale and Swain (1980) expanded on this idea, dividing communicative competence into four aspects: grammatical competence, sociolinguistic competence, discourse competence, and strategic competence. It's not just about using linguistic knowledge and skills to understand grammatically correct sentences, but more importantly, whether one can apply these languages and skills in a given situation to solve practical problems and achieve the goal of communication. The level of this communicative competence is an important indicator of foreign language proficiency.

Principal Zhang believed that listening and reading serve as input sources for spoken language, helping us gradually accumulate various language points (idioms, slang, sentence patterns, etc.). However, it is important to note that when selecting materials, the standard of "moderate difficulty" should be followed. Many students approach English learning with the mindset of "achieving everything at once" or "rapid advancement," starting with China Daily in hand and "Standard VOA" in ear at the beginner stage. As a result, they become disheartened from not understanding the newspaper and discouraged from not comprehending the news, eventually losing confidence in learning English over time, leading to a counterproductive outcome. American psycholinguist and teaching methodology educator Krashen once pointed out: When learning a foreign language, the key issue to focus on is CI (Comprehensible Input). Simply put, the difficulty level of the information being inputted should be "1+1," meaning slightly more challenging than your current level, taking one step rather than two steps forward. Therefore, materials that are 80% understandable are the best choice.
