UN Human Rights Council passes resolution condemning Syria

by lovet9v9 on 2012-03-02 11:10:13

The 19th meeting of the UN Human Rights Council passed a resolution on February 1 condemning the deterioration of human rights in Syria, expressing "grave concern," and criticizing the Syrian government for human rights violations. However, the resolution made no mention of demands from Syrian opposition groups. On the same day, the Syrian Interior Ministry announced its willingness to negotiate with Valerie Amos, the UN Under-Secretary-General for Humanitarian Affairs, regarding her visit to Syria.

Meanwhile, the largest external Syrian opposition group, the [Syrian National Council], announced the establishment of a military bureau aimed at uniting internal opposition armed groups like the [Free Syrian Army] to combat the Bashar al-Assad government. This move has raised concerns about pushing Syria further into civil war.

The resolution made no mention of Syrian opposition groups

The 19th session of the UN Human Rights Council held in Geneva passed a resolution on February 1 that expressed "grave concern" over the worsening human rights situation in Syria, criticizing the Syrian government for human rights violations while making no mention of Syrian opposition groups' demands.

The resolution was passed with 37 votes in favor, 3 against, and 3 abstentions. Russia, Cuba, and China voted against it.

The resolution heavily referenced the recent Friends of Syria conference in Tunisia, with content considered unbalanced, applying unilateral pressure on the Syrian government.

After the opening of the 19th session of the Human Rights Council on February 27, an emergency debate was held on the Syrian issue. The Syrian representative criticized the meeting for not consulting the Syrian side beforehand, accusing some countries of manipulating concepts like "civilian protection" and "humanitarian intervention" for political reasons, leading to the Syrian delegation's withdrawal from the meeting.

During the emergency debate, some countries condemned human rights and humanitarian law violations in Syria, calling for clear messages to be sent to the Syrian government and people. Many delegations pointed out that military intervention would not restore peace, believing that only domestic political processes could resolve the crisis peacefully in the long term.

After the emergency debate, the Russian representative proposed a vote on the resolution, considering it politically motivated and unhelpful in resolving the Syrian issue. The Cuban representative noted the resolution was unbalanced, ignoring human rights violations by Syrian opposition armed groups mentioned by the Independent International Commission of Inquiry on Syria authorized by the Human Rights Council.

Annan urged all parties to stop violence

On February 29, UN-Arab League Joint Special Envoy for Syria Kofi Annan urged all parties involved in the Syrian conflict to immediately cease violent actions and ensure humanitarian aid could enter Syria smoothly.

Annan met with UN Secretary-General Ban Ki-moon that day. Afterward, Annan stated that a crucial point for the international community to successfully resolve the Syrian crisis is "[to have one coordinated mechanism, namely the responsibilities jointly assigned to me by the UN and the Arab League]." He indicated he would work with the international community to form a peaceful resolution respecting the wishes of the Syrian people, ultimately achieving stability in Syria.

Annan was appointed as the UN-Arab League Joint Special Envoy for Syria last week. He said he would soon depart for the Middle East region to consult widely with relevant parties and seek entry into Syria.

Annan said the message he would convey to the Syrian government was "[very clear]—violence must stop immediately, and humanitarian aid must enter Syria." He said, "For the Syrian people, resolving the crisis through dialogue as soon as possible is currently the way forward."

Annan served as UN Secretary-General from 1997 to 2006. After leaving office, he successfully mediated the 2008 Kenyan election crisis.

Arab League denies arming Syrian opposition

On February 1, Arab League Secretary-General Nabil al-Araby denied any involvement in arming Syrian opposition groups in Cairo.

Al-Araby said during a press conference at the Arab League headquarters that certain countries had proposed arming the Syrian opposition, but the Arab League had no connection to this and had not issued any related decisions. He reiterated the Arab League's support for non-violent solutions.

Earlier that day, the Kuwaiti National Assembly passed a resolution recommending the government assist in arming the [Free Syrian Army]. In similarly Sunni-majority Gulf monarchies Qatar and Saudi Arabia, the idea of arming Syrian opposition groups gained popularity among political elites.

Al-Araby also held a welcoming meeting with the visiting Norwegian Foreign Minister on February 1. He stated that he did not believe the use of force could solve problems. If international efforts to resolve the Syrian issue remained ineffective, arming the Syrian opposition might be an option. However, currently, he had confidence in the international community's efforts to address the Syrian issue.

The Norwegian Foreign Minister said that Norway currently had no plans to arm the Syrian opposition, and whether to do so should be decided by the UN Security Council.

■ Syria

Government willing to negotiate humanitarian official's visit

Regarding reports that Syria refused the visit of UN Under-Secretary-General for Humanitarian Affairs Valerie Amos, the Syrian Foreign Ministry issued a statement on February 1 clarifying that Syria was willing to negotiate the visit date with Amos.

The statement said that Amos's previous press release mentioning Syria's refusal of the visit request was regrettable. "We have welcomed Amos's visit to Syria and are willing to cooperate with her to determine the visit date."

"However, she suddenly visited (the Middle East) region and requested a visit to Syria, but the dates she proposed were not suitable for us. We are willing to continue discussions to determine a mutually convenient time for the visit."

The Syrian Foreign Ministry spokesperson Jihad Makdissi said the previous day that as long as humanitarian duties respected Syria's sovereignty, Syria welcomed them. If humanitarian duties carried political objectives, "we completely reject them."

Government forces control conflict central area

According to a report by AFP citing Syrian security department officials, government forces fully controlled the Amr district of Homs, the center of conflict, on February 1, with the "last stronghold of the opposition having been taken." However, the leader of the [Free Syrian Army], Riad al-Assad, said that the [Free Syrian Army] and all other armed personnel had implemented a "tactical retreat."

The Syrian media quoted security department personnel the previous day saying that the Syrian army launched a large-scale clean-up operation in the Amr district, investigating all basements and passages, searching for "armed elements" and weapons ammunition. Several anti-government armed personnel died in the conflict, and some surrendered their arms.

In addition, according to the private Syrian news agency, government forces attacked a group of anti-government armed personnel attempting to enter Homs from Lebanon on February 29, killing three "armed elements" and injuring several others. One Syrian soldier was injured in the conflict.

Opposition sets up military bureau seeking unity

The [National Council] announced the establishment of a military bureau on February 29, stating that the military bureau would track opposition armed groups, organize and unite their combatants under unified command, define their offensive tasks. At the same time, the [National Council] would politically supervise them, coordinate their activities, and align them with the "overall strategy."

The chairman of the [National Council], Burhan Ghalioun, said at a press conference held the next day in the French capital Paris that the establishment of the military bureau was agreed upon by all Syrian opposition armed organizations, "We will function like a defense ministry."

He said that the domestic opposition armed groups would be centrally commanded and supervised by the military bureau, with the leader of the [Free Syrian Army], Riad, and the former government army general Mustafa Sheikh, who established the [Higher Revolutionary Committee], agreeing to join the military bureau.

The headquarters of these two opposition armed organizations are both located in neighboring Turkey. Ghalioun said: "The location of the military bureau's operations will be as close as possible to the action site, probably set up in Turkey."

"We know that some countries have indicated their willingness" to arm the Syrian opposition, Ghalioun said, the military bureau would play a "linking" role, transporting foreign-supported equipment into the country to avoid direct delivery of weapons by "specific countries."

The military bureau "will determine which weapons are necessary and for what missions," as well as from which countries these weapons can be obtained. However, the [National Council] has not yet received any "clear proposals" for foreign weapon supplies.

The [National Council] had previously called for arming the Syrian opposition groups but attempted for the first time to unify various opposition armed personnel militarily. AP believed this would push Syria further into civil war.

Some opposition figures had criticized the [National Council] for not openly supporting the [Free Syrian Army]'s armed overthrow of the Assad government. According to Ghalioun, the "rapidly changing reality" prompted this organization to join hands with the [Free Syrian Army].

However, when asked if Syria would head towards civil war, Ghalioun tried to downplay the risk of escalating conflict. "The task of the military bureau is to protect the peaceful protesters and civilians," he said, "We hope to control the use of weapons so that a civil war does not occur. Our goal is to help prevent a civil war."

■ Links

Switzerland closes embassy

The Swiss Federal Government announced on February 29 that due to security reasons, Switzerland officially closed its embassy in Damascus, Syria.

The Swiss Foreign Ministry stated that Swiss citizens living in Syria had been informed of the closure of the Swiss embassy in Syria, and consular services would be handled by the Swiss embassy in Lebanon.

There are approximately 150 to 180 Swiss citizens working and living in Syria, most of whom hold dual Swiss and Syrian citizenship. Switzerland recalled its ambassador to Syria last August and urged Swiss citizens to leave Syria last November.

UK withdraws diplomatic staff

British Foreign Secretary William Hague announced in the morning of February 1 that the UK had withdrawn all diplomatic staff from Syria and closed the British Embassy located in the Syrian capital, Damascus.

In his statement to Parliament, Hague said that due to security considerations, the UK had suspended all activities of the embassy in Syria, and British diplomatic staff left Syria on February 29 and would return to the UK in the short term.

Hague stated that the UK maintained the operation of the Damascus embassy to maintain contact with all sides in Syria and understand the domestic situation, but the deteriorating security situation now posed a threat to the embassy and staff. He requested British nationals still in Syria to seek assistance from embassies or consulates of other EU countries.

At the beginning of last month, the UK temporarily recalled its ambassador to Syria to "discuss" the Syrian issue. Hague indicated that the closure of the embassy would not affect the UK's continued pressure on the Syrian government. The UK would continue to work with the "Friends of Syria" and EU members to exert diplomatic and economic pressure on the Syrian government and support the Syrian opposition in "uniting."
