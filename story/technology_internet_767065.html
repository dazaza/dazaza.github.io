Research finds that most enterprises have blindness in investing in big data projects.

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 17:33:50

Today, the renowned blog site Readwrite published an analysis article stating that although big data is currently a hot topic, most companies that have invested in this field have not yet received reasonable returns, let alone positive returns. The content of the article is as follows:

As investments continue, the performance of big data is significantly below market expectations. Under the hype of the media, many companies plunged into this market without fully understanding how to extract value from big data. According to the latest analytical report, the value most companies obtain from big data is far below their expected goals, and they haven't even recouped their investments.

For every dollar invested in big data, only a few cents in returns are being generated.

So, how low are the returns those companies are getting? According to Wikibon's research, they aren't just low—they're extremely low.

Wikibon's findings reveal that 46% of big data investors can barely sustain their investment projects, while 2% of the companies have completely failed, losing all their investments.

However, this does not mean that big data is a scam destined for failure. That is not the case.

As Wikibon points out, one of the biggest reasons why these companies' big data projects fail is the lack of specific and measurable business applications when investing in big data technologies like Hadoop. They hear about a project related to big data and immediately invest money without actually knowing what their expected goals are. Market research company Gartner also reached the same conclusion through its analysis.

Hadoop acquired but data lost

For many companies, big data is equivalent to Hadoop, which one IT executive described as an "unsupervised digital junkyard." Julien Simon, Vice President of Engineering Technology at Criteo, said: "Big data may be vast, but it lacks actual data."

We store this data without knowing why we need it, and eventually, the collected data becomes some obscure and incomprehensible noise within our entire database. As famous statistician Nate Silver said:

"If the amount of information in the world is increasing by 2.5 trillion bytes per day, the quantity of useful information certainly isn't keeping pace. Most of the added information is just noise, and it grows faster than the useful information. There are too many hypotheses to test, so much of the information is useless—a relatively stable objective truth."

In other words, adding more data doesn't solve the problems of big data. Usually, it’s the cause of the problem and one of the key reasons why many companies cannot derive significant value from their big data investments.

What does success in big data look like?

According to Wikibon's research, the best big data projects are typically not initiated by the IT industry but are driven by business line departments, such as marketing departments. These projects focus on small applications rather than strategic solutions. "They fully utilize internal expertise and are very clear about what they can achieve with the resources they currently have," the article states.

These types of projects usually start small and gradually scale up after achieving initial results.

To avoid blindly investing in big data projects, we recommend you take this approach. Since all the best big data technologies are open-source, you can try them out before committing real funds and quickly find the big data technology that suits you best.
