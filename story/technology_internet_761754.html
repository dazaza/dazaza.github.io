Why Facebook Paid So Much for Instagram

by geekzhang on 2013-05-09 00:39:18

In the spring of 2012, Silicon Valley was shocked by Facebook's acquisition of Instagram for a staggering $1 billion, just a year and a half after its launch. To many, Instagram seemed like a lucky gold prospector who struck it rich, but only co-founder and CEO Kevin Systrom truly understood the twists and turns behind this success—it was far from the easy path that outsiders perceived. Systrom had missed several invaluable opportunities, including at least two chances to join Facebook and Twitter, as well as a lifeline that could have turned his fortunes around.

There is no photograph capturing the moment when Kevin Systrom's world changed forever. If there were, it might depict a tall, handsome man with dark hair leaning against a bench near a California train station, lost in thought. The stillness of the earth seems to envelop the swirling thoughts in his mind, pausing the flow of air around him.

It was an afternoon in April when this man nearing 30 waited for his business partner Mike Krieger, who was coming from Los Angeles. But Systrom seemed oblivious to the changes in sunlight and shadow around him; he had just left Zuckerberg’s nearby home and was still immersed in their previous conversation.

Systrom kept reflecting on the terms offered by Facebook’s founder and CEO: a $1 billion acquisition of Instagram, with $300 million in cash and another $700 million in Facebook stock. For the small team of Instagram—only 13 employees working out of a cramped office space just over a year and a half since its launch—this offer was excessively generous, almost incomprehensible.

However, Instagram was not without merit. In a year and a half, it had garnered thirty million iPhone users through its photo enhancement and sharing services. Its Android version launched just a week before Systrom's meeting with Zuckerberg and gained a million registered users in one day. Even without a clear monetization strategy, venture capitalists eagerly sought to participate in Instagram's next round of financing.

Instagram's valuation at the time was $500 million. Yet, Zuckerberg doubled this figure, leaving Systrom deep in thought.

Kevin Systrom's early life could be described as worry-free. Born in Holliston, a suburb of Boston, he grew up in a town populated by intelligent, ambitious middle-class parents raising equally smart and driven children.

Systrom was one of them. After graduating from Middlesex School, he smoothly entered Stanford University to major in Management Science and Engineering, all while being deeply fascinated by Silicon Valley. During this period, I always dreamed of starting an internet company to quickly become wealthy.

During his time at Stanford, he interned at a company that later became the globally renowned Twitter. After finishing his internship, he joined Google, then moved to Nextstop, a travel guide startup, a year later before resigning shortly thereafter. He then began his own entrepreneurial journey, developing Burbn, the precursor to Instagram, which was a location-based service with gaming elements.

Compared to Systrom, Instagram's other founder, Krieger, had a more traditional career as a tech professional. After graduating from Stanford, Krieger worked as an engineer and user experience designer at Meebo, a popular social media platform at the time. However, when Krieger first arrived in the U.S. in 2004, his aspiration wasn't to become a tech professional but a journalist, so he majored in Symbolic Systems, combining computer science and cognitive science—a field also studied by Yahoo CEO Marissa Mayer and LinkedIn co-founder Reid Hoffman.

According to Systrom, the other Instagram founder often overlooked by the media spotlight, Krieger, played a significant role as the soul of the team. The programs Krieger wrote for Instagram clearly demonstrated this.

The encounter between Systrom and Krieger lacked any dramatic flair. They had known each other during college and occasionally met at coffee shops in Los Angeles. "I had already met Kevin many times, and we would frequently exchange ideas, such as when I got something cool and geeky, I'd ask him, 'What do you think of this thing?' I believe our understanding of each other was established during those moments. I knew we were both interested in challenging things beyond our capabilities," Krieger said.

Until one day, troubled by the development issues of Burbn, Systrom approached Krieger and asked, "Hey, this time I want to really make Burbn happen. Would you be willing to be my co-founder?" Krieger immediately showed great interest, recalling, "A picture formed in my mind—me and Systrom somewhere in Los Angeles, working on what we loved."

Subsequently, Burbn received $250,000 in funding each from Andreessen Horowitz and Steve Anderson of Baseline Ventures. However, this did not alleviate Systrom's worries. At the time, Burbn was indistinguishable from other hot LBS check-in services, even its slogan seemed lackluster: "Burbn is a new way to communicate and share with the real world."

What disheartened Systrom even more was the news that Nextstop, where he had once worked, was acquired by Facebook. In other words, Systrom had twice missed opportunities to enter large corporations. "First, I missed the boat to Twitter, then I missed Facebook," Systrom said.

As with many startups, after Krieger confirmed his decision to join Burbn, Systrom expressed his desire to redesign Burbn, essentially abandoning everything about Burbn, including its original idea, and starting anew. Silicon Valley has a term for this phenomenon: pivot, a polite way to refer to companies that undo and redo their plans.

Systrom and Krieger began brainstorming ways to reconstruct Burbn. They eventually decided to create a separate independent service, giving it a semi-joking name Codename (meaning code name). Most importantly, they realized that developing desktop applications was not a wise choice, so they chose to focus solely on mobile apps, specifically designing iOS apps for the newly released iPhone 4 by Apple.

It was during this time that their thinking about the product underwent a qualitative change: why prioritize check-in services with photo uploads as an optional feature, instead of making photo uploads the main feature and check-ins secondary? Systrom said.

Systrom's long-standing passion for photography laid the groundwork for this seemingly sudden insight. When he was studying at Middlesex, he was already the president of the photography club. "I was practically born for photography; I love this art from the depths of my heart. It gives me more satisfaction than creating things with my hands because photography is so unpredictable. Different parameters can produce entirely different worlds." He used his Nikon camera to capture various artistic scenes—from the world reflected in car windows to the lesser-known side of Chinatown.

During his sophomore winter break, Systrom traveled to Florence, continuing to take photos. This time, however, his teacher convinced him to abandon his Nikon and use the Holga LOMO camera, which later influenced Instagram's LOMO-style filters. In Florence, Systrom also experimented with different chemical elements to develop photos, such as using selenium to produce purple-toned photographs.

In the busy summer of 2010, Systrom and Krieger gradually transformed their notebook ideas into app features. Both were extremely busy, to the point that Systrom could only afford a cheap vacation with his girlfriend Nicole at Todos Santos Art Village.

One day on the beach, Nicole told Systrom that she didn't want to use their app to take pictures because its effects weren't as good as her friend's.

"Do you know what they do to their photos?"

"They just take pictures, right?"

"No, they add filters to their app, completely changing the look of the photos."

"So maybe you should add filters too, right?"

"Hmm... That makes sense!"

Systrom immediately returned to the room and searched online for methods to create filters. After a series of trial-and-error attempts akin to incorrect chemical combinations, he finally developed his first filter that day, which he called X-ProII. Systrom decided to release the new product eight weeks later, featuring this filter as the main attraction.

Systrom and Krieger enthusiastically presented the new filter and plan to their investors, and neither Andreessen nor Anderson opposed it. Anderson even remained attentive during their pivot, worried about Systrom's relentless pursuit of success.

After finalizing the beta version, Systrom and Krieger began promoting their product to notable figures in the tech industry and media, leveraging their past Stanford education and Silicon Valley work experiences to generate buzz. Due to Systrom's infectious enthusiasm and Krieger's charm, Instagram received positive market feedback. Systrom, a former employee of Twitter during its Odeo days, met Twitter founder Jack Dorsey and showcased Instagram, instantly turning Dorsey into a fan.

"At first glance, Instagram seemed like a simple and easy-to-use product, but as I delved deeper, I was impressed by the intricate details behind it that created an excellent user experience. I still remember how Kevin at Odeo frequently mentioned photography; he has a genuine passion for it. Unfortunately, Twitter couldn’t provide him with the opportunity to express himself fully, but he found it in Instagram."

In June 2010, Instagram officially launched, and users began sharing their photos on Twitter and Facebook. On the first day of its launch, Instagram already had 25,000 users, setting off an unstoppable trend.

Systrom recalled attending a gaming event near ATT's office. He conducted a small survey on-site, observing how many users were using Instagram, discovering that 140 photos were taken via Instagram in the last two hours. He realized the potential for game interaction through the Instagram app. We began to understand that Instagram could do much more than just photo sharing.

The high attention from the media and public on Instagram was fleeting, followed swiftly by Systrom and Krieger's first setback in their careers.

In 2010, soon after investing in Burbn, Horowitz pivoted to invest in a similar photo service called PicPlz. Although PicPlz primarily targeted Android systems rather than the iOS-focused Instagram, and VC firms supported Systrom and Krieger's pivot, Horowitz still felt conflicted about investing in competing companies. Ultimately, Horowitz chose PicPlz and announced an additional investment of $5 million in PicPlz one month after Instagram's launch, ceasing further investments in Instagram (later, Horowitz netted $78 million from Facebook's $1 billion acquisition, 310 times the cost of investing in Burbn).

Systrom learned of Horowitz's additional investment in PicPlz through the New York Times. Upon seeing the news, he felt an indescribable sense of destruction: "Instagram was clearly on an upward trajectory, so why didn't we get fair treatment!?" Systrom said, "Horowitz is such a respected investor, but... for Instagram, he silently turned away."

Fortunately, other investors still saw potential in Instagram, and Systrom quickly seized a lifeline: Matt Cohler from Bain Capital. For entrepreneurs, the 36-year-old Cohler held a status nearly second to God. He was once an executive at LinkedIn and one of Facebook's earliest five members, stepping down from his position as product manager at Facebook to become an investor in 2008.

"In my eyes, Instagram is like an exhilarating symphony that fills a need that has long gone unsatisfied. It's the first application I've seen that was truly born for smartphones, allowing me to elegantly enjoy life." Cohler commented on Intagram.

In February 2011, Bain Capital led a $7 million Series A funding round for Instagram, valuing the company at $25 million. This round included participation from Twitter's Dorsey, angel investor Chris Sacca, and former Facebook notable technologist Adam D'Angelo, who was Zuckerberg's roommate at Exeter and later founded a Q&A website called Quora.

The crisis passed, and a month later, Instagram celebrated its achievement of millions of users at Bloodhound bar in Los Angeles, with Krieger personally grilling meat for Cohler and team members.

"Toasts to the good old days," Krieger said.

"Mike," Cohler followed up, "one day we'll say the current times are the good old days."

Less than a year after its launch, Krieger and Systrom were already overwhelmed by the geometric growth in users. With fewer than twelve team members, Krieger handled all technical issues, while Systrom managed product experience improvements and dealt with giants like Twitter's Dorsey and Facebook's Zuckerberg who were eyeing Instagram for acquisition.

"Kevin would sometimes call me, and I would occasionally call him," Zuckerberg recalled their relationship during Instagram's early days. They had known each other since Systrom attended Stanford University, occasionally meeting under fortuitous circumstances. However, after Instagram launched, Zuckerberg began frequently contacting Systrom, inviting him over to his home near San Francisco to discuss what he called philosophy.

Looking back, Zuckerberg's invitations to Systrom may not have been purely out of friendship. After all, Facebook started with photo services, and Instagram, as a rising star, could potentially become a direct competitor to Facebook. Zuck was aware of the risks.

Honestly, Instagram was siphoning off a lot of traffic from Facebook, Zuckerberg said. Then I thought, the best solution is to make Facebook and Instagram one company. From this, it's clear that Zuck recognized the threat posed by Instagram—not unfoundedly. Instagram was elegant, fun, and popular, focusing on the mobile sector, thriving in the mobile internet world. Meanwhile, Facebook, originating in the desktop era, struggled to find a clear path forward in the mobile internet landscape.

From a more abstract perspective, Zuckerberg may have feared a certain fate of large companies: in Silicon Valley, upstarts always have the potential to swallow up previously successful giants.

Simultaneously, Dorsey, who returned to Twitter as CEO, began expressing interest in acquiring Instagram, persistently convincing Systrom to bring his Instagram into the social media giant Twitter for collaboration. Coincidentally, Roelof Botha, an ambitious investor from Sequoia Capital, also extended an olive branch to Systrom. Botha had successfully invested in several famous startups in the social domain, such as Tumblr. He closely monitored the direction of Instagram and proposed an investment in early 2012, recognizing Instagram's unique user stickiness.

Too many startups can only watch as users come and go, unable to retain them, Botha said. But Instagram is different—it retains its users. Without hesitation, Botha proposed a $50 million investment in Instagram. Systrom was stumped by the multitude of options.

One day, as the CEO of a star startup, Systrom was invited to a prominent networking event in New York, where Jack Dorsey also attended. Dorsey, along with Twitter's then-CFO Ali Rowghani, formally proposed an acquisition plan to Systrom—an offer around $500 million, paid entirely in common and restricted shares, with no cash involved.

Later, recalling this meeting, Dorsey and Rowghani insisted they had presented a venture capital agreement on the spot, while Systrom maintained he never saw the document, and all commitments were verbal. The meeting ended on bad terms. Subsequently, Systrom called Twitter's CEO Costolo to inform that Instagram would remain independent and accept Sequoia Capital's investment.

Systrom also informed Zuckerberg of this decision. However, Zuckerberg did not give up because of Systrom's refusal. True to his usual decisive style at Facebook, the next day he sent an email to Systrom, hoping Systrom would give him a chance to discuss face-to-face why he was interested in Instagram. Posturing doesn't work; every tech company can spout a bunch of formalities, but I'm different—I'm serious about this, and I hope Systrom can feel my sincerity. Zuckerberg later recalled.

Two days after rejecting Twitter's acquisition, Systrom sat with Zuck at his home near San Francisco. They discussed Facebook, Instagram, and how Facebook's influence could help Instagram achieve its maximum potential. This conversation lasted a long time.

"I barely felt any formal negotiation atmosphere because talking with Systrom made us feel like true colleagues. I wanted to fight alongside him, and he felt the same way," Zuckerberg said. He was so eager to acquire Instagram that the day after they discussed future directions, he offered an acquisition amount double Instagram's valuation—$1 billion, with $300 million in cash.

Systrom was thoroughly moved by Zuckerberg. Partly due to the $300 million cash condition, more importantly, Zuck's irresistible passion changed Systrom's intention to keep Instagram independent. "I can't pinpoint exactly what changed, but Zuck presented a clear and feasible plan, a visible path, and an acquisition price twice the $500 million investment offered by Sequoia," Systrom said. Clearly, the scales were unbalanced—Facebook's offer far exceeded what Instagram was worth.

Another possible reason Zuck attracted Systrom was his promise that Instagram could continue operating independently within Facebook, unlike in other acquisitions where the acquired company usually faced dismantling and integration into the acquirer.

Speaking of that negotiation, Systrom did not consider it an exciting mysterious moment. People might imagine we reached an agreement in a dark room filled with intense rock music. My point is, people tend to dramatize these scenarios with intense confrontations, he said, subtly referring to a scene from the movie "The Social Network." But in reality, we quickly came to an agreement without any dramatic episodes.

Leaving Zuckerberg's house, Systrom called Krieger. He needed to decide Instagram's future with Krieger. They discussed the implications of Zuckerberg's offer on a bench near a California railway station. Finally, on the drive back to Los Angeles, Systrom simply told Krieger, "I really admire Mark, genuinely respect him and the Facebook he built. The ideals Facebook aims to achieve deeply inspire me." They ultimately decided to accept Facebook's acquisition.

This time, the person shocked by the news of Instagram's acquisition was Dorsey. He thought his personal relationship with Systrom had not been affected by business matters, but when he learned from his employees that Systrom sold Instagram, he realized he was wrong. "During that period, I wasn't completely disconnected from Systrom—we exchanged a couple of emails and met a few times at parties. But he never mentioned anything about selling Instagram to Facebook, and I was utterly disheartened." Perhaps Dorsey's last post on Instagram speaks volumes—a picture of an empty bus with all passengers gone.

Perhaps out of revenge, after Instagram officially became part of Facebook, Twitter disabled the feature to discover your Twitter friends on Instagram. Costolo explained this by saying, "We see Facebook and Instagram as one company, and I don't think pleasing their users will bring any value to Twitter."

Instagram reciprocated by disabling its filter service provided to Twitter, and later, as we all know, Twitter developed its own filters.

If the friction with Twitter was merely a minor external injury, then the privacy invasion accusations Instagram faced later were like severe internal wounds. The issue stemmed from a clause added in the new version: Facebook could obtain photos uploaded to Instagram for advertising purposes, and this acquisition could occur automatically without user consent. Headlines such as "Instagram sells your photos to advertisers" appeared in the press, and many celebrities avoided Instagram, fearing their photos might be exploited.

Although Systrom quickly reacted by removing the clause, the shadow cast on users was irreversible, and Facebook was labeled as a villain selling user privacy.

"The mistake this time was rushing into it. We hadn't even tested the acceptance level of this clause among users before recklessly launching it. Later, I asked myself, why prepare so far ahead for ads when we don't even have them yet?" Systrom said. "I should have asked myself this question earlier—it was my fault."

He viewed this incident as a profound lesson, telling team members to imagine Instagram as a country. If any change causes panic akin to changing all the road signs in the country to a different color, it should be carefully considered.

Since then, Instagram's growth has gradually stabilized. The number of users increased a thousandfold compared to the time of acquisition, reaching 100 million monthly active users and 200 million total users in just two and a half years, while Twitter took six years to reach this number.

Who accomplished all this? No one can say for sure. According to Silicon Valley thinking, perhaps people might feel Systrom and Krieger shouldn't have sold the rapidly growing Instagram so easily. But for Systrom, these discussions are meaningless. There are always Monday-morning quarterbacks, Systrom said. Silicon Valley is full of such stories—hot startups that reject offers from industry giants but end up veering off course or gradually fading from users' memories. In other words, before every Instagram, there lie countless graves of Friendsters.

In Systrom's view, with the help of Facebook as a teammate, Instagram can gradually explore profitable paths with endless possibilities in the future. I have no regrets about anything in the past; instead, I am grateful, he said.

Still, despite Instagram's rags-to-riches story becoming a topic of discussion in Silicon Valley, Systrom remains unwilling to open his cherished vintage whiskey to celebrate the achievements so far. "Once you open it, it's over. But I believe the truly celebratory moment has yet to arrive."
