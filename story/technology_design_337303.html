I. Preparation before passage: 1. Preheat the culture liquid: Place the bottles containing pre-prepared culture medium, PBS solution and trypsin in a 37℃ water bath to preheat. 2. Wipe the super-clean bench and both hands with 75% alcohol that has been exposed to ultraviolet light. 3. Correctly arrange the instruments: Ensure sufficient operating space for easy operation and reduce contamination. 4. Ignite the spirit lamp: The flame should not be too small. 5. Prepare sterilized empty culture flasks, place them in the microwave oven on high heat for 8 minutes to disinfect again. 6. Retrieve the preheated culture medium: After wiping with an alcohol swab, place it inside the super-clean bench. 7. Retrieve cells from the incubator: When taking out the cells, make sure to tighten the bottle cap and wipe the microscope stage with an alcohol swab, then observe the cells under the microscope. 8. Open the bottle caps: Disinfect each bottle cap by burning it over the spirit lamp.

II. Trypsin digestion: 1. Add digestive solution: Carefully aspirate the old culture medium, wash with PBS (rinse), add appropriate amount of digestive solution (trypsin), note that the volume of digestive solution should ideally cover the cells, and the optimal digestion temperature is 37℃. 2. Observe cells under the microscope: Observe the digested cells under the inverted microscope. If the cytoplasm shrinks and the cells are no longer connected in sheets, this indicates that the cells have been digested appropriately. 3. Aspirate digestive solution and add culture medium: Discard the trypsin solution, use a new pipette, and add fresh culture medium.

III. Detach and disperse cells: 1. Blow and detach: Use a pipette to blow the digested cells into a cell suspension. 2. Transfer the cell suspension to a centrifuge tube: Suck the cell suspension into a 10ml centrifuge tube. 3. Balance and centrifuge: After balancing, place the centrifuge tube in a desktop centrifuge, centrifuge at 1000 rpm for 6-8 minutes. 4. Discard supernatant and add new culture medium: Discard the supernatant, add 2ml of culture medium, gently blow the cells with a pipette to form a cell suspension.

IV. Dilute and subculture cells: 1. Subculture: Transfer the cell suspension to 2-3 culture flasks, add an appropriate amount of culture medium, tighten the bottle cap. 2. Observe cells under the microscope: Observe the cell quantity under the inverted microscope, count if necessary. Note that a low density will affect the growth of passaged cells; the density of passaged cells should not be less than 5×10^5/ml. Finally, mark accordingly.

V. Continue culturing: Wipe the culture flask with an alcohol swab, slightly loosen the bottle cap, and continue culturing in the CO2 incubator. Passaged cells begin to attach to the flask wall after 2 hours. When the growing cells cover 25% of the bottom area of the culture flask, it's marked as "+", 50% as "++", and 75% as "+++".

Precautions for passaging cells: 1. Strict sterile operation 2. Appropriate digestion: Digestion time is influenced by various factors such as the type of digestive solution, preparation time, and the amount added to the culture flask. During digestion, pay attention to the morphological changes of cultured cells. Once the cytoplasm shrinks, connections become loose, or there are signs of large-scale floating, terminate the digestion immediately.

EDTA (0.02% disodium ethylenediaminetetraacetate) digestive solution formula: EDTA 0.20g, 8.00g, KCl 0.20g, Na2HPO4 0.02g, glucose 2.00g, 0.5% phenol red 4ml, add distilled water to make up to 1000ml. Sterilize at 10 pounds for 20 minutes, adjust pH to 7.4 when using. Note that EDTA cannot be neutralized by serum, and the culture flask must be thoroughly cleaned after use to prevent cell detachment during subsequent cultures.

Cell revival principle - rapid melting: Cells stored at -196℃ in liquid nitrogen must be quickly melted to 37℃ so that the ice crystals outside the cells melt rapidly, avoiding damage caused by recrystallization due to slow melting.

Common mistakes made by scholars: 1. Water bath not preheated or not preheated to 37℃. 2. Too many cryopreserved tubes in the water bath leading to poor heat transfer and extended melting time. 3. Forgetting to balance before centrifugation leading to damage of the centrifuge and loss of cells. 4. Reviving too many cells at once and forgetting to change pipette tips and pipettes leading to cross-contamination of cells.
