But you can't see most of the relics by car.

by hjdsk28101 on 2011-05-29 11:03:26

It has been a long time since the last update, don't say I am lazy. Here we go again, let me share with you my travel experiences based on my itinerary.

I bought a soft seat train ticket on October 4th for an early morning departure to Chengde the next day (61 RMB per person). Since we couldn't book return tickets in Beijing, we decided to buy them once we arrived in Chengde. On the evening of October 4th, I booked a four-star Qianyang Grand Hotel on Ctrip, and everything went smoothly. Online, it was mentioned that along the way to Chengde, there might be people soliciting customers or promoting accommodations, so we had to be cautious to avoid being deceived. Reputable hotels wouldn't do this kind of thing (preying on newcomers, which happens everywhere in China). Accommodations in Chengde are generally more expensive (peak season from late April to early October, off-season discounts apply), so one should be mentally prepared. There are many hotels of various standards, but during peak weekends, rooms can get tight, so early booking is recommended. When we made our reservation, standard rooms were almost fully booked, and we managed to secure a room at 380 RMB.

On the train nearing Chengde, a woman approached us offering accommodation at the Sabine Hotel. Initially, we wanted to decline, but she said they could help purchase internal return tickets and guarantee availability. After some hesitation, we decided to wait until we got off the train to buy tickets first. However, when we reached the train station ticket office, all the return tickets to Beijing on October 8th were sold out. We were worried, and seeing the woman nearby, we followed her to check out the hotel. The location was good, right in the city center, but the space was smaller (later found out it was a two-star hotel), and the rooms were just barely acceptable. For the sake of securing three return train tickets, we decided to stay here (140 RMB + 20 RMB extra bed fee). It was much cheaper than Qianyang by over 200 RMB. Once settled, the hotel manager suggested we take a tour by chartered bus. We originally planned to take taxis ourselves, but due to persuasion and fear that the three tickets might have issues, we agreed to pay 40 RMB per person for them to handle our round-trip transportation. (Later we found out some paid 30 RMB, self-driving routes, others paid 60 RMB, and even heard of 80 RMB per person. They really gouged as much as they could. Another secret: this bus service was the most profitable part of their business. If we took a taxi, the cost for three people touring for two days would not exceed 60 RMB.)

With no other options, we proceeded with the arranged transport and started our sightseeing. Our first destination was the Outer Eight Temples. Five of these temples are currently open to the public: Puning Temple (commonly known as the Great Buddha Temple), Putuo Zongcheng Temple (Little Potala Palace), Xumi Fushou Temple (Panchen Lama's Palace), Anyuan Temple, and Pule Temple. It is recommended to hire a guide when visiting the temples. Puning Temple is the only one where lamas conduct Buddhist rituals and incense offerings (whether or not to burn incense is up to you; cheaper at the entrance, more expensive inside), definitely worth a visit at 50 RMB per person. You can see the world's largest wooden gold-lacquered thousand-armed Avalokiteshvara Bodhisattva, standing 23.5 meters tall, with a waist circumference of 15 meters, weighing 110 tons, incredibly grand. Putuo Zongcheng Temple has the largest building area, modeled after Lhasa's Potala Palace, very majestic. You can view its panoramic view from the north wall of the Summer Resort, extremely beautiful. However, it is a replica, so if possible, you should still visit Tibet. Xumi Fushou Temple is modeled after Shigatse's Tashilhunpo Monastery, where the Sixth Panchen Lama stayed in the 45th year of Emperor Qianlong's reign. The four standing golden dragons on the temple roof are the largest architectural feature of this temple, quite attractive. Moreover, the "Chacha" Buddha inside is the only one open to visitors for viewing. Anyuan Temple and Pule Temple are close to Qingchui Peak (Hammer Mountain), also the starting point for the Qingchui Peak cable car. The hiking trail starts near Pule Temple. Climbing Hammer Mountain is worth it, especially for men, haha, very spectacular.

After sightseeing, it's time to eat. Chengde's dining scene is pretty good, large portions, delicious food, reasonable prices, with many restaurants of varying sizes. One thing to note: it's best not to rely on guides for restaurant recommendations or accompaniment, as less ethical guides may collude with restaurants to set traps. Several people recommended eating roe deer meat and venison, but we avoided it, fearing potential scams. Chengde's local specialties include oatmeal dumplings and venison, otherwise, it's similar to other northern cuisines. We went to Batet Yangxie, the hottest place across from the main entrance of the Summer Resort. It was quite delicious, and despite the large crowd, our group of three spent only 71 RMB for both eating and hotpot. Regarding transportation, a latest Chengde city map suffices, it's a small city, and the map is detailed enough. Chengde's public transportation system is well-developed, buses reach all scenic spots for one yuan, taxis start at five yuan, but locals rarely use the meter, and neither should you, fearing it's specially rigged for tourists. Generally, inter-scenic spot rides cost five yuan, with the furthest being ten yuan. As for shopping malls, there's really nothing remarkable, truly lagging behind. We bought snacks and breakfast for the next day at a supermarket before returning to the hotel to sleep.

The next morning at six o'clock, we were rushed to visit the Summer Resort. Why so early? According to the hotel staff, they could help us get annual passes. Given that the Summer Resort's peak season ticket costs 90 RMB, off-season 60 RMB, and the annual pass only costs 60 RMB, we decided to get it. They took our photo the previous night, but the next day's revelation truly opened our eyes. It turned out they issued fake ID cards, and more shockingly, they had already arranged a deal with the gatekeepers at the Summer Resort, allowing "free passage". Truly everywhere they look for opportunities to make illicit profits, this is practically a zero-cost business, making me "open my eyes wide". At six o'clock, seemingly like Chengde citizens doing morning exercises, we entered the resort. Immediately, someone collected our fake IDs back, possibly fearing exposure, and replaced them with a useless ticket. Thus, their 60 RMB per person transaction was completed. Although it appeared mutually beneficial, it harmed the state. This situation arose due to inadequate supervision of Chengde tourism, resulting in non-standardized practices even years later, including the Outer Eight Temples. Let's not dwell on these matters. We entered through Lizheng Gate, the main entrance of the Summer Resort. Inside, we first visited the Main Palace, also called the Museum, where the emperor handled government affairs and lived. The Main Palace has plenty of content worth savoring. Exiting the rear door of the Main Palace, we arrived between the lake area and mountain area. Here, I must explain: to take the sightseeing bus up the mountain, you must do so here, requiring an additional 40 RMB per person. Of course, you can also hike up the mountain (no charge, multiple entrances available, but from November 1st to May 31st of the following year, the mountain is closed). The mountain area is the essence of the Summer Resort, very worth exploring, with numerous buildings, though most were destroyed before liberation (due to warlords and Japanese invaders), leaving ruins that still show traces of their former glory. Some have been restored or are in the process of restoration. However, taking the bus doesn't allow you to see most of the ruins because they are mostly located in valleys. Without a guide (even tour guides may not find them), using the map might lead to getting lost. The mountain area is vast with dense forests and numerous paths, but there are no dead zones for mobile phone signals. After hiking for two hours, due to discomfort in our legs, we descended to the lakeside for play. The lake area has three routes, all of which require half a day to complete. The routes are quite circuitous, but offer more detailed views. If you prefer, I recommend taking the middle route (Huanbi-Zhijingyunditi-Ruyizhou), which combines the scenery of the east and west routes. If you want a route closer to the mountain area or plan to hike up the mountain, take Shuanghu Jiajing-Yuanjin Quansheng-Wenjingge-Yunrongshuaitai, which is the route we took. Overall, the Summer Resort is worth a visit, being the largest imperial garden, though its magnificence does not match the Summer Palace. Additionally, Chengde's management is very irregular, streets are chaotic, and many hotels are generally expensive (around 300-1000 RMB), with the rest being average or somewhat unregulated. What satisfied us most was the food there, cheap and delicious.

Finally, we obtained supplementary seats sold privately by the train conductor, boarding an hour earlier than others. We truly felt the power of this travel agency, handling trains, the Summer Resort, cars, hotels, and engaging in various side businesses. It was terrifying and made me deeply disappointed in the inaction of certain places. Eventually, on the afternoon of October 6th, we returned to Beijing. Farewell to Chengde, which gave me many memories.

Lastly, I'll share a few photos with everyone.
