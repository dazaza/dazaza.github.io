The expansion list of purchase restrictions by the Ministry of Housing and Urban-Rural Development is difficult to implement due to local governments' covert resistance.

by o5vxvt74625 on 2012-03-01 14:05:28

Wang Ying

The Ministry of Housing and Urban-Rural Development (MOHURD) may be facing the most headache-inducing challenge since the start of real estate market regulation.

With just a month to go, MOHURD has still not announced the list of second- and third-tier cities that will expand purchase restrictions. Meanwhile, in some suspected cities set for restrictions, developers are rushing to launch new projects, even causing short-term price spikes.

In an ironic twist, recently, Langfang and Qinhuangdao have both reported rumors of unofficial negotiations regarding price caps. According to informed sources, many local governments want to meet their initial promises on housing price control targets but do not wish to be included on the restricted list, so they are attempting to avoid stricter purchase restrictions by imposing price caps instead.

Another source close to MOHURD revealed that the expansion of purchase restrictions has faced resistance from local governments involved in negotiations, and currently, only more than ten cities may have agreed to the restrictions. In the eyes of some local governments, their city's housing prices are not excessively high, and thus, they should not be included in the restriction list. Earlier, industry insiders predicted that over 60 cities would join the restriction list.

Wang Jianlin, chairman of Dalian Wanda Group Co., Ltd., stated in an interview that curbing housing prices requires comprehensive measures, and relying solely on purchase restrictions to suppress prices is naive.

Anxious Local Governments

Langfang’s private negotiation on price caps is not an isolated case. Recently, it was reported that developers in Qinhuangdao were "advised" by local authorities to keep the prices of upcoming projects below 7,000 yuan per square meter.

According to an insider from a well-known brand developer operating locally, a cluster of projects priced at 10,000 yuan per square meter recently appeared in the central area of Qinhuangdao, driving up citywide housing prices. As a result, relevant government departments in Qinhuangdao recently reminded some developers that future projects should not benchmark against these high-end developments; pricing must remain reasonable, and the government will strictly control the pace of housing price increases. Further information indicates that some developers in Qinhuangdao were required to keep the pricing of new projects preferably below 7,000 yuan per square meter.

According to data released by the National Bureau of Statistics on July 18th, housing prices in Qinhuangdao rose by 8.6% year-on-year in June, ranking second among the 70 cities in terms of housing price increase.

It is not merely Qinhuangdao that wishes to avoid purchase restrictions. A source close to MOHURD revealed that during recent negotiations with local governments regarding purchase restrictions, the vast majority of local governments could not accept restrictions being imposed on their cities. The source further mentioned that currently, only over ten cities have agreed, and the exact timing for the final announcement of the restricted list will depend on the opinions of higher-level leadership.

Although the restricted list has yet to be announced, housing prices in second- and third-tier cities without purchase restrictions continue to rise.

According to the China Index Academy's "July 2011 China Real Estate Index System Hundred City Price Index," in July 2011, the average residential price in 100 cities nationwide was 8,874 yuan per square meter, rising by 0.21% compared to June. Among them, 66 cities saw price increases, while 33 cities experienced declines, with one city remaining unchanged from the previous month.

According to data from the National Bureau of Statistics, from January to July, the national commercial property sales area reached 520.37 million square meters, increasing by 13.6%. Zhang Dawei, director of the Beijing Zhongyuan Secondary Market Research Department, analyzed that despite continued sluggishness in first-tier cities under purchase restrictions, national overall data shows that transaction volumes and amounts have grown compared to the first half of the year, mainly due to some second- and third-tier cities not under restrictions, where there has been a rush to buy before restrictions take effect.

"Currently, purchase restrictions remain the most effective means of real estate market regulation. Second- and third-tier cities not under restrictions face dual pressures: local investment demand and transferred investment demand from first-tier cities, concentrating national real estate market pressure on second- and third-tier cities. Continued price increases in these cities also affect the effectiveness of first-tier city regulations." Zhang Dawei believes that regulatory measures in second- and third-tier cities not under restrictions need to be strengthened.

The Gap Between 10 and 100

Hu Jinghui, vice president of 5i5j.com, expressed in an interview that the list of cities expanding purchase restrictions should be announced as soon as possible, without hesitation. If policy announcements are delayed, it could lead to a temporary policy gap, triggering concentrated increases in transaction volumes and housing prices in certain areas.

"This is a process of negotiation between the central and local governments. Purchase restrictions have a significant impact on local governments, and each city will argue that its housing price increases are not rapid enough to warrant inclusion in the restriction list." Zou Yi, general manager of Wuhetan Think Tank of Zhoulia Group, commented.

Hu Jinghui also admitted that the difficulties faced by purchase restrictions stem from China's rapidly urbanizing development. Once restrictions are imposed, urbanization will inevitably be affected, which is something local governments do not wish to see.

According to Zhang Dawei's understanding, the number of restricted cities should expand to 100. "There are 70 cities tracked for housing price indices, and currently, 40 cities have already implemented restrictions. The price increases in the other 30 cities must certainly exceed those already under restrictions, so they should also be restricted. Additionally, including third-tier cities surrounding first-tier cities, theoretically, the number of restricted cities should exceed 100." However, Zhang Dawei helplessly pointed out that if judged by policy standards, at least 10 cities around Beijing, such as Yanjiao and Gu'an, should be under restrictions.

Yang Hongxu, director of the Comprehensive Research Department of Shanghai E-House Real Estate Research Institute, was not as optimistic. According to his analysis, currently, among cities not yet under restrictions, three types of cities may be candidates: the first type includes cities with relatively high absolute housing prices, such as Zhuhai, Taizhou, Kunshan, Zhangjiagang, Huzhou, Jiaxing, Wujiang, Changshu, Jiangyin, Yangzhou, etc.; the second type, based on the housing price index of 70 large and medium-sized cities, includes cities with high housing price increases (over 6% compared to last year, with monthly increases around 0.3%), such as Yueyang, Dandong, Qinhuangdao, Mudanjiang, Xiangfan, Luoyang, Jilin, Changde, etc.; the third type includes small and medium-sized cities around Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, and Shenzhen, such as Zhuhai, Huizhou, Langfang, Kunshan, etc.

"We expect that the state will primarily consider the third category while taking into account the first and second factors when selecting new cities for restrictions. We estimate that the number will not exceed 20, and very likely be around 10." Yang Hongxu stated.

Regarding the effectiveness of purchase restriction policies, Yang Hongxu is not optimistic: on one hand, after restrictions are imposed, the adjustment in transaction volume is significant, and changes in transaction prices occur, affecting product structures and forcing companies to adjust their development strategies, such as residential-focused enterprises entering the commercial office real estate sector; on the other hand, the development of the commercial housing market is negatively impacted to a certain extent. "Some local governments, especially those in central and western cities who believe their housing prices are not excessively high, do not wish to see a significant reduction in land transfer fees and real estate taxes, nor do they want a sluggish real estate market to drag down local economic growth." Yang Hongxu explained.
