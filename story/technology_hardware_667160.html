Han Dynasty Rule Mirror

Some foreign scholars, for convenience, call this kind of mirror the "TLV mirror," while domestic scholars refer to it as the "rule mirror." This is mainly due to the "TLV" three patterns resembling the tools of a carpenter's compass and square. Therefore, the term "rule" resonates more with the Chinese than "TLV."

Some scholars believe that the Han Dynasty rule mirrors have astronomical significance; thus, they named them sundial mirrors. According to the sundial, L and V are thought to represent images of time. L represents the summer solstice, autumn equinox, winter solstice, and spring equinox, while V represents the start of the four seasons. T, on the other hand, carries spatial connotations.

The rule mirror was a popular bronze mirror during the Han Dynasty. A collector brought one such mirror. On its back, there is a round mirror button surrounded by a square grid. Each side of the grid has a T-shaped pattern, opposite which lies an L-shaped pattern. Outside the corners of the grid are V-shaped patterns. Between the TLV patterns are decorative motifs of auspicious beasts.

Other scholars argue that the square objects with TLV patterns on Han pictorial stones are liubo boards. They propose that the bronze mirrors borrowed these patterns from the liubo board, leading to a new name for the rule mirror: the liubo pattern bronze mirror.

The mysterious TLV patterns on Han Dynasty mirrors

This kind of mirror is called the rule mirror. Some scholars believe these patterns have astronomical significance. What does "TLV" mean? The Han people favored using a kind of rule mirror. Its back is decorated with TLV characters, supplemented by Azure Dragon, White Tiger, Vermilion Bird, and Black Tortoise. Many scholars have speculated about their meanings over the years, but opinions still vary widely.

The front of the bronze mirror is used for looking at one’s face, while the various patterns, decorations, and inscriptions on the back represent the social trends of the time.

Rule mirrors were most prevalent in the late Western Han Dynasty and during the Wang Mang period. What exactly "TLV" means has been debated by many scholars over the years.
