The bitterness and excitement of Chinese entrepreneurs studying abroad twenty years ago. People with insight are here on the Tianya blog.

by bhjdfghjsf41 on 2012-02-09 21:20:29

Twenty years ago, a group of students from prestigious Chinese universities went abroad to fulfill their dream of "plating themselves in gold," and now they have become elites in various fields. On Sunday evening, October 29th at 9:21 PM, two of the most representative figures from this group, Xu Xiaoping, one of the founders of New Oriental Education, and Mao Daqing, Vice President of Vanke, will be guests on the Asus "Hall of Fame" show on BTV's Finance Channel. Through the oral accounts of these two guests, we discover that there were many things about the lives of these entrepreneurs as overseas students twenty years ago that people might not have imagined.

In the 80s, applying for overseas study required bribes like watermelons and cigarettes. Although both were excellent students with good grades, they faced obstacles when applying to study abroad. For example, Xu Xiaoping said: "At Peking University, the personnel department wouldn't release my file, so I took two watermelons to the leader's house during the hot summer days — that's when I learned how to bribe. As for the plane ticket to the US, it cost $800. At that time, my monthly salary was only 100 RMB, so I borrowed the money from my wife's brother." Mao Daqing, who went abroad a few years later than Xu Xiaoping, also encountered resistance: "No matter what, we all faced one problem — going abroad was very troublesome. You couldn't even get a passport. First, you needed some kind of overseas connection, and your unit wouldn't give you the necessary certificates. While he used watermelons, by the time it got to me, it had turned into two packs of cigarettes. Getting a passport from the Public Security Bureau was harder than getting a hukou today."

The hardships of studying abroad twenty years ago

Nowadays, going abroad is no longer a novelty, but twenty years ago, studying abroad was seen as an absolute opportunity to "plate oneself in gold." However, the two entrepreneurs say: "When we went abroad, we didn't think it was about plating ourselves in gold. It was a way to realize our personal dreams. The path to achieving those dreams was through going abroad." Their generation of overseas students worked very hard and endured harsh conditions abroad — they all swept floors, washed dishes, and cleaned toilets.

The disparity and shock of studying abroad

Mao Daqing and Xu Xiaoping went abroad in the late 80s. What they saw abroad was very different from what they knew domestically. Xu Xiaoping said: "At that time, working a part-time job paid $5-6 per hour, which amounted to $40 a day. According to the exchange rate back then, that was equivalent to two months' salary at Peking University. There were two shocks: why did foreign countries have so much wealth? And secondly, the glory of labor immediately manifested itself in my life." Mao Daqing analyzed: "These people who went to study in the US and UK found that reality was completely different from what they had been taught for decades. This disparity and shock divided the wave of returning overseas students into two groups: one group studied hard and eventually returned, becoming today's old academicians, professors, and doctoral supervisors. Without this group, China would not have developed in the 80s and 90s. But another group chose never to return to China, finding it too terrifying. They decided they would absolutely never go back."

The illusion of returning overseas students becoming "seaweed"

Nowadays, many people believe that returning overseas students may end up as "seaweed" (a term implying they are stranded without progress). However, Xu Xiaoping tells everyone: "The idea that returning overseas students turn into seaweed is actually a misconception. If you truly obtain a good degree abroad and master the language and culture, such individuals have infinite development opportunities in China. This dream will never deceive anyone."

The two successful entrepreneurs who studied abroad, Xu Xiaoping and Mao Daqing, believe that the most important thing about studying abroad is: "To immerse yourself in the other side of the ocean, to really settle down and work hard. Beyond obtaining a degree, you must broaden your horizons and develop your character. In short, cultivate other qualities. Lower your pride and immerse yourself in their culture and society, including your studies. Many people can't truly immerse themselves. When you're here, you think you need to go out, get a degree, and use it as a stepping stone. So, their goal is to go there and bring something back. Once there, they feel they don't belong and are just there for a degree. No matter how many years they spend abroad, it's useless if they maintain this mindset."
