No alien bases on Mars: new evidence supports the existence of water ice

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 14:40:21

In recent years, multiple Mars probes from NASA have been exploring this red planet from orbit and its surface, forming a massive database of the Martian surface. Spacecraft such as the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter have conducted detailed observations of the Martian surface from orbit, discovering traces of liquid water that once existed on Mars. However, so-called "alien bases" have not been found. Instead, scientists continue to uncover new evidence showing that Mars once had a "comfortable" environment. Recently, researchers have discovered some unusual secrets about craters that do not align with traditional formation theories but are associated with "glacial landslides."

Most planets in the solar system without atmospheres bear traces of impact craters. When a rapidly moving small celestial body collides with the surface, it creates large amounts of dust or other materials, eventually forming the craters we see today. Scientists have discovered approximately 600 abnormal craters on Mars with two layers. These are not remnants of intelligent civilizations on Mars but rather caused by glacial landslides. This research was published in the August 7th issue of *Geophysical Research Letters*, authored by geologists David Weiss and James Head from Brown University in the United States. This discovery suggests that during the process of extraterrestrial bodies impacting the Martian surface, ice-water substances were left within the craters, forming thick "ice caps".

As early as the 1970s, NASA's Viking series of probes completed Mars exploration missions. These twin spacecraft consisted of orbiters and landers. Over the subsequent decades, scientists further validated evidence that Mars once had liquid water and large areas covered by ice sheets, especially in the northern and southern polar regions of Mars. Studying the ice layers on the Martian surface helps scientists infer the ancient Martian atmosphere and climate, leading to more accurate conclusions. Scientists believe that Mars' largest ice cap could extend to mid-latitudes, approximately 50 meters (165 feet) thick. Radiating streaks also exist at the edges of craters, similar to mountain (glacial) landslides on Earth. Scientists plan to conduct detailed investigations of each crater to study the interaction between glaciers and the Martian surface.
