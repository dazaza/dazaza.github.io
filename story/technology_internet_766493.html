Why has Apple's ability to keep new products secret diminished so much?

by anonymous on 2013-09-18 21:33:17

Not long after becoming Apple's CEO, Tim Cook firmly stated in an interview that he would further strengthen the company's confidentiality. However, from the current situation, this promise has not become a reality.

On Tuesday's new product launch event held locally by Apple, the company, as usual, received attention from countless media outlets and excited many Apple fans. Nevertheless, during this event where Apple released two new iPhones, there was little to be seen of any surprising elements. Typically, maintaining the secrecy of specific specifications of new products before their official release has always been one of the most challenging tasks for Apple and its competitors. However, repeated leaks have made these products feel like old news even before the launch event.

Months ago, numerous media outlets and blogs on the internet had already leaked photos, specifications, and video clips of these two new products from Apple. Information about fingerprint recognition technology, 64-bit processors, and the new color options for the iPhone 5s were also exposed during this period. Even more exaggeratedly, some websites had published the packaging images of the iPhone 5c online quite early.

In response, Apple's spokesperson refused to comment, and the company’s executives attempted to emphasize the positive significance of "early exposure" for the new products. Phil Schiller, Apple's Senior Vice President of Worldwide Marketing, said: "Some of you may have already seen the photos circulating on the internet, which is cool because it excites many people."

To be fair, there are indeed several companies in the tech field that try to attract consumer attention by occasionally releasing some spy shots. But so far, there is no information indicating that Apple meticulously planned this product exposure strategy. Moreover, regardless of how you look at it, Apple's stock price was negatively impacted by this launch, almost dropping by 6%. Some analysts attribute this to the high retail price of the iPhone 5c, as they believe it will severely limit Apple's ability to expand its market share in emerging markets.

Two Major Inducements

According to insiders within Apple's supply chain, another reason making the company's confidentiality increasingly difficult is the growing number of people involved in the new product design process. For Apple, those involved in new product development include not only the large team of research engineers working at the headquarters in Silicon Valley but also the assembly line workers receiving meager wages in Chinese contract manufacturers. It is precisely because of the latter that Apple can provide sufficient inventory to global dealers every quarter.

"Paying employees a few dollars a day obviously cannot guarantee that they won't leak product information," said Pierre Mercier, partner and director of supply chain research at Boston Consulting Group (BCG).

In this regard, Thomas Dinges, analyst at the well-known market research firm IHS iSuppli, estimated that the workforce of Foxconn, Apple's largest contract manufacturer, was less than 50,000 when producing the first-generation iPhone in 2007. However, the total number of people involved in the entire supply chain for iPhone production might have reached 350,000 at that time. And now, with the increasing complexity of relationships between Apple and its many supplier companies, this figure is estimated to have exceeded 1 million.

"An increasing number of people know your product information," said Dinges.

Besides, people's growing interest in consumer electronics has prompted various websites to closely track every detail of new products. Dinges believes that the rapid spread of new product spy shot information in emerging markets also helps create similar knock-off models in markets that usually cannot afford such products.

Ironically, Apple itself seems to have contributed to the escalating trend of product information leaks in another way, because it was after the advent of the iPhone that an increasing number of smartphones began to be equipped with high-definition cameras, which indirectly provided possibilities for product information leakage.

Mercier pointed out: "It has become easier to leak products because you can take pictures with your phone. People can take photos and upload them online within seconds, and even if their photo-taking behavior is discovered, it is already too late by the time the company catches them."

Countermeasures

Industry supply chain experts say that device manufacturers like Apple could avoid product information leaks by building their own factories. However, compared to outsourcing product assembly, this method would be extremely costly.

Joseph Carter, professor of supply chain management at Arizona State University's W.P. Carey School of Business, pointed out that there is not much Apple can do in controlling the flow of product information. Suing every company or website that releases spy shots would consume a great deal of the company's time, manpower, and resources. Moreover, even then, it would probably be difficult to stop dissatisfied employees from secretly taking pictures of new products.

Moreover, those who have the opportunity to take pictures of Apple's new products are not limited to assembly line workers. Those involved in transportation or customs agents may also come into contact with these goods and take "commemorative photos." Therefore, the key for Apple lies in delaying the leakage of product information as much as possible to avoid the negative impact of premature leakage on sales or the product.

"The reality is, there are no secrets left anymore," Joseph Carter concluded.
