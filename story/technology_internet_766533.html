The big data Wild West: The good, the bad and the ugly

Big data has developed to a critical stage. By 2017, the entire big data market will grow to $50 billion, but unfortunately, 55% of big data projects fail. Alongside opportunities come hype and misinformation; we are in the Wild West era of big data. The big data industry is at a standstill: those who understand it invest in this sector, collecting, storing big data, and benefiting from it; others buy into it with skepticism, not knowing how big data will affect their business.

Benign failure

Big data allows for a margin of error, which may sound counterintuitive, but there is a difference between benign and malignant failures.

Confused? Here's an explanation: The "test and learn" method can be applied to raw state big data. Companies need to identify these failures by formulating hypotheses and then validating them. This enables companies to develop truly coherent strategic solutions by delving deep into big data.

These "errors" are actually necessary steps to uncovering correct analytical results, creating significant opportunities across industries such as precise recommendations, risk management, equipment failure prediction, and streamlining logistics management processes.

In some optimistic cases, these companies are using insights derived from big data to develop new products, create new revenue streams, and even foster a big-data-driven corporate culture. To evolve to this stage, companies must break away from rigid quantification and cost-control thinking, developing flexible and agile analytical methods, gradually achieving optimal corporate development through large-scale automated predictive analysis. Only then can they truly find profitable business models through big data and develop new products based on data.

In the early stages of utilizing big data, speed is a key factor. The faster value is created through data-driven project completion and organizational skill accumulation, the quicker big data can be utilized more effectively at higher levels.

Quantcast perfectly illustrates the four-step model of growing into a big data company. Initially, it only provided free website traffic monitoring services. As market recognition increased—its daily calculations grew from thousands to billions—Quantcast quickly surpassed traditional database technologies. Business expansion enabled it to provide higher-quality analysis and judgments, enhancing audience analysis for corporate value addition and customer relationship maintenance.

Quantcast soon realized the necessity of investing in big data science, as identifying demographic and interest graph distribution states within massive activity data was highly challenging. Subsequently, Quantcast tested many products and services, one of its very successful projects being Lookalikes, which helped advertisers find new customers highly similar to existing ones. Quantcast now generates annual revenues of $100 million, with its flexibility allowing it to promptly seize opportunities in the development of big data.

Quantcast, Google, Facebook, and LinkedIn are pioneers among big data companies, having gone through these developmental stages. As big data businesses mature in internet companies, it is also the time for other enterprises to embrace big data to create value and accumulate competitiveness. For example, large IT suppliers are leveraging detailed technology product data and transaction data to generate predictive analytical models aimed at improving recommendation effectiveness and optimizing transaction experiences.

Malignant failure

Unfortunately, many companies remain stuck in very rigid patterns, still focusing on utilizing big data from the perspective of cost control and storage scalability. Perhaps they are still observing "agile analytics"—a method that breaks traditional cost-saving mindsets and flexibly applies data.

This means that many companies exploring big data are missing out on opportunities to improve their business and optimize services, as well as losing chances to develop new products based on big data rather than intuition. They are entering a stagnant phase in big data development—learning to store data but unable to extract value from it.

Big data requires human and resource investment—at the human level, more talent proficient in this technology is needed, whereas traditional cost-saving methods require layoffs. For big data companies, the situation is exactly the opposite. To achieve breakthroughs in big data, companies need to be willing to invest in people and money. Companies that cannot quickly adapt to changes and make timely investments will lose more opportunities to more agile small enterprises.

Unacceptable failure

Is there anything more embarrassing than the previously mentioned 55% failure rate? What causes these failures? One idea is that the Wild West era has too many charlatans making empty promises, boasting without showing results. They realize the hype around big data and mimic accordingly. Despite lacking proper certification, legacy consultants and system integrators position themselves as experts in this field.

Similarly, many traditional merchants sell outdated models as "big data." Many of them still use SAS (originating from 1976's Statistics Analysis System) on non-networked computers—is this big data?! Others focus on data mining and reporting, extracting, transforming, and loading content from small databases. These merchants typically use proprietary software disconnected from open-source parallel computing programming tools like Apache Hadoop.

We are at a critical point in the development of big data—requiring continuous data results to ensure sustained growth. Companies imagining outdated technologies or skills as big data will ultimately harm their businesses due to insufficient analytical capabilities. If their projects fail or provide inaccurate information, they will lose the competitive edge, handing over opportunities to competitors who truly understand big data technology.
