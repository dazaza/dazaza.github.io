College students encounter embarrassing situations when looking for jobs on other campuses. Can recruitment be done without tickets?

by 2blh921s9s on 2009-11-23 15:57:24

Recruitment tide, college students shuttle between schools to find jobs, but encounter the awkward situation of closed recruitment information between universities and prohibitions on non-students entering the premises. They appeal...

Zhou Ying, a fourth-year student at Hunan University of Chinese Medicine, was excitedly heading to another university in Changsha with her application materials for a job fair, only to be turned away because she wasn't a student of that university. Zhu Yu from Hunan University of Science and Technology encountered a "closed door" when trying to access other universities' employment websites; there were even instances of scalpers openly selling job fair entry tickets on campus BBS forums... Starting this week, multiple universities in Changsha have held campus recruitment fairs. However, incidents of universities closing off recruitment information and prohibiting non-students from entering are not uncommon. Graduates appeal for "shared resources" due to the difficulty in finding employment.

Being rejected at the scene and unable to access websites: On the 15th, Huang, a graduate from a university on the east bank of the Xiang River, set out at 6 a.m., transferred twice, and went to Central South University to attend a campus recruitment fair. Originally full of hope, he was instead met with embarrassment. "Please show your student ID and ticket!" The security guard at the entrance of the recruitment fair requested Huang. "I came from another school and don't have a ticket," Huang replied. "Sorry, our recruitment fair is only open to students of our school," and so Huang was denied entry. Seeing the bustling scene at the recruitment fair, Huang could only leave dejectedly.

Zhang Lei, a graduate from a university in Xiangtan who frequently attends recruitment fairs in Changsha, told reporters that it's not just on-site recruitment fairs but also university employment websites that are restricted. "Although many job postings are listed online, these are actually 'common goods.' Truly valuable important information is only accessible to students within the school, requiring input of student ID and password to enter."

The reporter subsequently logged into the employment information websites of 10 universities, including Central South University, Hunan University, Hunan Normal University, and Changsha University of Science & Technology. Among them, four universities' employment information websites were completely closed to the public. On the websites of Hunan Normal University and Hunan University, every time a job posting was clicked, a "Member Login" prompt would automatically pop up. Website registered users include employers and graduates, and students from other schools cannot register.

Even if one manages to sneak in, it doesn't guarantee a satisfactory result. Gao, a student from Changsha University of Science & Technology, told reporters that many companies recruiting require candidates to present the university's recommendation letter, and without it, they can only return empty-handed.

Despite this, during interviews, reporters found many students from other schools still taking their chances at campus recruitment fairs organized by prestigious universities. They said that recruitment fairs hosted by different schools vary in scale, and some well-known companies rarely favor non-prestigious university students. To apply to large well-known companies, one must attend recruitment fairs at prestigious universities.

Online, someone is selling recruitment fair tickets: "In fact, I've already made preparations." Facing the "no-entry" barrier, Zhang Lei laughed as he told reporters. He logged onto a campus BBS, opened a post titled "Seeking two tickets for XX University Recruitment Fair." This was posted several days ago by him, and now there were numerous replies offering ticket sales information. "Universities rarely shut down employment forums because everyone recognizes the rule of 'freedom of speech' on forums, so we often search for various information on different school BBSs," Zhang Lei explained.

As campus recruitment fairs increase, so does the number of posts seeking tickets on campus BBS forums. Many students from other schools are willing to pay high prices to purchase tickets from other students to gain entry, and some students use their resources to act as "scalpers."

Actually, there are always students on campus holding stacks of tickets, selling them outside recruitment venues, attracting many interested buyers. However, buying tickets on campus BBS forums is relatively safer, and those familiar with the "routine" usually contact sellers through campus BBS forums. A ticket-selling student revealed to reporters that these tickets belong to classmates who have secured jobs or plan to take the postgraduate entrance exam. Selling prices typically range from 10 to 30 yuan, and sometimes even 50 yuan for very good opportunities.

Schools complain about the helplessness of such measures: Regarding the "information barriers," a responsible person from the Career Services Office of a certain university in Changsha stated that it is mainly for safety considerations, controlling the number of people attending the recruitment fair, and secondly due to employment indicator pressures, prioritizing the employment needs of their own students.

But teachers familiar with employment work told reporters: "Our pressure is increasing day by day. We painstakingly contacted companies, only to end up helping graduates from other schools, and no one wants to do such thankless work." This teacher expressed that it is understandable to organize a recruitment fair using the university's manpower and resources, providing exclusive job information to benefit their own graduates. Due to competition among universities in employment, once fully open, it could potentially impact the school's employment work.

Some promotional meetings are attended early by many students from other schools, leaving the host school's students dissatisfied: "Who is the 'main character'?" An unnamed teacher said, "Many schools do this, and we follow tradition."

However, some experts believe that mutual construction of information barriers among universities artificially creates chaos and unreasonable distribution of employment information, making the already severe employment situation even worse and increasing students' psychological and economic burdens. Lack of transparency in job information also hinders companies from hiring suitable talents. In the short term, it may protect the employment of their own students, but in the long run, it will inevitably affect the overall interests of college student employment.

Students hope for shared employment information: How do students view the school's concerns? Chen Yiming, a fourth-year student at Central South University, believes it unnecessary, "Whether one is chosen by a company depends on one's abilities, and I don't mind competing with students from other schools. Moreover, each school's recruitment fair has its own characteristics, and I hope to visit other schools, naturally increasing my opportunities."

Many graduating students express dissatisfaction with universities blocking employment information. Most employers at social recruitment fairs and public recruitment websites seek experienced job seekers, making university employment websites the most practical source of information for fresh graduates. Many graduates state that the job-hunting battle is an information war, and gaining more information about recruitment fairs increases one's chances.

"Now the employment situation is very serious. Some positions may not attract much interest from this university's graduates but might appeal to students from other schools. Companies aim for optimal selection, and complete competition can happen with a long-term perspective. 'Local protectionism' will inevitably affect the overall interests of college student employment." Zhang Lei hopes that universities can establish a shared platform for employment information.

Relatively speaking, universities in Beijing are more lenient. On the 13th, reporters accompanied the Changsha Talent Attraction Group to Peking University, Tsinghua University, Renmin University of China, University of Science and Technology Beijing, and Beihang University. It was found that at any university's specialized recruitment fair, many students from other universities in Beijing come to participate. When asked how they obtained the information, it was mostly from the employment websites of various universities. Regardless of which school's students attend, they can freely listen to recommendations or participate in recruitment fairs in lecture halls without hindrance. (Reported by Xu Yuan, Intern Xiao Ye)

(Source: People's Daily Online)
