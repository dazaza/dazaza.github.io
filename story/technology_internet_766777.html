Google's development of a universal translation tool has achieved mutual translation between 71 languages.

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 21:02:03

Can technology break down the barriers of language? Google clearly believes the answer is yes. Under the leadership of a German computer scientist, the company is developing a universal translation tool for the world. Microsoft and Facebook are also researching similar technologies.

When science fiction writers envision humanity's future, there are always some recurring ideas to improve the world. For example, free and inexhaustible energy, spaceships that travel at the speed of light, and mini-computers capable of translating all languages in the universe.

The internet giant Google is attempting to turn the last dream into reality, led by a computer scientist from a small village in southwestern Germany.

Franz Josef Och, 41 years old, has long been focused on one goal: building the perfect translation computer. It is so low-profile and agile that "people easily overlook its existence, only hearing a whisper in their ear. This device can instantly translate conversations or texts written in any language—such as web content."

Och holds a doctorate in computer science, with his dissertation focusing on artificial intelligence. The U.S. Department of Defense’s Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA) recruited him to California. In 2004, Och received a call from Larry Page, one of Google's founders. He asked Och: Don't you want to take it to the next level? Thus, Och came to lead his own department—Google Translate.

Bold futuristic fantasies are one of Google's business models, from self-driving cars to research on extending life, this internet giant has ventured into many cutting-edge fields. Although so far, Google Translate seems to have shown little progress, which may be because Och and the team he leads have greater ambitions: they want to break down the barriers between different languages.

This German scientist and his team have made significant progress. Google Translate can now translate back and forth between 71 languages, such as English to German; Icelandic to Japanese, or vice versa.

You just need to paste the text you want translated into the blank box on the Google Translate page, and it will instantly translate it into the target language. With just a click of a button, an Italian website can be translated into German or any of the other 69 languages. You can also use your smartphone to take a picture of a confusing sign at a Japanese subway station, and the translation tool will automatically recognize the text in the image and translate it.

Last year, Google Translate was used 200 million times. So far, the service has been completely free. But many multinational companies would surely be willing to pay for automatic translation services; in the future, as the quality of machine translations improves, Google could start charging anytime.

Currently, the company's goal is still to refine this service and integrate it into smartphones. The translation team has already developed an application that turns a smartphone into a talking translation machine. So far, this app can handle about 24 languages. As long as the sentences being translated are relatively simple, the app works pretty well. For example, if someone wants to tell a taxi driver in Beijing that they need to hurry to a pharmacy, they just need to say the request in a familiar language (like German) into their phone, and the phone will immediately repeat their intention in Chinese: “Please take me to a pharmacy.” Och feels that currently this program is still "a bit sluggish and clumsy, because buttons still need to be pressed." More importantly, the quality of the translation isn't very stable. However, just a few years ago, even the current level of performance was unimaginable.

Decades ago, IBM laid the foundation for automatic translation, but the project progressed slowly and was soon abandoned. Due to this precedent, Google Translate was initially just "a pure research project" during its first year, but recently they have made tremendous progress, which Och even calls a "leap forward".

The translation team is growing in size at Google's Silicon Valley headquarters, with members including several German computer experts, but not a single linguist. Och himself is not skilled in linguistics. On the contrary, he says, "I find learning languages difficult, which is precisely the charm of machine translation: what matters is being good at mathematics and statistics, and knowing how to program."

Developing dictionaries, defining grammatical structures... these kinds of rules simulating human translation have nothing to do with Google Translate. In fact, these language rules are often too flexible and imprecise, making them difficult for computers to process. For instance, in English and German, adjectives come before nouns, whereas in French, they come after. Therefore, if translation software strictly adheres to grammatical rules, it quickly gets "lost".

The Google Translate team follows their own expertise: handling massive amounts of data and statistical analysis. "What our system does is compare existing translated texts, learn from itself, for example, what is the most common translation of a word or combination of words in another language," Och said, "Ultimately, we calculate the probability of a translation." The larger the amount of reference data available, the more accurate the system becomes. Therefore, only after the advent of the Internet did machine translation become possible. The World Wide Web provides an enormous amount of ready-made translated texts.

As the translation software collects data, it learns. For example, when asked to translate a sentence from French to German, the software first searches online for corresponding words and phrases, then calculates how to construct a new sentence based on the collected information. This method is quite effective, but far from perfect. Grammar, tone, and ambiguity remain major challenges for automatic translation software. While the results are understandable, they are a linguist's nightmare. Machines obviously don’t understand the beauty of words. Is it necessary to teach software how to elegantly craft sentences? Och thinks it's unnecessary. The key lies in "learning more effectively." In other words, computers need to master the art of imitation.

From the perspective of the translation program, the effect of translating between two languages is usually satisfactory when those languages have similar grammar and word structures and there is a large amount of reference data. Translation between English and Spanish is relatively flawless, while translation between English and Japanese is less satisfactory. "Unfortunately, English and German also belong to the challenging combinations," Och said, "which particularly troubles me."

But what is Google's ultimate goal? Does it want to monopolize human communication? Or does it hope to control global exchanges?

Och says business strategies are not within his jurisdiction. But he states: "Machine translation allows many people to access information that they couldn't obtain before, which makes other things possible." What's important are the indirect benefits they create. Google employs a similar strategy with other products. Its search engine and email services are currently free, but they have brought billions of users and related information to Google.

Meanwhile, Google is also facing increasingly fierce competition. A few weeks ago, social networking site Facebook acquired a small company. This company developed a language input and automatic translation system. Facebook hopes this system will facilitate communication between users from different countries. Currently, this social network has over a billion users in more than 200 countries and regions worldwide.

The European Union has also developed its own automatic translation software called MT@EC. It is mainly used to translate bureaucratic jargon. Due to budget cuts resulting from the financial crisis, the EU may have to reduce its translator workforce by 10% in the coming years.

Microsoft has also launched its own translation project. Last autumn, Rick Rashid, head of Microsoft's Global Operations, announced their research results at a press conference in China. Rashid speaks English, and this Microsoft translation tool simultaneously translated his words into Mandarin—not using a monotonous computer voice, but rather Rashid's own voice, assembled from previously recorded samples. This at least gives machine translation a touch of humanity.
