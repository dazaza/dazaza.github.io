Research on the Characteristics of Next Generation Network Services

by lk0pcdd on 2009-07-22 16:54:03

The Next Generation Network (NGN) is a research hotspot that the industry is focusing on, with all standard organizations, research institutions, operators, and equipment developers actively conducting research and experiments. The reason why NGN has received so much attention is closely related to the services it provides. It not only inherits various services provided by existing networks but also offers more diverse data services, multimedia services, and applications. Moreover, due to its layered structure and open interfaces, the way services are provided becomes more flexible and varied, with richer and more personalized service categories.

### I. Service Requirements of the Next Generation Network

NGN is a network capable of providing full-service offerings, including voice, data, video, streaming media, Internet access, digital TV broadcasting, mobile services, and various bandwidth, wired, and wireless services and applications. It also provides an open service interface, allowing multiple service providers to build and provide services. From the user's perspective, NGN includes all telecommunications services and applications provided in the current service environment (such as fixed telephone services, wireless/mobile services, broadcast and distributed services, and the Internet). From a technical standpoint, NGN involves various technologies such as telecommunications technology, computer technology, security technology, electronic application technology, and broadcast technology.

From current research, the demand for next-generation network services mainly manifests in openness, compatibility, mobility, interoperability, convergence, customization, security reliability, maintainability, manageability, controllability, and billing capability. NGN uses an open service architecture and system protocols, enabling multiple parties to participate in service provision through flexible framework structures or service engines. This allows for the flexible and rapid creation and delivery of services in various ways, adapting to and supporting different types of media, encoding schemes, service categories, methods, and bandwidth requirements. It supports multiple access technologies and terminals, user and terminal mobility, secure and reliable multimedia communication, emergency services, lawful interception, and other regulatory requirements. It also supports the convergence of fixed and mobile networks and services, allowing for shared network resources and management functions, and offering different real-time or post-processing billing strategies for different services and applications.

### II. Classification of Next Generation Network Services

The services provided by the next generation network to users are numerous and involve different levels and types. The transmission layer serves as the physical foundation of the network and can directly offer L1 VPN services, bandwidth and circuit wholesale services, pipe leasing, equipment leasing, fiber infrastructure, and wavelength leasing. The bearer layer is based on packet-switched networks and can provide broadband leased lines, ATM/FR access, L2VPN, L3VPN, and other Internet access and bearer services. The service layer controls and manages network services, offering users a wide variety of multimedia services and applications, including voice, data, and video. In essence, the service layer is the most diverse and crucial level where NGN provides services.

The types and characteristics of services at the service layer are very extensive, making it difficult to foresee all possible future applications and services, let alone classify them accurately without overlap. Currently, there are several classification methods, such as categorizing by the basic attributes and provision methods, functional features, communication attributes, session control, etc.

From the perspective of the basic attributes and provision methods, the service layer services can be divided into basic services, supplementary services, and enhanced services. Basic services are provided by the call/session control entity in the NGN network, representing the most common services and forming the basis for supplementary services. These typically include basic point-to-point voice, fax, and video services. Supplementary services are also provided by the call/session control entity in the NGN network, adding user service data and service features to basic services, such as number display, call forwarding, and multi-party communication. Enhanced services are provided by the service control entity within the NGN network or by third parties, featuring rich service characteristics. These services are controlled by the service control entity or third parties to complete service control and data functions, while controlling the call/session control entity in the network to handle calls.

According to functional features, the service layer services can be categorized into interactive and distribution types. Interactive services include conversational, messaging, and query-based services. Distribution services feature point-to-multipoint communication and one-way information exchange, which can be further divided into those without user-specific expression control and those with user-specific expression control. Conversational services provide end-to-end bidirectional real-time communication between users or between users and the network, such as telephone, video conferencing, high-capacity file transfer, real-time control, remote sensing, alarms, medical image transmission, and remote gaming. Messaging services enable communication between users through storage-forwarding units, mailboxes, and/or message processing units, such as electronic video mailbox services and email. Query services allow users to retrieve information stored in an information center, which sends the information to users on demand. This information can include mobile images, pictures, documents, and data. Distribution services without user-specific expression control continuously deliver information streams to authorized users, who can access the information at any time but cannot decide or control it, such as TV programs, electronic newspapers, and e-publications. Distribution services with user-specific expression control simultaneously deliver information from a central source to many authorized users, where the information is cyclically provided as ordered entities, and users can control the information, such as full-channel visual text broadcasts, remote teaching and training, and electronic advertising.
