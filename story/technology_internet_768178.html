Google Nexus 5: A Mediocre Effort

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 18:50:55

By Guoren

As a long-time Nexus user, I must discuss my disappointment with the Nexus 5. Has Android become mediocre? Is Google out of ideas?

In the past, before its official release, the Nexus 5 had already been leaked multiple times, but there were few standout features. Initially, rumors suggested that Google would hold a product launch event around October 18th. In the end, Google merely announced the Nexus 5 and Android 4.4 (KitKat) on its official website this morning. This marked the first time Google released a Nexus phone and a new version of Android without holding a press conference, possibly because they felt neither the hardware nor the software was particularly impressive. Let's take a closer look at these two products:

Hardware: Nexus 5

The Nexus 5 is no longer the "darling" or "flagship" device it once was; it has become a budget smartphone focused on offering value for money. In fact, the Nexus 5, still manufactured by LG, can be seen as a simplified version of LG's G2.

When comparing specifications, the Nexus 5 and G2 share very similar key configurations in terms of screen, camera, and processor. The much-discussed "optical image stabilization" feature is also present in the LG G2. Conversely, the Nexus 5 has a slightly smaller screen (4.95 inches vs. 5.2 inches), a smaller battery capacity (2300mAh vs. 3000mAh), and lower camera resolution (front 1.3MP, rear 8MP compared to G2's front 2MP, rear 13MP).

LG G2 vs Nexus 5 Specifications

The only distinguishing feature might be that the Nexus 5 supports wireless charging and uses ceramic buttons, but these points are not particularly innovative. Thus, the current Nexus phones are no longer geeky and unique devices; they have become tools for Google to popularize the latest version of Android.

Currently, the LG G2 retail price in China is 4299 RMB, while the 32GB version of the Nexus 5 costs just over 2400 RMB in the U.S. As such, cost-effectiveness has become the most prominent selling point of the Nexus 5.

Software: Android 4.4 KitKat

What upgrades does Android 4.4 bring? Here’s a brief list:

1. A smarter new dialer function.

2. Automatic system resource cleanup for low-end devices (with less than 512MB RAM).

3. A new lock screen interface and some new icons.

4. Always-on voice search activated by saying “OK, Google.”

5. Integration of Hangouts and SMS.

6. "Immersive mode," which hides the status bar during full-screen activities like reading.

7. An Emoji keyboard added.

8. Wireless printing functionality.

9. Full-screen wallpapers.

10. Screen recording capability.

11. Pedometer function, which is quite practical.

12. Semi-transparent interfaces (in some areas).

(Pedometer function interface)

While Android 4.4 introduces some useful new features, such as wireless printing and the pedometer, it lacks revolutionary upgrades. Many of its features are simply catching up with what iOS has already done, such as integrating instant messaging with SMS, wireless printing, full-screen wallpapers, and semi-transparent interfaces.

Even though Android smartphones now account for 80% of the global smartphone market share, Google cannot afford to stand still. Android needs more innovative features and application updates to stay ahead.
