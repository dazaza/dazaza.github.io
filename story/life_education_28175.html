Blog Chinese Translation » Advice for students: How to do research well

by huahuaniu on 2007-08-22 08:47:13

1. **Make a plan!** I tell my students that the first step in writing a research paper is to acknowledge that you really have a research paper to write. Write up a schedule that includes a series of important milestones to be achieved by certain dates (e.g., find 10 sources by September 20, finish preliminary research by October 15). Follow your schedule. You will also need time to see what materials you lack, what can be found in the library, choose relevant materials, read and take notes, and integrate them. Afterward, you need to clarify the points you want to make in your paper.

2. **Start with Wikipedia, and don't stop.** Wikipedia is a great place to start your research. You can search for keywords related to your topic, browse through the links on each page, and then look at the resources it suggests. Take notes, especially for any good resources it recommends. Our goal here is to get a relatively comprehensive overview of the subject you are writing about. And because of its hyperlink nature, Wikipedia is more useful than most printed materials. By the time you're ready to start writing, you should have gathered many better sources than those presented on Wikipedia. So, do not directly cite Wikipedia in your paper.

3. **Organize your bibliography.** Once you've found a good academic book or article on your topic, that's great. At the end of it, there will be dozens or even hundreds of references for you to explore. Usually, you can skim through the bibliography and note down titles that seem relevant to your research. Academic writers don’t usually try too hard to come up with creative names for their titles, so it's usually easy to understand their research direction from the title and subtitles. Next, go back to the beginning and see if you recognize any author names, which will be helpful. After completing this work for the first book, you can continue doing this for subsequent books. This way, you will quickly gather far more resources than you need. (But you do need to do this, because your library may not have all the mentioned books and journals, and inter-library loans are slow and not very valuable for students who need to complete papers by the end of the semester.)

4. **Keep a research question in mind.** Academically speaking, when data is presented to you, your topic should emerge from your research. When you conduct research, you need a "research question," which is the question you want to answer. When you encounter new material, ask yourself if it helps answer your question. You should discard materials that appear relevant but actually do not help answer your question. Finding a large number of materials may seem attractive, and indeed finding some materials is necessary, but too many will waste your research time. Find one or two good sources as background (what you initially found on Wikipedia is often enough), and then focus on answering your research question.

5. **Handle one argument at a time.** Don't try to tackle your entire topic at once. Get a general feel for the whole topic, and create an outline for what you need to understand. Then handle one argument at a time. After completing the first draft, you will discover connections between arguments.

6. **Use an information organization system.** When starting your research, you should have an idea of how to collect and organize your notes and data. Although I have written papers using index cards, my favorite system is still a notebook dedicated to a single topic. On the top of a new page, I write the name of the reference, then copy out quotations I need, and take notes. On the side, I add page numbers as labels, and jot down any thoughts that suddenly occur to me while reading. I like to use computers efficiently, so I have tried databases, Wiki software, outlines, and other software, but I have never found any system particularly effective. I spend all my time dealing with the software itself rather than doing my research. No matter what system you use, ensure that every quotation, fact, and idea can be traced back to its source. This way, you can easily add references when writing.

7. **Understand the resources you will use.** Spend some time understanding what online or offline resources your library offers. Most libraries provide introductions for students, or allow students to communicate with librarians. At least, you should walk around the library and know where things are located. Pay special attention to the microfilm storage room and the journal room, as you will use them in many research projects. Most university libraries subscribe to many academic databases, many of which are online. You need to know which ones can be accessed from home. For example, J-STOR stores full-text images of hundreds of academic journals, and they are all easy to search. You can have a sudden thought at midnight, log in, and print out two or three related papers to read in the morning.

8. **Seek help.** Not only should you utilize these materials, but you should also utilize the human resources available to you. Many professors spend time in their offices waiting for a student to come in with a question worth their time waiting. Be that student! Seek help when searching for and evaluating materials. You can also ask someone to help you identify the uses of the materials you have already collected. Another frequently overlooked resource is friendly librarians. In my eyes, they are the best people in the world: they know everything within their jurisdiction from cover to cover. They care deeply about the utilization of these materials, and their job is to make information more accessible. Most librarians are happy to help you find materials related to your project. Some librarians can even pinpoint hard-to-find information to a specific volume. Of course, don't forget to seek help from fellow students. Some may be working on topics closely related to yours.

9. **Carry a notebook to record ideas.** When you truly start your project, your brain is also searching through the materials you have read. If you're like me, you might get sudden insights at the most inconvenient times, such as in the bathroom, at the supermarket, or just before going to sleep. Carry a small notebook and pen with you. Record your inspirations, and transfer them to your research log (or software) as soon as possible.

10. **Keep your research current.** Pay attention to the publication dates of your materials—while using older materials is fine, you definitely want your references to come from the last decade. If research on your topic has almost dried up in the last ten years, it could mean the field has changed, but it could also mean funding disappeared, key researchers passed away, or other accidental factors. A small trick is to Google the main researchers and see if you can find their homepage. On the homepage, most people list their recent research activities and published articles. If so, you can try inter-library loans, or contact the researcher directly and ask if they can send you a draft or a copy. Have the courage to explain what you are researching, what you hope to discover, how your research is progressing, and what insights their articles might offer to your topic. Do not ask for a reference list or specifics about your topic; no one wants to do the work that should be yours.

These tips will help you obtain a good bibliography and thorough notes and data before you start writing. While evaluating materials is also an important part of good research, I will write about this separately because it involves too much to cover in one point. Librarians or your professor can help you, especially if your materials are limited to what your library can provide. Online resources are harder to manage. But remember, not long ago, we did successful research without the internet. Just typewriters. It's like climbing to the top of a mountain barefoot in the snow.
