In ultraviolet light, UV damage to the skin is the culprit. Tanning, swelling, and even blisters can occur, causing the skin to age quickly. UV radiation consists of UVA and UVB. UVB can burn the skin in a short time, causing redness from the sun (for most people, this occurs in about 25 minutes). The majority of sunscreen products available today focus on blocking UVB rays, with SPF being the standard measure of UVB protection.

UVA is the silent killer that penetrates the skin's surface, affecting deeper dermal tissues. It destroys collagen, elastic fibers, and other microstructures within the skin, leading to wrinkles, fine lines, loose skin, and rapid aging. In the sun’s ultraviolet energy distribution, UVA is 15 times more prevalent than UVB and is the primary cause of tanning. It reduces the skin's hyaluronic acid content, leading to dryness, accelerates melanin formation, darkens the skin, and is a major cause of skin cancer.

SPF refers to a product's ability to protect against UVB, specifically preventing redness and sunburn. Generally, human skin produces erythema in direct sunlight within 20-25 minutes. If a bottle of sunscreen has an SPF of 8, applying it extends the protection time to 20 (25) × 8 = 160 (200) minutes. Abroad, sunscreen packages often display both SPF values for UVB protection and PA+ signs for UVA protection. These represent the product's ability to prevent tanning. Products with these labels are usually more advanced technologically.

PA values are defined as follows:

- PA+: Effective protection for about 4 hours

- PA++: Effective protection for about 8 hours

- PA+++ : Super strong protection, found in higher-end imported sunscreens.
