Industry Characteristics of Data Analysis Explanation

by jintaimqr on 2012-03-05 16:25:09

Data analysis, except for pure manual laborers and assembly line workers who do not need to be involved, is very important for any industry and any position because it is an important standard that reflects professionalism, scientificity, and technical content. The logistics industry [http://www.jt077.cn/index.php?m=wlps] is no exception. In fact, everyone can perform data analysis, including elementary school students; it's just that the level of data analysis differs. One very important characteristic of data analysis is its industry-specific nature. That is, different industries determine that the methods of data analysis, data analysis tools, data analysis software, and the presentation of data analysis results have characteristics unique to their respective industries.

Logistics [http://www.jt077.cn/index.php?m=wlps] data analysis also exhibits distinct industry-specific colors, which hints at two issues. First, as a qualified logistics data analyst, one must be very familiar with the logistics industry and deeply understand the business processes, having profound experiences and insights into each detail. Second, one must have a clear understanding of the data characteristics of the industry: Is the data measurement-based or count-based? Is the average value reliable, or should we rely on the median? Which logistics data analysis tools should be mastered? Should we use graphical representations or statistical analysis tools? What kind of analysis software is appropriate? Minitab, Excel, SPSS, or JMP? Finally, the presentation of results is also very important. One needs to understand the corporate culture and leadership style of the company. Moreover, attention to detail is crucial. For example, when sending emails, is it necessary to attach the source data? Should recommendations be included along with conclusions?

In summary, the industry-specific features of data analysis indicate that when job hunting, we should not treat the role of data analyst or data analysis specialist as a separate job intention. From both the corporate perspective and personal career development, the recognition and prospects for such roles are extremely bleak. Very few companies specifically set aside a dedicated position for data analysis; more often, this role is combined with business specialists or handled by the operations department. Pure data analysts, even if they master numerous data analysis tools and possess proficient analytical skills, will find the process challenging without rich business experience, and the results may often lead in the wrong direction, losing focus.
