Mobile game developers: iOS depends on chart manipulation, Android relies on connections

by anonymous on 2013-09-08 19:43:46

On July 24th last year, there was an incident at the Kerry Center in Shanghai: a mobile game developer who had traveled from afar used the tea break during a conference to have a face-to-face talk with a representative of a domestic app market. However, before the developer could finish demonstrating the game's demo on his laptop, the other party clearly proposed an 80-20 revenue-sharing ratio. A similar situation also occurred during his cooperation negotiation with another channel provider.

This is the current state of the continuously heating up mobile gaming market, reflecting the true balance of power between distribution channels and game developers. For mobile game developers, both channel and marketing costs are rising, as the revenue-sharing ratio between channels and content providers is increasingly tilting towards the channel platform providers, shifting from an initial 50-50 split to a current 30-70 split (channels taking 70%, content providers taking 30%).

"If in the PC game era, game development companies still had the potential to grow into giants through a quality game, then in the mobile game era, channel providers are more likely to become giant companies," internet expert Cao Yueping told reporters.

Channel providers suppressing content creators

In fact, compared to previous PC games and web games, mobile games rely much more heavily on channel providers.

According to data from Analysys Mason: currently, the two most mainstream download methods are iOS and Android systems. Among iOS mobile game users, the majority choose to download games from the official Apple App Store, accounting for as high as 66.8%. In contrast, among Android system mobile game users, 52.4% choose to use "mobile management tools" for installation. This means that, to some extent, channels have become the invisible hand truly controlling the fate of mobile games.

On the other hand, currently over 80% of mobile game channel traffic is concentrated in the hands of joint operation platforms such as Tencent, 91 Mobile Assistant, and UC Jiuyou. As the content provider side, if a game is to succeed, its choice of channel cooperation is very important.

It is understood that if mobile game developers wish to promote their games through the aforementioned channels, they can generally adopt a joint operation model, ultimately sharing revenue according to game income; or they can choose to buy advertisements to gain better promotional positions within these app stores. However, for smaller mobile game developers, it is impossible to bear this cost. Therefore, channel providers prefer the joint revenue-sharing method.

However, with the expansion of the mobile game market scale, domestic mobile game channels are growing in reverse. The lack of clear industry rules has led to frequent conflicts between developers and channel providers.

iOS relies on ranking manipulation, Android relies on relationships

A mobile game developer told reporters that today's mobile game market presents a situation where "iOS relies on ranking manipulation, and Android relies on relationships." This makes channel integration concentrate power, increasing the influence of giants, such as contracts that could be signed at a 40-60 revenue-sharing ratio at the beginning of the year but have now effectively reversed to a 30-70 split. The reason lies in some platform vendors imposing various entry fees and advertising promotion fees as disguised charges.

CEO Wu Gang of Wanshi Interactive previously stated that due to significantly lowered barriers to entry and the influx of web game and PC game creators, mobile games experienced within one year what web games took three years to form - a vicious cycle where channels force products and game quality subsequently collapses.

CEO Wang Feng of Blueport Online believes that just like web games, mobile game developers have virtually no capital to fight against channels, and their fear lies in the fact that the mobile game market has already entered the prelude to disorder.

Huge disparity between domestic and foreign mobile game markets

The above conditions of the domestic mobile game market present a stark contrast to South Korea's mobile game market. Cao Yueping pointed out that even in South Korea where channels are strong, the market still maintains relatively reasonable revenue-sharing ratios, mainly due to the restraining mechanisms in South Korea's mobile game market. Taking KakaoTalk, South Korea's channel platform, as an example, its first round of external investment came from top research and development companies in South Korea such as Nexon and Hangame, which resulted in a mechanism restraint imposed by mobile game developers on channel providers.

"And in China, channels always aim to dominate alone, while developers of a certain scale dream of becoming channels themselves, so forming a mechanism similar to South Korea's mobile game market is impossible," said Cao Yueping.
