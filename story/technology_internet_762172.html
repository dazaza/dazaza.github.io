The collapse of the monopoly position of the Windows operating system

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 14:07:46

Fifteen years ago, the PC industry was at its peak, and its core technology, the Windows operating system, was also unmatched. Fifteen years later, PCs have no advantage compared to smartphones and tablets, and the monopoly of the Windows operating system is no longer.

In the late 1990s, there was an American tech company that could be considered unrivaled globally. The company was almost as rich as a country and had substantial technical strength. It not only dominated desktop operating systems but also saw increasing influence in other software fields. However, being too prominent invites attacks, and the U.S. government dragged this company into a prolonged antitrust lawsuit. Yes, it was Microsoft back then.

Microsoft eventually lost the case and had to accept strict legal restrictions. Many people were worried that this case would have significant negative effects on Microsoft, fearing that this idolized tech company would stagnate because of it. But facts show that over the past 15 years, what ultimately caused Microsoft to lose its dominance in the global personal computer market was not the antitrust lawsuit but changes in the market itself.

At that time, Microsoft had become the focal point of the global tech community, almost breaking records to become the world's first trillion-dollar company. In 1999, Microsoft reached its highest market value historically. According to Standard & Poor's data, Microsoft's record at the time was $620.58 billion. If we consider inflation factors at that time, Microsoft's total market capitalization on December 30, 1999, would be equivalent to about $850 billion today. However, the changes brought by the rapid development of the Internet were beyond Microsoft's control. Microsoft was merely one of the surging and beautiful waves in the global Internet tide.

The antitrust case back then indeed caused many losses for Microsoft, but they were not fatal. Due to the emergence and widespread adoption of new electronic products like cloud computing, smartphones, and tablets, the once-dominant desktop operating system no longer held the same prominence. As long as electronic devices can access the internet and run relevant programs, they can basically meet user needs, regardless of the operating system.

Through the following three tables, we can objectively understand the challenges faced by tech giants such as Microsoft, HP, Dell, and Intel before the new wave of the Internet.

Global Internet Device Shipment Statistics (2000-2012)

Firstly, let's look at the global Internet device shipment statistics: from 2000 to 2012, although PC shipments remained around 200 million units annually, after the appearance of smartphones and tablets, the market landscape underwent fundamental changes. Now, compared to smartphones and tablets, PC shipments pale in comparison.

Market Share of Major Global Platforms (Q1 2009 - Q1 2013)

From the table, we can see that the diversification of personal internet devices has significantly weakened the dominant position of the Windows operating system. This change has only appeared in recent years. Three years ago, 70% of global PCs used the Windows operating system. Now, due to the widespread adoption of Google's Android system and Apple's iOS platform, the global market share of the Windows operating system has decreased by almost 30% to 50% compared to before. The most noticeable change is that Android's global market share has surpassed Windows.

Finally, let's take a look at the statistical data analyzed by Horace Dediu, an analyst from the U.S. research firm Asymco. The PC business appears "small" when compared to the explosive sales data of smartphones and tablets, and the PC business itself has shown a clear downward trend. Now, internet devices have become diversified. Although PC technology is already quite mature, PCs are no longer the most cost-effective or convenient devices for internet access. The status of PCs has been overturned by smartphones and tablets, and personal computers have become specialized office equipment.

Annual Growth Rate of PC Shipments with the Windows Operating System

From the above graph, we can see that before the release of the iPad, PCs with the Windows operating system had impressive shipment growth. However, starting from the end of 2010, the growth rate of PC shipments has been sluggish, and recently, there has even been a noticeable negative growth.

In fact, it's not all bad news for Microsoft. Microsoft's performance in transformation is commendable, and Microsoft's Office suite remains an important source of revenue. Although Google and Apple have launched their own office software, they have yet to pose a fatal threat to Office.

Time flies, and it's been 15 years since Microsoft's antitrust case. The once monopolistic product — the Windows operating system — will never be sued for monopoly again, and the PC industry closely related to the Windows operating system also faces an uncertain future.
