Five photo sharing websites for you to choose - DoNews.com

by cacard on 2007-08-09 07:39:49

Yahoo recently announced that it will close its Yahoo Photos service on September 20th, forcing users to decide what to do with their photos. The photo storage and sharing service on the Yahoo website has been running for about seven years, but its popularity clearly lags behind newer services like Flickr.com. However, Flickr.com is also owned by Yahoo.

Now, users of Yahoo Photos must either transfer their entire uploaded albums to a new site or choose a new platform to start sharing digital photos. Facing such a daunting task, they are worried about making the wrong choice.

This week, I compared the features of five popular photo-sharing websites – Kodak Gallery, Shutterfly, Snapfish, Flickr, and Photobucket. Although there are many other sites offering similar services, such as SmugMug and Webshots, I focused my attention on the five photo-sharing sites recommended by Yahoo.

Among these sites, I already had free accounts on four of them, and registering for the fifth one was not difficult. Besides using the websites, I tested each company's services regarding privacy policies, community sharing, editing, storage limits, handling of dormant accounts, creating photo projects like books, and uploading images via email or mobile devices.

Each company offers countless features, which can be very confusing. Two sites, Kodak Gallery and Snapfish, require users to make at least one purchase per year; otherwise, their photos will be deleted (with prior warning via email). Each site provides free accounts, and if customers want to upgrade their accounts, except for Shutterfly, they charge an annual fee of $25. Photobucket and Flickr excel in creating shared communities for continuous sharing, while Kodak Gallery, Snapfish, and Shutterfly focus more on storing uploaded images. Shared sites have storage limits, while others do not.

Below, I will list the strengths and weaknesses of each photo service and briefly comment on the overall feel and practicality of each website.

Kodak Gallery

(www.kodakgallery.com)

This is a comprehensive and straightforward photo album-sharing site. While its photo-editing options may not be the most user-friendly, it might be the best among the five. Most sites expect users to edit their photos before sharing. Earlier this year, Kodak launched a new version of its EasyShare desktop software, enhancing editing capabilities such as full-screen image display.

In addition to the $25 annual fee for the Gallery Premier account, users can opt for a $50 annual account with discounts on Kodak prints (10 cents per print instead of the usual 15 cents). With a paid account, users can download high-resolution photos and obtain a password-protected personal photo-sharing URL. However, the other four sites offer free personal URLs, not just for paid accounts.

Shutterfly

(www.shutterfly.com)

Shutterfly seems to be the simplest of the five photo-sharing sites, though it’s not the most attractive or user-friendly. All its features are free. Unlike Kodak Gallery and Snapfish, Shutterfly users can retain their accounts without purchasing any related products, and their friends can browse shared albums without logging in.

However, Shutterfly's simplicity has its downsides. Users cannot upload audio, download high-resolution photos, or send photos to the site via email or mobile devices. The other four sites offer these functions either for free or through paid accounts.

Snapfish

(www.snapfish.com)

Snapfish is Hewlett-Packard Co.'s photo-sharing site, and it stands out for having the most restrictions. In addition to requiring users to make at least one purchase per year to retain their accounts, visitors who wish to view your albums must log in every time; this setting cannot be changed like on other sites. To circumvent this issue, Snapfish emphasizes its Group Rooms – personalized shared sites accessible via a specific URL and optional password.

In my opinion, both Snapfish and Shutterfly display photos too small on their sites, but Snapfish’s photo slideshow displays quite large – a strength. I wish the site would show larger photos under other circumstances as well. Users can download high-resolution photos for 25 cents for the first photo and 5 cents for each additional photo.

Flickr

(www.flickr.com)

Photobucket.com

(www.photobucket.com)

Between these two community-sharing sites, I prefer Flickr. It feels cleaner and less cluttered than Photobucket, with fewer ads. For those not accustomed to "trendy" sites, Photobucket and Flickr might seem extreme. They offer features like tagging and use various new terms that can often be confusing. On Flickr, "albums" are replaced by "sets," and photo organization occurs in "batches." Photobucket provides album management, allowing users to create sub-albums within a main album.

Neither site requires users to purchase anything, and both allow free downloads of high-resolution photos. Unlike one-time sharing, these sites continuously update photo blogs for friends to check at any time.

Through Flickr and Photobucket, users can easily post photos on blogs like Blogger and Typepad. Photobucket also supports posting photos on MySpace and Facebook.

On Flickr, users can find like-minded people by searching groups. I joined a group that shares tennis court photos from around the world. Without chatting or leaving overused comments to strangers, digital photos suddenly provide us with a new way to socialize online.

In other aspects, Photobucket allows users to create Remixes – presentations combining photos, audio, storylines, music, transitions, and some graphics. Creating a Remix takes only seconds, but the final "product" is very interesting and looks professional.

After testing these sites, I believe the ideal solution would be to combine the best features of all these sites into one excellent photo-sharing platform. In reality, each site has its shortcomings. However, Kodak Gallery and its EasyShare software program effectively combine editing and sharing functions. Flickr is my preferred photo community site, and both it and Photobucket offer fresh ways to share digital photos.

Katherine Boehret
