It was reported that a video has recently gone viral online. On the evening of February 16th, a fire broke out at a restaurant on Gui Street in Beijing. The fire truck responding to the alarm encountered social vehicles cutting in front of it and overtaking it along the way. From the video, it can be seen that the speed of the fire truck was affected, and the drivers of these social vehicles completely ignored the right of way granted to fire trucks.

Speaking of "privileges," it is somewhat repulsive. However, in fact, public authorities in any country are granted many privileges through laws, such as those for fire trucks and ambulances. For instance, according to the "Beijing Fire Regulations (Revised Draft)," it states: "Fire trucks... under the premise of ensuring safety, are not restricted by driving speed, route, direction, or traffic signals; other vehicles and pedestrians must yield and not overtake them. In case of emergency, obstacles and vehicles hindering the passage of fire trucks may be removed or forced to yield... Traffic management departments of the public security organs shall take necessary traffic control measures to ensure the rapid passage of fire trucks carrying out firefighting and emergency rescue tasks." Similarly, the "Road Traffic Safety Law of the People's Republic of China" also contains similar provisions.

In theory, since there are clear legal provisions, one should simply act according to the law. A relevant person from the Beijing Fire Brigade also stated that vehicles obstructing the passage could be fined or even detained according to the "Public Security Administration Punishment Law." However, there are issues in the enforcement process. First, the relevant departments may not have the time or capacity to handle it, and second, strict enforcement might lead to dissatisfaction among the public.

Why is this so? Because the common people have long been dissatisfied with various privileged phenomena, and "privilege" has become one of the main factors intensifying social contradictions. Actually, actively yielding to fire trucks, ambulances, police cars, etc., should be basic knowledge taught at the kindergarten level, something even children understand. However, due to the rampant abuse of extralegal privileges, people have developed a general attitude of rejection towards legally defined privileges. For example, in Beijing, "traffic control" is almost an everyday occurrence. No matter how urgent your situation—whether your house is on fire, you have a sick person in the car, or you're rushing to work or a business meeting—if a policeman waves his hand, you have to stop and wait until some VIP's convoy passes by. As someone who is not a legal professional, I wonder whether our laws and regulations clearly stipulate that officials' movements require silence and clearance. If, hypothetically, during the movement of a high-ranking official when traffic control is implemented, there is an emergency requiring immediate response, we don't know whether the privilege of the fire truck or the privilege of the VIPs would take precedence.

Saying all this is not to excuse those who obstructed the fire truck. Since the incident has been exposed, it is hoped that the relevant departments will strictly enforce the law and impose appropriate penalties on the parties involved. However, punishing one illegal act is easy, but winning the hearts of the people is far more difficult.
