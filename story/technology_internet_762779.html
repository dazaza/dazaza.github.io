"The Inevitable 'Death of Yougou': Belle's E-commerce Has Many Fatal Flaws"

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 17:17:03

Recently, Yougou.com, which has been suffering from a severe loss of senior executives, received another blow. On July 17th, it was reported that according to an insider at Yougou.com, the company's COO Zhang Xiaojun is set to leave and is currently processing his resignation. As one of Yougou.com's founding members, Zhang Xiaojun's departure holds significant implications, indicating that Yougou.com is indeed facing serious challenges.

Let’s take a look back at the e-commerce journey of Belle International, to which Yougou.com belongs:

A few years ago, Belle quietly tested the waters of e-commerce, developing steadily through three channels: Taobao distribution, Tmall, and B2C distribution (B2C distribution was completed via Taoxiu.com). Led by veteran group member Xie Yunli, this not-so-lavish e-commerce team first sold products under Belle's own brands before gradually introducing exclusive online models and then new online brands. This development path was considered a successful case of traditional brand enterprises entering e-commerce in a more solid manner than Li-Ning before 2011.

As Letao.com and Haolemai gained over $100 million in financing and rapidly developed in 2010, supplying these two companies became a concern for Belle Group. On one hand, they were worried about being constrained by distribution channels again; on the other hand, after evaluation, they realized they had enormous development advantages in the e-commerce sector. These advantages include:

One, a multitude of brands: Besides its several major women's shoe brands, the sales of internationally renowned sports brands such as Adidas and Nike have grown astonishingly fast;

Two, easier category expansion: For Belle Group, which owns its own women's shoe brands and acts as an agent for well-known sports brands, how to achieve absolute market advantage and status through multi-brands and multi-categories has been a question they’ve pondered deeply over the years. Expanding offline stores to add brands and categories obviously takes too long, whereas their ongoing e-commerce operations can easily expand brands but cannot quickly expand categories;

Three, financial advantage: In cooperation with e-commerce enterprises, Belle discovered that e-commerce is a capital-intensive business. With its financial strength, Belle could develop without relying on external capital, allowing it to outlast many vertical e-commerce sites that depend heavily on capital;

Four, talent advantage: After several years of e-commerce practice, Belle has already cultivated a highly professional and execution-oriented e-commerce team. The talent base allows them to handle the scale of e-commerce operations or even beyond;

Five, supply chain advantage: This is what excites Belle internally the most, and it is also the core reason why it attracts top executives from brand e-commerce or platform e-commerce. After all, for e-commerce, supply chain management is always fascinating because it seems to be the decisive factor besides capital. The supply chain includes product research and development, production, warehouse management, delivery, after-sales service, and a series of "business" contents. In traditional enterprises that prioritize "business" over "e-commerce," this is considered the greatest competitive advantage over pure e-commerce enterprises.

With these advantages, Belle e-commerce decided to go all out. First, they purchased the double-character domain name yougou.com,

and abandoned the somewhat counterfeit “topshoes.cn” Taoshuxiu website for redirection, stopping supplies to all distribution websites while retaining self-operated third-party online shops like Taobao and Tmall Mall but reducing investment and attention. They hired JD.com VPs and Vancl VPs as CMO and COO respectively, sent group veterans as CEOs, forming a golden triangle. Their goal was to create a full-category vertical B2C mall, implementing multi-brand strategies on one hand, and expanding into apparel and other categories on the other, realizing the dream of a department store empire that couldn't be achieved offline!

All of this sounds very promising, but every dream needs to face the harsh reality. The situation is as follows:

One, higher technical requirements for independent B2C: Belle's headquarters in Shenzhen struggles to recruit more excellent technical talents, and there are even fewer outsourcing service companies in Shenzhen. Technology determines user experience, backend management efficiency, and reliable data. Independent B2C involves dynamic technology, different from the relatively static technologies like ERP, continuous changes remain a hurdle for many traditional enterprises;

Two, increased management difficulty due to category expansion: Someone once said that doing well in women's shoes doesn’t necessarily mean success in men's shoes, something Belle must have felt deeply when expanding into men's shoes. It's easy to imagine the difficulties encountered in the backend when expanding into women's clothing and accessories within a year. For traditional brand enterprises accustomed to meticulous calculations, this rapid approach worked during Vancl's special period and was understandable within the systems of Vancl and JD.com. Although Belle has acquired Vancl's COO and JD.com's CMO, whether they can be understood and execute with "exaggerated costs" is highly questionable!

Three, system issues: Many people find it hard to understand the differences between e-commerce and traditional enterprises. Simply put, department settings (organizational structure), approvals, finance and budget, reporting methods, corporate culture, communication styles, execution methods, risk control, process systems, etc., form what is called the "mechanism" of a company. The conflict results in e-commerce trying to persuade the acceptance of the "system", while the traditional team tries but fails to fully understand the "system" of e-commerce, leading to low efficiency and instability in the execution team. Ultimately, the chairman or board might say, "E-commerce and traditional enterprises each have their strengths and weaknesses, and the future will definitely involve integration, so you must integrate in these aspects...". If this so-called "integration without harmony" appears, it leads to so-called management innovation, and thus "CEO rotation system" actually appeared in a company like Belle!

It was originally thought that Yougou.com of Belle e-commerce had taken the lead, and its senior executives were waiting for the glory of a spin-off IPO, enjoying fame and wealth, setting countless examples of traditional enterprises succeeding in the e-commerce era. Instead, they found themselves entangled and troubled by these underlying problems.

After all, whether it's the old-timers who grew up with Belle or the poached e-commerce VPs, it's clear that they aren't leaving because of lack of ability. When the VP from JD.com returns to JD.com, they continue to perform exceptionally well, and when the VP from Vancl ventures into entrepreneurship, they do so with courage. Are the underlying problems just the three mentioned above? Or are these not only Belle's underlying problems but also the common issues faced by many traditional enterprises attempting to enter e-commerce?
