Another Foreign Investment Merger and Acquisition: Philips Acquires Pentelec for 2.5 Billion Yuan?

■ By reporter Li Bing

Recently, media reports have claimed that the acquisition of Pentelec by Philips has been largely finalized, with both parties set to officially sign in the near future. The acquisition funds are estimated at approximately 2.5 billion yuan, and the acquisition targets mainly include Pentelec's full range of small household appliances products and equipment, brands, marketing networks, and operational teams.

If this acquisition is successful, it will be another case of a domestic home appliance brand being acquired after Supor was acquired by a French company.

The Securities Daily contacted relevant individuals from Pentelec and Philips but did not receive any positive responses.

"I've heard the price mentioned is over 1 billion yuan, but if the final acquisition price is indeed 2.5 billion yuan as rumored, then Pentelec has definitely sold itself for a good price," renowned home appliance industry observer Liu Budusten told the Securities Daily.

Is the Dark Horse Black Enough?

In the industry, Pentelec is widely regarded as a dark horse.

According to available data, Pentelec, which primarily focuses on lifestyle and personal care small appliances, has acted as an agent and supplier for Midea air conditioners and as an OEM manufacturer for Midea, Gree, Joyoung, and other small home appliance products.

At the end of 2003, Pentelec created its own brand "Pentelec." Based on its performance, it has shown steady growth: achieving sales of 1 billion yuan in its first year of going public in 2004, reaching 4 billion yuan in 2005, nearly 8 billion yuan in 2006, and breaking through 10 billion yuan in 2007. In a situation where other small home appliance companies grow relatively slowly, Pentelec's growth has become an industry "dark horse."

"In terms of the small home appliance market, Pentelec has done well," Liu Budusten said during an interview.

However, currently, China's small home appliance industry has formed two types of enterprises: all-around enterprises represented by Midea and specialized enterprises represented by Joyoung and Supor. Compared to these two types of companies, Pentelec seems neither specialized nor all-around, and its main focus on household electrical appliances still appears thin in market competition.

In recent years, Philips has gradually weakened and exited the consumer electronics business globally. According to available data, Philips has successively sold its chip, mobile phone, semiconductor, LCD panel, and other businesses.

The purpose of this acquisition of Pentelec seems obvious: to strengthen its competitiveness in the Chinese small home appliance market.

Liu Budusten stated, "Running a business is about making money, and selling the small home appliance business aligns with Pentelec's pursuit of maximizing interests. With the increasingly fierce competition in the domestic small home appliance market, given Pentelec's current strength, product layout, and brand influence, achieving growth from 1 billion yuan to 10 billion yuan might be easy, but scaling up from 10 billion yuan to tens of billions is difficult—not just for Pentelec, but for any company. For Pentelec to break through and transform in the small home appliance sector, it needs larger capital and more technical drivers, which is the most challenging part."

One is struggling to grow, while the other urgently needs to strengthen its small home appliance market.

It seems that both parties found common ground.

Marking the Boat to Retrieve the Sword?

Liu Budusten stated, "Philips' purpose for this acquisition is very clear: to strengthen its small home appliance market, which is related to Philips' established strategy in China."

As a European veteran home appliance enterprise, Philips hasn't developed smoothly in China in recent years.

On Philips' official website, one can see that apart from razors, the product line mainly consists of coffee makers, air purifiers, water purifiers, vacuum cleaners, etc. However, these products currently hold a relatively small share of the domestic small home appliance market. Moreover, with intense competition among brands, Philips doesn't seem to have a significant advantage in its products other than razors.

According to statistics, due to losses in recent years, Philips has successively withdrawn from the competition in computer monitors, televisions, mobile phones, and other markets. Furthermore, Philips' performance in the Chinese small home appliance market in recent years has not been satisfactory, with growth rates far behind those of other Chinese home appliance enterprises.

"The Chinese market is absolutely something Philips cannot abandon," Liu Budusten commented.

In the recent phase, Philips has frequently encountered product quality issues in the Chinese market in its quest for market benefits. First, Philips energy-saving lamps were found non-compliant in inspections, encountering the problem of energy-saving lamps not saving energy. Then, a large number of Philips hair dryers were recalled, undoubtedly exacerbating the declining trend of Philips' brand and market sales.

In May 2011, due to potential safety hazards of smoking or combustion under extreme conditions, Philips recalled over 1.75 million units of three models of Philips (Philips) hair dryers in the Chinese region. Regarding the reason for this recall of Philips hair dryers, Philips stated that under extreme conditions, there might be overheating phenomena, leading to safety hazards such as plastic deformation, smoking, or combustion. Philips indicated that the number of hair dryers involved in the recall in the Chinese region exceeded 1.75 million units. This would be Philips' largest recall action in China.

This recall also intensified consumers' concerns about the Philips brand.

"Many things Philips has done in recent years involve outsourcing production to other companies. To maximize profits, quality issues have frequently occurred. Besides strengthening the small home appliance market, acquiring Pentelec could also allow some products to be self-manufactured rather than outsourced to other companies," Liu Budusten said.

A person from the China Home Appliance Marketing Committee frankly stated in a media interview that, based on Pentelec's current business layout and competitiveness, it would be difficult for the company to sustain long-term development solely relying on its own strength. It even faces the risk of collapse. Whether partnering with Pentelec can save Philips' declining small home appliance business remains unclear.

"This acquisition, to a certain extent, is beneficial to Philips. But Pentelec belongs to the third-tier enterprise in the Chinese home appliance market, and Philips is a second-tier enterprise. If Philips wants to use this acquisition to compete against China's top-tier enterprises, it's clearly impossible. I believe that acquiring Pentelec might just be Philips' first step, and there may be further actions down the line," Liu Budusten told reporters.
