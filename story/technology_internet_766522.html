Sina Weibo: How to Make Profit from Twitter's Listing?

by anonymous on 2013-09-18 21:43:15

When Alibaba, caught in the "control and IPO" dilemma, was contemplating the location and timing of its listing amid uncertainties, Twitter across the ocean similarly did not want its path to the market to be seen too clearly.

Last Thursday, the famous social networking site Twitter officially announced that it had secretly submitted an S-1 form containing its IPO plans to the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC). However, as all information is still confidential, it remains unclear when Twitter's specific IPO date will be set. This could be the largest IPO since Facebook's market debut in May last year. But is Twitter really ready for its IPO? In December last year, Twitter announced that its monthly active users had reached 200 million, and CEO Costolo expected a 100% increase in Twitter users in 2013, reaching 400 million by the end of the year. However, data shows that Twitter's current total number of active users is 240 million, and at this growth rate, the number of active users by the end of the year will only reach 260 million, far below expectations. Even so, this figure is only one-fourth of Facebook's.

In May this year, Twitter was valued close to $10 billion, and its current valuation is roughly between $15 billion and $20 billion. On the other hand, China's "Twitter" — Sina Weibo — after Alibaba invested $586 million for an 18% stake in Weibo, was valued at approximately $3.3 billion.

Whether it is Twitter, the originator of microblogging, or Sina Weibo, the domestic imitator, both have been facing the same question since their inception: how to make a profit. Generally speaking, the influence of media should be proportional to its profitability. But why are the evaluations of the profitability of such influential entities as Twitter and Sina Weibo still stuck at whether they can make a profit?

Twitter's Continuous Experiments

The idea for Twitter, considered to have initiated the social era, began in 2006. At that time, the most mainstream social model in China focused on chatroom models represented by QQ chat rooms and community models represented by Chinaren. These two models eventually faded over time, while the rapidly growing user base of Twitter began seeking its own business model.

In 2009, current CEO Dick Costolo joined Twitter from Feedburner. He subsequently stated that Twitter would soon launch advertising revenue generation. The same year, there were reports that Twitter had already become profitable. In 2011, Twitter's valuation reached $8 billion, and the same year Twitter began placing ads at the top of users' tweet streams.

In recent years, with the emergence of Facebook and increasingly diverse social media platforms as well as the impact of mobile internet, Twitter has had to explore more ways to make a profit. Specific measures include: adding ads in mobile applications, reducing reliance on third-party applications, embracing television, expanding tweets, launching short video sharing services, and introducing ad APIs.

Currently, Twitter's main ad products fall into three categories: Promoted Trends, Promoted Accounts, and Promoted Tweets. These three modes respectively involve: advertisers purchasing positions for trending topics, placing ads at the top of tweets discussing trending topics. The highlight of these ads is that they can spark discussions about brands, enhancing brand influence; using paid promotion for Twitter accounts helps establish a more professional image, allowing specific accounts to gain more attention; Promoted Tweets are Twitter's most important product, appearing primarily at the top of search results related to specific topics and within users' timelines.

Among these three modes, Promoted Tweets have the highest profitability. This mode was launched in 2010, similar to Google's keyword-related search. In July 2011, a new mode called Timely Tweets was introduced, meaning Twitter would begin adding ads to timelines. If you follow promoted tweets, each time you log into Twitter, the promoted tweet will appear at the top of your timeline. When the timeline updates, the promoted tweet will sink normally. In September of the same year, the restriction requiring following recommended accounts was removed.

Data from eMarketer shows that Twitter's ad revenue was $45 million in 2010. In 2011, this figure grew to $140 million. The predicted data for 2013 is $400 million. Twitter, which has been relatively conservative in launching ad businesses, has introduced many ad products, making Wall Street more optimistic about Twitter's future.

Recently, Twitter acquired MoPub for $350 million to strengthen its mobile ad business. It is said that besides helping Twitter sell ads better, Twitter intends to retain MoPub's core ad exchange service, meaning that even if no one buys Twitter's ads, it can still earn money by helping others buy and sell ads. Twitter also plans to use MoPub to place its native ads on other publishers' mobile websites and apps, creating a Twitter ad network.

Sina Weibo’s Single-mindedness

Last year, Costolo hinted that the company might introduce other revenue models, such as e-commerce: "Every day on Twitter, we see a lot of business transactions. Short-cycle inventory goods like tickets are very interesting. We've been observing for a while and are considering ways to participate." This coincides with the focus of China's largest social networking site, Sina Weibo; after Alibaba acquired part of Weibo's shares, combining social networking with e-commerce was considered the best way to monetize the next era of China's Internet.

Sina Weibo's large user base and its extremely asymmetrical profitability have always made it a subject of criticism from outsiders. In April last year, Sina Weibo released a "Sina Weibo Q2 2012 Price List," indicating that Weibo had begun full-scale commercial operations. In its first quarter of commercialization, Sina Weibo's ad revenue exceeded $10 million, and the next two quarters both exceeded $20 million. Entering 2013, Sina Weibo launched an information flow ad system, and by the second quarter of this year, Weibo's ad revenue broke through $30 million for the first time. With the launch of Weibo information flow ads and cooperation with Alibaba, Sina's ad customers expanded from mainly brand advertisers to small and medium-sized enterprises and online merchants. However, this approach resembles the traditional method of selling space directly on Sina.com, which does not quite align with Weibo's "social" identity; its membership services and subsequently launched enterprise ad placement tool "Fan Pass" and e-commerce guide tool "Sina Showcase" have not yielded satisfactory returns.

More alarmingly, Sina Weibo's stickiness is fading. According to user activity data published earlier this year by market research firm Global Web Index, Sina Weibo's user activity dropped nearly 40% from Q2 to Q4 in 2012. The daily post volume per user has significantly declined compared to peak times in the past, and Weibo's overall traffic is also declining.

The only good news for Sina Weibo comes from its latest disclosed financial figures for the second fiscal quarter of 2013. The financial report shows that Sina's quarterly revenue was $157 million, the highest quarterly income since Sina's establishment. In the financial report, Sina expects total revenue for the third quarter to be between $176 million and $180 million, with ad revenue between $151 million and $153 million. These impressive figures come from Sina's comprehensive efforts across all its businesses recently: Sina.com was revamped on April 1, connecting Weibo with the portal; "Showcase Promotion" was launched on Weibo on April 17; and the mobile Weibo "PAGE Page" was launched on May 1. All these measures have driven the commercial development of Sina, especially Weibo.

Industry analysts predict that the "AliLang" launched on August 5 will bring profits to the company in the third and fourth quarters of this year. However, "AliLang" still needs to improve user experience; otherwise, given the current decline in Sina user activity, whether it can make a profit will remain uncertain. While some people believe that Alibaba's investment is undoubtedly a "timely rain" for Sina, others question whether Sina Weibo is "drinking poison to quench thirst."

What Sina Weibo needs to pay more attention to is that compared to foreign social application platforms like Twitter and Facebook, which have made various attempts in areas such as promoting popular topics, real-time searches, and revenue-sharing for microblogging applications, Sina Weibo still relies on traditional advertising for its business model.

Postscript:

For Sina Weibo and Twitter, these two companies that once bore great resemblance are now walking on completely different paths. Twitter, relatively conservative in advertising, is now facing the threshold of going public. If it successfully lists, the requirement for performance may prompt Twitter to become more aggressive in its business operations while exploring other profit directions, such as mobile internet; whereas Sina Weibo, known as China's Twitter, has partially become a subordinate of Alibaba. For a long time in the future, Alibaba's massive presence will loom over Sina Weibo, which may mean that apart from becoming an entry point for Alibaba to drive traffic, Sina Weibo has no other options.
