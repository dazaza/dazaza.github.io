Google's Android Team: People Have Changed, Where is Android Father Rubin Now?

by anonymous on 2013-09-08 19:54:17

Android's fate has become somewhat tied to the two characters "an" (安) and "zhuo" (卓). In the global mobile ecosystem competition, Android has performed "extraordinarily" well, continuing its rapid advancement and leaving iOS far behind. However, within Google, a series of personnel upheavals have led to significant staff turnover in the Android team, causing instability and an uncertain future, truly making its fate unsettling.

At the time when Hugo Barra, Google's spokesperson for Android, unexpectedly left Google to join Xiaomi as head of global markets, reports began mentioning the whereabouts of Andy Rubin, known as the "Father of Android" (with the name "Android" rumored to originate from "Andy"). Rubin was the first senior executive in Google's Android team to be reassigned. People also realized that it remains unclear whether Rubin is still within Google and what exactly he is doing.

After Rubin was suddenly sidelined and the Android business was transferred under Chrome, the outside world was puzzled by Google's actions and made various speculations about the integration trends of the two operating systems. To this day, the integration of Android remains shrouded in mystery, and the successive personnel changes indicate internal struggles within Google.

American tech media pointed out that starting with Rubin, followed by Android "spokesperson" Barra, up until now, the departure of high-level executives who have had significant influence on the Android system means that for Sundar Pichai, if any "surgical" changes are to be made to Android, the opposition faced would be minimized.

Tracing back through time, another important figure in Android left at the beginning of August. Jean-Baptiste Queru, who was responsible for maintaining the Android Open Source Project within Google, announced his resignation on Google+. His departure, ostensibly related to minor issues involving Qualcomm driver support, but many pointed out that Queru had long been dissatisfied with Google's overly closed policies regarding Android. His "open-source idealism" seemed to conflict with Google's commercial operations.

In March of this year, CEO Larry Page, after implementing large-scale "reforms and restructurings" in the company, took action against the outstandingly performing Android, announcing that Rubin would no longer be in charge of Android operations. Android would be integrated into the Chrome OS department, overseen by the increasingly influential Sundar Pichai within Google.

According to multiple media disclosures, although Android and Chrome OS belong to the same company, the two were almost completely disconnected. The default browser for the Android system was not Chrome, which Pichai leads, clearly indicating the lack of cooperation between the two systems.

Moreover, like Scott Forstall who was "adjusted out" by Apple's Tim Cook, and Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer who has announced retirement within 12 months, Rubin had a fiery temper and found it difficult to get along with or collaborate with others. Rubin liked to nitpick over his subordinates' work and verbally abuse employees.

When Page sidelined Rubin, he said, "Rubin himself realized that it was time to pass the reins of Android to someone else and write a new chapter within Google."

Since then, Rubin has disappeared from sight within Google. Clearly, for the "Father of Android" and "contributor" who once pioneered the global Android ecosystem and helped Google gain a foothold in the mobile internet era, Google's cold treatment of Rubin has drawn criticism from the outside world.

In early August, there were media reports citing unnamed sources claiming that Rubin might be working on robots in the Google X lab managed by Sergey Brin. Robots are one of the more futuristic projects in this lab, alongside self-driving cars, internet balloons, and smart glasses.

Regarding the news of Rubin working on robots, Google's spokesperson Leslie Miller stated: "We do not comment on rumors or speculation."

The outside world obviously does not agree with Page's claim of "Rubin voluntarily passing the baton." American media have analyzed various reasons for Rubin's sudden removal. One explanation is that Rubin was concerned that Samsung Electronics' large share in the Android camp could threaten the integrity of Android — Samsung might rewrite the Android source code itself or even abandon the "Android" name altogether. However, in Google's view, the relative strength of hardware manufacturers is irrelevant to them; as long as there are more Android users, Android can serve as an "entry point" for Google's internet application services. The WEB application system based on Chrome is also within Pichai's scope of responsibilities.

Another analysis suggests that Rubin strongly recommended that Google acquire Motorola Mobility to compete against Samsung and Apple. Google subsequently paid $12.5 billion to acquire Motorola, mainly for its patent assets. However, Google later discovered in a lawsuit between Motorola and Microsoft that the value of Motorola's patents had been grossly exaggerated, making the $12.5 billion acquisition a "bad deal." Rubin became the "scapegoat" for this unprofitable acquisition and was removed from his position in Android.

Faced with Rubin being sidelined, American tech media couldn't look away. TechCrunch recently listed Rubin as a candidate to replace Ballmer as Microsoft CEO, emphasizing that "the tech world doesn't know what Rubin is currently doing."

In reality, after Pichai took over Android, he remained cautious about the business. According to IDC data in the second quarter, Android held a 79.3% share of the global smartphone market, far exceeding iOS's 13.2%. However, Pichai did not implement major measures to address Android's three weaknesses — apart from Play content sales, Google lacks a profitable model for Android; the fragmentation issue remains unresolved; and in many markets worldwide, Android undergoes "cosmetic customizations," with all Google products and services completely removed.

Meanwhile, Pichai's Chrome OS, aside from sporadic updates and upgrades, has a market penetration rate that doesn't even come close to Android's.

Faced with unintended success and deliberate failure, industry insiders believe that Pichai, who shoulders the significant mission of Google's cloud services, cannot allow this awkward situation to continue indefinitely. Perhaps the consecutive departures of Android's top management herald an impending storm of change for Android.
