Comparison of the Live-action Versions of "The Prince of Tennis" between China and Japan

by opde55717 on 2011-05-22 16:59:01

Comparison of Chinese and Japanese live-action versions of "Prince of Tennis"

"Prince of Tennis", authored by Takeshi Konomi, is a story centered around Seigaku Middle School's tennis club members striving for the sport of tennis with the goal of winning the national championship. This serialized comic, which has now concluded, can be considered another successful sports-themed manga following "Slam Dunk". The "Prince of Tennis" consists of 379 episodes. After the manga was adapted into an animated series, it sparked a tennis craze in Japan. The "Seigaku Tennis Festival - 03" held on March 28, 2003, at the renowned Ariake Arena in Tokyo attracted 16,000 anime fans, marking a rare ACG event in Japan.

Japanese Live-Action Movie Version of "Prince of Tennis"

This version of "Prince of Tennis" was produced after the great success of its stage play adaptation, with some actors reprising their roles from the stage play. The main character, Somma Kanata, became one of the most popular child stars at that time due to this movie.

We often refer to it as the "Good Men" version of "Prince of Tennis" because many actors in the drama are from the show "Good Men, Go!" This version is the most controversial because it was released later and underwent the most changes. Since the original work is a Japanese manga, it naturally feels more natural when remade by the Japanese than by the Chinese due to differences in environment and culture. However, adaptations must be made when brought to China, so controversy is expected.

Ryoma Echizen is the protagonist of the series. In the original manga, he is a prodigious tennis player who is only 12 years old and stands at 151 cm (although much shorter than real-life tennis players, but remember, it’s a comic!). He is arrogant and competitive. In the Japanese version, this role was played by child actor Somma Kanata, who was 16 at the time of filming but looks very similar to the character due to his youthful face and small stature, especially during intense matches.

In the Chinese version, this role is played by newcomer Qin Junjie, whose name was changed from Ryoma Echizen to simply Longma. Although Qin Junjie is only 17, he appears older and stands at 177 cm, making it difficult for audiences to associate him with the young Ryoma. Additionally, Qin Junjie's kind appearance does not align well with Ryoma's competitive and slightly arrogant personality.

Tezuka Kunimitsu is the captain of the Seigaku tennis team in the original manga. He is 15 years old, stands at 179 cm, and is a left-handed player known for his serious and handsome demeanor, earning him the admiration of many female fans. In the Japanese version, this role is played by mixed-race actor Shiroda Yu, who also portrayed Tezuka in the stage play. Given the highly stylized nature of Tezuka in the manga, it is challenging for any actor to perfectly match the character, much like how any portrayal of Hanamichi Sakuragi is bound to be criticized. However, Shiroda Yu's interpretation is relatively close to Tezuka Kunimitsu.

In the Chinese version, Tezuka Kunimitsu is played by Bai Xuxu, a participant from the 2007 season of "Good Men, Go!", whose name was changed to Zhong Guoguang. As previously mentioned, portraying Tezuka Kunimitsu is inherently difficult, but Bai Xuxu's performance falls short. Besides wearing glasses, there is little about him that resembles Tezuka Kunimitsu, especially considering his nearly 30-year-old age and overly studious appearance, lacking the edgy charm of Tezuka.

Fuji Syusuke is a third-year student in the middle school division of Seigaku in the original manga, standing at 167 cm. Known for his cunning smile and unpredictable playing style, Fuji is a masterful tennis player who always maintains an elegant smile and rarely shows emotion. In the Japanese version, this role is played by Aiba Hiroki, who also starred in the stage play. Aiba Hiroki bears a striking resemblance to Fuji Syusuke.

In the Chinese version, this role is played by Chen Zeyu, one of the top ten finalists from the 2006 season of "Good Men, Go!", whose name was changed from Fuji Syusuke to Zhou Zhu. Chen Zeyu was originally a badminton player whose career ended due to injury and is now part of the new mainland group "Brotherhood." Just by comparing pictures, it's clear who performs better. The editor still feels that Chen Zeyu is too old for the role and his smile appears somewhat naive compared to Fuji Syusuke's sophisticated grin.

Oishi Shuichiro is 14 years old and stands at 175 cm in the original manga. He is calm, introverted, persistent, and practical, with a strong sense of responsibility and justice, easily earning the favor of seniors and coaches. In both the Japanese film and stage play versions, this role is played by Suzuki Yuki. To make the live-action version closer to the original, Suzuki Yuki even adopted a hairstyle almost identical to Oishi's. This demonstrates the meticulous attention to detail in the Japanese remake.

In the Chinese version, this role is played by Zhang Chao, another participant from "Good Men, Go!", whose name was changed from Oishi Shuichiro to Shi Yi. Not only does his appearance fail to resemble Oishi Shuichiro, but his near-30 age makes it even harder for audiences to accept him in the role.

Choutarou Ohtori is approximately 13-14 years old and stands at 173 cm in the original manga. He has a mysterious, self-centered, and easily angered personality, with an unfamiliar exterior but a pure and adorable heart. In the Japanese version, this role is played by Kujira Yasuaki, who may not be as handsome as Ohtori in the manga but compensates with a menacing appearance, especially when wearing a headband.

In the Chinese version, this role is played by Zhang Dianfei, the fourth-place winner of the 2007 season of "Good Men, Go!", whose name was changed from Choutarou Ohtori to Chen Haitang. So far, Zhang Dianfei's performance is the most praised among all characters in the Chinese version. However, the editor still feels that the same issue persists: his age is too old. Born in 1982, Zhang Dianfei doesn't resemble Ohtori in appearance, especially in terms of his non-threatening look.

Ryo Kawamura is 14 years old and stands at 180 cm in the original manga. He values brotherhood, human connections, and friendship, and is of the aggressive attacking type. In the Japanese version, this role is played by the same person as in the stage play, Ogata Kaichi, while in the Chinese version, it is played by Xue Haowen, whose name was changed from Ryo Kawamura to He Chunlong. Both performances feel rather weak, but Ogata Kaichi's appearance is closer to the original and his youthfulness gives him an advantage.

Eiji Kikumaru is approximately 14-15 years old and stands at 172 cm in the original manga. He is quick-witted and notices many subtle details that others overlook. His world retains a childlike innocence, making him a very playful boy. In the Japanese version, this role is played by Adachi Ryo. Due to Kikumaru's highly stylized and dreamy character in the manga, in the Japanese version, Adachi Ryo sports the same hairstyle as Kikumaru and even has a white sticker on his right cheek, enhanced by excellent makeup techniques, making him closely resemble the character.

In the Chinese version, this role is played by Mao Fangyuan, a member of the new mainland group "Brotherhood," whose name was changed from Eiji Kikumaru to Ju Wan. Mao Fangyuan's realistic face differs greatly from Kikumaru's dreamy appearance in the manga. Moreover, despite being only 21, his face doesn't appear to be 21, and his attempt to act cute is hard to endure.

Kintarou Tooyama is 14 years old and stands at 184 cm in the original manga. He is amiable and friendly towards juniors. In the Japanese version, this role is played by Araki Hiroyuki. In Japanese adaptations, likeness isn't as crucial as achieving a convincing portrayal through costume and makeup.

In the Chinese version, this role is played by Wang Chuanjun, a participant from "Good Men, Go!", whose name was changed from Kintarou Tooyama to Qian Zhenzhi. Despite being born in 1985, Wang Chuanjun looks much older and doesn't resemble a tennis player, appearing more like a bookworm.

Taichi Kawamura is 13-14 years old and stands at 170 cm in the original manga. He possesses resilience and boldness in the face of adversity, along with a free-spirited playing style. In the Japanese version, this role is played by Kaji Shoji, who achieves about 70% similarity to the character. In the Chinese version, this role is played by Yang Demin, whose name was changed from Taichi Kawamura to Tao Chengwu. One look at this costume design, and no further explanation is needed—it speaks for itself!
