The Most "Unfortunate" IT Companies: Tudou.com and Mecox Lane Make the List

by jdhgsa078 on 2011-08-16 15:47:39

The IT industry is always a case of some rejoicing and others grieving. The Digital Business Weekly will join numerous digital media outlets and professionals to initiate a vote, selecting the most unfortunate IT companies. Industry information at www.gtobal.com/info/

3G Portal

Winning at the starting line but losing along the way—perhaps this is the most fitting commentary on 3G Portal. As the company's performance continues without improvement, pessimistic views within the industry spread, and perhaps the two founders of 3G Portal, Deng Yuqiang and Zhang Xiangdong, feel somewhat bitter inside: after seven years of hard work, latecomers have either gone public or are highly profitable, yet they remain in a state of marginal profits, gritting their teeth to persevere.

Four years ago, in 2007, 3G Portal was a leading player in China’s mobile internet. However, by February 2011, 3G Portal only held 2.3% of the market share, significantly overtaken by later entrants such as UCWeb, a mobile browser manufacturer, and traditional internet giants.

Xunlei

The level of attention generated by Xunlei's IPO attempt is an unprecedented experience for the company since its establishment. In hindsight, Xunlei seems more like an unlucky child, with repeated postponements of its IPO and then the revelation that its ICP registration had been canceled. A search for "Xunlei" on Baidu reveals an overwhelming flood of negative news. This might resonate with the popular phrase today: why has Xunlei repeatedly postponed its IPO?

In the video client domain, Xunlei can undoubtedly be called the leader. But why did Xunlei take so long to go public? If Xunlei had executives familiar with the listing process and knowledgeable about capital market trends, the first domestic listed video company would not have been Youku, but Xunlei.

Mecox Lane

Going public is a good thing, but it doesn't necessarily mean success. Unlucky Mecox Lane encountered falling stock prices and class-action lawsuits. The performance of China's first B2C stock, Mecox Lane, is rather bleak. Reports show that Mecox Lane's net revenue in the first quarter was $48.1 million, down 2.4% from $49.3 million in the same period last year; its net loss was $3.9 million, up 153% from a net loss of $1.5 million in the same period last year.

Ku6

Ku6 is a tragedy. Ku6 faced fierce resistance from employees, eventually being judged as having conducted illegal layoffs. Two months later, there were further changes in Ku6's senior management.

Cisco

At the peak of the internet bubble, Cisco surpassed Microsoft to become the world's largest publicly traded company. Not long ago, Cisco spent billions of dollars acquiring WebEx, Tandberg, and Flip, among others, to enter new markets. However, on July 18th, Cisco announced plans to lay off 6,500 employees and sell a factory located in Mexico to Foxconn, effectively shedding another 5,000 employees. Prior to this, Cisco announced it would cease development of the Flip camera product and the Eos social platform.

Tudou

Facing situations where former wives demand money and competitors disrupt operations, Tudou's path to going public has been quite bumpy. In the grand drama of Tudou's IPO, Wang Wei turned out to be an inept director. On the eve of Tudou's IPO, his ex-wife suddenly came forward demanding a share of the money, something Wang Wei did not foresee. He founded one of the earliest video websites nationwide, built the most complete user growth system, but consistently lagged behind his competitors in key areas such as video licenses, attracting investment, and IPOs.

TOM Online

TOM Online, which has been declining steadily, once again faces massive layoffs. TOM Online lacks clear self-positioning, ineffective marketing promotions, struggles to capture user attention, and remains overly reliant on value-added services. As a former SP giant, its current predicament is inevitable. Compared to the year 2000, TOM should be more rational and composed in facing risks akin to bubbles.

SMIC

An unexpected battle for corporate control has plunged China's largest chip foundry into continued turmoil.

This battle represents the eruption of many accumulated contradictions at SMIC: conflicts between shareholders and management due to SMIC's equity and governance structure, performance declines brought on by market downturns, and interest conflicts arising from differences in treatment and values between employees from different regions. The sudden death of SMIC's former chairman, Jiang Shangzhou, became the direct trigger.
