TOEFL has already become numb and repulsive to blind dates but still runs around in the blind date field.

by hartyqekb on 2009-12-02 17:26:42

I'm still imagining myself as 18 years old, but I'll be 30 soon. Is that true? I don't want to think about it, but I have to face it. The arrogance of youth has long since disappeared, and the prime of our lives has quietly slipped away. Those of us born in the 80s are now standing on the cusp of 30, facing the responsibilities of adulthood. How many of us can still proudly say, "We are still young"? Yes, youth is capital, but with the rise of the 90s generation, are we still young? We once had grand ideals, wandered through unfamiliar cities in search of our dreams, believed that hard work would lead to success, and thought even a small room could nurture beautiful love. But when our careers stagnate and love becomes elusive, do those of us born in the 80s still believe that youth means no fear of failure?

One day, I realized that we who were born in the 80s have all grown old. We are no longer young, no longer carefree, no longer open, no longer smiling. What we have now is a fear of marriage, disappointment in the future, and hesitation in the face of difficulties. At work, we've lost our passion and only hope for stable jobs. Risky jobs are rejected. Once reluctant to use connections to find jobs, we now submit to mediocrity when our income can't even cover basic needs. We put aside our pride and seek to maximize interpersonal relationships. The hardships of work don't scare us; what matters is keeping the job. In matters of the heart, we no longer see marriage as the culmination of love, but as kinship. We no longer yearn for romantic, exciting love, but instead desire a happy, fulfilling marriage. Once believing that arranged marriages were for the 70s generation or earlier, we now find that they have become the mainstream way for the 80s generation to marry. One friend joked, "I'm either on a blind date, or on my way to one."

In terms of clothing, we who once obsessed over brands now feel estranged from them. Expensive clothes in our wardrobes make us, as salaried workers, hesitant to try them on again. After earning money, we realize how hard life is. We no longer care about brands, focusing instead on quality and purpose. We no longer impulsively buy discounted clothes, opting instead for ones suitable for specific occasions. Clothes aren't about quantity, but about looking presentable. Brands are just illusions.

In society, more and more friends with families no longer have the courage to talk about life because their conversations revolve around family. Single people of the 80s generation focus more on personal emotions. Bars and KTVs that we once loved are no longer appealing. We prefer the quiet of coffee shops or teahouses, reading books, listening to music, enjoying the bitterness of coffee or the fragrance of tea.

In family matters, family surpasses both love and friendship. Everything is prioritized for the family. We've lost our impulsiveness, understanding our parents' efforts and sympathizing with their helplessness. Family relationships that once seemed simple suddenly become clear. We realize that relationships between people are never simple. The most trustworthy people are our parents and ourselves. We increasingly dislike visiting relatives and friends because they always ask about marriage.

In entertainment, we find ourselves using our phones less frequently, sending fewer text messages. Sometimes we'd rather make a quick phone call than send a message. QQ doesn't flash incessantly like before. We've left many groups, some kept on mute out of politeness. Occasionally, we chat in a few groups then go invisible. We no longer lose sleep over games, preferring instead to quietly read a book.

In shopping, we always consider whether there are free gifts when buying things, not just what looks good. We no longer buy things we don't like just for the freebie. Shopping is no longer aimless wandering but purposeful behavior. After buying what we need, we leave quickly. Beautiful, delicate trinkets are admired in the store, then returned to the shelf without being taken home and stored in boxes.

Facing everything, we now think deeply and carefully. Our hearts no longer hold fairy tales. We've wasted too much of our youth, a time full of laughter and tears, vitality and decadence, sweetness and absurdity, confidence and loss.

We're sensitive, obstinate, and stubbornly pretend to be strong. We easily hurt others and are easily hurt by others. We pursue decadent happiness and indulge in the beauty of loneliness. We believe we're unique and that the world will change because of us.

We wake up to find we're no longer young, our futures perhaps no longer limitless. Was it ever truly limitless? We, the 80s generation, admit we're old, lacking the youth and passion of the 90s generation. All we have is the hustle of daily life. Our weary hearts occasionally smile, telling ourselves that at 30, our best years are just beginning. Playing a vigorous game of sports leaves us breathless, revealing bloated beer bellies after long periods without exercise.

I can't help but sigh: we of the 80s generation have all grown old, but we refuse to accept it. Today's difference from yesterday lies precisely in the lingering feelings of yesterday.
