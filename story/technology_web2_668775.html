iPad trademark Lin书Hao another war of domain name registration

by yyy852456 on 2012-02-18 14:51:08

The dispute over the iPad trademark between Apple and Shenzhen Proview is escalating. Although the focus is on the trademark registration dispute, the issues reflected go beyond just the competition for trademark registration. After investigation, the Yangcheng Evening News learned that with the rapid development of e-commerce, the rush to register website domain names has also become a trend, and has even become a new way to make money. If this chaos is not curbed, it might turn into another kind of "trademark battle."

The cost of domain name registration is not high. When the reporter conducted a trademark query on China's Trademark Network, entering "iPad" in the trademark name resulted in 47 entries. Among them, Proview Technology (Shenzhen) Co., Ltd. had 2 entries, while Apple Inc. had 16 entries covering 9 categories including computer products, sports goods, leather, etc. In addition, other companies have also registered the iPad trademark. Records show that Proview applied for the iPad trademark on January 10, 2000, with exclusive rights valid until June 20, 2021.

Yang Rongshan, chairman of Proview Technology, told the Yangcheng Evening News on the afternoon of January 16 that Proview began developing personal computers similar to tablet computers since 1998, and later launched a computer named iPad along with virtual hosting. The product was once sold in Europe, South America, and other countries and regions, but production was later halted. However, the trademark has been retained. Data from the trademark network shows that Apple also registered and filed the iPad trademark, first using it in July 2009, but it wasn't transferred until July 2011. Some legal experts say that due to the continuous emergence of electronic products in recent years, it has triggered a wave of trademark registrations in China.

In fact, besides trademarks, the rush to register internet domain names is also in full swing. According to the latest statistics from the international statistical agency "webhosting.info", as of January this year, the total number of ".com" domain name registrations exceeded 100 million, with China being the second largest in the world. When checking domain name registrations on China's Wanwang, the reporter found that English domain name fees range from 88 yuan to 500 yuan per year, while Chinese domain name fees range from 280 yuan/year to 480 yuan/year.

Among 17 English domain names, the familiar ".com" and ".cn" are the two most popular domain names in China, with fees of 139 yuan/year and 99 yuan/year respectively. The highest fee is for the ".tv" media domain name, which will be the mainstream domain name in the broadband era, costing 500 yuan/year.

"The cost of domain name registration is not high," said a related person in charge of Wanwang. The renewal fee for the second year is the same as the first year, or you can buy a package for three or five years, which would be cheaper. This person also revealed that once a domain name is registered, it is protected within the time limit, even if no operation is performed on the domain name.

Even "Lin Shuhao" has been registered. In 2009, PPLive acquired the domain name "pptv.com" for a high price of 1.8 million RMB, while the original owner only paid 3800 USD to obtain it, making nearly 70 times the profit. It is understood that angel investor Cai Wensheng has handled countless domain name sales over the years, from "g.cn", "520.com" to his self-founded "265.com". Although prices were never disclosed, it is rumored that "265.com" alone cashed out at least 20 million USD. Last year, JD.com acquired the domain name "jingdong.com", identical in pinyin to its name, for 3 million RMB. It is also rumored that last year Apple acquired the domain name "icloud.com" for 4.5 million USD.

As Apple gets embroiled in a trademark dispute, Facebook, which has yet to enter the Chinese market and does not have an official Chinese name, has already registered dozens of various types of trademarks in mainland China. According to records from China's Trademark Network, Facebook has registered a total of 61 trademarks, with registration times spanning from March 2006 to April 2011. The trademark names include THEFACEBOOK, FACEBOOK, F logo, and possible phonetic translations of Chinese names such as Feisibo, Fisiboke, Feisibuke, etc. From the international classification numbers, they cover community networks, electronic magazines, software, search engines, games, photo sharing, and even men's and women's clothing categories.

Even NBA rising star Jeremy Lin is sought after in the domain registration field. It is known that domain registrations related to Jeremy Lin include his name domains linshuhao.com/.cn/.com.cn/.net/.org, shuhaolin.com/.net, etc., all of which have been registered. Interestingly, there are also homophonic domain names like shuhowlin.com/.cn, shu-how.com, which have also been personally acquired, and the Chinese domain name linshuhao.com has long been registered. As expected, the trademark spelling JeremySHL has also been registered.

Domain grabbing isn't necessarily profitable. Recently, Taobao Mall suddenly changed its name to "Tianmao", surprising the industry. But Taobao had already preemptively acquired the phonetic domain names "tianmao.com/.com.cn/.cn". The Tianmao domain name "tianmao.com" was transferred by Zhuang Liangji from Xiamen, selling for 120,000 RMB last November. Another double-phonetic domain name he transferred, "huoxing.com" (Mars), fetched a transfer fee as high as 5 million RMB, netting him more than 4 million RMB in profit. According to relevant personnel, the value of a domain name is basically determined by how it is used and spread, and what commercial value it possesses, depending on what webpage it is attached to.

An insider revealed that domain investment has speculative components and contains significant bubbles. After multiple transfers, the price may rise, but the final buyer may not be able to sell it at a good price, and could even lose everything. Qi Lin, assistant director of the China Internet Network Information Center (CNNIC), once expressed to the media that malicious trademark grabs are firmly opposed and prohibited. Even if the malicious grabber bypasses strict relevant reviews, when the legitimate civil rights of enterprises are infringed upon, arbitration and other legal means can be used to seek fair resolution.

Legal experts point out that investing in trademarks and domain names requires investors to have foresight and strategic vision. Registering innovative vocabulary domain names may bring legitimate returns. Malicious grabs, however, hope to gain improper benefits by registering domain names identical or similar to others' trademarks and business names, then selling them to the owners of related civil rights or their competitors, or even imitating the brand products of civil rights owners using the domain name. Such actions should be stopped.
