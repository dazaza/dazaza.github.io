Google Accelerates the Deployment of Encryption Projects to Enhance Information Transmission Security

by anonymous on 2013-09-08 20:21:34

After the practices of the U.S. National Security Agency (NSA) in monitoring and eavesdropping on encrypted internet content through various means were exposed, Google has accelerated its deployment of encryption for data transmitted between its data centers, hoping to enhance the security of information transmission.

According to reports by the U.S. "Washington Post," Google's data encryption project was launched last year, but in the wake of leaks by former NSA employee Edward Snowden, this encryption project did little to restore Google's reputation. Google is also implementing a project to encrypt data on its servers.

Eric Grosse, Google's vice president responsible for security engineering, pointed out in an interview with the "Washington Post": "This is akin to an arms race, and the U.S. government agencies are the most technically proficient players in this game."

The role of encrypting data on servers and during transmission is similar to whether residents lock their front doors at night. Locking the door may not stop burglars from breaking in, but it can prevent many petty thefts and set more obstacles for larger thieves.

Independent U.S. information security researcher Ashkan Soltani, when discussing the U.S. government's access to encrypted data, said: "The government has a universal key for decryption. However, the U.S. government still cannot access all encrypted data."

Google's implementation of the data encryption plan will not affect the company's legal obligations. Google will continue to comply with the National Security Letters of the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) and other legal frameworks, and once it receives a request from the U.S. government for data, Google will still submit relevant data promptly. The purpose of Google accelerating the implementation of the data encryption project is to complete the deployment several months earlier.

Currently, Google is already using Transport Layer Security (TLS) technology in transit to encrypt emails sent via Gmail accounts. Google will also encrypt other forms of data sent through Google data centers, such as Google Drive content.

The media has raised many questions about Google's data encryption project, such as how much funding Google needs to invest to achieve this level of encryption, how many of Google's data centers are covered by the encryption project, and what encryption technology Google is currently using. However, Google's spokesperson refused to disclose specific details of the company's encryption project.

Google told the "Washington Post" that the company will use an "end-to-end" encryption process in this project, meaning that both the servers storing the data and the data in transit will receive strong encryption protection.

Google and Microsoft may file a joint lawsuit against the U.S. federal government next week, seeking authorization to disclose details of the U.S. government's requests for user data. This series of actions indicates that some tech companies may no longer remain silent about government requests for user data in the future.

Similarly on Friday, Yahoo released its first transparency report detailing data requests from governments around the world. The report showed that in the first half of this year, Yahoo received 12,444 requests for user data from the U.S. government. Among the countries globally that cooperate with Yahoo to share data collection information, the U.S. ranked first in terms of the volume of data requested.
