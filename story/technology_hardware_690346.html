American 12-Year-Old Boy Has an IQ Higher Than Einstein's, Seeks to Challenge the Absolute Theory

by aiipii12987 on 2012-03-05 09:47:39

Jacob Barnett

According to domestic media reports, there is a 12-year-old boy in the United States whose IQ is higher than Einstein's, reaching 170. Currently, he is studying at Indiana University, and his mentors are planning for him to pursue a doctoral degree.

This "prodigy" is named Jacob Barnett. In one week, he taught himself calculus, algebra, and geometry, and often tutors his university classmates. Now, he has begun to challenge Einstein's theory of relativity - naming it the "Einstein Relativity Expansion Version."

His mother, Kristin, was unsure whether her son was a genius or just talking nonsense, so she sent a video of Jacob's theories to the Institute for Advanced Study near Princeton. Astrophysicist Scott Tremaine confirmed its validity. In an email to Jacob's family, he wrote: "In his research, there are several of the deepest problems in astrophysics and theoretical physics. Anyone who solves these problems would win the Nobel Prize."

Kristin told the media that Jacob's knowledge definitely didn't come from her because "my math was failing."

Not only Kristin, but Jacob's entire family struggles with math. He once complained: "Every time I discuss math with my family, they just look blankly at me."

Reports indicate that Jacob suffers from Asperger syndrome and mild autism and did not start speaking until he was two years old. His parents were very worried about this. It wasn't until he grew older that they discovered his talent.

At the age of three, Jacob started playing with 5000-piece puzzles and even studied state highway maps, memorizing every single highway.

At eight, he graduated from middle school and joined the advanced astrophysics class at Indiana University-Purdue University Indianapolis (IUPUI). His classmates are all adults over 18 years old, but whenever they encounter problems, they seek out Jacob.

His teacher, Ross, said that Jacob often comes to him during breaks, asking many more detailed questions.

However, geniuses always pay some "price." Jacob's parents said that he finds it hard to sleep well at night, as numbers keep appearing in his mind. Now, he uses this time to study the theory of relativity. (Editor: SN016)
