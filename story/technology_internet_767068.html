US Blog: Ford's Mulally is good candidate for next Microsoft CEO

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 17:34:20

Introduction: The U.S. tech blog Business Insider today published an article titled "Why Ford CEO Alan Mulally Is A Good Choice To Run Microsoft (Despite His Lack Of High-Tech Experience)." It discusses how Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer recently announced his upcoming retirement, with Ford CEO Alan Mulally being considered a top contender for the next CEO position at Microsoft. Business Insider analyzes Mulally's advantages in becoming the CEO of Microsoft.

The following is the full text of the article:

Ford CEO Alan Mulally has become the leading contender for the next CEO position at Microsoft. For some, it may be surprising that Mulally is being considered as the next CEO of Microsoft since he has no connection to the tech industry. Before taking the helm at Ford, he was the CEO of Boeing’s commercial airplane business. He joined Boeing right after graduating from college and worked there for 37 years.

Even though Mulally lacks experience in the tech industry, he remains a suitable candidate for the role of CEO at Microsoft. The Wall Street Journal referred to Mulally as "one of the most respected CEOs across all industries." Barclays analyst Brian Johnson stated: "There is no doubt that Mulally fundamentally drove the transformation of Ford, simplifying its brand and developing a system conducive to scaling while also creating interesting products."

During the previous U.S. economic recession, under Mulally's leadership, Ford was the only major automaker that did not require government assistance. Mulally refinanced through private markets, enabling the company to survive on its own strength.

In 2009, Bloomberg Businessweek published an article detailing Mulally's two-and-a-half-year tenure at Ford. The article noted: "Under Mulally's leadership, decision-making became more transparent, previously very loose departments began to collaborate, and cars with better designs moved faster from design studios to showrooms. Ford's vast organization started working together, and the once inefficient, fragmented car development systems gradually aligned."

What Microsoft Needs

This sounds exactly like what Microsoft needs, and investors may agree with this assessment. On the day rumors emerged that Mulally might become the next CEO of Microsoft, Microsoft's stock price rose 1.5% against market trends. Political struggles have been evident within Microsoft for many years. The various groups within Microsoft work relatively independently, rarely collaborating. Baumer restructured the company to break this pattern and unite the entire company. When Ballmer devised the restructuring plan, he sought Mulally's advice.

Compared to organizational restructuring, Microsoft currently needs excellent products more. In 2007, after launching the iPhone, Apple underwent a complete transformation. Ballmer ridiculed the iPhone at the time, which now appears to have been a huge mistake. The iPhone sparked a revolution, and Google subsequently became the leader in the smartphone and tablet computing markets. The development of such products also impacted the PC market.

In 2009, Microsoft held approximately 70% of the market share for all connected computing devices, but this has now dropped to just 24%. There are increasingly more connected devices based on Android and iOS, and Microsoft is gradually being marginalized. Due to the rise of the iPad, the operating income of the Windows business has decreased by 55%.

At the age of 66, with no prior experience in the tech industry, can Mulally make a difference? We will have to wait and see.

A Fresh Perspective

At Ford, Mulally led the launch of several eye-catching vehicles. The Focus is an outstanding model, but it cannot be considered the iPhone of cars. Under Mulally's leadership, Ford's market share in North America has stabilized. However, according to Bloomberg data, Ford's current market share is similar to when Mulally first became CEO.

Comparing the automotive and tech industries from a product perspective is difficult and imperfect. However, what is crucial for Microsoft is that the disruption caused by the iPhone is already evident, and looking back won't bring any changes.

Microsoft currently has a comprehensive recovery plan. It has Windows Phone, Windows 8, and the Surface tablet. These products are still on the market's fringes, but they are heading in the right direction. Mulally will bring a fresh perspective, helping Microsoft focus on its strategy and regain its status as a leader in the tech industry.

Other Candidates Are Not Perfect Either

What is the next big thing in the tech industry? After all, technological progress isn't defined by past achievements but by the future. Microsoft has a large number of talented individuals who have developed excellent technology. Visitors to Microsoft's labs can see interesting products. Microsoft doesn't need exceptional foresight to think about what the next important product will be; there are already many such talents within the company.

What Microsoft needs now is a leader who can streamline business operations, encourage collaboration among everyone, and decide which projects should be supported and which should be abandoned. If Microsoft really needs better insight into technological developments, then just letting founder Bill Gates think about it in his spare time would suffice.

Mulally may not be the perfect candidate for the Microsoft CEO position, but neither are the other candidates. Reports suggest that former Nokia CEO Stephen Elop, who rejoined Microsoft, is suited to become Microsoft's CEO. However, Elop's work has been unstable over the years, and his performance as Nokia's CEO was not good. Before Microsoft announced the acquisition of Nokia's mobile phone business, Nokia was on the verge of bankruptcy.

Skype's head, Tony Bates, is also considered a potential candidate. However, he lacks experience running large public companies. Before Microsoft acquired Skype, Bates had served as Skype's CEO for less than a year. Can he lead a company as large as Microsoft?

Microsoft's "Jonathan Ive"

Mulally may lack technical expertise, but he was originally an engineer himself. He successfully transitioned from the aircraft industry to the automotive industry and could just as easily move into the tech industry. Moreover, Microsoft is a unique tech company. Although many people compare Microsoft to Apple, these two companies are actually quite different.

Apple focuses all its efforts on two products: the iPad and the iPhone. The company has other products like the iPod and Mac computers and continues to develop new ones. But at its core, Apple is very simple. Want to know how well Apple is performing? Just look at the sales of iPads and iPhones.

Compared to Apple, Microsoft is more like General Electric. It's a multi-sector company with products ranging from tablets, keyboards, mice, and gaming consoles to cloud computing services, enterprise services, and a large consumer segment. Microsoft touches almost every area of the tech industry. When one department is struggling, others can compensate.

Mulally has experience managing large companies like Boeing and Ford. He has the ability to unite Microsoft's talent and steer them in the right direction. He doesn't need to be the next Steve Jobs; he can be the next Tim Cook. However, he does need to find Microsoft's Jonathan Ive.
