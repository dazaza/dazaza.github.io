Chen Yizhou Talks About Three Elements of Successful Entrepreneurship: Persistence, Humility, and Awareness of Failure

by swsw007 on 2010-10-03 14:12:39

DoNews Special Report on September 15th: At the invitation of Mr. Tian Suning, Chairman and CEO of Oak Pacific Interconnect (Qianxiang), Chen Yizhou, delivered a speech at the Broadband Fund Annual Meeting. Below is a summary of his remarks:

Starting an internet business in China is a very challenging endeavor, and within the industry, there's often talk of the "three big mountains." Therefore, starting a business in China is harder than in the U.S., other developed countries, or even developing nations. However, because of these many challenges, it becomes even more necessary to discuss how we can increase the probability of entrepreneurial success. Here, I define success as the successful exit of a company where investors receive at least three times their initial investment. By this definition, the chances of a startup succeeding are quite slim. How then can one succeed? Over the past eight years of running Oak Pacific Interconnect, I've been contemplating this question. Today, I will share three fundamental elements of success for all aspiring entrepreneurs to consider.

The first element: Perseverance

I enjoy asking new hires at our company a question: In a particular field, such as playing a musical instrument or participating in a sport, how long does it take to become a world-class expert or competitor? Some say 100,000 hours, but this clearly doesn't align with normal logic since if you spent an average of three hours per day, it would take 100 years. The correct answer is 10,000 hours, as mentioned in the best-selling book *Outliers*. Calculating this, if you spend 10,000 hours achieving your goal, dedicating three hours a day, it would take 8-10 years to reach world-class expertise.

My second favorite question is why "10,000 hours" and not 5,000 or 30,000. The answer is that for ordinary people, practicing 10,000 hours represents the limit of human persistence. Only by reaching this limit do your competitors gradually give up, allowing you the opportunity to stand out. Taking piano playing as an example, you'll find that many who start learning alongside you eventually quit. Very few people actually reach the 10,000-hour mark, and those who do, provided they have sufficient talent, could become world-class experts. Therefore, the first condition for success is perseverance. To succeed, you need to outlast your competitors. Sometimes, it comes down to time—waiting until others lose patience, give up, get distracted, or move on to bigger, trendier fields. If you persist until the end, you're likely to be the last one standing and victorious.

The second element: Humility

Starting an internet company in China is difficult (as defined earlier). Among all the startups that have secured funding, fewer than 10% achieve this level of success. Why humility? Oak Pacific Interconnect has been around for eight years, during which we've achieved some milestones. However, every time we felt particularly proud, something bad seemed to happen almost immediately.

For instance, in 2003, when we began our wireless value-added services, our monthly revenue was exceptionally high, peaking at 6 million RMB per month—a significant income for a company with only dozens of employees. We were ecstatic. However, due to the unstable foundation of the entire industry, after 2006, both our own wireless value-added services and the SP industry as a whole rapidly declined.

In 2006, during the Web 2.0 craze, we became a banner company, and the news of Mopu going public was widely discussed. However, due to overly rapid expansion and weak foundations, the plans were put on hold.

After several such experiences, my conclusion is that success is a low-probability event, hard-won. After achieving phase victories, it's easy to misjudge the market and overestimate the company's capabilities. At such moments, it's crucial to remain humble and cautious. When things are going well, failure may be just around the corner.

The third element: Beware of Failure

A person I deeply admire, Warren Buffett's partner Charlie Munger, once said, "I want to know where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there." Most people smile knowingly at this statement, finding it reasonable, but if asked a few days later, they aren't entirely sure why it makes sense. Initially, I didn't fully understand this either, but I believed that such a brilliant person must have had a valid point. After much thought, I recently figured it out.

An entrepreneur has two ways to increase the likelihood of success: continuously doing the right things to boost the chances of success, or avoiding mistakes to reduce the probability of failure. Knowing "where we might fail" and then "not going there" means reducing the failure rate.

How do most companies fail? On the surface, it's usually because they run out of money. However, lack of funds is merely a symptom; it's determined by many deeper, more essential reasons.

When Chinese people treat illnesses, they follow the principle of addressing the root cause first. They think about solving more fundamental problems. But my perspective is that resolving fundamental issues is extremely difficult, as previously mentioned, success itself is a low-probability event, like treating the root cause of an illness—it's not easy. When it's impossible to solve the fundamental problem, it's better to address the symptoms first. For instance, taking aspirin when you have a cold helps alleviate symptoms, giving your body enough time for its natural resistance to fight off the illness.

For example, in 2000, Ding Lei told me that if he had delayed his IPO by a few weeks, it wouldn't have succeeded. If Ding Lei hadn't successfully gone public back then, I couldn't predict what he'd be doing now, but he'd definitely still be a remarkable individual. He might have lost hope in the internet and turned to pig farming instead. So, NetEase treated the symptoms first, securing funding, surviving a few more years, and when the opportunity arose, they seized it, expanding the company significantly.

Since success is a difficult achievement, rather than trying to force an increase in the chances of a low-probability event, sometimes it's better to lower the probability of failure. Knowing that the failure rate of internet startups is 90%, and that there are only a few ways to fail, if we can devise methods to reduce that rate to 80% or even 70%, it's equivalent to increasing the probability of success twofold or threefold!

Success is like a baseball flying towards a baseball player at light speed—it's not easy to hit. Failure, on the other hand, is like balloons slowly floating into view all day—they're easier to identify and hit. If you focus on the balloons and pop them all, what remains are the baseballs. Even if your skills are subpar, keep hitting, and eventually, you'll hit a baseball.

When you spend large amounts of time daily pondering how to achieve success, set aside 10% of that time to consider how to avoid failure. Doing so will significantly increase your chances of success. (End)
