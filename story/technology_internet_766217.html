The Backstory of the Microsoft-Nokia Acquisition: Four Rounds of Negotiations Led to Success

by anonymous on 2013-09-08 19:53:46

Recently, the American technology blog website AllThingsD published an article disclosing the ins and outs of Microsoft's acquisition of Nokia's mobile phone business. The negotiation started with a simple "Can we talk?" from Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer, and it took four rounds of major negotiations for Microsoft to finally achieve its goal.

Here is a summary of the article:

"Can we talk?"

This was the opening line in a call made by Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer to Nokia Chairman Risto Siilasmaa in late January this year. The transatlantic call occurred in the morning at Microsoft's headquarters in Redmond, Washington State, USA, while it was evening in Finland where Nokia's headquarters is located. Although the conversation lasted only five minutes, both parties agreed to meet at the upcoming Mobile World Congress in Barcelona.

Who would have thought that these few words would mark the beginning of Microsoft's acquisition of Nokia's mobile phone business?

Severe Resource Waste

Despite having established a very close partnership, Ballmer and several senior executives of both companies began expressing concerns about the slow development of Windows Phone. In fact, part of the reason for the slow progress of Windows Phone was due to the huge marketing funds invested by both Microsoft and Nokia in building their own phone brands and attracting app developers. Meanwhile, the engineers on both sides were either duplicating efforts in certain areas or unable to collaborate fully in others. As a result, Windows Phone's market share remained in single digits, marginalizing Microsoft in the competition in the mobile market and keeping it as a perpetual chaser of Google's Android and Apple's iOS.

More worryingly, without good sales performance to back it up, there were doubts about whether Nokia could continue to exist as an independent company. Nokia's performance in the stock market and financially has shown signs of decline. A key question lingering in the minds of investors is how fast Nokia's further decline will be. Microsoft was concerned that such a sluggish development trend might eventually force Nokia to join the Android camp. This was unacceptable for the future of Windows Phone, so Microsoft chose to further strengthen the relationship between the two companies.

After the deal was completed, multiple sources from both companies revealed that it took eight months and multiple rounds of high-level meetings across the globe to finalize the transaction plan. The success of the negotiations, which had been on the brink of collapse several times, was attributed to the perseverance and inclusive personalities of both parties, as well as unexpected factors like a glass coffee table that played a role.

Premier Engineering Tug-of-War

The location of the first long conversation between Ballmer and Siilasmaa: Hotel Rey Juan Carlos in Barcelona

As the Mobile World Congress in Barcelona approached, both teams attempted to evaluate their partnership and seek further solutions. At Hotel Rey Juan Carlos in Barcelona, Ballmer and Siilasmaa engaged in a one-hour long conversation. They discussed various options, ranging from minor adjustments to the existing partnership to deeper commercial collaborations, or even business mergers.

Following this meeting, Siilasmaa and Ballmer instructed several senior executives from both companies to further explore potential cooperation plans. Realizing that the direction of the dialogue might lead to acquisition negotiations, Siilasmaa and Nokia's board began evaluating all possible options, including non-Microsoft partnerships and acquisition scenarios.

Next came the tug-of-war involving the main executives from both sides. According to sources closely linked to both companies, the transaction announced on Monday can be traced back to the meeting in Barcelona and the three subsequent negotiations.

This acquisition case, codenamed Project Gold Medal internally by Microsoft (named after Edwin Moses, the former Olympic 400m hurdles gold medalist), was referred to by Nokia using the codename Paavo Johannes Nurmi (a nine-time gold medal-winning sprinter known as the "Flying Finn").

The First Negotiation Hits a Standstill

However, the completion of the transaction was more like a hurdle race, much more difficult than a short sprint.

On April 22, Nokia and Microsoft began negotiations at Skadden Arps Slate Meagher & Flom's New York office, Nokia's external legal advisory firm. The Nokia negotiating team included Chairman Siilasmaa, CEO Stephen Elop (formerly a Microsoft executive), internal Chief Legal Counsel Louise Pentland, and CFO Timo Ihamuotila.

The core figures on Microsoft's side were CEO Ballmer, Windows Phone department director Terry Myerson, then-CFO Peter Klein, and long-time Microsoft General Counsel Brad Smith.

In the April 22 meeting, Microsoft's Smith was unable to arrive on time because he was testifying in Washington D.C. for immigration reform. He hoped to be present at the start of the dialogue, but the testimony process took longer than expected. Ballmer had to communicate with him via phone, and Nokia's Chief Legal Counsel Pentland also contacted him via text message.

By the time Smith finally arrived at the negotiation venue, the negotiation had nearly reached a standstill. Microsoft had already stated its position, essentially proposing an acquisition intention, and Nokia was preparing to respond. Before reconvening, Smith had only 30 seconds to hastily discuss strategies with Ballmer, Myerson, and Klein.

After the meeting resumed, Siilasmaa spent 10 minutes calmly and politely explaining that there was too large a gap between the two sides regarding the valuation of Nokia's mobile phone business. Ballmer responded that he was pleased to understand Nokia's position. Subsequently, both sides decided that the meeting did not need to continue. After another 10-minute discussion in the meeting room, Smith and his Microsoft colleagues headed to the airport, possibly making it his shortest trip to New York.

Second Negotiation Hides Opportunities

Despite the deadlock in this negotiation, Smith still suggested that his colleagues reconsider for a day and re-examine the negotiation content. Someone from Nokia also indicated that the gap between the two sides might not be as large as it appeared. Perhaps they shouldn't have discussed the acquisition price too early, especially since Microsoft might not have fully understood many aspects of Nokia's business, thus failing to make an appropriate valuation.

The next day, April 23, Siilasmaa sent a text message to Ballmer discussing whether there was anything else worth discussing. Smith and Pentland also decided to meet to clarify the exact difference in their valuations.

According to insiders, a series of subsequent teleconferences convinced both sides that there was still room for negotiation. Both sides then decided to meet again on May 24. This time, the negotiation venue was chosen at Simpson, Thacher & Bartlett's London office, Microsoft's external legal advisory firm.

On May 24, the second round of negotiations began. Both sides started showing signs of reaching consensus on some issues, but still had disagreements on others. By nightfall, only the two negotiation teams from Microsoft and Nokia remained in the building. Each occupied one side of the floor, carefully studying the other party's viewpoints.

Just as Ballmer and Smith were walking back to Nokia's meeting room, suddenly a scream was heard and Ballmer disappeared. That powerful scream could only come from the leader of Microsoft with great lung capacity. The entire Nokia team was startled, thinking Ballmer was extremely dissatisfied with one of their suggestions. However, Microsoft's side was baffled. Then they heard sounds of people rushing around, with footsteps sounding anxious.

It turned out that Ballmer tripped over an invisible glass coffee table and fell hard, cutting a large wound above his eyebrow. Two Microsoft security personnel ran to find a first-aid kit. Eventually, Myerson informed the Nokia team via text message about what happened. While being bandaged, Ballmer conversed with Siilasmaa and Elop who came out to check on his condition.

Subsequently, both teams shared dinner together, with Ballmer sporting a large bandage on his face. When both parties returned to the venue the next morning, the offending coffee table had been moved from the center of the hallway to under a windowsill. By afternoon, the table had been completely removed.

Third Negotiation Demonstrates Sincerity

Nokia's mapping business became a focal point of contention between the two sides

However, the negotiations were still not going smoothly. Nokia's mapping business became a focal point of contention. Siilasmaa insisted that if Nokia wanted to continue as a company, the mapping business was crucial. Microsoft, on the other hand, firmly believed that mastering positioning technology was essential for success in the mobile market.

To break the deadlock, Smith and Ballmer personally flew to Finland on the afternoon of June 14 (Friday). They met at a residence owned by Nokia in Batvik, a town 30 kilometers west of Nokia's headquarters. This place, once belonging to the Russian military, featured several saunas, a swimming pool, a hunting lodge, and an old train carriage house, located near a famous winter swimming beach.

The Microsoft team negotiated for two hours with Siilasmaa and Elop, exchanging views on the difficulties in the negotiations. This negotiation did not reach a consensus, but both sides agreed to continue based on this foundation. During a traditional Finnish dinner, discussions shifted from the partnership to topics like the U.S. National Security Agency and the shocking Prism scandal. At 4:30 PM, Ballmer and Smith boarded a flight back to the United States. At least Smith stayed in Finland longer than during his previous brief stay in New York.

Fourth Negotiation Reaches Consensus

Entering July, both sides decided it was time to reassess whether the deal could be finalized. If no consensus could be reached, both sides would choose other options. Pentland contacted Smith and proposed a meeting. However, Microsoft had a lot to do at the time. Smith, Ballmer, and other Microsoft executives were still restructuring the company and busy preparing for MGX, the company's annual sales summit held every second week of July. Meanwhile, Nokia was busy finalizing its acquisition of Siemens' network equipment division.

Both sides eventually agreed to meet on July 20, and the negotiation venue returned to New York. They also decided that only eight key individuals would participate in this meeting: from Microsoft, Ballmer, Myerson, Smith, and CFO Amy Hood; from Nokia, Siilasmaa, Elop, Pentland, and Ihamuotila.

In the preparation week before the meeting, Microsoft's top management studied Nokia's conditions while balancing the preparations for MGX. The four-person group involved in the Nokia acquisition communicated via teleconference: Smith and Hood stationed at Microsoft's headquarters in Redmond, while Ballmer and Myerson were in Atlanta.

When Ballmer and Myerson flew back from Atlanta to New York, the map business issue still hadn't seen any breakthrough. Ballmer was very troubled by the inability to find a solution. He likened Microsoft's applications to oil tanks and Nokia's map data to crude oil, neither of which could be missing.

Microsoft wasn't the only "oil tank" that needed Nokia's "crude oil." Nokia hoped to provide its map services to a wider range of partners, including competing mobile products, cars, and other devices. Meanwhile, Microsoft wanted to apply the map business to its phones, tablets, computers, and web pages.

As the negotiations progressed, both sides suddenly realized that perhaps they didn't need to create this division. Since maps are also software, both sides could share the code. Nokia could retain the technical patents, while Microsoft could obtain an "equivalent to ownership" license. Essentially, Microsoft could not only use Nokia's map data but also utilize the data to accomplish necessary tasks. Ballmer and Myerson subsequently called Qi Lu, the head of Microsoft's Online Services Division, to ask if it was feasible.

Lu told them both that he believed it was fine.

Upon hearing this proposal, Siilasmaa asked Nokia's map business director Michael Halbherr in New York, who also agreed. At the end of the negotiations in New York, Ballmer and Siilasmaa shook hands amicably, although at that point they had only reached preliminary agreements on some principles outlined in the slides.

Ballmer's Retirement Doesn't Affect Deal Completion

In the following week, the legal and business departments worked to turn the concepts on the slides into actual transaction terms. Both sides were doing their utmost, hoping to reach a decisive consensus by September 3.

To achieve this goal, both teams met daily to decide which issues needed to be escalated to the four-person group consisting of Hood, Smith, Pentland, and Ihamuotila. The final outcome was not just a simple agreement but a series of agreements that included patent agreements, trademarks, the sale of the mobile phone business, and the fiercely debated map business.

All of this was happening when Microsoft underwent another major personnel change. After a series of complex events, Ballmer decided to retire from his position as Microsoft CEO, and Microsoft's board, including Director Bill Gates, agreed to his retirement application. Just before the announcement, he called Siilasmaa and Elop at Nokia to inform and gain their approval.

In the final days leading up to the agreement, communication between both parties intensified. Both sides were trying to take advantage of the time lag during the review period of materials submitted to each other, grabbing some rest time. Because they had to start reviewing the feedback returned by the other side early the next morning.

On Sunday, September 1, Ballmer was already on a plane to Finland. Upon arriving in Finland on Monday, both sides were fully prepared to sign the final acquisition agreement.
