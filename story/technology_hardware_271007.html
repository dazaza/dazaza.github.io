■ Reported by our reporter Liao Hongxiang

The term "knock-off" has never been positively interpreted. Recently, the scandal of built-in money-making SP services in knock-off phones has further made these already unpopular phones into public enemies. Recently, the MIIT quickly issued an "Urgent Notice on Further Strengthening the Management of Mobile Websites" to the operators, requiring them to conduct self-inspections, including mobile services. However, without the participation of the entire interest group, how could the knock-off manufacturers be so proactive and smooth in embedding "traps" into the phone terminals? Cutting off the money-making profit chain will be the key for knock-offs to get rid of the "money-making gate."

Knock-off phones exposed again in "money-making gate"

Once silent, knock-off phones have recently become a topic of public and media attention again, but not for good reasons. Due to being exposed for hiding consumption traps, all related information about knock-off phones has been exposed under the scrutiny of the media.

"Watching TV on my phone for about 30 minutes resulted in a bill of 581.5 yuan! It clearly says 4 yuan for 30 days, isn't this a scam?" Mr. Zhang's experience unveiled the long-hidden "money trap" in knock-off phones.

The incident started when Mr. Zhang bought a new knock-off phone for less than 500 yuan. The manual stated that the phone TV service fee was "4 yuan for 30 days." But later, when Mr. Zhang checked his bill, he found that watching half an hour of mobile TV resulted in an outstanding bill of 581.5 yuan. Recently, there have been a large number of complaints about this same phone model on the 3·15 Consumer Electronics Complaint Network, with almost a dozen complaints every day.

Similarly, Ms. Pang sent several text messages accidentally by pressing a button, each costing her two yuan in information fees.

These exposed infringement incidents are just the tip of the iceberg regarding the built-in "money traps" in knock-off phones. With the involvement of the media, the details of the incident began to surface gradually.

Unspoken rules under the transactions of interest groups

Subsequently, another party involved in the incident, namely relevant personnel from the knock-off phone industry, revealed to the media the "unspoken rules" of the knock-off phone industry: the built-in SP services in knock-off phones are actually provided jointly by SP service providers, knock-off phone manufacturers, and solution providers. Before the phones leave the factory, they are pre-installed with related services, offering users various value-added services such as games and music to meet their entertainment needs. As the mobile phone industry developed, the built-in value-added services began to take a wrong turn. Some SPs, tempted by high profits, ignored national regulations and disregarded consumer interests, starting to "meticulously operate" consumer traps.

In fact, this kind of non-compliance phenomenon has formed so-called "industry rules" within the industry, and even many large SP companies are also engaged in the business of built-in charging services. Accompanying the rapid development of the domestic mobile phone manufacturing industry, especially the knock-off phone sector, the number of participants in the "unspoken rules" has increased, making more and more consumers victims. Subsequently, a large number of rights protection incidents occurred, exposing this "unspoken rule" to the public.

Looking at the development of this incident, simply blaming the knock-off phone manufacturers cannot solve all the problems. Collusion is the keyword for these frequent infringement incidents. If it were not for the collusion among those "behind-the-scenes hands," how could the "money trap" succeed so easily? First, SP manufacturers collude with knock-off phone manufacturers to make huge profits through built-in services; secondly, the operators' tolerance or even cover-up during supervision makes this malpractice increasingly serious.

At the root, it is due to the temptation of high profits that all links in the upstream and downstream of the entire industrial chain have formed a common interest group. To gain more profits, all parties begin to "create and practice" so-called industry unspoken rules.

Cutting off the profit chain is fundamental

Without the warmth of the profit chain, everything wouldn't go so smoothly; without the coordination of the profit chain, everything wouldn't be so tacit. It is the profit chain that leads to numerous consumption traps, and it is the profit chain that causes repeated harm to consumers. Therefore, cutting off the profit chain is the key to rectifying the "money trap" in knock-off phones.

The MIIT has stated that it will severely crack down on SP enterprises that maliciously infringe on consumer interests. Once verified, they should be legally ordered to suspend operations and be prohibited from accessing any national telecom operator. On the 15th, the MIIT issued an "Urgent Notice on Further Strengthening the Management of Mobile Websites" to the operators, requiring them to strengthen management according to the relevant regulations issued by the MIIT and conduct a comprehensive inspection in the near future. At the same time, they should strengthen the management of business promotion channels and agency fee collection processes.

But relying solely on this passive regulation will hardly achieve satisfactory results. Handling one case after another is more like settling accounts after the fact and cannot truly prevent consumers from falling into consumption traps again. Removing the possibility of setting traps at the source allows consumers to consume with peace of mind. Only then can 3G develop smoothly without being hindered by consumption traps.

Returning to the key link in the industrial chain, which is the knock-off phone manufacturer. Since knock-off phones rely on low prices and multifunctionality to stand firm in the market, low cost is a principle that companies must follow. With the occurrence of the economic crisis and setbacks in overseas markets, knock-off phones have had tough times in recent years. Companies struggling for survival find it hard to continue developing. Therefore, to survive, some manufacturers have resorted to "perverse thoughts" and taken risks by engaging in "consumption trap" businesses.

Therefore, the key lies in how to allow knock-off manufacturers to move away from low-value-added cloning and imitation. Once these manufacturers find other ways to make money, they naturally won't be so enthusiastic about illegal activities.

Moreover, supervision should still be strengthened. Any non-compliant enterprise must be severely investigated, especially the operators, who should be urged more forcefully. Without the platform of the operator, the money that shouldn't have been consumed would naturally not flow into the pockets of unscrupulous businessmen.
