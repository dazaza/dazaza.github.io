Exclusive Interview with Wang Jianzhu: TD-LTE is all set, or it will be left behind if not implemented soon.

by anonymous on 2012-03-04 14:10:56

China Mobile Chairman Wang Jianzhou, in a special interview, said that there are no longer any technical issues with TD-LTE and it has reached a stage where everything is ready. He warned: "If we don't quickly adopt TD-LTE, we will fall behind."

Japan's mainstream operators have taken the lead in implementing TD-LTE. As soon as the topic of TD-LTE was brought up, China Mobile Chairman Wang Jianzhou immediately expressed a strong anticipation.

Throughout the day, Wang Jianzhou was mainly occupied with matters surrounding TD-LTE. In the morning, he led a TD-LTE seminar organized by China Mobile; at noon, he attended the opening ceremony of the ITU World Communication Exhibition and delivered a speech mentioning TD-LTE; in the afternoon, he visited the exhibition stands of TD industry alliances such as Datang Telecom.

He discussed TD sector cooperation with many international operators, exchanged views on TD frequency with former Minister of Industry and Information Technology Li Yizhong, and even discussed TD technology with the Secretary-General of the ITU (International Telecommunication Union). Finally, he came to a conclusion: "If we don't quickly adopt TD-LTE, we will fall behind."

Why is this? Wang Jianzhou said, because "TD-LTE has been commercially used in other countries, but not yet in our country. I met a CTO from Japan's SoftBank today who told me that SoftBank will launch its TD-LTE network next month. Overseas mainstream operators have started adopting TD-LTE. China was one of the earliest advocates for TD-LTE, but it has not yet been officially commercialized."

"TD-LTE has already reached a stage where everything is ready," Wang Jianzhou said, expressing both surprise and urgency. His surprise stems from the fact that China-led TD-LTE has been accepted abroad, not only extensively built but also commercialized. His urgency comes from the rapid development of FDD-LTE, as the two largest U.S. carriers Verizon and AT&T have begun adopting FDD-LTE, and the fourth-largest carrier Sprint has announced that it will stop doing WiMAX and switch to FDD-LTE.

According to the schedule, the International Telecommunication Union will determine the 4G standard next year. However, despite the 4G standard not being finalized, TD-LTE has already been commercially implemented in various countries. The so-called 4G standard is now more of a formal issue.

Regarding whether the TD-LTE technology is mature, Wang Jianzhou revealed that as of the end of September, the large-scale trial testing organized by the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology and China Mobile has concluded. Although the scale test results haven't been released yet, it already shows that there are no technical problems.

He gave an example from abroad: Masayoshi Son of Japan's SoftBank built TD-LTE base stations in Tokyo, personally experienced them on the streets, and then wrote a poem, one line of which means "Today is the happiest day for me," demonstrating Son's satisfaction with the progress of TD-LTE technology and network construction.

"TD-LTE has already reached a stage where everything is ready. Now globally, FDD-LTE lacks frequencies everywhere because, in the early stages of 4G, at least 40M frequency is required to do LTE, and future LTE-Andvice can reach 1000M speed, but it needs 80M frequency, and only TDD has so much frequency, so you cannot imagine how important FDD is globally," Wang Jianzou said.

"If we don't hurry, we will fall behind," Wang Jianzou warned.

He indicated that "if we aren't careful, TD-LTE will be like flowers blooming inside a wall, red outside, with TD-LTE construction and commercialization spreading widely overseas, and China will once again have started early but ended late."

Currently, domestic TD-LTE technology scale trials are ongoing. Wang Jianzou believes these trials can be accelerated. He said, "An advanced technology, if not tested commercially, will never be able to be commercialized."

He believes the main issues with TD-LTE are terminals and frequency. In terms of terminals, TD-LTE is still just data cards and single-mode phones, and developing multi-mode terminals will take time.

"The key is determining and allocating frequency bands for TD-LTE. Currently, TD-LTE trial cities have frequencies, additionally, 3G can smoothly upgrade to 4G, spending only 10% of the cost to upgrade to TD-LTE. 3G frequencies can be used for 4G, but the state still needs to clearly define the frequency band for TD-LTE."
