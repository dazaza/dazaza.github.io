"The Inevitable 'Death of Yougou': Belle's E-commerce Has Many Fundamental Issues"

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 15:28:57

Recently, Yougou.com, which has been suffering from a severe loss of executives, received another blow. On July 17th, it was confirmed by an insider from Yougou that Zhang Xiaojun, COO of Yougou, is about to leave and is currently processing his resignation procedures. As one of the founding members of Yougou, Zhang Xiaojun's departure carries significant implications, indicating that Yougou is indeed facing serious crises.

Let’s first review the e-commerce journey of Belle International, to which Yougou belongs:

Belle had been quietly experimenting with e-commerce for several years, developing steadily through three channels: Taobao distribution, Tmall, and B2C distribution (B2C distribution was completed through orders placed on Taoxiu.com). Led by veteran group member Xie Yunli, a relatively modest e-commerce team began by selling products under Belle's own brands, later launching exclusive online versions, followed by new network brands. This development path was considered before 2011 to be a more solid case of traditional brand enterprises successfully entering the e-commerce market compared to Li-Ning.

Following the 2010 funding of over $100 million for Letao and Haolemai and their rapid growth, Belle Group, which supplied these two companies, felt threatened. One concern was being constrained by distribution channels again; the other was recognizing its enormous development potential in the e-commerce field, including:

1. Numerous brands: In addition to Belle's several women's shoe brands, its agency for internationally renowned sports brands such as Adidas and Nike saw astonishingly rapid growth in online sales;

2. Easier category expansion: For Belle Group, which owns its own women's shoe brands and agents well-known sports brands, how to achieve absolute market advantage and status through multiple brands and categories became the most thought-provoking issue in recent years. Expanding physical stores to expand brands and categories was clearly too slow, and while ongoing e-commerce operations could easily expand brands, it couldn't quickly expand categories;

3. Financial advantages: During cooperation with e-commerce companies, Belle realized that e-commerce is a capital-intensive business. Belle Group's financial advantages allowed it to develop without relying on capital, thereby defeating more vertical e-commerce platforms that must depend on capital, becoming "the last man standing";

4. Talent advantages: Through several years of e-commerce practice, Belle already had a highly professional and strongly execution-focused e-commerce team. The cultivation of talent could meet the scale development needs of the e-commerce business or even higher;

5. Supply chain advantages: This made Belle internally very excited and was also the core reason for attracting executives from brand e-commerce or platform e-commerce. After all, for e-commerce, supply chain issues are always fascinating, as this seems to be the part that decides life or death aside from capital. The supply chain includes product research and development, production, warehouse management, delivery after-sales, and a series of "business" contents. In traditional enterprises that value "commerce" over "electronics," this is considered the most advantageous competitive edge over e-commerce enterprises.

With these advantages, Belle e-commerce decided to make a big move, starting by purchasing the double-barrel domain name yougou.com,

abandoning the slightly copycat “topshoes.cn” Taoshu website for redirection, stopping supplies to all distribution websites. Although retaining direct-operated third-party online shops like Taobao and Tmall Mall, the investment and focus were reduced. They recruited JD VP and Vancl VP at high salaries to serve as CMO and COO respectively, and sent a senior group veteran to serve as CEO, forming a golden triangle. They aimed to build a full-category vertical B2C mall, implementing multi-brand strategies on one hand, expanding into clothing and other categories on the other, realizing the dream of a department store empire impossible offline!

Everything sounded wonderful, but every dream needs to confront the harsh reality of the backbone. The reality is:

1. Higher technical requirements for independent B2C: Belle in Shenzhen headquarters could not recruit more excellent technical talents, and there was a lack of technical outsourcing service companies in Shenzhen. However, technology determines user experience, backend management efficiency, and more reliable data. Independent B2C involves dynamic technology, different from the relatively static technology like ERP previously used, continuous changes remain a hurdle many traditional enterprises cannot overcome;

2. Increased management difficulty due to category expansion: Someone once said doing women's shoes well and then men's shoes is hard, something Belle probably felt deeply when expanding into men's shoes. It’s easy to imagine the difficulties encountered in the backend within a short year of expanding into women's clothing and accessories. For traditional brand enterprises accustomed to meticulous calculations, this fast approach worked for Vancl during special periods, understood within the systems of Vancl and JD, but whether Belle can understand and execute with "exaggerated costs" remains questionable!

3. System: Many people don’t grasp the differences between e-commerce and traditional enterprises unless they’ve crossed both sectors. Simply put, departments settings (organizational structure), approvals, finance and budget, reporting methods, corporate culture, communication styles, execution methods, risk control, process systems etc., constitute a company’s so-called "mechanism." This conflict results in e-commerce trying to persuade acceptance of its "system," while the traditional team attempts to understand but can never fully comprehend the e-commerce "system," leading to low efficiency and unstable execution teams. Ultimately, the chairman or board might say, "E-commerce and traditional enterprises each have strengths and weaknesses, the future will definitely involve integration, so you must integrate in these areas...". If this so-called "integration without harmony" appears, it becomes what’s known as management innovation, thus the "CEO rotation system" actually appeared in a company like Belle!

Originally, it was believed that Yougou of Belle e-commerce was far ahead, and the top executives were waiting for the spin-off listing to enjoy success, wealth, and honor, countless examples of traditional enterprises succeeding in the e-commerce era, rather than being stuck in these hard injuries.

After all, whether it's the veterans who grew up with Belle or the poached e-commerce professionals, it can be seen that they did not leave because of incompetence. When the VP from JD returned to JD, he still achieved remarkable performance, and when the VP from Vancl had the courage to start a business. Are these hard injuries just the three points above? Or are these not only Belle's hard injuries but also the hard injuries of many traditional enterprises attempting to enter e-commerce?
