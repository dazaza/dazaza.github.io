I have always been puzzled by a passage from the humanist Erasmus in "The Praise of Folly" that has been repeatedly quoted: "If a stone falls on your head, you will certainly feel pain. However, shame, disgrace, and curses only exist when you feel them. If you don't pay attention to them, they won't bother you. As long as you can praise yourself, face the sea, with green mountains behind, and flowers blooming in spring - why should you fear the ridicule and mockery of the world? Stupidity is the only key to open the door to happiness."

At first, I thought this was just a form of spiritual triumph like Ah Q's, or an ostrich strategy. But later, I gradually understood that this is a celebration of the power of human reason. What is called stupidity is actually a reverse way of saying something positive. It means that only humans are aware of their own existence, possess subjective self-awareness and reflective ability, can discover and identify differences between themselves and nature, and have the ability to determine their own happiness. Whether a person is happy or not mainly depends not on external things, but on the person themselves. Aristotle once said: "Happiness is self-sufficiency," which conveys a similar meaning.

When you walk on the street or stay alone in a room, you suddenly remember an unfair event or someone annoying, and you cannot help but feel angry. You argue with them in your imagination, and at times, you even fantasize about throwing punches. At such moments, the moon is bright and the wind is clear, and the world does not notice your boiling anger and ignores you. Similarly, you suddenly recall a pleasant event, and you cannot stop smiling, mumbling to yourself while walking. At that time, the traffic flows like water and horses gallop like dragons, and the world does not notice your excitement and also ignores you. However, the former anger harms yourself, while the latter pleasure makes you feel comfortable. So, what should you choose?

Time is the most magical eraser. After anger, it gradually dissipates and is quickly replaced by new emotions. A person cannot live forever in anger; extreme anxiety leads to gradual forgetting, and you discover that what awaits you is not what you imagined in your anxious state. Since a person can never predict their mood at a certain day or time, why should they still be angry and anxious?

Sensations and concepts are inseparable Siamese twins. Sensations come first, followed by concepts, but concepts often interfere with sensations, and sensations can also trigger new ideas. If you constantly dwell on unfair, unpleasant, or unfortunate things, obsessing over them, you will eventually burst into tears, thinking that all the injustices of the world have fallen on your head. On the other hand, for the same person, in the same place, with everything you own, if you focus on pleasant thoughts, remain content and appreciative, you will become increasingly excited, from barely suppressing a laugh to bursting out laughing. For example, the most successful writer could cry if they wanted to. They might think, "For so many years, I haven't written a masterpiece or won the Nobel Prize; I'm truly a failure, living in vain," and thus sadness wells up inside them, leading to a good cry. Of course, they could also laugh if they wanted to. They might think, "To be born as a human and to have produced volumes of work, compared to ordinary people, I've truly enjoyed all the glory and made remarkable contributions," and thus joy fills their heart, making them laugh loudly.

Therefore, there may be some truth in Epicurean philosophy, which holds that the absence of physical pain and mental disturbance is the premise of happiness. Unfortunately, everyone feels that life is hard and envies others, saying, "Look how carefree he is." In reality, happiness does not depend on how big one's house is, how high one's rank is, or how much money one has; it does not depend on sacrificing present needs for fleeting vanity; nor does it depend on entrusting lifelong happiness to an abstract position. Happiness lies in one's own feelings. When Faust immerses himself in a beautiful moment, he says, "Stay, you are so wonderful," focusing on improving the quality of every instant, which is called happiness.

The greatest enemy of happiness is "raising oneself up" and thinking oneself great, believing that others only fit to adapt to oneself. Once you elevate yourself, the world changes color and taste, and happiness disappears.
