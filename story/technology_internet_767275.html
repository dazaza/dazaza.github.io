A letter Nokia received in 2008

by anonymous on 2013-10-11 19:13:23

The decline of Nokia always evokes sighs and nostalgia. The release of the iPhone was a landmark event, and Nokia's initial disdain for it eventually turned into acknowledgment, followed by a crisis. Many people assumed that the tragedy was caused by the arrogance of the giant. However, Nokia did not sit idly by but took countermeasures early on, which are often overlooked.

Fortunately, fragments of history can always be picked up again, allowing us to glimpse the texture of history from these scattered pieces.

In 2008, as night fell, Lauri Malkavaara, a journalist from Helsingin Sanomat (Helsinki News), sent an email to Nokia. This email listed the defects in Nokia's products and the crises they were facing. The email caught Nokia's attention, leading to multiple contacts with Malkavaara. Since their contact was private at the time, relevant information was only disclosed five years later. From the article Malkavaara wrote today, we can see that Nokia was already aware and secretly countering at that time.

One could say that this letter moved Nokia's upper management. In this letter, Malkavaara did not talk much about industry trends or cutting-edge technology, but rather started from the most minute and practical feelings. He listed the biggest defect of the phone he used — the Nokia E51 — which was its poor usability. For example, to change the ringtone, one had to go through five levels from the homepage to find it. And every time he opened the text editing function, it would make him choose from four options: text message, multimedia message, voice message, and email.

He listed many such user experiences, where these complex designs drove Malkavaara crazy. In contrast, he had an iPod touch, which was easy to use, had clear logic, and he had been using it continuously for six months.

This letter was not complicated; he wrote it quickly, originally intending to send it to the design department but couldn't find the right email address, so he could only send it to the Nokia news center displayed on the official website, waiting for what might happen next.

As Malkavaara predicted, the letter began to circulate within Nokia, causing a sensation and receiving attention. Writing a cautionary letter to the company and getting attention is a great honor for a journalist. But the events that followed exceeded his expectations. Perhaps Nokia was too nervous, and they frequently called Malkavaara. He had to explain that it was just a personal letter, although he was a professional journalist, he wouldn't report on Nokia.

Finally, this letter drew high attention from Nokia's upper management, who arranged a visit in the meeting room of Helsingin Sanomat.

Two people debated in the meeting room, where the senior executive detailed Nokia's strategy, insisting that consumer needs were diverse, and different phones were needed in the market. Malkavaara argued that consumers would not want a hard-to-use phone. Then he explained from multiple angles how this phone caused him usage difficulties, but the senior executive refused to give up defending.

Until the senior executive suddenly fell silent, staring straight at Malkavaara and saying, "This is a private exchange, right?"

Malkavaara nodded to guarantee.

Then he admitted that he completely agreed with everything written in Malkavaara's letter and what he just said.

Malkavaara was shocked at the time.

Following that, the senior executive told him that there was actually a highly confidential project underway internally, a completely new operating system, easier to use, which would change everything. This system was the well-known Meego later, but unfortunately, Meego failed a few years later.

Subsequently, the senior executive also told a story. When the first generation of iPhone hit the market, their intelligence agents bought a batch of iPhones and brought them back to headquarters. That evening, the senior executive took one home to study. His focus attracted his 4-year-old daughter, so to test the phone's ease of use, he handed the phone to his daughter, who quickly got the hang of it.

Before going to bed, his daughter appeared drowsily in front of his bed: "Can I sleep with this magical phone under my pillow?"

At that moment, he realized that his company was in big trouble.

Wandering and anxiety reflected Nokia's state at the time. Unfortunately, this conversation took place in 2008, and two years later, Nokia still launched the N8 equipped with the Symbian^3 system. The N9 with the Meego system wasn't released until 2011. Although Nokia had taken action in 2008, its efficiency in turning around was too slow, ultimately leading to its acquisition.
