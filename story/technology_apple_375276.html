Flattened Mac OS: Now Available

by diglog on 2010-12-19 13:51:26

The title of Apple's "Back to Mac" event this time has multiple meanings. Although the iPhone and iPad have caused wave after wave of industry excitement over the past two years, selling Macs is Apple's original forte; on a deeper level: iPad applications and hardware devices are also returning to the Apple computer. However, the implications of this homecoming journey for software and hardware are different.

Many features introduced in the new MacBook Air are similar to the iPad: slim body, flash storage, long standby time, instant start-up. The new MacBook Air does not have an optical drive like the iPad, which is a tradition in all generations of MacBook Air; and almost all netbooks and some "ultra-thin ultra-light" PC laptops do not consider CDs within their scope. It must be admitted that if a laptop wants to be portable, the hardware composition is basically this.

Of course, there are compromises, such as the inconvenience of upgrading flash memory compared to solid-state drives. Jobs described the new MacBook Air as the major direction of future laptop development; but probably within a certain period of time, most consumers buying Apple laptops will still choose the slightly more "old-fashioned" design of the MacBook Pro.

The iPad user interface will influence changes in Mac OS 10.7 Lion, offering another possibility for all future Macs: including the Launchpad screen display, its method of creating folders, support for full-screen applications, automatic archiving and automatic recovery.

However, hardware changes influenced by the iPad face trade-offs. Operations will become easier for beginners (which Apple has been striving for since the birth of the Mac). In this way, Mac OS will differ more from Windows; at the same time, products within the Apple ecosystem will be increasingly closely linked.

If you're an experienced player, this change may not look so appealing. For example, the auto-save function can sometimes save lives, but version control is more useful than auto-save for word processing applications.
