NetEase's "World of Warcraft" Suspended: What's Next for 5 Million Users?

by lay1987 on 2009-11-05 19:46:21

Is online gaming as profitable as drilling for oil in the Middle East?

From being an insignificant pawn to becoming a contested battleground, online games have developed amidst controversy. The online gaming market has successively produced two of China's richest people - Ding Lei and Chen Tianqiao - and allowed Shi Yuzhu, who had fallen on hard times due to business failure, to return to the list of wealthy individuals. The enormous potential of online games has attracted more people to invest in this industry. Some have described the exorbitant profits from online games as akin to drilling for oil in the Middle East; once you drill down, the oil gushes out.

According to statistics from the "2008 China Game Industry Report," in 2008, the actual sales revenue of China's online gaming industry reached 18.38 billion RMB, an increase of 76.6% compared to 2007. At the same time, it generated a direct income of 47.84 billion RMB for the telecommunications, IT, and other industries, surpassing the scale of the traditional three major entertainment content industries: movie box office, TV entertainment programs, and audio-visual product distribution.

Deputy Director of the National Administration of Press, Publication, Radio, Film and Television, Sun Shoushan, revealed in a speech that this year, the actual sales revenue of China's online gaming industry is expected to reach between 24 billion and 27 billion RMB, and will generate nearly 67 billion RMB in direct income for related industries such as telecommunications and IT.

The China Internet Network Information Center (CNNIC) released its "24th China Internet Development Status Statistical Report" in July this year, showing that by June 2009, the usage rate of online gaming services among internet users was 64.2%, with a user base reaching 217 million people, an increase of 30 million people compared to the end of 2008, and a rise of 1.4 percentage points in usage rate.

Where will NetEase go next?

According to data from iResearch, in the domestic online gaming sector, the top three are Tencent, Shanda, and NetEase, with revenues in the third quarter of 2009 reaching 1.432 billion, 1.297 billion, and 877 million RMB respectively.

NetEase, which ranks third, has encountered more trouble than Tencent and Shanda. Analysts believe that apart from Jiu Cheng, others might be envious of the agency rights for "World of Warcraft" obtained by NetEase, possibly involving several forces competing behind the scenes. IT commentator Hong Bo told the Youth Daily that the super popularity of this game will bring significant income to NetEase in the future and change the landscape of China's online gaming industry. However, currently, "World of Warcraft" hasn't brought much revenue to NetEase or become very important, so the suspension of approval hasn't greatly affected NetEase's stock price.

NetEase stated that it has learned about the General Administration of Press and Publication's announcement on its official website requiring NetEase to stop charging for "World of Warcraft" operations and new user registrations, but neither NetEase nor Shanghai Web Easy have yet received formal notification from the General Administration of Press and Publication requiring the cessation of charging operations. "World of Warcraft" by NetEase is still operating under a fee-based model, and new user registration has not stopped.

However, if this standoff continues or ultimately results in "World of Warcraft" failing to pass the review, the impact on NetEase would be significant.

Some estimate that "World of Warcraft" has over 5 million registered players, with more than 1 million concurrent online players during normal operations. According to the charge standard of 0.5 RMB per hour for prepaid cards, if forced to cancel charges or shut down services, the daily loss would be at least 2 million RMB.

Former Vice President of Shanda and CEO of Storm Entertainment, Zhu William, said in an interview with the Youth Daily that as long as it complies with the background and rules of domestic online games, we shouldn't close our doors. Domestic online games suffer from severe homogenization, and introducing some excellent foreign games can increase competition and thereby enhance the overall level of domestic online games.

"NetEase will not only face one game and a group of players but also undergo a severe test in its overall public relations strategy," Zhu William said.

Regardless of whether it's Jiu Cheng or NetEase, the Ministry of Culture or the General Administration of Press and Publication, they all overlook the 5 million "World of Warcraft" players. Hong Bo said.
