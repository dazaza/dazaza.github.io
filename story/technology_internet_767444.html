Is the key to Twitter's success having changed leaders in different development periods?

by anonymous on 2013-10-15 21:29:45

English Title: How Twitter’s Leadership Drama Explains its Success

A founder was sidelined early on, his name erased from the startup narrative. Another was ousted from the CEO position by a co-founder, previous boss, and seed investor. Not long after, this founder was also fired by venture capitalists (VCs), forced to watch as the original CEO, alongside another new CEO, retook control of daily operations. This is the story of Twitter.

Nick Bilton of The New York Times recounts it like a TVB melodrama. However, for those who have experienced several startups or tasted the bitterness of being a founder, such events are not surprising. Earlier this year, Harvard Business School professor Noam Wasserman published *The Founder's Dilemmas*, which includes nearly a decade of survey data showcasing the countless challenges faced by startups. In his view, Twitter's success stems precisely from these dramatic episodes. In other words, changing leaders at different stages of development was the key to Twitter's success.

Wasserman argues that founders can choose to become either "rich" or "king." Of course, some leaders don’t even achieve one of these roles, and according to Wasserman's research, very few people can simultaneously take on both roles.

The "rich" model allows the company to be more commercially valuable but forces the founder out by replacing their CEO position and control over major decisions. The "king" model gives the CEO control over the board and decision-making, but often results in a company with little value. For founders, neither option is inherently better; the key lies in whether each decision aligns with the original purpose of the startup.

The path to "value" is strewn with the thorns of "changing leadership." It’s not that VCs are malicious, but they realize that the company needs different leaders at different stages. As Wasserman writes: A technically-oriented CEO and founder may be the most important person in the early days of the company, but as it grows, the company requires a leader with a different set of skills.

Twitter's first CEO, Jack Dorsey, once collaborated with co-founder Evan Williams' previous company Odeo, working as the chief engineer to develop the product (Williams had previously sold Blogger to Google before founding Odeo). Evan Williams served as Twitter's second CEO for a couple of years. As the company continued to grow rapidly, leadership changes occurred again: the board promoted COO Dick Costolo to the CEO role.

Wasserman concludes that when founders celebrate the success of a product, it marks the end of an era. At that point, leaders face a series of new business challenges. They need to build a versatile company capable of going to market, selling products, and providing after-sales service to consumers. Financial management becomes more complex, and the CEO must rely on financial officers and accountants.

The organizational structure will become more hierarchical, requiring the CEO to establish formal processes, develop professional teams, and create a management layer. These CEO-level skills overwhelm founders, leaving them struggling to keep up. Even for the experienced Williams, this was no small challenge, making the team's transition unsurprising.

If the board can perceive the need for change, why do founders remain silent? Because founders exhibit flaws at this stage. Accustomed to being the darlings of investors, founders find it difficult to step down from their positions. In young companies, their resistance often becomes a stumbling block in leadership transitions.

Bilton believes that the motivation for change stems from the inadequacies of leaders. In a rapidly growing company like Twitter, both Dorsey and Williams undoubtedly faced their own management challenges, as is common in every startup. However, Wasserman argues that these transitions result from success, not failure.

The company's success highlights the shortcomings of the founders, alters the power structure, and makes them more vulnerable to attacks. "Congratulations, you've been successful! Sorry, you're fired," are the implicit messages sent by investors to founders.

Evidently, Twitter has been successful, at least in choosing the "rich" model over the "king" model. From the perspective of value creation, investors are most concerned about leadership changes, which are crucial for company growth. Therefore, leadership transitions are no longer personal narratives but keys to the success of startups.
