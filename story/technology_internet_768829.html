How Google Glass Will Revolutionize the Music Industry

by anonymous on 2013-11-25 11:23:17

Nowadays, more and more creative ideas are born on social media, and the collaboration between people is no longer limited to physical spaces but happens more online. Although modes like online collaborative writing are becoming increasingly mature, the music and entertainment industries have not truly embraced online collaborative work. For musicians, remote recording collaboration poses a huge hassle. However, Google Glass holds the promise of solving the issue of remote collaboration.

Producer Young Guru, who has worked with many singers and participated in producing Jay Z's albums, points out that remote presence technology can make collaborative work among music workers possible, such as Google Glass. These technologies can combine video, audio, and interactive components to create a unique "face-to-face" experience on the internet. "I think people will find collaboration an interesting thing because they can see each other's actions in real time, and they can free up their hands to do other things," he said.

During the process of making an album, some complex tasks can be achieved through remote collaboration, but the main parts still require face-to-face operations, such as recording. Collaborating via remote meetings (like Skype) is too complicated and makes communication lack intimacy—a feeling that is crucial for music workers.

Young Guru is a participant in the Google Glass Explorer Project. He says, "Imagine sitting there and being able to see the other person playing the drum kit or guitar; it would make collaboration much easier. At the same time, we can use applications to record music in real-time. Compared to sending audio files via email, which is inconvenient, Google Glass allows different workers to interact remotely." As part of his collaboration with Google, he provided many preset commands for controlling Google Glass. Now, Google Glass's headphones can distinguish the music the user hears.

Due to the rise of the internet, many famous music labels have suffered significant impacts on their release channels. Now, traditional music recording methods used by labels might also be replaced by internet collaboration. Some independent bands, like Canopy Climbers, are willing to complete music production over the internet. But the number of such bands is still small. The three members of Canopy Climbers originally lived together. Over time, when preparing to record their second album, everyone was in different cities or countries. They used cloud services to record their respective parts of the album individually and shared their tracks via Dropbox, eventually mixing the three tracks together. What role could Google Glass play in this recording process? Lead singer Alan Thomas said, "Collaboration is almost always a good thing. Everyone can understand each other's thoughts and energy."

When Google launched Google Glass, it brought a broad vision, but practical examples are still lacking. Giving Google Glass to people like Young Guru allows theories to be put into practice and measures the practicality of Google Glass. Young Guru's story is a great example where we can feel how music workers can achieve more effective collaboration through Google Glass.

For struggling independent singers and bands, the most important question is: Can Google Glass, as a musical tool, allow them to collaborate and record music from different locations? This possibility is significant, as Young Guru pointed out: "Google Glass maximizes what you can do. It frees you from the trouble of remote communication."
