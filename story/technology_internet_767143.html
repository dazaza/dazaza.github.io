Twitter vs Facebook: the former is like a square, the latter is like a skyscraper

by anonymous on 2013-10-08 18:37:39

Comparison of Monthly Active User Growth Between Twitter and Facebook

Can Twitter, which aims to achieve the heights of Facebook through custom ads in its feed, or even surpass it? The industry believes that Twitter cannot match Facebook; the former is like a town square, while the latter is akin to a skyscraper.

In May 2012, Facebook went public with monthly active users already reaching 845 million. If we consider the IPO timeline, Twitter's listing was about a year later than Facebook's. However, at the time of its IPO, Twitter’s monthly active user count was only one-fourth of Facebook’s when it went public—just 215 million. About half of Facebook's users access the site via mobile devices, whereas around 75% of Twitter users do so. Despite this, there is a significant difference in valuation between these two social networks. At the time of its IPO, Twitter’s annual revenue was only 13% of Facebook's. Facebook’s market capitalization once reached $100 billion and has recently exceeded that mark, whereas Twitter’s market value will be around $10 billion.

The above data indicates that the scale of these two social networks is entirely different and seems to suggest that their methods for attracting users also differ.

Facebook resembles a powerful digital city, composed of towering virtual communities, with a population of one-seventh of the world. Over a billion Facebook users design and manage their online identities, share trillions of pictures, links, interests, and wonderful moments from their lives through this social platform.

In comparison, Twitter looks more like a "town square," where people, possibly including those from Facebook, gather to share real-time updates, comments, and gossip using just 140 characters. However, on both platforms, users can freely exchange ideas.

Twitter describes its mission as: Twitter is a global platform that allows people to express themselves publicly and communicate with others. By creating a new platform for users to create, distribute, and discover content, we enable them to freely produce and share content, allowing their voices to reach every corner of the globe.

Similarly, Facebook describes itself as a platform that enables users to express themselves, discover, and create content. Facebook defines its mission as: Our mission is to make the world more open and connected. Users use Facebook to connect with friends and family, discover what's happening around them, and further share and comment on the people and things they care about. We believe that we are indeed at the forefront in promoting faster, easier, and richer communication between people, and Facebook has become an indispensable part of many users' daily lives.

Both Twitter and Facebook have the functionality to post text, images, and videos within their respective feeds and "content cards." However, Facebook appears more "verbose" and "trivial," while Twitter is more concise and real-time. Nonetheless, Facebook offers features such as status updates and "likes," while Twitter provides functions for posting and retweeting messages. Both social networks and instant messaging platforms aim for the same goals and gradually move towards their respective directions.

Twitter builds a more socialized social graph through its discovery function, which leverages the messages of people and events followed by users. Facebook has replicated Twitter's hashtag function. Moreover, both social networks have launched their own products to help users create short video content, such as Twitter's Vine and Facebook's Instagram, both of which are now seeking to establish similar mobile advertising revenue channels.

Through its messaging function, Twitter positions itself as a more real-time platform distinct from Facebook. Twitter states: Our platform allows users to quickly browse information by connecting various messages and more deeply explore links, images, media content, and other applications. Therefore, whenever something happens in the world, such as sports events or political or disaster-related TV content, it can be instantly disseminated through Twitter. Users can also discuss the events through Twitter and create powerful interactive experiences.

In contrast, Facebook focuses more on meeting the needs of the general public, requiring users' real identities, thus making it more like a socially functional website in the internet domain.

From the perspective of news sources, Twitter is akin to television platforms like CNN, TMZ, and ESPN, publishing news related to major events worldwide, including sports, entertainment, politics, and analysis. According to a Pew research report, only 18% of American adult internet users use Twitter, but 70% use Facebook. A positive sign for Twitter is that among American internet users aged 18 to 29, approximately 30% use Twitter.

Twitter executives understand that achieving Facebook's scale remains challenging. In the year ending June 30, 2013, Twitter's user base expanded by about 30%. Since May 2012, Facebook's user base has grown by 24%. Most of Twitter's growth comes from international markets. In the second quarter of this year, only 23% of Twitter's monthly active users were from the United States, yet during the same period, U.S. users contributed 75% of Twitter's advertising revenue.

Twitter's biggest risk lies in its difficulty to continue growing rapidly. In its S-1 filing, Twitter discussed important opportunities for expanding its user base, particularly in emerging markets. In terms of product development, Twitter claims it will "create and acquire new technologies to develop and improve products and services, thereby increasing the value of our platform and extending it to more global users."

Raising $1 billion in its IPO, having astute executives, and focusing on attracting more users will all drive Twitter's continued development. However, Twitter still feels more like a cable channel rather than a mainstream broadcast network.
