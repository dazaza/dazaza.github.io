The highest mountain in the world

by cqflw on 2009-10-18 20:09:49

Mount Everest

Mount Everest is the main peak of the Himalayas, with an altitude of 8848.13 meters and 29029 feet, making it the highest peak on Earth. It is located at 86.9°E longitude and 27.9°N latitude. It is situated in the eastern section of the China-Nepal border, with its northern slope in the Dingri County of the Tibet Autonomous Region of the People's Republic of China, and its southern slope within the Kingdom of Nepal. The Tibetan name for Mount Everest is Chomolungma, meaning "the third goddess"; the Nepalese name is Sagarmatha, meaning "sky god"; and the Western name is EVEREST.

Mount Everest has a giant pyramid shape, appearing majestic and grand as it stands high above the sky. Its terrain is extremely steep and its environment extraordinarily complex. The snow line height is between 5,800-6,200 meters on the north slope and 5,500-6,100 meters on the south slope. Between the Northeast Ridge, Southeast Ridge, and West Ridge lie three steep walls (North Wall, East Wall, and Southwest Wall). In these ridges and cliffs, there are also 548 continental glaciers distributed, covering a total area of 1,457.07 square kilometers with an average thickness of 7260 meters. The glaciers are mainly replenished by snow transformed from precipitation in two bands brought by the Indian Ocean monsoon. On the glaciers, there are ice towers of various shapes and rare beauty, ice cliffs as high as dozens of meters, and treacherous visible and hidden crevasses step by step, as well as dangerous areas prone to ice avalanches and snow avalanches.

Not only is Mount Everest towering and magnificent, but it is also overwhelming in momentum. Within a 20-kilometer radius around it, peaks cluster and mountain ranges overlap. There are more than 40 peaks over 7,000 meters in altitude alone. Some of the more famous ones include Lhotse (elevation 8,516 meters, the world's fourth-highest peak), located 3 kilometers to the south; Zuoqiong Peak (elevation 7,589 meters); Makalu Peak (elevation 8,463 meters, the world's fifth-highest peak) to the southeast; Zhangzi Peak (elevation 7,543 meters) located 3 kilometers to the north; Nuzi Peak (elevation 7,855 meters) and Pumori Peak (elevation 7,145 meters) to the west. Beyond these major peaks, there are other world-class peaks far away: Kangchenjunga (elevation 8,585 meters, the world's third-highest peak and the boundary peak between Nepal and Sikkim) to the southeast; Gasherbrum I (elevation 7,998 meters), Cho Oyu (elevation 8,201 meters), and Shishapangma (elevation 8,046 meters) to the west. Together, they form a magnificent scene of surrounding peaks bowing towards the central peak, creating a grand and tumultuous spectacle.

Other peaks over 8,000 meters include:

Shishapangma (Xixabangma), 8012 meters, the only 8,000-meter peak entirely within Chinese territory, located in the middle section of the Himalayas at 85'46'55 E longitude and 28'21'07 N latitude. It was first summited by a 10-member team from the Chinese Mountaineering Team in 1964, making it the last 8,000-meter peak conquered by humans.

Broad Peak (Bulo Aotu Feng), 8047 meters, a boundary peak between Xinjiang, China, and Pakistan-controlled Kashmir region in the Karakoram Range, located at 76'34'25 E longitude and 35'48'35 N latitude. It was first summited by an Austrian mountaineering team in 1957.

K2 (Qogir), 8611 meters, also known as K2, a boundary peak between Xinjiang, China, and Pakistan-controlled Kashmir region in the Karakoram Range, located at 76'30'51 E longitude and 35'52'55 N latitude. It was first summited by an Italian mountaineering team in 1954.

Nanga Parbat, 8125 meters, located in the western segment of the Himalayas in Pakistan. It was first summited by a joint German-Austrian mountaineering team in 1953.

Manaslu, 8156 meters, located in the middle segment of the Himalayas in Nepal, at 84'33'43 E longitude and 28'33'00 N latitude. It was first summited by a Japanese mountaineering team in 1956.

Makalu, 8463 meters, located at the border between Dingri County, Tibet, China, and Nepal in the middle segment of the Himalayas, at 87'05'20 E longitude and 27'53'23 N latitude. It was first summited by a French mountaineering team in 1955.

Lhotse, 8516 meters, located at the border between Dingri County, Tibet, China, and Nepal in the middle segment of the Himalayas, at 86'56'10 E longitude and 27'57'43 N latitude. It was first summited by a Swiss mountaineering team in 1955.
