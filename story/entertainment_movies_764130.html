Synopsis:

As the well-known producer of the TV show "Morning Challenge," intelligent and beautiful Abby Richter (played by Katherine Heigl) has always been the focus of everyone's conversations at the station. This is not only because her show is extremely popular, but more importantly, as Abby approaches her thirties, she remains single. This naturally sparks much speculation among her colleagues. As for Abby herself, she knows exactly why she is still single: her standards for a partner are incredibly high. Moreover, she once met a man who fit all her criteria - her "perfect gentleman" - but he left her. Consequently, Abby no longer fully believes in love, and she dedicates more of her energy to work.

This perspective on marriage and love quickly catches the interest of her colleague Mike (played by Gerard Butler). He believes that Abby's current views on love have serious issues. Therefore, he devises a small plan to challenge Abby to participate in one of his shows. In this program, Abby gradually uncovers the truth behind what she previously considered the "ugly facts."

Behind-the-scenes production:

Katherine Heigl's Personal Show

Hollywood is never short of female stars, especially beautiful ones. However, someone like Katherine Heigl, who is both young and experienced, is relatively rare. Starting as a child actor in 1989 when she was only 11 years old, her breakthrough came in 2007 with the box office hit "Knocked Up," which earned over $100 million in North America and instantly increased her value several times over. Her new work, "The Ugly Truth," like "Knocked Up," is a comedy, and together with her previous lead role in "27 Dresses," does Katherine Heigl prefer to take on this type of movie? Regarding this, she said: "Indeed, I really enjoy shooting comedies, and I enjoy the process very much because it is always filled with laughter, whether on-screen or off-screen. Everyone always comes up with many ideas to make people laugh. Since the entire shooting team is quite young, we don't confine ourselves to the script but rather explore more possibilities. This makes me feel like I'm not working but having a fun gathering with friends, so I really like this way of shooting movies."

When asked about choosing Katherine Heigl as the leading actress for the film, director Robert Luketic said: "This was decided before filming began. This movie revolves around the protagonist Abby Richter, who is the absolute core character in the story. We need an actress who can carry the film, and clearly, Katherine Heigl is such a person. I've watched her past works, and I really like the feeling she conveys in her films—she's very charming and convincing. This is exactly the kind of personality charm needed for the female lead, a TV hostess in this movie. Actually, I often tell Katherine Heigl on set, hey, this is your personal show, you'll definitely do the best, and she really surprised me, doing an excellent job. It made me realize that there are many possibilities in this young actress, and if possible, I look forward to continuing to collaborate with her."

The screenplay for this film was co-written by three screenwriters, with Nicole Isman, a newcomer, being the main author of the entire story and independently completing the first full version of the script. Regarding this story, Nicole Isman's view is: "I've always been preparing a topic like this, telling the story of a career woman choosing love in her work. This story includes some of my own life experiences and those of my friends around me. I want to make this story representative and reflect some real cases in today's society. The heroine in this play is the absolute protagonist, so I've been thinking, who would be suitable to be the leading actress of this movie? When the director told me Katherine Heigl's name, I remembered the movies I watched in the past, and I think she fits the requirements of this movie very well."

The Affairs of Contemporary Men and Women

Robert Luketic, who has directed films such as "Legally Blonde," doesn't have a high output, but most of the films he has directed are comedies. Last year's "21" also achieved good box office results. Regarding this new work, Robert Luketic said: "I hope this movie can present some topics that everyone cares about in today's life, such as how older unmarried women view the dilemma between marriage and career, and how they handle these problems. The heroine of the film is a hostess, a standard career woman, who values her career very much. This leads to various differences in her personal life. She hopes to spend more time on her career while being very picky about her other half. This is unfair to men because her focus is not on feelings, yet she expects others to be, thus causing dissatisfaction from the male lead in the movie, and the whole story unfolds accordingly."

"I hope this story can inspire men and women in today's urban society," Robert Luketic continued, "the title 'Ugly Truth' certainly does not refer to love itself, but rather to some undesirable things people may do for love. In the film, we focus on depicting these amusing situations. Gerard Butler plays the male lead who believes that pure romantic relationships are the foundation of maintaining feelings, but the heroine doesn't see it that way. This is similar to reality where women in society usually seek partners who can connect emotionally and give them a sense of identity, but perhaps for men, it's much simpler—they just need to get along well in bed. I think this is a regression in values, and it's the 'ugly truth' of today's society."

Regarding this point, Gerard Butler, who plays the male lead, also has his own opinion: "This looks like a simple comedy about a couple of sparring lovers, but it actually reveals many issues in contemporary relationships between men and women. However, I think there's no major problem with this. After all, young people nowadays are less easily influenced by certain viewpoints; they prefer to enjoy their own perspectives. My friends around me have different lifestyles. I think the 'ugly truth' refers to people who don't understand what responsibility is, especially men. Their attitudes towards relationships are too casual, which is something I completely disagree with."

Gerard Butler, who has been in the industry for more than ten years, has had a lukewarm career until his breakout role in "300." Since then, he has been offered continuous roles across various genres. Regarding the shooting process of this comedy, Gerard Butler said: "The director and screenwriters seem merciless; the script contains many straightforward dialogues involving arguments between Katherine Heigl and me regarding relationship issues. During actual shooting, the director wanted us to perform as naturally as possible, so we had to rehearse beforehand each time, making it really feel like we were arguing. Because of this, I was worried that it might strain my relationship with Katherine Heigl since it was difficult for us to shake off the characters after each shoot—it felt too real."

Fun Facts:

- Due to Katherine Heigl's outstanding performance in "Knocked Up," the producers decided on her as the leading actress during the early stages of planning.

- This is director Robert Luketic's second movie after "Legally Blonde" to feature a career woman as the protagonist.

- Similar to "Knocked Up," Katherine Heigl's character in this movie is also a professional woman in the television industry.

- The two screenwriters Karen McCullah Lutz and Kirsten Smith have previously worked on several of Robert Luketic's past films.
