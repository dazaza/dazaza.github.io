Goodbye, Nokia

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 22:26:40

After switching to a new phone, it felt like my life was completely held hostage. Occasionally, while craning my neck upwards due to the soreness and gazing at the sky, I found that the pace of such days was too consuming, too hard to bear. When I looked down, there was a vibrant world; but when I looked up, all I saw was emptiness. I started missing my nearly five-year-old Nokia phone. I am really loyal when it comes to phones — actually, except for clothes, shoes, bags, and cosmetics, I am quite loyal in most things. So much so that after a long time without new clothes, shoes, bags, or cosmetics, I began questioning my passion for life. Over the years, my five-year-old Nokia, under the constant friction of my flying fingers, had its letters and numbers on the buttons completely worn out. Each time I watched myself swiftly operate those barely identifiable buttons, a sense of accomplishment would arise within me. Unfortunately, the battery's aging speed far exceeded my expectations. It could barely last two calls before becoming almost dead. My parents, separated by hundreds of kilometers, would often shout at me: "Can you stop letting your broken phone hinder our communication with our grandson?"

Eventually, Brother Long couldn't bear it anymore. He threatened me with the new phone, saying if I didn't use it, he would give it to someone else. Naturally, I wouldn't let that happen, and happily picked up the new phone. Looking back, this pink Nokia has accompanied me through dating, falling in love, getting married, and giving birth to Tea Zi over the past five years. I sigh at the passage of time, as well as the fate of Nokia's decline. I can't help but recall how in "Ming Dynasty Those Things," when Former Moon talked about history, he said that no matter how great the glory, it only took up two pages in his view. The first page was incomparably glorious, and the second page was already cowering. Nokia's decline happened just like that, from one page to the next. I really wanted to keep this Nokia I've used for five years. I can't predict how technology will change our lives, but perhaps keeping the old phone has the same significance as preserving texts to commemorate time and life.

Recently, news came out that Steve Jobs' time capsule was unearthed. Amidst a pile of trashy news, this piece of information was a breath of fresh air, making me develop more affection for my new phone. Jobs was truly a man with a romanticist spirit. Unfortunately, the people involved forgot where it was buried, resulting in it not being dug up during Jobs' lifetime. Time is indeed a genuine potion of forgetfulness. This made me want to create a time capsule too, to dig it up after a few decades and see what happens. I imagine Tea Zi probably won't be very interested; he has already shown disdain towards my old phone, adopting an attitude of take-it-or-leave-it.

If I were to create a time capsule, what should I put in it? Besides my old phone, I really can't think of anything else right now. I don’t have a Lisa mouse, and besides, my attitude towards digital products has always been calm. Thinking about it, I decided against it. No matter where I bury it, it might get dug up and thrown away, and then tall buildings would rise on that spot. The result would undoubtedly be tragic. Therefore, I stopped worrying about what to bury for the future. In elementary school, we collectively sang songs about building towering skyscrapers, feeling particularly proud and united, believing in the greatness of human strength. Now, looking at these words, they seem irritating. All I can see are unscrupulous businessmen and cages like those in a zoo.

Time has changed some of our expressions. For example, when we were in middle school, the production relations of slave societies, feudal societies, and capitalist societies were the focus of exams. Back then, I loved to think deeply, chasing after the political teacher and incessantly asking questions. If the surplus value of workers in capitalist societies was taken by the evil capitalists, then where did the remaining profits go in socialist countries’ factories, since they weren't all distributed to the workers? The political teacher got annoyed with me, saying that knowing what was in the book was enough, and I shouldn't ask about extracurricular knowledge. Nowadays, students' political textbooks emphasize personal life experiences, mostly teaching children to love life, and rarely mentioning different societal systems.

My spirit of inquiry has remained intact until today. When it comes to natural sciences, I diligently seek answers, even though the forgetfulness caused by time makes me remember and forget repeatedly. Even when taking aspirin for a headache, I would chatter endlessly to Brother Long: "Do you know where aspirin comes from? Do you know its relationship with willow bark?" Fortunately, Brother Long also harbors curiosity about the wonders of nature, and listens earnestly to my various scientific show-offs with a learning attitude, occasionally raising questions to indicate that he has understood them thoroughly. This is probably one of the few intersections in our spiritual worlds. He has always been disappointed that I refused to play online games after marriage, hoping to drag me into his gaming world as his little follower.

But I just want to be Tea Zi's little follower, every moment of every day.
