Google I/O Lets Users Down: Focus Back on Developers

by geekzhang on 2013-05-16 17:20:44

Before this year's I/O, the whole world was guessing what kind of surprise Google would bring us at the conference; would it be the elusive Android 4.3/5.0? The XPhone, Moto's first device after being acquired by Google? Or perhaps a major update to Google Glass? Maybe even a smartwatch? And as a heavy user of Google Reader and Gtalk, I was also looking forward to Google’s follow-up handling of Reader and the rumored Google Babel.But sorry, none of these happened! (Though they really should have!)I still remember last year's I/O, where Google unveiled multiple headline products/services all at once: Nexus 7, Nexus Q, Google Glass, and Android 4.1. We were overwhelmed for a while; but after watching this year's I/O, I think many people might feel a little disappointed. However, behind the disappointment lies Google's reorganization and improvement of its product and service portfolio. Today's appropriate adjustments are for a better tomorrow.Living in the PresentSearch, Maps, Android, Chrome, and Google+—these products/services are currently Google's most fundamental and highly valued modules. At this year's I/O, the evolution and updates of these products took up most of the conference time.As the foundation of Google, search is delving deeper into the structuring and personalization of information. Whether it's the emergence of Knowledge Graph or improvements to Google Now, they're all sending a signal to people: Google knows you best! And the resulting privacy boundaries will receive more attention from users. (This point was raised during the Q&A session after the conference.)The update to Google Maps might be the longest part of all the topics at this year's I/O. Covering 200 countries, with street view covering 50 countries; integrating reviews and experiences from Zagat, further integrating with Google Offers, new UI, new iPad version, indoor navigation, personalization—all updates are starting to move towards heavier directions. Logically, this is similar to how Baidu Maps added group-buying around the area, photo sharing, social logins, taxi booking, etc., after version 3.0—that is, adding a service layer closely related to geographic location on top of the map, thereby achieving the so-called 'O' function in O2O. But do users really need these things? For users, perhaps I only need a map.As for Android and Chrome, both now managed by Sundar Pichai, frankly speaking, we didn't see any updates introduced. The introduction about Android itself only mentioned that the number of activated devices has grown from 400 million in 2012 to 900 million now, which perhaps no longer holds much significance. As for Chrome, it seems like Chrome is still moving forward on the path of faster, faster, faster browsers without end. Although all attendees received a free Pixel, where is our Chrome OS? Finally, the update to the photos section of Google+, I think, counts as a necessary feature for the normal evolution of community-type products.Looking comprehensively at the status of these Google flagship products at this I/O, under the premise that they are already doing well, they lack surprises, but at least they are all getting better.Returning to the Original PurposeGoogle I/O is an annual developer conference held by Google, so the original purpose of I/O is to better serve developers, and this year's I/O was especially evident in this regard. Compared to the mediocre performance of existing products, some tools/services provided for developers were truly eye-catching.The addition of three new geolocation APIs can only be considered an appetizer, while updates to Google Cloud Messaging, Google Play Games, Android Studio, and the developer platform are the main courses.Google Cloud Messaging is similar to Push services. It's well known that Android's PUSH system has become quite chaotic, and perhaps the official appearance of GCM can alleviate this awkward situation to some extent. But whether this can work domestically is another matter—one everyone understands.Google Play Games is a service akin to Game Center in iOS, providing features such as game progress saving, achievement systems, rankings, social invitations, multiplayer games directly to users, but for game developers, such channels are always welcome. Its official background means that Google Play Games will gain support from many game developers right from the start.Android Studio is a development tool launched by Google. Judging from its initial functions, its core advantage is solving the problem of resolution fragmentation and fragmentation of Android terminals, allowing one-time development to show results across different devices. According to Hugo Barra, Android product director, this is just the tip of the iceberg for Android Studio.In terms of optimizations to the developer backend platform, this year's I/O mainly showcased optimized notifications, automatic translation, better ad tracking, revenue chart displays, and app testing.BaaS platforms themselves should actually perform well. Previously, Android may have focused more on user expansion and OS experience; but entering the 4.X era, the team began paying more attention to developer services. Perhaps the outcome of the future battle between iOS and Android will largely depend on the health of their ecosystems, especially the developer ecosystem.Final ThoughtsActually, what impressed me most about this year's I/O was the hardware. Although we didn't see a new Nexus, Google did launch a phone with native Android—S4. This combination, which initially seemed a bit strange but makes sense upon closer inspection, is the most suitable one. Despite recent discussions and speculations about the conflicts between Samsung and Android increasing, this collaboration still generates anticipation. Priced at $649, it aligns with the current price of gray-market S4 units domestically. Friends who prefer the native Android OS experience might want to give it a try.
