Google's Chinese community helps create a new legend: top-tier private dinner gatherings

by geekzhang on 2013-05-14 18:55:20

At the end of January on an evening, Zhao Yong, founder of Grinntech, had just landed from his flight from San Francisco, USA to Beijing when he was picked up by Li Zhiping, his former colleague from Google who had especially come from Shanghai, to attend a gathering at Beijing's Gongti. With the Spring Festival approaching, it was the coldest day in Beijing, and despite several air conditioners working at full blast, they could not sufficiently warm up this Thai restaurant. However, shortly after, more than forty people arrived one after another, and as the wine flowed freely, many began to take off their coats.The above-mentioned discussants were members of a group calling themselves xGooglers (former Google employees). Since 2009, the members have maintained a frequency of meeting once every quarter. These gatherings are often initiated by Qi Qun, Founding Managing Director of Lightspeed China Partners. Zynga China General Manager Tian Xingzhi, with his high standards for fine dining, is always the go-to person for selecting the venue, while the wines during the meal are provided by Han Xuehao, CEO of TasteV. The bill is usually footed by Kai-Fu Lee, Chairman of Innovation Works, or by Wenbiao Li, Managing Director of Walden International, or by Qi Qun himself. The gatherings mostly take place in Beijing, but in Silicon Valley, Qi Qun has also organized exclusive dinner parties for over ten people.This was precisely the rare hunting opportunity for venture capitalists. For the Google gang, there were no formalities at these gatherings except for one strict requirement: you must have worked at Google in the past, but now you have left. Anyone can speak freely about marriage, having children, family matters, business models, entrepreneurship, and even hunting. Over four years, this private organization has quietly changed its contact methods from the initial email groups to social media platforms like Weibo, LinkedIn, and WeChat, and the number of people has grown rapidly from just over a dozen to more than 150.Topics discussed among the group usually range widely. Some people talk about Google's self-driving car project. Others share experimental results such as if two trucks travel from point A to point B, the normal fuel consumption is twice that of one vehicle; but if the two vehicles drive close together, it reduces air resistance, and the fuel consumption is only 1.3 times that of one vehicle. Driving closely not only saves energy but also alleviates traffic pressure.Zhao Yong and Li Zhiping also joined the discussion; both are members of xGooglers. xGooglers are typically highly intelligent and highly educated individuals. Just as PayPal Mafia (members of the American internet company PayPal's former club) spawned star companies like video website YouTube, electric car Tesla, and American space exploration technology company SpaceX, the Google gang is also extraordinary.Take a look at the guest list of xGooglers. Kaifu Lee, founder of Innovation Works (former Global Vice President and President of Greater China of Google), Zhou Shaoning, founder of Best Logistics (former Co-President of Google China), Qi Qun, founding Managing Director of Lightspeed Venture Partners (former Investment and Mergers & Acquisitions Director of Greater China of Google and also the first Chief Representative of the Google China Office), Wang Huinan, founder of Babytree (former Asia-Pacific Market Director of Google), Guo Quxi, founder of兰亭集势 (former Special Assistant and Chief Strategist of the President of Google China), Liu Jun, founder of Yunyun Search (former Deputy Dean of the Engineering Research Institute and Global Engineering Technology Director of Google China), Lin Bin, co-founder of Xiaomi Technology (former Deputy Dean of the Engineering Research Institute and Global Engineering Technology Director of Google China) - these are just the tip of the iceberg.Google's greatest contribution to China's internet industry is these googlers; it is a blood transfusion movement. Qi Qun said to "Global Entrepreneur." According to statistics by Kaifu Lee last year, more than 20 former employees of Google China have become founders or CEOs of internet companies, and at least 50 have become top executives or technical directors of China's top internet companies. This year, the number has increased by at least 50%, Qi Qun said.Premium R&DBefore returning to China to start his own business, Zhao Yong, aged 35, was a senior researcher at Google Research Institute in the United States (Googleresearch Senior Scientist). He was involved in specialized research hidden within Google X (Google's secret laboratory) for three years. Now this project has gained some influence; it is called Google Glass (Google Glasses). Zhao Yong casually told "Global Entrepreneur."Google always attracts the most astonishing top talents from around the world. Three years ago, during an internal meeting with more than 20 people, Zhao Yong finished his professional presentation, and suddenly someone behind him said: Perhaps we can accomplish it together! At that time, this person's attitude was very ambiguous, and I thought he might be gay. Zhao Yong said.Zhao turned around and discovered that this person was wearing a pair of glasses with a peculiar design. After the meeting, the two came to the meeting room to talk, but throughout the process, this person remained silent and did not say a word. Instead, he connected his computer with the projector. Zhao then surprisingly found that what appeared on the projection was what was presented in the other person's glasses. Zhao spoke with his mouth, while the other person typed faster than Zhao could speak.Zhao learned that this person was Thad Starner, a professor at Georgia Tech. Since the 1980s, he has been wearing such glasses on his face. In a television program 20 years ago, Thad Starner once told the host: I can answer any question about anything! The host asked if he knew baseball. Starner denied it. The host then asked about the life record of a player from the 1940s who had retired. Only three seconds later, Starner read out all the player's game data. In fact, Starner carried a computer with him and used a non-keyboard input device he developed himself. Within a few short seconds, the computer had transmitted the answers it searched online to the glasses. During conversations with people, this data was then read out. This event happened in the late 1980s and early 1990s, and our computers were still 286s and 386s at that time. Now, Thad Starner has been living with this thing for 20 years. Can you imagine? Zhao Yong said to "Global Entrepreneur."Even more shocking stories followed. Zhao asked Thad Starner if the glasses affected his social life. Starner denied it. After the meeting, Zhao described this strange person to his boss, Tom Dean, the former chairman of the Computer Science Department at Brown University. The latter said: I remember him. Twenty years ago, the arrogant and eccentric Thad Starner came to apply for a job at Brown University. Dean was one of the interviewers. After the interview, a professor said that if Brown University gave him a job, he would resign. Thad Starner ultimately did not get his wish.Afterward, Zhao Yong and Dean met with Thad Starner for dinner. Zhao mentioned Thad's experience during the interview twenty years ago. However, Thad completely forgot about the interviewer sitting across from him at the table. But a few seconds later, he exclaimed. The memories from twenty years ago reappeared before Thad Starner's eyes. Over the years, he had been using the glasses to record all video signals and convert them into knowledge stored in a searchable database, which could be retrieved at any time. Eventually, Thad Starner became Zhao Yong's partner on the Google Glass project.However, not every scientific geek gets Google's favor, Steve Mann being one of them. He once went crazy, spending the past thirty years wearing similar smart glasses devices. This glasses is permanently fixed to his skull, becoming part of his body unless removed with special tools. To avoid misunderstandings and trouble, he had to carry a physician's certification document. Even crazier, Mann hoped to control these hardware devices with his brain. Actually, I don't think Steve Mann has gone astray; he is a pioneer in this academic direction, at least fifty years ahead of ordinary people, so he seems a bit out of place in this era. Zhao Yong said.Google Glass is merely one of the projects in Google X. Google engineers always receive enviable salaries, interesting R&D projects, and substantial research funds, but Zhao Yong eventually chose to leave. Working at Google, especially at Google X, all projects are extremely innovative, and many are entrepreneurs or inventors bought or hired by Google. But I have a strong impulse; they can do these great things and make them happen, why can't I? Zhao Yong said.Talking about the motivation for starting a business, Zhao said: I want to achieve my ideals, not be an egg under Google's wings. What Zhao Yong wants to do is invent a new surveillance system. The images it collects differ from traditional cameras, but they produce different applications, such as security monitoring and intelligent analysis of customer flow behavior. For example, if a mall loses something, people know it's lost, but they don't know when it was lost, maybe a day ago, or perhaps a week ago, so they need to check the surveillance footage. Watching 24 hours of footage fast-forwarded takes three to four hours, but with the new surveillance system, everything can be completed within two minutes. If applied to intelligent analysis of customer flow behavior, merchants can understand purchasing behaviors and trajectories at different ages and times.Zhao Yong tried twice to find partners within Google, and two engineers were initially enthusiastic about joining, but ultimately decided to stay at Google. When you have a high salary and own multiple properties, making this decision becomes difficult. You must be crazy enough to do this; never encourage others to become your partner. Zhao Yong said.He prepared to leave Google and start his own business. In his spare time, he often conducted scientific experiments at home. One day, his colleague Li Zhiping brought his wife to see Zhao's new house and discovered this secret. Zhao revealed his entrepreneurial intentions, and Li Zhiping enviously said: Wow, that's amazing, I should think about whether I can start my own business too. In the end, Li Zhiping secured venture capital and started his business about half a year earlier than Zhao Yong. Li is now doing mobile intelligent voice search in Shanghai. Entrepreneurship is completely different from what you idealize; there are many setbacks, and the two of us feel a sense of shared destiny. Zhao Yong told "Global Entrepreneur."VeteranQi Qun can be considered the most experienced member of the Google clique. In 1999, Qi Qun received a job offer from Google when the company was just founded, with only about 20 people in total. Larry Page and Sergey Brin sat beside a ping pong table (which also served as a conference table) and talked to him. Qi Qun already had entrepreneurial ideas at the time, so he declined. In 2003, Google approached Qi Qun again, hoping he would focus on establishing the Google CJK (China, Japan, Korea) team based on Chinese operations. This time, Qi Qun agreed.This decision ultimately influenced Qi Qun's future life trajectory. In 2003, although Google already had a Chinese search engine, the quality was generally average, and the algorithm basically followed the English search algorithm with simple segmentation, without any engineer specifically researching Chinese search. In actual execution, Qi Qun quickly realized that there were numerous opportunities in China's internet sector, but not all of them were Google's strengths; some could be participated in through investment. In 2004, he made a move that seemed incredible today—investing in Baidu. Subsequently, he led investments in early-stage companies like Dianping, Tianya, Ganji.com, and Xunlei. At that time, my involvement in investment was accidental; on one hand, it was from a strategic perspective, and on the other hand, I myself was also an entrepreneur. Qi Qun told "Global Entrepreneur."In 1999, Qi Qun founded iTelco, raising $8 million in its first round of financing. iTelco was a global network communication product and service provider, aiming to integrate the internet, voice calls, faxes, emails, voicemails, and instant messaging to ultimately achieve the convergence of the internet and telecommunications. Its business model was to directly sell products and services to customers like China Telecom, China Unicom, and NTT Japan, a model that proved unsuccessful. This experience made Qi Qun realize that entrepreneurs needed more than just funding and technology; they also required business models and experience.During his time at Google, Qi Qun quickly assembled a Chinese team. At that time, Google's office was located in the Xinhua Insurance Building in JianGuoMen, Beijing, with only four or five rooms and about 20 people. Joiners included Kai-Fu Lee, Tian Xingzhi, Zhu Huican, Vice President of Tencent, and Wu Jun, Chief Engineer of Google.Subsequently, Qi Qun joined the venture capital circle, serving as the Managing Director of Lightspeed Venture Partners' global fund in China. As a former pioneer of the Google China market, he also became a core member of xGooglers. This group would seek his help whenever they encountered any entrepreneurial issues. Even if he didn't participate in the investment, Qi Qun was willing to offer advice. Some of the entrepreneurial projects by Googlers also caught Qi Qun's attention, such as the advertising company mediaV JuShengWanHe. Hu Ning, who graduated from Tsinghua University and obtained a Ph.D. in Computer Science from Carnegie Mellon University, participated in completing projects like Google Music. After leaving Google, Hu Ning and Yang Jiongwei founded mediaV, which entered the internet advertising field with algorithm technology, with clients including JD.com, Uniqlo, Nestle, etc.In 2012, Qi Qun embarked on a new entrepreneurial journey, founding Lightspeed China Partners, with a first-phase fund of $168 million, focusing on early-stage investments. The new fund has already invested in leading companies in various industries, such as Tujia.com, Meilele, and Rong360.At the most recent gathering, Qi Qun provided assistance in market promotion, positioning, and networking resources to Zhao Yong. People in Google are very 'googlely'. Everyone is excellent, passionate about what they do, trustworthy, and very team-oriented; it's a cohesive group. Qi Qun told "Global Entrepreneur."xGooglers have also attracted the attention of investors. Someone approached Qi Qun, offering to sponsor xGooglers' activities and hoping to obtain a list of members. However, this request was rejected. We want to keep it relaxed, so it remains private. But anyone needing help is welcome. He explained.This was the best time in Tian Xingzhi's eyes. Here, he met his wife, Wu Yuxin, who was also a Google employee. Initially, at a gathering of the Google clique, Wu humorously claimed she was the sister of director John Woo.Tian Xingzhi was born in Sichuan and moved to the U.S. at the age of 11, growing up in New York, and obtained a bachelor's and master's degree in computer science from MIT. In January 2000, he came to China and founded one of the earliest community and music social websites, Qzone. Later, he joined Boston Consulting Group, responsible for IT strategy and mobile projects, which were Google's focus in 2005, and Tian was selected to return to China.At that time, Google China was still in its early stages, with a rudimentary working environment where everything had to be done personally. Domestic navigation websites were popular then, and Google had to abandon its simple user interface and instead launch navigation products. There were many seemingly funny things back then. Users would search for "Google" on Google's page; the Korean team reported that many people opened Google's page without clicking due to its overly simplistic design, causing many netizens to mistakenly think the webpage wasn't finished. Many people didn't know how to use the search engine.The Chinese translation of Google was also born in a temporary office. At the time, Google employees and Ogilvy brainstormed dozens of options, and everyone voted for "Google". This translation was mocked, but people gradually accepted it. To let the U.S. headquarters understand China better, Chinese employees even arranged a series of skits reflecting various dynasties in China, showcasing Chinese history. At that time, Tian and Wu Yuxin weren't dating yet. Wu played Empress Dowager Cixi, and Tian played a loyal minister, whose head she chopped off. The final scene displayed modern life, where they played newlyweds. Later, when they really got married, they joked to everyone that it was actually their second wedding. That's fate. Tian Xingzhi said.Back then, the internal atmosphere at Google was very lively, and Tian Xingzhi often organized employee gatherings. As more people left, these gatherings evolved into xGooglers. At each quarterly gathering, an average of forty to fifty people attended. Tian Xingzhi was always the go-to person for picking the gathering restaurant, having been a food critic during chef competitions at Google.Besides enjoying good food, Tian also gained much work experience. In late 2005, Tian worked alongside Android operating system developer Andy Rubin and became one of the four people globally responsible for Google's mobile business early on, bringing Android to China and watching it grow step by step. Tian was responsible for expanding business with operators and phone manufacturers, while his colleague Di Shuo handled technical support. In 2007, Tian left Google and founded Spigot Games, developing and operating overseas games. This company was acquired by Zynga in 2010. A year later, Di Shuo also left Google and founded LeTouch Technology in Beijing, a provider of tablet computer system software and services.In Tian's subsequent entrepreneurial endeavors, the shadow of Google was everywhere visible. The decoration of Zynga's office was very Google-like, and the cafeteria had carefully selected chefs. Moreover, Tian hoped to create a Google-style culture. Zynga's culture inherits characteristics from both China and Google, representing a fusion. Compared to Google, Zynga has a stronger human touch. Tian Xingzhi said. Zynga also requires breaking rules. Once, a product manager at Zynga developed a product without informing Tian beforehand, but the product's data and results were good, and the operational cost was low, so Tian encouraged it. Additionally, Tian, like Google, encourages employees to spend 20% of their time on things outside of work.In 2010, when Google exited China, Tian Xingzhi was deeply saddened. We built it up with our own hands, but it was destroyed in one sentence. Tian said. To commemorate this special time, Tian and his former colleagues gathered to drown their sorrows in alcohol. Afterwards, he occasionally sneaked into Google to enjoy free meals and see who was sitting at his old desk. After the 2008 Wenchuan earthquake, Tian thought of his old employer and proposed that Google establish a missing persons platform. Soon, Google mobilized product engineers to complete this product. However, such opportunities have become fewer and fewer as there are now very few of his old friends at Google.At xGooglers gatherings, Tian has always been active. He never talks about work at these gatherings, preferring to let his imagination run wild, drink, and chat. There are enough meetings related to work; we're here just to relax. Tian said. In his eyes, xGooglers are all teachers to each other. Shen Si excels at snowboarding, Han Xuehao knows a lot about wine tasting, and Li Wenbiao is a golf expert. I can teach them dancing, photography, and martial arts. Tian Xingzhi told "Global Entrepreneur."Among xGooglers, Zhou Jie is arguably the most experienced. After graduating from Yale University in 2002, Zhou immediately joined Google.
