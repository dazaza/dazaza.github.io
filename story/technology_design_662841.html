Ultra-low Power Ultrasonic Heat Meter Based on GP21+EFM32

by zhihao2033 on 2012-02-10 16:45:20

**Ultralow-Power Ultrasonic Heat Meter Based on GP21 and EFM32**

With the improvement in living standards and increasing demands for residential comfort, heat meters are being widely promoted in buildings in Northern China for winter heating supply. Since 2009, Northern China has undergone a series of heating reforms, which have already achieved significant results. It is expected that billing based on heat consumption will be an important direction for future heating reforms in the north. The heat meter plays a key role in the heating system, responsible for heat calculation, recording, and data transmission. Due to its non-contact measurement method, low pressure drop, low energy consumption, and high measurement accuracy, ultrasonic heat meters are gradually replacing mechanical heat meters, becoming the preferred solution for heating measurement in Northern China.

The ultrasonic heat meter solution based on Energymicro's ultra-low-power 32-bit Cortex-M3 microcontroller (EFM32) and ACAM's highly integrated TDC-GP21 chip fully leverages the ultra-low power consumption and high computational capabilities of EFM32 as well as the high-precision measurement capability of GP21. This makes it one of the optimal choices for ultrasonic heat meter solutions.

### System Framework

As shown in Figure 1, the ultrasonic heat meter includes an ultra-low-power microcontroller (EFM32TG840F32), a time-to-digital converter (TDC-GP21) with a thermistor (PT1000) and ultrasonic transducer, an LCD display screen, operation buttons, an infrared communication circuit, and an MBUS communication circuit. The entire system is powered by a 3.6V lithium battery, and the voltage is converted to 3.3V considering the power supply requirements of the TDC-GP21.

### Hardware Design

1. **Main Control and Display Section**

The ultrasonic main MCU uses the EFM32TG840F32, which is based on ARM's 32-bit Cortex-M3 core. Compared to traditional 8-bit or 16-bit microcontrollers, it offers higher computational and data processing capabilities, higher code density, and lower power consumption. Actual data shows that the EFM32TG840 only requires 4 core clock cycles for 32-bit multiplication and 8 core clock cycles for 32-bit division, while the corresponding 16-bit microcontroller used in heat meters requires 50 and 465 clock cycles respectively. Since all the data collected from the time-to-digital conversion chip TDC-GP21 is 32 bits in length, both computation and heat calculation involve 32-bit data operations. Therefore, adopting the EFM32TG840 allows the ultrasonic heat meter to achieve better computational performance, thereby reducing the time the device spends in operational states and lowering overall power consumption.

The EFM32TG840 has five low-power modes (EM0-EM4). In EM2's low-power mode, the microcontroller can still run RTC, communicate or control via LEUART, LETIMER, and LESENSE, with a power consumption of only 900nA. Additionally, it features flexible wake-up methods and an autonomous PRS system that can be awakened by external I/O, I2C communication interfaces, LEUART communication signals, etc.

The EFM380TG integrates an 820-segment LCD driver that satisfies the direct driving needs of the ultrasonic heat meter's LCD screen, with a power consumption of only 550nA. The LCD driver also includes built-in voltage boosting and contrast adjustment functions, enabling the VCMP voltage comparator inside the chip to monitor the VDD voltage and adjust the LCD boost and contrast levels accordingly. This ensures good LCD visual effects even when the system battery voltage drops over time.

**Figure 2: Main MCU and Display Circuit**

The I/O of EFM32TG840 can be set to low-power mode wake-up and GPIO interrupt mode, allowing external operation buttons to achieve interactive control actions under low-power conditions.

2. **TDC-GP21 Ultrasonic Acquisition Section**

The TDC-GP21 is a new generation digital time converter specifically designed for ultrasonic heat meter detection and measurement, launched by Germany's ACAM in late November 2011. The TDC-GP21 chip uses QFN32 packaging and, in addition to having the functions of TDC-GP2, integrates the analog signal processing section required for ultrasonic heat meters, such as analog switches and low-noise chopped-stabilized (automatically performing temperature and voltage correction) analog signal comparators. The temperature part of the TDC-GP21 integrates a Schmitt trigger, which can directly connect a temperature sensor and reference resistor for high-precision measurement, far exceeding the requirements needed for heat meters. A 7x32bit EEPROM unit is available for storing the ID information and configuration register information of the entire heat meter.

The TDC-GP21 requires two supply voltages, namely the core voltage VCC and the I/O voltage Vio. In this solution, the two supply voltages recommended by ACAM use the same voltage source for power supply, and a dual-channel decoupling filter circuit is added to reduce system noise. Other parts of the circuit, such as the connection of transducers and PTC resistors as well as the crystal connections, are constructed using the official reference circuits provided by the manufacturer. In terms of clocking, the TDC-GP21 outputs a 32.768KHz clock, providing a low-frequency clock for the EFM32TG840F32, thus saving the need for a low-frequency crystal oscillator in the main MCU.

3. **MBUS Communication Section**

The ultrasonic heat meter communicates automatically through the MBUS (Meter Bus) bus for automatic meter reading. On-site heat meters can upload data to a concentrator via MBUS, and then the concentrator or a higher-level concentrator can transmit the data to the backend of the heating center via Ethernet or wireless GPRS communication modules for billing and management. In this solution, the TI MBUS chip TSS721A is used. The TSS721A is a transceiver integrated chip for instrument buses, containing interface circuits that can adjust the levels between master and slave devices in the instrument bus structure. Additionally, the transceiver can be powered by the bus without increasing the power demand on the slave device, and the bus can be connected without polarity. 4. **Infrared Communication Section**

According to the technical specification document "CJ/T 188-2004", the infrared communication of the ultrasonic heat meter uses a 38KHz carrier to modulate the communication data, with an effective communication distance greater than 2m. An infrared emitting diode and receiving diode with a wavelength of 940nm are selected. ### Software Design

The software part of the ultrasonic heat meter solution can be divided into three sections: the detection and measurement part of the TDC-GP21, the meter-reading communication part of the infrared and MBUS, and the display interaction part of the buttons and LCD screen.

For the detection and measurement software part of the TDC-GP21, reference can be made to the technical documents officially provided by ACAM, which provide the software configuration and implementation process for single-time acquisition of the TDC-GP21. The heat meter calculates the flow rate by calculating the time difference between the upstream and downstream of the ultrasonic wave, and then obtains the temperature difference between the hot water at the inlet and the cold water at the outlet through the measurement and calculation of PT1000. Finally, the heat value is calculated through the formula Q=cmΔt. In actual collection, to obtain a more accurate heat calculation value, the software designer can add corresponding compensation processing for nonlinear parameters.

For the software design of the communication and meter-reading part of the heat meter, after implementing the underlying serial communication of infrared and MBUS, the software designer can refer to the meter-reading commands, meter-reading communication data frame format, and meter-reading response data requirements specified in "CJ 188-2004 Technical Conditions for Data Transmission of Household Metering Instruments" to write the corresponding software.

The human-machine interaction software part of the heat meter mainly realizes the display of corresponding query data according to user button operations. For the LCD controller bottom-layer driver of EFM32TG840, the software designer can control it very conveniently. After completing the initialization of the LCD controller, operating the corresponding SEG segment registers with the corresponding data bits will light up the corresponding segments on the LCD screen for display. Combining the segment display content and user operations enables the design of the interactive software part.

### Advantages of the Solution

The advantages of the ultrasonic heat meter solution based on EFM32TG840 and TDC-GP21 include:

1. Compared to traditional 8-bit and 16-bit microcontrollers, the EFM32TG840, with its Cortex-M3 core, offers stronger computational processing capabilities, enhancing the overall performance of the meter.

2. Both the EFM32TG840 and TDC-GP21 have low-power advantages, resulting in lower overall power consumption and extending the battery life of the heat meter, indirectly reducing the cost of batteries required for the whole meter.

3. The EFM32TG840 integrates an LCD controller, RTC, and Flash for data storage, reducing the number of peripheral components in the overall solution and lowering the cost of the solution.

### Conclusion

In summary, the ultrasonic heat meter designed with EFM32TG840 as the main MCU and TDC-GP21 as the key detection component fully exploits the high performance, low power consumption, and good integration characteristics of the EFM32TG840, combined with the high precision and low power consumption advantages of the digital time converter TDC-GP21. This makes it the best choice for heat measurement in heating systems.
