What does the arrow triangle at the bottom of the beverage bottle mean?

Satisfactory Answer:

A twelve-year-old girl in the UAE got cancer because she used the same mineral water bottle for sixteen months continuously. The bottle contains a type of plastic material called PET, which is safe to use once. However, if you reuse it for the sake of frugality or convenience, there is a risk of cancer. I hope this article can help everyone understand the differences between beverage bottles and be aware of this hidden danger so that we can live healthily!

There is a triangle with arrows at the bottom of every beverage bottle. Please pay attention to the number inside the triangle.

PET (Polyethylene Terephthalate)

Commonly found in mineral water bottles, carbonated drink bottles, etc. It deforms easily when heated to 70°C, and harmful substances may leach out. After using a No.1 plastic product for ten months, it may release a carcinogen called DEHP. Do not place it in a car under the sun; do not use it to store alcohol, oil, or similar substances.

HDPE (High-Density Polyethylene)

Commonly found in white medicine bottles, cleaning products, shower products, etc. Do not use them as water cups or storage containers for other items. If not cleaned thoroughly, do not reuse them.

PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride)

Commonly found in raincoats, construction materials, plastic films, plastic boxes, etc. It has good plasticity and is inexpensive, hence its widespread use. It can only withstand heat up to 81°C. When exposed to high temperatures, harmful substances may be produced. It is rarely used for food packaging. Difficult to clean and prone to residue, do not reuse. If it contains beverages, do not purchase.

PE (Polyethylene)

Commonly found in cling film, plastic films, etc. Harmful substances are produced at high temperatures, and toxic substances can enter the human body through food, possibly causing breast cancer, congenital defects in newborns, and other diseases. Do not put cling film in the microwave.

PP (Polypropylene)

Commonly found in soy milk bottles, yogurt bottles, juice drink bottles, microwave meal boxes, etc. With a melting point as high as 167°C, it is the only plastic box that can be placed in the microwave. It can be reused after careful cleaning. Note that some microwave meal boxes have bodies made of No.5 PP but lids made of No.1 PE. Since PE cannot withstand high temperatures, the lid should not be placed in the microwave along with the body.

PS (Polystyrene)

Commonly found in instant noodle bowls, fast-food boxes, etc. Do not place them in the microwave, as they may release chemicals at high temperatures. After containing acidic (such as orange juice) or alkaline substances, carcinogenic substances may be released. Avoid using fast-food boxes to pack hot food. Do not cook instant noodles in the microwave.

PC (Other types)

Commonly found in kettles, space cups, baby bottles, etc. Department stores often give away cups made of this material as gifts. It releases toxic bisphenol A easily, which is harmful to the human body. Do not heat it, and do not expose it to direct sunlight.
