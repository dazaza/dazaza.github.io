Excerpt from "The Little Prince" At six in the morning, seventeen-year-old Gu Chengying woke up on time as usual. After getting up and folding the quilt, she put on her white slippers and walked into the connected bathroom. After brushing her teeth and washing her face, she cleaned the sink inside and out with a pure white towel, not allowing even a single drop of water stain to remain in the wrong place. Although there were maids at home for cleaning, she did not allow anyone to enter her room. In fact, her room was the cleanest and most organized place in the house. Unlike ordinary teenage girls whose rooms are always filled with idol photos and miscellaneous small decorations on the dressing table, Gu Chengying's room only had a deep blue single bed, a snow-white mattress and silk sheets, a desk, and a row of bookshelves in the same deep blue color. She tidied everything up neatly, and if it weren't for a faint indentation on the white pillow, people might have thought that no one lived in this room. However, this tiny flaw was quickly corrected. Gu Chengying, who had just come out of the bathroom, looked at what might be slightly messy but was intolerably disordered bedding in her eyes, went over, patted the pillow, and accurately placed it where it should be. Every item should be where it should be, not allowing even the slightest mistake: this may be due to the influence on her subconscious from experiencing several family economic crises during her childhood, the terrifying experience of losing everything in an instant. At 6:10, she opened the wardrobe, which similarly contained a whole row of black, white, and dark blue clothes that were ironed free of dust. She took off her pure white pajamas and changed into a white shirt and a dark blue pleated skirt. At 6:15, sitting in front of the dressing table, she combed her long black hair. With proficient movements, she tied a princess bun and then used a dark blue satin ribbon to tie a bow, making it the only decoration on her entire body. At 6:20, she left the bedroom, went downstairs, and came to the dining room. There was only one maid in the dining room, no one else. As usual, she was the first person to eat breakfast. Her parents didn't need to be mentioned; they wouldn't wake up before eight or nine o'clock. Her elder brother Chengxu was accustomed to coming downstairs at 7:30, and her younger sister Chengju would be even later, often lazily getting up when she was about to be late. The maid had already laid out her usual breakfast on the dining table — a glass of freshly squeezed orange juice, a plate of vegetable salad, an eight-tenths cooked boiled egg, and a piece of pancake. Even her actions while eating breakfast followed certain rules. First, she drank a sip of orange juice, not too much, stopping when she reached four-fifths of the glass. Next, she ate the salad, starting with the lettuce, then tomatoes, and finally green and red peppers. The number of lettuce leaves was six, each palm-sized, two small tomatoes, three small pieces of red pepper, and two small pieces of green pepper, with neither the numbers nor the sizes allowed to deviate by even a little bit. Once, a maid accidentally added an extra tomato, and Miss Gu turned around and left without touching her breakfast that day. She didn't say a word of scolding.
