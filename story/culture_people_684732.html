[Luxury in Qing Dynasty Cuisine] The Most Extravagant Diet

Among the Qing emperors, the most extravagant diet belonged to Empress Dowager Cixi. Her luxurious dining habits were famous in both Chinese and foreign history. For each formal meal, her dishes would fill three or four large tables, often exceeding a hundred varieties. These included cold and hot main courses, roasted delicacies, stoveside foods, and various snacks. At the sound of her command for a meal, eunuchs of all ranks would line up to wait respectfully, and the dishes would be arranged immediately. Some special dishes would be served as she ate. After the dishes were placed on the table, Cixi would glance over them, keeping those she liked and immediately removing those she didn't.

De Ling, a female official who once served by Cixi's side, wrote in her book "The Fragrant Memories" the following remarks about Cixi: "...Every day there are two fixed 'main meals,' during which exactly one hundred bowls of dishes must be neatly served. Besides these two main meals, there are two additional snacks. Though called snacks, they are still quite lavish compared to ordinary meals. However, the Empress Dowager was never overly casual. Therefore, each snack session included at least twenty bowls of dishes, usually around forty to fifty."

Of course, Cixi could not eat so much; "...at most she would eat only three or four dishes, while the rest would either be discarded or enjoyed in sequence by the female officials, palace maids, and senior eunuchs." This means that Cixi's purpose in dining was not just for eating but also for showing off her status and grandeur.

On October 10th of the 20th year of Guangxu's reign, Cixi celebrated her 60th birthday. Two years earlier, an imperial edict had been issued, and the banquet began more than a month before her birthday. Just the specially made porcelain items from Jiangxi Province, inscribed with the words "Wan Shou Wu Jiang" (Boundless Longevity) and auspicious festive patterns, including bowls, plates, and dishes, amounted to over 29,170 pieces. The entire celebration consumed nearly ten million taels of silver, an unprecedented extravagance in Chinese history.

Emperor Tongzhi ascended the throne at the age of six. Despite his young age, the imperial cuisine was provided according to the emperor’s standard. However, all these imperial dishes were eaten by a eunuch named Zhang Wenliang, who served near him. Zhang was deeply favored by Cixi. After finishing the meal, Zhang would express gratitude on behalf of the emperor to Empress Dowagers Ci'an and Cixi. Since the eunuch ate the imperial cuisine, what did the emperor eat? Emperor Tongzhi had two meals a day, eating food granted by the two palaces' Empress Dowagers: millet porridge, soybean porridge, coarse rice porridge, lamb soup, sweet rice porridge, willow leaf soup, noodle soup, tofu slice soup, etc. How could a growing child survive just on soup? Historical records show that Tongzhi once ran to the eunuchs to steal food, was caught, and the food was thrown away. Tongzhi's death was directly related to malnutrition.

During Daoguang's reign, corruption in governance, neglect of military preparedness, outflow of silver, and financial difficulties in the Qing palace forced Emperor Daoguang to live frugally to maintain the daily life of the royal family. His dietary lifestyle was the simplest among all Qing emperors.

In summer, the Qing emperors and empresses ate watermelon every day to cool down from the heat. However, Emperor Daoguang thought eating watermelons was wasteful, so he ordered the eunuchs: "Tomorrow, cancel the watermelons and provide only water." One day, Daoguang wanted to eat "noodle piece soup" and instructed the imperial kitchen to prepare it for him. Half a day passed, but the soup did not arrive. When Daoguang inquired, the person responsible said it would cost hundreds of taels of silver. Daoguang then told the person, "The best noodle piece soup is outside the front gate, costing forty coins per bowl. Let the eunuchs go buy it." The eunuch replied, "That restaurant has been closed for many years." Daoguang said, "I cannot waste so much money just for my appetite," and decided not to eat it. Throughout the year, Daoguang limited his diet to "five items" for each meal, meaning five dishes and five pastries for breakfast and dinner. Scrambled eggs were a daily staple. Even during New Year's Eve and the first day of the new year, Daoguang remained frugal. On the first day of the eighth year of Daoguang, his breakfast consisted of "one dish of dumplings in soup, one dish of mutton strips with sour cabbage hotpot, one dish of sautéed duck kidneys, one dish of stir-fried duck cubes with tofu, and one dish of scrambled eggs with meat."

Puyi recounted in his autobiography "My First Half of Life": "I ate six spring pancakes in a row, and the head eunuch found out. He was worried I might get indigestion from eating too many pancakes, so he invented a digestive method—two eunuchs lifted my arms from both sides and pounded me like driving stakes into the ground. Afterward, they were satisfied, saying I wasn't indigesting because of their treatment."
