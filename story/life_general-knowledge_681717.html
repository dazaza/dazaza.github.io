The "Five-Five Rule" That Must Be Read for Office Survival

Unspoken Rules in the Office

To Do:

One, love your work. Fall in love with your job and integrate it into your life. This way, you will establish a sense of responsibility and enjoy your eight hours at work.

Two, learn to smile. Not just to your boss, but to everyone from the gatekeeper auntie to the new intern. Remember to display your radiant smile to win the goodwill of everyone in the company. Affability is a crucial foundation for career development.

Three, be empathetic. Be a thoughtful person! Send a text message on a colleague's birthday, offer hot water when they're sick. Don't scheme maliciously; these are all simple acts. Good interpersonal relationships will benefit you greatly in the office.

Four, have principles. Being flexible in handling matters does not mean bending over backward and lacking opinions. Otherwise, the mask of hypocrisy will eventually be unmasked, leaving a bad impression of being brainless and incapable.

Five, respect others' privacy. Even in private, don't casually discuss colleagues' private matters, especially within the company premises, whether in the office, cafeteria, or restroom.

Not To Do:

One, refrain from expressing opinions lightly. Your workplace may have various unsatisfactory aspects, and you might have many ideas for improvement, but don't express them easily. What seems like enthusiasm to you might be perceived as dissatisfaction by your superiors. Focus on doing your job well.

Two, don't be late. Some people in the company may have a habit of being late, but that could become a topic of criticism. In companies where clocking in isn't required, being late, even just a couple of times, could be exaggerated into "frequent tardiness," and you won't have the right to defend yourself since there's no evidence.

Three, don't let personal likes or dislikes affect your work. You might dislike someone, but you must know how to collaborate with them at work. Working and making friends are two different things. Set aside personal feelings and complete tasks together.

Four, don't conflict with your superior. Venting anger by slamming the table might feel satisfying psychologically, but don't assume your superior will view it as a communication method like among friends. Even if the superior is wrong, communicate gently and give them a way out.

Five, don't be too strict. You might be right, and maybe the colleague didn't do their job well, but your stern attitude will be seen as harsh. If you don't greet colleagues regularly and only interact with them during meetings or when assigning tasks, sending emails in formal language, how can you win hearts?

Rules of Office Life and Laws

One, Inequity Rule: Capable people in the office always have endless tasks, while incapable ones have none. Those who do more work have higher chances of making mistakes, receiving criticism, and overtime. Those who do less or nothing tend to commit fewer errors, giving a better impression to leaders and scoring higher in year-end evaluations, leading peaceful lives.

Two, Transfer Rule: Whoever touches a task first is doomed. Don't think that after following procedures, delegating, and implementing, everything is fine. There will still be plenty of clean-up work for you to handle since you are in the general office, and you assigned the task downward, so you must close the loop.

Three, Benefit-Loss Rule: Everyone wants fewer responsibilities, less work, fewer troubles, and more opportunities to be good. Thus, the principle for most people is to push away tasks that offer no benefits and avoid those causing trouble. They absolutely refuse to do anything offensive.

Four, Procedure Rule: In the office, follow rules if there are rules, follow conventions if there are no rules, and follow leadership instructions if there are no conventions. Even for trivial matters, you must follow the procedure. Even if it causes delays, the blame won't fall on you.

Five, Greeting Rule: Even if you're overwhelmed with work, stressed out, or deeply engrossed in writing, when someone greets you or asks for help, you must drop everything, adjust your state promptly, and respond enthusiastically. Otherwise, others won't care whether you're busy or stressed, and they will surely have opinions.

Six, Treatment Rule: People always link their roles with their treatment. Although some might not take meetings seriously, their name cards must be placed on their seats because it signifies political treatment.

Seven, Distance Rule: Departments closer to the leader's office tend to start work earlier, leave later, and eat last. Those farther away don't concern themselves with these details. Individually, those farther from the leader perceive those close to the leader as influential.

Eight, Impression Rule: Even if you need to strengthen learning and mental adjustment to improve efficiency, it's better to browse news or listen to music when no one is around. Don't focus intently on meeting reports during working hours; otherwise, everyone will say, "Hey, you're so relaxed." Actually, your mind is racing at that moment.

Nine, Role Rule: A newly interacting leader might be very friendly towards you. If you mistakenly place yourself in the role of his buddy and speak without restraint, that's a big mistake. Within days, he will find a way to make you realize that leaders are leaders, subordinates are subordinates, and your relationship is not what you thought.

Ten, Awkward Rule: Hard workers don't fare as well as clever workers, workers don't fare as well as observers, and observers don't fare as well as troublemakers. Therefore, those in power are not necessarily the most capable, and those out of favor are not necessarily incompetent.

Eleven, Transfer Rule: If the leader's superior criticizes the leader, as a subordinate, you must be cautious as the leader might vent frustration on you. Of course, you can continue transferring it downward. Experienced spouses and children, noticing a gloomy expression, generally know to walk away and keep a distance.

Twelve, Attribution Rule: The most decent reason for personal stagnation is poor relations with the leader. Thus, managing relationships becomes the primary skill and task for many.

Thirteen, Conflict Rule: Getting too close to the leader invites gossip, while staying too distant risks lagging behind. Neither side offers relief, a constant dilemma for those with aspirations for promotion.

Fourteen, Face-Changing Rule: Some people behave obsequiously in front of superiors, jovially with peers, and sternly with subordinates. Changing faces three times a day, yet doing it effortlessly.

Fifteen, Liveliness Rule: Busy departments always maintain an atmosphere of "unity, tension, seriousness" without "liveliness." Idle departments, upon welcoming outsiders, liven up, using the opportunity to chat freely, laugh, and blow off steam.

Sixteen, Overtime Rule: Overtime equals dedication, regardless of efficiency. Browsing the internet on Saturday counts as overtime, and it's always the same few departments that consistently work overtime, while others function normally without it.

Seventeen, Conformity Rule: The head of a unit's personality and preferences subtly influence subordinates. Offices often discuss distinguishing characteristics of certain departments, attributing them to the department head. Since leaders set the tone, over time, everyone aligns with the leader
