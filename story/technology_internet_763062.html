Why did Surface RT fail while iPad didn't?

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 13:59:28

　　English Title: why-the-surface-rt-failed-and-the-ipad-did-not

　　To most consumers, Apple's iPad and Microsoft's Surface RT tablet seem to have little difference at first glance. Both are rectangular designs, both feature bright and clear screens, and both boast smooth and clean user interface designs.

　　However, the financial report Microsoft released this Thursday showed that the inventory of unsold Surface RT tablets had accumulated to a value of $900 million. In stark contrast stands Apple's iPad, which continues to break sales records, having now sold over 100 million units.

　　But why has one continued to succeed while the other failed? I have my own take on it, and it starts with a story.

　　I often receive tech-support calls from family members—my father, grandfather, cousin—seeking help with various computer problems. A few months ago, I received such a call from my sister. Her iMac had malfunctioned, so I began guiding her through the process of diagnosing the issue step by step. As I was explaining, I asked her,

　　"OK, what’s happening with your computer now?"

　　"My computer is restarting!" she replied triumphantly.

　　"Why? What happened?" I asked, confused. "Did it crash?"

　　"No, I just lost patience, so I unplugged it and plugged it back in."

　　This is precisely one reason why the Surface RT didn't sell well: impatience.

　　When Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer unveiled the Surface tablet a year ago, he stood on stage touting new and exciting features, trying to differentiate his product from the iPad. These features included additional ports, a USB interface, an SD card slot, the ability to use a pen for operations on the Surface Pro, and a built-in flip-out stand. This pen is sold by Microsoft and can also be used on the Surface RT.

　　Just thinking about all these options is enough to make one's head spin.

　　Nowadays, consumers don’t want to make choices. They lack patience. They want to unplug any external gadgets from the product and start using it immediately. They simply don’t have time to consider SD cards, USB interfaces, electronic pens, or flip-out stands. The Surface RT doesn't allow for that; consumers must think about these things.

　　Even the advertisements for the iPad and Surface RT differ. Apple showcases only the device, making the iPad the star and hero. Microsoft’s flashy ads, however, make the advertisement itself the star, not the product.

　　Last year, when the Surface RT was launched, I wrote an article. In it, I mentioned that among the new features of this device, the most appealing was its cover. It could double as a keyboard and made typing on the tablet more convenient. However, Sarah Rotman Epps—an analyst specializing in tablets at Forrester Research—reminded me that too many choices can overwhelm consumers.

　　"In this market, Microsoft will be its own worst enemy," Ms. Rotman Epps wrote at the time. "Not to mention Apple and Google. Apple understands this; it offers only a few choices: connectivity options, storage capacity, black or white."
