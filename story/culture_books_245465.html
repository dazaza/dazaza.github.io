"ON THE ROAD" Author: Kerouac

by jqrxass on 2009-10-08 00:06:19

From April 2, 1951 to April 22, in a span of 20 days, Jack Kerouac completed the first draft of "On the Road" with a typewriter and a roll of 120-foot-long printing paper.

"I'm about to start what might be the greatest adventure of my traveling life. Lying on a flatbed truck were around six or seven young men... I ran up and asked: 'Is there room for me?' They said: 'Yes, get on quickly, everyone who gets on has a seat.' Before I could settle down inside the compartment, the truck started moving. My body swayed, and one of the passengers steadied me, allowing me to sit down promptly. Someone handed me a bottle of cheap whisky... The drizzle in the Nebraska sky persisted without end, yet it had its own poetic charm, and I suddenly finished the drink. 'Ah ha, we're back on the road!' a young man wearing a baseball cap shouted... They said they planned to hitchhike across America this summer. 'We're heading to Los Angeles now.' ... 'For what?' 'For what? We're not sure either, no need to worry about it.'..."

"On the Road" is Kerouac's autobiographical masterpiece. The protagonist Sal, in pursuit of individuality, travels across the American continent several times by hitchhiking or driving with Dean, Marylou, and a few other young people. Eventually, they reach Mexico. Along the way, they indulge in excessive drinking, marijuana smoking, womanizing, and engage in discussions about Eastern Zen Buddhism. When they get tired, they block the road to stop cars, sleep in villages at night, wandering from New York to San Francisco, and finally disperse... Upon its release in 1957, "On the Road" caused a stir in public opinion, receiving mixed reviews. However, it is undeniable that this book influenced an entire generation of Americans' lifestyles and is widely regarded as a classic of the 1960s hippie movement.

Jack Kerouac was born in Lowell, Massachusetts, an old textile industrial town. His parents were French immigrants from Quebec, Canada. It wasn't until he was six years old that Kerouac began learning English. Both of his parents were Roman Catholics; his father Leo Kerouac owned a printing factory that sufficiently supported the family. Kerouac was the youngest boy in the family, enjoying a peaceful and happy childhood. At the age of four, his five-year-older brother Gerard passed away due to illness, leaving a deep impression on Kerouac. In his view, Gerard was exceptionally intelligent and especially compassionate towards small animals, but was mercilessly taken by death. The family's religious beliefs and the premature death of his brother made Kerouac believe in reincarnation since childhood. His later devout belief in Buddhism can be traced back to this event.

Kerouac was naturally shy as a child but enjoyed sports (horseback riding, baseball, football) and was passionate about reading literary works. From middle school, he developed the habit of carrying a notebook with him, jotting down the happenings around him - daily conversations of family, friends, neighbors, radio programs, and novel language from movie characters. He read extensively, from the works of British, French, Russian, German, and American writers in "The Harvard Classics" to contemporary authors like Hemingway. Literary allusions and the everyday vernacular of lower-class street people are two significant characteristics of Kerouac's work, clearly stemming from his early passion and familiarity with world classics.

Author Biography:

Jack Kerouac, born on March 12, 1922, in Lowell, Massachusetts, was the youngest child in a Franco-American family. He attended local Catholic and public schools, earning an橄榄球 scholarship to Columbia University in New York, where he befriended Allen Ginsberg, William S. Burroughs, and Neal Cassady, members of the Beat Generation.

Kerouac dropped out during his second year of university to pursue literary creation and worked intermittently for the U.S. Navy and commercial shipping companies. In 1950, his first novel "Town and City" was published. In 1957, with the publication of "On the Road," he became the spokesperson for the Beat Generation and entered the ranks of the most controversial famous writers of the 20th century. He also authored "Dharma Bums," "The Subterraneans," "Lonesome Traveler," and "Visions of Cody." On October 21, 1969, Kerouac passed away in St. Petersburg, Florida, at the age of 47.

His first novel "Town and City" (1950) was an epic describing family and society according to customs and historical events, but it did not attract much attention at the time. "On the Road" (1957) was written within weeks and remained unedited for several subsequent years. The novel's structure was loose and fragmented, depicting the absurd lives of a group of young people, reflecting the spiritual emptiness and aimlessness of post-war American youth. Kerouac's third novel "The Subterraneans" (1958) narrated the so-called lives of a group of "beatniks" in San Francisco who indulged in alcoholism, promiscuity, and drug use. "Dharma Bums" (1958) had a similar theme but contained profound Eastern philosophy. Soon after, Kerouac released two more novels, "Doctor Sax" (1959) and "Maggie Cassidy" (1959), both containing autobiographical elements, filled with the frustration, confusion, and passive resistance of faithless young people. In summary, Kerouac's works may lack artistic depth, but they offer unique insights into social reality. His later years were unfortunate, leading to his death due to excessive alcohol consumption.
