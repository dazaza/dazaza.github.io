International Fund for Animal Welfare: "Sitting Mountain Carver" returns to the mountains

by ditan360 on 2010-06-29 12:03:47

Exclusive to Low Carbon Network. Today, the Beijing Raptor Rescue Center of the International Fund for Animal Welfare (IFAW Beijing Raptor Rescue Center, abbreviated as IFAW BRRC) released two vultures in Dazhuangke Township, Yanqing County. One vulture was successfully released into the wild. The other vulture, which had been illegally kept by humans for a long time, still could not adapt to the wild environment and was taken back to the center by the staff for continued rehabilitation treatment. The vulture, commonly known as Zuo Shan Diao, is a second-level protected animal in China and also the largest bird of prey that can be seen in the Beijing area.

On January 17, 2010, Mr. Deng from Longwan Tun in Shunyi District found a large bird at the village entrance. According to Mr. Deng's recollection, the state of this large bird was very poor at the time, and it couldn't fly. Therefore, he took the bird home, fed it some meat, and contacted the raptor center for treatment. This vulture was extremely weak, with symptoms such as diarrhea and loss of appetite. After a veterinarian examination, it was discovered that due to bacterial infection, most of its toe tips and claws had fallen off. The center devised a detailed recovery plan for it. After five months of careful treatment, the current condition of this vulture is good, its body is strong, and it can return to nature.

On March 6 of the same year, the IFAW Beijing Raptor Rescue Center received another vulture from the Fengtai Forest Public Security Bureau. Compared to the vulture received on January 17, its physical condition was much better, with only some external injuries on the wrist joint. However, its behavior was abnormal, causing concern among the raptor rehabilitators: this vulture was "friendly" and "close" to humans, but exhibited more aggressive behavior towards its own kind. Zhang Guanghong from the Forest Public Security stated that this vulture had been illegally kept by humans for a long period.

Raptor rehabilitator Shang Yugang from IFAW BRRC said: "Birds of prey with behavioral problems will encounter many issues and harm in the wild. For example: they will seek food from humans rather than hunting themselves, thereby possibly being indirectly harmed by humans; during the breeding season, there might be mating behaviors towards what it perceives as its own kind, etc. Regarding the behavioral problems of this vulture, we usually reduce contact with it and place it with two other healthy vultures in the largest outdoor cage at the center. Some of its inappropriate behaviors have gradually been corrected, and its flight and physical conditions are good. Today, we hope to release them in the same habitat so it can continue learning survival skills in the wild from the other healthy vulture. It's regrettable that birds of prey, which should freely soar in the blue sky, are so unfamiliar with nature. The best course of action is to take it back to the center, allowing it to learn alongside another vulture undergoing rehabilitation at the center, while strengthening its flight training. We hope it can return to nature as soon as possible!"

The Beijing area is the habitat of many birds of prey and an important stopover on their migration route. However, due to the reduction in natural habitats and the acceleration of urbanization, many habitats where birds of prey depend on have disappeared, leading to insufficient food supply for them. If they encounter bad weather, their migration will face greater challenges, possibly resulting in malnutrition, weakness, and dehydration, making them unable to continue flying.

Hua Ning, Director of IFAW's China Program, said: "We are glad that many citizens can contact the center in the first instance when encountering injured or sick birds of prey for rescue. However, some people illegally keep and 'pamper' birds of prey, seriously harming their bodies while depriving them of the chance to return to nature." According to statistics recently done by IFAW BRRC, 29% of the birds of prey treated at the center were caused by illegal human feeding.

"When the vulture spreads its huge wings and rushes toward the blue sky, I feel like I'm soaring along with it. I hope everyone can protect animals. Do not keep, sell, or consume them!" said volunteer Liu Bin who participated in the release.

Birds of prey are at the top of the food chain and occupy an important position in the ecosystem. They play an irreplaceable role in controlling the population of rodents, maintaining environmental health, and ecological balance. Since its establishment in 2001, IFAW BRRC has already received more than 2,900 birds of prey. More than 60% of these birds of prey have successfully returned to nature.

About the International Fund for Animal Welfare

As one of the most influential animal welfare organizations globally, the International Fund for Animal Welfare (IFAW) has offices in 16 countries and operates animal protection projects in over 40 countries. Headquartered in the United States, IFAW improves animal welfare by reducing commercial exploitation of animals, protecting wildlife habitats, and rescuing animals in distress. IFAW protects both wild and companion animals through hands-on rescue operations and collaboration with governments. IFAW mobilizes public awareness through education and advocacy campaigns to prevent animal abuse and promotes policies that benefit both humans and animals harmoniously.

About the International Fund for Animal Welfare Beijing Raptor Rescue Center

The International Fund for Animal Welfare Beijing Raptor Rescue Center (International Fund for Animal Welfare Beijing Raptor Rescue Center - www.brrc.org.cn, abbreviated as IFAW BRRC) is located within the campus of Beijing Normal University, covering more than 1,200 square meters, integrating multiple functions such as raptor rescue, treatment, rehabilitation, management, and education. IFAW BRRC is one of the many animal protection projects of the International Fund for Animal Welfare (IFAW - www.ifaw.org.cn, abbreviated as IFAW) in China. Established in December 2001, IFAW BRRC is a non-profit wildlife rescue organization co-founded by IFAW, Beijing Normal University, and the Beijing Wildlife Nature Reserve Management Station, designated as the "Specialized Raptor Rescue Center" by the Beijing Forestry Bureau.

IFAW promotes and enhances the welfare of wildlife and companion animals worldwide by protecting wildlife habitats, reducing commercial exploitation and utilization of wildlife, and providing assistance to animals in distress. With the increasing awareness of wildlife protection among the Chinese public, more and more people are beginning to participate in wildlife rescue actions. To help wildlife conservation law enforcement agencies solve the proper treatment, rehabilitation, and release back to the wild of rescued animals, and to demonstrate the concept of animal welfare through practical work, IFAW BRRC combines international advanced raptor rescue techniques and experiences with scientific professional rescue methods. It provides treatment, care, and rehabilitation training for injured, sick, lost, and confiscated birds of prey during law enforcement processes in Beijing and surrounding areas, and promptly releases recovered birds of prey in suitable wild habitats.

The mission of IFAW BRRC is to introduce internationally recognized rescue concepts and standards, mature experiences, and advanced technologies to China's wildlife rescue work, which is still in its infancy. By combining successful foreign experiences with China's national conditions, IFAW BRRC explores a rescue model that helps improve the recovery rate of treated birds of prey, assists China's wild bird rescue efforts to align with international standards, and accumulates valuable experience for the scientific rescue of China's wildlife. As of October 2009, nearly three thousand birds of prey have been received and treated by IFAW BRRC, with more than half successfully returning to nature.
