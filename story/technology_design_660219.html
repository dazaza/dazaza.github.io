Guy bikes from Australia to Zhejiang for a graduation trip

by mingbb047 on 2012-02-07 14:57:41

Zhejiang's new school choice control rules will announce the information of admitted students and their parents. Recent photos of a young man. The world he recognized by bicycle, how is it different from what he read in books?

He Luojie, a 24-year-old guy, gave himself a special graduation gift - riding back from Australia to Zhejiang. He Luojie, 24 years old, abandoned the opportunity to continue his studies in Australia, took his tuition fees, and started cycling around Australia.

During the ride, he decided: ride home! From Australia all the way through Indonesia, Singapore, Malaysia, Thailand, Cambodia, Vietnam, Laos, finally to China, Guangzhou, and then to his home in Jiaxing, Zhejiang. This was like a graduation trip, but more like a prelude to life.

"Riding a bike always allows you to see the best and worst sides." Just as the cyclist in the movie "Pre-Ludes" said, He Luojie saw the most prosperous and poorest, met the most beautiful and the ugliest on the road. His worldview was gradually enriched. He wrote diaries, blogs, microblogs... striving to record everything he saw and felt along the way.

Reported by Guo Jing, pictures provided by the interviewee.

Hello, my name is He Luojie.

"It was this journey that made me fill an entire notebook in nine months."

Hello, my name is He Luojie. I was born in 1988 and am currently on a bicycle ride from Australia back to Jiaxing, Zhejiang.

I was born in Zhujia Mountain Lake Town, moved to Jiaxing with my parents at the age of four. My father is from Henggeng Village, Zhujia Mountain Lake Town, and my mother is from Luojia Bridge Village, Fengqiao Town, Zhujia.

My online name is "Shaojia Ren," and there's a legend online that I'm a "Shaojia hybrid," but actually, my initial introduction on Douban was a joke: "a hybrid of Zhejiang Zhuji and Jiaxing."

My high school academic performance was extremely poor, and I entered Shanghai Bond Vocational and Technical College for a logistics major. Fortunately, this major is jointly run with Tasmanian Polytechnic in Hobart, the southernmost city of Tasmania, Australia. After completing two years in Shanghai, on September 28, 2009, I went to Hobart to continue studying for the next one and a half years.

After graduating from Tasmanian Polytechnic with an advanced diploma (equivalent to a domestic associate degree), I could have transferred to the University of Tasmania to study for another two years to obtain a bachelor's degree like other classmates.

However, after living in Australia for over a year, I realized that spending another 40,000 Australian dollars for this degree was meaningless. In fact, most international students don't interact much with locals when they go abroad; they stay within a small circle... I thought it would be better to spend a fraction of that money to cycle across Australia and see what the real Australia is like.

Looking back now, this was the toughest but also the rightest decision I've ever made.

On December 29, 2011, while writing in my diary, I suddenly realized that I had filled up an entire notebook. During my schooling days, I never finished using an eraser or a pen refill, but this journey made me finish a whole notebook in just nine months.

The toughest decision

Heavy rain on Australia's east coast

"In the process of convincing my parents, I initially used many idealistic words..."

After several nights of psychological struggle and weighing pros and cons, I chose neither to switch majors nor to pursue further education but to drop out (actually not really dropping out since I did get a lower-level diploma. But saying 'dropping out' sounds cooler, just like Bill Gates and Mark Zuckerberg).

For the first time, I felt so close to an unreachable dream. In the process of convincing my parents, I initially used many idealistic words, which didn't work because in their worldview, all efforts are merely for survival, so I had no right to demand them to protect my "youthful" ideals. So I told them I would pay attention to safety on the road, learn knowledge, and see if I could find business opportunities or expand my network.

A famous quote whose source I forgot says, before doing what you like, you must do countless things you don't like.

I'm very lucky to have various conditions supporting me to do something I thoroughly enjoy at such a young age. As for the significance or purpose of this matter, I think the best assumption is that it has no meaning or purpose. There's a cliché saying, why climb a mountain? Because the mountain is there. Why go on the road? Because the road is there.

Heavy rain on Australia's east coast

"Seconds ago, I had never had this idea..."

This was the 12th day of my ride from Melbourne, following Australia's east coast to Sydney.

I had checked the Australian Bureau of Meteorology website, and in this month, in Tasmania or Victoria, it rains on average 10 to 13 days a month, meaning I had a high probability of getting soaked.

After winning the weather bet for 11 consecutive days, it rained heavily today. After being drenched for two hours with Akiko Fujita, the Japanese female rider I rode with yesterday, we arrived at a town called Nowa Nowa.

It was dim, and in the heavy rain, we saw a brightly lit coffee shop where an old woman was making cakes, a little girl was wiping tables, and the firewood in the stove beside the wall was burning vigorously, like a dream.

Behind the coffee shop was a caravan park with two wooden houses. The old woman said these were dormitories rented out to students on excursions, and today they could provide us beds for 20 Australian dollars each.

This was a very cheap price because it included a standalone wooden house, fully equipped public kitchen, and hot water showers.

Even though it was already late and I was very hungry, watching the spaghetti slowly boil made me feel content and patient. After all, if it weren't for this coincidental encounter, perhaps we'd have been forced to camp.

It was also during this night that I lay in bed, and suddenly, the idea struck me to ride a bicycle across the Australian continent, then go to Southeast Asia, and finally ride back to my hometown of Jiaxing, Zhejiang.

In the previous twenty-some seconds, I had never had this idea, including when I first started my bike tour. My original plan was to ride to Brisbane, the capital of Queensland, sell the bike, and take a boat or plane to South America, then return to China.

But now it suddenly changed, maybe overcoming setbacks gave me more confidence: isn't it just riding a bike for a year or so? It's nothing big.
