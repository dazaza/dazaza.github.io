How Samsung Electronics beats Apple in China: localization of products

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 17:54:57

According to a foreign media analysis article published on July 26, Beijing time, Apple is unable to shake Samsung Electronics' dominant advantage in the Chinese market. The reason lies in the fact that Samsung Electronics entered the Chinese market earlier, has a better understanding of the Chinese market, and launched more localized products. Below is an abstract of the article:

Apple CEO Tim Cook believes that "over time," the Chinese market will bring huge opportunities for this pioneering company. However, it seems that time is on the side of Apple's competitor Samsung Electronics, as Samsung Electronics has a much higher penetration rate and influence than Apple in the country with the largest population in the world.

Apple said this week that its revenue in the Greater China region fell 43% quarter-over-quarter to $4.65 billion. This figure also fell 14% year-over-year. The decline in Apple's revenue in the Greater China region was mainly affected by a sharp drop in revenue in Hong Kong. Cook pointed out in the analyst call that "it is unclear what caused the sharp decline in revenue in the Greater China region."

It is unclear what strategy Apple has taken against Samsung Electronics - let alone numerous small Chinese competitors.

Today, in this battle acknowledged by both sides as the most important market of the 21st century, Samsung Electronics is brutally beating its American rival. Market research firm IDC estimates that China's smartphone market will reach $80 billion this year and is expected to increase to $117 billion by 2017. Samsung Electronics currently holds a 19% share of this market. Compared to Apple, Samsung Electronics has a 10 percentage point lead in the Chinese market. Currently, Apple has dropped to fifth place in China's smartphone market.

Cook said that over the next two years, Apple plans to double the number of retail stores in the Greater China region. Currently, Apple has eight flagship stores in mainland China and three retail stores in Hong Kong. However, he stated that "Apple will make very cautious investments in distribution because he hopes to complete this work at a high quality level."

Samsung Electronics entered the Chinese market earlier, and currently has three times as many retail stores as Apple, being more aggressive in attracting consumers and building partnerships with operators. Former Samsung executives, market analysts, and industry insiders all said that over time, Samsung Electronics' position in the Chinese market will become even stronger, which is enough to withstand the impact from Chinese competitors such as Lenovo Group, Huawei, and ZTE Corporation. As of now, Apple has not commented on this report.

Multiple models

Samsung Electronics' history and corporate culture differ greatly from Apple, the innovative company founded by Steve Jobs and Steve Wozniak in Silicon Valley in 1976. Lee Byung-chull founded Samsung in 1938 when it was still just a manufacturer of noodles and candy. Over the following decades, through military-style iron discipline management, this family-controlled enterprise has grown into an industrial giant controlling South Korea's economy.

In stark contrast to Samsung Electronics, Apple is like a snapshot of Californians, which is the image this company obsesses over. This image has been transplanted to the Chinese market—Apple’s retail stores are routinely packaged—but this is not enough to overcome the more deeply rooted Samsung Electronics.

The electronics market in Shenzhen can explain part of the reason. Samsung Electronics' Galaxy series products and iPhones are placed side by side, with sellers aggressively recommending these products to consumers. Due to the large number of models, Samsung Electronics' smartphones sell at least four times as many units as iPhones.

Apple releases only one smartphone model each year, mainly targeting the high-end smartphone market; Samsung Electronics introduces multiple products with different specifications and prices to the Chinese market each year. Market analysts said that Samsung Electronics' products launched for the Chinese market come pre-installed with features specifically tailored for the local market, such as domestic picture original community POCO.CN or dual SIM card slots.

Technology consulting company RedTech Advisors General Manager Michael Clendenin said, "Chinese people like functions. They want their phones to have 50 different kinds of features, maybe they will never use these features. Apple doesn't play such tricks. But unfortunately, if you want to enter the Chinese mainstream market and want to gain a large market share, you must offer a Swiss Army knife-like phone."

Setting the pace

Market analysts believe that Samsung Electronics' further strengthening dominance in the Chinese market is also the key reason why Apple plans to launch a low-cost iPhone and a large-screen iPhone for the global market. A senior executive of Samsung Electronics said, "We definitely think we've set the pace in the Chinese market. Apple is forced to respond to our approach."

Samsung Electronics is more fearless, not only launching low-cost smartphones but also directly attacking Apple in the high-end smartphone market. Samsung Electronics Co-CEO J.K. Shin said, "We invest a lot of energy into the high-end smartphone market in China."

Samsung Electronics collaborated with China Telecom to launch a flip luxury smartphone for the Chinese market. This phone, priced at about RMB 12,000 (approximately USD 2,000), is endorsed by action star Jackie Chan. Samsung Electronics Mobile Business Vice President Lee Young-hee said, "There are many important VIPs in China, and we launched a product endorsed by Jackie Chan for them. This helps target niche consumer groups and build brand quality."

Although Samsung Electronics won't sell millions of such smartphones, this move expresses to operators Samsung Electronics' willingness to localize. IDC analyst TZ Wong based in Singapore said, "The key reason is that Samsung Electronics consistently adapts to the Chinese market. Chinese consumers all know Samsung Electronics is a Korean brand. But the message Samsung Electronics conveys has always been a localized face."

Retail presence

Samsung Electronics opened its first office in China in 1985. At that time, no one would have anticipated that Apple and Samsung Electronics would become the two giants of the technology industry. Like other Korean conglomerates, Samsung Electronics was one of the earliest Korean companies to enter the Chinese market, using China as a production base for its electronic products sold globally.

Unlike Samsung Electronics, Apple only started expanding significantly in the Chinese market recently. Entering the market early gave Samsung unparalleled advantages and allowed the company to quickly adapt to the rapidly changing market situation. Nomura Securities analyst Choi Chang-hee, who wrote about Samsung Electronics' development history in China, pointed out that by the mid-1990s, with the booming Chinese economy, Samsung Electronics made a strategic decision not only to view the Chinese market as a production base but also as a high-priced electronics market.

This strategic transformation means that Samsung Electronics has a much greater retail presence in the Chinese market than Apple. Apart from selling products through the retail outlets of the three major telecom operators, Samsung Electronics has a strong retail presence through alliances with Gome Electrical Appliances and Suning Electrical Appliances. Meanwhile, Samsung Electronics also has its own "experience stores" and nationwide small retailers in China.

Apple and Samsung Electronics have the same channels in the Chinese market, but entering the market later means that this company's presence is much smaller. For example, Samsung Electronics has more than 200 official dealers and retailers in Guangdong Province, while Apple has only 95.

Unlike Samsung Electronics' close relationship with Chinese operators, Apple has yet to reach a cooperation agreement with China Mobile. For years, the two companies have failed to reach a consensus on revenue sharing, which has prevented Apple's products from being used by hundreds of millions of China Mobile users.

Market research company IDC said that Samsung Electronics currently dominates the Chinese low-end and high-end smartphone markets. The average monthly wage of most Chinese consumers is only $640, and many users looking to upgrade from feature phones to smartphones cannot afford the high price of Apple phones.

By targeting segmented markets with different products, Samsung Electronics can build close relationships with consumers. Reports show that as consumers' incomes gradually increase, they will also choose to buy Samsung Electronics' higher-priced products. Competing on both the low-end and high-end markets has helped Samsung Electronics fend off attacks from local manufacturers such as Lenovo Group and Huawei.

Market analysts said that another tough battle for Samsung Electronics in the Chinese market may be Apple's launch of a low-cost iPhone.
