Premature ejaculation is one of the most common sexual dysfunctions in men under the age of 30. Most patients with this condition tend to be introverted and think pessimistically, easily experiencing operational anxiety (e.g., "Am I not good enough?") and anticipatory anxiety (e.g., "Will my performance be better than last time?") during sexual activity. Over time, this can form a conditioned reflex, which is often described as "once bitten by a snake, afraid of ropes for ten years."

Therefore, when treating premature ejaculation, building self-confidence is very important. In clinical practice, experts from the Urology Diagnosis and Treatment Center of Zhengzhou Huashan Hospital emphasize the use of behavioral therapy to reduce the sensitivity of the central nervous system and the penis, helping patients experience and enjoy sexual pleasure, overcome their fear of sexual behavior, and re-establish natural sexual responses. Common methods include:

1. "Stop-Start" training and penile squeeze technique: First, during sexual intercourse, the insertion and movement of the penis should be done slowly, and both partners can find topics unrelated to sex to distract the man's attention. When the man feels too much sexual pleasure and has an urge to ejaculate, stop the sexual activity immediately until the sensation subsides, then start again. This can also be called the start-stop method.

The penile squeeze technique requires the woman to stimulate the man's penis to increase its sensory tolerance. The specific method is: during sexual intercourse, the man lies on his back and focuses his attention on the penis; the woman sits between the man's legs and gently touches the penis. When the man feels excited and about to ejaculate, he can signal the woman to stop and place her thumb on the underside of the glans penis in the coronal groove, with the other four fingers on the dorsal side of the penis, squeezing tightly for about 15-20 seconds. This is usually repeated three times, and the fourth time ejaculation is allowed. Combining "stop-start" training with squeezing helps inhibit the ejaculation reflex. The latter can also be used during intercourse, where the woman, positioned on top, quickly disengages and performs the squeeze when the man feels highly excited and about to ejaculate.

2. Sensate focus training is an improvement on "stop-start" training, with the premise of gradually increasing the ability to distinguish sensations on the penis. It is suitable for premature ejaculation caused by guilt, uneasiness (such as concerns about past masturbation), or loss of confidence in sexual activity. It is divided into two stages: non-genital sensate focus training and genital sensate focus training. During the non-genital sensate focus training stage, both partners, free from interruption, focus on caressing parts of each other’s bodies other than the genitals, but without engaging in actual sexual intercourse. The focus of this stage is to experience the pleasure brought by caressing, eliminating fear, uneasiness, and repression associated with sexual behavior, allowing both partners to build confidence and intimacy, naturally reaching sexual arousal. This process usually takes one to two weeks.

In the genital sensate focus training stage, the area of affection expands to the genitals. The woman can touch her partner's scrotum and penis, stopping promptly if the man experiences high sexual pleasure and has an urge to ejaculate. This process also requires one to two weeks, and after the effect is consolidated, attempts at actual sexual intercourse can be made.
