Elop was accused of being a Microsoft spy: sticking to WP drove Nokia into a dead end

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 14:44:35

Microsoft will acquire Nokia's Devices & Services division for $7.17 billion, accelerating the development of the Windows ecosystem. The transaction is expected to be completed in the first quarter of 2014. Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer and Nokia President and CEO Stephen Elop have issued a public letter stating that both Nokia and Microsoft will fully commit to this new chapter.

The industry was shocked but also felt it was expected. While people are happy for Microsoft, they feel sad for Nokia, the former giant in the mobile phone industry. Criticism of Elop has resurfaced again; previously, Elop had been accused multiple times of being a Microsoft spy.

Before joining Nokia, Elop was the president of Microsoft's business division, overseeing information workers, Microsoft enterprise solutions, and unified communications group. He was responsible for developing the Microsoft Office system (programs, servers, and software-based services), Microsoft Dynamics (enterprise management software for small and medium-sized businesses, large organizations, and multinational enterprises), and Microsoft Unified Communications (a completely software-based communication tool provided for enterprises).

In the second month after Elop became Nokia's CEO, he announced a global layoff of 5,000 employees and reduced the number of people in the Symbian department, which became Nokia's largest layoff in ten years. Subsequently, Elop announced internal reforms for Nokia and gave it a brand-new positioning. This move directly accelerated the decline of the Symbian system and led to the faster rise of Android.

While streamlining the Symbian department, another thing Elop did was to cut the Nokia Android phone project planned by his predecessor, Nokia CEO Olli-Pekka Kallasvuo, and refused Google’s offer for Android cooperation. In February 2011, Elop canceled the collaboration with Intel on MeeGo development and announced a strategic partnership between Nokia and Microsoft, adopting the Windows Phone 7 operating system.

Elop's three initial actions pushed Nokia into an even more irreversible situation, especially after Microsoft announced that Windows Phone 8 would not be compatible with phones running Windows Phone 7.x, leaving Nokia in a very difficult position, greatly affecting its sales. With no other options, Nokia could only once again hope for the newly released Windows Phone 8.

The cooperation between Nokia and Microsoft was filled with humiliation and awkwardness. In the invitation to the previous Microsoft Windows Phone 8 launch event, the poster featured a phone with HTC's logo, showing everyone the fact that Nokia, their deep strategic partner, was becoming increasingly unimportant in Microsoft's eyes.

Regarding the decision not to adopt the Android platform at the beginning, Elop insisted he didn't regret it. Elop believed that choosing Windows Phone would help increase negotiation leverage with carriers: "We are not Apple, nor Samsung using Android; we are the third choice. Once operators want to exert pressure on handset manufacturers and need more choices, they will need the third option. So, strategically speaking, we still have opportunities."

However, the reality is that Nokia's debt rating has long been downgraded to junk status, and rumors about its possible acquisition by Microsoft have been rampant. Reviewing the calendar, Nokia and Microsoft's cooperation has exceeded 18 months. In the third-quarter financial report of 2012, Nokia's net loss was still as high as 969 million euros, with net revenue declining by 19% year-over-year. Criticism of Elop in the industry has grown stronger, with the most common demand being his resignation.

Nokia's former executive Tomi Ahonen even openly questioned Nokia's reform strategy, fiercely criticizing current CEO Elop, claiming that Elop's reforms ruined Nokia. Former Nokia manager and mobile industry analyst Tomi Ahonen attributed Nokia's further decline to the "Elop Effect" in a blog post, calling Elop "the worst CEO in all industries."

Even at the previous Global Mobile Conference, someone bluntly questioned whether Elop was a Trojan horse planted by Microsoft in Nokia. Although everyone could guess Elop's answer, this question showed many people's dissatisfaction and confusion with the cooperation between Nokia and Microsoft. Elop expressed that he was very sensitive to this question and felt embarrassed.

Surprisingly, Elop enjoys a high reputation within Microsoft, and there is even a view that after Nokia returns to Microsoft, Elop might become a strong contender for the position of Microsoft CEO after Ballmer retires.
