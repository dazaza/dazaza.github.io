Bitcoin Craze: Payment is Like Sending a Tweet, Could be Used to Buy Houses in the Future

by geekzhang on 2013-05-06 12:08:30

In 2011, people shopping for computers would notice a certain advertising slogan on some graphics cards: "Mining tool." At the time, many thought it was just jargon from an emerging online game.

Later, more and more people joined this "mining" game, running their computers day and night. Some even set up server rooms to increase the numbers in their alphanumeric accounts. People learned that these numbers were called bitcoins, but most ordinary people still considered it an irrational behavior of gaming enthusiasts.

By April 2013, everything had changed. On April 10th, the global trading price of one bitcoin surpassed $200, and on April 11th, it soared to a historical high of $264. Just two months prior, it was only slightly over $20. Amidst the current economic downturn, all other investment products paled in comparison to Bitcoin.

Despite a rapid drop in value within hours, the wealth potential behind Bitcoin attracted significant attention.

For the general public in China, Bitcoin gained attention due to the Ya'an earthquake.

The next day after the Ya'an earthquake, He Qingchuan, Director of Development at the One Foundation, posted on Weibo: "One Foundation's Bitcoin donation for the Ya'an earthquake has received an intention to donate 50Btc (approximately RMB 40,000 based on today's exchange rate). We will entrust the Chinese Bitcoin Community to sell the donated Bitcoins. We welcome geeks and hackers to donate Bitcoins to the One Foundation. This is the first NGO in China to accept Bitcoin donations, making Bitcoin a donation item for the first time in China."

People were mostly puzzled: What exactly is Bitcoin? Why is it so crazy?

Paying with Bitcoin is like sending a Weibo post

Many think Bitcoin is crazy, but few know what kind of currency it is, how it’s generated, how it’s used, or why it’s so popular. After research, we found that Bitcoin is a mathematically driven innovation.

Bitcoin operates without third-party supervision

Wu Wangbuhuan explained to reporters that in real life, face-to-face cash transactions generally don’t require third-party intervention or supervision because cash is hard to counterfeit. Once cash changes hands, it can't be reused by the same person. However, in online payments, cash is converted into digital form, creating two risks: digital information can be easily tampered with and duplicated, potentially leading to double spending. Therefore, traditional online payment processes require third-party institutions to ensure security and reliability.

In Bitcoin's system, creator Satoshi Nakamoto (whose true identity remains unknown) established an electronic payment system based on cryptographic principles rather than trust. This allows any two parties who agree to transact directly without the need for a third-party intermediary.

Unique logic prevents counterfeit money and double spending

Wu Wangbuhuan introduced that this system serves two purposes: preventing counterfeit money and preventing the same currency from being spent multiple times by the same person.

To prevent counterfeit money, Nakamoto proposed retaining all circulation information for every coin. During transactions, the source of each coin is traced back to its creation. Each transaction adds a circulation record broadcasted across a peer-to-peer network, ensuring all nodes (computers maintaining the network) store all circulation data. This way, any node can easily detect counterfeit coins before a transaction, preventing them from circulating.

To prevent double spending, Nakamoto adopted the proof-of-work method. Since each transaction must be broadcasted, repeated spending means broadcasting the same Bitcoin multiple times. Other network nodes bundle the received transactions into a data block A for validation and accounting. The method involves finding a specific character string based on the content of data block A. If successful, the node broadcasts the information across the entire network. If another node successfully validates and accounts for the next data block B based on data block A, it attaches B to A, forming a data block chain. For the same Bitcoin involved in multiple transactions, the longest chain determines which transaction is valid, discarding others. This ensures a Bitcoin can only be spent once by one person.

Donations using Bitcoin are transparent

Additionally, through the proof-of-work method, Bitcoin essentially eliminates the possibility of unauthorized tampering with historical transaction records. This is because altering records would require computational power exceeding the sum of all other network nodes. Wu Wangbuhuan said that storing all transaction records across all nodes and validating transactions through time-consuming calculations create a secure, reliable decentralized payment system. He vividly explained that Bitcoin transfers the workload of centralized regulation to a vast network where all nodes participate in oversight.

This characteristic made Bitcoin relevant during the recent Ya'an earthquake donations. Within two hours of the earthquake on April 20th, the largest Bitcoin community Bitcointalk initiated a donation campaign using Bitcoin. They established a seemingly random Bitcoin wallet address: 15EhGBC8JBBVDKQ9yp1AqCb1xnzQLk6QHX. On the blockchain website, everyone could see each Bitcoin donation to this wallet, from 0.0001 Bitcoin to 5 Bitcoin, clearly visible. Science fiction writer Chang Jia explained the biggest advantage of Bitcoin donations: if Bitcoin is used as a donation address, every donation is publicly transparent and shows its destination, preventing misuse by others. Additionally, there is no minimum limit for donations, and banks do not charge transfer fees.

In the next four years, 25 Bitcoins will be generated every 10 minutes

Since every Bitcoin transaction needs verification, to encourage nodes to fully commit to verifying and maintaining system operation, Nakamoto proposed an incentive mechanism: the first transaction in each data block is specially processed, generating several new electronic currencies owned by the data block creator (the first person to successfully account for all transactions in the data block). This process is called Mining, the origin of the Chinese term "mining."

Wu Wangbuhuan pointed out that submitting this record requires competition, depending on whose computer has faster computing power.

A Mr. He participating in mining told reporters that in the Bitcoin world, approximately every ten minutes, a data block is recorded on a public ledger containing verified global transactions within that period. The person who secures the bookkeeping right (the first person to successfully calculate a suitable string based on the data block information) is allowed to add an amount to their account, currently 25 Bitcoins, halving every four years. As transactions increase, the data volume becomes enormous, making it almost impossible for individual computers to secure the bookkeeping right. Thus, the mainstream approach now is for many computers to form a mining pool (Mining Pool). If the pool secures the bookkeeping right, after obtaining 25 Bitcoins, they distribute them according to each computer's contribution.

Wallet addresses have public and private keys

Many notice that Bitcoin wallet addresses are strings of numbers and letters. When using Bitcoin, everyone has one or several Bitcoin wallet addresses for paying and receiving money. Do you need to remember this address? What happens if it repeats?

Bitcoin expert Shui Kongkong clarified this doubt using mathematical knowledge. In general understanding, encryption and decryption use a single password, such as compressing a file with a password that can be unlocked by entering the same password. Until the 1970s, cryptography remained at this level, known as symmetric encryption algorithms. In 1976, mathematicians W. Diffie and M. Hellman proposed a new cryptographic idea: encryption and decryption use different passwords. Through mathematical methods, a pair of keys A and B are generated. Data encrypted with A must be decrypted with B, and vice versa. Based on A, B can be easily calculated, but not the reverse. A is called the private key, and B is the public key. As their names suggest, A is confidential, while B is public.

He said that when asymmetric encryption is applied to Bitcoin, the wallet address becomes the public key. Since public and private keys are generated using special algorithms, they cannot be manually set and appear irregular. According to Wikipedia, possible Bitcoin addresses exceed 2^160. To put it simply, there are about 2^63 grains of sand worldwide. If each grain contained an Earth, the total number of Bitcoin addresses would far exceed the number of all sand particles on all these Earths.

Shui Kongkong said the private key is usually stored in the wallet file, also merely a longer string than the address. You can copy or print it onto paper and keep it securely in a safe. That string carries your entire Bitcoin wealth.

He pointed out a more interesting creation of Bitcoin: brain wallets. On some websites generating online wallets, a phrase can generate a pair of public and private keys. By memorizing this phrase, you can regenerate the private key and access your Bitcoin from anywhere with internet connection.

Chang Jia reported that some tech enthusiasts designed offline hardware wallets, equivalent to encrypted USB drives. Transactions can only be completed when the hardware wallet is connected to a computer. Private key storage and digital signatures occur within the hardware wallet, preventing malicious software from redirecting transactions or recording keyboard inputs.

Regarding Bitcoin payment, Shui Kongkong said: Bitcoin payment is like posting on Weibo; the private key is the Weibo password, and the username is the public key. When A pays B, A posts on Weibo: "I pay B 1 Bitcoin," and miners verify whether A has sufficient funds. If verified, this post is bundled with other verified posts within ten minutes and forwarded again. When enough people forward it (usually six is sufficient), the payment is considered successful.

Transactions and mining occur 24/7

Due to Bitcoin's characteristics and media coverage of its wealth benefits, Bitcoin exhibits its wild side. Faced with profits and temptations, various types of people populate the Bitcoin world, engaging in stories of profit-seeking through price fluctuations or earning Bitcoin by running computations on computers.

Total supply capped at 21 million

On January 3, 2009, the Bitcoin network was born, and Satoshi Nakamoto released the first open-source Bitcoin client. On the same day, the first Bitcoin blockchain and the first 50 Bitcoins were created. According to algorithm design, the number of Bitcoins generated halves every four years, now reduced to 25. Those familiar with mathematics can calculate that by 2140, a total of 21 million Bitcoins will be issued, and the total supply will not increase.

Since every Bitcoin transaction is recorded, people can accurately track the whereabouts of each Bitcoin, allowing exact queries of the current number of issued Bitcoins on websites. On the Blockchain website, reporters saw that by last Sunday (April 28), a total of 11,072,300 Bitcoins had been issued.

This virtual currency, born under the framework of mathematical concepts with a fixed total supply, contrasts sharply with paper money over-issuance causing inflation. Basic economics suggests that if Bitcoin can exist long-term, it will appreciate over time. This is one important reason for Bitcoin's current madness.

Reports of massive Bitcoin hoarding abroad continue to stimulate public nerves. It was reported that the Winklevoss twins, Zuckerberg's archrivals, started stockpiling Bitcoin last summer. They are one of the largest single investors in Bitcoin, claiming to own 1% of all Bitcoins. One of the brothers views Bitcoin as follows: "We have decided to place our money and faith in a mathematical framework that is free from politics and human error."

Another report indicates that a Canadian Bitcoin supporter sold his property and purchased only Bitcoin.

24-hour trading activity

Currently, Mt.Gox is the largest trading platform, handling trading demands from around the globe. At the top of the website, there is an icon displaying real-time trading charts with five values for traders' reference: latest price, highest price, lowest price, trading volume, and weighted average price. There are over a dozen currency options including USD, EUR, JPY, and GBP. Here, Bitcoin can be exchanged for various currencies, and vice versa.

In today's globalized world, excitement from one continent can quickly spread to another. Domestic professional Bitcoin trading platforms have emerged rapidly. A website administrator told reporters that following extensive media coverage in April, trading volumes significantly increased, necessitating upgrades to handle demand. Currently, daily trading volumes reach 20,000 to 30,000 Bitcoins.

On domestic Bitcoin trading websites, prices are referenced against the US dollar and then converted to Renminbi based on the exchange rate. Similar to stock trading, there are buy and sell volumes, highest and lowest prices. Unlike stocks, Bitcoin trades 24/7, allowing participation anytime.

Downloading the Bitcoin client, generating a wallet address, and registering on the website allows participation in trading. If buying, similar to buying stocks, hang a buy order with the desired price. When the price aligns, the trade automatically completes, the administrator said. You can withdraw Bitcoin to your wallet or keep it on our site for sale at any time.

Prices can surge hundreds in a night

Xiaoliang is one of the luckier Bitcoin speculators. In early April, he bought a Bitcoin on a whim for about 400 yuan. The next morning, it was 500 yuan, exciting him. He thought, "If I bought more, with this rise, couldn't someone like me become rich overnight?" The rising Bitcoin price continuously stimulated his nerves, turning nightly chart watching and forum browsing into habits, vastly different from overtime work. Later, he gradually invested 12,000 yuan and, on a night when Bitcoin hit new highs, convinced himself to sell all his Bitcoin, earning around 30,000 yuan. However, he felt physically drained. Recalling the experience, he excitedly said, "This 30,000 yuan is very important to me."

Not everyone is as lucky as him. Lao Liu is one example. He bought Bitcoin at nearly 100 USD when it was particularly volatile. Recently, Bitcoin seemed to have entered a low point. When reporters found him, the trading price was 67 USD. With bloodshot eyes, he sighed, "This job isn't fit for humans—working 24/7, even fund managers aren't this tired!" However, Bitcoin recently showed clear recovery signs. Around 120 USD, he sold his Bitcoin, breaking even. "I need a good sleep!"

Buying 13 graphics cards at 6,000 yuan each

Compared to the huge risks of trading, many tech-savvy individuals chose the traditional method of mining. In Garage Cafe, Li Xiaolai, who owns the most Bitcoin domestically, recounted his early mining experience.

In early 2011, Li Xiaolai, originally a New Oriental teacher, first learned about Bitcoin through Google Reader. Subsequently, he thoroughly read Nakamoto's design paper, deeply attracted by the decentralized and anti-double-spending logical constructs. After analysis, Li Xiaolai sold all his stocks to purchase Bitcoin on Mt.Gox. Soon, Bitcoin rose from 1 USD to 6 USD, quickly climbing to 20 USD. Li Xiaolai realized that his selling speed couldn't keep up with Bitcoin's appreciation, so he turned to mining.

In July 2011, Bitcoin had risen to 25 USD. Li Xiaolai decided to sell three-quarters of his Bitcoin, using the profit to buy more graphics cards. At the time, capable graphics cards cost 6,000 yuan each, and most domestic sellers were out of stock. Eventually, Li Xiaolai went directly to the Shanghai headquarters of the brand. There was a small episode: he asked the agent, "How many of those graphics cards do you have? I'll take them all." After checking the warehouse, the agent replied, "13." He hesitated momentarily.

Ultimately, Li Xiaolai gathered 46 graphics cards, pairing two with the cheapest cases, CPUs, etc., forming 23 computers to mine non-stop. Problems arose immediately; the massive computational load required industrial electricity of over 20,000 volts. To meet the power requirements, Li Xiaolai chose a rural area between Beijing and Tianjin for mining. However, the first day of operation resulted in a power outage due to overload, and the issue was never fully resolved. Over five months, Li Xiaolai invested 600,000 yuan in mining, yielding fewer than 100 Bitcoin. Finally, he sold the equipment for less than one-fifth of its original value, ending the mining endeavor.

Graphics cards are basic configurations

Compared to Li Xiaolai's professional setup, Mr. He's configuration seemed amateurish, though he repeatedly emphasized that he only mined part-time. He said that currently, obtaining Bitcoin through personal computers is extremely difficult; a single machine might need to run for years to mine a Bitcoin. Joining a mining pool is recommended.

Six months ago, he joined a mining pool. The pool's role is to细分 data packets for various terminals, distributing them proportionally based on computational power to allocate Bitcoin rewards accordingly.

Mr. He said that although the algorithm for Bitcoin mining is complex, the actual operation is simple. Download the Bitcoin client, obtain a Bitcoin wallet, register on the mining pool, input the wallet address, install the mining software, select the data packet, and let the software run automatically. After the pool mines Bitcoin, it automatically distributes the Bitcoin to your wallet address based on each computer's computational power.

His current configuration includes two graphics cards: one integrated into the computer and another GPU card. The integrated card is used for movies and games, while the GPU card participates in mining pools. Watching movies and mining simultaneously doesn't affect either activity.
