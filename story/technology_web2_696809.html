Singing Techniques

The performance of a song requires expression in many aspects.

First, it is certainly the voice. This is a very professional knowledge, including breathing, vocal use, oral resonance, articulation, etc. In future articles, I will try to exchange with everyone what I know.

Secondly, it is emotion. This requires singers to have a strong sense of understanding of lyrics and melody. Many friends focus all their attention on the voice part when singing, while neglecting emotions, which will never sing a song well. The reason for overemphasizing the voice is that the singer's ability is limited, and during the singing process, they always think about where to pay attention to breathing, where to pay attention to inhaling, and prepare for high notes, etc., which are unavoidable. Therefore, if you want to achieve "a perfect blend of voice and emotion" in singing, practice is quite important. When your vocal exercises mature, your attention during singing will be on the emotion. There is a good saying: songs are not sung by the mouth, but by the heart. If the voice reaches the level of smooth and complete breath control, it will be easier to inject emotions into the song, making the performance more infectious.

Thirdly, it is perception. Here I emphasize again, musical perception is innate. You have as much as you have. It's hard to make up for it later. Pop songs need pop song perception, folk songs need folk song perception. For example, if you ask Yan Weiwén to sing "I Have Nothing," I guarantee it would make a bunch of people laugh! Different perceptions lead singers to handle songs differently. Some handle them plainly, some touchingly. These treatments are very subtle, such as a small inhalation, an emphasized heavy note, a long drag without inhalation, or a sudden ending... These can only be understood implicitly.

Fourthly, it is rhythm and pitch. Have you ever seen a child over three years old who could tap out embellished rhythms upon hearing a melody? Have you ever seen a child over three years old singing live while his father accompanied him on the accordion, changing the key at any time, yet he sang accurately? Have you ever seen an eight-year-old child humming the sound of "1" in any key like "E-flat" or "C-sharp" without hearing any sound beforehand, and then pressing the keyboard to verify it completely accurately? This is talent. Singing songs needs such a foundation, although they don't have to be so outstanding, but they must not be weak.

Fifthly, it is quality. This is the difference between amateur singers and professional singers. In fact, even comparing in terms of voice, emotion, perception, rhythm, and pitch, many amateur singers are not worse than professional singers. The gap lies in quality. Do you know your own posture and expression while singing? You may have a vague image, which is the image you imagine yourself while fully immersed in singing, and this image is likely to be the original singer's image of the song you are singing. But these are all your imaginations, and your imagined image is almost perfect. Do you know what others see? Have you ever filmed one of your formal performances? In fact, from the first glance or the first word when you go on stage, people can tell whether you are an amateur or a professional. The quality mentioned here also includes understanding of music theory, instruments, music history, and music common knowledge, etc. Of course, there are also many professional singers who are not comprehensive, but personally, I think that if you want to become a professional singer, you should adhere to these principles and deserve the word "professional."

Understanding Lyrics

Before learning a new song, how many times have you read the lyrics from beginning to end in your mind? And how many times have you pondered the thoughts of the lyricist and the realm he expresses? A song, whether it has lyrics first or music first, both authors carefully consider the realm and thoughts the other (lyricist or composer) wants to express. If it comes to you and you completely ignore their painstaking efforts and just sing according to your own feelings, I think it is inappropriate. Some individual singers like to express themselves according to their own understanding, and this way is not bad (and I like it too), but I would first understand the author's intention before developing further. Moreover, not every song allows you to express freely. For some songs, you cannot find a better way to express them, like the national anthem.

After deeply understanding the song, your emotional handling will definitely improve a lot. If your perceptiveness is strong, you will naturally emphasize a certain note, lightly pass over another note, and drag a passionate long note fully...

Talking and singing, besides melody, rhythm, and breath control, are there any other differences? Yes, that is the way of articulation. Please casually read "Let me love enough once" in the way you talk, then passionately sing it out... You will find that the mouth shape expands to the sides while singing, and it needs to be exaggerated. If you think it's about the same, then there is a problem with your articulation. Therefore, singing requires more exaggerated mouth shapes than speaking, so the articulation will be clearer, the breath more unobstructed, and more conducive to expressing emotions.

When singing Mandarin songs, accuracy is still the best. For example, the distinctions between "Z, C, S" and "an, ang" and "L, N" are easily confused. Hong Kong and Taiwan stars sometimes deliberately pronounce some words without rolling the tongue or with rolling, either because their tongues cannot keep up, or because they think it doesn't sound good, or they simply do not know the pronunciation. When we cover their songs, I do not have a clear stance; for imitation, not correcting is fine. However, if you want to sing your own style or compete, then corrections are necessary.

Regarding Cantonese songs, I advise you to learn well before singing, otherwise, it's easy to cause laughter. Like my previous experience singing Cantonese songs, I thought I was standard, but now I realize there was a gap. There are also subtle differences in Cantonese similar to those in Mandarin between "Z, C, S" and "an, ang", which cannot be learned well without serious study.

Breath Control

On this topic, I feel somewhat lost, because this is the hardest thing to express in words. Before I started singing, I practiced bel canto for two years, which gave me deep insights, and the breath foundation helped me a lot. People who haven't studied vocal music usually produce flat sounds using the normal speaking position (throat). We call it "white sound," lacking color and filtration. Below, I will describe the methods of bel canto practice.

The sound should originate from the lower abdomen, imagining the sound passing through the back spine, to the back of the head, to the root of the oral cavity, the entire sound should be vertical and backward. Try to find such a feeling: take a big bite of an apple, showing your upper teeth, and simultaneously bite down while emitting the "hmm" sound, feeling the sound at the back of the oral cavity and the upper part of the nasal cavity. This is one of the resonant points of bel canto. Next, sink the breath, expand the lower abdomen, slightly push the lower abdomen forward, and emit the sound at the previous position. Male voices press down the larynx, female voices focus more on the sound at the upper back of the head. You will find the sound much louder than before. Practicing against a wall regularly helps with vocalization, as leaning against the wall allows you to feel the posterior spine more easily and produces chest resonance with the wall, helping you find the chest resonance more easily. Start practicing with "mi～ma," gradually increasing the pitch, which helps practice high-pitched breathing; then practice from high to low, repeating this until one day you will find the right feeling. Bel canto emphasizes "transparency," often imagining your sound as vertical rather than flat; it also emphasizes "resonance," where the sound becomes rounder and fuller after passing through the chest or above. To experience resonance, you can close your mouth and emit the "hmm" sound, slightly adding a touch of sentiment, extending the "hmm" sound longer, feeling the vibration in your head and chest. If your breath control is good, this resonance will add much color to your voice. Listen carefully to some tall Americans talking, and you'll understand what chest resonance is (foreigners' chest structures are more prone to resonance).

Another point I want to mention is lung capacity. Singers need larger lung capacities, such as some long, non-breathing melodies, and you don't want to finish singing red-faced and gasping for air. Lung capacity must be maintained by regular physical exercise. Additionally, mastering certain techniques can compensate for insufficient lung capacity. First, you need to practice breath control. Good vocal techniques can effectively control the airflow through the throat. For example, when singing "...don't be afraid of your betrayal...", singers with unstable breath might burst out the "afraid" sound explosively, quickly exhausting the already insufficient air. If there are long non-breathing phrases afterward, it's surprising they don't suffocate. Singers with good breath control can regulate the flow of explosive sounds, avoiding popping sounds on the microphone. Therefore, I suggest placing a candle in front of your mouth while practicing, trying to keep the flame steady. This way, your breath will stay inside your body for a longer period. Think about whether you can persist longer by continuously exhaling or by holding your breath. Of course, this also requires the singer to relax, not to be nervous. Once nervous, the heart beats faster, making it harder to control the breath.

Here, I can only say so much. These require insight. However, I want to remind you that vocal exercises are the foundation of singing pop songs. Don't use vocal exercise methods directly to perform pop songs, as that would be overly artificial, like Yan Weiwén singing "I Have Nothing."

Regarding the use of voice in pop songs, my view is that as long as it fits the song's mood, shouting or breaking is not unacceptable. All these are absolutely prohibited in bel canto, so we need to take the strengths and make up for the weaknesses. We are not practicing bel canto to sing bel canto, but to sing pop songs well. It's like martial arts practicing horse stance; have you ever seen someone fighting while squatting in horse stance?

Some friends say such descriptions are hard to understand, asking me to name singers with transparent breath control, making it easier to follow and understand. This is also an effective method. Personally, I think female singers should listen more to "A Mei," whose breath control is naturally transparent; male singers should study "Man Wenjun," who truly sings with breath control. Also, Jacky Cheung has great breath control, but for teaching purposes, Man Wenjun is more suitable.

High Notes

This is the question friends like to ask most, because high notes are "cool," haha! Let me reiterate, good singing integrates many aspects, and one cannot say that the higher the high notes, the better the singing. Indeed, good high notes can enhance the climax of a song, but I advise friends not to only value high notes and ignore middle and low notes. Full-bodied middle and low notes are also very moving.

When singing high notes, the breath must be rooted in the lower abdomen and dantian. Peking Opera emphasizes "dantian qi," referring to this kind of vocal position.

Find the Feeling 1: If you are a girl and suddenly see a mouse on your shoe, what would you do? Most likely, you would let out a long "ah～" sound, and it might even be in a "falsetto" position. After calming down, try "ah～" again, and most likely, you won't be able to do it again. Why? At the moment of being startled, your lower abdomen tightens (think about the feeling of being startled, isn't your whole body muscle tense?). Precisely because of this foundation, combined with a powerful airflow (which can be understood as singing breath), rushing straight to the throat, the voice is bound to be high. Therefore, you can understand the feeling of reaching high notes as "tension" and "vigilance." Remember, everything is rooted in the lower abdomen. To sing high notes, it's not about lifting your head and pulling upwards; it's about the rebound force felt by focusing downwards.

Find the Feeling 2: Have you seen a rubber hose? How do you make the water in the hose shoot farther? Pinch the front end of the hose tightly, the smaller the gap, the greater the pressure, and the water shoots farther. High notes don't necessarily have to be very loud. Some singers produce a loud sound when singing high notes, we say that is using strength and lung capacity to sing, rather than using breath and technique to sing. Imagine the water faucet theory, and you should be able to understand some principles.

Find the Feeling 3: There is a large stone on the ground, pick it up. You bend over, counting in your mouth: "one, two, three, go..." I think this "go" must have used breath. I won't say more, just feel it. Look around for something heavy nearby... haha! These are some self-created methods to help you feel the breath, not formal, but very effective.

Also, I want to tell you one point, the ability to express high notes is related to your natural vocal conditions. If your voice is naturally high-pitched, it is a great advantage, like Sun Nan, whose voice is typically high, and performing high notes is not as strenuous. Singers with poor vocal conditions rely more on a lot of breath and methods. But regardless of whether the vocal conditions are good or bad, the voice is produced through breath, just like cigarettes passing through a filter, filtering the voice makes it more beautiful.

Additionally, I will tell you a psychological method. When singing high notes, imagine you are facing a vast sea and singing loudly, the endless horizon of the sea and sky is worth your loud shout, isn't it?

Middle and Low Notes

Middle and low notes generally should be full and emotional. However, like high notes, many people lack the resonance of middle and low notes. Low notes require chest resonance and a broad vocal structure to manifest, which is more innate. From my experience, it is difficult to train a voice that cannot reach low notes to go much lower, going down a few pitches is already good. If you must record a song rich in low notes, record it immediately after waking up without brushing your teeth or washing your face, the voice will go down significantly. Therefore, people lacking in middle and low notes should avoid such songs, playing to their strengths instead. People with rich middle and low notes should fully utilize its potential, as singing them well is very charming!

To sing low notes, keep your chest and throat relaxed, sighing can strengthen some resonance. For extremely low parts, adding some breath to produce oral resonance will make the sound feel thicker. However, no matter what, people with naturally thick voices have more room to play.

To sing mid-range notes, add more color, including beautiful articulation and breathing. Of course, this is related to singing skills and perception. Listen more to "Tsai Chin" for middle and low notes.

Overall and Style

Many singers only focus on the details of sentence by sentence, placing the unit of detail in "sentence" rather than the entire song.

Draw a wavy line for the entire song, and you will know where to be low-key and where to be high-pitched. Take a broader perspective, like positional awareness on a football field. How can you coordinate effective long-pass attacks by focusing on local details?

Unless you have a certain level of skill, don't talk about style. In the early stages, I support starting with imitation, but don't stick to one style. Sing various styles of songs by different singers, and you will unconsciously master some resonance, vocal tones, and basic breath control experiences. This is a good thing. After maturing, with your own vocal exercises and insights, uncovering your own style will be a natural process.

Performing requires dedication, but dedication should have a limit. It's not about forcing yourself to be overly dedicated, excessively sad or joyful. Based on understanding the meaning of the lyrics, measured dedication is sufficient. For example, I have seen people singing "…how could you bear to make me sad…" in extreme pain, which lacks感染力 and instead feels funny. But I have also seen a truly heartbroken friend singing this song at karaoke, with a blank face, singing very calmly,沧桑的嗓音, and the helplessness and loneliness expressed through the singing moved everyone present. I think this is just the right measure, no need for sobbing. But how many people can perform so perfectly at any time? This depends on the singer's musical perception.

Let me give you another example: Some friends exaggerate the characteristics of stars when imitating them. For example, when imitating Jacky Cheung, they overly press their throats to produce a strange thick sound (which was a feature of Jacky Cheung's early work, and is rarely seen now); when imitating Leslie Cheung, they exaggerate his tongue-like resonance at the back; when imitating Faye Wong, it sounds like a tongue-twisting weird sound. In fact, if you listen carefully to the original, it is not that exaggerated. I have a friend whose voice is very similar to Jacky Cheung, but every time he sings Jacky's songs, he does not grasp the measure, later I advised him to be more natural, and he ended up sounding more like the original. Sometimes, the observer sees things clearly. Seeking opinions from friends can be very beneficial.

In addition, people who are emotionally rich sing better than those who are emotionally bland. Resonating with the emotions in the song also requires singers to have rich life experiences. The more experiences, the deeper the insights.

This is something many friends pursue, haha! They think that without vibrato, it's not professional or impressive enough. Little do they know that vibrato in songs is just one way of expression. Can't you sing well without vibrato?

Vibrato, in essence, is the vibration produced by a powerful airflow impacting the back of the oral cavity and nasal cavity when singing with breath control, generated by the pressure applied by the lower abdomen. I don't advocate producing vibrato in pop music in such a rigid way. Some stars produce vibrato by shaking the larynx (Andy Lau) and tightening and loosening the throat (Jacky Cheung). These are small techniques or styles, but in many climactic parts, they also have powerful airflow vibrato. Therefore, breath control is still fundamental. Using breath control naturally produces airflow vibrations that create vibrato. For pop songs, some low-pitched places don't need every word to be on breath control like folk songs. Sometimes it's like a whisper, sometimes like a helpless sigh, without strong airflow supporting your vibrato. At this time, you need to use some small techniques to decorate your tail notes. I think that vibrato in pop songs doesn't entirely rely on breath control. Sometimes shaking the larynx and controlling the sound in the throat is also acceptable as long as it doesn't affect the overall atmosphere of the song.

Friends without vibrato who are determined to crave it can start with imitation - imitating ambulance sirens. Funny, isn't it? This is a self-study method without a vocal teacher nearby, though it doesn't belong to conventional vocal practice. Emit "woo woo" cyclically sliding high and low pitches (slightly fast), it can help you find the feeling of shaking the larynx. Slowly change the "woo woo" sound to "umm umm," then gradually to "you～" sound, and then sing some sentences ending with "you"… haha, this is just a way to feel the vibrato of the larynx, different from breath control. Voice teachers would surely be angry if they saw this! But everything needs gradual progress, slowly figuring it out. You might as well try.

Finally, I want to restate my opinion. If we look at a higher level, the current period emphasizes vibrato in singing, but maybe in five years, vibrato won't be emphasized anymore. Why worry about not having vibrato? Although vibrato can effectively enhance the atmosphere of a song, deep and penetrating, I also like listening to simple, pure voices. So, as long as you sing with your heart, you might have a more unique style.
