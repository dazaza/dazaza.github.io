Former A-League star confirms assistance in investigation; wide-ranging involvement may become a new breakthrough

by yinding999 on 2009-12-17 09:11:03

Former A-League star confirms assistance in investigation, wide-ranging involvement may become new breakthrough

According to reports, Qingdao native player Xing Rui, who once played for Shandong Taishan, Chengdu Wuniu, and Changchun Yatai, has confirmed that he has gone to Shenyang to assist police with their investigation. As a midfielder who was once very famous during the A-League era, and after retirement was revealed to have become an illegal gambling bookmaker, Xing Rui's "assistance in the investigation" could potentially become a new breakthrough for the "anti-gambling and anti-match-fixing storm" entering the Chinese Super League (CSL).

Once Renowned

To fans who only started following Chinese professional football after 2000, Xing Rui might be an unfamiliar name. However, as one of the first players to participate in China’s professional league, Xing Rui was absolutely a well-known name in Chinese football in the mid-1990s: from 1987, he began representing Shandong in the A-League, and after Chinese football entered the professional league era in 1994, Xing Rui was already the captain of Shandong Taishan. At that time, the midfield attack line he formed with Tang Xiaocheng and Su Maozhen was once renowned in the A-League. Especially after the 1995 league season ended, Xing Rui, who scored 7 goals, led Shandong Taishan to win the FA Cup championship, and he himself was rated as one of the "Top Ten Midfield Engines of the League," ranking alongside Ma Mingyu, Cao Xiandong, Gao Zhongxun, and others.

However, after Luneng took over Shandong football, Xing Rui also entered the late stage of his career. He no longer served as the captain of Shandong team, and after gradually being unable to enter the main squad, Xing Rui chose to leave Luneng and join Chengdu Wuniu, which was then in the B-League, in 1999, then transferred to Changchun Yatai, and announced his retirement in 2002.

Post-retirement Involvement in Gambling

After retiring, Xing Rui returned to his hometown of Qingdao to start a business, but it wasn't very successful. An informed source revealed that Xing Rui became a "small bookmaker" in Qingdao where gambling on football was becoming increasingly prevalent. Although there is no clear evidence to confirm this, given Xing Rui's identity and professional experience, he definitely had the opportunity to contact multiple CSL and second-tier league teams in Shandong, as many of his former teammates and "youngsters" are in teams like Luneng, Zhongneng, and Hailifeng. Even because Shandong players are spread across the country, Xing Rui's influence could easily extend to the entire CSL and second-tier league. As a "bookmaker," buying off players to manipulate matches would be effortless for Xing Rui.

There are reports that after the police announced the fake match case involving Hailifeng and Chengdu Football Club, Wang Shouye, who has been the general manager of Hailifeng since 2007, was summoned by the police to Shenyang for investigation, followed by Xing Rui. Given the concentration of so many interest elements on Xing Rui, if he is involved not only in Hailifeng's fake match and gambling case, but also in the other two CSL teams within Shandong province, Shandong Luneng and Qingdao Zhongneng, it will definitely become a new breakthrough for the anti-gambling and anti-match-fixing storm.

Since the beginning of the "anti-gambling and anti-match-fixing" campaign, Qingdao has always been at the center of the storm: from the fake match between Qingdao Hailifeng and Chengdu Football Club, to Hailifeng boss Du Yunqi fleeing abroad and the arrests of Leng Bo, Liu Hongwei, and others, and the "assistance in the investigation" of Wang Shouye and Xing Rui. Outsiders have long speculated: Qingdao's gambling culture ranks among the top nationwide, making it a key city in this "anti-gambling and anti-match-fixing" storm. Moreover, Qingdao has always been a cradle of Chinese football, not only having its own CSL and second-tier league teams, but Qingdao-native players are spread across the country, potentially forming a "Qingdao system fake gambling chain."
