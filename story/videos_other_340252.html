For many Chinese men, after sitting down, showing a part of bare legs or "thermal underwear" feels normal. Suit and tie conversation art: Suits are men's eternal fashion. They free men from many troubles but also bring them frequent confusion: how to distinguish time, place, occasion, and mood when wearing suits? This is the art of conversation between suits and ties.

Men's ties influence others' perceptions of his identity, status, credit, personality, and ability more than any other appearance aspect. Some men wear shirts and ties separately that are both of good material and color, but when combined, the colors obviously clash, which is a terrible situation that significantly reduces the impression others have of you. However, once you know the rules, choosing a tie becomes quite simple.

First, ties that are too long or too short are always ungraceful. An appropriate tie length is when the tip of the tie just touches the belt buckle, no more or less. There are many lengths and types of ties, with better quality ties often being longer. The standard length is 55 or 56 inches. Suitable tie length depends entirely on height and the method of tying the tie. The width of the tie is also crucial. Although there are no fixed rules, basically, the tie width should harmonize perfectly with the width of the suit lapel. Currently, the standard tie width refers to the widest part of the tie end being 4 to 4.5 inches.

Additionally, a tie should have a small hanging object (tie clip) behind the wider end, allowing the narrower end to hang smoothly without revealing the reverse side of the tie.

There are many patterns and colors of ties. The most common and practical style is one without any pattern or design, a solid color tie. A solid color tie can be paired with any style of suit or shirt. Whether paired with patterned shirts, large checks, or dark wide straight stripes suits, it performs exceptionally well. Sometimes, certain shirts or suits can only be paired with solid color ties. Solid color pairing is favored for its simplicity and broad adaptability, such as pairing gray suits with light blue or dark red ties. An expensive, well-made suit paired with a solid color tie emphasizes the luxurious material and exquisite tailoring, providing a complete aesthetic experience.

Ties printed with evenly sized patterns (geometric patterns) are also quite common, widely applicable, and easy to pair with clothing. The base color of the tie is the main color. You should choose a tie that matches either the same color family or a contrasting color family as the suit. The dot, mesh, or diagonal stripe colors on the tie should match the shirt color. For example, a royal blue base color tie with pure white dots should be paired with a white shirt because the white of the shirt enhances the white of the tie, while the suit can be chosen in the same color as the tie base, royal blue.

Shirts with more patterns should avoid ties with regular patterns, as the patterns on the tie can disrupt the overall pattern order. Typically, shirts with vibrant patterns and colors are unsuitable for conservative ties. The best material for ties is silk. Although the color is bright, it is not dazzling, and using this type of tie is almost unrestricted by time, place, or person. Polyester or polyester blended with silk has a similar luster to silk, being stiffer and having the luxury of silk, yet cheaper and more durable. Artificial silk ties look shiny when new but deform easily after multiple uses and can be discarded after some time. Avoid ties that expose poor dyeing materials, as their patterns or colors often bleed when washed, mixing together. Also, avoid unusual colors, patterns, shapes, or sizes, disregarding current fashion trends entirely.

After removing the tie, it is best to immediately hang it on a rack to naturally unfold and dry in a ventilated area. When the entire tie needs cleaning, it is best to take it to a laundry shop for dry cleaning. Do not throw it into the washing machine for stirring. Local stains can be cleaned with water or stain remover, then dried and ironed.

Attention to Suit Details

Men's suits are not just uniforms for everyone; their charm lies in shaping personal style. The nuances that many people overlook are precisely the essence of men's suits and reflect the wearer's aesthetic interest and appreciation level.

1. Collars: Suit collar styles vary based on personal preference. Among them, diamond and sword collars excel in rules and elegance, while shawl collars appear luxurious and solemn. Ordinary diamond lapels with widths of 8-10 cm make most men appear broader-chested. In the sixties and seventies, lapel widths became very large, with some upturned lapels nearly reaching the shoulders. However, in recent years, lapel widths have gradually narrowed. Meanwhile, businesspeople should avoid overly wide lapels during "business talks" as this gives an impression of lacking seriousness. Some prefer slightly narrower lapels, believing this makes them appear taller and more elegant. Generally, obese men can have narrower lapels; conversely, thinner people can have slightly wider lapels, making their chests appear fuller. Secondly, those with longer faces should wear shorter lapels, as the lapel closest to the face makes the face appear longer. Round-faced men, however, can choose longer lapel styles.

2. Shoulder Types: Basic shoulder styles of suits mainly include four types. First is the natural shoulder type, where the shoulder is not exaggerated and padding is minimal, giving a more fitting impression. Second is the drooping shoulder type, where the entire shoulder appears slightly rounded, looking very generous, with American-style suits adopting this shoulder type more frequently. Third is the square shoulder, where the shoulder head slightly rises, suitable for those who tend to shrug, softening and diluting the characteristic of protruding shoulder blades. Fourth is the concave shoulder, where the shoulder head rises, suitable for those with sloping shoulders, with European-style suits adopting this shoulder type more often, giving a soft impression.

3. Pockets: Patch pockets give off a casual style, while more formal suits should use welt pockets. Pocket flaps tend to widen the hip joint area, so for those who are overly robust, pocket flaps should be avoided.

4. Back: The only authentic decoration on the back of a suit is a vent or side slit. European-style suits generally use vents, adding a slim and elegant feel. Side slits are particularly suitable for those who like to put their hands in their trouser pockets. For those with wider hips, wearing suits with side slits improves visual effects, while suits without slits have an elegant traditional style.

5. Length: The appropriate length of the suit jacket varies by country. British-style jackets are longer, American-style jackets are medium-length, and European-style jackets are the shortest. For determining the appropriate length of the jacket for yourself, there is a simple calculation method: when standing, the distance from the neck to the ground divided by half is optimal. For shorter individuals, the lower hem of the jacket can be raised by about 1.5 centimeters above the hip circumference, making the legs appear longer and the figure more proportionate.
