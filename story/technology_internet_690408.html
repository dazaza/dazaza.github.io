The concept of growing pains was first proposed by Duchamp in 1823 and is most often applied in pediatric diagnosis, but its definition has not been very clear. Growing pains occur in children and young people over a certain age, with the location of the pain around the knee, showing irregular intermittent episodes of pain with an intermittent period lasting several days or months, often without structural damage. The cause of growing pains is not very clear, but it has been considered to be related to excessive bone growth, fatigue, familial factors, and psychological barriers. Most people believe that it is growth-related, resulting from childhood growth being too fast for peripheral nerves and tendons to correspondingly increase, thus generating referred pain. Data shows that the clinical diagnosis of 86 cases of children's growing pains actually consists mostly of clinical rickets, with a smaller part being subclinical rickets. The onset has seasonal characteristics (pain symptoms worsen in winter and spring and reduce or disappear in summer and autumn), their skeletal deformities are milder at night, and can be confused with growing pains. Children with limb pain are often considered to have growing pains without receiving due examination and treatment, thus losing the opportunity for diagnosis and treatment and causing adverse consequences such as skeletal malformations; attention should be paid. When encountering limb pain, late-onset rickets and similar diseases should be excluded. On this basis, the diagnosis of growing pains in children should be carefully made, especially in subclinical rickets where clinical manifestations are atypical, only lower extremity pain, fatigue, etc., without skeletal deformities, and blood biochemical and bone x-ray examinations often show no changes. Serum bone alkaline phosphatase increases due to vitamin D and calcium deficiency and disorders in calcium and phosphorus metabolism in children, increased osteoblast activity and bone alkaline phosphatase levels when ≥ 200U / L indicate a high likelihood of rickets diagnosis, when ≥ 300U / L it can basically be diagnosed as rickets... This change in blood chemistry and clinical manifestations is more sensitive than x-rays and is one of the widely used means of screening for clinical rickets. One of the causes of children's late-onset rickets is that children are in a strong phase of growth and development requiring various nutrients, especially vitamin D and calcium, dietary calcium intake is generally inadequate. Most parents, including some healthcare workers, only pay attention to calcium supplementation for children aged 3 years and lack knowledge on the need for vitamin D supplementation. Some patients in this group have a history of calcium supplementation but no history of vitamin D supplementation. Another important factor is the heavy learning tasks of school-age children, leading to less outdoor activities and lack of sunlight exposure. Therefore, children should have a reasonable diet, eat more calcium-rich foods such as dairy products, seafood, beans, etc.; appropriately add vitamin D in addition to infants (young children) tA’b, older children should also pay attention, especially in the winter season. In 2003, the American Academy of Pediatrics recommended that children and adolescents take at least 200U of vitamin D daily to prevent the occurrence of rickets; sufficient outdoor activities should be ensured. For children with growing pains, there should be a high alert to the possibility of late-onset rickets occurring, detection of serum calcium, phosphorus, alkaline phosphatase, calcium, phosphorus, and alkaline phosphatase compared to normal children, we must choose to quantitatively detect serum bone alkaline phosphatase reflecting the whole process of rickets bone changes, as well as use long bones x-ray enlargement radiography inspection, which is a more obvious and clear advanced and accurate means, providing clear evidence for early diagnosis J in order to prevent some Asian clinical rickets misdiagnosis, affecting children's growth and development.
