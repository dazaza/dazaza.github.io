Gary Hamel: Disrupting Traditional Management Made Samsung and Morning Star Rise

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 20:51:03

In an era where hope is fading, change is frequent, and competition is intense, what kind of company can own the future?

Over the past century, many names have lit up the commercial world's starry sky: Sony, Nokia, Microsoft, EMI Records, etc. There are a host of similar names. But in the last decade, Sony has suffered huge losses, Nokia was sold, Microsoft has waned, and EMI faced challenges from online music. Meanwhile, Samsung, Google, and Morning Star Company have emerged as new forces.

What kind of power drives such a reversal?

Gary Hamel, professor at London Business School and selected by The Wall Street Journal as one of the "most influential business thinkers in the world," believes that the era of "disrupting traditional management concepts" has arrived. He said that consumers' eagerness to exercise their "omnipotent" rights, the prevalence of social media, and the overnight disruption of century-old business models will ensure that only companies with flat organizations, those that beat bureaucracy, astonish consumers, and act faster than change can own the future.

Why did Sony and Nokia decline?

We once lived in an era that worshipped the "pyramid" structure. Under this organizational framework, leaders had supreme authority, efficiency was placed above everything else, and everyone took pride in being qualified, rule-abiding employees. The rise of Japanese enterprises was a successful example of this structure. Whether it was Sony Electronics or EMI Records, they all shared a similar pyramid corporate structure — as noted by Hasegawa Kazuhiro, a Japanese management master known as the "second Koan," and author of The President’s Handbook — their management had strong leadership capabilities, ensuring their propositions were always promptly implemented.

Despite differences in organizational structures, the "pyramid" structure was also ubiquitous in American companies. Gary Hamel mentioned that whether it was Microsoft and Dell, or Intel and HP, they all had powerful, imperial-like structures.

Having a smoothly running "pyramid" structure was once the hallmark of great 20th-century companies. Even the bureaucracy it brought was once considered inevitable. Max Weber once described it as follows: Bureaucracy is superior to any other organization in terms of precision, stability, strict discipline, and reliability.

However, the massive losses of performance-oriented companies like Sony, Sharp, and Panasonic provide a counter-interpretation of such "strong leadership" and "superiority." Despite still holding leading positions in their industries, HP, Dell, Microsoft, and Intel's market shares are increasingly eroded by newcomers like Samsung and Google. There are many more examples. Gary Hamel noticed that in today's era, every company at the top of its industry has about a 30% chance of losing its position within the next five years; and among the 30 "best CEOs in the world" selected by Barron's in 2008, only five retained that title in 2012.

There are many reasons for their "gradual failure." The numerous drawbacks of the "pyramid" structure come first, including expanding crises, increasing management costs, and creating friction. Theoretically, this structure is also believed to be responsible for decision distortion, power misplacement, suppression of dissent, misleading competition, overemphasis on experience, and suppression of initiative within the company. However, Gary Hamel believes that the core issue lies in the fact that the business world is becoming turbulent at an unprecedented speed, but companies are failing to keep pace. In a blog post titled "Wholesale Leadership" (Syndicating the work of leadership), this "leading global business strategy expert" in the eyes of Fortune magazine lamented: "The world has always been complex. So too are our corporate organizations. Usually, in traditional companies, when the small group at the top realizes the importance of comprehensive change, when the company's problems are serious enough or the opportunities clear enough to require immediate action, breaking all levels and demanding the attention of busy executives — it's already too late."

Past theories did not fail to offer possible responses for large institutions. But their solutions relied on "superman" type leadership. Gary Hamel wrote, "No era has ever craved leadership as much as today, yet it remains out of reach. This is not an individual problem, but a universal issue in corporate organizations — traditional pyramid-shaped organizational structures demand too much from a few individuals. This makes their knowledge diversity, expertise, and time insufficient when making decisions."

Changes in the environment also make extraordinary leadership forever insufficient. Gary Hamel summarized the changes in the global business environment as follows: companies that were once innovative now struggle on the brink of aging; compared to blue-chip companies, the younger generation prefers start-ups; century-old business models can be overturned overnight; investors are angered by greedy CEOs and self-satisfied boards; newly empowered consumers are eager to exercise their rights; social media has greatly changed the way people connect, learn, and collaborate. In short, this is a world where change is constant; businesses and consumers are connected as never before.

This makes our expectations for "superman-type" leadership even higher: he must be both confident and humble; strong-willed yet open to influence; able to analyze precisely and look ahead, while also being a practitioner, brave yet cautious.

But how many people in today's world meet our expectations? Gary Hamel frankly admitted, "I've never met many."

The Rise of Samsung and Morning Star

Everything seems to be in chaos. Gary Hamel believes that in fact, it already is. He said that the question we face now is no longer "How do we better respond to change?" — because change happens all the time and seems to never stop — but rather, how do we gain continuously improving competitive advantages? "Rebellion" is Gary Hamel's solution. Max Weber once referred to the bureaucracy accompanying the "pyramid" structure as "the best method known to mankind for achieving despotic control over others." Gary Hamel, however, believes that to give commercial companies a future, the first step is to replace this "soft tyranny" with a flat organizational framework. "If we want to build truly adaptive corporate structures, decentralizing power more widely is essential."

Morning Star is one of the recently emerging companies with a flat organizational structure. In its philosophy regarding organizational structure, there is this passage: "All team members are self-managing professionals. They decide on their own, along with colleagues, customers, suppliers, and peers, all communication and coordination matters related to work, without needing instructions." "Empowerment" means freedom. This completely contradicts the "control" concept under the "pyramid" structure. Concepts advocated by the latter, such as planning, budget control, distribution, measurement, organization, and monitoring, are replaced in flat organizational companies by social networks, opinion markets, online forums, and "choruses." Gary Hamel believes that only in a flat organizational structure can everyone enjoy the freedom to work, making it possible for "action to precede change."

Another benefit of a flat organization is that it allows employees to understand the significance of their work, thereby working proactively. Morning Star relies on a traditionally "unorganized, undisciplined" structure to ensure profitability based on one premise: all employees know what is happening daily with the company's financial numbers. Another reference practice is Unilever linking the soap produced by employees to the hygiene conditions of a village in India through full-process measurement and timely feedback, enabling employees to better understand the meaning of their work and thus improve their work processes voluntarily. This doesn't sound complicated. But data shows that although everyone knows talent is the most important capital, only 20% of people worldwide are truly committed to their work; correspondingly, only 21% of bosses are willing to share power with subordinates.

Gary Hamel also encourages people to reconsider "key principles," replacing efficiency and its representation of standardization, specialization, and formal hierarchy with adaptability and its representation of experimentalism, dissociation, and natural hierarchy. Correspondingly, concepts like discipline, neatness, obedience, and external incentives from the "pyramid" era should be replaced with internal markets, passion exchange, activism, and personal willingness.

With a flat organization and removal of bureaucracy, we still need extraordinary leadership: not necessarily as "omnipotent" as in the past, but capable of leading the company to create products and services that "astonish consumers." Clearly, this requires leaders to have a long-term vision and make continuous investments in key areas. Earlier this year, Hay Group surveyed 100 Asian companies, and the results showed that South Korea's Samsung and India's Tata had the highest EBITD rate (earnings before interest, taxes, and depreciation): 26.5%. There are many reasons explaining Samsung's success. Its CEO Kwon Oh-hyun once worked in R&D, and Hay Group believes this makes the company more aware that investing in "continuous improvement of customer experience" may not bring immediate benefits but is crucial for the company's future.

Every transformation will not be simple. But the core concept guiding the path to future companies is actually just one: activism. Gary Hamel would describe the birth process of companies likely to stand out in the future as follows: among 1,000 companies with ideas, 100 will take experiments, 10 will turn experiments into projects, and ultimately only one company will succeed.
