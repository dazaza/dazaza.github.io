This week, Campfire went down again (albeit for a short time). My distributed team at BankSimple needed an alternative group chat solution. After consulting the all-knowing Twitter, we tried out the most common few options. Although we ultimately didn't adopt any of them, these products were all very good, with friendly and approachable teams that were willing to listen to feedback. Nonetheless, they still didn’t get our business. This article isn't about group chat tools. It’s about platforms and treating customers well. It’s about a major misstep by some chat product companies. Too many companies are making the same mistake.

The chat services we tested had two ways of use: either through their web application via a browser or using their Adobe AIR-based application. After signing up, they strongly recommended using the AIR app, and certain key features were only available in the AIR app. We then experienced the common issues with AIR apps: poor performance, strange interface bugs, unresponsive keyboard shortcuts, and user interfaces that differ greatly from the operating system. The AIR app exists in an awkward limbo between a web app and a desktop app, resulting in a subpar user experience. Even though this service had better features and was reportedly more stable and reliable, we quickly switched back to Campfire (accessed via the native Mac client Propane).

I asked the developers why they used AIR. Their response was something like, "We're a small team. Most users are on Windows. Using AIR allows us to develop a cross-platform app with minimal effort to satisfy the largest number of customers." This is undoubtedly a typical case for using AIR: small web development shops can use the same technology to build desktop clients. What's wrong with that?

At first, I accepted this explanation. But the more I thought about it, the less it made sense: this decision was made from the company's perspective without considering the customer experience. Imagine a restaurant trying to make more money by using gutter oil: it's cheap, abundant, and there are no supply chain worries. Initially, profits increase. But soon, customers start complaining, and the restaurant’s reputation suffers, leading to a sharp decline in its standing. The owner is confused: gutter oil may not be ideal, but it doesn't affect edibility. And at first, it did bring in more profit and new customers! So where did it go wrong?

Like previous cross-platform component libraries and cross-platform application frameworks, AIR is the gutter oil of client-side software. Yes, it works, but it provides a suboptimal user experience. Those who used Java client applications in the 90s might be familiar with this: slow, ugly, inconsistent, and unreliable.

Do people really need native applications? Absolutely. In every support forum for AIR applications, you'll find customers requesting native apps. Usually, the administrator weakly explains that they don’t have the resources to develop native apps for every platform.
