Google Ruled to Have Violated Wiretap Laws by Scanning Gmail Content

Early on September 27 Beijing time, a U.S. federal judge ruled on Thursday that Google had violated both U.S. federal and California wiretap laws due to its use of computers to scan Gmail content for creating user profiles and delivering targeted advertisements.

In a class-action lawsuit accusing Gmail's business model of involving wiretapping, U.S. District Judge Lucy Koh made this ruling. Google had previously sought to persuade the federal judge to dismiss the lawsuit based on a clause in the Wiretap Act, which authorizes email service providers to intercept messages sent by users for the purpose of mail delivery or when service functionality malfunctions occur.

Judge Koh stated in her ruling: "The legal system indicates that Congress did not intend to give electronic communication service providers unlimited leeway to intercept communications for the benefit of their business models. In fact, if, as Google claims, information is broadly intercepted during normal business operations, then this statutory provision would be superfluous."

Gmail, including Google's office service Google Apps, is the world's largest email service with approximately 450 million global users. This ruling also disadvantages Yahoo!; its email service has over 300 million users, and the company similarly scans email content to display advertisements. Microsoft's newly launched Outlook web-based email service does not scan the emails sent by its 400 million users.

This is the second time this month that a U.S. federal court has determined that Google may be held accountable for wiretapping activities. This week, Google petitioned a federal appeals court to reconsider a prior ruling that deemed it a wiretapping act for Google Street View cars to collect data from open WiFi networks. Google argued that the September 10 ruling by the Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals was confusing because capturing open wireless signals, such as broadcast television signals, is protected under the Wiretap Act.

In this case, after Judge Koh's ruling, Google still has the opportunity to defend itself. However, there is currently no set trial date for this case. Jon Simpson, privacy project director at Consumer Watchdog in Santa Monica, California, stated: "This ruling means that both federal and California anti-wiretapping laws apply to the internet. It's a significant victory for online privacy protection. Companies like Google can no longer do whatever they want with users' emails and data."

In a statement, Google expressed disappointment with the ruling and said it would consider various legal options. Google stated: "Automated scanning helps us provide security protections for Gmail users and powerful features like inbox prioritization." Google also believed that according to the end-user agreement, Gmail users have consented to the scanning of their emails. However, Judge Koh considered that this agreement did not adequately inform users. Additionally, Google claimed that non-Gmail users who send emails to the Gmail platform have agreed to the scanning of those emails, but Judge Koh entirely rejected this argument.
