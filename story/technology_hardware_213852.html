Several issues to note when building ADSL access networks

by lk0pcee on 2009-07-24 16:47:57

The requirements for line pairs in ADSL systems are strict, and the transmission rate is highly sensitive to transmission distance, wire diameter, and copper wire quality. A large number of copper cable lines constructed during the peak development period in recent years were built under tight time constraints. Plastic cables were not subjected to air-sealed acceptance tests, and no gas maintenance was implemented. As a result, the problem of moisture ingress in cable joints is relatively serious, leading to low AC/DC performance indicators. Additionally, due to the influence of factors such as echo noise and crosstalk between lines, only about 15% of the current city telephone cables in China can support ADSL services.

Therefore, one should be cautious when using ADSL to transmit broadband services on existing telephone cables. ADSL can only be applied in areas where fiber optic access networks have not yet been established and where the cable transmission quality is good. ADSL is merely a transitional measure for the initial stage of broadband services. Once the penetration rate of broadband services exceeds 10%, ADSL will find it difficult to meet the demand. If a large amount of expensive transitional measures are promoted, it will inevitably miss the opportunity to build a bandwidth-extremely-high fiber network, creating new burdens and causing investment waste.

**DSLAM Configuration**

Based on user distribution maps, the number of users, the placement of cables, coverage area, and other factors, the DSLAM equipment configuration is determined. To ensure the minimum bandwidth for users, the number of users per DSLAM node should not be excessive. The construction of ADSL broadband access networks in various regions should be combined with the construction of urban fiber optic access networks. In the early stages of construction, according to the characteristics of ADSL technology and considering that the number of user demands may not be high and users are widely distributed, DSLAMs should be distributed across more offices, but the capacity of each node should not be too large temporarily. Before the urban fiber optic access network is completed, DSLAMs can be installed in existing central office rooms, utilizing existing city telephone cables. When the business develops to a certain extent, the DSLAM should be placed as close to the users as possible to facilitate user development and improve service quality.

**Configuration of Access Servers**

In traditional DSL network architecture, the central office equipment DSLAM connects through an ATM network or Frame Relay (FR) to a router. User data communicates via a Virtual Circuit (VC) between the DSL Modem and the router, which terminates the VC and restores the user data into IP packets. The main problems with this network are: routers do not have sufficient capacity to terminate data from users, poor network scalability; lack of user management and billing mechanisms, unable to manage users like narrowband dial-up methods, and unable to bill users based on multiple ways (such as duration, traffic, bandwidth, QoS, etc.); users cannot dynamically select services and ISPs.

As an access technology, ADSL does not possess user management and billing functions similar to narrowband access servers, thus requiring the cooperation of broadband access servers to truly implement ADSL services. Broadband access servers can handle PPP connection requests from users. Through the session process of establishing and releasing a PPP connection, they can separately calculate the duration and traffic of user internet usage and provide information required for various billing methods. Cooperating with Radius servers, using protocols such as PAP, CHAP, etc., for PPP user identification and authentication, and through the IPCP protocol for dynamic IP address allocation to users, thereby maximizing the utilization of limited IP address resources.

In the ADSL network architecture, the setting of broadband access servers can be divided into three methods: centralized, distributed, and centralized-distributed.

**Centralized**: At the exit point of a city's ADSL broadband access network, one broadband access server is set up to handle the PPP connections for all ADSL users in the city, providing information needed for billing by duration, traffic, etc., and offering dynamic IP addresses for virtual dial-up users. The centralized method has advantages such as saving on engineering investment and network operation costs, easy management, and facilitating business development. However, since the broadband access server becomes the center of network control and management, it might become a bottleneck for business development when the business reaches a certain scale. This method is suitable for small cities or during the initial phase of engineering construction in medium-sized cities where there are not many DSLAM nodes, fewer broadband users, and smaller business volumes.

**Centralized Distributed**: Several broadband access servers are set up in a city. Each server is connected to multiple DSLAM nodes within its region, handling partitioned ADSL business connections, billing statistics, and dynamic IP address allocation functions. Each broadband access server has an exit point to the Internet. This method is suitable for larger and medium-sized cities with more DSLAM nodes.

**Distributed**: Broadband access servers are installed at the access layer, completing ADSL business connections, billing statistics, and dynamic IP address allocation functions at the DSLAM node. Since broadband access servers are relatively expensive, unless there are more broadband users and larger business volumes, taking into account business volume and cost-performance ratio, it is generally not recommended to adopt this method.

**ADSL Network Transmission Mode**

Currently, there are mainly two network transmission modes for ADSL systems: Packet transmission mode and ATM transmission mode. Considering that the QoS mechanism of ATM transmission mode can guarantee the smooth implementation of large-scale real-time video services, most traditional telecommunications equipment manufacturers still prefer ADSL equipment with ATM transmission mode. In ADSL systems using packet transmission mode, the upstream data interface of the central office DSLAM equipment and the user data interface of the user-end RTU equipment are typically popular Ethernet interfaces. The transmission data format carried by the ADSL system usually adopts variable-length Layer 2 Ethernet frames or Layer 3 IP packets depending on the functional changes of the equipment at both ends.
