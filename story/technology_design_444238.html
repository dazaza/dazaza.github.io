Market analysis:

What deserves attention now is whether the A-share market can remain unaffected if surrounding markets continue to weaken? This remains to be tested. With the arrival of Friday, some sensitive factors will again influence the market, such as how high international oil prices will rise? Will the reserve requirement ratio be increased this weekend? These issues will cause anxiety among investors. Therefore, at this moment, it doesn't matter whether the market style shifts or not; what matters is that it's time to be vigilant about "changing winds."

Yesterday's topic was "K-line patterns suitable for buying in a volatile market." Today, after opening, the index slightly strengthened, with weighted blue-chip stocks like banks gradually rising and pushing up the index. Although large-cap stocks are gradually gaining market recognition, under the current regulation backdrop, it is unlikely for banks to continue their strong performance due to the sharp increase in foreign exchange reserves, which reopens the window for raising the reserve requirement ratio. Market volatility is unavoidable during this sensitive weekend period. Furthermore, the rise in international oil prices and commodities will increase production costs for enterprises and make it harder domestically to control inflation. As a result, it's unlikely for the market to see continuous upward momentum. Caution and good position management are still recommended in the near term.

Domestically, if risks in the commodities market accumulate with price increases and real estate transactions are administratively controlled, it's only natural for funds to flow into the stock market. Therefore, the rise in the stock market around the Spring Festival seems to be driven by new entrants. Confirmation of this judgment might come after the first-quarter financial reports of listed companies are disclosed, allowing us to form an idea based on changes in major shareholders. One phenomenon caught our attention last week: large-cap stocks were clearly pursued by many buyers. If new entrants have indeed bought a significant amount of these stocks, it would suggest that the market going forward could be quite resilient.

Speaking of this, China's stock market is often shrouded in "conspiracy theories" and tends to mythologize the power of hot money, as if it manipulates the most funds inside the market. So, hot money is supposed to inflate certain stocks and then smoothly exit, leaving behind a bunch of trapped investors. But I want to say that whether it's hot money or new entrants, they too fear being manipulated by the market and becoming the buyer or holder of previously inflated bad stocks. Ha ha, in the face of risk, everyone is equal, and who isn't afraid of "conspiracies"? So my consistent view is that those with low risk preferences will still buy quality stocks, even if their prices are higher. Those with high risk preferences naturally seek excessive returns from junk stocks. But the fundamental condition for buying stocks won't change: believing in the future of the market, which is the essence of being a bull investor.
