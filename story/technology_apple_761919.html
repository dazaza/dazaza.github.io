Google Official: The Relationship Between Android and Chrome is Like Mac and iOS

by geekzhang on 2013-05-16 16:57:52

When Andy Rubin left his position as head of Google's Android business in March, it came as a complete surprise to everyone. Even his successor, Sundar Pichai, the head of the Chrome department, was caught off guard.Pichai is now the most powerful person in Google's mobile business, overseeing both the Android and Chrome (OS/browser) departments. However, since taking over in March, he has not yet faced the media. With the Google I/O conference just around the corner, the 40-year-old Pichai gave his first interview to Wired, sharing some noteworthy insights.Regarding the relationship between Android and Chrome, it was widely speculated that Pichai's cross-departmental leadership role was aimed at merging the two. Pichai dismissed this notion:Users care about the apps and services they use, not the operating systems. Few people ask why MacBook uses MacOSX while iPhone and iPad use iOS? Why is that? They believe Apple is iTunes, iCloud, and iPhoto. In the long term, computing itself is bound to change. We are in a critical period. This is a world where multiple screens coexist, with smart displays, abundant low-cost computing, and numerous sensors being applied. In a year or two, things may be different, but in the short term, we have both Android and Chrome, and this approach will not change.About Facebook Home's lock screen:We want to be a very open platform, but we hope to do so in a way that provides a good experience for end users. There will always be some balance here. It’s no different from the decisions Facebook makes about its own platform. Currently, we don't plan to make any changes and are pleased with their excellent work.Regarding Kindle Fire, which has altered native Android beyond recognition:Under the licensing rules, Amazon can do this. Generally speaking, Google hopes everyone operates on a unified version of Android because it benefits everyone. But we won’t prohibit different approaches. Our focus is not on Facebook Home or Kindle Fire.And regarding the upcoming Google I/O conference, Pichai made an intriguing statement:It will be very different. This time we won't be launching many new products or new operating systems. For Android and Chrome, at this conference, we will focus on what we can do for developers to help them better develop applications. We will show how Google services can achieve excellent functionality on these two platforms.Does this mean we won't see any jaw-dropping hardware products at this year's Google I/O?
