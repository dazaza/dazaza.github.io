Explosion! Who is responsible? Rational purchase of laptop batteries

by jinniu2009 on 2009-02-04 11:03:39

Energy has always been the most robust capital in the world, and this is equally applicable to IT products. Although a mobile phone or a laptop does not demand too much power from its user, there is still such a saying in the industry: "Whoever masters the ultimate battery technology will become the pioneer of the entire industry." Despite the relentless pursuit of energy, civilian batteries are still subject to significant limitations.

Although they may not be as dangerous as "nuclear energy," the common lithium-ion batteries we use are not entirely foolproof in terms of safety. Looking back, from the early "fire incidents" involving batteries to the recent widely publicized "battery explosion incidents," all these events have clearly shown that the use of energy must follow certain rules.

### Lithium-Ion Battery Principles

Perhaps some of the statements above might sound alarmist, but I believe many frequent flyers know that batteries are not allowed in the aircraft's baggage compartment, including laptops with battery modules. The concerns of the aviation department may confirm some issues, similar to what I have observed in the second-hand laptop market in Zhongguancun.

Properly manufactured notebook batteries rarely cause problems, but those handled by second-hand dealers in computer markets can be different. Many people think that laptop batteries consist of 4 to 8 cells similar in size to AA batteries, but more importantly, there is also a square-inch charging protection circuit. Experience suggests that due to long-term overcharging, lithium batteries may instantly discharge a large current under specific temperature, humidity, or poor contact conditions, leading to self-ignition or explosion. The function of the small charging protection circuit is precisely to prevent such situations.

### Battery Composition

Batteries are made up of cells and control circuits.

In the second-hand laptop market at the computer mall, you often see batches of refurbished batteries. These batteries can restore some endurance capabilities, but very few of them have charging protection circuits. In other words, when your laptop runs for several hours a day, overcharging is almost inevitable, and the probability of explosion or fire increases significantly.

Some may argue that if so, just remove the battery while using the laptop and everything will be fine. But aside from questioning whether a "laptop" without a battery deserves the name, even the removed battery continues to discharge, especially after complete discharge (half a month to a month), bringing it closer to failure. Moreover, for batteries that have undergone rough "refurbishment," there may be inherent defects or internal short circuits, which are also reasons for spontaneous combustion or explosion.

In fact, many laptop users have experienced re-purchasing a battery after it fails. Original factory batteries work well but come with a high price tag. My suggestion is that if you really need a battery with effective endurance, the primary consideration should be whether the replacement battery can be used safely. While passively accepting the high-priced original batteries, we also hope that third-party battery manufacturers with true technical strength will emerge soon. By then, the era of high-priced laptop batteries will likely be eliminated.

According to unconfirmed third-party sources, the battery of the phone that exploded and caused serious injuries originated from some counterfeit manufacturers, which has similarities to the "refurbished" or "self-made" batteries found in laptops. As mentioned at the beginning, lithium-ion battery technology is already quite mature, but its use must still follow certain rules!
