All love affairs are the same at the beginning.

by haiptaowpp on 2011-09-19 21:23:52

Life after marriage is like biting into a pineapple; though the surface may not be appealing, it tastes good.

Later on, we matured.

With white hair, we walk hand in hand in the sunset.

Eventually, we grew old.

We encountered Eileen Chang, Gu Long, and Lin Huiyin. The love stories in Eileen Chang's works are mostly tragic, while those in Gu Long's are often incomplete and strange. In contrast, Lin Huiyin's love is picturesque. Tragedy, imperfection, and beauty—different hearts, different people, different loves. For people in their twenties, love is complicated, and amidst this complexity, our definition of love becomes even more ambiguous.

Therefore, during countless lonely nights, we always lament how life would have been better if we had only met for the first time, constantly sighing about things that seemed ordinary at the time. Occasionally, we speak tender words to the autumn wind, yearning for the deepest feelings buried within us. We might get drunk late at night and fall asleep reminiscing, hugging our spouses while missing someone deep inside.

Once, he asked me, "What exactly is romance? Why are we so anxious about gaining or losing it? Why are we so sensitive? Why does no one trust each other?"

As for me, I don't believe in the perfect love described in novels. Too beautiful things can only exist in books; reality always comes with many regrets, just like my friend who lost his seemingly perfect love overnight.

Listening quietly, we finally realized that the most important person in this lifetime is the one who walks with you until the end.

Everything changes, except for love, which remains constant. This kind of love is called growing old together.

Life settles down, career settles down, and marriage settles down, but what about love? Sometimes, marriage doesn't equal love.

I remained silent, as I certainly didn't know.

Yet, I admire the classic line from the Book of Songs: "Holding your hand, growing old with you." There's a touch of life's flavor in simplicity. But saying it is easy. In reality, spending a lifetime with someone is far from simple.

Time has smoothed out our edges. We smile watching the sunset, smiling at couples laughing under the trees, smiling at families strolling together. Walking in the sunset, the path we tread is shallow, just like the black hair faintly embedded among the white.

In middle age, the era of chasing love has passed. Health, career, and family are now the most important.

Speaking of this, I also recall a line from a Yuan Dynasty tune: "Ask the world, what is love, that makes people pledge life and death?" Of course, in my understanding, love hasn't reached the level of pledging life and death. Such love is too intense, not suitable for us.

Love is truly an unfathomable thing.

Marriage and love have become indistinguishable. Even if marriage isn't love, it feels no different.

Back then, instead of longing for grand romantic love, it was more like being imprinted by the poetry of love. Teenagers, what do they understand about love?

"Holding your hand, growing old with you." When we reminisce about this ancient saying, look at the person holding your hand beside you.

The neighborhood broadcast plays a song titled "Growing Old Together." I want to grow old with you, even if it's plain and simple, listening to your chatter every day, still smiling foolishly beside you. I want to grow old with you, cherishing every moment of love, until the day we both grow old, stars accompany our silver hair, still shining.

For most people, their twenties are the prime time for falling in love. It's like the moon rising above the willow branches, meeting after dusk. Youthful sweetness carries a bit of sourness, like a red apple, taking a bite brings sweetness with a hint of sourness. People are beautiful, flavors are beautiful. But apples, though beautiful, don't last long; a few bites and they're gone.

Love starts similarly for everyone, but later paths diverge. Some fade away in prolonged opposition, some break apart in the trivialities of daily life, others, after a burst of passion, vanish into memory. Like fireworks, dim yet brilliant, they interpret love in an extreme way. In life, we encounter many loves. Some are meant to be cherished in memory, others are to be consumed. Value the former, but need the latter more.

Back then, young and naive, we all longed for a passionate love. So, we watched "The Story of Minglan," sang about living freely in the mortal world, galloping horses to share prosperity, reciting melancholic ancient poems to express our feelings. Back then, love was pink, like two blooming peach blossoms in spring, shyly blushing in the breeze. Innocent and pure.

Just like love affairs in our twenties, we meet, love, hurt, suffer, and part ways. Love back then was always unpredictable.

Buddha said, "This is a sorrowful world, where sorrow means regret. Without regret, even if given endless happiness, joy cannot be appreciated."

So, I choose to let go.

From youthful naivety, youthful fervor, middle-aged depth, to the end, nothing compares to a love that lasts a lifetime.

Some say, in the journey of love, everyone meets three people: one you love, one who loves you, and one you marry.

At the onset of puberty, reading Liu Yong's "My belt grows loose, but I never regret it, wasting away for her," and Qin Guan's "If two hearts beat as one, why should they care if they see each other morning or night," we yearned for such bittersweet love.

But when sober, we still kiss our sleeping spouse, put aside those memories, and kiss our soundly sleeping child.

Ancient Orient has always been a place where hidden sorrows linger. Poems and songs, every line filled with heartbreak upon heartbreak. "Plucking mugwort, plucking mugwort, why not return?" Such touching love stories were sung even in humanity's infancy. Farewell, the earliest expectation of humans, makes waiting love always picturesque.

Yesterday, a friend broke up and was very sad. It was the first time I saw him cry, crying so bitterly, like a child, drinking much alcohol, sobbing uncontrollably as he drank. I think he must have really loved that girl.
