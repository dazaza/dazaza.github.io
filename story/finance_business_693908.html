Brand Development from a Management Perspective - 7

by rvktswr9 on 2012-03-06 20:19:57

Today, I chatted with an old colleague. He told me about taking his wife out for dinner on Christmas Eve. They saw more than a hundred people queuing up at Pizza Hut and decided to find another place nearby called B-Y-H, which is also a pizza chain store. The restaurant was less crowded, so of course there was no need to queue. In his previous understanding, B-Y-H should be a good pizza shop. However, after this meal, he told me that he would never go back to B-Y-H again. Why? B-Y-H pizzas used to be famous for their good crusts and fillings. My colleague was originally the quality control and R&D manager of Pizza Hut in Shanghai, and he has professional knowledge about evaluating the quality of pizza. He wouldn't make random comments. He told me that the quality of the pizza was very poor, with a hard crust and tasteless drinks. How did B-Y-H fall from being a brand renowned for its high-quality products to one with low quality and few customers, even during the peak dining season? I want to analyze it from two aspects.

Firstly, the operating rights of B-Y-H in mainland China are franchised. Our investors usually seek quick profits. To recover funds faster and expand operations, they immediately develop subsequent franchisees without having a mature brand operation system. B-Y-H itself has franchise stores, and its own management system is not yet mature. There are three levels between the American headquarters and the stores, resulting in a distorted copy of the management system. This is why chain restaurants must have a sound and effective system and process.

Secondly, the problems in product quality are mainly due to an immature operational forecasting system, lacking good data to support the quantity of products pre-processed at different times. Due to the immaturity of the cost analysis management system, each product's ingredient cost in the restaurant is different. Pizza, as the main product, has relatively higher costs, while other snacks and drinks have relatively lower costs. However, companies and private investors generally evaluate restaurants based on average comprehensive costs. If your pizza is good, customers will order it frequently, accounting for 70% of sales, while other products account for only 30%. How can the restaurant meet the comprehensive cost indicators? Without understanding the success rate of products, food classification cost management, and marketing strategies for different products, the restaurant cannot achieve the required cost data according to the superior requirements. The only feasible way is to discard ingredients past their expiration date or reduce the proportion of food ingredients to meet the targets set by the upper management.

Customers who love pizza will consume at different pizza brands. They assess the brand based on their dining experiences, thereby forming brand loyalty. Pizza Hut has today's customer base because, during its initial establishment when business was particularly slow, it preferred to discard semi-finished products past their expiration date rather than provide them to customers. No catering brand will establish a product discard system like Pizza Hut, KFC, and McDonald's do. Restaurant investors all plan to make a profit during the brand's establishment phase, whereas these three brands planned for losses during their establishment phase. The difference between long-term brand strategy and short-term profit strategy determines the longevity of the brand. Ultimately, the market and profits belong to those who follow this strategy. The establishment of concepts depends on the soundness of internal management and training. During Pizza Hut's loss-making period, an incident occurred at the Zhongshan Store in Shanghai where the store manager reused bread slices with mold spots. After being discovered, the store manager sincerely apologized and compensated the customers economically, severely punished the store manager, and informed and trained all operational employees. Similarly, within the company, someone questioned why the procurement department bought expensive mushroom cans instead of cheaper ones. At that time, Pizza Hut used mushroom cans produced by a large export food factory in Xiamen going to America. Pizza Hut had technical requirements for mushroom quality and evaluated the net content of the cans, so they selected this manufacturer. The price provided was 28.90 yuan per can, while Metro had mushroom cans of the same specifications for only 19.00 yuan per can, leading someone to question the procurement department. However, the company stipulated that procurement could not be done arbitrarily. First, the quality control department had to evaluate the product's quality, net content, and whether it could supply long-term. Their management of ingredients and suppliers is systematic, unlike some pizza brands whose procurement only cares about price, lacking quality control and supplier management, combined with the company's ignorance of cost management, causing the quality of ingredients to only decrease and not increase.

Can foreign catering brands succeed in China? Some people say that foreign monks chant scriptures better, but that's not always true. In Shanghai, there were once Friday's Restaurant, Tropical Rainforest, Apple Honey, and other American catering brands that have now disappeared. Why? Mr. Deng Xiaoping once said: "keep pace with the times." Exactly, Pizza Hut, KFC, and McDonald's came to China and kept pace with the times. McDonald's started selling chicken products, KFC started selling fried dough sticks, soy milk, and rice, and Pizza Hut is no longer an American fast-food restaurant; it's a leisure and happy restaurant. The restaurant decoration is like a mid-range Western restaurant, and the products are abundant, including afternoon tea, coffee, Chinese and Western dim sum, making meals from different cuisines, satisfying the Chinese palate, with colorful advertising and marketing strategies. I once discussed this issue with senior managers of these disappeared brands. They insisted that these brands operate successfully in America, and changing the business strategy would deviate from the brand's essence. However, they forgot a crucial point: China is a big catering country with its own catering culture. Foreign catering culture can only succeed by integrating Chinese catering elements and consumption habits. Success is not as simple as having capital. Is chain catering a systemic project? Today, I see (yesterday, the market spread news that Yum! Brands, the parent company of KFC, made significant progress in acquiring the chain hot pot enterprise Little Sheep (00968.HK). Little Sheep is only one step away from joining the KFC and Pizza Hut family. Moreover, Little Sheep has set the delisting time: February 2nd) this news. Little Sheep can be reborn. Without Yum! Brands, Little Sheep would surely die meaninglessly. This brand has already been on a downward trend. The founders of Little Sheep have far-sighted vision. And I can tell everyone here that under the management of Yum! Group, Little Sheep will become a world-renowned Chinese chain catering brand, successfully expanding worldwide.
