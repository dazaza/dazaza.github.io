Outside, at most, only exists in the early stages of marriage. As the length of marriage increases, romantic feelings will inevitably decrease. However, if this union is indeed of good quality, another kind of emotion will gradually grow. This new emotion stems from the original romance, and although it may not be as passionate and obsessive as romance, it possesses many factors that romance lacks. The most important ones are the mutual trust, tacit understanding in behavior, deep longing, and the sense of shared destiny formed through long-term coexistence. We can consider this emotion a form of kinship, but it differs from kinship based on blood ties. Instead, it is indeed a kinship produced on the basis of sexual love. I believe it fully deserves to be recognized as a form of love, and moreover, a mature form. To distinguish it from romantic love, I call it affectionate love. Love in marriage exists in this form. Following this line of thought, marriage is not only not the grave of love, but rather the soil for the growth of love - affectionate love.

Let's ask a married man a question: In this world, who is the woman that stirs your emotions the most? You say it's not your wife? That's normal, you don't need to feel guilty, it doesn't mean you don't love your wife. Let me ask another question: In this world, who do you think is your closest family member? You say it's your wife? Great, that's enough, it shows that you still deeply love your wife.

Love in marriage is the process of two souls continuously pursuing and attracting each other, and this process should not end because of marriage. Viewing marriage as the completion of love is a harmful concept. Under this concept, those who get married often think they have achieved great success, already obtained the other person, and no longer need to continue pursuing. However, love resides in pursuit; once the pursuit stops, love will inevitably disappear. A good marriage should keep love in an incomplete state, which means that both parties maintain necessary distance and tension. Each party views the other as an independent individual, thus always needing to be pursued again, and can never be possessed once and for all. In this state, both parties can continuously rediscover and appreciate each other, rather than being bound and becoming tired of each other, allowing love to gain space for continued growth.

In the world, there are various kinds of marriages, but truly unions based on love are not many, making them especially precious. However, paradoxically, unions based more on love tend to have their fragile aspects compared to marriages founded on traditional ethics and practical interests. What people see as ideal matches often cannot live happily ever after, and this is almost an old yet ever-new story. Investigating the reasons, perhaps it's because human inner feelings are harder to fathom and grasp than external norms and interests, and love is more changeable than secular marital bonds. Taking love as the sole basis for marriage logically implies that love is above marriage. Therefore, on one hand, if existing love has flaws, the marriage becomes problematic; on the other hand, once new love arises, the marriage must give way. Truly love-based marriages will never be completed or effortless, even the best marriage cannot obtain lifelong insurance.

Sexual love is essentially a very uncertain emotion. On one hand, it has a romantic tendency, as the saying goes, "Human nature values difficulty and undervalues ease, prefers novelty and despises the old." This psychology is particularly prominent in sexual love. People often beautify and idealize unknown and hard-to-get things, so the freshness of encounters and the freedom of breaking taboos become the main sources of sexual love pleasure. For this reason, the most unforgettable love experiences are either premarital loves or extramarital affairs. This situation cannot simply be attributed to moral defects, but also has psychological reasons. On the other hand, sexual love is a purely personal experience without objective standards. Whether one has fallen in love, whether two hearts truly resonate, and where the boundary between liking and love lies, are difficult for others to judge, and sometimes even the parties involved cannot determine. If such an unstable and unclear emotion is taken as the sole bond of marriage, the precariousness of any marriage becomes predictable.

To find a way out in the paradox of marriage, we should change our thinking and distinguish the love that serves as the foundation of marriage from romantic love. Romantic love may lead to the establishment of marriage, but it cannot serve as the enduring foundation of marriage. The foundation should be a kind of kinship developed from love, different from romantic love, which I call affectionate love. In this kind of love, romantic elements may still exist, but they have been reduced to a secondary position. The basic components are the mutual trust and empathy formed in long-term coexistence. This trust does not rely solely on good intentions, but is a capacity for mutual respect and approval in important behaviors cultivated over years, increasing with the length of marriage, giving a sense of security, and creating a solemn and harmonious atmosphere in marriage.

In fact, many families have not broken up not because they have never encountered the temptation of romantic love, but precisely because the parties value this hard-won trust in affectionate love, thus consciously avoiding such temptations, or even after falling into temptation, still make rational choices, and the aggrieved party is also willing to forgive. In my view, any marriage built on the foundation of affectionate love is not only stable, but also of high quality.

I do not deny the possibility of a new romantic love bringing a better marriage, but first, this is ultimately unknown, so it is a risk, second, even if it is true, after marriage, the new romantic love will eventually turn into affectionate love. I believe that recognizing the inevitability and necessity of affectionate love as the foundation of marriage, people will evaluate their current marital status more objectively, and once faced with the choice of staying or leaving, they will be much more cautious.

One of the important reasons why people see marriage and love as conflicting is the overly narrow understanding of love, limited only to romantic feelings between men and women. Such romantic feelings depend on some kind of encounter and freshness, manifesting as love at first sight, soul-stirring, intoxication, and inseparability. Such a feeling is indeed beautiful, but it cannot last, and this has nothing to do with marriage, even without marriage, it won't last long. Because once it lasts, any encounter will return to the ordinary, any strangeness will turn into familiarity. Trying to prolong this romantic feeling through marriage will inevitably fail, but the fault is not in marriage.
