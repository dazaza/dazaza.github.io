Six Commonly Asked Questions About SAT2 (1)

1) What is SAT2? When is the SAT2 exam held? Can I take it together with SAT1?

SAT2 is the common term for SAT Subject Tests. Unlike the SAT Reasoning Test, you can choose to take 1, 2, or 3 subjects from a variety of options. The subjects include math, physics, chemistry, biology, American/world history, literature, various languages, etc. Each subject has a total score of 800, and each test lasts for one hour. If you choose multiple subjects, the time will be extended accordingly.

SAT2 exams are available in January, March (only in the U.S. and some parts of the Americas), May, June, October, November, and December every year. However, please note that not all subjects are offered in every month. For example, world history or language exams may not be available in certain months. Pay attention when registering and planning your schedule.

The SAT2 exam time is the same as SAT1, both starting in the morning, so you cannot take both SAT1 and SAT2 in the same test date.

2) Which subjects should I take? Can I take Chinese?

First, check the SAT2 requirements of the schools you plan to apply to. Some schools don't require SAT2, while others do, and some even specify Math Level 2 + science subjects.

Chinese students commonly take Math Level 2 (don't take Level 1, as it's similar to SAT1 math but with a lower margin for error, making it harder to get a high score), Physics, Chemistry, American History, World History, and Biology (in roughly this order of popularity).

Your choice should first consider whether the exam is offered in the month you choose. Then, select subjects that match your strengths. For ordinary Chinese high school science students, choosing math, physics, and chemistry is the simplest and easiest to prepare for (details below). However, for those with weak science backgrounds or who have been away from science subjects for a long time, if physics or chemistry is your weakness (unable to handle high school level exams, unfamiliar with many theories, insufficient science foundation), you might consider taking Math + a stronger science subject + American History (USH). Alternatively, you could just take two subjects.

World History is more challenging and not recommended.

Biology requires a larger vocabulary, so most students prefer Chemistry over Biology.

If you are proficient in another foreign language, you can certainly take it, and it’s a good option. Rumor has it that foreign language exams are not too difficult. For specific advice, consult experienced seniors.

Of course, you can choose to take Chinese, but even if you score an 800, admissions officers (AOs) will likely think you're joking and throw it in the trash. (If you have a foreign passport and are bilingual, you might consider taking Chinese. I know friends who did this.)

Also, if you grew up reading Charles Dickens instead of Zheng Yuanjie, you might consider taking Literature. Few non-native English speakers take this test, but a high score can have a positive effect.

In summary, your choices should be within your capabilities. SAT2 isn't a good opportunity to study new knowledge for the sake of preparing for an exam; you can save that for AP tests the following year. Your choices should be something you can manage well, saving you the most time while maximizing your scores.

3) Why should I take SAT2? Does SAT2 have a significant impact on applications?

SAT1 is required by almost all schools, and SAT2 is also required by a large number of schools. Even if some schools don’t explicitly require it, other mainland applicants are taking it, and if you don’t, you might be at a disadvantage. Therefore, to make yourself more competitive, SAT2 has become an essential test for current applicants.

Moreover, SAT2 provides a great opportunity to showcase your ability in a particular subject. For example, if you've won awards in math or physics competitions, AOs may not understand what those awards mean, but excellent scores in Math Level 2 and Physics serve as solid evidence.

Although SAT2 scores may only account for a small percentage in admissions decisions, they are still indispensable. No one can clearly state how important SAT2 is, but good scores will certainly add value to your application. Schools like MIT place relatively more emphasis on SAT2 scores.

4) How extensive is the scope of the SAT2 exam? What is the difficulty level? What score is considered excellent?

I’ve only taken Math, Physics, and Chemistry, and the scope is basically similar to high school science subjects, with some content exceeding the high school curriculum. However, the difficulty level is quite low, roughly equivalent to the difficulty of high school final exams. If you understand the concepts, know what things are, and remember the formulas, there shouldn't be much of a problem. There's no need for complex thinking or calculations.

For Chinese students, achieving a perfect score is very normal. So your goal should be to aim for a perfect score. Scores below 750 in Math, Physics, or Chemistry would generally be considered less than ideal. A score of around 700 in American History is considered pretty good.

5) What should I bring for the SAT2 exam?

Printed admission ticket, Hong Kong/Macau Pass or passport, calculator (only allowed for Math), wear long pants, bring a long-sleeve jacket, and you can also bring snacks and water.

Most importantly, bring a clear mind.

6) Can I change my subjects on the day of the exam? Can I skip a subject even if I registered for it?

Yes. You will receive a thick stack of questions covering all available subjects. You can choose to take any subject in any order during the three hours.

(Though I haven’t personally experienced this, I suggest you avoid changing subjects on the spot. If you decide to change, didn’t you already review for the original subject? And if you reviewed, why didn’t you change earlier?)

You can abandon a subject during the SAT2 exam. Just don’t write anything on that page of the answer sheet.
