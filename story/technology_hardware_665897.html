Today, the new Women's National Football Team officially made its debut with Mexico Women's Football Team as its opponent. In theory, the three opponents of the Chinese team in this invitational tournament are arranged from weak to strong. Coach Hao Wei stated that the main purpose was to understand the recent situations of the South Korean and North Korean teams. It is worth noting that the press conference for the Chinese team lasted only 4 minutes, making it one of the shortest in history. Wei Di arrived here last night, marking his first appearance at a national-level match in over half a year.

Nearly 40 journalists applied to cover the event in Yongchuan, but by yesterday, only seven had checked in, including one local reporter from Chongqing. The cold atmosphere at the press conference evoked sighs regarding the current state of the Chinese women's football team. Coach Hao Wei seemed accustomed to this "small atmosphere" and went straight to the point: "Our overall goal is the Olympic qualifiers in 2015 and 2016, and the World Cup qualifiers. The average age of our team now is 22.84 years old, relatively young. We don't know much about the Mexican team. Both North Korea and South Korea have their own team-building goals, different from last year. With new personnel and combinations, we want to use this game to understand them better."

Subsequently, during the training session for adapting to the venue, the head coach of the North Korean team watched for a long time. Upon learning this, Hao Wei chuckled and said, "There's nothing wrong with this; I also watch their training." Some people were concerned that the tactics of the Chinese women's football team might be prematurely revealed, but Hao Wei didn't think much of it: "There’s no real need for secrecy in tactics; maybe just not letting them see set pieces."

Hao Wei is a coach who pays attention to details despite being rough around the edges. He did not bring Xu Yuan, the oldest player on the team, to the press conference but instead chose Han Peng, a newcomer from the Tianjin women's football team, signaling a clear message to the outside world: the future national team will thoroughly embrace youth. Han Peng stated, "This national team has many young players, and the new women's football team will display the spirit of a new era, bringing a different kind of match to the audience. I hope more viewers will support women's football." Slightly regrettable is that Han Peng got injured in the previous stage of training and will miss today's China-Mexico match.

A formal press conference lasting 3 minutes and 41 seconds; a supplementary interview after training lasting 1 minute and 3 seconds—altogether less than 5 minutes. Tickets for the invitational tournament cost 20 yuan, 30 yuan, or 40 yuan, allowing continuous viewing of two matches. At a ticket booth located downtown, there was no sign of any buyers within two hours yesterday morning. This is the current real "popularity" of Chinese women's football.

After the training session, the three goalkeepers engaged in a penalty shootout under the guidance of coach Li Wei. The three "beautiful goalkeeper" displayed quite remarkable foot skills, especially Zhang Yue, who scored all three penalties, leaving Li Wei utterly powerless, not only drawing applause but also raising some doubts: should Zhang Yue be assigned to take penalties in official matches? Hao Wei clarified: "No, we've already arranged for other players to take penalties in official matches." Such thoughts arose due to the concerning individual techniques of the female football players.

Yesterday's training session was relatively relaxed compared to recent ones, focusing only on basic offensive drills such as mid-field passing, crossing from the wings, and finishing shots without any opposition. Passing distances of about 20 meters often resulted in oversized ball control adjustments by the wing players, necessitating secondary corrections. In official matches, teams like North Korea and South Korea, which emphasize pressing, would not allow their opponents the time for such calm adjustments. A long-standing fatal issue for Chinese women's football has been an inability to score, seemingly due to a lack of forward strength but actually because the initial phase of the attack runs ineffectively, thus killing their own offense.

The flanks pass back to the central midfielder, who then sends through balls to the returning flank players. This tactic requires appropriately weighted passes; if too long, it equates to handing the ball to the opposing goalkeeper; if too short, it falls into the defenders' hands. According to incomplete statistics, the success rate of this offensive phase is only about 50%. Considering this was a training session without any opposition, achieving greater precision was still challenging, and in official matches, it might still be the old problem of thinking well but failing to execute.

At yesterday's pre-match press conference, Mexican Women's Football Team Head Coach Leonardo expressed, "I am very clear that the Chinese team needs a victory in this match. I also know that the Chinese women's football team is undergoing changes, and I hope to see them in the game. I can see that the Chinese women's football team is striving to return to the ranks of the world's top teams, and I hope the Chinese women's football team achieves success." A significant portion of this women's national team comes from the Youth National Team coached by Chief Coach Yin Tie Sheng. At this age, technical movements have largely solidified, and the room for improving basic skills is not substantial. The roughness of footwork may be the biggest stumbling block preventing this team from advancing towards higher goals.
