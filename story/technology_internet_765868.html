Big data, big but useless?

by anonymous on 2013-08-26 14:19:41

The development of the Internet can be measured by data, and big data has been called the new oil, a new category of assets by the World Economic Forum, with a revolutionary impact comparable to that of steam engines, power grids, steel, air conditioning, and radio.

In 2005, the total amount of data on the Internet was 3 billion GB, and Cisco estimated that the scale in 2013 would exceed that year's figure by more than 20 times. The period from the 1990s to 2005 is considered the first wave of the Internet, which brought us email, the Web, online search, and broadband. This second wave relies on big data to drive the economy.

However, there is a small problem: despite the surge in web traffic, the economy is in a recession. Thanks to the revolution in computers and the Internet, productivity steadily grew from the 1970s and continued until the 2000s. However, since 2005, it has declined, coinciding with the emergence of big data.

Therefore, some economists are beginning to question whether big data possesses the same influence as the first wave of the Internet, let alone compare with centuries of industrial revolution. One theory suggests that the big data industry makes the economy more prosperous by mobilizing existing companies to compete for customers rather than creating new fundamental opportunities.

Of course, some companies like Amazon and eBay indeed compete for customers. But other companies are eating away at traditional industries' markets, such as advertising, media, music, and retail.

Joel Waldfogel, an economist at the University of Minnesota, believes that the rise of the digital sector is at the expense of the physical world, so thinking that the rise of the digital will bring net growth to the economy is crazy. Robert J. Gordon, an economics professor at Northwestern University, thinks comparing big data to oil is just hype. In the last century, oil made the transportation revolution possible. If someone thinks personal data is comparable to oil and cars, they must not have grasped the reality of the last century (cars replacing horses, air transport replacing road transport).

But some economists believe that the economic impact of big data will come in just a few years—once universities train the appropriate data processing talent, and data-driven startups begin hiring. Of course, a recession might obscure the effects of the data revolution in ways that economists cannot grasp. Some even suspect that our current understanding of big data and "the cloud" may ultimately be nothing more than a mirage.

Data must fully realize its economic potential to matter. Of course, industries like marketing and pharmaceuticals are already using large amounts of data every day.

masFlight is one such company. It uses vast amounts of data to help airlines reduce fuel consumption and improve overall performance. Josh Marks is the CEO of this company, and although his primary mission is to help his clients compete with other airlines for customers, he also believes that the efficiency pursued by companies like his should eventually contribute to global economic development.

However, he also admits that most of the raw data streams on the Web currently have limited economic value; specialized data with deep understanding of specific industries in the hands of analysts is far more useful. He believes that the current hype about real-time processing capabilities for ultra-large datasets is somewhat overstated.

Some economists think that the true value of new technologies is often difficult to assess, and big data may have already benefited the economy without being reflected in official economic data. For example, should economists find a way to evaluate the intangible activities created by cat videos and TV shows on Hulu that bring joy to internet surfers? Do these activities contribute to GDP growth?

Additionally, infrastructure investments often take years to pay off. Shane Greenstein, an economist at Northwestern University, says that the investment in laying high-speed internet in 1990 only began to yield profits recently. However, he also points out that compared to the impact of the first wave on economic activity, the impact of the big data revolution is harder to see. This impact could either be delayed or may have no value.

But digital futurists like to compare the rise of the power grid with it. The idea is that ubiquitous Internet makes data and "the cloud" omnipresent, just as outlets make electricity omnipresent.

The comparison is tempting. Harold L. Platt's description of the dramatic increase in electricity in Chicago at the turn of the 19th and 20th centuries in "The Electric City" immediately brings to mind today's data growth.

But Platt, who is already 68 years old, does not agree with this simple analogy. He says that the revolution brought by electricity to manufacturing, family life, transportation, upper and lower society may not be replicated by the data age.

Nevertheless, Platt, receiving the interview via his mobile phone in Chicago, found himself unable to avoid this flood of data. While on the call, he received a text message from his adult child. "I have to text them back, otherwise they won't respond to me. So I have to go with the flow."
