Nokia is too early to launch tablet computers at this time.

by anonymous on 2013-08-20 14:49:09

Recently, there have been reports that mobile giant Nokia is set to launch its own tablet computer in September this year. The renowned U.S. investment information website SeekingAlpha has published an article by investor Jacob Steinberg commenting on this matter. Steinberg believes that it makes no sense for Nokia to enter the tablet market while its smartphone business has yet to stabilize and become profitable. Below are the main points of the article.

In the past few years, market observers have speculated several times that Nokia would release its own tablet. Each time, I said: "No, Nokia won't launch a tablet anytime soon because it doesn't make any sense." However, Nokia seems to have finally yielded to Microsoft, and may soon release a Windows-based tablet. I don’t think this is a good idea.

I know I've been arguing about this issue for a year, but it really makes no sense for Nokia to launch a tablet before its mobile phone business becomes consistently profitable. Nokia should first ensure absolute profitability in its phone business before considering entering the tablet market. That way, if the tablet venture fails, it can still rely on profits from its phone business to sustain operations. Currently, Nokia's phones aren't profitable, so launching a new product at this point makes no sense.

Microsoft is trying to build its own tablet, but it’s far from successful so far. Now, it seems like Microsoft wants to pass the buck to Nokia to minimize its own risks. Nokia shouldn’t take on Microsoft's burden unless Microsoft becomes a direct customer of Nokia, much like how Apple is a direct customer of Foxconn, which limits risk for the latter. Clearly, Microsoft won't sign such a contract with Nokia.

In the mobile phone market, Nokia has strong brand recognition, and many consumers will give it a chance. However, Nokia lacks similar recognition in the tablet market. A few years ago, Nokia attempted to launch its own tablet, but due to weak market demand, it temporarily abandoned the idea.

I know that if Nokia develops tablets now, their products will be much better than before. Even so, they are unlikely to be very profitable. The tablet market is getting increasingly crowded, and most manufacturers are struggling just to break even. Although Apple has lost some market share due to the emergence of low-cost Android tablets, it still captures most of the industry's profits. The smartphone market is similar to the tablet market, but Nokia has significant brand appeal in smartphones, whereas it lacks such advantages in the tablet market.

Moreover, the tablets Nokia plans to release might run on the Windows RT operating system, which isn't the most popular in the world. On smartphones, Nokia can add a powerful camera to divert consumer attention from its unpopular operating system. But doing the same for tablets is more challenging. Will Nokia equip its tablets with a super-powerful camera? And even if it does, will it be enough to attract consumers?

Developing a new product from scratch requires substantial initial investment, and Nokia's financial situation is not loose; it needs to tighten its belt. Nokia doesn't have the capital to take risks right now. Also, Nokia cannot rely on its NSN subsidiary (formerly Nokia Siemens Networks, now Nokia Solutions Networks) to balance the books or make up for failures in the smartphone market.

Please note, I haven't even delved into the specific details of the future Nokia tablet because I believe it's not important at this moment. Launching a tablet in a crowded market is risky and, even if successful, will only yield meager profits. Add to that the weaknesses of the Windows RT system, and this could be a disaster for Nokia. If Nokia's mobile phone division were highly profitable and had extra cash to fund projects that might lose money, I might support the idea of Nokia producing tablets. But that's far from the truth. In the last quarter, Nokia's Devices & Services segment had an operating profit margin of -1.2%, and in the first quarter of this year, the operating profit margin was -1.5%. Moreover, the non-IFRS operating profit margin for Nokia's Devices & Services segment was 0.1% in the first quarter and -1.2% in the second quarter. Remember, these numbers include patent fees paid to Nokia by over 40 manufacturers worldwide. In comparison, Apple's operating profit margins for the last two quarters were as high as 26% and 28%.

I’m not saying Nokia should achieve the same high profits as Apple. However, I believe Nokia should only venture into other risky projects when its operating profit margin reaches above 10%. Currently, Nokia should focus on its existing mobile phone business and map services rather than jumping into new fields. I still hold Nokia stock, but if the company announces the launch of its own tablet, I might sell them off.
