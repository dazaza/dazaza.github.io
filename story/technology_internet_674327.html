Foxconn is reported to raise salaries for its mainland employees by 16％-25％ again

by qingian007 on 2012-02-24 11:54:53

After the report came out, it aroused great response. On February 9, some apple consumers went to Apple's headquarters and speciality shops in Washington, New York, San Francisco, Sydney, etc. to hold protest demonstration activities.

Foxconn last raised wages for workers in June 2010, when it increased the salary level of workers in China by 30%.

[As a top manufacturing company in China, Foxconn provides basic pay for all primary workers in our Chinese factories far exceeding the minimum wage level set by the local government.] In this statement, Foxconn also said [In the future, we will provide more training and learning opportunities for employees, continuously improve process levels and efficiency, and increase salaries to create a good example for the manufacturing industry in China].

On the 17th, Foxconn (Hon Hai) Group announced that it would again raise the base salaries of grassroots employees in various factories on the mainland starting from February 1st, with an increase ranging from 16-25%. Foxconn stated that this was the third large-scale salary increase since 2010.

Multiple salary increases aim to set a good example for the manufacturing industry. In the statement issued by Foxconn, it was mentioned that this salary increase would raise the remuneration level of a primary-level worker from originally 1800 yuan per month to 2200 yuan if they could successfully pass the skill test. Three years ago, the monthly remuneration for primary-level workers was 900 yuan.

The timing of another salary adjustment may be dull or to respond to doubts about being a "sweatshop." From the recent public opinion environment surrounding Foxconn, the timing of this third significant salary increase appears somewhat dull. During the Chinese Lunar New Year holiday, The New York Times published a detailed report claiming that Foxconn secured orders from Apple due to its low labor costs, a factor that made it impossible for U.S.-based companies to compete, thus leading to job losses in the United States. This even prompted intervention from President Barack Obama regarding the matter. Additionally, The New York Times pointed out that working conditions at Foxconn were poor, and Apple had long been aware of this but took no corrective measures. Some other American media outlets followed up with reports and criticized Foxconn as a "sweatshop."
