How Small and Medium-sized Curtains and Fabric Dealers Can Create Profit Points

by bery201209 on 2012-03-07 16:13:58

With the continuous development of the curtains [http://www.beryhome.com/] industry, market competition is also intensifying. While some brands are increasing their market share, why do other brands struggle to boost sales? Facing pressures such as rising raw material costs and increasingly picky consumers, what should small and medium-sized curtain fabric [http://www.beryhome.com/] dealers do? What is the path to development for these dealers? Currently, where exactly are the profit points for small and medium-sized dealers?

Profit Point One: Store Retail

Many small and medium-sized dealers operate one or two stores. As a store, there will naturally be some walk-in customers with no prior business relationship. Of course, the amount of walk-in business is limited and requires time to accumulate; stores that have been open longer generally perform better than newly opened ones.

Store retail only accounts for a small portion of total sales. To grow their business, small and medium-sized dealers must transition from "sit-and-wait merchants" to "proactive merchants." Only by taking the initiative can they strengthen their distribution channels.

Profit Point Two: Residential Community Promotion

Under the guidance of some well-known brands, such as Huaren and Jiabao莉, many dealers have started promoting in new residential developments and communities. The typical approach is to hire one or two salespeople to canvas these neighborhoods. However, this method is costly, with wages and commissions accounting for about 15% of gross profits. After subtracting costs for sales personnel and advertising, the risks often outweigh the benefits. Even if capable salespeople are hired, retaining them remains a headache for small and medium-sized dealers.

Profit Point Three: Home Decoration Companies

Currently, many dealers are deliberately trying to develop partnerships with home decoration companies. In reality, this channel can be considered a "chicken rib"—not particularly profitable but hard to abandon.

Home decoration companies demand extremely low prices for paint and curtain products, resulting in only about 20% gross profit margins. Additionally, issues like advance payments, monthly settlements, and rebate requirements, along with some smaller home decoration companies frequently relocating and defaulting on payments, make this a problematic channel.

Therefore, most small and medium-sized dealers treat this channel like a hot potato.

Profit Point Four: Distribution Channels

Many brands are strengthening their distribution channel construction.

A certain curtain company from Jiangmen, Guangdong, invested heavily in 2007 to help its primary dealers develop over 4,000 county-level distribution stores nationwide, achieving a year-on-year sales growth of around 35%.

For small and medium-sized dealers with limited financial resources, this is indeed a fast and cost-effective development path.

However, due to the rapid expansion of distribution clients without subsequent support for helping these county-level distributors sell their products, customer loyalty began to weaken.

According to statistics, in 2008, the number of the company's distribution clients nationwide shrank by 30%, leading to slow sales growth that year.

For small and medium-sized dealers, following a similar path may temporarily help increase sales, but the assistance is only temporary.

Profit Point Five: Engineering Channel

Almost all small and medium-sized dealers envy the engineering channel but fear getting involved.

Engineering projects can indeed create overnight wealthy entrepreneurs, but they can also drag down a company.

Due to their lack of financial strength, social connections, and more importantly, engineering experience, small and medium-sized curtain fabric dealers find themselves hesitant when it comes to dealing with receivables, bidding processes, corporate qualifications, and on-site supervision. The entire process intimidates many dealers.
