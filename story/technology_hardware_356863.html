Yan Qinggu, the "Stephen Chow" on the Chinese drama stage - Wang Yu Ming. In "Buddha Hand Orange," Yan plays Qiu Xiao Yi; in "The Mistaken Inn," he portrays Zhang Wen Yuan; in "Hai Zhou Passes the Test," he is Hai Zhou; in "Nine Dragon Cups," he performs as Yang Xiang Wu; in "Rampage in Heaven," he brings to life Sun Wukong; in "Guan Sheng," he acts as the land god; and in "The Switched Babies," he plays Ning Zong Guan... With his painted clown face, Yan Qinggu's Beijing Opera comic characters always bring laughter to the audience.

Beijing Opera丑角 (chou jiao) art is a unique and highly aesthetic performing art. It is lively, humorous, and depicts all kinds of characters. On April 16th and 17th, the Shanghai Peking Opera Troupe will present a comedic feast titled "Clown Takes the Lead - Beijing Opera Chou Jiao Art." Veteran performers like Sun Zhengyang, Jin Xihua, Xiao Runnian, Yan Qinggu, Yu Wei, Hao Jie, Yan Runlei, Zhu Heji, among others, will take turns on stage.

Chou Jiao art reflects social phenomena behind its laughter, offering criticism and satire, while also conveying life philosophies. Thus, the artistic lives of chou jiao actors are rich and colorful. As the chief director and lead actor of this performance, renowned chou Yan Qinggu accepted an exclusive interview with our publication, humorously recounting his journey of learning the craft.

1. Learning the Clown Role at the Drama School

Unlike many Peking Opera actors who come from opera families, Yan Qinggu entered the industry through an advertisement for recruitment by a drama school published in a newspaper. "At the time, my mother saw the ad recruiting Peking Opera actors and asked if I wanted to try out. She told me that Peking Opera is a national treasure." Just entering the fourth grade, Yan Qinggu didn't know much about Peking Opera, "I had only seen the character Sun Wukong on TV, and found it quite interesting."

The entrance examination went smoothly, and at the age of 11, Yan Qinggu officially entered the drama school to begin his Peking Opera career. "The school was boarding, and everyone was excited on the first night. It was the first time living independently, and I met many new classmates." Yan Qinggu expressed gratitude for this experience, "It cultivated strong self-care abilities, exercised willpower, and allowed earlier exposure to society."

During the first semester, basic training was conducted, and in the second semester, Yan Qinggu was assigned to the chou role group. "I found learning the chou role quite interesting; the dialogues were fun, and sometimes, I would laugh while reciting lines with the teacher." Yan Qinggu mentioned that the atmosphere in the chou classroom was relaxed, and teachers would share their mischievous childhood stories with students, "But when practicing, we were extremely meticulous."

Within half a year of entering the school, Yan Qinggu fractured his hand, "It was my first time with a cast, unable to attend classes, and it was very difficult." As a chou actor, sometimes one has to portray dwarfs or play roles like Wu Dalang, requiring squatting during performances. Therefore, learning the chou role involves an important yet arduous fundamental skill—short steps.

In Peking Opera, the most crucial elements are "singing, recitation, acting, and martial arts." Listening to Yan Qinggu speak, his diction is impeccable. "In my profession, especially as a chou actor, one must use Jingbai on stage." Although similar to Mandarin, Jingbai requires a distinct Beijing accent, which Yan Qinggu jokingly referred to as a "job certificate." To help us Shanghai students speak standard Mandarin, our teachers once forbade us from speaking Shanghainese.

2. The Most Unforgettable Experiences

For Peking Opera actors, the voice-changing period is a critical juncture in their careers. After the ages of 15-16, Yan Qinggu often felt dissatisfied with his voice, "A bad voice cannot satisfy the audience, whether singing or reciting." Yan Qinggu explained, "Producing sound is abstract; the voice needs to be upright, paying attention to head resonance and utilizing every part of the body as a resonator... It sounds easy but is hard to do, requiring repeated contemplation, finding a notion, and continuous practice. This is the result of long-term accumulation and cultivation."

Not until the age of 25 did Yan Qinggu become relatively satisfied with his voice: "Only with a good voice can one feel confident." During his studies and performances at the drama school, there were two unforgettable experiences. "Both were adventures, but I persevered through them." At around 15-16 years old, during a performance of "Shi Qian Steals Chickens," when portraying a scene in the dark, he needed to perform the "spinning somersault" technique, where one spins in circles across the stage. "Spinning somersaults require space. Perhaps due to the table being misplaced, my foot caught the microphone while moving towards the edge of the stage, causing me to flip off and fall into the audience seats." The teacher watching the student performance from the side curtain panicked and almost rushed out. "I also panicked; at that young age, lacking performance experience, how did I end up falling off the stage?" Reflecting on this experience, Yan Qinggu laughed, "I was extremely nervous, so without thinking, I jumped back onto the 1-meter-high stage and continued spinning somersaults." After descending, the teacher did not criticize him but praised his quick reaction.

Another time, during a performance of "Wen Tan" at Yifu Stage, "In Peking Opera, actors' hats need to be worn tightly. Teachers usually helped me secure them firmly." Yan Qinggu recalled, "Halfway through the performance, I nearly vomited, my head went numb. That pain made your nerves and blood vessels feel blocked. My breathing became increasingly rapid, and my focus wavered." As a martial chou actor, injuries are inevitable, "Now, many joints are injured, and I dare not sit on soft sofas." Yan Qinggu said, this is a common occupational ailment among Peking Opera actors, "Sometimes, I feel like a weather forecaster; when my joints hurt, I know it's going to rain. We all prefer sunny days."

3. Studying Kyogen in Japan

Many people say Yan Qinggu resembles Liu Xiao Ling Tong, with an image fitting for Sun Wukong. "Sun Wukong is the most influential character in Chinese mythological novels. As a child, I loved listening to elders tell stories from 'Journey to the West,' admiring Sun Wukong's intelligence, bravery, and supernatural powers." Yan Qinggu inherited the Zheng style, "Founder Zheng Faxiang repeatedly studied 'Journey to the West' to shape Sun Wukong's image. He believed Sun Wukong was the unruly Monkey King who wreaked havoc in heaven and descended to the underworld, far surpassing ordinary monkeys."

To capture Sun Wukong's essence, Zheng Faxiang visited Qi Tian Temple in Fuzhou and Dafo Temple in Guangzhou to admire the statues of Qi Tian Dasheng and Doudou Shen Fo, hiring artists to replicate them and bringing them home for study. In the second half of 2001, Yan Qinggu chose to study abroad in Japan. "Traditional stage actors should not have narrow horizons." Yan Qinggu said that in Peking Opera history, figures like Mei Lanfang traveled to Europe, the United States, and Japan, participating in international artistic exchanges that significantly promoted Peking Opera's global influence. Later, Yan Qinggu began exploring Western and Japanese arts and found that the cultures were relatively close, with many shadows of Chinese traditional culture evident in their traditional arts. Thus, he decided to study in Japan for a year.

In Japan, Yan Qinggu learned kyogen at the famous Moriyama Senngoro family in Kyoto. "Kyogen is a traditional comedy with over 600 years of history. Characters typically number two or three, wearing simple costumes, with no makeup or complex stage setups, relying solely on the actors' bodies to convey meaning. In Japan, I witnessed their seriousness towards traditional arts. Even the oldest actors maintained strict discipline during rehearsals, demonstrating great rigor."

4. Cross-dressing as a Maiden

"Someone with your appearance playing the chou role seems like a waste." Hearing such comments, Yan Qinggu immediately countered, "Chou roles aren't necessarily ugly; as long as they're adorable and can express the character, it's fine. Painting a small clown face, though just a tofu block, contains patterns and lines, making it very beautiful."

Besides portraying chou roles, Yan Qinggu also cross-dressed as a maiden. This occurred during the 45th anniversary celebration of the Shanghai Peking Opera Troupe, featuring a large cross-dressing performance called "Eight Wax Temples," where Yan Qinggu played a young maid. "Although my role was minor, I was thrilled. I hadn't previously explored cross-dressing and directly acted as a flower maiden."

On the day of the performance, the theater was packed, with high audience expectations eager to see their special appearances. The backstage was bustling with everyone meticulously preparing themselves. "While applying makeup, many people surrounded me. After an hour of careful application, my facial transformation was finally complete. Next came wrapping my hair and attaching pieces, then wearing various exquisite accessories and flowers, making my head feel several pounds heavier, limiting my movements." Finally, applying white powder on his neck and hands, his skin appeared snow-white and delicate, Yan Qinggu joked that he truly indulged in being a woman, "Everyone complimented me as having a good appearance, a beautiful figure, and radiating brilliance." The performance was a success, blending laughter and applause, culminating in a climax of the entire play. Yan Qinggu said the sense of satisfaction surpassed that of playing his usual roles.

5. Rigorous Life Both On and Off Stage

Yan Qinggu on stage is witty and humorous, depicting mostly wandering knights capable of scaling walls and roofs, unrestrained and full of martial spirit. Is he similarly comedic in real life? "Thank you for using this term to describe me. In reality, I'm actually quite serious, introverted, and not adept at expressing myself." Yan Qinggu chuckled, saying his real-life persona contrasts greatly with his stage presence. He prefers tranquility and dislikes crowded places. During leisure time, Yan enjoys shopping, watching operas, plays, and exercising, including jogging and aerobic workouts. "To stay fit and maintain a good physique and condition, physical reserves are essential; otherwise, acting could easily leave one breathless."

Yan Qinggu leads a disciplined life, refraining from smoking and excessive eating or drinking. "My teacher Zhang Chunhua constantly warned me to cherish my health, citing numerous examples where poor health prevented showcasing one's artistry. Practicing is important, but so is rest."

Yan Qinggu shares a deep bond with his parents. Every time he gets injured during practice, his mother becomes particularly worried. "There were times when my mother knew I was injured and needed emergency care, and she anxiously rushed to the hospital to meet me." Yan Qinggu said sometimes after getting injured, he hesitates whether to inform his mother. "Nowadays, I am extra cautious during practice to avoid injury. I believe my body is given by my parents; any harm affects both of us deeply. While I may endure, my mother would be heartbroken."

6. English Interpretations of Traditional Plays

In the upcoming "Clown Takes the Lead" performance, veteran, middle-aged, and young chou actors will present classic excerpts from Peking Opera chou roles such as "Stealing the Silver Pot," "Hai Zhou Passes the Test," "Eighteen Parodies," "Lian Sheng Shop," "Flower Drum," and "Nine Dragon Cups." Among them, Yan Qinggu is likened to the "Stephen Chow" of the Chinese drama stage, as both have portrayed the Monkey King Sun Wukong, excelling in playing small characters, and are comedic stars known for their wit.

"I think I can't compare to him," Yan Qinggu said, "We each have our own art forms, but the commonality is that we both bring laughter to the audience." In this performance, Yan Qinggu will also sing Peking Opera in English, performing the English version of "Searching Alone to Save Orphans." Proficient in Japanese and English, does Yan Qinggu possess exceptional linguistic talent? "This is a traditional singing segment completed with English lyrics. I consider myself somewhat imitative, which is also a professional quality." Yan Qinggu said, with the World Expo approaching, everyone is learning English, and singing Peking Opera in English can be considered a trendy move.
