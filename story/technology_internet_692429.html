How to do a good job in the evaluation before patent application?

by keyizhang1 on 2012-03-06 09:35:55

A patent only has value when it serves a function. In recent years, the number of patent applications in our country has significantly increased. In just one year, 2011, the total number of domestic applications for invention patents, utility models, and design patents exceeded 1.63 million. Therefore, how to make patents worth their value and achieve appreciation as much as possible is a new challenge facing intellectual property work.

Currently, professional technology transfer agencies or intellectual property managers in enterprises are already paying great attention to the development and transformation of existing patents. However, in reality, whether a patent can be commercialized and how it can be commercialized should be considered from the very beginning of the patent application process. I believe that only by strengthening the evaluation before applying for a patent and formulating targeted application strategies can we change the passive situation of "what we have, what we sell" and create a new type of patent trading market where "what we sell, we will have."

A patent is the protection of a technical solution. Before applying for a patent, considerations include: whether the definition of the invention has been comprehensively described, how the effect is displayed, whether it is core technology or a technical improvement, etc. As the source of patents, only with innovation in technology does the patent, as a technical carrier, have a solid foundation. At the same time, the comparative advantage in technology preliminarily determines the scope and direction of patent protection.

Patent rights are the exclusive or proprietary rights granted by law to inventors or their assignees over their inventions within a certain period. Therefore, assessing the expected exclusive status of an invention is also indispensable, such as the scope and strength of patent protection, the degree of freedom to use the patent, the ability to monitor infringement, and the capacity to bear litigation costs. If after disclosing the technical solution through a patent application, it is difficult to detect others using your solution in their products or systems, or if monitoring requires significant costs, then even if the invention is technologically leading and could obtain broad protection, it may not be advisable to apply for a patent. Other forms of intellectual property protection, such as trade secrets or software copyrights, might be more appropriate.

The development stage of patent technology largely determines the cost and cycle of implementation, which is a highly concerned issue for patent transferees or licensees. Therefore, during the application stage, it is necessary to understand its status: whether it is just a concept, can be practiced, or is ready for production. If it is just a concept, continuous investment and secondary development may be required for it to be applied in the industry.

The purpose of evaluating the patent application to be submitted is to better grasp the commercial potential of the technology earlier, such as the ability to define products, expected demand, identity of end users, market size, and maturity. Whether to license it to a company of a certain scale, seek venture capital, or implement it oneself to prevent competitors from following. These all require in-depth research and investigation, and require collaboration between patent workers, the market, sales, and technical personnel. If the conclusion is positive, then investing quality resources during the patent application process, hiring experienced patent agents, deploying a complete patent portfolio, and selectively submitting international patent applications via the Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) route will all be valuable investments.

In addition, since the application and maintenance of patents require expenses, especially when submitting international patent applications via the PCT route, financial analysis is also essential. This includes R&D costs, patent costs, expected royalty income, the possibility of obtaining funding for subsequent research, product cost-sharing, etc. In fact, sometimes the result of cost analysis may indicate that obtaining a patent license from others is more economical than applying for a patent oneself. Therefore, introducing patents, digesting, absorbing, and innovating further can shorten the gap, enhance competitiveness, avoid risks, and achieve twice the result with half the effort.

In summary, I believe that the evaluation before applying for a patent is of great significance for controlling patent quality and subsequent transformation and implementation.
