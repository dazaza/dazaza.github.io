Analysis: Network information enters the fragmented era: micro-blog etc. become ties

by kunshanrencai on 2010-08-26 15:49:35

Bin Zhang, the host of CCTV's sports channel, is relatively satisfied with the viewership ratings of the World Cup matches. He said, "But how many people can watch an entire match from start to finish? I believe many people treat the World Cup as background noise. The TV is on, and they do whatever they need to do. When a goal is scored, they look over for the slow-motion replay. High viewership ratings only mean that everyone feels this summer should have the World Cup." More than ten years ago, Zhang Bin founded the 'Football Night' program. During its peak period, the program lasted four hours, then it was continuously shortened. The international football segment separated out to form the 'Global Football' program, airing every Monday evening. "That's basically a compilation of goals program. Even fans expect this - you talk less nonsense and let us see the goals quickly. The highest-rated program is still the compilation of the top 100 goals because the goals come one after another, constantly stimulating your brain."

Experts continuously describe our fragmented era - people find it hard to concentrate for long periods, children develop habits of browsing through hyperlinks, making it difficult to read a book in its entirety according to a logical sequence. Not just reading books, even leisure activities like football have become 'fragmented'. Few people can patiently watch a 90-minute game.

Another problem Zhang Bin felt was the trivialization of serious issues. In the news, he saw that the English FA began promoting a new youth training system, similar to China's 'sports school'. He wrote an article discussing this issue on his blog, resulting in only two comments. In contrast, blogs talking about celebrity gossip would receive more comments. He also started a 'Weibo' account online (http://t.sina.com.cn), "I really don't know how to use it, I don't know what to say with it."

But for many people, Weibo has become an important way to obtain information. Through the people they follow, their links, and their respective 'role-playing', they can easily know what is happening in the world and what the general public opinion looks like. The most active part of Kaixin Network is no longer small games like parking, but reposting, combining gossip news and entertainment content. The latest iPad application is Flipboard, its two most important sections are Facebook and Twitter. Users can certainly customize their information sources, but no mass media can resist social media and maintain its inherent authority. New types of social media and social networking sites bring a new type of social relationship, which in turn affects independent individuals.

The Atlantic Monthly recently published an article called 'A Death on Facebook,' written by a magazine editor. Ms. S once interned at the magazine. This lady had an extremely rich nightlife. The editor and Ms. S were merely nodding acquaintances; he suspected that a girl who frequented bars wouldn't be good at editorial work. In fact, he didn't do well either, and the magazine went bankrupt, leaving him unemployed. Initially, he could enjoy the beauty of loneliness, but soon he went on Facebook to see how his former colleagues were doing. He saw that Ms. S wanted to add him as a 'friend,' so he agreed. Soon, Ms. S's updates filled his screen. He saw Ms. S's whereabouts and pictures taken in bars. Then he saw a gentleman appearing in the photos with her. At first, the man's arm was just on Ms. S's shoulder, then he embraced her, then they sat together on a sofa in a living room. Ms. S showed all her Facebook friends the diamond ring on her hand. The editor wanted to send a congratulatory message but hesitated - I seem like a voyeur, watching her private life, but we're not really 'friends,' we've hardly spoken a few words.

Eventually, the editor had a chance to escape his online life and go to a friend's house in the countryside. One evening, he saw multiple unread messages on his Apple phone. Using his phone, he connected to the network and found out that Ms. S had unfortunately passed away. The editor recalled the changes in his life over the past year and felt a bit tearless - who am I crying for, for Ms. S? We barely know each other, we've never left messages for each other online. For her story? As a bystander, did I see a live-streamed story on the internet?

In the view of Google's researcher studying social experiences, "Social networks are not just a temporary trend, nor an addition to the currently known web, it is a fundamental change, a reconfiguration." Their research found that a large number of usage behaviors on social networks occur between strong ties with fewer people. On average, Facebook users have 130 friends, but only regularly interact with 4 to 6 people. People usually have around 150 weak ties. These 'friends' mostly belong to different circles, rarely intersecting in real life, but presented flatly online. What you say to friends in one circle might spread in another circle. Additionally, there are temporary ties on the network, including counter salespeople, those writing hotel reviews online, and sellers you encounter on shopping websites.

If we use Twitter or 'Weibo,' we will find that each of your 'followers' can form a temporary tie with you. If they leave you a comment or forward one of your tweets, this weak tie extends.

Google researchers remind us that about eight out of ten calls on our phones are still made to four fixed people, and early villages split when they reached 150 people. But we can no longer only interact with people within a 10-kilometer radius as we did 100 years ago, nor can we handle more than 150 weak ties.

Businesspeople are most sensitive to these social changes. A report released by Pennsylvania State University's Business School titled 'The Power of Twitter as Electronic Rumor' collected tens of thousands of microblog comments containing brand names. More than 50% of the content was negative, and 33% was critical. The report warns businesses that advice between close friends determines consumer purchasing behavior, and rumors on the network can significantly impact products and brands.

A market manager from a luxury car brand accepted our interview. She had just taken charge of the 'Online Marketing Department.' She said that many companies established e-Marketing as early as 2004 to conduct marketing on the Internet, but in recent years, the main focus of so-called online marketing departments has been on social networks and social media, with the core work being to do reputation marketing and ensure that their products appear positively amidst massive amounts of information.

"We know, of course, that electronic rumors are mostly negative, and negative messages spread more easily. So behind a market manager is a professional internet team. We judge which negative information comes from competitors, which are irresponsible rumors, and which are due to our own poor performance, and we handle this content seriously. On the other hand, we need to maintain visibility on social media, making it easy for consumers to find us. In fact, when you receive birthday wishes or text reminders on your mobile phone, that is part of online marketing. Our goal is to make consumers feel that we are right by their side."

Businesses will use various ties on the network to spread their brand, and one of the best methods is to post a tweet and randomly select winners from those who retweet to give away prizes. Many people simply click and forward it. To achieve better dissemination effects, participating in public welfare activities is effective: for every retweet of a tweet containing brand information, one dollar is donated to a certain charity fund. This latter method makes forwarders more conscious, and social activity researchers call it 'slacktivism.'

In October 2007, a website called Freerice launched an initiative: if you participated in interactive activities on the site and answered a question correctly, the site would donate 10 grains of rice to the United Nations World Food Program. After more than two years, they donated 157.6 million grains of rice, equivalent to 22 million bowls of food. This is a social activity characteristic of the fragmented era. Answering a question takes only a short time, and the contribution is only 10 grains of rice. Kristin Ivie's article on the social criticism website says: "We don't want the Revolutionary Armed Forces of Colombia" — this large protest against the Revolutionary Armed Forces of Colombia terrorist organization, planned through Facebook, attracted global attention, with more than 5 million people from over 100 cities gathering together. It was significant and moving! However, frankly speaking, sometimes the significance of these activities lies more in — letting us feel that we are part of this great movement.

Kristin Ivie refers to these actions organized and commented on groups online and forwarding posts as 'Activism 2.0,' distinguishing them from truly engaging in an action. He said, "Activism 2.0" is entering people's daily lives every day and is looking for a way to cultivate the habit of using technology and social media to contribute to social reform. Actually, people need only a small and convenient action to achieve — clicking the mouse.

Still on The Atlantic Monthly website, an article says that discussions and expressions of opinions on social networking sites and microblogs are all 'lightweight social postures'. This is just a random reaction after people accept fragmented information, completely different from the actions taken after deep thought. Regarding this, Mr. Ye Yincong, lecturer at Lingnan University in Hong Kong and engaged in social activity research, accepted our interview. He said, "More and more social actions are organized and mobilized through social networking sites on the Internet, especially among young people, this is a consensus. As for whether they are effective, it is still a topic of debate. My observation is that social networking sites are excellent tools, but they cannot replace substantive social organizations such as non-governmental organizations (NGOs). The networks formed by social networking sites are distributed, with unclear boundaries, adept at rapidly aggregating and spreading emotions, but not conducive to carefully handling different opinions, adjusting narratives and strategies. The recent 'Support Cantonese' campaign is an example. It lacked organization, was networked, and had strong mobilizing power, but when the government corrected the claim that Cantonese was being suppressed, the movement could not respond and adjust because there was no organization to respond."

Mr. Ye Yincong admitted that the Internet makes our lives more 'fragmented' and distracts our attention, "but this also causes young people to shift their way of paying attention to public topics. Taking social networking sites as an example, many young people do not rely on opinion leaders (Opinion Leaders), such as the media, scholars, officials, etc., to pay attention to public topics, but rather on friends in the social network. The Internet can rapidly stimulate emotions, and the enthusiasm of young people to participate in social activities will be high. This emotional aspect makes online discourse somewhat 'false.'"

Mr. Ye emphasized that the Internet provides a new environment for truth claims. Over the past few hundred years, modern society has undergone a process of disenchantment, and various traditional or theological authorities have gradually collapsed. Intellectuals have begun to attempt to re-establish authoritative discourse from the perspectives of natural science and philosophy, and truth claims have become an important foundation. This is a basic feature of the Enlightenment era. However, this foundation is usually built on individual elite reasoning or debates and consensus within small elite groups (such as academic systems, scientific communities, media, public spheres, parliaments, etc.). The Internet does not completely dismantle this foundation, nor does it stop claiming truth, but it expands this claim to a broader space that is no longer easily monopolized by cultural elites. A characteristic of this vast space is that truth claims are a more complex process, and the process of seeking truth is relatively open, so we continuously see falsehoods, errors, corrections, and truth-seeking in repeated cycles.

Mr. Ye said, "The issue of emotionalization, I think, is also a rational myth created by the Enlightenment era, or more gently put, it divides reason/emotion. There are many hidden emotional or emotional factors in the rational practices of the Enlightenment era. The key is not whether the Internet is emotionalized, but that the Internet can present emotions and rapidly reinforce them, making it impossible for rational reasoning and debate to hide emotions and feelings."

If the Internet only provided massive amounts of information, making us unable to keep up, it would seem easier to deal with. But the characteristic of this fragmented era is that your response to information plunges you into a fragmented lifestyle. Supporting Brazil in the World Cup is simple; you can tease Argentine fans on Weibo; while watching the game, you respond to Argentine fans' counterattacks on Weibo, and these emotional arguments make watching football more fun. Accepting information - providing feedback, this mechanism operates flawlessly on the Internet, occasionally cloaked in a technical outer layer. You can understand 10 pieces of news within an hour, issue 3 current affairs comments, promote two charitable donations, and give a bad review to a merchant on a shopping website, receiving a call from the merchant hoping you cancel the bad review. Dealing with information has already become your 2.0 version of action.

On August 12, Time Magazine placed a writer on the cover of all versions of the magazine after many years. This writer is named Jonathan Franzen. This 51-year-old writer previously had an essay collection called 'How to Keep Lonely', and the newly published novel is called 'Freedom'. His main point in Time Magazine is - the adult world is devaluing, we live in a childlike society, willingly accepting cultural products aimed at the children's market.
