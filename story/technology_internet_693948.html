Pokinsky and Antitumoron: A Half-Century History of Deception

by ucibv623 on 2012-03-06 20:54:33

The unethical "clinical trials" of antineoplastons

It is clear that Burzynski was a sufficiently bold individual in this aspect. Starting from 1978, Burzynski began to produce and sell antineoplastons himself without obtaining any clinical trial approval, giving patients the drug that had no clinical data and was not filed for an Investigational New Drug (IND) application. In March 1983, the FDA filed a civil lawsuit against Burzynski, and Regional Judge Gabrelle McDonald ruled to prohibit Burzynski from using antineoplastons across state lines. In May and June 1983, antineoplastons applied twice for IND but were both determined to lack sufficient clinical trial qualifications. In September 1983, Burzynski refused the FDA's audit inspection of his clinic citing lack of time. In December of the same year and February and October 1984, the FDA pointed out that his three IND applications did not meet the review requirements and thus rejected his applications. However, during this period, Burzynski continued to prescribe antineoplastons to patients without any efficacy testing or clinical trial authority.

In 1986, the Texas Board of Medical Examiners sent a letter to Burzynski questioning the efficacy and safety of his antineoplaston therapy. After two oncologists statistically analyzed the medical records of 60 patients treated by Burzynski, the conclusion was that "Burzynski's antineoplastons could not be proven effective from these results."

On the official U.S. clinical trial registration website Clinicaltrials.gov, there are 61 registered clinical trial history records related to "Burzynski" and "Antineoplaston." Among them, only one Phase II study is registered as "completed," while two are "terminated," seven are "withdrawn," one is "recruiting," and the remaining 50 clinical trial statuses are "unknown." Most of these trials with unknown status were registered within a month in March 1996, taking advantage of a congressional hearing opportunity for the FDA, with no follow-up information on whether patients were recruited, clinical results, compliance with ethical standards for clinical trials, or drug side effects. The only trial marked as "completed" (NCT00003509) is a Phase II randomized open-label study attempting to treat Stage IV melanoma without a control group. Patients were given intravenous injections of antineoplastons A10 and AS2-1, six times a day for three consecutive months.

In 1993, Burzynski was charged with using unapproved drugs on patients by the FDA. In 1998, the Texas court ruled to prohibit Burzynski from spreading false drug advertisements and ordered him to stop promoting these products to patients. Burzynski subsequently appealed on the grounds of interference with freedom of speech, but the Texas Court of Appeals upheld the original verdict, determining that "Burzynski's advertising language was illegal." The appellate court's ruling stated:

"Dr. Burzynski's patients are all gravely ill, their lives hanging by a thread. Even if there is only a glimmer of hope, they strive tirelessly, thirsting for survival, hoping even a faint chance is better than succumbing to fate. However, this desire for life can lead to irrational actions, providing an opportunity for those without scruples to take advantage, manipulating their desires for profit at the expense of lives. Texas and the FDA will make every effort to protect such vulnerable patients from being trampled upon. This court does not imply that Dr. Burzynski is such an unscrupulous person, but he is certainly not a virtuous one, failing to observe his duty to protect patients according to the law."

Burzynski's medical license was placed under supervision for ten years between 1994 and 2004. In October 2009, the FDA issued a warning letter to the Institutional Review Board (IRB) of the Burzynski Research Institute, stating that the IRB of the Burzynski Research Institute "did not meet the basic requirements set by the FDA to 'protect human subjects,'" including eight major issues with the IRB:

8. The review committee had fewer than five members.

It can be seen that the ethics committee established by the Burzynski clinic has committed almost every error an ethics committee could commit, and it would not be an exaggeration to call it utterly irresponsible for the lives and safety of patients.

In addition, the FDA's audit inspection department also found many problems with the entire Burzynski Research Institute, including incomplete informed consent documents, insufficient reliability of the drug itself, failure to comply with clinical trial protocols, incomplete and inaccurate clinical trial records, illegal use of the drug on subjects before filing for clinical trials, and failure to report adverse reactions and drug side effects as required.

The Texas Medical Board (formerly the Texas Board of Medical Examiners) filed multiple lawsuits against Burzynski in December 2010. This case will go to trial on April 11, 2012, to discuss whether his medical license should be revoked again.
