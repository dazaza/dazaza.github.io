Online Shopping Experience (Follow these points, and you'll likely buy both cheap and reliable products. I've bought thousands of items and received hundreds of positive reviews following these methods, without any neutral reviews, whether buying or selling):

1. First, look at the price of the item you want to buy. Is it drastically different from the real-world price? If the price difference is too large, it may be fake, but it could also be a seller promotion. In general, if the price difference is too large, there's an issue. Also, check if the store's items are actual photos. Actual photos are best, especially for large or expensive items. If possible, video chat with the seller beforehand. Best to communicate with the seller before buying to gauge their attitude and enthusiasm. If the seller doesn't respond quickly, you can wait (understandable, even gods aren't always available, and people have emergencies). Also, avoid haggling when deciding to buy (online shopping is already cheaper than market prices, otherwise, we wouldn't shop online, in my opinion). If you must haggle, ask about store discounts, gifts, or multi-item free shipping. Sellers will happily accept this (happy sellers pack more carefully! Most are pretty good).

2. Once in the seller's store, check if the store is well-decorated. It doesn't have to be luxurious, but at least show effort. Items should be neatly categorized with detailed descriptions and pictures. Everything should be organized. In general, stores run by diligent sellers sell trustworthy products.

3. This point is probably known by everyone—check the seller's credit and reviews. This is crucial in reflecting the quality of a product. Of course, some negative reviews don't necessarily mean the seller's product is bad—it could be a perfectionist buyer (no one is perfect, let alone things made by humans). It could also be malicious reviewers trying to get free stuff or refunds. Many sellers encounter such issues. So, we should look at the majority of reviews, as the minority should follow the majority. Everyone says it's good, so it must be good. More importantly, ensure the reviews aren't fake. How do you check? Look at the purchase records. Every purchase has a record showing the date and time. You can check the time difference between the review and the purchase to see if it's fake. Time doesn't lie. Of course, in rare cases, same-day delivery is possible for local purchases.

4. This point is crucial for finding cheap yet good products—compare multiple stores. Prices vary from store to store, and so do product quality and services. So, a lower price doesn't necessarily mean better, nor does a higher price guarantee quality. This depends on your judgment. To save more money, consider buying multiple desired items from the same store. If prices are the same for a single item, compare shipping fees. Don't overlook shipping costs—they can save a lot of money in the long run. At least, that's how it works for me.

5. This point is my personal insight—when buying items, you don't necessarily need to choose sellers with high credit ratings or diamonds. Personally, I feel sometimes choosing newly opened stores can be better. Such stores often sell items at lower prices because they usually have promotions to build credit. They value credit highly, so their after-sales service isn't necessarily worse than older stores. Their service is often enthusiastic because new sellers are excited when buyers inquire. This is a common experience for anyone running an online store. Of course, I'm not saying older stores have bad service; I'm just supporting new sellers (though if choosing new stores, prefer Paipai, as it ensures security through certified protection). However, I personally prefer stores with customer service, like Guangzhou VIPshop Information Technology Co., Ltd., because they provide professional shopping and after-sales service.

Online Store Experience (Though my store is new and my credit isn't great, here are some insights I hope will be useful. Please visit my store, thank you):

1. The first point is product quality. Whether you run a physical store or act as a reseller, ensure the quality of your products. If you're a reseller, it's best to buy a few items yourself before reselling them. This not only shows responsibility to customers but also gives you confidence in the products you sell. Visit other stores, especially those selling similar items, to learn how they promote popular items, and how they manage their stores. Learn from others' strengths to improve yourself. Knowing your competitors inside out helps you do better.

2. The second point is pricing. Prices should never be higher than those in physical stores unless you're selling directly from the origin (which is naturally cheaper). When pricing, aim for reasonable margins rather than excessive profits. Earning less per item is fine as long as you sell more—that's the essence of online store operation. Research the prices of similar items online and choose the best and most reasonable price—not necessarily the lowest. Sometimes, the lowest price raises suspicions about product quality. Choose a middle-ground price between the highest and lowest. This is the "popular price."

3. The third point is store decoration. Whether in reality or online, the appearance of your store leaves the first impression. Details matter, including the store name. A catchy, unique name with high SEO value is ideal (this is where I failed, so don't follow my example). A good store name is like a beautiful landscape—it attracts visitors and stays in their memory. Your logo should clearly indicate what you sell. Categorization is important too. Avoid using overly decorative graphics for categorization; simplicity is key. Think of Baidu's search interface—only a simple center point with blank space around it. Some might think it wastes space, but Baidu's design is user-friendly because it helps visitors find what they need quickly, saving time. We're not there for advertisements. Apply this principle to categorization. In-store ads, if any, should be simple. Add product usage instructions and selection tips in the announcement section. Include common bank accounts like Industrial and Commercial Bank, Agricultural Bank, Postal Savings, Construction Bank, etc., and leave a contact phone number. This reassures customers who don't have online banking and allows direct transfers (many of my sales have been this way, though I didn't earn credit, I was still happy). If possible, include a photo of the store owner or the physical store (though ID photos aren't necessary unless for specific purposes like dating). Overall, the more visible information you provide, the more trust you build with customers.

4. Regarding credit issues, this is something all new store owners want to know. If you're running a mall store, this concern is lessened (mall sellers probably don't worry about this either). New store owners often worry about having no credit, leading to fewer customers or constant bargaining. Bargaining mainly targets new sellers. So, don't immediately cave in when customers haggle. Don't sacrifice principles for credits by lowering prices, as this can lead to doubts about product quality or make customers accustomed to haggling. Instead, offer small gifts to discourage haggling. Running an online store isn't easy, so stick to your principles. The first month of running a store is largely a trial period. Don't worry about lack of business. You can participate in communities, forums, and groups to increase traffic to your store. During this process, you might meet potential customers. Use normal-sized or slightly larger fonts for advertisements instead of huge images everywhere, as this can annoy people (large image ads resemble spam commercials on TV, like "shocking prices" or "phones ringing off the hook"). Incorrect advertising can harm your product and store reputation. Advertising is crucial, so think carefully about how you approach it (if I ran an ad agency, you could ask me, haha). Study big brand TV ads—they're often simple and engaging, sometimes not directly promoting the product but attracting customers.
