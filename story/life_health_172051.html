Whitening: The Big Enemy of Osteoporosis?

by lk0shcs on 2009-07-07 10:42:53

Snow White remains the popular standard for beauty these days. In order to whiten their skin, women apply sunscreen daily before going out, determined to completely block harmful ultraviolet rays to prevent the formation of freckles and melanin. However, doctors warn that such a "never seeing daylight" lifestyle can directly hinder the body's absorption of Vitamin D, affecting calcium intake and increasing the risk of bone loss and osteoporosis. According to reports from Hong Kong media: A study by the University of Hong Kong showed that among Hong Kong women aged 30 to 40 surveyed, about 60% were deficient in Vitamin D due to deliberately avoiding sunlight, with half being moderately deficient—more severe than the group of women in menopause.

Exposing oneself to the sun for 15 minutes daily without sunscreen

Vitamin D is an essential element that helps the body absorb calcium and maintain strong bones. The simplest and fastest way for people to absorb the necessary amount of Vitamin D daily is to expose themselves to the sun for at least 15 minutes without applying sunscreen and not through glass; secondly, by consuming some foods rich in Vitamin D, as well as taking supplements like cod liver oil. Doctors specifically remind that relying solely on diet cannot provide sufficient Vitamin D.

In response to this, last month the Osteoporosis Center of the Department of Medicine at the University of Hong Kong School of Medicine conducted a telephone survey of 547 women over 50 years old and found that half of the women did not know that sunlight was the main source of Vitamin D, forty percent were unaware that Vitamin D benefits bones, and nearly forty percent failed to meet the minimum requirement of 15 minutes of sun exposure per day in the week prior to the interview. Most respondents also indicated they disliked being in the sun.

The University of Hong Kong tested the blood of nearly 400 men and women over 50 in menopause to understand their levels of Vitamin D and found that about 55% of the female group lacked Vitamin D, with 17.3% and 3.9% being moderately and severely deficient respectively; 68.5% of men lacked Vitamin D, with 23.2% being moderately deficient and 3.4% severely deficient. Compared to another group of women aged 30 to 40, approximately thirty percent each were mildly or moderately deficient.
