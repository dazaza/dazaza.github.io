[World Finance Report - Information] The American magazine Forbes website published an article not long ago with the title "China Is Not Yet a Superpower."

This article argues that it is difficult for China to become a superpower, and its reasons can be summarized in the following aspects:

First, Chinese society lacks ideals and concepts, and its ideological state is chaotic. American society has clear universal values representing global public sentiment, which are freedom and democracy. However, for China, Confucian thought predominated during thousands of years of feudal dynasties. The communist ideals and concepts once promoted have now been cast aside.

Second, in recent years, China's economy has maintained a high growth rate, but this growth has mainly benefited from large-scale investments in infrastructure, making it an investment-driven growth model. This model is hard to sustain over the long term. China has yet to establish an economic model proven by practice to withstand long-term tests.

Third, China's global strategy is merely a commercial one, prioritizing benefits, and its military spending is only one-sixth of America's.

Fourth, China does not have strong allies, even in Asia. Japan and India lean more towards the United States than China. In Asia, few countries wish for China to occupy a significant position on the world stage.

Fifth, China is not yet a rule-of-law country. China is a country governed by individuals, with serious corruption, favoritism, and profit-seeking. China’s future is full of uncertainties, including environmental degradation, population aging, political instability, social conflicts, and ethnic disputes.

Throughout several millennia, China was the most powerful country in the world, but due to the Chinese people's low-profile nature, Chinese influence has always been confined to a regional scope. After experiencing centuries of decline and calamity, growing and becoming strong again is something generations have aspired to achieve.

The Chinese are full of confidence but lack approachability. This is both the strength and flaw of the Chinese. Whether China can or wants to become a superpower is another matter, but some of the ideas and views presented in this article are worth pondering.

We will not discuss here whether China's economic model, defense spending, and status as a rule-of-law country are necessary conditions for becoming a superpower. The key issue is that the ideological state, corruption, and favoritism are indeed hindering China's economic growth.

In thousands of years of feudal society, Confucian thought has always dominated. The May Fourth Movement broke the shackles of Confucian thought, but new thoughts and value systems remain unclear in the minds of the citizens. To this day, the value system and behavior patterns of favoritism and profit-seeking in Chinese society are fully manifested. Such values not only fail to unite the people but also create divisions over interests, dispersing and weakening the cohesion of the nation. More importantly, Chinese society lacks a concept and perspective that connects with the world and aligns with globalization. These two aspects hinder further economic growth in China.

Economic rapid growth for a short period can be achieved through certain stimulus measures. In relatively underdeveloped circumstances, the most basic factors promoting economic growth are land, capital, and labor productivity. However, in the long run, institutional, systemic, and mechanistic factors should play a bigger role in promoting economic growth. Without a simple value system and philosophy connected to globalization, focusing solely on profit-seeking and material gain will significantly constrain economic growth when it reaches a certain level. Because the ultimate goal of economic growth is not just about the economy itself; it involves institutions, systems, and other aspects. To a considerable extent, at a certain stage of development, value concepts become more important. But even establishing advanced ideas remains a vague prospect for China!
