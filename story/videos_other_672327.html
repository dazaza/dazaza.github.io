Breast ptosis is a physiological phenomenon, commonly seen in middle-aged and elderly women who have been pregnant and lactated. It is caused by the hyperplasia of the mammary glands and connective tissues, which enlarges the breasts, followed by atrophy. As the skin and supporting structures are stretched with the enlarged breasts, their elasticity decreases. With the effect of gravity, they no longer contract back to their original state, causing the breasts to become loose and droop, shaped like a bag.

Breast ptosis can be divided into three degrees based on its severity:

Degree I: The breast sags, and the nipple is parallel to the inframammary fold.

Degree II: The nipple position is below the inframammary fold but higher than the lowest point of the breast.

Degree III: The nipple is at the lowest point of the breast. However, in some cases of severe breast ptosis, especially when there is hypertrophy at the distal end of the breast, the nipple may not be at the lowest point of the breast. This type should also be considered as Degree III ptosis.

The appearance forms of breast ptosis generally fall into three categories: spindle-shaped breast ptosis, triangular scarf-like breast ptosis, and bullhorn-shaped breast ptosis.

Spindle-shaped breast ptosis is characterized by a shortened transverse diameter of the breast base. The coronal plane of the breast base is smaller than that of the distal end of the breast, resembling a spindle, hence the name spindle-shaped breast ptosis. This type of breast ptosis deformity occurs when breast tissue herniates into the subcutaneous area of the areola region, often associated with macromastia syndrome. It is commonly seen in young and middle-aged women after breastfeeding.

Triangular scarf-like breast ptosis presents with the entire breast flattened, resembling a triangular scarf hanging on the chest wall, hence the name. The coronal plane of the breast base is elliptical, with the longitudinal diameter of the breast greater than the transverse diameter. There is no obvious herniation of breast tissue into the subcutaneous area of the areola region. It is commonly seen in middle-aged and elderly women, especially postmenopausal women.

Bullhorn-shaped breast ptosis has a basic equality between the coronal planes of the breast base and the distal end, or the distal end is slightly smaller. The longitudinal diameter of the breast is longer, and the nipple is located at the bottom line of the breast, resembling a bull's horn. The fibrous and fat tissues within the breast relatively increase, while the mammary gland tissue decreases. The breast feels denser, and the skin elasticity is better. This type of breast ptosis is commonly seen in young women.

Breast ptosis correction methods involve many surgical techniques, such as dermal fixation, breast lift fixation, double-ring fixation, etc. The basic principle is to lift and fix the ptotic and loose breast tissue and remove the corresponding "skin-areola" to achieve a normal breast appearance. However, since removing skin can leave scars, modern practices increasingly favor the double-ring fixation method for correcting breast ptosis. This method leaves only minimal surgical traces at the junction of the areola and skin, which almost disappear over time.

Correcting breast ptosis solely through breast augmentation is not advisable because breast augmentation cannot lift and fix the breast tissue and skin. It merely increases the volume of the breast temporarily, increasing skin tension. As the edema subsides, the continued relaxation of the skin and tissue and the descent of breast tissue will result in an unnatural appearance known as "double-peak breast," where the feel and appearance differ significantly.
