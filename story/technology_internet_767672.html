Twitter's longstanding problems: Niche and user loss

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 12:00:55

Donald Hovasse, a retired teacher, signed up for a Twitter account a year ago at the insistence of his daughter. However, after using it a few times, he lost interest. In this online social network, he found few friends but many celebrities.

Hovasse, who now resides in Las Vegas, said, "I just don't understand Twitter. Most of them are people I'm not interested in, and I don't care about what they say." Instead, he logs into Facebook every day to check on his friends' updates.

Twitter is expected to start trading on the New York Stock Exchange in mid-November, which has become one of the most anticipated IPOs (Initial Public Offering) in the United States this year. Hovasse's experience on Twitter serves as a warning to investors.

According to a joint survey by Reuters and research firm Ipsos, among the 1,067 surveyed Twitter users, 36% said they have accounts but haven't used them, while 7% said they had already canceled their accounts.

In contrast, among the 2,499 surveyed Facebook users, only 7% said they hadn't used Facebook's online social service, and 5% of respondents said they had canceled their accounts.

There are many reasons why users stop using Twitter, such as having too few friends who use the service or not knowing how to use it.

The user churn rate highlights an issue that has plagued this online information site over the years: although Twitter has managed to attract many well-known and loyal users, such as the Pope and US President Barack Obama, it still hasn't become mainstream like Facebook.

Making ordinary people see Twitter as an indispensable part of their lives is key for Twitter to attract advertisers and make profits.

Twitter claims that as of the end of September, it has 232 million monthly active users, an increase of 6.1% compared to the end of June. Since June 2012, the quarterly growth rate of active users on Twitter has never exceeded 11%.

When Facebook was the same size as Twitter today, its active users grew by more than 20% each quarter until its user base approached 500 million, when Facebook's growth rate dropped to 12%.

Dan Niles, Chief Investment Officer (CIO) of hedge fund AlphaOne Capital Partners, said, "Twitter is an excellent service with great growth potential. But from my point of view, I think Twitter's opportunities are less than Facebook's, and it should be reasonably valued."

Niles said, "From the data (referring to active user numbers), it seems that Twitter's fundamental revenue potential is lower than Facebook's."

Twitter's revenue in the third quarter increased more than doubled year-over-year to $168.6 million, but its net loss tripled to $64.6 million. Facebook will release its third-quarter earnings report at the end of this month, and analysts expect its Q3 revenue to be $1.9 billion.

Twitter CEO Dick Costolo once said that the company aims to become "the foundation of all global communication" and eventually let everyone on the planet use it. However, Twitter also admitted in its prospectus that "new users may initially find our product confusing."

Twitter has always been proud of sticking to its roots, i.e., messages limited to 140 characters with no other extra functions. Since the service was launched, Twitter has publicly opposed controlling how people use the platform and instead encourages users to naturally form their own habits.

Therefore, new Twitter users often find it difficult to grasp the flow of microblogs and express dissatisfaction with features such as "hashtags," basic function abbreviations (like retweets), and short links.

Larry Cornett, CEO of product strategy and design consulting company Brilliant Forge, said, "Everyone who comes here still feels confused. If they (Twitter) want to be widely accepted by users and increase daily interaction rates, they must make Twitter easier for users to accept."

According to the survey by Reuters and research firm Ipsos, among the 2,217 surveyed non-Twitter users, 38% said they do not find Twitter interesting or useful, and 13% said they do not understand the purpose of using Twitter.

Twitter has been taking measures to help new users. In December 2011, Twitter introduced a "Discover" section that displays trending topics based on users' locations and interests. Additionally, Twitter has simplified some functions and launched new tools to directly embed photos and videos into users' microblog streams, providing a richer and simpler user experience.

Analysts said these changes might mean that Twitter's user retention rate in the past few months could be better than the overall level. Brian Wieser, an analyst at market research company Pivotal Research Group, said that Twitter's current user base is already large enough to provide value to advertisers. He said that investors need to adapt to the idea that Twitter is not suitable for everyone. Wieser said, "The key question is, Twitter is a niche medium, and its appeal will never match Facebook's."

Twitter declined to comment on this article, stating that the company is in the "quiet period" before the IPO.
