Savings can be used to reinvest in yourself.

by lnov11iket on 2009-12-03 16:08:22

In the face of a "Ice Age" for full-time employment and a highly growing market for temporary and outsourced work, how can workers protect themselves to survive this cold spell? Chen Yu-fen suggests that in order to keep your job, you must first change your mindset: "It's not about choosing the job you love, but loving the job you have." Specific actions include:

1. Clearly articulate your value to the company

Regardless of whether the company is laying off employees or not, 2009 will be the time when business owners carefully evaluate the productivity of each employee. Therefore, you must make efforts to increase your own work value. The simplest way is to observe whether you can use the simplest words to express your contribution to the company in the shortest time possible. "Value" should be "quantified," even those in administrative support roles, such as the mail delivery staff, should record the number of documents they handle. If you cannot even articulate your contribution to the job, expecting your boss to do it for you might unsurprisingly land you on the layoff list.

2. Actively seek out tasks that no one else is doing

"This job wasn't assigned by the boss!" If you are accustomed to thinking this way, saying it, or even acting on it, please immediately remove this thought from your mind. Next year, companies may not fill vacancies or layoffs could lead to increased workloads. Since this is an unavoidable fact, the wise worker should take the initiative and inform the boss that they can take on more work; otherwise, silently accepting assignments from the boss and working hard is also safe. As for the least cooperative employees who complain after receiving tasks, "Be careful! Such people will definitely be placed on the layoff list."

3. Stop criticizing the company's actions

However, because companies usually have cost-saving measures during economic downturns, this is when true smart workers are tested. Smart people should demonstrate their willingness to share the company's difficulties, suppress any dissatisfaction they may feel, and remember not to casually criticize the boss or the company's cost-cutting plans.

4. Deepen your expertise with an innovative spirit

2009 is a good time to reflect on and improve your own work capabilities. Carefully consider whether the current job can be done better. Which process improvements can save time and create greater benefits for the company? Are there adjustments that need to be made to previous methods of working? What additional professional knowledge can help you perform better? It is suggested to approach your current job with the "entrepreneurial innovation" spirit and deepen your expertise.

5. Create flexibility in your work

Besides deepening your existing professional abilities, in times when job opportunities shrink, maintaining flexibility is crucial. The more flexible a worker is, the more opportunities they will have. For example, if administrative personnel worry that their contributions are hard to measure, can they apply for a transfer to the sales department to contribute performance? If a new job opportunity arises in the south, can they proactively apply to transfer from the north to the south?

6. Remember to save, store provisions to get through the winter

Although saving is not directly related to keeping your job, Chen Yu-fen reminds us that setting up a financial safety net allows you to turn crises into opportunities. Savings can be used to reinvest in yourself to cultivate professionalism, and in case of unfortunate unemployment, emergency funds will ensure that life does not fall into chaos.

*If unfortunately laid off and become unemployed, you should...

1. Quickly shake off negative emotions

Find ways to allow yourself only one week of sadness. In daily routines, be sure to go to bed early, rise early to face the sun, interact with more people, and expand your social circle, such as joining a book club, because expanding your social circle helps find jobs.

2. Seek career planning assistance from recruitment consultants

You can send your resume to various recruitment consulting firms and actively contact the consultants. Through the free consultation services provided by recruitment consulting firms, understand changes in the labor market and reassess your own value in the workplace.

3. View "dispatched work" as a piece of your personal career puzzle

If you cannot find a full-time job in the short term, you can also consider dispatched or outsourced work opportunities. Approach these opportunities with the best service attitude and professional ability. Once the economic winter passes, there will still be a chance to return to full-time work.
