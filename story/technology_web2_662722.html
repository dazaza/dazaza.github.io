Care for the first debt base QDII as a tool to disperse authority, then how about this more common category of investment to collect risks: the situation of bonds? Today I spent not long, and did some surveys. The survey was done, but the analysis is harder. The classification of bond funds still uses Morningstar's classification; funds holding more than 70% bonds are called general bond type funds, totaling 25 together. It is very clear that the higher the stock position, the better the returns over these two years. This is also very easy to understand. Moreover, it should be noted that the stock position here is the final data from the second quarter of 2007. Some funds have little change in their holding data, such as Galaxy Union Income. After one year, even if classified into conservative allocation type, its performance can still be seen. Therefore, when discussing bond funds, we must first understand the range of their stock holdings. The higher the stock holding, the higher the return, the higher the risk, and the less meaningful it becomes to use them to balance stock market risks. For example, the North Precious Yuan Bond Fund, although nominally a bond fund, had a stock position of 26% in the second quarter, thus being classified by Morningstar into the conservative allocation type, and hence not included in the list above.

Poor Country Capital and Jiazheng Bonds focus on new share subscriptions, which is written in their prospectuses, and indeed their holdings are almost all new shares. Shaoxing and Galaxy run faster because of their stock positions. Their subscription fees are also similar to stock funds.

Other funds seem difficult to summarize any rules from.

However, looking at the top four funds, with returns of 30-40% this year and 25-30% in 2006, compared to the yields of the Asia-Pacific region targeted by high-risk QDII investments, using bonds to diversify risks doesn't seem like a bad method.

Diversifying risk focuses on the correlation between investments. The greater the correlation, the more risks can be dispersed. Is the correlation between the Asia-Pacific stock market and the A-share market greater? Or is the correlation between the domestic A-share and bond markets greater? I don't know this, so the most important conclusion cannot be reached.

But bond funds could be worth considering, especially since last week bonds had a significant rise.
