Four Great Classical Love Stories of Buddhism

by vinuu on 2011-05-24 13:44:06

(1) Who Was You in the Previous Life

There was once a scholar who had agreed with his fiancée to get married on a certain day. However, when that day came, his fiancée ended up marrying someone else. Struck by this blow, the scholar fell seriously ill and no matter what his family tried, it was all in vain as he lay there gasping for breath. At this time, a wandering monk passed by, learned about the situation, and decided to enlighten him. The monk approached his bedside, took out a mirror from his bosom, and asked the scholar to take a look. In the mirror, the scholar saw a vast sea and a murdered woman lying naked on the beach. A passerby glanced at her, shook his head, and walked away... Another passerby took off his clothes, covered the corpse, and left... Then another passerby came, dug a hole, and carefully buried the body...

In his confusion, the scene changed. The scholar saw his fiancée. In the bridal chamber, the moment her husband lifted her veil... The scholar didn't understand.

The monk explained: "Do you see the female corpse on the beach? That was your fiancée's previous life. You were the second passerby who gave her a piece of clothing. She fell in love with you in this life to repay your kindness. But the person she ultimately has to repay for a lifetime is the last one who buried her, and that person is her current husband." Upon realizing this, the scholar suddenly sat up in bed, and his illness was cured.

(2) Zhu'er and Zhi Cao

Once upon a time, there was a temple named Yuan Yin Temple where many people came every day to burn incense and worship Buddha, making it very popular. On the crossbeam in front of Yuan Yin Temple, a spider wove a web. Due to being exposed to the incense and sincere offerings every day, the spider gradually acquired Buddhist nature. After over a thousand years of cultivation, the spider’s Buddhist nature increased significantly.

One day, coincidentally, Buddha visited Yuan Yin Temple, seeing its prosperity, which made him very happy. As he was leaving the temple, he casually looked up and saw the spider on the crossbeam. Buddha stopped and asked the spider: “We meet here today, which can be considered a kind of fate. I have a question for you. What do you think after cultivating for over a thousand years?” The spider was very pleased to meet Buddha and readily agreed. Buddha asked: “What is the most precious thing in the world?” The spider thought for a while and answered: “The most precious things in the world are 'what cannot be obtained' and 'what has been lost.'” Buddha nodded and left.

Another thousand years passed, and the spider continued to cultivate on the crossbeam of Yuan Yin Temple, increasing its Buddhist nature greatly. One day, Buddha returned to the temple and said to the spider: “Are you doing well? Do you have any deeper insights into the question I asked you a thousand years ago?” The spider replied: “I still believe that the most precious things in the world are 'what cannot be obtained' and 'what has been lost.'” Buddha said: “Think about it more carefully. I will come back to find you.”

Another thousand years passed, and one day, a strong wind blew, carrying a drop of dew onto the spider’s web. The spider looked at the dew, seeing its crystal clarity and beauty, and developed a fondness for it. The spider was very happy every day looking at the dew, thinking these were the happiest days in three thousand years. Suddenly, another strong wind blew, carrying the dew away. The spider instantly felt like it had lost something, feeling lonely and sad. At this moment, Buddha appeared again and asked the spider: “Spider, during these thousand years, have you thought deeply about the question: what is the most precious thing in the world?” The spider thought of the dew and told Buddha: “The most precious things in the world are 'what cannot be obtained' and 'what has been lost.'” Buddha said: “Good, since you have such an understanding, I will let you experience life in the human world.”

Thus, the spider reincarnated into a wealthy official’s family, becoming a rich young lady named Zhu'er. Time flew by, and soon Zhu'er turned sixteen, becoming a graceful and charming young woman, extremely beautiful and captivating.

One day, the newly crowned top scholar Gan Lu was invited by the emperor to hold a celebration banquet in the back garden. Many young ladies attended, including Zhu'er and the emperor's youngest princess, Changfeng Princess. During the banquet, Gan Lu performed poetry and showed off his talents, impressing all the young ladies present. However, Zhu'er was not nervous or jealous because she knew this was the karmic relationship granted by Buddha.

After some time, coincidentally, when Zhu'er accompanied her mother to burn incense and worship Buddha, Gan Lu also happened to be accompanying his mother. After burning incense and worshipping Buddha, the two elders started talking. Zhu'er and Gan Lu then went to the corridor to chat. Zhu'er was very happy, finally being able to be with the person she liked, but Gan Lu did not show any affection for her.

Zhu'er asked Gan Lu: “Don’t you remember the incident of the spider web at Yuan Yin Temple sixteen years ago?” Gan Lu was surprised and said: “Miss Zhu'er, you are beautiful and likable, but your imagination is too rich.” Saying this, he left with his mother.

Zhu'er returned home, wondering why Buddha arranged this karmic relationship but did not let him remember that incident. Why did Gan Lu have no feelings for her? A few days later, the emperor issued an edict, ordering the new top scholar Gan Lu to marry Changfeng Princess, and Zhu'er to marry Crown Prince Zhi Cao. This news was like a bolt of lightning to Zhu'er. She couldn’t understand why Buddha treated her this way.

For several days, she neither ate nor drank, pondering deeply, her soul almost leaving her body, her life hanging by a thread. Crown Prince Zhi Cao learned about this and rushed over, falling beside her bed and saying to the nearly dying Zhu'er: “That day, among all the young ladies in the back garden, I fell in love with you at first sight. I earnestly begged my father, and he finally agreed. If you die, then I will not live either.” Saying this, he picked up a sword, preparing to commit suicide.

At this moment, Buddha appeared. He asked Zhu'er's soul: “Spider, have you ever thought about who brought Gan Lu (dew) to you? It was Feng (Changfeng Princess) who brought him, and it was also Feng who took him away. Gan Lu belongs to Changfeng Princess; he is just a passing episode in your life. Crown Prince Zhi Cao was a small grass plant in front of Yuan Yin Temple thousands of years ago. He observed you for three thousand years and admired you for three thousand years, but you never lowered your head to notice him. Spider, I ask you again, what is the most precious thing in the world?”

After hearing these truths, the spider seemed to suddenly gain great enlightenment. She told Buddha: “The most precious thing in the world is not 'what cannot be obtained' and 'what has been lost,' but the happiness that can be grasped now!”

As soon as she finished speaking, Buddha left, and Zhu'er's soul returned. She opened her eyes, saw Crown Prince Zhi Cao about to commit suicide, immediately knocked the sword away, and embraced the prince deeply...

(3) A Thousand Years of Waiting

There was a young and beautiful girl from a wealthy family, talented in many ways. Her family's threshold was almost worn out by matchmakers, yet she still refused to marry because she was always waiting for her ideal partner.

One day, she went to a temple fair to relax. In the crowded crowd, she caught a glimpse of a young man and knew in her heart that he was the person she had been waiting for so long. However, due to the chaotic and crowded scene, she could not approach him no matter how hard she tried, and finally, she watched helplessly as her beloved disappeared into the crowd. Afterwards, the girl searched everywhere for this young man, but he seemed to have vanished from the earth, never appearing again. Disheartened, she spent each morning and evening praying to Buddha, hoping to see that man again. Her sincerity moved Buddha, who then appeared to fulfill her wish.

Buddha asked her: "Do you want to see that man again?"

"Yes, even if I only see him for a moment, it would be enough!"

"If you have to give up everything you currently have, including your loving family and happy life, would you?"

"I am willing," the girl replied with determination.

"You must cultivate for five hundred years to see him once, will you regret it?"

"I will not regret it," she answered resolutely.

Thus, the girl became a large stone, lying in the wilderness. For four hundred ninety-nine years, the wind and sun beat down on her, but she did not consider it bitter. What troubled her was that for over four hundred years, she hadn't seen a single person, unable to see any hope, which almost drove her to despair. In the last year, a stone quarry team came, selected her, chiseled her into a stone slab, and transported her into the city. It turned out that a stone bridge was being built in the city, and the girl became the guardrail of the stone bridge. On the very first day the bridge was completed, the girl saw the man she had waited for five hundred years! He hurriedly crossed the bridge, naturally unaware that a stone was staring at him intently. This man disappeared again.

Buddha's voice appeared again: "Are you satisfied?"

"No! Why am I the guardrail of the bridge? If I had been laid in the center of the bridge, I could have touched him, touched him lightly!"

"Want to touch him? Then you need to cultivate for another five hundred years!"

"I am willing!"

"Will it be painful? Will you regret it?"

"I will not regret it!"

This time, the girl became a big tree, standing on a busy road where many people passed by every day. The girl watched every day, but this was even more painful because countless hopes were met with countless disappointments. If it weren't for the five hundred years of cultivation before, the girl would have broken down long ago! Days passed by, and the girl's heart gradually calmed down. She knew that he would not appear until the last day. On the last day, the girl knew he would come, but strangely, her heart was no longer excited. He finally came! Still wearing the white robe she liked best, his face was still so handsome. The girl stared at him foolishly. This time, he did not rush past. Because it was too hot, he noticed a big tree by the roadside and decided to rest under it. He came under the tree, leaned against the roots, closed his eyes, and fell asleep. The girl touched him, and he was right next to her! But she could not tell him about her thousand years of longing. She could only try her best to gather the shade to shield him from the scorching sun. The man only took a short nap because he had other things to do. He patted the dust off his robe, looked back for a moment before he left, gently touched the trunk, and then walked away without looking back!

When the man gradually disappeared, Buddha appeared again.

"Do you still want to be his wife? Then you need to cultivate further."

The girl calmly interrupted Buddha's words: "I do want to, but it is unnecessary."

"Oh?"

"This is already good enough. Loving him does not necessarily mean becoming his wife."

"Oh!"

"Is his current wife suffering like me?" The girl reflected.

Buddha slightly nodded.

The girl smiled faintly: "I can endure it, but it is unnecessary."

At this moment, the girl seemed to notice Buddha sighing slightly.

The girl was somewhat surprised: "Does Buddha have worries?"

"To be good is better. A boy can wait for you a thousand years less. To see you once, he has already cultivated for two thousand years." Buddha's face bloomed with smiles.

(4) A Merchant's Four Wives

Once upon a time, there was a man who had four wives. His fourth wife was deeply loved by him, and whether sitting or standing, he was inseparable from her. His third wife was obtained after much effort, and he often sweet-talked her, but not as much as he pampered his fourth wife. His second wife frequently interacted with him, comforting each other like friends. They were content being together, and when separated, they missed each other. As for his first wife, she was practically a servant, shouldering all the heavy work at home. She endured various hardships silently, occupying almost no place in his heart.

One day, this man was about to go abroad for a long journey, and he asked his four wives: "Will you go with me?"

The fourth wife replied: "I don't want to go with you."

The man hated her lack of affection and called his third wife: "Can you accompany me?"

The third wife responded: "Even your most beloved fourth wife doesn't want to go with you, why should I?"

He then called his second wife and said: "Can you accompany me on this trip abroad?"

"I have received your kindness and can see you off to the city gate, but if you want me to accompany you abroad, I'm sorry, I can't agree," she replied.

The man also resented the second wife for her lack of emotion and asked his first wife: "I am going abroad, will you go with me?"

The first wife answered: "I left my parents and entrusted myself to you. Whether in joy or sorrow, life or death, I will not leave you. No matter where you go or how far, I will definitely go with you." The three wives he usually cherished did not want to go with him, so he had no choice but to take his first wife, who was not his ideal companion, and leave the capital.

In fact, the foreign country he was going to was the world of death. The husband with four wives symbolizes human consciousness.

The fourth wife represents the human body. Humans love their bodies as much as the husband loves his fourth wife. However, when the end comes and life ends, the soul always carries the blessings and sins of the present life, leaving alone and lonely, while the body collapses and cannot accompany them.

The third wife is no different from worldly wealth. No matter how hard one works to accumulate treasures, none of it can be taken with them when they die.

The second wife represents parents, spouses, siblings, relatives, friends, and servants. People living in the world love each other, miss each other, and are reluctant to part. When death approaches, they cry and send them to graves outside the city. Not long after, they gradually forget this event and throw themselves back into the hustle and bustle of life.

The first wife is the human heart, inseparable from us through life and death. It is so closely related to us, but we easily overlook it, focusing instead on the illusory physical form.

These four stories are highly philosophical, causing sighs and reflections. The love in this world truly makes people unable to extricate themselves and lose their way. In fact, if one can see the truth behind it, it is nothing more than the balance between gains and losses. Just as Buddha said in the story of Zhu'er and Zhi Cao: seize the happiness in front of you and cherish love, perhaps that is the best destination. The love that people pursue is not achieved by one-sided wishes or silent waiting. There is a destined connection, and once encountered, one must learn to seize and cherish it.
