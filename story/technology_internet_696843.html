[Qingdao Auto] Extend Tire Life: 10 Small Tips to Reduce Friction

by qssqwe215 on 2012-03-08 11:06:34

Whether the car driving method is appropriate or not has a great relationship with tire wear and driving safety. Good and proficient driving skills can effectively reduce tire wear, extend the service life of tires, and at the same time improve driving safety. How to reduce tire wear? I think:

(1) When starting the vehicle, don't be too aggressive. The release of the clutch and acceleration (pushing the choke) should be properly coordinated, allowing the car to start smoothly and naturally without shaking, avoiding excessive tire wear due to dragging against the ground.

(2) When boarding a ferry, use first or second gear and avoid sliding onto the ramp before starting, preventing damage to the tires. After disembarking, if climbing a steep slope or encountering muddy roads, choose a better road surface and use first gear to pass, preventing slipping and avoiding tire spin which can damage the tread.

(3) When going downhill, control the speed appropriately based on the steepness, length, and road conditions. Maintain a safe distance of more than 50 meters from vehicles ahead and stay to the right. In areas with limited visibility, pay attention to the braking of oncoming vehicles and sound the horn as a warning. On long and steep descents with narrow and dangerous roads, always be prepared to stop or yield. Use light braking or engine braking to control speed down the hill. This not only avoids or minimizes emergency braking and reduces tire wear but also ensures safe driving.

(4) When going uphill, try to use inertia. Change gears appropriately and shift in a timely manner. Do not let the car come to a complete stop before restarting to avoid rolling losses in the drive wheels and accelerate tire wear. Ensure that third, fourth, and fifth gears are not forced, and first and second gears are not overly rushed.

If the trailer cannot ascend and restarting proves difficult, block the rear wheels of the trailer with triangular wood or stones to prevent it from rolling back. Then slightly reverse the main vehicle to create a gap between the trailer hitch and the main vehicle's hook, temporarily releasing the main vehicle's traction. Allow the main vehicle to restart and then pull the trailer. If this method still fails, remove the hook and connect the main vehicle and trailer with steel wire rope, keeping the rope slightly bent. Start the main vehicle first, and when the steel wire rope approaches being taut, appropriately increase the throttle to pull the trailer and start moving.

(5) When turning, operate according to the road conditions, turning radius, traffic density, and vehicle speed. Generally, slow down appropriately and avoid high-speed turns to prevent cargo tilting to one side due to inertia and centrifugal force, causing single-sided tire overload and drag, accelerating tire wear, or even causing rollover accidents.

(6) On rough roads, choose the road surface to reduce the impact between the tires and the road surface, avoiding damage to the components and tires. Also, slow down to avoid tire bouncing and severe vibrations.

(7) When driving in complex situations, such as turning, overtaking, passing through busy urban streets, villages, intersections, narrow roads, railway crossings, etc., the driver should maintain an appropriate speed and pay attention to the road surface, pedestrians, and vehicle dynamics, always being ready to brake, reducing frequent braking, and avoiding emergency braking to lower tire wear.

(8) When driving through road maintenance construction areas, such as road resurfacing and rock blasting for rerouting, where the road is covered with sharp rocks but must be passed, use low-speed slow travel to choose a suitable road surface, avoiding excessive tire impact and severe punctures or scratches.

(9) When passing through muddy dirt roads or snow and ice-covered sections, observe beforehand and choose relatively firm and less slippery areas to pass through.

If the wheels slip, stop immediately, try reversing, and select another route forward. If reversing also causes slippage, remove the mud around the slipped area or lay stones, sand, or straw under and around the vehicle to help it pass through. Do not harshly accelerate, forcing the vehicle beyond obstacles, to avoid the tires sinking deeper and spinning in place, generating intense heat, and causing serious cuts or scratches on the tires and sidewalls.

When driving on very slippery surfaces, install anti-slip chains on the drive wheels. If no chains are available, ropes can be used as substitutes. Avoid using emergency brakes and foot brakes on these surfaces. If braking is necessary, preferably use handbrakes. If foot brakes must be used, apply [point braking] to reduce vehicle speed. Use low gear when going up and down slopes, especially uphill, ensuring continuous progress to avoid stopping midway.

(10) During the journey, when parking en route or at a station, develop the habit of safe coasting. Choose flat, clean, oil-free ground for parking, avoiding stones pressing against the tires and contact with oil. If the vehicle is overloaded, ensure that the frame does not deform and lift the rear wheels to prevent excessive deformation caused by heavy pressure.
