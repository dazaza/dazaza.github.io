**3 Discussion**

**3.1 Incidence of Escape:** From Table 1, it can be seen that during this stage (1994.1 ~ 1999.6), the incidence rate of escape was 1.71%, which is close to the domestic incidence rates of two other psychiatric hospitals (1.84% and 1.8%). However, compared to a retrospective study (1995 to 1996) conducted in two wards of a more closed-end managed mental hospital in Wuhan, the incidence of escape under open management is higher. (Note: The comparison involves both open and closed-end management control.)

The causes include both subjective and objective factors. Objectively, the hospital provided a large and comfortable free space (approximately 15 acres) for patients, which inadvertently increased the chances of escape. Subjectively, managers and staff were still in the exploratory stage of the new management model, and the care system needed continuous improvement.

**Table 2: Escape Time Distribution**

Occupational and recreational therapy times are from 8:40 AM to 10:40 AM and from 3:10 PM to 4:30 PM. During these times, all four wards are open, allowing patients free access to the large courtyard, playground, and recreational therapy activity rooms.

Open meal times involve collective meals in the cafeteria.

**3.2 Escape Methods:** Common methods of escape include doors, canteens, garden walls, wards, climbing trees, etc., with the door accounting for up to 18 cases (35%). The main reason is that our patients do not wear patient clothing, and there is no fixed staff at the porter position, often leading to mistaken identity and letting them escape. To eliminate this weak link, since 1999, the door porter has been assigned responsibility, and since then, there have been no further instances of patients escaping through the door.

Secondly, the back door of the cafeteria was another place where patients successfully escaped. Now, the rear doors and windows of the cafeteria have been reinforced with safety nets, putting an end to this approach.

Thirdly, the original courtyard wall of the hospital was too short, making it easier for tall, flexible patients to climb over and escape.

Fourthly, the back door of the ward, located outside for drying clothes, also served as a convenient way for patients to escape. This is related to both the lack of consideration by the staff and the overall hospital design, prompting us to designate specific areas for patients to dry clothes.

Fifthly, due to the need for greenery in the courtyard, many trees have been planted. Some of these trees have grown tall and, being close to the walls, provide patients with the convenience of climbing to escape. This reminds us that we must consider both preventing patients from escaping and maintaining the garden's greenery.

**3.3 Escape Times:** As can be seen from Table 2, patient escapes mainly occur during occupational and recreational therapy times (66.7%), describing the liberal freedom of time and space given to patients under open management, providing opportunities for patients to flee. Therefore, enhancing outdoor inspections is a necessary prerequisite for implementing open management. Additionally, the number of escapes during breakfast is higher than during lunch or dinner, probably mainly due to fewer nurses being present during breakfast times. Furthermore, the number of escapes is inversely proportional to the length of hospital stay, as found in the survey. There were 22 cases within one week of admission, 14 cases within two weeks, significantly reduced to five cases within three weeks, only one case within four weeks, none between 5-7 weeks, and started again after eight weeks (under 247).
