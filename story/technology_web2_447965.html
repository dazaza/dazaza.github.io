You should be both a skilled negotiator and an expert in being "sly". Success or failure may depend solely on whether you hold onto the last card. In battlefields, whoever can aim at the opponent amidst swirling sands has the chance to pull the trigger first. Whoever still has a "trump card" up their sleeve when the opponent is completely exhausted will win the final victory on the battlefield. Therefore, smart commanders always reserve a strong force as a reserve team when deploying troops, never risking everything by using all their forces too early. The hands of ever-victorious generals always have a "card" left.

In business, just like on the battlefield, trade negotiations and project collaborations must not let the other side fully understand your situation, allowing the opponent to predict that you have no other options. Because once each party understands the other's situation, they will increase the intensity of their attack, striking at the other's vital points and directly taking the "main camp." Remember a fable: long ago, the cat was the most powerful animal in the forest, with unmatched martial arts. The tiger always wanted to replace the cat and seize its throne, so it went to the cat for tutelage. The cat happily accepted the tiger as a disciple. Unaware that the tiger came to take over the throne, the cat diligently taught the tiger martial arts every day. A clever fox saw through the tiger's strategy and informed the cat. Enraged, the cat feigned calmness and continued teaching the tiger without reservation. The tiger's skills became increasingly formidable. One day, the tiger created trouble and wanted to eat the cat. The cat quickly climbed a tree, leaving the tiger dumbfounded. The tiger asked the cat, "Why didn't you teach me how to climb trees?" The cat angrily replied, "If I had taught you everything, I would be finished!" The cat preserved its life because it held onto the last "card."

You should be both a skilled negotiator and an expert in being "sly."

Whether in trade negotiations or project cooperation, businessmen need to command with authority, much like soldiers on the battlefield who wear "helmets" and carry "shields," otherwise they are courting death. Although a win-win result may be achieved during negotiations, such a win-win outcome is necessarily the intersection found after both parties have competed, within their own bottom lines and common interests, based on mutual sincerity in reaching an agreement. Each party will strive to maximize their own benefits, as the saying goes, "The world is bustling with people seeking profit." Thus, negotiation is also a battle without gunpowder. To win this battle, one must be "sly," though this slyness should not be apparent to others.

In Chapter 49 of "Romance of the Three Kingdoms," Zhuge Liang predicted that Zhou Yu would harm him after borrowing the east wind at Nanshan Mountain, so he arranged for Zhao Zilong, one of the Five Tiger Generals, to wait below the mountain. Indeed, after Zhuge Liang borrowed the east wind, Zhou Yu said, "This person possesses the ability to alter heaven and earth, and supernatural techniques! If we leave him alive, he will be a disaster for Eastern Wu. It's better to kill him early and avoid future troubles." He then sent Ding Feng and Xu Sheng to take Zhuge Liang's head. If Zhuge Liang hadn't reserved Zhao Zilong as his "card," we wouldn't have seen the series of brilliant stories about Zhuge Liang that followed.

Success or failure may depend solely on whether you hold onto the last card.

During negotiations, you must not let the other side drag you around everywhere. You should strive to understand the other side's bottom line rather than hastily declaring or revealing your hand. In other words, don't "invest" too much in the early stages; keep enough cards in reserve. Don't rush to reveal your last card after a fierce struggle. If you do, what you reveal won't be just a card but possibly the last piece of "modesty cloth" torn away, leaving you "naked." Afterward, even if the other side is willing to cooperate with you, you will no longer have the upper hand at the negotiating table, and they might even look down on you.

Therefore, success or failure often depends on whether you hold onto the last card. Never reveal all the cards in your hand, as the game can end prematurely, and the other side may play new cards at any time. Until the critical moment, don't reveal your strongest trump card. The ultimate winner is the real winner. Sometimes, there is no definitive answer to what the last card in your hand is. At that time, it depends on your experience and wisdom, as well as your ability to perform under pressure.

Often, your last card remains unused until the deal is done, turning into a dead card. This is actually fine, as it might be the best outcome where both sides achieve a win-win situation while saving face.

Maintain a proper distance appropriately.

A memorable love should be one filled with longing and concern due to distance. You should maintain a relationship with the other person that is neither too close nor too distant, ensuring that each date feels fresh and full of unexpected surprises. To keep your romantic partner interested in you, you must ensure that there are parts of you that remain unclear and mysterious to them during the period of romance. Similarly, in business negotiations and project cooperation, you should give the other side ample room for imagination, letting them have some dreams about you. Business ideals cannot be absent, but they also cannot be excessive; the key is to maintain the right balance.

Renowned master of crosstalk, Hou Baolin, also said, "Maintain a proper distance appropriately," which makes a lot of sense. Maintaining a proper distance means keeping a certain distance from the other side, not being overly familiar, as negotiation is not a matter of life and death. Leaving room means keeping the other side interested in you and preserving your cards, so you are not driven to extinction. However, your "hesitation" should not cause too much doubt in the other side or make them question your sincerity. Therefore, you should leave room for yourself and the other side, even if you are a seemingly approachable negotiation expert (as mentioned in the previous content). Appropriately, leave some maneuvering space for yourself and the other side. Because if you get yourself into a situation where you can't back down, this embarrassment will subtly change the psychology of both sides in the negotiation, potentially tipping the scales of victory and defeat. Unless you are sure that driving the other side into a dead end benefits you and you can fully control the situation, you must always leave room for the other side.

The crucial role of the last card also manifests in choosing the right timing to play it. Exactly when to play it varies depending on the individual. The basic principle is not to lose focus and play cards recklessly, especially at critical moments. Secondly, be vigilant against hidden opponents or third parties playing "ambushes." Regardless, you must not be the leading man under the spotlight, fully exposed to others. Meanwhile, if the other side is in the dark and you lack sufficient information about their bottom line, it could be dangerous. By then, you might find yourself, after this noisy and flag-waving competition, sending the "lady" into the bridal chamber while unknowingly becoming the "maid-in-waiting."

Remember: you must always hold the "ace" in your hand, not easily revealing it. It should be unseen in daily life, occasionally showing its prowess, only then can you remain undefeated in the battles of the business world.
