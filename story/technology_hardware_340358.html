"The Last Will of Old Dog Sanmao" Author: Jiang Zidan

That afternoon, if someone had walked down one of the streets of this southern coastal city, they would have seen me and my owner - a frail dog and a sturdy middle-aged man - sitting on the stone steps by the roadside in a posture of mutual dependence. To be more precise, I depend on him for survival because I feel that my emaciated body is so light that it could be blown off the tree of life by the wind of death at any moment. My owner obviously realized this and kept me warm with his arms, hoping to preserve a little more warmth in my icy trunk. We sat there quietly; he smoked while I panted. On this ordinary afternoon, no one paid attention to us, nor did anyone know that we were experiencing a moment of parting and impending death.

To tell you the truth, I haven't eaten for several days, even though my owner tried every possible way to feed me and gave me appetite-enhancing medicine, I just couldn't eat anything. I wanted to pretend to have a big appetite to make them happy when I saw their anxiety, but my stomach wouldn't cooperate. If I ate too much, it would throw it all up. My appearance quickly became gaunt, and my mind was somewhat confused. Finally, my owner called the doctor to give me an injection. When they injected me, I was very obedient and endured the pain silently. Don't look down on me as just a dog; I understand everything. I know that if the situation wasn't critical, my owner wouldn't have given me injections or medicine. He was trying to save my life, so no matter how painful it was, I had to fully cooperate. Otherwise, I might really die, which means I would enter eternal darkness alone and never see my owner again. This is what I fear most.

A small bottle of medicine refreshed me for a day or two, but as soon as the medicine stopped, it didn't work anymore. The medicine was administered intermittently, and the mood of the owners fluctuated like a rubber band. One day, my owner finally couldn't help but ask the doctor, "Can you estimate how old this dog is?" The doctor looked at my teeth and said, "It looks like he's at least over ten years old, a very old dog." After a moment of thought, my owner decided not to inject me anymore. Perhaps he saw that my front legs were already swollen from the injections, and my condition hadn't improved fundamentally, realizing that the medicine couldn't bring me back to life. He didn't want me to suffer anymore.

My owner picked me up and said to me, "Sanmao, let's go pick up footprints." So, we went onto the street.

I didn't quite understand what picking up footprints meant at first, but whatever my owner suggested, I would follow without objection. This was the tacit understanding between my owner and me over many years. Despite this, I was still curious about picking up footprints. My owner was very considerate towards dogs and understood my intentions immediately when he saw me struggling to look up at him. He then explained in detail the meaning of picking up footprints to me. It's a human legend that when a person dies, their soul will retrace the path they took in this life, picking up their footprints along the way. This allows them to review their entire life. But I am a dog, and whether a dog has a soul is still questionable, so he had to personally take me to places we usually go to give me the opportunity to recall our happy times together before I pass away. I licked the back of my owner's hand with the tip of my tongue to show that I understood what he said. My tongue was already dry and had no saliva, and touching anything caused fiery pain, but I still had to do it as usual. This was the main way I communicated with my owner, and I didn't want him to think I was so confused that I couldn't understand what he said.

Just as we were talking, I saw Nuo Nuo, the white puppy from the opposite building coming. As soon as it saw me, it immediately pulled hard on its leash, dragging its owner toward me. I suddenly felt very annoyed and quickly closed my eyes and buried my head. I didn't want another dog to disturb me and my owner at this time. Dogs don't hide their feelings; no one has ever seen a sad dog pretending to be happy or a happy dog pretending to be sad. Nuo Nuo clearly saw my state and seemed unhappy. It rushed over and started shouting at me. Didn't it see that I was already skeletal and on the brink of death? Fortunately, my owner understood me and told Nuo Nuo, "Sanmao can't play with you today; it's sick." Nuo Nuo listened and reluctantly grumbled twice before leaving reluctantly. I sighed in relief and licked my owner's hand again. I wanted to tell him not to let Nuo Nuo know about our footprint-picking, it was our secret.

Picking up footprints means reviewing one's life, and I have many secrets that will be taken with me when I die. My background is a mystery even to my closest owner. He once stared into my eyes and asked, "Sanmao, can you tell me where you came from?" I really wanted to answer his question, but I can't speak human language and cannot accurately express a complicated process, so my background remains a secret. By now, you may have guessed that I am a stray dog of unknown origin. If you've read the comic book "Sanmao's Wandering Story," hearing my name, you should already guess this point.

Dogs have memory, there's no doubt about that. I still remember clearly the night seven or eight years ago when my former owner started his small truck intending to leave but didn't plan to take me with him. For us dogs, there's nothing worse than not being able to go out with our owner. Dogs don't have a sense of time; each minute feels eternal to us. If you leave alone, we'll think you've left us forever and become depressed. That's exactly how I felt at the time. I barked and chased the small truck out of the alley where my owner lived. He noticed me, stopped the car, and told me to go back. I pretended to turn around and walk back, but when he sped up again, I followed behind. This time, I didn't dare bark, and he didn't notice me. When I chased the car lights onto the street, I realized something was wrong. There were countless cars with tail lights running all over the street, and I couldn't distinguish which one was my owner's small truck.

I panicked and ran aimlessly on the street, freight trucks' strong headlights blinding my eyes completely, leaving me unsure of the direction home. Mud covered my body, and I drooled uncontrollably from exhaustion, quickly becoming a strange-looking stray dog. I ran and ran, sniffing with my nose until it bled, trying to catch the scent of home. I realized I would probably have to join the ranks of unowned street dogs. Before, when I sat in my owner's small truck, I would always worry about these homeless dogs weaving through traffic with disheveled fur, but I never imagined that my own carelessness would destroy my once-happy life in an instant. I regretted it so much, my intestines turned green with remorse, and I wanted to cry loudly. But who could hear a dog's sobs?

When I was at a loss, a purple-red station wagon stopped near me, and a man and a woman got out. They were probably what people call a couple. In the dark, I couldn't see their faces clearly, but I heard the woman say, "This little dog seems panic-stricken, it must have lost its way home." The man added, "Let's take it home, otherwise it might get run over by a truck." The woman said, "How can we catch it? It might be scared of strangers." The man said, "Try anyway." He whistled and approached me, holding a large plastic bag. I knew he was using it because I was covered in mud and didn't want to dirty his car. I stood there without moving, hesitating for a moment before deciding not to run. I was too exhausted to run anymore, didn't know where to go, and instinctively judged that these two were kind people. Everything that happened afterward proved that my decision was correct. If I hadn't voluntarily followed the sound of the whistle and jumped into the bag, I might have been crushed under a wheel long ago and wouldn't have made it to this day of picking up footprints. Later, my new owner often introduced me to friends saying, "Sanmao is not particularly smart but very conscientious." I was somewhat dissatisfied with this evaluation because I've never been unclear in important matters; following the wrong car light was purely accidental.

So, I was lifted upstairs in a plastic bag, my heart still pounding. I worried that they might have only impulsively brought me home, and once they realized how much trouble keeping a dog would cause, they might change their minds. Sure enough, my worries were soon confirmed, but the problem wasn't with humans—it was with a cat.

Even before entering the house, I smelled the scent of a cat, an odor that makes us dogs uncomfortable. In theory, dogs and cats are the earliest and most successfully domesticated animals by humans, and they interact frequently, yet their relationship has never been particularly harmonious. I believe the responsibility mainly lies with the cats. Compared to us dogs, cats are petty-minded, devious, and often act self-righteous, leading people to believe they are more independent and decisive. Cats are wary of dogs, especially when a cat has already gained the owner's affection. It will fight tooth and nail to prevent another dog from entering the family, as doing so easily causes it to lose favor.

Unfortunately, I, who had narrowly survived a disaster and was still shaken, encountered just such an unpleasant cat. This chubby white cat wasn't some valuable purebred; it just looked fairly pretty. But it relied on having lived with its owners for over ten years, feeling like the king of the household. As soon as it saw me, it immediately started howling loudly, its fur bristling, its eyes flashing green with hostility. It also extended its sharpest weapon—its sharp claws—making it clear it was a difficult opponent. What frustrated me was that the kind couple didn't realize their cat was full of malice toward me; instead, they thought I had scared it. Actually, considering my unclear identity and my pitiful appearance, how could I possibly pose a threat to it? I'd be lucky to flatter it. But people tend to act emotionally, and with their long-standing bond with the cat, they naturally favored it. The female owner held the cat in her arms and comforted it, but it wouldn't stop until I retreated further and further, burying my head between my arms in the corner without moving. Only then did it calm down slightly. I knew that before the kind couple decided how to arrange for me, any unfriendly gesture toward the cat would be detrimental to myself. Sure enough, I heard them discussing, roughly planning to keep me at home for a few days to see if the cat could accept me. If it could, that would be great; if not, they would find another family for me. I glanced at the white cat with my peripheral vision and saw it also perking up its ears to listen to the owners' conversation. This truly worried me. Don't assume that since we can't speak human languages, we can't understand human speech. This cat had been living with them for over a decade, almost becoming a spirit. If it learned that the owners intended to decide my fate based on its attitude, things wouldn't end well.

The arrangement for that night was somewhat satisfactory to me. They gave me a bath and fed me a sausage. Although my stomach was starving, I tried to restrain myself, taking tiny bites to avoid appearing undisciplined. Before sleeping, they made a surprising decision: to lock the cat on the balcony and let me stay in the bedroom, so I wouldn't be afraid alone in an unfamiliar place. I was so grateful that tears almost welled up. I quickly found a non-intrusive corner to lie down, remaining silent and barely breathing. The two people on the bed soon emitted steady breathing sounds, likely already asleep. However, I stayed awake all night, thinking anxiously. How could a dog unable to control its own destiny sleep peacefully? Similarly, the precious white cat didn't sleep either. With my acute hearing, I heard it scratching the door and meowing loudly on the balcony, jumping up and down on the windowsill. I speculated that the cat must be suffering more than me right now, and I suddenly felt apologetic. Originally, it had lived happily with its owners, but my arrival disrupted the balance. I decided that the next time I saw it, no matter how ferocious it acted toward me, I would respond kindly and not hold a grudge. What could I do? I was a dog after all.

At that time, I didn't know why I thought this way. It wasn't until I moved to my later owner's home and he read me some books about dogs that I understood it was due to our inherent nature. One book called "Understanding Your Dog" stated: A dog's true nature is the innate need to be friendly to others. Calling this friendliness an "instinct" isn't an exaggeration because it's hard to suppress. The book used greyhounds as an example to illustrate a dog's natural friendliness. Greyhounds are the fastest dogs, capable of reaching speeds of sixty-four kilometers per hour. Though they are naturally gentle, they have never received human affection because people believed that racing dogs shouldn't have emotional attachments. Therefore, they spent their lives confined in narrow cages except during races. Once their short racing careers ended, they lost their utility and couldn't be kept as pets, so they were all destroyed. A compassionate person, distressed by this horrifying sport, rescued them and was astonished to find that these neglected, abused, and discarded dogs still looked at everyone approaching them with trust and friendliness, displaying an almost supernatural ability to forgive.

Let's return to that sleepless night. I lay quietly in the dark, waiting for dawn, missing my previous owner and his shabby little truck. I often accompanied him on cargo runs, enduring long and boring journeys together. Despite his difficult life, he never forgot his responsibilities to me. Whenever he had meat, he would set aside some for me to eat, and he never forgot to bring me the bones and scraps from meals shared with friends. Some people say that only rich people treat their pets well, poor people basically lack sentiment and shouldn't keep pets, which is entirely prejudiced. Based on my lifetime of experience, I can confirm that whether a person treats animals well has nothing to do with wealth; it depends on whether they have a kind heart. Unfortunately, I accidentally got separated from my poor but kind owner, and I truly hate myself for it.

As the curtains began to brighten, I heard the couple turning and talking in bed, immediately rushing over to greet them by vigorously wagging my tail at the side of the bed. This is the most common way we dogs show affection to humans. Perhaps because their beloved cat never showed affection this way, this action delighted them greatly. The woman said, "This dog is so sensible! Let's keep it and name it Sanmao." The man replied, "If we can keep it, that would be great, but we'll have to see if the cat accepts it." Over the next few days, they arranged multiple meetings between me and the white cat. Not only did the cat show no signs of reconciliation, it grew even more hostile because I slept in the bedroom while it slept on the balcony. Worse, its expression changed. It stopped baring its fangs and instead sulked far away from me and its owners, its green round eyes gleaming with resentment. Whether its cohabitants of many years or even I could bear to look at it, it evoked pity. Humans have a saying: "An aggrieved army is bound to win." The white cat was precisely such an army. For several days, it not only maintained a pathetic demeanor but also refused to eat or drink, looking sickly and losing weight. Its owners couldn't bear it anymore, fearing it would develop fatal depression. They urgently made phone calls, trying to find a good home to send me away. I could only accept this fate. Who would have thought I'd meet such sentimental but not loyal people, and such an experienced but stingy old cat? Besides, I'm just an ordinary dog. What virtue or talent do I possess to steal someone else's beauty? Thinking from the perspective of the old cat, sending me away was reasonable.

Thus, my owner was summoned by their phone calls to my presence, and from that day on, I became his dog, beginning a life of living together with them for nearly eight years.

Initially, the relationship between me and my owner wasn't particularly close; we both had divided loyalties. Later, I learned that my owner had brought me home at the request of a friend but hadn't yet decided to permanently take me in. It wasn't that he disliked me, but his deeply ingrained Confucian ideals became an obstacle in this matter. Confucian scholars agreed with the idea that indulgence in material pleasures leads to moral decline, and a proper scholar should not indulge in a life of leisure and pleasure. My owner was a serious writer and also a proper scholar, busy with both work and writing. Suddenly feeding a dog seemed somewhat inappropriate. No wonder at first, he always avoided walking me twice a day, acting furtively and lacking confidence. When he met acquaintances, he would always explain that a friend had picked up a stray dog that couldn't live with their cat, so they were temporarily fostering me. Watching and listening, I felt very uncomfortable. Scholars have these kinds of flaws; they always seek justification for everything, unlike my previous owner, a simple laborer who did whatever pleased him. My old owner openly expressed his love for me, sometimes even kissing me in front of others. Thus, on several occasions, a longing for my old owner overwhelmed me. When my current owner was chatting with others, I would seize the opportunity and sprint off into the street. Luckily, my owner always caught up with me in time. From then on, whenever he walked me, he always remembered to attach a leash to prevent me from getting lost again.

My behavior might have made my owner think I was ungrateful. Seeing that I was always distracted, he asked me, "Sanmao, was your previous life so wonderful that you're not content here?" I barked at him, indicating that he was wrong. Frankly speaking, the conditions in this home were much better than the previous one. Every meal included meat, and they bought a special straw basket for me to use as a bed. When they discovered fleas on me, they immediately went to the pet store to buy imported shampoo to bathe me. But why couldn't I forget my old owner? Wasn't it because the old owner accepted me wholeheartedly? My owner mistakenly thought I preferred wealth over poverty, showing that he was inexperienced in raising dogs and didn't understand us.

I wished I could recite a passage to prove the loyalty of dogs to him. It was something I heard on the radio with my old owner while we were on a delivery trip. Even though those words were spoken by a 19th-century American, I memorized them after hearing them just once. He said: Regardless of wealth or poverty, dogs are always with their masters. In the cold winter winds and snowflakes, they are willing to sleep on icy grounds as long as they can follow their master. They are willing to kiss hands that offer no food and lick wounds and pains brought by the cruel world. They guard beggars as they dream, just as they would a king. When all friends abandon us, the dog remains faithful. When wealth disappears and reputation is ruined, the dog continues to love us as the sun moves across the sky. When the final moment comes and death embraces the master, no matter if other friends scatter, the noble dog can always be found at the master’s grave, head resting between its paws, eyes filled with sorrow, but still vigilant, loyal, and sincere until death.

Listen, how beautifully he spoke! These are all the heartfelt words of dogs, and West, the person who said them, if he wasn’t a talking dog himself, must have been sent by heaven to speak for us. But at the time, my owner apparently hadn’t heard this broadcast, and even if he had, without personal experience, he would find it hard to believe.
