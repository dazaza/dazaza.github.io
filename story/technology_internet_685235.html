[Qingdao Community] The Focus of Rights Protection for New and Used Cars is Different

by qssqwe215 on 2012-03-01 14:20:49

For many consumers who are preparing to buy a new car, the car purchase contract provided by the merchant seems very complete when viewed from both sides. However, behind these seemingly rigorous contracts, there are many pitfalls that are hard to guard against. A small difference in wording could lead to vastly different outcomes in维权 (defending one's rights). Consumers buying second-hand cars have also reported troubles after purchasing a used car, with the costs almost matching the price of the car.

Stakes involved, cannot overlook a contract

In August last year, Mr. Wang bought a new car at a 4S store. At the time, the salesperson indicated that they would give away window film, and the words "give away window film" were also written on the purchase contract. However, after using the vehicle for a week, Mr. Wang found that the film was only applied to the two side windows of the new car. After negotiating with the 4S store, the in-store salesperson stated that although the contract indeed wrote "window film," it did not specify whether the front windshield needed to be covered. They insisted that applying the film does not include the front windshield. This behavior is an example of the dealer switching concepts.

The importance of a car purchase contract goes without saying. Some merchants sometimes manipulate the contract to gain more benefits or avoid responsibility. Consumers must be extremely cautious when signing a car purchase contract with a merchant.

First look at the contract then sign it—this is the most basic point. However, many people think "signing a contract will definitely involve looking at the contract first." In reality, in many car purchases, the soon-to-be car owner directly signs the contract after verbally agreeing with the dealer, assuming that the details have already been included in the contract. Reviewing the contract merely involves skimming through it without carefully checking each clause. Moreover, since there are many clauses in the contract, some contents are vaguely summarized, making it impossible to detect issues with just a general skim. After the owner signs, they discover they've been put at a disadvantage when conflicts arise. Therefore, the contract clauses must be carefully studied, and the detailed parts must be rigorously written into the contract.

The delivery time must be specified in the contract. Some dealers often leave the "delivery time" blank when signing the contract, which is very disadvantageous for consumers. If the dealer takes a long time to deliver the car, there is no penalty policy for the dealer. For the consumer, waiting a long time for the car without any compensation is problematic. Additionally, besides clearly specifying the delivery time, clear breach of contract responsibilities must also be noted to constrain the merchant. For example, if the car cannot be delivered on time, the merchant should compensate the consumer in a "double deposit refund" manner.

Vehicle information is a relatively easy content to be "switched". When signing the contract, consumers need to carefully check the vehicle information, including brand, car identification number, engine number, car code (chassis number), etc. They also need to verify major configurations, color (specific to seats), manual or automatic transmission, as well as accompanying documents, to prevent difficulties in defending their rights if vehicle components have issues later.

If it involves projects handled by the dealer, consumers need to pay special attention when signing the contract, especially clarifying the prices. For instance, a simple item like "registration fee" may also include smaller items like fuel fees and tolls, which need to be listed one by one to avoid having no evidence to rely on in the future.

Special attention needs to be paid to the details of the gifts. Salespeople, in order to attract customers, will often offer additional decorations or other discounts, which is a common situation. However, few people know that when signing the contract, these gift contents should also be written in, and the name, quantity, price range, etc., should be clearly defined. Importantly, the deadline and breach of contract responsibilities must be clearly written, not just verbally agreed upon.

When the contract involves vehicle quality issues, it often just uses "[new car follows manufacturer standards]" to gloss over. Once consumers find out that the purchased vehicle is a stock car or a refurbished car in the future, it is difficult to defend their rights. Therefore, when signing the contract, it should be noted that "[the car shall not be a refurbished car or a stock car]," and it should be agreed that if it is later found to be a refurbished car, stock car, or has collision marks, the buyer has the right to return the car. Additionally, if problems are found when picking up the car, they should be noted on the contract or refused on the spot to avoid future troubles.

Defending rights is not difficult, but second-hand cars require comprehensive inspection

Mr. Lin recently bought a second-hand Santana car. During the transfer process, he was informed that the car had multiple speeding and red-light violations records, and a fine of more than 4000 yuan must be paid to handle the relevant procedures. Since the contract did not mention whether the car had traffic violation records at the time of purchase, and the seller was nowhere to be found, Mr. Lin had no choice but to accept his misfortune.

The second-hand car market is inherently complex with low insurance coefficients, but this does not mean that the rights of second-hand car owners are not protected. If infringement occurs, active defense of rights is still possible. To avoid trouble after buying a car, it's best to check all items as much as possible before the purchase. Here, the reporter summarizes several common issues in second-hand car transactions.

Incomplete vehicle procedures. Complete procedures for a second-hand car should include: vehicle registration certificate (or original purchase invoice), driving license, vehicle purchase tax payment certificate, road maintenance fee procedures, and compulsory insurance, etc. If the vehicle registration certificate and driving license are missing and not promptly replaced, the car might be a "black car." If there are outstanding fees for compulsory insurance and road maintenance fees, the buyer will incur a large additional expense. In second-hand car transactions, if the seller cannot produce all the procedures for any reason, the consumer should be on high alert at this point.

Many consumers judge the condition of a second-hand car based on the mileage meter when selecting one. Sellers often take advantage of the buyer's mindset and tamper with the mileage meter. The mileage meter of a second-hand car can only serve as a reference. Based on the previous owner's purchase time or factory release time, one can determine whether the mileage data is normal usage. If the mileage is significantly lower than normal usage.

Many consumers focus too much on the "heart" of the car while neglecting the body's health when buying a car. Sellers precisely exploit this psychology by replacing engine parts, making the vehicle's "heart" appear younger to raise the price. If the old car has been repainted and there are repair marks around the front face, rear, and A, B, C pillars, the car likely has had an accident and probably has "external injuries."

In the second-hand car market, Fukan, Jetta, and Santana are the most favored by consumers, but beware of "commercial-to-private" vehicles mixing in. So-called "commercial-to-private" vehicles refer to cars previously used for taxi operations that enter the second-hand car market through illegal channels, masquerading as non-commercial vehicles. These cars usually appear newer, have shorter usage periods, and offer extremely tempting prices.

There are many second-hand cars on the market with unprocessed traffic violation records. Once such cars are purchased, the consumer has to "take responsibility" for the previous owner's traffic violations. As in the above example with Mr. Lin, some people believe that if the second-hand car is cheap enough, spending an extra 4000 yuan can be acceptable. However, if the amount of the violation record is higher, it could result in significant losses. Therefore, when buying a second-hand car, besides paying attention to the completeness of the contract and procedures, it's best to ask knowledgeable friends to help oversee the process.
