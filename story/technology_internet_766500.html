Google Ex-Staffer's Take on Twitter IPO: Exodus May Happen

by anonymous on 2013-09-18 21:34:28

Introduction: Twitter announced this week that it has submitted IPO documents to the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC). Subsequently, former Google employee Hunter Walker wrote an article on LinkedIn recalling the changes before and after Google's 2004 IPO. He pointed out that public companies attract a completely different type of employee—professional career individuals—but many older employees before the IPO may leave the company.

The following is the full text of the article:

Twitter announced this week that it has submitted Form S-1 to the SEC, marking the first step in its IPO process. This document remains confidential for now, so discussions about Twitter's financial status are currently based only on speculation. Twitter has become my most important social messaging platform where I can share ideas, find interesting new voices, discover quality content, and get a glimpse of what the world is thinking. I use other products too, but I live on Twitter.

The more than 2,000 employees at Twitter will be excited about the next chapter the company is about to embark upon. Besides bringing them substantial wealth, becoming a public company also represents a significant milestone symbolically.

I joined Google at the end of 2003, and Google filed its S-1 form in April 2004. There were many guesses and discussions inside and outside the company about Google's early development, but until this document was made public, no one knew how powerful a business model Google had created. During my interview, the Google HR department was vague about the value of the shares I would receive. In fact, they only told me the number of shares I would get. Since I didn’t know the total number of outstanding shares or Google’s enterprise value, knowing just the number of my shares didn’t mean much. But all they said was, “Trust us.”

So, what changed at Google after its IPO, and how might these changes affect Twitter?

**Data Confidentiality**

Before going public, Google was very open with data internally. We could access almost real-time revenue data through various means, and multiple tools were available to manage daily operations. Going public meant complying with regulatory requirements, so after Google went public, many tools required login credentials to use, and only a small portion of Google employees could access these data due to work needs.

If you submit an application to access this data, the approval process is usually very lengthy. Larry Page, Sergey Brin, and Eric Schmidt remained committed to being as open as possible, but our sales director could no longer publicly announce revenue figures at Friday meetings.

Google took many measures to ensure internal transparency, but some changes were inevitable after the IPO. If Twitter employees aren't prepared for this now, they'll soon find that they must use tools and data based on their job functions.

**Acquisition and Recruitment Capabilities**

It's unclear what Twitter's stock price is right now, but Twitter's stock could be very valuable in the future. After going public, liquid stock becomes a powerful tool for recruitment and acquisitions. Candidates can compare the total compensation offered by Twitter with their current employer's compensation.

From an acquisition perspective, Twitter may use cash or stock for future acquisitions. Google typically used cash for acquisitions but also used stock in the acquisition of YouTube.

**A Different Type of Employee Will Join**

After the IPO, it will be difficult to call Twitter a startup anymore. The reality is, they're no longer a startup: there's no start-up risk, they have thousands of employees, and generate hundreds of millions in revenue. After the IPO, this positioning will influence the composition of applicants. There will be fewer adventurers among the applicants and more professional career individuals.

This isn't entirely a bad thing; having more experienced leaders is crucial for maintaining stability within the company. Twitter has recruited a lot of talent from Google to build its management team. However, the corporate style of Twitter will change.

People sometimes ask me if Google started accepting "B+" level candidates after the IPO instead of insisting on "A" level talent. My answer is no. Google still recruits top talent, but the skill sets of these people differ. They are more practical and focused on scale. Google's founders worked hard to maintain the company culture, and during the first few years of Larry Page's tenure as CEO, he likely wanted to bring Google back to its early 21st-century state.

Twitter CEO Dick Costolo and his team will face similar challenges. Some people will reminisce about the "good old days," but they must also realize that Twitter in 2014 is very different from Twitter in 2008.

**Talent Drain**

In the six months following an IPO, companies often experience a talent drain, after which the flow of talent stabilizes. Many older employees who earn substantial income due to the IPO will choose to leave. Because many Twitter employees hold stock options, the company's IPO will allow these stock options to be converted into cash. It's worth noting that not all of this money will go directly into their pockets; they must also pay the corresponding taxes.

Since I have many friends at Twitter, I'm happy about Twitter's IPO. However, as a user of Twitter, this could be a bittersweet situation because going public means the company needs to be more stable, and Twitter might change the product users love in pursuit of growth.

When Google went public in August 2004, after a long registration process, there was a short period where people clapped and calculated their net worth. But soon after, everyone returned to work.
