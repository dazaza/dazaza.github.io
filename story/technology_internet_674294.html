Clinical Analysis of 38 Cases of Gastrointestinal Diverticulum

by ncpimqudxsx on 2012-02-24 11:38:03

Clinical analysis of 38 cases of gastrointestinal diverticulum: One case involved duodenal diverticula and jejunal diverticula with gastrointestinal bleeding patients, who received medical therapy and surgical treatment. The hospitalization period lasted from 3 to 10 days, during which they were cured. This article discusses the mechanisms and characteristics: a diverticulum forms as an acquired hernia when the mucosa and submucosa of the digestive tract protrude through a defect in the muscular wall, caused by increased intestinal pressure. Diverticula can be both true and false; the difference between the two is that false diverticula lack the muscularis layer. Duodenal diverticula are more common among digestive tract diverticula, accounting for 70% of all digestive tract diverticula, mostly single, with multiple tumors being rare. They are more commonly found around the ampulla of Vater, and are slightly more prevalent in males. Jejunal diverticula are rare, more frequently seen in the upper part of the jejunum, often multiple, with no significant difference between men and women. These conditions are more common in middle-aged individuals.

Clinical features:

(1) Most patients are asymptomatic. Symptoms only occur when concurrent diverticulitis causes abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, or even ulcers, bleeding, and perforation.

It was pointed out that in cases of multiple jejunal diverticula, such as Ahemeier's report of 62 cases, the diverticulum wall may contain heterotopic gastric mucosa or pancreatic tissue, leading to peptic ulcers, secondary hemorrhage, and perforation; wide and short diverticula often cause intussusception; diverticulitis fibers may lead to reverse volvulus or intestinal obstruction and compression.

(2) Multiple diverticula in the jejunum can result in chronic dyspepsia or dyskinesia associated with small intestine propulsion. The main part of the lesion in the jejunum shows uncoordinated strong movements that impede the small intestine, resulting in an obstructive state.

(3) Residual fluid in the duodenal diverticulum can compress the common bile duct and pancreatic duct, causing common bile duct inflammation and pancreatitis.

(4) Malabsorption syndrome: Residual intestinal contents in jejunal diverticula can lead to bacterial overgrowth, consuming vitamin B in the chyme and inducing megaloblastic anemia. Additionally, it can decompose bile salts, affecting fat absorption and causing steatorrhea.

Diagnosis: For middle-aged patients with diet-related abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting without a certain degree of timeliness and periodicity, especially in cases of upper and lower gastrointestinal bleeding with unexplained complications, this disease should be considered first. X-ray barium meal and gastroscopy examinations are important for diagnosing this disease.

Treatment: Medical treatment only alleviates symptoms. Usually involves peptic ulcer plus postural drainage, antibiotics, and maintaining water and electrolyte balance. If there is bleeding, perforation, or peritonitis, surgery is required.

Prevention and management experience of Class II and III abdominal wound infections (Report of 336 cases): Hai Yang Dongyu, Central Hospital of Nanyang, Henan Province, China 473009

Abdominal surgery involving digestive system tumors, cavity rupture, perforation, obstruction, inflammation, or abscess is still a common and frequently-occurring disease in clinical practice. Class II and III incision infections remain a common complication. A total of 336 cases of Class II and III incision infections were reviewed, and the following report outlines the experience and understanding gained:

Clinical data: General information: Among the group of 336 cases, there were 187 male and 149 female patients aged 3 to 81 years, with a median age of 43 years. There were 198 Class II incisions and 138 Class III incisions. The operation time ranged from ~6 hours, averaging 2.1 hours. Eight cases had operation times exceeding 6 hours. There were 27 cases of diabetes, eight cases of emphysema, and 18 cases of moderate to severe malnutrition. Out of the entire group, 212 patients underwent emergency surgery, while 124 underwent elective surgery.

Perioperative management: While actively preparing the patient for surgery in the hospital, comprehensive understanding and evaluation of factors that could cause wound infection were made. Blood glucose was controlled between 5.6 to 11.2 mmol/L, airway patency was maintained, and preoperative enemas and parenteral nutrition support were provided. During the surgery, protection of the wound was emphasized, especially in cases where abdominal pus in the peritoneal cavity might overflow and pollute the incision. After closing the peritoneal cavity at the end of the surgery, the wound was thoroughly washed with a 3% hydrogen peroxide solution and normal saline. Gloves were changed, and the skin around the wound was disinfected, followed by stamping with a dry surgical towel before suturing the layers of tissue tightly. Precise hemostasis was ensured, leaving no dead space. Wound blood transfusion catheters could also be chosen to cut more than one side wall of the hole for smooth drainage. Before closing the abdomen, if necessary, sutures were applied. Postoperatively, the type and size of the wound determined the choice of abdominal zone to reduce wound tension and maintain wound stability, preventing wound dehiscence and promoting healing. Nutritional support was provided to patients postoperatively, and factors susceptible to infection, such as diabetes, were managed. Effective antibiotics were empirically applied based on the patient’s incision type, type of surgery, wound size, intraoperative, and postoperative conditions. In addition, high concentration (80%) oxygen masks were used during and after the surgery for a short term.

Results according to the diagnostic criteria for infection cuts: Grade healed 229 cases, l2 cases of injury, infection of Class III cuts 1:39 cases, Class II cuts 1:33 cases; 10 cases of emergency surgery, and 2 cases of elective surgery.
