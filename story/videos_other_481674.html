Speaking of the moonwalk, the legend of Michael Jackson comes to mind. Do you still remember his classic Yuppie style? Dancing the moonwalk with a black billycock. The white shoulder patch on his black shirt became the symbol of Michael Jackson. He had a special love for shoulder patches and shoulder straps. If you keep an eye on his dress, he wore them every time. Just as shoulder patches and shoulder straps were to him, so cufflinks are to men.

From the Gothic Renaissance to the Baroque period, the European upper-class men who were into appearances already thought that ordinary ribbons couldn't match their noble cuffs perfectly, so cufflinks came into being. They hammered out a thin chain in precious metals, both sides of which were fixed on buttons of various materials and shapes. According to the interpretation of the Europeans, cufflinks were first a romantic piece of jewelry for upper-class men and then developed into a grade label for gentlemen. Such as the cufflinks worn by "Father of the US," George Washington, during his inauguration, which were collected in the Smithsonian Institute, Washington, D.C. Prince Charles is also a fan of cufflinks. In Britain, the most famous commemorative cufflinks are Platinum Cufflinks, which bear an inscription of the abbreviation of his name and his wife's.
