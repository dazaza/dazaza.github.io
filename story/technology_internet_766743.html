Innovation on the iPhone that you can't see

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 20:49:42

"Isn't it just about replacing the Home button?" Many people, upon seeing the iPhone 5s, immediately thought of this question. But such a question could also be applied to the iPhone 5: "Isn't it just about making the screen longer, lighter, and thinner?"

Then, if you ask those who bought the iPhone 5 now, they still love their phones very much. Yes, because many underlying innovations are invisible innovations that can influence things quite significantly. Who doesn't desire to use their tools with great ease and comfort?

For instance, only now have some people realized that the touch screen response speed of the iPhone 5 is 2.5 times faster than Android devices.

According to Agawi's TouchMarks test, the response speed of the iPhone 5s screen is 55 milliseconds, while the iPhone 4's is 85 milliseconds; as for Android devices, take Samsung's flagship model Galaxy S4 as an example, its screen response speed is 114 milliseconds. For Windows Phone devices, take the Lumia 928 as an example, the response speed is 117 milliseconds.

If you feel that when playing WeChat games on your Android phone, the plane seems not to keep up with your finger movements, then the above is the reason. "Milliseconds" is already a relatively microscopic unit, but sensitive human bodies can still perceive the difference - just like the refresh rate of computer monitors, which can also be felt by people.

Place your hand on the screen and then expect your operation to get the correct response. This is the basic experience in today's touchscreen phones, so even a slight improvement in this area is a meaningful enhancement.

As for the iPhone 5s, a Home button with an extremely complex design is solely for improving the speed and accuracy of fingerprint recognition. Speed and accuracy, compared to the stainless steel ring around the Home button, are hard-to-see innovations. And there are many other unseen innovations that Apple has made on the iPhone 5s, such as Oliver Bonaventure's recent discovery that iOS 7 began supporting a new technology called Multipath TCP developed at the beginning of this year.

As we know, for the Internet, TCP is a very important fundamental protocol that determines how data is transmitted over the network. Multipath TCP is an improvement to this complex protocol, aiming to increase the number of paths for TCP connections. Simply put, if your phone is connected to Wi-Fi and synchronizing data, even if the Wi-Fi suddenly disconnects, Multipath TCP can ensure the normal connection between applications and the network without affecting the data being transmitted.

The significance of Multipath TCP lies in the fact that regardless of the network environment, whether it's 3G or Wi-Fi, it ensures that the phone can send data normally. In short, it keeps the machine almost permanently "online," ensuring the normal connection between the machine and the network.

Users will find that the data on their phones will definitely sync to other devices, and this improvement in reliability greatly enhances the experience of network synchronization services or applications with synchronization features; for developers, with such a mechanism, they don't have to waste time explaining to users why the data cannot be synchronized.

This is an improvement that Apple doesn't intend to explain much, but it is still very important, both for users and developers. Therefore, even though the appearance remains consistent, the changes it brings are still different, determined by "unseen" innovations.
