In the middle is Bezos

What's it like to be friends with a billionaire? Brad Stone might have an answer for you. He's a contributor to Bloomberg Businessweek and has interviewed hundreds of "current and former" friends of Amazon founder Jeff Bezos.

Stone's new book, "The Everything Store: Jeff Bezos and the Age of Amazon," will soon be published. It is said to tell the story of how Amazon became a company that sells everything, but from some excerpts published in Bloomberg Businessweek, it seems that the employees under Bezos have had a tough time.

Amazon is a store that sells everything. More specifically, it's a store that competes with Walmart, a device manufacturer that rivals Apple, and a data service provider that goes head-to-head with IBM. This year, Amazon could bring in $75 billion in revenue.

The company's founder, Bezos, 49 years old, is confident in the transformative power of technology. His actions to continuously improve existing businesses and explore new ones often shock shareholders. In August this year, he bought The Washington Post, saying his optimism, innovation, and perseverance could revive the newspaper. Every week, Bezos spends one day at Blue Origin, the private space company he founded in 2000, stating its mission is to enhance the safety of space travel and reduce its costs.

CEOs who lack empathy are not uncommon

Amazon is famous for its high work demands. But you may not fully understand how the company operates or what kind of person Bezos is.

Even when nothing funny is happening around him, Bezos often bursts into nervous laughter, which can startle everyone. Rick Dalzell, Amazon's former chief information officer, said Bezos often laughs like this when others fail to meet his high standards, "It's clear that he does this to disarm your defenses and punish you."

In the tech world, authoritative CEOs are not rare. Stories about Steve Jobs being harsh to his subordinates at Apple are well-known; he once fired an employee in the elevator and yelled at underperforming executives. Bill Gates also lost his temper at Microsoft, while Steve Ballmer had a tendency to throw chairs. Andy Grove, Intel's former CEO, was so strict that one of his subordinates even fainted during a performance review.

Bezos falls into this category as well. His ambition and courage overshadow other ideal qualities of a leader, such as building consensus and politeness. Although he can be charming and witty in public, privately, his anger can erupt to what subordinates call "maniacal" levels. If a colleague doesn't meet Bezos' strict standards, he might become "maniacal." If an employee doesn't give the right answer, tries to bluff, takes credit for others' work, or engages in backstabbing, veins would bulge on Bezos' forehead. Below are some of his reprimands collected by former Amazon employees:

"Are you lazy or just useless?"

"Did you forget to take your stupidity medication today?"

"This question requires human intelligence."

"Do I need a certificate stating I'm the CEO of this company to stop you from challenging me on this issue?"

"Do you want credit for something you didn't do?"

"If I hear that idea again next time, I might as well commit suicide."

(After reviewing the supply chain team's annual plan) "I guess there won't be anything interesting happening in the supply chain next year."

(After an engineer’s demonstration) "Why are you wasting my time?"

Some Amazon employees believe that Bezos, like Jobs, Gates, and Larry Ellison, co-founder of Oracle Corporation, lacks empathy. Therefore, he views employees as expendable resources, completely disregarding their contributions. This, in turn, allows him to coldly allocate funds and manpower and make highly rational business decisions that a more empathetic CEO might hesitate over due to emotional and interpersonal considerations. However, these employees also admit that Bezos has improved the company's performance and customer service, and employee issues are secondary. "Bezos doesn't enjoy tormenting others," said Kim Richmiller, a manager who worked at Amazon for over a decade: "He's not that kind of person. He cannot tolerate foolishness, not even occasionally."

Bezos' criticisms almost always hit the mark, leaving employees both amazed and annoyed.

A seemingly contradictory aspect of Amazon's internal management

Bezos has a publicly accessible email address, jeff@amazon.com, where Amazon customers can directly send complaint emails. Not only does Bezos personally read many customer complaints, but he also forwards them to relevant Amazon employees, adding a question mark.

Such emails often cause panic within Amazon.com.

When employees see Bezos' email with a question mark, their reaction is akin to discovering a ticking bomb. They usually have only a few hours to defuse it: they need to thoroughly explain the situation and implement corrective measures reviewed by multiple managers before responding to Bezos.

Forwarding customer complaint emails with a question mark is one way Bezos ensures that employees continue to hear the voice of the customer.

Amazon employees live every day in this firefighting mode. An employee asked at a biennial company meeting, "Why must the entire team immediately drop everything to resolve a question-mark email complaint?" A supervisor replied, "Every incident described by a customer is important because it reveals problems in our processes. This is an audit conducted by the customer. We treat it as a valuable source of information."

This is a seemingly contradictory aspect of Amazon's internal management: almost every important decision, such as launching or canceling a feature or determining whether a new process will solve inefficiency issues, is made based on metrics. However, random customer narratives, despite being vastly different from cold hard data, can also change Amazon's behavior.

No amount of money is worth doing anything that damages customer trust

At the end of 2010, an unforgettable event occurred. Customers who had browsed Amazon's sexual health category lubricants received personalized recommendation emails for other adult products. Some customers complained to Bezos, and the marketing team received an email with a question mark.

Knowing that the matter was sensitive, the marketing team began nervously drafting explanatory letters. Amazon's direct marketing tools are decentralized, and managers responsible for specific product categories may send marketing emails to customers who viewed certain product categories but did not purchase. This promotional tactic often performs well, generating hundreds of millions of dollars in sales for Amazon annually. However, the lubricant-related complaint was caused by overreach by a junior product manager. But this time, the marketing team never got the chance to send out their explanation letter because Bezos requested a face-to-face meeting to resolve the issue.

Thus, Doug Herrington, Senior Vice President of Consumables, Jeff Wilke, Senior Vice President of North American Retail, and Steven Schur, Vice President of Global Marketing, waited in a conference room at Amazon headquarters in Seattle. Bezos entered the room casually. "Hello," he said as usual, "Today we're going to talk about Schur sending out lubricant marketing emails."

Bezos liked to say that when he was angry, "just wait five minutes," and the anger would dissipate like a tropical storm. But this time was different. He stood there, his gaze locked on Schur, who was responsible for email marketing. "I want you to shut down that marketing email channel," he said. "We can grow the company to a hundred-billion-dollar level without sending a single email."

Then an active debate erupted in the conference room. Amazon's corporate culture is known for its confrontational nature because Bezos believes that the clash of ideas and viewpoints brings out the truth. Wilke and his colleagues argued that lubricants can be purchased in supermarkets and pharmacies, and they are not that embarrassing. They also pointed out that these marketing emails significantly increased Amazon's sales. Bezos didn't care; no amount of money was worth doing anything that damaged customer trust. "Who in this room is going to shut down that channel?" he demanded sharply.

Eventually, the colleagues compromised. Email marketing activities for health and personal care categories were terminated. Amazon also decided to develop a central filtering tool to ensure that category managers could no longer recommend sensitive products. Books, electronics, and other items sold by Amazon continued to appear in marketing emails.
