The ending of "The Sixth Sense"

by w86x7n51dwg on 2011-06-06 16:44:58

The finale of "The Sixth Sense" reveals a stunning truth to Dr. Malcolm Crowe when he is confronted by Cole Sear's revelation that Malcolm himself is actually dead. In his wife's dream, Malcolm delivers his final testament, which might be the only declaration of love he has ever made in all these years.

Cole had long told Malcolm that those who have died don't know they are dead; they live as if they were normal people, seeing only what they wish to see. Cole can see them—he sees every day the spirits of those who met tragic deaths for various reasons. Malcolm didn't believe Cole's words, just like everyone else in this world, thinking this child was merely suffering from severe delusions. At one point, Malcolm even considered giving up on Cole. No one in the world believed Cole, so Cole stopped believing in anyone. He had no expectations for Malcolm and chose to escape even when Malcolm showed concern. Perhaps because Malcolm was determined to save Cole, Cole gradually felt that this person wasn’t filled with malice like others. Over time, Cole developed feelings for Malcolm and ultimately revealed his secret.

Malcolm’s relentless effort to save Cole stems entirely from an earlier case he handled long ago. It was very similar to Cole's situation. At the time, Malcolm thought the child he was dealing with was fine, but he didn’t know—and the child never told him—that he could see ghosts. In despair, without hope from even the most promising child psychologist in the region, the child grew up feeling abandoned. As an adult, unable to bear the mental strain, he went to Malcolm's house, killed Malcolm, and then committed suicide. Of course, Malcolm forgot the truth about the child killing him but remembered the fact of the child's suicide. His firm resolve to help Cole was initially driven by a desire for redemption—to make up for the harm he caused that child long ago by resolving Cole's issues.

In reality, we often overestimate our own nobility. This isn't really about making up for the harm done to that child; what Malcolm truly needed to heal was the damage to his own soul caused by the child's self-destruction. He was trying to eliminate his guilt towards that child. Malcolm represents human behavior in such situations: what we call "making up for harm" seems altruistic, as if it's done for others. But if the other party doesn't accept it, that's their loss. In truth, this is a selfish act of personal redemption—if the other party refuses to accept it, the ultimate loss is ours. Human actions are far less noble than what we claim.

The initial persistence was already a sign of selfishness, indicating that all active attempts at rescue can be understood as acts of personal redemption. Do you want to help someone? Such selfless assistance would surely bring you joy, wouldn't it? Why do you keep helping others? Because we lack happiness, because we need redemption, because we need to be saved.

But later, it wasn't just persistence anymore. When Malcolm accidentally discovered through a phone recording that the child from years ago had been talking to ghosts while calling Malcolm, Malcolm realized he had to trust Cole. Cole was afraid; he didn't understand why the ghosts were coming to him. At this point, Malcolm was no longer actively rescuing Cole. He realized that his previous efforts were unnecessary for Cole, whose needs transcended human knowledge. When Cole asked Malcolm why, Malcolm could only speculate that perhaps the ghosts wanted his help.

This is an ironic answer, not because of the answer itself, but because of the person giving it. Malcolm himself is a ghost—why did Malcolm approach Cole? Malcolm thought he needed to save Cole, but the truth is Malcolm needed Cole's help.

Malcolm's assumption was correct—the ghosts indeed wanted Cole's help. Cole was no longer afraid, and Malcolm unintentionally completed the rescue. Through Cole's situation, Malcolm came to believe in the existence of ghosts and understood how they forget they are dead. Finally, when he saw one night that his wife was holding the ring that should have been on his finger, and fireworks had been going off for an hour—it was lively!—he finally realized that the reason they hadn't spoken for a year wasn't because his wife was complaining about his over-involvement in work, but because he was, in fact, dead.

It's likely that Cole already knew Malcolm was a ghost. If not, why would Cole have advised Malcolm to talk to his wife when she was asleep? That is the only way for communication between the living and the dead.

Cole was also saving Malcolm, so Cole found redemption.
