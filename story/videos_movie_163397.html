Plot Summary of Full Episodes of the TV Series "Xiao Chun":

In 2009, farmers independently wrote, directed, and filmed a contemporary rural-themed TV series titled "Xiao Chun" with a total of 18 episodes. Originally named "Farmers' Children," "Xiao Chun" is a rural-themed TV series. It consists of 18 episodes, each lasting 45 minutes. Filming began on August 8, 2007, and wrapped up on November 9, 2007. The final editing was completed on May 8, 2008. In October 2008, it received the distribution license for domestically produced TV series.

Synopsis of "Xiao Chun":

In a fishing village on the outskirts of a city in Northeast China, Wang Xiaochun, who runs a restaurant, initially had no intention of participating in the village chief election. However, due to the support of the villagers, Wang Xiaochun gave up his long-established restaurant business to become the village chief. His adoptive mother, Aunt Er, knowing the influence of the Jin Pin San family, feared that he would not perform well and offend them, which could lead to unnecessary trouble. Despite Aunt Er's strong opposition, Village Chief Wang Xiaochun stood up against the family power of Jin Pin San and others, engaging in a struggle between collective and personal interests. He endured various pressures, overcame numerous difficulties, gained the support and trust of all the villagers, and led the fishing village step by step towards becoming a new socialist countryside...

Historical Breakthrough:

Originally named "Farmers' Children," it was later renamed "Xiao Chun." Each episode lasts 45 minutes. No one involved in this drama was professional; farmers wrote it, directed it, acted in it, portraying farmers. After being reviewed and approved by the National Radio and Television Administration, it became an 18-episode long drama. This is unprecedented, a pioneering effort by Northeast Chinese farmers. The entire production cost only 160,000 RMB, took 4 months to film, and involved 86 people. It marks a miracle in the history of Chinese film and television culture. Moreover, the entire filming process followed the requirements set by the National Radio and Television Administration, including obtaining shooting permits and distribution licenses. It is a legally compliant newborn project.
