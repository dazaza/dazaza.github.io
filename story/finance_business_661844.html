But I was always caught up in personal matters and never had the time to write this little travelogue.

by anonymous on 2012-02-09 14:15:05

If I keep procrastinating, it might be next year before I get to writing this down, so I should probably sleep less and write this down now.

Actually, I haven't particularly thought about going anywhere. My leave was requested early in August, but later I was indecisive, unsure whether it would be better to go or stay. As the days approached, I was still hesitant about where to go. In the end, I suddenly decided to book my flight and hotel. Because I knew that if I didn't cancel my vacation, I would definitely regret it immensely. Who knows what kind of restlessness and loneliness will follow after the holiday? Anyway, every day isn't necessarily a good mood day.

So with this ordinary yet slightly lucky attitude, I stepped onto the plane.

November 17th. Thursday

I very casually packed some clothes the night before, as I didn't know the weather, I was still a bit confused about the coat, not knowing if the one I brought would be warm enough or if I had brought too many. In the end, I decided to bring one coat.

At the airport, I bought some magazines, planning to read them for the entire flight and not get bored.

Miss Earth asked me if I wanted to sit by the window or the aisle, I said it didn't matter, so be it. In the end, I ended up sitting by the window, next to a father-daughter pair who seemed like university students. I felt she looked nervous, but maybe she thought I was too laid back. Later, I saw her with a laptop, either HP or Dell, and wondered if she was on a business trip.

When the plane landed, I saw that the area was quite desolate. Nanjing turned out to be surprisingly barren, all I could see were fields with tall cedar trees, all of which had shed their leaves, creating a strong autumn atmosphere.

I enjoy looking at these late-autumn landscapes - yellow leaves, red leaves, or even bare branches against a gray sky, forming a poignant scene. Due to the subtropical humid climate, although Hong Kong has cooled down considerably, the trees remain evergreen without any sense of "autumn." So even though Nanjing is desolate, seeing these scenes is still quite nice.

The taxi ride was bumpy, and every time the driver hit the brakes, my heart would race. People accustomed to such conditions naturally remained unfazed, but I was still worried and leaned forward to look around. Finally, we safely arrived at the hotel. After putting down my things, I lay down on the bed and didn't want to get up.

I really wanted to take a nap because I wasn't hungry after eating on the plane. And I always feel especially tired after flying (probably due to the air pressure). However, since there are people who need to eat, I couldn't just lie in bed.

It was a bit sunny outside, and Nanjing was warmer than I imagined, making me wonder if the coat I brought was too thick. Originally, I planned to wear the coat every day, so I didn't care much about what I wore underneath. But if the weather is this warm, what will I use to "cover up" my outfit? Sigh...

Our hotel was near Xuanwu Lake, and after walking two or three streets, we reached Shizi Bridge. Although it's called a pedestrian street, it's actually quite busy. The wide street had restaurants on both sides and stalls in the middle. Even though it was an ordinary afternoon, it wasn't very quiet.

After walking these two or three streets, I was already out of breath and my legs hurt. After eating (even though I said I wasn't hungry earlier, I still ate a set meal), I rested for a while and then continued toward Gulou.

Taking the subway from Xuanwu Gate to Gulou was only one stop, but when we walked, it took a very long time...

On the street, we kept encountering black dogs. Was it a coincidence, or do people in Nanjing particularly like raising black dogs? Regardless of the breed, they were all black. We had already encountered four or five black puppies the day before.

Finally arriving at Gulou, we couldn't see any drum towers. There were only a few tourists, and nearby elderly people were chatting under the big trees. It felt like a regular place for people to relax and cool off, but the status of Gulou was strange. It was surrounded by roads on all sides and didn't seem like a place for officials to relax after meals.

Walking around, we didn't see anything special, so we left.

After all this折腾 (trouble/tiring activity), we were quite exhausted and immediately returned to the hotel for a nap (though it wasn't really a "nap" but more like an "afternoon sleep"). This nap lasted until around six or seven in the evening, and then we were dragged out again.

Near the hotel was Yaohan, the long-unseen Yaohan! The Yaohan that we used to visit every weekend had closed its doors in Hong Kong many years ago due to poor business. Although I initially felt curious and nostalgic about the Nanjing Yaohan, after wandering inside, I couldn't find any traces of a "Japanese department store."

There was a supermarket in the basement of Yaohan, and upon seeing water for sale, I immediately bought a large bottle of several liters. If it was heavy, it would serve as good exercise, as relying solely on the two bottles of water provided by the hotel each day wasn't enough.

Next to Yaohan was also a bookstore, and someone like me who gets lost in bookstores wherever I go, ended up losing track of time and it was already 9:30 pm when I realized!

With the bookstore closed, we hadn't even eaten dinner yet...

So we returned to Shizi Bridge again, and none of the restaurants caught our eye. We ended up choosing "Simple Tea and Plain Rice," which indeed served very simple dishes. Since I wasn't very hungry (due to having eaten a set meal in the afternoon), it didn't matter much.

The duck blood soup was a bit spicy, and after drinking a little, I became thirsty.

The shop didn't sell soy milk (and during my few days in Nanjing, I never drank soy milk! The noodle shops didn't sell soy milk, which was strange. But that's another story), so I went outside to buy red date pear water. I couldn't taste the pear flavor, but the sweetness of the red dates was quite strong.

Because I was thirsty, I quickly finished the drink, but unfortunately spilled it on my pants. How embarrassing! So around ten o'clock, we raced against time and visited several stores to see if I could buy new pants. I managed to buy pants, and interestingly, the salesperson even asked me if I was from Northeast China! (Really funny.)

So that night, we returned to the hotel carrying newly bought water and wearing newly bought pants.

November 18th. Friday

Today is overcast, and with cloudy skies, it feels like drizzling rain. As a result, the Ming Xiaoling Mausoleum trip had to be postponed for a day, and today we went to Xinjiekou, the most bustling area in the city.

On the way, the silly fool made me angry again, so I stopped talking. When we got to Xinjiekou, I aimlessly wandered around the Central Mall and Dadi Department Store or just stood idly on the pedestrian paths.

In fact, after being angry for a while, I started feeling a bit embarrassed. Remembering last year on my birthday, I was also angry, spent the whole evening sulking, and later felt very regretful. I always thought our time together was so limited, and it was a waste to spend it being angry, even tainting the rare annual birthday celebration.

Later, I got hungry from anger and sat down in a Taiwanese beef noodle restaurant in the basement of Dadi Department Store for lunch. The noodles were pretty good; the handmade noodles had a chewy texture unlike the soft ones made by machines.

Afterward, we bought cream puffs from BEARD PAPA. I, as usual, only ate the original flavor, but they tasted much worse than those in Hong Kong. Mainly, the puffs were a bit soggy and lacked the crispiness.

The main department stores in Xinjiekou, like Zhongyang, Dadi, and Xinbai, all have the shadow of Japanese department stores. You can reach the basement of the department stores directly from the subway exit. The basements house restaurants and supermarkets (if there are any), the top floors sell cosmetics and more expensive items, the second and third floors mainly feature women's clothing, the fourth floor men's clothing, the fifth floor sports and casual wear, and further up are children's clothing, home goods, and larger appliances and furniture.

They all have similar layouts, so after visiting one or two, you can imagine what the other department stores look like.

I also wondered why there aren't any shopping malls in this style?

Next to Dadi is Nanjing IFC, where you can see famous brands on the road. However, strangely, there's no sign of Chanel (even though Bottega Veneta and Hermes are present).

Since there was nothing I wanted to buy, I didn't feel like shopping, so after lunch, we went straight to the Presidential Palace.

When we first got off the subway, I saw a building near the Nanjing Library that had a strong resemblance to a Japanese shrine. From afar, it looked quite grand, so I assumed it must be the Presidential Palace.

However, after walking around half a circle, I couldn't find any ticket booths or entrances (the only entrance-like one was closed), nor did I see any signs saying "Presidential Palace." Could it be that during the Anti-Japanese War, they were so low-key that they didn't even dare to put up a sign?

Later, we met a local who informed us that we had mistaken the Presidential Palace. The real one was behind us, near a Starbucks, and this unremarkable building remains a mystery, which we didn't bother to investigate further.

As soon as we approached the Presidential Palace, we were greeted by groups of tour guides waving flags and leading tourists inside. I suggested we sneak into one of the tour groups to save on admission fees. However, my guide claimed he knew the history better than those guides and could give a better introduction. So, we followed our private guide around the Presidential Palace.

The Presidential Palace is quite large and can be divided into east and west wings, as well as front, middle, and rear halls. Upon entering the main hall, you can see Dr. Sun Yat-sen's famous inscription, "The world belongs to the public." The important reception rooms and offices are concentrated in the front and middle sections, while the rear part mainly consists of living quarters and gardens. The reception rooms consist of dozens of sofas placed together, which are old-fashioned, with wooden legs supporting fabric seats (just looking at them makes you think sitting wouldn't be very comfortable).

Since there are many reception rooms, everyone just casually peeks inside. The President's office, however, is only one, so everyone crowds outside to take a look. I thought this office must have something extraordinary about it, so I struggled to secure a spot to peek inside. To my surprise, the President's office was rather plain, consisting of just a wooden desk and chair. On the desk was a green lampshade library-style desk lamp and a pen holder for writing brushes. The space wasn't very spacious, resembling the study of a wealthy family from the 1930s. However, even wealthy families' studies would have antiques or books on the shelves to create an appearance. Here, there wasn't even a single oil painting or watercolor painting. The only bright spot seemed to be a portrait of Chiang Kai-shek on the wall, and the rest felt so ordinary that it seemed like movie sets would be more elaborate. (Is this really the President's office? But the sign outside says so.)

Leaving the office building, we entered the garden and living hall at the rear. Inside the Presidential Palace are two small lakes (although called "lakes," they feel more like ponds). Beside the lake is a pavilion styled like a boat, likely used for drinking and enjoying performances, adding a touch of elegance. Opposite the boat pavilion is a small building where meals were taken. Imagine dining here while watching performers sing across the lake, a delightful experience. The pavilion beside the lake is unique, called the Mandarin Duck Pavilion, with a double-tiered roof design.

Behind the lake is the office used by Dr. Sun Yat-sen when he served as the Provisional President. Visible behind the office is a small workroom with a bed and a recliner, making it more life-like compared to the rigid official offices at the front. The bed and recliner seem small in size, and I had a strong urge to shake the recliner to see if it really moved. (Unfortunately, my hand couldn't reach inside, which was quite disappointing.)

In the courtyard behind the Provisional President's office stands a statue of Dr. Sun Yat-sen, inviting people to take photos freely. My guide and I sat under the surrounding trees to rest, watching tourists gather around Dr. Sun's statue for pictures. One batch finishes, and another comes. The guide joked that if someone shouted, "Mr. Sun, smile!" and Mr. Sun actually smiled, everyone would flee instantly, leaving the courtyard empty! Looking at the gloomy weather, I suddenly felt it had an eerie novel-like atmosphere.

After touring the Presidential Palace, we were tired again, so we returned to the hotel to sleep.

When I woke up, it was already six or seven in the evening. Taking advantage of the vibrant night, we headed to Confucius Temple. It's quite amusing; Confucius Temple is a place dedicated to Confucius, a scholar's holy ground, but surrounding it is a lively night market that only becomes active at night, which feels somewhat strange and dull.

After getting off the subway, we initially followed the crowd, thinking that since it was the weekend, everyone must be heading to Confucius Temple to eat. Following the crowd would surely lead us right! However, fortunately, we checked Google Maps at the crossroads because the majority were heading in a direction that wasn't towards Confucius Temple. If we had blindly followed, we would have repeated the "mistaking Zhang San for Li Si" situation from the afternoon.

The distance from the subway station to Confucius Temple was significant, but relative to me, as Hong Kong is a small place where each street doesn't require much walking to reach the intersection. Comparatively, a typical intersection in mainland China equals two or three intersections in Hong Kong. So, when told we'd walk through two or three intersections to arrive, I thought it wouldn't be too far, but halfway through, I was already out of breath.

Even though we walked this distance (passing AQUA CITY, where the trees were adorned with lights, making it the most Christmas-like mall I've seen in Nanjing!), arriving at Confucius Temple was worth it.

Confucius Temple is a night market with eateries and small shops on both sides of the Qinhuai River. To the left of the large archway, you'll first encounter a cluster of food stalls! My goodness! After traveling to so many places, I finally truly witnessed the food street often featured in travel specials on TV! Each stall sells unique and characteristic snacks - grilled meat skewers here, stinky tofu there, tofu pudding and sweet desserts elsewhere, and roasted crab, meatballs, eggs, and more...

Everything here seems quite hygienic. You first exchange cash for tokens, reducing the unhygienic direct handling of money by vendors (you know how dirty money can be!). The vendors loudly advertise their products - clang clang clang, delicious grilled food! Clang clang clang, regret it if you don't try the beauty crabs! Wandering among them, I felt like those female hosts in travel specials, excitedly buying and trying various snacks, only to realize that after two or three types, I was already full and couldn't eat anymore. Of course, it's impossible to eat everything like the hosts do...(I just had bean paste pastries, beauty crabs, and rice cakes. Nanjing has long, thin rice cake sticks mixed with a sauce that looks like tomato sauce but is slightly spicy, tasting quite flavorful.)

After eating, behind the snack street are stores housed in ancient-looking buildings but with modern interiors, including eateries and clothing shops, mostly mass-market brands. It resembles Shanghai's City God Temple. After walking through this street, we finally reached the main attraction - Confucius Temple. However, it was already dark, and most of us didn't enter the temple itself. Placing Confucius Temple here seemed more like a backdrop, I wonder what Master Kong would think about this.(What it looks like during the day remains unknown.)

Opposite Confucius Temple is the Qinhuai River, with small shops along the river selling items with traditional characteristics, such as Rain Flower Stones, silk products, paintings, and engravings. For tourists, these are essentially souvenir shops. After browsing a circle, we came out to admire the night view of the Qinhuai River.

Along the Qinhuai River, umbrellas were erected under which tables were placed. Nearby were a couple of stalls allowing people to buy food and sit to eat. Boats floated on the river, and for a fee, you could take a ride to cruise along the Qinhuai River. Dragon-shaped lantern decorations floated on the river, creating a bustling scene reminiscent of the Lantern Festival. Everyone gathered here to take photos as souvenirs, and photographers offered to take pictures that could be printed within minutes. It was exceptionally lively, like a major festival, and the atmosphere would likely be even better during actual holidays.

Not far from here was a bridge, from which you could clearly see the scenery of the Qinhuai River. Crossing the bridge to the other side, the atmosphere changed significantly. Restaurants seemed more upscale, and the streets were crowded with pedestrians. Light rain began to fall, so we opened our umbrellas and returned to the other side.

Initially, we planned to sit under the umbrella to shelter from the rain and leave once it subsided. However, the rain grew heavier with no sign of stopping, and it felt inappropriate to occupy the tables and chairs meant for diners. Thus, we ordered some food despite our already full stomachs.

We waited and waited, but the rain didn't stop. Combined with the wind, it felt much cooler than during the day. With no choice, we hailed a taxi and returned.

Only when we were in the car did we realize it wasn't even nine o'clock yet. We asked the driver to take us to Yaohan, where we bought some plum wine before returning to the hotel. Originally priced at 168, it was now marked down to 60! We found a treasure, haha. For drinking, we also bought disposable plastic cups (as we didn't want to use the hotel's large tea cups), only to later discover that the hotel had specific cups for drinking. Foolish of us, wasting 5 yuan on cups. Overall, we still saved money, and the plastic cups became our only "souvenir."
