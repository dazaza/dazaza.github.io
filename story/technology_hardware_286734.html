As of November 21, the average transaction price of commercial housing in Shanghai has already broken through the major barrier of 15,000 yuan per square meter. The phenomenon of "no ten-thousand-yuan houses within the Outer Ring Road" has led many college graduates who are just accumulating work experience to choose "ant living," while it has also prompted some people with a small foothold in Shanghai to choose "going abroad" and settle down elsewhere.

We must by all means ensure that young people have access to housing; this is our policy orientation. If we forget this, it would not be reasonable. Why? Shanghai's development depends on talent. If your high housing prices deter talented individuals from coming, what hope does Shanghai have?

Opening remarks: As young people step out of campus and into the post-graduation era, they enter our field of vision. What kind of pressure are they facing? How far apart are their ideals from reality? Secretary Yu Zhengsheng strongly appeals that Shanghai's development depends on talent. Shanghai is comprehensively advancing the construction of four major centers. While cultivating and absorbing high-end talents, it also needs to simultaneously improve the construction of echelon talents and give these young people at the base of the pyramid continuous attention. The upcoming series of reports titled "Post-Graduation Era: Investigation of Youth Survival Costs" aims to focus on the young footprints in this city and pay attention to their real-life survival conditions, calling for more attention from all sectors of society.

In Shanghai, how great is the pressure on young people to buy a house? How high is the cost for outsiders to settle down? Zhang Lin, an 80s-born youth with a monthly salary of 10,000 yuan and savings of over 200,000 yuan, told the reporter, "I really want to stay in Shanghai, but I simply cannot afford a house."

2005 - Happiness was: A monthly salary of 5,000 yuan, with a house rental of 800 yuan per month.

"Both my girlfriend and I felt that after enduring hardship for some time, we could own a house in Shanghai that belongs to the two of us."

In June 2005, Zhang Lin, a graduate of a well-known university's finance department in Shanghai, was smoothly hired by a public institution in Shanghai due to his outstanding professional background and achievements. Zhang Lin said that his position was futures trading, with a starting monthly salary of 5,000 yuan in the first year, which would increase according to his years of service, and there were opportunities for further study every year. "Such working conditions made many classmates envious at the time, and my parents back home were truly happy."

Zhang Lin's job was thus settled amidst the envious gazes of his classmates. Just after graduation, earning a monthly salary of 5,000 yuan made him delighted for a while, and he quickly brought his girlfriend who was working in Guangzhou to be by his side.

Even with such good jobs, it was impossible for two newly graduated young people to buy a house, and renting a house suitable for two people in downtown Shanghai was a significant expense. To save money and realize his dream of buying a house sooner, Zhang Lin and his girlfriend rented an old apartment in Yangpu District. "That was a house from the 1950s, with wooden floors that creaked when walked on, poor soundproofing, and I could know everything about my neighbors' children, like if they didn't do well on exams or if they were going on a spring outing tomorrow."

Although the living conditions were not comfortable, Zhang Lin said that transitioning from dormitory life to cohabitation satisfied him greatly. Moreover, the rent for a room less than 10 square meters was only 800 yuan at the time. "Both my girlfriend and I thought it was very suitable. After all, this was just our temporary foothold. After enduring hardship for some time, once we had saved enough for the down payment, we could own a house in Shanghai that belonged to the two of us."

2007 - Regret emerges: Monthly salary rises to 10,000, but house prices soar by over 300,000 yuan.

"After each overtime, I felt I was getting closer to my dream of buying a house, but I still couldn't keep up with the rising house prices."

Zhang Lin said that when he first started working, both he and his girlfriend worked extremely hard. "After each overtime, I felt I was getting closer to my dream of buying a house." In fact, due to Zhang Lin's efforts, he soon became a key business backbone in the unit. Within just one year of work, Zhang Lin was designated as an internal training target. "For this reason, I even got the opportunity to attend overseas training, and my salary also increased to 6,000 yuan."

In fact, Zhang Lin's calculations were quite precise. He told the reporter that since Shanghai was the core area of China's financial sector, for someone graduating in finance, Shanghai offered the most attractive "gilding" opportunities. "The experience gained from working in Shanghai for a year far exceeds what five years in the same position in my hometown could offer. My hard work was all aimed at settling down in Shanghai and realizing my career dreams."

In 2007, after working for two full years, Zhang Lin was recruited by a securities company due to his excellent business capabilities. Despite strong retention efforts from his original unit, due to the new company offering a monthly salary of 10,000 yuan, Zhang Lin resolutely gave up his stable job in the public institution and switched to this company to engage in IPO listing analysis work.

Despite the continuous rise in salary, Zhang Lin and his girlfriend remained very frugal. However, by 2007, Shanghai's housing prices had reached a peak. Zhang Lin said that a 90-square-meter apartment in Minhang that he and his girlfriend had initially looked at when they first graduated had risen from its original price of 500,000 yuan to over 800,000 yuan.

"In other words, although my salary has doubled, I still can't keep up with the rising house prices!" Working in finance, Zhang Lin saw the "bright prospects" of the real estate market. To buy a house as soon as possible, Zhang Lin decided to save a little more with his girlfriend. "Since I often traveled for IPO listings, my girlfriend shared a two-bedroom apartment with a colleague. When I returned on weekends, I squeezed in with her. This way, we saved a large portion of our rent!"
