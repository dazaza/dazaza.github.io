China's business godfather, Liu Chuanzhi, understands Alibaba's expectations of adopting the "partnership system"

by anonymous on 2013-10-13 13:54:00

Beijing, October 12 - In response to the recent heated discussion about Alibaba's decision not to list on the Hong Kong Stock Exchange due to its insistence on maintaining the "partnership system," Liu Chuanzhi, hailed as the Godfather of Chinese business, founder of Lenovo Group, and honorary chairman and senior advisor of Lenovo Group, expressed understanding for Alibaba's desire to adopt the 'partnership system' when listing in Hong Kong.

The first disclosure of Liu Chuanzhi's stance was made by Zhang Peng, editor-in-chief of the Business Value magazine and founder of GeekPark, on Weibo. Liu Chuanzhi stated: "I can particularly understand the original intention behind Jack Ma's partnership system, and I also fully understand his decision not to sacrifice the Hong Kong IPO."

Zhang Peng wrote on his Weibo account that Liu Chuanzhi believes that if a company does not carefully design its system after going public, it could easily become a situation where there are nominal owners in terms of assets but no actual owner for the future direction. Liu Chuanzhi also mentioned that he has personally witnessed the rapid decline of HP after losing its true owner. He emphasized that "this is a real danger that only those who have personally run a business can truly understand."

Yesterday, Niu Wenwen, publisher of Entrepreneurial Home magazine, published an article stating that "today people are discussing whether the partnership system overly protects the interests of the founders, but it seems the partnership system is more aimed at preventing the emergence of irresponsible parachuted CEOs."

Niu Wenwen believed that "high-tech and internet companies, even large ones like Google, Facebook, and Amazon, are still young companies. For the first time in human history, we have companies that are both large and young. How can these young internet giants maintain vitality and unique culture even when the founders' creativity declines or their attention shifts? This is indeed a fresh issue. We have already seen Yahoo, Microsoft, and even Apple fall into middle-aged decline after the departure of their founders. The professional managers hired by the board of directors find it difficult to keep the company creative and vibrant. These external professional managers are products of the manufacturing era; they do not adapt well to the internet era. Jack Ma seems to be using the partnership system to address the Silicon Valley pain point."
