In order to understand what cervical erosion is, we need to start from the beginning.

The female reproductive tract is divided into upper and lower parts, the upper reproductive tract includes the fallopian tubes and the uterine body, the lower reproductive tract refers to the vagina. The external genitalia are the external reproductive organs, but not cavities. The ovaries are sex glands and do not belong to the reproductive tract. The cervix lies between the uterine body and the vagina, with part of it protruding at the top of the vagina, which is a very important structure. Its importance lies in separating the bacterium-laden lower reproductive tract from the sterile upper reproductive tract. Another important function is that after conception, it allows the embryo to safely grow in the uterine cavity without falling out, but once the fetus matures, it can open the door in time, so the fetus can stay in the uterine cavity and come out, the goalkeeper role of the cervix is indispensable.

The upper and lower reproductive tracts of females are all cavities, all lined with epithelium. The morphology of the epithelium varies in different locations, with different functions. The epithelium of the fallopian tubes has cilia, which helps transport eggs, sperm, and fertilized eggs. The epithelium of the uterus (endometrium) undergoes periodic changes, shedding every month if there is no pregnancy, becoming menstruation. Some primates also have menstruation, while dogs and cats either do not have menstruation or have minimal menstruation. The epithelium of the uterus has glands rich in glycogen, aiming to provide nutrition for the fertilized egg after implantation. The epithelium inside the cervical canal secretes mucus, which has immune functions, allowing sperm to freely pass through while generally preventing bacteria in the vagina from passing through, thus separating the bacterium-laden vaginal cavity from the sterile uterine cavity. All these cavities' epithelia are only one layer (single-layer), whereas the epithelium of the vagina and the vaginal portion of the cervix, due to the need for sexual intercourse, has evolved into more than ten layers, appearing like fish scales under the microscope, hence called squamous epithelium.

Extensive research data shows that the cervix of a female fetus in her mother's uterus is eroded, which is the result of the pregnant mother's sex hormones acting on the fetal cervix; the cervix of elderly women appears smooth with no erosion because the ovarian function of elderly women has declined, and the level of sex hormones is very low. Therefore, cervical erosion in women of childbearing age is a physiological phenomenon, and if there is no infection, it does not require treatment.
