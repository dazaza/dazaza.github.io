Forbes: Who Says Microsoft's Days Are Numbered?

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 16:31:08

Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer

Beijing, July 24 - A Forbes online article recently authored by Tony Bradley claims that Microsoft is not facing an impending doom. The article points out that despite the decline in personal computer sales, resistance to Windows 8, a public relations disaster related to the Xbox One, and the failure of Surface RT, which may give the impression that Microsoft's glory days are over, the company is changing its strategy and still possesses its own "inertia" momentum.

Below is the full text of the article:

Microsoft's stock plummeted on Friday following the company’s disappointing Q4 fiscal 2013 earnings report released on Thursday. In this quarter, Microsoft recorded nearly a $1 billion write-down related to unsold Surface RT tablets. After the sharp drop in Microsoft's stock price, naysayers emerged claiming that the tech giant was headed for doom.

In the fourth quarter, Microsoft reported revenue of $19.9 billion, up 10% from Q4 of fiscal 2012 but approximately $1 billion lower than Wall Street analysts' expectations. Microsoft's net profit for the fourth quarter was close to $5 billion, better than the net loss of $492 million during the same period last year. For the entire fiscal 2013, Microsoft's revenue increased by 5.6% compared to 2012, and net profits grew significantly by almost one-third. As of the end of fiscal 2013, Microsoft held a total cash reserve of $77 billion; even after deducting debt, Microsoft still had over $60 billion in cash.

So why do some people spread panic about Microsoft's imminent demise? For investors, the decline in PC sales, resistance to the Windows 8 operating system, PR failures regarding the launch issues of the Xbox One gaming console, and the obvious failure of Surface RT might signal that it is time for Microsoft to step down from the top tier of the tech industry. Even if Microsoft doesn't completely collapse, given the current situation, it is probably fair to say that the company will endure some "growing pains" in the foreseeable future.

The reason Microsoft has become a "victim" in the current wave of technological development lies both in users shifting towards mobile and in the constraints brought by the company's own success. With the booming development of smartphones and tablets, the status of PCs has declined significantly, leading to a major drop in sales. In such an environment, the sales performance of the latest version of the Windows operating system to businesses and individual consumers has been less than satisfactory. However, even if users don't buy new hardware, they can still purchase a new Windows operating system. But in reality, Windows XP was an extraordinary operating system, and the subsequent Windows 7 was also worth its value, meaning there are hardly any incentives for users to upgrade to Windows 8.

However, Microsoft is changing its strategy, and the company still possesses its own "inertia" momentum. Microsoft does not plan to abandon its OEM (original equipment manufacturer) partners, at least not yet. But the company is working to decouple its fate from that of the PC industry. Microsoft has been steadily working to gradually instill a new culture of cloud services and subscription services to replace the old licensing model. Microsoft may be satisfied with the existing PC hardware, but this does not mean they won't run the latest Windows 8 operating system, nor does it mean they won't use services like Office 365.

This so-called "inertia" refers to the functions that Microsoft's dominant business footprint can perform. In desktop operating systems, Windows still stands alone; in web browsers, Internet Explorer still holds a dominant position; while the Office suite indeed faces some challengers, in the short term, this software will almost certainly not face the risk of losing market share to competitors. Microsoft's culture dominates the market, and both enterprises and individual consumers have deeply integrated into it, so Windows PCs will not be completely discarded.

I am not saying that Microsoft has no weaknesses, which would be too foolish. Historically, numerous examples exist where companies faded or vanished due to arrogance. No matter how strong a company's dominant position is, time could bring an innovation or a competitor that makes the company obsolete. We're just saying that Microsoft today has not deteriorated to that extent. Microsoft has indeed been slow to adapt to current technology trends, but the company still has time to correct these issues and get things back on track.

Microsoft will not vanish, and its stock price is merely in a temporary slump. What we need to do is observe how the company carries out its restructuring measures and whether these measures can help it regain strength. I believe this observation will be quite interesting.
