Tian vs University of Science and Technology Beijing Case Regarding the Issuance of Graduation and Degree Certificates (I)

by rtgfdxvj on 2010-05-18 08:53:51

In September 1994, the plaintiff Tian was admitted to the Department of Physical Chemistry at the College of Applied Sciences under the defendant University of Science and Technology Beijing, obtaining undergraduate status. On February 29, 1996, during a retake exam for electromagnetism, Tian was found with a note containing electromagnetism formulas on his person. When he went to the restroom midway through the exam, the note fell out and was discovered by the proctor. The proctor stopped Tian's exam according to examination rules. On March 5 of the same year, in accordance with Article 3, Item 5 of the "068 Notice," which states that those caught cheating, including writing on their hands, would be expelled, the University of Science and Technology Beijing determined that Tian’s behavior constituted cheating. Based on Article 1, which states that all students caught cheating would be expelled, it was decided to expel Tian. This decision was posted on the school bulletin board on the same day. On April 10, the Academic Affairs Office issued a notice regarding the change in student status, and the school ceased issuing food subsidies. However, Tian continued to participate in normal learning activities and events organized by the school as if still enrolled.

In the two weeks of March 1996, during the fourth semester, Tian did not register for the second semester of the 1995-1996 academic year. In September of the same year, Tian claimed his original student ID was lost and obtained a replacement. Thereafter, the University of Science and Technology Beijing collected tuition from Tian each year, registered him, issued living allowances for university students, and allowed him to participate in graduation internships and design projects. His thesis advisor also collected the graduation design completion fee on behalf of the school. Tian also took exams under the name of a student at the university and successively obtained certificates for passing the College English Test Band 4 and the BASIC language test for computer application proficiency. Over the four years Tian studied at the university, his grades were satisfactory, and he successfully completed his graduation internship, design project, and thesis defense, earning commendation for an outstanding graduation thesis and ranking ninth overall in his class.

In March 1998, when the school reviewed the eligibility list of the Class of 1994 graduates, Tian was not included.

Tian filed a complaint with the former State Education Commission about his enrollment status. On May 19, 1998, the Higher Education Department of the former State Education Commission sent a letter to the University of Science and Technology Beijing, deeming the handling of Tian's violation of examination discipline too severe and suggesting a reevaluation. On June 2, after reviewing the case, the University of Science and Technology Beijing maintained its original conclusion in a document submitted to the State Education Commission.

In June 1998, citing Tian's lack of enrollment status, relevant departments of the University of Science and Technology Beijing refused to issue him a diploma and consequently did not submit his graduation dispatch qualification form to the education administrative department. As a result, the school also did not include Tian in the list of candidates eligible for a bachelor's degree submitted to the university's Degree Evaluation Committee for review.

On October 5, 1998, Tian initiated administrative litigation in the Haidian District People's Court of Beijing, demanding that the defendant issue the "two certificates" (diploma and degree certificate), compensate for a loss of 3,000 yuan, and publicly apologize in the school newspaper.

On April 26, 1999, the Haidian District People's Court of Beijing made a first-instance judgment, ruling that the defendant should issue a graduation certificate to the plaintiff.

### Legal Arguments (First Instance)

Firstly, regarding the legal basis for not issuing the plaintiff's graduation and degree certificates:

Based on the spirit of the Urgent Notice on Strengthening Examination Management issued by the Higher Education Department of the State Education Commission on November 16, 1994, the University of Science and Technology Beijing formulated the Urgent Notice on Strict Examination Management [94] No. 068. This document clearly stipulates in its first article that "anyone found cheating in an exam will be expelled," and the third article defines nine forms of cheating, including one where students bring prohibited materials into the exam room or write information on their bodies. In this case, the plaintiff was caught with a note containing formulas during a retake exam on February 29, 1996, leading to his expulsion, effectively canceling his enrollment status. According to Article 28 of the Education Law, schools have autonomy in managing student records, implementing rewards or punishments, and thus the University of Science and Technology Beijing's decision to expel the plaintiff is part of its authority over student records.

According to Article 35 of the Regulations on Student Management of Ordinary Higher Education Institutions, "students with enrollment status who are morally, intellectually, and physically qualified and who complete all courses specified in the teaching plan shall be granted graduation certificates." Article 38 stipulates that "graduation certificates cannot be issued to students without enrollment status." Similar provisions exist in the Beijing Region's Methods for Managing Student Records at Ordinary Higher Education Institutions and the University of Science and Technology Beijing's Student Record Management Measures. Therefore, as the plaintiff's enrollment status has been canceled, he does not qualify for undergraduate graduation.

The People's Republic of China Degree Regulations and the Provisional Implementation Measures for the Degree Regulations stipulate that "undergraduate graduates with excellent performance may be awarded a bachelor's degree." The granting institution must review the graduate's performance and graduation appraisal materials. In this case, the plaintiff's graduation and degree qualifications were negated by the applied college, so the school could not issue the graduation certificate or bachelor's degree certificate.

Secondly, the factual basis for not issuing the "two certificates," which is the focal point and crux of the controversy in this case:

First, the nature of the plaintiff's act of carrying a note with formulas during the electromagnetism retake exam on February 29, 1996—whether it constitutes cheating or another violation of examination discipline—is the factual foundation of this case. As previously mentioned, the school's 068 Notice includes bringing prohibited materials into the exam as one of the nine forms of cheating. Such cheating is recognized as follows: if during an exam a candidate is found to possess material related to the exam content or writes exam-related content on their body, whether or not they look at it is not a necessary condition for determining whether cheating has occurred, as proctors cannot continuously monitor every candidate. In this case, after the exam began, the plaintiff hid a note with formulas in his sleeve, went to the restroom, and dropped the note on the floor, which was discovered by the proctor, fully meeting the criteria for recognizing such cheating. Regardless of the plaintiff's arguments regarding seating position, irrelevance of exam content to the note, or the proctor not seeing him look at the note, these do not affect the determination that the cheating was completed rather than attempted.

Second, expulsion is a legitimate and effective form of handling within the scope of universities' autonomy in managing student records. It carries a slight punitive aspect but is not categorized as a disciplinary action against students for misconduct. Its legal basis stems from Articles 29 and 30 of the Regulations on Student Management of Ordinary Higher Education Institutions. Expulsion involves the cancellation or termination of a student's enrollment status due to certain changes in circumstances after obtaining admission and completing normal registration. It can be divided into voluntary and involuntary expulsion. For those who marry without going through the proper procedures, the school treats them as having withdrawn, falling under involuntary expulsion. In this case, the school's handling of the plaintiff falls under this category. The essence of expulsion lies in canceling or terminating the student's enrollment status, with no reason recorded in the student's file, returning them to their original place or unit, allowing them to retake the college entrance exam, managed by the Academic Affairs Office. This differs from expulsion by force, which is managed by the Student Affairs Office, targeting serious violations of school rules and regulations, with records kept in the student's file and prohibiting them from retaking the college entrance exam. From this perspective, the school's expulsion of students for cheating is well-intentioned. It should be noted that many universities have similar practices for dealing with cheating students, not unique to the University of Science and Technology Beijing.

Third, after being expelled, did the plaintiff's subsequent reissuance of a student ID and registration restore or regain his enrollment status? This is the core issue of the case. As previously stated, the essence of expulsion lies in canceling enrollment status. The school's 1996 No. 003 work bulletin had already announced the decision to handle the plaintiff's expulsion. From that day onward, the plaintiff's enrollment status was terminated, reflected in various management processes within the school. During the second semester of the second academic year, following the incident, the plaintiff did not fulfill normal registration procedures, claiming the loss of his student ID. However, since students can enjoy discounts on train tickets and other conveniences with a student ID, the plaintiff should have fulfilled the replacement procedures between March 4 and 8, 1996, instead of waiting half a year to replace it. Claiming the loss of the student ID goes against convention, but regardless, it remains a fact that the plaintiff did not register that semester, which he did not deny in court. From a formal perspective, a student with normal enrollment status must register continuously across eight semesters over four academic years. However, the plaintiff lacks one semester.

In the process of replacing the student ID, there were institutional loopholes in the school, i.e., only requiring the department seal, personal explanation, and counselor's signature, followed by the Academic Affairs Office filling out the form, obtaining the steel stamp from the office, and simultaneously registering. The school once experienced a situation where more than ten people wrote their hometowns as Haikou or Guangzhou to enjoy discounted train tickets. The plaintiff exploited the deficiencies in the replacement process, succeeding in obtaining a replacement six months after the incident. Additionally, the specific teacher responsible for filling out the replacement form was a retired math teacher temporarily hired by the Academic Affairs Office, whose proxy behavior exceeded the authority to only issue IDs to students with enrollment status, making it invalid. Furthermore, the chemistry department should not have issued a certificate or stamped it for the plaintiff.

In the plaintiff's evidence, apart from the student ID, none directly and necessarily prove the plaintiff's normal enrollment status. These pieces of evidence mostly represent rights or obligations that follow-up students could also enjoy. Some are restored due to the plaintiff holding a flawed replacement student ID, such as food subsidies. However, whether a student enjoys normal enrollment status is generally evidenced by the student ID and registration, and more importantly, by the school's student registration cards, rosters, student files, and computer disks. The 1996 decision canceled the plaintiff's enrollment status, and the school repeatedly requested assistance from the chemistry department to urge the plaintiff to process withdrawal procedures. The 1998 review report still upheld the 1996 handling conclusion. Finally, the graduation qualifications and degree approval forms of the applied college denied the plaintiff's graduation qualifications, indicating that the plaintiff had long lost enrollment status and therefore could not obtain a graduation certificate or a bachelor's degree certificate.

Finally, how to understand and view several issues in this case:

First, how to evaluate whether the expulsion of the plaintiff was too severe. The severity is a relative judgment based on comparisons with similar comparable cases. The school's attitude toward enforcing the 068 Notice of December 22, 1994, has been firm and consistent. By January 1998, 24 people had been expelled for exam cheating, among whom eight were expelled for bringing prohibited materials. Of the remaining 23, including the other seven who brought prohibited materials, all processed their departure from the school themselves, except for the plaintiff. The same type of cheating received the same treatment; how can it be said that the plaintiff was treated too severely?

Second, whether the school's 068 Notice of December 22, 1994, serves as the legal basis for handling the plaintiff and whether such "quasi-abstract administrative acts" internal normative documents should receive support from the court in administrative trials. According to the Administrative Litigation Law, courts have the authority to review the legality of administrative normative documents below the level of regulations. In this case, within the scope of university autonomy, school rules and regulations that do not conflict with important national laws and regulations should be supported by administrative judicial power. Regarding the 068 Notice, its formulation and issuance process was legal, and its content was reported to the Higher Education Department of the State Education Commission, the Personnel and Education Department of the Metallurgical Industry Ministry (superior leadership agency), the Beijing Education Commission, and the Beijing Higher Education Bureau. None of these supervisory agencies raised objections, so the legality of the 068 Notice is beyond doubt.

Third, how to understand the execution of the school's decision on March 5, 1996. The plaintiff believes that the school's expulsion decision of March 5, 1996, was never executed, which is a misunderstanding. Canceling enrollment status means canceling the plaintiff's undergraduate qualification, and related procedures require the plaintiff to personally handle them. On April 10, 1994, the Academic Affairs Office indeed issued a nine-part form for executing the expulsion and changing the student's record. Some departments, like the Student Affairs Office, stopped issuing food subsidies and processing Beijing residency permits, while most departments needed the plaintiff to come in person to handle the procedures. To execute the nine-part form, one needs to obtain a transfer slip with stamps from fourteen departments from the Academic Affairs Office, providing a detailed address for the return of the archive to the original place, handled by the archive room. All these need to be personally handled by the plaintiff, as the school does not have the authority to forcibly remove students, but can only urge them to handle the procedures promptly. The school never allowed the plaintiff to continue studying.

Fourth, how to understand the relationship between the school's handling decision on enrollment status and the actions of individual staff members in departments and faculties.

Only the school administration has the authority to decide on the continuation or termination of enrollment status, which can be considered the exclusive jurisdiction of the school administration. Both the school's March 5, 1996, handling and its June 2, 1998, reply to the Ministry of Education's Student Affairs Department reached the same conclusion, namely, canceling the plaintiff's enrollment status. Any meaning expressed by individual staff members of departments and faculties related to enrollment status during this period, if contradictory to the two decisions, is invalid because it infringes upon the exclusive jurisdiction of the school administration. These expressions, as analyzed earlier, some exceed agency authority; some are made under significant misunderstanding by the handlers; others deliberately violate the school's decision, belonging to behaviors subject to investigation for disobeying orders and prohibitions.

Additionally, the administrative relationship between the school administration and the managed students in this case should fall under internal administrative law relations, governed by legal rules. According to legal principles, such handling decisions with the nature of internal specific administrative acts, once legally made, are determinative in effect. A party's appeal cannot naturally prevent the decision from taking legal effect. Only the party itself or a superior can change or revoke the decision through legal procedures. Therefore, the school's handling decision of March 5, 1996, remained in a determined state. The plaintiff's appeal, actual continued study, and individual opinions of department and faculty members could not shake or negate its legal effect, especially since it was upheld by the June 2, 1998, review report!

### First Instance Judgment

#### Administrative Judgment of the Haidian District People's Court of Beijing

(1998) Hai Xing Chu Zi No. 142

Plaintiff Tian, male, born in 1976, Han nationality, student at the Department of Physical Chemistry at the College of Applied Sciences, University of Science and Technology Beijing, temporarily residing in the school dormitory.

Plaintiff Tian sued defendant University of Science and Technology Beijing for refusing to issue a graduation certificate and degree certificate, filing a lawsuit on October 19, 1998. After accepting the case, our court legally formed a collegiate bench and held a public hearing. Plaintiff Tian and his commissioned agent; the commissioned agents of defendant University of Science and Technology Beijing attended the trial. This case has now been concluded.

The plaintiff argued that on February 29, 1996, during the retake exam for electromagnetism, he unintentionally brought a paper with formulas into the exam hall and did not check it during the exam. When he went to the restroom midway through the exam, the paper fell out and was discovered by the teacher. The school mistakenly believed he cheated and made the decision to expel him. However, the decision was not formally notified to him, nor was it implemented by the school and relevant departments. In September 1996, the school reissued a lost student ID for him, allowing him to continue participating in normal studies and all activities organized by the school, retaking the electromagnetism course, passing the exam, and participating in the school-organized English and computer grade exams, obtaining corresponding certificates; he also participated in the school-planned graduation internship design, thesis defense, and received the graduation design fee according to standards; he also participated in the school-mobilized voluntary blood donation activities. He paid educational fees and registered his enrollment status according to regulations, completing the teaching plan set by the defendant during his time at the school, achieving academic performance and a graduation thesis that met the requirements for higher education institutions. However, in June 1998, nearing graduation, the school notified his department that he lacked enrollment status as a reason to refuse issuing a graduation certificate, degree certificate, and processing graduation dispatch procedures. According to the Education Law of the People's Republic of China, the Degree Regulations of the People's Republic of China, and the Provisional Implementation Measures for the Degree Regulations of the People's Republic of China, the defendant should fulfill the statutory duties of issuing graduation and degree certificates. Therefore, he requests the court to order the defendant:

1. To issue graduation and degree certificates;

2. To promptly and effectively process graduation dispatch procedures;

3. To compensate for economic losses of 3,000 yuan;

4. To publicly apologize and restore his reputation in the school newspaper;

5. To bear the litigation costs of this case.

The defendant argued that based on the instructions of the former State Education Commission about maintaining examination discipline, the school formulated the Emergency Notice on Strict Examination Management (referred to as the 068 Notice) on December 22, 1994. This notice stipulated that any student caught cheating would be expelled and lose enrollment status. On February 29, 1996, during the retake exam for electromagnetism, the plaintiff was found with a paper containing electromagnetism formulas, discovered by the proctor, and the exam was immediately stopped. Based on the 068 Notice, the school announced the plaintiff's cheating behavior in the “End-of-Term Exam Work Bulletin”
