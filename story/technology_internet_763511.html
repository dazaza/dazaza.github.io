Facebook's New Feature: User Updates Can Be Embedded on Third-Party Web Pages

by anonymous on 2013-08-02 10:32:34

Facebook announced on Wednesday the launch of embedded posts, a feature that allows users to embed their status updates into any third-party webpage outside of Facebook. The content that can be embedded includes photos, status updates, posts with hashtags, and videos.

In the competition for real-time information with Twitter, this move will lead to direct confrontation between the two platforms. Prior to this, Twitter had already allowed users to embed tweets directly into third-party webpages, thereby helping these websites improve the interactivity of their content.

Twitter's strength lies in its public nature. By default, all tweets are displayed publicly unless the user manually locks their account.

Facebook has also recognized this value and is gradually increasing the openness of its social network. Last December, Facebook enabled by default the ability to search all profiles. Earlier this year, the company also embedded its photo-sharing service Instagram into webpages.

Even though Facebook already owns the world's largest private social network, the company also hopes to compete with Twitter in terms of real-time capabilities, pushing its content to third-party websites to expand content distribution channels and participate in global conversations.

For instance, hashtags need to be seen by many people to truly become a thread of conversation. So, if hashtags can now spread on websites like CNN, Mashable, and "Huffington Post," more people will see and join the conversation, ultimately adding their own hashtags on Facebook.

In recent years, Facebook has clearly indicated that real-time interaction could bring substantial revenue if it can attract top advertisers during prime time — aligning with Twitter's "second screen" strategy.

Facebook expects the embedded posts feature to roll out gradually over the coming weeks. However, third-party websites wishing to embed this content must install a plugin on their site, which may slow down the adoption process to some extent, but major websites are expected to participate first.
