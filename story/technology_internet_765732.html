Apple May Revolutionize Payment Methods with Fingerprint Technology

by anonymous on 2013-08-20 14:49:50

Beijing, August 18 - According to reports, Apple's next-generation iPhone is rumored to integrate fingerprint recognition technology. An analysis by Forbes magazine suggests that Apple could very well lead a new revolution in the payment sector in a highly stylish manner.

The report mentioned that what was originally an innocent statement triggered a storm of anger: Earlier this week, when New York City Mayor Michael Bloomberg mentioned the use of anti-theft locks with fingerprint technology in public housing, it immediately ignited the anger of candidates who want to replace him. Meanwhile, rumors that the next generation of iPhones will be equipped with a fingerprint scanner have thrilled the tech world.

So the question now is: Will the fingerprint scanner on the new iPhone be just a gimmick, forgotten in a year or two? Or will it give new life to this century-old technology?

In the United States, fingerprint technology is applied in multiple fields, but its closest relationship with the public is still within the criminal domain. People working for the government, those in the financial sector, or those frequently handling restricted information often record their fingerprints. Parents applying for adoption need to record their fingerprints; people obtaining green cards or applying for residency status also need to record their fingerprints. As early as 10 years ago, some PCs were already equipped with built-in fingerprint scanners. Installing fingerprint scanners on door locks in banks and government offices has become very common. However, most of the American public opposes these technologies, fearing that the government will establish powerful databases capable of identifying everyone.

Relatively speaking, people overseas record their fingerprints more frequently. For example, Europeans applying for passports need to record the fingerprints of all ten fingers, which are stored on a chip in the passport cover. The situation is similar in other developed countries.

Therefore, introducing a fingerprint scanner on the next-generation iPhone would make Americans angry but delight people in other countries. China has been using fingerprints as official identity proof since the Qing Dynasty, but it wasn't until the 19th century that this concept became popular in the West and was used in the criminal domain.

Fingerprint technology has long been used in non-criminal contexts in other countries. For instance, in the UK, students regularly record their fingerprints and authenticate them to access books at the library, attend classes, or pay for meals.

Last year, Apple acquired Authentec, one of the leaders in fingerprint technology, for $356 million, signaling that Apple took this field very seriously. Authentec isn’t the kind of company mentioned in various news reports because its technology is embedded in other devices. In fact, at the time of the acquisition, Authentec was providing fingerprint scanners for Samsung and Lenovo laptops. The company was also transitioning towards security services in the mobile sector, with Samsung being one of its major clients.

Apple could have obtained the technology through licensing like other companies and integrated it into their devices, but they believed this technology was important enough to warrant an acquisition.

The disappearance of this technology from the production lines of many of Apple’s competitors indicates that the acquisition abruptly terminated relevant fingerprint scanner licensing agreements.

But why does Apple favor this technology with negative connotations? Apple always looks at things differently and can often persuade consumers to change their behavior. With millions of credit card data in hand, Apple is sitting on a rich "payment data" mine. To lead a new payment revolution, the final step is to identify each user with a fashionable technology, and the century-old fingerprint technology perfectly fits Apple's needs. Therefore, Apple secured an important position in the fingerprint technology field through acquisition.

Imagine unlocking your iPad by pressing a button on the side of the screen with your finger, creating a completely different experience from before. Your girlfriend's installed apps or your child's favorite games disappear, instead, it becomes your independent space. Each desktop displayed on the iPad is associated with the recognized fingerprint.

Furthermore, take your phone to Starbucks or similar stores, all you need to do is scan your fingerprint, verify your identity information, and then the credit card linked to your identity successfully makes the purchase. Square would likely struggle to compete with such a solution, allowing Apple to reform the way money is transacted.

When embedded in the Airdrop app, the fingerprint scanner could become a way for friends to split bills at restaurants. Without cash, all procedures are completed by Apple's iWallet containing credit card information.

Now, when purchasing a new device at an Apple store, the method involves presenting your credit card and swiping it through Verifone's client. Tomorrow, all you need to do is press a button, provide your fingerprint information, and after the Apple client verifies your identity data, the transaction is complete. No NFC chips required, no credit cards needed, just your fingerprint.

If Apple can offer these use cases, it will undoubtedly gain a head start in the next generation of payment and identity management tools. In 2011, Motorola attempted to embed a fingerprint scanner into phones, but due to poor reliability, it failed to be accepted by a large number of users. These application methods will eventually extend to security areas (such as unlocking phones with fingerprints rather than passwords) and general identity verification areas (using phones as identity tools in corporate environments). As it continues to develop, Apple will build a powerful identity database.
