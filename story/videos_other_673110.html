Analyze the causes and consequences of how to guard against the risks of domain hijacking

by xwkj0983 on 2012-02-23 13:53:15

On the evening of January 23, a hacker group UGNazi hacked into the website of luxury brand Coach. During the hours when the domain name was hijacked, those who wanted to buy the new Willis handbag from Coach.com and those who wanted to purchase the Penelope shoulder bag from Coachfactory.com were all redirected to UGNazi's website. This incident truly frustrated the buyers.

Coach was lucky because the hackers were politically motivated rather than for commercial extortion. UGNazi targeted Coach because this heavily counterfeited luxury goods company supported the controversial SOPA (Stop Online Piracy Act). If UGNazi wanted to further threaten Coach and its customers, they could have controlled the emails sent to Coach.com or directed customers directly to phishing sites. UGNazi stated on their own website: "We don't want to steal user data; we just hope to make people aware of the harm that SOPA, PIPA, and ACTA bring to the internet."

A spokesperson for Coach told CIO.com that the domain hijacking did not significantly impact the company's business. Other companies whose domains had been hijacked might not be so lucky. For example, in 2008, when hackers attacked CheckFree.com, they redirected the webpage to a Ukrainian website and then downloaded malware onto CheckFree users' computers (the malware aimed to steal usernames and passwords). The users of CheckFree were not the only ones vulnerable to attack. Some small bank clients with partnerships providing online installment payment services were also affected because their websites were linked to CheckFree.com, said Lars Harvey, CEO of Internet Identity, an American security company.

Domain hijacking is serious because it can pose a threat to sensitive corporate information. Ram Mohan, CTO of domain registrar Afilias, said that hackers have the authority to view all emails received by the company, causing damage by sharing credential information.

Mohan said he knew of a company whose domain was hacked for five months without the company realizing it. They only discovered it when their domain was completely hijacked. The reason was that the hackers were very cautious: instead of redirecting users to another site, they directed them to a prepared domain but listened to all traffic. During this period, all data and emails from the company's website were routed through a set of servers installed by the hackers. Mohan said this kind of damage was very serious because it was highly concealed.

The Growing Threat of Domain Hijacking

Harvey and Mohan said that cases of domain hijacking are increasing because of their destructive power, as many companies now have online stores, and also because domain hijacking is easy to carry out.

"Criminals have found that the value of domain hijacking far exceeds other forms of attacks," Mohan said. "Hackers can now effectively conduct online identity theft. They already have access to a company's online identity, and the company's brand can be exclusively held by hackers."

Mohan added that his company has found that since 2005, the number of domain hijacking cases has tripled. In fact, the growth rate of domain hijacking exceeds the growth rate of domains. In 2005, there were fewer than one billion domains on the internet. By the end of 2011, the number of domains exceeded 2.2 billion.

Harvey said that many companies do not take seriously the fact that they cannot protect their domains. He estimated that this might be the result of domain registration always being handled by the legal department rather than the security department of the enterprise, domain registration.

Mohan said that when someone in the IT department wants to buy a domain, they may cancel the protection provided by the supplier because they don't want to pay extra fees or because they don't realize they need protection.

Harvey said, "Companies should view domains as valuable assets that are easily damaged."

Some Domain Registrars Are Just Going Through the Motions

Hackers can use a large number of techniques to attack a domain. One method is through the company's domain registrar. If the domain registrar does not have adequate security measures, it may allow multiple incorrect password entries, Mohan said. Hackers who know the administrator can repeatedly try usernames and passwords to enter the system.

"Data protected by username and password is not secure," Harvey added. "Hackers can socially engineer usernames and passwords through phishing emails. Hackers once attempted this with Comcast."

Hackers can also try the old trick of "forgot password." To obtain the password, they can pretend to be registered users who forgot their password. They click the "forgot password" link on the registrar's webpage. If the registrar allows them to enter the email used to receive the password or password reset instructions, the hacker can easily control the domain.

A third method is to exploit known security vulnerabilities on the server. Mohan said that not long ago, a client of Afilias had their website hacked because the technical department forgot to upgrade the latest MySQL patch. Hackers obtained the domain's username and password and exploited a vulnerability in the customer's MySQL database to gain access to the entire website.

Four Ways to Protect Your Domain

If every company took good protective measures, domains would not be easily hijacked, Harvey said. Fortunately, IT administrators can prevent their company's domain from being hijacked through simple steps.

1. Choose an Enterprise-Level Domain Registrar

Some domain companies target consumers and small businesses. As a result, they cannot provide the same level of security protection as enterprise-level domain registrars.

"Many companies usually choose the cheapest supplier or one that offers special services," Mohan said. "Your cost may only be $20, but the loss after your domain is hijacked will be much more than that."

Harvey added, "When your website transactions involve millions of dollars, you should choose the appropriate security level."

He mentioned that Coach.com is maintained by Network Solutions, a domain registrar and hosting provider. From their website, it appears that their service targets mainly small businesses.

You should look for specific security features when choosing a domain registrar:

Two-factor authentication or callback verification. Harvey said that if the domain registrar deploys advanced verification methods, most hijackings they have seen could be prevented.

Deploy multiple locking functions for your domain. Harvey said to ensure that both registration locks and registrar locks are turned on. Mohan said companies can lock their real domains. Some registrars also offer locks to prevent domain hijacking.

Some registrars automatically lock after three failed password attempts and do not send login credentials to any email.

2. Keep Security Patches Up-to-Date

Ensure that your web server uses the latest security patches so that hackers cannot exploit known software vulnerabilities. Otherwise, you're asking for trouble. Because domain hijacking is just a matter of time. This is the lesson learned by Mohan's clients who never applied the latest MySQL patch.

3. Monitor Where Your Website Traffic Goes

If you see mysterious website traffic heading to servers located in Ukraine, like what happened with CheckFree, something may be wrong with your site.

4. Request DNSSEC from Your Domain Registrar

Mohan said that DNSSEC adds security extensions to your domain system but cannot prevent domain hijacking. However, it is the only technology that ensures users won't be intercepted between clicking a link on the website and entering your page.
