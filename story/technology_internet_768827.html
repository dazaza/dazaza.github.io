Alipay PC Transfer Fee: All Because of WeChat

by anonymous on 2013-11-25 11:22:57

Over the weekend, the news that Alipay would start charging for PC-based transfers became a hot topic of discussion. This measure caused panic among many netizens, with some angrily criticizing Alipay for being unrighteous.

However, after consulting with industry insiders in the third-party payment sector, I came to the conclusion that the primary purpose of this adjustment is to counter WeChat, which has recently been gaining significant momentum. While increasing revenue and promoting Alibaba's IPO are also part of the agenda, they are not the most important goals.

First, let’s take a look at what Alipay announced. Alipay will adjust the transfer fee rate for account-to-account transfers on the PC platform. The monthly free quota will be abolished, and the fee rate will be reduced from 0.5% to 0.1%, with a minimum charge of 0.5 yuan per transaction and a cap of 10 yuan per transaction. The original fee rate and free transfer quota will be discontinued starting December 3, 2013. Alipay Wallet transfers will remain free.

Okay, why do I say that Alipay made this move to counter WeChat? Here are the reasons:

1. Alipay's move aims to accelerate user migration to mobile devices. Previously, most users enjoyed the benefit of free transfers within the Alipay system, aligning with the characteristics of small-amount consumption on mobile devices. Regardless of how Alipay reduces the PC-based transfer fee rate, the concept of "PC-end charging" has already taken root in people's minds, while the complete absence of charges on mobile devices will encourage these users to switch to mobile platforms more quickly.

2. For Alipay, the mobile end is not just a payment tool but an attempt to build a "personal wallet." Notably, the announcement about Alipay charging for PC transfers was actually released on November 6 but only drew attention over the weekend. More importantly, last Friday, Alipay proudly announced that its Alipay Wallet had become an independent brand with over 100 million users. Additionally, Alipay is currently promoting face-to-face payments, using Yintai as a pilot. All these actions follow a strategic plan.

3. Most importantly, it is to counter WeChat Pay. Whether it is guiding users to mobile devices or promoting physical store payments, all these efforts are aimed at competing with WeChat. Tang Bin from YiBao Payment stated that WeChat poses a significant threat to Alipay. In the mobile era, payments will become more O2O and personal. As an extremely heavy social app, WeChat holds advantages far beyond those of Alipay in the PC era. What worries Jack Ma even more is that after WeChat launched bank card payment functionality, this threat became even more apparent. Therefore, countering WeChat is the top priority.

4. Even Pony Ma said that Alipay's changes were to counter WeChat. In his famous 30,000-word speech, he mentioned: "Afterward, we saw (WeChat) mobile payments rise. Now, Alibaba is particularly focused on this because they see WeChat leading ahead, needing mobile payments. Of course, it may also be due to the stimulation of WeChat Pay, which suddenly dominated the market. It is rumored that Alibaba has mobilized its best hands to focus on wireless business, aiming to secure several so-called tickets, so Alipay Wallet followed suit."

Of course, Alipay's actions are also for profit. After all, in the mature PC market, Alipay firmly controls the majority of users. For those who care about the cost of fees, Alipay suggests you can use the mobile end. If you don't mind paying, well, then continue to pay Alipay a little extra money on the PC end.

Tang Bin pointed out that as a commercial company, Alibaba first offers free services and then starts charging, a tactic it often uses, much like Taobao did initially. Eventually, it also started charging in a trial and indirect manner. He even predicted that if Alipay's mobile end takes off, it will likely adopt the same charging method.

Another senior executive in the third-party payment industry noted that free PC transfers were essentially a loss-leader to attract users. After years of development, Alipay no longer needs such promotional strategies as its PC user base has stabilized.

Alipay is also cheering for Alibaba's upcoming IPO.

If you haven't forgotten, Alibaba Group is set to go public soon, although the listed company is separate from Alipay.

However, reviewing Yahoo's past announcements reveals a noteworthy point: "Before Alipay goes public, 49.9% of its pre-tax profits each year will be paid to Alibaba Group."

With the year-end approaching, charging for PC-based transfers at Alipay will certainly increase its overall profit, which will be included in Alibaba Group's financial report. Better-looking numbers could help Alibaba fetch a better price, right?

Finally, here are the transfer fee rates for Alipay and Tenpay for your reference:

Alipay: The fee rate has been reduced from the original 0.5% to 0.1%, with a minimum charge of 0.5 yuan per transaction and a maximum of 10 yuan per transaction. The original fee rate and free transfer quota will be discontinued starting December 3, 2013.

Prior to this, non-certified Alipay users had a monthly free transfer quota of 1,000 yuan, certified users had a quota of 10,000 yuan, and gold account users had a quota of 20,000 yuan. Any amount exceeding these quotas was subject to a 0.5% fee, with a minimum charge of 1 yuan per transaction and a cap of 25 yuan per transaction.

For example, if a non-certified user needed to transfer 1,500 yuan, previously they would have to pay 500 * 0.5% = 2.5 yuan, but after the adjustment, they will need to pay 1,500 * 0.1% = 1.5 yuan. Thus, the adjusted fee rate will primarily affect transfer transactions within the free quota, and transfer transactions exceeding the free quota may not necessarily incur higher charges.

Tenpay: Ordinary individual users have a monthly free transfer quota of 1,000 yuan, and verified users have a quota of 10,000 yuan. Any amount exceeding these quotas is subject to a 0.25% service fee, with a minimum charge of 1 yuan per transaction and a cap of 25 yuan per transaction. Similar to Alipay, Tenpay's mobile client transfers are also free.
