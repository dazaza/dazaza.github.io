Mobile phone pre-installed software must be audited by MIIT from next month

by anonymous on 2013-10-15 21:31:43

Starting Next Month, Pre-installed Mobile Apps Must Be Reviewed by MIIT

In response to the frequent reports of smartphone users being "held hostage" by pre-installed apps, the Notice on Strengthening the Administration of Mobile Smart Terminal Network Access (hereinafter referred to as the "Notice") that will be officially implemented next month by the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology (MIIT) clearly requires that mobile phone manufacturers must pass a review by the MIIT for any pre-installed software. Furthermore, this regulation prohibits manufacturers from installing software that collects or modifies user information without consent, causing issues such as data consumption, financial loss, and information leakage. However, industry insiders frankly admit that these regulations may not completely solve the chaos caused by pre-installed mobile apps.

In the era of "applications reign supreme" in smartphones, most users have been "held hostage" by pre-installed apps. Recently, Beijing Business Today learned through investigation that Android-based smartphones can come with as many as 20-30 pre-installed applications, which users cannot delete. Nowadays, having over a dozen pre-installed applications is common in Android phones. These include both the manufacturer's own applications and third-party software. The main issue consumers complain about is that pre-installed apps cannot be uninstalled, and there are security risks such as unauthorized charges and theft of user privacy information.

A salesperson from a mainstream retail store revealed: "Currently, there are roughly three ways pre-installed software gets onto domestic smartphones. First, when telecom operators customize phones, they require their related applications to be bundled inside the device. Second, when the phone leaves the factory, the manufacturer pre-installs its own or third-party applications. Third, during the distribution process, distributors might collaborate with software vendors for pre-installation or later install additional software through system reflashing."

This MIIT "ban" seems to offer hope for resolving the chaos of pre-installed apps. However, industry experts remain pessimistic, believing that this regulation may still fail to provide a permanent solution for users.

Li Yi, Secretary-General of the China Mobile Internet Industry Alliance, analyzed in an interview with Beijing Business Today: "This regulation only imposes certain constraints on manufacturers at the point of phone production but lacks accompanying supervision and punishment measures. There is a regulatory vacuum between the time the phone leaves the factory and reaches the consumer, providing opportunities for malicious software to be pre-installed."

It is worth noting that for low-end phone manufacturers with already slim profits, pre-installed software has become an important source of revenue. Even after obtaining network access permits from government departments before leaving the factory, these manufacturers can still pre-install software through distribution channels.

An observer pointed out that pre-installed software in China has formed a vast black-market interest chain covering smartphone manufacturers, telecom operators, various levels of distributors, application software providers, and channel promoters. Technically speaking, severing this chain is not difficult, but relevant parties lack direct motivation and enthusiasm to address the chaos of pre-installed software, thereby sacrificing consumer interests to a large extent.

Telecommunications expert Xiang Ligang stated that eradicating the chaos of pre-installed apps depends on the government's determination to rectify the situation and the strength of its regulatory punishments. If strict requirements are imposed on manufacturers, driving them to clean up all levels of distribution channels with effective cooperation from operators, it could truly free consumers from the troubles of rampant pre-installed software.
