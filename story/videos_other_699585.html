A UV Lamp in the Kindergarten Tanned All the Children in the Class

Some lamps can also tan and injure you just like the sun.

□ Times reporter, Jiang Dawei, Wang Yanwen

Intern reporter, Xu Bingbing, photographer, Lin Yanting, graphic designer, communicator Huang Chunyan

When her daughter, Tian Tian (a pseudonym), gradually became darker, citizen Ms. Lu did not take it seriously at first. However, during one of her trips to pick up her child from kindergarten, she was shocked by what she saw. The children in Tian Tian's class, more than a dozen of them, were all as dark as if they had just returned from Africa, and each one was darker than the other. Meanwhile, the children from other classes remained fair-skinned and rosy. What was going on?

The Always-On Sterilization Lamp

Tanned the Entire Class

Ms. Lu took Tian Tian to Hangzhou Third People's Hospital, where Deputy Director Song Weimin of the dermatology department immediately knew that Tian Tian had been tanned by ultraviolet rays. Subsequently, Tian Tian's classmates, accompanied by their parents, also found Dr. Song Weimin, and their diagnoses were the same as Tian Tian's.

Ms. Lu contacted the parents of several other children and together they approached the kindergarten where her daughter attended. However, the kindergarten staff also found this very puzzling because from 8 am to 4 pm every day, except for a 20-minute morning exercise session outdoors, the children spent most of their time indoors. So how could they have been tanned by ultraviolet rays?

All the parents were perplexed, but an unintentionally disclosed detail by the kindergarten teacher led Dr. Song Weimin to find the culprit - a UV sterilization lamp installed in the classroom.

According to regulations, the UV sterilization lamp in the kindergarten classroom should be turned on for about an hour daily. However, due to the teachers' unfamiliarity with the operating procedures, the UV sterilization lamp in the classroom had been left on continuously. Over time, excessive ultraviolet rays had tanned the children's skin.

Young Women Who Love Nightclubs

Get Tanned by Spotlights at Night

With summer approaching, effective sun protection has once again become a major concern for everyone. However, some cases seen at the dermatology outpatient clinic of Hangzhou Third People's Hospital indicate that not only sunlight but also various indoor lights can tan or even injure the skin, and some people even get tanned at night.

"Cases of being tanned or injured by lights are actually quite common, and those dozen children are not isolated incidents," said Dr. Song Weimin, a dermatology expert who has encountered many cases of people being tanned or injured by lights. One young woman, who was particularly careful about sun protection during the day, still got tanned. Upon inquiry, it was discovered that she loved going to nightclubs and was tanned by the various high-power spotlights in the bars which emit ultraviolet rays.

In addition, some female TV anchors frequently visit Dr. Song Weimin's clinic. After each program recording, their skin begins to redden, gradually becoming darker and deteriorating. The main culprit here is also the various lights that shine on their faces in the studio every day.

Two Types of Ultraviolet Rays

Most Likely to Damage Human Skin

Why can lights tan or even injure the skin? First, you need to understand ultraviolet rays.

Professor Lu Xuanhui, deputy director of the Institute of Optics at Zhejiang University, introduced that ultraviolet rays refer to electromagnetic waves with wavelengths ranging from 100nm (nanometers) to 400nm. Among these, wavelengths from 100nm to 200nm are vacuum ultraviolet rays (UV-D), wavelengths from 200nm to 280nm are short-wave ultraviolet rays (UV-C), wavelengths from 280nm to 315nm are medium-wave ultraviolet rays (UV-B), and wavelengths from 315nm to 400nm are long-wave ultraviolet rays (UV-A).

The wavelength of ultraviolet rays is inversely proportional to their energy. The longer the wavelength, the stronger the ability to penetrate the skin. The shorter the wavelength, the stronger the energy, making it easier to cause skin lesions.

UV-D cannot effectively propagate in the air, while the shorter-wavelength UV-C is almost completely absorbed by the ozone layer. Therefore, the ultraviolet rays in natural sunlight mainly consist of UV-A and UV-B, with UV-A accounting for about 98.1% and UV-B accounting for 1.1%.

"UV-A can penetrate the epidermis to reach the dermis and affect the melanin in the epidermis, leading to increased melanin deposition and darkening of the skin," Professor Lu Xuanhui explained. "On the other hand, UV-B almost cannot penetrate the epidermis. When high-energy UV-B acts on the skin for a long time, photodermatitis can occur, resulting in redness, itching, blisters, and swelling on the skin. Excessive exposure to ultraviolet rays can also lead to skin cancer."

Some Lights Also Emit Ultraviolet Rays

Halogen Lamps Not Recommended for Home Use

Now let's revisit the UV sterilization lamp in that kindergarten. To achieve the best sterilization effect, the wavelength of the UV sterilization lamp is usually set at 253.7nm, which falls within the range of the most energetic UV-C. It is no surprise then that the children were tanned.

"Some lights can also emit ultraviolet rays, and the intensity of the radiation varies depending on the type, power, color, and manufacturing process of the lamp," said Deputy Director Song Weimin of the Dermatology Department at Hangzhou Third People's Hospital. "Especially those high-power metal halide lamps, which emit medium- and long-wave ultraviolet rays similar to those emitted by sunlight, can damage the skin."

Professor Lu Xuanhui of Zhejiang University also pointed out that many public places such as bars, restaurants, malls, and stadiums often use lamps with strong ultraviolet radiation. The wavelengths of these radiated ultraviolet rays are approximately between 300nm and 400nm, falling within the medium- and long-wave ultraviolet radiation range, which has a significant impact on human skin.

"Many households now like to use metal halide lamps when decorating their homes, and some even install them in their bedrooms. Although these lamps are bright and aesthetically pleasing, prolonged exposure can have considerable effects on people," Professor Lu Xuanhui said.

Lack of Safety Quantitative Standards for Lamp UV Radiation Intensity

Although the ultraviolet rays emitted by lamps can tan and injure people, Professor Lu Xuanhui noted that there are currently no safety quantitative standards for lamp UV radiation intensity in China. However, the degree of harm caused by lamp UV to humans depends on various factors, such as the performance of the lamp, its distance from the body, and the duration of exposure.

Among the lamps we commonly encounter, special lamps such as metal halide lamps, UV sterilization lamps, and UV insect traps have relatively higher UV radiation intensity, while ordinary incandescent lamps do not produce UV.

The principle of fluorescent lamps and energy-saving lamps involves emitting heat electrons through electrodes to excite mercury atoms to release UV, but after the UV passes through the fluorescent powder coated on the inner side of the lamp tube and is converted into visible light, only a trace amount of UV-A range UV is emitted, which does not significantly affect human health.

People who work under lamps with higher UV radiation for long periods or are too close to the lamps, such as store clerks, restaurant waiters, and TV anchors, have a greater chance of skin damage. However, ordinary people do not need to worry too much as long as they do not stay under these lamps for too long or stand directly under them or get too close.

How to Combat UV Rays

Should You Apply Sunscreen Every 15 Minutes Like Big S?

Experts from Hangzhou Third People's Hospital: This Concept Is Worth Learning

Sunlight contains UV rays, and some lights also contain UV rays. In the face of omnipresent UV rays in life, how should we protect our skin? Should we follow the advice of famous artist Big S in "Beauty Master" and reapply sunscreen every 15 minutes to ensure our skin is not harmed?

Deputy Director Song Weimin of the Dermatology Department at Hangzhou Third People's Hospital said that although applying sunscreen every 15 minutes may seem exaggerated and impractical, this concept is worth promoting because the awareness of UV protection among Chinese people is still relatively weak.

Excessive UV Exposure

Can Easily Trigger Skin Diseases

Statistics show that Hangzhou Third People's Hospital diagnoses nearly a thousand skin cancer patients annually, and this number is increasing year by year.

"Compared to Caucasians, Asians have a relatively lower incidence of skin cancer and skin diseases, but our measures to protect against UV rays are still relatively insufficient," Director Song said. Many skin diseases are caused by excessive UV exposure.

"Moderate sun exposure every day can help supplement calcium and is beneficial to the body. Generally, 15 minutes of sun exposure per day is sufficient. If exposed to excessive UV radiation, it can damage the skin," Director Song said. Besides tanning and burning the skin, UV rays can also accelerate skin aging.

Physical Sun Protection is More Reliable

Reapply Every Two to Three Hours

In addition to using umbrellas and clothing to protect against UV rays, Director Song believes that correctly using sunscreens is also necessary, and these protective measures are not exclusive to women; men should also follow suit.

"Currently, there are mainly two types of sunscreens available in the market: physical and chemical. The former generally offers better sun protection and can effectively resist most UV rays that harm the skin, but the downside is that these sunscreens tend to clog pores," Director Song said.

The other type is chemical sunscreen. Using this kind of sunscreen makes the skin feel relatively fresh, but compared to physical sunscreens, its effectiveness is much weaker. Moreover, after using chemical sunscreen, it generally takes half an hour before it starts to work; otherwise, it will have no sun protection or UV resistance effect.

As for Big S's practice of applying sunscreen every 15 minutes, Director Song believes that although it might be somewhat exaggerated, many people apply sunscreen only once in the morning and then do not reapply for the rest of the day, which is even less advisable. The correct frequency of using sunscreen products is to reapply according to the environment every two to three hours, which can provide effective protection.
