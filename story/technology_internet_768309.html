Twitter Once Rejected a Facebook Buyout Offer

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 20:18:03

November 5 news, according to the tech blog Techcrunch, Twitter co-founder Jack Dorsey said that Facebook CEO Mark Zuckerberg had made two attempts to acquire Twitter.

Regarding the acquisition details, New York Times reporter Nick Bilton described them thoroughly in his new book "Hatching Twitter: A True Story of Money, Power, Friendship, and Betrayal," where he also recreated the three reasons why Twitter rejected the offer.

Please see the summary by Techcrunch editor Matthew Panzarino after reading the book:

In late October 2008, Dorsey had just been removed from the CEO position and only retained the title of chairman without voting or operational rights. The other two Twitter co-founders, Ev Williams and Biz Stone, were invited to Facebook to meet with Zuckerberg to discuss the acquisition of Twitter.

Bilton explained that Zuckerberg had spent months working on a plan for the acquisition with Dorsey. However, after Dorsey was forced out as CEO, all those efforts came to nothing. In an email Zuckerberg sent to Dorsey, he listed several reasons why Facebook and Twitter should merge. One reason was that Facebook would also "develop a product line similar to Twitter." The implicit threat was, sell to us, or we will compete directly with you.

Williams and Stone proposed a valuation of $500 million during the meeting. This number was within Zuckerberg's expectations because it aligned with previous communications with Dorsey.

However, the acquisition did not go through. The reasons can be seen from an email Williams submitted to the board, which is partially quoted in the book.

Williams wrote that for him, there needed to be three reasons to sell a company:

1. The price is high enough or the valuation reflects the company’s future potential ("We often say Twitter is a billion-dollar company. I think Twitter is worth much more than that," the email stated).

2. There is an urgent and direct threat from competitors ("Twitter does not face any external threats that could destroy it").

3. You have the opportunity to join an exceptional company ("I don’t use Facebook. I also have many concerns about their employees and business model").

Evidently, Williams already valued Twitter at far more than $1 billion in 2008. At the time, Twitter had 11 million users, and starting in early 2009, user numbers began to grow exponentially. Currently, Twitter's IPO indicates a company valuation of $11.9 billion, showing that even in its early stages, Twitter's management was confident in its potential.

This confidence is also reflected in the belief that no competitor could pose a substantial threat. Twitter's concept and operating model were unique, and even a company like Facebook would struggle to replicate it successfully. The book also mentions an interesting anecdote: Yahoo! once offered $12 million to acquire Twitter in its early days but was politely turned down. Even though the company only had 250,000 users at the time, the co-founders treated this offer as a joke.

Williams also had doubts about cultural incompatibility. Twitter's original intention was to democratize human network connections. Williams felt this was incompatible with Facebook's corporate culture, and he did not want to be influenced by Facebook. This shows that Silicon Valley is not just a place driven by money. Many people are still striving to transcend original sins.

We cannot ignore the threatening tone implied by Facebook during the negotiations. As we know, several cases have failed due to Facebook's threatening attitude. In 2011, three years later, Facebook launched the Subscribe feature to clone Twitter's "follow" function, which was later renamed "Follow."

Dorsey's attitude towards Facebook's acquisition offer was ambivalent. He said, "If the price is right, success can be achieved either way." At the time, he had just left the CEO position, and the hope of regaining real power in the company was very slim. Although he later returned to the management team with the support of friendly investor Peter Fenton, his viewpoint at the time is understandable.

The board ultimately agreed with Williams' perspective and rejected the acquisition offer. Zuckerberg then launched another aggressive campaign against Dorsey but refused to offer him a product director position. Dorsey has never visited Facebook again, and when Twitter went public, he regained shares with voting rights.

Williams published a blog post earlier this year about this acquisition, where he also listed the above three reasons, just without explicitly naming Facebook as the bidder.

We can imagine that if Facebook had successfully acquired Twitter, they would have created a global social networking monopoly. This would be another example of the entanglement between business acumen and moral standards. (Translated by Prosperous Tea)
