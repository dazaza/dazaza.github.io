It will also "expand its stores".

by wmakebn6 on 2011-07-12 11:05:37

After Gucci grandly opened its 2000-square-meter flagship store in a prime location in Shanghai, the next move came from Prada, another luxury brand that has set its sights on the Chinese market. Prada's new store is located across the street from Gucci’s in Shanghai, where these two luxury brands are now competing with each other. To prove their "top-tier" status, could there be anything more convincing than opening numerous large stores?

Gucci's flagship store at Heng隆 Plaza

Following Gucci's (Gucci) high-profile opening of a 2000-square-meter flagship store in a prime location in Shanghai, it was immediately reported that Louis Vuitton (LV), which had been present at Heng隆 Plaza for many years, would also "expand," aiming to at least match the size of Gucci's store. In response to this rumor, Louis Vuitton China did not confirm or deny it to CBN reporters but provided materials showing that its current store area at Heng隆 Plaza is 900 square meters, with no immediate plans to expand publicly available.

The competition between these top two global luxury brands remains unresolved, as another Italian luxury brand reveals its ambitions for the Chinese market.

Prada Store at Heng隆 Plaza

Across the street from Gucci's newly opened store in June, the brand making its next move is Prada (Prada). The sales staff at Prada's existing store at Heng隆 Plaza confirmed to CBN reporters that they will indeed open a new store in two months, which will span two floors. Originally, this space was occupied by a Japanese jewelry brand.

In fact, under the backdrop of the economic crisis, the financial condition of this actively expanding Italian brand isn't so good either. Due to a decline in comparable single-store sales last year, Prada experienced a reduction in disposable cash and an increase in costs, exacerbating their situation. After restructuring $1.6 billion in debt in April, they were able to secure funds for expanding new stores. Last year, Prada opened 20 stores, including outlets in London, Los Angeles, and Kuala Lumpur, as well as a showroom in Seoul.

CBN reporters learned from Prada's official website that Prada has already opened 8 stores in mainland China, including Chengdu, Qingdao, and second-tier cities such as Shenyang and Xi'an. This number of stores is only surpassed by Hong Kong (9 stores) and Japan (13 stores). By comparison, in its home country of Italy, they have 8 stores, while in the U.S., they have 9.

In the past year, luxury market consumption has shown a pattern of shifting trends, with both store layouts and operational figures providing compelling evidence.

CBN reporters learned that in the first quarter of 2009, the total sales of the high-end consumer goods group Gucci Group under the French PPR Group decreased by 3.4% compared to the same period last year (considering exchange rate factors), but the total income in the Asia-Pacific region (excluding Japan) surged by 25%, with Greater China contributing significantly with a 13% increase. Similarly, during the same period, the Swiss Richemont Group saw a similar trend: by the end of March, the group's sales increased by only 2%, but sales in the Asia-Pacific region (excluding Japan) soared by 14%.

Last year, the growth rate of luxury consumption in Europe was only half of what it was in 2007, while the U.S. remained relatively flat and Japan experienced negative growth. Against this backdrop, "the industry believes that the demand for international luxury brands has generally declined in the European, American, and Japanese markets. However, the results of this survey are reassuring: the economic downturn has not significantly affected mainland China, and the market still has tremendous growth potential." Christophe Cais, Executive Director of Albatross Consulting, a luxury market research company in Asia, analyzed for CBN reporters. Meanwhile, Jean-Michel Dumont, Chairman of Ruder Finn Asia, confirmed that after the test of the financial crisis, major luxury brands have further recognized the importance of the Chinese market. First-tier cities are strategic strongholds for establishing and solidifying brand images, while second-tier cities are the key to deep-rooted success in the Chinese market.

"As of January this year, the total amount of luxury consumption in China has accounted for 25% of the global market, reaching $8.6 billion, surpassing the U.S. for the first time and becoming the world's second-largest luxury consumption market," according to a report titled "2009 China Luxury Report" jointly released yesterday by Albatross Global Solutions’ Mission and Ruder Finn.

Based on the survey results of 1000 consumers with annual incomes of RMB 240,000, over 50% of respondents stated that their consumption behavior would not be affected, while only 20% expressed doubts about their consumption capacity in the next year.

The sampling survey results published in the report show that Louis Vuitton, Chanel, and Gucci rank among the top three in terms of mention rates by mainland Chinese consumers, while Prada ranks third alongside Gucci among Hong Kong consumers.

"Self-use, personal gifts, and business exchanges are the three main reasons for purchasing luxury goods. In second-tier cities, the number of people purchasing luxury goods for 'business exchanges' is more than three times that of first-tier cities," said Christophe Cais.
