Apple and Samsung's patent negotiations continue, as the two sides had once been close to reaching a settlement.

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 12:40:58

Beijing, July 20th - According to foreign media reports, based on court documents and insiders familiar with the situation, since last summer when Apple won a patent lawsuit against Samsung, the two companies have engaged in a series of private negotiations over patent disputes and were once very close to reaching a settlement. Currently, Samsung hopes to facilitate a comprehensive patent licensing agreement, but Apple has not made any comments.

One of the court documents revealed that both parties had conducted face-to-face negotiations in Seoul, the capital of South Korea, in December last year. In February, the two companies were even close to reaching an agreement, but it eventually fell through. Insiders stated that there are currently no signs indicating that both parties are close to reaching an agreement, but the negotiations are still ongoing.

A part of the files disclosed earlier this month by the US International Trade Commission mentioned sporadically that during the negotiations, Samsung hoped to promote a broad patent cross-licensing agreement aimed at resolving all lawsuits between the two companies. It is unclear whether Apple is interested in such an agreement.

Potential extensive settlement agreements or a series of individual agreements would be another testament to the intricate relationship between these two tech giants. Since 2005, Samsung has provided necessary components for Apple's iPod music players, but subsequently, the two companies became embroiled in fierce smartphone market competition.

In the smartphone field, Samsung mainly relied on Google's Android platform starting from the second half of 2011. Market research company Gartner's data showed that Samsung sold 24 million smartphones alone in the third quarter of 2011. In comparison, Apple sold 17 million iPhones in the same quarter. According to Gartner's data, in the first quarter of this year, Samsung further expanded its leading position in the smartphone market with sales reaching 64.7 million units, while Apple's iPhone sales were 38.3 million units. The two companies have maintained a "friend-enemy" relationship.

Apple filed a patent lawsuit in 2011, accusing Samsung of copying its product design, which sparked global legal disputes over technology patents and trademark designs between the two companies. Both hope to win courtroom battles while banning each other's products from the market.

Samsung accused Apple of infringing on a series of key patents in the wireless device sector, while Apple mainly accused Samsung of infringing on its unique mobile device functions and appearance design. So far, the outcome remains uncertain.

Last August, a jury at a court in San Jose, California, ruled that some Samsung devices infringed on Apple patents, ordering Samsung to pay Apple over $1 billion in damages. The jury believed that out of six patents infringed by Samsung, three involved the iPhone's appearance and screen icon design. The judge presiding over the trial later reduced the compensation amount, but Samsung still appealed.

In June this year, the US International Trade Commission made a ruling in favor of Samsung, issuing a ban on several models of Apple's iPhones and iPads, including the AT&T version of the iPhone 4. The commission has already submitted this ruling to the White House, and if President Obama does not overturn this decision within 60 days, the ban will take effect. So far, Obama has less than three weeks to make his judgment.

Both companies have reasons to reach a settlement. Their relationship is very complex; Samsung provides components for Apple's mobile devices, and Apple is Samsung's largest customer in the processor and memory chip sectors. Although Apple is trying to reduce its reliance on Samsung and recently signed an agreement with TSMC, which will start supplying chips to Apple from 2014, there are also local Korean media reports claiming that Apple still signed a contract with Samsung for chip supply in 2015. The authenticity of this report has not been verified.

Analyst Bob O'Donnell of IDC said: "The relationship between these two companies is a typical symbiotic one." He believes that despite Apple's continuous efforts to break free from its dependence on Samsung, it will still have to rely on Samsung for some components in the near future. O'Donnell said: "This is a painful transition period."

The history of the negotiations between the two companies can be traced back to 2010. A court document during last year's litigation showed that Apple proposed a patent licensing agreement to Samsung, charging $30 per phone and $40 per tablet. If Samsung agreed to a cross-licensing of patents, the price could be discounted. Samsung also proposed a patent licensing agreement to Apple, but Apple complained about the high price.

Most of the details of the negotiations are included in recent files where the US International Trade Commission explained its ruling on June 4th, mentioning most of the settlement terms proposed by both parties. The document states that in September last year, shortly after Apple won the lawsuit in California, it proposed a settlement agreement to Samsung.

However, the most serious negotiations took place between December last year and March this year. The file shows that multiple rounds of face-to-face negotiations were held in mid-January, but no agreement was reached. Both sides then agreed to meet again in February. On February 7th, representatives of both companies drafted a Memorandum of Understanding, proposing a settlement plan and submitted it to the senior management of both companies. However, there are no signs indicating that the management of both companies has accepted the terms of the agreement.

The negotiations continued until March, but soon broke down. On March 22nd, Samsung requested to restart the negotiations, but Apple did not respond before the deadline. However, Samsung indicated that some previous proposals could still be discussed.

The US International Trade Commission stated that it believed Samsung's proposed patent licensing fees were not unreasonable or lacking in sincerity. The document stated: "In fact, the ability of both parties' representatives to reach a Memorandum of Understanding itself demonstrates Samsung's negotiation sincerity. Simply put, both parties are on the same starting line."

Apple has consistently criticized Samsung's patent licensing fee requirements as unfair and inconsistent with its commitments to international organizations. Apple also claimed that Samsung has an obligation to first propose "a specific, fair, and reasonable licensing fee." The court did not agree with this view, stating that "existing evidence does not support Apple's position."

Apple also criticized Samsung's attempt to achieve a cross-licensing agreement for both parties' smartphone patent portfolios. The court also disagreed with this criticism, believing that "Samsung's proposal in this area is not without merit."
