Weight loss misconceptions! Don't let emotions affect your weight loss journey.

by xfnnv on 2009-01-09 16:39:28

Introduction: When you're bored or feeling down, do you always want to snack? When you're worried about something, do you feel like eating? When faced with heartbreaks such as divorce, unemployment, illness, or shattered dreams, do you get through it by eating? If your answers to most of these questions are "yes," then you are likely suffering from serious emotional eating.

When emotions turn bad, so does eating

When life becomes overwhelming with debt, unruly children, marital chaos, and work stress, irrational emotions in human nature begin to stir. You are no exception. You may fall into depression, become overly critical, filled with resentment or even hatred, living in guilt and shame. These emotions influence your behavior, causing you to eat excessively when you're feeling down.

When we feel stressed, anxious, or depressed, we instinctively want to eat. This is a natural physiological impulse. Food can affect the synthesis of certain natural chemicals in the brain that stabilize mood and psychological functions - a fact already confirmed by nutritional science. During these times, we often crave cookies, cakes, candies, or ice cream, known as "comfort foods." For many people, these foods are as hard to resist as smoking, drugs, or alcohol, leading to weight gain, physical deformation, or even a severe blow to self-esteem. Constant binge eating or improper eating to numb yourself results in greater guilt, anxiety, and stress, creating a vicious cycle.

Life rarely remains completely calm; like the tides, life has its ups and downs. It's inevitable to experience a variety of emotions, whether happy or unhappy. Therefore, regaining control over your emotions is one of the important factors in keeping life on track.

Once you start adopting this element and corresponding measures, you'll regain good feelings, reach an ideal state, and control your weight. As much as 50% of overweight individuals use food to relieve anger, stress, and disappointment. Are you one of them? Let's first conduct an emotional survey, and your answers must be absolutely honest. You will discover how much emotions drive your eating behavior. For example, do you always want snacks when you're bored? Even if you're not hungry, do you still like to eat with friends? When you're feeling down, do you want to eat something? Do you use eating to pass the time when you're lonely? When you're worried about something, do you feel like eating? When faced with heartbreaks such as divorce, unemployment, illness, or shattered dreams, do you get through it by eating? Even if you're not hungry, do you still eat? Do you think eating helps you relax and relieve stress? Do you feel like you eat too much despite being dissatisfied with your weight? If your answers to most of these questions are "yes," then you are likely suffering from serious emotional eating. Regardless of other contributing factors, it is essential to control the self-destructive nature of emotions.

It's the fault of stress

Overeating caused by stress is undoubtedly one of the main reasons for weight gain, as you consume too many calories. In response to stress, the body releases hormones that automatically stimulate appetite and intense hunger, tempting you to indulge in fattening foods. If left unchecked, stress can attack your health in other ways. Accumulated stress can lead to more severe consequences than obesity, making you more susceptible to diseases and shortening your lifespan. Prolonged exposure to stress can disrupt the natural immune system due to close contact between immune cells and nerve cells, resulting in decreased physiological function, manifested as tension headaches, migraines, back pain, fatigue, sleep disorders, ulcers, rising blood pressure, and even heart disease. Learning to control your thoughts is key to stopping this behavior, which depends on how you perceive these events in life and whether you can change your way of thinking. Many of our thought patterns are formed through personal "filters" - including our personality, attitudes, views, and beliefs - which are internal screening tools that influence how we see the world and react to it. These "filters" are largely the product of our past experiences and have a significant impact on how we interpret what happens in life. These interpretations, in turn, determine our responses to life. Thus, stress may seem insurmountable to some, while to others, it serves as motivation and transforms into an opportunity. Avoiding rather than effectively relieving stress harms physical health and shortens lifespan.

There is another type of "filter" called the "denial filter," which causes distress and stress beyond imagination. For instance, you might think obesity is merely an appearance issue and firmly deny that obesity brings health problems. Perhaps you vomit due to losing control of your diet, but you tell yourself that it's not a disorder, just something occasional, and everything will be fine once you reach your ideal weight or resolve life's stresses. The "denial filter" is dangerous because it conceals the true condition of your body. Living in denial is like a high-pressure cooker without a vent, where all the pressure builds up until the lid opens or blows off. If handled promptly, the problem might be resolved. However, if you continue to view dietary issues through the "denial filter," you allow self-deception to control your behavior.

There is also another "filter" called "automatic thoughts." Suppose someone at home criticizes you. Before you even realize it, automatic thoughts like "they never approve of me" or "they just don't accept me" are already running through your mind. Feeling hurt, you respond by absentmindedly scooping a big spoonful of ice cream into your mouth. Before feeling hurt, a negative thought flashes through your mind, one that has long taken root in your heart. When criticized by family, these latent thoughts quickly surface, so fast that you don't even realize their impact on your behavior. Fortunately, these thoughts can be questioned, controlled, and eventually changed. By altering your way of thinking, you can change your perception and behavior.

Slowing down thought processes

To change your emotional responses and the subsequent bad eating habits, you need to slow down, carefully listen to your inner voice, evaluate, and change your reactions. Rapid thoughts and inner reactions always precede emotions. If you currently feel irritated, excited, worried, lost, sad, or depressed, you are certainly saying something to yourself. From now on, whenever you feel restless, pay close attention to the voice within you.

Be sure to write down your thoughts, as writing helps you view your thoughts more objectively.

If you understand that emotional pain stems from thought, you must correct erroneous thoughts. By controlling your mental state, you can better adjust your emotions. When questioning automatic thoughts, you must ask these four questions: Is it true? Is it in my best interest? Does it protect my health? Will it help me achieve my weight loss goal?

Answering these not-so-simple questions by comparing them with reality allows you to change your unrealistic perceptions, judgments, and evaluations of yourself in a short period, regardless of how long your attitude has persisted. Use these questions as a turning point to change your inappropriate perceptions, judgments, and evaluations of yourself, stop self-criticism, and only then can you effectively control the negative emotions that revive your binge-eating behavior.

Closing the emotional floodgates

If sudden pain appears in life, you must resolutely close the emotional floodgates. Closing the floodgates means not carrying the problem or pain anymore but facing it and eliminating it.

If you persistently endure hatred, anger, and bitterness, it's because you haven't tried closing the floodgates of these negative emotions and healing the wound's pain. Choosing to carry hatred means choosing to experience life in a gloomy way, ultimately possibly seeking comfort in food.

The purpose of this step is to help you manage your emotional floodgates, giving you the ability to handle various situations and related pains in life, preventing binge eating from recurring and hindering your goal of controlling your weight.

Perhaps the most important reaction to free yourself from emotional bondage is forgiveness. Initially, forgiveness is extremely difficult because you believe it represents weakness rather than strength. However, this remarkable human act is the most fundamental positive force that closes your emotional floodgates and brings about change.

Without forgiveness, you are destined to struggle under the heavy burden of emotions and suffer from negative emotions. You might have just defended your hatred, thinking that someone who hurt you so deeply shouldn't be hated? But the cost of this belief is incredibly high because hatred is a highly permeable emotion that spreads like toxic gas throughout every corner of your life.

Forgiveness is the only way to free yourself from emotional captivity, elevating you emotionally to forgive those who have wronged you.
