Who are the real big bosses of Baidu, Tencent, and Alibaba?

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 20:09:03

According to a report by the Youth Daily, who are the real big bosses of several domestic Internet giants such as Tencent, Baidu, Alibaba, and Renren? The answer is definitely not Pony Ma, Robin Li, Jack Ma, or Charles Chen, these well-known website founders. Instead, it is the major shareholders who only appear in the financial information of each company.

Since the beginning of this year, various news and discussions surrounding Alibaba's IPO have been constant, and the relationship between Jack Ma, SoftBank, and Yahoo has been repeatedly interpreted. How to handle the relationship between the founder and the largest shareholder is a problem that domestic and foreign Internet companies must face. Usually, these major shareholders are mostly hidden from public view and only appear in the financial reports of each company. In fact, who is the boss behind QQ software, WeChat - China's largest Internet company with a market value of more than 250 billion yuan? The answer is not Pony Ma, but South Africa's MIH. The same situation also occurs in other domestic Internet giants such as Baidu, Renren, and Alibaba. This is probably why Jack Ma insists on keeping control in the hands of Alibaba partners.

Tencent: South Africa's MIH holds nearly 34% shares

Tencent is currently the largest Internet company in terms of market value in China. Its issue price was HKD 3.7 when it was listed in 2004, and yesterday it closed at HKD 416, rising more than a hundred times over the past nine years. In July this year, Tencent CEO Pony Ma became the top name on the "2013 Hurun IT Rich List" with 56 billion yuan.

However, Pony Ma is not Tencent's largest shareholder. During Tencent's early days, IDG and Pacific Century CyberWorks invested $2.2 million respectively, each holding 20% of Tencent's shares, while Pony Ma and his team held 60%. Later, South Africa's MIH Group acquired part of Tencent's shares from Pacific Century CyberWorks and Tencent's founding team.

Before listing in 2004, Tencent restructured its equity structure, with MIH and the founding team each holding 50%. Currently, South Africa's MIH Group holds 33.93% of Tencent's shares, while Pony Ma holds 10.22%, less than one-third of MIH's holdings. Fortunately, South Africa's MIH Group fully trusts Pony Ma and abandoned the voting rights of the shares it held from the start. Therefore, although Pony Ma holds a relatively small percentage of Tencent's shares, the specific management and operation of the company are mainly handled by him and several co-founders.

Baidu: US DFJ holds 25.8%

Among the Chinese Internet stocks listed in the United States, Baidu's market value exceeds $56 billion, far higher than other companies. When Baidu was listed on NASDAQ in 2005, its issue price was $27, and in 2010 it split 10-to-1. Its current stock price still exceeds $160.

Similarly, Baidu's founder and CEO Robin Li is not the company's largest shareholder. From its founding to before its listing, Baidu underwent three rounds of financing, with US investment fund Draper Fisher Jurvetson being the main investor. At the time of its listing, DFJ's shareholding ratio had reached 25.8%.

At that time, Baidu's executive team held 43.5% of the company's shares, with Robin Li personally holding 22.4%, making him the largest individual shareholder. According to Baidu's 2012 annual report, Robin Li's shareholding ratio changed to 15.98%, and his wife Ma Dongmin held 4.8%.

Although the combined shareholding ratio of Robin Li and Ma Dongmin is 20.78%, which is less than DFJ, the US stock market allows for a dual-class "AB share" structure. Through this model, Robin Li and Ma Dongmin's voting rights are as high as 68.17%, firmly grasping control of the company.

Alibaba: Japan's SoftBank holds 31.9%

In 2012, Alibaba and Yahoo signed an agreement where Alibaba repurchased 20% of its shares held by Yahoo for $7.6 billion. After the repurchase, Yahoo and SoftBank held 24% and 31.9% respectively, making Japan's SoftBank Alibaba's largest shareholder.

Alibaba's management currently holds about 10% of the company's shares, with Jack Ma alone accounting for 7.43%. If following Hong Kong's stock market principle of "one share, one vote," voting rights should be proportional to shareholding, meaning Alibaba's management would have only about 10% voting rights.

Originally, Alibaba's management had only two options: either adopt an "AB share" structure to list in the US, or follow Tencent's example and persuade the largest shareholder to give up voting rights. There were previous reports that SoftBank was willing to give up some voting rights to ensure Alibaba's smooth IPO in Hong Kong. However, Alibaba's management hoped to solve the problem once and for all through the "partnership system," leading to a stalemate with the Hong Kong Stock Exchange.

Renren: Japan's SoftBank holds 34.2%

In April 2008, Oak Pacific Interactive Group, the predecessor of Renren Company, announced that it had received an investment of 40 billion yen (approximately $384 million) from SoftBank. After the transaction, SoftBank held approximately 40% of Oak Pacific's shares, becoming its largest shareholder.

Renren Company was listed on the New York Stock Exchange in the United States in 2011, with Japan's SoftBank being the largest institutional investor, holding 34.2% of the shares. Masayoshi Son, CEO of Japan's SoftBank, once stated that he hopes to maintain Renren's shareholding ratio around 35%. Founder Charles Chen owns 22.8% of the company's shares but has 55.9% of the voting rights through the "AB share" structure.
