Where the Libyan war is going, the disaster for Libya's people

by ereia0354 on 2011-05-24 15:06:17

The battle for Misurata is in full swing. This city has been surrounded by Gaddafi's troops for seven weeks. The urban battle is becoming a symbol of the Libyan uprising. Should Misurata fall, Western nations will face the question of whether they should take other military actions beyond airstrikes. A swift end to Gaddafi's regime cannot be expected.

Western nations must prepare for a prolonged conflict. In Paris, there is growing concern that Libya could become a second Afghanistan. The most pressing question is: Is it still possible to reach a peace agreement with the dictator Gaddafi?

The Western nations intervening in Libya are aware that ultimately a political solution must be found, but how? There are five possible scenarios:

1. Gaddafi’s Exile

One scenario that could at least open the way for negotiations with representatives of the Gaddafi regime is his exile. The New York Times recently reported that the U.S. is considering this option. However, one issue remains: The International Criminal Court is investigating Gaddafi and his family. Therefore, only countries that have not ratified the statutes of the ICC would be able to host Gaddafi's exile. Many African and Arab nations might be willing to accept him.

2. Ceasefire and Dialogue

A ceasefire under international supervision could provide an exit strategy, which might also mean initiating dialogue between rebels and Gaddafi supporters. However, peace plans such as the one proposed by Turkey have not been adopted. The rebels' condition for peace talks is that Gaddafi must step down first.

An article co-written by U.S. President Barack Obama, French President Nicolas Sarkozy, and British Prime Minister David Cameron recently escalated the situation. They stated that if Gaddafi remains in power, the West would bear the shame of betrayal. By making this statement, they further limited the room for dialogue with the Gaddafi regime.

Libya’s leadership still hopes Gaddafi can remain in power. Libyan Foreign Minister Abdelati Obeidi told the BBC that if airstrikes stop, elections could be held within six months under UN supervision, addressing all issues important to Libyans, including Gaddafi's future as a leader. However, so far, there are no signs that the West or the Libyan opposition would accept such a ceasefire proposal.

3. National Division

Another possible scenario: a ceasefire leading to a division of the country into two parts—west under Gaddafi’s control and east controlled by the National Transitional Council. However, French Foreign Minister Alain Juppé recently opposed this idea at a NATO meeting. The problem is clear: the West would then have to ensure the security of the east, possibly through NATO forces, for a long period. Alternatively, the east could temporarily be placed under UN trusteeship.

Is it just about oil? No, there's also natural gas. Perhaps, but consider the following:

1) Libya has the ninth-largest proven oil reserves in the world and the largest in Africa, with high-quality oil, low production costs, and proximity to Europe.

2) In 2009, the U.S. Energy Information Administration predicted that global oil reserves had already peaked and that supply would decrease while prices rise over the next few decades. Experts debate this report, but it is generally agreed that easily accessible oil sources are becoming harder to find.

3) About 65% of the world's remaining oil reserves are located in the Middle East, along with significant natural gas deposits. Iran holds the second-largest natural gas reserves outside of Russia.

4) The world's largest economy, the United States, consumes approximately 21 million barrels of oil per day, of which only 7.5 million barrels are produced domestically. The rest, about two-thirds, depends on imported oil. The main sources (in descending order) are Canada, Mexico, Saudi Arabia, Nigeria, Venezuela, and Iraq.

5) The world's second-largest economy, China, consumes about 8 million barrels of oil per day, with demand expected to rise to 11.3 million barrels by 2015. Since domestic production is only around 3.7 million barrels, China heavily relies on imported oil. The main suppliers (in descending order) are Saudi Arabia, Iran, Angola, Russia, Oman, and Sudan.

It is estimated that between 2030 and 2050, China will surpass the U.S. to become the world's largest economy, provided it secures enough energy to meet its growing industrial needs. Ensuring the supply of oil and natural gas is therefore a key focus of Chinese foreign policy. Beijing is understandably concerned about its current supply routes, as approximately 80% depend on maritime transport, and all shipping points are currently controlled by the U.S.: The U.S. Fifth Fleet, based in Bahrain, controls the Strait of Hormuz, known as the lifeline of world oil, which handles the sea routes for Saudi Arabian, Iranian, and Omani oil. The Fifth Fleet also controls the Bab-el-Mandeb Strait, the Red Sea passage through which Sudanese oil is shipped to the Indian Ocean. Additionally, the Strait of Malacca between Sumatra and Malaysia, a major transit point for oil destined for China, is a chokepoint controlled by the U.S. Seventh Fleet.

From this, it is clear that China's sea routes are entirely controlled by the U.S., which would deal a fatal blow to China's economic development and political stability in the event of a conflict. It is understandable why China is extremely anxious about the security of its maritime oil supply. This is one of the main reasons why China is vigorously building a powerful navy, constructing ports in South Asia and Southeast Asia, and striving to create land pipelines through Russia, Central Asia, and Pakistan.

Recent developments in Iran are also unfavorable for China. As the second-largest supplier of oil and natural gas, Iran is currently reducing production due to international sanctions, directly affecting supplies to China. Beijing has invested over $120 billion in Iran's energy sector but has recently had to cut back because its banks risk being sanctioned for aiding the Tehran regime.

China has no doubt wondered why the U.S. bombed Libya but did not intervene in Bahrain or Yemen. In fact, Bahrain hosts the U.S. Fifth Fleet, and Yemen's Aden Port is used to control the Red Sea.

After the Cold War, the U.S. largely ignored Africa. So why has the U.S. suddenly taken such a strong interest in Africa? Three years ago, the U.S. temporarily established the U.S. Africa Command (Africom) in Germany, a joint combatant command. Unfortunately, so far, no African nation has been willing to host Africom, indicating that they see clearly that this new command is not for Africa's benefit but for the U.S.'s own interests. F. William Engdahl believes that the stated purpose of counter-terrorism is merely a cover, while the actual goal is to directly and indirectly control Africa's energy, resources, and routes and prevent closer ties between China and Africa. According to Engdahl's analysis, to achieve this goal, the U.S. takes major actions in regions that possess at least one of the following three characteristics: strategic energy transit points, abundant energy resources, or areas where Russian and Chinese defense space can be compressed.
