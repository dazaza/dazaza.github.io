In 2011, the global cement prices will generally maintain an upward trend.

by sffgskkgfel9 on 2012-02-14 09:32:33

nfl-blog.us In 2011, global cement prices are expected to generally maintain an upward trend – Domestic News - Industry News - Building Materials Network. As the post-crisis era of 2010 came to a close, many people wondered whether the global cement industry had truly recovered. This question was deeply pondered at the 13th Asian Cement Trade Forum, where Tim Cahill, senior equity analyst at Ireland's Davy Research, delivered a compelling speech via Skype despite not being physically present at the meeting.

Tim Cahill, based on statistical data from 2010, believed that although it was a challenging year, it also marked a turning point for the cement industry. He opined that the worst times were now behind us, and the cement industry would enter a recovery phase. While some regions might still face difficulties, this would not alter the overall global recovery trend. Over the next few years, global cement demand is expected to enter a peak season, inevitably leading to issues of overcapacity. Consequently, cement trade in certain regions will be very active.

Looking back at 2010, global cement demand was unstable, with significant differences between developed countries and emerging markets. Global cement demand is closely linked to GDP growth. The cement market began to show signs of weakness in 2007, hitting its lowest point in 2009. However, in recent months, clear signs of recovery have emerged. Developed countries were most severely impacted by the economic crisis, with demand facing setbacks as early as 2007, making 2009 the worst year. Emerging markets, on the other hand, became pillars during the crisis, offsetting the sluggishness in developed countries. From a pricing perspective, cement prices in 2010 experienced significant fluctuations, only stabilizing in recent months.

Future Market Predictions

In 2011, there are many uncertainties regarding the growth of developed countries, but various government economic stimulus plans are beneficial for growth in 2011. By 2012, the situation will undoubtedly improve, ultimately shaking off the shadow of the crisis. Demand in emerging markets will continue to grow, with Tim Cahill believing that growth in Asia and Latin America will outpace Africa and the Middle East. Future cement prices are a key factor, though price fluctuations will be significant. Prices may soften in some regions but will generally trend upwards.

For the period from the end of 2010 to the end of 2012, Tim Cahill divided the world’s main markets into three zones: hotspots, improving areas, and areas of concern. Hotspot regions are expected to see demand growth rates ranging from 5.5% to 10.0%, including India (9.3%), Indonesia (7.6%), Russia (8.0%), Poland (7.0%), Brazil (10.0%), and Argentina (5.5%). Improving areas are expected to see demand growth rates ranging from 2.0% to 3.6%, including Germany (3.0%), France (2.0%), and Canada (3.6%). Areas of concern are expected to see demand growth rates ranging from -14.5% to 5.5%, including China (5.5%), Spain (-1.6%), Mexico (2.2%), Southeast Europe (-14.5%), Egypt (5.0%), and the Middle East (3.8%).

Comparing global cement demand changes in 2010, Tim Cahill predicted the status of global cement demand by the end of 2011 as follows: Western European demand decreased by 5.6% in 2010 and is expected to increase by 1-2% in 2011. Eastern European demand decreased by 32% in 2010 and is expected to increase by 2% in 2011. North American demand increased by 1.3% in 2010 and is expected to increase by 2.1% in 2011. South American demand increased by 3.8% in 2010 and is expected to increase by 5% in 2011. African demand increased by 4.5% in 2010 and is expected to increase by 4-5% in 2011. In Asia excluding China, demand increased by 2.8% in 2010 and is expected to increase by 3-4% in 2011. In China, demand increased by 16% in 2010 and is expected to increase by 6% in 2011.

With increasing demand, many regions will face severe overcapacity issues, while in some areas, capacity expansion will far outpace demand growth. Therefore, many countries will encounter overcapacity and low utilization problems, such as the United States and Eastern Europe.

Global Hotspots

In hotspot regions globally, like India, demand will continue to grow. The primary drivers of India's cement demand are rural housing construction and government infrastructure projects. In 2010, cement demand grew by 8-9%, with capacity expanding by 10%. With the addition of new capacity, concerns about cement prices in India have arisen, especially in southern India, where future price fluctuations are expected to be significant. Overall, India's cement industry has high profit margins, and the market outlook is optimistic.

Brazil's demand remains strong and is expected to continue or even improve further. Brazil's cement demand increased by 15% in 2010 compared to the previous year. Driven by the upcoming Olympics and World Cup, demand is expected to continue growing in 2011-2012, with prices improving along with increasing demand. Egypt, however, poses challenges in the global cement market. Although Egypt's cement market performed well in 2009, its growth was halted by political factors. Demand growth in 2009 was 18-20%, but it returned to normal levels of 3.4% in 2010. Production is expected to grow by 17% in 2011, reaching an 85% utilization rate. However, prices have dropped to 500 Egyptian pounds per ton (equivalent to RMB 650 per ton) and are expected to fall further, making the outlook unclear.

The U.S. government is expected to spend $80 billion annually on highway construction, which will significantly boost cement demand in the U.S. market. Despite a 40% decline in cement demand, U.S. cement prices fell only 10% from their peak in 2004-2006 to their low in 2009. In contrast, steel prices in the U.S. fell by 50%, and paper prices fell by 40%. The real estate market in Europe continues to improve, with German construction orders rising by 4% in the past few months. The long-sluggish real estate industries in the UK and France have finally shown signs of rebounding.

Regarding global cement trade, Tim Cahill believes that regional overcapacity in cement production will lead some countries to increase exports to alleviate overcapacity, while Africa will become a major importing region. Global cement trade volumes will increase, with the top three exporting countries being China, Turkey, and Pakistan.
