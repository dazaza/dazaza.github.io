The application of computer technology in optical fiber splicing is based on the academic research of optical fiber communication and the transmission medium carrier light wave of optical fiber communication. Due to its long transmission distance, content of information, and high quality of communication, it has become a major means of information transmission. A practical optical communication system can cover hundreds of kilometers, while the standard produced cable length is only 2 kilometers, requiring multiple disk serial fiber optic cables. Each cable contains dozens of core fibers, so the workload of constructing fiber optic connections is very large. In order to speed up the rate of fiber optic splicing and reduce losses, computer technology is applied.

The application of computer technology in fiber optic splicing is divided into two major categories: non-welding methods and welding methods, with welding methods being more commonly used currently. The optical fiber splicer is an important tool, divided into five generations according to the level of technological development. The first generation manual optical fiber splicer did not introduce computer technology and had a large optical fiber splicing loss (around 0.2 decibels), now eliminated. The second generation uses a computer-controlled fully automatic optical fiber splicer, significantly reducing the optical fiber connection loss (0.05 decibels - 0.1 decibels), which is currently technologically outdated. The third generation optical fiber splicer features automatic calibration, automatic welding, screen display, and is also known as the core rod viewing optical fiber splicer. The screen display of the third generation optical fiber splicer uses a microprocessor, built-in miniature video camera for optical and electronic display and automatic welding and connection loss estimation, further reducing the machine's optical fiber connection loss.

The fourth generation optical fiber splicer functions not only include automatic alignment of optical fibers, joint detection, and connection loss detection, but also includes a thermal joint image processing system that automatically monitors and captures thermal images during the entire welding process, analyzing core deformation, displacement, bubbles, and other impurities related to connection loss information, allowing for more comprehensive and accurate estimation of splicing loss, resulting in very small optical fiber connection loss (approximately 0.02 decibels). The fifth generation optical fiber splicer, also known as the automatic welder, operates under computer control to reduce — remove the first layer coating — align the fiber structure, with a diameter size of 8-10 micrometers for the core and 100-150 micrometers for the cladding. Manual cutting is generally difficult to achieve, after which they will automatically melt the fiber size determination test procedure reduction.

As a fourth-generation automatic optical fiber splicer S176, a microcomputer stores the standard measured fiber size in the database, using fiber dimension measurement to quickly determine the fiber test results during the melting process.
