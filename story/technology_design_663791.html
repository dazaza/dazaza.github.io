Russian Air Force fleet approaches Japanese airspace, monitored by 13 Japanese and South Korean fighter jets

[Reported by the Independent Newspaper of Russia on February 10] Title: Russian military aircraft "pay a surprise visit" to Japan. On February 8, a formation of 5 Russian Air Force fighters flew near the airspace of Japan over the Pacific Ocean waters. The Japanese Ministry of Defense stated that it was unprecedented for 5 Russian aircraft to simultaneously appear near Japan's borders, and the Japanese government officially requested Moscow to avoid similar actions in the future. Just the day before, the Russian Foreign Ministry had protested against Japan's celebration of "Northern Territories Day."

On the evening of February 7, when Tokyo's nationalists gathered in front of the Russian Embassy in Japan to celebrate "Northern Territories Day" and demanded the return of the Southern Kuril Islands (Etorofu, Kunashiri, Shikotan, and Habomai), two Russian strategic bombers took off from the Ussuriysk airbase in Amur Oblast. Subsequently, two Su-24MP reconnaissance aircraft and an A-50 long-range early warning aircraft joined them. According to Japanese media reports, the Russian Air Force formation approached Japanese airspace at 9:00 AM local time on February 8 and flew along the northern borders of Hokkaido and Honshu.

The Japanese Self-Defense Forces Air Corps scrambled emergency flights upon receiving the alert. A total of 13 F-15 and F-16 fighter jets from Japan and South Korea tracked the Russian aircraft along their route. The Japanese military pointed out that it was the first time five Russian aircraft had come close to Japan's borders, and it was also the first time the A-50 early warning aircraft participated. Simultaneously, the Japanese military noted that the Russian military aircraft did not violate Japan's airspace.

Colonel Vladimir Deryabin, spokesman for the Russian Air Force, confirmed that the Tu-95MC strategic bomber indeed conducted a flight around the international waters of the Pacific Ocean near Japan, practicing navigation skills in landmark-free areas and completing aerial refueling tasks. The air patrol lasted nearly 16 hours. Deryabin emphasized: "The flight mission strictly adhered to the rules for using international airspace over open seas and did not infringe on any other country's borders."

Deryabin stated that the Russian Air Force's air patrols were routine flight missions that have been regularly conducted since August 2007. However, the flight mission near Japan's coast gained widespread attention because it coincided with "Northern Territories Day" for the first time. In the past year and a half, tensions surrounding the issue of the Southern Kuril Islands' ownership have intensified.

[Reported by the Chosun Ilbo website on February 10] Recently, Russian long-range strategic bombers capable of carrying nuclear weapons flew near Japanese airspace, prompting Japan to urgently deploy fighter jets in response.

The Japanese Ministry of Defense announced on February 9 that two Tu-95MC strategic bombers, two reconnaissance aircraft, and one airborne early warning aircraft, totaling five Russian Air Force aircraft, flew around the Japanese archipelago on February 8. The Tu-95MC bomber is equipped with cruise missiles capable of carrying nuclear warheads with a range exceeding 3,000 kilometers and has a flying distance of over 10,000 kilometers.

On February 9, the Russian Air Force stated through TASS: "This was a normal regular air patrol flight, and it does not violate relevant international laws. The Tu-95MC bomber flew for 16 hours and conducted aerial refueling exercises during the flight." The Russian side also disclosed that Japan and South Korea deployed a total of 13 fighter jets, including F-15s and F-16s, during the Russian Air Force's patrol flight. It is reported that South Korean fighter jets were scrambled when the Russian aircraft headed towards the East China Sea direction.

Japanese media analysis points out that Russia's large-scale flight patrol exercise is related to the Northern Four Islands (referred to as the Southern Kuril Islands by Russia) where territorial disputes exist between Russia and Japan. Japanese Prime Minister Yoshihiko Noda stated on February 7 during the "Northern Territories Day" event: "Regarding the issue of returning the Northern Four Islands territory, we will negotiate with Russia with a strong will." The Japanese government designated February 7 every year as "Northern Territories Day" and holds relevant events annually, demanding that Russia return the Northern Territories.

February 9 is also the anniversary of the self-sinking of the Russian cruiser "Varyag" after being damaged by Japanese ships during the Russo-Japanese War.

In September last year, two Russian Tu-95 bombers also departed from the eastern part of the Korean Peninsula, flew southward, passed Okinawa, and then flew northward over the Pacific Ocean, approaching Japan's surrounding airspace. Both South Korea and Japan had urgently dispatched fighter jets to monitor the situation. Five Russian military aircraft made a "surprise visit" to Japan, leading to the emergency deployment of 13 Japanese and South Korean fighter jets for surveillance.
