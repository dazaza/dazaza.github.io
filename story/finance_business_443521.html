Family businesses can be described as an ancient yet "fleeting" form of enterprise organization. It is called ancient because it is historically the longest-lasting form of business. Under private ownership conditions, the earliest enterprises in history were all family businesses. It is referred to as "fleeting" because up to now, family businesses have a lifecycle continuation rule of "wealth not lasting beyond three generations." Statistics show that the average lifespan of a family business is 24 years, which coincides with the average working life span of the business founder; 30% of family businesses can be passed on to the second generation, and less than two-thirds of these may reach the third generation, with only about 13% of the latter being able to continue beyond the third generation. The lifespan of Chinese family-owned private enterprises is even shorter. From these figures, we can see that there are not many family businesses that can sustain continuous growth.

Advantages of Family Businesses:

1. In the entrepreneurial stage, leveraging the unique blood ties, quasi-blood ties, kinship relations, and associated social network resources among family members, family businesses can quickly gather talent at a lower cost, fully commit themselves, unite in struggle, and even work without regard for compensation, gaining competitive advantages in a short period and accumulating primitive capital more rapidly.

2. Quick Response. From the perspective of the overall family interest, under normal circumstances, the consistency of interests naturally makes each member sensitive to changes in the external environment, especially market changes. Information about external changes can be quickly communicated to every member of the enterprise. At the same time, the authoritative leadership of the patriarchal system can make decision-making within the company faster. In execution, due to smooth internal information communication, members can easily reach consensus, leading to effective policy implementation and decision enforcement. The overall family interest inherently motivates family members to work harder, naturally helping the company achieve maximized value.

3. Low Psychological Contract Cost. This helps companies reduce monitoring costs, so the total agency costs of family businesses are relatively lower than those of other types of enterprises. The level of trust and understanding among family members far exceeds that of non-family enterprise members, allowing family business members to bear lower psychological contract costs. The special blood and kinship ties among members give family businesses strong cohesion. With lower psychological contract costs and the unity of operating rights and ownership, the total agency costs of family businesses can be lower than those of non-family businesses. (Junyuan Consulting)

Disadvantages of Family Businesses:

The particularities of family businesses lead to numerous inherent disadvantages that accompany their natural advantages. These inherent disadvantages are determined by the uniqueness of family businesses and depend on this uniqueness. When family businesses need to further grow and strengthen, these disadvantages become bottlenecks to corporate growth, hindering sustainable development. Below, we analyze the inherent disadvantages of family businesses.

1. Talent Bottleneck

Every business grows from small to large. In the early stages of a family business, the scale is small, and its core members are primarily family members connected by blood and kinship ties. The entrepreneur has natural patriarchal authority, and family-style management based on patriarchal authority ensures the smooth operation of the family business, even allowing for "board meetings during dinner." Simultaneously, in the early stages of growth, the total residual claim rights and residual control rights available in the family business are relatively small, meaning fewer entitlements for the enterprise. In such a state, conflicts over sharing benefits and claiming rights within the family business will not be too sharp, and the emphasis on patriarchal authority and familial ethics can effectively resolve conflicts among family members' interests. At this stage, the enterprise is relatively scarce in monetary capital rather than human capital, and the requirements for management are not high. As the business grows:

On one hand, the rapid expansion of the enterprise significantly increases the demand for human capital, while the supply speed of the family member population, influenced by the natural population growth rate, is generally much lower than the enterprise's need for human capital;

On the other hand, due to the expansion of the enterprise and the complexity of management, the demand for high-level human capital increases. For the family member group, the possession of high-level human capital factors is influenced by factors such as the probability of talent development. Within this small-scale group of family members, the probability of producing such talent is extremely low. Therefore, qualitatively speaking, the family member group also struggles to ensure the supply of human capital.

From the above analysis, it is clear that family businesses will inevitably encounter a talent bottleneck during their growth process.

2. Lack of Excellent Corporate Culture

Corporate culture represents the fundamental values and behavioral standards of a company. It is the value concept advocated, believed in, and must be practiced by the enterprise, as well as the intrinsic source of the enterprise's sustained operation and vitality. Its main content involves institutional arrangements and strategic choices of the enterprise. What kind of institutional arrangements and strategic choices a company has determines what kind of corporate culture it develops. In family businesses, power often concentrates in the hands of family members centered around the entrepreneur. This authoritarian decision-making system lacks effective supervision, feedback, and constraint mechanisms, making it unfavorable for scientific and democratic decision-making, thus prone to decision errors, which is a defect in institutional arrangements.

Simultaneously, in another crucial aspect—talent promotion—family businesses often adhere to particularism rather than universalism. Universalism refers to selecting talents mainly based on ability, with personnel appointments following institutionalized human capital management methods. However, family businesses often adopt a nepotism-based employment system, prioritizing blood relations first and considering ability secondarily. Applying particularism to family members and universalism to non-family members is a common ailment of family businesses. These actions deviate from basic fairness principles, severely discouraging the enthusiasm of non-family members and depriving family members of the motivation and pressure to improve their qualities. It is difficult to form an effective incentive and restraint mechanism. Under this particularism principle, human capital cannot be optimally allocated, and a reasonable talent structure becomes unattainable.
