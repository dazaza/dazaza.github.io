Replicant: A truly free version of Android

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 19:20:49

Since the birth of Android, there has been ongoing discussion about whether it is truly open and free. AOSP (Android Open Source Project) is indeed open source, but by itself, it can't do much. To make Android run on a phone, a large amount of closed-source software is still required, such as drivers for GPS chips, cameras, sensors, and other components. If we consider Google applications as well, the situation becomes even more complicated.

For users, Android does provide a choice, but this is quite far from being truly open and free. The Replicant team wants to change that by creating a genuinely free version of Android.

The team adheres to the spirit of the Free Software Movement. "It's about rejecting unjust powers imposed on your computer, or more broadly, imposed on your life," said Paul Kocialkowski, one of Replicant's lead developers, in an interview with Wired. "Anyone who understands and respects this will understand why free software is important."

Replicant was founded in 2010, and its roots can be traced back to the release of the HTC G1 in 2008. The open-source software community saw that running Android required a great deal of closed-source software, so some people began to think about alternative solutions.

Currently, Replicant is based on CyanogenMod. CM’s approach is to back up closed-source software and then install an alternative system. This way, users can use a customized version of Android without having to abandon closed-source software. However, Replicant's future plan is to completely remove all closed-source software from devices, forming a fully free system. In Replicant, Google Play does not exist. It has a free and open-source app store called F-Droid, where all apps are also open-source software.

For the Replicant team, the biggest challenge is reverse-engineering closed-source drivers. "We keep trying to uncover the magic inside locked black boxes, but we don't always succeed," Paul said. "This is why Replicant still cannot support all hardware features on certain devices."

The lack of good graphics acceleration software is their biggest challenge. Without solving this problem, running Android 4.0 or later versions would still require reliance on closed-source software. The Replicant team is attempting to use Mesa, an open-source implementation of OpenGL.

Android is not the only open-source mobile operating system, but Paul believes it offers the best hope for achieving a free mobile system. In his view, systems like Maemo, Firefox OS, and Ubuntu Touch, like Android, also require closed-source software to run. Android, however, has more developers and more supported devices. "Rather than waste time moving to a system that has the same flaws as Android, we'd rather continue building Replicant on top of Android," he said.

Free software is more trustworthy than closed-source software, but security issues on phones are unique. For example, carriers can track users' locations, monitor calls, messages, and data transmissions. Regarding this, Paul said, "Clearly, running Replicant offers security advantages, but making a truly secure phone will take a long time. This is a step towards security. If you really care about security, you should avoid using a phone altogether."
