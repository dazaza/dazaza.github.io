Rural woman writes a 410,000-word web novel to search for missing son (picture)

by anonymous on 2012-02-25 09:30:01

This is the missing child announcement that Xu Dafang posted many years ago. At first glance, Xu Dafang appears no different from an ordinary rural grandmother. She is not much of a talker, short but sturdy, with calloused hands. Her hair is casually tied up with a rubber band, her face slightly sallow, and she wears an old-fashioned pair of sunglasses perched on her nose. However, this unassuming rural woman hides a tremendous passion and love behind her simplicity. In real life, Xu Dafang works as a warehouse manager at a machinery factory in Beibei, dealing with countless bearings and steel ingots every day, earning only 1400 yuan a month. In her virtual life, however, Xu Dafang is the author of a 410,000-word online martial arts novel. Over the course of more than ten years, she handwrote a full six pounds of manuscript, telling the story of the grudges and affection between long-lost brothers during the chaos of the Yuan Dynasty. She does all this for one purpose: to find her son Wan Yu, who has been missing for 18 years. Chongqing Evening News intern reporter Yi Jing and intern Fu Lingge provided photographic coverage. "Son" visits, but brings only disappointment. For 18 years, Xu Dafang has searched far and wide, wearing out more than a dozen pairs of shoes. Xu Dafang told the Chongqing Evening News that in the middle of last month, she received a call from a stranger who claimed he had been abducted as a child. His background was very similar to Wan Yu's, and like Xu Dafang, he too hoped to find his long-lost family. Two days later, a thin, somewhat shy young man appeared before Xu Dafang's family. "He is about the same age as Xiao Yu, with a similar build and also single-lidded eyes," Xu Dafang introduced. The young man's name was Xiao Hu, from Dianjiang, 22 years old, studying at Chongqing Electronic Engineering College. From Uncle Hu, they learned that Xiao Hu had been picked up by an old lady at the Beicheng Bus Station when he was very young and wasn't sent to the Hu family until he was five or six years old... But it turned out that Xiao Hu wasn't their lost child. Nevertheless, Xu Dafang still transferred Xiao Hu's photo to her QQ space and listed him under the QQ "Family" group. "I thought, if my child is still alive, maybe he looks just like him..." For 18 years, she has persisted in searching for her son. So how did Xu Dafang lose her child? This story goes back to the cold morning of December 26, 1993. That day, Xu Dafang, living in Xiehe Team, Tuanshanbao Village, Beibei District, got up early as usual to prepare breakfast for her family. Recalling that morning, Xu Dafang still remembers it vividly, "I called him to get up and dressed him myself because it was cold outside, and I reminded him not to run around." After finishing breakfast and farm work, feeding the pigs, Xu Dafang turned around to look for her child, only to find that he was gone. "Her whole family was anxiously calling out the child's name throughout the village. Every corner of the village was searched, and hundreds of people from all three teams in the village were helping them look for the child." Wu Xueyan, the then deputy secretary of the Tuanshanbao Village branch committee, recalled that Wan Yu was only three years old when he went missing, with a handsome and adorable appearance. Later, Xu Dafang learned from the villagers that the child had left with someone named Cai from Chengjiang. They found Cai overnight, but he told them that while he originally intended to take little Wan Yu to the city to play, he accidentally lost the child at the bus station. Xu Dafang said, after that day, she spent her days wandering between the bus station and places where the child might appear, posting missing person notices, and looking everywhere for her child. In 2000, the birth of her younger son Wan Suyu brought some changes to the family. The couple named their child Suyu, a homophone for "Si Yu", expressing their hope that one day the two brothers could reunite. Writing novels to express feelings, hoping the son will see. Wan Yu's disappearance caused immense sorrow in Xu Dafang's heart. To divert her attention, she, who has always loved literature, especially martial arts novels, decided to create her own martial world with pen in hand. "The child loved listening to my stories the most, so I wanted to write for him, so he could read it." Xu Dafang said, she also had a more utilitarian thought, "I want to become famous, if I become famous, the child will be able to see me and know that I am looking for him." Thus, she began writing under the pen name "Xu Mengfei". Xu Dafang said that this novel tells the story of a broken family during the turmoil of the Yuan Dynasty, two brothers separated since childhood, gathering and dispersing due to the Dragon and Phoenix Sword, eventually engaging in a grudge and struggle. Although Xu Dafang denies placing her real-life emotions into the martial arts fiction, the reporter found after reading the novel that she subconsciously integrates these emotions into the story - Chapter One: Maileydi lifts the curtain, and finds the carriage empty, with no sign of Lin'er. She immediately becomes anxious and shouts around, "Lin'er! Where are you?" A distant high place echoes with Lin'er's cries for help, "Father! Mother! Save me!" Chapter Two: "Mother, please don't play hide-and-seek with Lin'er anymore, let's go home, alright? Lin'er really misses you so much!" Chapter Four: "... Heaven blesses us, we finally know each other is still alive! I told him, I gave birth to Huan'er, you, who is now twenty years old. He was delighted and wanted to find you immediately." Chapter Twenty-One: The mother's face suddenly turns snow-white, a sword pierces her back, blood gushing out, yet she still smiles faintly, saying, "Lin'er... Mother sees you... so happy..." Maileydi faints. Instantly, Han Lin understands everything, it's his mother, his mother used her flesh and blood to shield him from this sword! ... As for the future path, Xu Dafang says she doesn't know herself. But she says the one thing she knows clearly is to do one thing for a lifetime, which is to find her son. "I'm 46 years old, I've been looking for him for 18 years. I will continue to search and write until my son reads my novel." Comments are welcome.
