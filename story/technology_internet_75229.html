Internet operators and internet commentators - SweetIT.Net

by xjyzhenai on 2008-06-23 01:30:26

A few years ago, the Internet was flourishing all over the country. I often saw interviews on TV and other media - "What do you usually do online?"

I remember the most common answer was: "Look up information"... I thought it was funny back then. This is probably the most basic analysis of local Internet applications. Gradually, people started answering things like "playing games, chatting". And so the media stopped paying attention to what people did online.

Today, with various new technologies being applied to the Internet, countless new projects keep emerging. There are more and more things people can do online. In the past, going online was something you had to specifically "go" and do, but now without the Internet, many things cannot be done and the efficiency of many jobs would greatly decrease. This is thanks to the application projects in the era of widespread network usage and the IT industry built upon these projects.

As these projects become increasingly integrated into people's lives, people start to pay more attention to this industry. Many people are eager to enter this field and become an Internet operator. But out of so many Internet projects, how many can survive? It's not that one should not enter this field, but the success rate of Internet projects is indeed very low. When you start working on a project, it's also the time when you see it gradually decline.

Every day we browse the web and see many creative projects. Indeed, these must be based on people's needs. For example, Fanfou for Chinese short blogs, Feedsky for FEED hosting services, and ZhuaXia for RSS subscription services. If people don't have this need yet, the project must create the demand. When we see these projects, we first tend to evaluate them subjectively. If you form this habit, you begin to become an Internet commentator. When your comments gain recognition on the Internet, it acts as a driving force, pushing you to comment on every emerging project. You might establish your own column on famous IT communities like DoNews, or start your own IT review blog, such as Sharing Network 2.0. Of course, Jarry's Xiantian IT is also moving towards this direction.

I believe that before becoming an Internet operator, you must first be an Internet commentator. Jarry hasn't given this special "profession" the title of "expert." In this industry, there won't likely be any Internet commentary experts. What you say might be right, but it could also be wrong. Regardless of how you comment on others' projects, at least it's fundamental to becoming an Internet operator.

Internet commentators grow through learning. When you see someone else's project go live, you need to develop the habit of commenting, which requires learning. Experience the project, use the project. Once you understand its essence, you qualify to comment. Thus, the habit of commenting is cultivated through continuous learning and enrichment.

When this habit accumulates to a certain extent, Internet commentators may conceive the idea of operating their own project. Spotting a project, they plan to operate it. This project might be one they came up with themselves, or one they copied from others, confident they can do better. Remember, in the Internet world, there is no word like "invention." Therefore, there is no project birth that can be called "invention." For the Internet, this word is always outdated.

You might think you've accumulated a lot of experience. But even after your project gets underway, its future remains uncertain. Jarry has a friend who wants to create an online trading platform for virtual items and has already started implementing it. He said he wants to learn from Taobao. Indeed, Taobao's business model and the value it generates are beyond question. Jarry's friend wants to learn (or copy) Taobao's business model but to save on initial costs, he only focuses on a professional trading platform for virtual items. For this project, it definitely has its own value. But if Jarry's friend wants to become the operator of this project, his responsibility is to maximize the benefits generated by this business model. To maintain the operation of the project, he must continuously generate revenue. Since he isn't the "inventor" of the project but merely an "imitator," he must make changes, create differences, and build characteristics, making his project the most professional and authoritative platform in the field of virtual item transactions. Otherwise, his project will only have one path - death.

Internet projects are this cruel. But they don't just bring benefits to operators. Fame is also an important aspect. With fame comes value. Look at the early days of China's Internet, how many MBAs from MIT in the US, how many top graduates from Oxford and Cambridge returned home to start businesses? Indeed, their arrival brought fresh blood to the nascent Internet industry, accelerating the rapid development of Internet projects. But it also brought constant imitation and fierce competition. Now you rarely hear about highly educated individuals taking on CEO positions. Look at today's Internet projects?

Either you need to be very rich. After throwing in large sums of money, you still need to be prepared for the possibility of only gaining popularity but not profits. At least invest consistently for three to five years, increasing year by year, and see if your project has a place to survive?

Or you gather a small group of three to five people, or even two or three, and operate a small project. Then wait for it to slowly develop.

Or, from the beginning of the project, set your sights on venture capital. Then start pitching, explaining how great your project is, with its powerful and sound business model, and secure a large amount of venture capital. Under daily financial report pressure, lead a life that's neither human nor dog-like, pretending to be cool.

Alright, anyone can become an Internet commentator. But if you start getting excited about becoming an Internet operator, you should consider whether it's worth investing a lot of time and energy into operating a project that may not even make money. Not making money is already a very good outcome; you might end up in debt, disappearing from sight forever.
