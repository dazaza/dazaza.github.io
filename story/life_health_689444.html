What are the symptoms of osteophyte formation?

by po13929357028 on 2012-03-04 08:40:53

Osteophyte formation is a chronic, progressive joint disease, mainly affecting the proximal and distal interphalangeal joints of the hand, spine, and hip, knee, and ankle joints, characterized by joint pain, degeneration, and limited mobility. Joint movement, especially when bearing weight, exacerbates the pain, which lessens or alleviates after rest. Sometimes, finger joint pain occurs when holding objects or performing actions like opening bottle caps, possibly due to osteoarthritis in the hand. Hip pain after walking that lessens after rest may be caused by osteoarthritis in the hip joint. The main symptom of osteoarthritis in the knee joint is knee pain when going up and down stairs. Walking a certain distance can cause pain leading to limping, possibly due to lumbar osteophyte formation causing spinal canal stenosis. So, what are the symptoms of osteophyte formation? Here's a detailed introduction below.

1. Shortened height, hunchback: Often appears after suffering. The anterior part of the vertebrae is almost entirely composed of spongy bone, and this area is the body's pillar, bearing a large load, easily compressing and deforming, causing the spine to tilt forward, increasing the curvature of the back, forming a hunchback. With age, osteoporosis worsens, increasing the degree of hunchback, leading to significant contraction of the knee joints.

2. Pain: The most common symptom of primary osteoporosis, often seen in lower back pain. Pain radiates along the spine to both sides, lessening when lying down or sitting, worsening when standing upright or extending backward, or after prolonged standing or sitting. Pain is lighter during the day and worsens at night or upon waking in the morning. Bending over, muscle movement, coughing, or straining during bowel movements exacerbates the pain.

3. Fracture: This is the most common and most severe complication of degenerative osteoporosis.
