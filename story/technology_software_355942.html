It is also understandable that others give certain rewards out of gratitude.

by h1045136 on 2010-05-26 14:04:29

Yesterday, a reporter interviewed lawyer Hu Baijin from Guangxi Jinzhan Law Firm regarding Wang Lao Han's decision to sell his kidney. He stated that the underground trade of organs is certainly prohibited by law. However, if it is done out of altruism to help others, donating an organ is permissible, and it can be understood if others express gratitude and offer compensation.

In the "confession letter," Wang Mao wrote: In 2007, his son Wang Chunlei, only 20 years old at the time, was diagnosed with leukemia. To this day, they have been left destitute due to medical expenses. Additionally, his 80-year-old father suffers from numerous ailments and has stopped taking medication. His younger daughter has also dropped out of school to work in agriculture to save her brother.

Lawyer: Organ trading is illegal.

Citizens show both sympathy and suspicion.

Yesterday afternoon, the reporter went to Wancheng Culture Square. Under the shade of the trees along the street, Wang Mao held his granddaughter near his son, fiddling with the "confession letter" in the air.

She also informed the reporter that after Wang Chunlei’s former girlfriend learned he had leukemia, she left him, and now the child can only live with her maternal grandparents. Wang Mao holds her granddaughter while standing next to her son, begging on the street to sell her kidney to save her son.

Ms. Xu, a friend of Wang Chunlei's hometown, said that Wang Chunlei used to work in her factory. After falling ill, he was unable to continue working. Wang Mao brought her granddaughter and constantly stayed by her son's side to care for him. "I've tried to seek assistance for them, but I haven't succeeded," Ms. Xu said. Now, Wang Lao Han takes care of his grandson every day, and she herself is already exhausted.

Wang Lao Han's actions drew the attention of many citizens. Many sympathized with his plight, but there were also those who were skeptical about his intentions, fearing deception.

The girlfriend of Wang's son left him.

By/Photos: Reporter Dai Xikui

Ms. Xu said that Wang Lao Han's son does indeed have leukemia. She even showed the medical records and test results, as well as proof of being a low-income household. "You see, all these are real," she said. As a fellow townsman, she could only try her best to help, but she couldn't resolve the issue. "I've sought help from many departments, but none of them provided clear answers."

Yesterday afternoon, Wang Mao, a 58-year-old man from Suiping County, Hebei Province, begged on Wancheng Culture Square for help in selling his kidney to save his son who suffers from leukemia. "He is only 23 years old and has a 3-year-old daughter. If he passes away at such a young age, the family will be destroyed," Wang Lao Han said helplessly when asked by kind-hearted people.

However, some expressed that there are too many similar cases nowadays, making it hard to distinguish or address each one individually. Everyone has their own difficulties. Miss Liu expressed doubts about this situation but admitted it was quite pitiful. Still, she wouldn't take the initiative to help. "I hope relevant departments can assist them in resolving their issues," she said.

Lawyer Hu Chengyuan from Guangdong Jinzhan Law Firm stated that the underground trade of organs is definitely prohibited by law.

Wang Lao Han's act of selling his kidney attracted many onlookers at the square. The reporter observed that occasionally, compassionate citizens would approach and give Wang Lao Han a few yuan or even ten yuan. Upon receiving the money, Wang Lao Han would kneel down and kowtow. Mr. Wu, a citizen, said, "It's quite pitiful; raising children while caring for elders is not easy." He added, "Even if it's true, I'll still give some money so they can afford food."

However, regardless of the means used, if someone's intention is solely to obtain money through donation, it essentially equates to buying and selling. "If the sole purpose is to gain wealth, the law forbids it," he said. Although it may be difficult, hoping to obtain money through kidney donation is neither advocated nor permitted.

"My son is the hope of the entire family. If someone can save my son, I am willing to sell my kidney or even other organs in my body," Wang Mao said. He mentioned that they have already spent over 200,000 yuan and are in debt by more than 80,000 yuan, yet they still haven't cured his son's illness. "The doctor says there is still hope for my son, but it will require another 100,000 yuan in costs."
