In September 2010, the suspect Liu Yejun was captured by the police, and a large number of remittance transfer documents, forged maritime department seals, and agreements for the buying and selling of certificates with crew members were found in his home.

On August 1, 2010 at 10 a.m., the Wuhan Branch of the Yangtze River Shipping Public Security Bureau received a report from the Youjiang Maritime Bureau stating that since mid-July, someone had illegally intruded into the external information network of the Maritime Bureau of the Ministry of Transport through two servers of the bureau, breaking into the crew management database and generating 13 pieces of fake certificate information for crew members.

As early as May 2009, the Maritime Bureau of the Ministry of Transport had discovered during the investigation of crew certificates that some crew certificates in the crew management system had abnormal conditions. After internal verification, it was determined that the crew management system had been illegally intruded upon, and crew data had been illegally altered.

University student: While training crew members, he discovered a lucrative path to forging certificates.

A month earlier, another suspect, Guo Fei, was arrested at his residence in Shanghai, China, and the police found two home computers used to intrude into the website of the Maritime Bureau at his residence.

It is introduced that crew certificates are divided into four grades: A, B, C, and D, and there are strict regulations for issuance. Certificate holders must have professional academic qualifications, undergo specialized training, and have corresponding navigation qualifications and safety records. Crew members with Class C crew competency certificates can navigate in inland water areas.

According to the victim Lin, in 2006, he met Liu Yejun and asked him to help process a crew certificate. Liu Yejun asked him to pay an 80,000 yuan handling fee and a 5,000 yuan registration fee. Afterwards, Lin sent his information and 5,000 yuan to Liu Yejun. About a year later, Liu Yejun called Lin and said that the certificate was ready and could be checked online, and asked him to transfer the money. Lin went online and found that his crew upgrade information was indeed there, so he paid Liu Yejun another 45,000 yuan. In July 2010, after transferring another 20,000 yuan to Liu Yejun, Lin finally received the certificate.

On January 19, 2011, the Wuhan Branch of the Yangtze River Shipping Public Security Bureau transferred Liu Yejun on suspicion of forging and purchasing state organ documents and destroying computer information systems, and Guo Fei on suspicion of destroying computer information systems, to the Jianghan District Procuratorate of Wuhan City for review and prosecution.

Hackers: Unable to resist the temptation of benefits, they "professionally" modified the database.

The prosecutor pointed out that this case reflected inadequate security precautions on some government websites. Therefore, it was suggested that their internal and external networks should be physically isolated, and cybersecurity defenses should be strengthened. The prosecutor also reminded those who buy certificates and hope to take shortcuts not to have侥幸psychology; otherwise, the losses will outweigh the gains. Not only will the certificates be confiscated, but they will also bear legal consequences. To obtain professional qualifications, one must follow the relevant national department regulations, go through proper procedures, and rely on one's own abilities.

Liu Yejun claimed that his actions directing Guo Fei to intrude into the Maritime Bureau website only constituted the crime of illegal intrusion into a computer information system, not the crime of destroying a computer information system. In response, the prosecutors stated that the two not only intruded into the computer system but also modified and added data within it, causing particularly serious consequences. Therefore, they should be held criminally responsible for destroying the computer information system. To further solidify evidence for this crime, the prosecutor requested the technical department to collect electronic evidence regarding how the two intruded into the website and illegally added information, and to convert the electronic evidence into documentary evidence to prove the case.

Recently, after the Jianghan District Procuratorate of Wuhan City prosecuted, the court sentenced the defendant Liu Yejun to sixteen years in prison for fraud and destroying computer information systems, and co-conspirator hacker Guo Fei was sentenced to five years in prison for destroying computer information systems.

To test Ameng's ability, Liu Yejun uploaded two fabricated crew member profiles to the internet for Ameng (under arrest). Two or three months later, Ameng returned and said it was done, asking Liu Yejun to check on the internal network website of the Shanghai Maritime Bureau. After opening the website, Liu Yejun indeed found the information, so he transferred 20,000 yuan to Ameng's bank account.

Previously, Guo Fei repeatedly entered the database, and by the time of the incident, he had modified and added information for 38 fake crew certificates in the database, receiving over 410,000 yuan as payment from Liu Yejun.

At first, Liu Yejun only wanted Guo Fei to intrude into the external network of the Maritime Bureau, but to make things more seamless, in late 2008, he further instructed Guo Fei to intrude into the internal network ship management information system of the Maritime Bureau of the Ministry of Transport to add data. Meanwhile, Liu Yejun gathered information about various maritime network technologies from multiple sources to help Guo Fei intrude into the internal network of the Maritime Bureau. During this period, due to some technical reasons, Guo Fei paused for a while, but under Liu Yejun's continuous persuasion, he decided to continue trying.

The viewpoint of the procuratorial organs was later fully adopted by the court. Subsequently, the Jianghan District Court of Wuhan City sentenced Liu Yejun to sixteen years in prison for fraud and destroying computer information systems, and sentenced Guo Fei to five years in prison for destroying computer information systems.

32-year-old Liu Yejun is a teacher at a university in Guangdong Province, with a graduate degree. Since 2006, he has been responsible for crew training at the Zhanjiang Maritime Bureau, where he came into contact with many Fujian and Zhejiang crew members. Due to the severe shortage of crew members with Class C competency certificates in the crew market at the time, Liu Yejun began considering this as a way to make money.

Hearing about a shortcut, many crew members approached Liu Yejun for certification. After receiving money, Liu Yejun gave part of it to Ameng, keeping the rest for himself.

With the "business" growing larger, Liu Yejun's profits increased, and so did his audacity. By the end of 2008, Ameng revealed to Liu Yejun that he was just an "intermediary," and the person who could modify information online was Guo Fei. To reduce intermediate costs, Liu Yejun decided to cut out Ameng and directly cooperate with Guo Fei.

Using hacking software, Guo Fei investigated many maritime crew electronic declaration websites and eventually found vulnerabilities in the website of the Yangtze River Maritime Bureau. Through two servers of the Yangtze River Maritime Bureau, he logged into an internal server connected to the Maritime Bureau of the Ministry of Transport. In March 2010, after cracking the username and password, Guo Fei successfully hacked into the crew management database. He then tried adding a few crew member profiles, succeeded, and proceeded to add the first batch of crew member profiles according to Liu Yejun's requests.

After establishing contact with Guo Fei, Liu Yejun directed him to use hacking techniques to intrude into the external websites of the maritime bureaus of Shanghai, Guangxi, and other places, adding and modifying the fake crew certificate information provided by him.

After reviewing, the prosecutor believed that besides jointly committing the crime of destroying the computer information system with Guo Fei, Liu Yejun, with the intention of illegal possession, forged crew certificates to defraud others' money, which amounted to a particularly large sum, simultaneously violating the crimes of fraud and forgery and trading of state organ documents, constituting overlapping offenses. Therefore, he should be held criminally responsible for the more severe crime, i.e., fraud.

In July 2009, staff members of the Zhanjiang Maritime Bureau in Guangdong Province discovered inconsistencies between internal and external network information while conducting crew clearance reviews. The bureau subsequently sent assistance letters to various maritime bureaus in Guangdong Province, ultimately discovering six crew certificates not issued by the government. At the same time, the Shenzhen Maritime Bureau also found that 11 crew certificates in the crew information system were not self-issued.

Not only that, Liu Yejun also signed certificate processing agreements with certain crew members, encouraging them to introduce other crew members for certification. A crew member named Ye from Zhoushan, Zhejiang, claimed that he had introduced 20 crew members to Liu Yejun and paid 780,000 yuan for certificate processing fees. However, later, when these crew members attempted to board ships with the certificates, they were caught by the maritime department for using fake certificates and received administrative penalties.
