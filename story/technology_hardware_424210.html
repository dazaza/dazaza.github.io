The night view of Lulin Lake is so enchanting, and I took another photo at the same spot where I took pictures in the morning, recording this fairyland on earth.

The San Die Quan has a height difference of 155 meters, equivalent to the height of a fifty-story building calculated by three meters per floor. From the top of the mountain opposite it to the lower viewing platform of the San Die Quan, there are more than a thousand neatly paved steps. Two bamboo poles supporting a rattan chair become a simple means of transportation, and if one lets people carry them up and down, it would be quite expensive.

The front part of the viewing platform and the waterfall pavilion are all enveloped in water mist. The camera lens basically needs to be wiped after taking two photos to remove the water droplets! Taking refuge in the only shop on the viewing platform, the boss said that such a large volume of water is indeed rare. It can be seen that our trip was lucky, as we encountered the best time to see the San Die Quan.

Coming down is easy but going up is hard! Leaving the San Die Quan and heading back, we began a tough journey. We just climbed down as many steps as we now have to climb up. More than a thousand steps require a great deal of energy to overcome gravity and move upwards.

Due to the exhaustion from visiting the San Die Quan in the afternoon, we were particularly looking forward to dinner.

Mount Lu has numerous waterfalls; which one did the Poet Immortal Li Bai describe with "A waterfall plunges straight down for three thousand feet, making one suspect that the Milky Way has fallen from the ninth heaven"? There are various opinions: some say it's the Xiufeng Waterfall in the south of the mountain, others say it's the Shimenjian Waterfall, and still others say it's the Daguchi Waterfall in Tianhe Valley... But from the currently recognized perspective, the most scenic waterfall in Mount Lu is the San Die Quan. Since ancient times, there has been the saying, "Among the waterfalls of Mount Lu, the San Die Quan is the best." Without seeing the San Die Quan, how can one be considered a visitor to Mount Lu?

One can always marvel at the wonders of nature's creations everywhere in the natural world; on December 21, 2012, at 3:14:35 PM! In contrast to nature, humans always seem so insignificant.

Later, I heard that in another direction from the San Die Quan viewing platform, there is a mountain road that allows cars to drive directly there. However, the road is very narrow and steep, so safety must be especially noted…

Driving back to Taiji Hotel from the San Die Quan for rest, when we arrived at the edge of Lulin Lake, the night had already completely descended. Under the glow of colorful lights, Lulin Lake Bridge was like a splendid ribbon; the ripples on the lake surface divided the reflection of the bridge into countless small pieces, gently swaying with an indescribably wonderful ambiance.

On the way back, we passed by Tieshan Peak, which we didn't notice before. The unique geological structure on the cliff here is called a shear zone. Due to changes in the Earth's crust, the rock layers moved and were subjected to shearing and rotational external forces, forming the rod-like structures seen today.

15:16 San Die Quan Cable Car Station

After walking down for about ten minutes, the winding stone steps brought us close to the second cascade of the San Die Quan waterfall. Here, the swift flow of water was divided into two equally powerful streams, chasing each other as they flowed downward. The sound of water also changed from a solo to a duet, becoming even more majestic.

Stepping onto the viewing platform, a thousand-foot sheet of ice seemed to fall from the sky, fully deserving the description of "A waterfall plunges straight down for three thousand feet, making one suspect that the Milky Way has fallen from the ninth heaven." At the top of the waterfall, the spring water and rainwater from two mountains began to continuously surge downwards. Following the step-like terrain formed during the Quaternary glaciation period, it cascaded down in three sections: the first section resembling floating clouds dragging silk ribbons, the second section like shattered jade and crushed ice, and the third section like a jade dragon entering the deep pool. An ancient poem reads: Initially suspected to be frost rushing through the heavenly valley, then flipping over like clouds descending from the cliffs. Scattered like flying winds and light smoke, hanging like silver threads threading through jade pearls.

A couple on the viewing platform posed for a photograph against the backdrop of the waterfall, forming a "heart" shape with their hands. I thought that the water force of this waterfall varies between fast and slow, and the water volume fluctuates between abundant and scarce. Using this to witness love might not be appropriate. Regardless, cherishing the person in front of you and preserving this moment is enough!

Walking down a bit further, we suddenly saw a white streak appear on the distant mountain wall, which was the first cascade of the San Die Quan waterfall. "Looking afar at the waterfall hanging before the river" made us feel invigorated, involuntarily quickening our steps.

Not even one-third of the way down, my legs felt as if they were filled with lead; my head felt tight, and my chest slightly stuffy. As it grew darker, the path was filled with tourists returning, and everyone's state and expressions were similar: walking a bit, resting a while… The thick sound of the waterfall gradually faded away, replaced by the crisp songs of the streams in the nearby mountain gorges.

Continuing down into the height of the San Die Quan waterfall, a toll booth appeared! Surprisingly, each person was required to purchase a one-yuan badge, claiming it was for counting the number of visitors and could also serve as a collectible. This was clearly arbitrary charging! A sign of bad practices! It reflects the institutional defect of multiple management systems on Mount Lu!

Originally, the area above the second cascade of the San Die Quan waterfall was under the jurisdiction of the Mount Lu Scenic Area Administration Bureau, while the area below belonged to the Haihui Town of Lushan District, Jiujiang City. Hence, the ridiculous situation arose where tickets and cable car fees were charged again upon reaching here. Imagine who would come here and turn back without spending money?

The entrance fee for the San Die Quan does not include the cable car fare. Seeing the good weather, we decided to hike. The San Die Quan cable car is actually a horizontally moving monorail cable car, and its endpoint is only the mountaintop above the San Die Quan. The legendary thousand-step staircase still requires one's own two feet to conquer!

Advancing along the valley plank road below the track, this is the valley between Dayueshan Mountain and Wulaofeng Peak, where jagged rocks rise everywhere. The plank road goes up and down, twisting and turning, but fortunately, it is always accompanied by mountain springs, preventing boredom. While fully enjoying the pure air of the forested valleys, one can constantly get close to the spring water and feel its coolness.

The water flow on the surface of the waterfall is layered, splashing up sheets of water flowers upon colliding with the rocks. After the waterfall plunges into the bottom deep pool, steam rises, swirling and sprinkling with the wind, resembling a mix of rain and fog. The misty water vapor fills the eyes, as if this roaring spring water wants to sweep everything in the world away…

Many people say that the snails in Mount Lu taste great, and indeed they do! Each snail is fresh and full, stir-fried with scallions and chili peppers to bring out the freshness! Before cooking, the protruding parts of the snail shells are clipped off with pliers to make sucking out the snail meat easier. If it doesn't work, try sucking from the other side, and then from the front—it usually works. Of course, there are also refined people who don't want to suck with their mouths and use a bamboo skewer to pick out the meat slowly…

This dish of stir-fried bamboo shoots is a feast for the eyes, nose, and palate. The red, yellow, and green colors shine together, very appealing, and visually enhancing the appetite. The spring in Mount Lu comes about half a month later than at the foot of the mountain, so the current bamboo shoots must have just sprouted, being both resilient and tender at the same time.
