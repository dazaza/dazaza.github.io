We only select "Yes..."

by fearless on 2009-04-07 01:39:55

Song Jin (I have another friend named Song Yin) asked in her status message: "Campus users generally like to recklessly follow posts without discerning right from wrong!" If we follow common sense, this "strange" phenomenon is actually quite good, at least it appears that there's a lot of popularity.

A friend said that campus users reply to posts not based on the content of the post but on who posted it. This interpretation has some merit, but it doesn't seem entirely accurate.

Indiscriminately following and crazily liking posts is a single phenomenon that's hard to serve as an entry point for exploring user behavior. After all, the activity of "building floors" (adding comments in large numbers) is quite popular. Besides commenting, users also participate in activities without considering the details, they will definitely join if they receive an invitation, regardless of whether they actually attend or not. This is why many event-based sites have a system of deducting points for failing to show up.

We all know that behavior is outward activity governed by thought, and any kind of behavior is directed by thought. This makes the issue complex. To try to understand each case one-to-one is almost impossible. We can categorize the situation described by Song Jin and other similar cases into one category: affirmative response type. That is, when users encounter situations where they need to take action, they tend to give affirmative responses — replying to posts, participating in activities, accepting friend requests, agreeing to plugin invitations, choosing "yes" when faced with "yes/no" options...

Of course, "affirmative responses" are not absolute, just the majority. When encountering people who use reverse thinking or those with split personalities, it's hard to predict their behavior. Expecting to study user habits on them really requires extraordinary patience.

So why does this category of user habit called "affirmative response" exist? The reason is actually simple. It’s related to Windows systems. When the Windows system prompts users to make a binary choice, only clicking on buttons such as "Yes... Is... Confirm..." and other affirmative response buttons allows users to "normally" continue with the operation. On the contrary, there might be blank screens or error messages, even blue screens, black screens, automatic restarts or shutdowns. Users have been "scared" by Windows, so they won't easily choose negative buttons.
