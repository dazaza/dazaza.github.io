Weibo is trapped in the dilemma of e-commerce. The Chinese version of Facebook accelerates transformation

by anonymous on 2013-09-24 21:01:07

As the industry cheered for Facebook's stock reaching a new historical high, Twitter recently announced its intention to go public via its official website and has secretly submitted an S-1 document containing its IPO plans to the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC). However, it has not yet disclosed the timing of the listing or other details. This could be the largest IPO since Facebook's debut in May last year.

Abroad, social media is thriving, but how are China’s own versions faring? Known as "China's Twitter," Sina Weibo, and "China's Facebook," Renren Network, what challenges do they face? With profitability being a universal issue for these platforms and their transformation paths being controversial, where does the future lie for Chinese social media?

The困undation of Weibo's E-commerce

Stimulated by the positive news of Twitter's IPO, as of last Friday, Sina's stock closed at $83.21, up 1.64%.

In May this year, Twitter was valued close to $10 billion, with its current valuation estimated between $15 billion to $20 billion. Meanwhile, Sina Weibo, known as "China's Twitter," saw its valuation drop from a high of $8 billion to approximately $3.3 billion after Alibaba's investment.

According to data released by JiaThis on social media usage in July, Sina Weibo accounted for 14.94% of shares, surpassing QQ Space to take first place. Yet, this still doesn't mask the long-standing challenge of monetization that plagues Weibo.

In April last year, Weibo launched its first display ad system, seen as the official start of its commercialization efforts. From last year's financial report, the Weibo business contributed $66 million in revenue to Sina, but its costs exceeded $150 million, leaving Weibo far from profitability.

By April this year, Alibaba Group and Sina Weibo announced a strategic cooperation agreement where Alibaba invested $5.86 billion to acquire preferred and common shares issued by Weibo, representing about 18% of Weibo's fully diluted shares.

There are reports suggesting that this strategic cooperation is expected to bring Weibo approximately $380 million in marketing and social e-commerce revenue over the next three years. However, currently, Weibo's revenue situation shows no change in its loss-making state despite Alibaba's involvement.

Sina's Q2 financial report data shows that Weibo's advertising revenue was $30 million, up 209% year-on-year, with Alibaba contributing about $5 million in ad revenue to Weibo. Even so, Weibo's operations still incurred a loss of $11.6 million.

Sina CEO Charles Chao once publicly stated, "Weibo is currently a social media platform, but it can also become a social commerce platform, which would constitute a true ecosystem."

However, Weibo's shift towards e-commerce has become the biggest point of controversy regarding this capital marriage between Alibaba and Sina.

Especially with the launch of the "Weibo Taobao Edition" in August this year, Weibo has become a real carrier of Alibaba's social e-commerce dream. While sellers using the Taobao version celebrated a fivefold increase in traffic, many netizens complained that "Weibo ads have increased, becoming nothing more than Taobao's 'backyard garden'." Meanwhile, with the introduction of Weibo's information feed ads and its partnership with Alibaba, Sina's advertising clients expanded from primarily brand advertisers to small and medium enterprises and online merchants.

Regarding this, Wang Jun, a senior analyst at Analysys Mason, told South China Morning Post that increasing revenue through advertising is a necessary path for Sina Weibo. For social media, it is unfeasible to achieve profitability through a membership system in China. Even if it didn't partner with Alibaba, Weibo would still need to rely on advertisements to make a profit. "However, Weibo needs to manage the degree of its e-commerce integration carefully and find a balance between revenue and user experience. It can use big data such as users' online behavior, preferences, actual needs, and social circles to conduct precise marketing and recommendations. In this way, such marketing wouldn't be considered spam by users and wouldn't easily provoke negative reactions," said Wang Jun.

"Chinese Versions of Facebook" Accelerate Transformation

Just last month, Renren Network announced the shutdown of "Happy Farm," a game that became wildly popular across China in 2009 with over 100 million registered users. Notably, in today's booming era of online and mobile games, Renren's closure of Happy Farm might just be a strategy to streamline operations and better penetrate the gaming market.

Wang Jun, a senior analyst at Analysys Mason, believes that with the increasingly fast pace of life, especially with the rise of social forms like Weibo, users tend to prefer lightweight social media that is quick, direct, and concise. Moreover, with the development of mobile internet, the usage habits and patterns of users during the internet era have gradually faded, impacting products like blogs and SN S communities.

In fact, in the internet sector, Renren Company has long played the role of a capital player.

In May 2011, Renren, branded as "China's Facebook," listed on the NYSE with an opening price of $19.50 per share, up 39.28% from the issue price of $14. That same year, Renren acquired 56.com for $80 million. In the fourth quarter, Renren gained a one-time income of $50.9 million from selling shares of eLong, achieving profitability for the quarter.

In 2010, Renren ventured into group-buying with Nuomi.com, but its performance was consistently unsatisfactory. Now, Renren has handed off the hot potato "Nuomi" to Baidu, securing a $160 million strategic investment for Nuomi while Baidu obtained about 59% of Nuomi's shares.

In the gaming domain, Renren has chosen to focus on games as its next bet. Since the first quarter of 2012, the proportion of game revenue in Renren's total income has exceeded 50%. Based on its "cross-screen" strategy and the release of several new games, Renren's online gaming revenue reached $90.2 million in 2012, growing by 113.2% year-over-year. Currently, every game developed by Renren includes both iOS and Android versions. Industry insiders believe that mobile internet could be Renren's lifeline.

Nowadays, Renren Company is divided into five major divisions: Renren Network, Wireless, Games, Nuomi.com, and 56.com. "China's Facebook" has moved further away from the platform model and instead opted to independently develop applications, which brings along its own set of heavy burdens.

"At first, Renren only had users and traffic but lacked monetization pathways. Now, its ventures into gaming, group-buying, and video are inevitable needs," Wang Jun opines. He suggests that Renren should pay more attention to mobile internet and multi-screen social networking to enhance the user experience for multi-screen users.
