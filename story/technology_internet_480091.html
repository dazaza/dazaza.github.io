In the previous part of our introduction to the installation and maintenance of video surveillance systems, we discussed the maintenance of pan-tilt control and cameras. In this section, we will explore the maintenance of video optical transceivers, fiber optic transmission, and BNC coaxial cable transmission.

**Maintenance of Video Optical Transceiver**

The video optical transceiver is divided into transmitting end equipment and receiving end equipment. The transmitting end equipment is placed outdoors along with the camera, and engineers typically perform maintenance tests on the transmitting end. The inspection process for optical transceivers involves three steps:

1. Use a video surveillance comprehensive tester's multimeter to check if the power supply to the optical transceiver is normal. The typical input voltage is DC 5V.

2. Measure the fiber optic output power of the optical transceiver. The transmission wavelength at the transmitting end is 1310nm, and the reception wavelength is 1510nm. For a 20KM optical transceiver, the typical transmission power is around -9dBm.

3. Test whether the video port and RS485/RS232 communication of the optical transceiver are functioning properly. Send standard color bar image signals to the video optical transceiver using a surveillance comprehensive tester, and if the monitoring center receives the test color bars from the optical transceiver, it indicates that the video transmission portion is normal. Use the instrument’s RS485/RS232 data capture function to receive the control signal from the optical transceiver. If the control code sent by the monitoring center is received, it confirms that the RS485/RS232 communication transmission of the optical transceiver is normal.

**Fiber Optic Transmission Maintenance**

Fiber optic transmission has features such as high capacity, high speed, and high stability. Video surveillance systems increasingly use fiber optic devices like video optical transceivers and optical transceivers to transmit video images and control signals. Large-scale surveillance systems such as highway monitoring and safe city monitoring adopt fiber optic transmission to achieve highly stable video transmission. With the development of fiber optic communication technology, the prices of video optical transceivers and fiber optic cables have become lower, making fiber optic transmission very common in video surveillance systems. Fiber optic transmission is typically used for signal transmission between hundreds of meters to one hundred kilometers, and testing fiber optic cables is an essential step. For fiber optic maintenance, engineers must use professional fiber optic test instruments such as light sources, optical power meters, and OTDRs (Optical Time Domain Reflectometers). A stable light source emits laser light with high stable power, which attenuates after passing through the fiber. The optical power meter measures the power value after the fiber transmission, calculating the fiber loss in dB. To measure fiber loss, a light source and an optical power meter must be paired for accurate calculations. An OTDR is an instrument used to measure the break point location of fiber optics, determining approximately how many kilometers away the fiber breaks. However, due to the high cost of OTDRs, smaller scale engineering companies can use fiber fault locators to measure the break point location. For small-scale engineering companies, the most necessary fiber measurement tool is the optical power meter because long-distance optical cables are usually maintained by telecom operators or broadcasting systems. Small-scale engineering companies only need to measure the power and loss of fiber optic devices to identify issues.

When maintaining fiber optics, engineers use the optical power measurement function of the surveillance comprehensive tester, switching to the corresponding wavelength to measure multi-mode and single-mode fibers. Under normal circumstances, fiber-connected optical communication equipment (such as video optical transceivers, optical transceivers) will emit light signals. If the instrument does not receive a light signal, displaying -70dBm, it indicates that the fiber is disconnected and requires checking if the fiber jumper or cable is broken or damaged.

**BNC Coaxial Cable Transmission Maintenance**

Currently, traditional analog images dominate video surveillance systems. Due to compatibility issues, IP digital cameras have yet to become widespread. Generally, small area surveillance systems like factories and buildings directly use BNC coaxial cables to transmit images. BNC coaxial cables have weak interference resistance, and each BNC cable can only transmit one video channel. However, due to the small surveillance area, using coaxial cables for transmission is relatively cost-effective, leading to their widespread use. Due to aging, human damage, or other reasons, BNC cables may cause a decline in video image quality or even interruptions, requiring regular checks during maintenance.

When engineers maintain cables, they use a comprehensive tester to perform three steps:

1. At the camera end, connect the BNC cable to the instrument and use the instrument's image generator to send standard color bars. Under normal circumstances, the monitor at the monitoring center should receive the same color bars. This step determines if the BNC cable is disconnected and if the image colors at the monitoring center are normal.

2. At the monitoring center, use the instrument's video signal attenuation measurement function to determine if the video composite level after BNC cable transmission has attenuated too much. Normally, it should be 800-1000mV. Excessive attenuation can cause image jittering or darkening.

3. If the BNC cable breaks in the middle, since BNC cables are buried in conduits, engineers without test tools find it very difficult to locate the fault point. Using the line testing function of the comprehensive tester, the instrument sends signals and measures the approximate break point location, allowing engineers to quickly find the break point and significantly improving work efficiency.

**Conclusion**

The video surveillance maintenance comprehensive tester is designed based on the cable transmission and actual application environment of domestic video surveillance systems. It helps engineers solve more practical problems. Engineers can use the comprehensive tester not only to carry out maintenance work on cameras, PTZs, pan-tilts, video optical transceivers (product recommendation: 2-channel video optical transceiver), BNC cables, and fiber optics but also avoid frequently changing relevant test tools during high-altitude operations, thereby enhancing safety and work efficiency for engineers. This brings great convenience to the installation and maintenance of security surveillance systems.
