A Brief Exploration of the Inheritance and Development of Chaoyang Yingge Dance

by micro365 on 2010-03-31 16:48:54

This article is reproduced from: Guangzhou Song and Dance Troupe, Guangzhou Art Troupe, Guangzhou Dance Performance. Summary: Folk dances are the root of Chinese national dance art. The folk dance art of Lingnan is colorful. Among many types of dances, Chaoshan Yingge dance has a unique style and distinct characteristics. Yingge dance is a continuation of Han folk square dance and Nu culture relics. There have been certain changes in its continuation. By absorbing the northern big drum Yangko during the Ming Dynasty, it gradually evolved into a kind of folk dance art with unique expression forms represented by Chaoyang area.

Keywords: folk dance; Chaoyang; Yingge dance; inheritance and development

Yingge dance can be considered a representative of Chaoshan culture, which is an extremely important part of Lingnan culture and a significant branch of Chinese Han culture. Influenced by natural environment and historical changes, Chaoshan region formed a particularly prominent regional culture. The formation of Chaoshan regional culture went through several historical causes of immigration, blending the cultural characteristics of Central Plains Han culture, ancient Yue culture, Hakka culture, and Min culture represented by Fujian. After multiple mutual influences and absorptions, the local resident culture and immigrant culture of Chaoshan gradually merged to form the open cultural features of Chaoshan region. In this process, with the infiltration of several immigrant streams in Chaoshan region, the conflicts and intersections of foreign cultures, the indigenous culture of Chaoshan region and the ancient Yue culture, Central Plains culture, She ethnic culture, Min culture, Hakka culture, etc., both differ and show integration with immigrant cultures. The collision and convergence of various cultural streams formed the distinctive Chaoshan culture with regional characteristics, building up unique folklore and cultural consciousness, as well as the unique dialectal characteristics and graceful and elegant art.

Chaoshan folk dances have more than 40 varieties, roughly divided into three categories: the first category is animal dances; the second category is square dances; the third category is the deity-reward dances in religious sacrifices. Among numerous Chaoshan folk dances, the most famous one is undoubtedly Yingge dance. "Yingge dance belongs to Han folk dance forms, popular in Guangdong, Fujian and other places. Its main performance form is a large-scale group dance, where dancers hold a short wooden stick in each hand, striking them against each other in all directions, with strong and powerful movements and a strong rhythm. After the dance, there are song and dance plays called 'Yingge Houpeng'."

The activities of Yingge dance in Chaoshan region have obvious universality. Taking Chaoyang District as an example, Yingge teams are almost everywhere in the district. During traditional festivals and festive days, Yingge teams are active among the masses, adding brilliance to the festival and bringing joy to people. Then, when did Chaoyang Yingge appear, and how was it inherited and developed?

I. Historical Origin

Chaoyang Yingge is renowned for its boldness and vigor, hailed as the "typical dance of Chinese Han men." Regarding the origin of Chaoyang Yingge, scholars and experts have conducted much research. Although no definitive conclusion has been reached yet, there is generally a unified understanding that Chaoyang Yingge is a stream rather than a source, and its formation can be traced back to the Ming Dynasty or even earlier. Yingge originated in Puning and has a history of over 300 years. It is very popular in Puning, enjoying the reputation of "North Anse Waist Drum, South Puning Yangko". Popular in the Chaoshan region, it is regarded as a symbol of righteousness and peace, deeply loved by the masses and widely spread in the folk. As for the direct source, there are various different opinions. Some say it comes from the "Big Drum Yangko" of Northern Shandong and the "Liu Lin Yangko" of Western Shandong, some say it comes from the "Ba Wang Bian" of Henan flower drum, and others say it comes from the flower drum dance of Putian, Fujian. There are many theories. Southern Han folk dances have always been characterized by lightness and elegance. However, in the coastal areas of Eastern Guangdong, there exists a type of Yingge dance that rivals the majestic and robust Shaanbei waist drum for its grandeur and strength. According to records, there are several theories:

(1) Related to Sacrificial Exorcism

From the unearthed Neolithic, bronze, and Jin dynasty tombs in Gulan Mountain, it can be seen that the Yue culture of Chaoshan and the Nu culture and sacrificial rites of Central Plains culture are important parts of the origin of Yingge dance. Chaoyang is backed by mountains and faces the sea, with a perilous environment and frequent disasters. Due to limited conditions and inability to resist, they developed animistic beliefs and prayed for disaster relief through sacrifice. For example, in the fourteenth year of Tang Yuanhe, Governor of Chaozhou Han Yu sent someone to today's Dahu Lake in Haimen Town to sacrifice to the gods, with "blowing and beating pipes and drums accompanied by incense and cleanliness." In the thirty-sixth year of Ming Jiajing, "ghosts (horse spirits) galloped and attached themselves," "our county never ceased the beacon fires, and the sounds of cannons and drums were heard." Today's Yingge performers' face masks should be simplified versions of sacrificial Nu dance masks. The Nan Shan Yingge front team beats small drums. To this day, Yingge still participates in deity worship and exorcism, indicating that its formation is related to sacrificial exorcism.

(2) Influence of Western Spreading Min Culture

Starting from the Song and Yuan dynasties, Min culture gradually spread westward to Chaoshan and even Chaoyang. During the Song and Yuan dynasties, the largest number of immigrants from Min areas came to Chaozhou and Chaoyang. Later, Min became a transit station for large-scale migrations, and officials and immigrants brought Min culture to Chaoyang. Known as the singing Xilu Jianshan Yingge, team members hold small drums and perform with small steps. According to research, it originates from the flower drum dance of Putian. And Putian is the birthplace of many surnames in Chaoyang, with some tracing their roots back to Datiexiang. Clearly, the westward spread of Min culture is a major factor in the formation of Chaoyang Yingge.

(3) Theater and Folk Dance as Conditions for the Formation of Yingge

During the Ming Dynasty, "the custom in Chaoyang was to enjoy theater," with stages and earth platforms, performing "Tu Xi" and singing the predecessor of Yingge, which was originally storytelling but later adapted into its current form to suit the processions of deity worship and competitions. Local records state: "On the fifteenth day of the first lunar month, skilled singers gather in groups, dressing up to perform in square formations, welcoming deities through alleys," "to honor the gods, flutes and zithers sound, fragrant clothes cluster in shadows, filling the spring city." Today, characters are portrayed in the front section of Yingge performances, while the rear section includes excerpts from Chaoju operas like "Tao Hua Crossing the River," "Double Swinging Mill," "Immortal Master and Immortal Mother," folk dances like "Bird Fighting," and martial arts performances, truly integrating dance, opera, and martial arts.

II. Inheritance and Reform

Divided by dance rhythm, Yingge mainly has three families: slow board Yingge, medium board Yingge, and fast board Yingge. Slow board Yingge has a slower rhythm with classical court-style characteristics; medium board Yingge's gong and drum techniques are relatively simpler compared to the other two styles, showcasing the artistic beauty of "power" and "momentum," appearing more graceful; fast board Yingge has a faster, fiercer rhythm than slow and medium board Yingge, with tight drumbeats, dynamic opening and closing of formations, vigorous movements, and a lively atmosphere. Due to hundreds of years of productivity development and the needs of folk customs, Yingge performances have continuously enriched and improved, inheriting traditions and innovating in practice: slow board Yingge is only found in areas like Miancheng, Jingdu Huxi, and Haimen Hubian. Though its development is slow, it focuses on refining existing elements. In 1997, the Lingdong Yingge team's performance at the Yingge Art Festival had a free rhythm, steady, simple, and solemn actions, vividly portraying the inner world of characters and the heroic spirit. Medium board Yingge, where the drummer leads the advancement or pattern changes of the team while playing, originally had relatively simple gong and drum techniques. Later, Puzi introduced the character Yingge, and Wulian in Miancheng introduced the New Seven Hammer Yingge. Their gong and drum accompaniments added several large gongs from Chaozhou grand gongs and a small cymbal. Several teams participating in the 1997 Chaoyang Yingge Art Festival incorporated new content into patterns and formats, making them more flexible and vibrant. Fast board Yingge underwent the greatest changes, developing into the Nan Shan Martial School, Literary School, and Combat Format School. In the Nan Shan Martial School, half of the performers wield sticks and half drums, with vertical movement from the abdomen to the head corner covering 360 degrees. The spinning stick is lively and dynamic, embodying the heroic momentum of capturing tigers in the mountains and slaying dragons in the seas. This form is prevalent in areas like Liangying and Guyao. The Nan Shan Literary School differs from the Martial School in that its format includes a "crossing the river" dance scene, where performers use one or both hands to freely swing sticks above their heads, using freer steps and jumping off the boat posture, seemingly depicting the story of Liangshan heroes returning triumphantly from conquering the Great Name Fort. Currently, Xiancheng, Chengdian, and Simapu towns adopt this dance school. The combat format school is a product after 1931. That year, the "San He Xing Yingge Team" in Guiyu eliminated the hand drum in the front section, designing four combat formats—"fallen women" and "combat"—based on the teachings of boxing master Chen Zhuo San, creating a new combat format school that integrated martial arts into Yingge, stunning Chaoshan. In 1953, the Xiqi Yingge team invited coach Zhou Xiuxing from Jiashan to teach eight combat formats—"single combat" and "combat." In 1970, young coach Hong Wuren selected step methods such as "great warhorse," "cutting legs," "great iron clamp," "bow step," and "triangle horse" from Southern boxing "scattered hands," incorporating techniques like "all-encompassing rolling hands," "jade person reflecting in the mirror," "old tree rooted," "sharp knife cutting bamboo," "back stick," "capturing tigers in the mountain," "swallow flipping," "blocking tiger dry character," "continuous ring hand," "double dragon holding the moon," "Kui Star kicking the dipper," and "mighty lion opening mouth," redesigning single combat, combat, two-person combat, and four-person combat into upper and lower eight sets of combat formats. The outward horizontal spin stick was changed to fierce and robust, later developing to thirty sets with more than ten performance patterns such as butterfly, plum blossom, and bagua array, integrating dance, opera, and martial arts. They performed multiple times outside, winning three banners of honor at the "Chinese Dance Chengdu Autumn" competition. Yongfeng Yingge team absorbed the styles and characteristics of combat format, medium board Yingge, and other schools while retaining traditional flavors, forming a new combat format school characterized by ancient simplicity, beauty, ruggedness, bravery, and a lively atmosphere. They participated in the second "Plum Blossom Cup" competition of national art colleges in Beijing, gaining fame in Beijing. From the development process of these various schools, it can be seen that as long as traditions are inherited and innovations are courageously pursued, Chaoyang's Yingge will become increasingly wonderful and influential.

Since the founding of the People's Republic of China, due to the need for domestic and international performances, folk artists attempted to bring Yingge onto the stage. However, it was difficult for a few people to perform on stage to convey the overwhelming, brave, robust, and profound qualities of the mass square performance, and it was also challenging to combine the styles of various Yingge schools. Due to these reasons, Yingge had not made it to the stage until April 2007, when Chaoyang Yingge finally entered the CCTV broadcasting hall, participating in the fourth CCTV TV Dance Competition Yangko Contest, winning the Excellent Performance Award and receiving the highest support rate online, gaining the love of the entire nation. Gradually stepping onto the stage, Chaoyang Yingge has developed well. In the process of Yingge's development, female Yingge and youth Yingge appeared alongside the previous adult Yingge and elder Yingge, earning Chaoyang the title of "Chinese Folk Art Hometown (Yingge Art)" by the Ministry of Culture. At that time, Yingge had already entered schools, with urban and rural schools organizing student Yingge teams to participate in holiday parades, such as Huamei in Guiyu, Longgang, Fengnan, Fengbei in Miancheng, and several schools in Chengcheng and Xiqi. The inheritance of Yingge generation after generation depends on the efforts of our younger generation. Although organizing student Yingge teams is relatively difficult, having schools organize student Yingge teams allows students to perform Yingge, which is beneficial for cultivating and nurturing a new generation of individuals with moral, intellectual, physical, aesthetic, and labor education. So, how can we better enable the next generation to inherit Yingge? I believe that we can introduce Chaoshan folk art, including Yingge, into classrooms, develop school-based courses, and promote Chaoshan culture. By introducing the story background of Yingge, posting knowledge pictures of Yingge and other folk arts, and setting up exhibition halls about the development of Yingge, we can broaden students' horizons and stimulate their interest in learning Yingge. Whenever holidays come, teachers can re-choreograph Yingge based on its original form, creating new forms and meanings, allowing students to personally participate in Yingge performances to achieve the purpose of inheriting Yingge. Youth are the hope for the future of Yingge. To ensure this region-specific folk art can be passed down from generation to generation and to improve students' physical fitness and cultivate their heroic spirit, leaders at all levels in Chaoyang actively organize and advocate for Yingge to enter schools.

III. Innovation and Development

The development of traditional folk art is based on inheritance. Development is infinite, progressing in a process of continuous succession and improvement. The ancient, robust, and mighty Yingge dance is one of the most representative folk dances in Guangdong, having existed for nearly three hundred years. Since the founding of the People's Republic of China, Yingge has been highly valued and developed. In the continuous process of inheriting and developing Yingge, in today's rapidly changing world, how should we promote its further development? This is a question that requires thought. Today, when we talk about inheritance, we must take its essence, meaning not only preserving the essence of traditional Yingge in terms of its expressive forms, traditional routines, formation changes, action patterns, and unique styles, but also inheriting the excellent aspects that have emerged during its development. Today, when we talk about development, it means building on the essence of tradition and the essence of development to adapt to the aesthetic awareness and demands of contemporary times, further developing, enriching, and enhancing the artistic level of modern Yingge. How can we further promote the development of modern Yingge? I believe there are two main aspects: one is adhering to specific goal orientation; the other is measures for cultivating elite teams.

(1) Adhering to Specific Goal Orientation

Regarding adhering to specific goal orientation, the premise is what specific goals should include. Combining the current status of Yingge, I believe there are four areas that need further development: (1) Further development in action patterns, routines, and rhythm changes; (2) Further development in choreography for formation beauty and layer contrast; (3) Further development in arrangements for group performances and small group interludes; (4) Further development in shaping the characteristics of various characters. Among these four aspects, the most fundamental is the innovative development of action patterns, and this innovation must follow the traditional rhythmic style of Yingge, avoiding arbitrary combinations. "Routines" narrowly understood are action combinations. A new action pattern combined with an existing one brings novelty. Therefore, every action routine should include partially different action patterns, otherwise, too many routines would feel repetitive. Additionally, the rhythm changes of routine actions—fast, medium, and slow—are also important. A set of the same actions produces different effects due to rhythm changes. Therefore, from the perspective of folk dance, rhythm changes are also a form of innovation. Regarding "further development in choreography for formation beauty and layer contrast," first, when the formation moves and momentarily pauses and spreads out, it forms a picture composition, then transitions through formation scheduling to another picture. Therefore, in choreography, the transition process of formations should be shortened to highlight the continuous transformation of beautiful images. Secondly, in the conception of images, artistic atmosphere and artistic conception should be pursued, pursuing layers of progression and overall composition completeness. To achieve this, the artistic technique of "contrast" should be skillfully used, such as horizontal, vertical, diagonal, circular, square, curved, and triangular lines in formations. One line should not be repeatedly used, but variations should be sought. A single procession of Yingge cannot satisfy people's aesthetic senses anymore, and changes in processions and formations are also a form of innovation. Regarding "further development in arrangements for group performances and small group interludes," in other words, the interplay between "large scenes" and "small scenes," proper arrangement gives a refreshing feeling. Regarding "further development in shaping the characteristics of various characters," the representation and shaping of character traits must be embodied through action patterns consistent with their personality traits. Now, all characters with different face masks doing the same routine cannot distinguish the features between characters. Only when there are more and richer action routines can this issue be resolved. Moreover, the current female Yingge evolved from male Yingge, so the feminine characteristics need to be perfected. The basic dance steps upon entrance need to be feminized, and the rhythm should be slowed down. Other dynamics can refer to and draw from the martial dan roles in opera. In summary, these four aspects are interrelated and complementary. Dance is a dynamic art, and changing action patterns and postures are its language. The further enrichment and development of Yingge's unique action language will play a decisive role in promoting the further development of Yingge.

(2) Measures for Cultivating Elite Teams

Yingge is realized through people, first requiring dance performance talents, followed by coaching talents who can teach dance. To promote the further development of Yingge, creative choreographic talents are needed. Starting from the needs of promoting the further development of Yingge, not only existing talents need to be cultivated, but new backbone talents need to be constantly discovered, selected, and cultivated. The improvement of artistic quality in Yingge's performance, coaching, and choreographic talents will inevitably promote the prosperity and development of Yingge art. Then, how should talents be selected and cultivated? I believe that selection contests or creation contests within counties and towns can be held, gathering one or two elite Yingge teams through evaluations that integrate performance, coaching, and choreography talents. While general Yingge activities can be seasonal, the activities of elite teams should be regular. Through the following aspects of training elite teams, the goal of cultivating Yingge talents can be achieved: (1) Conducting textbook research; (2) Performing basic skills training for Yingge; (3) Conducting experimental and demonstrative rehearsals and performances; (4) Inviting folk artists for guidance and lectures. Regarding textbook research, it involves studying and summarizing the existing action routines and formation changes of this school or all schools, standardizing them; secondly, researching and formulating basic skills training materials for Yingge, especially decomposing technically challenging actions such as baton flowers, dividing them into multiple-step training procedures from easy to difficult, establishing normative standards for hand, eye, body, method, and foot training in Yingge actions. Regarding basic skills training for Yingge, basic training lays a long-term foundation for performances. For some technical and challenging actions in performances that require long-term training to take effect, daily basic training is essential; commonly used basic actions need to be standardized through basic training, and regular training ensures uniformity during rehearsals. Regarding experimental and demonstrative rehearsals and performances, they should focus on the innovation and development of Yingge. For instance, the interplay between "large scenes" and "small scenes" mainly concerns what should be arranged in the small scenes. It can be imagined: different character actions represent different personalities; arranging the action routines of various Yingge schools enriches the field of vision; arranging a solo performance of Yingge drums changes the form of Yingge, making it comprehensive; inserting segments of traditional Yingge "post-pavilion" songs and dances enriches the program content. When the small scenes perform in the central performance area, the large scenes should accompany with light movements and simple formation changes to maintain the continuity and artistic atmosphere of the performance. These four arrangements actually constitute various new versions, which must be tested through experimental performances. Successful versions can coexist, showcasing their own charm and being chosen according to the occasion. Regarding inviting folk artists for guidance and lectures, I believe that Yingge is passed down from generation to generation by the masses themselves. Contemporary Yingge should still be developed by the local masses, and folk artists can impart relevant Yingge theories and action essentials based on the actual needs of Yingge teams. Persistently training Yingge teams in this way not only cultivates more outstanding talents but also better passes down Yingge to the next generation. When significant results are achieved, a demonstration effect will emerge, further strengthening its artistic influence.

IV. Conclusion

Today's Chaoyang Yingge has undergone generations of evolution, especially since the reform and opening-up policy after the founding of New China, through the efforts of Chaoyang's cultural and artistic departments in deep excavation, meticulous organization, inheritance and development, and innovation. In November 1996, Chaoyang was named a "Chinese Folk Art Hometown" by the Ministry of Culture, and Yingge was included in the list of national intangible cultural heritage. In early August 1998, Pan Zhitao, Director of the Chinese Dance Department at the Beijing Dance Academy and Executive Committee member of the Second "Plum Blossom Cup" Chinese Dance Competition Organizing Committee, enthusiastically applauded and praised the brilliant performance of the Yongfeng Yingge team from Chaoyang, saying, "Folk dances like Yongfeng Yingge are the roots of Chinese dance." Chaoyang Yingge has experienced nearly half a century, evolving from square Yingge to stage Yingge, from single male Yingge to female Yingge, elderly Yingge, and youth Yingge. With its stirring hammer sounds, varied action formations, bright costumes, and characteristic Chinese face masks, it has won the love of the masses, becoming a rare gem in the garden of folk arts. With favorable factors such as timing, location, and people, the golden age of Chaoyang Yingge will last long. Continuing to promote the development of our folk arts is an inescapable responsibility for our younger generation. We firmly believe that through our joint efforts, Chaoyang Yingge will shine even brighter in the literary and artistic world in the future!
