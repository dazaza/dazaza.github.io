Twitter's closed platform strategy has led to a 170% surge in R&D expenses.

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 16:49:13

In the third quarter of this year, Twitter's R&D expenditure increased by 170% year-on-year, while its revenue growth in the same period was 105%. The fundamental reason for Twitter's R&D expenditure growth rate exceeding its revenue growth rate is due to the closed platform strategy of this social network: While Twitter promotes the concept of an open platform, it controls the advertising revenue from the platform. Therefore, they must rely on their internal talent to continuously develop innovative products, which has driven up operating costs. Below is a summary of the article:

Over the past four years, Twitter has been promoting that it is using external developers to improve its services, but many startups have found themselves excluded. In fact, to strengthen control over advertising revenue on its site, Twitter has imposed strict restrictions on external applications.

R&D expenditure growth outpaces revenue growth

Twitter is about to become the largest US tech IPO after Facebook. Therefore, the company must rely on its internal talent to continuously develop innovative products, which will drive up its operating costs. With many brands seeking to advertise on this platform with 230 million users, Twitter needs to increase investment to adapt to this trend.

Nihal Mehta, founder and venture capitalist of New York online advertising company LocalResponse Inc., said: "Twitter may never let any company grow on its platform. Once a company earns significant revenue on Twitter, Twitter will choose to do these things itself."

In the third quarter of this year, Twitter invested $87.3 million in R&D, more than half of its total revenue, most of which was used for talent recruitment. R&D expenditure increased by 170% year-on-year in the third quarter, while revenue increased by 105% in the same period.

Tense relations with developers

Similar to developing applications for Windows or Mac computers, an application can also use Twitter data and user networks to provide 'complementary' services. By selling advertisements within their services, these application providers can earn money on the Twitter platform.

Unlike Facebook, Google, and Apple, Twitter's relationship with developers is more strained. Everyone claims to be a platform. The word 'platform' appears 260 times in Twitter's IPO prospectus. Some companies have succeeded, such as game developer Zynga on Facebook, and thousands of games and applications on Apple iOS and Google Android platforms, with typical representatives being Angry Birds and Candy Crush Saga.

Attracting external developers is one way Facebook, Google, and Apple maintain low R&D costs. In the most recent quarter, Facebook's R&D expenditure accounted for only 19% of its revenue, and before its IPO in May 2012, Facebook's R&D expenditure ratio was only 14%. Google and Apple's R&D expenditure ratios are 14% and 3.3%, respectively.

Twitter will hold its IPO roadshow on Monday Eastern Time, and the company will also emphasize the concept of an 'open platform'. In July 2009, Twitter co-founder Biz Stone tweeted: "We believe that Twitter can nurture and support an ecosystem." In January 2009, as a startup in New York, Twitter held an event titled "Growing Together with Twitter" to attract companies hoping to conduct business on Twitter.

Joe Fernandez, CEO of U.S.-based influence assessment website Klout, was one of the attendees. Facing 700 tech enthusiasts, Fernandez promoted his product with all his might. Fernandez said: "Many companies were very interested in Twitter's 'platform' positioning. I myself was very fascinated." In 2010, Fernandez moved Klout's headquarters office to the same building as Twitter.

Part of Klout's success is because Twitter decided not to develop competing services. Klout generates revenue by helping advertisers find influential people who can promote their brand through social networks.

Other startups attending that conference weren't so lucky. CoTweet was acquired a year later, TwiTerra was just an unprofitable art project. StockTwits remains a company with only about 10 employees.

Companies like Klout and LocalResponse pay Twitter for data usage through Gnip Inc. and DataSift Inc. Gnip and DataSift are among the few companies that can access Twitter's massive database, and these companies pay Twitter licensing fees, accounting for as high as 9% of Twitter's revenue in the third quarter of this year.

This is one area where external companies can make money using Twitter. Twitter is currently investing in developing the 'Twitter Cards' feature, which allows content publishers and developers to customize how their content is displayed on Twitter.

Twitter is unwilling to share ad revenue generated on its own platform. In mid-2010, Twitter closed the function that allowed other companies to add ads to the 'tweet stream.' As a result, paid tweet brokerage startups like Adly Inc. and 140 Proof Inc. had to find other ways to make money, and Magpie was even acquired.

In 2011, Twitter also restricted services that provided users different ways to log into Twitter, such as through online dashboards or independent mobile websites. This move led to the closure of an app called UberTwitter. Another app, TweetDeck, was acquired by Twitter for $20 million in May 2011.

As competitors to UberTwitter and TweetDeck, HootSuite Media Inc. survived this change and grew by expanding to all major social networks and developing platform ad monitoring software for enterprises. In August this year, HootSuite raised $165 million in funding from venture capital firms such as Accel Partners and Insight Venture Partners.

Lack of comprehensive user data

Twitter is not the only platform that decides 'who stays and who goes.' In the past few years, Facebook has constrained developers by modifying its terms of service, such as changing payment methods and limiting spam email distribution.

However, Facebook allows some partners to receive 30% of in-game application revenue and lets developers use its over a billion users to post job advertisements and offer online dating services. Apple and Google have taken similar approaches, allowing developers to sell their products on these two platforms and participate in revenue sharing.

But Twitter does not yet have this capability. Analyst Charlene Li of Altimeter Group, a California-based investment agency, said: "Unlike Facebook, Twitter's ability to help brands target consumers is limited because users do not provide personal preference data on Twitter. Twitter does not know enough about its users. Twitter needs to collect as comprehensive data as possible on its platform because this is its weakness."

In fact, Twitter is also developing some products and services, such as Vine, a short video sharing application it acquired last year.
