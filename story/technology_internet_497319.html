Brand: How to Turn a Product into Pop Culture

by diglog on 2011-09-30 08:51:24

The name Stanley Hainsworth is extraordinary; he is one of the most outstanding branding masters in recent years. He started as Creative Director at Nike, then moved to Lego, and now holds the title of Global Vice President of Creative at Starbucks Coffee. Under his leadership, Starbucks' brand image and product sales have soared, making it one of the most beloved coffee brands. How did he transform "Starbucks" into such a contemporary brand, turning it into a cultural symbol that exudes irresistible allure?

At Starbucks, Stanley innovatively proposed five standards which are foundational design philosophies:

- Handmade

- Artistic

- Refined

- Human-centered

- Durable

"If you can't create a superior purchasing experience for consumers, don't expect them to pick up your products, or even want to try them," Stanley said in an interview, sharing valuable insights about brand building.

**Q: Stanley, how do you define 'brand'?**

**A:** A brand is the physical manifestation of emotion, connecting merchants and consumers.

**Q:** Is it an "emotional bond"?

**A:** When consumers have needs or desires, brands give them the most direct feelings, which are undoubtedly emotional.

**Q:** Alright, when a consumer has to choose between coffee and soda, what factors lead to different choices?

**A:** I believe the best brands create things that consumers haven’t even thought of (or even needed). For example, people need "high-quality, high-performance running shoes," so Nike gave everyone this feeling. When people are amazed that a brand can improve their lives—by adding more fun or increasing efficiency—they inadvertently connect with the brand.

**Q:** Creating something unprecedented is certainly a dream-like thing. But coffee is a common commodity. Among tens of millions of cafes worldwide, how does Starbucks create a unique experience? Don't consumers already know what they're buying?

**A:** I think great brands focus on their "final state." For instance, when we conceptualize a new product, we don't think about its formula but imagine what it will look like when it's finally in front of the customer. We discuss what it should present.

Just like Apple's famous 1984 ad, it didn't show specific products, but it portrayed the consumer's "final state."

**Q:** Is Starbucks deliberately creating an environment and scene for consumers to experience?

**A:** It has been this way from the start. When Howard Schultz (current CEO) first came to Starbucks, he began doing this. He personally went to Milan, experienced the local coffee culture, visited local cafes and bars, and carefully observed various customers. So from the beginning, Starbucks designed a unique "social atmosphere." People come here not just for coffee but also for the culture.

Howard clearly realized that there isn't only Starbucks in the world that provides quality coffee. How to stand out among many coffee shops? The answer is to build a "community," making it a third place beyond "home" and "work."

So, in my design, what furniture I select, what paintings I hang, and what music I play all revolve around this idea.

**Q:** What is the most critical part of building a brand?

**A:** For me, it's telling a story.

**Q:** What is "a story"?

**A:** Every brand has a story behind it, which could be the founder's story or the reason for the brand's creation. Some brands never tell their stories, so they don't receive love because there's no emotional connection. For example, the Microsoft brand, while Gates' personal story may be astonishing, people still perceive the Microsoft brand as cold.

**Q:** Would you conduct extensive market research?

**A:** Yes, I do a lot of research. However, the purpose of the research isn't to seek inspiration but to validate my ideas. If I ask a group of consumers, "Do you need a $4 cup of coffee?" no one would agree to pay, they'd prefer cheaper options.

These interviews come from Debbie Millman's new book *Brand Thinking and Other Noble Pursuits*, where the experiences shared are worth learning from for every entrepreneur.
