Musicians: Don't count on Apple's iTunes to make you a star overnight

by geekzhang on 2013-05-13 12:27:17

It has been a full decade since the launch of Apple's iTunes Music Store. From an initial offering of 200,000 songs at its debut to now surpassing 25 billion song sales, iTunes has undoubtedly become a vast online digital music empire.The new business model pioneered by Steve Jobs back then proved that his vision was at least ten years ahead of its time. Standing at this turning point in time, iTunes also faces the confusion of a midlife crisis: should it continue to rely on past achievements and comfortably rest on its laurels, or make up its mind to change?In the eyes of musicians who have experienced and accompanied the growth of iTunes, it has never been a quick-service platform for creating overnight sensations. Instead, it has taught people about sharing, taste, and opportunity.Overnight fame? Don't daydream.In the span of ten years, iTunes seems to have changed nothing. The cost to download a song remains at $0.99, and if you want the entire album, you still need to pay $9.99. While the price of a carton of milk has quadrupled or more, the pricing of iTunes remains the same.In a positive light, iTunes is persistent; but if we use a negative term, iTunes is a typical example of being stuck in tradition. In the U.S., it has already been criticized by some industry insiders as being like an electronic spreadsheet filled with music. One piece of evidence supporting this criticism is that it is nearly impossible to replicate the phenomenon of a rock guitarist like Joe King gaining global fans overnight through just one song on iTunes, as it happened ten years ago.Is it really possible that just because your song is uploaded to iTunes, you wake up to find the whole world knows you? This kind of thinking is simply a daydream. A few years ago, independent musician Sonny HU was playing his hip-hop music in the U.S. In his view, iTunes is merely a platform providing sharing and downloading services. You can't expect to make a lot of money from it. To date, I've only received a few thousand dollars in income, Sonny HU told the IT Times reporter. As a singer, you need to continuously accumulate more songs and more albums on iTunes until one day a headhunter from an advertising company or a commercial organization hears your music. That’s when your real chance to make money comes. Many of the jobs I get are because someone heard my songs on iTunes.Unlike YouTube and other video websites, or talent shows like American Idol, the nature of iTunes dictates that singers remain hidden behind their sounds. In fact, iTunes often plays the role of adding value to what is already there. Whether it's the most popular tracks from U2 on the first day of launch or all the tracks from Madonna that followed, these big names were already famous.From application to hearing your own song takes four monthsThe constantly expanding catalog of iTunes offers music fans more choices, but it also brings along a long and complex process. Without going into the preliminary preparations, starting from the day of application, it takes a full four months before you can hear your own song on iTunes. Especially for independent musicians, this is a very trying process. There are simply too many people ahead of you in line. Sonny HU said, plus other uncontrollable factors, new singers must be prepared to wait at least five months. The new song may be recorded for the high-energy summer, but by the time it goes live, you're already singing Christmas songs. This happens quite often. Therefore, three years ago, after returning to develop domestically, Sonny HU immediately decided to hand over matters such as uploading his music to a planning company abroad.Moreover, there are now WeChat, Weibo, Facebook, and other emerging platforms where, as long as you create music, there will always be a place for you. The only way is to brand yourself, carefully packaging and promoting from design to publicity. However, Sonny HU cannot deny that the service from iTunes is impeccable. Every two months, he receives a report from iTunes clearly marking how many listeners downloaded his songs in which countries and regions. You'll know that people in that area like listening to my style of music, giving him a clear sense.Actively inviting Chinese singersIn February this year, Apple's statistics showed that iTunes had 450 million active accounts, with song sales reaching an astonishing record of 25 billion. But at the same time, iTunes also faces an awkward reality: its share of the $2.9 billion U.S. digital music market has dropped to 63%, the lowest since 2006.In fact, the giant battleship that is iTunes is not unaware. On the contrary, it is also feeding itself with new nutrients. Previously, Apple launched iTunes 11, redesigning the user interface and integrating iCloud, the biggest update since the birth of iTunes. In terms of supplementing more types of music and filling with singers in various languages, iTunes is also making efforts. In late June last year, the main song "BB88" from Hong Kong singer Kahi Fang's album "Back to the Future" was uploaded to iTunes, which was a big news in the Mandarin music circle at the time. May Chan, the promotional officer at Warner Music Hong Kong, told the IT Times reporter that it was iTunes that proactively reached out to Warner, hoping to expand overseas business and enrich the library type. Besides Japanese and Korean songs, they wanted to invite Chinese singers to upload their works and albums. May Chan said that now, all Mandarin albums under Warner Music can be purchased on iTunes. As for specific download numbers and revenue-sharing ratios, she indicated that they signed a confidentiality agreement with iTunes, so it is inconvenient to disclose.In Hong Kong, paying 6 Hong Kong dollars allows you to download a new song on iTunes to listen to, which is the choice of many young people today. In mainland China, however, iTunes Music Store remains the most familiar stranger to many people. As the saying goes, those who feel the warmth of spring water first are the ducks. Whether to criticize or look forward to iTunes' future, the perception of iTunes comes more from other levels. According to the actual experience of tinyfool, founder of iApp4Me.com, he believes that iTunes is developing well. In 2010, tinyfool created a podcast on iTunes, attracting listeners ranging from friends, IT guys, students to Apple dealers, all kinds of people. In the past year, the number of listeners has skyrocketed, with around 4,000 people listening to each episode, a fourfold increase from the previous 1,000. tinyfool told the IT Times reporter that from a technical standpoint, there indeed isn't any other platform that can replace podcast. Perhaps, being irreplaceable and unmatched is precisely the reason why iTunes can still sit firmly on its throne, continuing to rely on its legacy.
