Synopsis:

In an era filled with war and witchcraft, Solomon Kane (James Purefoy) loses himself in endless greed and killing, almost becoming a puppet of the devil. After surviving a great calamity, Solomon returns to a monastery in his hometown of England, intending to live a life away from violence. However, under the guidance of a priest, he embarks on a journey back home. At this time, the outside world is full of killings and looting. Traveling alone, Solomon narrowly escapes danger but is fortunately saved by the Crusoe family, who are preparing to move to America. During his time with the Crusoe family, Solomon experiences the warmth and trust of family.

One morning, the Crusoe family encounters a group of evil warriors who kill Mr. Crusoe and his two sons and kidnap his daughter Meredith (Rachel Hurd-Wood). Before dying, Crusoe pleads with Solomon to rescue his daughter and tells him that only by saving Meredith can his soul be redeemed. Having vowed never to kill again, Solomon must pick up his sword once more to save Meredith and redeem his own soul...

The film is adapted from the eponymous novel created by American writer Robert E. Howard in 1928.

Behind-the-scenes Production:

In this era dominated by Hollywood movies, most fantasy films produced by major studios are aimed at children aged 13 and above. In comparison, Europe's handling of fantasy themes is somewhat worrying. Fortunately, the appearance of the movie "Solomon Kane" brings hope to the European fantasy film market. Directed by Michael Bassett, known for his World War II horror film "Deathwatch," the characters' costumes recreate the style of the Elizabethan era. The cast is quite impressive, with James Purefoy, who starred in the epic TV series "Rome" in 2005, portraying the hero Solomon Kane from American writer Robert E. Howard's works. Veteran actors such as Pete Postlethwaite, Max von Sydow, and Rachel Hurd-Wood will "assist" this knight in fighting against the "dark forces." The young British director Michael Bassett did not directly adapt Howard's novel for the big screen but instead created an original prequel about Kane's origin story, hoping to craft a British version of "The Lord of the Rings" trilogy. The film received many positive reviews when it premiered at the Cannes Film Festival last May.

Director Michael Bassett attempts to open an alternative path in the production of fantasy films, aiming to create both grand visual effects while making the audience feel that everything is real rather than fantastical. He believes that demons and frequently used elements in fantasy films can be completed through special effects, but the shooting locations and conditions must all be real. He states, "It’s too boring to act in front of a large blue screen, and for the actors, their performances will be better in a truly experiential and tangible environment." Therefore, the entire movie was filmed in France, Czech Republic, and the UK. Elements like strong winds, heavy rain, hail, and muddy grounds were all shot on location. The director integrates these real elements into the fantasy world, adding a lot of special effects design to the magnificent action scenes, crafting a more adult-oriented, complex, and less childish fantasy film.

James Purefoy says that his biggest commonality with Kane is the determination and courage to achieve goals. He claims he is not an academically trained actor, so he carefully studies each role. He read all of Robert E. Howard's related works as well as stories and poems about Kane. Then he began extensively reading books about Puritan beliefs, hoping to understand this person historically and culturally. Since Kane is a man of few words, he and director Michael decided to cut about half of the planned dialogue. "Every time I enter the character, I think I know who he is, what he thinks and does, and my job is to become his personality and do everything he would do."

Director Michael expressed how enjoyable it was to collaborate with James Purefoy. Initially, during casting, he didn’t consider James as the top choice because he thought James looked too modern. "I thought he was the type who wore a suit, held a glass of wine in one hand and hugged a beautiful girl in the other." However, after watching James’ performance in the epic TV series "Rome," his impression greatly improved. "I think his versatility is much higher than I originally imagined." After conversing with James, the director became even more convinced that James was the perfect choice for Kane. "His physical fitness and appearance are excellent, his combat skills are top-notch. Although the shooting conditions were tough, he was injured physically, he fully devoted himself to this role without complaint and never missed a day. His deep and meticulous understanding of the role aligns perfectly with mine."

Trivia:

- As early as 2001, there were rumors that Christopher Lambert would take on the role of Kane, and he himself said it was a very challenging role. However, due to failed agreements between producers and New Line Cinema, producer Murphy angrily left the project. The plan to shoot the movie was put on hold for a long time until the script for Solomon Kane's African adventure appeared, and subsequently, director Michael was appointed as the screenwriter and director.

- During a fight scene between James Purefoy and stunt actors, Purefoy's forehead was unfortunately pierced by a sword, requiring five stitches at the hospital. Besides this, knee ligament injuries were also common occurrences.

- To ensure authenticity and stay true to the original descriptions, the director rejected motion capture performances in front of blue screens. The storms, heavy rains, and muddy paths in the movie were all filmed on location in the Czech Republic, requiring actors and staff to brave the elements regularly and endure the cold, often rolling around in mud.

- To allow the actors to clearly understand the real effects of swords on flesh, director Michael went to great lengths to bring a dead pig to the set and demonstrated whipping, stabbing, and cutting on it.

- To match the character's physique, actor James Purefoy successfully lost 10 kg and spent a long time practicing sword-fighting techniques and horsemanship.

- Director Michael Bassett's 10-year-old daughter Isabella made a cameo appearance in the film, playing a strange little girl found by her family in an abandoned village. The crew praised the young girl's performance, and the director himself expressed pride in his daughter.
