MacBookAir (2010 Fall) Review

Not long ago, I experienced the boot speed of the MacBook Air. But winning at the starting line doesn't necessarily mean a smooth run to the finish. Does the 11.6-inch MacBook Air bring any surprises? For a complete review, please check out the content after the jump! In terms of hardware design, the two new models of MacBook Air (11.6 inches and 13.3 inches) haven't seen much astonishing changes in appearance. Thanks to the unibody aluminum shell manufacturing process, the overall assembly of MBA is quite solid, which also improves their durability compared to previous versions. In terms of size, the dimensions of the 13.3-inch MBA are now 33.5x22.7x0.3-1.7cm with a weight of 1.32 kg, while the 11.6-inch MBA measures 29.95x19.2x0.3-1.7cm and weighs 1.06 kg.

In terms of appearance, there's not much difference between the 13.3-inch MBA and its predecessor, except that it's slightly thinner. However, the more angular edges and the redesigned touchpad give the impression that it's smaller than before. Another change is the replacement of the touchpad with a large glass multi-touch pad. Some components have disappeared on the new MBA, including the ambient light sensor on the screen and the backlight on the keyboard. The former isn't a big issue as users might be better able to control the screen brightness they need, but the latter can be a bit troublesome. When using the MBA in low-light or dark environments, it can be difficult to identify which key is which (practice touch typing!). If the 11.6-inch version had to remove the keyboard backlight due to limited internal space, it would be understandable. However, even the 13.3-inch MBA lacks this feature, which is somewhat disappointing since the previous generation had it. Many components of the new 13.3-inch MBA are smaller versions, yet it still lacks the keyboard backlight, which is quite frustrating. Apart from the missing backlight and screen light sensor (as well as thickness, I/O ports, etc.), there aren't many differences between the new MBA and other MacBooks made with unibody aluminum shells. Simply put, they look like successful weight-loss paper-thin brothers, and the feel of the keyboard and touchpad is no different from others.

Hardware Specifications/Screen

Clearly, if you're looking for a gaming laptop, you can head back to work. These two paper-thin machines aren't designed for online use! For the 13.3-inch model, the base specifications include a 1.86GHz Core 2 Duo processor, an integrated NVIDIA GeForce 320M GPU, 2GB of RAM, and a 128GB SSD. It can be upgraded to a 2.13GHz CPU, 4GB of RAM, and a 256GB SSD. The 11.6-inch version features a 1.4GHz Core 2 Duo processor, the same GeForce 320M, 2GB of RAM, and a 64GB SSD. Upgrades can reach a 1.6GHz C2D processor, 4GB of RAM, and a 128GB SSD. Note that the SSD is not handled in the usual way like traditional hard drives but is closer to the flash storage used in iPhones/iPads, utilizing a mini-SATA interface. The main site tested two MBAs: one with a 1.4GHz CPU, 2GB of RAM, and a 128GB SSD (11.6-inch), and another with a 1.83GHz CPU, 2GB of RAM, and a 128GB SSD (13.3-inch).

I/O-wise, the 13.3-inch model has an SD card slot, and other aspects are similar, including two USB ports (on both sides to prevent conflicts between two USB devices), MagSafe, Mini DisplayPort, etc. Due to the lack of an Ethernet port and the unwillingness to spend money on a USB adapter, using WiFi for internet access is probably the best option. Important internal components, from the battery and memory (RAM) to the SSD, are not easily replaceable by users. The RAM is soldered onto the logic board, and the SSD has a special connection interface.

The screens have resolutions of 1440x900 for the 13.3-inch model (the same as the current 15-inch MacBook Pro), featuring a glossy display with LED backlighting. The 11.6-inch model has a resolution of 1366x768, also with a glossy display and LED backlighting. Using a resolution of 1366x768 on the 11.6-inch MBA makes the displayed content slightly smaller, but unless there are vision problems, it shouldn't be too hard to adapt. As for the screen itself, both models have good wide viewing angles and excellent color reproduction. Surprisingly, Apple seems to have used a new glossy coating on the new MBA, making it less shiny compared to the MBP when viewed side-by-side. The appearance feels closer to the old MBs/MBA, with a slightly purplish hue. Additionally, the Mini DisplayPort supports up to 2560x1600 output.

Performance/Battery Life

CPU performance testing results with Geekbench and Xbench were not particularly impressive, which wasn't unexpected, but it also can't be said that their performance was poor. The 1.4GHz version of the 11.6-inch MBA did seem a bit concerning. Although it operates smoothly with most software and daily usage, and Exposé works fine, it tends to lag involuntarily on 'content-rich' websites or when browsing large amounts of images. Additionally, playing YouTube HD videos often causes stuttering. On the other hand, the 13.3-inch MBA doesn't have these issues; it plays 1080p Flash videos smoothly and can handle the 1080p H.264 trailers on Apple's website without problems.

In fact, some data shows that the 13.3-inch MBA can closely follow the lower-end 13.3-inch MBP (2.4GHz Core 2 Duo CPU + GeForce 320M GPU + regular HDD). In gaming tests, Portal performed roughly between 30-60 fps on the 13.3-inch MBA, which isn't great compared to others, but it exceeds expectations. Also, soon after the game loads, the fans start spinning, though the noise isn't loud, it's noticeable compared to normal usage.

If users don't plan to use the MBA for HD video editing or the latest version of Quake, it should currently meet needs reasonably well. Sacrificing performance for extended usage time is something that some users can accept. So how about the battery life? Under normal usage (WiFi internet, watching Flash videos, screen backlight fully on), the 11.6-inch model lasts about six hours on a full charge. If used for more power-intensive tasks, it can last around five hours (actually a bit over four). The 13.3-inch model lasts about 7.5 hours under normal usage, and around six hours under heavy load (continuous video playback).

Conclusion:

Overall, the main site editor still considers these two new MBA models to be pretty good products. While they clearly fall short in gaming performance and execution efficiency, they offer solid construction and longer battery life in return. Additionally, the screen, SSD, and OSX are selling points. However, compared to similarly priced Windows laptops, they do cost significantly more. For the 11.6-inch model, it's currently difficult to use it as a primary machine. It can be used as a bedside toy, TV companion, or for short trips, but it struggles to displace mainstream laptops (such as MBPs) in daily personal use. Finally, whether or not one needs an MBA depends on brand loyalty. If sticking to the fruit logo is important, then there's no choice. But if not, there are plenty of good laptops available in the same class, such as the Lenovo ThinkPad Edge 11, Alienware M11x, Inspiron M101z, etc.
