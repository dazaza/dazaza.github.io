Google CEO says will reach a solution with Chinese government on google.cn - Nanjing SEO Bar

by maomaochaoren on 2010-03-11 08:47:24

Google's CEO said that a solution regarding google.cn would be reached with the Chinese government, but an official from China stated last Saturday that the Chinese government has not yet had direct contact with Google.

According to foreign media reports, Google's CEO Eric Schmidt announced on March 10th that Google is actively negotiating with the Chinese government and expects to reach a solution soon.

Schmidt refused to disclose more details or the negotiation timeline, merely stating that "there will be some progress soon." He indicated that these negotiations are between Google and the Chinese government, and do not involve American officials. However, a Chinese official stated last Saturday that there has been no direct contact between the Chinese government and Google.

On January 12th this year, David Drummond, Senior Vice President of Google, Corporate Development, and Chief Legal Officer, published an article on the official Google blog stating that due to numerous unreported attacks and surveillance endured by Google's operations in China over the years, as well as the government’s attempts to further restrict freedom of speech on the internet over the past few years, Google considered shutting down its operations in China and the website Google.cn.

The US State Department spokesperson then announced on January 15th that a diplomatic note would be sent to China regarding the Google incident. This spokesperson said, "It will express our concern about this event and request that China provide information explaining how this event occurred and how they plan to handle this issue."

However, shortly after, on January 18th, senior executives at Google changed their stance, indicating that business operations in China had returned to normal and they hoped to remain in China.

In response, on January 21st, Vice Foreign Minister of China, He Yafei, told reporters that issues encountered by foreign enterprises such as Google in China should be resolved through Chinese law, and the Chinese government is also willing to help resolve relevant problems. The "Google incident" should not be linked to the governments or relations between China and the United States; otherwise, it would be an over-interpretation.

In earlier years, Google launched the Chinese search engine Google.cn in 2006 under the condition of agreeing to review its search results. Since then, Google's operations in China have been fraught with difficulties, experiencing a series of setbacks including license issues, tax evasion allegations, disputes with Sogou, and allegations of pornography involvement. Last year, Kai-Fu Lee, President of Google Greater China, also announced his resignation.
