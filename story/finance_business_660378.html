Standards related to the optical radiation safety of LED lamps

by zhihao2033 on 2012-02-07 16:38:59

Abstract: This article introduces the main standards related to LED light radiation safety, including the guidelines of the International Commission on Non-Ionizing Radiation Protection (ICNIRP), the International Lighting Committee (CIE), the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC), the European Union (EU), and North American standards for the photobiological safety of laser products or lamps and lamp systems. It analyzes the differences and connections between these standards.

0 Introduction

With the development of lighting technology, especially the emergence of LED lighting sources, the issue of light radiation safety from light sources has gained increasing attention. Research into the effects of various spectra on the human body began in the 1930s. In the early 1980s, Sliney and Wolbarsht from the United States published a handbook on the safety of lasers and other light sources. Currently, many countries and regions around the world are continuously improving the photobiological safety requirements and light radiation safety standards for lamps and lamp systems. The aim of this article is to clarify the current major light radiation safety standards globally, making a simple comparison of their differences and analyzing their connections.

1 Guidelines Issued by the International Commission on Non-Ionizing Radiation Protection (ICNIRP)

One of the research scopes of ICNIRP is to determine the adverse effects of non-ionizing radiation on humans when exposed. Exposure guidelines are documents issued by the committee based on various scientific research results, establishing basic exposure times or exposure limits. These guidelines are generally published in journals such as "Health Physics." The currently effective ICNIRP light radiation-related guidelines are shown in Table 1.

Whether it's laser or non-coherent light, its light radiation safety foundation comes from the relevant guidelines of ICNIRP. These guidelines include specific parameters such as basic hazard-weighted functions, measurement parameters, and radiation time. Additionally, these guidelines are continuously updated with the accumulation of experimental results, better conforming to light radiation safety requirements. For the LED-related issues that many people are concerned about, ICNIRP suggests that safety assessments and corresponding tests for LEDs follow the guidelines for non-coherent light sources.

2 Laser Radiation Safety Standards

The IEC safety standard for laser products is IEC 60825-1 "Safety of Laser Products - Part 1: Equipment Classification, Requirements, and User's Guide." This standard is divided into three aspects: general rules, manufacturing requirements, and manufacturing guidelines. It proposes safe working levels for laser radiation safety evaluations for lasers with a spectral range of 180nm to 1mm wavelength and introduces a classification system based on the hazard level of lasers and laser products. Hazard limits in this standard originate from ICNIRP's light radiation-related guidelines.

In the measurement of LED radiation safety, IEC 60825 and IEC 62471 "Photobiological Safety of Lamps and Lamp Systems" can easily be confused. According to IEC 60825-1: 1993 + A1: 1997 + A2: 2001 "Safety of Laser Products - Part 1: Equipment Classification, Requirements, and User's Guide," the definition of a laser includes LED products, categorizing them as laser-type products for radiation safety classification. Since this standard tests and calculates energy for single-wavelength light, while the LED spectrum is actually a broad-band spectrum, it is not suitable to use the laser radiation safety classification method. Therefore, in the latest second edition of IEC 60825-1: 2007, it is explicitly stated that LEDs are not included in the IEC 60825-1 standard.

Due to the development of lamp radiation safety standards, LEDs, as a type of lamp, should use "Photobiological Safety of Lamps and Lamp Systems" to evaluate their light radiation safety. IEC issued a CTL resolution requiring that starting September 1, 2009, LED products officially adopt IEC 62471: 2006 and EN 62471: 2008 standards for safety assessment, with a one-year transition period. Thus, the validity of LED safety reports tested according to IEC 60825 ends on September 1, 2010. After this date, all LED product light radiation safety tests must entirely use IEC 62471: 2006 and EN 62471: 2008 standards.

3 Photobiological Safety Standards for Lamps and Lamp Systems

The International Commission on Illumination (CIE) released CIE S009/E: 2002 "Photobiological Safety of Lamps and Lamp Systems" in 2002. CIE rarely releases standards; most of its publications appear in forms like technical reports, demonstrating the importance CIE places on photobiological safety.

In 2006, IEC adopted CIE S009/E: 2002 and published the photobiological safety standard IEC 62471: 2006. The same year, China also adopted and published GB/T 20145-2006. In 2008, the EU published EN 62471: 2008, which considered some requirements of the EU Directive 2006/25/EC based on IEC 62471: 2006, making the standard limits stricter and testing requirements higher. In 2009, IEC published IEC/TR 62471-2, forming a vertical standard series with IEC 62471: 2006. Standard names are listed in Table 2.

3.1 CIE S009/E: 2002 Standard

CIE S009/E: 2002 applies to the evaluation and control of photobiological hazards of optical radiation in the wavelength range of 200 to 3000 nm for all types of lamps and lamp systems (including LED, incandescent, fluorescent, gas discharge, arc lamps, etc., but excluding lasers).

This standard primarily evaluates the degree of harm caused by light radiation on the skin and eyes through irradiance and radiance, classifying the risk level based on the severity of the hazard posed by lamps and lamp systems.

In the measurement of radiance values, the primary evaluation focuses on the harm to the retina caused by lamps and lamp systems. Due to the structure of the human eye resembling an imaging optical system, the wavelength range where light radiation reaches the retina is 300 to 1400 nm (other wavelengths are absorbed by the cornea, anterior chamber, lens, vitreous humor, etc.). Within this band, the blue light spectrum has the highest transmittance through various parts of the human eye, so blue light is particularly likely to reach the retina. Under the same spectral energy, blue light is more likely to cause retinal damage than other spectrums, commonly referred to as "blue light hazard."

Power-type lighting LEDs commonly used today emit light by mixing colors after exciting yellow phosphor with blue LED chips, especially high-color-temperature LEDs, which have more blue light spectral components, often exceeding the limit value of no-risk category during hazard level assessment.

Hazard limits related to the retina have a significant relationship with the area of the retina exposed to radiation. Since the cornea and lens focus visible light onto the retina, the measurement of the retina-exposed area must be associated with the angular diameter of the visible light source. When the exposure time exceeds 0.25 seconds, rapid eye movements enlarge the image formed on the retina, creating a larger angle known as the effective angular diameter. Considering different exposure durations' effective angular diameters when measuring hazard values under corresponding exposure times makes the measurement of radiance relatively complex.

Radiance measurement is simpler compared to radiance measurement because it simulates direct light reaching the skin and eye surface, unlike brightness measurement which simulates human eye imaging. Generally, cosine correction is performed using the small aperture method of an integrating sphere.

Corresponding irradiance or radiance values multiplied by the respective weighting functions yield effective hazard values, as shown in Table 3. These weighting functions originate from ICNIRP's guidelines for non-coherent light sources.

Thus, whether for lasers or non-coherent light, these guidelines serve as the scientific basis for assessing their hazards. In the CIE S009/E: 2002 standard, lamps and lamp systems are classified into four categories: no-risk, low-risk, moderate-risk, and high-risk.

3.2 EN 62471 Standard and EU Directive 2006/25/EC

EN 62471: 2008 was developed based on IEC 62471: 2006. The difference lies in its Chapter 4 Exposure Limits, which specifies that the exposure limits defined in IEC 62471: 2006 have been replaced by those specified in the EU Directive 2006/25/EC regarding artificial optical radiation sources in Europe. Therefore, all exposure limits adopted in EN 62471: 2008 are those required by the EU Directive, while the exposure limits of IEC 62471: 2006 are retained in Appendix ZB for reference.

A comparison of differences between IEC 62471: 2006 and EN 62471: 2008 is shown in Table 4.

The ultraviolet wavelength below 180 nm is generally considered vacuum ultraviolet, whose spectral energy is strongly absorbed by oxygen in the atmosphere, making it less harmful to the human body. The 180 to 200 nm wavelength represents the transition from vacuum ultraviolet to non-vacuum ultraviolet. The EN standard adopts a broader spectral range for specifying ultraviolet hazard values than IEC. Furthermore, the small-source blue light limit differs by 100 times from IEC because EN considers immobile eyes during ophthalmic procedures or surgeries, extending the irradiation time from 100 seconds under normal physiological movement to 10,000 seconds, thus differing by 100 times. Clearly, EN 62471: 2008 imposes stricter limit requirements than IEC 62471: 2006.

3.3 IEC/TR 62471-2: 2009

Although IEC 62471 provides a hazard classification system and test methods for all types of lamps, it does not guide manufacturers or users on how to safely use samples based on the hazard classification obtained from standard measurements. To address this, IEC published IEC/TR 62471-2: 2009 "Manufacturing Guidelines for Non-Laser Optical Radiation Safety," forming a vertical standard series with IEC 62471: 2006. Its purpose is to enable manufacturers or users to select lamps of different hazard levels based on different needs after determining the sample's hazard level according to the standard. For example, it guides the installation and use of lamps below a certain hazard level in specific workplaces. This technical report supplements the test conditions of IEC 62471 while clarifying how manufacturers assess hazard levels, stipulating the adoption conditions of hazard levels between light source manufacturers and lamp manufacturers, and requiring labels for lamp radiation safety.

An important clause in IEC/TR 62471-2: 2009 concerning LED lamps specifies that since LED light sources do not contain ultraviolet or infrared spectra, manufacturers can adopt the test conditions of this technical report when evaluating LED light sources. The obtained hazard level can be adopted by LED lamps using these light sources, thereby eliminating the need for repeated measurements.

In short, IEC/TR 62471-2: 2009 mainly serves as a vertical standard to IEC 62471, making the photobiological safety inspection of lamps and lamp systems a more complete measurement system. It enhances the operability of hazard level assessment within this standard system, facilitating testing institutions in conducting related work according to the standard system.

4 Recommended Implementation Procedures for Photobiological Safety in North America

As early as 1996, the Illuminating Engineering Society of North America (IESNA) and the American National Standards Institute (ANSI) took the lead in publishing the "Recommended Implementation Procedures for Photobiological Safety of Lamps and Lamp Systems" in the field of light radiation safety testing. This procedure includes general requirements, measurement techniques, lamp hazard level classification, and labeling, followed by subsequent publications of the second edition of this implementation procedure. The currently effective North American photobiological safety standard numbers and names are shown in Table 5.

This procedure applies to the evaluation of radiation safety for all lamps and lamp systems within the wavelength range of 200 to 3000 nm, excluding LEDs and lasers used in fiber optic communication systems. The limit values of this series of procedures originate from the critical value limits recommended by the American Government Industrial Hygiene Association and the relevant guidelines of the International Commission on Non-Ionizing Radiation Protection. It evaluates the hazard level of lamps and lamp systems from eight aspects: ultraviolet hazards, retinal thermal hazards, retinal blue light hazards, blue light small source hazards, lensless eye hazards, infrared radiation hazards, weak visual hazards, and skin thermal hazards; it also divides lamp hazard levels into no-risk, low-risk, moderate-risk, and high-risk categories; this procedure proposed label requirements earlier than the "Manufacturing Guidelines for Non-Laser Optical Radiation Safety." However, due to the low attention given to North American standards in China, this series of standards has long been neglected.

5 Conclusion

This article aims to clarify the current light radiation safety standards and the connections and differences between each standard, allowing users to better understand the current state of light radiation safety standards, facilitating manufacturers in selecting appropriate product standards, further understanding the limit values of photobiological safety standards, and enhancing people's attention to the light radiation safety of LED lamps.
