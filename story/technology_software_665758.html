The 44-year-old grandmother was awarded an honorary doctorate for cooking and sweeping for students

by anonymous on 2012-02-15 10:02:33

She never went to college, and she doesn't know what an "academician" is; in her entire life, she only learned how to write five words, but the University of Hong Kong awarded her with the title of "Honorary Academician"; she didn't accomplish any earth-shattering great deeds, she merely spent 44 years cooking and sweeping for students – at the award ceremony, this ordinary 82-year-old grandmother was called "someone who influences the lives of university students with her own life," and is "a treasure of the University of Hong Kong."

On September 22, 2009, when the University of Hong Kong awarded her the honorary academician title, this 82-year-old grandmother "looked extremely spirited."

She was scheduled to appear last. On that day, sharing the stage with her were awardees such as Cesar Ko, the former CEO of HSBC, M.K. Lee, the eldest son of Hong Kong billionaire Li Ka-shing, and veteran senior barrister Chor King-wai, who had received the Bronze Bauhinia Star.

The award ceremony used Chinese for her for the first time

Compared to these celebrities, the elder's resume appeared exceptionally thin - education: did not attend primary school, apart from her own name, she could not write other characters at the time; work experience: from the age of 29 to 73, she successively worked as an assistant chef and dormitory service staff at the University Hall of the University of Hong Kong.

This traditionally fully English ceremony broke precedent by using Chinese for her. The Student Affairs Director of the University of Hong Kong, Wilson Chow, first read the commendation written for this elderly woman in English, then again made a speech in Cantonese. It was only at this point that Yuan Su-mei, who had never received formal education, understood that the honor of being named an academician was to recognize her "unique contribution to higher education, influencing the lives of university students with her own life."

She obviously preferred another title - "Third Sister". Because her husband was the third son, this title has been called by the people of Hong Kong University for more than half a century.

Seventy-year-old Vice Chairman of Television Broadcasts Limited, Ngai-pang Leung, still remembers during exam periods "cramming books late at night," Third Sister would cook him a can of lotus seed chicken soup to boost his brainpower; Lawyer Xiangrong Chen, who graduated 15 years ago, recalled having a persistent fever before final exams, and Third Sister spent several hours brewing a bowl of herbal tea for him, which cured his illness.

Students often came specifically to the cafeteria to chat with her. Boys always confided their troubles to her, like not knowing how to please their girlfriends. Girls also sought out Third Sister, complaining about boys "only focusing on studying and not treating them well enough." Most of the time, Third Sister just patiently listened to their stories, saying some very simple truths like "cherish the person in front of you," or inviting them to drink a bottle of cola, "forgetting unhappy things," etc. Every graduation season, many students dressed in their academic robes would specially come to take photos with her as keepsakes.

Since installing a pacemaker in the 1970s, Third Sister could no longer continue working in the kitchen. This mother of five children then switched to being a cleaner. Boys always liked to throw parties in the dining hall, often celebrating until two or three in the morning, and she always waited until the party ended before going in alone to clean up beer, snacks, and stains from the floor. The hunched figure of the old lady cleaning the floor alone in the early morning dining hall made many students always "dare not forget."

She simply "poured her heart into dealing with people"

These various trivial "goodnesses" about Third Sister are still lurking in the memories of alumni after many years, but Third Sister herself couldn't clearly say where she was "good," In her view, "pouring her heart into dealing with people," life was actually so simple. While working in the dormitory, her own eldest son was studying astronomy in the United States, and she simply used a "mother's heart" to care for these children studying away from home.

Now she has already retired, but in fact, she has never really left the university campus.

In June 2009, Third Sister suddenly received a letter sent to her by the President of the University of Hong Kong, Lap-chee Tsui, inviting her to "accept the honorary degree of academician of the University of Hong Kong." She had no prior knowledge of this. Previously, the Alumni Association had repeatedly suggested the school award her an honorary academician title. Not until January 2009 did Vice-President Joseph Chow personally make the nomination. And before this, only "social elites" could possibly enter this nomination list.

"I believe there will be no controversy regarding Third Sister," said this nominator. As it turned out, Third Sister's nomination was "unanimously passed."

Three months later, Yuan Su-mei, who "didn't know what an academician was," went to attend the ceremony. Sitting on the stage facing more than 600 people, she tried hard to remember the route taken by those ahead of her, "when to wear the cap, how to bow," fearing "forgetting the entire procedure."

At the time, more than 20 "white-haired" alumni excitedly jumped up to applaud and cheer, forcing the ceremony organizer to have the staff walk over and ask these well-known alumni from political and business circles to "not get too excited, remain quiet."

"My life hasn't changed much," Third Sister said indifferently as usual. She still lived in a public housing complex built 12 years ago in North Point, folding the red-bordered black doctoral robe and felt hat carefully and storing them in a box.
