Three Wrong Choices in Home Kitchen Decoration

by longersing on 2009-09-11 18:25:43

Three wrong choices in home kitchen decoration: a showy but inefficient range hood, an improperly arranged operating area, and impractical pine countertops. These are common mistakes in decoration that we should carefully examine.

Popular search term: Kitchen decoration

1. Showy but Inefficient Range Hood

Ms. Li: I am a full-time housewife, and the kitchen was where I spent the most effort when decorating our new home. Our integrated kitchen is an imported brand, with a beautiful appearance and complete functions. The only regret is choosing an ultra-thin flat-panel range hood.

At the time, I liked its appearance, but now I find that its smoke extraction ability is too weak. After cooking a meal, the kitchen smells very strong, and it takes a long time for the smell to dissipate. It's troublesome to replace the range hood, and not replacing it doesn't solve the problem of heavy grease fumes, which troubles me greatly.

Designer: When the overall kitchens from imported brands first appeared on the market, their harmonious design of the range hood caught people's eyes. Indeed, the flat-panel design is simple and fashionable. Although expensive, it is still quite popular in the market. However, it is not suitable for Chinese-style cooking methods such as frying, stir-frying, steaming, and deep-frying because the smoke collector is too shallow or non-existent, causing serious overflow and diffusion of unextracted grease fumes. Over time, grease will adhere to the cabinet surface or walls, making kitchen hygiene another headache-inducing issue. To solve this problem, I suggest you switch to a deep-hood range hood. This type includes deep or deep-cabinet models, all equipped with deeper (larger volume) smoke collectors. When the fan runs at high speed, a certain negative pressure area forms above the smoke collector, accommodating the unextracted grease fumes, serving as a buffer, thus eliminating the phenomenon of large amounts of grease fumes overflowing and diffusing.

2. Improperly Arranged Operating Area

Ms. Wu: The distance between the sink in my kitchen and the side wall is less than 10 centimeters, right next to the stove, leaving almost no space for chopping boards or preparation. Cooking a dish requires washing vegetables to the right of the stove and cutting them to the left, back and forth, which is extremely inconvenient. Originally, the water pipes and gas pipes in the house were placed together against the wall. When designing the cabinets, the company simply installed the stove and sink near the pipes for convenience. I deeply regret not considering these details initially, which has led to the current awkward situation.

Designer: Many readers have also reported similar frustrations like Ms. Wu's. To fundamentally solve this problem, perhaps the pipes need to be rerouted, and the countertop replaced. Kitchen decoration must emphasize practicality. Not only should the operation area be reasonably arranged according to the workflow, but issues such as countertop height, cabinet height and thickness, and lighting brightness should also be adjusted based on the owner's specific needs. The opinion of the "main chef" in the household is very important. As much as possible, after comprehensive consideration, have in-depth communication with the cabinet designer. We designers use design to realize our ideas, but truly experiencing operational convenience requires more integration of the owner's personal experience and life experience.

3. Unrealistic Pine Countertops

Mr. Zhang: During decoration, to pursue my favorite rustic country style, the cabinets were designed as open without doors, using coarse hemp curtains for shielding. For the countertop, I abandoned ready-made countertops and chose rustic pine boards instead, having workers assemble them on-site and then brush waterproof paint on the surface. I thought the waterproofing would be fine, but since pine is solid wood, it inevitably contains moisture, and after dehydration, it gradually contracts, causing the cracks at the joints of the countertops to become larger and larger. Applying glue didn't help, and when there is standing water on the countertop, it seeps into the cabinet body. Now, I can only replace the countertop to solve the problem.

Designer: It seems Mr. Zhang was indeed too idealistic. Pine board countertops may look "cool," but since the kitchen countertop is the place that comes into contact with the most water, and wood is a material that absorbs water easily, cracking and deformation are inevitable. Nowadays, the trend of rural-style design is strong, so the materials for this style in the building materials market are becoming increasingly comprehensive. You can choose suitable materials to replace solid wood. In decoration, utility and aesthetics are always a pair of contradictions, but by selecting the right materials, this contradiction can be well balanced.
