SAT Reading Methods and Common Question Types

by thatseed123 on 2011-05-03 21:58:01

In response to the common difficulties faced by Chinese test-takers, a large amount of reading practice and methodology instruction is integrated into SAT classroom teaching. Simply put, here are some methods for reading:

1. When encountering an article for the first time, skim through it to find the core idea that the author aims to convey and develop an overall sense of the article's structure.

2. Read the questions after the article. If some questions seem difficult to answer on the spot, you can skip them temporarily and return to them after answering other questions.

3. For more complex questions, go back to the original text to find the initial description of the queried subject.

4. Try to describe what you believe to be the correct answer in your own words.

5. Finally, look at the five options under the question stem and select the correct one.

Of course, while reading the article, test-takers should also follow these basic principles:

☆ Pay attention to the brief introduction about the author and the article at the beginning. Since the SAT requires candidates to understand the author's way of thinking and writing style during the test, candidates should focus their attention on the provided information about the author and the article from the very start. By reading this content, they can grasp the basis of the article's structure and the author's purpose.

☆ Do not overly dwell on specific details in the article. The details in each article are merely to illustrate the author's thoughts and descriptions. Therefore, when reading the details in the article, candidates should use speed-reading methods. Many candidates often hope to precisely understand all the contents of the article within the short time frame of the exam, which is theoretically and practically unfeasible and unnecessary.

☆ Read the first third of the article more carefully, as important elements are often presented here. These "important elements" refer to the theme of the article. Generally, SAT reading articles introduce the theme within the first third of the text; usually, the author's writing intent and attitude towards the subject matter are also clearly revealed.

☆ Pay attention to the main idea of each paragraph and mentally describe the structural pattern of the article. Paragraphs are essentially the basic components of an article. Therefore, when reading SAT articles, candidates should focus on capturing the theme of each paragraph and sketching out the general framework of the article. Candidates can ask themselves questions like "What is the main point of this paragraph?" "How does the content of this paragraph connect with the preceding and succeeding parts?" "What role does this paragraph play in the article?" The better the understanding of these questions, the faster the candidate’s problem-solving speed will be, and the higher the accuracy of their answers.

☆ Summarize the content of the article promptly. After reading the entire article, it is best to take a little time to think about its structural pattern and overall layout, and abstractly summarize the central idea of the article in your own words.

Based on past SAT exam questions, the reading section generally includes the following types of questions:

(1) Theme-based questions. This type of question relates to the whole article, and the question stem may inquire about the main idea or basic purpose of the article. In the correct answer for this type of question, the option explicitly summarizes the main content of the article, while incorrect distractors often take the following forms:

- Irrelevant to the main idea of the article;

- Explicitly contrary to what the article says;

- Substituting the details of the article for the main theme;

- Exceeding the scope of the article, being too broad;

- Too extreme, exceeding the narrative scope of the article.

(2) Detail-based questions. This type of question often pertains to specific content within a certain part of the article. When answering these types of questions, candidates generally need to return to the original text about the issue to find the source of the correct answer. The distractors for detail-based questions often take the following forms:

- Confusing with narratives from other parts of the article;

- Using vocabulary similar to the original text but distorting its meaning;

- Contradicting the narration in the original text;

- Content stated is not expressed in the original text and cannot be logically inferred;

- Using extreme narratives.

(3) Inference-based questions. This type of question can examine both the thematic level and the detailed level of the article. It primarily requires candidates to infer, based on the information provided in the article, options that are not explicitly mentioned in the article but are logically correct.

(4) Vocabulary-based questions. Vocabulary questions in the reading section mainly assess the specific meaning of a word in the context of the article. What candidates need to do is deduce the exact meaning of the tested word from the context. It is worth noting that even if the word is familiar to the candidate, the final correct answer in the reading article is often not the commonly known meaning of the word, but rather a more obscure meaning. This situation is generally referred to as "familiar words with obscure meanings."

A thorough understanding of the various characteristics and specific question types of the SAT reading test lays a solid foundation for candidates to achieve high scores. As long as the above points are truly followed, candidates will no longer passively and ineffectively read SAT articles but will actively and positively engage with them. Through further detailed explanations in class and extensive article practice, candidates will surely conquer SAT reading, successfully achieve high scores, and realize their dream of pursuing advanced studies at top American universities.
