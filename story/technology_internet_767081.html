A Brief Talk on the Unspoken Rules of iOS App Pricing

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 17:53:19

This article's author, Marco Arment, is the founder of Instapaper.

In the latest episode of the podcast "Real World Price Dynamics with Lauren Smith," David Smith discusses with his wife Lauren the impact of iOS app pricing on consumers. Every iOS developer should listen to this conversation.

Lauren may just be one sample, but her experience is certainly not unique. You wouldn't believe how many people I've heard in the past few years express exactly the same opinion as Lauren:

Everyone outside the industry thinks that since I develop iOS apps, I work for Apple. Every iOS device user, professional (including my lawyer, accountant, and doctor), and family member thinks this way. Therefore, they can't understand why my app would cost money, let alone why most of the proceeds go into my pocket? Everyone believes that the value of an iOS app doesn't exceed $1, or even less than that. They're just programs on a phone. Almost everyone facing a paid download will turn to look for a free alternative. (And usually, there is one.) Many iOS device users have screens upon screens of apps installed, yet haven't purchased a single paid download. Users hate the current trend of "paid upgrades" (removing the old version and replacing it with a new, paid one). Apps that use in-app purchases don't hear nearly as much criticism.

I've spent a lot of time thinking about Overcast's monetization model (Note: Overcast is Marco Arment's newly developed podcast app for the iOS platform, not yet released). I'm definitely going to charge customers directly rather than through venture capital or advertising. But the specific form and content of the charges are still under consideration. One thing is certain: users have largely lost interest in paying for app downloads. If you have such an app in the store, you've probably already noticed the ominous signs.

This pre-paid model worked when there weren't many apps available, but today's market, filled with free high-quality apps, is a completely different world. Apps targeting niche markets that offer a clear advantage over their free counterparts can still find enough paying users, but the number of such apps is rapidly decreasing.

Figuring out Overcast's payment model is going to give me headaches for a while. Guess what the best iOS podcast app is besides the official Apple one? You might think Instacast, Pocket Casts, or Downcast. All these have great reputations, but they all lose to Stitcher. Stitcher is terrible, but it's the most popular app outside of Apple's official offering because it's free. Countless people have told me they use Stitcher just because it's free.

This is why Apple doesn't bother regulating paid upgrades: there's no market for it. The reality is that free apps get at least an order of magnitude more downloads than paid ones, and cleverly using in-app purchase features is the latest cash cow. This trend continues to strengthen over time.

The golden age of paid app downloads is over.
