Ten Sins of Express Delivery

Rapid development inevitably brings disorder, and such disorder has caused various strange phenomena in the express delivery industry. The ten experiences encountered by customers below are from media reports, reflecting relatively common phenomena in China's express delivery industry.

Compiled by Lei Shunli, Illustrations by Tiao Tiao

1. Delayed arrival

On February 13, 2010, Ms. Liu Jie from Shijiazhuang sent a box of red dates to her friend in Chongqing via YTO Express. However, half a month later, her friend still hadn't received the red dates. Thus, Ms. Liu called the express company to inquire about the reason. A week after the inquiry, her friend in Chongqing received the red dates, but they were already rotten beyond recognition.

2. Lost packages

On December 22, 2010, Mr. Mei, the head of Shen Dong Branch of Shenzhen National Travel Service, entrusted Sinotrans Global Express to deliver 120 invitations to their destination. However, these 120 invitations were discarded under a bridge by a courier from Sinotrans Global. Mr. Mei claimed compensation of 101,000 yuan from the express company, but the express company was only willing to compensate 12,000 yuan.

3. Improper delivery

On September 15, 2010, Miss Liu from Shanghai purchased five sweaters online. After the courier delivered the package to Miss Liu's workplace, as she wasn't there, the express company asked the gatekeeper to sign on her behalf. Subsequently, when Miss Liu opened the package for inspection, she found a small defect on the back of one of the clothes. Therefore, Miss Liu contacted the store owner, hoping to exchange it. However, the store owner stated that they required buyers to inspect the goods upon receipt. Since Miss Liu had signed for the package, it meant she acknowledged the product, making further exchanges inconvenient.

4. Refusal to inspect goods

In early November 2010, Mr. Xu from Beijing purchased a netbook online. Several days later, when the courier delivered the package to Mr. Xu's company, he was out on business and couldn't personally sign for it, so he asked his colleague to sign on his behalf. When his colleague requested to inspect the goods, the courier refused to unpack it, stating that it could only be opened after signing. When Mr. Xu returned to the office, he opened the unopened package. Upon flipping open the netbook's screen, he discovered broken traces across the entire screen.

5. Damaged items

On October 19, 2010, Ms. Chen from Hainan shipped a new lamp she bought from Beijing to Hainan via an express company. She specifically noted that the lamp was fragile. Before shipping, the express company charged her additional packaging fees citing the fragility of the lamp. However, when Ms. Chen received the goods, she found no extra packaging, and none of the lamps she bought for over 10,000 yuan were intact.

6. Failure to honor insurance

On December 15, 2010, Mr. Zhang from Nanjing received a damaged laptop computer he ordered online. As Mr. Zhang had purchased insurance before buying the goods, he immediately called the courier. The courier said he would consult with the company on how to handle the situation. In the afternoon, the express company called to inquire about the situation and promised to contact the sender to deal with this issue. The next day, Mr. Zhang called customer service to learn about the compensation situation. The operator said they needed authorization from the sender to handle the compensation issue. However, after Mr. Zhang obtained the authorization certificate, a courier staff member surnamed Liu informed him that there would be no compensation.

7. Difficulty in lodging complaints

At the beginning of September 2010, Ms. Wang from Beijing, after failing to communicate with the courier due to damaged packages, chose to call the express company's customer complaint hotline. However, Ms. Wang was unable to get through to the hotline repeatedly. Consequently, Ms. Wang decided to sue the express company.

8. Poor attitude

On January 4, 2011, Ms. Wang from Guangzhou ordered a desk lamp online. When the courier delivered the goods to the entrance of Ms. Wang's workplace, since she happened not to be in the office, the courier wanted to take the lamp away. After repeated requests from Ms. Wang's colleagues, the courier reluctantly agreed to leave the goods for her colleague to sign on her behalf. Afterwards, Ms. Wang learned from her colleagues that during the signing process, the courier used foul language. When the courier wanted to take the goods away, instead of using his hands, he kicked the package down the stairs from the seventh floor. Some male colleagues, angered by the courier's attitude, wanted to confront him. However, the courier, after looking at Ms. Wang's colleagues and then kicking the desk lamp several times, dropped the package and quickly ran downstairs.

9. Brutal unloading

On August 18, 2010, a netizen named "Meng Niu Baobao" posted a 3-minute-22-second video on Youku titled "Real Recording of How EMS Unloads Goods." The footage showed a worker, without any protective measures, casually throwing parcels from a truck marked with "Postal" onto the ground, producing frequent thuds as the parcels hit the ground. No one intervened during the entire process, and the parcels were haphazardly thrown everywhere. When some parcels blocked the way, the worker even kicked them away.

10. Violent sorting

On December 26, 2010, a video titled "Shentong Express Violently Sorting Items" showed more than 20 workers violently throwing and smashing express items. These items were chaotically smashed on the ground, shaking cars so much that their alarms went off. After this video spread wildly online, the brutal actions of the couriers sparked public outrage.

Even though we hate it, we can't live without it

No matter how irregular or criticized the express delivery is, our lives have undoubtedly been changed by it, and we can no longer live without it. We can only hope that it gets better.

South Weekly Journalist - Shi Yanyu

My name is Chen Qiong, and I want to share an experience that might be similar to what a friend around you has gone through.

I didn't touch a computer until I was in college, didn't use the internet until I started working, and didn't think about buying a house until I got married. I lived a passive life, dealing with things as they came.

Being passive isn't good; getting used to being pushed along makes you feel lost if you're not prepared. So now, thinking back, if it weren't for express delivery, how would I have survived the most important month of my life?

In April 2007, after my child was born, I realized I wasn't prepared. Before giving birth to my son, I bought things like bottles casually from the supermarket. But once my son was home, I was a bit dumbfounded. My maternity nurse complained about me, saying how could I buy such cheap stuff when I could afford better!

Following the list provided by the maternity nurse, I shamefully searched online and ended up purchasing far more than what was listed. For instance, regarding baby bottles, I bought brands like Pigeon from Japan, New Born from the UK, Dr. Brown from the US, trying wide-mouth, narrow-mouth, glass, and plastic versions. Within a month, my son accumulated over 20 bottles.

Sitting in confinement was boring, aside from eating, sleeping, and nursing my child, I spent most of my time lying in bed with my laptop browsing online. Every few days, I would place orders online for baby products, and when the doorbell rang, the courier would arrive.

On the busiest day, I received five couriers, with one courier still at the door while another was already waiting outside. On the day my child turned a month old, I summarized my shopping spree, having spent over 10,000 yuan, and within a month, I quickly became an expert in online baby product purchases. Over three years, I accumulated three diamonds as a Taobao buyer, those shiny yellow diamonds, all earned with real money.

In 2007, when I started online shopping, Taobao's annual transaction volume exceeded 40 billion yuan, with eighty percent being physical goods requiring logistics companies for delivery. Also in 2007, China's express industry's business revenue reached 38 billion yuan, growing at over 25%. From the first mention of "modern logistics industry" in the "Eleventh Five-Year Plan" in 2006, China's express business welcomed its golden five years. According to a report by The Beijing News, currently, forty percent of the express industry's business comes from online shopping.

Our family's spiritual sustenance also relies on express delivery; those books are all bought in bulk from Dangdang and Joyo. Except for toilet paper and sanitary napkins, almost everything we eat, wear, and use is delivered by couriers.

The more reclusive one becomes, the less inclined one is to go out. One of my most amazing experiences should be renovating our new house in the second half of 2007, where items like drains, hinges, and even solar water heaters were all bought online. Thanks to the omnipotent Taobao, after selecting the model, the courier delivers it to your door, and you arrange for the manufacturer to install it. All done without leaving the house.

Previously, I was a shopping enthusiast, feeling great even walking all day in high heels. Now, I can't walk for more than two hours without feeling sore. However, online shopping solves that problem. When tired from the computer, I can lie down or sit as I please, and buy whenever I want.

A few days ago, when the temperature dropped, I casually strolled through the mall and found that all the North Face women's down jackets were sold out. Back home, I quickly went online, and thanks to the express delivery, I managed to stay warm in winter with cotton boots from Harbin and long down coats from the US.

Over the years, the number of couriers I've encountered could line up for a hundred meters. Generally, I haven't met many friendly couriers, possibly because they're always tired. Sometimes, when I receive a courier's call outside, they rudely ask, "Why aren't you at home?" as if everyone who shops online should wait at home for deliveries.

With numerous deliveries, I don't always check the contents of the boxes. Once, I bought a protein powder bottle, and when I opened it later, I found it shattered into pieces. The seller agreed to send me a new one, but she also lamented that due to the rough handling by the courier, she lost a week's profit.

Often, the responsibility for damaged goods due to rough handling is transferred to online sellers, as even if complaints are made, there may not be a resolution. According to the latest data published by the State Post Bureau, last November, over 95% of consumer complaints related to express issues were handled through the "12305" postal industry consumer complaint hotline and the State Post Bureau website.

A week ago, a package from Shanxi had been sent for seven or eight days. Checking the tracking number online, I found it had been sitting there for two or three days without delivery. I called the express company to urge them, and they replied: "The courier said no one answered your phone!" Honestly, I never received a call. After verification, the courier admitted he was too busy to deliver.

This situation actually began in the express industry last November, known as "bursting warehouses." Due to the surge in business volume, the express industry entered the peak season earlier. Unfortunately, they faced severe manpower shortages, coupled with rising oil prices and diesel shortages. Weather-related traffic blockages in places like Guizhou and Hunan caused many areas' express services to stop. Goods couldn't be delivered, yet orders continued to increase, worsening the "bursting warehouse" phenomenon.

On October 1, 2009, the revised Postal Law was officially implemented. In the following year, 185,000 people nationwide participated in the express delivery personnel professional appraisal, leading to a major reshuffle in the express industry. The express delivery personnel frequently visiting my area, despite wearing uniforms from different companies, handed me a business card from another express company. After some time, he called to inform me he had switched to another express company. Professional appraisals indeed regulated some behaviors of express personnel, and now the issue of rude tones has decreased somewhat. Of course, when signing for goods takes slightly longer, couriers remain impatient, with "you're delaying my earning money" clearly written on their faces.

Online jokes about couriers earning over ten thousand yuan per month circulate endlessly. I have adapted to the changes brought by the courier era and sincerely hope they earn more through hard work, compete fairly, and enjoy the convenience brought by the logistics era.

Online Shopping Boosts Express Delivery

What do 12 million online stores and 140 million online shoppers mean? It means that China's express delivery industry has gained unprecedented momentum. In 2010, China's express delivery industry achieved an annual output value of 60 billion yuan, maintaining an annual growth rate of over 20%, according to Guangzhou VIPshop Information Technology Co., Ltd.

Investigative statistics show that one-third of the national express delivery industry's business volume is driven by online shopping, with online shopping packages exceeding 500 million in 2008 alone. Taobao's public data shows that in 2008, the actual daily logistics distribution support volume was approximately 2 million orders, rising to 3 million orders per day by June 2009. Online shopping delivery has become the largest pie in the express delivery industry.
