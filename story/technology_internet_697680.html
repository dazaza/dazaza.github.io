After the fierce competition, the curtain fabric industry may enter an era of high threshold.

by bery201209 on 2012-03-08 16:55:28

Fierce Combat and Reorganization

The reorganization process, for individual enterprises, represents a journey of death and rebirth, but it isn't necessarily a bad thing. In fact, the outcome of corporate battles in the curtain fabric [http://www.beryhome.com/] industry heralds the start of a maturation process, leading to a brighter future. Brand expert Zhao Long predicts that [this process will likely last 3 to 5 years].

[Large industries, small enterprises] is a characteristic of China's curtain industry compared to developed countries. China's total output value in curtains accounts for 70% of the world's total, yet in stark contrast, the curtain industry lacks any industry giants. Guangdong Province currently has more than 9,000 large and small curtain manufacturing companies, but there are zero enterprises that hold even 1% of the national market share.

The first word in industry maturity is [merger]. In the coming few years, [mergers] and [eliminations] will become the key words in the curtain industry. According to the Pareto Principle, in any group of things, the most important only account for a small part, about 20%, while the remaining 80%, although in the majority, are secondary. [It's about letting these 20% large enterprises acquire or eliminate the 80% small and medium-sized enterprises.] Zhao Long said.

Zhou Benquan's perspective slightly differs from Zhao Long's. [One-third of the enterprises will be eliminated, one-third will continue to do OEM (Original Equipment Manufacturer) work, and one-third will develop into the backbone of the industry through industrialization and brand operations.] Zhou Benquan still holds his [three-stream] view and predicts that this elimination competition will at least last until 2015, with surviving enterprises sharing the trillion-yuan value "cake" of the curtain industry.

In this elimination competition, some enterprises are like crocodiles, as one of the earliest and most primitive reptiles still alive today, they still possess fierce and tenacious vitality. Some enterprises are like pandas, although gentle and docile, they face significant survival challenges and need artificial insemination to propagate their offspring. The market economy naturally possesses jungle characteristics, and after the battle, the surviving enterprises will be like jungle overlords, becoming the dominant force in the curtain industry.

Compared to [crocodile] enterprises, [panda] enterprises always have weaker and more tender traits, easily evoking sympathy. But this may not be the whole truth. Survival of the fittest should be acknowledged. What needs special vigilance and thought during this phase is how to become a crocodile? How to learn from the crocodile? Instead of thinking about how to save the panda.

If the government takes on unlimited responsibility for panda enterprises, not only will the burden become heavier, but situations may arise where good and bad enterprises compete for funds, raw materials, and energy. [The curtain manufacturing industry needs more cultivation, but development-oriented cultivation is necessary; we cannot adopt a one-size-fits-all approach or provide uniform support.] Zhou Benquan said.

[Weaker curtain enterprises will shed tears as they exit the curtain industry or be acquired by other enterprises.] In the curtain industry, some people live and die for curtains, which is very touching. Zhao Long said, [We don’t want to see you leave, but I believe that the growth of enterprises and industries must go through such a process.]

Entering the High-Threshold Era

After a round of brutal combat and reorganization, experts predict that the low-threshold era is about to end, and the curtain industry will enter a high-threshold era. Enterprises will transition from extensive to intensive management, from excessive profits to slim profits. It’s like running a marathon; everyone is roughly the same at the beginning, but the athletes truly start to pull away in the later stages.

For 30 years, the curtain industry has been called a [low-threshold industry] and a [low-quality industry]. Has the industry made no progress? Industry insiders think the answer is negative; it just shows that the industry hasn't matured yet, and enterprise owners lack systematic thinking abilities. Perhaps what all curtain business owners dislike hearing most is that the entire curtain industry is currently still at the [low end of manufacturing]. However, a newspaper in Guangzhou listed the apparel industry, footwear industry, curtain industry, etc., as extensive industries, pointing out that low-end manufacturing is still in the doldrums.

From the start of entrepreneurship, the curtain industry had [viruses in its genes], but no [original sin]. Many curtain enterprises started with low beginnings, crude production, and evolved in forms such as father-son teams, husband-wife pairs, and brotherhoods. They began in small workshops with just a plane and a saw. Everyone worked hard together, developing and expanding the business.

But without clear planning in the early stages, they felt their way forward. Lagging management models and concepts, outdated marketing methods, severe homogenization, and other issues planted hidden dangers for curtain enterprises.

The relationship between curtain factories and dealers is loose, and a large part of the reason why they fail to form a harmonious supply chain stems from the aforementioned reasons. A company owner gave a regional manager a strict task —— to open three stores in the responsible area. The regional manager, lacking market management capabilities, directly passed down the target to the dealer, putting the cost of opening stores on the dealer. The dealer felt indignant: [Why should I open three stores for you?]

Currently, the entire industry is undergoing drastic changes. If enterprise owners continue to be stubborn, lack planning, and allow personalities to dominate corporate culture, the enterprise may stray off course.

The essence of the market is reality, and only those who grasp reality have a chance to seize the future! In the high-threshold era, a 3 to 5-year plan is crucial for enterprise development.

[Unfortunately, many Chinese enterprises, especially private ones, haven't realized this. Even if they do, they don't know whether to position 2012 as a year of reform, transformation, beginning, or strategic planning.] Zhou Benquan said.

A lack of entrepreneurs is also the reason why the curtain industry is called a [low-threshold industry] and a [low-quality industry]. Traditional merchants, bosses, and entrepreneurs represent three different levels of business owners. Merchants generally have short-sighted views, focusing only on the money from each transaction and product quality; bosses begin to consider and ponder the operating conditions of their enterprises; [Entrepreneurs differ from them in having many personal views and thoughts about the industry. They think about how to provide excellent products to society, how to lead the industry, establish industry standards, and consider internal issues such as improving the welfare and cultural literacy of company employees.] Zhao Long believes that cultivating an entrepreneur team is crucial.

In the high-threshold era, talent will be at the core of enterprises, and the direction of enterprise development will no longer be decided solely by the boss.

Currently, the talent view in the curtain [http://www.beryhome.com/] industry is distorted. On the one hand, enterprise owners desperately seek talent, while on the other hand, family-style production and management models keep talent out, making it difficult for talent to integrate into the company's decision-making level. The curtain industry is akin to a space for dwarfs; only those 1.6 meters tall can enter, while talents are 1.8 meters tall, bumping their heads every day as they enter and exit, bending over just to survive. Enterprise owners have high expectations for talent, hoping they produce golden eggs. Owners scrutinize them daily with a critical eye, making life tough for talent, who ultimately leaves after leaving behind a single solution.
