1. Aluminum pigments are used in coatings primarily for their appearance and ability to provide certain functional special metal effects. The pigments are often flake-shaped, with a particle diameter much larger than that of general pigments.

2. In most cases, the properties of metal pigments are determined by the production process. The production process of aluminum pigments:

1. Aluminum ingots are melted and sprayed to obtain irregular spray powder, which is then ground into flake particles under solvent and lubricant conditions through ball milling. The pigment suspension obtained after grinding is sieved, filtered by a press filter, and mixed to obtain a product with a solid content of 65% -70%, and a solvent content of 35%-30%.

2. Roll the aluminum ingot into aluminum foil, crush it, ball mill it, sieve it, press filter it, mix it, and get the product. The final product can be directly used by adding specific solvents or adding special additives in the filter cake. (The grinding parameters and the type of lubricant added are determined by the final application.)

3. Wetting performance: A lubricant must be added during processing to assist various production processes. This plays a unique role in the wetting performance of metal pigments. There is an obvious difference between floating and non-floating pigments (wetting performance) (1) Floating pigments: Due to its high surface tension, floating pigments cannot be wetted by the base material, so it is coated on the surface of the wet paint film. This effect is achieved by using stearic acid as a lubricant. In a coating system containing strong polar solvents or base materials, floating pigments may become unstable, be wetted by the system, and be converted into non-floating pigments. Therefore, this possibility must be considered when designing the coating formula. (2) Non-floating pigments can be prepared by adding strongly polar substances or drying agents to floating pigments, or by directly adding special lubricants (such as oleic acid) during the grinding process. Non-floating pigments can be completely wetted, so they can be evenly distributed throughout the paint film. Wetting performance of floating pigments: Characteristics: 1. Extremely high reflectivity (visible light infrared rays (heat) ultraviolet rays) applied to ordinary industrial coatings, roof coatings, such as tank coatings. 2. Shielding protection long diffusion path. Applied to anti-corrosion coatings. 3. Chrome plating effect. Applied to decorative coatings. 4. Floating property related to the polarity, acidity, and water content of solvents and base materials Disadvantages: The coating can be wiped off, and recoating is difficult.

Characteristics and applications of non-floating aluminum pigments: Characteristics: 1. Can be oriented in the coating. Applied to primer. 2. Completely resistant to wiping. Applied to tinplate coatings. 3. Can be topped with topcoat. Applied to coil material coatings 4. Can be used with colored pigments, with multi-color effects in paints and inks. Applied to marbling paints, three-dimensional coatings, automotive coatings, "flash" effect coatings, high-temperature glitter powder.

Particle shape, particle size, and particle size distribution The aluminum used in production has ductility, so during ball milling, the irregularly atomized powder can form flake particles, with a shape factor (thickness/diameter ratio) reaching 1:50 to 1:500.

Particle shapes can be irregular (pearl flower type) or perfectly round (silver dollar type).

The particle size distribution of metallic pigments has a very important impact on the optical characteristics when in use. Therefore, the particle size distribution must be measured during quality control.

Optical properties (aluminum pigments)

Aluminum pigments produce a special effect through the specular reflection and diffuse reflection of light on the pigment surface. The following factors directly determine the metallic effect of aluminum pigments:

1. Particle size distribution.

2. Particle shape (pearl flower type, silver dollar type).

3. Shape factor (thickness/diameter ratio).

4. Surface smoothness (substrate).

5. Parallel orientation degree of pigments relative to the substrate surface.

6. Wetting properties (floating/non-floating).

Sometimes it's hard to distinguish and measure metallic effects with the naked eye. Metallic effects mainly depend on the following factors: Hue Luminance/Whiteness Brightness (flicker and metallic luster) Tinting strength (covering power) Color saturation Gloss Angular color-changing flicker.

Hue: Aluminum pigments themselves have no color, but through the reflection of light, they create bright or metallic colors that differ from white and black pigments.

Whiteness, brightness, color saturation, angular color-changing, covering power, and gloss: The reflection and scattering of light on the surface of metal pigments give car paints a metallic shimmer effect, with these metal pigments being directionally arranged in the paint film. The visual effect depends on the ratio of reflected light to scattered light. The proportion of reflected light increases as the area of the pigment increases, and the proportion of scattered light increases as the light scatters at the edges of the pigments.

The coarser the pigment, the more circular the shape, the higher the proportion of reflected light, thus making it brighter, whiter, with higher color intensity, and greater angular color-changing flicker (flicker refers to changes in whiteness when observed from different angles). For example, observing the vertical and horizontal surfaces of a car or observing a car from different angles allows you to feel the angular color-changing flicker.

The finer the pigment particles, the less regular the particle structure, the higher the proportion of scattered light, appearing more uniform, with stronger light and dark changes, higher covering power, and better gloss. The higher the gloss, the clearer the image on the paint film surface, such as houses, clouds in the sky, figures, and trees. If the whiteness, brightness, angular color-changing flicker effect, gloss, and covering power are all excellent, there is no doubt that this silver slurry will be ideal; however, unfortunately, the relationship between these characteristics and particle size changes is opposite. The successful development of narrowly distributed round aluminum pigments (silver dollar type) largely meets the above performance requirements, except that the covering power still needs further research to improve. To some extent, an aluminum pigment can be produced that has good flicker, angular color-changing, brightness, color saturation, and high gloss. (Such as our company's electroplated silver and imitation electroplated silver series)

Pigment orientation: The quality characteristics introduced earlier, such as whiteness, brightness, color saturation, covering power, angular color-changing flicker, and gloss, are not only related to particle size and particle size distribution but also to the orientation of flake aluminum pigments in the paint film. When the flake pigments are parallel-oriented to the paint film surface, the effect is optimal. Poor parallel orientation can cause "haze" or diffuse reflection phenomena. The orientation of flake pigments is related to the formulation and construction conditions. The evaporation of solvents causes the contraction of the wet paint film, ultimately pressing the aluminum pigments into a horizontal orientation position. The higher the solvent content in the paint, the stronger this effect. This explains the phenomenon of flake pigment orientation. Therefore, the optical properties of low-solid-content paints are better than those of high-solid-content paints. The evaporation of solvents can cause what is known as Nard vortex, hindering the parallel orientation of aluminum pigments. (Causing haze phenomena) At this point, you can use some additives. They can fix the flake pigments. You can also use resins to promote the rapid evaporation of solvents (such as CAB cellulose acetate butyrate), but their functions should be tested before application.

Very thin metal pigments are extremely sensitive to high shear forces. Mechanical shear can cause deformation of the particles or the formation of many fine particles, thereby reducing optical properties. In the worst case, due to the many unprotected metal surfaces generated, chemical reactions occur, making the metal pigments chemically active and capable of reacting chemically with the paint system. Therefore, the mechanical properties of metal pigments must be taken seriously, especially during the dispersion process, and the same issue should be considered for metal paints during pumping or circulation lines.

Dispersion of metal pigments: To achieve satisfactory special effects, metal pigments must be fully dispersed in the paint system. Due to mechanical limitations, high-shear stirring methods should be avoided when dispersing metal pigments. Metal pigments should first be dispersed in a solvent, which will help with pigment dispersion. Generally, the ratio of aluminum silver paste to solvent is 1:1 to 1:2. The solvent and pigments should be slowly stirred and dispersed, noting that the solvent should not contain water. Halogenated hydrocarbons should not be used because chlorinated solvents release HCl under appropriate conditions, which can chemically react with fine aluminum pigments. After pigment dispersion, diluted resin should be slowly added to the pigments while continuously stirring. However, in practice, the reverse is often done—after pre-mixing the metal pigments, they are slowly added to the diluted resin. This operation must be slow to avoid pigment precipitation.

Preventing decomposition: In the automotive industry, most paints need to pass through pump circulation systems before spraying. During this process, high shear forces are generated. Therefore, a so-called "non-decomposable aluminum pigment" has been developed to adapt to circulation systems with higher shear forces. The thicker the flake aluminum pigments, the more mechanically stable they are. However, this geometric change will have some impact on their orientation and optical properties.

Chemical properties: The chemical characteristics of aluminum pigments greatly limit the selection of resins and solvents in the paint system.
