Behavioral science suggests that motivation can stimulate a person's drive, creating an inner desire for success and encouraging continuous efforts towards desired goals. However, managers should be aware of the knowledge required to operate a successful car beauty shop. What aspects of employee behavior should be motivated – reducing costs, accelerating project progress, or enhancing customer satisfaction? Once the goal is determined, employees can be motivated as follows:

1. Provide employees with challenging work. Routine tasks are the most demotivating. To see employees perform at their best, work must be challenging. Managers should guide employees in growing through their work and provide opportunities to learn new skills.

2. Ensure employees have the necessary tools to do their best work. Companies investing in leadership technologies generally boost morale. Employees take pride in having access to the latest tools in their industry. If they can proudly boast about their work, this pride serves as a powerful motivator.

3. During the implementation of projects and tasks, managers should provide employees with information to help them excel. This includes the company’s overall objectives, the work required from specialized departments, and specific issues employees need to focus on.

4. Employees doing practical work are experts in their field. Therefore, managers must listen to their opinions and invite them to participate in decisions related to their work. Honest communication not only makes employees feel like part of the operation but also helps them understand business strategies. If open communication and two-way information sharing become essential parts of the operational process, the motivational effect becomes more apparent.

5. Establish channels that facilitate communication across all levels. Employees can use these channels to ask questions, express concerns, or receive answers. Companies encourage open dialogue through methods such as employee hotlines, suggestion boxes, group discussions, and manager-led Q&A sessions.

6. Managers should maintain regular contact with employees. Scholar Graman believes: "By chatting with you, I invest my most valuable asset: time. This highlights the importance of our relationship and shows that I care about your work."

7. Understand the actual difficulties and personal needs of employees and try to meet them. This greatly boosts employee enthusiasm. For example, arranging childcare within the company or adopting flexible working hours.

8. Promote employees based on job performance. Promoting by seniority does not encourage employees to strive for excellence and instead fosters an attitude of waiting passively.

9. Develop a comprehensive set of standards for promoting employees internally. Employees have many aspirations and capabilities they wish to achieve; how many opportunities does the company provide to realize these goals? Employees will assess the company's investment in them based on these provided opportunities.

10. Emphasize the company's willingness to employ employees long-term. Employees should understand that job security ultimately depends on themselves, but the company strives to ensure long-term employment.

11. Employee salaries must be competitive. Pay should correspond to an employee's value. When employees feel their compensation is fair, they won't solely focus on their paycheck, benefiting the company significantly.

12. Seek their help. For leaders, seeking help from other employees may be the most effective way to make them recognize their own abilities and worth. Why is this? The key lies in making us vulnerable: it indicates we have weaknesses or lack necessary skills. Seeking help from employees not only shows respect for their professional skills but also demonstrates absolute trust from the leader.

13. Ask for their opinions. Similarly, ensure the content is unrelated to their job responsibilities. For instance, avoid typical questions like "What ideas do you have to improve efficiency?" Instead, find ways to utilize their skills or insights through other means.

14. Grant them informal leadership roles. For leaders, granting employees informal leadership over temporary teams brings significant benefits. It signifies trust in their skills and judgment. More important tasks and higher implicit praise greatly enhance their self-esteem.

15. Collaborate on tasks together. For bosses, there is a natural inequality with employees. Thus, an effective way to uncover employee value (especially unique value to the company) is through joint collaboration on tasks.

Of course, the projects chosen by leaders don’t necessarily need to be outside of work. The key is that participation must be equal, without distinctions between boss and employee. After all, inequality leads to disengagement, while equality means overall improvement.

16. Learn from price line companies. After completing outstanding work, leaders should congratulate the relevant employees and let them choose their own "prize." Leaders might say, "You've done an excellent job. What can I do to show the company's appreciation?"

Leaders may be surprised at how simple the rewards chosen by employees can be.

17. A community-like atmosphere within the company indicates that it has made every effort to establish a structure where everyone contributes. Backstabbing, office politics, and low morale can make even the most ambitious individuals lethargic.

In short, motivating employees boils down to respecting them. What employees need most is precisely this respect.
