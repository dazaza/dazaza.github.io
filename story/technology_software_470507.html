Mobile Internet Interview: Qin Dai, the "Big Head King" of Mobile Phones, Speaks on SoLoMoCo

by tuoqing1188 on 2011-08-04 09:53:15

Introduction: With the arrival of the 3G era, mobile internet has gained prominence and become a trailblazer in this technological age, riding on the development of mobile communications and the flourishing of the internet. Against this backdrop, Sina Technology has launched a series of interviews on mobile internet to discuss with industry practitioners the development trends, challenges, and issues facing the mobile internet sector.

Video: Qin Dai, CEO of Mobile Bighead, discusses SoLoMoCo. Media Source: Sina Technology.

Facing what I perceive as a falling void,

Like a madman losing confidence before a Buddha statue;

Book titles and famous names pass like fallen leaves,

Only upon seeing you does it dawn on me the essence of fundamentals.

-- Excerpt from Yuan Kejia's "Mother"

Among current mobile internet entrepreneurs, there are roughly three categories. One category transitioned from the internet industry into mobile internet, a natural extension of traditional internet. Another category chose mobile internet directly, foreseeing its potential even when the industry prospects were unclear, quietly striving until the dawn of the mobile internet era, or perishing just before that dawn. The third category transitioned from the telecommunications industry into mobile internet, shifting their focus after the "prosperity" of mobile communication and value-added services faded. Wang Qindai, CEO of Mobile Bighead, is an example of the third type of entrepreneur, humorously referring to himself as a "veteran."

Naturally, his comprehensive experience and ongoing involvement in the development of telecommunications, internet, and mobile internet is the reason I invited him for this inaugural interview in our mobile internet series. I believe that only through experience can one truly understand, gain insights, and have faith. Only through experience can one know the joys and pains of this industry.

This is already the third "face-to-face" interview with Mr. Wang Qindai. In this interview, regarding the judgment of entering and exiting industries, he emphasizes that being "interested" is the most important factor. He hopes that the industries he engages in can change people's lives, which is actually the perfect realm we all strive for in our work.

Regarding the theme of this issue, SoLoMo was first proposed in February this year by John Doerr, a renowned venture capital partner in the United States. It was further discussed at the Mobile Internet Conference in April by Charles Chao, CEO of Sina, and other practitioners in the mobile internet sector. Over the past few months, SoLoMo has become the most popular term in the mobile internet industry. From Wang Qindai’s interpretation, I discovered that SoLoMo is not merely a trendy concept; it is also the inevitable result of the development of mobile communication and the internet. Social networking, localization, and mobility will continue to influence the development trends of mobile internet.

Wang Qindai said that mobile internet has gone through three stages. The first stage is the regrettable SP (Service Provider) stage. The second stage is the “crouching, wanting to earn money but haven't yet” stage. The third stage is the phase where killer applications unique to mobile devices emerge. In this stage, SoLoMo provides the most appropriate description of mobile internet.

However, Wang Qindai adds Co (Commercial) to SoLoMo, representing business models. Indeed, without a business model, mobile internet is incomplete.

“Following this path to uncover unprecedented user needs related to SoLoMo, if you can satisfy these unprecedented user needs, you have the opportunity to become a disruptive or killer application.” Wang Qindai stated.

At the same time, he believes that merely identifying the SoLoMo concept without truly finding such a product application or service is meaningless.

“Has the ship of mobile internet bypassed the blue ocean and directly reached the red sea? Everyone suddenly shifts from the confines of the internet to the frontier of mobile internet, all flocking to the SoLoMo camp, indeed creating turbulent waves. But after the waves subside, only then will you discover who lies stranded on the beach and who reaches the other shore.” Wang Qindai expressed.

Below is the interview transcript:

Sina Technology: Hello netizens! I am Zong Xiuqian from the Sina Technology channel. It's great to meet everyone again. With the advent of the 3G era, driven by the development of mobile communication and the flourishing of the internet, mobile internet has gained prominence, becoming a trendsetter in this technological age. Against this background, naturally, Sina Technology cannot miss the opportunity to witness the development of the industry. Therefore, we hope to regularly invite guests to our interview room to discuss various aspects of mobile internet. We hope this interview will be long-term, continuous, and part of a series, and today is just the beginning.

As the opening of this series of interviews, we have invited a heavyweight guest to discuss with us a popular concept, SoLoMo. Please welcome today's guest, Mr. Wang Qindai, CEO of Mobile Bighead. Let's start with a greeting to the netizens!

Wang Qindai: Good afternoon, dear netizens of Sina! I'm delighted to have this opportunity to exchange views with everyone on the development trends of mobile internet and the possible emergence of killer or disruptive applications in mobile internet.

Sina Technology: Mr. Wang, we considered inviting you as the leading guest for this series of interviews. You have worked for world-renowned telecommunications companies such as AT&T, Lucent, and Nortel, later founded AsiaInfo and Huayou Century, and then started working in mobile internet. In between, you made a stunning transformation. How do you possess such strong judgment to predict the overall direction of mobile internet development? And each time, you enter during periods of great growth and exit just before sunset, re-entering another sunrise field?

Wang Qindai: You've summarized my career quite well—telecommunications is one block, the internet is another, and mobile internet is the third. What I initially studied had little relation to these fields. I've seen many colleagues, friends, partners, and co-workers whose work has little direct connection to their studies. Mine might still have a slight connection—I actually studied laser physics. At the time, I studied various types of laser equipment, the physical principles of laser equipment, and their potential applications.

One developmental area of laser physics is communication—for instance, the original signal source for fiber optic communication was a laser. When I graduated, I first chose communication. In that era, the real changes in people's lifestyles, work styles, and even entertainment were brought about by the deployment of communication networks and the popularization of communication terminals.

What interests me—or what I think interests all of us—is what kind of application technology and system deployment in this era truly changes our daily lives, work, and entertainment. If you can be involved, you'll find it fascinating. So, at the start of my graduation, communication achieved this purpose and produced this effect.

However, once you're immersed in the communication industry, doing various jobs in communication—whether sales, marketing, or technical—you'll realize that after some time, the rise of the internet is the future. To a large extent, the internet will compress and even replace traditional communication equipment, methods, and ways.

In the first phase, the company I worked for was AT&T, the American Telephone & Telegraph Company, equivalent to China's earliest postal and telecommunications department, telecommunications general bureau, plus today's operators. AT&T was then America's sole long-distance and local telephone company. It provided both equipment and telecommunication services. The second phase was Nortel, the largest provider of communication equipment, terminals, and technologies in North America, especially with certain expertise in wireless communication.

In the second phase, when you realize that the internet could significantly impact people's lifestyles, learning methods, and entertainment over the next ten or twenty years, you'd naturally get excited and want to learn and understand more about the internet. Around that time, I had the opportunity to join a team returning to China to start AsiaInfo. AsiaInfo performed exceptionally well, positioning itself as the architect of China's internet. Back then, China's internet had large-scale networks like 163, 165, and 169. Most of the equipment integration, design, system provision, and final system implementation were carried out by teams led by AsiaInfo.

After leaving AsiaInfo, I founded Huayou Century, entering wireless internet. The idea back then was very simple: the internet is the future, wireless is the future, and combining wireless with the internet leads to wireless internet. This combination would likely have the greatest impact on our surroundings and friends over the next ten or twenty years. Naturally, we believed it had better prospects and potential. Hence, at Huayou Century, we began offering services related to mobile internet.

From technology to telecommunications products, internet construction, and finally mobile internet services—we've come a long way. Throughout, I feel that going with the flow or having an interest in new things is crucial. More importantly, living a lifetime while staying updated with the times is quite interesting.

Sina Technology: Recently, I’ve noticed many people talking about the SoLoMo concept. This concept was first introduced in February this year by a famous venture capital partner in the U.S., John Doerr. Later, in April, Sina CEO Charles Chao also mentioned this at the Mobile Internet Conference, saying that future networks would develop towards SoLoMo—Social, Local, and Mobile—becoming the trend of future internet development. Do you think this concept is merely conceptual or can it provide guidance for development?

Wang Qindai: SoLoMo later became a joke among a few friends when we added 'CO' to it, making it SoLoMoCo. This concept was originally introduced around February this year when I saw some of our investors reference KPMG, a major investment firm, in a report by Mary Meeker, who was known as the first analyst of the American internet. Her report cited the SoLoMo concept and conducted some analysis. That was my first encounter with it in January or February. What you mentioned is interesting because at the Mobile Internet Conference, Mr. Charles Chao was the keynote speaker in the morning, but I didn’t hear him mention the network’s development towards SoLoMoCo. In the afternoon, I attended the first forum called the China Mobile Internet Forum, and I thought I was the only one heavily emphasizing SoLoMoCo.

Sina Technology: Because great minds think alike.

Wang Qindai: Exactly. I have a completely personal observation and opinion. I believe mobile internet may go through three stages. The first stage is what we jokingly call the unforgettable SP (Service Provider) stage. During the SP stage, companies like Airborne, Huayou Century, TOM, and Lingtong were leaders in this stage. They were pioneers due to their rapid movement, early advantage, and discovery of an opportunity. Fortunately, this opportunity coincided with a business model—providing ringtones, images, simple games, news, weather forecasts, etc., directly to users via phones—and they capitalized on it quickly.

Then came impulsive consumption—users encountering information and entertainment content on their phones for the first time, impulsively willing to pay for it. Growth was swift because the user base in China was large enough, and operators provided promotion and payment channels. China Mobile Dream Network played a significant role here. This was the first stage.

But the first stage passed quickly for two reasons. First, the mobile value-added services provided by SP lacked core value. Why? Providing pictures, ringtones, small games, or news and weather forecasts on phones could be obtained freely on the internet, lacking irreplaceable value. Over time, users felt paying one yuan for a picture or two yuan for a song wasn’t worth it unless they acted impulsively. It couldn’t sustain as a fundamental, everyday need.

Second, the value chain was incomplete. Service providers, whether content or service providers, couldn’t stand independently because the entire network was built, owned, and operated by operators. Any attempt to reach users for marketing or collecting payments relied entirely on one to three operators controlling all marketing, promotion, and payment channels. Operators promoting your service meant acquiring users, and operators collecting payments meant achieving a business model. Without operator support, you had nothing.

Because only a few entities could provide promotion and payment channels—just three powerful ones—while thousands of companies offered content like pictures, music, games, and information, the value chain was unsustainable. Thus, the SP business model lacked core competitiveness. The first generation of SPs would fade, as I wrote on Weibo: that generation earned money effortlessly.

The second era, I jokingly call it "crouching, wanting to make money but not yet." It includes today's Mobile Bighead and many other mobile internet companies. They transplanted good applications or services from the internet to phones. There are many examples in this industry—UC excels in navigation and browsers, 3G portals, clients, and platforms. Mobile Bighead originally provided news aggregation, music aggregation, video aggregation, and reading aggregation. However, all these services existed on the internet, merely extended and adapted to phones, allowing phone users to enjoy PC-based content, services, and applications. This is the second generation.

Why do I say "crouching, wanting to make money but struggling"? I believe it's a value chain problem. A good business must have a sound value chain. Mobile internet's value chain consists of three layers: the terminal (phones), the network, and the payment infrastructure. Early functional phones couldn’t access the internet, let alone MPKs. Then smartphones emerged, providing better internet access but still with discomforts. This is the first layer—the terminal. The second layer is the network. 2G networks were unreliable, 2.5G networks barely worked, and 2.75G networks were somewhat usable. The widespread adoption of 3G networks took years, calling for them since the early days. Finally, from 2009-2010, 3G became a reality and developed. The third layer involves having a groundbreaking application meeting unprecedented user needs. Even if you create such an application, how do you monetize it? Mobile internet lacks convenient third-party payment options. These are the three layers of infrastructure.

Thus, the second generation of mobile internet involves transplanting internet services, applications, and businesses to phones. Some have been doing this for over six years, others for three to four. Due to the incompleteness of these three infrastructure layers, efforts remain arduous without substantial profits.

The third stage, recently often mentioned, involves discovering killer or disruptive applications primarily for smartphones. Computers extend traditional internet, but smartphones enable unique applications. Alternatively, the SOLOMO era may arrive. Why? Among the three infrastructure layers, one has begun breaking ground and achieving substantial breakthroughs—smartphones. Simply put, iPhones transformed uncomfortable web browsing into enjoyment, while Android or gPhone systems democratized this experience to mid-to-low-end users. Initially, using the internet on phones was inconvenient or fragmented, but touch-screen iPhones made it easy and enjoyable. Now, during non-computer hours, users can conveniently browse the web. While iPhones target high-end users, Android systems bring iPhone-quality experiences to ordinary consumers, ranging from a few hundred to several thousand yuan. Touch-screen smartphones herald the first major breakthrough in infrastructure or value chains.

What can these smartphones do? For instance, smartphones allow constant mobility, unlike laptops. Secondly, wherever you go, smartphones can track your location, something computers lack significance in. Additionally, smartphones contain your contact list, can take photos and videos anytime, enabling capabilities previously unavailable on larger devices. These compact devices offer unprecedented, mature, and enjoyable application technologies.

On these application technologies, there's potential to maximize their effectiveness in fulfilling users’ unprecedented needs. What are these needs? They’re encapsulated in SoLoMo—Social, Local, and Mobile. Regardless of willingness, we live in a social era. Before virtual networks and computers, we socialized but were limited by distance and time. Virtual networks drastically reduced social costs, enabling interactions with more people regardless of distance. Don’t narrowly define socializing as merely dating—something I find uninteresting. Socializing is a fundamental human need, greatly expanded by the internet and significantly reduced in cost. Therefore, we now consider it a social era. Many activities, like watching news, movies, listening to music, or shopping, begin with social networks. We prefer to see what friends, role models, celebrities, or experts are doing, enjoying, listening to, recommending, watching, buying, and reviewing. Previously, we wanted similar information but lacked an omnipresent and easily accessible virtual social network. Once we obtain this information, we often shift from visiting Web 1.0 sites to exploring our social networks to see what our favorite and trusted friends or opinion leaders are engaging with—they may have already filtered out what we like.

I believe this is an inherent long-term demand, just released through the internet.

Next is Local—combined with phones, it emphasizes immediacy. Immediacy differs from mere mental visualization. What is immediacy? It means sitting here, accessing things within reach or walking ten minutes to fulfill needs—an experience unavailable on virtual internet. For instance, wanting to buy something right now is Local—it’s characterized by proximity. Mobile refers to anywhere-anytime access with a phone. We added CO for commerce. Together, these elements represent Social, Local, Mobile, and Commerce, best experienced on today’s smartphones. Along this path, we can uncover unprecedented user needs related to SoLoMo. If you satisfy these needs, you have the chance to create disruptive or killer applications.

Sina Technology: After this concept was introduced, many used it for hype and promotion. Do you think there might be blind bandwagon effects or potential bubbles?

Sina Technology: With a critical perspective, first, SoLoMo applications will certainly exist, but will they meet unprecedented demands here and now? This, I believe, requires practical testing—developing such products, launching them to the market, and gauging whether they meet users’ new and better needs. Second, are users currently inclined to use these services frequently and intensely with sufficient stickiness? Personally, I believe this opens a door and gives direction for good exploration and mining. Conversely, are SoLoMo applications necessarily killer apps? Is labeling any application as SoLoMo enough to guarantee success?

I joked on Weibo yesterday, “Does the ship of mobile internet bypass the blue ocean and directly reach the red sea?” Everyone suddenly shifts from the confines of the internet to the forefront of mobile internet, all flocking to the SoLoMo camp, indeed creating turbulent waves. But after the waves subside, only then will you discover who lies stranded on the beach and who reaches the other shore. From this angle, finding just a concept without truly identifying a product or service is meaningless. Second, even if you identify a potential service or product, if you lack the ability to develop, promote, maintain, and continuously iterate it, it remains ineffective. Returning to entrepreneurship, I still believe staying updated is essential, but balancing your foundations, abilities, and strengths with suitable ventures is crucial. Moreover, relying solely on concepts for sufficient funding should be a closed door.

Sina Technology: Including Chinese concept stocks, this issue arises. Perhaps some companies benefited from these concepts before, but Nasdaq will likely become more cautious in the future. Of course, the TOP3 rule applies to every industry—not just mobile internet. Ultimately, how many companies will settle into a definitive landscape?

Wang Qindai: I completely agree. All industries have TOP3, but when the industry foundation is enormous—like e-commerce, search, or mobile internet—involving massive user bases and scale, it will fragment. Fragmentation occurs in two ways: vertical and regional. Take a simple example—search. Baidu offers general parallel search, allowing you to search anything for the broadest, most timely, and common answers. What do you think we do on SouFun or Dianping?

Sina Technology: These revolve around our needs.

Wang Qindai: These are all searches. Our daily needs for search are extensive. When searching for, say, good apartments in Beijing, I could technically search on Baidu but wouldn’t. Instead, I’d go to SouFun or 58同城. These are vertical searches. Have you heard of Jingtanwang? Baidu partnered with Beijing News to form Jingtanwang, offering detailed, comprehensive, and timely information on Beijing’s dining, drinking, and entertainment. This is localized search. When a field or industry is vast, beyond general services, vertical and regional services emerge. Taobao is huge, but so are JD.com, Chen Nian, and many others—selling shoes, clothes, bags, etc. This mirrors traditional business models—if large enough, fragmentation occurs. Returning to mobile internet, it’s a sufficiently vast field where both general and specialized vertical and regional applications can emerge. On this basis, you can have N TOP3s.

Sina Technology: Thus, no matter how crowded the field becomes, finding your own business model is key, right? Identifying the right direction may present even greater opportunities than the internet, isn’t that true?

Wang Qindai: Yes, I believe mobile internet extends and vastly expands the internet. Simply put, there are far more mobile users than internet users, and mobile usage frequency and intensity will surpass the internet if it’s convenient enough. This is a bigger future. Nevertheless, I emphasize preparation, strength, and steady progress.

Finally, taking up one minute, I’d like to give a small preview to Sina’s netizens. In July, after thorough preparation, we’ll launch an application we believe has the potential to become a disruptive or even killer SoLoMo app. I sincerely hope to return to Sina’s live studio to discuss how we identified and created such an application and how users might try this potential killer app.

Sina Technology: We truly look forward to it. July isn’t far away, and we’ll surely have another meaningful discussion. Thank you, Mr. Wang, for joining our first episode of the mobile internet program. Much appreciated!
