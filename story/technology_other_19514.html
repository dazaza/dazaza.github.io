Reuters Online Financial Dictionary is more than just a simple translation of words. It provides detailed explanations of financial terms, helping you gain more financial knowledge.

Take the word "Price-to-Earnings Ratio" as an example. Below is the explanation provided by the Reuters Online Financial Dictionary:

Price-to-Earnings Ratio (P/E Ratio) Shiyinglv

Also known in English as earnings multiple. The P/E ratio is one of the most commonly used indicators to assess whether stock prices are reasonable. It is obtained by dividing the stock price by the annual earnings per share (EPS) (dividing the company's market value by the annual attributable profit to shareholders will yield the same result). When calculating, the stock price usually takes the latest closing price, and for EPS, if calculated based on the published previous year's EPS, it is called historical P/E; if calculated based on the market's forecast for this year and next year's EPS, it is called prospective/forward/forecast P/E. The EPS forecast used to calculate the forward P/E generally adopts the market consensus estimate, which is the average or median forecast collected by institutions tracking the company's performance from multiple analysts.

The P/E ratio is an important reference indicator for individual stocks, sectors, and the overall market. If any stock has a P/E ratio significantly higher than similar stocks or the market, there needs to be sufficient justification, which often revolves around the expectation of rapid future earnings growth for that company. A company enjoying a very high P/E ratio indicates that investors generally believe the company's future earnings per share will grow rapidly, such that in a few years, the P/E ratio will fall to a reasonable level. Once earnings growth does not meet expectations, the force supporting the high P/E ratio cannot be sustained, often leading to a significant decline in stock prices.

There is no fixed standard for what constitutes a reasonable P/E ratio, but for individual stocks, the P/E ratios of peers have comparative reference value; for sectors or the overall market, historical average P/E ratios are useful for comparison.

The P/E ratio is a highly valuable stock market indicator, easy to understand and data is readily available, but it also has many drawbacks. For instance, the earnings per share used as the denominator are calculated according to the currently prevailing accounting standards, but companies can often adjust them as needed. Therefore, theoretically, two companies with the same cash flow may have significant differences in their reported earnings per share. On the other hand, investors often do not believe that profits strictly calculated according to accounting standards accurately reflect a company's profitability on a going concern basis. Therefore, analysts often make adjustments to the net profit in the company's official formula themselves, such as replacing net profit with earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization (EBITDA) to calculate earnings per share.

Additionally, the market value used as the numerator in the P/E ratio cannot reflect the company's debt (leverage) level. For example, two companies with a market value of $1 billion each and net profits of $100 million each have a P/E ratio of 10. However, if Company A has $1 billion in debt while Company B has no debt, the P/E ratio cannot reflect this difference. Therefore, some analysts use "Enterprise Value (EV)"—market value plus debt minus cash—to replace market value in calculating the P/E ratio. Theoretically, the EV/EBITDA ratio can avoid some of the shortcomings of the pure P/E ratio.

In the above text, the terms attributable profit and net profit/earnings are used interchangeably. To be precise, all should refer to attributable profit. Net profit minus the profit attributable to minority shareholders and dividends to preferred shareholders yields the attributable profit to (common stock) shareholders. Minority shareholders refer to investors holding a minority stake in group subsidiaries.
