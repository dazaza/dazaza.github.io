The HR manager, holding a thick stack of evaluation forms, was recounting the same old story to department managers that repeats every year, and the expressions on everyone's faces seemed somewhat unnatural. The department managers, with another thick stack of evaluation forms in hand, were narrating the same tale to their department employees, and once again, the expressions on everyone's faces revealed the same unnaturalness.

The above two scenes are repeated in the annual evaluation process of many companies at the beginning of each year. Year after year, it's always similar. Yet, despite the similarities in the annual evaluation process, there is often significant turnover among participants in many enterprises, and each year’s evaluation brings about some disharmonious notes. Whether it’s the process or the results of the annual evaluation, they rarely leave people entirely satisfied. HR managers feel weary of the annual evaluation process, while regular employees approach it with a mix of anticipation and anxiety. Company leaders are perplexed by the outcomes of the annual evaluations, wondering why bonuses and red envelopes still fail to please employees, sometimes even leading to widespread dissatisfaction and employee turnover. However, in most companies, the annual evaluation remains an indispensable part of operations.

Is the annual evaluation necessary? And how should it be carried out? Is the company's annual evaluation really just an insignificant burden? To answer these questions and avoid the annual evaluation becoming a mere formality, I believe it is essential to address several key issues.

**Issue One: Must the annual evaluation wait until the end of the year?**

In general, performance evaluations can have cycles ranging from monthly, quarterly, semi-annual, to annual evaluations. Monthly evaluations typically occur at the beginning of the following month to assess the previous month's performance and are usually the most frequent type of evaluation in many companies. Unlike monthly evaluations, quarterly, semi-annual, and annual evaluations often incorporate a summarization effect. In reality, the evaluation process is distributed throughout the months of the year. From a business perspective, whether good or bad, the performance results at year-end cannot be altered. In this sense, process evaluations are more important than year-end evaluations. Therefore, if the routine evaluation work is done well, the year-end evaluation does not have to be a particularly cumbersome task but rather becomes a matter of course.

Of course, if the year-end evaluation does not have to wait until the end of the year, changing it to an annual assessment might be more accurate. This way, the year-end evaluation workload would not be so concentrated, and people wouldn’t feel anxious at year-end like students entering an exam without confidence.

**Issue Two: Is the annual evaluation applicable to every employee?**

The answer to this question is both yes and no. On one hand, companies need a basis to evaluate an employee's contribution over the past year, and the best method to obtain this basis is through the annual evaluation. It can use KPI indicators to assess work performance, attitude indicators to evaluate effort, and ability indicators to assess actual capabilities and potential. Analyzing from the category of indicators, some indicators are not suitable for monthly or other cycle evaluations, such as work ability evaluations. From the perspective of the company's human resource management system, the results of the annual evaluation can provide comprehensive references for employee training, recruitment, position adjustments, salary adjustments, etc., which other evaluation forms or methods cannot achieve. In this sense, the annual evaluation applies to every employee.

On the other hand, the annual evaluation should have corresponding evaluation indicators, especially annual KPI indicators. However, not every position in a company has its annual KPI indicators; for instance, setting annual KPI indicators for technical workers can be quite challenging. Therefore, the significance and methods of the annual evaluation may differ for different employees.

For executives or mid-level employees who have signed annual target responsibility agreements, the year-end evaluation is indeed a crucial time to test their performance over the past year. For this level of management, some indicators are difficult to break down into more specific evaluation cycles, such as collections and accounts receivable, which have uncertainties and instabilities, making monthly or quarterly evaluations difficult. The year-end evaluation offers a more appropriate cycle. These managers often sign agreements akin to annual target responsibility contracts at the beginning of the year, using them as the primary basis for year-end evaluations. The main content of these contracts involves KPI indicators used to evaluate their work performance throughout the year. Therefore, this type of year-end evaluation is genuine and substantiated. They should naturally become the key targets of the company's annual evaluation.

For ordinary company employees, they often lack clear year-end evaluation objectives. During the year-end evaluation, on one hand, a simple summary of the entire year's performance can be made, aggregating all monthly or quarterly KPI achievement conditions; on the other hand, a comprehensive assessment of their work attitude and ability is also needed. Some companies use 360-degree evaluations, while others simply rely on scores given by their direct supervisors.

From the perspective of the management philosophy underlying performance evaluations, the above concepts make sense. The human nature assumption in performance evaluations is "at birth, humans are inherently industrious," and evaluations serve as the "whip" to motivate. For regular employees, it is better to strengthen regular evaluations and emphasize process evaluations while de-emphasizing year-end evaluations, aligning more closely with Theory X. On the other hand, senior executives and middle managers are the backbone and core strength of the enterprise. They deserve more trust and authorization rather than frequent supervision and control. Appropriately relaxing regular evaluations and adopting year-end evaluations aligns with the characteristics of this level of management personnel, making their evaluation methods more consistent with Theory Y.

**Issue Three: Does the annual evaluation require clear indicators?**

This question is closely related to the previous one. If it is an evaluation, there should be evaluation indicators, and they should be clear. The answer to this question is self-evident. However, in many companies' annual evaluations, there indeed exists the phenomenon described at the beginning of the article—going through the motions seriously. Because these companies do not establish clear evaluation indicators for their annual evaluations, on the surface, the evaluation process seems very democratic, with everyone gathered together democratically discussing the employee's performance for the entire year. In fact, this apparent democracy cannot yield objectively fair evaluation results, ultimately ending in a harmonious conclusion among everyone. This is the inevitable result of "having no rules to follow."

Therefore, I suggest that the annual evaluation must set clear indicators, whether they are relatively objective quantitative indicators or more subjective qualitative indicators, having them is better than not having them. Even indicators such as teamwork ability, cooperative awareness, work enthusiasm, innovative spirit, etc., can make judgments on certain aspects of employees, which is better than pure guesswork. Setting indicators is also a sign of the scientific and standardized development of enterprise performance evaluations. Thus, using stacks of evaluation forms to conduct annual evaluations is, to some extent, necessary. Combining with the previous issue, it is recommended to set different indicator categories for various personnel and assign corresponding weights.

**Issue Four: What are the application forms of the annual evaluation results?**

In a company I am familiar with, the annual evaluation results can directly link to the following aspects:

- Annual bonus

- Salary adjustment

- Benefits

- Position promotion

- Job competition

- Training

- Outstanding employee selection

- Flag-raising qualification

This is a relatively comprehensive and systematic plan for applying the results of the annual evaluation. Analyzing this plan, it has the following features:

- Combines material incentives with spiritual incentives

- Combines short-term incentives with long-term incentives

- Combines explicit incentives with implicit incentives

Firstly, analyzing the first feature, annual bonuses, salary adjustments, and benefits belong to direct material incentives, while outstanding employees and flag-raising qualifications belong to typical spiritual incentives. Here, let me mention briefly that in this company with 1,600 employees, only one flag-raiser is designated per month, and a flag-raising ceremony is organized every two weeks. The flag-raising ceremony is grand and impressive, usually held on Monday mornings on the open square in front of the production workshop. The company's general manager leads all employees in a total mobilization. At this time, as a flag-raiser, one feels immense honor. This is an incentive effect that other material incentives cannot achieve.

Regarding short-term and long-term incentives, the annual bonus belongs to a short-term incentive for the current period, while salary adjustments, job competitions, and training have varying degrees of long-term incentive effects. Long-term incentives play a crucial role in talent cultivation and stabilizing the workforce.

Regarding explicit and implicit incentives, flag-raising qualifications and outstanding employee selections belong to clear explicit incentives. After the annual evaluation results are announced, various lists will also be published, and all employees in the company will know the results. In contrast, training opportunities, position promotions, and job competitions are relatively more hidden.

**Issue Five: Does the annual evaluation require personal summaries?**

In the usual thinking of performance evaluations, evaluations mainly focus on assessing people's work, and those being evaluated are often in a relatively passive state. However, in many companies' annual evaluations, personal year-end summaries are an important part. Is this self-inspection process necessary?

I believe this is a very important step. The ultimate goal of performance evaluations is not to implement rewards and punishments for employees but to use evaluations as a tool to improve the performance levels of both the company and its employees. Personal year-end summaries, through reviewing and reflecting on individual performance throughout the year, can achieve a self-critical effect, significantly promoting individual performance improvement. From this understanding, personal year-end summaries go beyond the scope of performance evaluations and are not merely for the purpose of the annual evaluation.

Additionally, analyzing the information provided by personal year-end summaries, it serves as an important source of information for evaluators conducting evaluations. Since annual evaluations cover a longer cycle, not every evaluator fully understands all the work achievements, attitudes, and abilities of those being evaluated. Considering these two roles of personal year-end summaries comprehensively, I believe that personal summaries are necessary forms of the annual evaluation. Furthermore, based on personal year-end summaries, it is essential to strengthen communication and feedback between evaluators and those being evaluated to maximize the effectiveness of the year-end summaries.

Overall analysis shows that the above issues involve the timing of the annual evaluation, the indicators for the annual evaluation, the applicable objects of the annual evaluation, the utilization paths of the annual evaluation results, and the necessity of personal summaries in the annual evaluation. I sincerely hope that these ideas can provide some guidance for companies' annual evaluations, making them truly meaningful and effective evaluation forms. Of course, considering the differences between individual companies, the above concepts are based on individual perceptions and cannot apply to all companies. Simultaneously, the annual evaluation is a broad topic, and I welcome further discussions.
