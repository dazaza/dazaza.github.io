Microsoft Chief Researcher Rebuts Ballmer: Keyboard and Mouse Are Gradually Becoming Obsolete

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 13:49:50

Microsoft Chief Researcher Andy Wilson

Bloomberg published a commentary article on Thursday titled "Ballmer's Exit Makes Xbox Spinoff More Likely." In the past few years, there have been repeated calls from investors for Microsoft to spin off its Xbox business. Now, with Ballmer, who strongly opposed such a move, announcing his retirement, this obstacle has been removed, paving the way for the separation of the Xbox business. This action could help unlock shareholder value and boost Microsoft's recently sluggish stock price. Below is an excerpt from the article:

Removing Obstacles

After 13 years of declining market value, some investors hope that Steve Ballmer’s replacement will take bolder steps to reverse the downward trend of the world's largest software maker. This may mean spinning off the Xbox video game business.

Ballmer's resignation as CEO of Microsoft could clear the way for a potential spin-off of the Xbox business, which is seen as beneficial for unlocking shareholder value. Despite its success in the consumer market and annual sales reaching $7 billion, the Xbox business remains one of the lower-margin units within Microsoft and has not helped drive the growth of the company's core enterprise services and software sales.

Data from Bloomberg shows that based on Nintendo's revenue multiplier, the Xbox is worth at least $17 billion. The U.S. investment firm Wedbush believes that given Nintendo's operational losses, the Xbox should be valued even higher.

Todd Lowenstein, fund manager at U.S. asset management company HighMark Capital Management, pointed out, “Xbox appears to be an attractive standalone business that can survive even if it is spun off. Among the businesses under Microsoft that are capable of existing independently, Xbox should be the most mature and have the greatest growth potential.” HighMark Capital Management manages $19 billion in assets, including Microsoft shares.

The new CEO of Microsoft will bear the mission of boosting the company's stock price and accelerating development. Microsoft's current market value is $275 billion, already surpassed by Google and Apple. Since Ballmer became CEO 13 years ago, due to failing to make significant gains in markets such as smartphones, internet search, and tablets, Microsoft's market value has eroded by $280 billion.

Reasons for Spinoff

Michael Pachter, a Los Angeles-based analyst at Wedbush, pointed out that the revenue and profit of the Xbox business unit mainly come from console and game sales, as well as online service subscription income, and its value after being spun off would be between $20 billion and $30 billion.

He also stated that due to the low profit margin of the Xbox business, some investors have long called for Microsoft to divest this business because they have never seen a case where entertainment devices help promote the sale of enterprise software and services. Pachter said: “Many investors have questioned Microsoft's involvement in the hardware industry. Whether or not to split the Xbox business mainly depends on Microsoft's thinking about how to develop this business once it grows.”

However, not all of Microsoft's shareholders support the spinoff of the Xbox business. Richard Cook, co-founder of U.S. asset management company Cook & Bynum Capital Management, pointed out that Microsoft must ensure the integrity of its business chain to have a more cohesive 'ecosystem' than competitors. He hopes that every link forming this integrity does not separate. The company also holds Microsoft shares.

Cook said in an interview, “I strongly oppose Microsoft's spinoff of Xbox. The benefit of this business is not only that it is already profitable but also that it allows Microsoft to have a place in the living room.”

Funding Risks

Brian Blau, an analyst at Gartner in San Francisco, pointed out that without Microsoft's protection and financial support, the Xbox may not be able to raise enough funds, which are also necessary for it to stay ahead of its competitors. Microsoft currently holds $77 billion in cash. Blau said in an interview: “Participating in the gaming industry is not easy, and there are countless companies that fall by the wayside.”

Spinning off the Xbox business would also be detrimental to the development vision of Microsoft's core products, which play a key role in attracting more consumers. Lewis Ward, a gaming industry analyst at IDC, stated that Microsoft should integrate more features of the next-generation Xbox (such as games) into the Windows 8 operating system and Surface tablet to inject momentum into these struggling products.

Ward said, “The possibility of spinning off the Xbox is small. Technically speaking, Microsoft is trying to integrate gaming into all of its system platforms.” Ballmer's restructuring plan further complicates the potential spin-off of the Xbox. In fact, he himself has always opposed the spin-off of the Xbox. According to this restructuring plan, Xbox hardware, video games, and TV series are grouped into one business unit, while the Xbox operating system and Xbox Live belong to another business.

Schwarz stated that whether Microsoft splits some business units ultimately depends on who the new CEO is. He said: “If Microsoft promotes Ballmer's successor from within, I think the likelihood of a split is small. But if Microsoft looks externally for a new leader with an open-minded vision and spirit of change, then I think he or she might consider splitting the business, and doing so would be significant.”
