The monopoly position of the Windows operating system collapses.

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 14:37:23

Fifteen years ago, the PC industry was at its peak, and its core technology, the Windows operating system, was unparalleled. Fifteen years later, PCs have no advantage compared to smartphones and tablets, and the dominance of the Windows operating system is also gone.

In the late 1990s, there was a US tech company that was undoubtedly leading globally. The company was almost as rich as a country and had formidable technical strength. It not only dominated in desktop operating systems but also had growing influence in other software areas. However, being too prominent invites trouble, and the US government dragged this company into a prolonged antitrust lawsuit. Yes, this was Microsoft back then.

Eventually, Microsoft lost the lawsuit and had to accept strict legal constraints. Many people were worried that this case would have a significant negative impact on Microsoft, fearing that this iconic tech company would stagnate because of the case. But facts have shown that over the past 15 years, what ultimately caused Microsoft to lose its dominant position in the global personal computer market was not the antitrust lawsuit but changes in the market itself.

At that time, Microsoft had become the focus of the global tech community and was almost about to make history by becoming the world's first company with a trillion-dollar valuation. In 1999, Microsoft's market value reached its historical high. According to Standard & Poor's data, Microsoft's record at that time was $620.58 billion. If inflation factors at the time are considered, Microsoft's total market value on December 30, 1999, would be approximately equivalent to $850 billion today. However, the changes brought about by the booming development of the Internet were beyond Microsoft's control. Microsoft was merely a surging and beautiful wave in the tide of the global Internet.

The antitrust case back then did indeed cause many losses for Microsoft, but they were not fatal. Due to the emergence and widespread adoption of new electronic products such as cloud computing, smartphones, and tablets, the once dominant desktop operating system has lost its former glory. As long as electronic devices can access the Internet and run relevant programs, they basically meet user needs, regardless of the type of operating system installed.

Through the following three tables, we can objectively understand the challenges faced by tech giants like Microsoft, HP, Dell, and Intel before the new wave of the Internet.

Global Internet Device Shipment Statistics (2000 to 2012)

Firstly, let's look at the global Internet device shipment statistics: from 2000 to 2012, although the annual PC shipment volume remained around 200 million units, after the appearance of smartphones and tablets, the market landscape underwent fundamental changes. Now, compared to smartphones and tablets, PC shipments pale in comparison.

Global Market Share of Major Platforms (Q1 2009 to Q1 2013)

From the table, it can be seen that the diversification of personal internet devices greatly weakened the dominant position of the Windows operating system. This change actually occurred only recently; three years ago, 70% of global PCs were running on the Windows operating system. Now, due to the widespread adoption of Google's Android system and Apple's iOS platform, the global market share of the Windows operating system has shrunk by almost 30% to 50% compared to before. The most significant change is that Android's global market share has surpassed that of Windows.

Finally, let's look at the statistical data analyzed by Horace Dediu, an analyst at the American market research firm Asymco. The PC business not only appears "small" in the face of explosive sales data of smartphones and tablets, but the PC business itself has already shown a clear downward trend. Now, internet devices have become diversified, and although PC technology is quite mature, PCs are no longer the most cost-effective or convenient devices for internet use. The status of PCs has been overturned by smartphones and tablets, and personal computers have become specialized office equipment.

Windows Operating System PC Annual Shipment Growth Rate

From the above chart, we can see that before the release of the iPad, PCs with the Windows operating system had a very impressive growth rate in shipments. However, starting from the end of 2010, the growth rate of PC shipments has been sluggish, and recently, there has even been a noticeable negative growth.

In fact, for Microsoft, it’s not all bad news. Microsoft's performance in transformation is commendable. Moreover, Microsoft's Office suite is also an important source of revenue. Although Google and Apple have also launched their own office software, they have not yet posed a fatal threat to Office.

Time flies, it has been 15 years since Microsoft's antitrust case. The once monopolistic product — the Windows operating system — will no longer be sued for monopoly, and the PC industry closely related to the Windows operating system also has a bleak future.
