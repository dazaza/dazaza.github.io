The Toy of the Sixty-Ninth Night

A monotonous life can sometimes have some very interesting events. I've even thought that if I hadn't met Ji Yan, I would be just like everyone else, living a mechanical nine-to-five life. Of course, occasionally, one might still encounter some very unpleasant people, such as the disheveled middle-aged man I met just now at the newspaper office entrance who insisted that I buy his goods.

His hair was long, his face unshaven, and his almost dirty yellow shirt was half tucked into his belt, while the other part hung out disorderly. The middle-aged man told me he had been unemployed for a long time. According to his own description, after seeing my articles about those interesting stories in the newspaper, he felt that I would definitely be interested in his story and hoped to sell it to me because he was in urgent need. Looking at him, it seemed like he really lacked money. I didn't originally intend to agree with him, but the man firmly held onto my sleeve and repeatedly claimed that I would definitely pay for his story.

How do you determine that I will spend money to buy your story? I curiously asked him, but made an effort to appear disdainful.

You will, you will, you definitely will. The man was delighted when I finally spoke to him. He clamped a thick stack of files under his arm and vigorously wiped his hands on his pants, which were already in bad condition, making them wrinkle up like pickled vegetables. He solemnly handed over the file folder to me with both hands.

You need to pay first. Otherwise, what if you walk away after hearing the story? He said another sentence, and I was somewhat agitated at the moment, so I actually negotiated the price, took the file folder, and only became troubled when the man happily walked away with the money. Although the money wasn't much, what if there was just a pile of waste paper inside? Isn't this just being taken advantage of by someone treating me as a fool?

I opened the bag, and fortunately, there were indeed stacks of handwritten manuscripts inside. The handwriting was beautiful, quite different from the appearance of that man. Perhaps, he used to be a well-dressed, high-status person. Who knows? The changes in this world always happen faster than I can imagine.

I brought the manuscript back to the office and took it out to read during my free time.

Before I lost my job, I lived in the old house passed down through my family. That area has since been sold off cheaply by me. There was no way; I couldn't repay the bank loan.

The buyers of my house were a peculiar pair, seemingly father and son. The older one was around thirty years old, tall, handsome, and extremely polite. Judging from his behavior, expensive car, and the fact that he paid the rent all at once, he must have been a rather wealthy person.

As for that child, truly, it was hard to accept. You can't imagine how a rude gentleman could have such a disgusting, no, nauseating son.

The boy was about twelve or thirteen years old, standing at only a little over one meter two, with a dirty outfit, hair messy like a bird's nest, always appearing aloof. His small single eyelid covered an almost motionless eyeball. His face was like a sponge soaked with sour milk, puffy and swollen, yet very pale. Strangely, the child's palms were quite large, almost the size of an adult's, and his finger joints were unusually coarse and large compared to other children his age. Moreover, the child tightly hugged a doll that was already in tatters.

That doll was almost completely ruined; the fake hair on its forehead had fallen off, and it had lost one arm. Its entire body was covered in dark red rust.

My doll is the best doll, its smile is as beautiful as a flower, its limbs move freely, its skin doesn't need repairs. If you ask me where I bought it, I'll tell you I made it. Since getting out of the car, the child had been singing this strange rhyme with his mouth open, not stopping for a moment, holding the damaged toy and looking at the house that was about to become their home.

I was talking to the gentleman, instructing him on necessary information and the geography and interpersonal relationships of this area, and the man was very polite and sincerely nodding his head saying thank you.

I am truly grateful. We came out of the exit ramp, and I was worried there might be something we didn't understand. After your introduction, I already have a preliminary understanding of here. If you're not busy later, I hope you can come visit more often. No need to be formal; since we've met, we are friends. Selling this house must have been very painful for you, so if there are any financial difficulties in the future, please come find me." The young man's words suddenly made my nose feel tight, warming my heart. After all, such friends are rare.

I was just about to leave when the child holding the old toy suddenly turned around, staring at me with wide eyes. I saw his mouth opening and closing, but it looked so unnatural. Those thick lips were like a fish gasping for air, breathing heavily for the little bit of air left.

Will you come again? If you do, remember to bring me a toy. After he finished speaking, he appeared very excited, his cheeks turning red, and his chest rising and falling. I was momentarily speechless, frozen in place, subconsciously nodding my head mechanically. Fortunately, the man noticed my confusion, smiled, patted my shoulder, and guided the child inside.
