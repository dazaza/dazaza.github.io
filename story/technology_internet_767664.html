The Big Data You've Never Known: Artificial Power

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 11:59:34

You've certainly heard enough about "big data," but still haven't quite figured out what it's all about. Even though you constantly feel some form of convenience it brings:

For instance, a week ago you took an interview test on a social networking site, and only after the interview did you realize that the real purpose of the test was to match how well your personality fit with the team. Another example is before the National Day Golden Week holiday, you received a push notification from a ticketing app suggesting that booking train tickets 16 days in advance by phone would have a higher success rate than doing so online on the same day. When you actually called, you found the phone booking system was busy, forcing you to wait two hours before making another call to successfully book the ticket. But it was better than being stuck on the 12306 website at the same time and unable to make a payment.

Big data is seeping into every industry and can even be closely linked to very daily-life scenarios such as your ability to take exams or the probability of contracting a certain disease. In the future, big data will be like water and electricity in our lives, improving the overall quality of information in society and making the use of information more efficient.

In this system, sampling and subsequent analysis of data still require human intervention.

Crowdsourcing makes data sampling more automated

"In the future, human intervention will become increasingly unnecessary, at least in the front-end data collection phase." Product manager James told Tencent Technology that a lot of data collection now comes from user interaction behaviors, such as searches, Weibo interactions, or actions like 'like,' 'upvote,' 'discard into trash' these small designs within applications. As long as users actively complete them, the backend can infer the quality of the data.

The fluctuation in onion prices determines the trend of India's inflation rate. A startup named Premise has over 700 users installing their developed application to upload real-time different retail onion prices in various regions every day.

Co-founder David Soloff of the company believes this is an effective way to sense global financial dynamics in real-time because local stores generally adjust product prices promptly based on changes in the economic environment (including wholesale prices and consumer confidence factors).

"The analytical methods proposed by Premise have proven that they can predict inflation indicators 4-6 weeks ahead in some economic environments based on the collected data. There's no need to wait for the monthly 'economic weather forecast' anymore," Soloff emphasized.

For retail stores, the placement of brands on shelves directly affects sales. How to keep the brand in a good display position among moving customers requires both time and effort and is rather tedious.

To address this, a company named Quri has developed an application called EasyShift, encouraging users to contribute their time and effort to collect this data in exchange for compensation. Users just need to accept tasks assigned by the application, take photos of specified places at designated locations, and upload them to Quri's server to receive a small reward.

The concept of EasyShift is easy to understand: most users carry smartphones nowadays. Brand owners want to know how their products are displayed in large retail stores, evaluate competitors' dynamics, report out-of-stock products and pricing information, monitor promotions and product launches. EasyShift pays consumers to collect this information while they shop.

During the Great East Japan Earthquake, there was an unexpected use of a car brand's real-time visualized navigation data in the "Connecting Lifelines" project which opened up "green life-saving channels."

The project leader, Shun Sugino, is the senior director of the Dentsu Creative Design Center. Before the earthquake, he accepted a cooperation project from a car brand. This project recorded dynamic data such as which vehicles were driving on a particular road segment, at what time, at which latitude and longitude, and at what speed towards which direction, with approximately ten thousand pieces of data recorded per minute in a car navigation database. Sugino integrated these data into a program and presented them in the form of a Japanese map.

These navigation data could be temporarily utilized during the earthquake.

"During the earthquake, communication signals were not smooth, and people could only confirm the safety of their loved ones through the network. The challenge we faced was how to send rescue teams to the disaster area," said Sugino.

Navigation data were originally collected for traffic congestion situations to gather vehicle movement data. "From another perspective, having vehicle movement data indicates that the road is passable," Sugino explained. After the earthquake, once there was vehicle movement, it was marked in green to form a passage trajectory.

Meanwhile, the team also organized users in real-time on Twitter to post current road conditions and sign information across Japan. After integrating these two types of information, the green lifeline channel data were published online for public download 20 hours after the earthquake occurred. Besides the web version, mobile developers quickly created a mobile version. Under the crisis, the spread of information was extremely fast, and soon multiple green lines appeared on the website and mobile apps, providing references for rescue teams to arrive quickly.

Human intervention remains necessary in the era of big data

Machine learning indeed plays a dominant role in big data, but is human intervention really unnecessary? For example, you've already gotten used to the rampant online marketing around you, but do you really trust marketing recommendations based solely on mathematical models and large-scale data analysis?

ZestFinance is a platform that uses machine learning and big data analysis to provide customer quality analysis for the payday loan industry (short-term high-interest loans similar to usury).

Unlike traditional analysis methods, ZestFinance can simultaneously operate multiple models to analyze massive amounts of data to determine various possibilities. With increasing data sources and types, these pieces of information are converted into tens of thousands of measurable indicators for borrower behavior, such as fraud probabilities, long-term and short-term credit risks, and repayment capabilities. Finally, the results from each model are consolidated into a final result. This platform can provide the most reliable results for users in seconds. Founder Merrill said, "We prefer to combine machine learning mechanisms with human intervention."

For example, in the medical field, data analysis based on machine learning is far from sufficient. "Because machine learning can calculate a certain proportion of probabilities, but it cannot reach precision and accuracy," CTO of Chunyu Doctor Zhang Baoyi explained to Tencent Technology. For example, designing a disease model involves retrieving all questions with a similarity of over 90% from the existing database, analyzing and summarizing the results, creating a probability model for disease occurrence, and summarizing the advice given by doctors for each question to provide the ratio of "no major issues" and "go to the hospital" as intuitive data references for patients.

"But this is also taking a certain proportion of probabilities, used for self-checking by users. However, whether it can accurately match the patient's actual condition still requires human analysis (doctor diagnosis). Our data analysts in the background also need to recheck and verify the accuracy of the data," the person mentioned above said.
