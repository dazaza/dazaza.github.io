The Story Behind the QWERTY Keyboard

by geekzhang on 2013-05-08 13:19:14

Original in English: smithsonianmag, translated by:@SuWanZiFantasy or Reality? The Legend of the QWERTY KeyboardFigure 1: US Patent No. 207,559: The first appearance of the QWERTY keyboard (Image: Google Patents)Which came first: the typist or the keyboard? The answer depends on the keyboard. Recently, an article on the Smithsonian's news blog SmartNews described a new keyboard system that provides a more efficient alternative to the widely used QWERTY universal keyboard (named after the first six letters on the top row). This new type of keyboard called KALQ is specifically designed for thumb typing on today’s smartphones and tablets. This interesting and commercially viable design reminded me of the fundamental principles behind the QWERTY keyboard. Unlike the KALQ keyboard, it could not have been designed to adapt to any specific input method because touch typing had not yet been invented at that time. There seem to be many myths and misconceptions about QWERTY, but these different theories all appear to agree that the QWERTY layout developed alongside early typewriters and became inseparably linked.In the 1860s, Christopher Latham Sholes, a politician, printer, journalist, and amateur inventor from Milwaukee, spent his spare time developing various machines to make his work more efficient. One of them was an early typewriter he co-invented with Samuel W. Soul, James Densmore, and Carlos Glidden, which was first patented in 1868. The earliest typewriter keyboards resembled pianos and arranged 28 keys alphabetically. The inventors were quite confident that this arrangement was the most efficient way. After all, anyone using the keyboard would quickly learn where each letter was, reducing search time and increasing typing speed. Why change? This makes the emergence of QWERTY somewhat perplexing.Figure 2: Sholes & Glidden Typewriter around 1873 (Original image: The Typewriter World)It is commonly believed that Sholes had to redesign the keyboard to address mechanical issues with early typewriters, which were slightly different from the styles often seen in second-hand stores and flea markets. The print bars, connecting the keys to the character dials, hung semi-circularly beneath the paper. If users typed a series of letters whose print bars were close together too quickly, the delicate machine would jam. It is said that Sholes redesigned the key distribution to separate common letter sequences such as "th" or "he." Thus, theoretically speaking, the QWERTY system aimed to maximize the separation of frequently used letter pairs. This theory can be easily refuted since "er" is the fourth most common character pair in English. However, one of the prototypes of the typewriter was only slightly different at the last moment; if it had gone into production, this article would have been about the QWE.TY keyboard instead.Figure 3: The 1873 prototype proving Remington's technology (Original image: The Typewriter World)By 1873, the typewriter already had 43 keys and a counterintuitive letter arrangement intended to ensure that the expensive machine wouldn't break down. Ultimately, function determined form, and such a keyboard trained typists. In the same year, Sholes and his research partners reached a manufacturing agreement with the firearms manufacturer Remington, a well-equipped company with skilled experience in precision machinery manufacturing. With the arrival of the Civil War [Translator's Note 1], there was undoubtedly an expectation to turn swords into plowshares. However, just before their machine known as Sholes & Glidden went into production, Sholes submitted another patent that included a new keyboard layout. Published in 1878, Patent No. 207,559 (as shown in the figure above) marked the first documentation of the QWERTY layout. Cooperation with Remington proved to be a huge success. By 1890, over 100,000 Remington-made typewriters based on the QWERTY layout were in use nationwide. The fate of the keyboard was finally decided in 1893 when the five major typewriter manufacturers—Remington, Caligraph, Yost, Densmore, and Smith-Premier—merged to form the Union Typewriter Company and unanimously agreed to adopt QWERTY as the de facto standard we know and love today.(Translator's Note 1: The original text has a logical error here, as the Civil War occurred earlier than the invention of this machine.)There are some related speculations that the business strategy of Remington before the merger also contributed to the widespread use of the QWERTY layout. Remington did not just manufacture typewriters; they also provided training courses—for a small fee, of course. Typists trained in their proprietary system would remain loyal to the brand, so companies hoping to hire proficient typists needed to purchase Remington typewriters. This is a business model that still works today, as Apple's iTunes, iTunes Store, and iPod demonstrate.Despite the undeniable help that the deal with Remington brought to the expansion of QWERTY's usage, researchers from Kyoto University have long doubted that its development was a response to mechanical failures. In a 2011 paper, Koichi Yasuoka and Motoko Yasuoka traced the evolution of the typewriter keyboard based on records of its early professional users. They concluded that the mechanical structure of the typewriter did not influence the keyboard design. More likely, the emergence of the QWERTY system was a result of how the first typewriters were used. Early users and beta testers, including telegraph operators, needed to quickly transcribe messages. However, these operators found that arranging letters alphabetically caused confusion and inefficiency when decoding Morse code. The Kyoto University paper points out that the evolution of the typewriter keyboard over many years was a direct result of input from these telegraph operators, for example:The code representing Z was often confused with the more frequently used double-letter combination SE. Sometimes, American Morse code receivers had difficulty determining whether Z or SE was appropriate before receiving the remaining characters, especially in the initial letters of a word. Therefore, S should be placed near Z and E on the keyboard so that Morse code receivers could quickly type them (Similarly, C should be placed near I and E, but in fact, C is more often confused with S).In this scenario, the birth of the typist preceded the keyboard. The Kyoto University paper also cites the design principles of Morse code to further refute the claim that Sholes rearranged the keys to slow down typists to protect his machine from jamming:Of course, the speed of Morse code receivers should match the speed of senders. If Sholes indeed arranged the keyboard to slow down telegraph operators, the operators would not be able to keep up with the speed of Morse code senders. We do not think that Sholes had such meaningless intentions during the development of his typewriter.Even without considering how this keyboard was developed, Sholes himself was uncertain that QWERTY was the best system. Despite selling his design to Remington early on, he continued throughout his life to improve and replace the typewriter, including some keyboard layouts he believed were more efficient, as shown in the following patent, which Sholes filed in 1889 (the year before his death) and was published posthumously:Figure 4: US Patent No. 568,630, published after Sholes' death (Image: Google Patents)However, the biggest rival ever to challenge QWERTY was the Dvorak Simplified Keyboard, developed by Dr. August Dvorak in the 1920s.Figure 4: The Dvorak Simplified Keyboard (Image: Wikipedia)Users of the Dvorak keyboard reported faster and more accurate typing rates, partly because the system significantly increased the number of words that could be typed using the home row of keys, where your fingers rest naturally. It was also considered that you hit those keys when merely trying to fill spaces. asjdfkal, sdfjkl, asdfjkl, asdfjkl, dkadsf, asdfjklasdfjk. Recent studies have also debunked claims that the Dvorak keyboard is more efficient, but this is irrelevant. Even by 1930, it was too late for any new system to gain a foothold. The Dvorak keyboard certainly has its supporters, but it never gained enough users to overthrow the king, QWERTY. After all, the world learned to type on Remington keyboards.When the first computer keyboards were produced, there was no technical reason to continue using this system—computers don’t jam. But of course, millions of people had learned to use QWERTY keyboards, which were prevalent in countries using the Latin alphabet. Not only that, even by 1910, this system had already been adopted by Teletype Corporation, which would continue to produce widely used electronic typewriters and computer terminals worldwide, further ensuring QWERTY's status under the new technological standards.Figure 5: KALQ Keyboard Layout (Image: Outlasvirta et al.)When a design relies on previous innovations and becomes deeply rooted in cultural consciousness making it unchangeable, this is called path dependence. This is why the new KALQ proposal is so intriguing. It attempts to break away from the reign of Christopher Latham Sholes, whose QWERTY system makes even less sense on virtual keyboards on tablets and smartphones than on computer keyboards. What is different about the new KALQ system? In some ways, the answer is clearly yes. It is based on a very specific, modern behavior—thumb typing. Similar to the theory that telegraph operators gave rise to QWERTY, users determine the structure of the keyboard. However, the KALQ system, or any similar system that may emerge in the future, can still be considered a product of path dependence. Because regardless of how the letters are arranged, the basic concept of individually separated letters distributed in a grid can be traced back to the efforts of Sholes and his collaborators in the Milwaukee factory. But this isn't necessary on a tablet. If you give someone who has never used a keyboard an iPad and let them develop a writing system, they might end up inventing a faster, more intuitive system. Perhaps a system based on quick finger gestures? Or one that involves swiping to type? This is not to say that such a system would necessarily be better, just to observe that the most exciting technologies still trace back to some tinkerers in their garages over 150 years ago. Indeed, the more things change, the more they stay the same.
