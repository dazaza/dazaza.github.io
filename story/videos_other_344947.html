Analysis and Resolution of Heat Treatment Process Problems

There are many quality issues in the heat treatment process because most of these processes are cyclic operations involving multiple steps. For example, low hardness in carburized quenched gears, cracks in bar quenching, uneven hardness in box resistance furnace annealing, and control of trace alloy elements in cast iron quenching are some common problems. Below, Jiangsu Will Furnace Co., Ltd., based on years of experience in heat treatment operations, proposes some solutions to these problems for your reference.

Problem One: Low Hardness in Carburized Quenched Gears

A batch of over 800 gears carburized and quenched in a Unicase dropwise gas carburizing and nitriding furnace required a surface hardness of 58-63 HRC after carburizing and quenching. However, during random inspection, the parts' surface hardness was found to be 52-56 HRC. Was this a carburizing issue or a quenching issue? Was the quenching problem due to heating or cooling? It was hard to draw a conclusion at that time. Due to the urgent production schedule of this batch of gears, I selected three already inspected gears, tied them with wire, reheated them in a salt bath furnace, and then quenched them in an oil bath for about 30-40 minutes. The final hardness test showed 63-65 HRC. After reheating and re-quenching the entire batch of gears, all the hardness tests were qualified. This decisive approach may not have identified the root cause but did solve the immediate production urgency.

Problem Two: Cracks in Bar Quenching

A batch of φ14mm × 240mm 40Cr bars had undergone tempering treatment but were found to have almost all cracked after about a week (during use). The crack pattern was a longitudinal single crack, with most cracks penetrating through both ends of the bars. Based on this, it was judged to be a quenching crack, but the operators on duty denied responsibility. Checking the operation records only revealed that the batch of bars was quenched by the second shift and tempered by the third shift, with no records of material, quenching temperature, or cooling medium parameters. I took one bar and joined it with a 45 steel joint, heated it in a salt bath furnace, and then quenched it in brine. About 20-30 minutes after cooling, the bar cracked with the same crack pattern as described above. Faced with the facts, the operator admitted mistakenly treating the batch of bars as 45 steel for quenching.

Problem Three: Uneven Hardness in Box Resistance Furnace Annealing

The pump shaft billets of our company's vane pump, made of 38CrMoAlA material, follow the process route: annealing → band saw cutting → rough turning → tempering → finish turning → grinding → nitriding. During band saw cutting, it was often found that the hardness was uneven on a single billet, with localized high hardness, leading to low cutting efficiency and rapid wear of the saw blade. Analysis showed that this was due to improper loading where the billets were either too long or positioned too far forward. The entrance of the box resistance furnace lacks heating resistor wires and has significant heat loss. Therefore, for general box resistance furnaces, parts should be loaded at least 200-300mm inside the furnace entrance to ensure uniform heating temperature within the furnace.

Problem Four: Control of Trace Alloy Elements in Cast Iron Quenching

Cast iron has poor thermal conductivity, and oil is generally used for cooling during quenching. The matrix of cast iron is similar to steel, consisting of pearlite and ferrite. Although cast iron has a high carbon content, increasing carbon does not significantly enhance its hardenability. Therefore, improving the hardenability of cast iron components depends on the role of trace alloy elements in the cast iron, and controlling the content of these alloy elements ensures the quality of heat treatment quenching.

For the stator of our company's vane pump, made of wear-resistant alloy cast iron, a heat treatment hardness of 50-56 HRC is required. Due to poor control of alloy element contents such as Cr, Mo, Mn, and Sn in the casting, there are often issues like uneven hardness and low hardness after heat treatment quenching. Someone suggested that the low hardness after quenching was due to a low proportion of pearlite in the as-cast base structure, suggesting adding a normalizing step before quenching. However, experiments showed that even after normalizing, the hardness remained low. In fact, under identical casting conditions, the proportion of pearlite in the as-cast base structure correlates with the content of trace alloy elements.

Many issues that arise during the processing of parts are actually due to lax process control and disorganized production management. Jiangsu Will Furnace Co., Ltd. provides you with some ideas and methods to resolve these problems for your reference, though they may not be the best solutions.
