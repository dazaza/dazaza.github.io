Zeng Guofan Talks about Health Preservation

by oecsvrcg55 on 2012-03-05 13:20:18

This is an essay for a seminar class. The teacher said it should be written with publication in mind and suggested submitting it to a newspaper. But I really don't have the confidence to put it out there, yet I don't want it to just sit lonely on my hard drive, so I've decided to post it online. It's a pretty serious piece, not something I write often. Everyone can take a look; purely for entertainment.

Zeng Guofan was a loyal minister of the late Qing Dynasty. On the occasion of his 200th birthday, he has been given the honorary title by later generations as "the only scholar through the ages." Zeng Guofan placed great emphasis on personal cultivation, which manifested in various aspects such as reading, dealing with people, and handling affairs. In the field of health preservation, he also had some research. Amidst the increasingly popular "health craze" today, we might as well take a look at Zeng Guofan's approach to health preservation, the man known as the "first perfect person through the ages."

If one were to say that Zeng Guofan's process of personality refinement lasted his entire life, then his methods of health preservation could also be said to have been practiced throughout his life. He established five rules for himself regarding health preservation: First, maintain regular sleep and diet patterns; second, control anger; third, practice moderation; fourth, wash feet before sleeping every night; fifth, walk three thousand steps after each of the two meals daily.

The first, fourth, and fifth of these five rules are concepts that most modern people have, but whether they actually follow them is another question. Zeng practiced these three rules strictly. Take the first rule of maintaining regular sleep and diet patterns, for instance. He often said, "The way of health preservation should be carefully experienced in the two words 'eating' and 'sleeping.' Eating means normal daily meals, but if eaten with pleasure, it is better than precious medicine. Sleep does not require much time, but if one can concentrate and dream sweetly, even a short while is beneficial for health preservation." There are examples to prove this: Zeng Guofan was arguably the most diligent official of the late Qing Dynasty. Once, when investigating the distribution of relief grain in Shandong, he was asking important details about government account records from local officials when he realized he hadn't eaten yet. Without hesitation, he went with the local officials to the county office dining hall to eat. Even if work was busy and the food was hard to swallow, he did not forget to eat at mealtime. Even eating just a sweet potato, he ensured he ate something at the right time. Zeng once wrote in his diary, "Maintaining regular sleep and washing feet are things that Star Gang practiced for over forty years, and I have been learning and practicing for seven years. Walking three thousand steps after meals has been tried recently, and I vow never to interrupt this practice." This shows Zeng's consistent adherence to these health preservation methods that align with modern health concepts. Not only did Zeng require himself to exercise daily to strengthen his muscles and bones, but he also encouraged his family members to practice together. Zeng once said in a family letter, "Seeing that the younger generation at home is physically weak," he advised his descendants to "walk a thousand steps after each meal, wash feet before sleeping, regularly practice archery (which helps cultivate decorum and strengthens muscles and bones, suitable for young people), and eat plain rice at dawn without any side dishes." In a letter to his son Ji Ze, he kindly wrote, "Ji Ze's body is also weak, so I teach him to focus on improving his sleep and diet. Sleep nourishes yin, and diet nourishes yang. Nourishing sleep requires having a fixed time and avoiding excessive thoughts; nourishing diet also requires having a fixed time and avoiding overeating." Beyond health preservation, this also reflects Zeng's loving care and education for his grandchildren, showing his painstaking efforts.

The second rule of controlling anger and the third rule of practicing moderation have some elements of Neo-Confucianism and philosophy. Controlling anger means minimizing anger, which is similar to the widely circulated folk poem "Don't Get Angry," but comparing the two perspectives on "anger," they are different. "Don't Get Angry" mainly explains that getting angry harms oneself and pleases others, thus persuading people not to get angry. Zeng Guofan's method of controlling anger focuses on spiritual adjustment, encouraging people to maintain a positive mental state, not just when they are angry, but also in everyday life, adjusting their mood naturally, achieving "controlling anger" through calmness and stillness. This is a method practiced regularly. Regarding practicing moderation, Zeng Guofan had a unique and interesting perspective. He believed that strong people are like rich people who become richer by refraining from extravagance; weak people are like poor people who gradually enrich themselves by being frugal. Zeng's uniqueness doesn't stop here. His concept of "practicing moderation" doesn't solely refer to restraining food and sexual desires. For example, although he repeatedly emphasized to his family that "reading cultivates my righteous spirit," he also believed that reading should be restrained and not excessive. From a modern perspective, not everyone may understand or agree with this, as it carries some traces of "Li" (Neo-Confucian) thought. However, upon further reflection, the rule of practicing moderation corresponds to the concept of "degree" in Chinese morality. Perhaps the key to practicing moderation lies in the word "degree."

After pondering over Zeng's five health preservation methods that span 200 years, under the modern medical and health preservation concepts, they still hold certain value. If we could strictly follow these health preservation methods like Zeng did and encourage those around us to join in, the physical and mental health indicators of the great Chinese people would be quite impressive. But, how many people can actually achieve this? This is the wisdom of Zeng Guofan being called the "first perfect person through the ages."
