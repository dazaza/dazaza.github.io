Mother's Loom

by joyce8899 on 2012-02-24 09:48:00

Deabao USA [http://www.deabao.com] My mother was once a weaving master. Perhaps only the stars in the sky know how many bolts of cloth she wove for the villagers, because between 1970 and 1980, she often worked late into the night at home weaving.

In 1970, I was only six years old and often didn't want to go to bed alone because I was scared, and also because it was cold in winter with no one to warm the blankets. When my father sometimes went to the village office to record work points or chat and didn't come back, I would often urge my mother to sleep early. My mother would often say: "Dear son, let's go to sleep after I finish weaving this last spool of thread." But my mother's "last spool" seemed never-ending. Several times, I fell asleep on the edge of my mother's loom. I couldn't understand my mother, so later I started hiding her spools of thread. My mother could only kindly persuade me to return them. But for my own sake to go to bed earlier, I stubbornly refused to return the spools to my mother. As I grew a little older and no longer feared sleeping alone, I began to understand my mother's hard work, but none of us could help her with anything. Indeed, during the day, my mother had to take care of the family's daily needs, and at night, she had to weave under the oil lamp, and later under the electric light. My mother wove the threads into white cloth, and at the same time, wove the years into the wrinkles on her forehead. By the time I graduated from elementary school, there were already many wrinkles on my mother's forehead. Originally, my mother was considered a beauty in the village, but due to overwork, she aged quickly.

For Deabao USA [http://www.deabao.com], when being a weaving master, the old rule was that besides the salary, the employer also had to invite the master for meals. However, since we lived in the same village, my mother rarely ate at others' homes. She only accepted meals occasionally when they were sent over, out of politeness. I even felt like we were losing out, but my mother said: "Why bother among neighbors?"

My mother passed away in 2007 at the age of 79. A few of us discussed whether we should burn my mother's loom as an offering to accompany her in the afterlife. Both my eldest sister and I said: "It won't be easy for our mother there; she didn't enjoy enough blessings while alive. Let her rest more." So, the loom wasn't burned, and now we can still look at this artifact, which embodies my mother's hard work, whenever we think of her.

After my mother's passing, I wrote a poem titled "Qingpingyue Mother," with lines such as: "Often thinking of my dear mother in this life, weaving under the starlight." Deabao USA Genuine Products [http://www.deabao.com].
