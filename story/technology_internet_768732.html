Lenovo's Liu Chuanzhi on internet agriculture: selling agricultural products like Lei Jun sells Xiaomi phones

by anonymous on 2013-11-20 11:56:10

On November 18, Joyvio Group under Lenovo Holdings announced its entry into the wine industry. Joyvio Group partnered with France's Jean-Pierre Moueix Group and Chile’s Emiliana Organic Vineyards, with Joyvio providing China's sales channels, raw materials (grapes), and brand support, while Jean-Pierre Moueix and Emiliana are responsible for wine production. Together, they aim to integrate global resources and align with China's consumer market.

Joyvio Group is one of Lenovo Holdings' six pillars, alongside Legend Capital (formerly Legend Investment), Lenovo Group, Digital China, Hony Capital, and Rongke Real Estate.

Through Legend Capital and Hony Capital, Lenovo Holdings has entered various traditional industries such as manufacturing, energy, and tourism. Joyvio Group operates independently because Liu Chuanzhi places agriculture on par with Lenovo Group and Digital China in terms of importance.

On the same day, Liu Chuanzhi, chairman of Lenovo Holdings, summarized in an exclusive interview with the 21st Century Business Herald that there are at least three reasons why Lenovo is vigorously developing the agricultural industry: first, new technology—beyond modern agricultural technology, computing and internet technology will be integrated into traditional agriculture; second, policy trends favor the upgrading and transformation of the agricultural industry; third, China will become the world's largest demander of agricultural products, making it the right time to connect China's market with global resources.

Liu Chuanzhi believes that due to the influence of small-scale peasant economy based on households, China's agricultural industry faces at least two opportunities for technological upgrades: the upgrade of modern agricultural technology and the upgrade of internet technology.

Besides wine, Joyvio Group has already launched products including kiwifruit (named "Liu Peach" after Liu Chuanzhi, establishing the world's second-largest kiwifruit base in Sichuan) and blueberries from Chile.

The New Internet Agriculture

How does the internet transform agriculture? Liu Chuanzhi believes that the first step is to transform sales, and in the future, selling agricultural products should be like Lei Jun selling Xiaomi phones.

Liu Chuanzhi believes that in terms of market sales, the internet will play a crucial role, possibly even surpassing current electronic consumer goods. For example, sending fruit to respect elders or friends can involve gifting a 1000 yuan fruit voucher. Through Joyvio's official website, Joyvio products can be purchased anytime, with online orders, settlements, and direct delivery via courier services to homes, which is much better than directly buying fruit.

Joyvio Group currently does not have its own e-commerce website, but Liu Chuanzhi stated that building one in the future is not ruled out. Liu Chuanzhi said: Building a website to sell fruit would be effortless for Joyvio.

Sun Shaopeng, president of Joyvio Group, believes that cloud computing technology and terminal computing technology can be applied to agricultural product production: through IoT technology, data such as temperature and humidity in the soil and air can be collected to understand conditions in real-time and make timely decisions. Besides production, during storage and operations, internet, cloud computing, and big data technologies also need to be applied.

In the process, Liu Chuanzhi believes that "knowing how to cooperate with grassroots rural organizations" is very important. Joyvio needs local governments to help create a land transfer environment in each location. If negotiations are done door-to-door, transferring just a few acres per household makes it difficult to achieve scale. The government, recognizing that Joyvio can help local development, will assist in this work, especially village committees that communicate easily with farmers.

Lenovo provides some start-up funds, the government mobilizes banks to provide loans, and the national inclusive agricultural support policies that Lenovo helps farmers apply for reports. With farmers' approval, these preliminary groundwork tasks can be completed jointly by Lenovo and the government.

Farmers benefit by receiving rent income from land transfers and can work in Joyvio's orchards to earn wages, becoming industrialized farmers.

After all these things are done, Lenovo introduces a complete set of management methods, agricultural technologies, and standards (such as quality standards and production process standards) into the orchard to realize agricultural modernization.

Liu Chuanzhi particularly mentioned corporate farmers: one farmer around whom multiple farmers have gone to cities for business or work abroad can entrust their land to the remaining person. Once this person accumulates 1000 acres, Joyvio Group can collaborate with them on a company-to-company basis, sharing profits. Other farmers either receive direct rent or profit-sharing.

Similarly, Lenovo hands over the enterprise to the corporate farmer, ensuring that they are competent in management ideas, technical means, and operational capabilities as "modern agricultural entrepreneurs," transforming agriculture through corporate farmers.

Sun Shaopeng said: It is too difficult to make a profit with too small a scale; 1000 acres is considered a relatively appropriate size.

New Business

Liu Chuanzhi observes agriculture from both technical evolution, land policy perspectives, and business considerations: whether a private enterprise like Lenovo can do agriculture hinges on whether it can make a profit. If doing so results in losses without profitability or involves excessive risk, it cannot be done.

The first reason it can be done is due to the vast technical space for agricultural transformation. Previous land policies themselves were based on families, dividing land into small plots where modern agricultural technology could not enter.

Liu Chuanzhi believes that compared to the PC manufacturing industry, China's agricultural technology lags further behind developed countries.

The second reason is due to changes in land policy, especially some new policy trends from the Third Plenary Session, such as land ownership and transfer, suitable for large-scale operation. Under this premise, modern technology, including PC technology, will integrate with agriculture, creating new forms of agriculture.

Another policy trend is that the state will reduce approval procedures, benefiting Lenovo's global purchase of agricultural resources, including land resources, technical resources, and advanced agricultural technology from developed countries that may be acquired through acquisitions; if resources are cheaper, they might also be obtained through acquisitions. The advantage of this approach is using China's market to connect with global resources, meeting China's increasingly expanding consumer demand.

Sun Shaopeng introduced that Joyvio Group purchased land in Chile, where an acre costs only more than 3000 yuan, and it is permanent ownership, much cheaper and more fertile than in China. The organic matter content of Chilean land is at least 5%-6% at its worst and up to 12% at its best, whereas China's average organic matter content is only 0.2%-0.3%.

Another benefit is the supply of off-season food: when winter comes to China and fruits are out of season, it happens to be summer in Chile, which is the fruit season. Shipping to China can fetch a good price. Sun Shaopeng said: Now Joyvio Group has blueberries in Chile, and on November 17, Joyvio Blueberries held a promotion event in Shanghai.

Liu Chuanzhi said that China, in many fields, like in the PC field, has become the world's largest consumer country.

The third reason is market demand. Liu Chuanzhi believes that a significant portion of people are willing to pay a premium for safe food, and Joyvio has the capability to invest in building a safe food brand.

Liu Chuanzhi believes that "agriculture is a business that Lenovo should undertake," not something any company can do. Agriculture differs from other businesses; it cannot provide quick returns. A private enterprise must reach a certain size, have sufficient investment capabilities, including continuous investment capabilities, to engage in such endeavors.
