CCTV: Who decides for my mobile phone?

by anonymous on 2013-10-15 20:58:54

Following the CCTV program "The Risk of Pre-installation," they now investigate whether mobile phone manufacturers and refurbishing companies closely related to the pre-installation of mobile applications are still engaged in such business. At a Samsung Electronics repair outlet in Beijing, service personnel told reporters that pre-installed software on Samsung phones still cannot be uninstalled.

Samsung Electronics repair staff: There's no way, it can't be deleted or uninstalled.

Reporter: After the TV report a few days ago, did Samsung have any response?

Staff: I'm not aware of anything.

Reporter: It still can't be uninstalled, right?

Staff: Yes.

This staff member also reminded reporters that if individuals change their phone permissions for uninstallation, the phone will no longer be eligible for warranty services. Subsequently, reporters contacted customer service from several domestic mobile phone manufacturers and were informed that most of the pre-installed software in their products cannot be uninstalled either. A mobile internet operations staff member from a domestic phone manufacturer revealed that their pre-installed orders are already booked up until 2014, leaving no space for new software.

Mobile Internet Operations Staff: Now there's actually no room left; we can only look around because we have some exclusive agreements. Our agreements go until May next year, meaning changes (to new software) won't be possible until then.

Reporters learned from industry insiders that several large-scale refurbishing companies remain very busy. Reporters contacted a refurbishing company in Shenzhen featured in last week's program.

Reporter: Can you do this software pre-installation now?

Refurbishing Company Staff: Yes, we can.

Reporter: Can you refurbish some domestic brand phones?

Refurbishing Company Staff: We can only handle Samsung's full series and most Lenovo models right now.

After further investigation, reporters found that the mobile app pre-installation business remains highly active. In several mobile phone sales locations in Beijing, reporters discovered many newly launched phones, including the much-anticipated Samsung Note 3. Does this new model come with pre-installed software?

Reporter: The Note 2 has too many apps inside. How about the Note 3?

Salesperson: Same.

Reporter: Can it be uninstalled?

Salesperson: Yes, rooting allows for uninstallation.

Reporter: Doesn’t rooting void the warranty?

If users alter phone permissions by rooting, they forfeit warranty services. This means that pre-installed software on Samsung's latest product is still difficult to remove. This salesperson revealed that the pre-installed software in the Samsung Note 3 is similar to the Note 2, with around 15-6 different types. Investigations showed that mobile app pre-installation has become an extremely common phenomenon in the industry, with the majority of smartphone brands competing fiercely in the pre-installation field.

A domestic internet institution conducted a sampling survey on pre-installed apps for 10 related models of smartphones from Apple, Samsung, Lenovo, HTC, LG, Coolpad, Huawei, etc. The results showed: only 3 brands had no pre-installed apps, 2 brands allowed users to uninstall pre-installed apps, while the remaining 5 brands forcibly pre-installed apps that could not be uninstalled. Reporters found that in a network survey involving 13,386 consumers, as many as 12,839 people indicated that they rarely used pre-installed apps, with only 154 people using them regularly.

Mobile App Security Expert Tang Wei: Users should actually have complete control over their phones. When users purchase a phone, they haven't been asked if they agree to install certain software. Without this condition, once users don't want to use or dislike certain products and aren't allowed to uninstall them, it becomes unreasonable.

After the September 29 broadcast of "The Risk of Pre-installation" on the "Weekly Quality Report" program, some consumers reported that their HTC phones came with as many as 48 pre-installed apps, causing significant inconvenience. Reporters learned that when phones initially leave the factory, the number of pre-installed apps is not high. So, how do pre-installed apps increase so dramatically before reaching consumers? Reporters contacted an insider who was previously involved in mobile app pre-installation, revealing the secret behind increasing pre-installed apps. This insider told reporters that well-known phone brands typically wouldn't choose obscure third-party software for pre-installation. Besides installing applications for strategic partners' customized phones, they generally prefer widely-used and well-known software to facilitate user convenience.

Pre-installation Insider: For example, branded weather apps or big browser providers are pre-installed and cannot be uninstalled.

This insider revealed that these apps are usually pre-installed at the factory stage, and the quantity is minimal. However, reporters observed that Samsung phones mostly come with 10-20 pre-installed apps, including lesser-known ones like novel reading platforms or game halls. The insider explained that after the phone leaves the factory, there are multiple stages where software is added.

Pre-installation Insider: Samsung has many such agents. For example, national distributors, second and third-tier agents, these agents receive the phones and usually keep them overnight. They can't immediately transport or sell the phones, so they bulk-load software into the phones.

Reporter: Can these added apps be uninstalled?

Pre-installation Insider: Generally, yes.

Reporter: But are there some that cannot be uninstalled?

Pre-installation Insider: Yes, there are.

Reporter: At the level of senior agents, how many apps are added?

Pre-installation Insider: Usually, 10 or 20 apps are loaded, with different software packages.

Thus, mobile phone manufacturers, various refurbishing companies, and agents all focus on the pre-installation market, layering more installations, which often confuses consumers with numerous apps upon receiving their new phones. The reporter observed that a Samsung phone had over 20 pre-installed apps, with three SMS apps alone, leading to repeated notifications of the same message, frustrating users.

Data shows that China's Android system application software approaches 300,000, and with the rapid growth of smartphones, application promotion inevitably targets this vast platform. An interest chain formed by software vendors, phone manufacturers, agents, sellers, and refurbishers gradually emerged, making pre-installation increasingly rampant. Insiders revealed that besides activation fees for pre-installed apps, there exists a profit-sharing model. Software vendors usually share profits in a 70-30 split, giving 70% of the profits to advertisers responsible for pre-installation. These insiders emphasized that e-commerce companies benefit significantly from this billing model, with mobile revenue accounting for 30-40% of total income.

Pre-installation Insider: Many e-commerce companies earn about 30%-40% of their annual billion-yuan profit from mobile ends.

Such profit margins encourage software vendors to pursue pre-installation business, while phone manufacturers and various agents naturally accept all pre-installation orders. For many counterfeit phone brands, pre-installation has even become the primary profit channel. Insiders mentioned that some counterfeit phones costing 300 yuan might sell at a loss for 200 yuan due to the profit-sharing from pre-installation. The existence of this pre-installation interest chain leads to the phenomenon of hard-to-uninstall apps, protecting the interests of collaborators but restricting consumer rights. Insiders disclosed that compared to battery drain, data consumption, memory occupation, and poor user experience, pre-installation poses a more serious threat through covert fee deductions.

Pre-installation Insider: Covert fee deductions occur through different channels, such as counterfeit phone or refurbishing channels. Software is quietly installed without users noticing. It runs stealthily, sending fee-deducting messages to operators, secretly deducting money from phone bills.

Insiders revealed that such software usually appears as tool or call-making apps, like compasses or free-call apps, designed specifically for fee deduction. Another type is legitimate software controlled by viruses, like weather apps, which add billing codes to secretly charge users.

Pre-installation Insider: If it deducts 10 yuan, the operator takes 3 yuan, leaving 7 yuan. Then, the 7 yuan is split 70-30.

Reporter: Who splits the 70-30 ratio?

Pre-installation Insider: The 7 yuan is split 70-30, giving 4.9 yuan to the refurbisher and 2.1 yuan to the developer.

Insiders told reporters that the scale of "covert fee deduction" by pre-installed apps is enormous and highly detrimental. These apps usually run covertly, charging small amounts like 3-5 yuan per instance, making it hard for users to detect. Malicious fee-deducting apps disguised as normal service apps may even block communication carriers' fee-notification messages, making detection even harder.

Pre-installation Insider: After the program runs in the background, you don't see or know that your money has been deducted. Many people don't check their phone bill monthly, so they might have been charged extra fees for years without realizing it.

This insider revealed that the amount of "covert fee deduction" by pre-installed apps is alarming, posing a major security risk to mobile internet and a huge trap for many phone users. During investigations, reporters found that besides the significant harm of "covert fee deduction," pre-installed apps also pose potential threats to user privacy. Many pre-installed apps require permission to access location information, SMS, calls, photos, etc., during updates. This dictionary app requires access to users' photos and videos, read phone status and identity, and have full internet access. Many phone users expressed that such domineering apps are common, and refusing to provide these permissions makes the apps unusable. Users believe such bundled requirements threaten personal privacy.

During investigations, reporters found that most people consider pre-installed apps on new phones unreasonable and unnecessary. However, phone manufacturers and agents ignore user feelings, driven by profit motives, continuing to pre-install numerous apps. Is there no one regulating this behavior deemed unreasonable by consumers?

In April, the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology issued a notice requiring phone manufacturers not to pre-install five categories of malicious software, including those collecting or modifying user personal information without consent, consuming data and causing losses without consent, and affecting the normal function of mobile terminals or network security. This ban is set to start monitoring pre-installation behaviors from November 2013. However, reporters noticed that there are currently no clear legal provisions or regulatory measures targeting pre-installation practices severely impacting phone experiences or usability. Consumer protection experts believe consumers should soon regain the right to freely choose.

Lang Dankuo, Complaint Legal Affairs Department of the Beijing Consumers' Association: This actually infringes on consumers' right to be informed, meaning users are not informed beforehand about the functions or characteristics of these (software) products, nor about the possibility of incurring charges or data usage. Consumers should have the choice to use or not use these apps, and this must be a clear operation. Consumers should have a clear way to disable features they don't need.

Experts believe relevant government departments should strengthen supervision, combat covert fee deductions and privacy theft in pre-installed apps, promote self-discipline in each link of pre-installation, and push for the establishment of relevant standards to jointly serve consumers in terms of basic application experience and rights protection.

Lang Dankuo: All parties involved, including our current operators, phone manufacturers, and third-party software designers, should ideally establish industry standards and regulations for software design (pre-installation) through industry organizations and self-regulation. Through self-regulation, we can standardize the rights and obligations of all operational parties.

Experts believe that in the issue of mobile app pre-installation, phone production and operation enterprises should exercise self-discipline, respect consumers' choice and usage experience. However, from the current situation, many phone operation enterprises have clearly not achieved this kind of self-discipline. A large number of pre-installed apps still appear in users' phones, possibly consuming data, wasting costs, and bringing risks such as malicious fee deductions and privacy leaks. Under such circumstances, regulators in the mobile phone industry should address this issue by introducing stricter and more effective regulatory measures to better protect consumers' legitimate rights and interests.
