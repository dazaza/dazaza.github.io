Unveiling India's Mars Mission: NASA's Influence

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 20:49:15

The Indian Space Research Organization successfully launched a Mars probe, the Mangalyaan, at 2:38 PM local time on November 5 (5:08 PM Beijing time), from the Satish Dhawan Space Center on Sriharikota Island. This marks the beginning of a 400 million kilometer journey to Mars. Of course, the probe needs to spend about a month in Earth's orbit to accelerate. If the Mangalyaan mission succeeds, India will become the first Asian country to complete a Mars exploration mission. In terms of global Mars exploration rankings, it will rise to fourth place, after the United States, the Soviet Union, and the European Union. Below are some facts from the Indian Space Research Organization about this Mars exploration mission:

1. The Mangalyaan is the first probe that the Indian Space Research Organization has sent beyond the Earth-Moon system.

2. Scientists predict that the Mangalyaan will reach Mars orbit around September 2014. If the probe successfully enters Mars orbit and completes equipment adjustments, it will begin investigating the Martian atmosphere, studying trace gases like methane related to life.

3. Behind the successful launch of the Mangalyaan was the support of an extensive network of ground tracking stations working in "relay."

4. The PSLV C25 rocket took more than 40 minutes to execute this mission, which differs from previous PSLV rocket missions.

5. Observation stations near Bangalore and Port Blair in India, as well as tracking stations in Indonesia and Brunei, participated in this launch tracking task.

6. The tracking platform located in the South Pacific consists of two Indian SCI ships equipped with 4.6-meter and 1.8-meter antennas, tasked with tracking the Mars probe.

7. Once the probe enters orbit, it will separate and be discovered by both ground-based and sea-based tracking stations. Stations from NASA’s JPL laboratory located in Goldstone (USA), Madrid (Spain), and Canberra (Australia) have also joined the mission team.

8. The Mangalyaan Mars probe carries five scientific instruments: the Methane Sensor for Mars (MSM), the Lyman Alpha Photometer (LAP), the Mars Exospheric Neutral Composition Analyzer (MENCA), the Mars Color Camera (MCC), and the Thermal Infrared Imaging Spectrometer (TIS).

9. The main task of the Mangalyaan Mars probe is to study the Martian atmosphere. The MENCA will investigate particles in the Martian atmosphere, while the MCC and TIS will facilitate imaging of the Martian surface.

10. In earlier stages of the Mars probe mission, the Indian Space Research Organization screened 33 different star-borne instruments, narrowing them down to 9 in the first stage, and finally selecting 5 instruments due to the small size and limited space of the probe, along with lower mission requirements.

11. The Mangalyaan Mars probe weighs 1.33 tons, carrying 852 kg of fuel and 15 kg of scientific instruments, and is expected to reach Mars orbit on September 14, 2014.

12. According to NASA statistics, although there have been 51 Mars exploration missions globally so far, only 21 of them have been successful.
