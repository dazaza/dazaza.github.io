1. Check the range of motion in the cervical spine: Slowly rotate your head in all directions and see if there is any pain in the neck. This is one of the diagnostic methods for cervical spondylosis.

2. Check the problematic areas in the cervical spine: Slightly lower your head, starting from the most prominent seventh cervical vertebra and moving upwards, gently press on the cervical spine and its left and right sides. If there is tenderness or if you feel hard lumps that resemble cords or sand particles, it may indicate an issue with the cervical spine. Dizziness when rotating the head and neck is a characteristic of this condition, also known as cervicogenic dizziness. This happens because the rotation of the head primarily occurs between C1-C2, where the vertebral artery is most easily compressed during head turning.

1. Vertebral artery torsion test: For diagnosing cervical spondylosis, the patient slightly tilts their head back, then rotates their head left and right. If dizziness occurs, it is considered positive.

X-ray, CT, and MRI examinations of the cervical spine reveal issues such as hypertrophy of the uncovertebral joints, narrowed intervertebral foramina, vertebral instability, and cervical disc herniation. Vertebral angiography can show narrowing or twisting of the vertebral artery, which is also a diagnostic method for cervical spondylosis.
