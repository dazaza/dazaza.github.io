The Blood and Tears Lessons for Entrepreneurs Left by Google Wave

by geekzhang on 2013-05-12 10:42:12

May 12th, Beijing Time - The once immensely popular Google Wave ultimately could not escape its fate of being discontinued, ending in a disheartening manner. While it's easy to lament its downfall, entrepreneurs should instead take this as a lesson to avoid repeating the same mistakes:1. Focus your energy on doing one great thing.Wave was a classic example of an engineering-driven product that incorporated almost every possible feature. However, for startups, adding features without careful consideration can be extremely dangerous. Just because something can be done or is easy for engineers, doesn't mean it should be implemented. This kind of thinking has become a silent killer for many startups. Each feature may seem beneficial at first glance, but when pieced together, they can lead to rapid deterioration:The product becomes unclear: As an entrepreneur, one of the biggest challenges you face is selling a clear product to a clear buyer. If you add too many features just because "we can," you risk losing focus and diminishing the sharp edge of what made your product stand out.Maintenance turns into a nightmare: Those seemingly trivial and harmless features might appear cost-free initially, but over time, as the product scales, these mistakes will exact a heavy toll. Customer support, code complexity, and future burdensome Q&A sessions are just some of the headaches you'll encounter.Therefore, my advice is to carefully consider features that are added simply because "we can." Treat them like cancer cells in software, and do everything possible to stop their spread. If you accidentally develop such a feature, don't hesitate—delete it immediately to purify the feature set. Google Wave tried to do too much, expanding far too broadly, and in the process, lost the clean simplicity that made it appealing.2. Don’t position your product as an X-killer.When Wave was launched, people lavished praise on it, calling it the "email killer." The Google employees involved with Wave repeatedly emphasized how outdated email was and confidently believed that Wave would replace it. However, things didn't turn out as expected. Wave never became the "email killer" but rather a decent team collaboration tool. The problem was that the latter wasn't exciting enough or grand enough to justify continued effort, so Wave was defined as the "email killer."Having a mission to become a "killer" product can distract you and even lead to poor product roadmaps. So-called "Google killer" search engines or "Facebook killer" social networks often find their dreams lofty but reality harsh—they fail to unseat leaders and end up killing themselves in the process.3. Avoid overly hyped explosive launches.This point may require further examination, but I've always been wary of excessively exaggerated product launches, which can be very dangerous for startups. Typically, overhyped launches rarely bring long-term value—just like the Digg storm, which quickly surged onto the internet scene only to fade away just as fast. It created unrealistic expectations among users, employees, and investors.Excessive hype around a product launch can easily lead to overwhelming disappointment later on, which is devastating for startups.Avoid hype like the plague—focus instead on celebrating small victories and concentrate on core business growth week by week, month by month. Although the journey may be long, it's more sustainable and enduring.If the hype comes knocking, bury yourself in the sandbox and ignore it. Tell everyone in the company who has heard about it—employees, investors, etc.—to spend two minutes of their day talking about the hype, then get back to work and forget about it. Ignore the hype in reports and use all feasible methods to forget about it. If you start believing the hype, it means you're about to run into trouble.4. The most important final pointIn your own playground, you can beat Google. Google does many things right, but that doesn't mean they're completely immune to competition and failure. For startups, Google's advantages may seem overwhelming—loads of money, a congregation of geniuses, a strong brand effect, etc. But believe me, if you apply a few strategies from "Moneyball" (highly recommended reading for entrepreneurs), you'll realize that as a startup, you have so many enviable advantages over those arrogant giant companies, and this isn't a joke. You can focus on small, precise entry points that large companies disdain and strive to become the king in that niche.
