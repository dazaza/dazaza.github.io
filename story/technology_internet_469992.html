A trader's mental journey that ordinary people cannot perceive.

by tuoqing1188 on 2011-08-03 13:13:34

Macro and micro are indeed almost perfectly integrated in speculative practice, no longer a fusion of philosophy and height. A friend of mine (or you could say my teacher), has indeed reached the realm of "winning without fighting" in trading, which I couldn't imagine before. Because I value details and technical routines at the micro level very much, that is what we often refer to as a "trading system." Through studying his trading decisions, I found that truly integrating all techniques (traditional analytical techniques, indicators formulas, waves and Gann theory, time cycles, trend following) into one is entirely achievable. However, it requires a very painful process of thinking, refining, rethinking, and refining again. Most of us, either due to lack of insight or perseverance, 99% of people cannot achieve their sublimation and transformation in the speculative market. This process, I think, calling it "alchemy" may not be quite fitting. I prefer to liken it to a "metamorphosis" process, painful and difficult as it may be. If we are committed to making professional speculation our ideal and career, then we must complete this possibly long transformation.

One of my realizations is that in the future, I will no longer be satisfied with improving and exploring the technical details of my trading system. I must spend more time on what some might even consider "meaningless or wasted" thought exercises. This abstract thinking is not just about achieving a certain level or broad strokes; this abstraction must be built upon many fine details, surpassing归纳and synthesis to reach a qualitative improvement. I don’t know if what I’m saying resonates with everyone. I once again feel the inadequacy of language, but I know I must persevere through the ambiguity within language and achieve a true breakthrough in my life and career.

I have fully recognized the difficulty, complexity, and uncertainty of such a breakthrough. I also know that there is a possibility I may never achieve this breakthrough in my lifetime. But I know that the direction my heart points to is the one that transcends speculation, traders, and even the market, almost approaching the way of life. Regardless of whether I can reach it, at least, I must calibrate my direction, aim for it, and take my steps forward.

People often say that in the futures market, discipline and mental control outweigh everything else. However, I must point out that the premise of all this is that you must have a well-established, market-tested trading system, otherwise, it risks becoming mere talk.

In my own practice, after designing and perfecting my trading system, my mindset naturally became more stable and confident. You will inevitably follow the signals indicated by your system with the strictest discipline because violating it would place you in a very disadvantageous position. When you and your trading system are fully aligned and you are confident that your trading system can successfully and sensitively handle most market changes, you will find that making money through trading is actually an easy, enjoyable, natural, and effortless process. Everything becomes harmonious, and you will eventually distance yourself from and bid farewell to losses—this is my personal experience.

Actually, these experiences of mine are useless for friends who are new to the speculative industry, or you might say, they are empty words. But my advice to friends is that you must establish a trading system suitable for yourself. No matter how bruised and battered you get by the market, you must stand up again and improve your system. Do not fear complexity and intricacy, because this is the only way to success and away from losses. Discipline and mentality must be built upon a scientifically effective system, which can be based on technical analysis, fundamental analysis, or other methods like Yi Jing prediction technology. I always believe that feelings cannot be relied upon long-term. Only an objective system can ensure you have an objective mindset.

A professional Hong Kong Hang Seng Index futures trader I know has survived in the Hang Seng Index market for nearly ten years. He mentioned that among those who used to trade the Hang Seng Index with him, some have changed careers, others have gone bankrupt. Among those he knows who entered this market with him and still survive in it today, he is the only one left. The power of time is stronger than we can imagine. Being right for a year or two and making profits for a year or two is not surprising. Only through the long-term processes of washing, polishing, and elimination can your true speculative talent be revealed. His computer-based trading system, established on a mathematical model designed by himself, is still being improved and corrected to this day. From him, I see the "professionalism" inherent in a "professional trader." Compared to him, I am merely a basketball enthusiast, while he is already a professional athlete playing in the NBA. It was only when I sat back in the car that I realized the integral and differential effects of time have just begun to show on me.

Actually, every professional trader understands the importance of a trading system. I recently had an idea to document my experience of establishing my trading system over the past five years, hoping it could provide some inspiration for everyone to eventually build their own system.

Establishing a mature, usable, consistent system that has truly been tested by the market, I believe, will go through two stages.

1. Accumulation

What I call accumulation is probably akin to the "accumulation" part of "thick accumulation and thin release." In this accumulation phase, it is the stage of storing a large amount of knowledge, skills, and methods, emphasizing the maximum possible effort to widely possess, absorb everything, and even promote the spirit of swallowing things whole, not seeking deep understanding, and tasting all herbs like Shen Nong, gathering all materials you can collect and depositing them in your brain for later use.

Where does the accumulation come from? Of course, primarily from books. You must have the ambition to sweep through all financial bookstores, without too strong a focus but with wide coverage, reading various financial and economic books both domestically and internationally. If possible, it's best to ask experienced hands for a reading list, so you can read more targeted and efficiently. This is somewhat similar to what the ancients said about "reading three hundred Tang poems thoroughly." My experience is to start with Western classic technical analysis texts, first creating a list of about twenty books. Those with high English proficiency should ideally read the original versions directly, using the simplest serial method to eliminate them one by one. The entire process allows for no shortcuts, though it is dull and lengthy, it is irreplaceable and unskippable. Moreover, for good books, you should read them repeatedly until you are extremely familiar with them.

The accumulation process uses memory and a small amount of reasoning more. Your primary task is to remember the methods and doctrines in the books, without needing too much skepticism or subjective initiative. These two aspects only become relevant after you have a certain amount of accumulation. If you can easily obtain the top two levels of evaluation in any standard IQ test abroad, I think this stage for you may only need 2-3 years, during which you also have time to read some philosophy and methodology books, serving the purpose of cross-referencing and mutual penetration.

During the reading phase, I do not belittle myself unnecessarily, I think you should try to read fewer domestic works and do everything possible (such as ordering from foreign websites) to tirelessly read Western classics, especially in technical analysis. Actually, the reading list in an experienced hand's library is the greatest wealth for beginners, allowing you to avoid many detours. Personally, after almost sweeping through all domestic books related to following major players and speculation, I took a big detour and, under a friend's guidance, started reading Western technical analysis classics, also reading a few original "bibles" and "Confucian" type books.

In addition to books, the internet is another source of accumulated material. You should have the determination to sweep through most of the most famous forums at home and abroad, reading everything written by experienced hands on the forum with humility and inclusiveness. Personal insights sometimes are more direct and profound than books. Personally speaking, the help I received from some skilled traders I met on other forums has been extremely far-reaching.

Besides these two, I believe you should devote all (remember, all) your time to watching the market, reading the market, and reviewing the market!!!

Watching the market, for beginners, is essentially just "watching the show" at first, but regardless, you have to keep watching despite the challenges.

If possible, watch all markets where historical data is available: forex, commodities, interest rates, index futures, stocks, etc. Especially indices, observing, analyzing, and predicting the trends of spot and futures indices across various markets should account for around 70% of your total screen-watching time. Even in the stock market, my belief is that indices rule everything, always acting according to the mood of the index, and absolutely not doing the foolish thing of "ignoring the market to trade individual stocks" as suggested by stock analysts. I don't believe that I can always luckily pick stocks that outperform or are independent of the market trend.

Moreover, the process of watching the market is also a great opportunity to summarize and apply the knowledge you've read.

During my process of watching and monitoring the market, I went through several stages:

1. Watching the Show Stage

Every day staring at the first board of the rise and fall rankings, seeing only the rising and falling curves with no feeling in mind.

2. Beginner Stage

After buying books, learning technical indicators such as volume ratio, comprehensive ranking, minute ranking, capital flow, hot sectors, and group hotspots, watching becomes slightly more purposeful and selective. Just like surfing the web, knowing how to use search engines to filter out the information you need.

3. Intermediate Stage

Purpose becomes clearer, having categorized self-selected items and sectors. Able to roughly judge the market's running trend from the movement of a dozen or so varieties, learned formula stock picking, creating custom indicators, testing transaction formulas (not yet a transaction system), and began rejecting traditional techniques and indicators, believing to have entered the core of transactions, indulging in writing formulas and so-called "transaction systems", and using self-designed indicators to measure indices and stocks.

4. Systematization Stage

Long-term monitoring and reviewing of almost all markets with historical data, especially the US Dollar Index, Nikkei, and Hang Seng Index. (These three are my favorite varieties.) However, the cost of monitoring external markets is significant, with all age-inappropriate diseases coming one after another, haha. After persistently researching across markets for a long time, I finally constructed my own trading system. Completely abandoned all indicators and formulas (except those needed in my system). Now, looking back, those "secret formulas or systems" calculated from high, open, low, close, and volume are laughable and not worth mentioning. Returned to the simplest and most primitive few technicals (if I say them, these techniques I dare say newcomers to the market may have heard of). The purpose of watching the market is to verify the system and improve the system.

5. Integration Stage

You finally realize that under the guidance of an effective trading system, you can quickly familiarize yourself with unfamiliar markets and become an expert in those markets, truly proving the saying, "stock price movements contain all information." You can imagine, despite knowing almost nothing and caring little about the supply and demand analysis fundamentals of natural rubber, soybeans, copper, etc., I still managed to profit without hindrance in the imperfect domestic commodity market. When I first entered the market, don't laugh, I didn't even know that China is the world's largest user of rubber and Thailand, Indonesia, and other Southeast Asian countries are the largest producers. After only a week of simulated trading, I started trading with my own margin. I don't understand fundamental analysis, and I look down on those pseudo-fundamental analysts who aren't professional. I only know that an effective system and psychological control can guarantee me profits in any market, it's that simple.
