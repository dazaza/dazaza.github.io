Capsule Apartments for College Graduates Built by an Old Man Receive No Attention

by ztt0288868 on 2010-04-07 14:14:58

Elderly Huang Rixin presents capsule apartments. Material pictures

■ Dialogue figure

Huang Rixin

78 years old, retired electrical engineer

■ Dialogue motivation

In Liu Lang Zhuang, Haidian District, Beijing, Huang Rixin designed 8 capsule apartments, each 2.4 meters long and 0.72 meters wide. A single bed, with internet access and TV. The elderly man said it provides transitional accommodation for university graduates, with a monthly rent of 200 to 250 yuan per unit. In the context of high housing prices, British scavenger elders are keen on stock trading, leaving behind a million-pound estate after death, capsule apartments have become a hot topic of attention as soon as they were exposed.

Learning computers to design apartments

Beijing News: How did you think of designing capsule apartments?

Huang Rixin: I saw an introduction to Japanese capsule apartments online, and I was very interested in such designs. Later, I looked up materials everywhere and started my own research.

Beijing News: How did the connection come about to provide them to university graduates?

Huang Rixin: I accidentally read a novel about Tang Jialing, and later I went to Tang Jialing myself to take a look. I found that there was random littering, poor air quality, and even six people squeezed into a few square meters.

These are all university students! The country will depend on them in the future! So I thought, my capsule apartment can do something for them.

Beijing News: Was the design process smooth? Were there any implementation issues?

Huang Rixin: I originally didn't know how to use a computer, but in order to carry out the design and communication, I started learning computers. In July 2008, this invention successfully applied for a patent, with the invention name being: Assembled Small Apartment. Last year, I started selecting locations and building, at the time I walked through more than 10 building material stores to find fire-resistant wall equipment, conducting repeated experiments, selections, and positioning.

Beijing News: Wasn't it successful at first?

Huang Rixin: In the earliest patent application, I designed fixed equipment, which means installing all facilities before moving them into the room. Later, I found out that it couldn't fit into the elevator, making it too troublesome.

Beijing News: Are there differences compared to capsule apartments in other countries?

Huang Rixin: My design allows standing inside, while Japanese capsule apartments have two levels, where people can't sit straight inside, nor do they have sufficient equipment and space for work and study. My kind of apartment can also accommodate 6 people like in Japan, with upper and lower bunks, but that doesn't align with my concept.

Beijing News: Is there a possibility of large-scale design and construction in the future?

Huang Rixin: I hope so. Not only do I hope the government has relevant policies to support it, but I also hope ordinary people participate. There are so many idle houses, they can be transformed into capsule apartments, providing conditions for university students.

Puzzled by no one renting

Beijing News: Some people online feel it's like a box due to the small space. Do you think living inside would feel oppressive?

Huang Rixin: I see some people online saying, why doesn't the old man live in it himself? But this is designed for temporary accommodation for transient populations.

University graduates just out of school have knowledge, privacy needs, but not much money. This is a transitional residence; once their income stabilizes, they won't live here anymore.

Beijing News: So far, no one has moved in. What do you think the reason is?

Huang Rixin: I'm also puzzled. I posted news online and put up rental information in Liu Lang Zhuang. There's a lot of discussion online, but no one comes to live. If no one lives here, my invention hasn't truly been recognized in a meaningful way.

Beijing News: Has anyone come to check out the house?

Huang Rixin: Yes, but they're all migrant workers. They think the house is nice, but they also think 250 yuan is a bit expensive. They don't need internet access, or even TV.

Beijing News: If it remains unrented, will you continue?

Huang Rixin: I want to find 8 graduates to stay free for half a month, preferably those who have lived in Tang Jialing, for comparison. Or, if any organization is willing to accept, I can display those rooms as exhibits.

No thought of recovering costs

Beijing News: You rented the house, did the landlord allow such major renovations?

Huang Rixin: The landlord supports it. My friend and I specifically established an organization to solve technical problems related to capsule apartments.

Beijing News: Do your family and friends support you?

Huang Rixin: At first, everyone disagreed, opposing voices. My children said, the old man is so old, isn't this doing something foolish! My wife is not in good health, she worries that if I collapse from exhaustion, what will we do.

However, seeing my determination, she gradually agreed. My children in New Zealand were happy when they saw online information about my invention, calling to say they fully support me.

Beijing News: You mentioned spending tens of thousands of yuan earlier, have you thought about recovering the cost?

Huang Rixin: From the design to now, I never thought about getting the money back. I just want everyone to recognize me, and through this capsule apartment, bring attention to the housing problems of transient populations.

I am already 78 years old this year, how many more years do I have left? What does my money matter? Being able to leave something for future generations is enough.

I hope society pays attention to the housing problems of transient populations and vulnerable groups, and hopes the government introduces policies to protect their rights.

□ By reporter Zhu Liudi and intern Li Yachen
