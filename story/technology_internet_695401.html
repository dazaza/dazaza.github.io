Beware of car repairs, stay away from traps

by jintaimqr on 2012-03-07 15:37:40

Data shows that quality problems have accounted for half of the overall car consumer complaints, and complaints regarding repairs are beginning to show an upward trend. Preliminary statistics indicate that complaints about the repair process mainly involve poor repair quality, unreasonable charges, and severe counterfeit and inferior parts. In this issue, we have specially compiled the hard-to-detect tricks in the repair process to warn car owners so they can better protect their rights.

1. Fabricating and exaggerating fault issues

Exaggerating problems and fabricating non-existent ones is a common trick used by many repair shops during the repair process. For example, one car owner took his car to a repair shop after noticing abnormal conditions. The repair shop informed him that the universal joint of the drive shaft was broken, and he had to replace the entire set of new universal joints at a cost of 3000 yuan. However, the car owner was quite suspicious. After consulting a professional master mechanic, he learned that simply replacing a dust cover would solve the problem, with total costs amounting to only around 100 yuan. Turning small problems into big ones, or making up problems where none exist, is a commonly used tactic by many repair shops.

2. Overquoting prices

Car problems vary greatly. How to price? How to charge? Currently, many decisions are still left to the merchants. Thus, many repair shops play with pricing. Mr. Wang's Xiaoli car accidentally got a small dent, and the specialized repair shop quoted 350 yuan for the fix. Mr. Wang thought it was too expensive, so he drove it to a professional car repair shop instead, where it was repaired for just 150 yuan. Mr. Wang helplessly expressed: "The prevalence of counterfeit and substandard parts makes us afraid to repair our cars outside, so we can only go to relatively reliable 4S stores, but the prices there are more than twice as high. When will we be able to haggle with repair shops like buying other goods, changing the situation where merchants decide everything?"

3. Substituting genuine parts with fake ones

To gain improper benefits, some car repair companies secretly replace certain parts of the vehicle without the owner's knowledge during the repair process. Mr. Yang was driving a Buick Regal on official business when he accidentally damaged the front headlights. He sent the car to a relatively 'reputable' repair shop, which fixed the light within a day. However, half a month later, the Buick Regal started experiencing frequent issues - first engine failure, then transmission problems, followed by a malfunction in the electronic ignition system. Upon inspection at a specialized repair shop, it was found that many original key components on the car had been replaced. A repair technician who wished to remain anonymous told reporters that there are 'principles' to part replacements; brakes, transmissions, and internal engine components generally cannot be touched, and only about 10% of a car can't be tampered with. With this knowledge, some repair shops freely substitute old parts while reserving newer ones for other uses.

4. Poor skills among repair personnel

Despite the fact that many standardized operation shops now emphasize the professional skills of repair personnel, the overall low quality of repair staff remains a prominent issue troubling the entire repair industry. Most repair personnel have not undergone professional training, relying on piecemeal experience gained while working, before starting to dismantle and repair vehicles. For highly technical and specialized problems, they often deceive customers, resulting in minor issues escalating into major ones. For instance, if a car gets into an accident and its covering pieces get damaged along with missing fixing clips, small repair shops might take shortcuts by drilling a hole with a flashlight and tightening it with a screw. Even worse, some repair shops introduce these semi-knowledgeable workers as experts to consumers, leaving them unaware and entrusting their cars to these individuals.

5. Passing off inferior parts as good ones

It's common for repair shops to install low-quality, high-priced parts as if they were good ones. Replacing car brake pads is especially prone to such practices, particularly for original brake pads from European and American imports, which usually come with higher prices. Meanwhile, domestically produced and Southeast Asian-made brake pads are cheaper, leading some repair shops to pass them off as imported goods. One car owner with a vehicle worth over 200,000 yuan brought it to a repair shop after an accident that damaged the car's front end. The shop promised that all repair parts were genuine Guangben factory components. After repairs costing over 40,000 yuan, the car developed unusual noises after just a few days of use. Upon inspection, it was discovered that ten components, including the battery, condenser, and horn, were all aftermarket products. It’s understood that the retail price difference between aftermarket accessories and genuine parts can be as high as ten times. Using aftermarket components to impersonate original factory parts and using old parts as new ones has long been an open secret in some small, non-standardized auto repair shops.
