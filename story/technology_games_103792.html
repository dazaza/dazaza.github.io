Financial storm winter, is the online game industry a safe haven? - Age1983

by hexiaoyao on 2008-11-15 20:59:23

The video website "Six Rooms" has recently announced a reduction in staff by two-thirds, prompting concerns from video and social networking sites about their fate. Many excellent internet companies have been placed on death watch lists by some research institutions.

Xiong Xiaoge, Senior Vice President of IDG Worldwide and President of IDG's Asia-Pacific region, as well as a founding partner of IDG's venture capital fund, recently stated: To do venture capital, one must first raise funds, and the financial crisis has had no small impact on this process. Secondly, it is not easy for invested companies to go public; they basically have no exit strategy.

Beijing Kylin Network Information Technology Co., Ltd. has recently announced its successful acquisition of $15 million in funding. Shang Jin, CEO of Kylin Games, said that Shenzhen Innovation Investment Group Co., Ltd., Beijing Huahuitong Company, and Little Elephant Investments participated in this round of financing for Kylin Games. In the context of a downturn in China's and even the global capital markets, this is one of the few recent successful financings in China's IT industry.

Why did Kylin Games, which was just established in July 2007 and has yet to turn a profit, receive such timely assistance from venture capitalists during this cold winter for the internet?

It's because Kylin Games' trump card - "Genghis Khan," according to Shang Jin. This game, expected to launch by the end of the year and seen as highly promising, is the most important reason why venture capitalists are interested in Shang Jin. "We're willing to invest hundreds of millions in a single game." Online gaming is such an industry: developing a game may require significant investment, but once the game is launched, it will continuously bring stable income to the company.

In fact, the optimism surrounding the online gaming industry isn't entirely baseless. Shang Jin believes that the main business of several listed Chinese gaming companies is online gaming, characterized by low per-unit consumption costs and a large number of consumers. The global economic recession has the greatest impact on bulk goods like real estate and automobiles, as well as services with high individual consumption costs. However, for online gaming, a form of inexpensive entertainment, consumer price sensitivity is not high, so it is less affected by this financial crisis wave. Additionally, before the financial storm began, the gaming industry was already a sector with abundant cash flow and great stability.

Simultaneously, he also believes that the business model of online games determines the industry's resilience during the financial crisis. Online games are not produced through piecework or work orders, and the sales process is very flat and direct, reaching users directly. It doesn't require too many channels, there's no pressure from upstream, nor does it need downstream orders; the company's revenue comes directly from its end users.
