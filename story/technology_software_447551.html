Gone is the dream of Nanke.

by bbfo4192 on 2011-06-17 12:00:16

In the 1970s, not long after the Club of Rome's tone of "singing down the economy" had faded, the "pipe dream" lasted for decades. Once the dream was over, the American way of life of "spending future money" came to an end, and everyone followed the Americans in tightening their belts.

Winter has come to the economy, without a dream, only worrying about food and clothing. So, in such a bleak era, how should businesses operate?

The other day, I met Professor Lang Xianping at the Nanhang Pearl Elite Forum, and we both gave speeches to the nation's "frequent flyers." Lang Xianping's answer was: "Go to sleep." Regarding predictions for the economic situation in 2009, Lang Xianping summarized it in one sentence: "Today is definitely better than tomorrow!" "The financial crisis is an extremely complex issue, difficult to control. Faced with the global financial crisis and the crisis of China's manufacturing industry, what can businesses do? Basically nothing, just go to sleep."

I hold an opposing view on this concept. Indeed, 2009 is a very cold "winter," but crises often occur without warning, leaving businesses unprepared. And once we can predict what will happen tomorrow, we will certainly have a response strategy. As said in "Red Cliff," any formation has weaknesses, and any deadlock also has a solution.

"Should we crawl into bed or go hunting?"

Winter is coming, hunters crawl out of bed and go hunting. People in the north buy sports equipment, preparing to go winter swimming and exercise during the winter. People in Guangdong start researching recipes, hoping to take advantage of the good bone qi for nourishment.

The economic winter has come, should companies crawl into bed or go hunting?

It is said: "Axes enter the forest according to the season."

The more extraordinary the times, the more extraordinary opportunities there are for companies.

Because your competitors are going through the same troubles as you. If your competitor chooses to exit at this time, you can take the opportunity to expand your market share. In the 1930s, the United States experienced its largest economic crisis in history. The retreat of state-owned film enterprises in Germany, Italy, and other countries in the face of economic difficulties gave Hollywood the chance to fish in troubled waters. Between 1945 and 1949, Italy imported 2000 films from the United States. Now, Hollywood has become the undisputed ace of the film industry.

Besides, during tough times, if among 10 companies doing advertising, you take one share, the effect will be better than when the economy is good and among 100 companies doing advertising, you take one share. This is because everyone's attention will focus on you. Building a brand firmly during the economic winter is like lighting a matchstick in the dark; it will shine brighter than a candle lit during the day.

"Winter Hunting Skills"

Going hunting requires adapting to local conditions and timing. How to hunt? Tactics are important. During tough times, Chinese companies' brand building needs to seize opportunities and concentrate efforts on urgent and important tasks. Specifically, there are four plans:

The first is precise targeting. When an airline went bankrupt, it said, "We are an airline that ordinary people can afford." However, people who take planes usually spend more money for saving time. In China, ordinary people mostly rely on trains. Turning a company into a low-cost airline is a positioning error. During tough times, brand building first requires calmly thinking about who your brand is selling to.

The second is internationalizing the image. Whether in tough times or prosperous times, globalization is an irreversible trend. With the rapid growth of our economy, joining the WTO, foreign enterprises and capital constantly flowing into the Chinese market, the globalization process of the Chinese economy is accelerating. The global spread of the U.S. subprime crisis further illustrates the arrival of the era of globalization. Enhancing an international image during a global economic downturn is the best time to surpass international brands.

The third is branding youthfulness. Does China lack brands? No. The People's Republic of China was founded in 1949, and we have more than 10,000 old brands. But a preliminary estimate last year showed only more than 1,000 remained. Why? Because they have been sticking to old ways and not keeping up with the times. This is a rapidly changing era, an era led by the Internet. The rise of the Internet has freed the younger generation from the constraints of "experience theory," allowing them to increasingly participate in politics, economics, and culture, gradually becoming the center of families and society. This makes brand youthfulness an irreversible trend. Under the backdrop of increasing social influence by the younger generation, only by actively promoting brand youthfulness and achieving emotional resonance with the younger generation can the most extensive support be gained. Obama's success is the best proof.

The fourth is terminal refinement. Why do people love watching Korean dramas? Because when filming Korean dramas, the food is made very delicately. When Chinese people shoot TV shows, the emperor eats things bought from small shops in the middle. An ancient Chinese saying goes: "Even rats have skin, but humans without manners, why not die?" For brands, it is the same. The intensification of international competition and the upgrading of consumer needs make refined brands the first essential factor for future brand survival.

Overall, such an economic winter is precisely the spring for corporate brand growth. Crawl out of bed to face it, strategically despise it, tactically value it, seize the "opportunity," elevate corporate vision, rejuvenate the brand image, differentiate brand personality, shape expert authority, spread brand stories, promote corporate leaders, pre-embed expansion pipelines, strengthen brand construction and communication. Tomorrow will definitely be better than today.
