The way of memory: the best way is actually the most primitive way.

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 11:28:51

Recalling this time last year, I was lying in bed, excitedly exploring the world of The Three-Body Problem through Wang Miao's journey. Apart from a few main characters' names, the general storyline, and the thrill I felt at that moment, most of the novel’s content has faded from my memory.

In the book *The Google Way: How One Company is Revolutionizing Management as We Know It*, Douglas Merrill suggests that the key to memory lies in forgetting. The human brain temporarily stores all information it encounters, but its capacity to retain is very limited. Therefore, quickly forgetting trivial information makes space for important details—something many people struggle to do.

Merrill believes that for critical information that needs to be retained, the best way to remember is "to get the information out of your head as fast as possible."

In everyday life, I enjoy using Evernote to jot down excerpts of great content I come across, even typing them laboriously on my phone screen. When I occasionally organize my Evernote content, I feel a sense of accomplishment—rekindling the joy I felt when I first copied those excerpts, which lifts my mood.

For less urgent information, storing it in a temporarily reliable place is also a good strategy.

The allure of text lies in its ability to evoke imagination without needing a physical medium. For photos, however, I might prefer something tangible like paper. Last week at the iFanr Smart TV Summit, I printed a photo I thought turned out well using an instant photo printer. Pulling the Lomo card from the bottom of the machine made me realize that taking this photo wasn't just another random snap stored with countless others in an album—it became a tangible catalyst for pleasant memories.

The sensory impact of physical objects seems more direct and intricate than virtual images. For emotional photographs, having a physical medium feels essential—you don’t need to see it all the time, but when you want to, you can find it easily.

In *The Eternal Life of Stars*, when Cheng Xin meets Luo Ji, the legendary Swordholder, for the last time, he is trying to preserve human civilization. According to Luo Ji's description: quantum storage devices can theoretically hold information for about 2,000 years under ideal conditions; after 2,000 years, internal decay renders the data unreadable. High-quality USB drives or hard drives can store information for around 5,000 years. Quality-printed materials, using special synthetic paper and ink, could still be readable after 200,000 years.

To preserve information for approximately 100 million years, the only feasible method is to inscribe it on stone.

For such grand, epic-scale civilizational information, the best method turns out to be the most primitive approach.
