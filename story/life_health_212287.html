Abnormal menstruation, be careful of gynecological diseases

by lk0lifeb on 2009-07-22 01:14:04

It is often said that women are like flowers, which are beautiful but also fragile.

According to incomplete statistics from the World Health Organization, the incidence rate of various gynecological diseases among women is over 65%. Relevant data from our country shows that the incidence rate of gynecological diseases among women of childbearing age is over 70%, and almost all women have been troubled by gynecological inflammation to varying degrees at some point in their lives. Among them, more than 5% of women have had their normal work and life affected due to severe conditions.

Experts' advice:

The monthly menstrual cycle is one of the signs that a girl has entered puberty. It's like a loyal friend who accompanies a woman through her beautiful life. From a medical perspective, menstruation is more like an indicator light for a woman's health. Any subtle changes in it may be related to endocrine disorders, inflammation, tumors, etc., reminding women that there may be a health crisis that requires timely professional diagnosis by a doctor.

Signal one: Lower abdominal pain

Many women experience cramping lower abdominal pain during or around their menstrual period. In severe cases, they may also experience pallor, cold sweats, cold hands and feet, nausea, vomiting, or even fainting and collapse.

Some women take painkillers during menstrual cramps. However, painkillers can only temporarily relieve the pain, and excessive use can cause nervous system dysfunction, reduced memory, insomnia, and other adverse effects.

Many women think that menstrual cramps are trivial and can be endured, believing that once they get married or have children, the problem will go away. However, it should be noted that menstrual cramps can be divided into two types: one is physiological menstrual cramps, which are generally harmless and will improve over time; the other is pathological menstrual cramps, which may be warning signals of reproductive system inflammation or endometriosis. If the pain persists without relief, it is necessary to seek timely examination at the gynecology department of a hospital to determine the cause and receive appropriate treatment.

Endometriosis is one of the important causes of menstrual cramps. The inner wall of the uterus has a layer called the endometrium, which undergoes cyclical changes influenced by ovarian hormones. If fertilization does not occur in a given month, the endometrium will shed, causing bleeding that flows out through the vagina as menstruation. Due to certain factors, the endometrium may move to places outside the uterus, resulting in endometriosis. These misplaced endometrial tissues will also experience cyclical bleeding influenced by ovarian hormones, but since there is no channel for the blood to flow out, it accumulates locally, forming cysts that grow larger over time. As the water inside is absorbed, the blood turns into a sticky substance resembling chocolate soup, which we call chocolate cysts. The most typical symptom of endometriosis is menstrual cramps, which worsen over time and may eventually lead to infertility. The peak age range for endometriosis patients is 25-49 years old. If left untreated for a long time, 20%-66% of patients may become infertile, so this condition should be taken seriously by women.

Signal two: Irregular menstruation

With the onset of puberty, women begin to experience menstruation. After a period of unstable menstruation, the vast majority of women will establish a regular and normal monthly menstrual cycle. Regular and normal menstruation refers to a menstrual cycle of about 30 days, a menstrual period of about 5 days, menstrual blood of a bright red color, moderate flow, and no abnormal clots. However, many women experience irregular menstruation due to various reasons such as physical fitness, work and living environment, diet, personality, and other aspects.
