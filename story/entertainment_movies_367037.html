Play with brains, test physical strength - Blu-ray review of "Die Hard with a Vengeance"

by diglog on 2010-10-06 12:35:09

The "Die Hard" series is possibly one of the few classic action films; the first installment attracted many audiences with its unique storytelling and character setup. The seemingly invincible McClane, paired with polite robbers, completely overturned the stereotypical image of police and bandits. Nonetheless, our hero finally begins to show emotion, knowing fear, using tactics to maneuver against enemies, and also suffering - whether physically or mentally. Moreover, the film features visceral combat, where characters transition from pristine clothing at the start to being battered and bruised later on, making the story feel grounded and realistic. The situation is perilous, but the protagonist truly rises to the occasion. In contrast, today's action films seem rather effeminate, relying heavily on computer-generated imagery, with actors posing in front of blue screens to complete so-called "grand" action sequences. The sound effects may be improved, and the visuals more dazzling, but they lack depth and are overly restless. After the sensory stimulation, it's rare for anything to leave a lasting impression. Of course, I don't deny that there are still exciting action films today, but the feeling has completely changed. I might be somewhat old-fashioned, but I still prefer the era of "fighting it out."

The story of "Die Hard 2" takes place at an airport, without director McTiernan this time. The Christmas backdrop remains, but with less McClane-style humor and more of Renny Harlin's hard-hitting style. The flavor is slightly different but can still be considered thrilling. Despite a somewhat boring plot, the scene of blowing up the 747 left a deep impression on me. By 1995, John McTiernan returned, bringing "Die Hard: With a Vengeance," filled with more explosions, humor, and blood. Leaving behind the building and airport, the movie moves to New York City, a larger playground with more things to destroy. The opening is accompanied by The Lovin' Spoonful's "Summer in the City," introducing the audience to a New York morning. A massive explosion follows—enough to send Arnold Schwarzenegger flying—signaling the start of a bumpy ride. Subsequently, there's non-stop car chases, puzzle games, with the protagonist being manipulated by the bad guys while enduring constant explosions and gunfire. The audience willingly follows along, fully immersed. The entire story flows smoothly without any dull moments, and even after countless viewings, it remains exhilarating.

My memory of this film starts in a theater-style auditorium, separated by a not-so-small stage, projecting onto a very ordinary screen that had been used for years—the grand opening explosion, with subway cars lifted onto the platform. At the time, I was in fifth grade, inexperienced, and completely stunned by these images. From then on, the cinema became my playground, my church, where I often watched movies on an empty stomach, toy cars in my pocket. This R-rated film that I shouldn't have seen became one of my best memories.

Despite the film's excellence, it never satisfied home cinema needs until the release of this Blu-ray disc. The previous 4:3LB DVD picture quality was disastrous, and although a new version improved the widescreen format, the enhancements were limited. The picture was filled with excessive digital enhancement artifacts, inducing nausea at times. The Blu-ray picture finally returns to normal, with some areas still showing digital traces, but overall, the image is clear and natural. The skin tones and lighting in the scenes are realistic, and the colors are vibrant, especially the green grass at Yankee Stadium. The grayscale handling is excellent, with distinct layers. Even in dark scenes like tunnels, with water chasing trucks, the texture of objects and splashes are well-presented. The bloodstains on McClane's body—increasingly messy—are depicted very realistically. Some scratches on the original film stock have been repaired, though the graininess is still noticeable. Despite not matching the quality of newer films, this film's transfer is the best it's been in a long time.

The English DTS-HD Master Audio 5.1 track supporting this film performs admirably. Rich surround details recreate the bustling environment of the Big Apple, with traffic, crowds, and people shouting as taxis charge through Central Park, all rendered clearly and accurately. The bass during explosions is solid, sounding fuller and more layered than previous mixes. Dialogue is clear, though still a bit dry, a problem that has annoyed me since the DVD era. I attribute it to the limitations of ADR mixing from that time. However, the better sound mix somewhat compensates for the original unsatisfactory foley work. The classic film score is perfectly presented through this audio track. Notably, the opening song features instruments and vocals coming from different channels, giving the old song renewed vitality.

The extras on this disc are mostly familiar, carried over from the original DVD content. There's a commentary track for those interested, an alternate ending (thankfully not used, as it's not great), a few behind-the-scenes documentaries, and some trailers. There's nothing particularly special, but this Blu-ray disc with restored picture and sound is worth purchasing. It's also one of the better ones in the series—the first one is too dated, the second one too ordinary, and the fourth one too childish (though, because it's newer, it still has some fans). Additionally, this disc should be region-locked to Region A, reminding us once again that staying away from Region C players is currently the best choice.
