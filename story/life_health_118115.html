The Spring Festival is coming. How to avoid holiday fat accumulation

by xfnnv on 2009-01-09 16:38:12

Preface: During holidays, the number of dinner parties inevitably increases. Women worry about gaining weight after indulging in delicious food, while men are also concerned about developing a beer belly from drinking and eating meat.

At the dining table, a man pats his stomach and says, "Back in the day, I was a scrawny 50-kilogram bean sprout, now I weigh 82 kilograms. Once you hit 30, it's like a steamed bun rising. From New Year's Day to the Spring Festival, there will be countless dinner parties and delicious foods, and more fat will accumulate on my belly. It's really worrying."

If you still believe in the law of conservation of energy, then you know how to avoid fat accumulation: simply reduce energy intake and increase energy expenditure. Reducing energy intake means choosing foods with less oil and more fiber at the dinner table; increasing energy expenditure means exercising after meals to "burn" off excess energy. Regardless of any miraculous weight loss methods, if these two points are not achieved, the final result will always be gaining weight again.

Many people admire me because, among peers, my appetite can be considered quite large, yet my waistline and weight have remained normal. There is no secret, just adhering to the above two principles. Specifically, here are some practices:

First, when eating, try to reach for low-energy, high-fiber foods with your chopsticks. For example, vegetables (like mushrooms, black fungus, seaweed), fruits, and seafood and tofu with little oil. If eating fish or meat, try to eat parts with bones and many thorns, as picking through bones and thorns is slower, so you end up eating less meat, and can appreciate the taste of food more carefully, appearing to enjoy your meal thoroughly.

Second, exercise after eating. At least walk more after meals, ideally run in the evening to promptly burn off excess energy. Walking for an hour can consume over 200 kilocalories. For instance, going shopping after dinner, walking two or three stops home instead of taking a vehicle, or doing housework like mopping the floor immediately upon returning home, are all effective activities.

If there is a third point, it would be maintaining a high metabolic rate, which means having a body that doesn't gain weight easily. This type of constitution isn't mysterious—it's just a state of good physical fitness and strong cardiorespiratory function. If you can run cross-country for four hours and still feel energetic, play tennis for an hour without feeling out of breath, run continuously for half an hour without feeling too tired, and climb eight floors of stairs in one go while still being able to chat and laugh freely, then you're likely someone who doesn't gain weight easily. Such individuals have firm muscles, good internal organ functions, and a high basal metabolic rate, consuming more energy than others under the same amount of exercise, naturally making it harder for them to develop flab around the waist.

The best way to test whether someone exercises enough is by looking at their fat distribution. If they are slim everywhere else, with no fat on their face, but have excess fat around the waist, then there is a 90% chance that this person does not exercise enough. As long as they engage in over an hour of aerobic exercise four times a week, they will gradually regain a slim waist.

Since we have so much time during holidays to indulge in fine dining, why not allocate a little time for exercise? Even half an hour of aerobic exercise every day is sufficient for us to maintain our figure.

The New Year is always an opportunity for a new beginning. On the occasion of this beautiful holiday, take the time to re-examine your life, make a resolution, start adjusting your diet, and exercise. By the time the bright spring sunshine returns to the earth, we can welcome it with a fit and light body. Nature will reward us with extra vitality and a sense of youth.
