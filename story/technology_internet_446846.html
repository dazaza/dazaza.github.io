I. People's Mediation Work

The street party working committee attaches great importance to the people's mediation work, requiring the judicial office to focus on people's mediation and strengthen its efforts to ensure stability in the area.

1. Organization of Screening

At all levels, the mediation committees adhere to the "combined investigation and mediation, prevention as the priority" work policy to timely resolve conflicts, settle disputes, prevent escalation, and avoid mass petitions for the purpose of maintaining social stability. Regular screenings are organized with monthly conflict dispute screening and mediation meetings held to clarify solutions. Each community summarizes and promptly reports screening situations. A "zero-report" system is implemented for those without discovered issues.

2. Training System

In response to personnel adjustments after the merger of street and community offices, and considering new situations and problems emerging in current mediation work, a training plan was promptly formulated and institutionalized. The district judicial bureau leadership was invited to provide professional training for mediation staff, teaching mediation skills and addressing practical issues encountered in their work. Feedback indicates satisfactory results with the business capabilities of each mediation committee's staff effectively enhanced. Throughout the year, 61 various types of conflicts and disputes were mediated with a mediation rate of 100% and a success rate exceeding 95%.

3. Archive Management

Each community mediation committee under the street established a mediation information collection system with registration records. Mediation dispute work archives and mediation work records were built to implement scientific and standardized management. This ensures one file per matter. For major disputes, a one-file-per-case system is fully implemented to execute archive management.

4. Fill the gap in grassroots mediation committees in non-public enterprises, continuously extending and deepening the large mediation network system by forming non-public mediation committees.

II. Resettlement and Assistance Work

Based on actual conditions, four measures were adopted to solidly carry out the resettlement and assistance work for released prisoners and those who have completed re-education through labor.

1. Establish a three-level organizational network of offices, stations, and volunteer teams. Within the jurisdiction, four communities rely on the judicial office to establish resettlement and assistance work sub-stations responsible for daily operations. In light of characteristics such as dispersed living arrangements, complex compositions, and uncertain movements of released individuals, a carpet-style large-scale screening was conducted from April to July in collaboration with local police stations. Clear numbers and conditions were identified for the 63 released individuals within the jurisdiction, forming a team of 189 grassroots assistance volunteers assisting the assistance workstations and groups.

2. Strengthen connection work for released prisoners. After receiving the notice of release, the community resettlement and assistance workstation is informed within seven days, promptly determining the assistance person in charge, forming an assistance group, implementing categorized management, and establishing assistance work archives. Connection work includes: 1) Timely home visits and processing of three-month low-income support according to regulations; 2) Monthly face-to-face meetings to understand ideological dynamics; 3) Timely signing of assistance agreements to reinforce assistance management objectives.

3. Implement the "3+1" assistance model in the work of assisting released prisoners, assigning a security director for each released individual to provide assistance politically, ideologically, legally, and morally. Employed individuals receive technical support from their enterprise, unemployed individuals are settled by the community, and a community cadre cares for them in daily life while providing timely feedback on assistance information.

4. Internal wall assistance, conducting no less than two sessions of assistance within prison walls annually, delivering warmth activities, exchanging hearts, and providing living necessities. Concerning both political and living aspects of incarcerated individuals, allowing them to feel the warmth of the government.

III. Legal Education

Conduct various forms of legal education activities to improve residents' legal literacy. According to the "two improvements, two changes" goals proposed in the "Fourth Five-Year Plan" for legal education, an implementation opinion for governing the street according to law and legal education was formulated at the beginning of the year, and legal education was strictly carried out according to the document requirements.

In the legal education work of XX year, some fresh and lively popular legal education forms were attempted. Lawyer forums, legal consultations, bulletin boards, and blackboard newspapers were used for legal publicity. Each community established a legal reading corner and legal service consultation station to provide legal services to residents. It can be said that the legal education in XX year was "audible, visible, and flavorful." Additionally, we strengthened legal education for youth, regularly and targetedly conducting legal education. We consistently provided legal education to eligible young recruits, explaining contents such as the "Constitution," "National Defense Law," and "Criminal Law." The development of legal education in XX year not only achieved satisfactory results but also played a positive role in guiding future legal education work.

IV. Legal Services Entering Communities

To meet the continuous needs of community residents for legal services and create a good legal environment in the streets and communities. XX Street focuses on promoting the harmonious development of "three civilizations" construction and widely carries out the "Legal Services Entering Communities" campaign, highlighting the convenience and benefits of legal services to the public, achieving "zero-distance" contact between legal services and residents.

The leadership of the XX Street Party Working Committee and the Office takes the "Legal Services Entering Communities" work very seriously, placing the comprehensive development of "Legal Services Entering Communities" and deepening grassroots community legal aid services as the top priority of street work. Four community legal worker contact cards were established, forming a horizontal-to-edge and vertical-to-bottom legal aid model with vulnerable groups, laying a solid foundation for legal education and legal services entering communities.

Firstly, communities conduct different content legal lectures based on their actual needs, using legal needs questionnaires such as "What form of legal services do residents need?" and "What suggestions do you have for the 'Legal Services Entering Communities' campaign?". Meanwhile, community legal education galleries are used to extensively promote laws closely related to residents' lives.

Secondly, establish community legal service consultation reception stations and conduct "one matter one law" legal service activities. For example, currently prevalent labor dispute cases, house dispute cases caused by family conflicts, house relocation dispute cases, and debt dispute cases caused by economic contracts, different legal services are provided based on different cases.

Lawyers Ma Pingyuan and Fan Dongsheng from the Xiuzhu Community Legal Service Consultation Station persistently serve as voluntary legal advisors for the community, receiving widespread welcome from the masses. They carefully answer various legal inquiries raised by the public; patiently mediate various conflicts and disputes arising between families and neighbors, improving the public's understanding of legal knowledge and enhancing their ability to use legal weapons to protect themselves from harm. Resident Song Yufeng and her husband, a remarried couple for 16 years, recently faced marital conflict due to her inability to visit her hospitalized husband suffering from cerebral thrombosis, coupled with perceived stinginess in financial matters, leading to dissatisfaction from her husband and his children. Subsequently, they disallowed Song from visiting her husband, escalating family conflicts. Song Yufeng planned to file for divorce. Through patient and detailed work by the lawyers, the elder calmed down. Eventually, Song Yufeng agreed with the lawyer's suggestion, abandoning the intention to file for divorce and waiting until her husband's condition improved for further adjustment.

Liu Dongying, a low-income resident at No. 18 Longsheng Alley, due to conversion from agricultural to non-agricultural status, had no job. Her husband Zhang Jun suffered from mental illness for many years with worsening conditions. Unable to endure Zhang's abnormal behavior, Liu requested a divorce from the Zhang family, involving housing issues which the Zhang family opposed. Liu sought legal resolution from Lawyer Fan. Through legal education and explanation to the Zhang family, they agreed to the divorce request, granting Liu ownership of the house and custody of the child.

All four community legal service consultation stations under the XX Street are equipped with two to three lawyers. Various relevant legal education, consultation activities are carried out vigorously. Currently, the communities have held four legal lectures, answered 36 various legal inquiries, mediated eight types of conflicts and disputes, contributing to maintaining social stability in the jurisdiction.
