Three sentences from Ballmer perfectly explain why Microsoft's mobile business failed.

by anonymous on 2013-10-13 14:12:37

Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer

Steve Ballmer, the CEO of Microsoft, is stepping down for a reason. In the past, Microsoft may have been the most powerful company in the world, but now it has been pushed to the sidelines. Although it remains an important tech company, its influence is clearly not what it used to be. The rise of mobile devices like tablets and smartphones has helped Google and Apple take the spotlight from traditional Windows PCs with their Android and iOS systems.

In recent years, Ballmer has made several wrong statements, three of which best explain why Microsoft's mobile business has failed. These statements, when considered in context, better explain why Microsoft lost its dominance in the operating system market.

The first statement: Ballmer commented on the iPhone as follows:

"$500, fully subsidized by the carrier?! That’s the most expensive phone in the world, and it doesn’t appeal to business customers because it doesn’t have a keyboard."

Perhaps nothing has harmed Microsoft's mobile business more than Ballmer's short-sightedness. When Apple launched its first iPhone, it was priced high, lacked a physical keyboard, and broke industry norms. Despite this, it succeeded not only with consumers but also with enterprise customers who had previously favored BlackBerry phones.

Eventually, Microsoft had no choice but to copy the iPhone operating system and business model, first trying with Windows Phone 7 and then WP8, but it was too late. Currently, Microsoft holds just a 4% share of the global smartphone market, which is still a significant increase from last year.

In comparison, Apple holds a 13% market share, while the iPhone accounts for over 40% of the U.S. smartphone market.

Microsoft hasn't given up on smartphones. In fact, acquiring Nokia's phone business was a higher-level imitation of Apple, giving Microsoft the ability to develop its own phones. Perhaps Microsoft hopes to eventually catch up with Apple, but the likelihood of that happening is low. It is already six years behind Apple and lacks the ecosystem and marketing capabilities of its competitor.

The second statement: Ballmer commented on Google's Android system as follows:

"It's hard to get excited about Android phones. Apple is a good competitor, a different competitor."

Ballmer showed considerable disdain for Android. At the time, he preferred to view Apple as a potential threat and mocked Android extensively. During interviews, Ballmer sarcastically remarked that to use a phone based on Google's Android operating system, you needed to understand computer technology.

As it turned out, there were many consumers who understood computers. According to statistics, Android currently holds a 79% share of the global smartphone market and is particularly popular in emerging markets like China and India.

Ironically, the method Google used to develop the Android ecosystem was borrowed from Microsoft, similar to the way Windows worked with traditional PCs. Google allowed many hardware manufacturers to produce Android devices, resulting in a wide variety of Android phones, ranging from entry-level to high-end models. While this caused so-called "fragmentation," it also propelled Android to the top of the global smartphone market.

The third statement: Ballmer on who matters most:

"Developers! Developers! Developers!"

This statement perhaps best explains why Microsoft's Windows Phone system has ended up in its current sorry state—because it lacks developer support.

When Ballmer made that statement years ago, he surely understood the principle that the quality of an operating system can only be demonstrated through the applications that run on it. For years, Windows led the pack because it received support from developers worldwide. An application might not run on Mac or Linux devices, but it would definitely run on a Windows PC.

For Windows Phone, the situation is reversed. Microsoft's mobile operating system still lacks support from key applications, such as Instagram, Flipboard, and HBO Go. These apps may eventually release versions compatible with WP, but the companies behind them are unwilling to invest much time supporting Microsoft's platform.

Moreover, when new popular apps, such as new games or social networking sites, emerge, it usually takes months before WP-compatible versions are available. Therefore, consumers buying WP phones must accept the fact that they will have limited access to apps, which is one of the main reasons why most consumers avoid purchasing WP phones.

Microsoft's Windows business is on a downward trend

In recent years, PC sales have been underwhelming. Recently, market research firm Gartner reported that global PC shipments declined by 8.6% year-over-year in the third quarter, marking the sixth consecutive quarter of declining PC shipments. While traditional Windows PCs won't disappear overnight, mobile devices have undoubtedly sounded their death knell.

Microsoft failed to seize the opportunities presented by these market trends, and ultimately, this can all be attributed to Ballmer. He neither appreciated Apple's iPhone nor understood the appeal of Google's Android, and he seems to have forgotten the importance of developers altogether.
