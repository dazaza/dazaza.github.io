Insiders offer Microsoft 11 pieces of advice: need to change the corporate culture

by anonymous on 2013-08-29 16:40:54

During the nearly 14 years that Steve Ballmer served as Microsoft's CEO, the company's revenue more than tripled to nearly $78 billion, and its net profit was leading in any industry.

However, even so, the majority of technical experts agree that Microsoft needs a restart. Under Ballmer's leadership, because this company failed to launch products with sufficient significance or keep up with the pace of technological trends, Microsoft was also seen as a slow-moving giant.

Under Ballmer's leadership, Microsoft has achieved some success in several new areas, such as the Xbox video game system, and the company is working hard to promote the sale of integrated backend technology to enterprises.

After Ballmer announced his retirement within the next 12 months, The Wall Street Journal interviewed 11 business leaders, technicians, and management experts, asking them what kind of CEO Microsoft needs in their eyes to help reshape the company’s image as a technological innovator.

Former General Motors Global Product Development Vice President Bob Lutz:

The next Microsoft leader will either be the so-called technical executive or someone who provides users with a free-to-run operating system. In terms of the automotive industry, it is necessary to let someone with a sixth sense about what the next generation product will be become the leader.

Microsoft is currently in the same position as General Motors and Ford Motor were back in the day - always accustomed to analyzing the past, observing the development of the world, and always distrusting those with foresight. Because products are always based on retrospective analysis, your products will always lag behind the industry leaders by three years.

Enterprises must be run by managers who can accept risks or like new ideas and product-oriented thinking. A high-tech company that doesn't manage to think outside the box is destined to fail.

Facebook former director and Accel Partners partner James Breyer:

Microsoft must start over in mobile and social platform businesses. The biggest mistake this company made was not having enough experience in these new fields and emerging areas.

There are many long-term opportunities in areas including cloud computing, data centers, and big data. Microsoft must be very willing to reallocate existing products. I often think that Microsoft's products are inconvenient to use. There are also opportunities in positioning simple products.

Microsoft has a group of outstanding talents who will become the main force of the next generation of management. However, there will be conflicts in management philosophy between product-oriented and business-oriented managers, and Microsoft must work hard to overcome this issue. Moreover, Microsoft must significantly improve the speed of decision-making.

Singularity University Innovation and Research Vice President Vivek Wadhwa:

Microsoft has some of the best talents in the world, but they have been affected by the company's bureaucracy, internal struggles, and protectionist sentiments. Releasing the energy of these talents will make Microsoft capable of anything.

Digital payment company TrialPay CEO Alex Rampell:

As Marc Benioff (Salesforce.com CEO) put it, software is dead. Microsoft must eliminate numbers and years from various versions (such as Office 2003, Office 2007, Office 2011). Updating software every four years is indeed embarrassing. Facebook makes daily patches to its website. More importantly, the subscription-based revenue model is better and easier to predict. Office 365 is a start, but Microsoft's transformation needs to be more aggressive.

Microsoft also needs to acquire companies – whether acquiring talent or technology. Microsoft's $6 billion acquisition of aQuantive was somewhat of a failure, but consider this: Yahoo's former CEO Terry Semel once had the chance to acquire Google and Facebook. Neither deal would have cost Yahoo much money. eBay's acquisition of PayPal, Priceline.com's acquisition of Booking.com, these deals all revived the parent companies through new businesses. The right acquisitions can also bring in talent. PayPal spent $240 million to acquire Zong, bringing David Marcus as the new president of the company.

Industry analyst and venture capitalist Jean-Louis Gassee:

Microsoft should develop a reliable tablet – not a hybrid product – just a tablet. Microsoft should also enter the smartphone market itself, because Nokia won't work exclusively for Microsoft in the future. Microsoft must continue to implement its service strategy, which includes making Office substantially move to the cloud. Moreover, Microsoft should make Windows 8 only applicable to PCs, rather than adopting dual standards. The reason is very simple: if the product is too complicated, it is doomed to fail.

Quip founder and former Facebook CTO Bret Taylor:

Microsoft's recent strategic transformation focuses on "services and devices" – which essentially turns Microsoft into a mix of its largest competitors Google and Apple. I am skeptical of whether this strategy will succeed because the service model and the device model are extremely different.

The device model requires tight, vertical integration of software and hardware, with profits mainly coming from hardware devices. The service model, on the other hand, requires services to cross platforms and devices, maximizing adoption by potential consumers. I suggest that Microsoft's new CEO rigorously review the company's new strategy and choose a single route, a single business model. To achieve this, Windows may no longer be the same strategic asset as before, and this will be the greatest challenge for Microsoft's true transformation in the future.

Harvard Business School Assistant Professor Gautam Mukunda:

Microsoft must completely rethink its corporate culture. Microsoft is now known for its bureaucracy and politics, and the company's layered ranking system has had a toxic effect on corporate culture.

Whoever becomes Microsoft's CEO, his first task should be to change the company's corporate culture and release the talents of Microsoft employees. Microsoft's new CEO should immediately abolish the ranking system after taking office and rebuild the company's corporate culture from the ground up.

DataGravity CEO Paula Long:

What does Microsoft represent today? Is it its office suite, tablets, or something else? The market isn't clear about these things, and Microsoft's next CEO must clearly explain this issue.

As one of Microsoft's customers, I am very excited about Microsoft's potential opportunities. Microsoft is one of the largest data creators and owners, and the company has the opportunity to be more than just a raw material pipeline. Instead, the company can create added value for users.

Executive Search Firm Egon Zehnder International Partner Chris Patrick:

Microsoft's new CEO must have a strong technical product background and the leadership qualifications to manage large enterprises. A company must have an outstanding product-oriented leader, but if he cannot innovate, drive, and encourage employees, the company will not reach the desired height.

Microsoft's new CEO must be the fundamental axis of the company's strategy. He must not only convince technical personnel but also integrate the development strategy with sales and marketing.

Cheezburger Network CEO Ben Huh:

Microsoft needs more chaotic space. Institutionally, there must be room for some failures, which can nurture great successes. In the past eight years, the morale of Microsoft employees in Seattle has changed from arrogance to depression.

Microsoft's best asset is its employees, and now they should be relaxed to create a less strict environment. To achieve this, Microsoft's new CEO needs time and the ability to invest in corporate culture and chaos at the expense of short-term profits. Microsoft's 72% gross margin is indeed excellent, but it cannot come at the expense of innovation.

Wall Street certainly won't like this, but focusing on short-term profits will affect future innovation.

Kabam CEO Kevin Chou:

The process of Yahoo seeking a CEO over the past decade should provide some experience and lessons for Microsoft.

Like Microsoft, Yahoo has ventured into many areas through large mergers and acquisitions, but lacks a clear strategic direction. Due to the ambiguity of the strategy, not only has the company's search business been surpassed by Google, but its news, photos, and local services have also been eroded by competitors.

Marissa Mayer is changing all of this. Mayer has formulated a step-by-step product strategy for Yahoo, which has made investors very happy. She also cut off products unsuitable for Yahoo and bought products that were very needed. The audience size of Yahoo began to rebound. Last month, Yahoo's unique visitors in the United States exceeded Google for the first time in two years.

What kind of CEO does Microsoft need? Whether business-oriented or product-oriented, this person needs to ultimately define Microsoft's future.
