The prospect of public relations soft articles from the editorial concept of the "First Financial Weekly" is worrying_2265

by fmakehg4 on 2011-09-15 17:34:20

Due to professional needs, I read every issue of the "China Business Weekly" carefully, and have learned a lot about magazine production. However, precisely because of reading this magazine, I am increasingly becoming worried. The reason for my worry is not because our magazine does not match up to "China Business Weekly" - so far, I haven't thought about competing with them - but because if media peers continue to develop according to the philosophy of this magazine, the public relations industry will face significant challenges. Many companies' business models and profitability will be unsustainable, thus making it hard for them to survive.

The executive editor of this magazine, Yi Xianfeng, has a strong personality. This is because of his preface. Usually, the preface of a magazine either presents profound personal views or provides an elegant summary of the current issue. Moreover, as someone who can serve as chief editor, he must possess excellent writing skills. Therefore, I have developed a habit of reading magazines: when I get a new magazine, I always read the preface first, which gives me a kind of reading pleasure that cannot be provided by reporters' articles.

Yi's prefaces are extraordinary. He generally doesn't write much about the content of the magazine itself, but more about content management or systems and methods. Since the founding of the magazine, he has been promoting one idea in the preface: fair attitudes and objective reporting of business news should not be influenced in any way by any interest group. Because "China Business Weekly" does not have its own website, I cannot check all the prefaces, but in my impression as a reader, Yi has mentioned PR companies multiple times, showing quite a bit of aversion towards them. He was also very unhappy about the harassment from PR companies directed at himself and the magazine's journalists.

I vaguely remember him writing a story: a PR company called Yi to try to secure an interview with their client's CEO, but after being rejected, they arrogantly asked, "How much money would it take to do an interview with the CEO?" As you can imagine, Yi told them, and also informed all PR companies through the preface: in "China Business Weekly," news cannot be bought.

In a recent issue of "China Business Weekly" I purchased a few days ago, there is a segment of the preface:

1. Editors and journalists (including trainee journalists) shall not accept any gifts, including customary festival gifts delivered via courier services, which will be returned to the original address.

2. Editors and journalists (including trainee journalists) shall not accept red envelopes or transportation fees provided by interview subjects or third parties, nor shall they accept traffic and accommodation expenses provided during interviews.

We re-emphasize this rule and hope to receive supervision from all sectors. If any staff member of the weekly violates the rules, please report directly to the weekly, or report to our supervisory institution and industry management body. Once verified, we will take the strictest measures.

So, does "China Business Weekly" have paid soft articles? Yes. The paid soft articles in this magazine are called "Marketing Department Planning," with the English term ADVERTORIAL, and the font differs from the magazine articles. Essentially, this is telling readers that this is an advertisement. Only, the absence of the Chinese word for advertisement makes it feel somewhat bashful about charging for advertisements. And the final statement feels somewhat hypocritical: "This article is published by 'China Business Weekly' for the purpose of conveying commercial trade information and does not represent the views of this publication. This column is planned and produced by the Marketing Department of this publication."

Regardless, "China Business Weekly" strictly maintains the distinction between news reporting and advertising soft articles.

Where is the line drawn? "China Business Weekly" is a magazine mainly focused on business reporting, and each issue reports on many companies. In this magazine, many "Marketing Department Planning" articles look quite similar to news reports. So why do "Marketing Department Planning" articles charge while reports strictly prohibit any form of transaction? On this difference, I once discussed with the editors during a meeting of "International Public Relations" magazine, and the conclusion was that for enterprises, whether positive or negative, the content and sources of news reports are diversified, and conflicts, phenomena, or concepts are presented in the reports. On the other hand, soft articles are one-sidedly positive, with singular concepts, and generally lack conflict, contradiction, and contrast in their content arrangements. The reason I discussed this with my colleagues is to help us grasp the essence of news reporting and avoid reducing pseudo-news to a minimum.

I don't know if the above text has caused any concern or enlightenment among PR company readers? In my view, the concern is: the current practice of PR companies publishing soft articles or seemingly news-like paid publications may lose its meaning due to being contrary to the development trend of media; the enlightenment is: PR companies might keep pace with the times and help clients engage in genuine information communication and news planning with media, rather than simply buying space and publishing articles.

Actually, we should have already seen that purchasable media space has little communication value; media space with great communication value is often hard to influence. If you don't believe me, PR company readers can try to "break through" "China Business Weekly" and spend money to buy a report for your client; or during a press conference, see if their journalists really don't accept transportation fees. To be honest, I can't fully trust what Editor Yi says, since the editor-in-chief has policies, but the reporters have their own tactics. If anyone verifies this, please let me know the result.

Wang Ran: The Mixed Style of "China Business Weekly"

In the last year or two, the entertainment-ization of financial content seems to have become a trend. Typical examples on TV include "Hall of Fame" and "Boss Hall," and in print media, a typical example is the recently launched "China Business Weekly" ("CBW").

I first saw this magazine at the airport. Since I had already started paying attention to the cross-media platform of "First Financial," and knowing that this newborn was a "child" of my friend He Li, I naturally bought a copy to read on the plane. After flipping through it quickly, I found three or four pieces that made me want to read further (I can't remember exactly what they were, but I do remember one was about NBA China), and I immediately developed a good impression of it. From then on, whenever I see this magazine at the airport, I definitely buy it.

If I were to describe my initial impression of this magazine with two words, they would be sensitivity and mixed style. It has a sharp and clear perception of urban economic life, and stylistically, it seeks to make financial information more relatable and even fashionable, transforming high-end finance into something more accessible to white-collar workers. If you could put the completely different magazines "Caijing" and "Le" under the jurisdiction of Wang Boming into a blender and produce a thoroughly mixed fresh juice, perhaps it would be "CBW."

As for the latest two issues, the contents that caught my eye included the cover stories "Bund Revolution" and "The King of Gambling Loses in Beijing," as well as "Ferragamo's New Path," "Airlines' Trivial Comparisons," "Shai or Share," "Cat and Dog Economy," "Extraordinary Lin Yihua," "Pay for Carbon Emissions at URBN." (In contrast, topics like RMB appreciation, Buffett, Eastern Airlines, Yahoo, domestic strongest brands, talent competition, etc., which are relatively "hard finance," I have no interest in reading in this magazine.)

Last week, I met He Li, the editor-in-chief of "CBW" at an event. I told him: "Your magazine is a bit like CCTV's new building, though it hasn't been fully twisted into place yet, it has already begun to develop its own style."

I advised him: You might as well remove the half-baked hard finance (about 1/3) entirely and go all out for popular finance. National affairs have Shu Po (editor-in-chief of "Caijing") worrying about, and entrepreneur circles have Dong Hua (publisher of "China Entrepreneur") protecting. Instead, focus on creating lifestyle-oriented finance aimed at urban white-collar workers, with some entertainment and lifestyle guidance. Don't compare furrowed brows and deep insights with "Caijing" because they are constantly looking down on global events, and don't envy the convivial gatherings of "China Entrepreneur" because they are truly dedicated to operating circle culture. Just turn this magazine into something we can read leisurely before landing on the plane, or flip through without annoyance while waiting for someone at a coffee shop, mixing finance and fashionable living, where high-end audiences feel young, and ordinary white-collar workers feel sophisticated. That's how you carve out a niche in the midst of all these entrepreneurs and financiers.

In media, the absolute number of people applauding, whether it's ten thousand or a hundred thousand, isn't the most important thing. What's most feared is unclear positioning. There are two situations for unclear positioning: one is really not thinking clearly, and the other is thinking clearly but lacking the ability to execute.

Recently, I've decided not to accept interviews from a certain mainstream financial newspaper in China that was once very popular. The reason is that they have sent three different reporters to interview me on different topics recently, but their questions were all disappointingly poor. Imagine the state of talent reserves at this newspaper if such level of reporters are being sent out for external interviews. This is a classic case of overextension.

It must be acknowledged that in the long run, hard finance (whether on TV or in print media) will have a larger market than popular finance. Therefore, I will continue to be optimistic about mainstream financial print media like "Caijing" and "China Business Daily," and also eagerly anticipate the efforts of the First Financial Channel to accelerate national coverage, as well as its potential competitor, Hubei Satellite TV, which is leveraging its satellite platform to create a Chinese version of CNBC. However, the true golden age of hard finance won't come until our stock citizens stop treating stocks like lottery tickets, which may still require at least three to five years. You must hold out until that day, and before it arrives, seize (mere cultivation is insufficient) and reserve talent as much as possible.

In comparison, "CBW" has chosen a cleverer and easier-to-operate direction. Its requirements for professional financial journalists are not as high as those for hard finance, but at the same time, it effectively caters to the entertainment-oriented trend of Chinese society moving from serious faces to natural, relaxed expressions, as well as the need for semi-reclined lazy reading among the mainstream population whose life pace is accelerating. It also helps cultivate a group of white-collar audiences with high advertising value for the cross-media platform of First Financial.

I wonder if the stylistic choices of "CBW" offer any small amount of enlightenment to those private enterprise bosses who have made money in this industry and are now lining up, crying and shouting, determined to invest heavily in the media.
