Hugo running away for love? In fact, he just wanted to change companies to do Nexus!

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 14:00:49

Hugo Barra left Google to join Xiaomi, not just for a reckless love or a spontaneous trip, but possibly to recreate Nexus.

After Hugo Barra came to Xiaomi, what would he do? MIUI has already deviated far from the original Android ecosystem. Xiaomi phones are just products for the mid-to-low-end market. Is it worth it?

What Hugo values is not the market that Xiaomi has occupied.

Hugo's arrival at Xiaomi is probably not for continuing the glory of the Xiaomi series. Although Xiaomi’s marketing strategy isn't very high-end or luxurious, domestically, it has already surpassed other local manufacturers by leaps and bounds. Additionally, as a "third-party smartphone operating system based on in-depth optimization, customization, and development of the Android system," MIUI has become quite mature. It targets a clear group: satisfying both the desire of so-called enthusiasts to tinker with their devices and meeting the basic needs of domestic novice users who require easy accessibility. However, overall, the proportion of the latter is larger.

Even though his role at Google was mainly focused on Android marketing, he probably wouldn’t get involved too much with Xiaomi phones. While HTC and other large manufacturers are gradually moving closer to the original Android experience, MIUI continues to go further down the path of customization. This is easy to understand from a commercial perspective. For large manufacturers like HTC and Samsung, if they were to fully customize their systems, why would Google spend so much effort creating this system? To prevent its own creations from eroding itself, Google must gradually restrict permissions, and the final result might be either don't use my system, or use the original version —— closure is just a matter of time.

However, all of this faces obstacles domestically, in an environment where only a censored version of Google can be used. Hugo definitely knows all of this, and he surely also knows that MIUI now is merely flying under the banner of Android. If we call iOS the 1.0 era of operating systems, representing closed ecosystems, then Android should be called the 2.0 era, representing openness. Similar to Web 2.0, the User in UGC has shifted from the user to the manufacturer. Poor execution leading to a lack of user experience will, in turn, greatly harm the Android system.

Once you enter the world of tablets, it's deep waters.

But these factors aren't the real reasons Lei Jun poached Hugo. To truly understand why Xiaomi brought Hugo into the fold, we need to look at the products Hugo handled at Google. Google is an internet company that has recently started selling hardware. So far, the only hardware from Google that stands out is Glass and Nexus, and Hugo was responsible for Nexus. Last year, among the Android tablets sold, the Nexus 7 accounted for more than 10%. This gives us some insight into why Hugo joined Xiaomi —— possibly to help Xiaomi create tablet computers.

Then let's discuss Nexus. Even being Google's direct product, its performance is rather mediocre, showing just how deep the waters of the tablet market are. It also indicates from another angle that as long as they're running the Android system, users don't care whether it's the original version or not, nor the difference in user experience. Tablets are not entirely equivalent to smartphones; from the perspective of user habits, tablets have higher requirements for ecosystems because they lack the function of smartphones as communication tools (which also shows that Android's ecosystem still has a lot of room for improvement). Even with apps like Google+, Pocket, and Flipboard integrated into Nexus, compared to iPad, they are incomparable.

Mi Pad?

Considering that the development speed of hardware is slower than software, the competition in tablets is more concentrated on the ecosystems provided by different operating systems. But the development of ecosystems is largely restricted by whether the mobile operating system is open or not. Building an ecosystem is mainly about binding users to make money, and Apple does this well. Different terminals but the same system, whereas Google doesn't. Since Android is open, you shouldn't expect all manufacturers using your system to build your ecosystem on their phones, nor should you expect me to spend money making phones while you sit back and count the money.

Big manufacturers like Samsung can easily set up an app store, with low marginal costs. But for Google to produce hardware, the cost is significant. From Xiaomi phones, it's evident that the intention to build its own ecosystem is already clear, having long since migrated native applications like Gmail to its own app store. Expecting all manufacturers to obediently use the original ecosystem is unrealistic. Instead, finding a well-customized system and branding it with Android could make more money. For example, if you flash MIUI onto another phone, you become part of my ecosystem.

Xiaomi phones have already sold well enough, accumulating a large number of phone users who are already familiar with this operating system. Not making tablets at this point would be against reason. This idea originates from Apple —— accumulating users through phones, building a closed ecosystem, and then making tablets!

Hugo has arrived. As for why he abandoned Nexus and chose Xiaomi, a July interview may offer some clues. In the interview, Hugo mentioned that the construction of Android's ecosystem on tablets hasn't reached the ideal level yet. He praised HTC One's restoration of the original ecosystem and pointed out that one of the reasons for the slow construction of the ecosystem on tablets is price-related:

"I think we are perhaps coming close to it, but this is a $200 device……If you were to price it at $300 or $400, you could do something a little bit more in terms of polish and finish and materials and so on. Why hasn't that happened yet?"

Since Lei Jun can control costs so well, along with the cheap labor force domestically, most importantly, there are already so many enthusiasts accumulated, and Google has been censored domestically, Hugo seems to see a glimmer of hope in Xiaomi. As for how much this tablet will cost, I guess it won't be cheap.
