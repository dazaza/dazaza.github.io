If you really value each user, you should send them emails like this.

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 13:59:17

Because of work, my email inbox is always filled with various emails sent by companies. Many of these stem from product trials during which I left my email address. Afterwards, whenever they have updates or reminders, they send an email over. Typically, I don't bother reading them unless it's for products I use regularly, like Kickstarter’s featured project information or Coursera’s course updates.

These emails are usually bland and formulaic, showing no sense of the person behind the message. But one company stands out: Honey.

Honey is a collaborative social tool designed for enterprise use. It aims to allow users to not only focus on their tasks but also engage in playful banter without affecting productivity. A classmate, Chen, invited me to try it out, so I registered and took a look. As usual, I found myself uninterested in such products, turned around, and never came back. Two weeks later, I received this email: "Keep Track of Honey without leaving your box" (Stay updated on Honey without leaving your inbox).

I clicked through and saw that the email read as follows:

Hi Yang Li,

Did you know that Honey can send you a daily or weekly digest of the most popular content within your company? Our users find this feature quite useful for staying informed about what's buzzing in the office.

It briefly explained the technical details of how to receive these emails and concluded with a sentence: "I'll try to make these emails helpful, but if you don’t find them useful, you can unsubscribe from Honey emails anytime by clicking here."

A week later, I received another email: "Easier ways to post to Honey" (How to post more conveniently on Honey).

Hi Yang Li,

I noticed that you haven't posted anything since joining Honey. I understand that sometimes going to a specific site just to share something can be bothersome. There are actually some simple ways to do it.

You can use our bookmarklet to share directly from your browser or even post via email. For more details, check here: https://honey.is/home/#tools/uploading

Many Honey users find these features great, and we hope you will too.

Three weeks later, another email arrived: "We miss you!" This time, they were genuinely concerned because, from registration to now, I had never opened Honey.

Hi Yang Li,

We haven’t seen you use Honey in a while, and we miss you!

Did you encounter any issues while using Honey? Do you think Honey is useful? Is there anything we can do to make Honey better?

I hope you’ll consider giving Honey another chance. If you’re not interested, I sincerely hope you'll let us know why. Your feedback is incredibly valuable and can help guide us in the right direction.

Thank you!

In terms of effectiveness, it's hard to say exactly how great these three emails were. Yes, these emails aimed to provide inactive users with a few tips and casually ask for feedback. I didn't reply at all since I initially had no intention of using the service. However, I was truly touched by Honey’s dedication. All these emails were personally sent by CEO Dan, and the wording, content selection, and organization were genuinely impressive. Perhaps my translation doesn't fully convey the nuance, but those interested can check out the screenshots of the emails at the end of the article.

Zaarly co-founder Koester shared a perspective in "What Software Makers Can Learn from Hardware Makers." Due to the inherent limitations of hardware products, founding teams must pay close attention to every early adopter and ensure each user follow-up is done thoughtfully. In contrast, software entrepreneurs often get lost in the vast number of users. I believe they could learn something from Honey.
