Amazon's success formula is replicated, or it may not rule the world

by anonymous on 2013-11-25 11:12:49

In the newly published book on Amazon's history, *The Everything Store*, author Brad Stone recounts a conversation between CEO Jeff Bezos and the company’s first employee, Shel Kaphan. In the mid-1990s, when Amazon was still just a bookseller but growing rapidly, Kaphan expressed his doubts to Bezos: Was it really necessary for the company to keep pursuing expansion?

"When you're small, bigger people can take away what you have," Bezos reportedly replied. "We need to create an even playing field."

This strategy has been remarkably effective. However, as overseas online retailers begin to use the same tactics against Amazon, this success may soon hit a wall. Compared to other traditional American retailers, Amazon has been particularly successful in international markets. But if Bezos wants to replicate Amazon's growth curve in the U.S. overseas, it might already be too late—other companies may have already learned Amazon's lessons well.

**Alibaba**

Earlier this week, Chinese consumers celebrated the annual Singles' Day shopping festival. Alibaba Group reported that its platforms generated nearly $6 billion in sales on that day alone. That figure is almost 10% of Amazon's total revenue for all of last year—achieved in a single day.

It should be noted that Alibaba's websites operate more like eBay than Amazon, meaning it only takes a small cut of that $6 billion in sales. Even so, it's still an astonishing number. Last year, ComScore reported that U.S. consumers spent close to $1.5 billion on "Cyber Monday." Their spending during the entire five-day Thanksgiving holiday didn't match Alibaba's one-day sales.

Moreover, Alibaba's record-breaking sales on Singles' Day in 2013 were up over 80% from the previous year. If growth and scale mean everything in online retailing, then Alibaba has perfectly followed Bezos' playbook. It also means that for Amazon to surpass Alibaba in the Chinese market would be as difficult as it would be for other online retailers to beat Amazon in the U.S.

**Snapdeal in India**

A company backed by eBay aims to become the Alibaba of India, also attempting to follow Bezos’ philosophy. Snapdeal recently announced that it expects to generate $500 million in revenue this year, a sixfold increase from last year. While that amount isn’t huge, the growth is impressive. Snapdeal CEO Kunal Bahl pointed out that India's overall retail market is about $600 billion, with e-commerce making up less than 1%.

Like Alibaba, Snapdeal provides a platform for brands to sell their products, hoping to drive the growth of online retail in this way. Its platform aims to address the "trust deficit" regarding online shopping locally. It has a long way to go, but the growth potential in a country with a population of 1 billion is staggering.

India resembles China a few years ago in terms of lacking logistics infrastructure. Freight delivery, payment networks, and supply chains are not as stable or efficient as in developed countries, which forces potential online retailers to build their own complete e-commerce systems. They can't simply plug into UPS's delivery system like Amazon does.

But importantly, the lack of infrastructure support encourages companies like Snapdeal to remain flexible, which aids rapid growth. Unlike Amazon, which has heavily invested long-term and seen profits suffer, Snapdeal hasn't poured vast sums of money and effort into building large warehouses to centrally store large quantities of goods. Instead, as Bahl put it, Snapdeal "is aggregating the long tail of supply."

"That's where the center of gravity lies."

This platform model allows companies like Snapdeal and Alibaba to expand their product categories much faster than Amazon. However, it also makes it harder for them to ensure a consistent experience—an unshakable stability akin to the jet fuel in Amazon's growth engine.

Another lesson taught by Amazon works against its prospects for success in India and China—the power of being a first mover. As *The Everything Store* points out, in the mid-1990s, many smart people never foresaw that Amazon would eventually overshadow Barnes & Noble. The physical bookstore giant reacted slowly, launching its sales website much later to compete with Amazon, and has never caught up.

During Amazon's last earnings call, CFO Tom Szkutak said the company is expanding its B2C sales platform in India. It uses the same business model there as Alibaba and Snapdeal. Amazon is a powerful force, but whether its actions are too late and whether it will fail to become the leader in India just as it couldn't beat Alibaba in China remains to be seen. Besides cooperation and support from brands, companies like Snapdeal have the unique advantage of "local knowledge." In their home markets, they don't face the cultural learning curve that Amazon encounters when venturing outside its own borders.

"Emerging markets require businesses to have a very deep understanding of the local market and local consumers," Bahl said.

"I think they'll definitely get there," he said of Amazon. "But they're not there yet."
