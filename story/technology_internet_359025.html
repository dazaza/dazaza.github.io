360 Data Center Inventory: Technical Means to Keep the Data Center Clean and Tidy

by lovee1f8a3 on 2010-06-24 15:40:50

<div>Most data centers are carefully designed, and many people mistakenly believe that such data centers only need to pursue high efficiency and low energy consumption. Therefore, people often forget the important meaning of a clean environment for data centers. Some data centers might even be in harmful environments due to the entry of outdoor particle or gas pollutants. In some cases, pollutants may also form inside the data center. To prevent data center managers from forgetting this important point, today we will have a simple discussion on data center environmental issues.<br/>

<br/>

The impact of air pollutants on data centers is mainly divided into three categories: chemical effects, mechanical effects, and electrical effects. Copper creep corrosion in circuit boards and silver plating corrosion in small surface-mounted components are two common chemical failures. Mechanical effects include heat sink contamination, optical signal interference, increased friction, etc., and electrical effects include changes in circuit impedance and arcs. Please note that reducing the size of the functional components of circuit boards and achieving miniaturization of components is a necessary condition for improving hardware performance, but it also makes hardware more susceptible to pollutants in the data center environment. Manufacturers are constantly striving to maintain the reliability of hardware while continuously shrinking the size of functional components without taking additional high-cost measures to strengthen all IT equipment. Most of their IT equipment is not installed in corrosive environments, where such environments would subject the equipment to higher risk of failure.

It is the responsibility of data center administrators to maintain the reliability of hardware by monitoring dust and gas pollutants in the data center. The cleanliness of the data center must reach the standard of ISO14644-18 level. Generally speaking, the following corresponding filtration schemes can achieve such a level of cleanliness:

1. Continuously filter indoor air using MERV8 filters as recommended in ANSI/ASHRAE Standard 127-2007 "Method of Testing for Rating Computer and Data Processing Room Unitary Air Conditioners";

2. Filter incoming air to the data center using MERV11 or MERV11 filters as recommended in the ASHRAE book titled "Particulate and Gaseous Contamination in Datacom Environments." Dust sources inside the data center should be minimized.

Gas pollutants should comply with the modified safety level G1 of ANSI/ISA-71.04-1985 to meet the following conditions:

1. Copper reaction rate below 300C/month;

2. Silver reaction rate below 300C/month, and for data centers containing large amounts of gas pollutants, it is strongly recommended to perform vapor-phase filtration on both incoming air and internal air in the data center. It is crucial to follow the requirements listed here to maintain the high reliability of IT equipment and avoid the cost of hardware replacement outside the warranty scope.

Failures caused by dust include (but are not limited to) the following (ASHRAE2009a):

Mechanical effects

These effects include obstructing cooling airflow, interfering with moving parts, wear, optical interference, interconnection interference, surface deformation (e.g., magnetic media), and other similar effects;

Chemical effects

Dust falling on printed circuit boards can cause component corrosion and/or short circuits between adjacent functional components;

Electrical effects

These effects include impedance changes and bridging of conductors in electronic circuits.

Dust is ubiquitous, and even with the best filtering measures, there will still be dust in the data center. This dust will fall on electronic hardware. Fortunately, most dust is harmless. Only in a few cases does dust erode electronic hardware.

Generally, harmful dust in data centers contains a large amount of ions, such as chlorides. These harmful dusts mainly come from coarse outdoor dust with diameters of 2.5-15μm and fine outdoor dust with diameters of 0.1-2.5μm (Comizzoli1993). Coarse dust particles contain various mineral and biological pollutants (mostly formed by wind erosion) and can remain airborne for several days. Fine dust particles are generally formed by mineral fuel combustion and volcanic activity and can remain airborne for several years. Various large saltwater bodies are also a major source of air dust pollutants in data centers. Strong winds in coastal areas can blow sea salt inland up to 10 kilometers (6 miles) or further, and these sea salts can damage electronic equipment within this range (Bennett1969; Crossland1973).

Absorbing moisture from the environment is one way dust damages the reliability of printed circuit boards. Ionic contaminants in wet dust reduce the surface insulation impedance of printed circuit boards, and worse yet, they can cause short circuits between adjacent functional components through ion migration.

Hygroscopic relative humidity refers to the relative humidity at which dust absorbs enough moisture to become wet, causing corrosion and/or ion migration. This determines the corrosiveness of the dust. When the hygroscopic relative humidity of the dust is higher than the relative humidity of the data center, the dust remains dry and will not cause corrosion or ion migration. However, in a few cases, when the hygroscopic relative humidity of the dust is lower than the relative humidity of the data center, the dust absorbs moisture, becomes wet, and causes corrosion and/or ion migration, reducing hardware reliability. A study conducted by Comizzoli et al. in 1993 showed that leakage currents caused by dust resting on printed circuit boards increase exponentially with increasing relative humidity around the world. This study led to the conclusion that keeping the relative humidity of the data center below 60% can keep the leakage current caused by resting dust within an acceptable sub-μA range.

In rare cases, harmful dust may also be generated inside the data center. Humidifiers increase indoor humidity by evaporating water droplets in the air. If the water added to the humidifier has a high salt content and the hygroscopic relative humidity of these salts is lower than the relative humidity of the data center, it may cause harmful indoor dust pollution. Even if the concentration of these salts is very low, they can still pose serious corrosion and ion migration threats. These humidifier-related corrosion problems can be mitigated by treating the water in the humidifier with reverse osmosis (ASHRAE2009a).

Fibrous dust from paper, cardboard, or textiles can contaminate heat sinks and interrupt equipment cooling processes. Data center operators should avoid extensive use of these materials in the data center. For example, new equipment should be unpacked outside the data center, and large printers should be placed elsewhere. Overall, most dust is harmless.

In rare cases, when the hygroscopic relative humidity of resting dust is lower than the relative humidity of the data center, corrosion and/or ion migration problems may occur. Generally, the relative humidity of the data center must be kept below 60% to avoid any dust corrosion of hardware. Zinc whiskers are another way that particulate pollutants severely affect the reliability of hardware; they are the most common conductive particles found in data centers.

To prevent corrosion, the bottom of some raised floor steel tiles are coated with zinc. The beams and bases supporting the steel tiles are also coated with zinc. This zinc may be applied through electroplating or hot-dip galvanizing. Although zinc whiskers can appear in both types of galvanizing, they are more likely to occur in electroplating (Brusse2004; Lahtinen2008). Sometimes, zinc whiskers can grow up to 1 to 2 millimeters long. When steel tiles are disturbed during removal or underground cables are pulled/removed, zinc whiskers may move and become exposed to the air, threatening IT equipment. If IT equipment inhales zinc whiskers, circuits with voltages above 25V may experience short circuits, arcing, signal disturbances, or catastrophic failures (Miller2007).
