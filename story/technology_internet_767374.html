This is the world's first QWERTY full keyboard phone.

by anonymous on 2013-10-13 14:12:27

Many users tend to associate physical keyboards with BlackBerry, largely due to the Canadians' expertise in the development and design of full-keyboard communication terminals. However, in reality, the era of full-keyboard devices was actually ushered in by Nokia at the end of the 20th century. In 1996, catching many competitors off guard, Nokia pioneered a full-keyboard personal digital assistant (PDA) system solution within the mobile phone industry, which later became the widely discussed full-keyboard PDA device. By 1998, Nokia successfully launched the world's first QWERTY full-keyboard product, the Nokia 9000 Communicator. Both its naming and functional settings tightly revolved around the theme of "personal digital assistant communication."

### Hardware Marked by Its Era

The Nokia 9000 Communicator weighed approximately 400 grams with a thickness of 38 millimeters. In today's context where most smartphones weigh less than 100 grams and are thinner than 10 millimeters, this undoubtedly qualifies as a bulky device. However, at that time, even such a product was highly sought after and loved by many users due to its novel concept of a full keyboard.

In terms of hardware, the Nokia 9000 Communicator featured a 24MHz Intel i386 processor with a total storage space of 8MB, divided into three parts: 4MB for application memory, 2MB for program memory, and 2MB available for user use. It boasted an impressive standby time of up to 33 hours. Although these specifications seem outdated today, during a time when PCs were not yet widespread, such configurations not only made task handling effortless but also represented a cutting-edge hardware standard.

### Nokia's PDA Philosophy

The Nokia 9000 Communicator was referred to as a new personal digital assistant communication terminal rather than just a mobile phone for two main reasons:

Firstly, most PDAs at the time had exposed screens and relied on stylus-based human-computer interaction. Nokia, however, introduced a flip-style full keyboard solution. When closed, it functioned as an ordinary phone, and when opened, it transformed into a personal digital assistant. The trade-off for this combination was an unusually thick body.

Secondly, the Nokia 9000 Communicator ran on the GEOS 3.0 operating system, specifically designed for personal digital assistants. The human-computer interaction mode was optimized based on PC operating systems, reflecting a bit of PC product DNA. Functionally, this unique PDA not only supported basic note-taking, SMS, email, and fax features but also included calendar, scheduling, and WAP browser functions, along with a multi-party conference call hands-free feature.

Nowadays, many mobile phone manufacturers attempt to enrich the user experience beyond the terminal device, such as through accessories. Similarly, Nokia experimented with providing richer and more practical features for its personal digital assistant, the Nokia 9000 Communicator, around the turn of the century, including charging stands and car kits, all closely aligned with the habits and needs of PDA users.

### Transformation in Differentiation

The Nokia 9000 Communicator marked the beginning of the full-keyboard personal digital assistant era, but this differentiated advantage did not last long. Competitors like Ericsson, Motorola, and Palm soon launched their own flagship products, prompting Nokia to symbolically improve its first-generation product, introducing the lighter and thinner 9110 and 9110i models in the Communicator series. However, since the overall appearance and layout remained unchanged, they could not quickly outpace similar products until the launch of the Symbian OS-powered 9210, which helped Nokia gradually establish a foothold in the PDA market with its rich software functionality.

Nokia’s debut in the PDA market was through the 9000 Communicator, while its maturity began with the 9210. Accompanying the iteration of the Symbian operating system, to unify the user experience and meet strategic positioning needs, Nokia gradually downplayed the "personal digital assistant" product concept, transitioning towards the smartphone era represented by Symbian. As a result, few people later referred to its products as PDAs, opting instead for the term full-keyboard phones. However, some iconic elements from that period continued to be carried forward in subsequent products, such as the once highly popular Nokia E71, which combined ultra-thin design with a full keyboard.

### Transitional Human-Computer Interaction Solution

From the perspective of achievements in full-keyboard phones, Nokia was undoubtedly a strong force. Despite facing deep-rooted and powerful competitors like BlackBerry in this market, the significance of Nokia's leadership in initiating the full-keyboard phone trend is remarkable. This new form of human-computer interaction, represented by button-based interaction, served as a moderate solution that allowed users to become familiar with, accept, and quickly adapt to the transition from traditional feature phones to modern smartphones dominated by touchscreen interaction.

Of course, this human-computer interaction solution also had its shortcomings, such as overly focusing on replicating the PC user experience onto mobile platforms. This design and R&D mindset restricted breakthroughs in mobile product human-computer interaction methods. Fortunately, the era of touch-based human-computer interaction eventually arrived.
