Introduction to Flower and Green Plant Rental (Part Two): Suitable Flowers for Office Environments

1. With the widespread adoption of office automation, equipment such as barcode printers, label printers, and single-print machines, along with carpets, wallpapers, and adhesives, have all contributed to varying degrees of indoor air pollution. To effectively manage office pollution, apart from enhancing ventilation, the simplest and most effective method is to place potted flowers in the office, because green plants are "absorbers" of harmful substances within the office environment. Pollutants in the office can be absorbed by ornamental plants through the tiny pores on the underside of their leaves, and microorganisms around the roots of these plants can also break down pollutants and absorb them through the root system.

2. To effectively eliminate multiple pollutants in the office, it is advisable to place different types of plants simultaneously. For every ten square meters of space, at least one to two types of pollutant-absorbing plants should be placed. Suitable ornamental plants for office environments include: Spider plants, Chrysanthemums, and various flowering varieties that primarily absorb formaldehyde but can also handle benzene emissions from printers and barcode label printers, as well as nicotine. Ferns, Ivy, Cycads, and Chrysanthemums can decompose three harmful substances: formaldehyde found in carpets and plywood, xylene hidden in wallpapers and printing ink solvents that are harmful to kidneys, and toluene found in dyes and cleaning agents. Red Poinsettias can absorb xylene, toluene, and ammonia present in synthetic fibers, solvents, and paints. Dragon Blood Trees (Ironwood), Daisies, and Peace Lilies can remove trichloroethylene originating from printers, laser printers, and cleaning agents and adhesives.

How to Properly Care for Ornamental Plants?

1. Maintenance of Ornamental Plants: Pay attention to light regulation. These plants need light, but they should not be left under strong indirect sunlight for extended periods. In a week, ornamental plants should be exposed to indoor light for at least a day or a day and a half. However, avoid exposing them to direct sunlight at noon; instead, expose them in the morning or late afternoon to prevent leaf scorching. For some plants that require more light, artificial lighting like fluorescent lamps can be used as supplements. If plants like Snake Plants, Philodendrons, Rubber Trees, Variegated Peace Lilies, etc., experience prolonged lack of light, their leaves may turn yellow or pale green. In such cases, quickly move the plant to a location with adequate light and rotate the pot 90 degrees regularly to ensure even light exposure on the leaf surfaces.

2. Watering Ornamental Plants: The principle of "only water when necessary, and thoroughly when watering" applies. During winter, in addition to watering the soil, misting the leaves keeps them moist, which benefits growth. In summer, since most ornamental plants originate from tropical and subtropical regions and prefer warmth, overwatering should be avoided to prevent issues like yellowing leaves, poor growth, or even plant death due to excessive moisture.

3. Ventilation: Most ornamental plants thrive outdoors, but modern homes often have good insulation. Good ventilation in well-lit areas is beneficial for plant health. Poor ventilation can lead to overheating and high humidity levels indoors, so maintaining proper airflow helps plants grow better and reduces damage caused by dampness or heat stress. Additionally, good airflow minimizes pest and disease problems.

4. Fertilization: Generally, fertilize ornamental plants once a month or every 15 days. Use diluted fertilizer rather than concentrated ones. Avoid fertilizing during summer and high-temperature periods.

5. Heat Protection and Insulation: Most ornamental plants require protection from cold in winter. Species like Variegated Peace Lilies, Pothos, Cycads, Calatheas, Bromeliads, Aglaonemas, Kentia Palms, Dieffenbachias, Alocasias (Elephant Ears), and Phalaenopsis Orchids prefer warmer temperatures. They should be placed in rooms with heating equipment where the temperature remains above 12°C. Some hardier species like Miniature Palms, Philodendrons, and Chinese Evergreens can tolerate slightly lower temperatures.

6. Pot Selection for Ornamental Plants: Plastic pots, clay pots, and purple sand pots can all be used, but clay pots are preferred due to their excellent air and water permeability, which promotes vigorous plant growth. However, they might not be aesthetically pleasing or lightweight enough, so many people now use colored plastic pots as decorative outer covers, known as "double-pot systems."

Soil Types Best Suited for Growing Potted Plants:

Different flower species and stages of growth require different soils. Commonly used potting materials include:

- Sandy loam: Clean texture, low clay content, good drainage, suitable for seed sowing, seedling cultivation, and cuttings. Also an important material for making potting mixes.

- Garden soil: Semi-clayey sandy soil, containing a certain amount of humus, suitable for planting robust shrubs or adjusting potting mixes.

- Peat soil: Acidic or neutral, contains a certain amount of organic matter, suitable for slow-growing evergreen shrub cuttings or mixed with sandy loam for general flower seed sowing or cuttings, also useful for making potting mixes.

- Compost soil: Also called leaf mold, porous, loose, acidic or slightly acidic, rich in humus, suitable for growing acid-loving flowers, and an important material for making potting mixes.

- Roadside accumulated soil: Loose, with good permeability and fertility, suitable for growing general flowers or making potting mixes.

- Mountain soil: Slightly acidic, commonly used for growing orchids or making potting mixes for Camellias, Azaleas, etc.

- Pond mud: Rich pond mud, soft texture, good drought and flood resistance. Often used for growing black orchids imported from the south, requiring soil replacement after one or two years.

- Coal ash: Lightweight and porous, containing trace nutrients, can replace sand in potting mixes, partially soluble in water, suitable for flower absorption.

Flower Species Not Suitable for Indoor Placement:

- Roses: Their fragrance can cause discomfort, shortness of breath, and breathing difficulties in some individuals.

- Orchids: Their scent can cause excessive excitement and sleeplessness if inhaled for long periods.

- Judas trees: Their pollen can induce asthma or worsen cough symptoms if in contact for too long.

- Night-blooming Jasmine: Emits strong odorous particles at night, causing dizziness and discomfort in people with hypertension or oral diseases, potentially worsening their conditions.

- Tulips: Their flowers contain toxic alkaloids, prolonged contact can accelerate hair loss.

- Oleander: Its flowers emit a gas that can make people sleepy and reduce mental acuity if inhaled for too long; its secreted milky liquid can be toxic if in contact for too long.

- Pine and Cypress: Their fragrant emissions can irritate the gastrointestinal tract, affecting appetite and causing nausea and dizziness in pregnant women.

- Hydrangeas: Their emitted particles can cause skin allergies and itching in some individuals.

- Rhododendrons: Their flowers release toxins, ingestion can cause poisoning, severe cases leading to respiratory arrest and serious harm to health.

- Lilacs: Their fragrance can cause excessive excitement of the central nervous system if inhaled for too long, leading to sleeplessness.

Eight Things to Avoid When Fertilizing Flowers:

1. Do not fertilize newly planted plants: New transplants are already stressed, additional external stimuli could prevent healing and lead to root rot.

2. Do not fertilize during early blooming: Early fertilization can cause bud drop, flower drop, and fruit drop.

3. Do not fertilize during dormancy: Dormant plants have slowed metabolism and photosynthesis. Fertilizing would disrupt dormancy, causing unnecessary growth and depletion of reserves needed for next year's blooms.

4. Do not fertilize directly at the base of the roots: As plants grow, their root systems expand. Fertilizing directly at the root base is less effective. Instead, apply fertilizer appropriately between the roots or around the edge of the pot to enhance root absorption.

5. Do not use excessively diluted fertilizers: This could result in insufficient nutrient supply. Generally, follow the "frequent light fertilization" principle, using a mix of three parts fertilizer to seven parts water being optimal. Using unfermented fertilizers can attract pests, produce foul odors, pollute the environment, and cause fermentation that damages plant roots upon contact with water.

6. Do not overuse nitrogen fertilizers: Balanced application of nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium is recommended. Overusing nitrogenous chemical fertilizers can cause excessive vegetative growth, delaying blooming or preventing blooming altogether, resulting in smaller, paler flowers.

7. Do not fertilize sick or weak plants: Weak plants with thin stems and poor photosynthesis have slow metabolism. Applying fertilizer arbitrarily can cause further damage due to inability to properly utilize nutrients.
