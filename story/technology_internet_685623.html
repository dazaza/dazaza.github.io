Media reports: 13 volunteers in Syria were killed while escorting journalists to leave the country

by abcurtbt on 2012-03-01 15:56:08

According to foreign media reports on February 28, a team of Syrian volunteers known as the "Dare-to-Die Squad" attempted on the evening of February 26th to escort four foreign journalists out of the country. Among them were two wounded journalists from Homs. However, they were continuously attacked by government forces and only managed to successfully escort British journalist Paul Conroy to Lebanon. The whereabouts of the remaining journalists are currently unknown, and thirteen members of the "Dare-to-Die Squad" were killed in the operation.

**26-hour life-and-death escape**

On the evening of February 26th, during a lull in the shelling, the Syrian "Dare-to-Die Squad" set out under cover of darkness from the Baba Amr district of Homs on this perilous escort mission. They aimed to transport the injured British journalist Paul Conroy, French journalist Edith Bouvier, and two other journalists to neighboring Lebanon. Bouvier, due to severe injuries, had to be carried on a stretcher by the volunteers. Along the way, they had to avoid patrols and snipers from the government forces.

A Syrian activist coordinating the evacuation stated: "When they reached the edge of the Baba Amr district, they were subjected to heavy fire, including rocket attacks and tank bombardments. They could only move from one house to another." At this point, three members of the "Dare-to-Die Squad" were killed, forcing the entire group to retreat.

According to Wisam Tarifi, spokesperson for the Avaaz organization sponsoring the evacuation, the team escorting Conroy managed to continue their journey and entered Lebanon through a smuggling route. The entire evacuation process took 26 hours. However, this team also came under attack, losing three members. The remaining three journalists and their escorts seemed to have retreated back to Baba Amr, tragically resulting in seven Syrians being killed by shelling during the retreat. Tarifi expressed high respect for these "brave and passionate Syrian youths" who assisted in the evacuation.

**Complicated situation leads to reporting errors**

On February 28th, multiple media outlets and French President Nicolas Sarkozy announced that French journalist Edith Bouvier and British journalist Paul Conroy had safely entered Lebanese territory, expressing relief that "the nightmare was over." However, Sarkozy soon retracted his statement: "The situation is very complicated, I cannot make any comments, and I apologize for my earlier remarks." The French Interior Ministry stated that it was still unable to confirm Bouvier's location, and France's Le Figaro newspaper pointed out that previous reports about her safety in Lebanon were incorrect.

Ambulances from the Syrian Red Crescent had entered the Baba Amr district of Homs multiple times. There were reports that the stranded journalists had repeatedly "refused" to leave with the convoys, with the last refusal occurring on February 27th. Although Red Crescent personnel did not see any wounded journalists, intermediaries indicated that the journalists were unwilling to withdraw. In reality, these journalists had already begun their secret escape mission on the evening of February 26th.

**Secret smuggling routes commit crimes**

Paul Conroy and his escorts entered Lebanon through a secret smuggling route. In the six days prior to February 26th, 40 Syrian casualties had entered Lebanon via this route. Ten months ago, the human rights organization Avaaz collaborated with Syrian activists to develop this route, primarily using it at night to transport medical supplies and food, while also evacuating injured people from Homs.

An Avaaz spokesperson said: "That evening, all four of them unanimously decided to exit via the smuggling route. This route was very complex because they had to avoid Syrian troops, minefields, and a large military base. Along the way, they would quickly exchange information via satellite phone at each checkpoint to ensure safety before continuing. Sometimes they also rode motorcycles."
