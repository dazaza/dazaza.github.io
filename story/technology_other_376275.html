"Use 'gather, fight, calculate' to defeat 'set, nurture, kill'_ Patent Search_ Baidu Space"

by zhihui009 on 2010-12-28 16:48:36

— A Brief Discussion on the Essentials of Patent War Rights Protection

The strategy that foreign patent holders use to charge Chinese enterprises for patent fees is similar to the tactics of major players in the stock market: trapping, nurturing, and killing. Trapping refers to capturing, where patent holders establish a patent pool through patent applications and layouts, enveloping Chinese manufacturing under the control of patents. Nurturing means "raising fish by releasing water," which involves cultivating users through a series of strategies, enticing manufacturers to invest while charging low or no patent fees, allowing Chinese manufacturing to continuously increase its investment until it becomes deeply entrenched; killing refers to waging a patent war, collecting high licensing fees.

These three strategies are most classically represented by the fate of DVD manufacturing companies. Trapping refers to applying for an airtight series of patents, forming patent pools such as "3C", "1C", and "6C"; nurturing refers to ignoring or testing the waters before 2003; killing refers to the large-scale crackdown starting from 2003, collecting $20 in patent fees per DVD player. After these three steps, Chinese manufacturing feels the pressure, but foreign patent holders have made substantial profits.

To counter trapping, nurturing, and killing, I believe the correct strategy Chinese enterprises should adopt is gathering, fighting, and calculating.

Gathering refers to King Goujian of Yue's "ten years of gathering, ten years of lessons" approach. What Chinese manufacturing lacks most now is a repository of patents. Only by spending a considerable amount of time accumulating strength like King Goujian can one have the opportunity to compete with international patent holders. Gathering can be done in two ways: acquisition and "production."

Mergers and acquisitions (M&A) are an important way for Chinese enterprises to expand overseas markets. Many Chinese enterprises focus on tangible properties such as factories and machines, as well as sales channels, but rarely consider the patents of the acquired company, and even if they do, the acquisition may not succeed. Two years ago, TCL loudly proclaimed obtaining Thomson's TV patents and TCL's mobile phone patents, but it turned out that they had no intention of giving you the patents. Patent fees still need to be paid, and if the joint venture fails, the patents remain theirs. It is evident that seriously considering patent assets during M&A is very necessary. Lenovo has done this well; when acquiring IBM's personal computer division, Lenovo simultaneously acquired over 1500 patents and a laboratory in Japan capable of producing patents.

Patent acquisition can only address urgent needs; for sustainable development, the key is for enterprises to have the ability to develop their own patents. I refer to the process of enterprises developing patents through self-research and applying for patents as "production." In the knowledge economy era, patents have become conventional weapons, and enterprises must purposefully and systematically produce patents.

Fighting refers to engaging in intelligent and brave struggles against Western patent holders to strive for reduced or exempted patent fees and secure manufacturing rights with limited stakes. Chairman Mao once said, "Struggle for unity and unity will exist; compromise for unity and unity will not exist." The essence of fighting against Western patent holders lies in daring to fight and skillful fighting.

Chairman Mao also said, "First, fear nothing; second, oppose." Although Chinese enterprises have grown and developed, they are still small compared to multinational corporations. Fighting in unfamiliar foreign courts and under conditions of significant disparity requires even more courage. Not long ago, Zhejiang's Tongling Technology Group won a lawsuit against Leviton, one of the world's top 500 companies. To win this U.S.-initiated intellectual property lawsuit between China and the United States, Tongling Technology spent over $2 million just on litigation fees. The court ruled that Tongling's ground fault circuit interrupter products did not infringe on Leviton's patents. Chairman Chen Wusheng said, "This cost was not insignificant, but it was worth it!" This is courage!

Besides courageous fighting, there is also clever fighting. In 2004, Huawei was sued by Cisco for infringing on five patents and several copyrights. To resolve the situation, Huawei reached a cooperation agreement with Cisco's competitor 3COM. Eventually, 3COM negotiated with Cisco, resolving the lawsuit through settlement.

Calculating refers to planning. Sun Tzu's Art of War mentions "temple calculations," which involve comprehensive planning before going to war, ensuring that one "knows oneself and the enemy, and will never be imperiled in a hundred battles." Facing the pressure from Western patent holders, we must calculate — calculate patent layout, calculate patent licensing fees, calculate the validity of patents, calculate strategically and tactically, so that we can secure manufacturing rights at the lowest cost.

Everyone knows that patent litigation costs overseas are high. In fact, according to overseas lawyer peers, Chinese enterprises lack experience in patent litigation, and in many cases, they are taken advantage of by patent lawyers. For example, in the lead attorney aspect, since American lawyers charge based on case handling time, if Chinese enterprises don't request otherwise, patent lawyers will arrange as many lawyers as possible to participate in the litigation or insert unnecessary investigative activities, which do not help the litigation but significantly increase expenses. Overseas large companies generally pay great attention to saving litigation costs, sometimes entrusting only two lead attorneys for a patent case, and sometimes hiring non-litigation lawyers outside the litigation team for settlement negotiations to reduce litigation fees.

In addition to litigation fees, other patent activities must also be calculated, including conducting patent layout analysis before application and eliminating non-critical patents during management. All of these require appropriate tools. Patent analysis tools include patent analysis software and patent maps. There are many patent analysis software options abroad, and in recent years, related companies and products have also appeared in China, which is a trend. Patent maps are the product of patent analysis software, visualizing patent analysis data into charts for reference by corporate decision-makers. The Japanese Inventors Association provides its members with over 40 types of patent maps annually. Chinese enterprises are relatively small, and except for a few large enterprises, none have the ability to establish their own patent research repositories or draw patent maps. Therefore, organizations like the Chinese Inventors Association need to step forward, drawing professional patent maps for key industries to guide Chinese enterprises in their struggles against overseas patent holders. (Intellectual Property News, Wang Jigang)
