Scholar impostors question Cao Cao tomb fugitive who has repeatedly rejected face-to-face interviews

by bi1r8063ha on 2012-02-22 20:58:03

"Yan Peidong" claims that the stone plaque labeled as "Wei King's Regularly Used Great Halberd" is a forgery. (Reference Photo) Once impersonated a journalist and was wanted online by the police, in 2010 he gained fame by questioning the authenticity of Cao Cao's tomb in Anyang, Henan. The police have confirmed - the "pioneer in exposing Cao Cao's tomb" turned out to be an escapee.

Yesterday afternoon, the Qiaodong Branch of the Xingtai Public Security Bureau announced that "Yan Peidong," who claimed in 2010 to possess irrefutable evidence proving the fabrication of "Cao Cao's Tomb," has been verified to be Hu Zegun, a fugitive wanted online by law enforcement agencies.

This morning, reporters from the Legal Evening News learned from the Qiaodong Branch of the Xingtai Public Security Bureau that the fugitive Hu Zegun is still at large. The police hope to gather information about the fugitive through media reports and will reward individuals providing case-solving clues with 500 yuan.

Police Revelation: "Yan Peidong," the Three Kingdoms expert, turns out to be a fugitive online.

At 4:50 PM yesterday, the Qiaodong Branch of the Xingtai Public Security Bureau revealed via a microblog post under the name "Xingtai Qiaodong Public Security Network Spokesperson" that "Yan Peidong," a well-known internet figure in 2010 for accusing the fabrication of Cao Cao's tomb, is actually Hu Zegun, a fugitive currently being pursued.

According to the data provided by the police, Hu Zegun was listed as a fugitive online for pursuit by the public security organs on February 23, 2006, and remains at large.

At 8:20 AM today, the Legal Evening News confirmed from the command center of the Xingtai Public Security Bureau that the content of the aforementioned microblog post is true. The staff of the command center disclosed that "Yan Peidong" is indeed the fugitive Hu Zegun, who remains at large, and the police are making every effort to capture him.

Investigation Process: Impersonating a journalist to defraud money, he was wanted online six years ago.

In early 2005, Hu Zegun impersonated a journalist from a certain media outlet, claiming publicly in various settings that he could provide legal consultations and handle wrongful or unjust cases. He even found multiple issues of the media's newspaper, noting his contact number on them and distributing them widely.

In May 2005, Hu Zegun, under the pretense of helping Wang, a villager from Jinci Dao Village in Qiaodong District, win a land dispute case within the village, defrauded the victim Wang of over 56,000 RMB.

On February 10, 2006, after receiving a report, the Qiaodong Public Security Branch conducted an investigation. On February 21, the case was officially filed, and on February 23, Hu Zegun was listed as a fugitive online for pursuit. In this fraud case, the police discovered that Hu Zegun used the pseudonym "Ren Jian'an." During subsequent investigations, the police found that Hu Zegun had impersonated multiple names and was suspected of involvement in several fraud cases.

Rewarding "Three Kingdoms Expert Yan Peidong" Exposes Identity

After the police launched the Clear Web Operation, the Qiaodong branch of the Xingtai police increased efforts to encourage Hu Zegun to surrender, repeatedly persuading him through family members and village cadres. Meanwhile, the police also intensified their investigation into the case.

On November 10, when visiting one of Hu Zegun's friends, officers of the Qiaodong branch learned that during the spring and summer of this year, Hu Zegun had frequent interactions with this friend. Hu Zegun proudly claimed to his friend that he was the much-discussed "Yan Peidong" in the media. Additionally, he attended a certain event in the western mountainous area as a specially invited guest under the name "Yan Peidong."

Subsequently, the police compared Hu Zegun's photo with the photos of "Yan Peidong" published in the media and determined that the "physical characteristics matched." The police then located Hu Zegun's rented residence in Nantou Street, Ren County. The landlord said that Hu Zegun registered under the name Hu Jun, and "Hu Jun" did not often return to the rented place.

When the police showed the photo to the landlord for identification, the landlord immediately recognized that the fugitive Hu Zegun was the "Hu Jun" who rented his house. However, by this time, Hu Zegun had moved out a month ago.

Meanwhile, the police noticed that since then, the blog of "Yan Peidong" online had no further updates, and the phone number published on the blog had stopped being used. The police believed that during the October visit to Hu Zegun's relatives, they "probably startled him." Taking all factors into account, the police ultimately determined that "Yan Peidong," who made a big splash in the controversy over Cao Cao's tomb, was Hu Zegun.

Reporter's Experience: "Yan Peidong" Repeatedly Refused Face-to-face Interviews

After the confirmation of Cao Cao's tomb in Anyang, Henan in late 2009, doubts persisted. Among these, "Yan Peidong" caused the biggest stir.

The Legal Evening News reporter contacted "Yan Peidong" several times by phone. During the interviews, he refused requests for face-to-face interviews, citing safety concerns due to his stance against the authenticity of Cao Cao's tomb in Anyang.

During conversations, Yan Peidong generally spoke incoherently, especially when questioned by reporters, showing signs of nervousness and sometimes stuttering.

In late August 2010, media reported that the "National High-level Forum on Three Kingdoms Culture" held in Suzhou on August 21 featured a scholar from Hebei named "Yan Peidong." He claimed to have obtained substantial evidence that could expose the entire fabrication process of the so-called "Cao Cao Tomb Evidence." If revealed, his evidence would overturn the claim of Cao Cao's tomb, turning it into a colossal hoax.

"Yan Peidong's" statements sparked heated debates between the "anti-Cao" and "pro-Cao" factions regarding the authenticity of Cao Cao's tomb, making "Yan Peidong" a representative figure of the "anti-Cao" camp.

On the morning of August 26, 2010, our reporter first contacted "Yan Peidong." He stated that on January 6, 2010, when he visited Xigaohexue Village in Anfeng Township, Anyang, many villagers involved in the excavation of the tomb told him that Cao Cao's tomb was originally empty, and the items inside were custom-made and buried later at a forgery site.

"Yan Peidong" mentioned that he went to the forgery site referred to by the villagers, spending 170 yuan to make a Ming Dynasty dragon-patterned stone plaque. The boss of the forgery site informed him that the "Wei Wuwang Changsuoyong Gehu Da Ji" unearthed from Cao Cao's tomb was produced there.

"Yan Peidong" also indicated that he did not record audio or take photos during his visits to the forgery site. However, the reporter noticed that before accepting our interview, "Yan Peidong" told other media outlets that he possessed all the evidence of Cao Cao's tomb forgery, including audio and video recordings taken at the forgery site. His statements to different media outlets contained contradictions.

Later, "Yan Peidong" disappeared for a while, and attempts to contact him failed, either because his phone was off or because he frequently changed numbers. The mystery surrounding "Yan Peidong's" supposed release of a series of evidence proving Cao Cao's tomb forgery remained unresolved.

Investigation Progress: Multiple Identities Suspected of Forgery

The reporter noticed that during the months when "Yan Peidong" was most famous, his real identity had already been questioned.

During that period, "Yan Peidong" appeared in the media with multiple identities: Secretary-General of the "United Nations World New Economy (China) Research Institute," Executive Editor-in-Chief of "Chinese Cultural Development Internal Reference," Advisor to the "Chinese Three Kingdoms Culture Research Center," and Director of the "Beijing Longteng Shengshi Travel Culture Information Consulting Center."

However, according to media investigations at the time, all four of these identities were suspected to be fabricated.

Another important clue was that according to searches by Hebei public security authorities, there were only two people named Yan Peidong in the entire province at the time: one was a farmer from Weixian in Xingtai, and the other was a 17-year-old student in Zhangjiakou.

According to investigations by Hebei police, there was only one Yan Peidong in the entire Xingtai area at the time: Yan Peidong from Liyuan Village in Weixian. And this Yan Peidong was not the same person as the highly visible "Yan Peidong."

"Yan Peidong" Remains at Large; Police Offer Reward

This morning, the Legal Evening News learned from the Qiaodong Branch of the Xingtai Public Security Bureau that "Yan Peidong," the fugitive Hu Zegun, remains at large, and the police are actively pursuing his capture.

An officer from the Qiaodong Branch of the Xingtai Public Security Bureau introduced that in recent years, the Xingtai police have been following up on this case and have done countless rounds of work with his family. "But Hu Zegun's family has always been inconsistent, and he has never surrendered." The officer explained that Hu Zegun's phone number kept changing, and he often used aliases, making it difficult for the police to capture him.

When discussing how Hu Zegun turned out to be Yan Peidong, the officer also expressed surprise. "We were quite shocked when we confirmed that 'Yan Peidong' was Hu Zegun." The officer introduced to the Legal Evening News reporter over the phone that the police discovered that "Yan Peidong" became extremely famous in 2010 for publicly pointing out the fabrication of Cao Cao's tomb. "We didn't expect 'Yan Peidong' to be so well-known."

According to the Xingtai police, the Qiaodong Branch of the Xingtai Public Security Bureau has now published a hotline number and promised to reward individuals providing case-solving clues with 500 yuan.

Famous for Accusing Cao Cao's Tomb Forgery

"Yan Peidong's" fame stems from the 2010 debate over the fabrication of Cao Cao's tomb.

In 2010, as the archaeological work on Cao Cao's Mausoleum progressed, the authenticity of Cao Cao's tomb sparked intense debates.

On June 15, 2010, "Yan Peidong" revealed through the media that Pan Wei-bin, captain of the Henan archaeological team, participated in the forgery and buried fake artifacts in the tomb before digging them out to pass them off as genuine, causing a stir in public opinion.

After August 25, 2010, when interviewed by various media outlets, Yan Peidong claimed that the most crucial evidence of "Cao Cao's Tomb," the stone plaque inscribed with "Wei Wudi Chang Suo Yong Ge Hu Da Ji," was forged.

On the evening of August 27, 2010, Yan Peidong made his first public appearance, giving an exclusive interview to a Hebei media outlet, claiming to possess a series of evidence proving the fabrication of "Cao Cao's Tomb," forming a complete chain of evidence. However, when asked to present the "irrefutable evidence," Yan Peidong dodged the question, claiming that an important piece of evidence (a fake stone plaque from Nanyang, Henan) had not yet been acquired.

Around September 10, 2010, just as everyone hoped that Yan Peidong would soon reveal the evidence of the fabrication of "Cao Cao's Tomb," he began deliberately avoiding interviews with journalists, and the "irrefutable evidence" became increasingly elusive.

Subsequently, the once-famous "Yan Peidong" faded from public view as attention to Cao Cao's tomb diminished. His true identity also became a mystery.
