Synopsis:

Thirty-five-year-old Bubby has never left the dirty, cramped small apartment where he lives alone with a cat and his tyrannical mother. He not only endures his mother's beatings and verbal abuse but also engages in an incestuous relationship with her. Despite being mentally stunted due to this environment, Bubby possesses a unique talent for perfectly mimicking anyone. One day, a suddenly appearing drunken old man claims to be his father and kicks him out of the house. Enraged, Bubby suffocates his parents and steps out of his home. In this unfamiliar world, Bubby imitates everything he sees, causing numerous laughs and setbacks. By chance, he dons his father's priest costume, steps onto the stage, and begins mimicking all the voices he hears, becoming an overnight sensation and gaining many fans. He also discovers his ability to communicate with cerebral palsy patients. Eventually, Buby finds his true love.

The R-rated black comedy directed by Rolf de Heer in Australia in 1993 caused quite a stir at the Venice Film Festival in 1993 and won the Special Jury Prize. It received four awards at the Australian AFI Film Awards, including Best Director and Best Actor, and is considered one of the most important works in the history of cinema in the 1990s.

In this wild yet profound film, director Rolf de Heer directly points his lens at society: Bubby, who is good at imitation and honest, is like a mirror reflecting the myriad aspects of the world. In his ignorant and laughable ways lies a strange solemnity and sadness. The veil of pretense in worldly attitudes is lifted under his imitation, leaving behind only a chaotic and lamentable essence. The lead actor, K. Hooper, shows astonishing acting potential in this film, portraying Bubby's innocent ignorance and pitiable nature vividly. This film caused a stir at the Venice Film Festival that year and won the Special Jury Prize.

Film Review:

Everyone Loves a Fool

The god of cinema favors fools because fools are purer. The brain grooves of fools may be rougher and more direct, highlighting the hypocrisy of the masses. And actors playing fools often win awards and accolades because they can endure and act foolishly. Although limited by the obscure subject matter and bold imagery, the R-rated black comedy "Bad Boy Bubby" did not enter the mainstream market, its appearance still caused a great stir. As an Australian film, "Bad Boy Bubby" caused strong shock at the Venice Film Festival in 1993 and won the Special Jury Prize. At home in Australia, it deservedly won four AFI Film Awards, including Best Director. Lead actor Nicholas Hope also won Best Actor for his outstanding performance in this film. We don't know how much of this was due to the "fool effect." To this day, the tragic "Bad Boy Bubby" seems to have faded into obscurity, yet many critics still believe that "Bad Boy Bubby" deserves a place in cinematic history. This phenomenon arises not only because the incestuous theme of the film is shocking and filthy, making it unacceptable to the mainstream perspective, but also because the complex and profound humanistic concern expressed in the film is difficult for ordinary audiences to grasp.

I. Born Wild

Labeled as a "black comedy," "Bad Boy Bubby" has no comedic elements at the beginning. The 35-year-old Bubby trapped in a confined room has a vacant and dull gaze. His only friends are cockroaches and an unfortunate cat. His daily entertainment consists of imitating his mother, torturing the cat and cockroaches. His mother confines him, insults him, beats him, and engages in incest with him. He pulls off all the legs of a cockroach and watches it struggle helplessly. His mother needs only a word to make him sit alone in an empty room, unmoving. Bubby is sad; he wants to know about the outside world, but his mother tells him there is poison gas outside. He wants to understand the basics of survival but can only sit alone in an empty room. He wants to understand the meaning of "breathing" and even survival. But his mother restrains his neck with a thick arm from behind and covers his face tightly with another hand. After a moment of silence, Bubby begins to struggle due to suffocation, and his mother suddenly lets go, saying, "This is—breathing."

If the mother represents the extreme ugliness of humanity, then Bubby's father surpasses that limit. When he first appears, he is a close-up of a man with a scruffy face, reeking of alcohol, and full of deceitful words. He speaks smoothly and lies effortlessly, yet he can deceive Florence, Bubby's mother, into smiling broadly. This man teaches Bubby to call him "Dad." Bubby also learns obscene language and how to flirt with his mother from him. Ironically, Bubby learns the Roman collar used by priests and the uncanny ability to flirt with his mother from his father. Thus, when he flirts with his mother in the same obscene tone, he is beaten up by his enraged father and fearful mother who doesn't want their incest exposed. At this time, he still believes the outside world is full of poison gas, so he wears a gas mask, screaming and struggling to breathe for the first time for his own "survival."

Literarily, the "man in a case" is Belikov, who confines himself in a case. In the film, the man in a case is Bubby himself. The dual shackles on his spirit and body are given by his parents. His mother deceives Bubby by telling him the outside world is full of poison gas, so she makes him wear a gas mask whenever he goes out, confining his body. She preaches to Bubby that "God is watching, I am the executor," confining his spirit. Moreover, she sexually abuses him, completely losing all sense of morality. Perhaps this is the concept that director Rolf de Heer wants to convey: a child born into this world will be physically and spiritually confined, and his body will be defiled. But the defiled is not just Bubby; it is all of humanity. Bubby is the son of humanity. Such a film opening would deter most viewers, shocking them with its outrageous and filthy imagery, and startling them with the oppressive and ugly aspects of humanity reflected in the film. It is said that during the early stages of creation, Rolf de Heer tried shooting the story set in the room with a distorted 1.66:1 aspect ratio, making the already oppressive plot even more unbearable, almost inducing claustrophobia. Therefore, Rolf de Heer had to cancel this distortion. Even so, the reputation of the film's "dark thirty minutes" remains widespread among viewers.

The 30-minute turning point is the first climax of the film. After finally realizing the false world created by the tiny prison, Bubby angrily smashes this fake world. In the laughing and beating hell created by his drunk parents, he picks up cling wrap, wraps it around his parents' heads, and the world becomes silent. From this point on, like a bird breaking out of its shell, Bubby finally spreads his heavy wings and flies towards the outside world. The human son who unintentionally committed patricide and matricide now carries the original sin and is truly born.

II. Mirror Image, Copycat

Children start imitating their parents from birth, and Bubby confined in a dungeon is undoubtedly the best practitioner. Isolated from the world, he practiced imitation for 35 years, making his ability perfect, like a mirror reflecting others' words and deeds.

Whether intentionally or unintentionally, the plot setting of this work coincides with French psychologist Lacan's "mirror stage" theory. The mirror stage, as the name suggests, refers to the meaningless imitation behavior infants exhibit when they lack the ability to distinguish between self and others. Babies recognize their existence through the misconception of becoming others, and this process of recognizing oneself is filled with anticipation and illusions for any human. Bubby uses imitating others' images as a way to improve himself. In the prison, he imitates cats, mom, dad; after leaving the prison, he imitates everyone he meets.

Bubby's transformation process is full of pain. The film gives a side close-up of Bubby escaping from the prison. Behind him, in the night, lights shine brightly, symbolizing countless lives. A peculiar smile appears on his simple lips as he crosses through the garbage field created by urban twisted machines, his lonely and small figure trembling under the huge lamp. He encounters women other than his mother while listening to hymns for the first time. He is driven out of stores, confused by the daylight world, shocked by the destructive power of a chainsaw wielded by a coarse man, infected by children's innocence but thrown out of the store by clerks, scolded and abused, repeating the thugs' foul words to police and getting beaten down, and unexpectedly beating up supermarket clerks. Meanwhile, the unchanging shabby room and the mirror that once confined him in a corner gradually break.

In the movie, Bubby repeatedly experiences the absurd misunderstandings caused by the "mirror stage" imitation behavior. He imitates his mother wrapping tape around the cat, killing it, then wrapping it around his parents, leading to their deaths. The world gives him insults and violence, and he returns them with insults and violence. He is both an unconditional receiver and an unreasonable avenger. He copies humanity's absurdity and returns it to humanity. He keeps the dead cat with him until it emits a corpse smell. The term "copycat" is also the alias for imitators in criminology, and the repeatedly appearing unfortunate kitten might just be Bubby's mirror reflection. On the surface, he seems to be mindlessly repeating others' words and actions, but after experiencing the joys and sorrows of life, he gradually begins to selectively use these "tools" given by the world. Through these experiences, he realizes that he does not need to live through imitation. Because through recognizing "differences," he initially forms a concept of self. When a child can clearly understand their image in the mirror and the difference from others, the mirror stage comes to an end.

III. Redemption through Religion, Music, and Women

Mother and father, two beautiful words, represent love and understanding in family social attributes, with the father symbolizing rationality and strength. Together, they shape a complete social personality, transforming a tender and ignorant newborn from a biological entity into a social one. This process should be perfect and sound, but the film strips away the existence of parents and uses religion, music, and women to enlighten Bubby's wisdom.

Jesus' cross becomes the sword of Damocles hanging over Bubby's head, and the Roman collar of the clergyman becomes the deception tool of his father. In Bubby's tragic growth journey, religion plays a crucial role. He always repeats his mother's words: "God sees everything! If He tells me you moved, I'll smash your brains out!" without realizing how many absurd things were done to him in the name of religion. Thus, the most intense revelation in the film comes from the mouth of a scientist:

"...Believing that God does not exist is the responsibility of all humans, only then will we have a future, because only then will we take responsibility for our own actions, and that's what you need to do! Bubby: Believe that God does not exist, take responsibility for your own actions!"

Such words inevitably cause discomfort among religious people and polarize the film's evaluations. Coincidentally, Nicholas Hope, the lead actor of Bubby, is a Catholic. Despite Rolf de Heer's recognition of his talent, when talking about the film's attitude towards religion, he still finds it hard to accept. Viewers criticize the film for exposing female nudity, discriminating against women, and even animal protection organizations join in, protesting the death of kittens during filming.

Here, perhaps Rolf de Heer does not intend to bring religion down from its pedestal, but he harshly rebukes some people who commit sins in the name of religion. There is a thought-provoking dialogue between Bubby and his bandmate in the film: crowds of the same faith killing each other; people believing suicide leads to heaven; non-violent believers killed by violence, killings and being killed are meaningless. Through this dialogue, the director expresses his view again: Do not commit sins in the name of God; humans should bear their own responsibilities.

If religion plays a negative role in Bubby's growth, music is undoubtedly positive and full of vitality. Bubby's world progresses with music; he follows hymns to meet women, listens to rock music to make friends, is summoned by the organ into the church, and harmonizes with guitar sounds on the stage. The guiding role of music towards goodness is shown in two highly artistic scenes in the film: one is the evil-doing Bubby robbing a supermarket passing by a warehouse, seeing an angel-like girl gracefully playing the violin. After the tune ends, the lingering sound fades, and she gently lifts her hand, seemingly saving the thug Bubby; the second scene is Bubby in prison, longing to escape when hearing the loud and free bagpipe sound, but being dragged deeper into confinement. In these two scenes, music becomes a symbol of beauty and truth, noble, pure, and divine. Bubby, who is connected to music, makes people chuckle while also provoking deep thoughts. When he is in dire straits, it is the rock band that takes him in and composes songs for him. After enduring trials, Bubby finds them again following the music. Encouraged by his friends, Bubby stands on the stage, grabs the microphone, and starts roaring at the world along with the singing. He repeats curses, jokes, and obscene words, yet the audience is moved by his sincerity. On stage, Bubby has a spiritual influence akin to "the world laughs at my madness, I laugh at their blindness." His irritability may be ridicule or bitter medicine, truthfully reflecting the illness and madness of modern society.

Obsessed with his mother, killing his father, Bubby's actions have long been explained by the Oedipus complex. In the film, his mother is not the last stop in his pursuit of women. Due to lacking a sense of shame, every time Bubby encounters a woman as plump as his mother, he approaches her with flirtatious words and touches. This instinct-driven behavior is unmasked, interestingly, different women react differently. He has encountered choir girls, rich mistresses, been saved by a violinist girl, seduced by a rocker... Too many women appear in his life. Women either clean his face, shave him, bathe him, give him food, reveal themselves, or hit him with fists and feet. They embody the greatness of motherhood, the intertwining of sexual desire and physical desire, and also the resistance to sexual harassment. Women make men grow up, turning Bubby from a beast driven by instincts into a man with restraint, all because of the appearance of his destined lover, interestingly named Angel (ANGEL).

The film's most brilliant part lies in its ending. Bubby stands on the stage, singing the song that flirts with his mother. As the song reaches its climax, he combines with the inflatable doll, symbolizing the pure mother, the real "angel." The rock music fades away, hymns resound, and religion, music, and women finally become one. From this point on, Bubby, the child burdened with humanity's original sin, is finally saved by the woman "Angel."

Lonely Wanderer, the Interwoven Lives of Director and Actor

It is difficult to find two similar films by Rolf de Heer. He seems capable of handling any genre, and his filmmaking style is elusive. In "Perfect Revenge," he leaves us with the wife's merciless revenge on her husband; in "Tracker," he tells the story of intense racial struggles; in "Ten Canoes," he explores family relationships through an indigenous unit; in "Dr. Plonk," he pays tribute to science fiction silent films. From his films, it seems he has criticisms about religion, race, and marriage. Looking closer, one can perceive his language in making films and telling stories. During interviews about "Tracker," he described wanting to create an isolated mini-society and mini-environment, and most of his films indeed do this. In Bubby's room, in the scientist's factory, on the stage of a small tavern, he narrows the space limited by the audience's vision. Through "narrowing," he amplifies certain things, which is his characteristic. Born in 1951 in the Netherlands, he spent his childhood in Indonesia before moving to Australia at the age of eight. His wandering life did not give him the lifestyle concept of an Australian white person. Unbound by land, religion, and ethnicity, his wandering life gave him a transcendent reviewing perspective. When unable to quickly integrate into an environment, this transcendent perspective creates a lonely world for himself.

Rolf de Heer's transcendent perspective also discovered a great performing genius, Nicholas Hope. The director found his remarkable acting ability in a 19-minute autobiographical short drama "[Confessor Caressor]." He played a rapist-murderer confessing in front of the camera. While this short film contains elements of paying homage to pseudo-documentaries like "Man Bites Dog," it fully reveals his outstanding talent and ability to grasp the atmosphere of performances. Nicholas Hope's performance in "[Bubby]" stands out particularly. Hope excels at capturing the subtle psychological activities and facial details of characters. When Bubby's intelligence was not yet developed, his eyes were vacant and monotonous, and his emotional expression was rigid and weak. As Bubby ventures out into the world, Hope progressively reveals these emotions. With increasing experience and richer emotions, Bubby's expressions, eyes, and overall demeanor become more abundant and complete. It can be said that Nicholas not only gives life to the character but also gives the character a life cycle. Since this film that launched his acting career, Nicholas Hope has been active in Australian and American television and film industries. To prove his correctness, the director included Nicholas Hope's interview and the short films he previously acted in as DVD extras. Some details of the film's production are also worth noting.

The film's budget was only $750,000, yet it employed a record-breaking 32 cinematographers. Surprisingly, Rolf could combine the footage shot by these different cinematographers, selecting, cutting, and ultimately producing a complete, organic, nearly flawless whole. Many of the materials give the impression of being watched from a human eye perspective. Additionally, he utilized advanced sound recording technology, faithfully recording the low-frequency buzzing sound at the beginning of the film and the tremors in Bubby's throat when he spoke, giving viewers a peculiar sense of realism while experiencing these inexplicable noises. His beloved music not only reflects truthfully in the film but also enhances the performances and adds color to the film. More impressively, the script for this movie was completed almost ten years ago. After a decade of refinement, "[Bubby]" finally made a stunning debut.

The Rolling Stones sang the famous line "I can't get no satisfaction" in 1965. Nowadays, people living in urban jungles and amidst steel monsters seem increasingly inclined towards endless material desires, just as the song describes. Once upon a time, humans were like Bubby on stage, madly seeking, or like Angel's parents, endlessly cursing. People persecute dissenters for religion and ideologies, killing others and themselves in the process.

Nowadays, why can't people still find satisfaction?

Rolf de Heer provided us with the answer back in 1993—the ending of the film: in a green corner amidst the urban jungle, Angel raises children and teaches them, while Bubby chases and plays with children. Facing the sea of humanity, spring warms and flowers bloom.
