Pinterest traffic increased 10 times in half a year, but it's not suitable for a business model yet.

by nif2dfoe on 2012-02-19 08:32:36

Introduction: A foreign media outlet recently wrote an article pointing out that the number of users for the American image sharing social networking site Pinterest broke the 10 million mark in January this year, with traffic increasing tenfold within half a year. However, this currently popular start-up is unable to convert its traffic into revenue, which will become one of Pinterest's biggest challenges going forward.

The following is the full text of the article:

Business Model

If you've never heard of Pinterest before, you might soon. In the last six months, Pinterest's traffic has increased tenfold. In January this year, Pinterest's visits were almost a third of Twitter's. But this start-up also faces a challenge - it doesn't know how to profit from its traffic.

Pinterest board member and venture capitalist at Bessemer Venture Partners, Jeremy Levine said: "Pinterest's commercialization strategy isn't even close to being baked, it's not even near the baking counter. We have a hundred ideas, but none have been implemented so far."

For any internet start-up, Pinterest's situation is perfectly normal; some might even say it has become a rule. Previously, Facebook and Twitter both grew rapidly without a business model initially, and given this, Ben Silbermann, co-founder of Pinterest, suggested he would follow the same growth path.

This 29-year-old former Google engineer said: "I think if we create a service that allows many people to easily create goods, then this service will be quite valuable." Silbermann co-founded Cold Brew Labs Inc in 2008, and launched Pinterest, the company's only product, the following year.

To date, the company has raised $37.5 million in funding from Silicon Valley angel investors such as Yelp CEO Jeremy Stoppelman and well-known investment firms like Bessemer and Andreessen Horowitz. As it is not a public company, Pinterest does not disclose financial data, but its revenue is indeed low, and it has not yet turned a profit. According to informed sources, Pinterest's current valuation is around $200 million.

Social websites are generally free for users to use, and monetization has always been a challenge. So far, analysts' commercialization plans offered to Pinterest revolve around targeted advertising sales and user personal data development, but these plans are not mature and may alienate users - this is actually also the problem faced by social networking giants like Facebook.

User Surge

However, users are flocking to Pinterest. According to the latest data released by market research firm comScore, Pinterest had over 11 million unique users in January this year, more than double the 4.9 million in November last year. At the same time, visitors spent nearly 100 minutes on Pinterest, while they only spent 19 minutes on professional social network LinkedIn.

comScore analyst Andrew Lipsman said: "Before Pinterest, I have never seen an independent website's user base grow so quickly to break 10 million." comScore stated that 68% of Pinterest users are women, many of whom live in the Midwest. Users need an invitation to join Pinterest.

Craft trading website Etsy.com has 50,600 fans on Pinterest, and the site is currently using Pinterest's price display feature. This means that when Pinterest users "retweet" information about an Etsy chair on their information platform, the image of the chair automatically includes its name and a banner displaying its price.

Creating Value

Moreover, Pinterest's service has indeed created value for some small businesses. Amy Squires, co-founder of wedding gift retail website The Wedding Chicks LLC, said: "Our traffic can be converted into sales." The company's revenue in 2011 was $540,000, significantly higher than $340,000 in 2010. Squires said that Pinterest contributes twice as much traffic to The Wedding Chicks website each month compared to Facebook and Twitter.

According to Neil Blumenthal, co-CEO of eyewear retail website Warby Parker, direct visits to the company's website from Pinterest have quadrupled in the past four months. He said, "Pinterest allows us to promote our products in a more organized and solid way."

Like other user-generated content sites such as YouTube owned by Google, Pinterest also has policies in place to handle copyright images and other content releases. According to these policies, content owners can report copyright infringement to Pinterest, and if true, Pinterest will immediately delete the content. Pinterest stated that "copyright issues have been minimal so far."

Criticism

In recent days, Pinterest has been criticized by some netizens for not disclosing its practices in affiliate marketing. Affiliate marketing is a common online advertising format that allows merchants to embed links to their online stores on related websites, rewarding them with a sales commission. Companies like Amazon also adopt this practice.

However, according to Pinterest and its venture capitalists, affiliate marketing is not currently a significant part of Pinterest's business model. Another concern some people have is that Pinterest's terms of use are broad, giving it the right to "sell" and "modify" member content, which could potentially lead to privacy issues in the future.

Nevertheless, this hasn't stopped New York gift retail website UncommonGoods from using Pinterest's services. According to company founder David Bolotsky, in October last year, UncommonGoods noticed that traffic to its website was coming from Pinterest, so it started using Pinterest from then on.

Bolotsky said that in the past few weeks, the traffic UncommonGoods website receives from Pinterest has begun to approach or even surpass the traffic from Facebook. He said: "The overlap between Pinterest users and our users is perfect." Near the icons linking to the company's Twitter and Facebook pages, UncommonGoods recently added a new icon that reads: "Please follow me on Pinterest."
