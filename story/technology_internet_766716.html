Businessweek: IBM Should Reposition Itself for the Cloud Era

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 20:39:42

IBM Needs to Reposition Itself for the Cloud Computing Era

Introduction: The latest print edition of BusinessWeek features an article titled "IBM Needs to Reposition Itself for the Cloud Computing Era." While IBM remains at the forefront of technological innovation in terms of R&D, it struggles to convert these inventions into commercially viable products, particularly in the fields of cloud computing and mobile technology. To fully adapt to modern demands, the company should abandon its previous profit targets and stock repurchase programs, reinvesting funds into product development to stay current with the times.

The following is a full translation of the article:

Market Transformation

Consider this: The average tenure of IBM's executive team dates back to 1985, a time when the internet was still an internal project of the U.S. government and Steve Jobs had just begun making waves with Apple. Ginni Rometty, who became CEO in 2012, joined IBM in 1981, just two years after graduating from Northwestern University’s School of Engineering.

This management team undoubtedly has extensive experience. However, the question is whether this tech giant has the right strategy for the challenges they face in 2013? IBM found itself heading in the wrong direction amidst significant technological transitions. In the era of cloud computing, enterprise customers can rent server storage and processing power at costs far lower than hiring IBM consultants or purchasing hardware.

Ginni Rometty, who took over as IBM's CEO in January 2012, must implement strategic changes that position IBM as a leader in the rapidly growing markets of cloud computing, data analysis, and mobile devices. She must lead IBM out of stagnation—its core business revenue has been stagnant for five years—and achieve organic growth, moving away from financial engineering and asset sales that characterized its past strategies.

IBM clearly has the capacity to reinvent itself: In the early 1990s, when the company was losing billions of dollars and underwent massive layoffs, it decisively shifted its focus from mainframe businesses to servers, software, and technical services, regaining prosperity under the leadership of CEOs Lou Gerstner and Sam Palmisano.

Tied Hands

However, Rometty is constrained by a promise made by her predecessor: Sam Palmisano committed in 2010 to investors and Wall Street analysts that IBM’s earnings per share would increase by 74%, rising from $11.52 at the time to $20 by 2015. Currently, IBM is steadily progressing toward this goal through multi-billion-dollar stock repurchases, cost-cutting, business divestitures, and accounting adjustments—Bloomberg estimates that IBM's earnings per share will reach $16.72 in 2013.

As Credit Suisse analyst Kulbinder Garcha puts it: IBM does everything except actively invest in new businesses. He recently downgraded IBM's stock rating to “Sell.” IBM shares have risen by about 1.5% this year, compared to an 18% increase in the S&P 500 index during the same period. “Their model of the last eight years isn’t going to work going forward,” said Garcha.

Despite being the inventor of cloud computing, IBM was ranked last among the top 15 cloud service providers in a report by Gartner on August 19th. Furthermore, Rometty only began investing in mobile application development quite recently.

Profit Growth Challenges

IBM may no longer be able to effortlessly sustain profit growth: although their last quarter's performance exceeded expectations, this was achieved only after excluding $1 billion in costs related to layoffs. According to ISI Group analyst Brian Marshall, “IBM’s model of driving profit growth through financial optimization has run its course.”

Cutting Costs

To cut costs, IBM has carried out large-scale layoffs—this year’s layoff-related expenses amounted to $1 billion, compared to $803 million last year and $440 million the year before. Rometty also forced American hardware employees to take unpaid leave at one-third of their salaries. The company has also exited some low-margin industries. On September 10th, IBM sold its $1.3 billion customer care outsourcing business to IT services company Synnex for $505 million.

At IBM’s annual investor meeting in February, Rometty defended the layoffs and asset sales, emphasizing the importance of profitable growth. "If we didn't do this, we would definitely be much larger and grow faster, but our profit margins might decrease," she said.

Rometty declined interviews but told investors last year that she had met with the CEOs of 500 clients to understand their needs from IBM—a typical approach of hers since joining IBM as a systems engineer in Detroit and quickly ascending to various managerial roles.

In public appearances, she often envisions the future of technology over the next 20 years—when the world will be run by computers capable of thinking like humans. She also frequently promotes cloud computing and mobile computing. However, once in charge, she hasn't invested sufficiently to realize this vision. Since taking the helm, IBM's R&D expenses have not grown. Nonetheless, IBM spokesperson Michael Fay stated that the company continues to invest in new growth areas.

Lagging Behind

IBM frequently tops the U.S. patent list with billions of dollars in R&D spending, yet converting these inventions into commercially valuable businesses remains a significant challenge. "The market drivers are social, mobile, data analytics, and cloud computing, but they aren't major players in these areas, despite what they claim in promotional materials," said Tigress Financial Partners analyst Ivan Feinseth.

Since 2007, IBM has spent $6 billion acquiring companies in the cloud computing sector, most notably SoftLayer. The company also claims its cloud computing revenue will reach $7 billion by 2015. However, most of this revenue comes from shifting existing IBM businesses to the cloud.

In cloud computing, IBM remains overshadowed by market leader Amazon. This year, Amazon beat IBM to secure a $600 million contract with the U.S. Central Intelligence Agency (CIA). "We used to collaborate and compete with IBM, but today, I no longer see them in our deals," said Acquia CEO Tom Erickson.

IBM’s best hope may lie in its data analytics business, for which it has hired hundreds of scientists and invested in commercial research projects such as Watson, a high-performance computer that defeated human contestants in the American quiz show Jeopardy! IBM has raised its data analytics revenue target for 2015 from $16 billion to $20 billion. During this period, the company will focus on developing areas such as medical diagnostics and financial market analysis.

Credit Suisse’s Garcha believes that if IBM wants to make comprehensive adjustments according to the needs of the times, it must abandon Sam Palmisano’s short-term profit goals, forget about stock repurchase programs, and redirect funds into product development. Specifically, in the software field, "at least some more modern-thinking people need to be brought in." (Translated by Siyuan)
