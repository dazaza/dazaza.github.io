Dianping: A Bone the Giants Can't Chew, Living in the Zone of Discomfort

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 17:36:03

Zhang Tao, CEO of Dianping.com

Written by Yuan Yin, edited by Yang Jing, photographed by Xiao Nan

Zhang Tao, CEO of Dianping.com, has once again postponed the company's IPO timeline.

In 2011, when Dianping secured a $100 million Series C funding round from Sequoia Capital and others, its valuation reached $1 billion. Following this, it should have chosen an appropriate time to go public within the next one to two years, but it didn't. Instead, in 2012, it raised another $60 million in Series D funding.

Given Dianping’s scale and development momentum, going public would not be difficult. However, Zhang Tao feels that an IPO at this stage would be too costly. He breaks down the greater internet (PC + mobile) into four elements: people, information, goods, and services. Each connection point represents an opportunity for a giant to emerge. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, and Alibaba connects people with goods. The space between people and services is where the fourth giant could potentially reside. Since 2003, Dianping has been diligently accumulating resources to fill this gap, finally capitalizing on the explosion of mobile internet. Mobile phones have bridged the gap between "location" and "service." According to Zhang Tao's logic, no company seems more suited than Dianping to take this fourth spot.

However, the outside world doesn’t see it this way. In recent months, with frequent acquisition moves by BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent), Dianping has become a hypothetical acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition candidate; under any of the BAT brands, it could accelerate their path to greatness. On the other hand, Dianping's size is far smaller compared to BAT, and it’s premature to declare it as a future giant. Moreover, whether it’s Tencent WeChat’s public platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after acquiring Gaode and Meituan, all are targeting Dianping’s territory - local life services primarily focused on food, drink, entertainment, and leisure.

Before proving the following two points, Dianping going public would equate to selling itself at a discount. First, Zhang Tao needs time to demonstrate that what Dianping does now cannot be achieved by BAT. Second, Dianping is about to engage in the ultimate battle of group-buying. If it secures the top position, its valuation could increase several folds.

2013 is Dianping's "decisive year." Regarding speculations and rumors, Zhang Tao remains indifferent. He is clear: "The internet is indeed entering an era of great convergence, but Dianping will not be acquired."

Zhang Tao categorizes companies that might be acquired into three types. The first type has platform attributes but is better suited to develop within the ecosystem of a giant, such as maps and email services. These products can easily attract a large number of users but struggle to find an independent business model. Some, like browsers, can make money, but the revenue from navigation services is much less than their strategic value to large companies. “Giants are willing to spend money maintaining email products because they help enhance brand and user stickiness, but I've never heard of a standalone email company succeeding.” The second type involves industry consolidation, such as in the video industry, using acquisitions to expand market share. The third type is when a company faces financial difficulties and sells itself for survival.

Now, Meituan has already joined forces with Alibaba. Theoretically, Dianping should quickly seek a "backer" to avoid being overwhelmed by competitors gaining capital and rapidly expanding. However, Zhang Tao says, "The giants' assistance to us is limited. We do need users, but execution is more important. Mergers and acquisitions would reduce our execution capabilities and may lead to marginalization by larger companies." Furthermore, he wants Dianping to maintain the most precise "distance" from the giants. Getting too close to any one of them would amount to "taking sides," making Dianping an enemy to the others.

Thus, logically, the acquisition of Dianping has reached a dead end. Zhang Tao counters, "Why should we take their money? We're not short of money; what we need are resources. If a giant is willing to invest a small stake and provide us with many resources, I would consider it. But would they? Would a giant give you resources if they only hold a small stake?"

Weapon One: Dirty and Hard Work

In the mobile internet space, opportunities for pure internet product companies are becoming fewer. New opportunities come from those combining with traditional industries. Zhang Tao believes that life services mainly revolve around food, drink, entertainment, and leisure. Other niche markets, such as ride-hailing, can form decent business models, but there is little chance for any of them to grow into giants.

For food, drink, entertainment, and leisure, user demand is simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is the core of life services, which is precisely where Dianping started. Whether in content, member accumulation, or brand, newcomers find it hard to surpass Dianping.

The most representative product form of discounts is group-buying. Group-buying has now entered the championship battle, and Dianping plans to face off against Meituan in 2013. This is a winner-takes-all market. As long as there are two strong players, the industry's gross profit margin will be lowered, making stable profitability impossible.

A foreseeable future includes reservations. Reservations can create a true online-to-offline closed loop, but they must be built on Dianping having an absolute advantage in both information and discounts. Otherwise, creating a closed loop would be difficult. In 2012, Dianping began experimenting with reservations. Zhang Tao said, "Both user and merchant feedback has been good so far, but if reservations were done separately, it would be hard to solve the chicken-or-egg problem."

Of course, life services present some even "heavier" opportunities. For example, food delivery is a rigid user demand, and merchants can increase sales. In the past couple of years, food delivery companies like "To Home Gourmet" have thrived. However, the core of food delivery is distribution—getting meals delivered within a very short time while solving issues like soup spilling. The logistics requirements are higher than those of fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang Tao said, "If food delivery is executed well, there is still an opportunity, but it is harder dirty and hard work. What Dianping is doing now is already dirty and hard enough."

If judged by age, Dianping is already an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has painstakingly accumulated merchant data and user reviews, which Zhang Tao calls "dirty and hard work." This primitive growth model once limited its speed, but the pain of the past has become Dianping's core barrier today. It can be said that currently, except for Meituan, Dianping has no real rivals in the life services field.

However, he still needs to beware of BAT's "claws." Baidu's intervention is the most direct. Baidu Maps already has over 100 million users, and users can use the map to search for nearby restaurants and other life services. Tencent, believed to have secured a ticket to mobile internet, launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest goods trading platform, and expanding goods from physical items to services is logical. Besides, it has also included Meituan and Gaode into its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there are no signs of explosive growth in terms of reality and data," Zhang Tao said. Brand positioning is crucial; the key is which product users turn to first when they want to eat, drink, play, or relax.

Moreover, in the life services sector, which has immense imaginative potential, competition among giants will inevitably cross boundaries, and no one can completely monopolize the field. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained on Weibo and WeChat. Zhang Tao feels that in this case, Dianping is safer. "In细分fields like car rental and business travel, the huge user base directed by the giants could easily overwhelm existing companies."

As for Alibaba, which has invested in Gaode, invading Dianping's territory won't be easy either. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, which can be categorized regardless of how peculiar they are. A diner's experience depends on various factors such as the environment, waitstaff, and chef on the day. Managing merchants cannot rely solely on systems and tools like Alibaba's; it requires building a 'ground force' managed by humans.

"The three giants all want to take this path, and there is room for imagination, but this is not a highway; it's a rugged mountain road," Zhang Tao believes. First, providing life services necessitates deploying ground teams across various levels, something BAT lacks the genes and capability to manage offline teams. Second, they lack the willingness; life services require focus and dedication, not just sending a small team to complete tasks, and making money is slow. "A company finds it hard to excel in two important things simultaneously, let alone they must consolidate their advantages in their original fields."

Weapon Two: Agile Maneuverability

When Dianping received $100 million in financing in 2011, Zhang Tao told the public that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, times change fast, and before Dianping went public, his analogies had fallen from grace.

He wasn't overly worried about it. "They didn't become big companies because they lacked offline teams, and now they all want to be America's Dianping."

Today's path is one Zhang Tao has painstakingly explored step by step. When he felt that selling keyword ads and coupons was too outdated, the emergence of group-buying lit up his eyes. Initially, like the Americans, he understood group-buying as a marketing platform—one deal per day. But as orders increased, he realized that group-buying had the characteristics of a trading platform. Thus, he envisioned Dianping's future as a life services trading platform. The two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were used precisely to transition Dianping from an information platform to a trading platform.

Without this transformation, Zhang Tao might not have had the confidence to compete for the fourth seat among giants today. But this transformation sounds simple yet is extremely painful in practice, akin to Dianping's second entrepreneurship.

First, the gross margin of a trading platform is lower compared to selling ads alone, requiring stricter cost control and efficiency. Second, a trading platform demands higher cross-departmental communication skills.

Previously, to maintain Dianping's impartiality and independence as an information platform, the content team and sales team were completely separated. Zhang Tao even deliberately established a "firewall" between departments—no matter how important the client, sales couldn't alter information on the platform, which once led to mutual animosity between the content and sales teams. Now, as a trading platform, editors and sales teams need deep communication to create good marketing plans. Besides planning, editorial cooperation is necessary. He said, "Maintaining information quality is still important, but there's no need to reinforce hostility. We need to dismantle the firewall."

Though editors can logically accept this, individuals have behavioral and value inertia, and when aggregated across the entire company, the inertia becomes even greater. To address this, Zhang Tao specially convened several meetings to encourage more communication between departments. Several months later, an editor manager casually mentioned to a sales manager, "I used to think you were the enemy, but now I realize you're a colleague."

The sales department also needed transformation. To promote conversion, Zhang Tao cleverly gave the offline team a dual identity. First, for merchants, salespeople should transform into marketing consultants—not just selling products but helping merchants devise marketing strategies. Second, for the company, marketing consultants act as procurement agents. Dianping is like a supermarket; not only must the product quality be good, but the variety must be rich. Marketing consultants should aim to help users procure group-buying products. Of course, dual identities will inevitably generate a series of complex problems. For frontline personnel, this is quite headache-inducing—they must balance the scales themselves.

Substantially, Dianping's "dual identity" is very similar to Alibaba's division into 25 business units. Decentralizing decision-making gives the company faster response times but increases management difficulty. Similarly, compared to frontline employees, Dianping's management team suffered more during the transformation.

Every year, Zhang Tao decomposes Dianping's company goals into various departments, then executives further break them down layer by layer, but this decomposition isn't simple arithmetic. Li Jing, co-founder of Dianping responsible for the sales team, sometimes finds unresolved merchant demands due to sales issues affecting performance. Therefore, Li Jing created a new position, setting up communication specialists who gather information daily from marketing consultants, submit it to other departments, and track feedback. He often tells his team, "Being adept at embracing change doesn't mean liking change. No one likes change; it makes everyone uncomfortable when encountered. All employees must go through uncomfortable zones, including me."

Numbers illustrate the "weight" of Dianping's execution team. Out of over 3,000 employees, more than 2,000 work offline. The ground force directly communicates with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group-buying, and bundled marketing solutions.

For this core team resisting BAT's influence, Dianping has a unique management and evaluation system.

They aren't called "sales"; from the first day, their business cards read "Marketing Planning Consultant." Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the era of watching news and chatting on QQ, lagging behind Taobao merchants by at least three to five years in their understanding of the internet. Marketing planning consultants need to inform merchants about online user demands and how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing said, "Even for a high-end restaurant, marketing consultants wouldn't recommend a groupon for abalone and shark fin; perhaps they'd suggest other dishes, as online users differ from offline users."

An outstanding marketing consultant must be a jack-of-all-trades. They need to explain clearly to restaurant owners and hair salon owners the essential differences between discounts and group-buying; which kind of discount aligns better with their own positioning. If facing a hot pot restaurant owner, the marketing consultant should know whether it's the off-season or peak season, which products have rising costs during certain seasons, and which have decreasing costs, proposing a group-buying menu that pleases both merchants and users.

This places high demands on marketing consultants, requiring them not only to be internet marketing experts but also industry experts. Li Jing said, "I must admit, it's impossible for every advisor to be a double expert, so we set star ratings for advisors." Similar to hotels, marketing advisors range from one-star to five-star, with the best ones called Super Stars.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline workers aren't tech-savvy graduates from prestigious universities, making it challenging for them to understand internet products. Dianping has established a knowledge base for marketing advisors, containing knowledge points and related cases accessible to both new and old employees. If encountering new problems, employees can ask questions within the knowledge base, and thousands of consultants nationwide will assist in answering.

Most of the knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees based on their practical experiences. Dianping has established a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, with employees who help others the most each quarter receiving rewards. Every quarter, Dianping conducts skill assessments for employees, with results directly tied to promotions.

Compared to internet knowledge, industry knowledge is harder to obtain. Some experiences are summarized by traditional merchants after years of working in the industry. A Super Star marketing advisor told Li Jing that hair salons should avoid offering shampoo-cut-blow-dry group-buying packages because men lack motivation to buy them, while women mostly blow dry their hair without needing the whole package. Li Jing was astonished, "We men would never think of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing advisors are not only divided by city but also by industry sectors such as catering, hairdressing, and leisure. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is further subdivided into hot pot, Western cuisine, and buffets, with each marketing advisor responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing advisors can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, making merchants less resistant when selling products," he said.

Each marketing group holds its own case-sharing sessions: what challenges a particular type of merchant encounters, what marketing needs they have, what solutions the company provides, and the effectiveness. Outstanding cases are reported upwards, and Dianping officially shares one case each week.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition called "Entrepreneur Winner." Within the first three months of employment, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products but listening to merchants share their experiences. If they are responsible for hot pot restaurants, after three months, they must submit a business plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hot Pot Restaurant Owner." Like graduation, employees defend their presentations on-site, with industry supervisors evaluating and rewarding the winners.

From a strategic perspective, whether it's Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, their objectives are quite similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O, when anyone in the industry mentions them, sound almost identical. "The era where storytelling and concept creation could lead to success is gone. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model lies in management and execution, not strategy," Zhang Tao said.
