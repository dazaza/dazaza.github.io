What should expectant mothers in early pregnancy pay attention to?

by nxcon999 on 2012-02-24 10:30:42

Early pregnancy: during the first month after conception, pregnant women will not feel the beginning of new life. However, there are some important signs that can remind women of childbearing age that they may be pregnant.

Many expectant mothers experience dizziness and fatigue shortly after becoming pregnant. In medical terms, this is known as early pregnancy reaction. Early pregnancy usually occurs around 6 weeks after a woman's menstruation stops, at which time blood HCG levels significantly increase. Blood HCG reduces gastric acid secretion and weakens gastrointestinal peristalsis, leading to certain symptoms. The main symptoms of early pregnancy include dizziness, fatigue, excessive sleepiness, drooling, loss of appetite, craving for sour foods, aversion to greasy foods, nausea, and vomiting.

The severity of early pregnancy reactions may be related to the level of a special hormone produced in the body called human chorionic gonadotropin (HCG), as well as the mental state of the pregnant woman. Some pregnant women do not feel anything unusual in the early stages and continue with their normal routine. Most pregnant women only experience mild reactions. Getting more rest, adjusting dietary habits, eating small meals frequently, and maintaining emotional stability generally cause these symptoms to subside on their own by around the 12th week of pregnancy without affecting physical health. A very small number of pregnant women experience frequent vomiting, even to the point of expelling foam-like mucus, bile, or blood, and cannot drink even a drop of water. This can lead to dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, and acidosis within the body, necessitating medical treatment at a regular hospital.

Early pregnancy reactions are not diseases but rather common physiological changes that occur during the early stages of pregnancy, representing a necessary process of pregnancy. Therefore, one should not be overly anxious but try to eat as much as possible, as things will improve after this stage.

Pregnant women should ensure adequate rest and sleep, while husbands should provide necessary comfort and support to alleviate any concerns. After experiencing early pregnancy reactions, one should attempt to eat something to suppress nausea and avoid foods and smells that make them uncomfortable. Meals should be taken in small amounts throughout the day. If adjustments such as these do not reduce the frequency of vomiting, especially if urine ketone bodies are positive, then hospitalization may be required. In some cases, it might be necessary to terminate the pregnancy.

In general, early pregnancy reactions have no significant impact on either the mother or fetus and will gradually subside after a period of time. However, if the pregnancy reaction becomes severe and persists for a long duration, it can negatively affect the growth and development of both the mother and fetus. Severe loss of appetite, nausea, and vomiting prevent the pregnant woman from obtaining sufficient nutrition. As the fetus grows, its nutritional needs increase, and if the mother experiences dehydration and acidosis, along with ion imbalances, it can directly threaten the lives of both the mother and fetus.
