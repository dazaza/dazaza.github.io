Who are Android's consumers, anyway?

by geekzhang on 2013-05-16 17:19:14

Ever since I started getting into Android last year, I have been curious about who exactly the consumer of Android is. Despite Android's growing presence in the market, this question has only become more confusing for me. Interestingly, Google's problem isn't a lack of consumers for Android; rather, it’s that there are too many consumers, and this is what concerns me.

Google has consistently provided device manufacturers with a free, open-source, and excellent operating system. Manufacturers can customize the OS according to their needs, but this has turned out to be a disaster for software. The reason is simple: manufacturers simply don’t understand software. Strange customizations, poor hardware and driver choices have led to countless issues with devices. These problems mean that manufacturers spend months catching up every time Google upgrades Android. Outdated software is the first issue with Android.

Besides, Google must also deal with carriers' desire to control devices. Carriers have seen the impact of the iPhone on ATT's network and are therefore concerned about companies like Apple that crave data usage damaging their networks. However, Google ultimately has no choice but to compromise: they cannot let Apple take over the market. As carriers, they inevitably need to leave their mark on what they sell. They fill Android phones with their own media apps, partner applications, and all sorts of useless and ugly things.

Carriers have significant say over the timing of OS updates. Since all OS updates must rely on Over-The-Air (OTA) technology, Google has to depend on carriers to support Android. To maintain their own pace, Google must treat carriers politely. Slow updates are detrimental to users, which is the second concern.

Google also needs to convince developers to create applications for them. Due to the instability of the development platform, device fragmentation, and the disadvantage in user numbers compared to Apple, Google still lacks sufficient appeal to developers. Android merely offers developers higher permissions, but the smoothness, aesthetics, and stability of iPhone applications are what users care about most. Google still needs to work hard to provide better tools for developers and showcase world-class examples of Android application development. If you ask developers which platform they would develop for first, I suspect Android wouldn’t be their first choice. This is the third concern.

Finally, end-users are the most important consumers, and this is where I believe Google hasn't fully understood yet. When the G-1 was first launched, Google simply viewed it as an alternative to the iPhone. You heard that right, an alternative. It didn't offer any qualitative improvement; despite some commendable aspects, it mostly fell short of the iPhone. I'm talking about the shortcomings from the perspective of ordinary internet users, not geeks, Silicon Valley types, or business users. Undoubtedly, Google wants more people to buy Android phones, but many of its designs target geeks rather than regular users. For regular users, installing programs is a terrible experience, and the cumbersome process frustrates users. Although Android has improved, it still feels more like a mini-computer than a highly user-friendly smart device. I've used the HTC Hero, Motorola Droid, and Nexus series, but none of them made me want to continue using them. This is the fourth concern.

Perhaps all of this sounds like a death sentence for Android, but that’s not my intention. My concern about Android is that it seems lost. On one hand, it needs to meet the demands of big players while failing to satisfy each one of them. On the other hand, it must build an operating system that provides a great experience and is loved by users. Google must realize that most users don’t need multi-tasking, root permissions, or other unnecessary features.

Apple's success lies in the excellent user experience of the iPhone, and this is what Android needs to learn: focus on improving the user experience so that users will choose to buy your phone and love it. To achieve this, Google must be firmer with device manufacturers and carriers while focusing on UI improvements. I look forward to seeing Android's progress.
