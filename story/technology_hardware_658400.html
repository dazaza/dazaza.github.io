[Keywords] Protein aging refers to the gradual process of functional and organic decline in the body with increasing age. The skin, located on the surface of the body, is the most visible tissue showing the aging of the organism, making it easy to observe and an important target organ for studying aging [1]. Collagen, as a structural protein, is widely found in the skin, tendons, and other connective tissues of animals. Tissues rich in collagen protein easily show some physiological changes related to age, so collagen protein has always been regarded as an important indicator for exploring the mechanism of aging and has received much attention [2,3]. This article reviews the correlation and research progress between collagen protein and skin aging.

1. Physicochemical characteristics of collagen protein

Collagen is a natural protein widely present in the skin, bones, cartilage, teeth, tendons, ligaments, and blood vessels of animals. It is an extremely important structural protein in connective tissues, providing support to organs and protecting the body. Collagen is usually white, transparent, and unbranched fibrils, surrounded by a matrix composed of glycosaminoglycans and other proteins. The collagen protein family includes 19 types of collagen proteins and more than 10 types of collagen-like proteins. The molecular weight of collagen protein is approximately 300 kD. A common feature of its primary structure is that there is one glycine every two amino acids, which can be represented as DDlyyDXDYYnn―, where X and Y positions are proline and hydroxyproline or other amino acid residues. This tripeptide repeat sequence restricts the rotation of the polypeptide chain. Its tertiary structure consists of three left-handed helical polypeptide chains intertwined to form a rope-like right-handed superhelix structure. The triple helix often contains 4DD hydroxyproline, forming hydrogen bonds and oxygen bridges, making the structure relatively stable. There are also globular sequences inserted, making the collagen molecule more elastic. Due to extensive cross-linking within and between collagen molecules, its structure is very stable and has low immunogenicity and sensitivity. Collagen protein is the main molecular compound in the extracellular matrix. With the development of molecular biology, biochemistry, and cell biology, people's understanding of collagen protein has gradually improved. It has now been confirmed that collagen protein is not a general term for proteins but, like immunoglobulin, has diversity and tissue-specific distribution, being a functional protein related to the functions of various tissues and organs [4]. Its tissue specificity is manifested as follows [5]: (1) The skin mainly contains type I and type III collagens; (2) The tendons mainly contain type I collagen; (3) Cartilage mainly contains type II collagen. According to their distribution and functional characteristics in the body, collagen proteins can be classified into three categories: interstitial collagens, basement membrane collagens, and pericellular collagens. Collagen protein is the protein constituting human connective tissue, accounting for more than one-third of the total protein content in the body, and over 5% of the protein in the skin. These structural proteins not only provide a site for nutrient metabolism for surrounding cells but are also related to the morphology, structure, growth metabolism, and movement of surrounding cells. It has been reported that the proportion and distribution of proteins in the skin change under different physiological conditions. The presence of collagen is closely related to the health degree, water retention, and elasticity of the skin. A concentration of 1 mg/ml of collagen is optimal; lower than this, collagen cannot form properly; higher than this, although the adhesion of epidermal cells is good, the proliferation rate of single cells decreases [5].

Collagen has strong biological activity and functions, participating in cell migration, differentiation, and proliferation, giving bones, tendons, cartilage, and skin certain mechanical strength. Due to its weak antigenicity and good biocompatibility, collagen has wide applications in medical and health fields such as burns, trauma, corneal diseases, beauty, orthopedics, hard tissue repair, and wound hemostasis.

> Application of Collagen Protein in Beauty

Collagen protein is the main protein component of skin tissue, synthesized by fibroblasts. As age increases, the synthetic capacity of fibroblasts declines. If there is a lack of collagen protein in the skin, collagen fibers will become cross-linked and solidified, reducing the amount of polysaccharides between cells. The skin will lose softness, elasticity, and luster, leading to aging. At the same time, the dermis' fiber breaks, fat atrophies, sweat glands and sebaceous glands reduce secretion, causing the skin to develop spots and wrinkles, among other signs of aging. In the facial skin, due to long-term exposure to air and ultraviolet radiation, collagen fiber bundles lose their physiological elasticity and straighten, resulting in loose, cracked skin. In foreign countries, extensive research has been conducted on the role of collagen protein in beauty. Studies have shown that even a 0.01% pure solution of collagen protein has excellent anti-radiation properties and can form a good moisture-retaining layer, providing comprehensive hydration needed by the skin. Animal experiments have also shown that applying a gel containing collagen protein to the skin can effectively improve the structure of the epidermis and dermis and promote collagen synthesis within the skin. > Changes in Aging Skin

1. Epidermal cells in aging skin gradually become flattened, the boundary between the epidermis and dermis becomes flat, the epidermal pegs become shallower and less distinct, and the combination between the epidermis and dermis becomes less tight, making the skin susceptible to external damage and blister formation. Keratinocytes (keratohyalin cells) enlarge, with partial incomplete keratinization, blurred contours, reduced vitality, loose intercellular connections, and decreased hydration capacity. The water content of elderly skin is only 50% of that of normal young people, so it often appears rough.

2. Changes in the dermal structure are the main cause of skin aging. The thickness of the aging dermis becomes thinner, and the density increases. Ultrasound or direct measurements both show that the skin thickness decreases linearly after the age of 30. Gman believed that women have thinner skin than men, possibly explaining why women's skin ages earlier than men's. After the age of 30, the number of dermal fibroblasts gradually decreases, the total collagen content decreases by 1% annually, the collagen fibers become thicker and abnormal; simultaneously, the density increases, making them harder to break down by collagenase, thus increasing the stability of collagen. The expression of type III collagen genes in aging skin decreases, and the synthesis of type III collagen reduces, while the expression of type I collagen genes increases. Typically, infant and young adult skin contains 80% type III collagen and 20% type I collagen; however, when the skin ages, the ratio gradually reverses, decreasing stress transmission and shear resistance. Elastic fibers in the papillary layer and reticular layer of the dermis decrease and degenerate, and the arrangement of collagen and elastic fibers becomes disordered, leading to wrinkles, looseness, and sagging.

3. Many metabolic enzymes in aging skin have reduced activity, hyaluronic acid and sulfates decrease, and the clearance of chemical substances weakens. The vascular system relatively decreases, microcirculation weakens, temperature regulation ability decreases, and Langerhans cells decrease, lowering immunity and increasing susceptibility to infectious diseases. Pigment cells in aging skin significantly decrease, making them more vulnerable to sunlight exposure; lipofuscin markedly increases, presenting age spots and other localized pigmentation changes.

4. Sebaceous glands and sweat glands in aging skin atrophy, secretion decreases, sweating response weakens, insufficient emulsifiers on the skin surface lead to weakened hydration capacity of the stratum corneum, causing rough and dry skin.

> Mechanisms of Skin Aging

Skin aging is a long and complex evolutionary process. There are many types of research on the mechanisms of skin aging, including representative theories such as genetic regulation of skin aging, free radical theory of skin aging, metabolic imbalance theory of skin aging, and photo-stimulated aging theory of skin.

1. Gene Regulation in the Process of Skin Aging

The mechanism of gene regulation in the process of skin aging is very complex, involving many regulatory sequences, enhancers and inhibitors of genes, and other factors causing gene changes. The characteristics of genes affect the entire function of cells and regulate the aging process of cells. Skin aging is mainly due to the increased expression of inhibitory genes in chromatin DNA and mitochondrial DNA, suppression of many genes related to cell activity, and oxidative stress-induced damage affecting replication, transcription, and expression. Therefore, gene regulation is the root cause of skin and cell aging. Studies have shown that skin aging is related to the content and nature of dermal collagen proteins. With age, the inhibitors controlling DNA synthesis increase, leading to a gradual reduction in RNA and DNA content, further reducing protein synthesis, resulting in a decrease in collagen quantity and quality. Pieringgg et al. proved that DNA replication is directly related to skin aging. They discovered DNA synthesis inhibitors in cultured skin fibroblasts, which slow down cell replication speed, increase error rates, ultimately causing gene mutations. Simultaneously, with age, the repair capability of cellular DNA variants or defects decreases, leading to cell aging and death.

2. Free Radical Theory of Skin Aging

Harmann (1956) proposed the free radical theory of aging. Free radicals have extremely strong oxidation capabilities, which can cause unsaturated fatty acids in biological membranes to undergo peroxidation, forming lipid peroxides. One of the intermediate products, malondialdehyde (MDA), is a strong cross-linking agent that binds with proteins, nucleic acids, or lipids to form insoluble substances, leading to membrane hardening, reduced permeability, affecting cellular substance exchange, subsequently causing rupture and death. Whether free radicals produced by biological metabolism or those generated by physical radiation and chemical substances, they all damage large molecules in the body. However, the body also has defense mechanisms against free radical damage: one is the repair mechanism for damaged large molecules, such as glutathione peroxidase, which can restore oxidatively denatured large molecules to their original functions; the other is the clearance mechanism, such as vitamin E and catalase (CAT) being scavengers of hydrogen peroxide, especially superoxide dismutase (SOD), which effectively clears superoxide free radicals. With age, the levels of these antioxidant enzymes in the body decrease, weakening the protective function or causing obstacles, thus accumulating free radicals, causing damage to various macromolecules in skin cells, leading to loss of skin elasticity and flexibility, appearance of wrinkles, dryness, scaling, dullness, and excessive deposition of pigments and lipofuscins.

3. Metabolic Imbalance Theory of Skin Aging

This theory suggests that the regularity of aging in living organisms is expressed through cellular metabolic processes. Studies have shown that whether internal or external factors leading to metabolic disorders in the body can promote cellular aging, thereby causing organism aging. Experiments have shown that improving the metabolic functions of animals can delay the onset of aging. Age-related antioxidant research focuses on inhibiting the production of reactive oxygen species in mitochondria. Providing a diet rich in antioxidants through metabolic pathways can effectively prevent mitochondrial oxidation and subsequent cascade reactions. Adding natural nutritional supplements rich in vitamins C and E, antioxidant enzymes, and various trace elements to the feed of elderly rats, after 5 days of feeding, it was found that the content of hydroxyproline in the rat's skin and collagen-rich tail tendon significantly increased, and it could reduce the risks of age-related immune disorders and arteriosclerosis.

4. Photo-Stimulated Aging Effect of Skin

The skin exposed on the body surface is most sensitive to "photo-stimulated aging" caused by ultraviolet (UV) light in sunlight. Studies have shown that during early irradiation, fibroblasts shift from synthesizing collagen fibers to synthesizing elastic fibers; in the middle stage, reticular fibers proliferate, and newly synthesized collagen fibers increase. Meanwhile, UV-induced inflammatory infiltration causes mononuclear macrophages and neutrophils to release proteolytic enzymes, further reducing mature collagen. Additionally, elastin fibers in the dermis absorb UV light, causing elastin denaturation, thickening, clustering, presenting the hyperplastic manifestation of "photo-stimulated aging." In the late stage, it presents "atrophy."

> Role of Collagen Protein in the Mechanism of Skin Aging

During the aging process, collagen protein clearly exhibits a persistent increase in anti-degradation ability. Fluorescent substances resembling old-age pigments in collagen tissues such as human and animal skin and tendons gradually increase with age. At 44°C, the action of collagen protein shows a decrease in the amount of collagen protein that can be extracted by neutral salts, acetic acid, and pepsin from the skin, bones, tendons, muscles of rats, and human skin with age. The thermal stability of collagen protein usually improves with age, which has been proven in the skin of rats, pigs, and humans and the tail tendons of rats and mice.

Studies have shown that with age, the colony-forming ability of epidermal cells decreases correspondingly, and their responsiveness to collagen decreases, preventing the release of cell growth-promoting factors. Furthermore, the basal layer of the epidermis contains interferon-like substances that inhibit cell proliferation, making adults far more sensitive to these substances than infants, indicating that the age-related decrease in proliferative capacity is the result of a combined decrease in responsiveness to collagen and an increase in responsiveness to growth inhibitors. Dermis fibroblasts gradually lose high-affinity receptors or signal transduction abilities, reducing their responsiveness to collagen.

Studies have found that the number of epidermal growth factor receptors (EGFR) is proportional to the proliferative ability of the epidermis and inversely proportional to the degree of differentiation. Aging cells have relatively fewer EGFRs, higher degrees of differentiation, and lower proliferative abilities. During the aging process, fibroblasts increase the expression of laminin and type III collagen genes while decreasing the expression of type I collagen genes. Matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) mainly degrade collagen and elastin and other components of the dermis, and together with their specific inhibitors (TIMPs), maintain the structure of the dermis. Recent research has focused more on the relationship between these two and skin aging. An increase in MMP secretion and a decrease in TIMP secretion both lead to intracellular degradation of collagen molecules.

As age increases, covalent cross-links formed between the original collagen triple helices become increasingly robust. This led to the proposal of the collagen aging theory. The exact mechanism and structure of aging cross-links have always been subjects of continuous exploration and debate by researchers. With age, lysine residues form intermolecular cross-links in type I collagen in various tissues, and reactions occur producing oxime groups from hydroxylysine and aldehyde hydroxylysine. These bivalent cross-links further react to produce more complex multifunctional intermolecular cross-links. Aldehyde lysine and aldehyde hydroxylysine are generated by the oxidative dehydrogenation of lysine by copper-containing lysyl oxidase. Studies on mouse bones and rat and rabbit lungs have shown that lysyl oxidase activity initially increases to a peak value and then begins to decrease with age.

Appell first proposed that the oxidation of polyunsaturated lipids releases malondialdehyde (MDA) and other active carbonyls, which can react with proteins such as collagen, generating intra- and intermolecular cross-links. When gelatin films containing unsaturated lipids are exposed to ultraviolet and visible radiation, collagen cross-linking occurs; if free radical scavengers are present, cross-linking is inhibited. Lipid peroxidation damage experiments by Ichss et al. showed that the thermal cracking time of collagen in rat tail tendons was prolonged, indicating that the incubation of tail tendon tissue with peroxidized lipids accelerates the aging process of collagen.

Non-enzymatic glycation (NEG) refers to the reaction of the aldehyde or ketone groups of reducing sugars with free amino groups in large molecules such as proteins in the absence of enzymatic catalysis, forming irreversible advanced glycation end-products (AGEs). As organisms age, AGEs accumulate continuously in the body, causing adjacent proteins and other substances to cross-link, affecting not only the structure of these substances but also causing changes in biological functions. The dermis of the skin is rich in collagen, accounting for 50% of the dermal connective tissue. Collagen metabolism is slow, and its molecules contain more lysine and hydroxylysine. The --amino groups of these two are prone to non-enzymatic glycation reactions with glucose in extracellular fluids. The accumulation of AGEs causes collagen fibers to form intermolecular cross-links, altering their physical properties and biological characteristics. On one hand, excessive cross-linking of collagen fibers reduces the permeability of connective tissues, weakening the diffusion of nutrients, gases, and waste, increasing tissue hardness. On the other hand, they are difficult to hydrolyze by collagenase, reducing solubility and toughness. These changes lead to decreased skin elasticity, difficulty in flattening wrinkles, and their deepening, promoting the aging process of the skin. The skin aging induced by ultraviolet light may also be related to the accumulation of AGEs in the dermis. Through AGE receptors binding to proteins, cell functions are altered and oxidative stress is induced. Free radicals damage normal tissue functions, destroy normal components of the matrix, reduce collagen synthesis, and increase the release of matrix metalloproteinases, leading to greater collagen degradation than synthesis.

The formation of senile pigments involves two major biochemical side reactions: oxidation and glycation. Most of the early damage caused by free radicals and oxidation can be easily identified, engulfed, degraded, cleared, or repaired by the organism. However, the consequences of --amino reactions, especially the aging of tissue structures, are often difficult to repair and reverse, lasting throughout life.
