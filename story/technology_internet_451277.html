The Ministry of Commerce recently released five draft supporting regulations for the Anti-Monopoly Law and solicited opinions. The Coca-Cola acquisition of Huiyuan case, which has attracted widespread market attention, may be reviewed according to the new rules.

The five drafts that are publicly soliciting opinions are respectively the "Interim Measures for the Examination of Concentrations of Undertakings" (deadline: February 16, 2009), the "Interim Measures for the Declaration of Concentrations of Undertakings" (deadline: February 16, 2009), the "Draft Interim Measures for the Collection of Evidence in Cases of Suspected Monopolistic Concentrations of Undertakings Not Reaching the Declaration Standards" (deadline: February 16, 2009), the "Draft Interim Measures for the Investigation and Handling of Concentrations of Undertakings Not Declared According to Law" (deadline: February 16, 2009), and the "Draft Interim Measures for the Investigation and Handling of Concentrations of Undertakings Suspected of Monopolistic Behavior Not Reaching the Declaration Standards" (deadline: March 6, 2009).

These drafts detail the declaration and acceptance procedures. The "Interim Measures for the Declaration of Concentrations of Undertakings" (draft) makes detailed provisions on the procedures, materials preparation, etc., for the declaration of concentrations of undertakings by industry operators and the acceptance of declarations by anti-monopoly law enforcement agencies, and provides clear explanations of relevant concepts such as "control" and "turnover" related to determining the declaration standards.

The draft clearly stipulates that the Ministry of Commerce is the anti-monopoly review law enforcement agency for concentrations of undertakings, and the Anti-monopoly Bureau of the Ministry of Commerce is responsible for the specific law enforcement work of accepting and reviewing declarations of concentrations of undertakings. If a concentration of undertakings reaches the standards specified in the Anti-Monopoly Law and the Provisions of the State Council on the Standards for Declarations of Concentrations of Undertakings, a declaration must be made. Before formal declaration, the operators involved in the concentration can first apply to the Ministry of Commerce for consultations.

The declaration requires submission of a declaration form, an explanation of the impact of the concentration on relevant market competition, the concentration agreement and related documents, as well as financial accounting reports and other files and materials.

Among these, the "explanation of the impact of the concentration on relevant market competition" is the most critical document. Its content specifically includes: an overview of the concentration transaction; definition of the relevant market; market shares and market control of the operators involved in the concentration; major competitors and their market shares; market concentration; market entry; current status of industry development; the impact of the concentration on market competition structure, industry growth, technological progress, national economic growth, consumers, and other operators; evaluation and basis of the impact of the concentration on relevant market competition; and opinions from relevant parties, such as local governments and competent authorities.

The "Interim Measures for the Examination of Concentrations of Undertakings" (draft) clarifies the specific procedures for anti-monopoly reviews of concentrations of undertakings.

During the review process, the declarant and other relevant parties have the right to defend themselves, to state their views, and to present defenses. The Ministry of Commerce may solicit opinions from relevant government departments, trade associations, operators, consumers, and other entities or individuals as needed, and may decide to hold a hearing on its own initiative or at the request of relevant parties to investigate and gather evidence, listening to the views of all relevant parties.

The hearing may notify representatives of the operators involved in the concentration and their competitors, upstream and downstream enterprises, and other relevant enterprises to attend, and may invite experts, trade association representatives, relevant government department representatives, and consumer representatives to attend as appropriate.

After conducting a preliminary review of the concentration of undertakings, if the Ministry of Commerce deems it necessary to conduct further review, it shall make a decision to conduct further review and notify the declarant in writing; if it decides not to conduct further review or fails to make a decision within the prescribed time limit, the operators involved in the concentration may proceed with the concentration.

During the further review stage, if the Ministry of Commerce believes that the concentration of undertakings may eliminate or restrict competition, it shall notify the opposing opinion in writing and set a reasonable time limit for the operators involved in the concentration to present written defense opinions.

In order to pass the anti-monopoly review, during the review process, the operators involved in the concentration may adjust their transaction plans and propose restrictive conditions that can eliminate or reduce possible adverse competitive effects, such as structural conditions like divesting part of the assets or businesses of the operators involved in the concentration, behavioral conditions such as opening up networks or platforms and other infrastructure, licensing key technologies (including patents, proprietary technology, or other intellectual property), terminating exclusive agreements, or a combination of both.

After further review, the Ministry of Commerce may make a decision to prohibit the concentration of undertakings or a decision not to prohibit the concentration of undertakings.

For those not prohibited, the Ministry of Commerce may also decide to impose relevant restrictive conditions to reduce the adverse effects on competition caused by the concentration, which is conditional approval. For this purpose, the Ministry of Commerce will establish a corresponding supervision system to supervise and inspect the implementation of restrictive conditions by the operators involved in the concentration.

Additionally, the "Draft Interim Measures for the Investigation and Handling of Concentrations of Undertakings Not Declared According to Law" sets out detailed norms for the investigation and handling of concentrations of undertakings that reach the declaration standard but have not been declared according to law, including investigation procedures, rights and obligations of the investigated party, investigation content, and handling methods.

If information obtained through legitimate channels such as reports, media information, opinions of relevant departments, etc., indicates that a concentration of undertakings is suspected of reaching the declaration standard but has not been declared according to law, the Ministry of Commerce may initiate an investigation. The investigation content includes whether the concentration of undertakings has reached the declaration standard but has not been declared according to law, and whether it may have or may potentially have the effect of eliminating or restricting competition.

If it is found that the concentration of undertakings has reached the declaration standard but has not been declared according to law, and the concentration has not yet been completed, the Ministry of Commerce may order the cessation of the implementation of the concentration and make a handling decision based on the investigation results.

The "Draft Interim Measures for the Collection of Evidence in Cases of Suspected Monopolistic Concentrations of Undertakings Not Reaching the Declaration Standards" stipulates preliminary analysis before initiating the evidence collection procedure. After considering factors such as the market share of the operators involved in the concentration, the geographical scope involved in the concentration, reactions from peers and upstream/downstream enterprises, it decides whether to initiate the evidence collection procedure, and then decides whether to initiate an investigation.

The scope of evidence collection includes: the market share of the operators involved in the concentration in the relevant market; the size, market concentration, and market competition status of the relevant market; the ease of entry into the relevant market; the reaction of consumers and other operators to the concentration of undertakings; the opinions of relevant trade associations, industry regulatory departments, and local government departments; records of monopoly behaviors verified before the concentration of undertakings by the operators involved in the concentration; and the purpose of the concentration of undertakings.

It is understood that the Ministry of Commerce will separately formulate corresponding rules for the investigation procedures.

China's Anti-Monopoly Law came into effect on August 1, 2008. This law stipulates that a pre-notification mandatory declaration system will be implemented for concentrations of undertakings, and the anti-monopoly regulatory department under the State Council will conduct reviews. On August 4, 2008, the State Council issued the "Provisions of the State Council on the Standards for Declarations of Concentrations of Undertakings," which only specifies the declaration standards for concentrations of undertakings, while issues such as the declaration procedures, review procedures, and standards remain unclear.

The Ministry of Commerce is the principal department responsible for anti-monopoly law enforcement regarding concentrations of undertakings in China.
