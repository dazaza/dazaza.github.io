Citing Classics - Winning with Ease

This phrase originates from "Sun Tzu's Art of War - The Chapter on Reality and Appearance": "Those who first occupy the battlefield and wait for the enemy are at ease, while those who arrive later and rush into battle are weary." This strategy emphasizes that placing the enemy in a difficult situation does not necessarily require an offensive approach. The key lies in taking the initiative, acting opportunistically, responding to myriad changes with constancy, maintaining calmness in the face of action, actively maneuvering the enemy, creating opportunities, and ensuring that the enemy does not dictate one’s movements but instead is led by one’s own actions.

Responding to Changes with Constancy - Taking the Initiative in SAT Writing

The essence of "winning with ease" lies in achieving motion through stillness, responding to myriad changes with constancy, and subduing the opponent after they have acted. As the first section of the entire SAT exam, the writing portion plays a very important role as it represents a candidate's reasonable combination of thinking, sentence structure selection, and vocabulary within the limited time of the test. The writing score is also an important reflection of the applicant's overall quality. How to successfully overcome this type of writing within 25 minutes is an indispensable task. SAT writing is analytical writing, and part of the grading criteria requires candidates to achieve outstanding critical thinking skills, which we refer to as excellent dialectical thinking. This necessitates the ability to think about problems from different angles to achieve deeper analysis and problem-solving. This standard has already far surpassed the difficulty of the TOEFL essay exam, so the most crucial and time-consuming part of the essay exam process is undoubtedly the thought construction process. Fortunately, the scope of SAT writing questions is basically confined to a few fixed categories. If these categories can be mastered beforehand, it will be of great help for candidates to quickly solve problems during the exam.

Through our research on the exam questions, we have found that the majority of historical questions have traces of repetition in their themes. For example: in the themes of SAT questions, there is one category specifically examining the comparative relationship between individuals and groups, such as: what the masses or the authorities controlling the masses consider correct is actually wrong, but a few people discover this error and oppose it, leading to innovation and eventually promoting the development of the entire discipline. For similar questions like these, we only need to thoroughly study the correct and incorrect relationships between individuals and groups. Given SAT's high requirement for candidates to use examples in the exam to illustrate issues, we specifically search for such examples in history, such as:

John Nash, who questioned Adam Smith's views and won the 1993 Nobel Prize in Economics with his own "Game Theory"; Nicolaus Copernicus, who opposed the "Geocentric Theory" and proposed the "Heliocentric Theory"; Charles Darwin, who proposed "On the Origin of Species" (i.e., the theory of evolution);

Abraham Lincoln, who opposed slavery and granted freedom and equality to African Americans through the "Emancipation Proclamation," etc.

When candidates use these classic historical facts and figures in their essays during the exam to answer questions, it not only reflects their detailed understanding of history to the examiner but also provides profound instance support for the argumentation of the article. Therefore, categorizing the questions and finding classic examples to answer them, fully preparing for SAT writing before the exam, and achieving motion through stillness, becomes an important step in overcoming the SAT writing challenge!
