Facebook's delightful attempt at ecommerce has failed

by anonymous on 2013-08-26 14:19:11

Last September, Facebook had just gone public and launched its Facebook Gifts service to diversify its revenue streams. Through this service, users could purchase gifts (including physical items) to give to designated users, with the entire process, including payment, completed within Facebook.

This service was somewhat unique because, as users shopped on the Facebook website, it gave Facebook an e-commerce label, becoming a new attempt for social networking sites to monetize.

The creation of Facebook Gifts stemmed from many wonderful ideas. Since users generally submit their birthdays on the site, Facebook would push notifications to close friends of users who were about to have a birthday, reminding them to buy birthday gifts. Or during major holidays like Thanksgiving or Christmas, Facebook would push gift information to users.

Normally, social media websites like Facebook drive a large amount of traffic to e-commerce platforms. If they did it themselves, the traffic would stay within the Facebook site, and they could enjoy a share of the sales. Why not do it? This was a very reasonable combination of a social network and e-commerce.

However, before even a year had passed since its launch, Facebook announced that it would shut down its physical gift service, planning to convert all gift products into digital goods or more popular gift cards among users.

According to Lee Linden, who was responsible for Facebook's gift business, less than 20% of the current gift business came from physical goods, with the rest being digital goods or gift cards. Therefore, Facebook decisively closed this business and shifted its focus to areas with greater user demand. In the second quarter of this year, the revenue from the gift service was only 5 million US dollars, negligible compared to the company's overall income.

A function that seemed so reasonable encountered obstacles for many reasons. The main reason lies in the fact that e-commerce is a very complex system. Due to Facebook's cooperation with hundreds of retail stores, and some of these partnerships being quite diverse, Facebook needed to handle product display, payment systems, track inventory and logistics with partners, and also have customer service personnel. Once it involved physical sales, it often couldn't be done lightly, which made Facebook feel out of place given its expertise in online services.

Moreover, when users needed to buy gifts, Facebook Gifts lacked strong appeal. Its product variety couldn't match Amazon or Best Buy, and the prices weren't cheap either, leaving users no reason not to go to more specialized sites.

Additionally, as a social platform, Facebook couldn't be as professional as companies specifically providing such services. For example, Wrapp, founded as early as 2011, specializes in gift card giving services based on Facebook relationships. By now, Wrapp has over 1 million users, with over 15 million gift cards sent, and global cooperation with over 200 retailers. By June of this year, Wrapp's total financing had reached 25.5 million US dollars.

Facebook Gifts provided a way of thinking for the integration of social networks and e-commerce, but Facebook just couldn't figure it out yet. On the contrary, it can serve as a reference for domestic social services, such as Weibo and WeChat, which can offer similar services, especially since Weibo has a strategic partnership with Taobao, and Tencent has YiXun. Shopping integrated with social interaction will inevitably be a future development direction.
