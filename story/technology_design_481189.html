Some logistics companies embezzle the insurance fee, e-commerce companies are forced to build their own logistics system

by jdhgsa078 on 2011-08-24 16:30:34

Recently, the third party logistics distribution exposed another scandal. The valuation fee which is supposed to be used as insurance for damage compensation in the logistics process was actually taken by the logistics company as extra profit and pocketed instead of being handed over to the insurance company, resulting in significant losses for e-commerce merchants. This revelation has caused a major headache for all the major e-commerce companies, forcing them to sacrifice profits and build their own logistics systems.

The CEO of AiNai Mall, an air-conditioner B2C vertical website, Mr. Zhang Chuan recently revealed that some logistics companies have been embezzling the valuation fees from e-commerce companies, taking these fees as additional profits without handing them over to the insurance companies they are supposed to.

Previously, the price hike trend by logistics companies directly led to several e-commerce enterprises investing heavily in building their own logistics systems. The exposure of this scandal will undoubtedly accelerate the pace of e-commerce companies building their own logistics systems. However, e-commerce operates on a light-asset model. How should home appliance e-commerce companies balance between the huge cost of self-built logistics and corporate profits?

Logistics Companies Embezzle Valuation Fees; Small E-commerce Can't Afford It

Mr. Zhang Chuan disclosed to the reporter: On July 29, I sent two high-end air conditioners worth 28,500 yuan to Shenyang, with full valuation plus freight totaling 679 yuan. On August 3, the customer called me to say that the air conditioner was damaged and refused to accept it, returning the goods. I asked the Beijing logistics company to compensate me, but their staff told me that they did not hand over the valuation fee to the insurance company, so they could only compensate 3,000 yuan. I disagreed, and later their leader said they could only compensate 5,000 yuan.

However, the logistics company also expressed helplessness. A responsible person from a small logistics company who wished to remain anonymous said that due to too many loopholes in the logistics delivery process, insurance companies basically do not provide insurance services for single products from e-commerce companies now. If courier companies have to compensate, they cannot afford it either.

In fact, with the rapid development of e-commerce, online shopping companies have seen a surge in orders, but the development level of domestic logistics in China is far from meeting the needs of e-commerce development. Third-party logistics companies are under great pressure. Under such strong pressure, the logistics industry often experiences problems such as delivery delays, poor service attitudes, and inconsistent transportation standards, causing consumers to complain endlessly and troubling e-commerce companies.

Mr. Tian Yuan, brand director of New Seven Days Electrical Appliances Network, complained to the reporter recently: We originally cooperated with a well-known courier company, but they had no experience in delivering large appliances like TVs. As a result, the screen-breaking rate during transportation reached up to 10%, leading to customer rejections. This greatly affected our sales and user experience, causing us heavy losses.

After going through previous issues such as warehouse overflow, suspension of operations, and price hikes, this time the logistics company was exposed for embezzling valuation fees. An insider in the logistics industry revealed that big logistics companies like ZTO Express, STO Express, YTO Express, HTKY Express, SF Express generally do not embezzle the valuation fees from e-commerce companies, but the local franchisees of these companies usually do not hand over the valuation fees collected from customers to the headquarters as insurance premiums. This leads to untraceable cases when home appliances are damaged during transportation.

It is understood that some e-commerce companies with larger order volumes sign special contracts with logistics companies. For example, if the loss and damage ratio is within 2‰, the logistics company can be exempt from compensation. If it exceeds 2‰, regardless of the reason, the logistics company must compensate at 70-80% of the retail price. Smaller e-commerce companies that suffer are those with fewer orders. Larger e-commerce companies generally sign dominant contracts with logistics companies and even require them to pay deposits in advance.

E-commerce Forced to Sacrifice Profits to Build Their Own Logistics

With the continuous exposure of scandals such as price hikes, suspension of operations, and embezzlement of valuation fees by third-party logistics companies in recent years, major e-commerce companies have no choice but to accelerate the construction of their own logistics systems.

Traditional physical retailers like Gome and Suning use their inherent advantages in both online and offline operations to switch to electronic malls. Currently, Gome, along with the earlier acquired Kuba.com, forms a dual-brand operation in e-commerce. By integrating Gome's powerful logistics and procurement platform with Kuba.com's network technology and experience, Gome effectively combines and complements its online and physical stores.

Gome insiders stated that currently, Gome Electronic Mall has established more than 30 self-owned warehouses, possessing nearly ten thousand regional fast delivery capabilities. In the next three years, Gome plans to take several regional centers as cores, including building a core network operation system, over 100 large-appliance logistics warehouses; over 600 distribution points; and over 2,000 self-owned delivery personnel.

Kuba.com CEO Wang Zhiquan told South China Morning Post that after being acquired, Kuba.com relies on the advantages of Gome's physical stores, significantly reducing the logistics pressure. Currently, the proportion of self-built logistics accounts for the absolute majority, with only a few regions using third-party logistics.

Similarly, YiPurchasing, a subsidiary of Suning, shares Suning's national logistics system for its physical chain stores. It is understood that Suning YiPurchasing has already achieved half-day delivery in 83 cities for large appliances and next-day delivery in 220 cities. For small items, half-day delivery is available in six cities, and next-day delivery covers over 300 cities nationwide.

Clearly, building their own logistics and distribution systems is becoming a new focus for domestic e-commerce websites. Statistics show that 95% of the top ten e-commerce websites have built their own logistics warehouses, and 50% have their own delivery teams. Mr. Tian Yuan also told the reporter that now we have built our own logistics and cooperate with quality-assured third-party logistics like CR Logistics, reducing the damage rate to about one per thousand.

However, industry insiders point out that the cost for e-commerce companies to build their own logistics is very high. This is not a simple concept, and implementation will face significant financial pressure. Taking JD.com as an example, in its first and second rounds of financing, 70% of the $31 million went into logistics. But such cost expenditures are difficult for e-commerce companies in the investment and development phase to bear. Even though some e-commerce companies have started building their own logistics, it is only limited to a certain extent, and the difficulty of nationwide popularization is extremely high.

Facing the high cost pressure of building their own logistics, e-commerce companies show a sense of helplessness, having no choice but to proceed. Mr. Wang Zhiquan stated that even if a huge capital investment is required to build logistics, leading to thin profits, neither Kuba.com nor Gome will slow down the pace of building their own logistics and have prepared for non-profitability in the first few years, focusing solely on expanding territory.
