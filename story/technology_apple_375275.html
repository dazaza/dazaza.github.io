As Chinese developers, how can we address the piracy issue of iOS software in China?

by diglog on 2010-12-19 13:51:26

I just read the open letter that Hao Peiqiang sent to Steve Jobs on October 27th, titled *As a Chinese Developer, I Hope Apple Takes the Piracy Issue in China Seriously*. I think anyone who is selling software on the App Store or planning to write software for the App Store faces the same issue. Though the intent of this letter is good, judging by Jobs' past behavior, if he were to respond, it would probably be along the lines of "You should have written to your own fucking government instead." The piracy problem is still something the Chinese people themselves need to face.

If you're an iOS developer and your software primarily targets users in mainland China, you want them to buy your software through the App Store rather than steal it using tools like 91 Mobile Assistant.

What can you do? Honestly, there’s not much you can do. But there are still some things you can try:

1. Make your software great. You’ll never completely eliminate piracy, but you can offer a better alternative than piracy. Today's Chinese people (especially those born after 1985) grew up in more affluent environments compared to previous generations. If your software provides a great experience—fast, full of care, and reasonably priced—they are willing to pay for it. You might not believe this, but you'd better believe it because the software you create with this belief has a chance to be something people are willing to pay for.

2. Don’t use soft language. Piracy is piracy, stealing is stealing. If a friend asks you to help them jailbreak their device, don't hesitate to take the moral high ground—it's about defending your own livelihood. (I know some people will jump in to emphasize that jailbreaking isn’t the same as piracy. But if someone needs your help to jailbreak their device, their purpose is likely not to develop unlock software to compete with Geohot or comex.)

3. If you can afford the software, buy it. Transmit costs about as much as a meal for three or four people in a first-tier Chinese city. Besides, most iOS apps cost far less than that.

4. Educate the people around you about data security. Online shopping with a credit card isn’t as scary as it seems; what’s truly frightening is using QQ, 360 Security Guard, or IE 6, using WEP Wi-Fi passwords, using online banking without protection on public Wi-Fi without a password, opening attachments from unknown sources, thinking you have nothing to hide and thus no need to protect your privacy, etc.

5. Encourage more people to write paid software for sale on the App Store. Only creators can truly understand the position of other creators.

I don’t know if doing these things will work, but I do know they’re the right things to do.
