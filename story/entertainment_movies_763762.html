Synopsis:

Patience Phillips (Halle Berry) is a gentle and sensitive woman who leads a simple and content life in Salt Lake City. She works as an art designer at a cosmetics company named Hedare Beauty and is seen as a carefree unmarried white-collar worker by her colleagues. However, in this city filled with crime, misfortune slowly approaches Patience. By chance, Patience discovers that the company's boss George (Lambert Wilson) and his wife Laurel (Sharon Stone) are involved in criminal activities. Subsequently, Laurel sends a hitman to kill Patience to silence her.

At this moment, a miracle occurs when the Egyptian cat that Patience once saved appears before her. It turns out that this cat is actually a cat deity! To repay Patience, the cat god uses its magic to bring her back to life. After resurrection, Patience finds that her body has undergone tremendous changes. She gains a mysterious power, becoming agile, highly perceptive, and skilled in combat. Thus, Patience transforms into the mystical Catwoman, declaring war on the evil in the city.

To seek revenge, Catwoman embarks on a path of ultimate confrontation with evil. A detective named Tom Lone (Benjamin Bratt) begins investigating Catwoman and becomes captivated by her mystery and charm. Meanwhile, it seems like the entire city has fallen into a deep valley of crime, making the situation increasingly uncontrollable...

Behind-the-scenes Production:

Bringing the Legend of Catwoman to Life

Cunning, mysterious, dangerous, and sexy, Catwoman has never lacked fans or enemies. As a protector of the underprivileged, Catwoman does not cross legal boundaries to achieve her goals. She moves between darkness and light, embodying a combination of superhero and extreme villainy. Actress Halle Berry, who plays Catwoman, says, "Catwoman is a symbol of wonder; all women want to be her, and all men want to have her. In many ways, cats reflect the true psychology of women—they are elusive, and you must work hard to gain their trust and respect. As they say, dogs have masters, but cats have servants."

Since her debut in the first issue of Batman in 1940, the image of Catwoman has gone through a long evolution. Producer Edward McDonald says, "Because of her inherently mysterious nature, we were free to interpret her character. Our film retains the essential characteristics of Catwoman while infusing contemporary elements." The filmmakers also introduced a new element to the Catwoman legend—the urgent need for strength in Patience Phillips, whose transformation into the enigmatic and decadent Catwoman makes her feel stronger and more exhilarated than ever before. Producer Dennis T. Noroi says, "We wanted to tell the real origin story of Catwoman, showing how a woman becomes Catwoman and acquires the unique abilities of felines. We chose Halle because she is not only adorable and compassionate but also sexy and strong. She convincingly portrays three distinct characters seamlessly: the fragile and shy Patience before the transformation, Patience struggling with her Catwoman persona during the transition, and finally the mature and complete Catwoman."

Filming began at the end of September 2003. To create a vivid Catwoman, Berry started rigorous training in June of that year, which continued throughout the filming process. Noroi praised her saying, "Halle performed a large number of stunts herself right from the start." Stunt coordinator Steve Davison, who called Berry an 'action genius,' said, "Halle's professionalism and astonishing focus and decisiveness were unprecedented."

Choreographer Anne Fletcher taught Berry Capoeira, a Brazilian martial art that incorporates dance and gymnastics movements into traditional martial arts. While low center-of-gravity positions, high kicks, and jumps may seem effortless for Catwoman, they were quite challenging for Berry. She remarked, "Capoeira is probably the hardest martial art to learn in the world, and I had to perform all the moves in heels!"

Although CGI technology can generate some humanly impossible movements, Fletcher still carefully designed Catwoman's signature moves and taught Berry to think like a cat. Berry said, "Anne should add feline expert to her resume. She helped me understand the movement characteristics of felines and Catwoman's habits and thought processes. Additionally, I watched many videos about cats, lions, and tigers." During filming, Berry interacted closely with animal coordinator Boone Narr, head trainer Mark Harden, and 43 cats to better understand feline behavior.

Besides extensive martial arts training, Berry also mastered Catwoman's unique skill—the use of a whip. Berry recalls, "Of all the training I received, using the whip was the most fun. The whip is perhaps the most unpredictable tool, but it is also the sexiest and most fascinating. Once you perfect a swing, you'll realize it's incomparable."

The Way of the Cat

Before filming began, the production team intended to use computer-generated cats in the movie. Convincing the producers to use real cats became one of the most challenging tasks. Animal coordinator Narr said, "People think domestic cats are untamable, but in reality, they are easy to train. We demonstrated to director Pitof and several producers how obedient house cats could be, and they agreed to use them extensively in the film. Ultimately, 99% of the cats in the movie were real."

Most of the cat actors came from animal shelters across California, and "Midnight," the cat that brings Patience back to life, is a rare Egyptian cat. "Midnight" was portrayed by three Egyptian cats from New Jersey named "Cairo," "Nile," and "Scarab." They had no prior acting experience and were originally just show cats. Their personalities varied greatly—"Cairo" was the main actor and performed most scenes with Berry, "Nile" was calm and suited for sitting scenes, and "Scarab" was active and became the "stunt cat." It took four months to train these three Egyptian cats into disciplined cat actors. The opening scene features cats wildly chasing mice on city streets. Narr recalls, "Obviously, we couldn't use real mice, so we decided to let the cats chase red laser pointers. In post-production, a CG mouse would replace the laser."

Fun Facts:

- Ashley Judd was initially considered to play Catwoman.

- Michelle Pfeiffer declined to appear in the film due to discomfort with her costume from Batman Returns.

- Josh Lucas turned down the role in the film.

- After the film wrapped, Halle Berry adopted a kitten that acted in the movie.

- Due to scheduling conflicts, Graeme Revell and William Orbit were unable to compose the score, and Klaus Badelt was chosen as a replacement.

- The fight scene between Catwoman and Hathaway took nine days to shoot.

- Due to poor test screening results, the production team reshot some scenes with only one month left until the release date.

- Filming was briefly interrupted when Sharon Stone answered her cell phone.

- Halle Berry was hospitalized after colliding with studio equipment while performing a chase scene.

- Halle Berry won a Golden Raspberry Award for her role in this film.

Goofs:

- Patience's motorcycle helmet disappears and reappears intermittently.

- In the fight scene with Hathaway, Catwoman's high heels change from thin heels to thick ones.

- In the jewelry store robbery scene, the robbers fire 16 shots from two shotguns at Catwoman without reloading, even though each shotgun holds only five rounds.
