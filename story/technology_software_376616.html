Baidu Xiaosheng: The Android app I'm using

by xdash on 2010-12-31 11:12:08

(The author of this article, Xiaosheng, is currently a product manager at Baidu Mobile Division and a co-founder of Mobile Design China)

As professionals in the mobile internet industry, we need to study all kinds of popular Android/iOS applications, especially those similar products related to our own projects. We need to understand them from the aspects of visual design, interaction design, information architecture, and operation. However, as a Moto Milestone user, I also need to use various applications in daily life. Due to my background in industrial design, I have a preference for simplicity, like MIJI, IKEA, 1984, and Douban. Therefore, I naturally have higher requirements for the visual interface when choosing mobile applications. This is akin to how programmers love cutting-edge technology; professional background can even influence the way we choose products in daily life.

Invited by XDash to talk about some commonly used applications, familiar ones like Gmail, Gtalk, and browsers go without saying. So, I will pick four less exposed "daily items."

Jorte

Jorte Notebook is like a simplified version of Google Calendar, with simple and easy-to-use main functions. Less frequently used features are placed in the settings. When you open the app, the first page displays the calendar, allowing you to quickly check your schedule. You can choose different visual styles and arrangement methods, with dynamic effects for view transitions, and swipe left or right to flip pages. It can sync with Jorte Calendar or Google Calendar, supports data export and import, which would be appealing to those who love synchronization.

The functions can basically meet most users' needs for calendars quickly and efficiently. It offers up to 16 different styles of desktop widgets, more than any other application, satisfying any screen display space requirement.

When clicking on a date, a dialog box pops up to create a new event, and you can also choose "previous day" and "next day." Actually, these two options are unnecessary. The Moto Milestone has a resolution of 480*854 pixels, even with a horizontal resolution of 320 pixels, each clickable date area can reach 45 pixels. After converting precision, it already meets the click area for an adult user's fingertip, with little chance of accidental clicks.

Clicking on a single task allows you to view task details, set status, and edit. The underline style for "show details" is a web design norm, but personally, I dislike seeing such designs in client applications. This might have something to do with Android's own control design. Hiding some less commonly used "advanced" options in the page is quite consistent with user operating habits.

Compared to Jorte, the design of the sixth-ranked 365 Calendar in the Android Market is slightly more complex, with more information on the page and more functions. 365 Calendar is a typical example of transplanting iOS design entirely onto Android, with sluggish responses to physical back key operations. Personally, I find its operations quite awkward.

365 Calendar includes lunar terms, making it suitable for the local context. The schedule and calendar just differ in viewing modes, with significant repetition of information between two adjacent views. Initially, the default display shows Beijing weather, assuming that you can modify it in "more" or "settings," but after failing twice, I realized that you need to change it in the weather section under "tools," which doesn't support automatic location. This is a common issue when there are too many functions. In terms of desktop widgets, combining with time makes the widget more convenient to use than Jorte, occupying the user's main screen position.

Baidu Around Me

Baidu Around Me was just released in December, with both WAP and Android versions available. The cute white bear on the startup screen looks very friendly. From my personal aesthetic perspective, the interface design of the previously used Dianping client needs a major overhaul, and after half a year of use, the interface design has hardly changed. New LBS products like Baidu Around Me, Shanda Check-in, and QQ Food are much more aesthetically pleasing. However, Dianping still holds a clear advantage in terms of data at present.

Personally, I don't focus on what sign-in functions LBS products offer, but rather what convenient information services they provide. After asking several colleagues in the LBS industry about improvements in user sign-ins, the answer I got was that if users like signing in, they can publish their current location and activities. Personally, I think this idea is somewhat naive, as it could make the product overly simplistic. There are still many designs around sign-ins or user geographical locations that can be explored.

For Baidu Around Me, I personally focus on how to quickly discover nearby merchants, obtain information to help me determine my action goals, and what I can do upon reaching my destination. The interface details of Baidu Around Me still need some fine-tuning. Seeing so many "＞" on the homepage drives me crazy. What is displayed on the main interface of a mobile product reflects what the product design prioritizes. For example, the main page of Dianping shows business districts and channels, while StreetPong's main page displays friends' sign-in information.
