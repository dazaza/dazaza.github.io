1. Cleaning or soaking shiitake mushrooms too much will wash away the nutrients. Shiitake mushrooms contain ergosterol, which turns into vitamin D after sunlight exposure. However, excessive cleaning or soaking before eating can lead to nutrient loss. When cooking mushrooms, avoid using iron or copper pots to prevent nutrient loss.

2. Overcooking frozen vegetables leads to nutrient loss. Most frozen vegetables have already been blanched and do not need to be cooked for a long time. Otherwise, they will become mushy and lose many nutrients.

3. Eating raw bean sprouts is harmful. Bean sprouts are tender and nutritious, but they must be thoroughly cooked before eating. Otherwise, it may cause nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, dizziness and other adverse reactions.

4. Eating tomatoes before meals frequently is not advisable. Tomatoes should be eaten after meals. This way, the acid in the stomach mixed with food significantly reduces acidity, avoiding increased gastric pressure that causes bloating, leading to abdominal pain, discomfort, etc., in children.

5. Mixing carrots and radishes into a puree is not recommended. Carrots contain an enzyme that can destroy vitamin C, which would completely break down the vitamin C in radishes if they are mixed.

6. Excessive intake of beta-carotene. Although beta-carotene is very nutritious for children, it should be consumed in moderation. Excessive consumption of vegetable juices made from carrots or tomatoes may lead to carotenemia, causing facial and hand skin to turn orange-yellow, resulting in poor appetite, unstable mental state, irritability, sleep disturbances, night terrors, crying, talking in dreams, etc.

7. Feeding children too much spinach. Spinach contains a large amount of oxalic acid, so children should not eat too much. Oxalic acid combines with calcium and zinc in the body to form calcium oxalate and zinc oxalate, which are not easily absorbed and excreted, affecting the absorption of calcium and zinc in the intestines. This can easily lead to calcium and zinc deficiency in children, causing poor bone and tooth development, as well as affecting intellectual development.

8. Feeding children unblanched bitter melon. The oxalic acid in bitter melon can hinder calcium absorption from food. Therefore, bitter melon should be blanched in boiling water before eating to remove the oxalic acid. Children who need to supplement a lot of calcium should not eat too much bitter melon.

9. Storing cooked leeks for too long. Overnight leeks can turn toxic. Leeks are best prepared and eaten immediately and should not be stored for a long time. If stored for too long, a large amount of nitrate will turn into nitrite, causing toxicity reactions. Additionally, children with poor digestion should not eat leeks.

10. Cooking leafy greens for a long time. Leafy greens should not be simmered for a long time during cooking. Otherwise, the nitrates in the leafy greens will turn into nitrites, easily causing food poisoning in children.
