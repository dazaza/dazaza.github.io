When will the economic crisis be over and what can we do about it?

by langmzz on 2008-11-13 21:10:44

When will the economic crisis be over? This is a huge question, and it is such a big issue because it is far more complex than people imagine. It encompasses everything from a sweet potato to an entire country or even the whole world. Some say half a year, others say one year, and still others say two to three years. Personally, I think the third opinion is more scientific, because this economic crisis was triggered by the U.S. "subprime crisis," meaning it started in the financial sector, where the financial system encountered problems. Only through reforming the financial system can we end this economic crisis. And financial system reform is not something that happens overnight; it requires gradual coordination among countries worldwide. Moreover, even if the reforms are effective, the crisis can only be gradually reduced, not abruptly stopped. Hence, time is needed. Therefore, no one, not even the world's top economists, can provide an accurate answer. At most, they can only inform people about the trends in the development of the economic crisis.

So, what can we do in these two to three years? Should we just wait for the economic crisis to pass? Should we retreat? Stand still? Or move forward? I believe no one wants to stand still, and even fewer people want to retreat.

What is a "crisis"? Separating these two characters, you have "危" (danger) and "机" (opportunity). "危" plainly means danger, while "机" refers to opportunity. We must marvel at the wisdom of our ancestors, who, when creating this term, intended to tell people that when there is danger, there is also opportunity. The key lies in what comes to mind when people see these situations.

Take the internet as an example. Affected by this financial crisis, some video network operators and social networking services (SNS) have been hit, starting layoffs. This has dealt a significant blow to smaller network operators and those planning to invest and start businesses in the network market. Especially during an economic crisis, investing and starting a business gives the impression of being foolishly brave—like knowing there’s a tiger on the mountain yet deliberately heading toward it.

However, people should also remember another saying: "rowing against the current—if you don’t advance, you retreat."

On one hand, those network operators already listed and operational have already invested, so they can only use this crisis to strengthen their adaptability, seize opportunities, turn pressure into motivation, upgrade technology and market applications, fill gaps, and take advantage of any chance to make a comeback, refueling for longer-term development.

On the other hand, those planning to invest in the network market shouldn't just focus on the "危" (danger) in the crisis. Remember, opportunities are everywhere, especially when others are scared off by the dangers. In practical operations, lessons can be learned from the problems this crisis has caused for the network industry. By identifying vulnerabilities in network operations, finding new paths in technology and operational strategies, one can more easily see others' problems during a crisis and find solutions more readily. Larger network operators find transformation more difficult, making it easier for new entrants to gain an advantage. That’s why I say that a crisis is the best time for entrepreneurship—it all depends on whether one can seize the opportunity.

Let me give a simple example: It's like buying stocks when they plummet. Costs are lowest then, and eventually, they will rise again according to stock market rules. When they do, you'll make a lot of money. Of course, this requires analysis, forecasting, and strategy—you can't just buy any stock. Similarly, in entrepreneurship, as long as the direction and strategy are appropriate, the cost of starting a business during an economic crisis is the lowest.
