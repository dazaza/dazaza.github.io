Baidu: Don't just focus on searching

by anonymous on 2013-08-26 13:57:00

On August 22, the annual Baidu World Conference once again became the industry focus.

From "Box Computing" in 2009, the application open platform in 2010, personalized entry in 2011, to the "seven weapons" of cloud services in 2012, every year's Baidu conference is a grand roadshow where Baidu outlines its strategy for the coming year and showcases its strength and direction.

So this year, what did Robin Li say?

Undoubtedly, it could only be mobile, and it would only be mobile. The competition focus in the Internet world has shifted from PCs to mobile, an unavoidable fact. Baidu, which spent $1.9 billion acquiring 91 Wireless, must quickly secure its ticket to this new world.

What is this ticket? Robin Li said: it's "lightweight apps."

So-called lightweight apps refer to applications that can be used without downloading. For example, if you input "from Baidu Tower to Guomao" on Baidu Mobile, you no longer need to open Baidu Maps to directly see the map and route. This way, users don't need to download a large number of application clients; they just need a search engine to solve their problems.

This is obviously the most suitable path for Baidu.

In the PC era, the browsing web pages as the main mode of internet use, along with the scattered distribution of massive information, made search engines the largest entry point of the entire Internet. Baidu and Google rode this wave to become the largest-scale Internet giants in China and the U.S., respectively.

However, over the past few years, differences in screen size, processing speed, and operation methods of mobile phones have led to the development of a business system centered on client applications in the mobile Internet. Users have gotten used to downloading different application clients, leading to the rise of various app stores and putting Baidu, which focuses on search, in an awkward position. Even though it opened up an application platform and cloud services, it was still hard to avoid being marginalized.

But since 2012, circumstances have taken a turn for the better.

On one hand, Android, riddled with malicious programs, has continuously increased the security risks of downloading and using APPs; on the other hand, the long-tail effect of app stores has become increasingly prominent. Additionally, with the maturity of technologies like HTML5 that support the webification of mobile applications, the possibility of users directly using applications through search has re-emerged, and the entry value of search has thus returned.

The reason is very simple: the PC era has already proven that only search is the most effective way to address long-tail demands.

In such a situation, Robin Li attempted to re-establish Baidu's key entry position in the mobile Internet world with the combination of "acquiring 91" and "lightweight apps." The former makes up for Baidu's shortfall in mobile application distribution, while the latter allows Baidu to capture developer and user needs in the long-tail segment that app stores find difficult to cover.

From this perspective, Baidu's grasp of market trend directions and strategic layouts for future competition are much more critical and accurate than 360's tactical-level moves like "Tomato CtrlCtrl."

After completing the entry layout, Baidu's ultimate goal is clearly a "super distribution platform" that integrates different entries and platform resources, gathers merchant resources from different developers, and reconstructs the value chain of the traditional PC search ecosystem.

Therefore, at the 2013 Baidu World Conference, Robin Li asked the question "Developers, are you happy?" in the style of CCTV's happiness survey to attract the 99.9% of developers who harbor resentment towards app stores.

Of course, I still want to mention one point: although this is currently the best strategy for Baidu to seize the voice in the mobile world from both strategic and tactical perspectives, the Baidu World Conference still has some shortcomings.

Currently, BAT has undoubtedly become the three major players in China's network industry. However, if we broaden our view globally, China's Internet industry is still catching up. Without discussing the gaps in network infrastructure and root servers in basic fields, China still lags behind in operating systems and application innovation.

Besides historical debts and the environment for innovation protection, a more important point is that Silicon Valley giants often put more effort into application innovation and then encourage everyone to follow them, whereas Chinese giants mostly focus on building platforms, integrating resources to provide logistics and tools, encouraging others to charge ahead for them.

For example, Google, beyond its foundation in search, constantly expands into cross-field areas such as mobile operating systems, wearable devices, unmanned cars, etc. Meanwhile, Baidu, although still robust in innovation, mainly focuses on its old search business.

If viewed from the perspective of the enterprise itself, both paths have their merits. Focusing on one field or expanding innovation each has its advantages and disadvantages. But as leaders at the level of BAT, who gather the largest resources of the entire industry, I think they should actually raise their sights and look further.

Even if 360 is beaten down day after day, how meaningful is that really?

In fact, there are too many fields where innovation can happen, such as the cross-integration of information technology with industry, the Internet of Things, biomedicine, and even with numerous traditional industries. This is a vast area and will also be the focus of emerging industry competition between countries in the future.

In these areas, Internet giants actually have great potential, but so far, we haven't seen any strategic-level products or plans from them in these aspects.

Finally, a small piece of gossip: the sponsors of the 2013 Baidu World Conference were SONY and Wahaha, which is quite interesting.
