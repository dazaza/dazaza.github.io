Professional Criminal Defense: Sentencing System Reform in Comparative Law Perspective

by lilan2333 on 2009-12-02 11:03:14

Shangquan Law Firm in Beijing   Chang Zheng     

The principle of legality for crimes and punishments, as well as the principle of proportionality between guilt and punishment, place the issues of conviction and sentencing at the core of the criminal trial system. However, reviewing China's procedural setup and judicial practice in criminal trials reveals a characteristic of "emphasizing conviction, neglecting sentencing," meaning that conviction and sentencing are integrated into one process. Under this model, conviction becomes the focus of the trial, and the impact of sentencing procedures on the defendant’s rights is not given sufficient attention, which is detrimental to the full exercise of the right to defense. This weakens the balance between the right to defense and the power of adjudication, potentially affecting the fairness of punishment.

Here, through examining the systems of conviction and sentencing under different litigation models, I propose some ideas for reforming China's sentencing system.

### 1. Comparison of Sentencing Systems Under Different Litigation Models

#### (1) Anglo-American Legal System's Sentencing Model

The Anglo-American legal system adopts a model where conviction and sentencing are separated, dividing criminal trials into two relatively distinct stages: "determination of guilt" and "sentencing hearing." In the first stage, an adversarial litigation model is used, strictly constrained by evidence rules. Fact-finders listen to evidence presented and cross-examinations by both prosecution and defense to authoritatively determine whether the defendant is guilty of the charged offense. [①] Once the defendant is found guilty, the judge will hold a specialized hearing on sentencing, during which probation officers or social workers present a "pre-sentencing investigation report," listening to sentencing opinions from the prosecutor, defense counsel, and even victims before making a sentencing decision.

The sentencing recommendation system in the Anglo-American legal system gradually emerged alongside pre-sentencing investigation (presentence investigation) and plea bargaining systems. The UK and the US represent the Anglo-American legal system, but there are differences between these countries regarding the issue of whether prosecutors can make sentencing recommendations. Unlike the US, UK prosecutors cannot make sentencing recommendations, even in plea bargaining scenarios. In contrast, multiple entities in the US have the authority to make sentencing recommendations, with the prosecutor's sentencing recommendation system being the core part of the US sentencing recommendation system.

#### (2) Civil Law System's Sentencing Model

Compared to the Anglo-American model, the civil law system determines that the same trial organization, following the same trial procedure and applying the same procedural and evidentiary rules, simultaneously resolves both conviction and subsequent sentencing issues. [②]

Prosecutors in most civil law countries have explicit or implicit legal bases to make sentencing recommendations, which is a "traditionally more common practice" in civil law countries. [③] Unlike in Anglo-American countries, prosecutors are the sole entities authorized to make sentencing recommendations in civil law countries. This authority typically stems from the role and tasks of prosecutors in criminal proceedings. Differences in roles and tasks lead to variations in the scope or content of sentencing recommendations made by prosecutors. For example, in countries like France and Germany, prosecutors are allowed to make both general and specific sentencing recommendations in court. In a few countries like Russia, prosecutors can make sentencing recommendations but not specific ones.

#### (3) Current Conviction and Sentencing Model in China

Since the reform of China's criminal trial methods in 1996, adversarial elements have gradually been incorporated into the criminal trial model, reducing the influence of officialism. However, overall, China still maintains a model where conviction and sentencing procedures are integrated. Nevertheless, China's criminal trial system differs from other civil law countries in that the core of criminal trials in China is conviction, while sentencing is not the primary issue to be resolved in criminal trials, becoming an ancillary procedure to conviction. Sentencing issues are not specifically included in court investigations but are only mentioned during the court debate phase. Prosecutors supporting public prosecution do not have much authority to make sentencing recommendations, and parties involved in litigation, such as defendants, defense counsel, and victims, also lack opportunities to make suggestions on sentencing issues. There is no specific procedure for either side to present evidence, question witnesses, or debate on sentencing circumstances, types of punishment, ranges, mitigating factors, reductions, or exemptions from punishment. Determining the sentence becomes an independent activity conducted by judges outside the courtroom. Currently, there are no clear regulations on the right to make sentencing recommendations or relatively independent sentencing procedures in judicial practice.

### 2. Issues with China's Current Conviction and Sentencing System

From the perspective of litigation efficiency, the integrated model of conviction and sentencing is not without its advantages. Compared to the Anglo-American model where conviction and sentencing are separated, this integrated model addresses both the conviction and sentencing of the defendant within a continuous trial process, enhancing litigation efficiency. However, more and more scholars, both domestic and foreign, point out two fundamental flaws in the integrated model of conviction and sentencing practiced in civil law countries: First, it easily undermines the effectiveness of the presumption of innocence, lowering the defendant's litigation status; Second, it gives judges too much discretion in sentencing, making it difficult to obtain sufficient factual information and impossible to hear opinions from both prosecution and defense during the sentencing decision-making process. [④]

From the perspective of China's judicial practice, the trial process emphasizes conviction over sentencing, with conviction being the core of criminal trials. Both sides mainly investigate and debate around whether the defendant is guilty, what crime they committed, and often lack corresponding factual information about sentencing circumstances, especially discretionary sentencing circumstances, as well as related sentencing circumstances and their specific ranges during sentencing. Courts do not conduct comprehensive investigations into the defendant and their actions, nor fully examine the consequences of the crime. Additionally, due to the lack of an independent sentencing supervision institution, it is impossible to scientifically evaluate the effects of punishment. In current judicial practice, although both sides may propose relevant opinions on major sentencing circumstances, there are no specific sentencing recommendations afterward. These sentencing informations have not undergone thorough court investigations and debates, leaving sentencing entirely up to the judge's evaluation and determination. Judges also neglect fully explaining the reasons for sentencing in the ruling documents. Such non-transparent sentencing procedures can easily cause defendants and their families to question judicial fairness, leading to social conflicts.

Particularly, in cases where the defendant does not admit guilt and the defense lawyer makes an innocent defense, the disadvantages of the integrated model of conviction and sentencing become more evident. When the judge finds the defendant guilty, during previous trial processes, an innocent defense leaves the defense lawyer unable to sufficiently argue on the defendant's sentencing issues: Choosing an innocent defense means having no opportunity to suggest leniency in sentencing; If leniency in sentencing is suggested, it creates a contradictory situation where the defense argues both innocence and leniency based on the same facts.

### 3. Justification for Reform in China's Sentencing System

Firstly, sentencing defense is a part of the right to defense, and separating conviction from sentencing can ensure the full exercise of the right to defense.

Article 35 of the Criminal Procedure Law stipulates that "The responsibility of the defender is to, based on facts and laws, present materials and opinions proving that the suspect or defendant is innocent, has a lighter crime, or should have their criminal responsibility reduced or exempted, protecting the legitimate rights and interests of the suspects and defendants." Clearly, sentencing is as important as conviction and is an important part of the defense.

Separating conviction from sentencing and establishing an independent sentencing procedure can avoid insufficient sentencing defense in cases of innocent defense. At this time, the defendant can freely choose an innocent defense without worrying about losing the opportunity to personally defend themselves on sentencing issues; And when the defendant voluntarily admits guilt, the court's thorough investigation and debate on sentencing circumstances precisely provides another guarantee for the fair application of punishment.

Secondly, the independence of the sentencing procedure can effectively constrain the power of adjudication.

Standardizing sentencing can, to a certain extent, limit the judge's discretion. However, without the participation of both prosecution and defense, victims, and other litigants in the sentencing procedure, sentencing remains independently decided by the judge. Only when both prosecution and defense truly participate in the sentencing procedure, establishing an independent sentencing procedure, allowing defenders to exercise their right to sentencing defense in a public and transparent process, can the power of adjudication be effectively constrained.

### 4. Proposals for Reform in China's Sentencing Procedure

China's sentencing procedure reform must combine with the country's judicial practice status and not simply imitate foreign practices. It should be rooted in local conditions and gradually establish a system separating conviction and sentencing procedures. I propose distinguishing between cases of innocent defense and voluntary admission of guilt, treating them differently.

In cases where the defendant does not admit guilt or the defense lawyer makes an innocent defense, separate the conviction procedure from the sentencing procedure and establish a sentencing hearing procedure, which refers to the court presiding over hearings on sentencing issues for specific defendants, listening to opinions from both prosecution and defense, victims, and the public.

In cases where the defendant voluntarily admits guilt, a unified procedure can solve both conviction and sentencing issues, simplifying the investigation and debate on conviction facts and focusing the trial on investigating and verifying sentencing information and debating it.

At the same time, incorporate the sentencing recommendation system, legally granting both prosecution and defense, and victims the right to make sentencing recommendations. These sentencing recommendations can be general or specific and will ultimately be chosen and evaluated by the court in the ruling document.
