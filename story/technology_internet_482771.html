With the acceleration of urbanization, a large number of houses on rural collective land have been included in the demolition scope. In order to solve the housing problems of the displaced persons, the government has built special relocation安置 houses. Relocation安置 houses enjoy relevant policies such as land allocation and fee reduction, and have the nature of social security. Their supply targets are families of residents relocated from collective land. Compared with ordinary commercial housing, the biggest feature of relocation安置 houses is the specificity of the purchasing objects and the restriction of trading time, that is, only qualified displaced persons are eligible to purchase relocation安置 houses, and within a certain period of time (usually five years) after obtaining the property certificate of the relocation安置 house, the displaced person cannot transfer the house to others. Although the transaction of relocation安置 houses is restricted, in actual life, due to the usually large price difference between relocation安置 houses and ordinary commercial housing, an actual transaction market for relocation安置 houses has formed among the people, and even "scalpers" who buy such houses in bulk and then resell them for profit have appeared. Although the practice of both parties signing a purchase contract, handling the procedures for the payment and delivery of the house, and then processing the transfer of the house after five years does not violate legal regulations and is considered a legal act; compared with ordinary commercial housing, the transaction risks of relocation安置 houses are much greater. This is mainly reflected in the following aspects: First, rising housing prices can easily induce the seller to breach the contract. According to policy regulations, the seller must wait five years after obtaining the property certificate before transferring the house to the buyer. During this long five-year period, it is difficult for anyone to predict the trend of housing prices. When housing prices rise sharply, the seller may completely sell the house again to a buyer offering a higher price. In some extreme cases, a set of relocation安置 houses may have been bought and sold more than ten times before the final transfer. Buyers of relocation安置 houses cannot obtain sufficient protection from the purchase contract. The ultimate ownership of the house depends on the transfer situation: whoever ultimately obtains the ownership certificate of the relocation安置 house is the owner of the house, regardless of whether they were the first buyer. Many buyers of relocation安置 houses think that since the purchase contract has been signed, the payment has been made, and the house has already been renovated, even if the seller breaches the contract, how could they be driven out of the house? However, in fact, the buyer who completes the transfer procedures will legally obtain the ownership of the house and has the right to request the original purchaser to move out of the relocation安置 house. Second, the buyer cannot obtain compensation benefits from another demolition of the house. In the process of urban expansion, some newly built relocation安置 houses face demolition again, which is not uncommon. At this time, the compensation paid by the demolition department is often higher than the transaction price of the relocation安置 house, and therefore, disputes over the distribution of the demolition compensation often occur between the buyer and the seller: the seller believes that since the house has not been transferred yet, the demolition compensation should belong to the seller; the buyer believes that the payment has been fully settled, they have already moved in, so the demolition compensation should belong to the buyer. From a legal perspective, the demolition compensation should still belong to the seller: on one hand, the demolition compensation is a transformed form of the demolished house, not interest. The transformation usually takes the disappearance of the original object as its premise; while the production of interest usually does not depend on the disappearance of the original object, but rather occurs under the condition that the original object exists. The rule that the ownership of interest changes with delivery does not apply to demolition compensation. On the other hand, the seller obtains the demolition compensation based on legal regulations and not without legitimate grounds, thus it does not constitute unjust enrichment. Therefore, even after having received the purchase payment, the seller still has the right to obtain the demolition compensation. Third, it is easily affected by uncertain factors. A long transaction time means that many unforeseeable factors will trigger disputes. In one case, before completing the transfer, the seller passed away, and the heirs of the seller disputed the sales contract, believing that the price sold to the buyer was too low. In order to complete the transfer, the buyer had to negotiate with the heirs whom they had never met, and became involved in the seller's family affairs. Eventually, the buyer chose to resolve the issue through litigation, but faced new difficulties. The seller had multiple heirs, some of whom had settled overseas. In this situation, the court would have to deliver documents through diplomatic channels. As for how long it would take to complete the delivery, it was hard to predict, and the litigation would be prolonged as a result, preventing the buyer from obtaining the property certificate. In addition, changes in national tax, credit, and other macro-control policies will also cause both parties to re-evaluate the gains and losses of the transaction.

When buying relocation安置 houses, buyers should not only see their relatively low prices but should also recognize their special risks. I believe that buying relocation安置 houses is not a wise decision, because the time required to successfully complete the transaction is too long, and the potential legal risks are difficult for anyone to foresee and avoid.
