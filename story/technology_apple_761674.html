JD.com's Challenging Year: What are Liu Qiangdong's 4 trump cards?

by geekzhang on 2013-05-06 15:08:40

Jingdong's Year of Trials: What Are Liu Qiangdong's Cards?After completing the fourth round of financing, the noise about Jingdong's impending demise has nearly disappeared. However, Jingdong's external environment hasn't changed; formidable rivals still loom large. What cards does Jingdong hold?Yuan YinIn just over half a year, JD.com CEO Richard Liu lost over 20 pounds.You could make several guesses about his weight loss: JD can no longer afford price wars like on August 15, and he feels weak inside; strong enemies make him feel uneasy: Alibaba is building a large logistics network, Tencent-backed YiXun is making aggressive moves, and Suning continues to fiercely compete with JD; or JD, moving at high speed, is being tested more in its internal capabilities as a B2C company, testing Liu Qiangdong's ability to steer the JD chariot.Of course, there could be another interpretation: for instance, last year JD underwent its third organizational restructuring, aligning its CXO management team, freeing Liu from daily operations, giving him more leisure time; this spring, with the completion of another round of financing, JD now has money, and rumors of a broken capital chain did not materialize as expected, relaxing him. Once again, Liu Qiangdong told "China Entrepreneur" that JD has no plans to go public within two years.For his weight loss, Liu Qiangdong explained that he is exercising, persistently running. For the long and enduring e-commerce battle, good health is absolutely necessary.In 2013, Liu Qiangdong set a goal for JD to reach 10 billion yuan in online transactions, while simultaneously planning four strategic sectors: e-commerce, logistics platforms, technology platforms, and internet finance. In other words, this year is JD's year of trials; achieving the 10 billion yuan target not only means that JD's massive flywheel can accelerate to millions of rotations, similar to Tmall, but also suggests that it may enable the second and third large flywheels to start spinning.In the same year, e-commerce became a field of heavy capital and asset competition; the entire internet was at a turning point where mobile technology was changing destinies; user tastes became increasingly picky, and demands and habits kept evolving. Can Liu Qiangdong achieve his goals? In this complex card game of e-commerce, what cards does Liu Qiangdong hold?The first card: $700 millionWill JD survive? Speculations about its capital chain and board control have accompanied JD for ten years and are still present today.On February 16, JD confirmed the completion of a $700 million equity financing in common stock. Investors included Canada’s Ontario Teachers' Pension Plan Board, Saudi Arabia’s Kingdom Holdings Company, and some existing shareholders, temporarily dispelling the notion of an inevitable demise.This is JD's fourth round of financing. To date, JD's total funding amount is quite astonishing, nearing $2.3 billion. Although Liu Qiangdong repeatedly stated externally that JD is not short of money, outside observers like Li Chengdong also said JD is not lacking funds, but this doesn’t mean JD isn't under financial pressure.Liu Qiangdong recounted the perilous moments amid the rumors to "China Entrepreneur." JD has never faced a situation where the capital chain was about to break or the company would die. Of course, there was one time when sleep wasn't good, during the financial crisis.He recalled 2008, when rumors spread that JD couldn't raise funds. There was a brief period of hesitation, but the reality was, our shareholders would provide whatever amount we needed, but we hoped to find new venture capitalists, fearing that old shareholders might take too many shares. In other words, the subsequent two rounds of financing totaling $2.2 billion were obtained very easily by JD, which still maintained control over the entire board.In terms of personality, Liu Qiangdong doesn't seem like the type of entrepreneur investors typically favor. He is extremely assertive, demanding absolute control over the company and the board. When negotiating with investors, he responded to growth questions, "You ask me how I prove it? You can think it yourself; if you think I can't achieve it, don't invest." The same applies to old shareholders: "If you believe in me, hold on; if not, sell. It's not like you can't sell; I can also buy back these shares."Investors may not like the domineering Liu Qiangdong, but JD's potential is incredibly enticing. JD publicly disclosed data showing sales of approximately $1.6 billion in 2010, reaching $9.7 billion in 2012, with an annual growth rate close to 200%.Please note, 2012 was a bleak year for the e-commerce environment, with many B2C and group-buying websites collapsing due to broken capital chains. Previously well-funded companies like Vancl also encountered survival crises. Capital began concentrating on the leading players, and JD was the natural choice.With the new funds, JD built a robust barrier. $700 million is equivalent to RMB 4.4 billion. Last year, there were reports that JD's 2013 logistics investment would reach RMB 3.6 billion. Liu Qiangdong did not deny such speculation, telling "China Entrepreneur" that besides logistics, JD would increase investments in new businesses and technologies. Previously, JD's investments were mainly to meet the needs of e-commerce itself, but starting this year, new business attempts will begin. It is reported that JD is undergoing a large-scale system upgrade and investing in big data, with an estimated budget of RMB 1 billion.Liu deliberately emphasized that he wouldn't waste money anymore. In the past, it was common to raise $10 million in funding and generate only RMB 10 million in annual sales. Such behavior severely violated basic commercial rules and occurred every year. He said, "I believe that this phenomenon will almost certainly not happen this year." This implies that price wars lead to losses, and Liu Qiangdong doesn't intend to play that way anymore. In the past, JD's rapid expansion relied on price wars, quickly entering markets with low prices (though Liu claimed the low prices came from JD's high efficiency rather than burning money), with the shortfall subsidized by marketing expenses. The most typical example was the battle against dangdang in the book market and the events of August 15.He reflected on the classic battle of August 15. "It was a coincidence, inevitably happening on the same day at some point, engaging in short-term competition. This is fair competition in a market economy, nothing unusual. It doesn't mean it won't happen again, nor does it mean it will happen every day. If they come, let them come; if they leave, let them leave. Their arrival isn't surprising, and their departure isn't regrettable." But competitors won't let up easily. Mo Daiqing, an analyst from the China E-Commerce Research Center's online retail department, said: "JD's new round of financing will undoubtedly exert intangible pressure on potential competitors including Suning's Suning.com, dangdang, Tencent's e-commerce, and Amazon China." Liu Qiangdong faces another group fight. Not long ago, YiXun launched a targeted price protection service, promising refunds if items drop in price within 24 hours after purchase. In terms of funding alone, JD is slightly weaker compared to Tencent. Although YiXun lags significantly behind JD in scale, it is undeniably a "rich second generation" backed by Tencent. Tencent not only has strong financial resources but also abundant and nearly free traffic within the QQ ecosystem, saving significant marketing costs. YiXun's CEO, Bu Guangqi, confidently told "China Entrepreneur," "Under the premise of low-cost operations, few can lose money faster than Tencent." Similarly, Alibaba possesses formidable financial strength. Although the Alibaba ecosystem manages merchants rather than goods, offering less control compared to JD, platform-oriented Alibaba is unlikely to guide merchants into price wars, but this giant remains JD's unavoidable strong rival.JD must also face Suning. Years of accumulation give Suning a procurement cost advantage. However, as a listed company, price wars could lead to a sharp drop in stock prices. Moreover, returns on many real estate investments require three to five years, and Suning needs e-commerce infrastructure more than JD does. If a price war truly occurs, Suning's financial base is slightly inferior to JD's.The second card: User ExperienceIn the business world, Liu Qiangdong is unyielding. As he described, aside from disdain for investors, he also shows contempt for competitors, saying, "We must always, daily, annually, and at any moment face competition, so who the competitor is doesn't matter."However, he lavishes attention on users. He openly admits his sense of crisis stems from user experience, stating that the final few large platforms left will become increasingly painful as competition intensifies. While platforms may engage in differentiated competition, facing consumers, price and service remain the ultimate deciding factors. JD must maintain a sense of impending danger; even a slight decline in user experience could lead to failure. If JD's user experience surpasses that of competitors (Liu calls them 'friends'), then we can rest easy.At JD, user experience is divided into three elements. The first element is the product itself.Liu says, "To this day, JD only sells genuine products." When selling 3C products, JD is the only merchant that voluntarily and mandatorily provides invoices. Last year, JD issued one or two hundred million invoices, a significant cost. Previously, Xiaomi founder Lei Jun revealed that each invoice costs about 0.2 yuan, meaning JD spends tens of millions annually just on this.The second element is price. Liu describes low prices as a policy that will never change in a hundred years. However, due to JD's prominence, it is often targeted, making it nearly impossible to offer the lowest price for every item. JD COO Shen Haoyu interprets low prices as competitive pricing. "Relying solely on irrational low prices is unrealistic. Losing money every day until JD disappears would leave users worse off. I prefer the concept of consistently low prices; if you add a few items together, it will definitely be cheaper than others."The third element is service. In 2013, JD implemented a series of plans to enhance service.This year, JD established a special department called the User Experience Department, directly overseen by Liu Qiangdong. This small team consists of young employees drawn from various departments within JD, all avid online shoppers. Their job is to evaluate and test JD's user experience, reporting results directly to Liu, who will demand relevant departments to make improvements immediately.Liu is confident, claiming JD compares favorably against competitors in these three aspects. JD strives to maintain a lead over followers, especially in details.In 2013, JD aims to upgrade passive services to active ones, converting over 90% of customer service calls to proactive outreach within a year.Active service means identifying issues before users do and proactively providing assistance. For example, if delivery is delayed due to weather, JD will preemptively inform and possibly compensate users; return exchanges will bypass cumbersome procedures with users simply scanning a QR code to initiate processing. Or when collecting items, notifying customers of collection times and collectors.This project is led by Shen Haoyu. He gives examples: previously, JD provided little active service for abnormal orders, only responding when users called to inquire. "Fantasizing that users won't call and problems will resolve themselves automatically is unacceptable. Users will contact you if their orders have issues, so it's better to identify problems early and proactively reach out." However, Shen is mindful of the balance. With fast-paced lifestyles, everyone is busy, and marketing-style outbound calls can easily annoy users. Expansion must be cautious.Shen Haoyu oversees JD's warehousing, logistics, and customer service—three departments closely related to service. Recently, he has been reviewing active service strategies internally, following delivery personnel in several cities to study which processes can be improved and what additional services users need. In the future, frontline delivery personnel can handle much marketing work. "The goods belong to the company, and the people belong to the company; this advantage is hard for others to replicate. Leveraging this advantage ensures all users feel it." Shen frequently tells frontline delivery personnel, "You represent JD."JD's RMB 1 billion technical investment is closely related to this. Active services rely more on systems and data. Using big data systems, if an order encounters an issue at a certain stage, the system will alert and directly transmit information to the call center. Before joining JD, Shen had experience organizing and mining big data, preferring to abstract human tasks into tools. JD streamlined its customer service knowledge base, automatically displaying relevant information when users inquire about specific products. He hopes customer service representatives can solve problems, sell products, and understand categories and products. Content unfamiliar to representatives, requiring offline consultations with relevant departments, is saved, organized, and sometimes placed directly on product pages, providing answers to users during shopping.Simultaneously, big data can manage user expectations more reasonably. Shen believes JD's order call-in ratio still has room for improvement. He and many around him frequently shop online without ever calling customer service. Repeated calls indicate unresolved issues, increasing call volume and reducing user experience. "Who is calling? At what time? About what kind of problem? These data points exist in the system but haven't been analyzed." He says, "Our goal through data analysis is to reduce unnecessary calls, ensuring each incoming call is handled satisfactorily. This immediately becomes productivity, improving efficiency, saving costs, and enhancing user experience." Compared to companies like Baidu, Tencent, and Alibaba, JD started its big data initiatives relatively late. Liu Qiangdong explains, "Now is the right time; the data must accumulate sufficiently for meaningful insights."However, JD's potential in big data is already evident. JD will provide data services upstream in the supply chain. Allocating goods based on experience previously resulted in significant errors, and later adjustments were costly. Big data services can help suppliers adjust regional supply ratios and even assist manufacturers in regulating production. Nationally, JD's user experience is undoubtedly a strong card. But JD's user experience isn't flawless. At least JD cannot yet ensure consistent experiences between its POP platform (open platform) and its self-operated platform. Additionally, since many city deliveries rely on third-party logistics, JD struggles to precisely control efficiency and outcomes.All e-commerce companies are seeking JD's weak points in user experience to break through. Within the Alibaba ecosystem, merchants are encouraged to emphasize "small and beautiful" for personalized experiences distinct from JD. Suning implemented uniform online and offline pricing in cities like Beijing, where physical store experiences often trump virtual ones. In densely populated areas, Suning finds it easier to leverage physical stores for quality delivery and services.YiXun's strategy directly targets JD. Bu Guangqi aims to concentrate resources intensely, aiming for breakthroughs in local markets, replicating successes in key cities. In Shanghai and East China, YiXun offers three daily deliveries within 4-6 hours, with possible innovations like slow delivery and scheduled delivery, cultivating many loyal users. Currently, first-tier cities are the main contributors to e-commerce sales. In 2013, YiXun expanded into markets like Beijing and South China. This nibbling strategy prompts Liu Qiangdong's defense, which is offense—continuously enhancing user experience.The third card: LogisticsIn April 2013, "China Entrepreneur" explored the mysterious Asia No.1 warehouse. Near a highway exit in Jiading District, Shanghai, amidst a bustling construction site hung JD's flag—this was JD's Shanghai Asia No.1 warehouse. Phase one spans 94,000 square meters, with the main structure completed and equipment installation about to begin. Adjacent land is reserved for phases two and three, with construction to follow. Upon completion, the Shanghai Asia No.1 warehouse will cover 230,000 square meters.Currently, warehouses of comparable size in China are mostly used for bulk logistics and unsuitable for e-commerce. JD is an exception. The medium-sized warehouse awaiting equipment installation stands 24 meters tall, divided into three zones with dual-level operations. One zone is fully mechanized with operator stations on the platform. The other two zones are semi-mechanized but require only 32 employees. It is understood that the entire warehouse is custom-built for JD.By the end of 2013, JD's total warehousing area will exceed one million square meters. Expanding warehousing capacity, Liu Qiangdong calculated from JD's three operational indicators: front-end focusing on user experience, back-end focusing on cost and efficiency. These indicators determine profitability, which hinges on logistics management.In March, Tencent CEO Pony Ma said the future trend in e-commerce is that standardized category products yield no profit, relying instead on non-standardized products like clothing sold through open platforms for profit. However, Liu Qiangdong disagrees, emphasizing operational and managerial capability. "At the same price, others lose money while Walmart profits because Walmart achieves a low-cost, high-efficiency supply chain. This is JD's core goal." Regarding inventory turnover speed, Gome and Suning average 60-70 days managing 10,000 SKUs. In 2012, JD managed 2 million SKUs with an average inventory turnover rate of 28-32
