Will WeChat be an opponent of Alipay?

by anonymous on 2013-10-19 11:55:21

In the era of super apps and light applications, the perspective on "Alipay's rivals" has changed. For Alipay, which was once invincible, competitors have now emerged.

In mid-September, users in Beijing found at subway stations that by using WeChat Pay on a vending machine, they could purchase any beverage for only 1 yuan by simply scanning the product QR code.

Behind this simple and minor action of "scanning to shop," there are two main messages being conveyed: first, an ideal shopping scenario is being built and realized where consumers can efficiently complete purchases using fragmented time, turning any place with a QR code into a store; secondly, WeChat has made a strategic move in the payment domain.

From a functional perspective, Alipay's rival should be companies like Tenpay. However, as per a metaphor used by Jack Ma, whether in terms of user numbers or market share, Alipay’s "value presentation" has consistently outperformed other third-party payment companies, making it a decisive force that rivals cannot even see through a telescope.

But in the mobile internet world, the current emergence of super apps represented by WeChat and the unstoppable trend of light applications are constructing simpler and better application scenarios and user experiences. They are beginning to shake up the seemingly unshakable forms from the PC era, such as payments.

What does WeChat bring?

Comparing Tenpay and Alipay horizontally might seem a bit rough, as their significance to their respective parent companies differs fundamentally: starting from its inherent e-commerce attribute, Alipay is one of the most crucial links, especially after the birth of the mobile product Alipay Wallet, which in some sense serves as an entry point. On the other hand, Tenpay has been comprehensively integrated into Tencent's organizational structure as a foundational tool, serving the relational chain.

Compared to Alipay, which stands independently within the Alibaba system, Tenpay is "invisible."

But the internet landscape changes rapidly. Despite holding the second position with a 20% share of the third-party payment market, Tenpay's expansion space on the PC end remains limited, yet it still has a chance to turn the tables, and this opportunity comes from WeChat's large platform.

Currently, WeChat's payment scenarios based on goods, dining, entertainment, etc., are omnipresent. Tenpay General Manager David Lai once stated that "O2O micro-payments" will rely on WeChat and Micro Life to gradually integrate Tenpay's new mobile payment model into users' daily payment scenarios.

Let's look at what else WeChat Pay is doing: embedding small payments like games, emoticons, membership fees, forming user micro-payment habits; cooperating with airline WeChat PR accounts to complete ticket transactions anytime; connecting with video websites to realize home entertainment payment models; and of course, integrating mobile e-commerce payments with its own Yixun platform. Within WeChat, payment is more like a lightweight background function that appears as needed.

WeChat Pay is quietly penetrating every corner of life, and these areas are also the application scenarios that Alipay desires.

Looking back at payments, payments have always existed at the "tool" level. But just like how email was once a function and later evolved into a basic infrastructure, when payments transform from an "organ" into a "gene," the priority of application scenarios clearly outweighs the tool itself.

When an application is powerful enough and the payment scenario is smooth enough, is connecting to Alipay still necessary? Moreover, the time window during which Alipay benefited from the "lagging online banking services" is disappearing. As long as banks open secure interfaces to external platforms, the payment process will become increasingly simplified. Convenience will become a standard feature, even without a payment tool.

The emergence of WeChat seems to begin shaking the necessity of Alipay as a "tool." These two parties, which were not previously on the same frequency, have started a "different kind of competition."

These super apps with massive user bases, along with the inevitable trend of light applications, will have the ability to more effectively solve the problem of aligning applications and services with mobile user needs. Or rather, users only need the service; they don't care about the specific implementation form.

This means that being a general-purpose tool in the future is somewhat risky. The implicit embedding of payment capabilities in super apps will inevitably lead to the segmentation of the payment market. And in this process, while no one may disappear, all will face the harsh reality of value degradation as they gradually narrow down to their own systems.

Alipay's Response

In fact, every step Alipay has taken since initiating O2O payments via QR codes has not been missed. For example, Alipay has partnered with "Youbao" and ten other vending machine operators, allowing users to use Alipay Wallet to purchase products from vending machines at any time; it has also cooperated with 300 offline catering merchants for mobile payments, among others.

However, the segmentation of the payment market is happening faster than imagined. Not long ago, JD.com terminated cooperation with both WeChat and Alipay on mobile ends, seemingly ready to launch its own payment system; Sina and Baidu have recently obtained third-party payment licenses; and industry rumors about companies like Xunlei venturing into secure payment fields have never ceased.

In such an external landscape, facing competition from WeChat, how should Alipay respond?

It is understood that Alipay will soon release a brand-new feature, introducing a "public account" platform within the Alipay Wallet app. The first batch of cooperative accounts will go live shortly.

Why does Alipay want to create public accounts? The underlying logic is not difficult to understand: once the transaction bottleneck between public accounts and users is resolved, corporate accounts will break free from the limitations of mere customer service and information communication, opening up vast possibilities—this is why many public accounts connect to WeChat.

Taking the most active aviation enterprises as an example, users can check and directly complete payments at any time by simply opening WeChat. Such an experience easily attracts and retains users. It is rumored that China Southern Airlines launched special WeChat discounts to promote its WeChat ticketing channel, offering tickets purchased via the WeChat Official Account platform at a 6% discount compared to its official website and mobile app.

For Alipay, achieving this connection is effortless. And this group is precisely the audience Alipay wants to capture and mobilize. According to sources, Alipay has already provided a payment integration cooperation model in preliminary negotiations with the first batch of users and has offered personalized solutions for public accounts in different industries.

To date, Alipay remains the most well-known and excellent payment tool domestically, and Alipay Wallet is still a powerful killer application. Especially after the launch of Yu'e Bao, it not only opened the curtain on internet finance but also brought about enormous effects in mobile wealth management that many companies find hard to match. If the internet sees a peak every five years, Alipay undoubtedly maintained its dominant position in the PC era. However, under the rapid change rate of mobile internet, in three years, a completely different result is entirely possible.

Regardless, facing today's WeChats, Alipay must expedite the expansion of its user value.
