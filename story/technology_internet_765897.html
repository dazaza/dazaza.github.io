Forget the tablet, Nokia has a much bigger plan

by anonymous on 2013-08-29 16:28:44

Nokia seems to be almost certain to launch a tablet. Compared with the Nokia Windows RT tablet that has been criticized even before its release, Nokia has an even bigger plan. Michael Halbherr, Executive Vice President of Nokia's Location and Commerce department, told GigaOM that the Finnish hardware manufacturer intends to enter the automotive industry. Of course, Nokia will not manufacture "indestructible" polycarbonate cars but instead plans to launch a connected car service platform.

Nokia is already somewhat involved in the automotive sector; the Here Maps led by Michael Halbherr offers car navigation services. However, this shallow level of cooperation does not satisfy Nokia. According to Michael Halbherr, Nokia will use its map service, which has a good relationship with car manufacturers, as a stepping stone to establish a close connection with cars and provide information and entertainment services.

Historically, Nokia has indeed provided maps and traffic updates to the automotive industry, and in this aspect, it can even be considered an industry pioneer. In a recent interview, Michael Halbherr stated:

"An increasing number of connected cars give us the opportunity to transition from being content providers to platform providers and service providers."

Michael Halbherr did not elaborate on the specific details of this transformation. However, it is certain that Nokia’s thinking will not be limited to car dashboards or autonomous driving technology but rather seeks to integrate cars into a larger transportation network and "smart city" network. At the GigaOM Mobilize conference in October, Michael Halbherr will discuss more about integrating private cars into public networks.

Although there are no specifics about Nokia's plans, Michael Halbherr still mentioned some benefits this plan will bring to automobile transportation:

- Helping guide the fastest route to the destination

- Reducing carbon dioxide emissions (how?)

- Collaborating with traffic management systems to coordinate congestion (suggest testing in Beijing)

Nokia has years of experience in software, hardware, and service integration, and Microsoft currently holds a decent share in the in-car information system market, although the industry leader is BlackBerry's QNX. With its relationship with Microsoft, Nokia will have a smooth start.

Taking Ford as an example, providing services for Ford cars based on a Windows-based in-car infotainment system platform would be effortless for Nokia.

Currently, Google Maps and Apple's Siri voice control offer convenience for drivers through these free services, but they pose a risk of losing control over cars for car manufacturers. Both Google ads and Apple's attempt to replace dashboards with Siri are things car manufacturers do not want to see.

For the automotive industry, they have always maintained strict control over the entry points for applications and services into cars. Compared to the internet industry, the automotive industry is a conservative group, but they have realized that they must become more open, even if they won't follow Google's pace. Nokia and the automotive industry share a similar temperament, and their relationship is also good.

So, will Nokia's new plan align seamlessly with the automotive industry that needs a gentle transformation?
