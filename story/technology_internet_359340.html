Nuclear safety bureau: China to be a major nuclear power country in 2012

by ditan360 on 2010-06-28 16:36:09

China’s nuclear power policy has been adjusted from "moderate development" to "active development". It is expected that by 2012, China will become a major nuclear power.

On the 26th, Tian Jiashu, director of the Nuclear and Radiation Safety Center of the Ministry of Environmental Protection (National Nuclear Safety Administration), revealed this news when attending the "2010 Fifth Green Wealth (China) Forum".

According to the mid-to-long term development plan for nuclear power approved by the State Council in 2007. By 2020, the national installed capacity of nuclear power will reach 40 million kilowatts, with about 18 million kilowatts under construction, totaling 58 million kilowatts, accounting for 4% of the installed power capacity.

"The plan is being adjusted. It is estimated that by 2020, the total capacity of operating and under-construction nuclear power units will reach 75 million kilowatts, with more than 40 million kilowatts under construction, accounting for 5-6% of the installed power capacity," he said.

Currently, there are a total of 11 operating nuclear power units in our country, with a total installed capacity of 9.068 million kilowatts, accounting for 1.1% of the total installed power capacity in our country. From 2006 to November 2009, 22 nuclear power units have obtained construction permits.

Tian Jiashu said that it is expected that by 2012, the number of operating and under-construction nuclear power units in our country will reach 50 (the current scale of France and Japan), becoming a major nuclear power; by 2020, it will reach 100 (the current scale of the United States), becoming one of the top two nuclear powers in the world.

Tian Jiashu believes that due to the limited role or utilization of hydropower, wind power, geothermal energy, and solar energy, they cannot meet the growing energy demand. Therefore, vigorously developing nuclear energy is inevitable.

According to the investigation by the Nuclear and Radiation Safety Center of the Ministry of Environmental Protection, a one-million-kilowatt coal-fired power plant consumes 200-300 tons of coal annually, while a nuclear power plant of the same power only requires 30-42 tons of nuclear fuel annually. The use of nuclear energy can not only save a large amount of coal and oil but also greatly reduce the corresponding transportation pressure.

"Compared to the large amount of greenhouse gases and waste slag produced by conventional thermal power, the waste discharge of nuclear power plants is very low, which can be considered zero discharge," Tian Jiashu said. Nuclear power plants also need to discharge radioactive exhaust gas, wastewater, and waste slag, but before discharge, they undergo strict "three-waste" treatment to ensure that their radioactivity and quantity have an impact on the environment within safe limits.

Regarding the economic issue of nuclear power, Zhao Renkai, academician of the Chinese Academy of Engineering, stated, "As long as the cost of nuclear power is reduced to $1,500 per kilowatt, nuclear power can have economic competitiveness."

According to the economic study of nuclear power plants built after 2000 by the National Nuclear Safety Administration, except for Germany and the UK, the base price of one-million-kilowatt nuclear power plants is all below $1,500 per kilowatt, among which South Korea is $1,495 per kilowatt, the United States is $1,237 per kilowatt, and France is $1,179 per kilowatt.

"It can be seen that nuclear power can fully compete with coal-fired power," Tian Jiashu said.

Currently, the domestication rate of nuclear power in our country is relatively high. Tian Jiashu said that reducing the cost of nuclear power plants is an important measure to enhance the competitiveness of nuclear power, and the domestication of nuclear power is an important way to reduce the cost of nuclear power.

According to the survey by the National Nuclear Safety Administration, with the increase in the domestication ratio, the cost of nuclear power generation has significantly decreased: when the domestication reaches 50%, nuclear power is about 10% higher than thermal power; when the domestication reaches 70%, nuclear power is slightly lower than thermal power.

At the forum, Tian Jiashu also refuted some rumors that some countries are reducing the scale of nuclear power. He cited examples: In 2001, the United States passed the "Energy Assurance for America's Future" bill, supporting the construction of new nuclear power units, adding 50 million kilowatts of nuclear power capacity by 2020; Russia plans to significantly increase the proportion of nuclear power, raising it from the current 17% to 25% by 2030; India plans to build 50 million kilowatts of nuclear power by 2030 (currently at 3.48 million kilowatts); Japan plans to increase the proportion of nuclear power to 40% by 2030 to reduce CO2 emissions; South Korea announced that nuclear power will increase from the current 38% to 65% by 2035.

Tian Jiashu stated that the International Energy Agency recently predicted that by 2050, nuclear power generation may account for 25% of global power generation, and at that time, global carbon dioxide emissions will be reduced by 50%.
