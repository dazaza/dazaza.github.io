Neglecting to manage enterprises' transactions of bank acceptance bills

by loveyan5 on 2012-02-18 15:42:50

Translation:

Allowing companies to trade bank acceptance bills

The next shockwave will directly hit banks

The act of buying and selling bank acceptance bills must be effectively stopped by law

(By Lawyer Guo Yumian, Hebei Tianjie Law Firm)

Currently, for the behavior of trading bank acceptance bills without a genuine trade background or real debt-creditor relationship, China's governance level has not paid enough attention either in terms of awareness or from an operational legal standpoint. The enormous hidden dangers posed by the simple act of buying and selling bank acceptance bills have surfaced. If effective measures are not taken in time, sooner or later, the next wave of hidden dangers will directly impact the banks, potentially leading to their bankruptcy and affecting the property of innocent depositors, further impacting social stability.

I. The enormous hidden dangers posed by the simple act of buying and selling bank acceptance bills have surfaced:

Case Review:

1. A deputy branch manager of the Agricultural Bank of China in Hohhot severely violated regulations by issuing acceptance bills, resulting in China's largest acceptance bill fraud case with an involved amount of 370 million yuan, causing the country a loss of 75.99 million yuan.

2. Female staff at a Shanxi bank lost 113 million yuan through the resale of bank acceptance bills.

3. Li Qun of the Agricultural Bank of China in Fuyang, Anhui lost nearly 100 million yuan through the resale of bank acceptance bills.

4. The "Hou Xiaorong Special Large Ticket Fraud Gang" is suspected of defrauding victims like Chen Yong of 180 million yuan in ticket funds.

5. Changhong was involved in a 2 billion yuan case of issuing fake VAT invoices for bank acceptance bills.

6. In Zhejiang, seven people were sentenced in the first case of reselling bank acceptance bills, involving 1.084 billion yuan.

7. In Yixing, Jiangsu, a 500 million yuan bank acceptance bill fraud case was exposed, with the main culprit still at large.

8. Agricultural Bank employees in Shijiazhuang lost 200 million yuan through the resale of bank acceptance bills.

Currently, similar cases in Shandong, Henan, Shanxi, Inner Mongolia, Jiangsu, Shenzhen, etc., are under investigation...

II. Why do such cases result in many victims losing everything, yet people still rush forward? Why does history repeat itself, failing to serve as a lesson for future generations?

The reason is simple:

Firstly, many entities involved in the resale (including individuals and corporations) have a侥幸 psychology. They believe that as long as the capital chain remains unbroken among all parties involved in the resale process, they can continue the "passing the parcel" game. (Of course, from a moral standpoint, pursuing profit is a common human trait and is nothing to criticize. However, the enormous hidden dangers it poses are something we must guard against.)

Secondly, throughout the entire management of this type of financial order, whether it's the banks, the CSRC, or even the judicial precedents set by public security, procuratorate, and court after cases occur, there hasn't been a strategic height adopted to maintain financial stability, prevent bank bankruptcies, ensure depositor fund safety, and thereby achieve social stability. It's merely treating symptoms rather than addressing root causes.

III. How to effectively manage this chaos disrupting financial order? I believe there are two key management directions: First, it is necessary to effectively "intercept" and sanction relevant enterprises' continuous large-scale "acquisition" of bills in the market. Second, strictly monitor and sanction the illegal actions of banks in the bill splitting market, truly preventing banks from "adding fuel to the fire" by engaging in the resale of bank acceptance bills solely for the purpose of increasing deposits.

Some enterprises, especially some large enterprises (after contacting some cases, according to my understanding, some state-owned enterprises and listed companies also engage in the acquisition of bills), have each formed a relatively fixed channel for acquiring bank acceptance bills, creating a group with relatively fixed downstream sources providing tickets regularly, gradually forming a pure "ticket resale industry." Pursuing profit is a common human trait, and it is nothing to criticize. However, the hidden dangers of this behavior disrupting financial order are enormous.

One reason is the existence of "profit margin," which leads to some people specializing in absorbing public deposits for pure resale profits. However, the daily discount rate is as volatile as the stock market, with prices changing every day, while those accepting others' deposits need to pay high interest daily. As a result, in order to cover borrowing costs and ensure the smooth absorption of public deposits in the future to continue the "passing the parcel" game, the phenomenon of "buying high and selling low" naturally occurs. Sooner or later, a situation of "platform collapse, broken capital chain, and numerous victims" will arise!

Secondly, this ticket resale behavior without a genuine trade background also invisibly exacerbates inflation. Currently, the industry has not given sufficient attention to the significant inflation caused by this behavior.

Therefore, to effectively prevent this disruption of the financial market, it is necessary to first intercept enterprises from massively "acquiring" bank acceptance bills in the market, cutting off the pure "buy-side market" for bills. This way, the suppliers (future victims, including enterprises but mostly individuals) will lose their basis for existence.

Strictly regulate the behavior of banks engaged in bill splitting, prohibiting the splitting of bills without a genuine trade background. While splitting creates opportunities, it must not create risks!

A few years ago, I read a financial newspaper. It promoted how a certain bank branch had developed a bill-splitting business, significantly increasing the bank's intermediary service fees within just one year. It also effectively achieved the deposit-increasing targets set by the higher-level bank. The title was prominently marked in bold letters as "Splitting Out a New Horizon," encouraging other branches to emulate or replicate the practice.

At the time, I was very puzzled: how could a small branch have so many and such large amounts of bank acceptance bills requiring discounting and splitting? After later encountering related cases, my doubts were answered: the bank likely colluded with its account-holding enterprises to forge contracts and tax invoices. Engaging in such business poses enormous risks to the bank. Banks have the responsibility to understand the annual business volume of enterprises to determine the credit limit for bank acceptance bills. For example, if an enterprise produces goods worth 1 million yuan per year, but applies for discounting (splitting) of bills amounting to 10 million yuan, where does the difference of 9 million yuan come from? Once such cases are uncovered and litigation ensues, the bank will bear primary liability.

On the other hand, once the actual trade volume of enterprises is "emptied" by this formal resale of bank acceptance bills, if the bank-issued acceptance bills expire after six months and the enterprise cannot pay the bank's exposure on time (meaning: when applying for issuance of bills, the enterprise first pays 30% of the guarantee fee, and after six months, should deliver the remaining 70% of the funds to the bank, otherwise the bank will have to advance the remaining 70% of the acceptance funds), the bank will have to use its own funds to cover the shortfall. Over time, the risk to the bank is enormous, and such losses will become real and difficult to recover. Ultimately, bankruptcy is not impossible.

IV. In judicial activities, when handling bank acceptance bill fraud cases, public security, procuratorate, and court must not lose sight of the bigger picture. They must not simply emphasize the independence of bills. The independence of bills is not "lawless and chaotic," and the independence of bills must be premised on the legality of bill transactions.

In some places, public security, procuratorate, and court handle such bank acceptance bill fraud cases by not investigating where the defrauded bills ultimately flowed to, which specific "bill-acquiring" enterprises or units (bank acceptance bills can only be used by units; individuals are merely intermediaries during the resale process)? Whether these "bill-acquiring" units have paid the corresponding price? Instead, they focus on investigating and punishing the "supplying" entities. Their abstract reasoning is that bills are independent and ensuring their circulation is paramount.

However, I believe that neither from the legislative intent nor the judicial pursuit of results, such reasoning is sufficient.

First, theoretically, according to the "Bill Law" and relevant judicial interpretations, as well as banking laws and regulations (such as "Payment Settlement Methods," "Discount and Rediscount Regulations," etc.), the independence of bills is not "lawless and chaotic." The independence of bills must be premised on the legality of bill transactions. Only by ensuring the legality of bill transactions first can the circulation of bills truly be guaranteed.

Secondly, in practice, not investigating the flow of defrauded bills towards the "downstream" of endorsements, but only investigating and punishing the supply of bills from the "upstream" of endorsements, means: punishing the supplying entities while not punishing the purchasing units, cannot effectively standardize and stop the behavior of buying and selling bank acceptance bills. As mentioned earlier: pursuing profit is a human nature, as long as there is profit to be made and a sense of侥幸 exists, as long as the "bill-acquiring" market continues to exist, the "supplying" market for reselling bank acceptance bills cannot be interrupted.

Therefore, in handling such cases, public security, procuratorate, and court must first clarify: what is the ultimate purpose of handling such cases? The purpose of the judiciary is not about punishing how many people, nor collecting how much fines, nor solving how many major cases; the ultimate purpose of the judiciary in such cases is to effectively warn and protect potential victims' financial security through the judiciary, effectively prevent financial risks through the judiciary, and effectively standardize financial markets to avoid bank bankruptcies and maintain social stability.

V. Finally, I believe that there should be explicit legal provisions established to punish such behaviors.

Although Article 5 of the "Amendment to the Criminal Law (Seven)" defines illegal payment settlement services as illegal business operations, this regulation is "hidden too deep," affecting public understanding, and many people still believe that reselling bank acceptance bills is a legitimate voluntary "trade" between equal entities. Therefore, it is necessary to establish clear legal grounds for punishing the resale of bank acceptance bills. A new crime category called "Reselling Bank Acceptance Bills Crime" should be established, and the punishment for such behavior should be increased.

VI. Lastly, four important points are reiterated:

1. The ultimate goal of judicial authorities handling such financial cases is not about punishing how many people, nor collecting how much fines, nor solving how many major cases; the ultimate goal of the judiciary in such cases is to effectively warn and protect potential victims' financial security, effectively prevent financial risks, and effectively standardize financial markets to avoid bank bankruptcies and maintain social stability;

2. The key and effective measure to prevent this bill resale behavior is to "intercept" the act of enterprises "acquiring bills" without a genuine trade background;

3. Do not simply emphasize the independence of bills. The independence of bills must be based on the legality of bill transactions. The premise and foundation for protecting the normal circulation of bills is also the legality of bill transactions.

4. Strengthen monitoring of the bill splitting market between banks. While "splitting out a new horizon," do not "create a field of hidden dangers"! Do not simply think that this is just banks seeking intermediary business income, nor simply assume that this is just for increasing deposits. The enormous hidden dangers it poses to banks and society are: banks ultimately being unable to honor their commitments, leading to bankruptcy and harming depositors' savings, thus affecting social stability.
