Wisdom Education - Basic Introduction to SAT

by thatseed123 on 2011-05-05 23:18:07

SAT is the abbreviation for Scholastic Assessment Test, and its Chinese name is Academic Ability Assessment Exam. It is hosted by the Educational Testing Service (ETS) headquartered in Princeton, New Jersey, USA. The SAT score is an important reference for high school students from all over the world to apply for studying at prestigious American universities and for scholarships. Currently, if high school students want to apply to top universities in the USA, apart from providing results from language proficiency tests like TOEFL, 90% of schools require SAT scores as it is the only comparable measure of student ability that American universities can use, coming from different regions and schools. Chinese high school students with only TOEFL scores have almost no chance of being admitted to the top 100 universities in the USA. Moreover, SAT scores are also a decisive factor in determining whether Chinese high school students can apply for scholarships. Therefore, we can roughly understand SAT as the "college entrance exam" of the USA. (However, the admission process in American colleges is different from that in China, so simply equating SAT with the American college entrance exam is not comprehensive. When American universities assess whether a student should be admitted, SAT scores generally account for no more than 50% of the evaluation criteria. Furthermore, most schools that are not ranked particularly high explicitly state that they do not require SAT scores from regions like mainland China where there are no SAT testing sites. Therefore, it is not impossible to get into lower-ranked schools without SAT, but having SAT scores definitely provides a significant advantage. In similar circumstances, students with SAT scores will be given priority.)

More than 2 million American high school students take the SAT each year. It is an exam acknowledged and required by nearly all American universities, especially the Ivy League, serving as a crucial reference for admissions decisions and scholarship evaluations. Therefore, in 2005, nearly 2,000 Chinese high school students took the test, and this number has been increasing dramatically year by year.

The SAT is divided into two parts: SAT I Reasoning Test and SAT II Subject Tests. SAT I mainly tests candidates' writing, reading, and math abilities, while SAT II covers subjects such as mathematics, physics, chemistry, and biology. The most commonly selected subjects by Chinese students are three out of mathematics, physics, chemistry, and biology. Generally, Chinese high school students applying to American undergraduate programs need only participate in the SAT I exam. Compared to the TOEFL test, although SAT is not a language test, it is a unified examination for high school students worldwide, including those in the USA, so the word difficulty alone is much higher than that required by TOEFL. Especially since the reform of the SAT in March 2005, the newly added writing section is a test of pure English language skills, a requirement that neither TOEFL nor IELTS exams can match. Therefore, for Chinese students, merely understanding SAT as a reasoning and subject test carried by language would be a big mistake; on the contrary, its requirement for language proficiency is a level higher than that of TOEFL.
