Facebook Cuts Back on Intrusive Ads in News Feed

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 10:56:57

Facebook announced on Friday an adjustment to its advertising policy, aiming to reduce the number of ads for products and services that users are not interested in within the News Feed.

This is Facebook's latest attempt to improve advertising on the News Feed, a business that is becoming increasingly important for this social networking company.

"When deciding which ads to show to which audiences, we will place more emphasis on user feedback regarding ads, including how often people report or hide certain ads," said Facebook.

"If someone frequently hides electronic ads, we will reduce the number of such ads shown to them," the company added.

Facebook has been striving to highlight advertisements while also hoping to avoid causing dissatisfaction among users. This world's largest social network boasts 1.15 billion users, with 85% of its revenue coming from ads. In July, Facebook stated that for every 20 pieces of content users see in their News Feed, one is a paid advertisement.

Although big brands like Toyota and AT&T advertise on Facebook, the platform also hosts many smaller brand ads for weight loss and teeth whitening. Analysts say some users may not welcome these less well-known products appearing in their feeds.

Nate Elliott, an analyst at U.S.-based market research firm Forrester Research, suggested that Facebook should place the latter type of ads in less intrusive areas, such as the right side of the webpage, reserving the News Feed for high-quality ads.

"If Facebook allows unappealing ads to be placed here, they will run into trouble. It will harm the user experience and diminish the value of premium ad spaces," Elliott said.

Facebook indicated that some marketers might "see changes in their ad channels in the coming weeks," but did not provide further details.

On Friday, Facebook's stock price rose by 1.7% to $51.24 during regular trading on the Nasdaq, marking its highest closing price since its IPO in May 2012.
