This year, I prepared to spend my holiday in Bali. As soon as we landed, the hotel driver came to pick us up and brought us to the hotel. It was a great hotel with very attentive service. Taking five days off from the busy life and work to relax made me come to Bali for the second time. "The wind blows gently, the night sleeps quietly. The wind blows gently, the night is deeply drunk." Walking on the beach of Nusa Dua, Luo Dayou's song echoed in my mind. The wind was actually pretty strong, and the waves of the Indian Ocean had a mesmerizing power in the midnight darkness. The seawater was cold, so we marveled at the horror of the Titanic sinking. The sky was still clear on the night of Bali in June, and the horizon was full of stars. The only sound in my ears was the sound of the waves. The night at Nikko was really quiet. I spent two such nights at Nikko.

After resting briefly at the hotel, we went out for dinner. Unexpectedly, the streets of Kuta were quiet, and it started drizzling. Isn't Kuta known as the busiest place in Bali? I wonder what the former Bali was like with its music and dance. We chose to have dinner at the famous Poppies. In the following days, we tried several well-known and less famous restaurants. Regarding the restaurants in Bali, I think: First, the few restaurants widely recommended online, like Poppies and those in Ubud, are mostly characterized by deep courtyards or mountainous charm. It's better to visit these places during the day; there's no feeling at night when it's pitch black. Second, the above-mentioned restaurants don't follow the romantic route. The romantic restaurants in Bali are mostly located in seaside hotels, and there are some at the Discovery Shopping Mall near the sea in Kuta. Third, Indonesian cuisine is quite ordinary; there's no need to be too particular about choosing a restaurant when coming to Bali. Many restaurants with average reputations have good taste. Finally, the dinner at Jimbaren Beach is worth a try. Although it has become a tourist project, I always miss the incredibly comfortable feeling of having dinner by the sea. Back at the hotel, I pushed open the floor-to-ceiling window and lay on the sofa that resembled a bed by the window while outside, the band from the themed bar sang songs. Each room in the hotel has a small set of speakers. Turning on the radio, there are always beautiful songs on the Balinese radio station.

We woke up around 10:30 the next morning, had breakfast at the nearby McDonald's, and then wandered aimlessly on the beaches and streets of Kuta. The beaches of Kuta are quite average, possibly due to the large number of tourists. There were many debris, making it look somewhat dirty. In comparison, the beaches of Nusa Dua visited later were better. We booked a car at 2 PM through the hotel. Although the time was short, one must tour the island after coming to Bali. I love photography and prefer natural landscapes over historical culture, so generally, I don't visit temples or appreciate folklore unless they themselves have high aesthetic value.

Our first destination was Tanah Lot, which is roughly one of the closest attractions to Kuta, about a 45-minute drive. I was glad to see many tourists there; a tourist spot without people lacks vitality, and I don't like a quiet Bali. However, having many people also causes trouble, as people line up one by one to take photos, and there are always crowds in my lens. To me, Tanah Lot is a place that neither surprises nor disappoints; you can visit if you have time, but it doesn't matter if you don't. After staying for more than half an hour, we headed towards Uluwatu. On the way, we asked the driver to stop by the Blue Point Bay Resort, as the photos posted by a friend on Ctrip were truly impressive. Apart from normal security checks, the hotel's security does not prevent sightseeing tourists like me. Thanks to Grandpa N for recommending this multi-leg tourism option.

Entering the hotel gate, it was easy to see through the lobby to the swimming pool built on the edge of the cliff and the glass church. A sense of transparency arose in my heart, and the beauty here is indeed commendable. Coming to Uluwatu, the biggest concern was the monkeys because we both dislike animals. However, our two experiences at Uluwatu told us that as long as protective measures are taken, the monkeys here won't bother people. From the entrance all the way inside, although the numerous monkeys affected my mood, when the cliff lines of Uluwatu stretched before my eyes, the ocean under the sunlight sparkled with white waves crashing onto the shore, my chest expanded to its widest point.

Some say the seafood dinners by the Jimbaran beach lack originality, while others claim the sunset over the Indian Ocean is spectacular. But I think having a seafood dinner by the sea is always a good choice, especially since it's not far from Uluwatu. The weather wasn't great, and the sun was hidden behind thick clouds until it got dark. But it didn't matter; the warm sea breeze, the vast coastline, the fragrant grilled fish, drinking two bottles of Bintang beer, the mood at that moment was truly serene and relaxed, something rarely experienced in daily life. When we arrived at Jimbaran, we let the hotel car go back because the restaurant would send guests back to the hotel if you stayed close to Jimbaran. Then, we soaked in the hotel's Center Stage listening to music; Hard Rock music always makes sense. What a perfect half-day!

The third day in Bali was simple and leisurely. In the morning, we basked in the sun by the pool, checked out, and spent the afternoon at a spa near the airport. Similarly, the spa provided transportation, saving us the cost of going from the hotel to the airport, haha. The spa itself wasn't particularly special, costing $30 for two hours. The first trip to Bali was pleasant, though it was regrettable not having time to visit Ubud and Kintamani. We all said we could come again, just casual talk, but unexpectedly, we returned so soon. In Bali, we're no longer rookies, so we quickly planned our itinerary, and the first stop was naturally Ubud. Tegal Sari was clearly less famous locally than it was online. The driver missed the turn initially, but fortunately, we noticed the hotel sign on the side of the road. We booked the car for the next day at the front desk and requested a TV, then strolled along Monkey Forest Road towards the center of Ubud. Indeed, there were many shops, and people were selling art pieces on the street. I guess the older I get, the less taste I have; Ubud's artistic atmosphere, admired by many friends, held no attraction for me, and I couldn't even finish visiting ten stores. However, unlike Kuta, wandering aimlessly and without expectations in Ubud made me feel good. The Night Market was lively, but just looking around once was enough. Frankly, we didn't even visit Ubud Palace or ARMA in Ubud, let alone museums. I'd rather get a massage, haha, I have no interest in Balinese history. Let's go back to the hotel and watch the World Cup instead. On the way back, we saw the Dirty Duck Dinner that everyone often mentioned, and we probably wouldn't have the chance to try it, but the lunch at Café Wayan in the afternoon was also good. At 10:45 the next day, we left the hotel and headed to Kintamani. Tegal Sari's car costs 60K per hour, and since our final destination was Nusa Dua, we added another one and a half hours for the return trip. This price isn't cheap, but the car was a new Toyota SUV, very comfortable, and we trusted the hotel's vehicle relatively more. The friendly Balinese people easily became familiar, chatting about the previous day's soccer game and talking about Tegal Sari's past. On the way, he took us to a coffee plantation, which was somewhat unique. Every vehicle entering had to open and close the gate, looking almost like a drug-dealing place. Saying it was a plantation, it was actually a valley with beautiful scenery and a fragrant aroma throughout the mountains. Tourists sat on chairs facing the valley, and the host served them a cup of coffee and cocoa each. The driver even helped bring some fruits I hadn't seen. Although it wasn't in our plan, we were happy to spend half an hour here and bought some coffee and cocoa to bring back as gifts for our colleagues. We soon arrived at Kintamani, and the charm of Lake Batur and Mount Batur delighted us, not letting down our feelings of coming to Bali again. However, the vendors there were particularly persistent, trying to make you buy things, which was a bit annoying. Since we had lunch a bit late, there weren't many people in the restaurant, so we picked a good seat by the window and invited the driver to eat and chat together. Only when I reached Kintamani did I realize that I should have chosen to stay there for a day, allowing myself to delve deeper into the shores of Lake Batur and climb a volcano, which is the kind of adventure I like. But it wasn't possible this time, and besides, the person beside me wasn't physically fit for such activities, so this idea remained a dream, haha. After dozing off all the way, we arrived at Nusa Dua. Although defeating Japan is an unwavering national policy, the hotel was truly beautiful. Just walking around the hotel passed our second day.

Most of the third day was spent by the sea and the swimming pool, and the results of the sun exposure that day are still visible now. At 4:30 pm, we went to Uluwatu again because we liked it there and wanted to see the sunset at Uluwatu. Following the old routine, we then went to Jimbaran beach for dinner. Since the restaurant we visited the first time was the first one on the beach, we easily found this familiar place. There were clearly more visitors in June than in February, and we were lucky to encounter a band. Due to the large number of guests, we listened to a lot of free songs. The band was sincere, with one of the five members playing the cello, their attitude worthy of affirmation, obviously not just fooling around. On the fourth day, after waking up, we sat on the balcony, looked at the sea, and let our minds wander. I like these days accompanied by the sound of the waves. At noon, we left again. Another holiday ended, meaning we would start a brief separation again. These years, we have lived in different places. At Pudong Airport and Changi Airport, one of us always stays while the other flies away. Even in vacation spots like Hong Kong and Bangkok, we always take different flights to leave separately. I've talked about many reasons why I like Bali, but there's one more thing I haven't mentioned: in Bali, we come together happily and leave together, only in Bali do we not have to say goodbye.
