Why Tencent and Chery are fighting for the QQ trademark: neither can afford to lose

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 14:15:52

Does Tencent produce cars? Although the answer is negative, as early as 2005, the company applied for the registration of the "QQ" trademark on Category 12 goods including automobiles, and was approved in 2008. However, Chery Automobile Co., Ltd., which produces cars, did not agree. In 2009, Chery filed a request to cancel Tencent's automobile-related trademarks with the Trademark Review and Adjudication Board (TRAB) on grounds such as identical or similar trademarks on similar goods, and received support. Subsequently, Tencent sued the TRAB of the State Administration for Industry and Commerce. Yesterday, the Beijing No. 1 Intermediate People's Court heard the case, where plaintiff Tencent, defendant TRAB, and third party Chery faced each other in court.

It is well known that in China, two enterprises are associated with “QQ”: one is Tencent Technology, and the other is Chery Automobile, both belonging to different fields. Tencent has secured its place in the internet sector, while Chery's QQ car sales have accumulated to over a million units. In terms of the usage timeline of "QQ", Tencent launched the instant messaging software QQ in 1999, while Chery introduced the microcar Chery QQ in 2003. The question of who should own the rights to "QQ" has been a point of contention between these two companies for nearly 10 years.

On May 19, 2005, Tencent applied to register the "QQ" trademark under Category 12 goods including automobiles, and was granted registration on March 7, 2008. On November 26, 2009, Chery requested the cancellation of this trademark from the TRAB on grounds such as violation of the Trademark Law by replicating famous trademarks, identical or similar trademarks on similar goods, and infringement of prior rights of others. After deliberation, the TRAB ruled that the registration of the contested trademark constituted the preemptive registration of another person's already used and influential trademark as stipulated in Article 31 of the Trademark Law, and thus revoked Tencent's registered trademark.

Tencent argued that the disputed ruling made by the TRAB contained obvious errors and omissions, stating that the contested trademark did not violate any laws and should be maintained. Its representative explained that on August 31, 2001, Tencent had applied to register the "QQ" trademark under Category 38 services including "information transmission, computer terminal communication". Through extensive promotion, the number of registered users of QQ communications grew from 5 million in 2000 to over 200 million in 2003. Therefore, before Chery Automobile used the "QQ" trademark, Tencent's "QQ" trademark had already become a well-known trademark. In 2009, Tencent obtained the certificate of qualification for a well-known trademark from relevant national departments, and any other company using the "QQ" trademark would constitute an infringement.

Chery Automobile, however, believed that Tencent’s registration of the "QQ" trademark in the automotive field was malicious. As the third party in this case, Chery's representative pointed out that the trademark Tencent registered in 2001 was not the "QQ" letters but a graphic. Chery Automobile, on the other hand, registered the "QQ" letter trademark in the automotive field in 2003. Thus, the two companies used their respective trademarks in different fields. Chery Automobile's "QQ" trademark in the automotive field preceded Tencent's, and Chery QQ has sold over a million units, with advertising expenses amounting to more than 200 million yuan, deserving protection.

Furthermore, Tencent argued that its registration of the "QQ" trademark across multiple fields was a protective registration for "QQ". However, Chery Automobile contended that from the initial planning of the car name to later promotional campaigns, it was all based on the already well-known "QQ" trademark and penguin mascot to achieve promotional effects, constituting an infringement that cannot generate legitimate prior rights.

The court will announce the verdict at a later date. For both Tencent and Chery, having operated the "QQ" trademark for nearly 10 years, neither can afford to lose this lawsuit.

Is there a third way in the QQ dispute?

Tencent QQ is the first domestic instant messaging software launched by Tencent in 1999, and Chery QQ is a microcar launched by Chery in July 2003. Both "QQ"s enjoy high recognition in their respective fields.

Upon searching, it was found that as early as when the "QQ" car was introduced in 2003, Tencent had raised a trademark infringement dispute with Chery. Tencent claimed that due to its busy schedule preparing for its IPO, it did not file a public lawsuit. Later, Tencent sent a lawyer's letter to Chery, but Chery never gave Tencent a substantive response.

At the end of 2005, news about the legal battle over the QQ trademark appeared in the media again, but no further developments were seen in this case, with some media even labeling it as hype.

This time, the trademark dispute between the two parties was brought to court with Tencent as the plaintiff, TRAB as the defendant, and Chery as the third party. Trademarks are the signboards for enterprises to stand firm in the market. For Tencent and Chery, who have cultivated their brands for around ten years, neither can afford to lose. Some people believe that in this lawsuit, whoever is identified as infringing will face huge compensation. Is there only a win-lose situation in this trademark dispute, or is there a third way? Tang Guangliang, an intellectual property expert at the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences, told reporters that Tencent and Chery could fully collaborate to achieve a win-win situation.

Tang Guangliang said that the function of a trademark is to attract consumer attention, serving as a link between merchants and consumers, and also as the image of merchants in the minds of consumers. A trademark itself is a means rather than an end, and how to use this means depends on each merchant's consideration. In recent years, China's trademark protection has become increasingly mature. Whether or not there is infringement does not solely depend on the similarity of two marks; the key is whether consumers will be confused. If they are not confused, then the use by two or more merchants can expand the influence of the trademark, benefiting all users of the trademark.

"For example, with the 'QQ' trademark, if 'consumers buy Chery's QQ car because they think it is produced by Tencent,' or 'consumers use the QQ chat tool because they like Chery QQ cars,' then confusion exists," Tang Guangliang said. If the above situations do not occur, then both sides do not need to fight over it. Because regarding trademarks, it is not simply who says it is theirs that makes it so, and even if it is theirs, it does not mean they must eliminate every competitor. As long as the trademark is not maliciously defamed or causes loss of market share, it might even have a promoting effect, and both sides can coexist peacefully and share benefits.

Moreover, Tang Guangliang said that from a rational market perspective, Tencent and Chery do not conflict. Chat tools mainly target young people, and QQ cars are also widely loved by young people. Both sides can fully collaborate, operate together, share the market, and achieve a win-win situation, completely unnecessary to fight over trademark ownership without damage.
