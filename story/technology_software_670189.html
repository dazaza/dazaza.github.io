Penetrating the Fog of Housing Prices, Understanding China's Economy: Researcher Zuoppei from the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences Explains the Latest Economic Situation

Houses are a hot topic in China, garnering attention across all social strata.

Recently, researcher Zuoppei from the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences discussed several economic events during a lecture at Beijing Forestry University, including rising prices, housing issues, and the phenomenon of Wenzhou bosses fleeing.

In Mr. Zuoppei's lecture, a significant portion was dedicated to analyzing housing prices with particular clarity. Regarding the current trends in real estate and changes in housing prices, Mr. Zuo’s insights provide readers with an entirely new perspective.

Introduction: Three Phenomena Everyone is Watching Recently

There have been a few economic phenomena recently that have caught the public's attention.

Firstly, inflation continues, but according to official statistics, the rate may have slightly decreased compared to the previous months, though price increases remain evident.

Six years ago, the Consumer Price Index (CPI) was very low, around zero, indicating no inflation at the time. Now, we do have inflation, and it's quite apparent.

The second issue is housing prices. I gave a report early in 2010 and conducted specific research in 2007. At that time, I stated that China's housing prices were abnormal, simply put, they shouldn't be this high. Why did they get so high? It's due to factors like credit.

Currently, housing prices can basically be considered stagnant and show signs of decline, which validates my past assertion: as long as the government is determined to control money and credit, housing prices cannot avoid falling.

The third issue is the so-called phenomenon of entrepreneurs fleeing, referring to Wenzhou business owners running away. Using Western standard terminology, these entrepreneurs are debtors who borrowed money but defaulted on their loans without repayment, breaching agreements severely enough to make creditors lose patience, leading them to flee.

Excessive Credit Expansion Leading to Economic Overheating

Why do these phenomena occur simultaneously? Simply put, the entire economy is overheated, primarily caused by excessive expansion of monetary and credit systems.

Excessive expansion of monetary and credit leads to excessive demand. Companies produce based on demand, and overproduction occurs when production exceeds potential capacity. This kind of excessive expansion directly results in price hikes in a market economy.

Due to rising prices, there is a counteraction from the monetary and credit authorities responsible for policy-making. What is this counteraction? It's monetary contraction.

The effects of contraction have already begun to show: housing prices are not increasing anymore and even starting to fall. However, a large number of companies that expanded excessively find themselves in trouble. During the economic overheating period, they were borrowing heavily, believing that borrowing now would lead to big profits later. Now, they suddenly realize that the economy is contracting, and market conditions are worse than originally estimated.

Simply put, economic overheating leads to price increases, which in turn prompts the government to implement a tightening policy on monetary and credit institutions.

In general economics, inflation has a certain inertia. Like braking, even if you apply sudden brakes, people still move forward; it doesn’t stop immediately. Therefore, we still see rising prices, but the tightening policy has started to take effect.

Under this effect, housing prices begin to fall, but many entrepreneurs complain bitterly because their capital chains have broken.

Cause Analysis: Excessive Total Demand, Monetary Growth is the Main Reason

These three phenomena represent three aspects of macroeconomic dynamic fluctuations. Our country currently has excessive total demand, leading to excessive output. The relationship between these two can be explained in one sentence: we are now a market economy, and enterprises produce based on sales demand. Therefore, the production of enterprises and the macroeconomic production overall are too large, exceeding our country's current production capacity.

China experienced its first obvious inflation in ten years in 2007, with economic growth reaching 14%. According to our estimates, this growth significantly exceeded the country's production potential in 2007. Thus, there was a noticeable wave of inflation from the end of 2007 to 2008.

Of course, the average annual growth rate in recent years in our country has been about 9.7%, fluctuating each year, mainly due to demand factors. The most significant factor influencing demand in this context is undoubtedly monetary credit, specifically monetary growth.

From 1997 to 2002, our M2 (the definition of money supply. M1: M1 = commercial bank current deposits, referred to as narrow money supply. M2: M2 = M1 + fixed deposits and savings deposits, referred to as broad money supply.) increased every year, with the lowest being a 12% increase per year, and the highest being 13% or 14%. There was no inflation during that time; instead, discussions revolved around deflation, with price levels decreasing, and most items becoming cheaper. According to statistical data, the CPI growth rate during those years was negative.

Credit Issues: Changes in the Quantity of Money Basically Synchronize with Loans

Speaking of housing prices, there was no crazy rise in housing prices in previous years. At that time, commercial housing began to be sold, but sales were slow, resulting in a large number of unfinished buildings.

When did China's housing prices start to rise and continue to accelerate? In 2003. What does this indicate? The amount of money indeed increased, and so did M2.

After 2003, China's M2 growth rate significantly increased, with an annual growth rate consistently above 16%, whereas before it was below 15%, reaching a maximum of 14%. After 2003, the rates climbed to 16%, 17%, 18%, and even 19%. As a result, since then, our growth rate has been over 10% annually, reaching 14% in 2007.

Price levels and inflation rates gradually rose. First, price levels transitioned from declining to stopping the decline, then to an annual increase of 1%, finally reaching 3-4% in 2007, with a peak month in 2008 seeing a 10% increase. Thus, the driving force of money is very clear.

The quantity of money is almost closely tied to loan amounts. M2 roughly equals loans plus foreign exchange reserves, so while M2 and loans are not equal, they generally move in sync, especially in terms of percentage growth.

After 2008: Too Many Loans

By the end of 2007, inflation was already evident, and the economy was overheating. The government implemented a tight monetary policy, which mainly manifested in restricting loans.

By November 2008, compared to the previous year, loans only increased by 15%. The result of this 15% increase in loans led to our M2 growing very slowly, indicating that this tightening policy was effective in curbing the price and housing price increases seen in 2007.

In 2008, due to the U.S. financial crisis, there was widespread panic in the West. Under such panic, total demand changed. This total demand change is simple: everyone didn't want to spend money, especially no one wanted to borrow.

All those who could act as creditors became unstable; people stopped depositing money in banks, banks stopped lending to each other, and banks stopped issuing loans, causing societal demand to change. Naturally, Western countries were extremely panicked. We know that President Bush started bailing out markets then, and China also introduced an economic stimulus package.

Due to the Western financial crisis in 2008, China's exports were indeed hit hard, prompting the government to change its policies. The tight monetary policy from the end of 2007 turned into a major expansionary policy by the end of 2008. This major expansion was reflected in both currency and credit.

In 2009 alone, China's loan balance, the total amount of loans outstanding (which refers to loans already issued but not yet repaid), increased by 30%.

As a result, with the increase in loans, M2 also had to grow. M2 increased by 27%. From 2003 to 2008, our M2 increased relatively more, eventually leading to the price hikes in 2007, which were around 18-19% per year. However, in 2009, M2 grew by approximately 27%. The reason? Loans increased by 30% in one year.

In other words, by the end of 2008, the total loans in our country amounted to 30 trillion yuan, which quickly rose to 40 trillion yuan, an increase of 10 trillion yuan.

In just one year, newly issued loans reached 10 trillion yuan. Consequently, many people now criticize us, saying that our current inflation is due to this. But I disagree; the problem in China is not the 4 trillion yuan but rather the increased credit, which is 10 trillion yuan.

At the beginning of 2009, housing prices hadn't risen yet, but by the end of 2009, they skyrocketed. Why? Because of those 10 trillion yuan — it was these 10 trillion yuan that caused the surge.

House Problems: 10 Trillion Yuan in Loans Pulled Up Housing Prices

In those two years, the sales of commercial housing in China were only around 3 to 4 trillion yuan. Imagine what you could buy with 10 trillion yuan in loans.

In 2009, everyone was criticizing the sharp rise in housing prices, but actually, the increase was still relatively low. If all that 10 trillion yuan were used to buy houses, the price hike wouldn't be what it is today — we don't know how much higher it would have gone.

Professor Li Daokui from Tsinghua University said that the 10 trillion yuan could sustain us for five years because it has momentum. It's simple: it doesn't take effect immediately. Many people borrowed money, bought houses, and didn't do anything else afterward. Gradually, this money circulated and drove up prices, taking about one to two years to manifest, right around 2011.

This 10 trillion yuan pulled up housing prices, which is why we all said at the end of 2009 that housing prices surged dramatically. All developers came out claiming it was due to rigid demand, asserting that housing prices would only rise and never fall — this is deceiving those who don't understand.

Thus, when I gave a report at the beginning of 2010, I told them: if in the next two years, China's annual credit growth remains at 15%, continuously for two years, housing prices will definitely plummet sharply, because the data supports this.

Deep Reasons: 2011's Inflation Was Caused by Too Much Lending

When I was talking about this at the beginning of 2010, I already knew that the government had set a rough budget for the credit growth in 2010, which was about 18%. You should know that 18% is a very high number.

From 2003 to 2007, our annual growth was about 17% to 18%. Then, in 2009, we had a 30% credit growth, and in 2010, we still had 18%. These two combined meant that China's demand expansion, monetary demand expansion, couldn't be contained.

Therefore, the price increase in 2011 and the inflation were inevitable. It was simply due to too much money and too much lending.

So, what did this set of policies lead to? To put it simply, the initial intention of this policy was good, given the Western financial crisis, aiming to save the market and prevent a decline in the Chinese economy.

We mentioned that the economy indeed declined. If the growth rate drops too low, unemployment will rise, making society unstable, necessitating market intervention. However, the actions were too hasty and crude, going too far, like stepping on the gas pedal too hard.

To Respond to the Crisis, an Appropriate Growth Rate Was Needed

But by 2010, the situation had already been salvaged, yet the policy didn't stop, continuing with 18% credit and monetary expansion. What were the consequences?

Firstly, housing prices soared; secondly, the original intention wasn't necessarily to drive up housing prices but to boost the entire economy, meaning to raise GDP growth rates and economic growth rates.

Our economic growth rate was around 14% in 2007. Due to the tightening in 2008 and the impact of the financial crisis, it dropped to 9% in both 2008 and 2009.

Under such circumstances, in 2010, we still didn't hold back, resulting in a growth rate of around 10%, surpassing our 9.5%.

If our growth rate in 2011 exceeds 9%, we might enter a genuine phase of expansion.

After the economic growth rate reached double digits in 2010 (of course, driven by monetary demand), inflation began, leading to price increases.

Monetary Shifts: Certain Measures Taken Years Ago Might Have Been Misled by Americans

Although the trend of rising prices has passed, prices are still increasing.

To sum up based on China's past experience, to truly curb this wave of inflation, I believe moderate tightening is still necessary. What needs to be tightened? The target for 2012 should be brought down to 15%, and if it goes a bit lower, I estimate this expansion can be halted.

Looking back now, it might seem like hindsight, but at least allowing credit to grow by 30% in late 2008 was somewhat excessive.

I recently read a report. In late 2008, we attended a G20 summit where everyone jointly decided to rescue the market, saving both China and the United States. Some people were still promoting this.

In reality, the situation faced by China at the time was very different from that of the United States. When the financial crisis erupted in the U.S., it wasn't experiencing economic overheating; the economy was basically stagnant, whereas ours was somewhat overheated.

Inflation in the U.S. was almost non-existent, whereas China already had clear signs of inflation in 2007. Therefore, the situation in China was different from that in the U.S.

The U.S. needed to rescue the market because its entire financial system collapsed during that crisis.

China's financial system remained largely intact. Did it really need such a massive rescue? So, I suspect we might have been misled by the Americans.
