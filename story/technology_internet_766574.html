Revealed: The True Mission of Apple's iPhone 5C Launch

by anonymous on 2013-09-18 22:00:10

The iPhone 5C is now caught in the "wolf in sheep's clothing" skepticism. Previously, analysts believed that Apple’s release of a cheaper iPhone was aimed at reclaiming the smartphone market below $300. However, on the day of the 5C's launch, its price exceeded $500, leading analysts to speculate about its true intention. What is Apple's real purpose behind releasing the iPhone 5C? Let's uncover it together!

If you want to understand what Apple is up to? What is the true purpose of launching the iPhone 5C? Why does this make sense? Then the following two reasons are key:

1. Apple did not release a new iPod Touch this time.

One of Apple's regular actions every September is to release a new iPod, but this year there was none. The iPod is still available for sale on various online stores, but this product will soon come to an end.

In July, Apple reported that iPod sales were declining at an annual rate of 32%, which was significant for Apple because the iPod had once been one of its best-selling products. In 2011, the sales volume of the iPod Touch could rival that of the iPad.

The iPod Touch is very popular among children and young people, who can enjoy the benefits of iPhone apps via Wi-Fi. This explains why Apple sold 80 million iPod Touch units from 2007 to 2012, during which time it also sold 244 million iPhones. Although not the main source of revenue, the sales of the former should not be underestimated.

The entry-level iPod Touch has never been lower than $199, the same as the two-year contract price for the iPhone, with the only difference being that the former does not require a monthly payment of $70 for phone service.

2. American children are flocking towards smartphones.

In the past year, U.S. wireless carriers have started introducing family data plans, making it easier for parents to buy smartphones for their children. A couple can add a data plan for their child by paying an additional $10 (T-Mobile), $20 (Sprint), $35 (AT&T), or $40 (Verizon).

Thus, from 2011 to now, the percentage of teenagers owning smartphones has grown from 23% to 37%. According to the Zact 2013 Mobile Families survey, 44% of American children aged 12 to 17 were using smartphones in 2013. It is predicted that this number will grow to 51% in 2014, 59% in 2015, 66% in 2016, and 73% in 2017.

This means there could be 40 to 50 million children customers of the iPod Touch in the U.S., and this group is very likely to shift to smartphones, presenting a huge market opportunity, especially in the increasingly saturated markets of the U.S. and Europe.

Besides, these children have already invested significantly in music and apps, giving Apple a natural advantage in transitioning them to the iPhone.

Therefore, the colorful iPhone is designed to meet young people's need for "self-expression." So stop obsessing over whether Apple has created a cheap iPhone; it may not even care, as it is simply using it to expand its market share and reclaim the market previously held by the iPod Touch.
