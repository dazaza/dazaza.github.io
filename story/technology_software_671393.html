The commonly used carrier frequency for infrared remote control is 38kHz, which is determined by the 455kHz ceramic resonator used at the transmitting end. At the transmitting end, the crystal oscillator needs to be divided into integer frequencies, and the frequency division factor is generally taken as 12, so 455kHz / 12 = 37.9 kHz ≈ 38kHz. Some remote control systems also use 36kHz, 40kHz, 56kHz, etc., usually determined by the oscillation frequency of the crystal oscillator at the transmitting end. The characteristics of infrared remote control are that it does not affect the surrounding environment and does not interfere with other electrical appliances. Since it cannot penetrate walls, household appliances in different rooms can use a universal remote control without interfering with each other; circuit debugging is simple, generally no debugging is required if the given circuit is connected correctly; encoding and decoding are easy, multi-channel remote control can be performed.

In a multi-channel control infrared remote control system, the infrared transmission part usually has many buttons representing different control functions. When a button is pressed at the transmitting end, there will be different output states at the receiving end accordingly.

Due to the production of a large number of infrared remote control-specific integrated circuits by various manufacturers, one can simply follow the instructions when needed. Therefore, infrared remote control has been widely applied in household appliances and indoor short-distance (less than 10 meters) remote control.

The output status at the receiving end can roughly be divided into five forms: pulse, level, latch, interlock, and data. "Pulse" output refers to the situation where, when pressing a button on the transmitting end, the corresponding output end at the receiving end outputs an "effective pulse", which usually lasts around 100ms. "Level" output means that when a button is pressed at the transmitting end, the corresponding output end at the receiving end outputs an "effective level", and when the button is released, the "effective level" disappears. Here, the "effective pulse" and "effective level" could either be high or low, depending on the static condition of the corresponding output pin. If the static state is low, then "high" is effective; if the static state is high, then "low" is effective. In most cases, "high" is effective. "Latch" output means that every time a certain key is pressed at the transmitting end, the corresponding output end at the receiving end changes its state once, i.e., from high level to low level or vice versa. This kind of output is suitable for power switch, mute control, etc. Sometimes this output form is also called "inverted phase". "Interlock" output means multiple outputs clear each other, and only one output is valid at the same time. Channel selection on TV falls into this category, as well as dimming, speed adjustment, audio input selection, etc.

"Data" output refers to assigning numbers to some transmission keys, using several outputs at the receiving end to form a binary number to represent different key inputs.

Under normal circumstances, in addition to a few data outputs, there should also be a "data valid" output end at the receiving end to fetch data in time. This kind of output form is generally used for interfacing with microcontrollers or computers.

In addition to the above output forms, there are also "latch" and "temporary storage" forms. "Latch" output means that for each signal sent by the transmitting end, the corresponding output at the receiving end is "stored" until a new signal is received; "temporary storage" output is similar to the aforementioned "level" output.

1. Transmission Power: Greater transmission power results in longer distance, but consumes more electricity and easily generates interference.

2. Reception Sensitivity: Higher reception sensitivity increases the remote control distance, but is prone to interference causing misoperation or loss of control.

3. Antenna: Using straight-line antennas and keeping them parallel extends the remote control distance, but occupies more space. In practice, extending and straightening the antenna can increase the remote control distance.

4. Height: Higher antennas result in greater remote control distance, but are limited by objective conditions.

5. Obstruction: Currently used wireless remote controllers operate in the UHF band specified by the country, whose propagation characteristics are similar to light, with straight-line propagation and little diffraction. Walls between the transmitter and receiver will significantly reduce the remote control distance. If the wall is made of reinforced concrete, due to the conductor's absorption of electromagnetic waves, the impact will be even more severe.
