He would rather go to the forest than go to school; he loves nature but dislikes discipline. His brothers are already engaged, but he only thinks about how to complete the task of collecting waterfowl eggs. His knowledge of animals is much richer than his knowledge of people. He believes that in one issue we value most - the issue of love - we fall short of animals. He sees that when the female nightingale is incubating her eggs, the male nightingale stays by her side all night, singing for his dear wife: "Dit dit! Zhi zhi! Ge ge - li!" Such things, Bill would never do, or even think of doing. When the stork mother sleeps with her children in the nest, the stork father stands on one leg all night on the roof. Bill couldn't stand like that for even an hour.

One day, while studying the contents of a spider's web, he suddenly completely abandoned the idea of getting married. The male spider busily weaves his web to catch careless flies - young, old, fat, and thin flies. He lives to weave his web and support his family, but the female spider lives solely for her husband. She eats him up out of love for him - she eats his heart, his head, and his belly. Only his long, thin legs remain in the web as a memorial to his efforts to provide for his family. This is the absolute truth that Bill learned from natural history. Bill witnessed this event and studied the issue. "To be so loved by one's own wife, to be eaten up by her in the heat of passionate love. No, no human could ever love to such an extent, but is such love worth it?"

Bill decided to never marry! He didn't even want to kiss or be kissed, because kissing might be the first step toward marriage. But he did receive a kiss - a kiss that all of us will receive eventually: the firm kiss of death. After we have lived long enough, death receives the command: "Kiss him to death!" And then a person dies. God sends forth a ray of intense sunlight, blinding the eyes. The soul, which arrives like a meteor and departs like a meteor, no longer rests in a flower or dreams under the petals of a lotus. It has more important things to do - it flies to the eternal kingdom. However, no one can describe what this kingdom looks like. No one has been inside to see it, not even the stork, who can see far and knows many things. Even the stork knows little about Bill, although he understands Bert and Betto well. But we've heard enough about them, I think you agree. So this time, I said to the stork: "Thank you." But he demanded payment for this ordinary little story - three frogs and a small snake, because he prefers food as payment. Would you give it to him?

I wouldn't. I don't have any frogs or snakes.
