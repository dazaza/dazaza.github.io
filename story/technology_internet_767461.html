Microsoft is accelerating the pace of product upgrades, but enterprises will stick to using Win7.

by anonymous on 2013-10-15 21:38:54

Market research company Gartner anticipates that, for many years to come, corporate users will continue to stick with Windows 7 and Office 2010. Despite Microsoft accelerating the upgrade pace of its operating systems and office suites, corporate users are unlikely to follow suit.

Gartner analyst Michael Silver, while hosting Gartner's IT Symposium in Orlando, Florida last week, told the press: "Enterprises are very likely to stay on Windows 7 for as long as possible."

Silver based his prediction on the tactics enterprise users have adopted in response to Microsoft's accelerated release schedule for Windows and the transition to the new user interface known as "Modern" or "Metro." He said, "In one to two years, Microsoft will release another version of Windows 8 with an updated version of IE. Meanwhile, the Windows desktop won't change significantly and can essentially support a long period of time."

Silver displayed a slide during the conference showing that enterprises are adopting a "steady-as-she-goes" approach to deal with Microsoft's accelerated software release cycle, sticking with the mature and stable Windows 7. He stated at the conference that the combination of Windows 7 and Office 2010 could be used for ten years, which is considered a standard lifecycle.

Microsoft has committed to providing ten years of support for Windows 7. It will continue to offer both security and non-security patches until January 13, 2015, referred to as "mainstream support." Afterward, Microsoft will provide "extended support" until January 14, 2020.

Similarly, extended support for Office 2010 will continue until October 13, 2020. This means that Microsoft will still provide over six years of support for the Windows 7 and Office 2010 combination. Therefore, corporate users can confidently continue using them.

However, this strategy requires enterprises to migrate their data to the new system before the old system retires. Hence, Gartner advises corporate users to begin migrating to Windows 7 by the end of 2017 and to Office 2010 by the end of 2018.

Silver mentioned that if corporations do not stick with Windows 7, another option would be to keep up with Microsoft's product release rhythm, constantly upgrading to new systems. This would require more frequent hardware upgrades, testing of new software and applications, and deploying the latest operating system within the next 12 months. Moreover, this process would need to endlessly repeat.

Corporate users must prepare on both fronts—testing and patching the latest operating system while also deploying previous versions. When Microsoft released Windows 8.1 this week, the testing/patching phase began. However, most analysis firms and analysts, including Gartner, expect Microsoft to release Windows 8.2 within the next year.

Successful Strategy—Steady-as-She-Goes

The deployment window for Windows 8.1 is 24 months, primarily due to Microsoft's decision to mimic its former support policy for service packs and patches during the lifecycle of the operating system.

A marketing manager at Microsoft confirmed in July: "The General Availability release of the Windows 8.1 upgrade will be supported throughout the lifecycle of Windows 8. Afterwards, Windows 8 customers will have two years to migrate to Windows 8.1."

Silver remarked that such an upgrade rhythm is unacceptable for most enterprises.

When discussing the challenges enterprises face in response to Microsoft's changes, Silver said, "Accelerating the upgrade pace is undoubtedly the biggest issue. With faster OS release cycles, corporate users are uncertain whether their applications will work on newer versions of Windows and IE."

Silver added that on average, each enterprise application has 10 to 20 users. For large enterprises, many business-critical applications have hundreds of users. Ensuring these applications run smoothly on every new version of Windows and IE consumes significant financial resources and time, almost impossible to achieve within 24 months.

However, Microsoft doesn't see it that way. Outgoing CEO Steve Ballmer, during an interview at Gartner's symposium, claimed that corporate concerns about Microsoft's faster software update schedule were unwarranted.

Gartner analyst David Cearley pointed out, "Corporate users are now very concerned about Microsoft's accelerated new software release cycle." Ballmer shook his head and said, "Not necessarily. If our customers buy software from us, install it, and use it locally, you'd tell us 'Don't update the software too frequently; updating every two to three years is best.' But if we provide a service, like Office 365, customers will tell us 'We want the service we use to always be up-to-date.'"

Silver found Ballmer's explanation amusing but lamented his inability to recognize the issues enterprises face in adapting to Windows' accelerated update schedule. Silver said, "Ballmer believes that as long as Microsoft puts everything online, users won't have any problems. To a large extent, Microsoft thinks that isn't an issue."

Silver pointed out that this does not align with reality. Although Windows software no longer needs to be sold on DVDs, it is not a service like Office 365. Silver said, "Enterprise users will certainly worry about the future. Once they get on Microsoft's train, Microsoft will take them where it wants to go, or worse, lead them to a crash."
