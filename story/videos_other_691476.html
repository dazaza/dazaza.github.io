Zynga Builds Platform to Shake Off Facebook Dependency: Third Parties Can Move In

by xue23awsh on 2012-03-05 16:20:07

Introduction: Through its partnership with Facebook, Zynga has become the leader in the social gaming sector. However, due to the risks inherent in this model, Zynga decided to independently build its own platform, not only to promote its games but also to assist competitors in their development, aiming for mutual benefit within the industry.

The following is a full translation of the article:

Backup Plan

In the early months of 2010, Zynga's engineering team halted all new game developments. As the developer of popular social games such as "FarmVille," this San Francisco-based startup was engaged in tense negotiations with Facebook. Then and now, Facebook provided the platform and vast audience for Zynga’s games. Zynga CEO Mark Pincus realized he needed a backup plan in case the negotiations failed. He ordered his engineers to start developing their own website.

"Everyone was working day and night back then," Pincus recalled. "It was an incredible engineering feat. We quickly became ready to host our games ourselves." But the website was never launched. Tensions eased, and both Facebook and Zynga recognized their mutually beneficial relationship, leading to a five-year contract.

Two years later, Zynga is now a public company, and Facebook is about to go public. (Their collaboration has become one of the most profitable in the internet industry: Facebook disclosed in its prospectus that Zynga contributed 12% of its revenue.) But on March 1st, Zynga finally unveiled the long-planned website under the domain Zynga.com, which used to be just an informational site. Going forward, it will not only offer Zynga's games but also host products from competitors. Other companies can use the service to release their games and promote them to Zynga players, but in exchange, they must pay Zynga a portion of their income.

Development Plan

Zynga aims to become a platform for other businesses to develop on, similar to Facebook, Twitter, and other tech companies. "We want to grow the market for everyone. Our vision is to have a billion people playing games together," said Pincus.

On Zynga.com, players can log in using their Facebook accounts and make purchases through Facebook's payment system for virtual tractors and crops. Pincus says using Facebook's tools significantly lowers the barrier to entry for online gaming. However, he admits the integration has never been perfect—Zynga estimates that about one-third of Facebook's social gamers are casual players who get annoyed when friends talk endlessly about cows in "FarmVille." This is one reason why Zynga decided to branch out on its own.

The site will begin testing later this month, featuring highlighted games such as "CityVille," "Zynga Poker," and "Words With Friends." When a player selects a game, the interface nearly fills the entire browser. On one side of the screen, the zFriends feed continuously displays messages related to other players' gaming activities. While still relying on Facebook's platform to identify friend relationships, Zynga has developed its mechanism to pair strangers who enjoy the same game and spend similar amounts of time playing.

In addition to the new website, Zynga Platform will also host games for other companies. Analyst Michael Pachter from Wedbush Securities believes this indicates "Zynga acknowledges they cannot monopolize innovation." The biggest draw for developers is access to 240 million players who play Zynga games at least once a month.

In return, Zynga will take a cut of the revenue, though the exact percentage has not been disclosed. An informed source revealed that after Facebook takes its 30% cut from the payment system, Zynga will take 30% of the remainder. In other words, original developers will only receive 49% of the revenue.

External Recognition

Paying commissions to two companies might seem excessive, but at least one developer approves of the model. San Diego-based game studio Mob Science plans to launch a game called "King World" on Zynga Platform this spring. In this game, players create a character and save the queen. "Relying entirely on Facebook is risky," said Mob Science CEO Michael Witz. "We need significant funds to attract users, and it's challenging to gain widespread adoption on Facebook. Instead, we're willing to invest time creating the game and let Zynga leverage their extensive experience to drive its success."

In recent years, Zynga has spent hundreds of millions of dollars building its data centers to reduce reliance on Amazon's cloud computing services. These new servers allow Zynga to provide more flexible and comprehensive services to other game developers. Later this year, the company plans to launch an analytics console to help game developers understand which features enhance or detract from game performance. Zynga executives also stated that the company may one day serve as consultants for small game developers.

"Many services we previously provided internally to our game studios will now be offered externally," said Zynga CTO Cadir Lee. "We hope to improve all games. Even large companies face technical challenges during growth." Electronic Arts (EA) is an example; despite owning the most popular online game "The Sims Online," it has experienced multiple server outages.

Pincus and his company have not discussed potential collaborations with competitor EA, but they believe they possess the best skills in the social gaming field. If they can train smaller enterprises, their capabilities will increase significantly. However, many developers remain cautious about Zynga's specific services and pricing. "Everyone we've spoken to is willing to participate to some extent, but they need to see the final product we offer," said Pincus.
