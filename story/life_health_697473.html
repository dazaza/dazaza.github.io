The causes of Type 1 diabetes include:

Autoimmune system defects: Multiple autoimmune antibodies can be detected in the blood of Type 1 diabetes patients, such as glutamic acid decarboxylase antibody (GAD antibody) and islet cell antibody (ICA antibody). These abnormal autoantibodies can damage the B cells in the human pancreas responsible for secreting insulin, preventing them from functioning normally.

Genetic factors: Current research suggests that genetic defects are the basis for the development of Type 1 diabetes. This genetic defect manifests as abnormalities in the HLA antigens on the sixth pair of chromosomes. Scientific research indicates that Type 1 diabetes has a familial pattern— if your parents have diabetes, you are more likely to develop the disease compared to those without such a family history.

Other factors: Such as milk, oxygen free radicals, some rat poisons, etc. Scientists are still studying whether these factors can cause diabetes.

Viral infections may be triggers: Surprisingly, many scientists suspect viruses can also cause Type 1 diabetes. This is because Type 1 diabetes patients often experience viral infections before the onset of the disease, and outbreaks of Type 1 diabetes often follow viral epidemics. Viruses, such as those causing mumps and rubella, as well as the Coxsackie virus family that causes polio, can play a role in Type 1 diabetes.
