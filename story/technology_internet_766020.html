Gossip Bar: A New Role for Lei Jun in the Xiaomi Story?

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 14:10:09

Over the weekend, I met with a friend from the U.S., and he asked me which companies in China's tech sector are currently the most innovative. I gave him a few examples: in the internet space, I mentioned Alibaba, Tencent, and 360; in telecommunications, I cited Huawei and ZTE. These companies were familiar to him as he had heard of them in the U.S., and we exchanged our views on each. What surprised me was that he eventually asked, "What kind of company is Xiaomi? Is it the most innovative company in China?"

I countered by asking, "Why would you ask about Xiaomi?" He replied that he had seen reports about Xiaomi on multiple American tech blogs, so he wanted to learn more. After further investigation, it turned out that the gossip reported by U.S. tech blogs caught his attention — specifically, Hugo Barra, Google's Vice President of Android Product Management, who resigned angrily after learning that his girlfriend, Amanda Rosenberg (currently the marketing lead for Google Glass), was involved with Sergey Brin, one of Google's co-founders. Coincidentally, Barra's next move was to join Xiaomi in China. The U.S. tech blogs reported that Xiaomi had surpassed Apple in smartphone market share in China and was leading a revolution in China’s mobile phone industry.

I expressed my opinions to this friend. First, Xiaomi indeed stands out in China's smartphone sector, carving its own niche, but within the vast Chinese mobile phone market, Xiaomi belongs to the third tier, and labeling it as revolutionary might be an exaggeration. Second, Xiaomi's innovation is primarily in its marketing model, lacking foundational innovation and certainly not leading the innovation in China's mobile internet. Third, Xiaomi's model thrives under the unique market conditions in China, and its largest potential market remains within the Asian Chinese-speaking market circle, making it difficult for Xiaomi to expand beyond Asia.

After returning home, I reviewed recent reports on this matter from both American tech blogs and domestic Chinese media. Abroad, the coverage was almost entirely focused on gossip, casually mentioning Xiaomi as Barra's next destination. In contrast, domestic reporting was uniformly celebratory, highlighting Xiaomi as the big winner, attracting the so-called Google vice president. Some media even claimed that Barra's joining Xiaomi symbolized the ambition of China's smartphone industry. It seemed that Barra's hiring not only elevated Xiaomi but also propelled China's smartphone industry into a qualitative leap forward, making Xiaomi a representative of Chinese enterprises going global.

In addition, Lei Jun revealed another $10 billion funding round, following the launch of the Redmi priced at 799 yuan, and the upcoming release of Mi 3. For a time, all eyes in China's tech sector were on Xiaomi. This starkly contradicted my own views on Xiaomi, prompting me to further examine what magic made the industry believe Xiaomi was China's "Innovation King."

Regarding innovation itself, the tech world generally categorizes it into foundational innovation, product innovation, and application innovation. For Chinese companies, most focus on product and application innovation, which is indeed where the most attention-grabbing developments occur because technology ultimately needs to be presented through products and applications to users. However, from the perspective of innovation weight, product and application innovation account for only one-third, while foundational innovation forms the core.

Taking smartphones as an example, foundational innovation mainly includes chips (CPU, GPU, DSP, RF, etc.), core components (cameras, displays, storage, etc.), and operating systems (Android, iOS, Windows, etc.). It is well known that these foundational innovations are largely monopolized by companies from the U.S., Japan, and South Korea. Although China is the world's largest smartphone manufacturing and consumption country, Chinese enterprises possess very little foundational technology, often outdated or low-end, or simply unprofitable areas others have abandoned. Ultimately, most Chinese smartphone manufacturers are merely ODMs or OEMs performing minor assembly work, while the true winners remain Qualcomm, Google, Apple, Samsung, ARM, Broadcom, and other companies leading in foundational technological innovation.

To put it bluntly, the core competitiveness of Chinese smartphone manufacturers lies in offering ultra-high cost-performance ratios.

As for application-level innovation, I can only say that a glance at the app stores filled with copied, repetitive content reveals just how poor China's application innovation truly is.

Given these perspectives, I believe Xiaomi, despite its unique aspects, does not represent the pinnacle of China's technological innovation and should not become a model for other Chinese enterprises to emulate. Instead, Chinese companies should learn from firms like Huawei, investing more in foundational research and innovation to genuinely become stronger. Relying solely on capital-based, marketing-driven product and application innovations will always result in fleeting success, like meteors that appear and disappear quickly.

Regarding Barra joining Xiaomi or Xiaomi acquiring Barra, given Xiaomi's inherent marketing nature, this is undoubtedly a highly exploitable opportunity. Currently, whether it's the U.S. media linking Xiaomi through gossip or the exaggerated, irrational reporting by Chinese media, Xiaomi has gained immense attention from this event.

Last Friday, I met a senior figure in the smartphone industry. This gentleman stated that through Xiaomi's online marketing strategies, building a brand now requires less investment and yields faster results than before. As such, he plans to step into the limelight and launch his own branded smartphone. This person was recently responsible for the ODM of North Korea’s Arirang phone. However, I remain skeptical, as Xiaomi's marketing gimmicks aren't easy to replicate.

It is evident that Xiaomi's marketing model has captivated many, leading to widespread imitation. Yet among those attempting to follow Xiaomi's internet and mobile internet-based smartphone approach, few besides Xiaomi have succeeded, illustrating both the randomness and inevitability of Xiaomi's rise.

Simultaneously, I believe Xiaomi's future holds too much uncertainty. Of course, this can only be judged over time.

As for Barra himself, what he brings to Xiaomi remains unclear at present. In Google, people like Barra are abundant. The role Barra will play at Xiaomi hasn’t been officially announced yet, nor do I wish to speculate unnecessarily. Over the years, China's tech sector has recruited numerous foreign executives from international companies, but their impact remains minimal due to cultural and managerial differences. Thus, using foreign executives in Chinese companies still seems more about appearances than substance.

However, the gossip surrounding Barra reported by U.S. tech blogs is quite intriguing. So-called “fury for a beauty,” such incidents have occurred throughout history, both domestically and internationally. If one's beloved is taken by a superior, choosing to resign quietly and without recourse isn't unreasonable. Moreover, it's challenging to find a logical reason why Barra would leave a major platform like Google to join Xiaomi, a company far smaller and less influential.

Therefore, I can only speculate that Xiaomi seized the opportunity when Barra was emotionally vulnerable, offering him a high position and salary along with the promise of a thriving market in China and globally, thus bringing him onboard to elevate Xiaomi's brand marketing to another peak.

Lei Jun's insight cannot be overstated. Barra's resignation provided Lei Jun with an opportunity, albeit perhaps a brief one. A vice president responsible for Android projects leaving due to a romantic entanglement already draws significant media and public attention. On many mainstream foreign media outlets and tech blogs, Xiaomi's name appeared alongside these news headlines. Through this, Lei Jun successfully launched Xiaomi's first overseas marketing campaign.

Currently, Xiaomi's model relies heavily on consistently releasing explosive news for炒作on the internet. Whether it's a new product launch, a funding round, or the Barra incident, constant hype boosts Xiaomi's brand recognition and allows it to gain traction in the capital markets, earning it a reputation for innovation and development. For instance, Xiaomi, founded merely three years ago, has already reached a valuation of $10 billion.

As for whether recruiting Barra aims to enter the U.S. market, I am skeptical. As mentioned earlier, Xiaomi struggles to expand beyond Asia, let alone penetrate the U.S. market.

At this stage of Xiaomi's development, there is one crucial issue that must be addressed: how to allow shareholders from previous funding rounds to cash out. Remember, no matter how high the valuation, it remains theoretical until realized. The best method for cashing out is through an IPO. Where to list is another matter. Interestingly, the majority of China's internet companies choose to list in the U.S. rather than domestically, and there are stories behind this choice. Despite Xiaomi's current popularity, Chinese stock markets, investors, and the domestic smartphone industry see things clearly but prefer not to reveal everything. Therefore, I believe listing in the U.S. represents the best way for Xiaomi to cash out, and Barra could potentially be Lei Jun's ideal character to continue writing Xiaomi's narrative—aristocratic roots, surrounded by scandals...

All of this aligns perfectly with Xiaomi's business model: first establish Xiaomi's brand presence in the U.S., allowing more Americans to recognize Xiaomi's achievements in China and across Asia, thereby paving the way for Xiaomi's eventual U.S. IPO. Ultimately, this will enable the shareholders who have supported Xiaomi to cash out.

This demonstrates that Xiaomi's strategy is quite grand. Lei Jun's statement that Xiaomi won't go public for five years makes sense, as each phase requires carefully orchestrated hype to complete, none of which can be skipped. Barra plays a new role in Xiaomi's scripted drama, showcasing Lei Jun's astuteness.
