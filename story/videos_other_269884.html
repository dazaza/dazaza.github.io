The empirical rule of volume and price changes

by gpgp6486 on 2009-11-22 12:03:22

Empirical rules of trading volume and price changes:

1) Under the limit-up and limit-down system, if a stock experiences its first limit-down without trading volume, it will continue to fall until significant volume appears for rebound or reversal; similarly, if a stock experiences its first limit-up without trading volume, it will continue to rise until significant volume appears for retracement or reversal.

2) There is always a reason for increased trading volume: in high-price zones, some major players may engage in fake trading to create high volumes, often placing large sell orders at certain prices before buying them back. This is just an illusion, as the real movement of the center can be identified. If such fake trading occurs at low prices, it indicates institutional fund switching or preparation for a bull market, providing an opportunity to follow up.

3) After a long-term decline in stock prices leads to a bottoming out of trading volume, if the price rebounds but the subsequent trading volume does not increase with the price rise, and if the price rises again only to fall back near the previous bottom (sometimes higher than the previous bottom), but the second bottom's trading volume is significantly lower than the first bottom, it shows that there is no further downward momentum, indicating a new wave of upward movement. Consider buying at this point.

4) Regardless of whether there is trading volume during a decline, once the price pattern breaks down, timely profit-taking or stop-loss measures should be implemented.

5) In high-price zones, if a long black candlestick appears and cannot be absorbed by two consecutive large bullish candles, it indicates the formation of a top price, and one should promptly liquidate holdings. In high-price zones, regardless of positive or negative news, if a large candlestick appears with significant volume, one should be cautious about the formation of a head.

6) When trading volume reaches a new high but the closing price fails to reach a new high the next day, it suggests that the price will retreat. Similarly, when trading volume reaches a new low while the price stops falling, it indicates that the price will stabilize and recover.

7) In a bear market, if both volume and price break through the previous high during a rebound, it often indicates the end of the bear market. In a bull market, if the price sets a new high followed by a new high in volume, it frequently signals the end of the bull market and the start of a bear market.

8) The longer the time spent building a base with stable volume and price, the greater the strength and height of the subsequent rebound. As they say: the longer horizontally, the higher vertically.

9) Volume-price analysis is not suitable for short-term analysis of small-cap fully controlled stocks, but medium to long-term analysis still cannot escape the volume-price analysis system.

10) Observing changes in volume must be combined with K-line trends and patterns.

11) Trading volume is the thermometer of the stock market. Many stock surges are not due to fundamental changes but rather to short-term supply and demand relationships in the chip market.

12) In an uptrend, when relatively low trading volume appears, and the stock price retreats to important moving averages (5-day, 10-day, 30-day), it is often an excellent short-term buying opportunity.

13) The size of the trading volume determines whether a stock will experience a rights issue rally or discount before and after the rights issue. After the rights issue, if the trading volume increases and bullish candles appear, it indicates a rights issue rally; if no volume or reduced volume appears, it indicates a rights issue discount.

14) There are two characteristics of changes in trading volume for dark horse stocks at the bottom: one is that the trading volume suddenly increases at the bottom from a certain day onward and almost remains at this level daily, with the stock price slightly rising and forming cross star patterns when declining; the other is that the trading volume gradually increases from a certain day onward and maintains this increasing trend, with the stock price slightly and continuously rising, indicating that the main player has lost patience to slowly accumulate positions and has to push the stock price higher while buying.

15) At the end of the bottom consolidation phase of the stock price, the fluctuation range gradually narrows; when the trading volume shrinks to the extreme and then increases, the stock price breaks through the consolidation pattern with a moderate bullish candle and stands above the 10-day moving average; the trading volume continues to expand and the stock price keeps closing positively, adhering to the principle of leaving the bottom price for three days; after the breakout, the overlapped moving averages turn into a bullish arrangement. This is the best short-to-medium-term buying point, and also the coordination of volume, price, and moving averages.
