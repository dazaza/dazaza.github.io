Amazon beats IBM to $600m CIA cloud computing deal

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 15:31:29

There's an old saying in the tech world: No one ever got fired for buying IBM. For decades, that has made Big Blue a perennial leader in computing services.

But IBM may now be facing an unusual challenge from Amazon.com, which is rapidly expanding its cloud-computing business after landing a $600 million contract with the CIA.

For years, Amazon has provided Web-based computer services to startups and small businesses. But last month, Amazon Web Services beat out IBM to win a $600 million contract with the CIA.

IBM has protested and temporarily halted the deal. But regardless, it shows how far Amazon has come from being just an online retailer of books to becoming an information technology supplier to large corporations and government agencies.

That news helped push Amazon shares to a record high on Thursday ahead of the company’s second-quarter earnings report. Amazon does not break out revenue figures for AWS, but Wall Street analysts believe the business is growing faster than its retail operation and is more profitable.

"AWS is certainly one of the big factors driving the stock," said Ron Josey, an analyst at JMP Securities. "Software and IT investors know and are trying to figure out what AWS is worth and what kind of impact it will have on adjacent markets."

With the highest research and development budget in the industry, IBM continues to dominate corporate IT computing services, and it will probably continue to do so for some time. But as the cloud market grows, AWS will increasingly get attention from companies like IBM and Oracle.

AWS was the first to introduce public cloud computing services in 2006. Businesses can rent computing power, storage and other services from data centers shared by multiple customers rather than creating and maintaining their own data centers, which tends to be less flexible and more expensive.

Amazon has already attracted a host of marquee customers, including Samsung, Pfizer, the Public Broadcasting Service and NASA.

Like tossing a pebble into a still pond, Amazon’s rise in cloud computing has created ripples across the entire cloud market. After two quarters of declining revenue, Oracle announced a partnership in June with former rivals Microsoft and Salesforce.com to counter AWS’s aggressive moves.

"AWS has had a meaningful impact on the overall IT market and the large players like IBM are responding," said Colby Synesael, an analyst at Cowen & Co.

From bookstores to tech

Amazon started as an online bookstore, but over the past few years, it has expanded into areas as diverse as tablets and video. Some have criticized Amazon for investing heavily while paying little attention to profits.

Yet Amazon shares hit a record high of $309.39 on July 16, and they are up 22 percent this year. By contrast, Oracle shares have fallen 4 percent this year. IBM shares are up 1 percent.

This month, AWS cut prices on its popular EC2 service, sending Rackspace shares tumbling more than 45 percent this year.

Analysts estimate AWS contributes at least $2 billion in annual revenue, and the cloud services market is expected to grow beyond $60 billion. Thanks to increased government spending on cloud computing, AWS could generate more than $10 billion in revenue over the next several years, four times what it makes now.

The competition against IBM for the CIA contract burnished Amazon’s reputation and gave Wall Street more confidence in its competitive abilities. Analysts believe AWS can go toe-to-toe with other corporate IT vendors.

According to a report released by the Government Accountability Office, five companies competed for the CIA contract: AWS, IBM, Microsoft, AT&T and another firm whose identity has not been disclosed.

When AWS won, IBM protested, prompting the GAO to release its report. The agency said in June that the CIA had reopened parts of the negotiating process, giving IBM a new opportunity.

But the GAO also said AWS offered the best combination of services and price.

“Amazon scored better than IBM on every single technical criteria,” said Carlos Kirjner, an analyst at Bernstein Research. “It has become one of the most sophisticated and powerful IT companies in the world.”

According to the GAO report, the CIA was concerned about IBM’s ability to provide auto-scaling, or automatically increasing or decreasing computing power based on application use.

“Auto-scaling is a very complex technology and there aren’t many cloud providers who do it well, but Amazon is one of them,” said Kyle Hilgendorf, a cloud computing analyst at Gartner. “I don’t think anyone would say IBM does it better.”

Clint Roswell, an IBM spokesman, said the government did not evaluate IBM’s CIA proposal thoroughly enough.

He added: “IBM remains committed to providing enterprise-grade security solutions and practical cloud solutions, and IBM looks forward to having a new opportunity to demonstrate its ability to meet government requirements.”

An Amazon spokesman declined to comment on the CIA contract. A CIA spokesman also declined to comment.

In June, IBM paid $2 billion to acquire SoftLayer Technologies, a competitor of AWS. Bill Moran of Ptak Associates said that acquisition might help IBM when the CIA reopens bidding.

According to people familiar with the matter, such moves are part of the business strategy. He added: “They don’t need issues like auto-scaling to reopen negotiations.”
