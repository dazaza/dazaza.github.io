Too many precious works of art were destroyed in the Cultural Revolution.

by fbfshtwk0423 on 2012-02-14 15:27:20

"During the Cultural Revolution, too many precious works of art were destroyed. Now, most of the antiques remaining in the domestic market are in poor condition and have lost their luster, so the goods that the domestic market can provide are really limited. In comparison, these antiques that have drifted to Japan are better preserved, and the asking price here is not high."

"For the Japanese, the memory brought by the craze for Chinese antiques can be said to be a mixture of joy and sorrow. During the period of Japan's rapid economic development in the last century, thousands of Japanese people bought back a large number of Japanese cultural relics lost abroad from overseas. Subsequently, with the arrival of the economic bubble era, the antique market also declined, and people's interest in antiques plummeted.

"People who came to Japan during the Meiji period were all very rich and carried extremely valuable antique gifts with them. Of course, in the following years, a large number of fakes were produced, so many Chinese people believe that the authenticity of Chinese antiques first discovered in Japan is more credible." Nakamura Miyuki also said that the handicrafts sold in her antique city are all reasonably priced. The highest-priced antique in Ginza Antique City is a ceramic makeup box from the Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms period, which was auctioned at a reserve price of $300,000. This contrasts sharply with a Qing Dynasty vase that was sold for $68 million at an auction in London last year.

"In China, antique collection has become a highly profitable industry, growing rapidly with the development of the economy. According to Nakamura Miyuki, the famous porcelain from the Ming and Qing Dynasties and the exquisite carvings from the Tang Dynasty are currently the most sought-after and valuable treasures. National honor is an important driving force behind the expansion of treasure hunting activities overseas. The strong interest in antiques has made the businesses of professional dealers, antique shops, and even private museums flourish. However, at the same time, the increased interest in antiques has also given counterfeit sellers opportunities to speculate and make money through deception.

"Because of its proximity to China and the presence of a large number of precious Chinese antiques here, Japan has become the most attractive antique market for collectors and enthusiasts today, including the Shanghai antique market. Years of continuous wars, prolonged natural disasters, and the outbreak of the Cultural Revolution have caused a large number of precious artworks to flow overseas. Nakamura Miyuki, manager of Ginza Antique City, stated that wealthy Chinese people from the Ming and Qing Dynasties brought a large number of precious antiques to Japan, many of which were given as gifts to Japanese nobles, especially during the reign of Emperor Meiji."

"Recently, a luxury antique auction was held in Ginza, Tokyo. Before the official start of the auction, a treasure hunt game was played. Antiquities enthusiasts entered a smoky room with magnifying glasses and flashlights to search for and admire treasures such as vases and statues."

"The rapid development of our country's economy has brought positive stimulation to the antique market, causing the prices of these precious artworks to soar in China," said a Shanghai-based antique collector surnamed Zhang, who wished to remain anonymous because he feared it might affect the high price of the antiques in his possession."

"Recently, some Japanese antique collectors whose cash flow chains have been broken have been forced to sell their family treasures, resulting in a rush purchase by a large number of Chinese buyers, including some Chinese antiques and artworks that were gifted to Japanese nobles decades ago. At the same time, Japan's tourism industry has also been greatly stimulated by regularly organizing Chinese antique collectors to participate in auctions in Japan to buy goods."
