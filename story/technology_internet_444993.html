American professor advocates "slow reading"

by zzf000zxyn7 on 2011-06-13 10:30:28

(Source: Reference News, June 23, 2010)

[AP Concord, New Hampshire, USA, June 16] In an era where people spend large amounts of time quickly browsing websites, sending text messages, and checking emails, an English professor at the University of New Hampshire is advocating for "slow reading," a method he believes allows readers to uncover more meaning and enjoyment from texts.

Thomas Newkirk is not the founder or a well-known supporter of the "slow reading" movement, but he feels that "slow reading" has become more important in today's cultural and educational systems. People often approach reading like fast food, wanting to consume it as quickly as possible.

"In schools, reading has become a competition. Students are timing themselves to see how many words they can read in a minute," Newkirk said.

"This gives students a wrong impression of reading. It’s like telling students that faster is better."

Newkirk is encouraging educators at all levels to return to older methods such as reading aloud and memorization, techniques that help students truly 'taste' the written word. He uses these methods in his own classroom. His students have told him that they have become so accustomed to quickly scanning web pages online that they now experience difficulty concentrating when reading physical books.

"One student told me that when he reads physical books, the words on the page seem to him like hyperlinks on a webpage. This distracts him and makes him think about other things. I think they realize they're missing something," Newkirk said.

However, "slow reading" does not mean that everything should be read as slowly as possible. John Miedema, author of the book "Slow Reading," said that similar to the slow food movement, the goal of the slow reading movement is to shorten the distance between the reader and the information being read. His 2009 book "Slow Reading" explores this movement.

Miedema works as a technical specialist at IBM in Ottawa, Ontario, Canada. He said there has been little formal research done on slow reading; most studies focus on health issues like reading difficulties. However, he mentioned that the slow reading movement is growing: The 2004 book "Slow Down: The Global Slow Movement and Its Challenge to Speed Worship" initiated the "slow life" movement. Author Carl Honoré was inspired to write this book after reflecting on a book-buying experience. At the time, he was considering purchasing a set of "One-Minute Bedtime Stories" for his children but suddenly realized that his obsession with speed had spiraled out of control.

Lindsay Waters, Executive Editor of Humanities at Harvard University Press, detailed a global reading crisis in a 2007 article in "The Chronicle of Higher Education" and called for a "reading revolution."

Miedema said that literary criticism at the university level and other fields have long emphasized "slow reading," but now elementary schools are also getting involved.

Third-grade teacher Mary Ellen Webb at Mast Way Elementary School in Durham, New Hampshire, requires her students to memorize poems longer than 40 lines and recite them to their classmates and parents. She does this primarily to boost the students' sense of pride, but she noted that having students memorize poetry helps enhance their ability to understand complex texts.

"Memorization is one of the things we've lost. It went out of fashion a long time ago. People place great emphasis on reading speed. Some believe that reading speed is a measure of reading ability. I think speed is important, but we may have made too many mistakes in this area," Webb said.
