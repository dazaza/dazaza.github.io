[The Precious Land of Northeast China] Rushing to Northeast China

As a social custom, "rushing to Northeast China" has been widely accepted. The east gate of the Shanhai Pass city defines the boundary between the outside of the pass and the Central Plains. From the Qing Dynasty to the Republic of China for hundreds of years, the phenomenon of Shandong people leaving their hometowns and rushing to Northeast China began. In the 19th century, the lower reaches of the Yellow River suffered from disasters year after year, but the Qing government still banned access to the Northeast. Bankrupt farmers disregarded the ban and ventured into Northeast China in the tens of thousands, risking punishment, thus giving rise to the term "rushing to Northeast China." By 1840, the population in Northeast China had exceeded 3 million people, an increase of seven to eight times compared to a hundred years earlier when the national population was over 400 million. At the end of the Qing Dynasty, Russia invaded Northeast China. In 1860, the Qing government partially lifted the ban on land reclamation in certain areas of Northeast China and fully lifted it in 1897. By 1910, the total population in Northeast China had increased to 18 million people. During the Republic of China period (1912-1949), the wave of "rushing to Northeast China" surged, with nearly 40 million people by the eve of the founding of New China (data from "Geography of China's Population" by Zhang Shanyu). Over the 38 years of the Republic of China, the number of Shandong people rushing to Northeast China reached an average of 480,000 per year, totaling over 18.3 million people, with around 7.92 million Shandong people settling down there. This can be considered one of the largest population movements in human history and an unprecedented large-scale migration in modern history. Therefore, the ancestors of most native residents of Northeast China today come from provinces like Shandong —— the lower reaches of the Yellow River region.

Rushing to Northeast China: After the Qing dynasty entered the pass, they implemented ethnic hierarchy and isolation policies, strictly prohibiting Han Chinese from cultivating the "Dragon Rising Place" of Manchuria —— issuing the "Ban Guan" order. Emperor Shunzhi warned the Manchu nobility to retreat to Northeast China if necessary. With the entire Manchu clan entering the pass, the population in Northeast China drastically decreased. Under the pretext of protecting the "ancestral place of origin" and the benefits of ginseng mountains and pearl rivers, the Qing government long implemented a policy of closure and prohibition in Northeast China. Starting from the Shunzhi era, the Manchu territory constructed more than a thousand kilometers of "Willow Hedge" fence walls —— the Manchu Great Wall (Willow Hedge, Willow Wall, Willow City, Strip Border), which was completed during the middle of the Kangxi era. The Willow Hedge extending from Shanhai Pass through Kaiyuan, Xinbin to Fengcheng in the south is called the "Old Border"; the section from Kaiyuan northeastward to today's Jilin City in the north is called the "New Border" (according to the Cihai dictionary). Hence, in folk terms, there are sayings like "people inside the border" and "people outside the border."

Three Periods:

(1) From 1644 to 1667, the "Liaodong Recruitment for Cultivation Regulations" stipulated that "those who recruit a hundred people will be granted the rank of County Magistrate or Garrison Commander," leading to many Shandong people migrating to Northeast China during these 23 years. Many regions were developed due to immigration, resulting in "greatly expanded land and increasing households."

(2) From 1668 to 1860, to maintain the traditional customs of Manchuria and protect the livelihood of the Eight Banners, in the seventh year of Emperor Kangxi's reign (1688), the Qing court ordered the cessation of recruitment and granting of official positions in Liaodong, implementing a closed-door policy in Northeast China.

(3) From 1861 to 1911, after the Opium War, the Qing government's control over the frontier gradually weakened, and Russia continuously encroached upon Heilongjiang's borders. The Qing government adopted General Te Puqin's suggestion and officially lifted the ban on land reclamation in the tenth year of Emperor Xianfeng's reign (1860).

Historical Background:

From "exiles" to "refugees," and then to the "immigrant society" in Northeast China. In the early Qing dynasty, ethnic tensions were severe, and the Qing rulers adopted extremely cruel suppression measures. Rebels were exiled to remote "smoky and foggy" lands, and many criminals from Shandong were sent to the harsh cold of Northeast China. This practice of exiling prisoners is historically known as "flowing people." The people of Shandong are typical examples of being attached to their homeland. Population pressure, natural disasters, man-made calamities, and the policies of the Qing government constituted the external causes for Shandong people rushing to Northeast China. Taking the construction of the Chinese Eastern Railway at the time as an example, according to Japanese scholar Tao Ye Junshan in "The History of Northeast Development," "Chinese laborers gathered like ants, and Shandong, the main supplier of labor, took the opportunity to send countless workers out of the pass to assist. It is not an exaggeration to say that the 1500-mile-long Chinese Eastern Railway was completed by Shandong laborers." Regarding the resistance against foreign invasion in Northeast China, the "Wangou Commune Annals" of Hunjiang City, Jilin Province, records: "In 1921, a farmer named Wang Zhenbang, originally from Shandong, fled with his wife and eldest daughter to the Xichuan area. In 1931, when the Japanese army occupied Northeast China, Wang Zhenbang gathered over a hundred fellow Shandong natives to resist the Japanese. By the end of 1934, they engaged in several battles with the Japanese, with the second battle being the most successful, inflicting heavy casualties on the Japanese, making the righteous army feared by the Japanese."

Due to Northeast China being a place of exile and prohibited cultivation, the area west of the border wall was the grazing land of Mongolian nobles. However, the collapsing Qing government could not stop the historical tide; its more than two hundred years of rule promoted the great fusion of the Manchu, Mongolian, Han, and other ethnic groups. Even historical events like the "Manchu-Mongol Independence" movement and the "Puppet State of Manchukuo" did not change the fact that the Manchu people became the Manchu ethnic group of China. The sense of attachment to the homeland among the people of Shandong, the land of sages, diminished, while their adventurous spirit strengthened, prompting the flow of refugees to shift from returning in winter to taking root in Northeast China. The Xinhai Revolution brought tens of millions of Han people from within the pass back into the embrace of China, "to the point where cultural geography maps indicate that the region is entirely populated by Chinese people" (from "The Empire of the Steppes" by French author René Grousset).

Rushing to Northeast China represents a tragic history and a grand migration. There were specific "routes for rushing to Northeast China" and particular historical backgrounds. Thus, rushing to Northeast China is a social-historical migration phenomenon, with spontaneous objective factors and deep internal political influences. "The essence of Shandong people rushing to Northeast China is the spontaneous and unstoppable tragic movement of impoverished farmers striving for survival on the brink of death." In 1899, Japanese scholar Koetsu Hirata recorded the true historical scene of the time in "A Journey Through Manchuria": "Traveling from Fengtian to Xingjing, we saw people pushing wheelbarrows, women sitting atop them, some children crying and others sleeping. The men pushed from behind, younger brothers pulled from the front, elderly women leaned on walking sticks, young girls clung together, staggering along the road. The men cursed their young wives, and old mothers called out to their children. Teams of people were all heading towards Tonghua, Huairou, Hailong Cheng, Chaoyangzhen, visible one after another. On the journey from Fengtian to Jilin, the travelers shared beds with Shandong immigrants..." As the world's largest ethnic group —— the Han people, population pressure over the past few centuries has caused the Han to continuously spread outward: going west, moving south to Nanyang, Inner Mongolia and Xinjiang also became immigrant destinations for the Han people. Thus, rushing to Northeast China became a balance of population and economy.

Cultural Background:

In the mid-19th century, as the gates of the Shanhai Pass opened wide, a flood of migrants surged forth, powerful and unstoppable. People are carriers of culture and information, and the movement of people is essentially the movement of culture. The rising waves of "rushing to Northeast China" signify the large-scale advancement of Central Plains culture into Northeast China, bringing cultural exchanges into a new phase. If cultural exchange during the "closed-door" era was characterized by the "erosion" of Central Plains culture onto the existing culture of Manchuria, influenced by artificial "closure and prohibition," then under open historical conditions, Central Plains culture rapidly spread throughout Northeast China, becoming dominant. "For the vast majority of Shandong immigrants, the three northeastern provinces are no more than an extension of Shandong Province."

The "replication" of Shandong villages and Hebei villages in Northeast China essentially represents the horizontal transplantation of Central Plains culture. Given their overwhelming numbers, they have ample reason to preserve Qi-Lu culture or Yan-Zhao culture, as described by the saying, "living in clusters, their language and customs remain unchanged." They do not need to alter themselves to adapt to local social customs, religious beliefs, or use local languages and scripts. In a certain sense, this is also a form of cultural conservatism. Zhao Zhongfu once remarked on the significance of "rushing to Northeast China": "Socially speaking, the three northeastern provinces are essentially an expansion of the agricultural society of North China. Despite geographical distance, there are no significant cultural differences between the two. In terms of language, religious beliefs, customs, family systems, ethical concepts, economic behaviors, and other aspects, North China and the three northeastern provinces are largely similar. Most importantly, members of the immigrant society in the three northeastern provinces do not have the notion of distinguishing themselves from their cultural roots."

Faced with the overwhelming presence of Qi-Lu culture and Yan-Zhao culture, Northeast culture could not help but feel some apprehension, nor could it avoid the emergence of "native vs. newcomer" conflicts. For instance, the "Brief Account of Heilongjiang" states: "Regarding hired labor for cultivation, the majority comes from Zhili and Shandong provinces. Every year after the ice melts, the roads in Fengtian and Jilin provinces are filled with travelers. The locals deeply resent them and often bully them." An example is Anguang County in Liaoning (now the New Ping'an Town of Da'an City in Jilin Province). The "Local History of Anguang County" records: "Before the establishment of governance in the county, the Mongols were unfamiliar with farming and relied solely on herding, leaving the land barren. Subsequently, Han civilians came to cultivate the land, allowing the Mongol princes to gain rental profits. However, due to different ethnicities, language barriers, and lack of mutual understanding, the Mongols often bullied them... Until the thirtieth year of Guangxu's reign (1904), officials were dispatched to survey wastelands and recruit households to claim land. Immigrants flocked to the area... The Mongols gradually began farming."
