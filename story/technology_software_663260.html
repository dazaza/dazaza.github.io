The future is an era of technology and talent, and the talent model is innovation-driven.

by bhhgsyfk4143 on 2012-02-11 13:05:59

Therefore, the basic method for enterprises to build a talent team is: for senior management, selection is more important than training; for middle management, both selection and training are necessary; for grassroots employees, training is more important than selection. This indicates that the stage of low labor costs has passed, replaced by a new development model of high income and high output. This is the important reason why today's private enterprises in China need a talent strategy. In the past, it was the era of scale and quality, and the talent model was mainly imitation; in the future, it will be the era of technology and talent, and the talent model will be mainly innovation. In such a transformation stage, the importance of talent goes without saying. China is entering a phase of industrial upgrading and consumption transformation. Take Beijing as an example. In 2011, Beijing's population exceeded 20 million, GDP reached 1.6 trillion RMB, per capita GDP exceeded 12,000 US dollars, reaching the level of "rich countries" internationally. The national per capita GDP has reached 4,500 US dollars, also reaching the level of "upper-middle" countries internationally.

The stronger a person's ability, the greater their contribution to the company, but the greater the difficulty of change. If values do not align, then it is basically impossible to reform them. Keeping someone for performance reasons alone harms those employees who are loyal to the company's values. In most cases, the efficiency of talent cultivation is inversely proportional to the level of talent within the enterprise. The lower the level, the higher the training efficiency; the higher the level, the lower the training efficiency.

Given this, why do so many private entrepreneurs focus on cultivating senior executives, especially newly recruited ones? What they call "cultivation" is not about ability, but about character. Most private enterprise bosses spend a lot of time on this, earnestly teaching and trying to gain loyalty from capable individuals through personal feelings. This may be effective in the short term, but in the long run, this is actually a behavior with a very low return on investment, as the saying goes, "A forced melon does not sweeten."

Cao Cao once said something to the effect that during war, ability is emphasized over morality, and during peace, morality triumphs over ability. This statement precisely reveals the dilemma of corporate talent strategy: using capable people means not only a high price but also whether their alignment with corporate culture during "peace" times can be guaranteed? For those capable individuals with "the courage of ten thousand men," on one hand, companies vie to hire them, and if one place doesn't keep them, there will always be another place willing to. On the other hand, changing oneself means pain, so why should they transform themselves for a particular company?

How should internal talents be selected? I suggest using more open selection and less personal nomination. The best way for open selection is public competition. Now government agencies all use this method to select officials, yet companies instead favor nepotism? Open selection not only brings excellent talents to the company but also allows more employees to join the growth system of corporate executives, enabling the company to obtain a transparent and fair employment culture.

In terms of talent cultivation, employee training yields the highest return on investment. Grassroots employees, especially new employees, are generally younger and weaker in ability. At this point, investing in their values will yield high returns, and the return on investment in their ability cultivation is also the highest.

For senior management, "selection is more important than training" due to their impact on the system. "One bad soldier is just one bad soldier, but one bad general ruins the whole army." The method of using senior management must take employees as the starting point. Using the wrong senior manager will lead to "one bad general ruining the whole army." It should be noted here that "selection" includes both external and internal choices, or rather, internal choices are even more important. Each company's culture and operating methods have unique aspects. External hires have their advantages, but internally grown talents understand and know the company culture and operating methods better, making them easier for employees to accept.

Middle management is the backbone of the enterprise, playing a role in bridging upper and lower levels. In the sense of "bridging upwards," middle management is even more important than senior management because the company's key strategies and decisions are implemented through middle management. Therefore, the method of building middle management talent is both selection and training.

Jack Welch once said that his entire job was to choose the right people; Steve Jobs also said that one outstanding talent could match fifty mediocre employees. It is clear that excellent companies all consider talent as the core of managing the company. The current issue is how enterprises can obtain outstanding talents and how they can build a strong team.

More importantly, due to the generally low income of grassroots employees, their willingness to learn at this time is also the strongest. Most multinational companies have clear training plans for grassroots employees, with costs amounting to many times that of senior management training fees. Unfortunately, many of our private enterprises do the opposite. Bosses or senior management spend a lot of money on learning, but invest little in grassroots employee training. They are indifferent to the growth of grassroots employees but overly indulgent towards senior management. Naturally, the return on investment in building a talent team will not be high.

In building a talent pool, special attention should be paid to daring to use some people who voice opposing opinions. There shouldn't be too many of these people; otherwise, the enterprise would become a "university" or research institute, engaging in empty talk without practical value. But there shouldn't be none either. People who dare to voice opposing opinions may make you uncomfortable, but they will constantly remind the enterprise of the existence of crises, prompting you to continuously reflect on and improve yourself. What enterprises truly need to guard against are those seemingly loyal but lacking independent personality and initiative individuals. Their excessive compliance will cause the enterprise to lose its clear understanding of the situation, leading to gradual decline and even death in comfort.
