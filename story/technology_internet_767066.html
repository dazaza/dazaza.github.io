Top Ten Milestones: A Brief History of the Development of Google's Search Business

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 17:34:00

On September 30, according to foreign media reports, September 27 of this month marked the 15th anniversary of Google's founding (note). A website listed ten milestone events in the development of this search engine company.

1. Google went online

The establishment of the company was undoubtedly the biggest milestone in the history of Google Search. Between 1997 and 1998, Larry Page and Sergey Brin, co-founders of Google, began preparing the company in a garage in Menlo Park, California. A few days after its establishment, the company registered the domain name Google.com. The concept behind this service, which is the word "infinity" (googol), shows that the company aims to organize infinite information on the World Wide Web in a constructive way to help users find the answers they need.

2. Taking the first successful step with Yahoo's help

Before 2000, Google was not yet mainstream in the search industry. The leading position belonged to the veteran search engine Yahoo! founded in 1994. It was this company that helped Google gain prominence through a search cooperation deal — Yahoo abandoned Inktomi and used Brin and Page's services to support their native search results. Interestingly, the result of this deal 15 years later turned out to be quite significant.

In the end, many disputes arose between the two companies, including over technical patents. In 2004, Google settled patent disputes with Yahoo through its subsidiary Overture Services by issuing 2.7 million Class A common shares to Yahoo. This led to the establishment of Google's advertising product AdWords.

3. Self-service profitable search model

In the past, search engines like Lycos AskJeeves and Excite earned revenue through banner ads. This advertising model was very popular in the late 1990s, but after some time, no one clicked on them anymore. Search companies and advertisers faced income problems.

In 2000, Google launched the AdWords product. This model seemed very novel at the time — brands could manage themselves instead of communicating with ad agencies about ad matters, saving time, effort, and costs.

More attractively, AdWords had a personalized attempt where ads could be created based on the content of search queries rather than appearing randomly. Moreover, Google used a bidding model to increase revenue: the higher the bid, the higher the position of the customer's text ad would be.

Yahoo and Microsoft quickly emulated this approach. This type of advertising accounted for a large portion of Google's revenue — $42.5 billion in total advertising revenue in 2012.

4. Is Microsoft plus Yahoo greater than Google?

Due to dissatisfaction with Google's dominant position in the search engine market, Yahoo and Microsoft formed an alliance in 2010, using Microsoft's technology to handle Yahoo's search algorithms and paid search platforms. Simultaneously, Yahoo became the "exclusive cooperative seller" for both companies, selling advertisements to advertisers of paid search from both companies.

This deal was massive and required approval from regulatory authorities in the US and Europe, which ultimately approved the deal in early 2010.

It was unclear how effective the partnership was, but Yahoo extended the cooperation for 12 months starting April 1, 2012. However, when Marissa Mayer took charge of Yahoo, she indicated that this agreement did not deliver the promised market share and revenue.

Yahoo signed a "global, non-exclusive content-related advertising deal" with Google earlier this year, proving it still preferred Google more, marking the formal end of the Microsoft-Yahoo partnership.

Sorry, Microsoft.

5. Why does my website rank low?

Companies continuously claimed that their products ranked lower in Google searches, accusing Google of fraudulent behavior and treating competitors differently from their own products. Google denied any misconduct.

In March 2006, KinderStart, a parent tutoring site, sued Google, claiming it manipulated the "PageRank" of the KinderStart website. The company alleged that Google violated the Second Amendment of the Constitution and the Sherman Antitrust Act, and engaged in "religious and political discrimination."

A district judge in San Jose ultimately dismissed the plaintiff's lawsuit request, stating that Google's "PageRank" was not a factual statement that could be litigated, but merely a thought of Google. The court stated:

"Kinderstart claims that it is mathematically impossible to assign a PageRank value of zero. This claim assumes that Google views PageRank as a purely objective standard in some way. As discussed above, PageRank is Google's invention and does not necessarily have to have an independent visible assignment. In fact, Google can arbitrarily assign PageRank values, whether integers or numbers with many decimal places, but this cannot lead to 'incorrect' PageRank values."

Other companies also sued Google for manipulating rankings, and in September 2011, the U.S. Senate Antitrust Subcommittee got involved in a related lawsuit. Yelp's CEO testified in court, stating that websites must endure Google because it is the gateway for users: "Google forces review sites to provide content for free to its own similar products — not to customers," he said. "Then Google gives better treatment to its own products."

In response to such criticism, Google Chairman Eric Schmidt said, "Senator, I can assure you we manipulate nothing."

6. The rise of the Knowledge Graph

In today's era of platforms and big data, industry leaders all use "graphs" — Facebook has the Social Graph, Eventbrite has the Event Graph, LinkedIn is building the Economic Graph, and Google has the Knowledge Graph.

Based on how Google's Knowledge Graph gathers information from sources like the CIA World Factbook, Freebase, and Wikipedia, its working method might resemble AskJeeves and Wolfram Alpha. Some people say this platform helps Google move towards the semantic web, a smarter search engine capable of solving complex problems rather than just analyzing input box contents literally.

It is estimated that the Knowledge Graph contains over 570 million data points — namely the most commonly searched people, places, and things — along with 18 billion cross-references. Google can integrate many of its products into this large database, and one example is the intelligent voice assistant software Google Now.

7. Is this the image you're looking for?

At one point, the method to search for things on the Internet was to enter relevant descriptive words. But in 2011, Google's image search made it possible to search based on an image in hand or find similar images. This function equates pictures with search terms.

The process is simple: when users are looking for information about a particular subject but only have a related picture, they can upload an image to Google, and it will display relevant search results.

8. Helping people monetize their own websites

Although Google allows advertisers to market their products on Google's site (which brings tremendous traffic), it missed something. In 2003, the company launched a product called AdSense, allowing ads to break free from the original closed system and expand to third-party websites.

Anyone can place ads on their own site to make money without relying on banner ads that no one clicks. Management, maintenance, and dealing with advertisers are all handled by Google, giving advertisers new opportunities to reach interested customers on high-value websites. For instance, if you search for photography materials and visit a site, you might see an ad for a Canon camera or a photography course — this is what Google aims to achieve, and ad agencies pay for it.

Google integrated AdSense into many of its own services. Content, feeds, search, mobile, websites, and video.

In 2012, AdSense generated $2.88 billion in revenue (accounting for 27% of the company's total revenue).

9. When did Google start monopolizing?

It’s not surprising that the U.S. government would sue Google for monopolizing the search market. In 2013, Google reached a settlement with the Federal Trade Commission (FTC), ending a two-year investigation by the agency and avoiding a lawsuit.

Rivals like Microsoft and Yelp had long been urging the government to file lawsuits, arguing there was enough evidence to show Google engaged in monopolistic practices. However, due to this settlement, Google avoided the fate Microsoft faced in the 1990s. Time magazine reported:

"Google voluntarily made numerous commitments to change its search behavior. The company also signed a consent decree regarding allegations that it used smartphone patents to block competitors — essentially a binding judicial order."

Clearly, Google couldn't avoid antitrust investigations — in November 2010, the company was investigated by the European Commission. Vice President Joaquin Almunia issued an ultimatum to Google's chairman, asking for responses to four areas questioned by the EU. Schmidt responded, but the case remains unresolved, although Google proposed a solution earlier this month.

According to comScore statistics, Google's search market share was 67% in July 2013, increasing by 0.3 percentage points. Bing by Microsoft holds a market share close to Google's at 17.9%.

10. Mobile Search

Among all the new tools, products, devices, and services launched by Google, one thing is clear: all the data being used and provided will be incorporated into the company's search algorithm, improving it and providing richer, more relevant answers. Just look at our search methods — whether on Google.com on desktops, in browser address bars, or on mobile devices or apps.

Google was the first company to bring search to mobile devices — its app is available on both Android and iOS. Users can also use Google Now on Android devices or through the Chrome app. As Amit Singhal, Google's senior vice president, mentioned earlier on the 27th, when Google started, finding answers on the internet meant finding a desktop computer. That wasn't a quick way to check movie times, maps, or background information.

Expectations are high; eMarketer estimated in August that nearly one-fifth of Google's total U.S. advertising revenue would come from mobile search. In short, going mobile is a win-win-win situation for consumers, advertisers, and Google.

Search has evolved from a simple text box into a platform supported by the Knowledge Graph and integrated products like Gmail, Books, YouTube, Google+, Docs, and Maps. However, to do so, Google must balance data collection with its "Don't Be Evil" creed. While consumer data is crucial for Google and helpful to users, some people don’t want to be disturbed.

Google has released various tools, browser extensions, and settings to prevent user data from being tracked by its services, but some consumers still have concerns. Amid the fallout from the "PRISM" scandal, Google finds itself in a difficult position — it needs to reassure users that it is not involved and continues to work hard to ensure the confidentiality of all search queries. Of course, all this while keeping a backdoor open for ad clicks.

Note: Although Google considers September 27 as the company's birthday, the "officially recognized date", Page and Brin actually registered the company on September 4, 1998. The company was established three days later, and the domain registration date was September 15.
