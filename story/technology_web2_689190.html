Uniqlo's slogan is democracy and freedom.

by wgyuvs8a on 2012-03-03 20:02:09

Uniqlo's previous slogan was "freedom and democracy," which was perhaps too serious for a fashion brand. Sato and Yanai Tadashi had multiple direct discussions and sketched out a vision: Uniqlo needed to be "aesthetically conscious ultra-rationality." The meaning of this complex phrase is that Uniqlo products are not only aesthetically pleasing but also offer great value for money.

"I want to strongly convey this characteristic that can proudly stand up to the world," said Sato. He immediately removed all of Uniqlo's old logos, changing it from a dark red to pure red and simplifying the font to just its skeleton. Sato also designed a katakana version of the Uniqlo logo in Japanese, and he didn't care at all if it appeared in Japanese in overseas markets.

Yanai greatly appreciated this logo. He said, "Even if foreigners can't read it, it sufficiently expresses the essence of Uniqlo, 'surely able to release strong power overseas.'"

When this logo appeared on the Uniqlo New York SOHO flagship store that opened in November 2006, American media gave two evaluations: "reminds one of the Japanese flag" and "unprecedented audacity."

This was exactly the effect Sato wanted. Due to historical reasons, Japanese companies usually act low-key in the European and American markets, even well-known brands like Toyota rarely do extensive corporate promotions. Uniqlo's actions were completely different; apart from the katakana logo, there was also the slogan "From Tokyo to New York."

All of this was done to reshape Uniqlo's brand recognition. Sato introduced Yanai to Japan's "Flash Master," multimedia designer Nakamura Yuji, asking him to redesign Uniqlo's American website. In addition, Sato brought in top international models to shoot print advertisements for Uniqlo, and these fashionable posters were quickly posted in Uniqlo stores around the world.

The New York flagship store became an instant hit, and the overseas market began to shift towards Uniqlo's Taobao flagship store. Sato thus earned the title of "design magician who can drive sales." He then designed a new corporate identity mark for Uniqlo's parent company Fast Retailing - a pure red inverted triangle cut into three pieces. In addition to Uniqlo, Fast Retailing also manufactures and sells mid-to-high-end brands such as Theory. Yanai was very satisfied with this design, feeling that his sense of "climbing upward" and "sharpness" were fully expressed.

This kind of atmosphere permeated the products. Compared to H&M and ZARA's style of closely following international runways, Uniqlo tends to subtly "become trendy." They don't miss any popular elements, such as leggings (tights), striped marine styles, and intricate prints. But Uniqlo has more unique tricks up their sleeve - the professional t-shirt brand UT.

Sato Kazuhiko expanded a completely new business model for Uniqlo. He told Yanai: "The Uniqlo brand is like a media platform; there must be something that only Uniqlo can do." After reviewing all the products, Yanai answered: "That's t-shirts."

T-shirts are the simplest product among all clothing items and have great potential to become an ideal business model. Uniqlo launches five hundred varieties of t-shirts each season. But this could also become a disadvantage - being able to buy them anytime indeed provides reassurance, but it also means lacking the important labels of fashion and personality. Moreover, once the variety of t-shirts increases, after customers spread out the t-shirts to confirm the patterns, the store becomes messy.

It's time for the organizing technique to take effect.

Sato attempted to propose a commercial system. System development is more important than product production. "Just like Google and YouTube, we must be a brand that provides a groundbreaking system. Once the infrastructure is completed, adding content will allow us to become a global commercial brand and actively promote various collaboration projects."
