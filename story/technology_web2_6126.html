Web 2.0 Revolution (Part 4): The Google Paradigm

by prisco on 2006-09-15 09:42:19

◎Google Can Profit Without Monopolizing

In February 1999, the author proposed the following rule: "Media must monopolize or operate in an oligopoly to profit solely from advertisements, but the internet cannot be monopolized. Therefore, it can only seek other ways to profit beyond advertisements." This was based on a deep reflection of the nature of media itself, though few believed it at the time.

Although this line of thinking has ultimately been proven correct over time, the author overlooked the continuous decline in storage and bandwidth costs on the internet, combined with constant technological advancements. These factors have accumulated enough energy to significantly alter the cost structure of online media, thus overturning the old rules.

"Profit from advertising without monopolizing the internet"—this is the new paradigm that Google has brought to the world. "Users don’t need to stay on the search engine for long; the best outcome is for them to quickly find what they’re looking for and leave early"—this too is a new paradigm introduced by Google.

Ten years ago, search engine operators worried about the low "average page views per visitor," so they developed countless services in hopes of keeping visitors on their sites longer to generate more page views and sell more ads. However, Google took the opposite approach, delivering what felt like a wake-up call to past operators.

There are two key technical aspects behind Google's success: improving search accuracy and reducing average search costs (a point rarely mentioned). On the business side, there are two critical factors: AdWords, the keyword-based advertising system, and AdSense, which shares profits with other website operators.
