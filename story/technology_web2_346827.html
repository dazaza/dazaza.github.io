Synopsis:

Cinema Paradiso is an Italian film about movies, a classic that must be mentioned in the history of cinema. Directed by Giuseppe Tornatore, a new force in Italian Neorealism, who later directed films such as "Starring Role" and "The Star Maker," each with excellent results. "Cinema Paradiso" won numerous awards including the Cannes Film Festival Jury Special Prize, Golden Globe Best Foreign Language Film, and Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film.

The story follows a young boy named Toto growing up in Giancals village on the island of Sicily. Alfredo is the projectionist at the "Paradise Cinema," and through their shared love of movies, they form a mentor-student bond. Alfredo serves as a guide throughout Toto's childhood, adolescence, adulthood, and even old age, guiding his growth. After Alfredo's death, he leaves Toto a reel of film, rekindling thirty years of lost memories and emotions.

In the southern Italian town of Giancaldo, young Toto not only loves watching movies but also enjoys seeing Alfredo "cut" films. In the 1940s, movies had to be inspected by priests before being shown, and any scenes deemed inappropriate (like kissing scenes) were strictly cut out. When the audience noticed that kissing scenes were missing again, they would boo or even spit; during emotional moments, the audience would collectively burst into tears. The cinema was always lively.

Little Toto treasured the reels that brought magical images when they spun, and his dream was to become a movie projectionist like Alfredo. However, Alfredo saw Toto's intelligence and believed he had a bright future ahead of him. He advised Toto to leave the small town: "Don't stay here too long, or you'll think this place is the center of the world."

Though Toto struggled to understand Alfredo's advice, he continued to learn about movie projection from him every day. Kind-hearted Alfredo organized an outdoor screening so more people could watch, but the film caught fire. Toto saved Alfredo from the flames, but Alfredo lost his sight. Toto became the only person in the town who knew how to project movies, taking over as the town's projectionist.

Toto grew up and fell in love with Elena, the daughter of a banker. Their pure first love was beautiful, but their vows were interrupted by Elena's father. Toto went off to serve in the military, while Elena went to university. Heartbroken, Toto left the town to pursue his dreams...

Middle-aged Toto returned to his hometown and met Elena again, discovering Alfredo's painstaking efforts: Alfredo had secretly hidden Elena's messages, entrusting his unfulfilled life dreams onto this bright and sunny youth. To avoid Toto's future being hindered by love, Alfredo concealed that Elena had come looking for Toto. Toto found the note Elena left him amidst the ruins of the cinema, recalling past events, where the twists and turns of life's opportunities have already passed, making it impossible to calculate the gains and losses of partings and reunions.

In the small town of the homeland, television has replaced movies, and the once bustling cinema is now dilapidated and about to be demolished to build a parking lot. People from the town gather to watch the demolition, curious young people and nostalgic elders alike. The Paradise Cinema, which once housed the joys and sorrows of the townspeople, collapses with a loud crash.

Toto returns to the city and opens the gift Alfredo left him: a reel of film containing all the deleted kissing scenes. The reels that little Toto once desperately sought but couldn't obtain are now given to him by old Alfredo, along with some cherished memories, lost years and dreams, and the era of movies that once brought people heavenly happiness.

Behind-the-scenes Production:

About the Film:

This film is the breakthrough work of Italian director Giuseppe Tornatore and one of the most familiar and moving European contemporary art films to Chinese audiences. It has a heartwarming bittersweet quality that releases the infinite charm of cinema. This film is a heartfelt tribute to the beloved filmmakers and moviegoers, winning the Cannes Film Festival Jury Special Prize and the Oscar for Best Foreign Language Film.

For those living in the mundane world, the cinema may indeed be a worldly paradise. The characters in "Cinema Paradiso," whether famous directors, projectionists, or viewers, exhibit a special way of life: their connection to movies far exceeds daily life. Their central scene of activity is the cinema. Relatedly, it seems that the theme of their lives is also cinema. Touching is the fact that the love scenes in the movie are cut one by one, and when people reconnect them, this thought-provoking, reflective, and impactful theme becomes even more profound and powerful.

Memories form the basic structure of the film. Nostalgia naturally flows into these memories, becoming a prolonged tone. The rise and fall of the Paradise Cinema correspond very clearly yet emotionally to the rise and fall of a culture. The constant flashback shots intertwined with present-day shots create a strong contrast: Little Toto grows up to become a great director, the old projectionist has passed away, and the once-packed cinema is instantly reduced to ashes. This intense contrast technique gives the film's temporal changes a powerful charm that reveals time itself.

Starting from childhood Toto's recollections, then young Toto's, until Toto leaves the small town, the recollections end, flashing back to reality, where Toto takes a plane back to the small town. This is the backbone of the film's narrative structure. It should be noted that the recollection part develops smoothly like a stream, without a single flashback to reality. The purpose of choosing a simple and natural narrative structure is clear: when reminiscing, people often choose a quiet place and don't want to be disturbed. The deepest fragments of memory need to be carefully combed through. The various elements of the film are handled naturally and smoothly, for example, when middle-aged Toto lies in bed reminiscing, the sea breeze blows into the room, and the wind chimes jingle. The camera then cuts to young Toto sleeping in church, holding the priest's bell in his hand. Another example is Alfredo's hand touching young Toto's face, and when the scene unfolds, Toto has grown up.

On the main narrative line, there are several vivid branches that complement the main line beautifully. For instance, Alfredo symbolizes the development of cinema; he started projecting films in the silent movie era. When he and Toto were happiest together, it was also the time when people loved movies the most. When he lost his sight in the fire, Toto took his place, continuing to bring joy to people. Meanwhile, the interwoven films also demonstrate the development of cinema in terms of technology, form, and content (such as the evolution of film stock, from kiss scenes being cut to pornographic films, from black-and-white to color films, etc.). When Toto falls in love, television is born, quietly launching an attack on cinemas. After Alfredo's death, the Paradise Cinema is destroyed, seemingly symbolizing the end of an era where movies are no longer an essential part of people's lives.

About the Director:

Giuseppe Tornatore is a rising star in the Italian neorealist school of filmmaking, born in 1956 in Bagheria near Palermo. Tornatore began working at a very young age, initially as a photographer, and his photos were published in different photography magazines. At the age of 16, he participated in two plays by Pirandello and De Filippo, after which he began to enter the film industry, mainly producing documentaries. This early work experience greatly influenced his later film style.

From 1979, Tornatore closely collaborated with the Italian national television station RAI, producing a large number of programs. He also participated in scriptwriting and assistant directing, undoubtedly accumulating valuable experience for his future endeavors. In 1986, Tornatore directed his first feature film: "The Man Called Professor," which made him an overnight sensation, earning him the Silver Ribbon Award for Best Director from the Italian Film Journalists Association and the Golden Globe Award for Most Promising New Director. The film that truly put Tornatore on the map was "Cinema Paradiso" in 1988. This warm and touching film not only showcased the magic of cinema but also allowed us to reflect on our childhood dreams with a light-hearted attitude. The film's success was unprecedented, winning both the Oscar and Golden Globe Awards for Best Foreign Language Film, and receiving numerous accolades at major European film festivals. Although Tornatore's filmography is not extensive, most of his works are set against the backdrop of his hometown, Sicily, with themes focusing on youthful aspirations and elderly reminiscences, and almost every one is a meticulously crafted masterpiece.

About the Music:

Ennio Morricone is currently the most prolific film score composer, starting with his debut work "The Fascist" in 1961, he has composed music for nearly 500 films! He even holds the record of scoring music for 27 films in one year. Versatile Morricone can handle the music for any type of film. In 2006, the Academy Award presented him with the Lifetime Achievement Award.

As for Morricone's collaboration with "Cinema Paradiso," it was indeed a classic partnership. The slow piano melody is tender and serene, evoking a sense of nostalgia and beauty. All the love around Toto is perfectly highlighted. The most famous song is the theme song sung by Ennio Morricone and Dulce Pontes, which is elegant and touching.

Fun Facts:

After the fire destroyed the projection room, the new projector installed was modeled after the Victoria IV era, handmade by Cinemeccanica S.p.a in Milan, Italy.

Director Giuseppe Tornatore initially viewed this film as an obituary, announcing the end of traditional cinemas (as depicted in the film) and the conventional way of making films. However, after the film's huge success, he stopped mentioning this.

When Salvatore returns home, he sees his mother preparing his room. The painting on the wall is from Federico Fellini's movie "The White Sheik" (1952), starring Leopoldo Trieste, who played Father Adelfio in the film.

Twelve years after the original release, a director's extended edition was released, adding fifty minutes of content, mainly focusing on the love story of young Toto and his reunion with his former lover as an adult.

In the official version, Salvatore returns home without meeting his first love. In the director's cut, not only does he meet Elena, who is married to one of his classmates and has a daughter, but they also have a relationship. This might be one of the reasons why the film received an R rating; another reason is Salvatore's encounter with a prostitute on the cinema's bench. When saying goodbye to Alfredo, he encounters some acquaintances at the cinema entrance, and Salvatore spots her from afar. Additionally, the director shares some personal reflections on the shooting process, explaining some editing decisions, such as whether or not to include the meeting with Elena.

Bloopers:

When Toto was a child, during the school exam scene, we see he is left-handed, but as an adult, he uses his right hand to mark the calendar.

Factual Error: The 1954 calendar is incorrect. The calendar shows April 1, 1954, as Friday, but it should be Thursday. Other dates are correct.

Anachronism: In one scene, the movie shown on the screen is "And God Created Woman" from 1956. Several scenes later, Salvatore is marking a calendar labeled 1954. "And God Created Woman" was released in 1956.

When Toto was a child, during the exam scene where he throws answers to Efreet, in the first shot, he throws with his left hand, but in the next shot, he catches with his right hand.
