Compared with European and American countries, domestic enterprises lag far behind in risk management. To enumerate, there are five major black holes in enterprise risk management.

High-risk investment management black hole

With the improvement of the scale and internationalization level of domestic enterprises, many large enterprises have begun to use financial derivatives to carry out investment activities. Initially, this seems to be a beneficial experiment in capital operations, but due to insufficient understanding of high-risk investments and the lack of effective management control from regulatory departments and within the enterprise, failures are not uncommon. China Aviation Oil (Singapore) Corporation lost $550 million in crude oil futures trading, causing huge economic losses to the country, but the disaster does not stop there. The Singapore stock market once heavily suppressed Chinese stocks, causing other innocent enterprises to suffer collateral damage. In the long term, it seriously damaged the international image of domestic enterprises.

"Central enterprises should correctly understand and use financial derivatives, strengthen risk management and control of financial derivative transactions, and must not blindly participate in or speculate on high-risk investment businesses, nor should they avoid them altogether." Li Rongrong, Director of the State-owned Assets Supervision and Administration Commission (SASAC), set the tone for limiting central enterprises' high-risk investments. He also emphasized that using financial derivatives to hedge and offset risks is a method that international enterprises should master. To enhance supervision over central enterprises, SASAC successively issued the "Interim Measures for the Supervision and Management of Central Enterprises' Investment" and the "Guidelines for Comprehensive Risk Management of Central Enterprises." This year, SASAC will also specifically study and formulate regulatory systems for overseas investments by central enterprises to standardize their overseas investment activities and reduce the risks associated with such investments. It can be predicted that a series of regulatory measures will standardize the high-risk investment behavior of central enterprises, but the degree of improvement will depend on the efforts of central enterprises themselves to implement comprehensive risk management. Considering their industry position and exemplary effect, the standardization of central enterprises in this area will have a positive impact on other state-owned enterprises and private enterprises.

Major investment project management black hole

The basis for SASAC's management of major investment projects by central enterprises mainly comes from the "Interim Measures for the Supervision and Management of Central Enterprises' Investment," while local state-owned enterprises mainly follow the relevant policies issued by local governments.

In recent years, local governments have increased their supervision of state-owned assets and have successively issued policies for the risk management of major investment projects by local state-owned enterprises. Shandong Province has promoted the implementation of an argumentation system for major investment projects, improved the board system of provincially managed enterprises, fulfilled the provincial enterprise dispatched finance director system, established a complete organizational system for the supervision of state-owned assets, and implemented a responsibility pursuit system for major investment decision-making errors. Guizhou Province has strengthened intellectual property protection in major economic activities through the establishment of special review mechanisms.

In 2007, SASAC will further strengthen its supervision of enterprise investments. Based on continued research into post-evaluation indicator systems for new types of investment activities such as equity investments and asset mergers and acquisitions, SASAC will increase the intensity of post-evaluation of specific projects. It initially considers conducting post-evaluations of investment projects from one enterprise in each business sector to further promote enterprises' improvement in input-output efficiency.

The state's enhanced supervision of major investment projects is also reflected in foreign acquisitions. In recent years, foreign capital entering China has shown trends of large scale, multi-industry involvement, and pursuit of controlling stakes and industry monopolies, which have brought significant impacts on domestic enterprises and industries, and even posed threats to China's industrial security in some areas. For this reason, the state issued the "Regulations on Foreign Investors' Acquisition of Domestic Enterprises," and the National Development and Reform Commission completed a report on the review of foreign acquisitions, recommending key reviews of major foreign investment projects in nine industries involving the national economic lifeline and nine key areas.

Subsidiary management black hole

For enterprises, establishing subsidiaries is an effective way for异地operation and rapid expansion. Since subsidiaries are closer to the local market than headquarters, they can respond quickly to market changes, better grasp local market needs, collect local market information for headquarters, and promote the enterprise image locally. However, the saying "distant mountains and remote emperors" suggests that in the management of subsidiaries by headquarters, the choice between centralization and decentralization becomes difficult. Centralization allows strict control over subsidiaries but loses the ability for quick responses; decentralization grants subsidiaries autonomous operating space but makes it easy to lose control. Therefore, balancing centralization and decentralization directly affects the risk level of headquarters' management of subsidiaries.

China Aviation Oil (Singapore)'s failure in crude oil futures trading was mainly due to the lack of effective internal risk management. Relevant departments had already issued the "Measures for the Management of Overseas Futures Hedging Business of State-Owned Enterprises," imposing strict restrictions on related businesses of state-owned enterprises. However, China Aviation Oil lacked supervision over the funding authority and stop-loss points of its Singapore subsidiary, leading to repeated losses in futures trading until it became irretrievable. President Chen Jiulin concurrently served as vice president of the group, dominating the subsidiary and making it difficult for the group board to restrain him. The internally formulated "Risk Management Manual" was practically useless.

Overseas accounts receivable management black hole

Under the impetus of globalization, domestic enterprises have accelerated their "going out" steps. The Ministry of Commerce estimated that China's total trade volume in 2006 would exceed $1.75 trillion, with exports reaching $963 billion, ranking high globally. However, accompanying the increase in trade volume is the domestic enterprises' overseas accounts receivable. To date, more than $100 billion in overseas accounts receivable are expected to be unrecoverable, making overseas accounts receivable a significant risk for domestic enterprises.

With the improvement of marketization levels, credit transactions gradually replaced cash transactions as the mainstream settlement method for enterprises. In European and American countries, credit transactions account for up to 80% of total transactions, while in China, this proportion is only 20%. Despite this, the bad debt rate of European and American enterprises is only 0.25-0.5%, whereas that of domestic enterprises is several times higher. The average collection period for enterprise accounts receivable is 45 days in the United States and 125 days in China, indicating the low level of credit risk management in domestic enterprises. Nevertheless, domestic enterprises extensively adopt credit transaction methods in foreign trade. The main reasons are that most of the products exported by domestic enterprises have low technological content, so they can only compete on price, settlement methods, and payment terms. For example, choosing credit transaction methods to save 5% of the letter of credit handling fee, after goods are shipped, most companies wait until the end of the payment period to handle collection business. Additionally, the performance target pressure of state-owned enterprises in foreign trade also prompts them to take export risks to achieve performance targets. It cannot be ignored that China's long-term strict control over overseas investment and foreign exchange also leads some enterprises to manipulate overseas accounts receivable to avoid approval and alleviate the financial pressure on overseas companies.

Changhong was once a leading domestic home appliance enterprise with excellent performance and a good corporate image. However, to expand its overseas market, it engaged in substantial credit transactions with the US company APEX, resulting in overseas arrears amounting to 3.9 billion yuan, severely affecting the normal operation of the enterprise. Changhong's trust managers made a one-time provision of 2.6 billion yuan for bad debts in 2004 to show their determination to make a comeback, but this also caused the enterprise to suffer severe shocks in the securities market and failed to recover its strength.

Changhong's case is not unique. Large-scale credit sales are widespread in the export trade of domestic enterprises, especially among state-owned enterprises, making the management of trade risks a major issue that domestic enterprises urgently need to resolve. To solve the problem of overseas accounts receivable, enterprises can entrust professional collection companies, but they must bear high collection commissions; arbitration methods can also be used, but enterprises need to invest substantial human and material resources, and the enforcement of arbitration results poses challenges; enterprises can also apply for import-export credit insurance, such as factoring business. Fundamentally, enterprises should establish an internal credit management system of "3+1". "3" refers to the pre-credit management stage's credit investigation and evaluation mechanism, the mid-credit management stage's debt guarantee mechanism, and the post-credit management stage's accounts receivable management and recovery mechanism. "1" refers to establishing a credit management department.

State-owned enterprise financial audit black hole

The most convincing example regarding the financial audit black hole of state-owned enterprises is SASAC's surprise inspection of the financial audit reports of 181 central enterprises in 2004. The inspection found that 120 enterprises had a considerable portion of their financial conditions not included in the financial audit reports; 30 enterprises' financial audits were incomplete or superficial; 13 enterprises' financial audit reports conclusions were contrary to reality; and 13 enterprises' financial audit reports had technical issues. Although it has been more than two years since that inspection, various signs indicate that the financial audit status of state-owned enterprises remains unsatisfactory.

Based on this, the state has strengthened auditing supervision of central enterprises. The National Audit Office determined three priorities for auditing state-owned and state-controlled enterprises during the "Eleventh Five-Year Plan" period: First, checking the authenticity of enterprise accounting information, especially profits and losses, severely punishing all kinds of falsification behaviors and major violations of laws and regulations. Pay attention to the management and use of marketing expenses, combating commercial bribery. Second, evaluate the fulfillment of economic responsibilities by enterprise leaders, check problems in major business decisions and business management, promoting the enhancement of management awareness, responsibility awareness, and benefit awareness. Third, pay attention to prominent problems in enterprise management, check, analyze, and evaluate the quality status and preservation and appreciation of enterprise assets, revealing the loss of state-owned assets and harm to employee interests.

In addition, SASAC has made strict regulations on the financial final account audits of central enterprises listed companies, requiring central enterprises to strengthen the management of financial final account audit work for affiliated listed companies (including offshore listed companies with entities in mainland China). Specific requirements include: Reasonably arranging the work plan for listed company financial final account audits, not allowing delays in listed company annual financial final account announcements to affect the overall progress of enterprise group financial final account work; carefully clearing investment, dealings, and related party transactions between the enterprise and its affiliated listed companies to ensure the authenticity and completeness of audit results; and ensuring that the format and content of information disclosed in affiliated listed company audit reports meet the unified requirements of financial final account work.

Saudi Mecca Light Rail Project made China Railway Construction undergo a severe test, winning respect from around the world. At the same time, it posed a major challenge for the company: how to further enhance the company’s risk prevention awareness and risk control capabilities in the process of going global or future growth!

Chairman Meng Fengchao of China Railway Construction Corporation mentioned in the 2011 work conference that one of the prominent issues facing China Railway Construction was the “weakness in risk control capability.”

The weakness in risk control capability has become a "soft rib" for enterprise growth.

In the past, we often said that the construction industry was a high-risk industry, but this was mainly from the perspective of safety. Undoubtedly, compared with other industries, construction has obvious characteristics such as diverse working environments and uncertain engineering geological conditions. Safety production has always been the top priority in enterprise risk control. However, the Saudi project and the experience of enterprise growth show that risks in enterprises are not limited to the field of safety production.

Lü Duojia, chairman of Beijing Huaida Risk Management Consulting Company, said: "In 2008, the first risk for central enterprises was investment risk, including price, cash flow, safety, and environmental protection." He believed that the top ten global enterprise risks had changed compared to previous ones, in order: the major challenges of the international financial regulatory environment to enterprises, obtaining credit, slowing economic recovery or a second bottom recession, talent management, emerging markets, cost reduction, non-traditional market entrants, stringent environmental measures, social recognition risk, enterprise social responsibility, and alliances and mergers expanding business. Social responsibility was included in the risk list for the first time.

In 2004, the Enterprise Comprehensive Risk Management Research Group led by SASAC conducted a questionnaire survey in some state-owned enterprises. Quite a number of managers of state-owned enterprises believed that the biggest risks were intensifying market competition, national policy orientation, strategic decision-making mistakes, etc. Meanwhile, a statistical company in the U.S. conducted a questionnaire survey on a typical multinational company and found that the various risks encountered in the process of enterprise growth amounted to tens of thousands, of which about 2,500 could be controlled and managed by enterprises.

Although there are differences in statistical methods among countries worldwide, differences in survey objects, differences in thinking methods for classifying and confirming risks, and differences in results and data, one point is the same: risks arise due to the presence of a large number of uncertainties. In other words, where there are uncertainties, there are risks.

Generally speaking, risks can be divided into strategic risks, financial risks, operational risks, and hazardous risks. From the source of risks, they can be divided into external and internal enterprise risks. From the external environment, factors such as political instability, financial storms, changes in international exchange rates, influences of religious customs, unpredictable natural disasters, sudden events, changes in national leaders, adjustments in national economic policies, misinterpretations of foreign contracts and legal policies, etc., can lead to various risks in construction enterprises, especially in overseas engineering construction. From the perspective of internal enterprise operation and management processes, blind investment expansion, lack of prudent acquisitions and mergers, indiscriminate bidding and contracting, reliance on affiliations for contract undertakings, irregular operations, subcontracting and reassignment, the "big package mode" of dumping tasks, lagging management, inappropriate personnel selection, shortages of production factors, internal conflicts within the enterprise, tight funds, heavy debts, cash flow or supply chain breaks, etc., have all made enterprises pay heavy prices and exorbitant "tuition fees."

According to relevant data, among the Fortune Global 500 companies, one-third of the companies disappear from the list every 10 years, either declining or going bankrupt. Including Barings Bank, WorldCom, Ping An, Arthur Andersen, Delong, Bear Stearns, Merrill Lynch, Lehman Brothers, Morgan Stanley, and China's Giant Group, Sinopec, etc., all fell into irredeemable situations because they ignored the existence of risks when the enterprises were flourishing, even leading to bankruptcy.

On the other hand, companies that have consistently maintained a stable position among the Fortune Global 500 have deep insights and preventive measures against risks and crises. Weathersrup, head of PepsiCo, proposed the "Doomsday Management" theory at a time when the company was thriving. He frequently used impressive amounts of information and facts to make employees realize that "doomsday" was not far away, thereby stimulating employees' positive upward spirit. Bill Gates is also a person with strong risk awareness. He believes that risk and crisis awareness are the original driving force for Microsoft's growth. Microsoft's slogan is "No matter how great your product is, you are only 18 months away from failure." It is precisely this kind of risk awareness that has enabled Microsoft to grow to today.

Almost every risk or crisis faced by enterprises has happened before, but tragedies keep repeating themselves in different enterprises. The fundamental reason lies in the fact that enterprises have not incorporated risk control and risk management into their strategic management from the strategic height of enterprise growth, have not valued, studied, or excelled in drawing lessons from previously occurred crisis events. Zhou Fangsheng, former deputy director of the Enterprise Reform Bureau of SASAC, said: "Central enterprises are making investments and carrying out projects every day. Risk assessment is very important. Major projects should conduct full-process engineering risk assessments and risk management."

Risk management refers to the process and systematic methods by which enterprises control the influence of market changes and other uncertainties within acceptable and manageable ranges during the process of achieving future strategic goals. Currently, more than 40 countries and regions around the world, as well as local organizations, have passed legislation on risk management, formulated risk management standards and related procedures. In September 2004, the COSO organization in the United States published the framework for comprehensive risk management, which had a significant impact worldwide. Japanese companies such as Shiseido, JR West Japan Railway Company, and Panasonic successively established integrated risk management committees and risk management systems to comprehensively manage all risks across the entire company. The risk management system includes organizational structure, management system, committee responsibilities, risk classification, responsible departments, preventive measures, emergency response procedures, execution procedures, management supervision, corporate culture concepts, etc. Moreover, risk management is placed on par with strategic management, with annual risk assessments conducted. The assessment objects include those from political, economic, and social aspects, natural disasters, emergencies, environmental protection, business growth, business management, and basic work aspects. For the identified risk objects, according to the probability and impact degree, the first step is to establish a set of standardized and comprehensive early warning systems, which include prevention and avoidance, establishing contingency plans and organizational systems, and conducting simulation drills. Subsequently, information is collected and controlled for multidimensional risk identification. Then, isolate risk crises, build "firewalls," establish abandonment principles, ensure internal communication, adjust personnel reasonably, adopt appropriate decision-making models, and communicate effectively with the media in real time to achieve expected goals. Finally, through timely aftermath handling, conduct independent investigations, diagnose "risk sequelae," and implement systematic changes to fundamentally provide security guarantees for preventing and eliminating risks and crises.

There is a famous saying in China: "Born in adversity, die in comfort." Applying this saying to enterprise risk control is quite appropriate. One can say that our understanding of risks has gone through a long phase, and our exploration of risks is endless, with a long road still ahead. But one thing is certain: risk control is an inevitable choice for enterprises to achieve sustainable growth!
