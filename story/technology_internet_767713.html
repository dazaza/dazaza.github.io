Zuckerberg: Copycat craziness trips up Facebook's international growth plans

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 12:48:04

For market pioneers, imitation can be considered the sincerest form of flattery. However, when imitation starts to become annoying, it's a different story.

Facebook CEO Mark Zuckerberg stated that this social networking giant has begun to face such an irritating situation, as some social websites in markets outside the U.S. are too similar to Facebook both in appearance and functionality. In fact, this situation has already affected Facebook’s international growth prospects.

"Ultimately, copying always ends up being quite a boring thing. Outside the U.S., people have copied Facebook so much that it has made it harder for us (to grow internationally)." Zuckerberg said during the "Y Combinator Startup School" conference held over the weekend.

Zuckerberg pointed out that in these overseas markets, especially Russia, they were particularly adept at imitating Facebook’s features. "They copied Facebook so terribly that even Facebook finds it difficult to beat them," he said. "Since we founded Facebook almost 10 years ago, but to this day, we still cannot defeat the Russian copycats."

Zuckerberg provided an interesting reason for the predicament Facebook is facing. He pointed out that apart from sites copied by local Russians, many startups also build Facebook-like websites and push them to European countries. Zuckerberg noted that in these markets, the first movers among imitators enjoy the network effects brought about by "viral growth," which makes it more challenging for Facebook to expand its business in these markets.

Zuckerberg also pointed out that another issue is that the websites copying Facebook in markets like Russia aren't just simple social networks; they are also major hubs for illegal file upload hosting services. This means that for users who want to obtain pirated music and movies for free, these copycat sites are more attractive than Facebook.

Industry insiders point out that this is indeed true. If you compare Facebook's quarterly user growth rate in Europe with other regions (such as Asia, where Facebook faces fierce competition from local social networks), you will find that the former's growth rate has significantly slowed down over the past year. And for Facebook, Europe is a very important market because it has more consumers with disposable income compared to developing countries.

In other words, this is a problem that must be resolved for Facebook.

As for solutions, the "lockdown" policy adopted by Zuckerberg and another co-founder of Facebook, Dustin Moskovitz, in the early days of the company might work.

Another solution could be: if you can't beat them, join them. In the past, Facebook has also copied services from some competitors (such as Snapchat, Quora, and Foursquare). Industry insiders suggest that perhaps Facebook could adopt the "copying" strategy mastered by its overseas competitors.
