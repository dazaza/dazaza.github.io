If I drive a private car to sweep the streets in the United States, I think it's an ordinary thing.

The elderly sanitation worker rests on the car

Written by Xu Shuguang, Chairman of GreenTree Inn and 519.com Wine Direct Sales Network

In Shenyang, an elderly sanitation worker nearing sixty years old drives a private car worth more than 80,000 yuan to work sweeping the streets every day because he cannot bear to part with the sanitation work he has done all his life. This not only becomes a street scene in Shenyang, but also a hot topic among netizens recently. (October 26, New Express)

When I first saw this news, I thought it was incredible and suspected whether it was a hype. But after reading the whole news, I began to believe that there really is such an elder who loves his job so much. If this happened in the United States, I would think it's an ordinary thing, but in China, this looks too "strange".

First of all, sweeping the floor is considered as a "not decent" job in the eyes of ordinary Chinese people, and is often used by parents to scare children who don't study hard, saying "If you don't study well, you will end up sweeping the streets". However, this common cleaning job has been done with pleasure by this elderly sanitation worker for 34 springs and autumns. Even when he is old and facing retirement, he still cannot bear to give up this job, showing how deeply he is emotionally attached to this job.

Decades ago, the popular phrase "There are champions in all 365 professions" is rarely mentioned today. Nowadays, there are regular releases of the top ten industries with the highest salaries, and the difference in the number of applicants between popular and unpopular university majors is huge. Everyone wants to go to places where they don't have to work hard, get good treatment, and can enjoy a comfortable life. Jobs that require hard work, real skills, and fierce competition are avoided like the plague. A survey conducted by a US consulting company among 215 HR representatives and 862 employees in China found that the length of employment is getting shorter and shorter, and the frequency of job-hopping is increasing. Moreover, 61% of Chinese employees are ready to switch jobs. Another survey showed that 50% of people change jobs to pursue higher salaries. The restless attitude towards careers has become a kind of "virus" in China's current workplace. The professional loyalty cultivated during the planned economy era has disappeared, let alone loyalty to a seemingly "lowly" career.

What moved me most about this old man is that he said "I love this job". However, now it is probably difficult to find someone who genuinely loves their work from the bottom of their heart. More people regard their jobs merely as tools for making money, a bowl of rice, rather than a career to be achieved with one's entire life. In ancient China, many professions were passed down from father to son, and then from son to grandson, thus giving rise to many families renowned in medicine or carpentry. Oral tradition led to the creation of century-old brands. Although this family-style inheritance may not necessarily be worthy of promotion, it at least illustrates one point: any great achievement requires generations of people devoting their entire lives, continuously striving, refining, and contributing to achieve. This accomplishment first stems from the love and loyalty of treating a profession as a cause.

If every employee of our GreenTree Inn could be like this elderly sanitation worker, no matter how our lives change, not only treating work as a means of livelihood, but also as a part of life, and as one's own career to accomplish, I believe that it would lead to extraordinary improvements both for one's personal value and for the brand value of GreenTree Inn. If there ever comes a day like that, I will feel extremely gratified.
