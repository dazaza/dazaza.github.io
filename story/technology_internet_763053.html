3D Printing at the Crossroads: A Wealth-Building Wonder or a Money-Burning Gadget?

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 13:57:57

Nike's 3D Printed Concept Shoe

At the beginning of this year, the concept of 3D printing was extremely popular. Recently, it has once again become a focal point of attention across various sectors—following Nike’s release of the world's first 3D printed concept soccer shoe, both New Balance and Adidas, one of Nike’s competitors, have also begun using 3D printers to manufacture functional parts for athletic shoes. Meanwhile, at this month's Paris Fashion Week, Dutch female designer Iris van Herpen unveiled her "Wilderness Embodied" series, created using 3D printing and laser cutting technology.

However, it is worth noting that despite the increasing commercialization speed of 3D printing, even entering the realm of mass consumer goods and being frequently hyped in capital markets, it continues to face skepticism, with some questioning whether its supposed claim of sparking an industrial revolution is merely a buzzword.

Bullish Supporters -

Driving Personalized Products

The most impressive aspect of 3D printing lies in shortening the distance between ideas and physical objects. For many who anticipate personalized products, 3D printing is essentially a "dream-making machine."

From a commercial production chain perspective, 3D printing offers unique advantages: no need for large factories, truck fleets, transportation, supply chains, or even tariffs—it almost requires no intermediaries. Orders are customized according to demand, thus eliminating the need for inventory and warehouses. All that is required is a printer, raw materials, software, and a design blueprint.

"The future of 3D printing technology is undoubtedly bright," stated Shen Zhen, associate researcher at the Institute of Automation of the Chinese Academy of Sciences, in an interview with Yangcheng Evening News. Although its commercial application may not see significant development within the next one or two years, its growth will accelerate over the next five to ten years. Shen added that 3D printing holds significant cost advantages in producing small-batch personalized and custom-made products, especially those requiring high precision components, which would otherwise incur higher costs when produced using traditional manufacturing techniques.

In addition, 3D printing technology can make market research easier and more precise. Merchants simply need to print a few industrial product models using a 3D printer and display them in stores to directly test market and consumer preferences. The ability to accurately predict market expectations is crucial for determining the future success of any product, regardless of the industry.

Shen believes that while 3D printing itself might not constitute the third industrial revolution, the changes it brings to traditional manufacturing processes will trigger this revolution. Furthermore, the popularization of personal 3D printers will be a trend in the development of 3D printing technology over the next three years.

Related Stocks Experience Repeated Surges

Since the end of last year, the 3D printing concept has been one of the sectors most favored by investors. According to Sun Lipeng, investment advisor at Jufeng Investment Consulting, since December 4th of last year, the 3D printing concept index has seen a peak increase of nearly 100%. Despite recent adjustments, the increase still reaches 80%, far outperforming the overall market index of 4.77%. This has also led to the emergence of several strong-performing stocks, including Haiyuan Machinery, Nanfeng Shares, and Guangyun Da.

The popularity of the 3D printing concept originated from U.S. President Barack Obama listing it as a key direction in his "National Network for Manufacturing Innovation Plan" in 2012. The public began paying attention to 3D printing following the successful trial of the world's first 3D printed gun on May 5th of this year. Since then, 3D printing has received widespread media coverage. It can produce bones and hearts for medical purposes, satellite components demonstrating its potential in aerospace, food, clothing, shoes, cars, and even realistic dolls. Some have even started promoting the idea that the "third industrial revolution is imminent."

Peng Jun, investment advisor at Ping An Securities, considers 3D printing a revolutionary rapid prototyping technology, hailed as the third technological revolution, already industrialized in the United States. China has also introduced policies to vigorously support the 3D printing industry. From an industry perspective, listed companies have vast prospects for development.

Pessimists -

Just Supplementary, Not Mainstream

"If 3D printing were truly useful, I’d write my surname 'Guo' upside down," publicly declared Terry Gou, chairman of Foxconn. He expressed his lack of optimism towards 3D printing technology, stating that it cannot be applied to mass production. Therefore, he considers the notion of "3D printing being the third industrial revolution" to be mere hype. He further illustrated that, for instance, 3D printing cell phones is impossible due to the inability to add electronic components, making mass production unfeasible. Moreover, restricted by material limitations, there are very few other products that 3D printing can currently produce.

The traditional printing industry also believes that 3D printing technology will not pose a threat to it in the short term. Yu Shuzhang, president of Fuji Xerox (China) Co., Ltd., stated that digital printing still has significant growth potential over the next 5 to 10 years. He explained that despite digital printing existing for over 20 years, it still holds considerable growth potential, especially in the high-end printing market. On the other hand, although office printing may decrease, introducing color printing and providing printing solutions can still boost corporate revenue. "We are also researching 3D technology, but I still believe that over the next five years, the impact of 3D printing will not be too significant."

"3D printing is just a supplement to traditional craftsmanship." Dr. Wang Huaming of Beihang University stated that 3D technology is not revolutionary; it is merely an incremental (or additive) manufacturing technology. It does not bring entirely new finished products; similar results can be achieved through other methods with better mechanical properties. However, such technologies are suitable for design validation and small-scale production, so they do have their place. 3D printing technology is still developing, and the industry is far from mature. It cannot replace traditional manufacturing, and overstating its importance could mislead industrial development.

Material Restrictions and High Costs

"Opponents" cite another reason: 3D printing technology is limited by materials and has high cost barriers. Wu Cheng, vice general manager of the Rapid Manufacturing Center at Huazhong University of Science and Technology, once stated that using 3D printing to manufacture an automobile engine would cost 50 times the price of the car. Zijin Lide's 3D printer takes 8 hours to "print" nine golf balls.

In contrast to the speculative frenzy in the stock market, the capital returns of 3D printing in actual applications are disheartening. As one of the first domestic companies to venture into 3D printing, Wuhan Binhu Mechanical & Electrical Technology Industry Co., Ltd. has experienced more years of losses than profits. Its general manager, Zhou Jianguo, stated that most peers currently struggle to survive. Similarly, Chairman Lian Ning of Zijin Lide, China's largest and most widely commercially applied 3D printing company, admitted: "Our current cash flow and financial structure can only sustain normal operations. Expanding scale, making new investments, or recovering previous investment costs is basically impossible."

On the other hand, the popularization and application of 3D technology face bottlenecks due to material restrictions. Zhao Honghan, Deputy Secretary-General of the China Unsaturated Polyester Resin Industry Association and an expert in composite materials, believes that not all materials can be transformed into adhesives or powders suitable for printing. Currently, 3D printers can mainly handle a few types of materials, such as high molecular composite powders. Therefore, restricted by material processing technology, the performance of products made through 3D printing struggles to meet the stringent requirements of metal materials and cannot be used in critical component areas. (Reporters Liu Shan, interns Zhao Ping, Zeng Yanyan)
