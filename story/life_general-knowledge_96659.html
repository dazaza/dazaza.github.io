In-depth Analysis: How Scam Text Messages Make Money - Age1983

by hexiaoyao on 2008-10-15 22:27:39

Today, on the way back to Shenzhen, two young ladies who looked like junior high school students were discussing how to check the balance of a Unicom mobile phone. They both agreed that calling 10086 could help them check the balance of a Unicom card. What happened next? One girl said she couldn't check it because the response was in Cantonese...

This really shocked me……

A few days ago in the morning, I received such a text message:

My account can't be used anymore, please transfer the money directly to the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China. Card number: 6222 0219 0100 2162 xxx Name: Chen Xiaoke. (15102615xxx)

In general, we just laugh it off when we see such messages, delete them, or report them to the mobile company or the police. It's obvious that this is a fraudulent text message, so why do people still send these kinds of messages to defraud millions? Why would anyone believe the content of the message?

(A fraudulent text message using earthquake relief as an excuse)

Let's assume the following scenario:

1. The cost of sending one text message is 0.1 yuan.

2. Mass texting doesn't require much human resources.

3. Assume that out of 10,000 people, exactly one person believes the content of the message and transfers 10,000 yuan to this student named Chen Xiaoke (we're not discussing the reasons for now).

4. Then, the scammer can earn up to 10,000 - 10,000 * 0.1 = 9,000 yuan in income. Of course, other costs must also be deducted.

You can see that in this assumption, the probability of falling for the scam is 0.01%, but the scammer still gains a "profit" nine times relative to the cost.

Once you understand that this probability cannot equal zero, and it may even be higher than 0.01%, and furthermore, the amount transferred might be tens of thousands or hundreds of thousands, you will understand why so many people mass-send fraudulent text messages and why this "industry" is so profitable.

Compared to "one-ring scams," fraudulent text messages are more "effective." Below, we analyze why some people fall for certain types of fraudulent text messages:

One, One-time Fraudulent Text Messages

So-called one-time fraudulent text messages are those sent only once to the recipient, after which there is a certain chance (even if small) that the recipient will fall for it.

1. Fraudulent text messages from telecom operators

Don't think that only "criminals" send fraudulent text messages; telecom operators might also engage in such behavior, such as the following message:

"A telecommunications friend informs: To celebrate the breakthrough of 100 million telecommunications users, we express our gratitude to all users. If you forward this message to ten users, your account will increase by 188 yuan in phone credit. I've just tried it, and it's true."

When you actually fall for the temptation to mass-send this message, the operator secretly rejoices.

Reason for falling for it: Greed for small benefits.

2. Money Transfer SMS 1

As mentioned earlier:

"My account can't be used anymore, please transfer the money directly to the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China. Card number: 6222 0219 0100 2162 962 Name: Chen Xiaoke"

Why would anyone believe it?

Let's consider the following scenarios where the recipient might transfer money to the scammer's account:

(1) The party knows someone named Chen Xiaoke.

(2) The party has borrowed money from someone and suspects that this is a reminder to repay.

(3) They need to transfer money to another person and mistakenly believe this is the temporary new account.

Is it difficult for this text message to encounter any of these three types of people?

The answer is yes, it's difficult, but the probability is not zero. Based on the assumption mentioned earlier, scammers can optimistically wait for their prey.

Reason for falling for it: Carelessness.

3. Money Transfer SMS 2

Perhaps you have received a text message like this before:

"Mom, I'm on the road, my stuff and wallet were stolen, please transfer 200 yuan to ICBC XX, this is my friend Xiao XX's phone, I don't have money on me, remember."

If you are a man, obviously you wouldn't believe this text message no matter what, but what if you are a mother with a son studying far away?

Because of concern for your son, you might very well believe this text message.

Similar to "Money Transfer SMS 1," this type of text message operates on a certain probability basis, but compared to the previous text message, the likelihood of the recipient falling for it is greater, though the amount of each fraud is relatively smaller. Because if you assume you are a student asking your mother for 2000 yuan at once, it would raise a lot of suspicions.

Reason for falling for it: Worry about family members.

4. Prize-winning SMS

"One-ring scams" often use winning prizes as a means of deception, and text messages can do the same. Since this type of fraud has become very common and familiar to us, most people no longer believe it, thus reducing the frequency of such fraudulent text messages.

The reason for falling for it is simple: Greed for small benefits.
