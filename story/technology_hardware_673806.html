Insiders Sigh That Actors' Salaries Are Rising Faster Than Housing Prices, Market Chaos Is The Main Reason

by dahslkh6 on 2012-02-23 22:12:50

There is a saying: what has been rising fastest in China in recent years? It's not the stock market, not housing prices, nor oil or gold prices... The answer is: actors' salaries! Yesterday, a post by industry insider and President of Mathe Media Culture, Chen Lizhi, also expressed such sentiments. Ren Zhiqiang, a well-known real estate developer, forwarded his post, agreeing and humorously adding: "It's rising faster than housing prices."

Producers, screenwriters, and others have complained about the rapid increase in actors' salaries. "A certain female actress who was paid 20,000 RMB per episode at the beginning of last year is now being paid 300,000 RMB per episode. A male actor who was paid 150,000 RMB per episode last year is now being paid 500,000 RMB per episode. Another male actor has even quoted 700,000 RMB per episode. As for movies, prices have suddenly skyrocketed; an actor who was paid 800,000 RMB is now demanding 3 million RMB, while one who was paid 1.2 million RMB a year or two ago is now asking for 6 million RMB," reports within the entertainment industry constantly mention exorbitant salaries. Yesterday, a post by Chen Lizhi, President of Mathe Media Culture, once again expressed concerns about this phenomenon. In the conclusion of his post, Chen Lizhi made an appeal: "Whether it's TV series or films, they have not developed healthily, but have instead experienced malignant growth, awaiting a reshuffle!"

Subsequently, when reporters contacted Chen Lizhi to further understand the situation mentioned in his post, he declined, stating that it was "inconvenient to say more." However, he did indicate that the rise in actors' salaries had become a significant trend in recent years, a feeling shared by those in the industry. Indeed, apart from Chen Lizhi, many producers have also complained about the "soaring" phenomenon of actors' salaries. Director Feng Xiaoning once told the media: "The combined remuneration of a few main actors amounts to astronomical figures, taking up over 50% of a film's total cost, yet their performances increasingly disappoint." He also lamented: "The excessive increase in actors' salaries constitutes a kind of economic crisis for production units."

Many screenwriters have also voiced their grievances. In one interview, a top-tier screenwriter told the reporter: "We first-tier screenwriters can earn only about a million RMB for writing a good script, whereas a well-known actor can make nearly ten million RMB from shooting a single movie. This situation will definitely affect screenwriters' enthusiasm for creation."

The madness of price increases: outrageous salaries, with monthly fluctuations being normal

Has the surge in actors' salaries truly become a major phenomenon in the industry? Yesterday, journalists contacted some television production companies. When discussing the issue of actors' salaries, almost everyone unanimously stated: "They've become outrageously high, especially in the past two years, rising very quickly."

Mr. Lu, a person responsible for negotiating with actors at a television institution, told reporters that the skyrocketing star salaries began in 2009. "Previously, filming one episode of a TV series, second-line actors' salaries ranged from 30,000 to 50,000 RMB, and now the basic quote is around 100,000 RMB. As for actors on the cusp between first-line and second-line, their quotes were generally around 100,000 RMB two years ago, and now these prices have reached 300,000 to 400,000 RMB. First-line actors go without saying, as their prices, which were approximately 150,000 RMB two years ago, are now generally between 400,000 to 500,000 RMB, with some even approaching a million RMB."

Miss Wang from a TV production company gave reporters some examples. "Two years ago, a second- or third-line male actor WT earned only 30,000 to 50,000 RMB per episode, but now it's around 150,000 RMB; a female actress GTT previously earned about 120,000 RMB per episode, but now her salary exceeds 300,000 RMB; another male actor WZ saw his salary multiply several times in the past two years, earning less than 100,000 RMB per episode two years ago, but now it's over 700,000 RMB; a big-name male actor SHL earns close to a million RMB per episode..."

Although actors' salaries have already reached astonishing heights, the price hikes continue unabated, becoming increasingly absurd. Mr. Lu told reporters that he is currently negotiating cooperation plans with some actors, and every time they communicate, the actors' salaries keep increasing. "Generally, they increase by half a million to a million RMB every half month. This isn't an isolated phenomenon; the price changes every month. And for top-tier celebrities, their salaries might even increase every few days."

Severe consequences: six to seven tenths of the investment goes to actors' salaries

The crazy surge in actors' salaries directly leads to increased costs in TV drama production. "In 2007, we could produce a very good piece with 8 million RMB, inviting big-name stars from Hong Kong, Taiwan, and other places, while still ensuring quality post-production. But now, let alone 8 million RMB, even 10 to 20 million RMB might not be enough just to pay actors' salaries." Mr. Zhang from a TV production company calculated for reporters that if a series invites two top-tier stars, at a minimum, these two would require 800,000 RMB per episode, resulting in a total of 24 million RMB for 30 episodes. Adding other actors' salaries, the total easily reaches 30 to 40 million RMB. "Now producing a TV drama, actors' salaries typically take up six to seven tenths of the overall investment, sometimes even more." Mr. Zhang pointed out that for a TV series produced last year with an investment exceeding 40 million RMB, a single actor's salary amounted to over 10 million RMB.

With too large a proportion allocated to actors' salaries, it certainly affects investments in other areas. "Some viewers may notice that the costumes in a certain drama appear shoddily made, props seem like they're from Taobao, and post-production seems incomplete... Yes, but what can we do? Most of the money goes to actors' salaries, so we have to save costs elsewhere." Miss Zhao from a TV production company lamented that the practice of robbing Peter to pay Paul is quite common in today's TV drama production. The current uneven quality of TV dramas is somewhat related to the excessively high proportion of actors' salaries.

Complicated reasons: chaotic markets lead to arbitrary price demands

Why has there been such a crazy surge in actors' salaries in the past two years? Several insiders analyzed the situation.

Firstly, the TV drama market has grown larger, attracting more people. Especially many big bosses and large companies have ventured into the film and television market. However, due to their lack of understanding of this market, they blindly throw money around, directly leading to the crazy surge in actors' salaries. "They have plenty of money, see a promising script, and insist on hiring a certain actor. If the actor refuses, they simply raise the salary, offering much higher than the market rate. There are only so many well-known actors domestically, and once one raises their price, others follow suit. When first-line actors raise their prices, second- and third-line actors won't lag behind either."

The influx of substantial funds is an important reason for the crazy surge in actors' salaries. The implicit rule of TV stations selecting shows based on actors also provides leverage for actors to demand higher salaries. "Now TV stations select shows based on actors. One can say that actors with salaries under 400,000 RMB hardly make any impact. TV stations look for top-tier celebrities with salaries over 400,000 RMB." Mr. Zhang said that this actor-based show selection allows these actors to confidently demand higher salaries.

Helpless solutions: production companies are at a loss, prefer not to produce shows

Are there any ways to mitigate the alarming rise in actors' salaries? Interviewed industry insiders all expressed helplessness: "We don't want them to rise so high either, but it's really hard."

Miss Zhao indicated that current countermeasures are somewhat helpless. "Generally, the only option is to promote new talent. But this comes with significant risks. Although new talents have lower salaries, they also have weaker market influence. If no one watches after production, it's a loss. So the most common approach is to use one or two top-tier celebrities along with newcomers to try to reduce the proportion of salaries."

Another method is to sign actors directly. Mr. Lu explained: "Now many TV production companies cultivate their own artists, which makes it cheaper to hire them. However, this also carries great risks. Once these actors become famous, they often leave for better opportunities. After gaining fame, their market expands, and they have more choices."

An even more helpless solution is to cut corners by reducing investments in costumes, props, and post-production to lower overall costs. "This directly affects the quality of TV dramas, but many production companies adopt this method nowadays."

Another approach is for multiple companies to jointly produce a TV drama. "Previously, one company could easily handle the production of a drama, but now, to ensure quality and market performance, several companies often need to collaborate. However, this significantly reduces the producer's profits, so some people choose not to produce shows anymore." Miss Wang expressed this sentiment.

During interviews, several industry insiders stated that they hope the industry implements measures to regulate the issue of excessively high actors' salaries. "People often bring this up at industry meetings, but there hasn't been a good solution yet. Of course, relying solely on policies won't work; the market must control it."
