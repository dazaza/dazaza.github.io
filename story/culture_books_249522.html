Give your child a good personality: Slow parenting

by jqrxass on 2009-10-19 21:17:08

Mr. Hei Youlong has long enjoyed the title of "Father of Chinese Carnegie." Despite his remarkable achievements, he believes that his greatest success in life does not lie in the fame, status, or wealth he has attained but rather in raising four outstanding children using the method of "slow parenting."

"Slow parenting" does not mean letting children do whatever they want; it means giving them opportunities to learn and grow through their experiences. From two books, one can see that Mr. Hei Youlong and his wife also went through phases where their children were rebellious, disobedient, and behaved poorly. The children were not the "good" kids that others might have perceived them to be. After more than 20 years of patient "slow parenting," all the children have grown up to establish their own families and careers. Each of them leads a fulfilling life both at work and at home, proving that Mr. Hei's "slow parenting" philosophy has yielded rich rewards.

In the narratives of each family member of the Hei family, readers can feel a deep sense of familial affection. From the stories shared by every member of the Hei family, readers can also sense the cohesion of a harmonious family and the positive, optimistic influence possessed by each individual.

**Slow Parenting - First Chapter: Opening the Time Capsule of Home**

Since the concept of slow living was introduced, "slowness" has become a quality of life that urban dwellers strive for. I recently came across a book on family education titled *Slow Parenting*. My first thought was whether the achievement-oriented Chinese parents would accept such a gentle and unhurried educational philosophy. Interestingly, there is another parenting book with a cover headline reading: "Admitted to Ivy League at Age One." It's clear that such a dazzling fast-track promise would attract more parental attention compared to the idea of slow parenting.

**Slow Parenting - Second Chapter: Home as a Concentric Circle**

In today's families, both parents often work full-time and return home late. Children are busy with homework, and parents are occupied with household chores. This hectic schedule leaves little time for parent-child interaction. Occasionally, on weekends, families may go out for a walk in the countryside. However, I suggest that parents, whether they go hiking or fishing with their children, should try to allocate more time to sit down and chat with their kids. These moments of heart-to-heart communication are the most valuable and important.

**Slow Parenting - Third Chapter: Gifts from Children to Parents**

My mother excels at praising her children. She often tells me, "Among all the children, I like you the most, and I love you the most!" But she says the same thing to my other siblings. While this is well-intentioned, it creates a situation where we compare ourselves to each other and become jealous. Everyone strives to be better than the others to gain Mom's praise and attention. Therefore, parents need to be cautious when praising their children, as it could lead to jealousy in social situations later in life.

**Slow Parenting - Fourth Chapter: When We "Get Stuck": The Hei Family's Carnegie**

Later, Mr. Hei Youlong considered leaving Guangqi Society and even ran for a legislative seat, which wasn't successful. The Hughes Aircraft Company in the U.S. wanted to rehire him, but I felt that going back to the old path wouldn't be meaningful. Fortunately, I still had Carnegie's contact information, so I encouraged Mr. Hei to give them another call. Treating it like a last-ditch effort, since it was an opportunity he truly desired, why not try again? Six months later, we made the second call to Carnegie. This time, their response wasn't as cold. They asked Mr. Hei to provide his background information and promised to consider it.
