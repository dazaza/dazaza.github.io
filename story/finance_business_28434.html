Fortune reviews 15 companies that are about to change the world.

by fenqu on 2007-08-29 15:05:59

Recently, the American Fortune magazine selected 15 companies that will change the world. It is believed that these companies have changed the rules of their respective fields and may disrupt existing industries while bringing new opportunities. Below is a brief introduction to these 15 companies.

First: Blinkx

CEO: Sulen贾·Kandretelrek

Blinkx was founded by Sulen贾·Kandretelrek, a 26-year-old computer science graduate from Cambridge University, and Kathy·Litewig, who is currently the company's CEO and marketing director. Although its reputation cannot yet match Google's, they specialize in using speech recognition software to convert video and audio data into text content, achieving more accurate full-text searches than other search technologies. Blinkx is also widely recognized as a leading player in the "video/audio search" niche market. After entering the video advertising market, the company will undoubtedly pose a serious threat to Google and Yahoo.

Second: Raydiance

CEO: Barry·Schuler

Raydiance's CEO, Barry·Schuler, is the former CEO of AOL. Raydiance is a company dedicated to applying ultra-short pulse laser technology to real-world applications. Ultra-short pulse lasers can perform a variety of precision tasks, such as etching semiconductors, killing tumors, detecting and dismantling roadside bombs, but so far there has been little commercial market scale.

Third: Expensr (www.coko365.com)

CEOs: Raman·Child and Sean·Gupta

Expensr (www.coko365.com) is a simple online income and expenditure accounting service. It’s not called financial management because its functions are too basic—apart from accounts, categories, and transaction records, it only allows for pie chart output by category and transaction timelines. However, the simplicity of its functions ensures the interface remains clean.

Fourth: ZipCar

CEO: Scott·Griffin

Zipcar, located in Cambridge, Massachusetts, is the largest car-sharing company in North America. It operates a self-service car rental business, which is a service calculated on an hourly or daily basis for immediate use. Today, Zipcar has over 70,000 consumers and business users, with nearly 2,000 cars distributed across ten states from Washington to Toronto. Customers can book cars via the internet or phone. Unlike traditional rental companies, Zipcar's parking spots are spread throughout its entire service area, making it just as convenient for short trips as for long ones, much like using one's own car.

Fifth: MFG.com

CEO: Fei Moqi

MFG.com, headquartered in the United States, is the largest platform for global manufacturing buyers and suppliers. In 2000, Fei Moqi began building MFG.com. At the time, his concept was to innovate and go beyond the traditional boundaries of the manufacturing industry. E-commerce websites targeting the manufacturing sector merely provided directories of buyers and suppliers along with names, telephone numbers, and faxes, thinking that would be enough to do business. He realized that in the manufacturing enterprise field, only by completing online transaction processing seamlessly and ensuring that buyers' requirements are fully communicated to suppliers, matching demands with production capabilities, could an effective trading platform be created.

In the past 12 months, MFG.com's total transaction amount has exceeded $2 billion. Last October, MFG.com established its China branch in Shanghai.

Sixth: Virgin Charter

CEO: Scott·Duffy

Virgin Charter is a network company specializing in booking air taxi tickets. As air taxis become increasingly popular, Virgin Charter hopes to become the Expedia of this industry.

Seventh: PatientsLikeMe

CEO: Ben·Haywood

The internet holds vast amounts of disease data, and PatientsLikeMe is a website that collects and organizes this data to assist patients in finding appropriate treatments. Despite having relatively few registered users currently, it has already become a gathering place for medical researchers.

Eighth: Bloom Energy

Bloom Energy's goal is to power homes using solid oxide fuel cells. The biggest challenge Bloom Energy faces now is the cost of its products; the company needs to reduce the cost of its equipment to below $10,000.

Ninth: Vanu

Vanu is a company that uses IP technology to develop multi-mode infrastructure for wireless operators. Vanu's Anywave base station upgrade software is expected to be available later this year. It is reportedly the first software radio product approved by the U.S. Federal Communications Commission, supporting both CDMA and GSM simultaneously through Vanu's portable waveform software on generic processors and industrial standard servers. This is significant for emerging small operators, meaning that GSM Anywave base station operators can upgrade to the CDMA system simply by downloading software to their current RAN infrastructure.

Tenth: Zink

Zink revolutionized the concept of digital cameras by combining a digital camera and printer into one device. Seeing this function might remind you of Polaroid instant cameras. This isn't surprising, as the core technology of Zink-brand digital cameras comes from Polaroid.

Eleventh: A123 Systems

A123 Systems is a high-tech company specializing in producing high-energy storage new materials, with partners including world-renowned tech companies like Motorola and Qualcomm. The company currently develops two types of products: one is an extremely high-energy battery based on new negative electrode materials, and the other is a revolutionary production technology that produces high-capacity, long-life batteries at a lower cost.

Twelfth: Renewable Energy Group

Renewable Energy Group specializes in biofuel development, and its current market share has reached 40%.

Thirteenth: Desktop Factory

3D printers have been used in the industrial field for over a decade, mainly during the design phase of automotive and aircraft components. Previously priced at about $100,000 each, the price has now dropped to $15,000 per unit. Desktop Factory's goal is to launch a 3D printer priced under $5,000 this year.

Fourteenth: Cree

Cree is an advanced semiconductor company that primarily uses its expertise in silicon carbide and gallium nitride raw material technology to produce new active semiconductors. Its products include blue and green light-emitting diodes, radio frequency, microwave devices, and power switching devices, and it is also developing ultraviolet laser-like bodies.

Fifteenth: One Laptop Per Child

"One Laptop Per Child" is a useful learning tool specially designed for impoverished children living in remote areas of the world. This laptop was collaboratively designed by many experts from academia and industry, showcasing outstanding wisdom and practical experience in this non-profit humanitarian project. This result reflects a unique collaboration in form and function, producing a clever, ultra-low-cost, energy-efficient, responsive, and durable machine. Through it, developing countries can rapidly advance in children's education, improving teaching content and quality in a timely manner. The current selling price of this laptop is $176.
