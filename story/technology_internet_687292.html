How to do early reading for babies before the age of 1

by lmxbbwudi23 on 2012-03-02 14:58:16

Cultivating a good habit of reading in babies can start before they turn one year old. However, infants under one year old often encounter many reading challenges, such as difficulty focusing attention, disliking reading, preferring to bite or tear books, etc. Meanwhile, parents are also confused about how to choose books and how to read them to their children. Now, we have compiled six common problems regarding reading for infants under one year old, which will surely be helpful to you.

1. When to start reading

When is the right time to pick up a storybook and engage in parent-child co-reading with your baby? In fact, you can start reading from the moment your baby is born because babies already possess auditory and visual functions at birth. However, during the first half-year after birth, most parents focus on feeding, diapering, and sleeping schedules, rarely considering sharing the joy of reading with their babies.

So, how do you choose the first book for your baby? For infants just starting to read, Ms. Xu suggests that parents buy black and white picture books or use large images (simple contrast patterns) found in daily life to start parent-child co-reading. Once the baby can reach out and grab the book, you can try letting him start reading on his own. Picture books, especially colorful and bright hardcover books, are more suitable for babies under one year old. One sign of a baby's reading behavior is liking to bite books. When a baby can focus his eyes on the pictures in the book, it indicates that he has developed reading cognition.

2. What posture should be used for reading

Hold the baby close to your chest, holding the book with both hands, and place it directly in front of the baby.

3. What methods should be used for reading

a. Point at the pictures in the book while telling the baby what they are, or directly recite the text in the book.

b. The tone of adult reading does not necessarily need to have ups and downs. At the beginning, you can read as if having a normal conversation. Once the baby shows more interest in reading, you can adjust the reading emotion according to the storyline.

4. What are the reading taboos

a. Babies under one year old have short attention spans and cannot focus their eyes for long periods. Do not force them to listen to a particular story.

b. Actions like biting, flipping pages, and hitting the book are common behaviors during this period and are part of the reading pleasure the baby gets. Therefore, these behaviors should not be considered strange or stopped, as doing so might reduce the baby's interest in reading.

5. How to choose books

Expecting a baby under one year old to gain specific education from reading is unrealistic. The purpose of reading at this stage is to make the baby friends with books and cultivate an interest in books and reading. Therefore, for babies under one year old, books are small, hard, safe, easy-to-grab-and-bite toys.

Types: Bath books, cloth books, doll books, hardcover books, etc., are all suitable.

Content: Focus on pictures, and the colors should be as bright and vivid as possible.

Size: Preferably lightweight, thin, and small-sized books that allow the baby to hold and flip pages by themselves.

Quality: Choose printed materials without added fluorescent color systems that can withstand grabbing, holding, biting, chewing, pressing, and squeezing without deformation.

6. Why do babies love to chew, tear, and throw books

When you want to enjoy the joy of parent-child co-reading, the baby often suddenly snatches the book from your hand and starts drooling profusely while tasting it. Even after "eating" it, the baby may carelessly throw it aside. What’s going on?

Experts say that putting things they can see and reach into their mouths to chew is a necessary sensory stimulation required for all one-year-old babies during their development. Therefore, parents' attitude should be to let it happen naturally instead of prohibiting it.

Of course, parents need to prepare for the issue of babies tearing and biting books. For example, choose specially designed toy books made for babies to chew (please pay attention to quality to avoid pigment or heavy metal contamination issues). Most of these books can be washed repeatedly. Additionally, since babies in this age group often drool, chewing books can help expel bacteria from the mouth. As long as the books are cleaned regularly, there is no need to worry too much about hygiene. Moreover, throwing things is also a good way to train fine and gross motor skills. If the baby likes to throw books like balls, parents don't need to worry and shouldn't mistakenly think that this indicates the baby doesn't like reading.
