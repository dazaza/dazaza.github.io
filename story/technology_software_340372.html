Abstract: Differential settlement at the junction of new and old road embankments has always been a challenge in highway widening projects. To reduce this issue, common methods used in plain areas during highway widening are proposed to minimize differential settlement.

Keywords: New and old road embankments, differential settlement, technical methods

0 Introduction

During the renovation of old roads in plain areas, in order to maximize the reduction of workload and avoid large-scale encroachment on farmland, water conservancy facilities, houses, etc., except for partial line adjustments, the existing highways are generally utilized, with single-side or double-side widening designs being adopted. The old road embankments, after years of settlement, have become relatively stable. However, the newly formed embankments must go through a settlement process, causing differential settlement between the new and old embankments. This leads to cracks and damage on the road surface, affecting the usability of the road. Currently, China's highway sector lacks mature and unified pavement widening design methods, making it necessary to propose more reasonable construction plans during construction to minimize the occurrence of differential settlement as much as possible.

1 Basic Ideas of Combined Section Treatment Technology

To prevent differential settlement at the joint sections, it is essential to enhance the overall performance of the new and old embankments. Therefore, the core idea of combined section treatment is to ensure the overall performance of the joint sections. Enhancing the overall performance requires starting with the foundation, reducing foundation settlement, then addressing the embankment by widening lateral connections, and finally avoiding the occurrence of differential settlement on the road surface.

2 Technical Methods

2.1 Original Ground Treatment

Due to limited funding in road network renovation projects, the foundation treatment plan is usually conservative, with the technical solution aiming to minimize financial input. As a result, conventional measures are typically adopted for ground treatment.

2.1.1 Water-free Foundation

In areas where the water table is deep, after clearing the surface, direct compaction construction can be carried out, or appropriate replacement of soil within a depth range of 30~50cm at the base with plain soil can be done. Once the specified compaction degree is achieved, embankment filling can proceed.

2.1.2 Permanently Waterlogged Foundation

In areas with shallow groundwater levels, it becomes difficult to compact the original ground. Generally, drainage ditches and sump pits are dug outside the slope angle line of the embankment, and water is concentratedly pumped out into nearby rivers. After the water level significantly drops, all silt is removed, and the base is replaced with plain soil, low-dose lime-soil, or crushed brick material within a certain range. Compaction is followed by compaction degree testing or settlement observation. Once relevant requirements are met, normal embankment filling can commence.

2.2 Embankment Construction

2.2.1 Old Embankment Slope Treatment

The soil at the old embankment slopes tends to be loose, with a lot of garbage and roots. The slope soil is generally excavated (usually around 0.3-0.5 meters) until reaching the compacted embankment boundary. Steps should be dug on the slope where the old and new roads meet to facilitate good bonding between the old and new embankments. If the old embankment slope is intact, the excavation line for the first step should ideally be 1~1.5 meters inward horizontally from the foot of the old embankment (after surface clearance), making the bottom corner line of the excavated step parallel to the old embankment line. The steps should have an inward tilt of about 3%. Each step height should be controlled between 0.5m-1.5m, but also determined according to the actual engineering situation for optimal dimensions.

2.2.2 Filling

Earthwork embankments must be constructed according to the designed cross-section, with layered filling and compaction. The maximum loose laying thickness per layer generally should not exceed 30cm, and the minimum compaction thickness of the last layer up to the top of the roadbed should not be less than 8cm. The width of the embankment fill should be wider than the design width on each side, ensuring that the compaction width is no less than the design width, and finally trimming the slope. Generally, horizontal layered filling construction is adopted for embankment filling. That is, the entire cross-section is divided into horizontal layers and filled layer by layer upwards. If the original ground is uneven, filling should start from the lowest point, and each layer should be compacted to meet specified requirements before proceeding to the next layer. The compaction degree detection of the embankment strictly follows the "On-site Testing Procedures for Highway Subgrade and Pavement" (JTG-2008). Only after achieving the specified compaction degree can the upper layer filling proceed. After completing the embankment construction, to ensure construction quality, high-tonnage (above 50 tons) vibrating rollers are often used to roll over the embankment several times, or one to two layers of lime-stabilized soil are added within the 20cm~40cm range at the top of the embankment to ensure that the embankment deflection meets the design standard.

2.3 Fill Material Selection

The embankment fill material can affect the compression deformation of the new embankment itself on one hand, and on the other hand, it influences the deformation modulus of the new and old embankments, thereby affecting the mechanical response of the pavement structure at the joint sections. Different embankment fill materials can lead to differences in the deformation modulus between the new and old embankments, resulting in varying anti-deformation capabilities. Therefore, the selection of fill materials for the new embankment is extremely important.
