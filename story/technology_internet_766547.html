Twitter IPO: some are glad while others are worried, spawning a large number of millionaires.

by anonymous on 2013-09-18 21:53:25

Twitter confirmed last week that it has filed an S-1 document with the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC). This marks the official start of Twitter's IPO process. In response, The New York Times published an article stating that Twitter's IPO will instantly create a large number of millionaires, with several venture capital firms benefiting by over a billion dollars. However, this has also left some investors who previously sold their Twitter shares regretful.

The following is the main content of the article:

In June 2007, Evan Williams was still looking for investors for the internet communication service he co-founded called "Twitter." He had already signed investment agreements with some well-known Silicon Valley investors, but he still sent an email to Dick Costolo, an old friend who had just sold his company to Google, asking if he was interested in investing $25,000 to $100,000 in Twitter.

Just three minutes later, Costolo replied to Williams saying, "I will invest $25,000 in Twitter. Thank you, Evan, I believe this will be very interesting."

Currently, Costolo has become the CEO of Twitter and one of the few independent investors who will benefit significantly from Twitter's IPO. On Thursday, Twitter confirmed that it has submitted its IPO documents to regulators and expects to go public by the end of this year or early next year.

Although many details of Twitter's IPO are still unclear, such as the starting stock price, according to sources familiar with Twitter's internal financial information, Costolo's initial investment in the company will eventually bring him a return of over $10 million, and he has also received millions of dollars worth of stock options after becoming the company's CEO.

Twitter declined to comment due to the confidentiality of its IPO documents.

It is worth noting that Williams' substantial wealth is not accidental, as his investment provided development funds for the early stages of Twitter, and he is still the largest shareholder of the company, which will certainly make him a billionaire after Twitter goes public. According to sources, venture capitalist Chris Sacca and venture capital firms Union Square Ventures and Spark Capital will each hold shares worth over $1 billion after Twitter's IPO, while other existing investors in Twitter will receive tens of millions to hundreds of millions of dollars in profits.

Some gain, some lose

However, not everyone is so lucky.

In the early days of Twitter, the company once faced difficulties and saved money through layoffs. Just two years ago, there were doubts about Twitter's survival and profitability because the company had not clearly stated how it would generate revenue from user posts limited to 140 characters. In 2011, many early investors and employees of Twitter sold their shares to DST Global, the Russian investment company eager to buy Twitter stocks at the time.

Dom Sagolla, one of the early employees of Twitter who was laid off in May 2006, said, "It was heartbreaking to watch Twitter enter my life and then be taken away."

Although Sagolla was one of the early employees of Twitter, he never owned any shares of the company. After leaving Twitter, he wrote a book introducing new users to Twitter and conducted paid public speaking events. Despite earning considerable income from these activities, Twitter's IPO will not benefit him in any way.

Analysts believe that although Twitter's IPO may not reach the scale of Facebook's last year (Facebook raised $16 billion in its initial public offering last year), it will not prevent the company from creating a large number of millionaires during its IPO. Bijan Sabet, a partner at Spark Capital and an early investor in Twitter, said, "For me personally, this is a once-in-a-decade, or even a once-in-a-lifetime investment opportunity."

If history can serve as a lesson, Twitter's successful IPO could provide much-needed seed funding for the next generation of tech companies that can compete with Twitter. James Moore, senior director of the Entrepreneurship Program at Columbia Business School and founder of technology industry consulting firm J. Moore Partners, said: "When you have a successful IPO, people gain more confidence in both public and private markets, and these two are directly related."

In fact, Williams and the other two founders of Twitter, Jack Dorsey and Biz Stone, are no longer involved in the company's daily operations and have embarked on their own new careers respectively. Meanwhile, dozens of other early employees of Twitter have already left the company, and many of them hold shares worth over a million dollars. Of course, the exact amount of their wealth will depend on Twitter's final IPO price, which will be determined by investment bankers evaluating investors' interest in Twitter and working with the company.

In March this year, private data provider VC Experts revealed that Twitter offered employees shares valued at $17 per share, meaning that the company's valuation after a round of financing in 2011 has exceeded $8 billion. Regarding this, Michael Pachter, an analyst at Wedbush Securities, said, "Since then, some interested investors have been willing to offer up to $30 per share in private transactions."

In recent stock market trading, internet company stocks have seen significant increases. Pachter revealed that this week, investors heavily invested in companies like Facebook, Netflix, and Pandora, pushing these companies' stock prices to record highs.

"The valuation of internet companies has approached madness, and investors are willing to pay more money to buy more internet company stocks, and Twitter will benefit from this," Pachter said.

Although most investors who sold their Twitter shares earlier now regret it, an early investor who sold millions of dollars worth of Twitter shares to the Russian investment company DST Global in 2011 revealed, "As one of the original investors in Twitter, we have already received quite substantial returns."

The only exception

It is worth noting that some early employees of Twitter did not receive any shares of the company, including Florian Weber, one of Twitter's earliest programmers. Weber was involved in the Twitter project even before it separated from Odeo. Weber said, "At the time, I was just a contractor, not a regular employee, so I didn't get any stock options. Moreover, I am from Germany, so getting company stock was not something I always pursued."

It is understood that Weber worked remotely from an office in Hamburg, Germany, and he had grown tired of this work style.

"As far as I know, I am the only person who did not get stock options," yet Weber does not feel particularly regretful about it. Because he has since founded his own company Amen in Germany and recently sold it to Tape.tv.

"I am very satisfied with my life," Weber said finally.
