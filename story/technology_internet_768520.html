Facebook's acquisition of Instagram does not help develop strategies

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 19:06:40

The acquisition of photo-sharing service Instagram by the social network Facebook seems to be a pretty good deal. However, in reality, it does not align with Facebook's development strategy.

When Instagram was acquired by Facebook in April 2012, it only had 30 million users; now, after 18 months, its user base has surpassed 150 million. This naturally brings to mind YouTube, which Google acquired, and whose popularity has grown day by day since its acquisition at the end of 2006.

However, the difference is that YouTube is already the dominant platform for online video sharing; whereas Instagram is far from being the dominant mobile photo-sharing platform.

Facebook's latest statistics show that 55 million photos are posted daily on Instagram, while 450 million photos are posted daily on Facebook. In comparison, photo-sharing app Snapchat also sees 450 million photos posted daily, and cross-platform chat app WhatsApp sees 400 million photos posted daily. Moreover, we do not know how many photos services like Line, WeChat, or other similar services post daily. Meanwhile, Instagram has 55 million monthly active users, while WhatsApp has reached 350 million users. Furthermore, there are many other similar services that post more photos daily than Instagram.

Therefore, Facebook's acquisition of Instagram did not resolve the strategic development issues it faces. It merely acquired one among many mobile social products, and not the largest one.

The rise of all these new services is due to the fact that smartphones have eliminated the insurmountable barriers Facebook faced on desktop computers:

- The smartphone address book serves as a social graph available to all applications;

- The smartphone photo library can be used by all applications;

- The smartphone push notification function eliminates the hassle of checking multiple websites;

- Switching between icons on the smartphone home screen is more convenient than switching between different websites.

People don't use Instagram specifically for posting photos, WhatsApp for text messages, or Line for stickers. Instead, they may use any service for anything. For example, using Instagram to notify others that you're running late, using WhatsApp to share holiday photos, or using Snapchat to arrange evening activities, etc. WhatsApp and Instagram are not entirely different services; rather, they are direct competitors vying for users' time and attention. Instagram, Snapchat, Line, and all other services provide users with various options for engaging in different social activities.

This is nothing surprising: we already have three social applications on our phones, including voice calls, text messages, and emails. And we don't rigidly stick to using a particular application for a specific task. New applications simply give us more choices.

Of course, acquiring Instagram was still a good deal — if Facebook were to acquire it today, it would likely cost much more than it did back then. However, this acquisition now appears to not align with Facebook's development strategy. Will Facebook continue to acquire services like WhatsApp, Snapchat, Line, Kakao, and others? Indeed, some of these services will eventually disappear, but Facebook cannot dominate as it did in the desktop space, defeating competitor after competitor. In the mobile space, Facebook has become just another player among many.

Perhaps the best course of action for Facebook is to rethink and rebuild its core product rather than acquire a service that isn't outstanding.

This reminds one of another famous acquisition case: Yahoo's acquisition of the photo-sharing site Flickr. For a period, AOL and Yahoo were enthusiastic about acquiring emerging web services because their portal status was threatened. Later, due to poor management, many of these web services were forced to shut down. But that's not the key issue. The key is that no matter how skilled they were at acquisitions, they could not always acquire the best sites. Facebook certainly can't either.
