Twitter's Secret IPO Filing Accused of Keeping Investors in the Dark

by anonymous on 2013-09-18 21:31:36

Twitter's secret submission of an IPO application to the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) has once again sparked debate among industry insiders regarding the JOBS Act, signed into law by President Obama in 2012. The act aims to help small businesses more easily obtain financing from investors but has been criticized for not giving investors enough time to digest the financial information submitted by companies during their IPO applications, while also reducing market transparency.

Twitter was able to secretly file its IPO application due entirely to provisions in the JOBS Act. The act stipulates that companies with annual operating revenues below $1 billion enjoy a maximum "incubation period" of five years. During this time, eligible enterprises are not required to disclose any financial information or submit audited reports to regulators, thereby reducing costs and bureaucracy to allow smaller companies to list faster and more easily. To encourage small business financing, the act even relaxes certain parts of federal securities regulations, which is expected to boost employment rates.

For companies that do not meet the requirements of the JOBS Act, they must publish hundreds of pages of IPO documents several months before going public, including financial conditions, risk factors, company ownership, and management structures. This gives investors, financial analysts, and media sufficient time to assess the prospects of the listed company.

However, Twitter and other qualifying companies need only disclose their financial information to investors three weeks before starting the roadshow process. This leaves investors with just a few days or one to two weeks to consider and evaluate the information. In addition, these companies can reduce the amount of information disclosed when revealing executive and director compensation. They can also assess interest from sophisticated investors before submitting IPO documents to the SEC.

The JOBS Act truly helps genuine start-ups understand the regulatory listing process without worrying about exposing too much data if they later withdraw their application, avoiding prolonged exposure to competitors. This act achieves three goals at once.

Different Views

However, some critics argue that although Twitter meets the requirement of being an "emerging growth company" with annual operating revenue below $1 billion, it is absurd to classify the company as eligible under the JOBS Act. If successful, Twitter's valuation after listing is expected to exceed $10 billion.

Ironically, Twitter, which can easily spread large amounts of data in the blink of an eye, refuses to disclose its own financial data.

Edward Ketz, a professor of accounting at Pennsylvania State University, said: "The idea of allowing companies to secretly submit information is completely wrong. This violates all SEC rules. The SEC first requires companies planning to go public to submit complete and comprehensive data reports."

Supporters of the JOBS Act, however, argue that the critics' views are unfounded. They insist that this legislation allows start-ups to avoid disclosing financial information due to early communications with regulators and not be constrained by the 'quiet period' rule, which prevents them from discussing information related to the prospectus externally. Supporters also emphasize that publishing financial information 21 days before the IPO roadshow provides sufficient evaluation time for investors, media, and analysis institutions.

Ketz pointed out that Twitter's actions were neither illegal nor inappropriate, but the decision made shareholders feel passive. He stated: "If Twitter's management considered investors more, they wouldn't make such a choice."

But others hold different opinions, including Kate Michell, head of IPO Task Force, who advises the U.S. Treasury Department and lawmakers. She argued that although secretly filing IPO documents may sound "evil," it allows companies to have full dialogue with the SEC regarding information disclosure and accounting methods.

This can also prevent companies from getting into awkward situations, such as having to disclose part of their commercial information to potential investors and Wall Street analysts without being able to publicly discuss it.

She pointed out that Facebook and Groupon’s recent IPOs serve as good examples. Groupon was criticized in 2011 for using unconventional accounting methods. Facebook faced questions for not including mobile advertising as a risk factor in its IPO documents. Michell stated: "These issues all stem from the IPO documents, but buyers and sellers can only engage in limited static communication." She further emphasized that potential investors should carefully review the content highlighted by the SEC in the listing documents.

Manchester United as a Counterexample

The JOBS Act is one of the few pieces of legislation that can easily pass through Congress with bipartisan support. Due to the strong appeal of promoting start-up development during economic downturns, opposition voices have not had much impact.

Besides enabling growth-potential start-ups to secretly apply for IPOs, the act also opens doors for hedge funds to promote private equity transactions and crowdfunding financing. According to SEC filings, as of June 2013, approximately 250 start-ups used the secret IPO channel, but the SEC did not disclose specific company names.

However, among the known start-ups that went public via the secret IPO channel, some have far exceeded the scale of start-ups. For example, last year, Manchester United Football Club in the UK went public in the U.S. through a secret IPO after attempts to list in Hong Kong, Singapore, and the UK were rejected. Former U.S. Congressman Michael Oxley pointed out at the meeting: "Manchester United has been 'undercover' for 120 years."

Democratic Senator Charles Schumer, on the other hand, pointed out that the secret application clause has always been the most successful part of the JOBS Act. He stated that investors still have ample time to analyze all the information they need before the IPO.

Schumer said: "If Twitter qualifies as an 'emerging growth company,' then it fully enjoys the rights granted by the act."

Protecting Investors

Previously, some industry insiders questioned whether the definition of "emerging growth company" was too broad. Mary Schapiro, the former chair of the SEC, attempted several times to lower the $1 billion threshold but failed.

In a March 2012 letter to Senator Tim Johnson, chairman of the Senate Banking Committee, and the highest-ranking Republican senator on the committee, Richard Shelby, Schapiro expressed her recognition of the importance of lowering the threshold for small businesses applying for IPOs but was uncertain if the JOBS Act found the best approach.

Schapiro wrote: "Setting a lower annual revenue threshold would reduce risks faced by investors and better focus on the benefits these new clauses bring to small businesses, as these small businesses are the engines driving future American economic development."

Barbara Roper, Director of Investor Protection at the Consumer Federation of America, stated that Twitter's secret submission of its IPO application undermines the original legislative intent of the JOBS Act.

Roper pointed out: "This proves that the basic premise of 'emerging growth company' is simply ridiculous."
