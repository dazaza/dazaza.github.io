The first thing on your mind

by diglog on 2010-09-27 10:20:45

[Author: Paul Graham, Original Link]

I recently realized that the things one thinks about while taking a morning shower are often much more important than I had imagined. I knew that this is a time when good ideas or inspirations easily occur. Now, I want to take it further: I want to say something even more radical – if you don’t think about something while you’re in the shower, it’s very hard to truly excel at it.

Everyone who has dealt with difficult problems may have encountered this situation: you try your hardest to solve something but fail, and then when you're doing something else, the answer suddenly becomes clear. This kind of thinking isn't something you deliberately force. I'm increasingly convinced that this type of thinking isn't just helpful for solving difficult problems; it's necessary. However, the tricky part is that you can only indirectly control this kind of thought process.[1]

I believe that most people, at any given time, have one top idea in their mind. That is, if you allow your thoughts to drift freely, they will tend toward that idea. And this idea will consume all your mental energy, even though there are other secondary ideas that also need consideration. In other words, if an incorrect idea becomes your top priority, it can be disastrous.

What made me realize this was having an idea that I didn't want become my top thought, and it stayed there for too long.

I had noticed before that startups slow down when they start raising money, but I didn't understand why until I went through it myself. The problem isn't that meeting with investors takes up a lot of actual time; rather, once you start preparing to raise money, "raising money" becomes your top thought. It becomes the idea that comes to mind when you're in the shower. This means that other problems cease to exist.

When I founded Viaweb, I really disliked raising money, but I don't remember why I disliked it so much. But when I was raising money for Y Combinator, I do remember. Because financial issues easily become your top thought. The reason is simple: they must be. Raising money isn't easy; it's not something that happens unless you put a lot of effort into it. Unless you're thinking about it while you're in the shower, it won't happen. Then you'll find that the things you actually want to do have made little progress.[2]

(I've also heard similar complaints from some of my professor friends. Nowadays, professors seem to have become professional fundraisers who do research as a side job. Perhaps it's time for a change.)

This issue hit me hard because for most of the last ten years, I've been able to freely think about what I wanted. So, when I couldn't control my own thoughts freely, the contrast became very sharp. But I don't think this problem is unique to me, because almost every startup I see, when they start raising money or considering acquisitions, everything else slows down.

You can't directly control the direction of your thoughts. If you could, they would stop drifting. However, you can indirectly control them by controlling the situations and circumstances you place yourself in. This was an important lesson for me: be careful about things that will lead you into dangerous situations; strive to place yourself in situations where the most pressing problem is exactly what you most want to do.

Of course, you can't fully control it. An emergency might crowd out other thoughts and occupy your mind. But aside from emergencies, there are many ways to indirectly control what becomes your top thought.

I've found that there are two types of thinking that should particularly be avoided – one type is like the Nile Perch, which drives away other more interesting thoughts. For example, the point I mentioned earlier: thinking about money is one such case. "Wanting money" is almost the classic attention black hole. Another is controversy. They attract attention in the same wrong way: as genuinely interesting thoughts, they grab people like velcro, but lack substance. Therefore, to do real work well, you should avoid controversies.[3]

Even Newton fell into this trap. In 1672, after he published his theory on color, he found himself unable to focus on work due to controversies for several years. Eventually, the solution was to stop publishing papers:

"I find that I have made myself a slave to philosophy, but if we can rid ourselves of Mr. Linus (Newton's opponent), then except for what I write for private interest or posthumous publication, I shall firmly resolve to quit philosophy forever. For I know that a man must either resolve not to make anything new or become a slave to protect it."[4]

Linus and his students in Liege became increasingly obsessed with nitpicking criticism. Westfall, Newton's biographer, seems to have felt that Newton's reaction was excessive:

He wrote later, recalling that period, that Newton's "slavery" consisted of five responses to Liege over more than a year, totaling 14 pages of printed text.

I feel great sympathy for Newton. The problem wasn't the 14-page response itself, but the pain caused by the fact that this silly controversy kept occupying the top spot in his mind – time he could have spent thinking about things he truly desired.

Tolerance brings some special benefits. If someone hurts you, you are hurt twice: first by the harm itself, and second by spending time afterward ruminating on it. If you can learn to ignore harm, you can at least avoid the second injury. I've found that by telling myself "this isn't worth occupying space in my mind," I can avoid thinking about unpleasant things others have done to me. I'm always happy to discover that I forget the details of controversies, meaning I haven't thought about them at all. My wife even thinks I'm more forgiving than her, but my motivation is actually quite selfish.

I suspect that many people aren't sure what their top thought is at any given time. I often get it wrong too. But I tend to think it's the idea I "hope to place at the top" rather than the one that "has already taken the top spot." It's actually quite easy to figure it out: take a shower. See which thought emerges? If the one that emerges isn't the one you "want to place at the top," perhaps you want to change some things.

Notes:

[1] There's no doubt that this type of thinking already has its name, but I call it "peripheral thinking."

[2] This was very evident in our case, even though both rounds of fundraising went smoothly, each still took a few months. No one treats large sums of money casually, and the required attention is usually proportional to the amount of money involved – perhaps not linearly, but definitely monotonically increasing.

[3] Corollary: Avoid becoming a manager, otherwise your job will consist of "dealing with money and controversies."

[4] Letter to Oldenburg, quoted from Richard Westfall's "Life of Isaac Newton," p. 107.
