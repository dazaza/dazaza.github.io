Treatment of 66 cases of hepatic trauma with combined injuries, mainly including rib fractures complicated with pneumothorax (6 cases), traumatic brain injury (8 cases), intestinal rupture (3 cases), splenic rupture (3 cases), and kidney contusion (5 cases). Two treatment methods were used: non-operative treatment in 19 cases, which included bed rest, blood volume supplementation, pain relief, use of hemostatic drugs, and dietary management. Operative treatment was performed in 47 cases, including laceration suture for hemostasis, debridement, partial hepatectomy, hepatic artery ligation, hemostatic tamponade, and peripheral hepatic drainage. Among the patients, 59 were cured and 7 died. The causes of death included hemorrhagic shock (4 cases), traumatic brain injury (1 case), respiratory failure (1 case), and multiple organ failure (1 case).

Discussion:

1. Diagnosis of liver trauma: Based on the history of abdominal trauma, clinical symptoms and signs, simple abdominal paracentesis can provide a clearer diagnosis of liver trauma. If the patient's condition allows, B-ultrasound or CT imaging can be performed. However, for critically ill patients or those with complications, diagnostic procedures should not be excessive to avoid delaying the timing of surgery.

2. Non-operative treatment of liver trauma: It is believed that once liver rupture is confirmed, immediate surgical exploration is necessary. However, based on clinical experience, some patients with hepatic rupture and bleeding may stop bleeding without surgery due to the three-stage branching of the intrahepatic Glisson system from the liver surface at about 3 cm. Thus, superficial liver injuries do not usually result in major bleeding or biliary fistula, and B-ultrasound or CT imaging can provide dynamic observation, making conservative treatment possible. In this group, 19 cases were treated non-operatively, with only 1 death due to brain trauma, and the rest were cured without complications. Indications for non-operative treatment should include hemodynamic stability, systolic blood pressure not less than 90 mmHg, pulse rate not more than 100 beats per minute, no obvious signs of peritonitis, B-ultrasound or CT identifying grade 1-2 liver injury, and no other intra-abdominal organ injury.

3. Operative treatment for severe liver injury: The aim of surgery is to control bleeding, prevent biliary fistula, remove devitalized liver tissue, and ensure sufficient drainage. Hemostasis can be achieved by ligation of vessels or ducts, or by tamponade using gelatin sponge or omentum if exact hemostasis is difficult. Blood transfusion: If there is no hollow organ or gallbladder rupture, reinfusion of blood is possible. Even if there is a small amount of bile leakage, it can be returned without anticoagulants after filtering through 7-10 layers of gauze into a physiological saline bottle, with possible addition of saline for blood dilution to facilitate infusion. Drainage of the abdominal cavity: Most liver injuries require placement of drains during surgery. Adequate drainage of oozing blood and bile is very necessary to reduce postoperative subphrenic abscesses.
