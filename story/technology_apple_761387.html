If Jobs saw Apple's report: he would definitely write to Cook to throw away the report.

by geekzhang on 2013-05-03 13:22:51

Reported by Chih Lou on April 24, Tencent Technology

As market analysts have noted, Apple is currently experiencing a rather awkward moment of financial report exposure.

In the early hours of today, Apple released its second quarter fiscal year 2013 earnings report. The report showed that despite an 11% increase in revenue to $43.6 billion, setting a new record for quarterly revenue, profits also saw their first decline in 10 years, dropping 18% to $9.547 billion. The revenue from the much-anticipated Chinese market failed to grow significantly and did not become a breakout growth point for Apple's performance. This is probably not what Apple, including Tim Cook himself and the late Steve Jobs, had hoped for.

Tim Cook, as the head of Apple, has been facing numerous troubles recently. In the past few weeks, some players in the capital market and media have launched a series of propaganda attacks against him. Some even claimed that Apple's board was looking for a new CEO. The public opinion war under the guise of saving Apple is not simple; the main participants were identified by Fortune magazine as not being true friends of Apple.

The criticism of Cook mainly stems from the stock price drop. Under Cook's leadership, Apple's stock price plummeted from a high of $702 per share in September 2012 to $390 on April 19, 2013, marking a new low in 52 weeks. Critics attribute the stock price collapse to Cook, ignoring the fact that the $702 high was also achieved during Cook's first year as CEO.

Fortune columnist Philip Elmer-DeWitt believes that the rise of Apple's stock price to over $700 and its subsequent fall to below $390 is more related to the dysfunction of the securities market than to Cook's actions as CEO.

This explanation fails to account for such a dramatic stock price drop. Setting aside these premeditated attacks and excessive defenses, how is Apple performing today?

The earnings report shows that Apple sold 37.4 million iPhones and 19.5 million iPads in the last quarter, slightly exceeding analysts' expectations. Clearly, this number would be enough to celebrate with champagne at any other company. Even for a giant like Apple, considering the overall health of the PC market and consumer electronics market, it still represents a satisfactory result. However, Apple seems to suffer from complications of Steve Jobs' "reality distortion field." Despite impressive revenue and solid fundamentals, it can no longer continue to impress the capital markets that once adored it.

What’s wrong with Apple? The Wall Street Journal believes that Apple is facing an identity crisis on Wall Street. Apple continues to be viewed as a hardware company. With the increasing homogenization of smartphones and tablets, the growth momentum driven by products like the iPhone and iPad will soon fade. If categorized as a software-hardware company, it would be valued more like an internet or software company, which often have recurring revenue streams and typically higher P/E ratios than hardware companies.

But this is a false proposition. Since the birth of the iPod in 2001, over the past 12 years, countless articles have been written about how the combination of iPod and iTunes changed the traditional music industry, and discussions on how App Store complements iPhone and iPad to create ecosystems are also abundant. For a long time, Apple's unique integration of software and hardware was considered one of the reasons for its soaring stock price.

Perhaps Nokia's story can offer some insight:

In 2010, Apple, already the global smartphone leader, released the iPhone 4. Affected by this news, analysts predicted Nokia's third-quarter profit to be only €183 million on average, with some even believing it would hit a ten-year low. However, Nokia ended up making €634 million in profit. In the fourth quarter of 2010, analysts predicted its profit to be €529 million, but Nokia again reported a net profit of €745 million. After the second quarter of 2011, however, Nokia suffered losses for six consecutive quarters and completely became a marginal player in the market.

Of course, today's Apple has not yet encountered a truly formidable opponent. Even though Samsung's position in the smartphone market is strengthening, Apple remains the undisputed king of the tablet market, and the App Store is still the most complete ecosystem in the market, unlike the awkward role Nokia played three years ago.

The only answer might have nothing to do with performance, management, corporate nature, or even market competition—it may just be because Apple no longer has product launches that can captivate people. Losing innovation capability might be an old argument, but in the new wave of hardware enthusiasm since 2012, we haven't seen Apple's presence among products like 3D printers, light-field cameras, new thermostats, and various other innovative items. The iPad Mini serves more to perfect the product line rather than redefine the world, a slogan that has become increasingly difficult to associate with Apple.

If Steve Jobs were watching from above, how would he view today's Apple? He might send this letter:

To the Apple Board and Tim Cook:

I remember strongly recommending Tim Cook as Apple's CEO when I resigned from the position. Today, I still hold this view—Cook is still the person who can lead Apple forward.

I know the external voices have shaken you, but I hope you remember one thing: Over the past few decades, Wall Street has alternated between praising and criticizing Apple, profiting from manipulating numbers and curves; while Cook has step by step transformed Apple's bloated and inefficient manufacturing and distribution departments into their current state, creating miracles repeatedly after I stepped back from management.

I am familiar with those despicable tricks. People emphasize how Apple's stock price fell from $702 per share in September 2012 to today's $390, but they absolutely won't mention that on the day before Cook officially took office in August 2011, Apple's stock price was only $373.72. Ignore these noises and continue moving forward—this is our mission.

Of course, this doesn't mean we should avoid addressing existing problems. We indeed have bottlenecks. After redefining the phone and creating the tablet market out of thin air, how do we maintain our position in the market and continue to innovate? These are challenges Apple faces now.

But what disappoints me most is that while we stand still, Google Glass has been defined as the coolest product of the moment. Even though it is far from commercialization, it is enough to shake everything we have defended for years.

From another perspective, our unreleased TV, watch, or other terminals still haven't left the realm of screens, while Google has started trying to directly monopolize eyeballs. Once Google succeeds, any other product relying on screen interaction will pale in comparison. This is a fatal blow.

Dividends for shareholders, strengthening ties with the Chinese market, launching the 7-inch iPad mini—these are actions you've taken based on your own judgment, not inspired by my spirit, and commendably, they haven't disappointed me. Now, we need more changes, introducing more revolutionary products instead of being fixated on numbers and reports. You know that focusing too much on these often marks the beginning of a company's mediocrity.

Time is pressing. The mobile phone market is no longer our home turf, and the tablet market is already facing more challenges from competitors. Maintaining our achievements while creating new glories is not easy, but I believe you can do it. While Cook is busy with supply chains and various markets, Ive and others should focus more on the products themselves. We should acquire more cutting-edge companies and organically integrate their unique innovations and technologies to unleash more stunning products in less time.

Finally, I am eagerly looking forward to the next Apple event, because it concerns not only my past but also your future.

Yours,

Steve Jobs

April 23, 2013

Although this letter is purely fictional, the problems Apple faces are very real. From greatness to mediocrity, from affirmation to doubt, this is a fate that most companies in the technology industry cannot escape, whether it was Fairchild in the past, HP earlier, or many famous Japanese tech companies. They all inevitably followed this parabolic path. As a business entity, Apple, sooner or later, will eventually follow this trajectory. Steve Jobs once said, "Have the courage to follow your heart and intuition. They somehow already know what you truly want to become. Everything else is secondary." At this moment, Cook, Ive, and other Apple employees should ask themselves: Am I ready to reclaim the honor that belongs to Apple?
