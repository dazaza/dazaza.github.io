Elop sold Nokia for 18.8 million euros, Finnish Prime Minister says it doesn't make sense

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 20:59:51

The Finnish Prime Minister and Minister of Finance have joined the chorus of criticism against Stephen Elop. Former Nokia CEO, Stephen Elop, received a compensation package worth 18.8 million euros after selling Nokia to Microsoft for 5.44 billion euros. This has led to rare criticism from Finnish government officials regarding the operations of a private company.

Jyrki Katainen, the Prime Minister belonging to the center-right coalition, stated over the weekend on Finnish television that such bonuses are unjustifiable during tough times for the company. He described the situation as "ridiculous," adding: "Clearly, the reward practices of this multinational giant are so special they cannot be understood by common sense."

Jutta Urpilainen, the Finance Minister from the center-left coalition, expressed in her blog that "this bonus could undermine trust in Nokia. It may also poison societal attitudes and threaten social harmony."

The intervention of Finland's top two political figures shows that anger has spread nationwide following Microsoft's acquisition of Nokia's mobile phone division. As part of the deal, Nokia's former CEO and ex-Microsoft manager, Stephen Elop, received an 18.8 million euro payout and returned to his old employer.

This ignited anger among politicians, workers, and unions alike. Under Elop's leadership, Nokia effectively paid him one million euros for every billion euros in market value.

In November, Nokia approved the transaction with Microsoft at a special meeting and revised Elop's contract proxy materials. Last Sunday, Nokia stated that these actions were taken to prevent Elop from resigning and included a non-compete agreement to ensure he would not work for Microsoft.

Risto Siilasmaa, Chairman of Nokia's Board of Directors and Interim CEO, issued a statement late Friday night saying, "The compensation paid to Stephen Elop is purely based on the CEO contract terms and conditions approved by the board in 2010."

Ms. Urpilainen called for reconsideration of the rules to allow shareholders to decide on bonus payments at the general meeting. "It is now decided by a small group of directors," added Mr. Katainen, who felt the award had an unfair tinge. Following the national wage agreement, workers accepted an additional 20 euros per month and only a 0.4% wage increase in the second year. This compensation significantly exceeds that agreement.

The intervention of both political figures has put pressure on Elop. Privately, some Finns have started suggesting that Elop should return part or all of the compensation.

After announcing the deal with Microsoft, Elop became the head of Nokia's mobile phone business under his former employer, returning to Microsoft. Microsoft plans to establish a large data center in northern Finland and continues to view Finland as an important R&D base.
