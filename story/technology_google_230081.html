1. Butterfly Effect

In the winter of 1961, Edward Lorenz, a meteorologist at MIT, was using a computer to solve 13 equations simulating Earth's atmosphere in an effort to predict weather patterns. To examine the results more closely, he took an intermediate solution, increased its precision, and re-entered it into the equations. When he returned after drinking a cup of coffee, he was astonished. He found that his new simulated weather pattern had deviated significantly from the previous printed data. Initially, he thought there might be something wrong with his computer, but then he realized that nothing was amiss with the computer’s operation; the answer lay in the numbers he had just inputted. In the original program, he used six decimal places: 0.506127. During the second run, he rounded off the number to 0.506. He believed such a tiny difference — omitting a thousandth — would not have any real impact. He was wrong — this minuscule difference had already caused a huge divergence! This slight change in the input quickly led to entirely different results!

The formal name for this phenomenon is "extreme sensitivity to initial conditions," and its informal, more common name is "the butterfly effect." It means that a butterfly flapping its wings occasionally in the Amazon rainforest of South America could cause a tornado in Texas, USA, two weeks later. The reason being that the butterfly's wing movement causes changes in the air system around it, generating weak air currents, which in turn cause corresponding changes in the surrounding air or other systems, leading to a chain reaction that ultimately causes significant changes in other systems.

Similarly, Dr. W. Edwards Deming, the world-renowned management scientist, after over 50 years of statistical research, came to a very similar conclusion. He pointed out that every process has a starting point and an endpoint. If you ensure the correctness of the first 15% of the process (initial conditions), then you can at least guarantee obtaining 85% of the expected output. In other words, as long as you focus on the first 15% of anything, the remaining 85% will come effortlessly.

Please remember, the initial stage is the most important!!!

The reason why millions of English learners fail to learn English well is because they did not follow the most scientific method of learning English right from the start—"characters—words—sentences—texts." Instead, within just a few days of starting, they were swept into the vortex of "test-oriented English." From then on, all they could think about was not how to learn English well, but how to score high marks. Under this deformed way of thinking, students could only follow their teachers in studying English test-taking techniques every day, and thus, after ten years, their English proficiency still only manifested itself on a test paper.

2. Pareto Effect

The Jews are known as the world's foremost merchants. What is their first secret of business? The answer is: they adhere to the ancient cosmic rule, the most important one being the 78:22 rule!

How did they arrive at this ratio? It was calculated based on the relationship between a square and its inscribed circle. Assuming the area of a square is 100, then the area of its inscribed circle is 78.5, leaving the remaining area as 21.5. Expressed in whole numbers, this is 78:22. Coincidentally, in the proportion of gases in the air, nitrogen accounts for 78%, while oxygen accounts for 22%. In the human body composition, it is made up of 78% water and 22% other substances. Therefore, this 78:22 becomes a cosmic rule that humans cannot resist.

This magical ratio, however, is not called the "Jewish Rule," but rather the "Pareto Effect" or "Pareto Principle." In 1897, Italian economist Vilfredo Pareto (1848-1923), while conducting economic research, accidentally noticed the wealth and income patterns of people in 19th-century Britain. In his survey sampling, he found that most of the income and wealth flowed into the hands of a minority. In today's society, this in itself is not surprising, but he also discovered two facts that he considered very important. One was that there was a consistent mathematical relationship between the percentage of a population group and the total income or wealth enjoyed by that group.

What truly excited Pareto, however, was another discovery: this uneven pattern repeated itself. Whether examining different periods or different countries, he observed this phenomenon. Whether it was early Britain or other countries of his time, or even earlier data, he found the same pattern recurring with mathematical accuracy.

From Pareto's research, we can deduce the following result: if 20% of the population enjoys 80% of the wealth, then it can be predicted that 10% of the population owns 65% of the wealth, and 5% of the population owns 50% of the wealth. Here, the emphasis is not just on percentages, but on the fact that the distribution of wealth among the population is uneven, and this is a predictable fact!

The Pareto Effect has other names, such as the 80/20 rule, the Least Effort Principle, the Imbalance Principle, etc., advocating that a small inducement, investment, and effort usually produce large results, outputs, or rewards. Literally speaking, this principle suggests that 80% of the results in your work come from 20% of the effort you put in. Thus, for all practical purposes, four-fifths of our efforts — that is, most of the effort we expend — are unrelated to the outcome. This situation goes against what most people expect.

In summary, there are two different types of imbalance between cause and effect, input and output, effort and reward:

― The majority, which only has a minor impact.

― The minority, which has the main, significant impact.

The 80/20 ratio is quite close to the 85/15 ratio mentioned by Deming in the context of the butterfly effect. However, Deming emphasized the "first 15% of doing things," while the Pareto Principle emphasizes the "overall imbalance!"

Perhaps someone might ask, "What does the Pareto Effect have to do with learning English?"

Of course, there is a connection!!! If you pay attention, according to the Pareto Principle, as long as we master 20% of the key content in English, we can solve 80% of the problems in English learning. For example, although English grammar knowledge is exceptionally complex, in actual reading practice, you only need to grasp a limited number of grammar points to read without hindrance. These grammar points mainly include the number of nouns, five tenses of verbs, comparative forms of adjectives, attributive clauses, and subjunctive mood, etc. Learning these grammar points takes adults only a mere two hours!!!

3. Pygmalion Effect

Pygmalion is the name of a beautiful statue carved by the king of Cyprus in Greek mythology. The king was deeply infatuated with this beauty, forming a strong desire. He was so devoted to the goddess he sculpted that he treated her as a living girl, pouring out his love and praying for her to gain life day and night. Under his devout protection, later, this statue actually turned into a live person and became his wife. Psychologists borrowed this mythological story to describe how deep expectations placed on others or oneself can become an internal driving force, resulting in the miraculous effect of turning expectation into reality, calling it the "Pygmalion Effect."

In English learning, the vast majority of students lack confidence, alternating between a desire for success and fear of failure. If today's study goes well, the student becomes highly motivated, feeling that nothing in the world can stop their progress. But if they encounter a question or word they don't know, they may feel immense frustration and worry deeply about their English learning.
