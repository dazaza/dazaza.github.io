"Bloodthirsty Scepter" - from a rural teacher to a powerful minister of the Southern Song Dynasty

by jqrxass on 2009-10-24 16:06:42

"The Bloody Scepter" Part 1 is the first biography of Qin Hui, telling how he rose from an unknown private school teacher in a village to a key player in the turmoil at the end of the Southern Song Dynasty through strategy and luck, as well as some talent. He served twice as the Chancellor of the Southern Song Dynasty, holding office for a total of 19 years, gradually ascending to the pinnacle of power. In this book, readers will see an image of Qin Hui as a talented individual who was inherently deceitful and skilled in wielding power. The author of this book has spent many years studying history, with much of the content sourced from ancient texts such as the "History of the Song Dynasty." Written in a humorous and straightforward style, employing novelistic techniques and empathetic thinking, the book aims to provide historical reflections beyond those typically associated with figures like Hu Xueyan and Zeng Guofan, through the universally known negative character of Qin Hui.

This story should start with Emperor Shenzong of the Song Dynasty. Since this ambitious emperor made up his mind, stomped his foot, and resolutely decided on reform, the court ministers educated under the imperial examination system, with their heads full of the Four Books and Five Classics and mouths full of classical phrases, could no longer sit still. They engaged in what intellectuals were best at - internal strife, splitting into two factions. One faction advocated for reform and change, while the other insisted on following ancestral rules. These two factions fought each other, taking turns to come onto the stage, demoting each other back and forth, making quite a spectacle. A person who enjoys self-mutilation by having their left hand fight their right and vice versa obviously has issues with their head, even a child in diapers would know that.

"The Bloody Scepter" Part 1

Reading books? Back then, it wasn't as simple as it is now, where you just reach a certain age, take out your household registration book, and can sign up for school nearby.

All professions are inferior, only scholarship is exalted. Not everyone could afford to read books. Children from wealthy families usually had renowned teachers hired at high salaries to tutor them privately at home; children from middle-class families had to pool money together to hire a teacher shared among several families; as for children from poor families, most of them couldn't afford schooling.
