Ballmer's Microsoft: An MBA Company That Lacks a Developer Soul

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 13:52:44

Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer to retire within 12 months

Introduction: U.S. tech blog ReadWrite recently published an article titled "Ballmer's Microsoft: All MBA, No Developer Soul," which argues that during Steve Ballmer's tenure, Microsoft exploited its flagship products like Windows and Office to generate as much profit as possible while neglecting technological development — a strategy that ultimately failed. Following Ballmer's announcement of his retirement, Microsoft may need to select a technologically-focused CEO.

The following is the full text of the article:

Microsoft was once an ambitious company. However, over the past decade, Microsoft has used every means possible to generate as much profit as it could from just two products: Windows and Office. Meanwhile, the industry has moved toward social networking, web applications, and mobile internet. As expected, Microsoft's golden days gradually came to an end after Bill Gates retired in the early 21st century and handed over the reins to Steve Ballmer. In fact, this also reflects Microsoft's transition from a technologically-oriented CEO to a commercially-oriented one.

However, the results have not been good. Ballmer has announced that he will retire within the next 12 months. Therefore, Microsoft must reclaim its "roots" among developers. To achieve this, all of Microsoft’s products must join the open-source community.

Ten Years of Stagnation

There is no doubt that Bill Gates did not leave Microsoft overnight. However, his influence on the company waned over time, coinciding with a downward trend in Microsoft's stock price.

Despite still achieving quarterly profits that are hard for other companies to match, this too reveals part of the problem: Ballmer showed a clear tendency to exploit Microsoft's "cash cows," namely Windows and Office, while ignoring future-oriented development.

Focusing on CIOs, Ignoring Developers

The issue with Ballmer lies in his inability to foresee where the tech industry would go. Although renowned tech expert Tim O'Reilly suggested that we should pay attention to the geek community to discover technology trends, Ballmer, over the past 13 years, merely focused on feedback from corporate Chief Information Officers (CIOs). The CIOs told Ballmer how Microsoft should improve its current products to meet their needs. This model gave rise to some excellent but non-revolutionary technologies, such as SharePoint.

CIOs typically avoid risks and therefore do not care about Microsoft's future direction. During this process, Ballmer's Microsoft completely missed the shift of developers towards Node.js server programming languages, NoSQL databases, and cloud computing development and deployment. This shift represents the most significant technological development of the past decade: the rise of open-source developers. Open-source strategies allow developers to experiment with new technologies without being tied to any single company or needing permission.

Ballmer aggressively protected Microsoft's "cash cows" and actively fought against the open-source community. As a result, Microsoft lost the support of a large number of developers. Microsoft's recent redesign of its developer website MSDN further demonstrates that the company is now more focused on marketing rather than supporting developers.

Profit: A Myopic Focus

Instead of paying attention to the growing developer community, Ballmer was satisfied with the feedback provided by CIOs and the appearance of strong profit margins. Based on this approach, Ballmer failed to keep pace with mobile app and web app developers. For example, Ballmer once believed that the iPhone was an overly expensive phone and unfriendly to enterprise users.

At the time, Ballmer claimed that phones running on Microsoft's system would sell tens of millions annually and gain the favor of many enterprise users. However, after Apple released the iPhone, Microsoft's phone business quickly fell into an awkward situation. It turned out that what kind of phone is used in enterprises is not decided by CIOs, but by ordinary users.

The Way Forward

After Ballmer's announcement of his impending retirement, Microsoft's greatest hope lies in promoting or hiring a technically-focused CEO who can earn the respect of developers. Additionally, Microsoft needs to treat open-source technology as a "first-class citizen." Although Microsoft has made significant changes by allowing developers to develop on its platform, it has yet to fully embrace the open-source community.

Take the Kinect motion control system as an example. Although Microsoft opened this platform to developers, it should be noted that no developer can truly access Kinect's proprietary code. Microsoft allows developers to "tinker" but does not permit modifications to the platform. This might be the maximum extent Ballmer could tolerate, but in the 21st century, it is also a failed strategy. (By Jin Wei)
