How to raise centipedes? How to manage centipedes? What is the market prospect for centipedes? The Agriculture Science and Technology Benefit-for-People Special Program will tell you about the living characteristics of centipedes.

1. Habitat Environment: Centipedes avoid sunlight, are active at night and rest during the day, preferring dark, warm, sheltered, and well-ventilated places to live. They mainly inhabit low-altitude areas with more rocks and less soil. Although they can be found in plain regions, their numbers are fewer there. Centipedes have a strong ability to burrow into crevices. They often use their sensitive antennae and flat head plates to explore cracks and holes. Most rock and soil crevices can be passed through or inhabited by them. When the density is too high or they are disturbed frequently, it can lead to mutual killings and death. However, under artificial breeding conditions, if bait and drinking water are sufficient, dozens of centipedes can cohabit.

2. Activity and Diet: Centipedes are typical carnivorous animals, being fierce and having a wide range of food preferences, especially favoring small insects. They have jaw claws that can shoot venom, even capable of killing animals larger than themselves. There are also instances where centipedes kill each other within the same species due to poisoning. The insects eaten by centipedes include crickets, locusts, June beetles, cicadas, grasshoppers, as well as various flies and bees. They may even eat spiders, earthworms, snails, and animals much larger than themselves like frogs, mice, sparrows, lizards, and snakes. In early spring when food is scarce, they can also eat tender shoots of grass and moss. In artificial breeding, some people feed them loaches, fresh fish, frogs, shrimp, and crabs, but the food must be fresh; any slight decay will prevent them from eating.

Egg-laying and Hatching Techniques: Centipedes are oviparous. Every late spring to early summer, the eggs in the ovaries gradually mature. The general number of eggs laid is between 20 to 60, mostly 40 to 50, with some laying fewer than 10. The egg-laying season is from late June to early August, i.e., from the Summer Solstice to the Beginning of Autumn, with the peak egg-laying period being in early to mid-July. Before laying eggs, the abdomen of the centipede almost tightly adheres to the ground, digging shallow caves on its own. During egg-laying, the centipede's body curves into an S-shape, with the last few pairs of legs propping it up, the tail raised upward, and the antennae stretched forward. Then, the eggs are discharged one by one from the reproductive opening. Under undisturbed conditions, the smooth egg-laying process takes about 2 to 3 hours. After laying the eggs, the centipede cleverly turns its body sideways, gathering the eggs into a cluster using its legs and hugging them to incubate. If disturbed during egg-laying, the centipede will stop laying eggs or eat all the eggs that are currently hatching. This is the so-called protective reaction of centipedes. The incubation period for centipedes lasts 43 to 50 days. During this time, the mother centipede stays close to the eggs or young ones, carefully guarding them. Sometimes her lower body and antennae sway left and right, driving away nearby small insects. She often uses her feeding claws to manipulate or lick the egg cluster and young ones. It is speculated that centipedes may secrete substances from their oral glands and coxal glands to prevent bacterial infections or contamination of the egg cluster. The eggs are elliptical in shape, varying in size.

Generally, the diameter of the eggs is about 3 to 3.5 millimeters, light yellow, and semi-transparent. The egg membrane is elastic. There is no significant change in the first five days, only gradually turning white from light yellow. Half a month later, the eggs grow into kidney shapes, with middle trace lines splitting open, growing to 5 millimeters. After 20 days, they become crescent-shaped, faintly showing tiny foot claws, measuring about 7 millimeters. One month later, they initially resemble young ones, with a body length of about 1.2 centimeters, able to wiggle slightly in the mother centipede's embrace. After 35 to 40 days, the young centipedes grow to 1.5 centimeters, able to climb up and down, but still not leaving the mother. After 43 to 45 days, they grow to 2 to 2.5 centimeters, separating from the mother to independently move around, seeking food. During the incubation period, the mother has already accumulated enough nutrients, so feeding is unnecessary, otherwise, the eggs may get contaminated by food and be consumed by the mother. From hatching, the development and growth of the young ones until adulthood, centipedes need to molt several times, growing significantly larger after each molt. Adult centipedes generally molt once a year, occasionally twice. Before molting, the back plate rises without luster, the body color changes from black-green to pale green with a slightly burnt yellow hue, the legs change from red to yellow, the whole body becomes thick and slow-moving, refusing food, with reduced vision and tactile abilities. During molting, the centipede uses the front end of its head to push against the stone wall or mud wall, prying open the head plate, then relying on its own contraction movements to shed the skin sequentially, allowing the body along with the legs to molt from front to back. When molting reaches the 7th-8th section of the body, the antennae are shed. Finally, the tail is shed. The old skin shed appears wrinkled, stretching out to form a complete centipede shell. Adult centipedes generally shed one section every 4-6 minutes, taking about 2 hours to completely shed. During molting, disturbances should be avoided, otherwise, it will prolong the molting time. For artificially bred centipedes, care must be taken to prevent attacks by ants during molting, as centipedes lack the ability to resist during molting, and their new skin is tender, easily bitten by ants. The growth rate of centipedes is not fast. From the first year when the eggs hatch into larvae to just before winter hibernation, they only grow to 3-4 centimeters. In the second year, before emerging from hibernation, despite abundant food, they only grow to about 3.5-6 centimeters. In the third year, they grow to over 10 centimeters. Therefore, from the start of the egg stage to developing into adult insects that lay eggs again, it takes a full 3-4 years.

The size of the larvae born in the same year differs greatly depending on whether they were born from early or late-laid eggs. The speed of growth in the same year is closely related to whether the food supply is sufficient and the length of the feeding period. It has been observed in artificial breeding that centipedes grown in isolated natural conditions do not grow as fast as those fed indoors.

Centipede Breeding Management Techniques:

1. Control Breeding: Various forms such as breeding pools, breeding tanks, and breeding jars can be used for controlled breeding of centipedes, but all should prevent escape. The walls of the breeding pool can be made of glass or plastic sheets, or water ditches can be built around to prevent centipedes from escaping. Ensuring the survival rate of centipede offspring during artificial breeding is a very important task. Centipedes require certain environmental conditions for egg-laying, hatching, and rearing young ones. Therefore, timely management and protection are necessary to ensure the quantity of reproduction and the successful completion of centipede hatching.

2. Isolation of Pregnant Females: Centipedes require a quiet environment for egg-laying and hatching. Disturbances can cause females to eat the eggs or even the young ones. Observations show that in the same breeding pool, the egg-laying times of female centipedes vary greatly. The activities of non-egg-laying females and male centipedes often interfere with and disrupt the normal progress of egg-laying and hatching. Some even snatch the eggs. Therefore, before egg-laying, the females should be separated into different tanks for breeding, or in large breeding pools, isolation can be done using glass sheets, bottomless glass cups, cans, or tiles.

3. Increased Feeding Before Egg-Laying: Female centipedes do not eat or drink during the hatching period, relying on their own nutritional reserves to maintain activity. Before egg-laying, females have the habit of consuming large amounts of food to accumulate nutrition. At this time, the feeding amount should be increased, and attention should be paid to adjusting the types of food to encourage females to consume more and increase their nutritional reserves before hatching.

4. Supervision During the Hatching Period: During the period when centipedes hold eggs and rear young ones, they react to disturbances, vibrations, strong light, loud sounds, etc. Therefore, the breeding room must be chosen to be quiet and dim. It is best to install red lights indoors and cover the windows with cloth or bamboo curtains to prevent strong light exposure.

After the hatching cylinders are placed inside the room, they should not be moved easily once the eggs are laid. Observers should move cautiously and avoid moving the covering glass (tiles) randomly or shining flashlights. No feeding is needed during the hatching period, but since this is the peak of summer, if the humidity in the hatching nest is too low, it will affect the normal development of the embryo. Therefore, water should be added to the hatching cylinder appropriately. Water should be slowly poured along the wall of the cylinder, making the inner wall around the hatching nest slightly damp. Do not directly sprinkle water on the nest, nor make the nest overly humid. After hatching, although the young centipedes temporarily gather in clusters, they can also move and search for food individually. At this time, the female centipedes gradually leave the hatching nest and move independently. Because centipedes compete for food and have the habit of eating smaller ones, the female centipedes should be removed promptly or the young ones should be separated for separate breeding.

5. Individual Growth Patterns: Under artificial feeding conditions, the body length of centipedes can reach the medicinal standard of small strips (8 cm) within two years, and approach the standard of large strips thereafter. Therefore, it can be inferred that artificial breeding of centipedes, from capturing adult centipedes, laying eggs, hatching until providing medicinal use, requires 2-3 years.

Medicinal Processing Techniques for Centipedes: Medicinal centipedes are dried whole bodies. After catching them, they are scalded to death in boiling water, then fine pointed bamboo sticks are inserted into the head and tail parts, using the elasticity of the bamboo to straighten them, and then dried under the sun. After processing and preparation, the centipedes appear as flat long strips, approximately 14-16 cm long, about 0.6-1.0 cm wide, with a total of 22 segments, the last segment being small, called the tail. The head is reddish-brown, with a pair of sensory organs and poison hooks; the back is dark brown and shiny, with two prominent ridges; the abdomen is yellowish-brown and shriveled; each segment has a pair of legs, which are yellowish-red, curved backward, and the last segment resembles a thorn. It has a fishy smell, with a special pungent odor, and tastes spicy and slightly salty. The centipede contains two toxic components similar to bee venom, namely histamine-like substances and hemolytic proteins; in addition, it also contains tyrosine, leucine, formic acid, fat oil, cholesterol, etc. Medicinal centipedes have the functions of calming wind and relieving spasms, reducing swelling, and detoxifying. They are mainly used to treat children's convulsions, tetanus, seizures, facial paralysis, lymph node tuberculosis, abscesses, and ulcers. Recently, it was discovered that centipedes have anti-tumor effects.
