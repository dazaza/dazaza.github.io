Nokia 1050: A Tribute to the Lost Youth of Chinese Consumers

by geekzhang on 2013-05-08 10:39:21

Nokia 1050, the legendary phone that costs only 162 yuan and has a standby time of 35 days. This summer, Chinese consumers will pay tribute to their lost youth through Nokia 1050, but Nokia views the entire matter as a surefire good deal. Repeatedly crushing user loyalty with commercial thinking might be the final poison that consumes Nokia.

Long-sunken Nokia once again became the focus of attention, and the trigger was the Nokia 1050, known as the cheapest phone. This device mainly targets the low-end market, with an official price of just 162 yuan RMB.

In terms of hardware, the Nokia 1050 has absolutely no standout features: it does not support 3G networks, cannot install apps, has a display screen of merely 1.4 inches, and does not support touch operations. This is almost a configuration from ten years ago. The only advantage is the battery life: talk time can reach up to 12.5 hours, and standby time can even last for a month. However, the enthusiastic coverage by domestic media and the unstinting praise from loyal fans have elevated the Nokia 1050 to the status of a "godly" phone even before its launch.

Nokia executives were even more confident in interviews: despite the very low price, the profit margin of this phone is no lower than that of any high-end phone from other brands. Once a frenzy of buying occurs, Nokia will still make substantial profits. The Finnish giant's plan is: relying on the price advantage, to ship large volumes in emerging markets like China, compensating for the shortcoming of relatively low absolute profits, and recovering market share.

However, Nokia may have guessed the result correctly but failed to foresee the process. This low-end phone may sell well, but it won't slow down Nokia's decline. The popularity of Nokia 1050 might be a cup of poison, merely relieving Nokia's current hunger.

High-class riches lowering their status

All signs indicate that in the near future, Nokia is likely to move forward vigorously along the path of low-end and cheap phones. The former high-class brand is preparing to lower its status, becoming ordinary, to earn blood and sweat money. The Nokia 1050 is neither the first step towards ordinariness nor the end point of decline. In today's mobile phone market, where screen size and chip performance are fiercely competed over, why did Nokia launch a low-end phone?

On the surface, after aligning with Microsoft, Nokia seemed to have acquired elite qualities, and the design and craftsmanship of the Lumia series of phones made both the industry and consumers quite impressed. However, the smartphone market dominated by Apple and Samsung could not tolerate a third party's interference. In the first quarter of this year, Nokia sold only 5.6 million Lumia smartphones, equivalent to 15% of Apple's sales during the same period and 8% of Samsung's, and this was already Nokia's best performance since switching to the Windows Phone platform.

Even worse, Nokia's total smartphone sales (including Lumia and Symbian) in the previous quarter were only 6.1 million units, a year-on-year decrease of 49% and a quarter-on-quarter decrease of 8%. This means that Lumia's sales came at the expense of eroding the Symbian phone market. As Symbian gradually fades out, if Lumia cannot successfully counter Android phones and iPhones, its growth space will further shrink.

The progress of Nokia's smartphone business has been slow, so Nokia can only continue to explore the potential of its feature phone business to preserve its hopes of a comeback. In the previous quarter, Nokia sold 55.8 million feature phones (including Asha series), accounting for more than 90% of total sales, making them the mainstay. However, the replacement of feature phones by smartphones has become an industry trend, and Nokia is powerless to reverse the situation: the sale of 55.8 million units looks quite good, but it represents a year-on-year decrease of 21% and a quarter-on-quarter decrease of 30%.

Facing rapidly shrinking sales, Nokia's response strategy is to repeatedly lower the bottom line. According to the previous quarter's financial report, the average selling price of Nokia's feature phones has dropped from $33 (approximately 203 yuan) a year ago to $28 (approximately 172 yuan), and the company expects this figure to continue to drop. The 162 yuan price of the Nokia 1050 is not the end point.

Chinese consumers don't care about price

Perhaps the only thing that comforts Nokia is the gross profit margin of 22.9% from its feature phone business, which exceeds the 20.7% from its smartphone business. Although incomparable to Apple's 68% gross profit margin, this data gives Nokia's upper management hope, planning to continue moving towards the lowest level of the mobile phone market, using nearly rock-bottom prices to exchange for massive shipping volumes, compensating for the weakness of its smartphone business.

As usual, Nokia chose China as its breakthrough point, the largest global market that once brought it boundless glory. But this time, it miscalculated.

Although considered one of the emerging markets, compared to India, Africa, and South America, China's young consumers have higher incomes and purchasing power, with lower price sensitivity; more importantly, consumers here are willing to spend one or even two months' salary to buy a high-end smartphone, and extreme behaviors such as selling kidneys to buy a phone occur frequently.

For example, the bare machine price of the iPhone 5 basic model is $649 (approximately 4000 yuan), equivalent to one-third of the average American monthly salary, which already makes most Americans exclaim it's too expensive; while in China, many young people with monthly incomes of three or four thousand yuan would rather save on food and drink to buy an iPhone 5. This indirectly indicates that price is not the primary consideration for Chinese consumers when purchasing a phone.

Nokia obviously didn't realize this. In the official promotional page for the Nokia 1050, the low price is prominently displayed. Ten years ago, most phones were expensive, and pricing as a selling point was indeed effective; but now, phones have become common electronic products, and low prices alone are insufficient to attract consumer favor.

Business thinking crushes user loyalty

However, just like other low-end phones from Nokia, the 1050 may still sell well in China. More than a decade of accumulation, a generation's emotions, are enough to let this outdated model fight for another ten years.

Since establishing a research and development center in Beijing in 1998, Nokia has cultivated the Chinese market for 15 years. Before 2006, Nokia could be said to have had smooth sailing, with domestic market shares once exceeding 70%, and models like 1100, 7610, N-Gage, E71, and N95 becoming star phones. In that era when the internet was not yet developed, Nokia established a good reputation among students and the younger generation through limited marketing methods such as TV and print media, and relatively affordable prices, which was the most effective user education. More commendably, this user stickiness was not accumulated through advertising campaigns, personal worship, or scarcity marketing, but through word-of-mouth promotion, allowing Nokia's user goodwill to remain fresh over time.

As they grew older, the poor students of yesteryear gradually became today's white-collar workers, with greatly improved aesthetic standards and purchasing power. Unfortunately, Nokia did not adjust its product line and marketing strategies according to these changes, but blindly believed in classic designs, constantly rehashing old products and launching them back onto the market. This led Nokia's brand image to linger in the rut of being cheap and durable, and its user base struggled to break through and even continuously shrank.

Interestingly, Nokia's low-end phones remained widely popular for a long time. The reason is that Nokia sells cheap products, but what consumers buy is emotional attachment. For example, this Finnish company's recent troubles, suffering continuous huge losses, should not have much connection with ordinary consumers, but whenever there is negative news about Nokia, a large number of Chinese netizens express regret and sorrow. Nokia accompanied a generation's growth over the past decade and has become a memory of youth alongside other retired electronic products like Xiaobang learning machines, BP machines, and Xiaolingtong phones.

However, Nokia did not carefully cherish the deep feelings of its old users, nor did it cleverly transfer them to its new generation of Lumia phones. The sharp drops in high-end phone prices and the unchanged low-end phones slowly eroded user loyalty. Ultimately, it boils down to Nokia's management not understanding the mindset of Chinese users, focusing solely on product combinations. The previous N series was like this, and the current 1050 hasn't deviated from this logic.

Yesterday afternoon, Nokia changed its China regional business director again, which is the fourth change in three years. Over the past year, Nokia's market share in China plummeted sharply from 30% to less than 4%, and the sales volume in the first quarter of this year fell more than 60% year-on-year. This has turned Nokia's emerging market strategy into a laughingstock.

Changing personnel probably won't solve Nokia's Chinese puzzle. Whether the new boss, Eric Portman, who previously managed Russian affairs, understands China better is concerning. On the other hand, under the protection of loyal users, the sales volume of the Nokia 1050 is likely to still be impressive, but this outdated phone that has long lagged behind the times will be a poison pill, continuing to corrode Nokia's image and soul, and making the Lumia series, which strives to be high-end, even harder to survive.

This summer, Chinese consumers will pay tribute to their lost youth through the Nokia 1050, but Nokia views the entire matter as a surefire good deal. Repeatedly crushing user loyalty with commercial thinking might be the final poison that consumes Nokia.
