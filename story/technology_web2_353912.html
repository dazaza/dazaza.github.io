Dragon's Valley Free External Cheat Proof - My View on Thesis Writing [Original]

by sdhyxcu4a on 2010-05-01 23:24:22

(Today, I chatted with a friend about writing papers. During the conversation, I felt that I should summarize the train of thought in writing papers and share it with my friends so that they may benefit from it. The paper is purely of an essay nature, and there are bound to be errors and inadequacies. I hope that the readers will be lenient.) Many people, especially students entering the graduate stage, have to write academic papers as part of their academic requirements. Many schools require students to publicly publish 1 to 2 papers during their graduate studies. However, in reality, many academic papers written by graduate students have many problems. These problems include unclear objects, unclear arguments, unreasonable or forced evidence that cannot adequately explain the issues at hand, etc., which are the most common problems. There is another very common and serious problem, namely patchwork papers. They often find several other people's papers, put them together, cut and paste, and can produce two papers in one night. Such papers have neither their own viewpoints nor their own evidence. Even the content written has few sentences expressed in their own words. The lack of value in such papers is self-evident.

We often say that a graduate student needs to learn a few things: (1) Learn to criticize, possess good critical thinking and a spirit of criticism; (2) Master the basic knowledge of your field, especially the cutting-edge knowledge of a certain professional direction; (3) Be able to independently use professional theory to solve problems; (4) Based on existing theories or targeting current existing problems, be able to conduct in-depth research and innovate; (5) Be able to convincingly argue for the viewpoints and solutions you propose. These are the most basic requirements for master's degree students, but in reality, many graduate students cannot study earnestly. Especially in recent years, with the large-scale expansion of graduate admissions and the pressure of employment, many students choose to take the graduate entrance exam. Graduate school has quietly become a refuge for some students who fail to find employment, leading to a situation where they just get by day by day. In this situation, many graduate students encounter numerous obvious problems when writing academic papers. The author believes that the reasons for these problems are at least the following: (1) Not studying regularly, not reading papers, not writing, and last-minute cramming, thus resorting to cutting and pasting; (2) Studying hard regularly but lacking in critical thinking; (3) Unclear target of criticism, unable to articulate the reasons for criticism; (4) Having critical thinking, being able to criticize problems, but merely acting like an angry youth without delving deeper into the issues and failing to form their own views, i.e., unclear "argument"; (5) Some students write well, with clear theories, but cannot substantiate the correctness of their thesis, failing to convince readers.

These are the major surface-level problems and also the most fundamental ones. The widespread existence of these problems forces us to worry about the quality of graduate education. Facing these issues, the author believes that the process of writing academic papers is actually a "criticize-prove-argue-prove" process. Below, using some simple language, I will discuss my understanding of the process of writing academic papers:

1. Criticize

By criticize, we mean criticism, which involves criticizing the views of existing papers or issues present in society, setting up targets for criticism, and laying the groundwork for highlighting the theme of the article. As a graduate student, one should not blindly accept all problems and statements regardless of their correctness, but instead should possess this critical spirit, be able to find "targets" for criticism, dare to criticize, and be capable of criticism. Good critical thinking and a critical spirit are essential basic qualities for a qualified graduate student. Humanities graduate students, unlike science and engineering graduate students who face many practical problems and simply solve them, mostly deal with articles, theories, and views. Therefore, the requirement for critical thought and thinking is even stricter. Faced with others' papers, one must first think about others' views, make judgments, find places in others' views that you believe may be unreasonable, and delve deeply into thinking, striving to identify their problems and shortcomings. One should have this mindset: any issue under discussion by people, even those that have become consensus, may still have flaws and can be criticized to establish one's own view. With such an attitude, one should read others' published papers and face real-world problems. Regardless of what kind of scholar, writer, or authoritative work, one should dare to criticize boldly, even if one's views may initially have problems, it doesn't matter.

2. Prove

By prove, we mean substantiation, which involves using some already recognized evidence, theories, and views to justify the legitimacy of your criticism of the established targets. This step is the most important before presenting your argument. If you criticize a problem but cannot find reasonable theories or views to substantiate the correctness of your criticism, then the legitimacy of your paper and the upcoming argumentative section will be greatly reduced, weakening the paper's persuasiveness. It would leave readers confused about why you criticize, making your criticism perplexing. For the writer themselves, if you cannot find evidence to substantiate the rationality and correctness of your criticism, you might even doubt your own reasons for criticism, let alone convincing others to agree with your views.

3. Argue

By argue, we mean presenting an argument, which refers to the author's proposed viewpoint, opinion, or solution to the problem based on criticism and substantiating its correctness. This argumentative section is crucial because it is the author's own proposal, a clear solution or viewpoint targeted at the criticized content. This is the most important and difficult part of the academic paper writing process, and also the soul of the paper. A paper, no matter how well you criticize and how fully you substantiate, if you cannot clearly propose your own views, it becomes a "critic" who criticizes for the sake of criticism, and may even make people feel that your criticism is too broad and lacks focus. Since readers cannot see the author's clear viewpoint, they will feel disappointed with your paper and suspect that you criticize just for the sake of criticizing.

4. Prove

Here, "prove" also means substantiation, but it differs from the substantiation in the second step. The former substantiates the correctness of the criticism content, while here it substantiates the correctness of the author's proposed views. Since you have proposed your own views, opinions, or solutions to the problem, whether they are reliable, correct, or can solve practical problems must be proven. Without proving the correctness of the author's proposed theory, others will doubt the correctness of your views/solutions, and your views/solutions will lack strength and vitality. Therefore, it is very important to prove its correctness using existing theories, solutions, or methods. It relates to the reliability of your views. After having these substantiations, you can draw a clear conclusion in the concluding part of the paper. Otherwise, the correctness of the conclusion will also be questioned.

The above are the author's simple views on the process of writing academic papers. Since this is an essay-like piece, the views may not be comprehensive, and I humbly share it with my friends.
