Steve Paul Jobs: The Rise of Jobs Resembles a Revenge Story

In his speech at Stanford University's graduation ceremony in June, Jobs mentioned the event of being fired from the city where he grew up and later explained that "being fired from Apple was the best thing that ever happened to me."

From Jobs' perspective, this process of returning to being an ordinary person after being a successful one gave him a sense of freedom. It also allowed him to enter the most creative phase of his life. From another angle, this period is an indispensable part of Jobs' legend. However, it is necessary to remind readers that it was still a stormy month, which ultimately promoted the personal growth of this leader in an extreme way.

The fall and rise of individuals are usually unpredictable. However, on the other hand, it reveals the secrets of the business world's ups and downs. His downfall was mainly due to excessive indulgence in technology while neglecting the overall management of the company, failing to achieve "balance." The escalation of his conflict with Sculley was essentially the result of Apple's attempt to transition from its early stages to modernization.

As the founder of Apple, Jobs found himself in the most difficult times without completing his personal transformation. Ten years later, his re-emergence demonstrated the essential elements in the governance of modern companies: inexhaustible creativity, skill in seizing opportunities to shape authoritative images, increasingly mature personality, the ability to form alliances, and an indomitable will in adversity.

iMac, iTunes, iPod... Behind these groundbreaking consumer goods stands the genius figure Steve Jobs. His success story you must have heard: At 21, he founded Apple with his good friend Wozniak in his own garage; at 22, he launched Apple II, setting a precedent for personal computers; and by 25, he had become a millionaire.

However, let us not forget his glamorous image as a businessman. In this article, Jobs is first and foremost an irreplaceable exile.

Young and Successful

Steve Paul Jobs, one of the founders of Apple, born in the United States on February 24, 1955, grew up in what is now Silicon Valley.

After graduating from high school in 1972, he studied at a college in Portland but only for one semester.

In 1974, Jobs found a job as a video game designer at a company.

A year later, at the age of 21, Jobs and 26-year-old Wozniak founded Apple Computer in Jobs' garage. Their product, the Apple II, featured 4K memory and used their own operating system display monitor, making it the first personal computer sold in the market.

Jobs later recalled: "I'm lucky because when I got into the computer field, there weren't many people who knew about computers. These people were all outstanding talents who came halfway from fields like physics, music, biology, etc. They were deeply interested in this and not just for the sake of learning computer operations."

In November 1980, Apple's stock rose to $22 per share, turning Jobs and Wozniak into overnight millionaires.

In 1986, Jobs acquired Pixar, a digital animation company. This company, now known for producing animated films like Toy Story and Finding Nemo, represents the second peak in the career of the then 46-year-old Jobs.

Falling Out

The young and successful Jobs did not foresee that he would hit a rough patch in his thirties.

Apple's main cash cow, the Apple II, started to decline after sustaining for eight years, and the sales of the newly born Macintosh were unsatisfactory.

Jobs' original intention with Macintosh was to replace IBM's leading position in the personal computer market. Therefore, he adopted a closed architecture approach, where all peripherals were independently developed by Apple. The lack of supporting software significantly contributed to Macintosh's failure.

This stubborn adherence to old ways became the beginning of Jobs' departure, and it was further exacerbated by his ally, Apple's CEO John Sculley.

In 1983, Jobs recognized the managerial talent of Sculley, the president of PepsiCo. After persuading Sculley for four months, Jobs finally convinced him with the question: "Do you want to spend your life selling sugary water or do you want to change the world?"

What Jobs didn't anticipate was that three years later, it was this trusted ally who directly caused his downfall.

Their conflict stemmed from different views on the company's development direction. Coming from the beverage industry, Sculley prioritized cost control. However, the new IT industry required high research investment: the R&D cost for Apple II was $50 million, and for Macintosh, it had soared to $800 million.

On one side, there were high R&D costs, and on the other, sluggish sales performance, causing the company's deficit to grow rapidly.

Jobs' obsession with technology led the entire company into an enterprise culture that completely worshipped technological innovation, ignoring costs and user needs.

Sculley could no longer tolerate this and decided to reform Apple's existing structure.

At the height of his power, Jobs insisted on his own way and refused to accept Sculley's proposals.

Their relationship deteriorated sharply, and by May 1985, it had reached a point where either he or Sculley had to go.

Sculley pressured the board of directors: if Jobs stayed at Apple, he would leave. In this critical moment, the board chose Sculley.

At the end of May, during a senior management meeting, it was announced that Sculley would conduct a comprehensive internal restructuring, and Jobs would no longer be involved in Apple's daily affairs.

An enraged Jobs immediately sold all his Apple shares, retaining only one share to attend the annual meeting.

This remaining share would later play a key role in his return.

Preparing for a Comeback

After leaving Apple, Jobs went through a period of depression. His friends even suspected he might commit suicide.

However, while conversing with a Nobel laureate, he suddenly had the idea to start a new company: providing powerful configurations for research institutions and universities to accelerate the research process. This would later become the Next company.

"To start again with a new group of geniuses and create something cool," was Jobs' sole thought.

So he began recruiting former Apple employees. This alarmed Sculley so much that he feared half the employees would leave the next morning.

Subsequently, Apple sued Jobs for "poaching" employees. This lawsuit severed the ties between Apple and its co-founder but also, in another way, promoted Jobs' maturity and advanced the development of his new venture.

Initially, Jobs made similar mistakes with Next as he did with Macintosh: highly precise, closed architecture, and unable to meet market expectations.

But this time, Jobs cleverly pivoted by leveraging his grasp of technology and quickly refocused Next on the development of the next-generation operating system, successfully transforming it.

Next's software system, perfectly matching Apple's needs, would later directly facilitate Jobs' return to Apple.

Meanwhile, Jobs opened another chapter in his career.

In 1986, Jobs bought Pixar Animation Studios from George Lucas for $10 million. This marked another significant chapter in Jobs' life story.

Jobs invested ten years of effort into Pixar. Starting in 1995, films like Toy Story, Finding Nemo, and The Incredibles captured the hearts of people worldwide, generating substantial box office revenue.

This remarkable individual, Jobs, thus earned the title of a successful entrepreneur, blending Silicon Valley expertise with Hollywood flair, paving the way for his triumphant return.

His Greatest Lesson

Sculley, who forced Jobs out of Apple, laid the foundation for the PowerPC alliance with IBM and Motorola (Motorola).

However, the massive investment in the Newton PDA product failed, repeating the same mistake as Macintosh due to market expectations being too far ahead.

In 1993, Sculley had no choice but to step down quietly.

Gilbert F. Amelio, who joined Apple in 1996, reported a staggering loss of $740 million in the first quarter.

A year later, Apple's stock plummeted to a historical low of $12.75.

Twelve years had passed, and it seemed that Jobs' time had come.

Having weathered the ups and downs of his career, married, and raising children, the man approaching middle age began to frequently visit Apple's management team.

Simultaneously, he told his wife: "I want to give them one last lecture!"

Jobs' confidence stemmed from the decade-long nurturing of Next.

In the early 1990s, Apple had realized that MacOS could no longer meet the demands of the next generation of applications and sought technical support from other companies.

Microsoft's Windows NT, Sun Microsystems, and IBM's Taligent were among the potential solutions.

While Apple's management debated various options, Jobs proposed that Amelio introduce the Next product.

Great business leaders are often great salespeople.

During the presentation to Apple's top executives, Jobs explained that, in terms of technical content, Next's system was particularly suitable for Internet and multimedia operations, aligning well with Apple's market positioning.

His passionate speech convinced Apple's executives that adopting Next's operating system was Apple's only viable path forward.

In December 1996, Apple officially announced the acquisition of Next Software for $427 million, and simultaneously, Jobs was appointed as Amelio's special advisor, thereby gaining access to Apple's affairs.

Since Amelio consulted Jobs on major policy decisions, Jobs' influence within the company gradually increased.

At the beginning of 1997, during a major suppliers conference, Jobs' charismatic speech quickly ignited the audience's enthusiasm, standing up spontaneously, applauding enthusiastically.

All of this sufficiently indicated that the struggling Apple urgently needed a charismatic leader to boost morale, and Jobs was the only candidate.

In July of that year, following the fifth-quarter loss, Apple's board dismissed Amelio, and Jobs began acting as CEO.

This time, he reignited Apple's full passion.

Jobs reconvened the team to formulate a new product strategy for the company;

To boost employee morale, he announced on the company's bulletin board the consideration of adjusting employees' stock options, signing off as "Jobs and Management Team."

At the Macworld Expo held in Boston in August, it was Jobs' first public appearance since returning to Apple.

At the event, Jobs announced a series of stunning news that shocked the entire industry: Larry Ellison of Oracle joined Apple's board of directors;

Microsoft invested $150 million in Apple stocks and agreed to a five-year cross-licensing agreement, etc.

Some insightful analysts saw hope for Apple's revival.

A month later, the board announced Jobs as Apple's interim CEO.

In the first quarter of 1998, Apple turned profitable, and its stock price soared.

In January 2000, Jobs received a stock option worth over $2 billion offered by the board and was formally appointed as CEO.

This longest exile ended with Jobs' most dramatic comeback.
