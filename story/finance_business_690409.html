Scenic spots in Paris:

Louvre Museum

This world-famous art palace was originally built at the end of the 12th century and was used for defensive purposes at the time. Later, after a series of expansions and renovations, it gradually became a magnificent royal palace. Since the 16th century, Francois I began to collect various artworks on a large scale, and each generation of emperors continued this tradition, enriching the collection of the Louvre. Today, the museum has collected more than 400,000 works of art, including sculptures, paintings, fine arts, and crafts, as well as ancient oriental, ancient Egyptian, and ancient Greek and Roman in seven categories. In 1981, the French government carried out large-scale repairs on this exquisite building, and since then the Louvre has become a professional museum. It is worth mentioning that there is a transparent pyramid building at the main entrance of the Louvre, and its designer is the famous Chinese-American architect I.M. Pei.

Eiffel Tower

Built in 1889 for the World's Fair at the time, it was criticized after it was built, saying that it was a pile of scrap iron that destroyed the beauty of Paris. Nowadays, this iron tower, which once held the world's tallest building record for more than forty years, has become the most important symbol of Paris.

The romantic Parisians gave the tower a beautiful name - "Shepherdess in the Clouds." The Eiffel Tower was built to solemnly commemorate the 100th anniversary of the French bourgeois revolution of 1789 on the occasion of the world-renowned international exposition. It was named after the famous French architectural engineer Gustave Eiffel, and a half-length bronze statue of Eiffel was erected under the tower.

In 1889, it was the 100th anniversary of the French Revolution, and the French government decided to celebrate grandly, holding a world exposition of unprecedented scale in Paris to display achievements in industrial technology and culture, and to build a monument symbolizing the French Revolution and Paris. The preparatory committee originally hoped to build a classical group of memorials with statues, monuments, gardens, and temples, but among more than 700 submitted plans, they chose the design of bridge engineer Eiffel (G·Eiffel, 1832-1923): a giant tower symbolizing machine civilization that can be seen from any corner of Paris.

Notre Dame Cathedral

Located on the birthplace of Paris, the Île de la Cité, it was built in 1163 and took four hundred years to complete. It is a representative work of Gothic cathedrals. The interior decoration of Notre Dame Cathedral is solemn and dignified, with colorful glass windows that are eye-catching, and the flying buttresses and grotesque water spouts are vividly lifelike.

Versailles

Originally a place for royal hunting, it was the royal palace from the reign of Louis XIV (1682) until the French Revolution. This is also a must-visit place for everyone coming to Paris. Behind the palace is a huge imperial garden. The Palace of Versailles is located 20 kilometers west of Paris and was built by Louis XIV. It is famous worldwide for its luxurious splendor and imaginative architectural design. The building complex is 580 meters long, including the royal palace, gardens, and Trianon Palace.

Arc de Triomphe

Located in the wide Place Charles de Gaulle. This is the end of the Champs-Élysées, and also the highest point of the Chaillot Hill. Extending from the Place Charles de Gaulle in four directions, there are 12 avenues. The magnificent and splendid Arc de Triomphe stands on the roundabout in the center of the square. This arch was started construction in 1806 by Charles Percier under the command of Napoleon. According to Napoleon's order, it was used to commemorate the French army. The arch was completed in 1836. It has only one archway, topped with a barrel vault, and its scale surpasses the Arch of Constantine in Rome. It is 50 meters high and 45 meters wide, with huge reliefs on each face.

The most famous and exquisite relief is the one located on the lower right side facing the Avenue des Champs-Élysées, depicting the scene of the volunteer army setting out in 1792, and this masterpiece is named "La Marseillaise". The scenes of the celebration ceremony for Napoleon's great victory are carved in other positions above this relief, and the names of each battle are engraved on the shield-shaped decorations at the top. In 1920, a "Tomb of the Unknown Soldier" was built under the arch, and every evening, an eternal flame is lit here. There is also a small memorial inside the building, recording the history of this memorial building. There, visitors can see the names of 558 generals, and some of them are underlined, indicating that these people died in battle.
