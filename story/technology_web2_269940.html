Specific actions to understand the betting odds display

by gpgp6486 on 2009-11-22 13:29:07

Understanding specific actions in the context of stock trading: I remember a seasoned insider mentioning in one of his works, "Give me a K-line chart, and I'll tell you what the major players are planning." I believe this represents the most ideal state when conducting post-market analysis.

Post-market analysis has various advantages. It allows traders to make calm judgments about individual stocks and the overall market trend without being influenced by the rapid changes in real-time market conditions. However, for short-term traders, buying and selling decisions made during trading hours can directly affect their success or failure. These seemingly simple buy-sell decisions, though honed through post-market analysis, are often disrupted by the manipulative actions of major players at the order book level. Real-time market conditions are a synthesis of various factors including the market, major players, retail investors, etc. Relying solely on post-market analysis to predict future trends may be too extreme and can easily be thrown off by random market fluctuations, leading to passive trading situations. Below, I will share some personal insights on how to identify and interpret some of the major players' trading actions and intentions from the order book.

Any action taken by a major player in the order book generally serves one of three purposes: lifting the price, shaking out weak holders, or unloading shares. Understanding the specific meaning of these actions is an essential basic skill in practical trading.

Lifting: After completing the accumulation phase in its initial position-building area, a major player will take advantage of favorable market conditions to start a lift, quickly moving the stock price away from its cost zone to prevent more low-cost shares from falling into others' hands. On the chart, this is represented by largely detaching from the index's influence and showing a relatively independent short-term upward breakout trend. In practical trading, the following aspects can help identify a major player’s lifting actions:

1. Opening Price Patterns: For strong major-player stocks, the first 20 minutes after opening are crucial during their initial jump. If the index shows typical up-and-down oscillations after the market opens, the impact on the stock price is minimal. When the index falls, the stock remains stable above the previous day's closing price, with the average price line and the stock price basically parallel. The volume ratio exceeds 1.0. Even if there are sell orders that temporarily lower the stock price, it can quickly return to the consolidation area. During this period, if there are overly aggressive large upward lifts, one should decide the buying timing based on the stock price and the average price line's positions. Do not chase high prices when the stock price rises more than 2% above the average price and the average price line fails to rise correspondingly, as the stock price will likely have a short-term regression towards the average price line. Buying around the average price line would be more prudent.

2. Intraday Patterns: Stocks showing breakout signs on the daily chart usually exhibit relatively steady intraday movements. One characteristic is that the major player rarely lets the stock price drop below the average price line because there is no need to do so on that day. If you notice the stock breaking below the average price line and struggling to recover, be cautious whether the opening pattern was merely a bullish trap. A key to identifying this action is that after an hour of such a bullish opening, the stock will inevitably fall below the average price line and decline, indicating the major player's unwillingness to protect the stock price and intentional oscillation. Therefore, if you miss the strongly performing stocks at the opening, promptly identify stocks that can break away from index oscillations and run steadily above the average price line with moderate increased volume. Buying near the average price line is relatively safe.

3. Closing Patterns: If the intra-day order book is strong, it will trigger a wave of followers in the last half hour, causing the stock price to detach from the day's market trend and move upward in a single direction. At this time, the major player will use large-scale upward lifts to seal the downward space for the next trading day. Since the follow-up buyers at this time all have a strong desire to realize short-term profits, if the stock price rises by more than 5% during the rush-buying at the close, be careful about the selling pressure caused by profit-taking the next day and the volatility brought by the major player's opening downward pressure the next day. Therefore, avoid chasing high prices excessively at the end of the day to prevent being caught in a passive situation due to the major player's short-term oscillation the next day.

Shaking Out Weak Holders: This action can occur in any area where the major player operates, with the basic purpose of clearing excess floating chips from the market and raising the overall holding cost of the market. Major players typically shake out weak holders in two ways: direct pressure and wide-range oscillation.

Direct Pressure: This method is more commonly seen in the major player's accumulation area, aiming to scare away floating chips at the same cost. Intraday manifestations include an unusually high opening price with only a few active buy orders before turning sharply downward, continuously dropping below the previous day's closing price. Retail holders then rush to exit at higher prices. Here, don't simply assume that the stock price deviating far from the average price line is a good opportunity to buy, as the opening pattern largely determines the day's trend. If the major player intentionally depresses the price at the opening, this action won’t be completed in a short time. A more stable buying point would be when the stock price undergoes several rounds of waterfall declines, dropping 3-5% or more below the average price line. At this position, short-term floating chips on the same day are no longer willing to sell, and the major player also realizes that further depression could cause some degree of chip loss. This position should be a relatively stable buying point during the shakeout process. Even if the stock doesn't reverse upward on the same day, it still represents a relatively low-price area for the day, allowing for a calm observation of its subsequent changes and enjoying the short-term profit differences during the shakeout oscillation. The waterfall decline at the close is a cost-effective tool used by the major player to create a bearish daily candlestick during the shakeout process. The manifestation on the order book is that several huge sell orders suddenly appear in the last few minutes before the close, driving down the stock price. You can discover this action from the 5-minute decline ranking list. This buying opportunity is difficult to grasp, and it is recommended not to rely on luck in actual combat to find such a buying opportunity.

Wide-range Oscillation: This method is more commonly seen in the middle of the rise and is easily mistaken by operators as the major player unloading shares. The key to identifying this action is to observe whether the major player uses the fast upward surge action before the noon close. Generally, a rapid rise in stock price just before the noon close is to open up space for the afternoon oscillation. At this time, the market generally only needs a few small buy orders to push up the stock price, and the slope of the upward surge is hard to accept, while the moving average only slightly tilts upwards. In this case, it is best to reduce holdings at high prices if you already hold the stock, because the stock price will soon return significantly to the area near the average price line, or even show a downward pressure and waterfall decline. In this situation, the average price line may remain stable despite the stock price's ups and downs, making the average price line a good reference point for entering and exiting.

Unloading Shares: As long as emotions do not interfere, it is generally easier to identify the characteristics of unloading shares at the order book level. The major player often uses a high opening price with a large volume in the pre-market auction, but the stock price cannot continue the strong momentum of the previous day and turns downward, breaking the average price line with high volume. Although there are large buy orders in the market, the stock price movement is clearly restrained by the counter-pressure of the average price line. There is no resistance at the previous day's closing price, and the downward speed of the average price line is basically consistent with the stock price, which is caused by the concentrated unloading of the major player.

A simple way to distinguish between falling during the shakeout and falling during unloading is that during the shakeout, there will be a significant waterfall decline, while unloading does not have this feature. The former will create a large distance between the falling stock price and the average price line, and the average price line has a clear restraining effect on the stock price. The latter manifests as a high-volume continuous decline, with the average price line exerting great counter-pressure on the stock price.

A heavily traded stock that performed well the previous day but opens significantly lower the next day with high volume is a rather ruthless method of unloading shares. Generally, the stock opens 3%-5% lower, with a small fluctuation throughout the day. The average price line and the stock price intertwine, and the stock price struggles to rebound to the previous day's closing price. The purpose of doing this is twofold: First, it prevents the followers from the previous day from realizing profits, creating a reluctance to sell, reducing non-major-player-related selling pressure, and eliminating unnecessary trouble in protecting the stock price; Second, it attracts empty-handed investors at a lower price to achieve the goal of smooth unloading. For such a trend, once holders notice it, they should not entertain any illusions and should choose to sell as the best strategy.
