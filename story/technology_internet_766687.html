Venture capital offers cash rewards for hackers who crack the iPhone fingerprint security technology

by anonymous on 2013-09-24 22:19:15

Global hackers are focusing on this Friday's release of the iPhone 5s by Apple in major markets, and a rapid competition has begun to crack the fingerprint recognition technology Touch ID used for the first time in the iPhone 5s. At the iPhone 5s launch event, Apple stated that the fingerprint recognition technology would ensure user data is even more secure.

According to Reuters, some security researchers, together with a micro-venture capital company, are offering a $13,000 cash prize, as well as several bottles of fine wine, bitcoins, books, and other prizes, to the first competitor who successfully breaches the defenses of the iPhone 5S. Arturas Rosenbacher, co-founder of Chicago-based IO Capital, is one of the sponsors. He said that this move will gather top talents from the hacker community to help Apple find vulnerabilities and deficiencies that it may have overlooked. Rosenbacher added: "Our original intention is to solve problems before they become issues. This will make the product more secure."

Meanwhile, according to Forbes' online edition, Jose Rodriguez, a 36-year-old soldier living in the Canary Islands, Spain, has discovered a vulnerability in the newly released iOS 7 that allows access to the photo album, email, Twitter, and other apps without entering a password.

In response, Trudy Muller, an Apple spokesperson, informed Reuters reporters that the company will address this issue and release it as part of an iOS 7 update when it is ready. Muller said: "Apple takes user data security very seriously."

David Kennedy, a former cyber intelligence analyst for the U.S. Marine Corps, is also fully prepared to participate in this hacking competition. He now runs his own consulting company, TrustedSec LLC. As the founder of the DerbyCon hacker conference, he said at last week's Thomson Reuters Global Markets Forum: "I am doing everything I can to find a way to crack the fingerprint recognition technology. I will stay up all night trying various methods to break it."

Cracking could take as little as two weeks

Security professionals are concerned that using fingerprint recognition technology to allow users to access sensitive data on their phones or complete mobile purchases may lead to many problems. The fingerprint scanner on the top-of-the-line iPhone 5s allows users to simply place their finger on the Home button to unlock the phone or make purchases on iTunes. This technology has long been considered a significant advancement in biometric technology for personal electronics.

Charlie Miller, a security engineer who is famous in the hacking circle for discovering major vulnerabilities in iPhones and bypassing the security alerts of the Apple App Store, pointed out that Kennedy or other top-tier hackers could likely crack the fingerprint recognition technology of the iPhone 5s within two weeks. Once cracked, they could easily access the vast amount of valuable data stored on the user's iPhone and make purchases on iTunes and the Apple App Store. Miller declined to comment on the hacking competition and potential security vulnerabilities in the fingerprint scanner.

Security professionals stated that based on the information Apple has disclosed so far, they have not found any fatal vulnerabilities in the fingerprint scanner. Critics praised the usability and reliability of the technology this week.

This sapphire sensor-equipped fingerprint scanner is embedded in the Home button of the iPhone 5s and scans the user's fingerprint when they touch the Home button, verifying their identity. The data used for verification is encrypted and stored in a small secure area of the iPhone 5s A7 chip. No remote server, including Apple's iCloud system, receives any related information.

HD Moore, a hacking expert and chief researcher at security software provider Rapid7, said: "This level of protection indicates a higher security rating, but it won't discourage hackers from trying to crack the new technology. This is undoubtedly a technology that hackers are very interested in, and people will continue to pay attention to it."

Cracking new technology is not uncommon

Apple should not assume that hackers only care about cracking its products.

In fact, all major electronic products with new features have undergone similar cracking processes. Google, Microsoft, and Samsung products are no exception.

In 2012, Charlie Miller led a team to demonstrate how to completely take over a Google Android smartphone via its near-field communication (NFC) technology. This wireless technology is used for data sharing or purchase transactions at POS terminals.

Software vulnerabilities are usually first discovered by so-called "white hat" hackers, who then report them back to the manufacturers for fixes, preventing criminals from exploiting them. But there is a prerequisite: "white hats" must discover these vulnerabilities before other hackers do.

Since Apple launched its first iPhone in 2007, "white hats" have discovered numerous security risks in iPhones, iPads, and the App Store. They say that such inspections have helped the iPhone become one of the most secure devices on the market today.

Kennedy said he needs to conduct comprehensive tests on the new iPhone to find the best way to attack it. He pointed out that the methods he plans to use include cracking the software used to analyze fingerprint data, or even disassembling the iPhone 5s and connecting it to a custom device that simulates the working principle of Apple's fingerprint scanner. Another possible method of cracking is: extracting fingerprints left by users on other devices and copying them onto the iPhone 5s.

The iPhone event will be held on September 10th.
