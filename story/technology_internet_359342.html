China Association for Science and Technology disclosed: the "environmental impact assessment" of coal mines has big loopholes

by ditan360 on 2010-06-28 16:37:37

The investigation results published by the Chinese Association for Science and Technology (CAST) on August 25 showed that the "cumulative effect" problem of ecological environment degradation caused by coal mine development has long been neglected, and there are significant loopholes in the current environmental impact assessment of coal mines.

This investigation was completed by China University of Mining and Technology. At a press conference held by CAST on August 25, relevant personnel from CAST stated that this investigation is the first systematic research both domestically and internationally on the cumulative resource and environmental effects of coal mining and its evaluation results. It provides a new perspective for better coal mining evaluation and planning design, and explores the scientific development path of the coal industry, with important theoretical and practical value.

According to Wang Yunjia, professor of China University of Mining and Technology and head of the coal mine ecological environment investigation project team, coal mine exploitation often causes surface subsidence, gas, dust, gangue pollution, and other ecological environment degradations. The characteristics of coal resource extraction, such as long duration, high disturbance intensity, and gradually expanding influence range, determine that the ecological environment degradation in coal mine areas has obvious "cumulative" features.

Previously, investigators studied and analyzed the ecological environment problems, main sources of influence, and pathways of influence in the Luan, Huainan, Pingdingshan, Yanzhou, and other mining areas.

Taking Shanxi, a major coal-producing province, as an example, the investigation mentioned that in 2003, the losses caused by environmental pollution and ecological destruction in the coal industry accounted for about 11.5% of the province's GDP that year; by 2005, the area affected by coal mining subsidence had reached 1,084.886 square kilometers, affecting 170,524 households and involving 394,388 residents; in many mining areas in our country, unreasonable coal development has endangered the resource and environmental safety of the mining area. The number of "triple-nothing" farmers (no land, no water, no housing) and "environmental refugees" in the mining area is increasing, and the "four-mining" (mining industry, mining mountains, miners, mining cities) issues are severe.

Wang Yunjia said that the generation of ecological environment problems in mining areas is the result of mutual interaction between the socio-economic development, population concentration, and resource extraction in the mining area, and the fact that the cumulative effect has not received due attention is a very important factor.

He said that traditional coal development environmental impact assessments have exposed many inherent defects. Coal development zones are often composed of several or even a dozen mines, and the mining of each mine is gradual, with a cumulative and superimposed impact on the ecological environment. However, traditional coal mine environmental impact assessments often only consider individual engineering projects and their influence over time and space, ignoring spatiotemporal effects and the comprehensive or cumulative impacts on the environment generated between one project and others.

"Our new round of coal development is mainly concentrated in the ecologically fragile regions of Jinsha-Mengning (Shanxi-Shaanxi-Mongolia-Ningxia). To ensure the resource and environmental safety of coal development zones, it is necessary to comprehensively consider the spatial configuration relationship between coal, water, environmental resources, etc., and reasonably determine the scale and mining areas under full consideration of resource and environmental capacity," Wang Yunjia said.

Regarding the loopholes currently existing in coal mine environmental impact assessments, the project team stated that it is necessary to evaluate and predict the cumulative effects of resource and environment caused by different development schemes. However, the investigators indicated that there is currently a serious lack of research in this area in our country, and the theoretical preparation is clearly insufficient.

The project team suggested mastering the mechanism of the impact of coal development on resources and the environment, establishing a model representing the cumulative effects of resources and the environment. In this way, mining and related activities can be controlled or optimized from aspects such as time-space, layout, and scale, reducing the occurrence of cumulative effects, thereby effectively reducing the damage caused by coal development to the resource and environmental systems of the mining area.
