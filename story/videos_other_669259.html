Mango:

The variety of mango: Egg mango (called in Fujian), with a strong smell, more oval shape, orange flesh, as big as a small egg, extremely sweet and fragrant, suitable for juicing, very concentrated juice, can be mixed with light honey water; slightly inferior to the egg mango is the waist mango, shaped like a small pig kidney, yellow flesh, some are light yellow, very thin core, this kind of mango is very sweet, but not as fragrant as the egg mango; there are also ivory mango, Empress mango, will have some astringency and sourness when not fully ripe. There is also a green mango called Green Mango, which is sweeter than the yellow one.

Selection: When selecting mangoes, choose those that are relatively full and round, neither too soft nor too hard, purely yellow in color, with a strong fragrance from afar, without black spots (those with freckles are rotten inside). Smell the fragrance and select those that are about 80% ripe, which can be eaten after being left for one or two days to fully ripen. For fully ripe mangoes ready to eat, check the base of the mango, it should be very fresh, without water oozing out, and the skin should not be wrinkled, otherwise the moisture has already been lost.

The juice of mango contains components that are not good for the skin, so it is best to cut it into small pieces and avoid contact with the skin and lips, then it will be fine.

Grapes:

Choose grapes that are plump and tightly packed together. First, smell them; buy only if they have a strong fruity aroma. If choosing grapes in winter, make sure to pick fresh ones. Don't judge by the size of the berries, but by the stem — fresh grapes have firm, bright green stems. Once the stem turns dark brown and feels soft, it means the grape has been off the vine for a long time. Even though such grapes might appear firm, it's actually due to low-temperature storage, not true freshness. Be especially careful when buying "Rose Fragrance" grapes, as these small berries don't keep well.

Durians:

Choose durians with a strong fragrance, slightly cracked ones where you can see the soft and glutinous flesh inside, not overly wet, with elongated, golden-yellow fruit segments. Durians with a more plump shape tend to have thinner shells and more segments. Rounder-shaped ones usually have thicker shells and less fruit. When picking, shells that are yellow-green are preferable.

Papayas:

Papayas come in male and female varieties. The ones with larger bellies are female and tend to be sweeter. Generally, choose papayas with protruding bellies, many surface spots, and a just-turned-yellow texture that isn't too soft. If there’s a bit of gel-like substance on the surface, that’s fine—it’s sugar gel, indicating sweetness. If you intend to eat immediately, choose yellow-skinned ones, but they shouldn’t be too soft, as such papayas are sweet but not mushy. For dishes like papaya spare rib soup, unripe green-skinned papayas are ideal—they're generally harder and not eaten raw. When ripe, the skin turns yellow and can be eaten as a fruit. For desserts, red Hawaiian papayas are recommended for their sweetness.

Lychees:

When selecting lychees, go for those with uneven, deep-textured skin—these usually have smaller seeds! Smooth, flat-skinned ones typically have large seeds. Besides having a bright red luster, they should feel firm and elastic when squeezed—if lacking elasticity and feeling soft and hollow, they've been sitting for too long. You can gently press the bottom of the lychee; if it feels soft without any hard object beneath, the seed is generally small. If you feel a hard object, it likely has a larger seed.

Kiwi:

Select kiwis with pointed heads, resembling a chick's beak, rather than flat ones resembling a duck's beak. Flat ones have been treated with hormones, while pointed ones haven't or have had minimal hormone use. Truly ripe kiwis are entirely super soft. When picking, choose those with a slightly deeper color, nearing a soil-yellow hue, indicating sufficient sunlight exposure and greater sweetness. Choose kiwis with tender green near the stem for freshness. They should be consistently firm throughout; if one part is soft, it indicates spoilage. Darker colors around the stem area also indicate sweetness.

Apples:

Fuji apples should be selected based on their appearance—those with a streaked look, yellowish-red hues. Such apples have normal yellow flesh, are crispy, sweet, and slightly acidic with a pure taste. Avoid those that are green with a hint of red, as these often have a bland taste, possibly with plenty of water content, but lack sufficient sweetness. Genuine Red Fuji apples placed on a table are always tilted, never perfectly upright, otherwise, they aren't authentic. Their color shouldn't be excessively red, but slightly pinkish, not uniformly red. Choose those with many red streaks, and the "navel" should be deeply indented for sweetness. Apples with more spots on the skin, which produce a crisp sound when lightly tapped with your finger, are both sweet and crunchy.

For apples (especially Red Fuji), it's best to select those with strip-like red patterns ("strip red") and some yellow within the red. These apples are crispy, juicy, and very sweet.

Strawberries:

Avoid strawberries that are too red; the brighter the color, the more sour they tend to be. Strawberries with a hint of white in their red are the sweetest. When purchasing strawberries, avoid those that are abnormally large or oddly shaped. Instead, choose strawberries of consistent size, smaller ones being safer, with colors not overly red but ideally containing small white dots inside.

Tomatoes:

When selecting tomatoes, avoid those with sharp edges or feel unusually light, as these are likely caused by ripening agents. Opt for tomatoes with a faint powdery surface and a smooth, rounded stem area. If the stem area has a slight green tinge, it signifies the sweetest, sandiest texture. Avoid tomatoes with sharp tips or high bottoms; instead, choose those with a smoother overall appearance. Tomatoes treated with excessive rapid ripening agents in spring often have sharp tips. Select tomatoes with a pinkish hue, round shape, and small white dots on the skin.

Peaches:

When selecting peaches, primarily rely on the fragrance—the sweeter the smell, the sweeter the peach. Usually, they don't look particularly appealing, so pick those that aren't too soft, with a small peak at the top.

Tangerines, Pomelos, Oranges:

"High-bodied oranges, flat-bodied mandarins, smooth-skinned tangerines." Oranges should be tall and slender, mandarins flat, and tangerines should have shiny skin. Regardless of the type, those with a ring-shaped feature near the center are considered "female" and are usually sweeter. Those with a dot-like feature are "male," not as sweet as the females. Choose those with thin, elastic skin. Early-season tangerines have more juice and sweetness, while late-season ones are rougher and fibrous. For pomelos, tangerines, and oranges, choose heavier ones, with smooth skin like a baby's. Avoid rough, pockmarked skins unless referring to winter's popular sugarcane tangerines, which have coarse skin, whereas smooth ones are of lesser quality. When buying oranges, prefer those with a circle at the bottom. For navel oranges, those with an inward-dipping hole at the bottom tend to be sweeter. Generally, firmer pomelos and honey pomelos are better. Heavier ones of the same size are preferred. Pressing them, the firmer the better, and the thinner the skin the better. Honey pomelos with smoother, more evenly golden-yellow skin are better. Such pomelos have ample fruit water content, higher sweetness, and a purer taste.

Here are some insider secrets from fruit sellers on how to pick fruits, take notes!

Firstly, here's a quick tip for the impatient: The secret to picking fruits is that regardless of the type, the sweeter the fruit, the deeper the indentation near the stem.

Choose fruits of moderate size; overly large ones are likely induced by hormones or growth enhancers. In general, overly large fruits—or even crabs, fish, and shrimp from rivers—are best avoided!

Most southern fruits sold in the north are artificially ripened using various chemicals because ripe southern fruits do not transport well. Thus, they are shipped unripe and processed locally. Therefore, eating local fruits is another way to avoid this issue.

Generally, follow these steps: first smell, second look, third squeeze. Smell to ensure the fruit has its characteristic fragrance and no other strange odors. Look for any black spots or signs of rot. Squeeze to test the firmness, no further explanation needed.

The criteria for selecting fruits include choosing those with vibrant, glossy colors. Also, check the base of the fruit to see if it is deeply indented and has a circle, which indicates it is a "female" fruit and thus sweeter.

Pears:

Choose "female" pears, which are finer, juicier, and sweeter. Female pears have an even number of dimples at the top, deeper navels, and a more rounded area around the navel with a thicker stem base.

Cherries:

Check if the stalk is fresh. Fresh cherries have green stalks, while older ones have blackened stalks. Then, check if the skin is wrinkled; wrinkles indicate the fruit has been stored for too long and has lost water content.

Bananas:

Avoid bananas with green ends; these have been treated and are not naturally ripe. Also, avoid overly large bananas; smaller, rounder ones without edges are better. Ripe bananas are typically yellow with small brown spots.

Cucumbers:

Never buy cucumbers with large bellies and small stems; these have been treated with hormones.

Watermelons, Hami Melons, Cantaloupes:

When selecting watermelons, choose those whose stripes seem stretched apart, with smaller tails at the blossom end, and an inward-dipping "belly button." The surface should not be smooth but bumpy. The color should be greener with a deeper hue. Hold the melon with one hand and gently tap it; it should sound hollow, and your hand should feel a slight vibration. This indicates a sandy, less dense interior. If unripe, it will sound dull.

For white-fleshed sweet melons, choose smaller ones with no navel at the larger end but a slight green tint. These are the first fruits of the plant and are easier to pick because they are smaller. Another option is to choose melons with a navel; the larger the navel, the better. Pressing the navel area should feel soft. Smelling the "bottom" of the cantaloupe, if it has a fragrance, it is a good and sweet melon.

For Hami melons, choose ones with thick and dense net-like patterns and a strong fragrance.

When buying any melon, if the end opposite the vine is indented, it is ripe, good, and sweet.
