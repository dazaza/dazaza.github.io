Getting sick five times is beneficial to physical health.

by meimei234 on 2011-12-23 12:03:25

Medical research shows that not all illnesses are harmful to the body. When pathogens invade the body or the body undergoes pathological changes, a defensive response occurs, which is what we call "getting sick." Therefore, being sick is not always detrimental and can sometimes be beneficial.

**Fever:** A temperature above 37°C is considered a fever. Medical studies have proven that the combat capability of immune cells increases with rising body temperature. At the same time, high body temperature reduces the concentration of iron ions in the blood, leading to insufficient supply of trace iron ions required for pathogen growth, thereby inhibiting the development of pathogens. These are the main forces in fighting diseases, while drugs are just external aids. Therefore, taking antipyretics at the onset of a fever may worsen the condition. If the body temperature does not exceed 38°C and there is no significant impact on mental state or appetite, you can place ice on your forehead to relieve discomfort while drinking more water and appropriately supplementing vitamins and minerals. You can also eat some nutritious yet light foods. If the body temperature exceeds 38.5°C, or if there is persistent low-grade fever, or if symptoms such as rashes, severe headache, joint pain, or convulsions occur, immediate medical attention should be sought.

**Cough:** Coughing is one of the most common symptoms in the respiratory system and is a protective measure for the human body, which is beneficial to the organism. When the respiratory mucosa is stimulated by foreign objects, inflammation, secretions, or allergic factors, it reflexively triggers a cough, helping to eliminate foreign objects or secretions from the outside that invade the respiratory tract and remove irritants from the respiratory tract. If antitussive drugs are used immediately upon coughing, it will leave the dirt that exacerbates inflammation in the respiratory tract, hindering the elimination of inflammation. When coughing occurs, you should stay warm while consuming large amounts of water and avoiding spicy foods like peppers and onions that stimulate the respiratory tract. If frequent coughing is accompanied by fever, chest pain, weight loss, etc., immediate medical attention should be sought.

**Nosebleed:** We can consider nosebleeds as a way for the body to "detoxify" itself. When people drink less water, consume more spicy foods, and live in dry environments, leading to "internal heat," the body often chooses the weak nasal mucosa to "detoxify." So when nosebleeds occur, there's no need to worry excessively, as they usually stop on their own within a short period. However, if the nosebleed continues after some time, an early checkup is recommended. In this case, you can lean forward and let the blood flow naturally, using paper towels to gently absorb the blood flowing out of the nose. If the bleeding is heavy, pinch the soft part of the nostrils for a few minutes, or raise the arm opposite the bleeding nostril to help stop the bleeding. If the nose bleeds heavily and continuously, immediate medical attention should be sought, as this is often caused by hardened blood vessels or excessively high blood pressure.

**Diarrhea:** Diarrhea, especially when the excrement has an unusual odor, is often the body expelling toxins from food or other sources. When diarrhea occurs, it's important not to fall into treatment misconceptions, such as: using anti-diarrheal medication for every instance of diarrhea; assuming diarrhea equals inflammation and thus requiring antibiotics; using painkillers to alleviate diarrhea-related pain; taking medicine only during severe episodes and stopping when it subsides; drinking more water to replenish lost fluids; eating less during diarrhea and more afterward. When encountering diarrhea, it's advisable to adopt a method that follows the natural course, such as drinking a cup of hot water to help the intestines quickly expel toxins. If there is severe diarrhea with frequent urges to defecate, small amounts of stool, anal spastic pain, bloody stools, or accompanying abdominal pain, fever, or vomiting, immediate medical attention should be sought.

**Vomiting:** Vomiting is also aimed at expelling substances harmful to the body, reducing absorption, and is a defensive reflex mechanism of the body. Using antiemetic drugs at this point would leave harmful substances lingering in the body, endangering health. For simple vomiting, an appropriate amount of water should be consumed to avoid water and electrolyte imbalance. If gastrointestinal discomfort accompanies the vomiting, hydration should be combined with massaging the stomach and abdomen. After vomiting, do not rush to eat; instead, engage in slight activity to allow the body to relax before eating. If vomiting recurs or is accompanied by abdominal pain, fever, etc., immediate medical attention should be sought.
