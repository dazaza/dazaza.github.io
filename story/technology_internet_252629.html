Face and Development

Today, as I passed by the New World Plaza in Shanghai, I saw a group of people surrounding a small band that was dancing and singing. It moved me deeply. Perhaps many people look down on street performers, thinking it is embarrassing. In fact, many successful bands and artists around the world started their careers by performing on the streets or in cafes.

During this process, they gain experience through direct feedback from the audience and build confidence in performing on stage. I remember that the difference between professional athletes and amateurs lies in an extra 10,000 hours of effort. When they accumulate enough experience and work hard enough, these singers transcend from being amateurs to professionals. Quantitative changes gradually lead to qualitative changes, and with practice comes skill. Unconsciously, they become top-tier singers and artists.

Whether a hotel excels depends crucially on the quality and ability of its manager. Relying solely on giving orders to other employees to manage a hotel well is impossible; such a manager lacks management skills and capability. A person who cannot solve problems cannot become a math teacher, and someone who has never solved equations cannot become a university math professor! Therefore, a manager without practical experience cannot manage a hotel well.

To become a competent manager, one needs not only some basic theoretical knowledge but also personal involvement in every specific task at the hotel. Through your own practice, you will understand the various job roles and facilities, truly realizing the challenges our employees face in their work. Only then can you help them overcome these issues and have the ability to guide other employees.

Many managers care about "face," and I understand this. However, those who are unwilling to leave their offices, unwilling to take off their suits, personally participate in engineering repairs, cleaning rooms, frontline sales, or bending down to pick up garbage from the ground, cannot possibly gain much experience or capability. If a person sitting in the position of a manager does not even understand a small engineering problem, how can they have the "face" to command our maintenance personnel? Being incompetent and having high expectations without matching abilities, how can they earn respect? Where does the so-called "face" come from? Therefore, this so-called "face" is self-deception and an obstacle to self-development!

If the managers of our GreenTree Inn appear on the front lines, working alongside employees to solve problems and serve guests, these managers not only gain valuable experience but also win the respect of all employees and guests alike. This not only doesn't lose face but, on the contrary, I believe this is true dignity, real respect.

I hope not only for the managers of GreenTree Inn chain hotels but for all hotel managers to have enough "face." I hope all our administrators are recognized by our employees and society. I hope this love for "face" becomes pressure for your own development and transforms into motivation. What we want is the genuine "face" earned through hard work and respect, not the false "face" bestowed by others' favoritism.
