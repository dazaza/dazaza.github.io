Samsung's logic

by anonymous on 2013-09-08 19:33:17

Two years ago, Samsung experimented with the Phablet form factor, which lies between mainstream smartphones and tablets. The Galaxy Note was still somewhat puzzling, but the Galaxy Note 2 quickly validated market demand.

Today, a host of manufacturers are wielding large-screen phones, sharpening their knives. How will the Galaxy Note 3 defend its honor?

Will it be through stunning industrial design, intelligent user experiences, or technological innovation?

Samsung, firmly seated as the global leader in smartphone shipments, has all the capital to provide a satisfying answer.

As before

The anticipated features did not appear. A top-tier processor, 3GB of massive RAM, and 4K video recording that's more hype than practical were all expected. Additionally, Samsung continues to enhance the S Pen, the best companion for the Note series, to highlight the business functionality of this phone.

With 5-inch screens becoming mainstream and the large screen advantage of the Note fading, how likely is it that the Galaxy Note 3 can continue to dominate with just a pen?

Not without surprises, such as covering a distinctly plastic core with a faux leather back.

The same issues

The problems with the Galaxy Note series are the same as those with the Galaxy S series.

After enduring patent troubles with the Galaxy S and Galaxy S2, Samsung introduced the Galaxy S3, a comprehensive solution to avoid patent disputes, calling it "inspired by nature."

The Galaxy S4 indeed took a step forward in exploring the division of labor among processors, theoretically being more efficient and energy-saving. However, the so-called octa-core inevitably carries the suspicion of being a marketing ploy. Moreover, Smart Pause, Smart Scroll, and S Translator remain beautiful in concept.

Who is thinking differently

The Lumia 1020 integrated a 41-megapixel lens into the phone and employed advanced supersampling technology for zooming. It did not opt for a brute-force approach with a large optical zoom lens.

The Moto X featured a complex X8 system processor aimed at providing better intelligent experiences and battery life, despite often being misunderstood as a mid-range dual-core device. It did not choose the currently popular Qualcomm Snapdragon 800.

Apple boldly embraced flat design, aiming to explore more intuitive and efficient user experiences, even if it risks losing users. It did not stick to the long-popular skeuomorphic design.

Even with plastics, the N9 chose a vibrant, sturdy, and tactile type, showcasing hardware design widely praised.

You may think they were forced by market constraints, but I prefer to believe it relates to courage, choice, and taste.

Peak can also manufacture shoes as visually appealing as Nike’s, yet it hasn't followed Nike’s persistent exploration in ankle protection and shock absorption technologies.

Full industry chain

Controlling the entire industry chain, Samsung can minimize component costs and ensure massive supply demands. Thus, it can freely offer a full range of phone sizes, ensuring one always hits the market blind spot.

Do you remember HTC's sorrow when there was a Desire screen shortage?

Samsung's logic

When Samsung's flagship phones first hit the market, their prices could rival the latest iPhone models, only to start plummeting rapidly months later, creating an impression of extremely high cost-effectiveness for users.

"Exaggerated pricing" is Samsung's logic.

You won't see exquisite material backs on Samsung's flagship phones, but you will see huge Samsung logos across Victoria Harbour.

Facing the choice between increasing costs to refine hardware design and seeking technological innovation, or cleverly launching an overwhelming marketing campaign, the latter is Samsung's logic.

Whether it's the appearance of the Galaxy S4 or the Galaxy Note 3, both align with the public demand for "no personality equals smoothness, no standout means most amicable."

Just like Taobao's chaotic homepage, it's a typical case of prioritizing commerce over design.

Because getting the most people to buy is Samsung's logic.

Final words

Idealism has never been a competitive weapon in commercial society and sometimes can even become an obstacle.

But products imbued with emotion, belief, and courage,

will endure.
