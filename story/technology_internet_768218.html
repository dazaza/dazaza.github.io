Facebook Ads Warning Sounds Alarm Bell for Twitter on Roadshow

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 19:10:05

Facebook's investors and supporters believe that, as the social network company with the most users worldwide, it will inevitably bring boundless opportunities to advertisers. However, this belief was cooled by a statement from the company's CEO David Ebersman on Wednesday: he indicated that the advertisements Facebook can show to its users might be limited.

Ebersman's warning not only affects Facebook itself but also other social media companies, including Twitter, which is currently on a roadshow. Twitter has yet to turn a profit, but its advertising model is very similar to Facebook's.

"Investors must understand that there are limits to mobile advertising revenue," said analyst Jeff Sica of U.S. investment firm Sica Wealth Management, "This is Facebook's problem, and it will also become Twitter's problem."

In its seven years of existence, Twitter is facing an increasing number of challenges: although the company has 230 million active users, its growth rate is far lower than Facebook's, partly due to their inability to retain newly registered users. A recent Reuters survey showed that 36% of Twitter users do not use the service.

"You don't know how many people sign up and then stop using it, or how many have abandoned their accounts," said analyst Adam Crossman of investment company Middleton & Co., who attended Twitter's roadshow in Boston on Thursday.

Twitter has set its IPO price range at $17 to $21, aiming to raise up to $1.6 billion. At this price, Twitter's valuation could reach up to $11 billion, lower than the $15 billion estimated by some analysts.

An investor who attended Twitter's roadshow on Thursday said that Twitter's famous brand will attract some investors. "This is a company that has changed the world, so I won't bet against it," said the investor, who wished to remain anonymous, "But they haven't created a business model that can grow by 100% annually and achieve profitability."

Third-party Advertising

Twitter is trying to expand its advertising business through other means. This week, the company completed a $350 million acquisition of mobile ad network MoPub.

"In the eyes of users, there is a limit to the number of ads that can capture their attention without irritating them," said Rich Wong, partner at U.S. venture capital firm Accel Partners, who previously invested in MoPub and AdMob, the mobile ad network acquired by Google for $750 million.

Rich Wong stated that after acquiring MoPub, Twitter can increase revenue by placing ads in other apps, rather than being confined to the users' Twitter feed. "With the leasing model, you can scale the eyeballs significantly," he said.

Whether MoPub can uncover new revenue opportunities for Twitter remains to be seen. However, some industry insiders believe this deal is similar to Google's $3.1 billion acquisition of DoubleClick in 2008. That acquisition helped Google deliver targeted ads to other websites, no longer limited to Google.com.

In the case of MoPub, Twitter will place ads in third-party mobile apps, not websites.

According to multiple tech blogs, despite pausing its mobile ad network project earlier this year, Facebook restarted the project in late September. The reason for expanding this new revenue-generating plan is that the proportion of ads published in the user's News Feed has approached its upper limit. Ebersman stated on Wednesday that the 5% News Feed ad display rate would not increase much in the future.

This statement surprised some analysts and investors who had high expectations. "5% is a bit low, I'm surprised it's only 5%. I expected more," said analyst Brian Blau of U.S. market research firm Gartner.

More Precise Targeting

The prospectus shows that Twitter currently generates $0.65 per user, less than Facebook's $1.72. Analysts believe that Twitter has great growth potential in this area because the company's CEO Dick Costolo has been cautious about inserting more ads into the Twitter feed.

Blau believes that Twitter's model and the fast-scrolling nature of its ads differ from Facebook's, meaning that Twitter users are more tolerant of ads. However, if Twitter's ability to display more ads is restricted, it will seek higher prices through more precisely targeted ads.

In the past year, Twitter has expanded its ad targeting functions, delivering precise ads based on the regions or topics of interest to some users. Twitter determines users' interests based on the accounts they follow and the messages they post. In July, the company began using cookies to track the web pages users have visited, a technique already widely adopted by internet companies.

However, while marketers praise these highly personalized ads, social media companies must also weigh the negative effects, since excessively mining user interests may make some people feel uneasy, or even cause them to leave.
