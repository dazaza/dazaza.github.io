CN domain name opens to overseas registrants, domestic market size hits bottom

by qiyeseo5774 on 2012-02-27 22:32:35

CN Domain Names Open for Overseas Registration

Station Network, February 16th News - Not long ago, the Singaporean domain name registration company IP Mirror claimed that individuals and organizations from the UK, Hong Kong, Singapore, Malaysia, and Australia will be able to directly register Chinese domain names, using .CN, .中国 (China), .公司 (Company), and .网络 (Network), without having to pay for a local presence certification service fee. According to CNNIC's approval process, these domain names will then be released from their server-hold status, allowing them to resolve to websites and activate email services. This means that overseas entities from these regions registering domain names also need to go through CNNIC's approval, and during use, must comply with CNNIC’s policies regarding website content management. If violations are discovered, the domain name may be banned by CNNIC.

At the end of 2009, relevant national departments conducted a large-scale cleanup of illegal and inappropriate content on the internet, targeting domestic internet environments. A number of websites with issues such as illegal content and incomplete filing information were shut down during this cleanup. The strict one-size-fits-all policy also affected many legitimate websites to varying degrees. Subsequently, CNNIC tightened the registration policies for CN domain names, stipulating that starting from December 14, 2009 at 9:00 AM, individual user domain registrations would cease, and also closed the channel for overseas CN domain name registration. This became a turning point for CN domain names, with the total number falling from tens of millions at its peak to around three million today. In just two years, CN domain names plummeted from heaven to hell, and it has been difficult for them to recover.

This time, the re-opening of CN domain names for overseas registration has led many industry insiders to speculate that it could inject a strong stimulus into the long-depressed CN domain name market, potentially triggering a new wave of CN domain name registrations. Admin5 editor believes that CNNIC opening CN domain names for overseas registration can be seen as a largely meaningless [self-rescue]. Promoting CN domain names abroad is more symbolic than practical for CN domain names, with reputation effects outweighing market effects. In the long term, it may enhance the image of CN domain names but is unlikely to bring substantial increases in numbers. There are mainly three reasons for this:

Firstly, we all know that the development of CN domain names began around 2006 when the domestic internet emerged from the bubble and began to recover and warm up again. The development of the internet economy spurred the birth of a new batch of websites, both personal webmasters and large websites saw some development. In 2007, CN domain names launched the [One Yuan Registration] campaign, which drastically lowered the threshold for CN domain names, promoting their rapid popularization with populist policies, making them the preferred choice for webmasters building sites. As a result, CN domain names experienced explosive growth, becoming the most widely used country-level top-level domain globally, rivaling COM domain names. However, the explosive growth of CN domain names also revealed certain problems due to the special environment of China's internet and the savage growth of webmasters, leading to various illegal and infringing behaviors, with pornographic and junk websites proliferating. The subsequent blanket cleanup policy caused a large number of CN sites to fail, and the introduction of new CN domain name policies prohibiting individual registrations further eroded webmaster confidence in CN domain names. Even though enterprise terminal values increased instead of decreasing, it was difficult to drive overall CN domain name growth again. Although opening overseas registration could lead to some growth, trying to remedy the astronomical drop over two years is unrealistic.

Secondly, for overseas institutions, the application significance and value of CN domain names are limited. Although the areas opened this time are all places where there are large concentrations of Chinese people, with many Chinese enterprises and Chinese-language websites. Opening CN domain name registration in these places could indeed drive some growth, but the numbers should not reach significant scale. On one hand, CN domain names belong to country-level top-level domains, and their application scope is generally concentrated domestically. Even if there are a large number of Chinese enterprises and websites abroad, to establish themselves locally, their own country-level top-level domains and COM domain names are the first choice. Unless overseas organizations launch websites to enter the domestic market, CN domain names cannot demonstrate their own value. The true meaning of a country-level top-level domain lies in widespread domestic use. With the domestic market in disarray, seeking a breakthrough in overseas Chinese regions is akin to avoiding the main issue and not finding the real solution to the problem. Going global may give CN domain names some reputation and image promotion, but the debt accumulated over the past two years is not easily repaid.

Thirdly, from the perspective of the open policy, during the period of overseas open registration for CN domain names, they need to comply with CNNIC's relevant policies regarding website content management. If violations are found, the domain name may be banned by CNNIC. Foreign website and domain name management policies differ greatly from those in China, with almost no common ground. Overseas institutions registering CN domain names to build websites will inevitably adapt to local website and domain name policies. If the website does not violate local policies but conflicts with CNNIC's management policies, it will inevitably be banned. From previous CN domain name cleanups, it is clear that CNNIC finds it difficult to formulate short-term management methods suitable for overseas website content and local internet policies and environments. Governing overseas website domain names with domestic policies will inevitably lead to conflicts and various troubles and problems.

The total domestic market volume has hit bottom; going global is hard to save the market.

From the latest statistical charts released by CNNIC, in December 2011, the total number of CN domain names was 3,528,511, reaching the highest level of the year. However, throughout 2011, CN domain names have been stuck in a cycle of rising one month and falling the next, with fluctuations of about 100,000 each time. Its fundamental nature of having fallen to rock bottom and being unable to rebound has not fundamentally changed. Perhaps stimulated by the news of overseas open registration and the possible reopening of domestic individual registration in 2012, CN domain names may set new growth records in 2012, but wanting to return to the tens of millions of users from back then seems like an impossible task. Its new strategy of going global is only treating the symptoms and not the root cause, putting the cart before the horse. Without making more improvements in policy and management and aligning with internationally renowned domain name management structures, whether domestically or overseas, it will be difficult to seek higher development. Going global is not a magic bullet for saving the market.
