Google Aims to Upend HTML5 Standards: New Video Encoding Reduces Size by 60%

by geekzhang on 2013-05-17 08:16:10

Beijing, May 16th news - In recent years, major software and hardware companies in the industry have been rushing to release the new HTML5 standard, with various parties joining different camps. For example, Opera, Adobe, AMD, ARM, and other companies support Google's WebM standard; while Sony, Fuji, Samsung, Microsoft, and others are patent holders of the H.264 standard proposed by Apple.

The H.264 standard has always been one of the main compression technologies for online video and has a growing trend to replace Flash video formats. Its main supporters are Microsoft's Internet Explorer (IE) browser and Apple's product line. The former ensures H.264's advantage in the desktop device market, while the latter ensures H.264's advantage in the portable device market. In fact, most of H.264's patents are held by Microsoft and Apple. A senior official from Microsoft's IE browser team publicly stated that H.264 would be the only supported HTML5 video codec in IE9.

In China, due to the unification of the Blu-ray format, the vast majority of high-definition videos on the market use H.264 encoding. Mainstream video websites generally support the H.264 decoding format. It is understood that China's broadcasting system and telecom operators once regarded H.264 as the primary direction for promotion. In already operational IPTV projects by telecom companies, almost all adopted H.264; and in the conversion from analog to digital, as well as in network bidirectional renovations within the broadcasting system, H.264 technology standards were widely adopted.

After H.264 united the hardware player camp and developed for many years, it was once considered the standard for next-generation video technology. Under pressure, Google's YouTube announced around the end of 2010 that it would open its doors to H.264.

At today’s I/O conference, Google announced an upgrade to the web video format WebM: its video encoder has been upgraded from VP8 to VP9, reducing the size of generated videos by 63% compared to H.264.

Google also announced that YouTube will henceforth support VP9 video (as early as the beginning of 2011, Google decided that its next-generation browser Chrome would support the new WebM video codec technology but not H.264). Clearly, Google hopes to use VP9 to end H.264's leadership in the next generation of video standards. However, rumors suggest that H.264 is also being upgraded to H.265, which might gain more hardware support.

Of course, Google's confidence in challenging the H.264 standard dominated by Apple is related to Apple secretly restricting the development of HTML5 in iOS over the past two years to protect its own interests. Although Steve Jobs was once a key force in challenging Flash and promoting HTML5 development, in the post-Jobs era, Apple has done little to maintain its existing advantages in the mobile internet business model. In the future layout of HTML5, Google has gradually taken the lead over Apple due to its more open attitude.
