Precautions for raising egg-laying hens in winter and spring

The precautions for raising egg-laying hens in winter and spring are provided by the 5830 Agricultural Website Navigation: In winter, when egg production is at its lowest or stops completely, based on ensuring the health of the egg-laying hens, you can feed the chickens without grain or with minimal grain to maintain their feeding. Implement coarse feeding techniques and clever fine feeding methods to maximize the reduction of feed costs, lower breeding costs, and pick up the economic benefits of chicken farming from subtle details and put them into your pocket. The specific approach can be divided into three steps:

When egg production begins in the following spring, replace the coarse feed with carbohydrate-rich feed such as corn, melon dry, and sorghum, and increase the amount of fishmeal, soybean cake, peanut cake, bone meal, trace elements, and amino acids. Fully utilize the production performance of the chicken's body, improve feed efficiency, and achieve the best economic benefits of chicken farming.

After mixing various coarse feeds evenly, mix all kinds of additives with the coarse feed evenly (coarse feed can account for 80% to 85%, while fine feed and various additives can account for 20% to 15%) before feeding. This not only saves a large amount of grain but also gives the chickens a sense of fullness. Under the action of various additives and fine feed, coarse feed undergoes full nutritional conversion, which is digested and absorbed by the chicken's body, producing heat energy to maintain the physiological and biochemical metabolic needs of the chicken and its body temperature, and may even continue to lay a small number of eggs.

Collect various grain hulls and vines (such as sweet potato vines, bean pod skins, rice bran, empty grains, sorghum bran, and corn cobs). Use a grinder to crush these materials (the finer, the better), then prepare some additives like soybean meal, fishmeal, bone meal, trace elements, and various amino acids. The more varieties of coarse feed, the better.
