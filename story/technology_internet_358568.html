Practical Feng Shui Knowledge

March 12, 2010

As times progress and people's living standards continue to improve, more attention is being paid to living conditions and the environment. Therefore, choosing what kind of living environment, selecting what type of home, and how to construct, layout, and decorate a new house have become challenges for countless modern individuals seeking happiness and fulfillment in life.

When we view ancient Chinese feng shui from the perspective of modern science, you may surprisingly discover that this ancient practice, although cloaked with an aura of mysticism, encapsulates the essence of various disciplines over thousands of years. These include ancient Chinese philosophy, visual aesthetics, geophysics, hydrogeology, ecological architecture, meteorology-climatology, environmental landscape design, psychology, and human life information studies. It is a treasure of traditional Chinese architectural culture and holds extremely important reference value in modern architectural fields.

1. What is a "Qi Kou" (Energy Entrance)?

So-called "Qi Kou" refers to the entrance where energy enters, originally pointing to the opening or depression at the front of a village or residence, through which fresh energy flows in. Therefore, the main gate should face it directly to gather auspicious energy. Later, "Qi Kou" was extended to mean the main entrance of a residence, i.e., "the door for entry and exit is called Qi Kou."

Nowadays, with the densification, apartmentization, and increased size of windows and doors in residences, the orientation and main gate (Qi Kou) of modern homes are not as crucial as those of ancient dwellings. Hence, "Qi Kou" can be extended to include windows, doors, and openings or depressions in adjacent buildings where fresh air often comes from.

This "Qi Kou" is best located in the east, southeast, and south directions, allowing gentle breezes to flow in, aligning with the ancient aspiration of "auspicious energy coming from the east."

Now many people living in apartments in the south reside in Xun houses (facing northwest and sitting southeast), with public entrances in the north (Kan) position, and the main door of the residence either in the east (Zhen) position or west (Dui) position. According to ancient feng shui theory, the main and main doors are Qi Kou, but in reality, they are merely passageways. Large windows on the southeast side have become the primary Qi Kou. This situation is quite common.

Feng shui most abhors "dead energy," "sha energy," "scattered energy," and "leaking energy."

A wind tunnel will cause "scattered energy" and "leaking energy." Ancient Chinese feng shui believes that "energy dissipates when carried by the wind" and "attracts misfortune," thus requiring "concealing the wind and gathering energy," meaning avoiding the wind. Wind tunnels are precisely the source of "attracting misfortune." Why? Because high wind speeds and rapid changes in the energy field make it difficult for humans to adapt, disrupting the balance between the human body and the environment, causing discomfort, even leading to illness due to energy loss.

No wind will cause "dead energy" and "sha energy." The human body itself and daily household activities emit large amounts of exhaust gases. If ventilation is poor, it becomes difficult for indoor and outdoor air to exchange, causing waste gases to stagnate indoors and self-pollute.

In summary, feng shui follows the principle of harmony and beauty in the "Book of Changes," opposing two extremes—too much wind or no wind—and emphasizes concealing the wind and gathering energy to achieve vitality, meaning ensuring air circulation to maintain a certain level of freshness.

The most ideal residential environment has soft winds gently blowing, giving a refreshing sensation.

2. What is a "Shui Kou" (Water Mouth)?

So-called water mouth refers to the entrance and exit of water flow. On one hand, ancients recognized that water flow affects energy: "Yang energy moves with the wind, Yin energy moves with water." "Harmonize yin-yang energy to respect human habitation." On the other hand, they believed water represents wealth, so they particularly valued water mouths, seeing them as protectors and lifelines.

The hallmark of an appropriate water mouth is: the heavenly gate opens, the earthly gate closes. "Wherever water comes from is called the heavenly gate; if water comes without visible source, it is called the heavenly gate opening; wherever water goes is called the earthly gate; if water goes unseen, it is called the earthly gate closing. Water inherently represents wealth; the open gate brings wealth, the closed gate ensures inexhaustible wealth use." (Volume Seven of "Mountain Eye Diagram Explanation") The heavenly gate and earthly gate define the entire village area, symbolizing the gateway and soul of the village.

Having water in front of the door is called gathering water in front of the hall, traditionally considered a sign of good fortune, making water a symbol of blessings.

Modern architecture compensates for the lack of natural water by artificially digging ponds or building fountains. The Oriental Plaza in Beijing, built by Li Ka-shing along Chang'an Street, features a huge fountain pool used for praying for blessings, with ascending fountains symbolizing hopes for abundant wealth.

Later, the water mouth specifically referred to the water outlet, usually located at the lowest point of a village, requiring gentle water flow equivalent to "closing the earthly gate." The water mouth gathers the spirit within the area, managing the vital points of a region. With tight closures, the spirit multiplies, and the size of wealth depends on this.

Water is the source of life for humans and also seen by some as a source of wealth. Traditional feng shui believes that if the door is positioned in an auspicious place and there is water in front of the house, naturally wealth is generated, bringing prosperity to the owner; however, if the door is in an inauspicious place, then this water source could bring disaster to the owner.

3. What is "Bao Shui" (Hugging Water)?

So-called hugging edges refer to hugging water, indicating the inner bend of a river (now called the convex bank), suitable for building residences; so-called water reverse edge refers to the outer bend of a river (now called the concave bank), generally unsuitable for building residences.

In a village in Guangdong, backed by mountains to the north, a river flows gracefully from east to west through the middle, with a significant curve. The northern bank of the river is the right bank and the inner bend (so-called "inner position"), where families thrive; the southern bank, located on the left bank and the outer bend, sees a decreasing population, and eventually the residences become deserted and desolate.

The aforementioned convex bank suitable for building homes and concave bank unsuitable stems from natural environmental considerations under ancient low productivity conditions. The concave bank erodes due to water impact, with steep banks prone to collapse, and deeper riverbeds making life inconvenient; the convex bank accumulates sediment continuously, with fertile land and rich underground water sources beneficial for human life.

If the concave bank is reinforced, it can serve as a dock and be developed into a city, something the convex bank cannot match. For example, Shaoguan City is built on the concave banks of the Dian River and Wu River. Clearly, the concave and convex banks of rivers should be viewed dialectically.

Why did the ancients believe that the right bank of the river was suitable for building homes while the left bank was not? This is because rivers, influenced by the Earth's west-to-east rotational inertia, experience a force pushing to the right in the Northern Hemisphere. Energy "stops at water," compressed by the force to the right, concentrating and enriching nutrients in the atmosphere on the right bank of the river, hence auspicious; conversely, the energy on the left bank disperses, making it relatively inauspicious.

The right bank being auspicious and the left bank inauspicious is not absolute and should be viewed dialectically, as auspiciousness also depends on factors such as terrain, soil fertility, and transportation convenience. Therefore, a comprehensive assessment is necessary.

During the static periods between mountain winds and valley winds, pollutants are least likely to disperse, accumulating heavily in the valleys, causing air pollution.

4. How to Avoid Air Pollution?

Due to poor air circulation in valleys and basins, they are easily affected by air pollution. During the day, slopes warm up due to sunlight, resulting in higher temperatures than in the valleys, forming temperature inversion, with valley winds blowing upward. At night, slopes cool down quickly, with temperatures dropping lower than in the valleys, cold air descends, forming mountain winds blowing from the slopes toward the valleys.

Just like tides ebbing and flowing, during the intermission period between mountain winds and valley winds, pollutants are least likely to disperse, accumulating heavily in the valleys, causing air pollution. Therefore, it is not advisable to build homes in valleys, but rather on slopes, preferably sun-facing slopes.

Homes built in valleys or basins, surrounded by high ground, block incoming water and energy, preventing natural airflow, making it difficult to become a lively place.

Moreover, in such environments, people may feel confined, with suppressed emotions affecting their health.

5. Is it Suitable to Live Close to Roadsides?

If a home is too close to railways or roadsides, it suffers from dust pollution, noise pollution, and exhaust pollution from trains and cars. Additionally, continuous vehicle traffic destabilizes the environment, making it difficult for the body to adapt, leading to fatigue, lethargy, and even illness.

Homes too close to railways or roadsides are also prone to traffic accidents, making children and the elderly nervous and anxious.

Furthermore, railways and roads often expand, change routes, and pose constant threats of demolition.

6. Avoiding Road Intersections

Homes at road intersections, ends of roads, alley entrances, and alley ends have two main issues: First, dirty air from nearby areas tends to linger here, causing air pollution; Second, the environmental field is unstable, making it difficult for the human field to adapt, depleting internal energy and weakening physical health, possibly leading to illness.

If unavoidable, how to handle living at road intersections, ends of roads, alley entrances, and alley ends?

Currently, there are two methods: One is to enhance ventilation inside the home, preventing dirty air from lingering indoors; Two is to set up a screen or barrier in front of the door or window facing the alley or road to buffer direct impacts.

The road in front of the main gate of a home should not directly face the gate nor form a "T" shape.

7. Unsuitable to Live in Areas with Strong Electromagnetic Waves

Any place with active ground electricity is prone to combine with atmospheric electricity, causing lightning, and thus unsuitable for building homes. It's best to install lightning rods on rooftops as a precaution.

Homes should not be built under high-voltage power lines, and houses constructed under various power lines must maintain sufficient safety distances.

Natural lightning protection mainly occurs through raised objects on the Earth's surface. If a home is backed by large mountains or surrounded by mountains on three sides, it is most conducive to eliminating lightning.

According to the characteristics of lightning strikes, groundwater outlets, river beds, lakeshores, wetlands, damp buildings, tall trees, etc., are typically prone to lightning strikes. Therefore, residential sites should maintain a certain distance from these areas to ensure safety.

8. Mountain Ends and Cliffs Below Can Cause Illness

Homes facing the end of mountains or valley entrances are prone to illness.

The so-called mountain end refers to the slope below where the mountain ends and connects steeply to flat land. Valley entrances are narrow valleys between two mountains entering flat land gradually. Broad plains usually have rivers flowing through them. Living in such terrains poses potential dangers from floods and landslides.

Geographically, this terrain is called a fan-shaped terrain. Rapidly descending narrow valley streams suddenly slow down upon entering flat land, depositing sand and soil at the valley entrance. Especially during flood seasons, sand and soil caused by landslides flow down with floods, accumulating at the stream exits, forming new land in a fan shape.

Such land, backed by mountains and facing vast plains, offers excellent views and convenient transportation, possessing many favorable conditions as a residential environment.

However, looking back at its rear, the floods and landslides that created this fan-shaped land could return at any time, so its danger can never be eliminated. People living in such areas become anxious during heavy rains, and prolonged exposure could lead to illness.

9. Wind Blows at Road Ends

Road ends can be divided into two types: T-intersections and cul-de-sacs.

In the case of a T-intersection, there are two inauspicious factors. One is that such places are easily attacked during enemy invasions. In terms of defense, these places are often used as strongholds to stop enemies.

Another inauspicious factor is susceptibility to wind. During storms, strong winds blow directly into homes at the end of streets. Similarly, during fires, flames often follow the wind to the end of streets.

Due to these inauspicious factors, homes at the end of roads are considered ominous. In modern times, though enemy invasions do not occur, car accidents frequently happen. Therefore, "building a home at the end of a road is highly inauspicious."

If it is unavoidable to build in such places, remember to leave a passage to the back houses to prevent entrapment during fires.

10. Avoid Big Trees in Front of Doors

In Yang residence geomancy, having big trees in front of doors blocks yang energy, increasing yin energy. Indeed, having big trees in front of doors makes access inconvenient and increases the risk of lightning strikes, polluting the living space with fallen leaves.

From a modern perspective, having large trees in front of doors causes great inconvenience because modern transportation primarily involves vehicles. People walking can sidestep through gaps between trees, but cars cannot conveniently navigate through tree-lined paths. Thus, homes with big trees in front are less convenient now than before.

Additionally, big trees in front of doors obstruct people's vision, causing feelings of frustration and depression, affecting the homeowner's health.

The main gate is the "vital energy" entrance, similar to a person's throat. If obstacles are placed at the "qi kou," the life-sustaining "qi" will be blocked, making it difficult to obtain vital energy, and people cannot remain healthy and long-lived.

According to Yang residence geomancy, homes are generally not favored to have "wood." However, this taboo does not apply to bamboo. Even in modern times, some people believe planting non-tall, graceful bamboo around or in front of homes beautifies the environment.

11. Avoid Wetlands and Mold

The ideal soil type for foundations is sandy loam for two reasons:

First, sandy loam is dense and sturdy, preventing the risk of home collapse.

Second, sandy loam is relatively dry with strong water infiltration properties, favoring aerobic bacteria growth and ensuring soil self-purification.

Clay wetlands, with overly compact structures and poor water infiltration, hinder soil self-purification. Homes in such areas are prone to dampness, water accumulation, and mosquito and mold breeding.

12. Avoid High Water Tables

The groundwater table should ideally be low, at least 0.5 meters below the foundation, to prevent damp, cold homes and avoid groundwater contamination.

If the groundwater table is too high, homes may become damp and cold, risking collapse and frequent groundwater contamination.

Living in such homes long-term can easily trigger various rheumatic diseases and is a major taboo for homes.

If building in areas with high water tables is unavoidable, pay special attention to backfilling and raising the foundation to prevent dampness and moisture.

When building on problematic soil, use modern materials like cement, steel, bricks, and ceramics as much as possible.

13. Avoid Soil-Related Diseases

Soil affects human health through water in the soil or through food chains involving plants and animals. If the soil contains too little or too much of certain elements, it can cause disease.
