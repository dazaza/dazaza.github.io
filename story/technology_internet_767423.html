Lei Jun and Chen Nian are true lovers, and Vancl is about to undergo a "Xiaomi-style" transformation.

by anonymous on 2013-10-15 21:19:05

Fanke always manages to create a stir every year. Last year, during the 5th anniversary celebration, Fanke used "pulling the plug" on its servers as a publicity stunt; this year, as the 6th anniversary approaches, threats of supplier suicides have become the new buzz. Another new angle is Lei Jun, who is both mentor and friend to Fanke's founder, Chen Nian, since their time at Joyo.

This once-celebrated internet-native brand company has, in recent years, seemed perpetually on the brink.

It hasn't been doing well. Before the end of 2011, it was an industry star, with an IPO seemingly just around the corner. One wonders if Fanke and its shareholders regret not going public then when the valuation was close to $4 billion. Despite that time not being an ideal window for an IPO and despite the main investor of the self-operated B2C giant privately sending mass emails to fund managers in North America intending to block Fanke, had Fanke gone ahead, things might be much better now.

Back then, including the Alibaba ecosystem, Fanke ranked fourth in the entire industry. Now, it can barely make it into the top ten, and by next season, it probably won't even be in the top ten.

Timing, destiny.

Actually, Fanke isn't as bad off as the outside world makes it out to be. Its investors know that Fanke has already passed through its toughest times - that was last year when there was an unprecedented massive sale to clear inventory, with CEO Chen Nian himself saying it was a matter of survival.

The surviving Fanke became a "Chinese Inventory Clearance Expert" for most of 2013. After successfully clearing inventory and finding its rhythm last year, Fanke turned into a platform for other similarly struggling clothing brands to offload their excess stock.

However, is this really Fanke's future? We've often asked: what is Fanke's true identity? Is it a product brand or a channel brand?

As a clearance platform, it ranks tenth in the industry and third in certain categories, but the value isn't significant, at least that's what investors would think.

Signs suggest Fanke has wavered. A couple of months ago, one of Fanke's suppliers told ZenNew that Fanke had planned to completely abandon its own brand, only placing orders for this year's autumn and winter collections...

But everything quickly reversed.

Fanke has stopped wavering. It will continue to focus on product branding, and this time it's all-in.

An important investor of Fanke recently asked ZenNew, what do you think Fanke's biggest problem is? We answered: strategy fluctuation, unclear image; he then asked: how about Chen Nian? ZenNew answered: his passion for Fanke is more than enough, almost excessive, but his attention to products has yet to be proven.

We asked in return, how long will Fanke continue to waver? The investor very confidently replied: it won't waver anymore, it's back to products, back to the brand.

He said this because in the series of board meetings starting from June, Fanke's fate and direction were repeatedly reviewed.

In May, Fanke was still trying to introduce third-party brands, considering adopting the "Brand VIP.com" model. But the results weren't as expected, that mixed model showed no hope, and the board intervened, requiring the management team led by Chen Nian to find a new direction.

Fanke's new direction originated from a drinking session.

Mid-June, Chen Nian and Lei Jun drank together. Halfway through the session, Lei Jun suddenly asked, Chen Nian, are we still brothers? If we're brothers, let's talk straight: Fanke's products aren't good, and neither is Fanke's attitude towards products.

Chen Nian's first reaction was indignation: after six years in the clothing business, having made so many products, how could they say he and Fanke's team weren't up to par? Isn't Lei Jun's success with Xiaomi due to good luck, riding the wave of the smartphone boom and mobile internet?

This dialogue ended with Chen Nian drunk. He later said that his higher tolerance for alcohol didn't prevent him from collapsing first that night due to the shock, feeling both ashamed and angry. This was the first time he and Lei Jun discussed such a sharp and sensitive topic. Even during their time at Joyo, he had never encountered such intense questioning from Lei Jun.

Around the same time, Fanke began its G轮融资 (G round of financing). The board and investors kept challenging and questioning Fanke and Chen Nian.

Fanke's investors discovered that the company's costs were too high, fixed costs and variable costs added up to basically equal sales revenue. If this continued, Fanke would be a company without a future. Shareholders could only invest in new opportunities, not just to save it.

The board wanted to see substantial changes from Chen Nian. Lei Jun's conversation made Chen Nian think about Xiaomi's experience, and he invited Lei Jun to help diagnose the situation.

From mid-June to August, even during the busiest phase of launching Redmi and preparing for Xiaomi's annual conference, Lei Jun and Chen Nian had intensive personal exchanges totaling over 60 hours. The topics were very sharp and unreserved: Fanke makes factory goods, Fanke lacks focus, Fanke doesn't have a good attitude towards products.

Lei Jun introduced Chen Nian to Xiaomi's "seven-character mantra" - "focus, extreme, reputation, fast." Chen Nian started to question: Xiaomi makes phones, which can advance single models, but Fanke makes clothes, an industry that traditionally has a large number of SKUs per category. Can these two be compared?

Lei Jun countered: why did Apple succeed with just one phone model? Could the boss of Nokia name all the models Nokia released over the years?

When introducing Fanke's products, Chen Nian said they were made by factories that also produce for Uniqlo and GAP. Lei Jun responded, this in itself doesn't mean much. The key is how much control Fanke has over materials, processes, and design. For Apple and Xiaomi, contract manufacturers only provide capacity, while those aspects are strictly controlled in-house. In Fanke's case previously, even pattern making and materials relied on contract manufacturers.

After these 60 hours of conversation with Lei Jun, Chen Nian admitted he was convinced both intellectually and emotionally. He invited Lei Jun to share his thoughts with Fanke's executives.

At Fanke, Lei Jun focused on two categories. The first was down jackets. As a skiing enthusiast, Lei Jun asked if the Velcro on the sleeves would still adhere tightly at minus 20 degrees Celsius. But the category manager's response left him shaking his head: "I don't know. This product is sold to urban residents who don't travel, so that's not an issue."

Lei Jun then asked about Fanke's lightweight down jacket from last year, comparing it to Uniqlo's. What advantages does it have? The response was, it weighs slightly more, using more material. Lei Jun conveyed the doubt raised by Xiaomi colleagues: is heavier weight an advantage for a lightweight down jacket?

Chen Nian said these two questions should serve as profound lessons for the company.

Before Lei Jun's visit, Fanke had prepared a polished PPT, but he didn't look at it, instead starting with direct questions. After discussing the products, Chen Nian said he felt Fanke had made big mistakes, "mistakes that were ridiculously off-base!"

Later, during an internal management meeting at Fanke, Chen Nian told his colleagues that Fanke had been lost for too long. After focusing on shirts in 2007, he hadn't seriously followed up on specific products, and he hadn't even used all of them himself, "which is truly embarrassing." None of the "seven-character mantra" had been achieved, and he was too accustomed to making excuses for his laziness. "Self-defense and敷衍users" was Fanke's biggest and most fundamental trap.

This was Chen Nian's latest reflection.

In fact, since the end of 2011, Chen Nian has been reflecting. This reflection surprisingly set a record in the industry: it has lasted nearly 24 months. Only, before the end of 2012, the reflection was about the greed of expanding too quickly, and now the reflection on products is a deeper continuation.

Lei Jun summarized ten points of Xiaomi's experience for Chen Nian. When we obtained this brief document, we found it to be the most concise detailed description of the "Xiaomi model" to date:

One. Big market. A product needs to have a sufficiently large market.

Two. Single item. Focus on a single item rather than a category.

Three. High ground. Attitude must be excellent; even if you can't be like Apple, your attitude should be better than Apple's.

Four. Experts. You must have expert advisors, but don't burden them with tasks; just get ideas from them.

Five. Genuine materials. Pay particular attention to product details.

Six. Cost price. Selling price equals cost price. Start by setting the industry cost price, then move to destroy the industry cost price. Don't deceive yourself with gross margins; relying on current gross profits to make money won't lead to excellence.

Seven. Users are friends.

Eight. Design thinking should involve user participation from the start. Only by treating users as friends will they actively market for you.

Nine. You have used the products you make.

Ten. Surprise. Exceed user expectations.

Lei Jun also gave Chen Nian three suggestions: eliminate management layers; Chen Nian shouldn't be called president anymore, just founder, and there won't be vice presidents or assistant presidents within the company; eliminate KPIs, allowing each division to focus on making good products; eliminate greed, there's no need to make too many products.

Fanke's final determined direction is to fully restart research on products, build reputation, streamline product lines, abandon categories that perform poorly or whose performance is uncertain, and concentrate efforts on limited core categories.

Therefore, many underperforming categories like children's wear were abandoned, leaving only a limited number of seven or eight categories such as T-shirts, shirts, pants, canvas shoes, ultra-light down jackets, POLO shirts, etc. At its peak, Fanke had as many as 19 major categories. Accompanying this process, layoffs occurred. Currently, Fanke has over 1,000 employees (excluding Fengda logistics and customer service), whereas at its peak in 2011, it had over 10,000 people. During this period, Fanke moved from South Second Ring Road to Yizhuang in the Beijing suburbs, significantly reducing rental costs from 6 yuan per square meter to 1 yuan. In terms of management, each product division will discard KPIs and focus entirely on creating excellent individual products to open up the market.

Recently, Chen Nian has stayed indoors at the company, continuously reviewing products with various project teams. Voices inside Fanke say this time has become unprecedentedly strict. Simultaneously, Fanke explicitly proposed that the design team should establish a unified brand tone plan; technicians and fabric experts should unify the specifications and process standards of fabrics and accessories for clothing; and establish a pattern room, recruiting and building its own pattern master team, no longer relying on contract manufacturers, taking control of product design, materials, and processes to ensure sufficient product quality.

After accepting the "Xiaomi transformation" mindset, Chen Nian's priority was to create products that could ignite the market. He particularly explained in the company that it wasn't about best-sellers in the Taobao ecosystem, but about achieving extreme versions within basic items, aiming for "a launch that sweeps the market."

This is the path to rebuilding the brand and redoing reputation. Correcting chaos and returning to初心has precedent. For example, Muji, due to excessive pursuit of sales performance, neglecting design development concepts, rapid store expansion, and encountering low-cost competitors, suffered a loss of 3.9 billion yen in 2001. The company was near bankruptcy, and the third president, Matsui Zhong San, underwent a three-year reform to get back on track. His core reform that led to renewed brilliance was returning to product quality and improving design.

Interestingly, the then CFO of Fanke also suggested burning off large amounts of dead inventory, allowing the company to start anew. Though late, better late than never.

One of the senior executives at CITIC, a shareholder of Fanke, has bought stocks of almost all domestic listed apparel companies. Chen Nian asked him, among the companies you've invested in, is there any where you'd especially want to buy things? After some thought, the executive replied that he didn't know where he'd go.

Chen Nian thus expressed his feelings: the factory goods mindset is a problem for all Chinese apparel brands. Over the past five years, the entire industry grew rapidly, leading to today's greed and difficulties. Fanke distributed a large number of domestic brands' clearance products priced at 19 yuan, indicating that the inaction of the previous generation of brands has been rejected by the new generation of consumers.

Chen Nian said in an internal meeting that the main responsibility for Fanke's problems lies with the so-called leaders of the company, mainly himself. Starting now, time is running out, and the whole company needs to transform into a "focused, extreme, reputable, fast" Fanke.

It sounds interesting, the dream is beautiful.

ZenNew has a strong interest in observing this closely, as it concerns the way forward for China's first large-scale internet-native brand, and also serves as a key experiment on whether the "Xiaomi model" can be transplanted, not to mention it's also a renewal of the connection between Lei Jun and Chen Nian.

This time, Fanke's adjustment shows a determination to risk everything, breaking away from all constraints, marching forward regardless of the millions opposing.

Wishing Fanke well.
