The Black Death (1347-1351)

by henguai on 2009-11-23 21:41:14

The Black Death (1347-1351) is one of the most fatal plagues in human history. It is generally believed to be caused by a bacterium called Yersinia pestis, but recently some people have suggested that it may have been caused by other diseases. The origin of the plague has sparked extensive controversy among experts. Some historians believe that the Black Death originated in China or Central Asia in the 1320s or 1330s and was carried to Crimea in southern Russia by merchants and soldiers over the following years. In the 1340s, the epidemic spread from Crimea to Western Europe and North Africa. The Black Death resulted in up to 75 million deaths worldwide, including 25 to 50 million deaths in Europe alone.

One symptom of the Black Death was the appearance of many black spots on the patient's skin, which is why this particular plague was called "Black Death." For those who contracted the disease, a painful death was almost inevitable, with no possibility of cure.

The bacteria causing the plague were carried by fleas hidden in the fur of black rats, which were abundant in the 14th century. Once the disease broke out, it spread rapidly. Between 1348 and 1350, 25 million Europeans died from the Black Death. However, this outbreak did not end there. Over the next 40 years, it occurred repeatedly.

In the 1320s, before the plague bacteria erupted again, they had lain dormant for hundreds of years in the Gobi Desert of Asia. Then they quickly spread through flea-infested rats along trade routes from China to Central Asia and Turkey, and then were brought to Italy by ships, entering Europe. The dense population of Europe became a powder keg for the disease. Over three years, the Black Death ravaged the European continent and then spread to Russia, resulting in the death of nearly one-third to half of Russia's population.
