Neglected and Awkward: What's Next for LED Display Advertising?

Over the past decade, with the continuous development of LED technology, more and more affordable high-quality large LED screens have been installed in densely populated indoor and outdoor locations. Meanwhile, the increasingly mature technology of LED large screens has also attracted the attention of advertisers and clients. The reason is simple; the first association advertisers and clients have with LED large screens is undoubtedly: this large screen is good, like a big outdoor TV that can continuously play TV advertisements. It reaches a wide audience, exudes grandeur, and enhances brand image. As a result, in recent years, there has been a significant increase in the value, construction, and high-cost investment in LED large screen advertising media, with alcohol companies being particularly enthusiastic. In many first-tier cities and provincial capitals, as well as bustling areas around train stations and pedestrian streets across various regions, alcohol companies' advertisements frequently appear on LED large screens.

However, time has tempered the frenzy surrounding LED large screens. With the entire alcohol industry becoming more rational, alcohol companies' attitudes towards LED large screens have also changed.

The author had detailed discussions with advertising executives from several well-known domestic liquor companies regarding issues related to LED large screen advertising. Compared to the preference advertisers showed for LED large screens in previous years, those who had previously advertised on LED large screens conveyed a common sentiment — LED large screens have become like a chicken rib, tasteless to consume but regrettable to discard. Despite their seemingly strong presence, LED large screen advertisements do not live up to expectations and instead waste a significant portion of the advertising budget.

Yet, neither advertisers nor advertising agencies have delved deeply into this issue. On one hand, LED large screen advertisers are eager to secure business, while on the other, advertisers harbor uncertainty about this advertising model. This has led the originally superior LED large screen media to an awkward position.

Through research, the author found that markets across the country can be divided into four levels based on city size: Level 1 markets (Beijing, Shanghai); Level 2 markets (provincial capitals); Level 3 markets (prefecture-level cities); Level 4 markets (county-level cities). All these markets are filled with LED large screen advertisements of varying sizes.

In Level 1 markets, once highly sought-after LED large screens such as those at Beijing West Railway Station and Beijing Railway Station now sell less than 1/6 of their total capacity (calculated based on 30 customers airing 30-second ads), with execution prices only at 30% of the listed price.

In Level 2 markets, the number of LED large screens has become excessive. For example, in central areas of cities like Shenyang, Wuhan, and Chengdu, the number of LED large screens has exceeded 20. However, sales are less than 1/8 of the total capacity, and the quality of customers is difficult to ensure. These screens have even become clubs for regional small brands, undoubtedly devaluing themselves. The media class of LED large screens in Level 2 markets has significantly decreased.

In Level 3 markets, taking the central area of Jinhua, Zhejiang as an example, there are currently 12 LED large screens built or under construction. Sales are less than 1/8 of the total capacity, and most of the broadcasting customers are local supermarkets, cinemas, and hospitals, with few well-known brands investing.

In Level 4 markets, there are already quite a few LED large screens, with an average of 1-2 per county. The operating status of these LED large screens can barely cover basic operational costs, and many counties do not have fixed advertising agents for their LED large screen media.

The high operational cost of LED large screens makes it difficult for their prices to decrease, and their effects often fail to meet the high expectations of advertisers when paying premium prices. This undoubtedly leads to the increasingly awkward situation of LED large screens.

Currently, the increasingly awkward state of LED large screen media reminds the author of two emerging media types that have recently gained popularity among advertisers — airport brush screens and electronic roll-up banners.

Brush screens, electronic roll-up banners, and LED large screen media all originate from outdoor giant banner media formats. The fixation, miniaturization, and high-class transformation of giant banners form brush screens; the miniaturization and portability of giant banners form electronic roll-up banners; and the vividness and multimedia transformation of giant banners form LED large screens.

Among the family of advertising media consisting of giant banners, electronic roll-up banners, brush screens, and LED large screens, LED large screens are undoubtedly the most technologically advanced and vivid media terminals. In discussions with multiple liquor companies, the author discovered that advertisers would rather invest in a city's outdoor giant banner than in city LED large screen advertisements. Advertisers for liquor companies are the main force in media investments. They believe that although LED large screen advertisements look impressive, they are largely wasted.

Understanding LED Large Screens

What exactly are LED large screens? Perhaps it is precisely because advertisers initially defined them as large television screens that they went astray. Although the audiences for LED large screen advertisements and television screen advertisements may be similar, factors such as environment and mindset during ad viewing make LED large screen audiences fundamentally different from television advertisement audiences. When watching television, viewers are generally relaxed and absorb advertising information while leisurely watching programs. However, LED large screens broadcast the same advertisements in crowded environments where audiences are tense and anxious, making it unsuitable to air television advertisements. The initial understanding of LED large screens overlooked the environment and mindset in which audiences receive advertising information.

We must recognize that LED large screens are not televisions but outdoor media.

Who Is Watching Television Advertisements?

Do television advertisements aired on LED large screens actually work?

When moving through crowded outdoor areas or inside train stations, the environment is noisy and chaotic, and the audience's mindset is tense and anxious. Take a 15-second television advertisement as an example: the first 5 seconds are the introduction, the middle 5 seconds are the climax, and the last 5 seconds are the conclusion along with corporate and product information. These three 5-second segments, combined with sound effects and voiceovers at different stages, create an advertisement with full advertising effect. Otherwise, advertisers wouldn't spend large sums hiring professional planners, directors, and shooting teams to produce these advertisements. A perfect 15-second television advertisement can even make the audience feel like they've watched a blockbuster, but this is premised on the audience receiving the advertising information in a quiet environment and a relaxed mindset. In crowded, noisy, and tense outdoor environments, very few people will stop quietly to spend 15 seconds watching an advertisement. Even if they do stop, due to the loss of some sound elements, they cannot fully absorb the information from the advertisement.

Television advertisements are specifically tailored for audiences in front of television screens and cannot be universally applied to LED large screens.

Why Are LED Large Screens Wasted?

Assume an advertiser broadcasts a 15-second advertisement on a city center LED screen with a daily foot traffic of 500,000 people, airing 120 times a day. This totals 1,800 seconds, equivalent to just half an hour. Within this half-hour, approximately 30,000 people pass by the LED screen. The actual time spent transmitting product and brand information within the 15-second advertisement does not exceed 10 seconds, and the effective audience is roughly around 20,000 people. Of these 20,000 people, how many actually pay attention to the information broadcasted on the LED screen? Calculated this way, it undoubtedly represents a high advertising cost with a negligible number of effective audiences.

No wonder many liquor company advertisers reflect that instead of airing a 30-second LED screen advertisement 180 times a day, it would be better to spend less money and place a month-long product giant banner advertisement near the LED screen. Because giant banners remain unchanged 24 hours a day, transmitting information directly and simply, and can even serve as an effective advertising form that creates a landmark impression for the audience.

Therefore, first, LED large screen advertisements are wasted due to the non-closed nature of the audience, meaning the audience cannot be forced to accept advertising information as they would with in-car or building TV advertisements. Second, it is also because LED large screen advertisements change too quickly, transmitting too much information, causing the audience to merely know that there is an LED screen somewhere displaying various information, without any possibility of forming a memory.

Vividness, multimedia capabilities, and immediacy are the strengths of LED large screens, but aimlessly playing television advertisements is undoubtedly using its non-closed shortcoming to compete with television, inevitably leading to failure.

How Can LED Large Screens Leverage Their Strengths?

Given the current situation, how to leverage strengths is a topic worth studying in depth. To solve this problem, we need to consider what LED large screens should broadcast in crowded, noisy, and chaotic environments, where the audience is tense and anxious.

The trial of electronic roll-up banners as a new medium is a preliminary attempt to address this major challenge.

The advertising elements of electronic roll-up banners are simple. The entire screen is the size of a "roll-up banner," with 2/3 or even the entire picture remaining unchanged, changing frames like a slideshow. The remaining 1/3 can either play television advertisements according to the advertiser's preferences or display slideshows across the entire screen. This way, the audience can absorb advertising information at a glance, maintaining the quality and vividness of the medium, making it easy for consumers to remember.

The author believes that the advertising model of electronic roll-up banners provides valuable reference for LED large screens. Imagine redefining LED large screens not as large TVs but as vivid "electronic giant banners." The vividness and high quality of LEDs can be utilized, and since the transmitted information is simpler, it becomes easier for the audience to absorb advertising information silently in an instant, thereby avoiding the shortcomings of non-closure. Moreover, the production cost of electronic roll-up banner images is also relatively low. Referencing the electronic roll-up banner model is undoubtedly a good solution.

Secondly, we might look to the world. The LED large screens located in New York's Times Square and Tokyo's Ginza are invaluable. What they broadcast is certainly not television advertisements for specific products but specially planned, directed, and produced advertisements tailored for specific screens. Such advertisements usually do not rely on sound effects or voiceovers but use extremely simple, communicative images to transmit advertising information frame by frame in 15 or 30 seconds.

Additionally, LED technology stems from continuous improvements and developments of neon light technology. In Japan, this improvement trajectory is completely preserved in the creation and placement of LED media advertisements. The LED large screens in Ginza are even used as powerful neon lights — long-term provision to a fixed client, attracting attention through simple high-brightness blocks and line color changes, transmitting the simplest and most direct advertising information (such as product names, corporate logos) to the audience.

Therefore, alcohol companies can fully refer to practices in developed regions of the U.S. and Japan, specifically planning and producing advertisements for LED large screens with simple and easily communicable information, or even neon-lighting LED large screens, dedicating them to permanent brand and product advertisements. By leveraging strengths and mitigating weaknesses, LED large screens can become effective platforms for companies to showcase their brands and images.
