1. Drug treatment: Under the guidance of a doctor, some traditional Chinese medicine can be used. When taking these drugs, patients must follow the course of treatment and not stop or reduce medication on their own when they feel symptoms have improved. This not only affects the efficacy of the treatment but can also lead to a recurrence of the condition.

2. Supportive treatment: Patients can increase their intake of nutrients and engage in appropriate physical exercise to enhance the body's resistance and strengthen its ability to resist disease.

3. Physical therapy: In cases where drug treatment is not very effective, physical therapy can be used as an adjunct, such as laser, microwave, or iontophoresis treatments. Physical therapy promotes local blood circulation, improves the nutritional state of tissues, enhances the body's metabolic capacity, and helps with the absorption and regression of inflammation. It should be particularly noted that acute adnexitis must be treated actively and thoroughly to prevent it from becoming chronic.

Chronic adnexitis is difficult to cure completely. During treatment, antibiotics must be used reasonably. Misuse of antibiotics is a common misconception in the treatment of pelvic inflammatory disease. Clinically, broad-spectrum antibiotics or antibiotics selected based on sensitivity tests are mainly used, such as tetracycline, sulfadiazine, fleroxacin, metronidazole, penicillin, or other cephalosporins. The hardest part about using these antibiotics is determining when to stop them. Stopping too early often leads to a recurrence of the patient's condition, while long-term use of these drugs not only imposes a heavy economic burden on the patient but may also damage other organs.

Symptoms of chronic adnexitis:

1. Infertility: The fallopian tubes themselves are damaged by the disease, leading to adhesions and blockages that cause infertility, usually secondary infertility. Even if pregnancy is possible, there is a higher chance of ectopic pregnancy.

2. Abdominal pain: There are varying degrees of pain, often a hidden discomfort, with soreness, swelling, and a feeling of heaviness in the lower back, back, and sacral areas, which often worsen due to fatigue. Due to pelvic adhesions, there may be bladder or rectal fullness pain or pain during emptying, or other bladder and rectal stimulation symptoms, such as frequent urination or tenesmus.

3. Dysmenorrhea: Caused by pelvic congestion resulting in stasis dysmenorrhea, most often starting about one week before menstruation with abdominal pain, worsening as the period approaches, until the onset of menstruation.

4. Menstrual irregularities: The most common is excessive menstruation and heavy menstrual flow, possibly due to pelvic congestion and ovarian dysfunction. Chronic inflammation leading to uterine fibrosis, incomplete uterine involution, or abnormal uterine position due to adhesions can all cause excessive menstruation.
