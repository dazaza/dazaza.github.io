In-depth interpretation of new technology in iPhone 5S: paving the way for wearable computing devices

by anonymous on 2013-09-18 21:59:20

iPhone 5S Integrates Apple's Future Product Technology

Apple's new high-end iPhone, the iPhone 5S, provides a glimpse into a series of products that this world's most valuable company will introduce in the future.

Analysts in the tech industry who study Apple said that the new sensor chip equipped in the iPhone 5S lays the groundwork for future wearable computing devices; the fingerprint sensor offers more opportunities for smartphones to be used as shopping tools. Furthermore, the new processor adopted by the iPhone 5S may provide an opportunity for the company's future Mac computers to break free from their reliance on Intel.

Timm Bajarin, an analyst at the consulting research firm Creative Strategies, believes that "these are important steps taken by Apple." Since Apple showcased its two latest iPhones on September 10th, investors and analysts have been searching for clues about the company's future devices and services. This is because since Apple launched the iPad in 2010, it has not introduced any new product categories. Launching wearable devices similar to watches or new payment platforms clearly could add new revenue channels for Apple.

Apple launched two iPhone models last week. Among them, the high-end iPhone 5S with a mobile carrier contract is priced at $199 in the U.S. market; the starting price of the iPhone 5C is $99. The iPhone 5C began accepting pre-orders on September 13th but does not include the sensor chip, fingerprint recognition chip, or the 64-bit processor configured in the iPhone 5S.

Wearable Devices

Before launching the new iPhone, Apple was already developing wearable devices. Apple is currently seeking trademark rights for "iWatch" in countries such as Japan. Sources familiar with the company's plans previously revealed that Apple has formed a team of approximately 100 designers and technical personnel to develop a watch-like computing device. As of now, Apple spokesperson Tom Neumayr has not commented on this matter.

Apple's entry into new markets may disrupt the nascent mobile payment and wearable computing device markets. Nike, Fitbit, and Jawbone have previously launched wrist health tracking devices, while Square and PayPal, owned by eBay, have been developing payment networks for phones.

At the same time as Apple released the iPhone 5S, the company also showcased health and fitness applications developed for the M7 chip. Phil Schiller, Apple's senior vice president, also mentioned during last week's launch event that compared to other major chips in the iPhone 5S, the M7 chip, which tracks user movements via accelerometers, gyroscopes, and compasses, consumes less power.

Sarah Rotman Epps, an analyst at Forrester Research who has been studying wearable computing devices, said that Apple initially intended to use the M7 chip to improve the health application on the iPhone, and this chip may eventually be integrated into new devices like the iWatch. The chip can easily handle data generated by wearable devices. The analyst said, "Regardless of whether Apple releases the iWatch or another type of wearable device, the M7 chip will make such devices more powerful and enhance the user experience."

Secure Payments

Greg Sterling, an analyst at Opus Research, said that the fingerprint sensor on the iPhone 5S is an ideal security accessory for users making mobile payments. Apple's iTunes currently has over 435 million credit card users, making the iPhone their ideal new digital credit card. Sterling said, "There are many possibilities for Apple around mobile payments. Because they have so many credit card users, the company is poised to reap significant rewards from this."

Fingerprint recognition technology will first be used to purchase songs, movies in iTunes, or apps in the App Store. This technology is currently not open to external developers. Analyst Carl Howe of Yankee Group said that if Apple opens up this technology, app developers will create a series of business-focused applications. The analyst believes that business users concerned about security view fingerprint recognition as a way to protect their data, as employees need to input their fingerprints to access company documents. He said, "Because of fingerprint authentication, Apple might become the most popular smartphone provider for enterprise IT departments."

Mac Processor

Apple also launched the A7 processor last week. Analyst Bajarin believes that adopting a 64-bit processor in smartphones means Apple might merge mobile and computer operating systems. This is not good news for Intel, as Apple's Macs currently still use Intel processors.

Bajarin said, "Although currently only the iPhone is equipped with the A7 processor, this processor is expected to be adopted by Apple's iPads and even computers in the future. Moreover, the A7 processor may also be used in Apple's future TV products." As of now, Intel spokesperson Chuck Mulloy has not made any comments on this matter.

The iPhone event will be held on September 10th.
