The post-80s entrepreneurship experience: Zhang Ailing once said, "One should become famous as early as possible." However, up to today, success also seems to be something that is better achieved earlier rather than later. At the age of 25, what are you doing? Larry Page's answer would be just founding Google, while Charles Zhang's answer would be pursuing a doctorate degree at MIT. And if your answer is just starting to work or running around for your entrepreneurial dreams, then you might want to listen to a different answer given by Gurbaksh Chahal. This Indian-American youth born in 1982 had already achieved entrepreneurial success twice by the time he was 25, becoming a wealth label of the post-80s generation that media competed to report on. And this autobiography, "At 25, I Made $300 Million," is not only a record of his success but also a wealth story unrelated to age or race.

Gurbaksh's life has been legendary from the very beginning: as an Indian Tamil who immigrated to America, Gurbaksh's soul seemed closer to the rebellious and restless elements of American culture. In 1998, when the internet craze swept across the globe, Gurbaksh decisively founded his first company, ClickAgents, without any capital and made the same bold decision as Bill Gates — abandoning his studies to fully commit to entrepreneurship. With its unique concept and outstanding performance, ClickAgents earned Gurbaksh his first $40 million. At this point, discussing whether university education was necessary was meaningless; this millionaire only proved one thing — if the opportunity in front of you is excellent, then it is worth giving up everything for it.

Although he encountered some trouble after selling ClickAgents, Gurbaksh still favored the internet advertising industry. Three years later, he once again demonstrated his keen sense for the forefront of industry development and started his second venture — BlueLithium. This company, which conducted advertising business from a behavioral targeting perspective, grew rapidly at an astonishing speed and was purchased by Yahoo! in September 2007 for $300 million in cash. Gurbaksh officially joined the ranks of billionaires.

Like all successful entrepreneurs, Gurbaksh did not stop there but began his third venture in 2008. Will this company called gWallet allow Gurbaksh to reach another peak in his career? The story continues.

This autobiography is a declaration of the success of the post-80s generation and a challenge to the established success model by the people of the 1980s who are gradually becoming the main body of society. Their thoughts and behavior patterns are completely different from the traditional ones, and although there may be doubts about this, no one can deny the strength of this force. The post-80s do not blindly follow history, but they do not deny history either. Just like in this book, Gurbaksh continuously emphasizes the power of faith and persistence, encouraging every entrepreneur to face their dreams and make persistent efforts for them. At the same time, he analyzes each detail of his entrepreneurship with readers, trying to provide strong support to entrepreneurs in similar situations.

This is not only a manual for success but also an inspiring book about pursuing and achieving dreams, just like the 27 pieces of advice Gurbaksh proposed at the end — dreams and interests are the "key to success," and the right attitude and personal strengths will be a good start for entrepreneurship; learning to listen will help you make your own decisions, and courage to admit mistakes will allow you to move forward; refusing mediocrity and fearlessness will make you stand out among competitors; finally, only perseverance will let you see the day of ultimate success.

Perhaps Gurbaksh's achievements cannot compare with those of Bill Gates or Bezos, but for all young entrepreneurs with dreams, his experiences may offer more realistic inspiration.
