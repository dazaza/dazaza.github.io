Product Introduction: Martell Noblige is carefully selected from aged Cognac eaux-de-vie to create a pure, smooth and mature taste with a long-lasting aftertaste. The extraordinary streamline bottle design with faceted lines matches the charm of people who are born with noble qualities. Today, Martell Noblige contains purer and more concentrated Borderies eaux-de-vie, which gives Martell Cognac a unique style - light taste with scattered floral fragrance. It also includes the essence of Cognac from other Cognac production areas - Grande Champagne, Petite Champagne and Fins Bois.

Martell's initial freight was to Jersey and Guernsey islands, which were traditional trade routes to England. Then he expanded trade to Rotterdam, Hamburg Hanseatic port and White Bove. By 1721, he exported 53,000 gallons of Cognac. Seven years later, he bought land and buildings in Cognac, which remain the company's property today.

With passion and enthusiasm, Jean Martell systematically participated in local markets, contacting vineyard owners. He was often seen personally directing the filling of barrels in Tonnay-Charente. He also frequently visited Orleans and Saumur to meet clients and agents outside the Cognac region.

In 1726, Martell married the daughter of a Cognac merchant, but unfortunately they soon divorced. Later, he remarried the daughter of another prominent Charente family. He passed away in 1753, leaving behind a prosperous and famous company managed by his wife and two sons.

As the business developed, Martell made its first shipment to North America in 1784. Other milestones include the first export of bottled Cognac in 1797, the first shipment to Russia in 1803, and the first use of the Extra designation in 1819 when Theodore Martell wrote a letter from London to his brother ordering 15 barrels. The first VSOP shipment (also the first use of this designation) to London was in 1831. Martell began exporting to Australia in 1851, China in 1861, and Japan in 1868. In 1880, the Cognac vineyards were severely attacked by phylloxera, causing a major setback to his business as 80% of the grapes died. After many years of effort, the crisis was resolved by grafting European vines onto American rootstocks and replanting them. Exports resumed shortly thereafter.

The famous Martell designation Cordon Bleu (Blue Ribbon) was created in 1912. Martell Cognac was selected for the Armistice signing ceremony on November 11, 1918.

In 1975, the company went public and was acquired by the Canadian drinks giant Seagram in 1988. This was followed by new varieties: Napoleon Special Reserve (Special Reserve) in 1990, Gobelet Royal (Nightlight Cup) a year later, and L’Or (Gold Medal) in 1992. The latter was housed in a prominent 24K gold medal glass short bottle. In 1994, Noblige (Precious) was launched, followed by Creation (New Ideas) a year later, the latter being a variety to commemorate the company's 280th anniversary.

Currently, the family still controls the main operations of the company, with Patrick Martell (Patrick Martell), the eighth generation, serving as General Manager.

Wine Review: The wine has a delicate floral aroma, smooth and round taste, perfectly balanced, and distinctive.

Recommended Occasions: Warm family dinners, friends gatherings, business banquets, courtesy visits, etc.
