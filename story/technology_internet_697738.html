Yelp's High Stock Price Accused of Lacking Solid Foundation: Lacks Better Profit Model

by bingo123 on 2012-03-08 17:22:39

Introduction: Last Friday, the review website Yelp went public, sparking enthusiasm among investors. However, MarketWatch columnist John C. Dvorak pointed out that while the company may have some potential, to justify its current high stock price, it must find a better profit model.

The following is the full text of Dvorak's commentary:

Before we begin this article in earnest, I believe many readers are not very familiar with Yelp's website, so I obviously need to explain the main differences between it and other review sites that have existed up to now. After all, can it be cloned by competitors? And can it be improved by the company itself? These are questions that investors very much hope to clarify.

Yelp (YELP) has become one of the most useful review websites because you can read public reviews before consuming at a business - mostly restaurants - which helps you make a decision. Since these reviews come from various different people, their content inevitably has biases or inaccuracies, but if you can understand a reviewer's comments on other businesses, you can naturally gain a better understanding of the overall quality and reliability of their reviews. That's the key.

People can certainly continue to rely on numerous reviews from industry experts, newspaper columnists, and various loose forums, all of which are very reasonable ways to learn about a place, but none of them have reached Yelp's update speed. Here, a user can update their reviews of businesses daily. For example, once the chef at a restaurant resigns, you might learn about it on Yelp before it even hits the newspapers.

Most review websites are filled with negative comments from competitors and former employees, or advertisements placed by businesses themselves and their investors to attract customers. Before Yelp came along, this had already become the norm for review sites on the internet. The premise of Yelp is that reviewers will leave a large number, hundreds or even thousands, of reviews, allowing readers to understand what a particular reviewer has said about other places to determine whether the review they're reading is fair, and whether the reviewer's taste aligns with their own.

I remember reading years ago about someone giving a one-star review, the worst possible, to the upscale French Laundry restaurant in Napa Valley, California. Further investigation revealed that the reviewer was a vegan who was upset that French Laundry did not accommodate his dietary preferences. On Yelp, malicious reviewers, eccentric individuals, "plants," or vegans like the one mentioned earlier, can be easily identified.

If there's anything that bothers me, it's the excessive criticism of restaurants by vegans, but these are all easily detectable, so it's not difficult to take them into consideration.

On Friday, when Yelp finally went public, its offering price was considered too expensive by many observers, yet the stock still attracted investor attention. Apparently, buyers believe that at this price, Yelp still has great potential.

So where does the potential lie? Based on the way the company operates, it doesn't seem like there's much potential. There are few ads, and their business model involves having restaurants and stores pay to prioritize their reviews - in other words, pushing five-star reviews to the front.

The efficiency of this model is highly questionable. First of all, it could anger users and damage the site's credibility. Yelp also tries to use its homepage, such as listing their "Yelp Best" lists, but users often overlook these lists. Additionally, when browsing some pages, you'll see snippets of cheap banner ads.

In fact, Yelp doesn't have particularly impressive profits. But could hiring some outstanding salespeople unlock some potential? I think if they adopt Groupon Inc.'s (GRPN) model, providing group-buying opportunities or discounts for various restaurants, I wouldn't be surprised. It seems quite natural, after all, they already have a long list of merchants.

Actually, since they've thought of making money by manipulating the order of reviews, why not try helping businesses improve their products and services? This would be fully utilizing Yelp's strengths, or they could collaborate with OpenTable Inc. (OPEN) (whose reviews are truly terrible) or other potential companies.

Yelp could indeed become a cash machine, but at least for now, if they don't break out of their narrow thinking patterns, the idea of a cash machine remains a mirage. (Translated by Zi Jin)
