Apple Plummets Over 5%: The Unmet Expectation of a Budget Model Erases $35 Billion in Two Days

by anonymous on 2013-09-12 12:14:04

Apple's stock price closed down more than 5% on Wednesday, following a 2.28% drop the previous day, erasing over $35 billion in market value in just two days. This decline was primarily due to the company’s strategy of attracting more users with a "cheap" iPhone failing to impress analysts, prompting several investment firms to downgrade their stock ratings for the company.

On that day, Apple's stock fell by $26.81 in regular trading on the Nasdaq, closing at $467.83 per share, a decrease of 5.42%. Over the past 52 weeks, Apple's highest stock price was $705.07 and its lowest was $385.10. Compared to its historical high of $705.07 reached in September last year, this closing price represents a drop of 34%.

At a product launch event held on Wednesday, Apple unveiled two new models: the iPhone 5s and the iPhone 5c. However, analysts were concerned that the latter, being the lower-priced model, might still be too expensive for consumers, thus failing to attract buyers. In China, the standalone price of the iPhone 5c is RMB 4,488 (approximately $733). By comparison, official data published by the *China Daily* showed that the average monthly income in Beijing in 2011 was around $730.

Based on these concerns, several investment institutions including Bank of America Merrill Lynch, Credit Suisse Group, and UBS have all downgraded Apple's stock rating to "Neutral," expressing doubts about its pricing strategy.

Below are the views of major investment banks that have criticized Apple:

—UBS analyst Steve Milunovich downgraded Apple's stock rating from "Buy" to "Neutral," while also lowering his target price from $560 to $520.

"We are downgrading Apple from 'Buy' to 'Neutral' due to concerns that Apple's pricing strategy will impair its ability to compete in key growth regions of the smartphone market, especially in China. We were impressed by the specifications of the iPhone 5s, the addition of Japanese wireless carrier NTT DoCoMo, and yesterday's news regarding China Mobile. However, we were surprised that the standalone price difference between the iPhone 5c and the iPhone 5s is only $100.

We believe that even if Apple reaches a cooperation agreement with China Mobile in the near term, it will still face challenges competing with comparable Android smartphones (4-inch display, quad-core processor), as Android models are generally priced 40% to 50% lower than the iPhone 5c. According to a recent survey report published on the website of the *China Daily*, among 35,000 Chinese consumers surveyed, only 2.6% said they would consider purchasing the iPhone 5c at RMB 4,488.

Based on weak demand at the low end, we have reduced our iPhone sales volume forecast from 175 million units to 158 million units. Despite a 10% reduction in sales volume forecasts, our revenue and earnings-per-share forecasts for Apple remain unchanged, mainly due to a 10% increase in the average selling price of the iPhone and a slight rise in gross margin. We have slightly increased our earnings-per-share forecast for Apple's fiscal year 2014 from $44.65 to $45.46."

—Credit Suisse Group analyst Kulbinder Garcha downgraded Apple's stock rating from "Outperform" to "Neutral," maintaining his target price at $525.

"Overall, we are disappointed with Apple's decision to maintain its identity as a provider of premium-priced smartphones, which will continue to expose it to fierce competition and limit its total sales volume and future growth. We expect that over the next 12 months, Apple's iPhone lineup will still hold a significant position in the smartphone market above $400, not much different from the situation over the past few years. However, the growth rate of this segment has already peaked, with annual total sales reaching 300 million units, and market expansion is slow. In this low-growth segment, Apple continues to face strong competition from Samsung, which holds 50% of the market share outside of Apple.

Critically, due to ignoring the mid-tier smartphone market, Apple's user base will continue to migrate to the Android ecosystem. Based on our latest estimates, Apple's share of the smartphone market will decrease from 18.1% last year to 15.5% this year, and further to 13.1% next year."

—Susquehanna Financial Group analyst Chris Caso reiterated Apple's "Neutral" rating, maintaining his target price at $440.

"We expect that the addition of NTT DoCoMo and China Mobile will mean an increase in Apple's total sales volume, but it will not bring significant sales growth like introducing a low-cost smartphone for emerging markets would. Therefore, we maintain our view that this year's iPhone product cycle will not provide significant momentum for Apple. However, we expect Apple to gain significant momentum next year, as we anticipate that Apple will release an iPhone with a larger screen size, and the new product cycle will allow China Mobile to adopt a more aggressive pricing strategy for the iPhone 5c."

—Citi analyst Glen Yeung reiterated Apple's "Neutral" rating, maintaining his target price at $430.

"We expect that 60% of China Mobile's 3G phones in the first quarter of 2013 will be priced below $150. We had already believed that the expected average selling price of $450 would make it difficult for the iPhone 5c to gain traction in emerging markets. Our concerns have now intensified, leading us to expect that Apple's stock price will come under pressure after the recent upward trend. Although NTT DoCoMo has become the latest distributor of the iPhone, Apple did not mention any progress regarding cooperation with China Mobile.

Data from Multi-Level Market Research shows that Apple's market share in China declined in the second quarter (down 100 to 400 basis points depending on the data source), ranking fifth or seventh among manufacturers, contrasting sharply with its top position in the U.S. market. We believe the reason for Apple's declining share is the growth of China's domestic phone manufacturers (according to Canalys, Lenovo, Huawei, ZTE, and Xiaomi all had higher market shares in China than Apple in the second quarter), whose phones offer lower prices compared to the iPhone while having competitive specifications."

—Bank of America Merrill Lynch analyst Scott Craig downgraded Apple's stock rating to "Neutral."

"We are downgrading Apple's stock rating to 'Neutral' because: (1) The company lacks a 'low-end' iPhone, and its (iPhone 5c) pricing is too high, making it difficult to penetrate the smartphone market in emerging economies; (2) There has been no cooperation agreement with China Mobile yet."

However, some analysts remain optimistic about Apple's prospects. Cowen & Co analyst Timothy Arcuri reiterated Apple's "Outperform" rating, maintaining his target price at $550; Lazard Capital Markets analyst Ed Parker reiterated Apple's "Buy" rating, raising his target price from $500 to $570; Canaccord Genuity analyst Mike Walkley reiterated Apple's "Buy" rating, raising his target price from $530 to $550.

In addition, Evercore Partners analyst Rob Cihra reiterated Apple's "Overweight" rating, maintaining his target price at $600; Bernstein Research analyst Toni Sacconaghi also reiterated Apple's "Outperform" rating, maintaining his target price at $600.
