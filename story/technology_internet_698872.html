Dietary patterns associated with an increased risk of adenocarcinoma

by abckvuul on 2012-03-09 11:36:26

Prostate cancer is related to the level of living standards and dietary structure

　　Prostate cancer tends to occur in the elderly, and its incidence increases with age. The incidence in people with a family history of prostate cancer is significantly higher than in the general population. Epidemiological surveys have found that prostate cancer is also related to living standards and dietary structure; the more fat, protein, and cholesterol components in the diet, the greater the risk of prostate cancer.

　　Blindly supplementing androgens can increase the risk of cancer

　　There are many injectable or oral male hormone products on the market now (mainly used to enhance male virility). Experts indicate that blindly trusting advertisements and indiscriminately taking these male hormone products may increase the risk of prostate cancer.

　　Expert comments

　　People whose testicles do not develop or who do not have testicles (such as eunuchs) will not develop benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH), nor will they develop prostate cancer, indicating that prostate cancer is closely related to male testicles and androgens in the body.

　　[If you feel your androgen secretion is insufficient, it's best to seek medical advice at a hospital and appropriately supplement androgens. However, if you take or inject excessive amounts of androgen products on your own, it may increase the risk of cancer.] In addition, experts point out that if prostate cancer already exists within the body, but male androgen products are still consumed, it could worsen the condition.

　　Early symptoms of prostate cancer are easily confused

　　[Many patients ask me whether prostatitis or BPH can turn into prostate cancer?] In response to this, experts state that most cases of prostatitis are chronic non-bacterial prostatitis, which studies suggest may be closely related to androgens; whereas BPH is a common disease among elderly men, causing symptoms of urinary difficulty, and is a benign condition.

　　[So far, there is no evidence definitively showing that prostatitis or BPH can transform into prostate cancer. However, BPH can indeed coexist with prostate cancer in patients.]

　　Although there is no necessary causal relationship between the three conditions, patients should not be complacent. Many clinically confirmed late-stage prostate cancer patients previously thought they had BPH, thus missing the optimal treatment time. Experts explain that the symptoms of the two conditions are very similar and are often confused.

　　[Frequent urination, increased nighttime urination, difficulty urinating, and painful urination, etc., are often mistaken by many ordinary citizens as prostatitis or BPH. Therefore, patients are advised to seek medical attention early and undergo a prostate-specific antigen (PSA) test to rule out the possibility of cancer.] Experts indicate that compared to other malignant tumors, prostate cancer progresses more slowly. If detected early, the natural survival rate can reach over ten years.
