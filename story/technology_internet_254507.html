Four Living Details to Help Babies Stay Away from H1N1

by alexsgxu on 2009-11-01 08:54:13

Today, the focus of all major media and websites has once again shifted to H1N1 influenza A (hereinafter referred to as swine flu). According to the latest influenza monitoring results, among the influenza viruses, H1N1 virus accounts for nearly 80%. It is worth noting that in the cumulative report of 1502 clustered cases, 96.4% occurred in schools.

In April this year, I wrote an article about swine flu. At that time, I was in California, USA, where the development of the epidemic was not serious. Half a year has passed, from the rectification of the name of the disease to remove the stigma from pigs and rename it H1N1 influenza A, to the accelerated development of vaccines, people's efforts to face and combat this global epidemic have never stopped, but the scale of the epidemic continues to expand. Relevant personnel of the World Health Organization predicted that with the second winter, there would be 100,000 new patients every day until 80% of the world's population were infected before the epidemic would end.

Fortunately, the H1N1 vaccine has been successfully developed and put into use. News reports say that more than 85% of the population are protected after receiving the H1N1 vaccine. However, among the groups prohibited from receiving the vaccine, the first are children under the age of 3. This is because the H1N1 vaccine approved for domestic use has only conducted clinical trials on people over the age of 3, and there is still no data on the safety and efficacy of the H1N1 vaccine for children under the age of 3.

All these remind us that as the H1N1 influenza becomes more severe, our children, especially those under the age of 3, are a very vulnerable group. In addition, children aged 0-6 are still in the period of incomplete immune function and have weaker resistance. Moreover, the younger the child, the more difficult it is to clearly express the impact of pain through language.

At GreenTree Inn, I always emphasize the need to care for and love everyone around us, our guests, our colleagues, starting from the details, because details determine success or failure. From these concepts, we should start from the details around us, pay attention to our family and friends, especially infants and toddlers of younger ages, to prevent and stay away from H1N1.

The first detail is for parents to pay special attention to some symptoms in their children. If sudden coughing, fever, muscle pain, fatigue, diarrhea, vomiting, and red eyes occur, one should carefully observe the severity and judge whether to seek medical attention promptly. However, if any of the following symptoms appear: 1) rapid or difficult breathing; 2) bluish skin, refusing to drink enough fluids; 3) excessive sleepiness or lethargy; 4) irritability, refusing to be held; 5) improvement in flu-like symptoms followed by recurrence with fever and severe cough, accompanied by rash; 6) severe or persistent vomiting, then you must take your baby to the hospital immediately.

The second detail concerns daily diet. Experts suggest consuming more high-quality proteins, such as colostrum and soy products, which contain various immunoglobulins beneficial to the human immune system. Especially Immunoglobulin A in colostrum can enhance the anti-virus ability of the respiratory mucosa, while soy polysaccharides can boost immunity. For infants and young children between 6 months and 6 years old who are in the phase of incomplete immune function, taking colostrum appropriate for their respective age stages can enhance human immune function.

The third detail involves daily living habits. Children should be discouraged from playing in crowded places or areas with too many pets, and contact with objects should be minimized as much as possible. Physical contact, including handshakes, kisses, and shared meals, should also be avoided as much as possible. Help children maintain adequate sleep, exercise regularly, wash hands frequently, especially after coughing or sneezing. Pay attention to indoor ventilation and cultivate good personal hygiene habits in children.

The fourth detail is the cleanliness and disinfection of the home environment. Everyone knows about regular cleaning, but there may be some overlooked hygienic blind spots, which can easily become breeding grounds for bacteria.

For example: 1) Keyboards, telephones, mobile phones, mice - these small items we deal with every day are most likely to spread viruses. 2) Sofas, carpets; 3) Televisions, both inside and out accumulate dust. The TV screen attracts a lot of dust, and the backside inside the television also accumulates a lot of dust; 4) Drainage pipes, toilets, garbage bins; 5) Pots, pans, cutting boards; 6) Door handles and power switches. These small places should be wiped regularly with alcohol or disinfectants.

Details determine success or failure. In the efforts to combat the H1N1 pandemic, we must always care for our families and friends from the details and protect them from H1N1.
