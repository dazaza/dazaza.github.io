Cai Chongxin's article: Why does Alibaba introduce the partnership system

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 10:38:56

Introduction: Today, Joseph Tsai, co-founder and Executive Vice Chairman of Alibaba Group, published a signed article discussing the IPO of Alibaba Group and related issues concerning the "partnership governance structure", providing a detailed interpretation of the original intentions and core purposes behind the setting up of the "partnership structure". He also raised the following questions: "We did not expect the Hong Kong regulatory authorities to make changes for Alibaba alone, but we are convinced that Hong Kong should seriously explore an innovative regulatory environment suitable for future development trends. Today, as a Hong Konger, I would like to ask: Should Hong Kong's capital market regulation be left behind by the rapidly changing world, or should it make changes for its own future and innovate quickly?"

In his article, Tsai stated, "We proposed a partnership corporate governance mechanism that allows Alibaba partners - the core managers of the company’s business - to have greater strategic decision-making power, reducing the impact of short-term fluctuations in the capital market, thereby ensuring the long-term interests of customers, the company, and all shareholders."

He also emphasized, "We hope that the future of the company will far exceed the lifespan of any founder, and we are seeking a mechanism to ensure the company's continuous development for 102 years. We firmly believe that maintaining the company's innovation ability, culture, and mission is the key to success in this constantly changing and disruptive world. The partnership system is a creative way to ensure the core interests of shareholders while adhering to 'who we are'—this is something we cannot and will not change."

A few days ago, Zeng Ming, the Chief Strategy Officer of Alibaba Group, also made similar remarks. Zeng Ming believed that the partnership system is a better internal decision-making mechanism that can also ensure the long-term interests of shareholders. "Our goal is not to get an exception from the Hong Kong market for us, but we hope that the Hong Kong market can also introduce such a mechanism so that other companies in the future can also use a similar mechanism, or even other innovative methods to go public. As long as there are enough investors who recognize this concept and both parties reach a consensus, it will be a good and reasonable arrangement, ensuring the long-term interests of shareholders and employees."

On September 25, Charles Li, CEO of the Hong Kong Stock Exchange, published a lengthy article seemingly responding to Alibaba's IPO matter. Li acknowledged that Hong Kong's current listing system indeed needs discussion.

He said, "What is most needed here is to objectively look at things, not be swayed by negative emotions, not be affected by criticism, and not be influenced by the specific circumstances of individual companies or cases. Ultimately, we need to make decisions that are most suitable and beneficial for Hong Kong, rather than the safest and easiest ones. I just hope that everyone can engage in sincere, open, balanced, objective, and respectful discussions on this important issue involving public interest."

Hong Kong public opinion analysis suggests that the partnership system has a certain degree of rationality. After most companies go public, they often face pressure from outsiders pursuing short-term interests, disrupting the original development rhythm. In the context of capital expansion, they are bound to the fast lane. Especially for Internet companies, flexible management and quick response are needed. Typical examples include Facebook, which designed a dual-class share structure to ensure the founders' rights in the company while minimizing the influence of profit-seeking capital on the company's development decisions.

Moreover, the Alibaba platform has more than 7 million merchants, with e-commerce sales exceeding 1 trillion yuan in 2012, and it is expected to exceed 1.5 trillion yuan this year. Its social significance goes beyond being just a company, and the partnership system helps maintain its long-term business philosophy.

At the institutional design level, with the increasing adoption of multi-layered equity structures by high-tech companies and private equity groups in recent years, if the Hong Kong Stock Exchange does not have corresponding mechanisms, it will lose the opportunity to attract such companies to list in Hong Kong.

The discussion triggered by this matter is still ongoing. However, the general view in the market is that regardless of whether this proposal is ultimately realized, the Hong Kong Stock Exchange needs to review whether the current policy design can keep up with the needs of corporate governance instead of rigidly sticking to outdated rules.

In the past few years, due to the inability of Hong Kong Stock Exchange's institutional design to meet the demands of the times, several companies, including Jardine Matheson and Manchester United, turned to the US for listing after short-term consultations with Hong Kong. The ranking of the Hong Kong Stock Exchange's fundraising capabilities in the global rankings over the past three years has been quite embarrassing.

Below is the full text of Tsai's article:

Why Alibaba Launched the Partnership System

Previously, Alibaba Group had multiple discussions with Hong Kong regulatory authorities on how to align our management innovations with the effective governance of the capital market. As a company whose main business is in China, Hong Kong was naturally our preferred choice for listing.

We proposed a partnership corporate governance mechanism that allows Alibaba partners—the core managers of the company's business—to have significant strategic decision-making power, reducing the impact of short-term fluctuations in the capital market, thereby ensuring the long-term interests of customers, the company, and all shareholders.

There are rumors that Alibaba's proposal threatens the "one share, one vote" principle advocated by Hong Kong regulators. This is not true.

We have never proposed adopting a dual-class share structure (Dual Class). A typical dual-class structure allows those with higher voting rights to enjoy these rights in the voting of any company affairs. However, our plan fully protects the important rights of shareholders, including the unrestricted right to elect independent directors, voting rights in major transactions and related party transactions, etc.

Why do we insist on this partnership governance structure? Our primary goal is to ensure the cultural inheritance of the company. In the past 14 years, Alibaba's mission has been: "To make doing business easy globally," helping entrepreneurs and small businesses succeed. A clear mission, pursuit of long-term goals, and adherence to values truly define "Alibaba culture" and have enabled us to achieve today's results.

At the same time, we have observed that many excellent companies quickly decline after the departure of their founders, but some successful founders also commit fatal errors. The mechanism we eventually set up replaces the founders with partners. The logic is very simple: a group of like-minded partners is more likely than one or two founders to sustainably inherit and promote an excellent culture.

We believe that the partnership system can allow us to last for generations: partners are equal, they will abandon bureaucracy and hierarchy, and solve problems through cooperation. Partners are not only managers but also owners of the enterprise, with a strong sense of responsibility. The partnership system injects fresh blood each year by accepting new partners, continuously invigorating vitality. Through this mechanism, we believe that we can maintain continuous innovation and continuously enhance Alibaba's talent pool.

People who do not understand the true intent of our partnership may criticize our plan as merely a way for the founders of a company to retain control. In fact, we have completely different goals. For 14 years, we have never thought about controlling this company through the setting of equity structures, and certainly not now! We just want to establish and improve a cultural safeguard mechanism so that this company can develop healthily and lastingly. We hope that the future of the company will far exceed the lifespan of any founder, and we are seeking a mechanism to ensure the company's continuous development for 102 years.

We firmly believe that maintaining the company's innovation ability, culture, and mission is the key to success in this constantly changing and disruptive world. The partnership system is a creative way to ensure the core interests of shareholders while adhering to "who we are"—this is something we cannot and will not change.

As an e-commerce company, we deeply feel that the Internet will bring revolutionary changes to all industries, and the capital market is no exception. As a social enterprise, we strive to drive such innovation. We welcome discussions on what kind of governance model is more reasonable in the 21st century for emerging companies like Alibaba.

We did not expect Hong Kong regulators to make changes for Alibaba alone, but we are convinced that Hong Kong should seriously explore an innovative regulatory environment suitable for future development trends. Today, as a Hong Konger, I would like to ask: Should Hong Kong's capital market regulation be left behind by the rapidly changing world, or should it make changes for Hong Kong's capital market future and innovate quickly?

As the ancients said: "A thousand sails pass by the wrecked boat..."

Joseph Tsai, co-founder and Executive Vice Chairman of Alibaba Group.
