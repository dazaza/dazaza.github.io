China's excess

by 0deq589k6s on 2009-12-02 14:41:28

The Chinese luxury goods industry has maintained growth at a rate of over 20% in the past few years, and this was also the case during the economic crisis in 2008 and 2009. The British consulting firm OC&C predicts that the size of China's luxury goods market will increase to $12 billion next year, surpassing Japan to become the world's largest luxury goods consumer. But luxury goods, these items marked with the value of time being "rare, useless, expensive", have, in China, shifted from being exclusive to the power elite to becoming a consumption spree for the middle class. Not only are the original nobles and business tycoons using luxury goods; China's middle class also takes pride in wearing or owning a luxury logo. What path have luxury goods taken in China? Is the future of luxury goods in China moving from consumption to production, or from flaunting to understatement? Does owning luxury goods merely signify status, or can it be given more meaning? Luxurious China represents another side of China's present.

China: A Step Into the World of Luxury

Luxury goods are tools they use to try to prove their worth. These people like to buy, but pay more attention to discounts. They haven't become rich enough to not care about prices.

This magazine's reporter/Wang Jiamin (reporting from Beijing and Shanghai)

In the winter of 2009, at the Guomao subway station in Beijing. Amidst the throngs of people, a young girl carrying a Gucci bag hurriedly boarded the subway. Behind her was Gucci's newly hung large fashion advertisement.

In China, it is no longer rare to see young girls carrying Gucci or Louis Vuitton bags on the subway. The office workers weaving through the city's subways now have access to the same luxury handbags as the wealthy madames in the Palm Springs mansions.

This winter may be the moment when luxury goods have come closest to mainland consumers.

In October, KPMG's report "China's Luxury Consumers: Keeping Up With the Trends" showed that in the past two years, Chinese consumers' familiarity with luxury brands has increased, recognizing an average of more than 60 brands. In Beijing and Shanghai, consumers recognize 70.5 and 73.3 brands respectively.

Correspondingly, the total amount of luxury goods consumption in China has skyrocketed in recent years. In 2004, the total amount of luxury goods consumption in China (according to statistics from Ernst & Young) was only $2 billion; by November 2009, this number (according to statistics from the global strategy consulting company Bain) is expected to reach $9.6 billion.

PricewaterhouseCoopers is even more optimistic. They predict that by 2015, China will become the largest buyer of global luxury goods.

A major luxury goods consuming country is being born. Who creates these consumption figures, social celebrities, Dullara types, or big tycoons?

Hong Huang: Admiration for Craftsmanship and Details

Social celebrities seem to be the natural consumers of luxury goods.

Hong Huang, daughter of Zhang Hanzhi and granddaughter of scholar Zhang Shizhao, was sent to New York for study at the age of 12 by the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, graduating from Vassar College in New York State, USA, in 1984. Now, Hong Huang is the publisher of three fashion magazines, iLOOK World Metropolis, Youth Tribe, and Brand World Fun, and frequently attends various fashion banquets and social events as a social celebrity.

She is well-versed in classic luxury brands, Hermes silk scarves paired with suits, Dior saddle bags, Chanel lipsticks, and she can even introduce excellent designers, such as Tiffany's Spanish designer Elsa Peretti.

But she doesn't buy much herself. The set of clothes Hong Huang most wants to own is Chanel's classic suit. She also pays attention to the limited edition bags Louis Vuitton releases each year. Like all women, Hong Huang is fascinated by high-end jewelry. "But you know, it's too expensive. But even without buying, you can still appreciate it, enjoy part of its value."

"Many people think luxury goods are for others to see, but in fact, they are just for your own enjoyment," Hong Huang feels that the most important thing about a luxury item is understanding its craftsmanship and consciousness. When she eats with "such thin bone china bowls," the food tastes the same, the bowl has no special function, but "your posture will be different, your feeling will be different, including how your mouth bites the rice will be different." Luxury goods mean savoring every detail of life to the extreme, as Hong Huang would say, "it's pretentious."

In Hong Huang's view, the true way of luxury lies in the connotation of "taste." This kind of thing can be felt and touched, but is hard to express. For example, Hong Huang likes Fujian meat floss, exquisite cheongsams, and jasmine flowers, things her grandmother liked. "Taste must be related to life. My grandmother was very particular. Our family's meat floss was always made by a master craftsman in Fujian, and my grandmother's cheongsams didn't have many styles, but she knew very clearly which tailor made each one, and every stitch had to be precise."

The most special thing was her grandmother's favorite jasmine flowers. There was a Master Zhang in Beijing back then, and everyone knew Master Zhang's jasmine flowers were the most fragrant. Every early morning, Master Zhang would send a basket of jasmine flowers over, and her grandmother would pick one to pin on her cheongsam as her perfume.

"For me, growing up watching my grandmother's attentiveness, I thought life should be like this," Hong Huang said, "Of course, all this was before the Cultural Revolution." Revolutionary fashion was practicality, Hong Huang believes this is one of the reasons why many people today find it hard to understand the feeling of luxury, "During the Cultural Revolution, chairs that came out couldn't have curves or carvings. They absolutely could only be chairs for sitting."

Contact with modern luxury brands as we know them today began after Hong Huang graduated from university. At the end of the last century in America, Hong Huang spent $800 on her first classic Chanel quilted bag. "For me, it was an astronomical figure, but I wanted it so badly. As a teenager, I encountered the Cultural Revolution, and all that princess-like spirit inside me never got to express itself fully. Chanel gave me the feeling of being a young lady."

Hong Huang says that back then she didn't understand craftsmanship, knew nothing, and didn't know what luxury brands were. Her real understanding came after entering the fashion industry, "The simpler something is, the more sophisticated its craftsmanship and design."

She saw during some brand visitation activities the craftsmanship and refinement Europeans adhere to, such as the strict procedures for Birkin Bags, and the hundreds of parts in tourbillon watches.

In 2009, Hong Huang visited Cartier to observe the cutting and setting of diamonds. This rare opportunity led Hong Huang to take a small notebook to record every detail of the production process. What moved Hong Huang the most was the polisher. The polisher was responsible for polishing the jewel settings before embedding, and one old master had been doing this job for 23 years. "His job is to thread a cotton string through the hole where the jewel will be embedded, and polish places that won't be visible after embedding until they shine brilliantly."

"Since no one will see the part you polished later, don't you feel it's a waste of time?" Hong Huang asked the master. The old man looked at her and said, "Just because no one sees it doesn't mean it doesn't exist. God is there."

Hong Huang admires this old man who has been doing the same job for 23 years. Hong Huang advises the young people who consume luxury goods now, "Don't wait for someone to tell you what craftsmanship is, that's useless. You need to be cultured, need to experience it."

After luxury goods consumption became widespread in China in 2008, the movie "The Devil Wears Prada" also became a popular topic. Someone asked Hong Huang if she would become China's "fashion diva"? Hong Huang said, "To be a diva, you must have your own unique voice. People used to call me a cultural person, and I was happy about it, but when they called me a fashion figure, I frowned. Now I've opened up, I am a fashion figure, but to be a diva, I need more industry knowledge."

"Dullara" Class Dreams

Hong Huang bought her first Chanel bag in 1984. More than 20 years later, the consumers of luxury goods on the mainland are no longer from the celebrity class. At the beginning of 2009, Hong Huang wrote in the magazine "ILOOK", "The world is flat, our symbols are brands. You're a Hermes man, I'm a Chanel woman, and all of us are Louis Vuitton bags."

Hong Huang has friends from all walks of life, and these friends' attitudes towards luxury goods are not consistent. Hong Huang's mother, Zhang Hanzhi, and most of Hong Huang's friends, especially intellectuals, including Zhang Yonghe and Liu Suola, have specific preferences for certain luxury goods. Zhang Yonghe likes Yohji Yamamoto's clothing designs, Liu Suola likes Chanel cosmetics, and her mother, Zhang Hanzhi, likes Louis Vuitton shoes.

But beyond these intellectuals, Hong Huang has many urban middle-class friends. This group has gradually become the backbone force of luxury goods consumption in China.

A book called "Dullara's Promotion" vividly records this urban class. They are wrapped in something called survival rules, and in life, apart from workplace competition and fashion handbags, there are also living pressures and life confusions. They continuously strive and chase, just to secure a place in society.

Luxury goods are the tools they use to try to prove their worth. These people like to buy, but pay more attention to discounts. They aren't rich enough to not care about prices. Hong Huang told the reporter from China News Weekly, "I have a friend who can tell you that it's best to buy Rolex in Bangkok, it's cheaper than on the mainland or in Hong Kong. I don't know how she figured it out, but she's really good at it."

Amanda Ma is one such "savvy" individual. She values the design and price of a certain luxury brand, but her shopping channels aren't big shopping malls; instead, they are airport duty-free stores, asking others to purchase for her, or even online purchases, paying more attention to discount information from direct sales stores. "You need to know, the pleasure of shopping passes quickly, I must make sure it's worth the money," Amanda said.

As for why she needs it, Amanda thinks that working in a high-end office building makes avoiding cocktail parties as impossible as avoiding the nine-to-five routine. Shoes paired with evening gowns must be Manolo Blahnik or Ferragamo, "If you don't have a big brand, people in the office building will think you're 'too young'?"

Amanda feels like she's "being pushed forward by trends," and if she stops, she'll appear out of place in this bustling city. In Shanghai's metro, neatly dressed "Dullaras" carry Louis Vuitton, Chanel, or Gucci, while at Beijing's Wudaokou subway station, security checks provide plastic bags for ladies. A security officer told the reporter from China News Weekly, "Every day we encounter many such situations, they worry the conveyor belt at the security check will dirty their branded bags."

Amanda starts to wonder whether "Louis Vuitton" is classic or mainstream?

Hong Huang said, "This is a natural confusion, Dullaras and luxury goods have a natural contradiction."

The concept of luxury goods changes with the times. Luxury goods have a sacred pursuit of exquisite craftsmanship, this ideal started in Europe in the 18th century and gradually faded after World War II. "For a long time, people believed that craftsmanship was a part of human civilization. When the machine era arrived, manual labor depreciated, people thought machines did better than humans, stitches were tighter, and production was higher."

"Dullaras" emerged along with the industrial era and multinational corporations. They have no connection to craftsmanship, nor do they feel it. At this point, what they consume is innovation. Hong Huang believes, "It's not just about how good the craftsmanship is, it's important to look at the design, a new style. Classic luxury designs change with the times. Women's bags used to be very small, now they're getting bigger because women need to carry more things, like cosmetics, or even a laptop for the internet."

This is the reason why many luxury brands can continue to maintain their charm in new environments. "Although appreciation for craftsmanship is no longer promoted by people, they indeed enter the lives of the masses more closely." Tsinghua University School of Economics and Management professor Li Fei told the reporter from China News Weekly, "Most consumers of luxury goods are from the middle class, they don't necessarily need to understand craftsmanship, nor can they afford top-tier luxury goods, possibly just a wallet, a bag, or a silk scarf. These entry-level products satisfy ordinary people's dream of experiencing luxury."

The first luxury item Amanda owned was a bottle of No.5 perfume. Sometimes she even mocks herself slightly proudly, "Can't afford a mansion, can't marry into the elite. But I can sleep with No.5 perfume, just like Marilyn Monroe."

Symbols of Wealth

No matter how fond the middle class is of luxury goods, those who truly spend extravagantly on luxury goods are always the wealthy. According to Hurun's "2009 Wealthy Consumption Price Index" report, among China's top luxury goods consumer groups on the mainland, there are 825,000 people with assets over 10 million RMB, and 51,000 with assets over 100 million RMB. Another report, "2009 China Millionaire Brand Preference Report," shows that luxury goods such as luxury cars, watches, and jewelry mostly appear on the consumption list of the wealthy, and these consumptions are not significantly affected by the economic crisis.

"The neighbors around here either drive Mercedes-Benz or BMW! If you drive a Japanese car, you're embarrassed to even greet people!" This line from a movie reflects the lifestyle of mainland tycoons today. However, a newly released movie in 2009 attempts to tell the public, "Driving BMW or Mercedes means you're nouveau riche, you need to have some taste."

It's unclear from when exactly luxury goods seemed to be equated with nouveau riche. Hong Huang said, "At least there are some rich people, I don't know why they buy things the way they do, spending money leaves me speechless."

These rich people who leave Hong Huang "speechless" have their consumption behaviors hidden behind these numbers: At the 2005 Shanghai International Top Private Goods Exhibition, 7,000 tycoons created 2 billion RMB in sales, with some tycoons making immediate purchase decisions; at the 2008 International Top Private Goods Exhibition, more than 10,000 tycoons from the Beijing-Tianjin-Tangshan area, Northeast, and Shanxi provinces attended, with 60 international top brands participating, showcasing over 300 rare treasures, more than one-third of which were making their debut in China or even Asia. To maintain a high-end private atmosphere, the exhibition was not open to the public, only to invited VIPs. The transaction amount remains undisclosed to this day.

From these mysterious consumption behaviors, status and hierarchy can be clearly divided. Robert Frank, in his book "Class (Hierarchy)," says that in the West, members of European royal and noble bloodlines are the "visible upper class," while various entrepreneurs are the "upper-middle class." Generally, traditional luxury brands are primarily consumed by royalty and nobility, while the "propertied class" also "feels proud to own luxury brands."

In China, this broad "vanity" is amplified several times. The British Economist recently said, "The Japanese were once considered the most blind followers of consumption groups, and now the Chinese are showing a strong trend to replace them. They accept well-known brands that they do not fully understand and consume them according to their own understanding."

"I don't think this understanding means you have to be a nobleman, drinking wine for three years and still not tasting anything?" Hong Huang believes that initially, luxury consumption might be for status or vanity, but one's taste cannot stay at this level forever, "People are always in a learning process. When you appreciate it, you establish a direct relationship between life and this object."

Some luxury companies also hope to build such relationships. Gucci has prepared concept stores for Chinese consumers, and the company's CEO Patrizio di Marco believes, "An increasing number of high-end Chinese brand consumers are seeking fashion authority and the craftsmanship quality assurance represented by 'Made in Italy.'"

Vacheron Constantin has created a club for top guests within a mansion on Huaihai Road. They hold a limited number of VIP events annually, including wine parties and appreciation events, with small scales, only about 20 people each time.

Vacheron Constantin's store layout includes bookshelves with books about watch history and craftsmanship, as well as watchmaking tools brought from Switzerland. A repair room is specifically set up on the second floor of the club. Alexandre Kerguen is the first watchmaker to come from Switzerland to China, and he receives approximately five guests per day. Alexandre told the reporter from China News Weekly, "The guests coming to repair watches are all half-experts. Initially, they just buy something, wanting this or that, later they start talking to me about watches, talking about time."
