Jack Ma's Partnership System Defense: How Important is it for Alibaba?

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 17:16:43

　　Alibaba Equity Structure

　　How important is the partner system to Alibaba? It's even worth taking the risk of going public.

　　Last week, the game between Alibaba Group and the Hong Kong Stock Exchange over the partner system intensified. On the eve of the HKEX's routine meeting on the 26th, Li Xiaojia, CEO of the Hong Kong Stock Exchange, summarized the various views of the market on whether to exempt Alibaba from listing with its partner system using nine different identities, ultimately indicating that there might be a public consultation on multi-tier equity listing structures.

　　On September 26, Joe Tsai, co-founder of Alibaba, also published a signed diary entry titled "Why Alibaba Launched the Partner System," posing a counter-question to Hong Kong: Is Hong Kong's capital market regulation being left behind by the rapidly changing world, or should it make changes for the future of Hong Kong's capital market and innovate quickly?

　　The focus of the partner system lies in whether it threatens the "one share, one vote" principle advocated by Hong Kong regulatory authorities. Raymond Wang, a partner at Anli Law Firm in Beijing, told Southern Metropolis Daily that such a system is acceptable within private companies, but once a private company enters the public investor market, this system may be questioned. "This system deprives small and medium shareholders of their right to 'nominate' directors —— their agents."

　　Alibaba's IPO controversy has been accompanied by the battle between innovation and regulation all along. A Hong Kong investment banking professional, speaking to Southern Metropolis Daily, indicated that unless Alibaba is willing to compromise, the possibility of the Hong Kong Stock Exchange approving it is not great.

　　"Nomination" vs. "Election"

　　Just as when Alibaba B2B was listed in Hong Kong six years ago, whether Alibaba Group will list in Hong Kong again has become the focal topic of this small piece of land.

　　The market originally expected that the Hong Kong Stock Exchange would seek preliminary clarification of principles regarding the controversy over Alibaba's application for listing in Hong Kong during its regular meeting on September 26. However, in an email sent to Southern Metropolis Daily that evening, the Hong Kong Stock Exchange replied: "We do not comment on individual companies' listing matters."

　　That day, foreign media cited messages claiming that negotiations between Alibaba and the Hong Kong Stock Exchange had broken down, and they planned to list in the United States. After the news broke, the stock price of the Hong Kong Stock Exchange fell by nearly 1%.

　　Regarding the listing location, Alibaba stated that it would not comment.

　　So far, information obtained from multiple channels by Southern Metropolis Daily indicates that Alibaba still prefers to list in Hong Kong rather than go to the NYSE or NASDAQ.

　　The point of contention lies in a corporate governance mechanism called the "partner system" that Alibaba has implemented internally since 2010. Over the past three years, the group has produced 28 partners in three rounds concerning relevant partnership charters. Alibaba partners are required to have "worked at Alibaba for more than five years, possess excellent leadership skills, highly identify with the company culture, and actively contribute to the development of the company, willing to spare no effort to pass on the company culture and mission."

　　From the process of the partner system, Alibaba's senior management decides the list of partners; these 28 partners (and potentially more in the future) have the right to nominate more than half of the board of directors of the listed company. If the shareholders' meeting does not approve the list, the partners have the right to nominate new directors until they are approved by the shareholders' meeting.

　　From the perspective of the board's functions, the board makes strategic decisions for the company and ensures its long-term interests. The general interpretation is that if Alibaba's partner system is accepted by the Hong Kong Stock Exchange, then despite Alibaba's senior management and Jack Ma holding only about 10% of the equity, they can continue to control Alibaba with a minority stake, which contradicts the "one share, one vote" principle.

　　Raymond Wang, a partner at Anli Law Firm in Beijing, analyzed to Southern Metropolis Daily reporters that according to current media reports and open letters from Alibaba executives, the "partner system" that Alibaba hopes to adopt is not the same concept as the partnership in partnership enterprise laws in mainland China, Hong Kong, or the Cayman Islands. This is a special clause set in the articles of association for nominating director candidates.

　　"This system deprives small and medium shareholders of their right to 'nominate' directors —— their agents. Although C.C. Joseph Tsai mentioned issues like cultural inheritance, which are very reasonable, there are significant problems from the perspective of corporate governance. You cannot both gain the convenience of financing as a listed company and retain the benefits of private company autonomy, trying to have both fish and bear's paw."

　　It should be noted that these directors of Alibaba still need the approval of the shareholders' meeting. Therefore, in his open letter, C.C. Joseph Tsai mentioned that the plan fully protects the important rights of shareholders, including the unrestricted right to "elect" independent directors and voting rights on major transactions and related-party transactions.

　　Last week, Yahoo! and SoftBank, major shareholders of Alibaba who are not among the partners, issued public statements supporting the "partner system." Masayoshi Son, president of SoftBank Group, said in a statement, "These years, Alibaba has created tremendous value for shareholders, achieving amazing results. Alibaba's unique corporate culture is at the core of its success, and maintaining this culture is crucial for the company's continued development. Therefore, we strongly support Alibaba's partner system."

　　However, Raymond Wang believes this is not surprising. "If these two parties did not agree, this partner system would not have been established. Since this is set in the articles of incorporation, it must be approved by the company's shareholders."

　　But once a private company enters the public investor market, this system may be questioned. "Jack Ma's so-called partner system is a special management clause set up in the overseas holding company where Alibaba is registered in the Cayman Islands. Such a system can be accepted within private companies, as it pertains to agreements made by shareholders on the articles of incorporation." He believes that once it reaches the level of listing, it goes beyond just the company level and involves the protection of small and medium shareholders, such as conflicting with the existing regulatory framework in Hong Kong.

　　An Unstable Dual-Class Share Structure

　　So far, the Hong Kong Securities and Futures Commission does not seem inclined to give the green light to the partner system proposed by Alibaba.

　　In Hong Kong, currently only the Hong Kong Stock Exchange has the right to nominate directors, as confirmed by the Ordinance passed by the Hong Kong Legislative Council, the "Exchange and Clearing House (Consolidation) Ordinance." Among the 13 directors of the Hong Kong Stock Exchange, only 6 are elected.

　　Another option for Alibaba is to list in the U.S. with a dual-class share structure, commonly known as Class A and Class B shares. In a dual-class share structure, stocks are divided into Series A and Series B. External investors hold Class A shares, with one vote per share; while management holds Class B shares, with N votes per share (usually N=10), ensuring that management has voting rights exceeding their actual shareholding percentage. Currently, technology companies like Google and Facebook use this dual-class share structure for their listings.

　　However, Raymond Wang believes that given the current shareholding levels of Alibaba's executives, even with the A/B share structure, the converted voting rights may not be sufficient to secure control of the company. A reference point is Renren Inc., also listed in the U.S., where founder Charles Chen owns 22.8% of the shares, corresponding to 55.9% of the voting rights.

　　"These two systems are both structures where the minority controls the majority, with actual voting power exceeding shareholding percentages. But Alibaba's partner system seems unfamiliar to everyone, and I've consulted some American securities lawyers who haven't seen similar precedents either." Raymond Wang believes that any proposal by Alibaba, whether in Hong Kong or the U.S., requires adjustments within the existing regulatory framework to be accepted, which isn't easy.

　　In agreement with most market opinions, two Hong Kong investment bankers contacted by Southern Metropolis Daily believe that unless Alibaba is willing to compromise, the likelihood of the Hong Kong Stock Exchange approving it is slim because they are concerned about shareholder protection and compliance with regulations. David Webb, a well-known Hong Kong shareholder rights activist, also believes it's difficult to make an exception. "If regulators make exceptions for a new issue, future applicants will demand equal treatment, and already-listed companies will complain they could have done the same."

　　If the IPO in Hong Kong fails, Alibaba turning to the U.S. capital market will face more unknown issues and stricter regulatory requirements. Many analysts believe that for U.S. investors, the Alipay incident will be a topic Alibaba cannot avoid, as well as issues regarding the source of Taobao's products and the U.S. class action lawsuit system, all of which Alibaba needs to consider.

　　Can They Wait?

　　In fact, regardless of where they eventually succeed in listing, Alibaba's timetable for going public this year is already quite tight.

　　These days are decisive moments for Alibaba's potential listing in Hong Kong. According to the new sponsor rules of the Hong Kong Stock Exchange, effective October 1st, all companies applying for listing after this date must appoint sponsors two months before submitting their applications to ensure sufficient time for due diligence. In other words, if Alibaba fails to submit its listing application today, the earliest it could happen would be December.

　　On the other hand, as the negotiation between Alibaba and the Hong Kong Stock Exchange continues, the market valuation of Alibaba keeps rising. An unnamed Hong Kong stock analyst from Shen Yin Securities told Southern Metropolis Daily that initial valuations by investment banks ranged from $70 billion to $100 billion, but there are still many variables. "Given Alibaba's good profitability, the valuation could approach $100 billion." Bankers estimate that this IPO could exceed $15 billion in scale.

　　According to the equity repurchase agreement signed in May 2012, if Alibaba Group goes public before 2015, it has the right to repurchase 50% of the remaining shares held by Yahoo! (approximately 10% of Alibaba's shares) during the IPO, or allow Yahoo! to sell during the IPO. Otherwise, Yahoo! has the right to handle its shares independently.

　　Moreover, the substantial financing involved in Alibaba's previous round of repurchases, involving numerous investment institutions, dictates that it cannot delay its IPO timing for too long.

　　In terms of market capitalization, according to foreign media reports, in early 2011, Jack Ma proposed to Yahoo! to acquire 15% of the shares for $3.5 billion, valuing the entire group at approximately $23.5 billion. By 2012, when Yahoo! and Alibaba signed the repurchase agreement, the overall valuation reached $35 billion, and starting last year, Alibaba's valuation at investment banks has reached $70-100 billion.

　　"Time drags on longer, the higher the cost of repurchase for Alibaba will be. It needs to meet the conditions for repurchase (the IPO must raise at least $3 billion in cash before various fees, and the issue price per share must be more than 1.1 times the repurchase price), yet it cannot excessively release asset value. This is a test for Jack Ma," said an investment industry person to Southern Metropolis Daily.

　　A Forewarning of $2.5 Billion

　　From a management structure perspective, Jack Ma is not easily challenged. Since 2010, after multiple rounds of structural adjustments, a multi-layered structure has formed within Alibaba, consisting of the Strategic Management Executive Committee, the Strategic Decision-making Committee, and the Partner System. In the new system, the Strategic Decision-making Committee is responsible to the board of directors, while the Strategic Management Executive Committee is led by the CEO.

　　Zeng Ming, Chief Strategy Officer of Alibaba Group, stated at a media briefing last week that after trial operations for three years, the partner system is considered a good internal decision-making mechanism for the company, potentially growing to over a hundred members in the next few years.

　　At the management level, Jack Ma tightly controls Alibaba's future, but from the perspective of control rights, how is it?

　　Tracing back to August 11, 2005, during the traditional Chinese Valentine's Day, Alibaba and Yahoo! announced in Beijing that they had signed an agreement where Alibaba acquired all assets of Yahoo! China, receiving a $1 billion investment from Yahoo!. In return, Yahoo! received 40% economic interest and 35% voting rights in Alibaba.

　　At that time, Yahoo! was one of the top internet companies in the world, while Alibaba was a nascent entity in need of nurturing. Yahoo! China's assets included its portal site, YiSou search engine, IM service, 3721, and all assets on Yipaiwang.

　　Xie Wen, former General Manager of Yahoo! China, analyzing for Southern Metropolis Daily, said that Alibaba accepting Yahoo!'s investment mainly stemmed from three considerations: first, Yahoo!'s investment represented a "great spiritual victory" for Alibaba given its influence in the global internet industry at the time; second, Alibaba lacked technological advantages, and introducing Yahoo!'s advanced search technology could fundamentally enhance Alibaba's technical foundation; third, there was "pressure" from some founders, employees, and investors to find suitable exit methods.

　　According to insiders close to the transaction, out of the $1 billion, Alibaba received $250 million. SoftBank sold its Taobao shares for $360 million and then purchased Alibaba shares for $150 million, resulting in a net profit of $210 million. Meanwhile, Alibaba's management and other shareholders cashed out $540 million, part of which came from Yahoo!'s $390 million purchase of shares and SoftBank's $150 million purchase of shares.

　　Publicly available data shows that before this transaction, the entrepreneurial team represented by Jack Ma held 47% of the shares, SoftBank held 20%, Fidelity held approximately 18%, and other shareholders collectively held around 15%. After this transaction, Yahoo! became Alibaba's largest shareholder, holding 39% of the shares but only 35% of the voting rights.

　　Soon after this deal, Alibaba announced an additional $1 billion investment in its auction site Taobao and supported Taobao to remain free for another three years. Ultimately, in the C2C battlefield, Taobao defeated EachNet, establishing its early dominance in the development of China's e-commerce industry, which has continued to this day. Public opinion generally believes that the $250 million in development funds from Yahoo! played a crucial role during this period.

　　However, the cost paid for this was a long road to regain "control rights." Was it worth it? Only Jack Ma knows the answer.

　　Key Year 2010

　　Regarding evaluations of shareholder relations, Jack Ma has made many brilliant comments. For example, "Customers first, employees second, shareholders third." Or, "Capitalists are always uncles; you are the parents of this enterprise, and you must control the future of this enterprise."

　　In reality, throughout Alibaba's development over the past decade, doubts about Alibaba's low equity have never ceased. However, insiders close to Alibaba revealed to Southern Metropolis Daily that Jack Ma's first relatively "obvious" challenge to control rights actually appeared in 2010.

　　After the Yahoo! transaction, Alibaba's board consisted of four people: Jack Ma and Joe Tsai representing Alibaba, Jerry Yang representing Yahoo!, and Masayoshi Son representing SoftBank, with Jack Ma serving as Chairman of the Board.

　　According to their agreement, after the five-year expiration of the contract signed in 2005, starting from October 2010, Yahoo!'s voting rights would increase from 35% to 39%, while Jack Ma and management's voting rights would decrease from 35.7% to 31.7%, and SoftBank would maintain its 29.3% shareholding and voting rights unchanged.

　　Additionally, "The total number of directors Yahoo! can appoint shall be the higher number between the directors they can appoint at that date and the directors Alibaba Group management shareholders can appoint at that date." The agreement also stipulated that as long as Jack Ma holds one share, he has the right to appoint one director on the board.

　　This clause meant that Yahoo! had the right to increase the number of board seats from one to two, changing the structure of Alibaba's board from "2:1:1" to "2:2:1," where Yahoo! and SoftBank combined might form a majority on the board.

　　Interestingly, in this year, Jerry Yang, who got along well with Jack Ma, was marginalized within Yahoo!, while Carol Bartz, then CEO of Yahoo! and described as "the woman who embarrassed Jack Ma," took office. Just before the October deadline, Carol Bartz publicly stated that she "might" join Alibaba's board later that year.

　　This move provoked strong opposition from Alibaba. Wang Shuai, then Senior Vice President of Alibaba and Chief Marketing Officer of Taobao, clearly expressed, "Yahoo! should first focus on improving the current state of Yahoo! in America; perhaps that wouldn't be a bad choice."

　　Another event that occurred this year subtly hinted at another "hidden" challenge to control rights.

　　The Wall Street Journal reported at the time that AOL was discussing the possibility of acquiring Yahoo! with several private equity firms, and these private equity funds were "very interested" in transforming Yahoo! into their private company.

　　It's easy to imagine that if Yahoo! itself underwent changes, Alibaba might face greater uncertainties. "This is not favorable for Jack Ma. If Yahoo! views Alibaba from an asset perspective, PE firms would view Alibaba more from an investment angle, making Alibaba's future even less predictable," said someone close to Alibaba. Jack Ma and Alibaba's management team have never shied away from their desire to reclaim control rights, but this pivotal year of 2010 heightened their sense of "urgency."

　　Racing Against Time

　　Xie Wen believes that when viewed in a broader context, it becomes easier to understand why Alibaba began implementing the partner system, transferring Alipay's equity, and repurchasing Yahoo!'s equity starting in 2009. "All of these actions were ways to compensate for the excessive dilution of equity back then." He thinks, "No one wants to kick out the management team, but equity is like a sword of Damocles hanging over the heads of the management."

　　In 2010, the 18 founders of Alibaba resigned their "founder" status and began internally piloting the "partner" system, selecting new partners annually.

　　According to multiple sources, that year, Joe Tsai represented Alibaba in at least two rounds of talks with Yahoo! regarding the equity repurchase but failed to reach an agreement. Later, Jack Ma criticized Yahoo! openly at the shareholders' meeting of Alibaba B2B, stating, "Everything was going well, Yahoo! had agreed, but when it came to finalizing the specific plan, they terminated the negotiations."

　　By 2011, Jack Ma used the argument that the VIE structure did not meet the requirements for the central bank's payment license issuance, separating Alipay from Alibaba's system and transferring it to Zhejiang Alibaba E-Commerce Co., Ltd., owned by Jack Ma and Xie Shihuang. This further escalated tensions between Alibaba and Yahoo!. The matter was eventually settled with a one-time cash return to Alibaba upon Alipay's IPO (no less than $2 billion and no more than $6 billion).

　　However, soon after, Carol Bartz's departure made Yahoo! shift from a hardline stance to compromise, driving the situation in favor of Jack Ma.

　　In September 2011, Alibaba launched the Dawn Plan, selling approximately 5% of the shares held by management and employees to Yunfeng Fund, Russian venture capital firm DST, Silver Lake Partners, and other institutions at a valuation of $35 billion. These institutions voluntarily handed over all voting rights to Alibaba Group's management.

　　Finally, in May 2012, Alibaba Group reached a consensus with Yahoo! on the equity repurchase.

　　Based on a valuation of $35 billion, Alibaba Group would repurchase 20% of the group's equity held by Yahoo! for $6.3 billion in cash and up to $800 million in newly issued preferred shares of Alibaba Group. Institutions such as China Investment Corporation, Boyu Capital, CITIC Capital, and China Development Bank Financial Leasing Co., Ltd participated in the subscription. Information from sources close to the transaction revealed that most of them agreed to vote alongside the management team.
