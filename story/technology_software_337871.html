Data recovery, does Google really speak justice?

by lesile7hl0 on 2010-03-31 18:25:01

Google initially entered China because it was "profitable," and now it wants to "withdraw without retreating" by citing "human rights and freedom of speech" issues in China. What was originally a well-calculated plan for both fame and profit has now resulted in Google losing both in China. The recent extensive government lobbying and "media blitz" that Google has conducted in the U.S. is a sign of the company's desperate situation, akin to a schoolchild who lost a fight at school running home to complain to their parents (the U.S. government) and asking them to intervene. However, this crisis management approach by Google further highlights its lack of understanding of the Chinese market, Chinese culture, Chinese politics, and the way Chinese people think.

Moreover, laws in many other countries around the world also require Google to take measures to review search results. For example, France prohibits the publication of "neo-Nazi" information online; in Italy, child pornography is banned. If Google can comply with the laws of other countries, then Google’s operations in China should also comply with Chinese law, just like all companies operating in China.

In fact, Google has never implemented a "global unified standard."

If Google genuinely hopes for all people worldwide to live according to a global unified, American-standard lifestyle, including having absolute online freedom, it could start by implementing a global unified wage standard. The minimum wage standard in California, where Google's headquarters is located, is $8 per hour. In that case, according to the California standard, Google employees in China, working 160 hours per month, should have a monthly minimum wage limit of RMB 8704. And this is only the minimum wage limit, which is basically the wage standard for cleaning staff at Google's U.S. headquarters; the wages for technical personnel should be at least doubled. If Google hasn't implemented such a "unified standard" in other countries, can employees in those countries sue Google for intentional discrimination?

Firstly, the Chinese government's management system regarding internet companies or any media company operating in China existed before Google's "landing" and isn't specifically targeted at Google alone. Google entered the Chinese mainland market after explicitly promising to abide by China's management system. If Google is as "noble" as its own data recovery publicity claims, why did the company compromise with the Chinese government four years ago on such a "principle-based" issue? These contradictory actions themselves prove Google's hypocrisy, lack of principles, and lack of credibility.

Is Google righteous? That might just be what it says about itself. In reality, Google has been rapidly expanding over the years, infringing rights everywhere, and does not possess the moral strength it boasts. This so-called "exit" by Google is not a true exit from China; it has not abandoned its commercial interests in China. Currently, Google has invested in five enterprises in China that are engaged in technology research and development, software and hardware, and advertising businesses, all of which are still operational. Now, Google has only moved its news, image, and search services to Hong Kong, but Hong Kong is still part of China, ultimately indicating its desire to continue making money in China.

Even though Google has consistently proclaimed that it will "never do evil," in reality, it lacks a unified standard of good and evil, often adopting a double standard instead.

Secondly, differences in systems and cultures are almost always the first lesson for all multinational companies entering another country. For instance, McDonald's restaurants in India do not offer beef burgers out of respect for the Hindu prohibition against eating beef. If we were to follow Google's logic, even in India, the "Big Mac" should be replicated exactly as it is elsewhere to be considered "authentic." But if they really did that, not only would the product not sell, but it could also lead to religious conflicts locally. Therefore, the so-called "global unified internet freedom standard" promoted by Google is unworkable. Governments and peoples of different countries interpret and accept internet freedom differently and cannot all manage according to the American standard.
