Centipedes prefer fresh animal feed and eat soft-bodied juicy small insects, such as crickets, Tenebrio molitor, locusts, spiders, mole crickets, grasshoppers, earthworms, flies, maggots, sparrows, rodents, frogs, and other carnivorous animals, as well as pig blood, chicken blood, tender grass buds, watermelon rinds, etc.

The centipede nest must be kept clean, and any leftover feed and dead bodies found must be removed promptly. If dead bodies and leftover feed are not cleared, they will rot, mold, and spoil, and centipedes may get sick after mistakenly eating them. Every month, potassium permanganate can be dissolved in water until it turns light purple, and then sprinkled on the bricks and tiles in the nest for disinfection.

(1) Temperature required for centipede farming:

The suitable temperature for centipede growth and reproduction is 20°C-32°C. Below 10°C, their activity slows down, with less movement and food intake. Below 5°C, they enter hibernation.

(2) The relative humidity of the air in the nest is generally around 70%, and the moisture content of the bedding soil is 30% to 35%.

(3) Centipede stocking density:

Generally, about 600 adult centipedes, 1800 medium-sized centipedes, and 3000 small centipedes are stocked per square meter. Centipedes of different sizes must be farmed separately to prevent bullying of the weak by the strong and mutual cannibalism.

(4) Feeding time for centipedes:

Feeding usually begins around 6 PM, as centipedes are nocturnal. They come out at night to feed, between 9 PM and 3 AM. Feeding occurs every two days, and the feed must be sufficient to avoid fighting due to lack of feed. Although centipedes have a wide range of food sources, they require fresh food and do not eat rotten food. A single feeding can reach 1/5 to 3/5 of their body weight, and they can survive without food for ten to fifteen days after being fully fed.

(Eight) Seasonal management of centipedes:

(1) Spring management: In spring, attention should be paid to management before and after the Awakening of Insects. At this time, the temperature is low, and although centipedes begin to recover, their activity and digestive abilities are weak. Feeding too early often leads to indigestion. Generally, feeding occurs once every five days.

(2) Summer management: Summer is the peak season for centipede activity. Besides providing enough food, attention should also be paid to hygiene. Any rotten carcasses or leftover feed should be removed, and water should be provided. Drinking water must be clean, and indoor farming can involve opening windows for ventilation.

(3) Autumn management: For young centipedes born in August and September, grouping work should be done before winter. Feed more active feed to enhance physical fitness and store energy within the body. This applies to other aged centipedes as well, and attention should be paid to changes in the temperature of the activity area.

(4) Winter management: As temperatures drop before and after Frost's Descent, centipedes stop eating and begin hibernation. After hibernation, attention should be paid to frost prevention.

(Nine) Management of different aged centipedes:

(1) Management of young centipedes: After 42 days of incubation, centipede eggs hatch into young centipedes. At this time, their appetite is high, and feeding must be adequate. Provide water for sucking and offer palatable feed.

(2) During the growth, development, and seed production process of adult centipedes, the quality of feed is relatively high. At this time, insect meat is generally the main feed, and the frequency of feeding should be increased.
