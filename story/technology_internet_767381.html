Google Exploits User Information for Commercial Use, Only to Be Retaliated Against by Users

by anonymous on 2013-10-13 14:13:37

Recently, Google announced that it will begin using photos, comments, and recommendations from users' profile pages in its display ads starting on November 11th, and it will not pay users any fees for this. This announcement has caused a great uproar. Many Google users are angry and have started to devise countermeasures. Some have even suggested that Google users use an image of Eric Schmidt (Eric Schmidt), the executive chairman of Google, as their profile picture.

In protest against Google's actions, many Google users have come up with various "creative tactics." For instance, some suggest placing a PS-ed裸体 photo of Larry Page (Larry Page), one of Google's co-founders, holding a rose on every user's profile page.

Others have posted messages on Twitter suggesting that Google users replace their profile pictures with Eric Schmidt's mugshot. As a result, Schmidt's photo would appear in every display ad instead of the users' own pictures.

Such practices could potentially become popular among Google users. Perhaps one day, all Google users might stop using their own photos on their profile pages and switch to other images, such as a hippo, a serial killer, a character from a TV series who met a tragic end, or a former Russian president.

In this way, Google's display advertising policy could quickly turn into a farce. The company may be forced to win people over by offering users a share of the revenue generated from display ads.

On the other hand, the issue is not that Google wants to make money from any information we post, but rather our strange psychology of being eager to tell the world who we are, what we like, and what we don't like.

Of course, Google does offer users the option to "opt out." This is precisely where its cleverness lies; it won't get into legal trouble over privacy issues like Facebook did.

Perhaps, the practice of using Schmidt's headshot will have an effect and force Google to compromise.
