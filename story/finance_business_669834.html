Chinese-American takes care of urban homeless for 24 years and is awarded by Obama

by ming1980k on 2012-02-20 11:46:07

The Chinese Mother of 500 Homeless People

For 24 years, she has tirelessly cared for the homeless, growing from helping four individuals to now assisting five hundred. Every day, she cooks meals for 500 people, helps them build bathhouses, and even arranges funerals for those who have passed away. She says her motivation stems from her own childhood experience of homelessness and the constant hunger she endured. She was awarded the "Presidential Citizens Medal" by Obama.

When Guan Huiqun received the "Presidential Citizens Medal" from President Obama, she tried hard to conceal her excitement, repeatedly reminding herself: "He's just an ordinary person."

This scene took place on August 4th last year. Prior to this, the 50-year-old Chinese-American woman had never imagined that she would be mentioned in the same breath as former US Secretary of State Colin Powell, actress Elizabeth Taylor, or boxer Muhammad Ali, all of whom were previous recipients of the same medal.

Born in Guangdong, she never attended school and can only write her name in Chinese. When announcing the reasons for her award, Obama summarized her achievements in just a few sentences—caring for the homeless in Eureka, California, day after day for 24 years.

"This isn't my job; I didn't plan it. It just gradually grew," Guan Huiqun said in her heavily accented Cantonese during a phone interview with the China Youth Daily reporter.

At 3:30 AM on June 20th local time, Guan Huiqun woke up as usual, started brewing coffee, and began making cakes. In her own kitchen, there are two large ovens capable of baking 100 small cakes each and five commercial coffee machines that can make 100 cups of coffee each. With these, she single-handedly prepares breakfast for 500 homeless people.

She is familiar with every step in her kitchen. 24 years ago, she began doing this, but back then, it was much easier since she was only caring for a family of four living in a scrapped car. As she came to know more homeless people, she started waking up earlier. At her busiest times, she would rise at 2 AM to prepare breakfast, sleeping only four or five hours a day.

At 7 AM, she starts her professional meal delivery van, driving to 11 different locations where groups of homeless people gather. Over the years, through repeated outdoor visits, she has gradually discovered these people living under bridges, beside railway tracks, and in bushes.

These people have become accustomed to Guan Huiqun's delivery schedule. Even before the food truck arrives, long queues have already formed. To save time, she sometimes hands out meals in bulk to familiar homeless "leaders" and then quickly moves on. However, in places where there are more mentally ill patients, she stays for half an hour to "watch them eat personally."

In areas considered "illegal" by the local government, the homeless wait hidden among the trees. Guan Huiqun carries a stack of cakes and a heavy coffee pot, walking half a mile into the bushes.

After not too long, her long-standing leg pain may flare up. When she can no longer walk, she waits for the homeless to come help her.

Jesse is one of the homeless people who often helps carry the coffee pot. For the past seven years, Jesse has slept in the mountains, on street corners, and in tents in the woods, but his breakfast has almost always been the coffee and desserts delivered by Guan Huiqun. This 41-year-old white man sometimes wonders how Guan Huiqun can keep going.

"I just can't bear to see others go hungry; it makes me uncomfortable when I see it," Guan Huiqun explained.

"Hunger" is almost the entirety of this woman's childhood memory. Born in Kaiping, Guangdong, in 1959, her parents were both local doctors. During the Cultural Revolution, her originally wealthy family fell apart, and Guan Huiqun became a street child at the age when she should have been starting school. Starving, she spent her days searching for food in garbage piles, sometimes even having to "fight dogs for food."

Four years after wandering the streets, Guan Huiqun was smuggled to Hong Kong by relatives and soon immigrated to the United States. Later, she met her current husband. After marriage, her husband taught physics at Humboldt State University in California, and Guan Huiqun settled in nearby Eureka.

Even after becoming a mother of two children, Guan Huiqun still couldn't shake off the shadow of her childhood. She kept a large stockpile of food at home, including hundreds of pounds of rice, and always cooked far more than necessary. She constantly worried about "not having enough food."

When her eldest son was in the first grade, while working part-time as a physical education assistant at his school, she noticed a white girl in her son's class who kept asking her son for food. She later learned that the girl's father was a car washer who had lost his job, plunging the family into financial difficulties. They were starving and couldn't afford rent, so their home was a dilapidated car.

"She reminded me of myself when I was little," Guan Huiqun said. From then on, she began sending food from her home to the girl's family. During Thanksgiving and Christmas, she would cook a meal at home, bring along her ceramic tableware, and join the family in their car for dinner.

Gradually, her concern extended beyond just food: seeing the moldy sofa in the car, she brought extra bed sheets from home to cover it; noticing that the girl's sister wet her pants, she brought diapers; she also introduced a job at McDonald's to the girl's father.

Now, over 20 years later, Guan Huiqun has expanded what she did for that girl's family to include over 500 homeless people in Eureka, ranging from a three-month-old baby to an 82-year-old elder. Guan Huiqun remembers each of their names.

On June 20th, while delivering breakfast, she was also thinking about when to take a pregnant homeless woman to the hospital for a check-up, when to send a homeless young man who wants to attend university to meet a social worker, and how to help a runaway youth return home.

Initially, she only wanted to ensure that others wouldn't go hungry, but now, Guan Huiqun hopes that these homeless people can "regain dignity and reintegrate into society."

For these homeless individuals, dignity might start with taking a shower. Last year, Guan Huiqun invested $2,500 and partnered with a charity organization to build a bathhouse. Every day, about 60 homeless people visit this bathhouse with three showers, enjoying 20 minutes of free bathing.

At 9 AM on June 20th, after finishing breakfast delivery, Guan Huiqun rushed to the bathhouse to register the arriving homeless people and repeatedly urged them not to forget to "brush their tongues." Due to long-term alcoholism or drug addiction, many homeless people have lost their sense of taste, and Guan Huiqun accidentally discovered that "brushing the tongue" can help restore it.

"Mom, is this okay?" After brushing, the homeless people would stick out their tongues for Guan Huiqun to "check." This group of homeless people, regardless of age, always habitually call Guan Huiqun "mom."

In this bathhouse, one homeless man once begged her to let him "stay longer in the bathroom to feel like being home." Another time, a 60-year-old man hugged her and cried loudly after stepping out of the bathroom. He hadn't looked in a mirror or taken a bath for 16 years and had forgotten the original color of his skin.

Guan Huiqun understands this kind of life deeply. She remembers collapsing on the streets of Kaiping, Guangdong, countless times due to hunger, feeling powerless even when flies crawled on her face.

In that turbulent era, Guan Huiqun's mother couldn't remember her exact birthday, as she was the eighth among twelve siblings. Guan Huiqun, wandering alone on the streets, always told herself: "As long as I wake up tomorrow and can still move, it's my birthday."

Now, Guan Huiqun has found that the best way to stop being afraid of hunger is to continuously help others by cooking for them.

At 1 PM, she rushed again from the bathhouse to a large kitchen at the church. Here, she spends two and a half hours cooking pasta and beef in two 1.4-meter-high large pots. Since the church provided her with this kitchen for free four years ago, the amount of food she prepares has been increasing.

On June 20th, she prepared the largest meal of her life—700 pounds of pasta. To transport these noodles, she had to divide them into 36 smaller pots, place them on a cart one by one, and push the cart back and forth to load the noodles onto the meal truck.

"I'm almost going crazy," she said. Near the end of the month, many people's wages from the beginning of the month were almost used up, leaving them without money for food. Recently, those coming to eat aren't all homeless; there are also economically struggling families. Guan Huiqun doesn't mind. "Come and eat if you're hungry!" she always says while handing out the noodles.

Some Americans in business thought Guan Huiqun was indulging lazy people and shouted loudly at her doorstep: "Get on your boat and go back!" Guan Huiqun didn't respond directly but humorously replied: "Now I can take a plane!"

In reality, this woman who always smiles in every photo often feels anger and helplessness.

On stormy nights, Guan Huiqun often drives to the beach to look for those without shelter. She lets everyone take turns sitting in her car to avoid the rain and pulls the disoriented homeless people away from the sea. At such moments, she can't help but ask herself angrily: "Why am I the only one here?"

What makes her feel even more helpless is that over the years, she has lost count of how many "children" she has seen pass away, and the undertakers in Eureka have become her friends. Due to insufficient funds, Guan Huiqun can only cremate most of the deceased homeless people, and only buys land for those who particularly wish to be buried.

On the evening of June 20th, Guan Huiqun visited Sandy under the bridge again. Sandy, 56 years old, suffers from advanced lung cancer, and the doctor has advised her to abandon treatment. Based on her experience, Guan Huiqun judged that Sandy "won't live for more than a week." All she can do is wipe Sandy's body with bathing powder every night and then apply moisturizer to her dry skin.

However, what slightly comforts this "mom" is that some "children" are doing better, like Jesse, who recently moved into a car she bought from the used car market and no longer has to endure sun and rain. Also, Willy, 21 years old, moved from a tent beside the railway track into a house belonging to her friend and recently started attending community college.

The little girl she helped for the first time 24 years ago is now married and has children in another city, working as an accountant. In her own kitchen, she keeps a set of tableware similar to the ones Guan Huiqun once brought, reminding herself not to forget the kindness she received back then.

In recent years, more and more people have joined Guan Huiqun's efforts. A coffee shop in Eureka leaves its unsold desserts for her every day, beef suppliers occasionally deliver free beef, doctors provide free medical consultations for the homeless, and some citizens have begun volunteering. Guan Huiqun's neighbor Mike Craghead even set up a personal homepage for her, paying for the domain name out of his own pocket and managing online fundraising.

Even the application process for the "Presidential Citizens Medal" was entirely handled by her neighbors. In the summer of 2009, Tim Wozniak learned online that the 42-year-old "Presidential Citizens Medal" had for the first time implemented a public nomination system.

"Guan Huiqun was the first person who came to my mind; she does what the government should do," Tim said in a phone interview with the China Youth Daily reporter. He simply filled out a form online, wrote a short essay of about 500 words, and completed the nomination.

To Tim's great surprise, Guan Huiqun was eventually officially selected as one of the 13 awardees.

At the award ceremony held at the White House, Guan Huiqun specially wore traditional Chinese clothing with Tang-style buttons and maintained a smile throughout. It wasn't until she stepped down from the stage and hung the medal around her neck that she couldn't help but cry.

Recalling the moment afterward, she said that at that instant, she remembered the sign hanging around her neck as a child, which read "the child of demons and snakes," giving her a feeling of being transported to another world.

Last year, Guan Huiqun returned to Hong Kong. "Maybe I'm getting older, and I increasingly want to go back to my hometown," she said, adding that she still hasn't returned to mainland China. While in Hong Kong, she specifically asked her friend to drive her to the New Territories and look across the river at Shenzhen.
