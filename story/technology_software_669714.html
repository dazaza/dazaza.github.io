It's just a "dream".

by nfhlvpug on 2012-02-20 10:30:50

Share to: Welcome to post comments I want to comment. Cheng Jun said that he most admired the Hong Kong lyricist, Chris Tang. "When 'Chris' and 'Tang' are put together, it forms a 'dream' character," Cheng Jun said, "and my dream is to promote my lyrics just like him."

Singing praises is his fantasy. In the summer of 2007, while working at a certain construction site, Cheng Jun's right foot was injured, and he was sent to a nearby large hospital by his co-workers. The doctor directly used hydrogen peroxide on Cheng Jun's wound, causing him to cry out in pain: "Why don't you disinfect it?" The doctor gave Cheng Jun a sideways glance and coldly said, "Am I the doctor or are you?"

Cheng Jun could not express his gratitude, so he picked up a piece of paper and wrote: "Happy times do not know the existence of troubles / Lost in sadness without knowing how to wake up / Thank you for your care..."

The hand-written drafts are his children. Life is his teacher. He hopes to find a collaborator who can help him turn the lyrics into music, accompany the singing, and record it into a complete song. He also hopes for a stage where he can loudly sing his own songs. "I want to give my dreams an answer!" Cheng Jun repeatedly told the reporter. Just as he had written on the file folder where he kept his drafts in 2004: I want success, and I want failure, everything is possible.

Cheng Jun parted the bedding to reveal a thick stack of draft papers. He boldly opened them one by one, treating them like his own children. These were the lyrics he had written over the past few years. Some were written on advertising flyers, some on restaurant order slips, and some even on building material packaging paper. "When inspiration strikes, I will immediately jot it down, even in the bathroom, I would suddenly take out my phone and save the inspiration in the text messages."

In this vast city, he was alone, yet he could easily transform the minutiae of life into lyrics. In the eyes of his co-workers, he seemed a bit aloof, yet he deeply admired the famous Hong Kong lyricist, Chris Tang.

Cheng Jun angrily transferred to a smaller clinic. There, a young nurse not only helped clean his wound but also brought him water and bought him lunch. What moved Cheng Jun the most was when the nurse said, "We are all workers, seeing you is like seeing our relatives who work outside."

During the past decade, Cheng Jun has already created more than 200 lyrics. "At that time, I read a novel called 'Dreams of Falling Flowers,' and was moved by its rich language, so I also began trying to express myself with delicate language," Cheng Jun said.

Due to his creations, Cheng Jun gradually became a bit isolated. While his co-workers played cards and went to internet cafes, he stayed in the bedroom organizing his drafts. When his co-workers asked what he was writing, he simply replied, "Diary."

Stepping over discarded wood chips and walking through muddy soil, the reporter followed Cheng Jun to an empty building within a construction site in Chen Jiawan, Wuchang. Bending down to enter a short door, they arrived at a ten-square-meter earthen house - this was the dormitory he shared with three other workers. Wooden boards served as his bed, and plastic buckets as his desk.

Most of Cheng Jun's beloved lyrics still lay in those repeatedly reviewed draft papers and in his computer. "Maybe because I am getting older, I cannot settle down to learn how to make accompaniments and record songs," Cheng Jun, who had long wandered between various construction sites and lived in basements and unfinished houses, remained steadfast in his desire to turn his lyrics into songs.

Cheng Jun always had a continuous stream of creative inspiration. This inspiration came from everything he saw and heard in his life.
