Apple soared by 5%, slapping the Apple bashers with three sets of data.

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 21:53:30

Introduction: Apple (AAPL) announced on Monday that the sales of iPhone 5C/5S had exceeded 9 million units in three days, and for a moment, the world was silent, then noisy again. The silence came from the "Apple haters" who had been chanting work songs to bury Apple in the past ten days, while the noise came from the various "Apple fans" who were almost buried with it. Apple's stock price soared by 5% on Monday.

On September 10, the much-anticipated Apple new product launch was held at its headquarters. Unlike previous years, two new iPhones, 5C and 5S, were released this time. Apart from the 64-bit A7 processor equipped in the 5S, almost all the "surprises" had been revealed by earlier media reports. Apple's confidentiality system was thus declared completely broken, and the stock price plummeted accordingly, dropping nearly 3% within 30 minutes, eventually closing down 2.28% at $494.64.

When the wall falls, everyone pushes. Before the opening of the next day, Bank of America, UBS, and Credit Suisse simultaneously announced the downgrade of Apple Inc.'s stock rating and target price, causing Apple to open down 6%.

On September 17, the news that China Unicom's iPhone 5C pre-sale data dropped by half compared to the iPhone 5, and China Telecom reduced subsidies on iPhone contract phones once again shocked Wall Street's fragile nerves, causing Apple to fall another 3.18%. By then, Apple's market value had evaporated $51 billion in five trading days, equivalent to RMB 312.2 billion.

What does RMB 312.2 billion mean? The permanent population of Beijing, Shanghai, and Guangzhou is 58.04 million, and the price of the naked iPhone 5S in China is RMB 5,288 — the market value that Apple lost in five days could buy each permanent resident of Beijing, Shanghai, and Guangzhou an iPhone 5S, even giving them an extra RMB 90 worth of phone credit. Another way to understand it, $45 billion is equivalent to twice Nokia's market value and nearly ten times BlackBerry's market value.

Tim Cook cried, shouting to the mountains, forests, and seas: "Where did I go wrong, where did I go wrong?"

Wall Street professionals strongly agreed with the opinions of Chinese netizens: the low-end iPhone aimed at the Chinese market is priced too high, and the market share will decline again...

Amidst the curses, Bloomberg journalist Sam Grobart found Tim Cook, who was accompanied by Senior Vice President of Software Engineering Craig Federighi and Apple's unofficial king Sir Jony Ive, for a rare long interview. In this interview, Cook rarely responded to Wall Street's doubts about Apple's management team.

He implied that the iPhone 5C was not positioned as a low-end phone from the beginning, and Apple has no products targeted at the low-end market. "I think in all consumer electronics markets, there is always a large segment prepared for junk. We don't do the junk business. We don't produce products for the junk market. What we want to do is make truly great products and provide an excellent experience. And I am sure that we have enough customers willing to buy such products. We want to please them."

Does this sound familiar? In August 2007, Jobs said something similar when answering CNET reporter Molly Wood's question: "Our goal is to make the best personal computer in the world, a product we can proudly sell and recommend to our families and friends. We want to make such products at the lowest possible cost. But I must tell you, there are some things in our industry that we cannot proudly put into consumers' hands, some things we cannot proudly recommend to our families and friends. We cannot make such things. We just cannot make junk."

It seems as if those who complained that the iPhone was too expensive and cheered after Apple's stock price plummeted, left obvious red fingerprints on their own faces.

Four days after Cook's interview, on September 23, Apple hit them again with the right hand, announcing sales exceeding 9 million units.

But obviously, the old hands on Wall Street are all about money and not about face. Apple's stock price gapped up 6.1%, closing up 4.97% at $490.64. The already rotten apple suddenly became fresh again, making Wall Street analysts drool.

Not only the iPhone but also the "ugly as hell" iOS 7 — the newly flattened iPhone operating system — delivered the third slap. No one admits to liking "My Heart Will Go On," this "kitschy song," no matter how perfect Celine Dion's voice is; nor does anyone dare admit to liking iOS 7.

Claiming to hate Jony Ive's flat design is the most effective way to gain "taste," while admitting to staying up until 1 AM upgrading and loving it is more dangerous than coming out of the closet. The latter is just a sexual orientation issue, which anyone cannot discriminate against, while the former concerns aesthetics, and will surely be trampled upon by ten thousand feet, forever nailed to the pillar of shame in history.

Fortunately, within a few days, over 200 million people upgraded to iOS 7, breaking the fastest software upgrade record again. Apple fans can breathe a sigh of relief now, with so many people joining in, they no longer need to walk around with tails between their legs.

Apple has always been one of the most watched companies by both Wall Street and the general public, yet Apple's stock price has been irrationally fluctuating around its value, and no one knows what the real value is. The controversy surrounding Apple, or even insults, will continue. It doesn't matter; this is also one of the pleasures of investing. No company can provide so many interesting topics. Just remember, arguments aside, calm down, figure out the market rules, and make money — that's the ultimate goal of investors. Have fun, but don't forget the main event.
