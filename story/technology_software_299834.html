Criminal Defense - Basic Criteria for Conviction -- Objective Judgment Precedes Subjective Judgment

by guanlu369 on 2009-12-09 10:43:42

The conditions for establishing a crime include both objective and subjective conditions. Under the two systems of criminal composition, these conditions must be met, but the question lies in whether to make an objective judgment first or a subjective judgment first? We believe that an objective judgment must be made first to ensure the accurate determination of guilt. This rule is determined by the logical progression structure of the three-tier system. In the practical activities of the four-element system, the rule that objective judgment precedes subjective judgment has not been institutionally guaranteed, and the order of objective and subjective judgments can be carried out arbitrarily. On the surface, it seems to be just a simple issue of order, but in fact, the impact of this order issue is enormous. In current judicial department practice, due to the lack of clear rules constraining objective judgment before subjective judgment, many cases will lead to incorrect conclusions.

There was a case: How to characterize taking a company car away while working as a driver under a false identity. The case details are that Wang used fake identification to apply for a driver position at a clothing company. On his first day of work, he took the opportunity to drive away with the company car during a business trip and claimed it as his own. Afterwards, Wang used the same method to possess cars from three companies, with illegal gains ranging from 100,000 to 200,000 yuan (refer to the third edition of the Procuratorial Daily on October 14, 2009 —— editor's note). In this case, Wang had the intention to illegally possess the car before applying, so he forged an ID and a driver's license to apply for the job and took the opportunity to drive away with the car while performing driving tasks assigned by the company, claiming it as his own. Some people believed that Wang committed fraud, with the following reasons: First, Wang’s subjective intent to commit fraud ran throughout the entire case. His intent to illegally possess the car occurred before he obtained the position as a driver. Under the guise of fabricating facts and concealing the truth, he implemented a series of behaviors such as applying for the job, accepting employment, approaching the property, and obtaining the property. His goals were clear, and his actions were meticulous, with a clear intent to commit fraud throughout the process. Second, Wang's objective behavior conforms to the characteristics of fraud crimes. It was precisely because he fabricated facts and concealed the truth, causing the victim to fall into a cognitive error and voluntarily deliver and dispose of the property, which allowed Wang to achieve his criminal purpose. This objective manifestation fully meets the characteristics of fraud crimes. Here, the disposal of property rights should not only be understood as the disposal of ownership of the property, but should also include the disposal of control over the property and possession rights. Moreover, in judicial practice, as long as the act of transferring property is carried out, the victim already faces a huge risk of losing the property, and when delivering the property, the victim does not think they are transferring ownership of the property. From these reasons, we can see that the author of the case first said that Wang subjectively intended to commit fraud, then said he carried out fraudulent behavior, making a subjective judgment first and then an objective judgment, leading to errors. If the three-tier theory is followed, this case should clearly be classified as embezzlement by an employee. The key difference lies in whether to make an objective judgment first or a subjective judgment first. If an objective judgment is made first, the defendant's application for employment would not be considered a fraudulent act; even if the application was made with the intention of committing fraud, the application itself does not belong to the constitutive elements of fraud. The constitutive element of fraud is fabricating facts, concealing the truth, causing the victim to fall into a cognitive error and dispose of the property, and illegally possessing others' property. However, the act of applying for a job, even using a false ID, cannot be considered a constitutive element of fraud. His constitutive element behavior is using his position as a driver to possess the company's property, and such an objective element determines that the behavior is embezzlement by an employee. After confirming this objective judgment, then judge the subjective elements; there is subjective intent to illegally possess. A principle needs to be explained here: the objective constitutive elements have the function of regulating intent. Its meaning is that objective behavior exists independently of subjective intent. However, subjective intent depends on objective behavior. In this case, he used his position to possess the property of his unit, which objectively constitutes embezzlement by an employee; subjectively, he had the intent to defraud, based on the fact that his act of obtaining his position had the subjective intent of fraud. Therefore, it does not affect the conviction but affects sentencing. If you make a subjective judgment first, believing that the perpetrator subjectively intended to commit fraud, then the objective behavior is a fraudulent act, having a subjective intent to commit fraud, how could the objective behavior not be a fraudulent act? One error would lead to other errors. Such errors are very common in the judicial process.
