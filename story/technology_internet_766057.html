Microsoft to acquire Nokia's devices and services business, accelerating the growth of the Windows ecosystem

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 14:41:53

　　The Next Chapter: An open letter from Steve Ballmer and Stephen Elop

　　One of the biggest "what-if" speculations in recent years has come true. On the morning of September 3rd, Beijing time, Microsoft announced the acquisition of Nokia's Devices & Services division. The company will acquire Nokia's business unit for €3.79 billion and its patent portfolio for €1.65 billion, totaling $7.17 billion.

　　According to the US tech blog TheVerge, this move will enable Windows Phone 8 to achieve "software-hardware integration," allowing Microsoft to offer an integrated solution spanning both software and hardware.

　　This transaction is expected to be completed in the first quarter of 2014, subject to approval by Nokia's shareholders and relevant institutions.

　　Stephen Elop and Steve Ballmer jointly released an open letter stating that the collaboration between Microsoft and Nokia will accelerate the development of "Nokia's devices and services business":

“This will accelerate the momentum of Nokia’s devices and services business, bringing the world’s most innovative smartphones to even more people, while continuing to connect the next billion users through Nokia’s rich range of feature phones.

Microsoft's commitment and resources will drive Nokia’s devices and services business forward. We can now realize the full potential of the Windows ecosystem, delivering the best mobile experiences for people’s work and life.”

　　In terms of specific acquisition details, Microsoft will acquire Nokia's Devices & Services business, including the Mobile Phones and Smart Devices business units, industrial design team, production equipment, related market, sales teams, and support teams. After the acquisition is completed, 32,000 employees will transfer from Nokia to Microsoft, with 4,700 coming from Finland. Nokia's CTO and patents will remain within the company — by the second quarter of 2014, the company's workforce will eventually stabilize at 56,000 employees.

　　Regarding the acquisition costs, the cost for the commercial department is €3.79 billion ($4.99 billion), and the cost for the patent portfolio is €1.65 billion ($2.18 billion). The first part of the payment will mature within five years, with an annual bond coupon rate of 1.125%, and an initial conversion price of €3.9338 every six months. The second part of the payment will mature within six years, with an annual bond coupon rate of 2.5%, and an initial conversion price of €4.0851 every six months. The third part of the payment will mature within seven years, with an annual bond coupon rate of 3.625%, and an initial conversion price of €4.2364 every six months.

　　After the acquisition of the Devices & Services division, Nokia will retain three businesses: Network Infrastructure Services (NSN), Maps Service (Nokia Here), and Technology Development and Registration Services (Advanced Technologies). Nokia also announced that Risto Siilasmaa, chairman of the board, will serve as interim CEO, while Stephen Elop will return to Microsoft to manage the Devices and Studios team, responsible for content including Microsoft's existing devices and Nokia's entire product line.

　　Julie Larson-Green, the previous head of Microsoft's Devices and Studios team, will continue to oversee the project, focusing on Xbox and Surface accessories. After the acquisition is completed, Larson-Green will join Elop's team.

　　As part of the acquisition, Nokia's key executives responsible for engineering design will join Microsoft. The involved Nokia executives include: Jo Harlow, who will continue to lead the smart device team; Timo Toikkanen, who will continue to lead the mobile phone team; Stefan Pannenbecker, who will take charge of design; Juha Putkiranta, who will lead the integration of Nokia's interests.

　　Also as part of the acquisition, Nokia agreed to authorize Microsoft to use the Nokia brand for 10 years on current Mobile Phone products — this may indicate that Microsoft will produce and manufacture non-smartphones under the Nokia brand, including devices based on Series 30 and Series 40. Nokia remains the owner of the Nokia brand.

　　Comments:

　　Current Nokia CEO Stephen Elop previously served as president of Microsoft's Business Division and was a close ally of Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer. Elop left Microsoft in October 2010 to become Nokia's CEO. After Elop became Nokia's CEO, Nokia made a major decision: to completely abandon the Symbian smartphone platform, which had been in use for ten years, and switch to Microsoft's latest Windows Phone platform. Because of this, Elop was questioned by many Nokia insiders and media as being Microsoft's "Trojan Horse."

　　After Ballmer announced his retirement last month, Elop was seen as one of the most likely successors. Is this acquisition of Nokia a way for Microsoft to "welcome back" Elop?

　　Forbes: It's not surprising that Microsoft sold its devices division to Nokia, but what is surprising is the timing and price. Nokia currently appears to have turned things around with Lumia and its strategy of using low-end feature phones to boost its smartphone business has been quite successful. Why sell now? And at such an extremely low price.

　　Additionally, Elop's motives are suspect. After jumping from Microsoft to Nokia, Elop quickly decided to bet everything on Microsoft when Nokia's quarterly phone sales were still as high as 24 million units. With this sale, Elop returns to Microsoft.

　　Even the people of Finland, his home country, highly question Elop's loyalty over these years. (Let alone us?)

　　AllThingsD: This move sends a clear signal: Microsoft firmly believes it must succeed in the mobile phone business. Even though Nokia has placed its fate in Microsoft's hands, Microsoft still needs to keep control of its own destiny.

　　Wall Street Journal: This deal reveals a strong signal from Microsoft: in the competition against Apple and Google, Microsoft needs stronger measures, which is the most pressing threat that Ballmer's successor needs to address. Nokia also acknowledges that rather than fighting alone, it's better to pool resources and survive together.

　　According to BusinessWeek, Sydney telecom operator consultant Paul Budde believes, “In terms of smartphones, Nokia and Microsoft really missed the boat, and it's hard to grab hold of the railing and climb back on. The question is, will the merger of two weak companies result in a strong new company? I'm skeptical.”

　　A New Chapter: An Open Letter from Ballmer and Elop

　　— Microsoft to acquire Nokia's Devices & Services division, accelerating the development of the Windows ecosystem

　　Nokia and Microsoft both have great dreams — we once dreamed that there should be a computer on every desk and a phone in every pocket, and after our long-term efforts, these dreams have gradually become reality today.

　　And today marks a new moment of innovation.

　　Nokia carries a legacy of 150 years of tradition, innovation, excellence, and constant reinvention, this DNA of transformation began in Finland and continues to spread globally. From the original paper mill to producing rubber shoes and tires, then to mobile phones, the spirit of innovation is embedded in Nokia's blood.

　　Nokia will write a new chapter, continuing its innovations in mobile networks, maps and location, and cutting-edge technology.

　　So too with Microsoft, today is a bold step towards the future, a big leap towards our new journey: creating a series of devices and services that delight consumers and assist businesses of all sizes.

　　In our past two-and-a-half-year partnership, both parties have built a new global mobile ecosystem with their strengths, achieving remarkable results: Nokia's Windows Phones, through award-winning phones and outstanding mobile applications and services, are becoming the fastest-growing smart terminals globally.

　　On this basis of successful cooperation, we announce important news today: Microsoft intends to acquire Nokia's Devices & Services business, providing customers with more choices, faster innovation, and more surprising devices and services.

　　This will accelerate the momentum of Nokia's Devices & Services business, bringing the world's most innovative smartphones to more people, while continuing to connect the next billion users through Nokia's rich range of feature phones.

　　Microsoft's commitment and resources will drive Nokia's Devices & Services business forward. We can now realize the immense potential of the Windows ecosystem, delivering the best mobile experience for people's work and life.

　　We will continue to produce the phones you love, while investing in the future — new phones and services that combine the best resources of Microsoft and Nokia.

　　Nokia and Microsoft will both fully commit to this new chapter.

　　Together, we will redefine the boundaries of the mobile field.

　　Elop and Ballmer

　　Ballmer's Open Letter to Microsoft Employees: Elop is Back

　　Following Microsoft's announcement of acquiring Nokia's phone business for approximately $7.2 billion, Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer sent a letter to all Microsoft employees, outlining how the company plans to integrate this business after the transaction is completed.

　　Below is the full text of the email:

　　From: Steve Ballmer

　　To: All Microsoft Employees

　　Time: 8:00 PM, Pacific Time, September 2nd

　　Subject: Accelerating Growth

　　We have exciting news today: We have reached an agreement to acquire Nokia's Devices & Services business, including their smartphone and phone businesses, the award-winning design team, global production and assembly facilities, and operations, sales, marketing, and support teams.

　　For Microsoft, this is a bold step into the future and the next important phase following our July 11th announcement of transformation.

　　We are delighted that this plan will bring together the best mobile device businesses of Microsoft and Nokia. In the past two and a half years, our cooperation on Windows Phone has achieved excellent results, with the stunning Lumia 1020 being the best proof. Our cooperation has already brought about rapid growth. In fact, Nokia's Windows Phone is the fastest-growing smartphone in the market.

　　It's time to move forward on this momentum, increasing the market share and profitability of our mobile phone business. Clearly, greater success in the mobile phone business will give us and our partners better opportunities to implement our strategy, developing a series of devices and services for personal and enterprise users, serving users worldwide at home, at work, and on the go, helping them engage in the activities they value most.

　　We have explained the strategic rationale behind Microsoft's transaction in a document. I encourage you to read it.

　　This is a smart acquisition for Microsoft, beneficial to both companies. We will gain excellent talent, technology, and intellectual property rights. We have already seen the outstanding results produced by the cooperation between Nokia and Microsoft.

　　Considering our long-term partnership with Nokia and the fact that several key Nokia leaders will join Microsoft, we expect a smooth transition and excellent execution.

　　As with all other acquisitions, our top priority is to ensure the completion of the transaction. We expect the transaction to be completed in the first quarter of 2014, requiring Nokia shareholder approval, regulatory approval, and satisfaction of other conditions necessary for the completion of the transaction.

　　However, I know many are curious about what will happen after the transaction is completed. Although the details have not yet been finalized, here's what we currently know and generally how we will drive the integration process:

　　1. Steven Elop will return to Microsoft to lead the expanded device team, which will include all our current device and studio businesses as well as most of the teams from Nokia. He will report directly to me.

　　2. Julie Larson-Green will continue to lead the Devices & Studios team, focusing on important products to be released this fall, including Xbox One and new Surface tablets. After the transaction is completed, Larson-Green will join Elop's team and collaborate with him to build the new organization.

　　3. As part of the acquisition, several key Nokia engineering leaders will join Microsoft, reporting to Elop.

　　- Jo Harlow will continue to lead the smart device team.

　　- Timo Toikkanen will continue to lead the mobile phone team.

　　- Stefan Pannenbecker will be responsible for design.

　　- Juha Putkiranta will be responsible for the business integration work on Nokia's side.

　　4. Regarding the sales team, we plan to retain Nokia's field team led by Chris Weber unchanged, continuing to focus on device sales, so we can continue to drive sales growth. After the transaction is completed, Weber and his team will report to Kevin Turner. We will establish a unified team focused on carrier sales. There may be some additional opportunities for integration. Turner will collaborate with Weber and Chris Capossela to develop plans.

　　5. The Operations Systems team led by Terry Myerson will remain unchanged, but its mission will include supporting first-party and third-party hardware innovation. We are committed to working with our partners to help them develop excellent products and businesses based on our platform. We believe this transaction will enhance the value of our partners in the future. The existing rhythm and ways of working between Myerson's team and the newly added Nokia team will serve us well, ensuring our progress is unaffected.

　　6. We plan to integrate global marketing activities, led by Tami Reller and Mark Penn. It is crucial that we quickly achieve a unified branding and advertising strategy.

　　7. Functions such as finance, legal, human resources, corporate communications, DX/evangelism, customer care, and business development will be integrated into Microsoft. Procurement, customer logistics, and supply chain will become part of the device business led by Elop. The ICM/IT function will also be integrated into the traditional IT department. Given the different manufacturing processes and systems owned by Nokia and Microsoft, we need to study the factory system situation.

　　8. We plan to provide unified support services for all devices. We need to study how to integrate Nokia's excellent work into Microsoft's services during the integration process.

　　9. During the integration process, we do not have significant plans to change the locations where work is conducted globally. Therefore, we expect the Nokia team to basically remain at their current locations.

　　10. Tom Gibbons will lead the business integration work on Microsoft's side.

　　Although we have announced an important message today, we still need to focus on the operation of our current business. We are facing an important autumn and holiday season, so we need to execute flawlessly and continue to drive business growth. I have no doubt that we can achieve this.

　　Steve
