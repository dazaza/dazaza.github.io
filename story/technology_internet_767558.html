Vancl plans to transform in the style of Xiaomi: Lei Jun once criticized Vancl for being inadequate, which upset Chen Nian.

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 10:48:14

Chen Nian Talks About Lei Jun's Influence: Vancl to Adopt Xiaomi-like Strategy with 20% Staff Reduction

Vancl, once a shining star in the internet-native brand company sector, has taken a path opposite to JD.com and Tmall over the past two years, always teetering on the brink. After encountering inventory accumulation and a failed IPO attempt in 2011, Vancl faced a "power cut" controversy during its 5th anniversary celebration last year, and as its 6th anniversary approached this year, it became embroiled in a scandal involving delayed payments to suppliers.

Following threats from suppliers to jump off buildings to demand repayment, rumors surrounding Vancl grew increasingly intense, ranging from cash flow issues that could lead to a repeat of PPG's fate, to speculations that Lei Jun, an investor in Vancl, and Chen Nian, CEO of Vancl, had a "cozy" relationship where Lei might take over Vancl. There were also claims by former employees that investors had issued a "final ultimatum" to Chen Nian, not ruling out the possibility of replacing the CEO.

Beneath these rampant rumors lies Vancl's stagnation in the e-commerce industry over the past two years, contrasting sharply with its confident strides just a few years ago. In 2012, Vancl narrowly escaped disaster after a massive inventory clearance sale, while in 2013, it was seen as a platform for other struggling clothing brands to liquidate their excess stock after gaining fame through its own inventory clearance efforts.

In May this year, Vancl attempted to introduce third-party brands, considering adopting a "brand + VIPshop" model. In September, Vancl underwent a major restructuring and integration of its V+, Flash Sale, and third-party departments, preparing to return to its brand-oriented strategy. Amidst Vancl's series of actions, its positioning became unclear, leaving outsiders puzzled whether Vancl was a product brand or a channel brand.

Amid Vancl's wavering strategies, investors expressed doubts about Vancl’s current approach. According to insiders, starting from June this year, investors significantly increased their intervention in Vancl. During a series of board meetings, Vancl's future and direction were repeatedly debated. Ultimately, Lei Jun influenced Chen Nian deeply, prompting him to decide to stop wavering and refocus Vancl on products and branding.

Chen Nian revealed in an interview with Tencent Technology last night that since June, he had interacted with Lei Jun multiple times over a period of 60 hours. Especially before the launch of Xiaomi 3, Lei Jun earnestly discussed Xiaomi's product and brand philosophy. Lei Jun's attitude and methods with Xiaomi greatly impacted Chen Nian, directly influencing Vancl's subsequent series of drastic adjustments.

These adjustments include relocating the headquarters from the city center to the remote Yizhuang Development Zone, making the decision within a month. The integration and adjustment of Vancl's V+, Flash Sale, and third-party departments involved hundreds of employees and thousands of suppliers. This relocation and adjustment "optimized" away 20% of Vancl's workforce, leading to disputes over delayed supplier payments due to poor communication and personnel turnover.

"V+, Flash Sale, and the third-party departments involve hundreds of people and thousands of partners; misunderstandings are normal during changes. The delayed payment issue to suppliers is mainly due to communication problems," Chen Nian said. Vancl's investors are under pressure, but not to the extent of demanding a CEO change yet. Payments for delayed amounts are being expedited, and Vancl's new round of financing is in process and will soon be credited.

Regarding the recent supplier payment controversies, Chen Nian believes the media is "sensationalizing" the situation. He complained that although many media outlets reported that suppliers cornered him on the 3rd floor, this did not actually happen last week.

However, this does not mean Vancl will remain unchanged. After the adjustments, Vancl will undergo a "Xiaomi-style transformation," focusing less on a wide variety of products and prioritizing creating market-explosive items. On the product side, the main focus will be on ensuring no inventory buildup while maintaining profitability. Additionally, Vancl plans to drastically reduce its SKU count, returning to simple combinations and achieving the highest quality standards.

Lei Jun's influence led to dramatic adjustments at Vancl.

The theme word for Vancl over the past few years has been "adjustment."

It is said that this new direction of Vancl originated from a drinking session. A report claimed that in mid-June, Chen Nian and Lei Jun drank together. As the evening progressed, Lei Jun suddenly asked, "Chen Nian, are we still brothers? If we are, let's be honest—Vancl's products are not good enough, and Vancl's attitude towards products is not good enough." This greatly impacted Chen Nian, making him feel both ashamed and indignant. It was the first time they discussed such a sharp and sensitive topic.

The report further pointed out that Chen Nian initially felt wronged, believing that after six years in the clothing industry, how could his work be deemed insufficient? Wasn't Lei Jun's success with Xiaomi just due to good luck and the booming smartphone and mobile internet markets? Despite having a higher alcohol tolerance than Lei Jun, Chen Nian ended up drunk.

Chen Nian confirmed that he had communicated extensively with Lei Jun over the past few months. Lei Jun, who is both mentor and friend, as well as an investor, has always paid close attention to Vancl. "I saw a particularly vivid description, including internal meeting records, which contained elements of truth. However, I won't comment on some of the more exaggerated parts," Chen Nian said.

However, Chen Nian admitted there were indeed initial disagreements between himself and Lei Jun, as he couldn't accept Lei Jun's viewpoints due to differing perspectives. Lei Jun thinks in reverse, discussing ideas like eliminating KPIs, profit margins, and organizational structures—radically different from Vancl's traditional management style, causing confusion during their early discussions. Yet, Vancl's existing model was unsustainable.

Chen Nian recalled, "At first, I thought Lei Jun was talking nonsense, but later realized there was truth in his words. For example, when you reach a certain scale, profits naturally emerge instead of setting profit margins upfront."

Ultimately, Chen Nian accepted all of Lei Jun's suggestions: removing layers of management (Chen Nian would no longer be called President but Founder, and the company would have fewer Vice Presidents and Assistant Presidents); eliminating KPIs to allow each division to focus solely on product development; avoiding greed by not producing too many products, and adhering to Lei Jun's seven-character motto: "Focus, Extremes, Reputation, Speed." Chen Nian began to refocus on product development.

In recent months, Chen Nian has stayed in the office, closely reviewing products with various project teams. Just ten minutes before the media interview yesterday evening, Chen Nian was still communicating with a product team. By the time the interview concluded at 9:30 PM, Chen Nian had already moved on to another meeting, planned to continue until the early hours of the morning.

"Primarily, we need to return to the product itself, focusing more on the core. Think about the product from the perspective of a user," Chen Nian said. He and his executive team buy back all Vancl products available on the market to try them daily, reorienting themselves to view products from a user-centric perspective.

This differs from Vancl's previous approach, which was very similar to traditional enterprises. They used to plan based on historical experience, deciding the number of SKUs and categories needed to achieve a certain scale, then dividing tasks accordingly to support that scale.

"When you prioritize sales volume and growth rate, it inevitably forces the product into a secondary position. We're not discussing clothes anymore, but rather the size of the room. Because the room determines how many clothes we should produce. Isn't this mindset flawed? Today, it seems naive, but for a while, that's exactly what we were doing," Chen Nian reflected.

Afterward, Chen Nian internally reflected that Vancl had lost its way for too long. Since 2007, after focusing on dress shirts, they hadn't seriously scrutinized their products again. Even worse, none of their products were fully utilized by themselves—"truly shameful." None of the internet "seven-character principles" were achieved, and they had become too accustomed to excusing their laziness. "Self-defense and complacency towards users" was Vancl's biggest and most fundamental trap.

Now, in the process of learning from Xiaomi's model, Vancl, which once had 19 major categories, has narrowed down to a limited selection of seven or eight categories, including T-shirts, dress shirts, pants, canvas shoes, ultra-light down jackets, and polo shirts.

Vancl has also consolidated its previously scattered systems, clearly defining a unified brand tone by the design team; standardizing material specifications and processes by the technologists and fabric experts; establishing a pattern-making workshop and recruiting a team of pattern makers to ensure product design, materials, and craftsmanship control quality.

This process has also accompanied Vancl's "bloody" phase. Vancl has undergone fierce staff optimization, involving 20% of its employees. For instance, internal controls, which previously required 12 managers for 12 lines, may now only need one manager after integration.

In fact, to demonstrate Vancl's determination to change immediately, it relocated from the southern second ring road to Yizhuang on the outskirts of Beijing. Rent costs dropped from 6 yuan per square meter to 1 yuan, and the relocation date was moved from May next year to September this year.

Inventory Devours Everything

In November 2011, Vancl reached the final stage of its IPO journey. Almost simultaneously, the dangerous symptoms behind its steep growth curve gradually surfaced through various rumors: layoffs began, followed by revelations of staggering inventory levels reaching billions of yuan, massive losses, and senior management departures... Suddenly, everything seemed out of control at Vancl.

That year, excessive expansion caused Vancl's inventory to reach 1.445 billion yuan by the end of 2011, with total losses nearing 600 million yuan, and only completing 3.8 billion yuan out of its initial 10 billion yuan sales target.

Over the following two years, Vancl's status declined. Many e-commerce industry insiders believed that Vancl was an excellent company, and if it hadn't made such serious mistakes in 2011, it would have already gone public and surpassed VIPshop in prominence.

However, history has no "what-ifs." The rapid expansion of Vancl after delegating authority resulted in painful lessons learned.

"The blow in 2011 left us dazed," Chen Nian remarked yesterday. After reaching a 5 billion yuan annual sales scale, Chen Nian increasingly recognized the importance of management and began learning from traditional enterprises.

Scarily, traditional brands also made serious mistakes those years and struggled similarly.

"What troubles everyone most is why companies hit inventory explosion issues around the 5 billion yuan mark. Achieving 10 billion yuan in sales with a 20% inventory means each brand must discard 2 billion yuan worth of goods annually once it hits that threshold," Chen Nian explained. Inventory compression devoured gross margins. From 2007 to 2010, Vancl thrived with very high gross margins because it had no inventory issues.

The 2012 inventory clearance cast a deep shadow over Vancl. At that time, Vancl's inventory was five times what it is today, marking Chen Nian's toughest moment. To clear such a large inventory, Vancl had to sell many products at extremely low prices. An important measure now is to return excess warehousing and logistics facilities.

For a while, Chen Nian was baffled by Vancl's numerous issues. Under Lei Jun's guidance, Chen Nian realized that Apple and Xiaomi provided Vancl with a direction.

"Why can't you buy Apple phones whenever they launch? Is it due to insufficient production capacity? Definitely not. Apple is also afraid of inventory," Chen Nian mused, "At this point, Xiaomi gave me the most crucial inspiration."

Conclusion

After adjustments, Vancl will adopt a "Xiaomi-like transformation" mindset, focusing less on a wide range of products and prioritizing creating market-explosive items. On the product side, the main focus will be on ensuring no inventory buildup while maintaining profitability. Additionally, Vancl plans to drastically reduce its SKU count, returning to simple combinations and achieving the highest quality standards.
