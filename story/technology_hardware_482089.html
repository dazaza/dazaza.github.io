"Thoughts of Home on the Winter Solstice Night in Handan" - A Winter Solstice Poem

by niunai092 on 2011-08-26 19:16:29

Appreciation of Bai Juyi's "Winter Solstice Night in Handan, Missing Home"

In the inn at Handan I meet the winter solstice,

Hugging my knees before the lamp, my shadow keeps me company.

I imagine my family sitting up late at night,

Still talking about the traveler far away.

Explanation of the Title

This poem was written at the end of the 20th year of Zhenyuan (804), when the author was serving as a proofreader in the Secretariat, aged thirty-three. "Handan" is now part of Hebei Province. "Winter Solstice" is one of the twenty-four solar terms in the lunar calendar, roughly equivalent to December 22nd or 23rd in the Gregorian calendar. In the Tang Dynasty, the winter solstice was very important. On this day, the court would take a holiday, and it was even more lively among the common people. Everyone would wear new clothes, exchange food and drinks, offer mutual congratulations, presenting a festive scene. Bai Juyi wrote this poem while he was traveling for official duties, staying overnight in an inn at Handan.

Line-by-Line Interpretation

"In the inn at Handan I meet the winter solstice,"

"Hugging my knees before the lamp, my shadow keeps me company."

The Winter Solstice festival should be joyfully celebrated with family at home. Now far away in the inn at Handan, how will I celebrate? I can only sit hugging my knees before a solitary lamp, accompanied only by my shadow in the quiet night. The first line narrates the experience of celebrating a festival away from home, planting the root of "missing home." The second line, with the words "hugging knees," vividly portrays the posture of sitting idly. The phrase "before the lamp" both creates the atmosphere and points out the "night," naturally leading to "shadow." And the word "accompanying" connects "body" and "shadow," giving the "shadow" human emotion. Both "shadow" and "body" are sitting idly hugging their knees; the feeling of loneliness and longing for home has already overflowed beyond words. "Inn" refers to a post station where ancient officials could rest during their travels.

"I imagine my family sitting up late at night,"

"Still talking about the traveler far away."

On this Winter Solstice festival, because I am far from home, my family must also not be enjoying themselves. When I sit hugging my knees before the lamp, thinking of my family until deep into the night, they probably haven't gone to sleep either. They must be sitting before the lamp, talking about me, this "traveler far away"! The third and fourth lines are deeply moving and intriguing: the scenario imagined by the poet while missing his family is actually how his family misses him. As for what they "say," it leaves room for the reader's imagination. Each person can supplement it based on their own life experiences.

Critique

Bai Juyi's five-character and seven-character quatrains total seven hundred and sixty-five poems, accounting for about twenty-seven percent of all his works. This poem is an early fine piece among them, reflecting the homesickness of a wanderer, with every word revealing deep nostalgia. Its excellence lies in two aspects: First, it uses straightforward and simple language to express a common life experience, with sincere and touching emotions. Second, its conception is ingenious and unique: First, there isn't a single "thinking" character in the poem, yet it conveys "thinking" everywhere in its plain narrative; Second, instead of writing about his own longing for home, he writes from the opposite perspective, akin to Wang Wei's "On the Ninth Day of the Ninth Month Missing My Brothers East of the Mountain" ("From afar I know my brothers have climbed high, but there's one less person wearing dogwood") and Du Fu's "Moonlit Night" ("Tonight the moon shines over Fu Zhou, but she watches alone in her chamber"), achieving similar effects through different means. Song dynasty's Fan Wen commented in "Night Talk Beside the Bed": "Bai Letian's 'I imagine my family sitting up late at night, still talking about the traveler far away' is rather direct, not as good as Wang Jian's 'At home seeing the moon they expect my return, which is exactly when I'm missing home on the road,' which has a twist." This commentary is not entirely accurate. Both have their unique merits, and there's no need to belittle one to exalt the other.
