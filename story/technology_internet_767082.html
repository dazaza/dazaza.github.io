What is "Microsoft Design"?

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 17:53:29

Microsoft, the tech giant with a 38-year history, over $50 billion in annual sales, and more than 90,000 employees (excluding Nokia acquisition), has long been criticized by mainstream media and industry experts for its complacency in the post-PC internet era. With little progress in mobile and online businesses, it often undergoes major internal personnel adjustments at regular intervals. The recent core senior management restructuring was only five years after the last one. Now, people are no longer talking about the colorful flag that once dominated the Seattle skyline, but rather Apple Design without Steve Jobs and Google's efforts to bring Hollywood's stunning sci-fi movies into reality.

Is Microsoft really as outdated as the experts and media have analyzed? And on the other side, have consumers really become indifferent to Microsoft? Today, I will talk about what I know about Microsoft. Luckily, I go to Microsoft several times a year, sometimes just visiting with friends or sometimes on business.

Rebranding

From the moment I saw Windows 8, Microsoft seemed ready for a complete transformation, bidding farewell to the past. From the second version of the Microsoft Windows logo until now, never has any version been as grandly redesigned by the most famous American graphic design company "Pentagram" to redesign the visual brand communication of Windows. This highly perspective new Windows brand design is very appealing. The original four-color flag has been redefined as a geometric shape of one color, giving the impression that it has floated in from nowhere, shaking your senses.

This kind of rare rebranding within a company reflects on those old enterprises that have long shown no improvement and lost competitiveness. Many people do not believe that Microsoft can emerge from such a major change. Its second largest revenue-generating business, Office logo branding, has also been completely updated accordingly. The new generation of Office logos are all designed around one theme style: Windows. Even the latest version of Microsoft's homepage and all its websites have almost entirely adopted the large color block layout design of Windows Modern, achieving a unified style and design. I think Microsoft did a thorough job this time.

The new Office logo design looks more refreshing than all previous versions. However, when it comes to the dozens of components under Office, some logo designs look less than ideal, lacking overall line aesthetics, some looking too hasty, others not dignified enough. Privately, I redesigned a whole new brand identity logo for this star software worth hundreds of millions.

Windows 8 Modern evolved gradually from Windows Phone 7, marking the biggest change in the history of Windows versions. Compared to the most popular past versions like 95, XP, and 7, this change is like night and day, originating from the reorganized revitalization of the mobile smartphone system. This is the result of repeated failures in Microsoft's pursuit of competitors in entertainment consumer software and hardware. However, when compared to the outdated interface designs commonly seen in the industry, regardless of Apple’s transition from Skeuomorphism to Flat Design or Google’s Holo, Microsoft's Modern stood out as soon as it was launched, leaving an impression that phone interfaces could be designed like this.

Inspired by the clear and simple signposts at airports, Modern didn't bring Microsoft substantial market returns in mobile terminals and the new generation of Windows. But as a novel design style, it broke all the old design rules that all manufacturers had to follow when designing mobile user interfaces, such as: App logo sizes must be designed according to certain pixels, always square, and App arrangements must be in groups of four. Considering solely from a "design" perspective, this is Microsoft's greatest contribution.

In addition to innovating in user interfaces, Microsoft also leaves a strong mark in the design of physical industrial products. For example, the Surface tablet computer's rear cover with a foldable stand design, which I mentioned in an earlier article: Microsoft does not pay as much attention to the smoothness between product lines as Apple does. If Surface were designed by Apple, there would absolutely be no slot on the back cover to create a stand for users to manipulate, then combined with a Touch Cover to function as a laptop. This compromise solution represents Microsoft's consistent product design thinking, prioritizing functional use "experience".

Since the 1980s, Microsoft's mouse hardware business has adopted this mindset in designing its Arc series mice. The foldable tail of the Arc Mouse greatly reduces its size for portability, while the folding control of the Arc Touch switches the power on and off. The special design of this mouse lies in its foldable tail made of DuPont's highly resilient POM material, which can withstand tens of thousands of folds and frictions by users. Due to its outstanding material design and comfort, it won the Red Dot 2011 Product Design Award and IDEA 2011 Gold Award.

Among all the mouse manufacturers I have seen, Apple sacrifices functionality purely for "beauty", whereas Microsoft focuses on powerful functional convenience. In the field of mouse design and application, either they are good to use but ugly, or they are beautiful but not practical; the fusion of art and engineering seems difficult to intersect here.

Designs from Science Fiction

Every March, TechFest held at Microsoft's headquarters in Redmond, Seattle gathers the latest research results from the six top-notch research institutes around the world. These six research institutes aim to solve the hardest problems in the computer field and provide long-term reserves for Microsoft's products. I attend every year. Among them, a few Demos in the 2010 edition made me rediscover the magical power of computers.

For instance, Natural User Interfaces with Physiological Sensing interprets the signal exchange between the human body and the computer through sensors (remote sensing technology) on the arm, activating finger gestures to infer spatial and surface reactions in the computer, making the entire body organs hardware devices for the computer, so we don’t need to use a mouse or keyboard to communicate freely and smoothly with the computer. This sounds very similar to what Kinect, Leap Motion, and Google Glass are doing today. Indeed, three years ago, the prototype of the natural human-computer interaction interface researched by Microsoft has been realized by a few companies at the forefront today.

It is worth noting that among all these "wearable devices" that replace all hardware devices through gestures, Kinect was the first real finished product launched. From its initial launch in early November 2010 to the end of the year, the sales volume of Kinect reached an astonishing 8 million units in just two months. The unprecedented response made its sales speed surpass the iPad released by Steve Jobs at the time, earning Kinect a place in the Guinness World Records for that year. In 2011, Microsoft released a Xbox 2010 Year in Review video on its Showcase advertising video website. From this video, you can feel the crazy excitement when Kinect was first launched. It freed the hands in gaming, just like the current hot Google Glass aims to liberate people's phones, which is impressive.

Another memorable demo from the 2010 TechFest was Mobile Surface, consisting of a camera and a miniature projector, forming a powerful computer. Through the projected virtual interface, you can interact with it naturally by placing your hands on it, just like you would on a tablet. What's special about this demo is that it eliminates the traditional display screen, performing tasks entirely in the projected illusionary interface.

Three years later today, this technology is still in the lab, but its potential allows us to foresee a future where your living room walls, tables, and beds will be your PC. You just need a clean surface, plus Kinect gesture recognition, and all the hardware devices we use today: keyboards, mice, displays, etc., will be condensed into a small box the size of a phone, completing all your interactions with the PC today. This is a revolutionary advancement in human-computer interaction. Perhaps the concept of Google Glass has already brought today's people into the future, but I think it is limited to playing an extraordinary role in "sharing" tasks. However, as a direct way for people to see and touch and interact with the PC, the combination of Mobile Surface + Kinect represents the future of PCs.

Another very innovative technology came from last year's TechFest with a "IllumiShare" demo, which allows people in different regions to interact as if they were in the same virtual interface, feeling as if the other person is right in front of them. The highlight of this technology is that it breaks the barriers of traditional PC dialog boxes and allows for a more immersive experience.

Conclusion

In the post-PC era, Microsoft cannot combine engineering and art as beautifully as Apple, nor can it timely grasp various user needs to achieve uniqueness. I deeply admire a sentence from the beginning of Eslinger's "A Fine Line" (Between Art and Commerce):

"Taste" can achieve great success, design and production can become soulmates, one person's vision can shape a product or even a company. Ultimately, the product designed can change the fate of a company and become an indispensable partner in our lives.

Today's Microsoft is also trying to reshape its brand taste, with bright blue Windows, deep red Office, from Windows Phone 7 to Windows 8, from Arc Touch to Kinect motion sensing; on the other hand, some intangible products are quietly acting as industry movers behind the scenes. Microsoft Research is located in the most dazzling technology and education areas around the world, and each new iteration of products lets me feel a little difference in Microsoft.
