Guzang Festival Origins and Development

—— Xijiang Qianhu Miao Village

  No one among the elders in various villages can clearly state the exact year when the Guzang Festival started. It is only known to be passed down from generation to generation. Many elder leaders and Guzang heads recount that they heard from their elders that the Miao people's Guzang Festival originated a long, long time ago when the Miao ancestors, led by Jiang Yang Gong, sacrificed to their mother “Mei Bang Mei Liu” (Butterfly Mother). Legend has it that after Jiang Yang Gong and his wife established a family and had children, every year was blessed with favorable weather and bountiful harvests. However, there were two consecutive years of severe drought, resulting in no grain yield. To find out the reason, Jiang Yang invited a witch doctor. The witch doctor said it was because Jiang Yang's eldest son did not properly manage the water buffalo, which had stepped on Butterfly Mother’s grave several times, angering her. She demanded that Jiang Yang sacrifice a water buffalo to honor her during the New Year. Reluctantly, Jiang Yang followed the order. The following year, indeed, the weather was favorable again, and they had a good harvest. From then on, the Miao ancestors began the annual ancestor worship.

  A few years later, inspired by the ancient practice of making hollow wooden coffins for burials and combined with their frequent relocations and sacrificial needs, since Butterfly Mother was born from the heart of a maple tree, they hollowed out the maple wood into cylindrical drums, symbolizing her soul's dwelling place. They covered either or both ends with animal skin, tightened it, and secured it with bamboo nails. People called this drum the Ancestral Drum, representing the resting place of ancestral spirits. The Ancestral Drum was a sacred instrument that could not be struck casually, except during the ancestor worship festival when it was necessary to awaken the ancestral spirits to enjoy the sacrifices and share joy with descendants. Afterward, it was returned to a specially designated cave for safekeeping. Since it was often kept outdoors, it was prone to damage, so almost every few years, a new one had to be made. Because the Guzang Festival involves awakening the drum, replacing the drum, sacrificing, and storing the drum, it is named the Guzang Festival. As it is held by a drum society unit, it is also called the Drum Society Festival. Due to its purpose of honoring ancestors, it is also referred to as the Ancestor Worship Festival.

  Li Tinggui pointed out in his article "A Brief Discussion on the Social Organization of the Miao People" that the basic unit of the ancient Miao society was the drum society. When the Miao ancestors first held ancestor worship activities, they only worshipped female ancestors, and the maple wood drum used by the drum society merely symbolized the resting place of the female ancestor Butterfly Mother. Wei Qiguang mentioned in his article "On the Origin of the Miao Drum Society - Yi Lang" that this indicates that the Xijiang Guzang Festival (Drum Society Festival) originated in the period of the Miao ancestors' matrilineal clan society, at which time the drum society was an exogamous group.

  Later, the Miao ancestors specifically made another type of wooden drum – the Descendants Drum – for the worship of Jiang Yang Gong and Jiang Yang Nai, hoping for the prosperity and safety of their clans. This drum could also be used for entertainment during the New Year or the Guzang Festival, bringing joy to both ancestors and themselves. After the rituals and entertainment, it could be stored safely in the house, so unless destroyed by fire or war, it could last over a hundred years before needing replacement. People realized from experience that to celebrate the Guzang Festival well, it had to be organized by democratically elected Guzang heads and their assistants.

  Tang Chunfang stated in his article "The Meaning and Origin of the Miao Drum Sacrifice Festival" that the Miao drum sacrifice custom originated in the early matriarchal society, and the wooden drum evolved from a coffin. One of the authors of this article, Li Guozhang, also discussed this in "Miao Wooden Drums," stating that the drum storage custom was already very prevalent in the Tai Shan, Lu Shan, and Yimeng Mountain areas of Shandong during the prehistoric era. However, due to limitations in productivity and calendar civilization, the drum sacrifice customs in the early matriarchal society lacked fixed rules, with diverse forms and free expression. It wasn't until the lunar calendar was established that a more standardized Guzang Festival was formed. The Guzang Festival began with Jiang Yang's sacrifice to Butterfly Mother, which took place in the second month when butterflies start gathering flowers. Nowadays, some Miao villages within Leishan County still celebrate the Guzang Festival in the second month.

  Perhaps because of the annual ancestor worship and celebration of harvest, along with the numerous ceremonies such as awakening the drum, raising the drum, establishing the drum (rotating the drum), sacrificing the drum, sending the drum, and storing the drum, the Guzang Festival gradually changed to a small annual ceremony (Miao New Year) and a grand ceremony every few years (3, 5, 7, 13, 25 years, currently 13 nominal years for Leishan County), continuing until today.

  Later, Chi You, the cultural ancestor of the Miao people, engaged in a decisive battle with the Yellow Emperor near Zhuolu, Hebei Province. Chi You was defeated and killed in mid-October of the lunar calendar. His remains were secretly collected by a Miao clan with a dragon totem and buried in Puyang, Henan Province. Thereafter, Miao people across various regions began celebrating the New Year in October, the month when Chi You was killed, to worship Butterfly Mother, Jiang Yang, Chi You, and their clan ancestors. Most Miao villages in Leishan County still follow the tradition of celebrating the Miao New Year and the Guzang Festival in October according to the lunar calendar.

  After the defeat of the Jiuli tribal alliance led by Chi You, many Miao ancestors had to flee and migrate. Therefore, clan leaders and village elders convened a meeting and decided that after splitting into different clans, each clan would independently hold the festival according to a twelve-year cycle based on the Earthly Branches (Chinese zodiac signs), rather than gathering at the original residence. Some drums were changed from maple wood (black drum) to nanmu or camphor wood (white drum). White represents snow, symbolizing coldness, sorrow, and grief, signifying a mourning sacrifice. The reason is that when Chi You was captured, the Miao people's ancestral tree, the maple incense tree, was intentionally used as shackles by Ying Long to humiliate them. Hence, the Miao people avoided using maple wood for drums and instead used nanmu or camphor wood. This is why later it was said that the Miao drum societies had black drums and white drums, with white drums evolving from black drums.

  Due to the countless long-distance migrations of the Miao ancestors, clans continuously split and divided properties. Different branches and clans lived in different places, leading to slight differences in their use of wooden drums. Some still maintained the Ancestral Drum and Descendants Drum, stored separately in nearby caves and homes; others combined them and stored them in drum towers, drum halls, or the residences of respected village elders; yet others used them both for ancestor worship and as musical accompaniment for dances, such as the current Wuliou Village in Leishan County.

  Approximately since the appearance of bronze drums in the Shang and Zhou dynasties, the Miao people also widely used them during the Guzang Festival. In modern times, some Miao villages even replaced wooden drums with bronze drums, stored in civilian houses. Their functions, besides ancestor worship, also include celebrating harvests, rallying clan unity, and providing entertainment, enriching the content of the Guzang Festival activities and intensifying the atmosphere.

  Nowadays, while holding the Guzang Festival, some Miao villages still follow the procedures of the drum-raising year, drum-rotating year, and drum-sending (hiding) year, but most Miao villages have simplified the three-year cycle to be held in the third year. These various evolutionary processes from simple to complex and back to simple, formed in different historical periods, can all be seen in the Guzang Festival celebrated by Miao villages in Leishan County today.
