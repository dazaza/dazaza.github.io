Dialogue on Snow Treasure Peak: The Yangtze Glacier Rescue Operation

by ditan360 on 2010-08-04 18:49:09

On July 30th, the world's highest-altitude environmental forum was held at Xuebaoding in Sichuan Province. Zhang Ke/Photography

At around 10 o'clock on July 30, 2010, the mountaineering team of the "2010 Yangtze Glacier Rescue Action" ascended to the peak of Xuebaoding, the main peak of Minshan Mountain. Sanlang Ruo'er/Photography

This is a dialogue at the world's highest altitude concerning responses to climate change:

From the base camp at an altitude of 4200 meters at the foot of the main peak of Minshan Mountain, Xuebaoding, to the top of the snow-capped mountain at an altitude of 5588 meters, five climbers and 23 college students from 13 universities both domestically and internationally discussed strategies for addressing climate change via walkie-talkies;

This is also a difficult climb that verifies the fact of glacier melting with blood:

Due to global warming and glacier retreat, the rock ice on Xuebaoding has become very fragile and slippery. During his descent, high-altitude collaborator Sanlang Ruo'er accidentally fell about 70 meters, suffering severe muscle strain in his waist and a fracture of his right index finger.

On July 30th, the forum "2010 Yangtze Glacier Rescue Action - Future of Youth Response to Climate Change," organized by the China Environmental Protection Foundation and Green River, was held at the base camp of Mount Xuebaoding in Songpan County, Sichuan Province. Simultaneously, five climbers ascended Xuebaoding. The journalist personally witnessed this historical moment.

Difficult Climb

At around 1 PM on July 29th, after a 15-hour car ride and a 4-hour hike, the journalist finally arrived at the base camp of Xuebaoding.

This was the journalist's first time seeing Xuebaoding, one of the seven sacred mountains of Tibetan Bon religion, known as "Xia'er Dongri" (Eastern Conch Mountain) by the Tibetan people.

Under the noon sun, Xuebaoding appeared pure and white. Unlike the multiple times the journalist had seen Mount Everest, the glaciers on Xuebaoding were not large, and the lower part of the glacier had broken into blocks. The waist and foot of the mountain were mostly covered with loose rocks. From the base camp, the naked eye could see the exposed black-brown rocks at the summit.

"The changes are really significant." Team leader Luo Rigesi told the journalist. Luo Rigesi couldn't remember how many times he had led teams to climb Xuebaoding. As a witness to glacier melting, he found that in recent years, the glacier area on Xuebaoding has become smaller and thinner.

The snowline of Xuebaoding used to be around 4700 meters, but it has now retreated to around 5200 meters. The C1 camp located on the ridge at an altitude of 5100 meters used to be permanently covered with snow, but now it has turned into a rocky slope.

The ridge leading to the summit is called the "Camel's Back" by climbers. It has a vertical drop of about 7 meters at its highest point, with steep cliffs on both sides, requiring safety ropes to climb.

"It's much harder to climb the 'Camel's Back' compared to before," said Luo Rigesi. After the glacier accelerated its melting, the "Camel's Back" became very steep.

At 5:50 AM on July 30th, while the journalist was still in the sleeping bag, they heard Yang Xin, the planner of the "2010 Yangtze Glacier Rescue Action" and president of the Sichuan Green River Environmental Protection Promotion Association, communicating with Luo Rigesi via walkie-talkie. Luo Rigesi informed Yang Xin that the climbing team had already set off at 5:30 AM, but due to the icy and slippery surface, their progress was very slow, and it was estimated that the summit time would be more than three and a half hours away.

At 7:08 AM, Luo Rigesi reported that they had reached an altitude of 5300 meters. The surrounding scenery was very beautiful, and the team members were changing their crampons. This meant that the snowline of Xuebaoding this year had already retreated to an altitude of 5300 meters. However, through the telescope, the journalist observed a large amount of rocky areas along the climbers' route forward.

The next section was the "Camel's Back." Due to the slippery surface, it took approximately two hours to climb the 100-meter route.

At around 10 AM, the five climbers, including Luo Rigesi, successfully reached the summit. They started descending ten minutes later.

Twenty minutes later, the most worrying event occurred.

High-altitude collaborator Sanlang Ruo'er's foot suddenly stepped on a piece of ice that broke, causing him to fall rapidly down the steep icy rock. His entire body continuously rubbed and collided with the ice layers and rocks. After falling about 70 meters, he was caught by the safety rope, but he landed heavily on the rock wall.

"I've been doing this for over ten years, and this is the first time I've encountered such an incident," said Sanlang Ruo'er, who had climbed Xuebaoding multiple times, sometimes four to five times a year. He told the journalist that mainly the ice was too thin, and there were already numerous ice cracks at the summit. Sanlang Ruo'er liked to call those thin pieces of ice "blue ice" and said, "There is more blue ice at the summit now. Only white ice (solid glaciers) is good for climbing."

During the fall, Sanlang Ruo'er suffered serious muscle strain in his waist and a fracture in his right index finger.

"I estimate that within ten years, climbing Xuebaoding won't require crampons anymore," said Luo Rigesi. Once the rocks beneath the glaciers are exposed or there are numerous ice cracks, the speed of glacier melting will be very fast. This will also increase the danger of climbing.

Dialogue Among Snow Mountains

The "Future of Youth Responding to Climate Change" forum is the third stop of the "2010 Yangtze Glacier Rescue Action." Previously, the "2010 Yangtze Glacier Rescue Action" organized glaciologists and scientific expedition volunteers to conduct the first stop: the Yangtze River source glacier investigation, and the second stop: the Qia'er Mountain ecosystem survey.

Yang Xin told the journalist that the biggest feature of the third stop was that two university students and three high-altitude collaborators were recruited and selected to climb Xuebaoding during the forum period, engaging in dialogue with other students at the base camp via walkie-talkies.

The two students who reached the summit were Yang Gaoge from Chengdu University of Electronic Science and Technology and Xiang Tianyu from Beihang University. The remaining students came from Guangdong University of Foreign Studies and Trade, Tianjin University, Shenzhen University, Southwest University of Political Science and Law, Chengdu University of Information Technology, Southwest University for Nationalities, Wuhan University, etc. Two special students were Sangjie, a sophomore at Minzu University of China who grew up at the foot of Xuebaoding, and Jake Keznick, a student from Carleton College in Minnesota, USA.

At 8 AM on July 30th, the students sat on the ground at the base camp, facing the snow-covered mountains and glaciers, and the forum officially began.

Liang Jing'en and Yao Han from Guangdong University of Foreign Studies and Trade were the first to speak. "Yesterday, we arrived at Nami Village at the foot of Xuebaoding and witnessed how pure the water flowing from the glacier was. We thought of the Pearl River, and we felt a strong contrast," Liang Jing'en said. She grew up near the Pearl River and personally witnessed how clean river water gradually became murky, making people afraid to take water from it and eventually avoiding it altogether.

"What exactly has improved or deteriorated in our quality of life?" asked Yao Han to the students present.

Wen Dan from Southwest University of Political Science and Law originally didn't understand what "water quality shortage" meant. "There's so much water in the rivers, how can there be a shortage?" Later, she realized that it was precisely due to some environmentally unfriendly behaviors, especially the unrestricted discharge of sewage by enterprises along the rivers, that caused severe pollution of the river water, making it impossible to use, even for agricultural irrigation.

At 9 AM, the walkie-talkie transmitted the voice of Yang Gaoge, who was climbing Xuebaoding. "From Nami Village to the base camp, while hiking here, we saw the bridges and streams along the way, the lush grasslands, and the diverse alpine vegetation, all nurtured by the glacier water," said Yang Gaoge.

"It's hard to imagine, if one day the glaciers melt completely and the rivers dry up, how will those lives survive? If the glaciers retreat, and there's no more snow, and Xuebaoding becomes 'Snow Bald Top,' will the charm of climbing remain?" Yang Gaoge kept asking breathlessly.

"I come to Xuebaoding once or twice every year, but each time I find that the glaciers I passed before have retreated significantly," asked Sangjie to the students present. Are the villagers at the foot of Xuebaoding like Nami Village powerless in the face of climate change and glacier melting?

Actually, it's not entirely true. When interviewing Sangjie's father, Keboya, he told the journalist that the villagers of Nami Village care very much about the environment. For many years, the villagers of Nami Village only cut branches and trees broken by falling rocks, and live trees are not allowed to be cut down. Household garbage is not dumped into the river but classified, and non-degradable items are burned outside the yard.

Although everything the villagers do cannot change the process of global warming, at least they haven't exacerbated the deterioration of the local ecological environment.

When Xiang Tianyu reached the summit, he was knocked unconscious by a fist-sized rock. After waking up, he told the classmates via walkie-talkie, "Glaciers are the blood of the high mountains and the pinnacle of biodiversity; when the blood dries up, the high mountains lose their life, and residents in the high mountains and lowland residents alike will lose their last home."

In the evening of July 30th, the five climbers who returned from the descent and the other students, faced with the increasingly retreating snow-capped mountains and glaciers, issued an initiative titled "Practicing Low-Carbon Living, Saving Ancient Glaciers," calling on Chinese youth and every university student to reduce unnecessary consumption, save all energy and resources that can be saved; prioritize green energy, support the development of renewable energy; reduce or even refuse single-use items; enrich oneself with knowledge, influence others with actions; pay attention to global climate change, and care about the future of residents in high-altitude areas.

Melting Yangtze Glaciers

Glaciers are the most important markers of climate change and the most crucial "samples" for studying global climate change. In the process of global warming, only glaciers can display intuitive "data" in the short term.

The first stop of the "2010 Yangtze Glacier Rescue Action," the Yangtze River source glacier investigation, discovered that between 1969 and 2007, the Gangjiaquba Glacier at the source of the Yangtze tributary Garqu retreated more than 4000 meters at its maximum, with an average retreat distance exceeding 2800 meters. Between 1969 and 2010, the northern branch of Jianggudiru Glacier at the source of the main stream of the Yangtze River, Tuotuo River, retreated more than 700 meters, while the southern branch of Jianggudiru Glacier retreated more than 1200 meters.

The Qinghai-Tibet Plateau is hailed as the "Third Pole" of the Earth, featuring the largest area of valley glaciers on Earth. The meltwater from these glaciers has always been the source of major rivers in China, such as the Yangtze River and Yellow River, and is also the most important water source for Southeast Asian and South Asian countries, often referred to as the "Asian Water Tower."

For many years, Yang Xin has been closely monitoring the melting of Yangtze River glaciers. He told reporters, "In the past 30 years, the retreat distance of the main glaciers at the source of the Yangtze River has exceeded 600 meters."

The Yangtze River is the river with the most glaciers in the world. The glacier melting caused by climate change not only affects the water supply of the Yangtze River but also severely impacts the local natural environment, biodiversity, and the livelihoods of indigenous people.

From 2005 to 2009, Green River collaborated with scientists from the Cold and Arid Regions Environmental and Engineering Research Institute of the Chinese Academy of Sciences to implement the "Yangtze River Source Glacier Retreat Monitoring" project. Based on continuous surveys and monitoring data over five years, combined with various sources of information, the basic judgment was made that under the influence of climate warming, glacier melting would affect biodiversity in the upper reaches of the Yangtze River, potentially leading to biodiversity loss. Residents at high altitudes in the source region of the Yangtze River face a series of ecological issues brought about by glacier disappearance and may gradually become ecological refugees.

How important are the glaciers of Xuebaoding for Nami Village and the surrounding environment? Glaciologist Zhang Wenjing from the Chengdu Institute of Mountain Hazards and Environment of the Chinese Academy of Sciences analyzed that the glaciers of Xuebaoding belong to marine-type glaciers. A key characteristic of these glaciers is that their ice temperature is at zero degrees Celsius. In summer, glaciers help lower the temperature in local areas; in winter, when temperatures drop below minus several degrees Celsius, glaciers release their heat, helping to warm the surroundings.

Clearly, the residents affected most by glacier melting are the locals. Liu Yuan, a Ph.D. candidate in anthropology at Minzu University of China, has conducted long-term research in the Three Rivers Source Region. She told reporters that due to long-term adaptation to their survival environment, Tibetan people have grown accustomed to living in environments characterized by glaciers, snow-capped mountains, streams, and grasslands. However, with the dual impact of population growth and rising temperatures, the ecological environment in the Yangtze River source area has gradually deteriorated, with grassland degradation, making life increasingly difficult for herders.

Botanist Tang Ya from Sichuan University, who served as chief scientist for a project at the United Nations Mountain Center and has long studied plants in the Qinghai-Tibet Plateau and its southwestern regions, told reporters that in years with abundant precipitation and relatively low temperatures, precipitation is stored in the form of glaciers in high mountains; in years with reduced precipitation and higher temperatures, more glaciers melt, providing more meltwater. Glaciers act as solid reservoirs that regulate water volume, and places with glaciers are better equipped to cope with droughts.

However, during interviews, the reporter learned that the retreat of Yangtze River glaciers is an irreversible fact, and what people can do is adapt.

A report titled "Vulnerability and Adaptation to Climate Change in the Yangtze River Basin" published earlier by the Institute of Geographic Sciences and Natural Resources Research of the Chinese Academy of Sciences stated that with global warming, glaciers in the Yangtze River source area will continue to retreat, and permafrost will melt faster. In the short term, this will benefit water resource increases. However, in the long term, it will severely reduce the upstream water supply, leading the source area ecosystem toward aridification and desertification. By 2060, the glacier area in the Yangtze River source region will decrease by 11.6% compared to 1970, and the concurrent runoff will increase by 28.5%, with the zero-equilibrium line rising by about 50 meters.

Experts recommend strengthening infrastructure construction within the basin, enhancing economic development levels, and improving the capacity to respond to climate change as measures to adapt. In climate-dominated areas, focus should be on protecting permafrost and biodiversity while reducing human activity interference.

Challenges in Saving Glaciers

Yang Xin was named one of the "2006 Green China Figures of the Year" by the Publicity Department of the Central Committee of the Communist Party of China, the Ministry of Environmental Protection, and six other ministries. He designed the "2010 Yangtze Glacier Rescue Action" which consists of six stages.

Besides the three stages already completed, it includes the fourth stage, planned for September, where domestic and international entrepreneurs, with the help of high-altitude collaborators, will climb Habaxueshan in Yunnan's Shangri-La, following glaciologists for glacier investigations and botanists for plant studies. Finally, participants will gather at the foot of Habaxueshan to participate in the "Corporate Social Responsibility at High Altitudes" forum.

The fifth stage is scheduled for October, hosting the "Chinese Media in Action" investigation, where media representatives from Beijing, Shanghai, Guangdong, and Sichuan will form teams, led by glaciologists and botanists, to traverse the glacial zones under Gongga Mountain via four routes. Through media reports, they will document glacier and environmental changes under the backdrop of global warming.

The sixth stage is the "Sounds of Nature Concert" held in November at Siguniang Mountain. Yang Xin told reporters, "The celestial sounds emanating from the snow-capped mountains, glaciers, and forests will awaken people's inherent desire to admire nature and care for the environment, influencing more individuals to join the low-carbon lifestyle."

"The performers will play music facing the glaciers," Yang Xin envisioned a scene where a child or several children (musicians or music enthusiasts), even dozens of them, face the snow-capped mountains, glaciers, and golden forests to play harmonious natural sounds, expressing themselves to nature.

This is a scene almost without an audience. The music is dedicated to the snow-capped mountains, glaciers, streams, forests, and grasslands, with nature being the listener.

Yang Xin also designed an even more heart-stirring scene:

Led by the main venue of the natural music concert at Siguniang Mountain, musicians in Shenzhen by the sea stand in the water and play music facing the ocean, while students play music facing the mangroves; in Shanghai at the mouth of the Yangtze River, musicians play music at the wetlands of Chongming Island facing the vast East China Sea; in Chongqing beside the Yangtze River, musicians play music facing the surging Yangtze River; in Yueyang by Dongting Lake, musicians play music facing the eight hundred li Dongting Lake... More cities, more music lovers, simultaneously playing the same set of compositions, praying together for the snow-capped mountains, glaciers, rivers, lakes, and oceans.

However, plans are just plans. On the evening of August 1st, at a roadside restaurant in Songpan County, Yang Xin helplessly told the reporter, "It might not be possible to carry out the last three activities." Without much financial sponsorship, the first three activities had already consumed most of Yang Xin's savings. "If it doesn't work out, the last three activities will be postponed until later," he said.

The reporter learned that for many years, Yang Xin has been raising funds in a unique way by publishing books and selling them for charity.

At the end of the last century, tens of thousands of Tibetan antelopes were poached annually in Hoh Xil. In 1997, Green River sold "The Spirit of the Yangtze River" to recruit volunteers and established the first civilian nature conservation station in Hoh Xil - the Sonam Dajie Nature Conservation Station.

Later, Yang Xin published the "China's Yangtze River" picture book with the joint funding of the World Wildlife Fund (WWF), The Nature Conservancy (TNC), the Critical Ecosystem Partnership Fund (CEPF), and American philanthropist Joseph. He sold these picture books to companies, which either kept them or donated them to designated school libraries through Green River.

The "2010 Yangtze Glacier Rescue Action" partially raised funds through the sale of the "China's Yangtze River" picture book. Yang Xin plans to raise more funds to establish the second civilian nature conservation station in China - the Qia'er Mountain Nature Conservation Station - to monitor glacier retreat and vegetation changes and help high-altitude residents adapt to climate change.

"2010 Yangtze Glacier Rescue Action"

In 2010, the China Environmental Protection Foundation, Green River, the Chinese Mountaineering Association, and relevant media organizations, including the First Financial Daily, jointly launched the "2010 Yangtze Glacier Rescue Action." The plan is to conduct specific activities close to snow-capped mountains in six key areas where Yangtze River glaciers are distributed - the source of the Yangtze River, Qia'er Mountain, Xuebaoding, Habaxueshan, Gongga Mountain, and Siguniang Mountain - through climbing, scientific expeditions, crossings, forums, concerts, etc., to showcase the past, present, and future of Yangtze River glaciers on a large scale. This aims to promote scientific investigations of glaciers, public participation in environmental protection, and media attention, reminding the public to pay attention to changes in the ecological environment of the Yangtze River, urging everyone to start from themselves and their surroundings, participating in addressing climate change and slowing global warming through low-carbon actions, preserving more glaciers and snow-capped mountains for the Earth, humanity, and future generations.

"World Forum on the Future"

"World Forum on the Future" is one of the series of projects of the "2010 Yangtze Glacier Rescue Action" and the third station of the entire project. The forum invites 23 student representatives from 13 universities both domestically and internationally to hold a "Youth and the Future of Responding to Climate Change" forum at the Xuebaoding climbing base camp, calling on young people to take responsibility for future climate change. The forum connects with the climbing progress in real-time and interacts with the summit team at the peak. During the forum, glaciologists, ecotourism professionals, and forum representatives jointly investigate the glaciers around Xuebaoding and the impact of glacier melting on local biodiversity, residents' production, and daily life.
