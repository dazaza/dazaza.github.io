Xiangxi cured meat Weibo gets the attention of over ten thousand people, Xiangxi man Shi Dun sells cured meat on Weibo.

by edivista on 2011-12-21 11:17:03

Click to contact Xiangxi cured meat Weibo

I run a Taobao store selling Hunan specialties. If it's not sold within a week, you can contact me to see if I can help. Today's newspaper reported that Shi Dunrang, a man from Xiangxi, made a thousand catties of homemade cured meat for sale in Changsha but met with a cold response. Since then, Shi Dunrang's cured meat has become the focus of our newspaper's news hotline and official microblog. Similar calls and messages have come from all sides.

Tens of thousands of people on Weibo are paying attention to Xiangxi cured meat

Last night, this reporter was the first to release on Weibo the news about the Xiangxi man making a thousand catties of cured meat and worrying about sales channels. In just half an hour, forwards and comments poured in like snowflakes. By this afternoon, the message had been forwarded thousands of times, and tens of thousands of people were paying attention. Can it be mail-ordered? Is it easy to transport? People in Hainan who want to buy Hunan cured meat, sign up quickly. Let's all buy a little! Good stuff! Requesting purchase! Life is not easy! Regarding Shi Dunrang's predicament, readers and netizens are particularly concerned, and most said they were willing to buy cured meat to alleviate Shi Dunrang's worries.

Mr. Hu from Changsha immediately found Shi Dunrang and bought 50 kilograms of cured meat after reading the report in this newspaper. He told reporters that he planned to provide the cured meat he had bought at low prices for customers to eat in his restaurant.

Dear kind-hearted people, thank you

Compared to the liveliness on Weibo, Shi Dunrang's mobile phone was also very busy. From last night until now, my phone hasn't stopped ringing, with many kind-hearted people calling. This afternoon, Shi Dunrang told reporters that he had already received hundreds of calls from kind-hearted people.

Some asked about the situation, some said they would come to take a look, some wanted to buy cured meat, and some consoled him and told him not to worry. And some readers who couldn't get through to Shi Dunrang's mobile phone simply called our newspaper's news hotline to inquire about purchasing cured meat.

Shi Dunrang said that the words of a kind-hearted person from Beijing moved him deeply. This kind-hearted person asked him to persevere for another week, and if the cured meat wasn't sold out after a week, he could come and buy it all.

Besides answering calls, citizens who came to buy cured meat also kept Shi Dunrang very busy. A kind-hearted citizen wanted to buy more than 1000 yuan worth of cured meat. To avoid troubling me, he refused to let me deliver it and drove here himself. According to Shi Dunrang's statistics, by 4 pm today, citizens who came to buy cured meat had already purchased more than 200 catties of his cured meat.

In my hardest time, so many people came to help me. Thousands of words converge into one sentence: Thank you, kind-hearted people. Shi Dunrang repeatedly entrusted the reporter to write an article to thank all the kind-hearted people on his behalf. ■ Reporter Zeng Lili

Reporter's Notes

Shi Dunrang and others still need to make up for their knowledge of the market

From the moment I saw Shi Dunrang, I felt the hardships and difficulties of this man, so I really wanted to help him. The article was published, and I was happy to see that it got a lot of attention, and the sales predicament was quickly resolved, smoothing out Shi Dunrang's furrowed brows.

But amidst the joy, reason also told me that the sudden opening of sales channels was mostly due to the love of citizens and readers pushing it forward. If there were a few more Shi Dunrangs, could we still help?

From helping fruit farmers sell surplus tangerines and bananas, to helping vegetable farmers sell surplus tomatoes and cabbage, in these various cases reported in the media, people can easily find that all agricultural product surpluses are often caused by blind starts without rational market research or finding good sales channels. If the media didn't amplify these individual cases, if kind-hearted people didn't offer help, any effort would be hard to solve these problems.

Of course, helping the weak and solving people's difficulties is our responsibility as media professionals, but if we truly want to prevent Shi Dunrang's case from happening again, we must often make up for our knowledge of the market.
