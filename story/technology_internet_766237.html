The Truth Behind Mobile App Security Vulnerability Incidents

by anonymous on 2013-09-08 20:03:56

Security issues may not be a particularly hot topic for IT professionals in China, but the "first-ever high-risk phone malware vulnerability" recently exposed by 360 has drawn my attention. I just want to reveal the truth behind 360's complex public relations hype.

Event Background: On September 5th, 360 and its CEO Zhou Hongyi widely publicized a mobile security vulnerability on Weibo and WeChat, claiming a "shocking discovery": "China's first-ever high-risk phone malware vulnerability." They also claimed that it could be exploited on a large scale, exposing multiple Android applications from Tencent, Baidu, and Kingsoft as having serious mobile malware vulnerabilities, causing widespread panic. However, the very next day, authoritative institutions exposed similar vulnerabilities in 360's mobile browser and mobile guard, essentially slapping themselves in the face.

So, what exactly happened? After several days of research, I have basically figured out the ins and outs of the matter. Please allow me to break it down for you:

Truth One: This vulnerability originated from Android.

First, this is an Android vulnerability, so all APPs based on the Android system are affected. Since clicking a link to open a malicious website is required to be compromised, mobile browsers are hit hardest. UC, QQ, 360, and猎豹browsers are all affected.

Second, this Android vulnerability actually existed two years ago, not suddenly appearing today.

Experts explain: "This issue can be traced back to a 2011 paper titled 'Attacks on WebView in the Android System' (http://www.cis.syr.edu/~wedu/Research/paper/webview_acsac2011.pdf). This article pointed out some risks brought about by the addJavascriptInterface method, such as if your app implements a class for reading and writing files, and then uses the addJavascriptInterface interface to allow JavaScript calls, attackers might directly call this class to perform read and write file operations. This is the most primitive risk and does not directly point out the exploitation of the getClass() method."

Third, this vulnerability is actually quite difficult to exploit, and its harm is not as alarming as 360 claims.

Experts say, "To perfectly exploit this vulnerability, other factors need to be involved."

Fourth, many manufacturers have gradually fixed this vulnerability, and it has not significantly impacted users.

Truth Two: 360 is taking advantage of the situation for PR hype (very skillfully).

Why did 360 make such a big deal out of such a small matter? In the hands of 360, which excels at PR hype, this became an excellent opportunity. Here's a brief analysis of their hype steps:

Step one, the official Weibo and WeChat accounts of 360 Mobile Guard simultaneously released the news using the typical sensationalist title approach of 360 PR: "China's First-Ever High-Risk Phone Malware Vulnerability Can Be Exploited on a Large Scale." The title is indeed well-crafted.

Step two, all 360 accounts joined in retweeting on Weibo, including 360 Navigation, 360 Browser, 360 Cloud Disk, and even Zhou Hongyi himself. Various Weibo accounts worked together in unison.

Step three, release online news to substantiate the matter.

With this set of moves, the general public might basically believe in 360's expertise and authority in the field of security.

However, unexpectedly, on September 6th, 360's mobile browser and mobile guard were also exposed to have the same vulnerability. Of course, 360 would not mention this. But since they attacked others, others will certainly retaliate. Such self-inflicted damage is truly unnecessary.

Truth Three: The real purpose of 360's PR hype is to expand its market share in mobile applications.

In fact, leveraging security incidents to scare users and maliciously attack competitors is a common PR tactic used by 360. Upon reflection, there are two benefits for 360:

First, by exposing the security deficiencies of competitors, it establishes its own authority in security.

Second, by attacking the shortcomings of competitors like Baidu, Tencent, and Kingsoft in mobile applications, it aims to increase its own market share in mobile applications.

Event Recap:

Qihoo 360 issued a risk warning yesterday. Recently, popular Android applications like WeChat have been exposed to have high-risk "malware vulnerabilities." Just by clicking on a URL sent by a friend or shared in Moments, a phone could automatically execute hacker commands, leading to severe consequences such as installing malicious fee-deducting software, sending fraudulent messages to friends, and theft of contact lists and text messages. A large number of applications, including the Android versions of WeChat, QQ, Tencent Weibo, QQ Browser, Kuaibo, Baidu Browser, and Kingsoft Browser, were exposed to have similar vulnerabilities.

The term "malware vulnerability" originates from "malware attacks." A malware attack refers to when attackers gain control of a website, alter its content, and embed malicious code within the pages. After detecting information about the malware vulnerability, the 360 Security Center analyzed it and found that these vulnerabilities were caused by application developers improperly using Android's WebView development interface. Previously, Google had explicitly warned in the Android developer documentation, "This interface in WebView can cause serious security problems. Avoid using this interface unless you ensure that all content loaded into WebView is written by yourself." Many domestic application developers ignored this security standard, resulting in a large-scale outbreak of this phone malware vulnerability.

According to the analysis of the vulnerability's attack pathways, this phone malware vulnerability mainly affects chat applications and mobile browsers. In chat applications like WeChat, simply clicking on a malicious URL sent by a public account or a friend could lead to compromise. Users should avoid clicking on unfamiliar or suspicious links.
