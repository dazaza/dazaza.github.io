Recommendation of a good-looking novel, "The Fable of Marking the Boat to Seek the Sword," and its investment implications.

by qiurui5493 on 2010-05-27 09:22:12

Forget the price at which you bought the stock." Anthony Bolton, a primary school-educated citizen, should be familiar with the fable of "Waiting for Hares by the Stump" from the "Chajin" section of the "Spring and Autumn Annals of Mr. Lü". The story goes: A man from Chu was crossing a river by boat when he accidentally dropped his sword into the river. He immediately made a mark on the side of the boat and reminded himself: "My sword fell here." After the boat docked, he went down into the water at the marked spot on the boat to search for his sword.

The soaring stock price of Shan Ying Paper Industry has increased the arbitrage space [Choose to sell off? Wait and see? Or enter the market while it's low?]. The dull afternoon market sees an increase in adjustment pressure. The short-term adjustment is roughly established in the mid-afternoon review on June 16th, but the adjustment space will not be too large.

In life, buying a movie ticket (assuming it cannot be refunded) means that regardless of whether you watch the movie or not, the money for the ticket cannot be recovered. This cost is your sunk cost. When you go to the cinema to watch the movie, it might turn out to be terrible. At this point, you have two options: one is to endure the pain of loss and force yourself to finish watching; the other is to leave immediately and do something else. From a rational perspective, we should choose the latter. If we choose the former, continuing to watch not only wastes money but also forces us to endure a boring plot. Choosing the latter, at least we save time.

However, in investment, we sometimes unconsciously inject emotional factors: For example, after purchasing a particular stock, we often think about the initial purchase cost. When deciding whether to sell, we are often constrained by sunk costs, blindly holding onto stocks that continue to decline. Similarly, in the field of fund investment, many investors remember their investment costs and fall into the psychological disturbances of "avoiding regret" and "seeking pride," thus redeeming profitable quality funds too early and holding poorly performing funds for too long, contrary to the goal of asset appreciation.

"To seek a sword like this, isn't it confusing!" The moral of rigid and inflexible investment is easier said than done. We should evaluate investment targets rationally and beware of letting fixed purchase prices dictate our investment thinking!
