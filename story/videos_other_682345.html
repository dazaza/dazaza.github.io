The other end of the broken bridge - an expansion company

by chandadada on 2012-02-28 20:21:10

On the other side of the broken bridge, there might be a person encouraging you, cheering for you loudly, and shouting for you. You must not betray its expectations; you need to bravely leap across. On the other side of the broken bridge, there could be an infinitely beautiful scenery. We rarely have the opportunity to reach such a high place. The broken bridge is set up on a small flat area of the mountain. As we climb the mountain, when we see the scenery on the opposite side, perhaps we will linger a bit longer to satisfy our eyes' desires. But we still need to resolutely jump over it. One end of the broken bridge might be a tall wall. Standing on the broken bridge, the wall fills your entire field of vision, making you feel oppressed and helpless, as if jumping over would mean colliding with the wall. However, we must persist in our goals and courageously leap over it. At one end of the broken bridge, there might also be teammates participating in grabbing the bar. At this point, your competitive mindset kicks in again, and without much thought, you leap across because you want to compete with them to see who completes the task first.

A broken bridge set up in different places can give people different feelings, and the scenery on the other side of the broken bridge can even influence the team members standing on the broken bridge. If one end of the broken bridge has experienced coaches standing there, their patient guidance and direction can greatly reduce the team members' fear of heights. Instead, they may develop a great sense of gratitude and trust towards the coach. Successfully leaping across the broken bridge then becomes no problem. It's the same in our work. When we complete a task alone, if we encounter a huge challenge, what we need in front of us is care, guidance, and encouragement. Only then can we regain confidence in the face of difficulties and firmly overcome them. Otherwise, we may perceive the difficulty as growing larger, leading to the loss of necessary confidence, reducing work efficiency, and causing us to become disheartened. This shows that in the face of difficulties, whether we offer employees help or mental pressure is crucial. While work pressure is naturally important, encouragement and trust within the pressure are the essence of our team leadership.

When you use all your strength to climb an 8-meter-high broken bridge, looking up suddenly reveals that standing at a high altitude allows you to see so far. Why wasn't the view from the office window ever this good? Looking out in the city, all you see are skyscrapers or endless streams of traffic. But on the other side of the broken bridge, the scenery is different. There are no tall buildings or cars, not even their shadows. Even the air seems sweeter. With your nose happy, let your eyes enjoy the blessings too. With such a good view, we should take a little more time to look at it. In the midst of enjoying it, you hear the cheers and applause of your teammates ringing in your ears. At this moment, you suddenly realize that you still need to jump the broken bridge. The expansion training must continue. Slowly, you leap across and return to the ground back and forth.
