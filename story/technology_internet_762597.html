Dianping: A Bone the Giants Can't Chew, Living Outside the Comfort Zone

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 16:17:23

Zhang Tao, CEO of Dianping.com

Written by Yuan Yin, edited by Yang Jing, photographed by Xiao Nan

Zhang Tao, CEO of Dianping.com, has postponed the company's IPO once again.

In 2011, after receiving a $100 million Series C funding round from Sequoia Capital and other institutions, Dianping's valuation reached $1 billion. According to convention, it should have chosen to go public within the following one to two years, but it did not. Instead, in 2012, it raised another $60 million in its Series D round.

Given Dianping's scale and development trend, going public would not be difficult at all. However, Zhang Tao feels that an IPO now would be too costly. He breaks down the big internet (PC + mobile) into four elements: people, information, goods, and services. Each connection point offers an opportunity for giants to emerge. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, and Alibaba connects people with goods. The space between people and services is the seat for the fourth giant. Since 2003, Dianping has been diligently accumulating data, finally waiting for the explosion of mobile internet, where phones merge "location" and "service." According to Zhang Tao's logic, no company seems more fitting than Dianping for the imagination of the fourth giant.

However, the outside world does not think so. In recent months, with the frequent acquisition strategies of BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent), Dianping has become a hypothetical acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition target; regardless of which surname it takes on from BAT, it could help them achieve greatness early. On the other hand, Dianping's size pales in comparison to BAT, and it is still too early to declare it as a future giant. Moreover, whether it's Tencent WeChat's public platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after acquiring Gaode and Meituan, they all directly target Dianping's territory — local life services centered around dining, drinking, entertainment, and leisure.

Before proving the following two things, Dianping going public would be equivalent to "selling at a discount." First, Zhang Tao needs time to prove that what Dianping is doing now cannot be achieved by BAT. Second, Dianping is about to engage in the ultimate battle of group buying. If it secures the top position, its valuation could increase several times over.

2013 is Dianping's "decisive year." Regarding speculation and rumors, Zhang Tao turns a deaf ear. He is very clear: "The internet has indeed entered an era of great integration, but Dianping will not be acquired."

Zhang Tao categorizes companies that might be acquired into three types. The first type has platform attributes but is better suited to develop within a giant's ecosystem, such as maps and email. These products can easily attract a large number of users but struggle to find independent business models. There are also products like browsers that can make money, but the revenue from navigation services is far less than their strategic value to large companies. "Giants are willing to spend money nurturing an email product because it helps with branding and user retention, but I've never heard of any standalone email company succeeding." The second type involves industry consolidation, like in the video industry, where acquisitions expand market share. The third type is when a company encounters financial difficulties and sells itself to survive.

Now, Meituan has already been brought under Alibaba's wing. In theory, Dianping should quickly seek a "backer" to avoid being defeated by a capital-infused competitor. However, Zhang Tao says, "Giants offer us limited help. We do need users, but execution is more important. Mergers and acquisitions would reduce our execution capabilities and risk marginalization by a larger company." Not only that, he aims to keep Dianping at the most precise "distance" from the giants. Getting too close to any one of them would be tantamount to "choosing sides," making Dianping an enemy to the others.

Thus, logically, the acquisition of Dianping has reached a dead end. Zhang Tao counters, "Why should we take their money? We don't lack funds; what we need are resources. If a giant is willing to invest a small stake and provide us with many resources, I might consider it. But would they? Would a giant give you resources if they only hold a small stake?"

Weapon One: Dirty Work, Hard Work

In mobile internet, opportunities for companies focusing solely on pure internet products are becoming fewer. New opportunities come from those that combine with traditional industries. Zhang Tao believes that lifestyle services primarily revolve around dining, drinking, entertainment, and leisure. Other细分 markets like ride-hailing can form good business models, but it's hard for any of them to grow into giants.

For dining, drinking, entertainment, and leisure, user needs are simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is the core of lifestyle services, which is exactly where Dianping started. Whether in content, member accumulation, or brand, later entrants find it hard to surpass Dianping.

The most representative product form of discounts is group buying. Group buying has now entered the championship contention phase, and Dianping plans to face off against Meituan in 2013. This is a winner-takes-all market; as long as there are two strong players, the industry's gross profit margin will be pulled down, and stable profitability cannot be formed.

Another foreseeable future is reservations. Reservations can form a true online-to-offline closed loop, but this must be based on Dianping having absolute advantages in both information and discounts. Otherwise, building a closed loop is difficult. In 2012, Dianping began experimenting with reservations. Zhang Tao said, "Currently, both users and merchants are giving positive feedback, but if we were to focus solely on reservations, it would be hard to solve the chicken-or-egg problem."

Of course, lifestyle services offer some even "heavier" opportunities. For instance, food delivery, which is a rigid demand for users and can help merchants increase sales, has seen companies like Daojia Meishi gain momentum in the past one or two years. However, the core of food delivery is delivery—getting meals delivered within a short time frame while solving issues like soup spills. The logistics requirements are even higher than those of fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang Tao said, "If food delivery is executed well, there's still an opportunity, but it's harder dirty work and labor-intensive. What Dianping is doing now is already dirty and labor-intensive enough."

If judged by age, Dianping is already an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has painstakingly accumulated merchant profiles and user reviews, which Zhang Tao calls "dirty work, hard work." This primitive growth model once limited its speed, but the past pain has become Dianping's core barrier today. It can be said that currently, aside from Meituan, Dianping has no real competitors in the lifestyle services sector.

Nevertheless, he still needs to guard against BAT's "clutches." Baidu's involvement is the most direct. Baidu Maps already has over 100 million users, and users can use the map to search for nearby catering and other lifestyle services. Tencent, considered to have secured a ticket to mobile internet, launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest physical goods trading platform, and expanding goods to services is natural. Besides, it has also included Meituan and Gaode in its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there's no sign of explosive growth in terms of reality and data," Zhang Tao said. Brand positioning is crucial; the key is which product users turn to first when they want to dine, drink, entertain, or relax.

Besides, in the vast field of lifestyle services, competition among giants will inevitably cross over, and no one can completely monopolize the domain. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained on Weibo and WeChat. Zhang Tao feels that this makes Dianping safer instead. "In segmented fields like car rental and business travel, if giants redirect their massive user base, they might flood out existing companies."

As for Alibaba, which invested in Gaode, it wouldn't be easy for them to invade Dianping's territory either. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, which can be categorized no matter how peculiar. A user's dining experience depends on various factors such as the day's environment, waitstaff, and chefs. Managing merchants cannot rely solely on systems and tools like Alibaba uses; it must involve a 'ground force' managed by people.

"All three giants want to follow this path, and there's room for imagination, but this is not a highway; it's a rugged mountain road." Zhang Tao believes that first, doing lifestyle services requires ground teams deployed at various levels, which BAT lacks the genes and capability to manage offline teams. Second, they lack the intention; lifestyle services require dedicated effort, not something a small team can accomplish, and earning money is slow. "It's hard for a company to excel at two important things simultaneously, let alone consolidating their advantages in their original domains."

Weapon Two: Small Boats Are Easier to Maneuver

When Dianping raised $100 million in 2011, Zhang Tao told the media that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, times change fast, and before Dianping went public, his analogies fell from grace.

He wasn't particularly anxious about it. "They didn't become big companies because they lacked offline teams; now they want to be the American version of Dianping."

The current path is something Zhang Tao has gradually explored step by step. When he felt that selling keyword ads and coupons was outdated, the emergence of group buying caught his eye. Initially, like the Americans, he viewed group buying as a marketing platform—one deal per day—but as orders increased, he realized group buying had transactional attributes. Thus, he saw Dianping's future as a lifestyle service trading platform, and the two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were used precisely for Dianping's transformation from an information platform to a trading platform.

Without this "genetic modification," Zhang Tao might not have had the confidence to compete for the fourth major seat today. But genetic modification sounds simple, yet in reality, it's equivalent to Dianping's second entrepreneurship, a process extremely painful.

First, compared to selling advertisements separately, a trading platform lowers the gross margin and requires stricter cost control and efficiency. Second, a trading platform demands higher cross-department communication skills.

Previously, to maintain Dianping's impartiality and independence as an information platform, the content team and the sales team were entirely separate. Zhang Tao even deliberately established a "firewall" between departments—no matter how important the client, the platform's information couldn't be modified for sales purposes, which once led to mutual hostility between the content and sales teams. Now, as a trading platform, the editorial and sales teams need deep communication. Achieving a good marketing plan requires not just planning but also editor cooperation. He said, "Maintaining information quality is still very important, but there's no need to reinforce animosity anymore; we need to dismantle the firewall."

Though editors can logically accept this, individual behavior and values have inertia, and when combined across the entire company, the inertia becomes even greater. Zhang Tao specially held several meetings to encourage more departmental communication. After a few months, an editorial manager casually told a sales manager, "I used to think you were my enemy, but now I see you as a colleague."

The sales department also needed genetic modification. To promote conversion, Zhang Tao cleverly gave the offline team a dual identity. First, from the merchant's perspective, salespeople should become marketing consultants—not just selling products but helping merchants devise marketing plans. Second, from the company's perspective, marketing consultants are the company's buyers. Dianping is like a supermarket; not only must the product quality be good, but the variety of goods must also be rich. Marketing consultants should aim to help users purchase group-buying products. Of course, dual identities will inevitably lead to a series of complex issues. Frontline staff find this quite challenging as they have to balance the scales themselves.

Substantially speaking, Dianping's "dual identity" is very similar to Alibaba's division into 25 business units. By decentralizing decision-making power to frontline employees, the company gains faster response speeds but increases management difficulty. Likewise, compared to frontline employees, Dianping's management team suffers more during the genetic modification process.

Every year, Zhang Tao breaks down Dianping's company goals into various departments, then senior executives further break them down layer by layer. However, breaking down goals isn't a simple arithmetic operation. Li Jing, co-founder of Dianping responsible for the sales team, sometimes finds that unmet merchant demands stem from sales issues but affect performance. Therefore, Li Jing created a new position, setting up communication specialists who collect information daily from marketing consultants, submit it to other departments, and track feedback. He often tells his team, "Being good at embracing change doesn't mean liking change. No one likes change; everyone feels uncomfortable when encountering change. All employees must go through a non-comfort zone, including myself."

The numbers reflect the "heaviness" of Dianping's execution team. Out of over 3000 employees, more than 2000 work offline. The ground force is responsible for direct communication with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group buying, and bundled marketing solutions.

For this core team that can resist BAT's clutches, Dianping has a unique management evaluation system.

They are not called "sales"; from the first day, their business cards bear the title "Marketing Planning Consultant." Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the era of watching news and chatting on QQ, lagging at least three to five years behind Taobao merchants in understanding the internet. Marketing planning consultants need to tell merchants what online users need and how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing said, "Even for a high-end restaurant, marketing consultants wouldn't suggest offering abalone or shark fin group buys, possibly replacing them with other dishes because online users and offline users have different demands."

An outstanding marketing consultant must be a versatile player. They need to explain to restaurant owners and hair salon owners the essential differences between discounts and group buying and which discount aligns better with their positioning. If facing a hot pot restaurant owner, the marketing consultant should know whether it's the off-season or peak season, which products have rising costs in this season, and which have decreasing costs, proposing a group-buy menu that both merchants and users like.

This sets high requirements for marketing consultants—they must not only be internet marketing experts but also industry experts. Li Jing said, "I must admit, it's impossible for every consultant to be a double expert, so we rank consultants by stars." Similar to hotels, marketing consultants range from one star to five stars, with the best ones called Super Stars.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline employees are not tech-savvy graduates from prestigious universities, making it challenging for them to understand internet products. Dianping built a knowledge base for marketing consultants, containing knowledge points and related cases, accessible to both new and experienced employees. If they encounter new problems, employees can ask questions within the knowledge base, and thousands of consultants nationwide will help answer them.

Most of the knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees based on practical experience. Dianping set up a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, rewarding employees who help others the most each quarter. Each quarter, Dianping conducts skill assessments for employees, linking results directly to promotions.

Compared to internet knowledge, industry knowledge is harder to obtain. Some experiences are summarized by traditional merchants after decades of practice in the industry. A Super Star marketing consultant told Li Jing that the hairdressing industry should avoid offering wash-cut-blow package deals because men lack motivation to buy, and women prefer blow-drying without washing. Li Jing was astonished, "We men would never think of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing consultants are divided not only by city but also by industries such as dining, hairdressing, and leisure. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, dining is further subdivided into hot pot, Western cuisine, and buffets, with one marketing consultant responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing consultants can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, making merchants less resistant when selling products," he said.

Each marketing group holds its own case-sharing sessions: what difficulties a certain type of merchant encounters, what marketing needs they have, what solutions the company provides, and what effects they bring. Outstanding cases are reported upward, and Dianping officially shares one case each week.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition called "Entrepreneur Winner." Within the first three months of joining, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products but listening to merchants share experiences. If they are responsible for hot pot restaurants, after three months, they must submit a business plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hot Pot Restaurant Owner." Like graduation, employees present their defense live, and industry supervisors evaluate the winners, with the company providing certain rewards.

Strategically speaking, whether it's Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, their tasks are quite similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O are familiar to anyone with a basic understanding of the industry, and their spoken words sound alike. "The era when success relied on storytelling and creating concepts has passed. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model lies in management and execution, not strategy," Zhang Tao said.
