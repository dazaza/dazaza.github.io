Interview "makeover" skills

by bbeij62s on 2009-12-08 16:05:30

   Job-seeking Image Design -- Handing out a Beautiful Business Card

When job hunting, using certain techniques to fully display one's knowledge, abilities, and skills is an art. To achieve this goal, it is necessary to study clothing styles, pay attention to detail embellishments, appropriately design one’s image, and practice speech and answers. Three experts have given specific explanations to job seekers from their respective fields of study.

Body Language

Case: Xiao Wang walks into the interview site, sits down opposite the examiner, leans back on the chair, crosses his legs, folds his hands in front of his chest, and stares at the examiner with a nervous look.

Comments:

The body language unintentionally displayed by job seekers during interviews can be crucial for the success or failure of the interview. Sometimes, even a single glance or gesture can affect the overall score. Here are some suggestions that job seekers can use to practice:

Eye Contact: Eyes are the windows to the soul. Appropriate eye contact can reflect intelligence, confidence, and enthusiasm for the company. Correct eye expression should be: politely looking directly at the other person, but avoid staring too long as it may give a sense of being overly aggressive; eyes can move every three seconds, focusing on the examiner's nose-eye triangle area (social zone); eyes should be calm and focused without appearing rigid, and do not let nervousness cause your gaze to wander. Avoid sidelong glances, downward or upward looks, and certainly avoid wandering, distracted, or even flirtatious gazes.

Gestures: Some job seekers, due to nervousness, don't know where to put their hands, while others get overly excited and wave their hands around when talking. Neither of these behaviors is acceptable. Don't have too many small movements, as this is a sign of immaturity. It's also important to avoid scratching ears, rubbing shoulders, or patting the other person's shoulder to appear friendly.

Posture: Good posture is one of the key factors in leaving a good impression on the interviewer. The body can be slightly relaxed, otherwise, it will appear stiff. When sitting on a chair, it's best to sit only 2/3 of the way back, without leaning against the backrest. Keep the upper body straight to appear energetic. Women should keep their legs together, and the body can be slightly tilted, avoiding shaking legs or crossing arms in front of the chest.

Clothing and Accessories

Case: Xiao Zhang, with dyed hair, walked into the interview site wearing casual clothes, with various little bells hanging on her backpack and shiny accessories around her neck and wrists.

Comment (Professor Xu Jiahua from Shanghai Theatre Academy):

Clothing and accessories are the first impression left on the interview examiner by the job seeker. A well-dressed appearance and hairstyle design can add points to job hunting. At the same time, appropriate attire can increase confidence, thereby performing better in subsequent interviews.

Clothing: The key to choosing clothing is based on the job requirements. For positions in banking, government departments, or secretarial roles, clothing tends to be more traditional and formal; for public relations or fashion magazines, you can add some trendy elements to your clothing, showing your ability to capture fashion information. The most important thing in personal grooming is cleanliness and tidiness. Do not overly emphasize individuality unless applying for jobs in entertainment, film, or advertising industries. Too striking clothing choices should be avoided. For recent graduates, a slightly student-like appearance is acceptable. Even when interviewing for big companies, casual suits can be worn. Compared to formal suits, they offer more freedom in fabric, shoes, and color combinations. Note that too many accessories should be avoided during interviews, as they can distract the examiner and sometimes leave an immature impression.

Makeup: Women should avoid heavy makeup, and men should avoid exaggerated tattoos. Makeup should be natural and leave no obvious traces, weakening individuality and emphasizing commonality. Women can use thin and transparent foundation to create a healthy complexion, light-colored lipstick to enhance natural beauty, brown eyebrow pencils to adjust eyebrows, and mascara to make eyes more spirited. Men can use some cleaning cosmetics to give a clean and sunny impression. Be especially cautious with perfume usage. Avoid strong or strange-smelling perfumes; a faint fragrance can easily make others feel pleasant.

Hairstyle: Hairstyles should not only match face shapes but also coordinate with age, body type, personality, clothing, and job requirements to reflect overall aesthetics. First, avoid exaggerated or strange hair colors. Men should avoid long hair or bald heads. Secondly, hairstyles should be correctly matched with clothing. For example, if wearing a suit, it's best to tie up the hair, which appears more energetic. Thirdly, skillfully use visual illusions to change face shapes. For example, people with long faces can have longer bangs and try to make the sides of the hair fluffy so that the long face doesn't appear too obvious. People with short necks can choose neat and short hair to visually lengthen the neck. People with round or square faces generally do not suit ear-length haircuts or center-parted hairstyles. Instead, they should increase the volume on top of the head to make the forehead part appear fuller and reduce the width of the lower part of the face in terms of visual effects. Finally, depending on different professions, hairstyles should vary accordingly. For example, when applying for a flight attendant position, tied-up hair is more suitable. Art-related work has broader requirements for hairstyles, allowing for slight coloring or longer hair for males. Regardless of what hairstyle is designed or combed, hair hygiene should always be maintained.
