In the 1960s, it was pointed out that Hong Kong had a good education system but lacked non-degree education as an important means in academic work. At that time, outdoor training companies had not yet developed or been established in Hong Kong. People's training was always classroom-style education, making it difficult to achieve the short-term training effects of modern outdoor training companies because people cannot sit in the classroom all day listening to the teacher's lectures. Some people could maintain a listening state throughout the day, but it was still hard to achieve true experiential effects. In summary, Hong Kong’s educational model lacked comprehensive educational development. Soon after, the earliest outdoor experiential training institution in Asia appeared in Hong Kong. Several years later, Beijing outdoor training companies also emerged, further promoting and strengthening the development of Beijing outdoor training globally.

Hong Kong's first experiential training company was established in such an educational environment in 1970, initially as a mode of heart-oriented education focusing on four aspects: promoting communication, accepting responsibility, building confidence, and boosting self-esteem. It was seen as a representative of short-term and intensive training institutions, guiding young people through transitional periods in their lives until they found jobs and successfully integrated into their work positions. The world's first outdoor training company was called Outward Bound, established in 1941, originating from naval warfare, as a training method to develop experience and skills for young sailors. The term "outdoor training" has since evolved through global institutional recommendations, with the aim of helping people discover themselves spiritually, learn to take care of themselves, and make them care about the people around them.
