"Three Smiles" - A Group Crossover Without Punchlines

Guo Degang makes movies...

Such two seemingly unrelated and unreliable things actually have a day of intersection.

I am definitely not a "mainstream ideology supporter of crosstalk".

But I must admit that countless sleepless nights were made less boring by Mr. Guo's crosstalk, which damages others without benefiting himself, making the audience laugh.

But Guo Degang making movies?

And using the material of "Tang Bohu Points at Qiu Xiang", which has been fantasized about by countless literati for nearly half a century?

Mr. Guo is bound to face a group of movie enthusiasts who appear solemn or passionate, pouring cold water on him...

In today's entertainment industry where "crossing boundaries" has become a trend,

It's not surprising that Guo Degang, who has never faded from the public eye and always refers to himself as a "non-famous crosstalk actor", has crossed over.

Starting a blog, recording songs, acting, situational dramas, hosting, clothing stores, "Guo Family Cuisine",

Regardless of whether he is famous or not, mainstream or not,

Mr. Guo's "money-making" ability is extraordinary, like being in an empty field.

I've also had the honor of reading Mr. Guo's blog.

Between laughter and scolding, it's hard to hide the occasional bursts of thought.

In his writing, he deeply captures the essence of traditional Chinese literature.

After all, whether elegant or vulgar, crosstalk is a traditional art form steeped in five thousand years of Chinese civilization.

For Mr. Guo, who has strived for this art until now, even without the increasingly devalued "diploma" as proof,

His personal intellectual realm will not be too bad.

Guo Degang as a host brings his crosstalk skills of jesting to a certain extreme.

His intimate and popular "Beijing rogue" demeanor resonates with the audience.

Even if some programs escape accusations of "low-level interest, vulgar boredom,"

To a large extent, it stems from mistakes made by professionals in the pre-production planning, mid-production filming, and post-production editing.

As a host, Guo Degang may not be outstanding, but at least he is qualified - perhaps better effects could be achieved if he didn't dress like a quilt cover...

And repeatedly making "startling appearances" in movies - whether as a photographer in "The Founding of a Republic" or a certain character in "Mars Is Alright,"

Mr. Guo's hearty appearance and innate sense of humor can make the audience laugh.

Thus, in the genius director Lau Kar-leung's "Time and Tide," Guo Degang was given an important role alongside Stephen Chow, Sun Li, and other beloved actors of Director Lau.

That shoulder-baring, long-haired Cao Cao surely evokes more laughter than the overused "silly sister" and "creepy man" images.

All these are positive examples of Guo Degang's successful "crossing boundaries."

However...

Some people are naturally expressive, so they can become actors.

Some people are naturally likable and in control, so they can become hosts.

But the field of "movies," although never lacking in talent, even if you are talented, you may still be overlooked.

What about a crosstalk artist? (Note: I mean no disrespect to crosstalk artists, just discussing the matter at hand.)

Guo Degang's one-sided "enthusiasm" for movies, and his lack of control over crosstalk,

Has created an odd hybrid called "Three Smiles: Talented Scholars and Beautiful Ladies."

Regardless of the grandiose "change of female leads,"

Fan Bingbing and Yao Di both count as top beauties, the former taking the international muse route, the latter awkwardly interpreting the new "Spicy Sister."

Comparatively, the increasingly picky "international style" might not want to use a "silly Qiu Xiang" to disrupt the carefully cultivated "actorial" image.

A scheduling conflict, perhaps, was a blessing in disguise for her.

Yao Di, though a new face, is refreshingly adorable, foolishly pure, and endearingly silly.

In front of a weak plot, her "Qiu Xiang," who wasn't fully developed by the film, still has a certain three-dimensionality.

Her infatuation, devotion, and bashful innocence are portrayed satisfactorily.

Moreover... her counterpart Tang Bohu is... Guo Degang!

Mid-aged Guo Degang seems unaware of the vast difference between himself and the legendary "handsome and talented" Tang Bohu.

Of course, descriptions like "handsome as Pan An" are overly idealistic.

Creating a personalized version of Tang Bohu is not wrong per se.

But with Zhou Xingchi's version setting a high standard, the influence of classic models is hard to surpass.

Moreover, Zhou Xingchi ingeniously portrays Tang Bohu as a man skilled in both literature and martial arts, known for his womanizing but actually unlucky in love, making him a world-class good man.

This inevitably appeals to women of all classes who lack security, are emotionally irrational, and have overflowing maternal instincts.

Zhou Xingchi also uses segments like timed painting and couplet competitions to exaggerate yet appropriately display the talents of this top-ranked "talented scholar."

In contrast, Guo Degang's version of Tang Bohu, bald head, wrinkles, bulky muscles, short stature,

This appearance might win laughs on stage, but on the big screen, it fails to justify the ticket price.

One cannot help but feel sorry for Ms. Yao.

As for showcasing the literary talents of Scholar Tang, perhaps only the seven-character quatrain at the end of the movie, hinting at "I Love Qiu Xiang," manages to sound decent.

The opening ancient punk concert featuring Tang Yin clearly borrowed from "River East Lion Roar."

However, Guo Degang's singing style remains that of the太平歌词 pronunciation method, indelibly marked by the quyi tune.

The so-called "impressionist" painting style ultimately proves too impressionistic; the pond fish scene offers more comedy than the stunning effect of Zhou Xingchi's "Spring Trees Autumn Frost Picture."

As for the chaotic couplets and exaggerated "guess what I'm doing" game, they leave viewers utterly confused.

One cannot help but lament the beautiful face of Ms. Gao Yuanyuan...

And "Before my bed a pool of light—Is frost descending from the air? I lift my eyes to look at the bright moon, I lower my head and think of my lunchbox."

OMG, Mr. Guo, can you only mock this one poem by Tang Bai?

Halfway through the movie, I couldn't help but wonder if it was impossible to showcase Tang Bohu's extensive knowledge of poetry,

So the great scholar was pitifully made to bump his head, lose his memory, and call it a day.

Let's take a look at the plot. The story framework of "Three Smiles" appears to be hastily pieced together.

Nine Thousand Years Old, real estate, night pearls, pomegranates, and the thoughts of the maid and the butler...

All these unrelated storylines are patched together, bursting forth with stiff jokes unexpectedly.

Throughout the movie, even the basic cause, development, climax, and conclusion are poorly handled.

Even if efforts are made to fill in elements that create atmosphere,

The lack of structure dooms the movie's completeness, wasting the guest stars Mr. Guo invited through his connections...

Speaking of actors, one must mention the forced performances by the popular figures of Deyun Society.

Guo Degang, despite his age, tries to act innocent and naive, romancing actresses of his granddaughter's generation.

Even with all the "must-kill" scenes of watching the moon and setting off fireworks from romantic dramas, the visual impact still doesn't feel pleasant.

Li Jing and He Yunwei, one a top student from Beijing University of Science and Technology, the other hosting a program at Beijing TV with cool humor,

Unfortunately, the movie gives them too few lines, and in their limited roles, they mostly pretend to be crazy.

Even if they bring out their signature fast clapper skills, the two brothers from the Hua family remain tragically extras.

Typical forgettable characters, incomparable to the "successive" brothers in Zhou Xingchi's version of Tang Bohu.

I can't help but shout "wasted talent" for these two.

Fortunately, both Li and He are either Guo's junior or disciple, participating in the movie purely out of brotherhood, without involving any interests.

If they don't take it seriously, why should we nitpick?

However, there are some bright spots in the movie...

Firstly, Yu Qian's cross-dressing as Madam Hua, a role eagerly anticipated, arrives halfway through the movie.

Audiences marvel at Yu Qian's dignified appearance, suitable for a wealthy family's madam (and someone who enjoys life).

Mr. Yu is indeed broad-minded, not only being innocently teased in crosstalk (I know that's artistic need, three parts joke seven parts support~),

But also sacrificing himself for art by cross-dressing, showing the deep bond between the golden duo Guo and Yu (may they be happy...)

Also noteworthy is Cao Yunjin's portrayal of Chief Steward Hua, young and handsome among the relatively abstract-looking members of Deyun Society.

Moreover, Cao Yunjin's personality most resembles Master Guo, excelling in comedic antics with a "new generation pushing the old" momentum.

Compared to others' overt acting, Cao Yunjin understands the "self-forgetfulness" required in front of the camera, barely reaching the level of "acting" (not a common feat).

Alas, flaws overshadow merits, judged by movie standards, "Three Smiles" still has too many shortcomings...

Guo Degang turned the movie into a star-studded group crosstalk performance.

But, it lacks even the punchlines of crosstalk, rushing the performers on stage.

Well, well, making people laugh is still good.

Just a word of advice to Mr. Guo: your crosstalk should return to the theater, television isn't a suitable medium for crosstalk, and neither is the movie.

You're better off staying in the theater...
