China Mobile suspected of hijacking mobile browsers, affecting WeChat

by geekzhang on 2013-05-03 14:41:01

Yesterday, well-known self-media person Pan Yuefei exposed a picture. The picture showed that when some netizens were reading his article using the WeChat embedded browser, a reminder of remaining mobile data and advertising information popped up above the article. The upper end of the window was marked with "Mobile 139 Navigation - Data...". It was suspected that the WeChat embedded browser had been hijacked by China Mobile. Upon investigation, it was found that the netizen used Hangzhou Mobile's network. This netizen said that according to his understanding, similar cases of WeChat browser hijacking also occurred in Jinhua, Zhejiang.

Subsequently, Pan Yuefei initiated a survey on his personal WeChat public account (panyuefei2013). Three Hangzhou-area netizens responded that they had encountered this situation, and advertisements from China Mobile also appeared when they used their browsers to surf the Internet. Pan Yuefei himself also said that he personally encountered the same situation when surfing the Internet using his browser.

Netizens who are familiar with related technologies pointed out that this is China Mobile's hijacking of mobile browsers, using the very mature http unpacking and adding advertising technology on PCs. After searching through search engines, we found that there are already quite a number of netizens online who have reported that their mobile browsers have been hijacked by Mobile 139 Navigation.

At this year's 315 evening party, CCTV once exposed the providers of PC-end networks hijacking browsers. CCTV reporters discovered that some broadband operators in Sichuan, Shanghai, and other places were forcibly pushing pop-up ads to users.

However, some users tested using China Mobile's network in Beijing but did not find any related issues. For this, Sohu IT communicated and exchanged with middle-level management personnel of local branches of the three major telecommunications operators. According to employees of Unicom and Telecom, although telecom operators are very sensitive to WeChat, many of them also want to hijack WeChat, but it cannot be done technically. Because the technical support management of operators is very strict, group approval is required for allocation, and the process is very cumbersome, not controllable by individuals.

On the contrary, employees of China Mobile complained bitterly that there was no need for them to do so. Remaining data reminders can be implemented, which is beneficial to users, but the group has not yet required a unified implementation. If external links are added for traffic increase, it will bring no benefit to them. Because the more external links, the greater the traffic, the more inter-network settlement between China Mobile and Telecom Unicom, the bigger the bill they pay for Tencent. If it is purely for advertising information, this income does not offset the inter-network settlement fee. Therefore, if this phenomenon is true, it is entirely possible that it was done by third-party traffic companies or advertising companies, which is actually the same issue as the PC ad implantation exposed by CCTV at the 315 evening party.
