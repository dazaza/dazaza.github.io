How lawyers can play a bigger role in medical dispute cases

by lyg on 2009-11-25 11:02:42

Kangxin Law Firm, Shanghai

Lu Yiguang

At the end of 2008, I was invited to attend the Huangpu Lawyer Forum at Mount Sheshan. The following are my personal views, and I welcome any valuable comments.

I mainly want to exchange ideas with everyone on the following aspects: First, the current status of medical disputes in our country and the historical evolution of the medical dispute resolution system; Second, focusing on introducing the case handling process for general medical dispute cases, combined with specific cases, discussing what role lawyers can play in these processes and what legal assistance we can provide to the parties involved; Next, I will talk about the difficulties commonly encountered in medical dispute cases and hope to hear your experiences and valuable opinions; If time permits, I also want to introduce several special types of medical dispute cases.

1. Current Status of Medical Disputes in China and Historical Evolution of the Medical Dispute Resolution System

(1) Current Status of Medical Disputes in China

Medical disputes refer to situations occurring in healthcare, preventive health care, medical aesthetics, and other legally qualified medical enterprises or institutions where one (or multiple) party believes that another (or multiple) party has made a mistake in providing medical services or fulfilling their legal and contractual obligations, causing actual harm consequences, and should bear liability for breach of contract or tort liability. However, both (or multiple) parties have different understandings of the disputed facts or differ in compensation amounts, leading to mutual arguments and each holding their own views.

In fact, wherever there is a doctor-patient relationship, there are medical disputes. This happens in all countries. A few days ago, I read in the newspaper about an old man in the UK who was diagnosed by the hospital with advanced liver cancer and was expected to live only six more months. As a result, this old man distributed his life savings of 18,000 pounds to his friends and family, selected a top-notch urn for himself, and pre-paid the funeral home and crematorium fees. After half a year had passed, he received an apology letter from the hospital stating that the diagnosis of advanced liver cancer was incorrect. By this point, the old man had nothing left and decided to sue the hospital for compensation.

This is not a joke; misdiagnosis and mistreatment almost occur everywhere in the world. "On average, every Chinese doctor may encounter two medical disputes per year, which is much less compared to American doctors (15 per year). On one hand, this indicates that Chinese doctors are currently in a relatively stable environment, but on the other hand, it also shows that the tension in doctor-patient relationships cannot be overlooked." At the recently held 2008 Hospital Management Cadre Legal Knowledge Training Course organized by the China Hospital Association, Judge Lin Wenxue of the Supreme People's Court Research Office drew the above conclusion based on a set of data statistics from his research.

According to calculations, around 13 million medical disputes occur nationwide each year. In 2006, the total number of outpatient visits nationwide was 2.4 billion, so the proportion of medical disputes occurring was approximately 0.5%. If allocated according to the number of medical personnel nationwide at the end of 2007, which was 6.65 million, each doctor might encounter two medical disputes per year on average. While the overall situation remains stable, since 2002, serious incidents of medical disputes have occasionally occurred, attracting high social attention and having significant social impacts.

Based on an analysis of the total number of medical dispute cases accepted by courts nationwide, from 2002 to the present, the number of cases filed under the category of medical accidents has remained stable at 9,000 to 10,000 cases per year. Including cases filed under other categories, the total is between 45,000 to 50,000 cases.

(2) Historical Evolution of the Medical Dispute Resolution System

First Stage (1950-1959): Extensive use of criminal law led to fear among medical personnel.

Second Stage (1959-1977): Administrative determination and handling were primarily used, especially during the Cultural Revolution.

Third Stage (1978-2002): The "Methods for Handling Medical Accidents" was issued, resolving medical disputes through administrative means combined with judicial avenues.

Fourth Stage (2002 to Present): The "Regulations on Handling Medical Accidents" was issued, reducing administrative handling gradually while more cases were resolved through judicial channels.

However, during the implementation of the "Regulations on Handling Medical Accidents," some problems have gradually emerged, such as overly simple analysis opinions from medical associations and the absence of death compensation items. Therefore, calls for revising the "Regulations on Handling Medical Accidents" have also grown louder.

2. Practical Issues for Lawyers in Medical Dispute Cases

Here, I focus on introducing matters that lawyers should pay attention to when representing patients in medical malpractice compensation dispute cases.

(1) Pre-litigation Preparation

1. Consultation and Reception: Due to the unique characteristics of medical dispute cases, such as strong professionalism and high risk, these issues need to be fully communicated with the parties during consultation and reception. When unable to grasp medical issues, it is recommended to hire expert assistants.

An expert assistant refers to individuals with specialized knowledge or experience in science, technology, or other professional knowledge areas. Based on the hiring by the parties and court approval, they assist in explaining or commenting on specialized issues related to the disputed case facts in court. In medical cases, expert assistants are individuals with corresponding medical knowledge who analyze whether specific medical actions contain errors, whether the patient's damage is causally related to medical actions, and explain professional medical terms, diagnostic norms, and conventions.

2. Evidence Preservation: Includes copying and sealing medical records as well as preserving physical evidence. According to relevant laws, patients have the right to copy and seal medical record materials. It is important to note that when sealing medical records, both ends of the sealed bag should be affixed with seals, and a sealing certificate should be requested from the medical institution. Additionally, suspected adverse reactions caused by intravenous fluids, blood transfusions, or medications should be sealed promptly.

3. Autopsy: The main purpose of autopsy is to determine the cause of death. The autopsy process can involve forensic experts, and the deceased’s family can send representatives to observe the autopsy process.

4. Case Analysis: Focus on the qualifications of hospitals and medical personnel, the authenticity of medical records, the causes of patient damages, whether medical actions comply with diagnostic norms, and the legality of drugs and medical devices. Expert assistants significantly help in analyzing the case.

(2) Litigation Process in Medical Cases

1. Evidence Exchange

During the evidence exchange stage, the attorney should pay attention to verifying the qualifications of physicians, the completeness, authenticity, and legality of medical records.

2. Possible Appraisals in Medical Cases

(1) Medical Accident Technical Appraisal: Established according to the provisions of the "Regulations on Handling Medical Accidents." According to the latest "General Rules for Judicial Appraisal Procedures," appraisals commissioned by the court to clarify case facts are considered judicial appraisals. Therefore, our previous distinction between medical negligence and causation appraisals as judicial appraisals, while excluding medical accident technical appraisals conducted by medical associations from being called judicial appraisals, now seems outdated and needs revision. Medical accident technical appraisals are entrusted to medical associations, and in many regions like Shanghai and Jiangsu, cross-regional medical association appraisals are implemented to ensure objectivity and fairness, avoiding biases from familiar individuals.

(2) Medical Negligence and Causation Appraisal (hereinafter referred to as Medical Negligence Appraisal): In current judicial practice, medical negligence appraisals receive much praise. Reasons include: the appraisal institution does not belong to the health administration system but rather to the judicial administration system; medical negligence appraisals only assess whether medical actions contain negligence and whether there is a causal relationship with damages, without determining whether it constitutes a medical accident; appraisal documents provide detailed analysis opinions and thorough reasoning.

(3) Disability Appraisal: This appraisal is usually proposed by the patient, and the appraisal institution only evaluates the damage suffered by the patient without analyzing causality, making the scope of appraisal worthy of attention (related to medical disputes).

(4) “Three Periods” Appraisal: Appraising nutrition, nursing, and rest periods.

(5) Document Appraisal: Generally appraising whether medical documents have been tampered with, requiring considerable appraisal knowledge from the proposing party. Types of appraisals include text formation time, print formation time, handwriting appraisal, etc.

(6) Sealed Bag Appraisal: The purpose is to determine if the sealed medical records have been opened after sealing.

(7) Drug Quality Testing: Primarily targeting suspected counterfeit or substandard drugs causing damage.

(8) Medical Device Quality Testing: Targeting the quality of medical devices.

(9) Autopsy: Conducting autopsies on deceased individuals, mainly to identify the cause of death.

Additionally, there are some rarely used appraisals. It is worth noting that any type of appraisal is essentially a form of expert testimony. How to apply for an appraisal? Who to choose for the appraisal? How to conduct the appraisal? And how to challenge the appraisal afterward? Are all questions worth considering.

3. Key Points in Medical Accident Technical Appraisals

(1) Writing Statements and Points of Contention

According to the current design of the medical accident technical appraisal system, after receiving the appraisal commission, the medical association will require both parties to provide points of contention and statements, which are sent to the appraisal experts before the appraisal meeting. Therefore, clearly defined points of contention are the first step in helping parties obtain satisfactory appraisal conclusions.

(2) Drawing Lots

Many people do not take this procedure seriously or only care whether a forensic expert participates. In reality, drawing lots is a crucial part of the appraisal process, significantly influencing the appraisal conclusion.

The first thing to note during the drawing process is what discipline of experts to draw for the appraisal. Normally, within the legal framework, the medical association gives a suggestion. For example, if an appendectomy went wrong, the medical association would arrange for general surgeons to conduct the appraisal.

However, many cases actually involve multiple disciplines. For instance, a patient diagnosed with thyroid adenoma underwent a major thyroidectomy, after which hoarseness was discovered. The pathology report indicated thyroid enlargement with nodular formation. In this case, if an appraisal is conducted, the medical association might arrange for general surgeons to conduct the appraisal. There is a problem here: hoarseness is due to damage to the recurrent laryngeal nerve, so having general surgeons conduct the appraisal is fine. But, is thyroid enlargement best treated surgically? Ideally, an endocrinologist should participate in the appraisal.

Also, even for common surgeries like appendectomies performed by general surgeons, consideration should be given to whether other disciplines should participate. For example, in a case where a patient developed kidney failure after an appendectomy, the selection process should include not only general surgeons but also nephrologists.

Since specialties differ, the perspectives and recognition levels of appraisal experts vary. Without selecting appropriate experts, the appraisal conclusion could be very different.

The medical association also pays attention to the opinions of the parties. If the parties raise objections regarding the category of experts during the drawing process, the medical association will halt the drawing and submit the party's opinion to the medical association's work conference for discussion before proceeding with arrangements.

After selecting the specialty, the next step is determining the primary discipline, which is self-evidently important. The more specialists involved in the controversial focal point, the more thorough the discussion will be, preventing "one-man rule."

After determining the types of disciplines and the number of people in each discipline, comes the issue of disqualification, which I will not elaborate on but is still a matter worth paying attention to.

(3) Matters to Note During Medical Appraisal Meetings

There are currently two misconceptions in medical cases: the first misconception is that the patient side thinks they should proceed with the appraisal first, then file a lawsuit if it is determined to be a medical accident (or negligence); the second misconception is that appraisals are the experts' responsibility, and just attending and explaining the situation is enough. In reality, the core of medical cases lies in the appraisal, determining the success of litigation. If successful, it also determines the thoroughness of the victory. Therefore, not taking the appraisal seriously can have very serious consequences.

We have already introduced expert assistants above. I believe that hiring expert assistants to participate in appraisal meetings is greatly beneficial for resolving medical disputes and safeguarding one's legitimate rights and interests. During the appraisal meeting, it is important to note that appraisal experts are not legal professionals, so the speech at the appraisal meeting differs greatly from courtroom debates. If participating in the appraisal meeting is metaphorically described as a war, it can be divided into three strategies: the lower strategy involves narrating the factual process and letting the experts judge whether there was medical negligence; the middle strategy involves targeted questioning for the appraisal experts to answer; the upper strategy involves analyzing problems and persuading appraisal experts to accept and form appraisal documents; it is important to remind that appraisal experts are neutral, neutrality includes passivity, precisely because of this reason, one should not expect appraisal experts to become your agents. It is unrealistic to thoroughly review thick medical records within one or two hours, so the parties must actively and proactively participate in the appraisal.

Understanding the medical knowledge involved in the case is the foundation and prerequisite for participating in appraisal meetings. Besides that, one must combine the actual situation of appraisals in China and return to an old saying: all appraisals are merely expert testimonies. How to provide information to experts? What information to provide? How to help experts ascertain facts and make appraisal conclusions? These are all very worthy topics for discussion.

(4) Bearing of Appraisal Fees

Please note Article 34 of the "Regulations on Handling Medical Accidents," which states that medical accident technical appraisals can charge appraisal fees. If determined to be a medical accident, the appraisal fees are paid by the medical institution; if not a medical accident, the appraisal fees are paid by the party that applied for medical accident handling.

4. Key Points in Court Hearings

(1) Selection of Cause of Action

As mentioned earlier, today's focus is on medical malpractice compensation dispute cases. That is, apart from medical malpractice compensation disputes, there are other causes of action. According to the "Provisions on Civil Causes of Action" officially implemented on April 1, 2008, causes of action involving medical dispute cases also include medical service contract disputes, product quality damage compensation disputes, radioactive pollution tort disputes, etc.

(2) Application of Evidence Rules

Everyone knows that medical tort cases apply "burden of proof reversal." I recommend that lawyer friends gain more understanding of "burden of proof reversal." In fact, not all the burden of proof is borne by the hospital; the patient side has the preliminary burden of proof. Moreover, in the application of evidence rules, the burden of proof can also shift.

(3) Submission and Cross-examination of Evidence

In medical dispute cases, the cross-examination of appraisal conclusions should be a key focus, mainly concerning issues related to the degree of responsibility, disability level, and medical dependency in the appraisal report.

We have already discussed expert assistants. During the cross-examination of appraisal conclusions, expert assistants can provide more professional cross-examinations of medical appraisals, which will provide professional assistance to improve the quality of the trial. One of the main reasons judges overly rely on appraisal conclusions is that if they deny the appraisal conclusions themselves, they will bear enormous professional risks and easily be criticized as "laymen judging experts," abusing power, and disrespecting science. Without expert assistants, even if appraisal experts appear in court, the appraisal conclusions cannot be effectively cross-examined, making it difficult to achieve ideal trial results. If the expert assistant system is introduced, as experts with medical knowledge, they can carefully study the appraisal process and conclusions. Under the guidance of the judge, they can question the appraisal experts to restore the process of forming the appraisal and the facts and materials relied upon to reach the appraisal conclusion, enhancing the transparency of the appraisal. This makes it clear to the judge and both parties whether the materials submitted for appraisal are appropriate, whether the appraisal procedures are legal, and whether the facts and theories relied upon for the appraisal are reliable.

Some special types of evidence, such as witnesses and audio/video recordings, also play a significant role in many cases.

(4) Court Debate: After the factual investigation concludes, the court debate begins. Medical dispute cases are highly specialized and have significant social impact. The agent lawyer should fully realize this and pay attention to grasping the trial atmosphere, guiding the parties to a more rational level.

3. Common Confusions in the Judicial Practice of Medical Dispute Cases

(1) Dual Legal Application

Different compensation bases, mainly reflected in the death compensation item. My view is that death compensation should apply in medical malpractice compensation disputes. Below is an excerpt from a judgment document on the China Court Network for your reference ([2006] She Min Yi Chu Zi No. 889, Civil Judgment of the Sheyang County People's Court, Jiangsu Province, in the case of Plaintiff Yang vs. Defendant Sheyang County Hospital over medical accident damage compensation):

The compensation items and standards stipulated in the "Regulations on Handling Medical Accidents" and the "General Principles of Civil Law" are inconsistent: the former has fewer compensation items than the latter, with the typical missing item being death compensation. The direct consequence of this dual legal application phenomenon is often that the results of the two types of medical dispute cases are obviously unfair: medical actions constituting medical accidents, with higher degrees of hospital fault, result in smaller compensation amounts; medical actions not constituting medical accidents, with lighter degrees of hospital fault, result in larger compensation amounts. Such unfair treatment deviates from the principle of similar handling for similar cases, deviates from the standard of severe punishment for severe faults and light punishment for minor faults, and deviates from the socialist rule of law concept emphasizing "fairness and justice." It also reduces the public acceptance and social credibility of judgments in medical dispute cases. Courts cannot convincingly explain to the public and society the legality and rationality of heavier punishments for minor faults and lighter punishments for severe faults in the two types of medical dispute cases. Courts should not make excuses like "this is legal but unreasonable." Court proceedings should neither be arbitrary nor mechanical but should reflect the essence of the law and meet the public's demand for fair treatment. A distinguished judge once pointed out: "An excellent judge should correctly grasp the value pursuits preset by the legal system and integrate their understanding of the value of law into the interpretation of law to make fair judgments consistent with the spirit of the law. However, quite a number of law enforcers in our current stage lack a high level of legal value cultivation, resulting in frequent deviations from the value spirit of the law when implementing specific legal systems and norms. When this situation occurs, although they feel there may be some problem and the implementation of the law contradicts the logic of life, due to their lack of legal value cultivation, they cannot clearly recognize the existence of the problem. Thus, they lament: 'Legal but Unreasonable!' Some even boast of being strict law enforcers." "What is legal should be reasonable, depending on how judges understand the law and grasp its value. Applying legal norms to handle specific cases is not a rigid and mechanical process but a creative activity. Truly understanding and grasping the spirit and value of legislation can... correctly interpret and apply the law, making fair judgments that conform to both law and reason."

2. Basic Conclusion of Theoretical Analysis: Medical malpractice compensation dispute cases caused by medical accidents, especially the "death compensation" item in such cases, applying the "General Principles of Civil Law" and relevant judicial interpretations, conforms to legal principles.

(1) The legal application of the two types of medical dispute cases should be unified, which is a trend and has become a consensus among all sectors. Road traffic accident compensation cases and non-road traffic accident compensation cases also long existed with inconsistent legal applications, which were satisfactorily resolved with the issuance of the Supreme Court's "Interpretation on Several Issues Concerning the Application of Law in Personal Injury Compensation Cases." This should serve as a beneficial reference for resolving inconsistent legal applications in the two types of medical dispute cases.

(2) The "General Principles of Civil Law" is basic law, while the "Regulations on Handling Medical Accidents" is an administrative regulation, whose effectiveness is lower than that of the law...

(3) Whether or not there is death compensation is a typical difference in the dual legal application of the two types of medical dispute cases. According to the Supreme Court's "Interpretation on Several Issues Concerning the Determination of Civil Tort Liability for Mental Damage Compensation" issued on March 8, 2001, mental damage consolation money includes death compensation. The "Regulations on Handling Medical Accidents" did not list death compensation separately based on this.
