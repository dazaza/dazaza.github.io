Foxconn insider: It's common for a mobile phone to have one more or one less component.

by geekzhang on 2013-05-03 14:19:31

Apple's Return Policy Puts Foxconn in CrisisThe yield rate for the iPhone5 agreement was 90%, but it had previously hovered around 70%Foxconn, which saw a 19.21% decline in Q1 performance due to Apple's declining sales, has once again been dealt a heavy blow by its close partner. There are reports that recently Apple returned on a large scale the iPhone5 units that did not meet appearance and functionality requirements, demanding that Foxconn rework them. Although Foxconn officials have not admitted to a large-scale rework, it is worth noting that this is not the first time Foxconn has clashed with Apple over quality issues.Evidently, the continuous quality problems with the iPhone5 highlight the serious challenges facing Foxconn's contract manufacturing model.This news is trueFoxconn's denial casts doubt over this return crisis.On April 22, regarding the news that Apple required Foxconn to rework 5 million iPhone5 units, Foxconn's official statement was that it involved customer confidentiality and would not comment. Hon Hai Precision's spokesperson, Hsing Chih-ping, also stated externally that the news about Apple requiring rework had not been verified and thus would not be commented upon, although he firmly denied the circulated numbers.It is worth noting that neither Foxconn nor Hon Hai completely denied the news of the iPhone5 rework; instead, Hsing Chih-ping's emphasis on the numbers makes things more subtle.During an interview, a Foxconn insider confirmed to the reporter the news that Apple required a large-scale rework of the iPhone5. Indeed, Apple did return a batch of iPhone5 units around March 15th to Foxconn for rework. This news is true. He mentioned that according to his knowledge, the Zhengzhou factory had already started reworking, and employees within the park were discussing this matter. However, he also expressed uncertainty about the figure of 5 million units.He explained to the reporter that these phones were returned after leaving the factory due to issues with their appearance and functionality. The iPhone5 is difficult to manufacture, and mass production as demanded by customers often results in paint peeling or parts being installed incorrectly.What makes the outside world particularly concerned about the return event is the impact it has on Foxconn's slim contract manufacturing profits. It is reported that the labor cost for reworking a single iPhone5 is around 200 yuan.In response, an investment professional familiar with the electronics industry joked to the reporter: "Reworking means using the original parts to assemble a new phone, and you have to spend extra effort disassembling the phone." He indicated that based on data he had in the past, an iPhone takes nearly 17 hours on the assembly line. In the context of Foxconn Technology Group Chairman Terry Gou's high-profile announcement last year to raise ordinary workers' wages in mainland China to 4400 yuan, he stated that 200 yuan is definitely insufficient now, but Foxconn's investment in robotics is likely to reduce some costs.In fact, this isn't the first time Foxconn has suffered from Apple's actions. Reports suggest that in August 2010, Foxconn failed to meet Apple's quality requirements, resulting in an 800 million yuan compensation to Apple. However, this piece of news from three years ago has not been confirmed by Foxconn.The Woes of Low Yield RatesThe conflict between Foxconn and Apple appears on the surface to be Apple's strict demands due to the complex craftsmanship of the iPhone5, but in reality, the root cause lies in Foxconn's long-standing problem of low yield rates.Reports indicate that Terry Gou visited the Guanlan factory in Shenzhen on April 16th due to the return issue and proposed a twelve-word policy: improving yield rates, specialized training, and boosting morale. To demonstrate his emphasis on yield rate issues, Terry Gou even set up an office in Guanlan and stated that he wouldn't leave until the yield rate met the requirements.The aforementioned Foxconn insider confirmed this news. He revealed that Mr. Gou came specifically for the yield rate issue and visited factories in Shenzhen, Zhengzhou, and other locations. Mr. Gou was very upset and scolded all the executives. Now, there is a major overhaul across all parks within the next month.Low yield rates not only make it difficult for Foxconn to explain itself to Apple but also significantly impact its own operations.According to the above-mentioned Foxconn insider, the agreed-upon yield rate for iPhone5 between Foxconn and Apple is 90%, but it has consistently hovered around 70%. Any part exceeding the agreement will undoubtedly be borne by Mr. Gou.The low yield rate of the iPhone5 undoubtedly exacerbates Foxconn's already thin contract manufacturing profits. The insider said: if one part on the iPhone5 production line fails, the entire production line must stop. Every day of operation is a day of loss. He mentioned that due to the low yield rate of the iPhone5, many production lines have ceased operations, with the lowest operational rate being approximately 15%.Besides, the tension among employees caused by low yield rates further troubles Terry Gou.According to the journalist's understanding, before leaving the factory, the iPhone5 undergoes internal quality checks by Foxconn’s quality inspection department. Due to Apple's stringent requirements for the iPhone5, Foxconn has intensified its internal inspections, leading to conflicts between the quality inspection department and frontline production workers like explosives sealed in a can ready to explode at any moment.Last September and October, large-scale strikes occurred in the Taiyuan and Zhengzhou plants producing the iPhone5. Reports suggest the reason was the overly stringent quality control standards of Foxconn's quality inspection department and dissatisfaction among employees with being forced to work overtime to meet iPhone5 deadlines.Therefore, Terry Gou's efforts to improve yield rates are no mere words. Apart from replacing the general manager responsible for iPhone orders in the iDPBG business group, Zhong Yi-hua, starting from early April, Foxconn began comprehensive shutdowns for rectification in Shenzhen, Zhengzhou, and Taiyuan plants. Under the personal supervision of Terry Gou stationed in the park, the aforementioned insider stated that the Guanlan plant in Shenzhen began small-scale mass production from mid-April, with the yield rate now reaching 90%, but whether the Zhengzhou plant has reached the same level remains uncertain.Contract Manufacturing Model Faces ChallengesImproving the yield rate is undoubtedly good news for Foxconn. However, it is worth noting that the frequent quality issues with the iPhone5 and the imminent conflicts between the quality inspection department and frontline employees clearly indicate that Foxconn's large-scale assembly line contract manufacturing model is encountering severe challenges.Labor shortages are Foxconn's eternal theme. After the series of jumpings due to intense labor conditions, Foxconn significantly reduced overtime work. Facing massive orders, apart from deploying robots, Foxconn had no choice but to recruit a large number of new workers.Frequent turnover and lack of training have resulted in a shortage of skilled workers on Foxconn's production lines. This is undoubtedly another reason for the low yield rate, aside from the complexity of the iPhone5's process. Many newcomers receive just a few days of training before directly assembling the iPhone5. This obviously leads to problems. The aforementioned investment person told the reporter. But apparently, Foxconn doesn't have time to take care of so much; the flood of orders during peak production seasons dictates that they must continue production even if it's substandard.Moreover, relying heavily on Apple orders in a few large business groups like iDPBG undoubtedly causes Foxconn to suffer from 'Apple dependency syndrome.' According to the journalist's understanding, Apple orders already account for 60%-70% of Foxconn's business, making Apple Foxconn's largest single client.However, while Foxconn depends on Apple, Apple evidently cannot do without Foxconn either. When asked whether Apple might reduce iPhone5 orders due to Foxconn's low yield rate, the aforementioned Foxconn insider laughed and said, "How could that happen? When only one supplier can produce the iPhone5, how could Apple hand it over to someone else?" This view clearly aligns with what Terry Gou previously said: "Why does Jobs give me the Apple order? Because only I, Foxconn, can do it."Clearly, despite the severe challenges faced by Foxconn's contract manufacturing model, in the short term, no one can replace Foxconn's stable position in Apple's supply chain. According to the insider, Foxconn is currently preparing fully for the production of Apple's next-generation products.
