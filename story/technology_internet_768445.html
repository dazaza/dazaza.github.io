Andy Jesse, the head of Amazon cloud services: Success comes from continuous innovation

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 18:26:40

Amazon AWS Business Director Andy Jassy

　　It is undeniable that cloud computing has impacted the entire enterprise IT industry. In this field, no one does it better than Amazon.

　　Amazon's Amazon Web Service (AWS) cloud service has now become a giant in the cloud computing sector. Other tech companies like IBM and HP are also developing their own cloud services, but they all seem to be imitating AWS.

　　Amazon will hold its re:Invent conference next week in Las Vegas, where customers will have the opportunity to learn about the latest features of AWS services. Amazon AWS business director Andy Jassy will deliver the first keynote speech at the conference. AllThingsD had the privilege of interviewing him, and here are the main points of the interview:

　　Q: We already know that AWS is a big part of Amazon's business, but we don't know exactly how big it is. You're hosting an important conference next week, can you give us a sneak peek?

　　A: I'm really looking forward to the re:Invent conference next week, it's the second edition this year. Unlike other conferences, the re:Invent conference isn't promotional. It focuses more on sharing information and education, which is why last year's re:Invent conference was so popular.

　　We have 175 sessions scheduled for this year's re:Invent conference, 40% of which will be led by customers or partners. We sincerely hope that attendees will gain a lot of knowledge about AWS, such as what the service is, how it works, its architecture, and case studies from other customers. They can acquire the necessary knowledge to use and develop applications on this platform during the conference.

　　I want to emphasize two key themes: innovation and flexibility. The poor macroeconomic conditions have made many companies feel helpless. We've heard that many employees feel lost when they walk into the office because the company doesn't allow them to try and innovate. There are too many barriers between ideas within most companies today. If the cost of trying to innovate is too high, then those attempts need approval from many committees, project planning may take weeks, and these companies find it difficult to form a true culture of innovation.

　　The flexibility you get from cloud computing will change all of that. With AWS, you can try out new ideas simply and cheaply. If the attempt fails, it won't cause much damage. This is fantastic because it eliminates employees' fear of failure, which is usually one of the major factors that kill innovation.

　　In fact, our customers tell us that their organizations keep generating new ideas, and employees are excited to be able to try out innovations on behalf of clients.

　　I will also talk about hybrid IT architectures at the re:Invent conference, which is very different from some conservative IT vendors. Those conservative IT vendors believe that companies want all software to run locally, preferably behind a firewall, and only occasionally use cloud services when needed. But we think that there are very few companies that can afford to have their own data centers, and most computing tasks should still be done in the cloud.

　　Q: It seems that you release new services every few days, and your pace is so fast that others find it hard to catch up. Can you talk about the latest services AWS has released?

　　A: The latest service might be Mobile Push. It's part of our notification service. It supports mobile devices, and customers send millions of messages every day.

　　You're right, we release a large number of new services every year. Let me give you some specific numbers: in 2010, we released 61 products and services that we considered very important, 82 in 2011, 159 in 2012, and from the beginning of this year to the end of September, we have already released 179 important products and services, and I believe we can exceed 200 by the end of the year.

　　Another latest service is Cloud HSM hardware security modules. These are tamper-proof devices where customers typically store highly sensitive keys. If someone unauthorized tries to access it and fails multiple times, it automatically shuts down, and you can no longer use it or open it.

　　Two of our customers wanted to keep their HSMs in our data center. We didn't want to do that initially, but after hearing that this was a very common service requirement, we decided to develop a service around this requirement.

　　Q: You recently won the CIA's cloud computing contract. This came as a surprise to me because most people think the CIA is very conservative in using IT services. Why would they accept a public cloud service? What does having a customer like the CIA mean for Amazon?

　　A: I must carefully consider my answers to these questions. The CIA and intelligence agencies are trying to change the way they use computing services. They want a platform that can quickly improve productivity, scale at any time, and have a wide range of services available. They eventually chose us, and we were certainly happy and grateful. They were looking for a very stable and feature-rich infrastructure platform.

　　Q: Can you talk about the progress of GovCloud, which is the cloud computing service for government departments? Before Amazon launched it, former U.S. Chief Information Officer Vivek Kundra urged the government to adopt cloud computing services from a cost-saving perspective. Does AWS have other government customers?

　　A: Of course. Around 600 government departments worldwide are using AWS services. The U.S. Navy has stored non-confidential information on AWS and reduced costs by half. NASA JPL uses AWS services for the Mars Exploration Rover. The rover takes pictures on the surface of Mars and sends them back to JPL for processing, then decides the next action plan. All of this work is done on AWS. During Obama's presidential campaign, he also used AWS, building 200 applications over 18 months. On election day, they set up a call center and built a database to understand the status of the campaign and the voting situation of other candidates.

　　Q: For the rest of this year, what are your priorities? And for next year?

　　A: There are many. I think you'll see us continue to add new features to almost every service. We will increase more instance types in our EC2 service. We have a lot of cross-regional features. I think you'll see us continue to expand into other regions. We currently have nine large service regions globally, and that's not all; we will add more service regions in the future. Customers tell us their needs, and we've added a lot to our roadmap. I think you'll see more services that make it easier and cheaper for customers to use various services.

　　Q: IBM recently acquired SoftLayer, attempting to expand its cloud services. How is the competitive landscape in this area recently?

　　A: Every major company has been trying and developing cloud computing services, and AWS has been developing for at least 7.5 years. We wish they started doing this from the beginning. We expect many companies to join in. When we ask customers what they value most about AWS, it's usually local solutions. Now there are a lot of discussions and comparisons about cloud services versus local services in the market. We let them try running workloads in the cloud, and once they try it, they get excited and want to move more work to the cloud. We often see this happen.

　　We always thought that the cloud computing field would have many successful players. Look at AWS, we provide infrastructure, software, hardware, and various data center services. If we estimate the size of this market, I guess it's probably several trillion dollars. A market this large cannot have just one service provider succeed.

　　Q: Have you noticed that customers are becoming increasingly trusting of cloud computing services?

　　A: I think the adoption rate of cloud computing services is much higher than it was a year ago. If we compare our current development in the enterprise market and the public customer market with four years ago, it's a world apart. From the adoption rate of AWS, it's still in the early stages of development. Customers find that it has been running for more than seven years and has a certain level of stability.

　　When we first launched the AWS cloud service platform, it had very limited functionality compared to now. That's also the problem competitors face when entering this market segment; they are now in the same position we were in four or five years ago.

　　We have been rapidly updating and evolving. Take our data warehouse service Red Shift as an example, we launched it in February and added about 20 new features within eight months. Rapid innovation not only helps you solve more load problems but also makes enterprise customers more confident that you will be a good partner. If we cannot provide all the features they want, we will quickly add new ones.

　　Q: How did you achieve all of this in such a short period of time?

　　A: That's a great question. I don't know if the answer I give is the best one. I think it relates to many things, including the talent we hire. We love to recruit builders. I mean, they are both entrepreneurs and innovative thinkers. They believe that the launch of a product or service is just the beginning.

　　We have fostered a corporate culture of continuous innovation. Some companies say "no" to new ideas. But at Amazon, especially in the AWS service department, the leadership team is always trying out new ideas and saying "yes" to them. This innovation-focused corporate culture has a huge appeal to our team; it encourages people to come up with new ideas to help customers.
