[Repost] Summary of Etiquette Points in Business Negotiations

by koucai4472 on 2012-02-27 17:11:36

Negotiation is, at this point in time, a form of communication that is greatly needed. It is also a relatively special way of communicating. Through negotiation, both parties can achieve a situation of mutual benefit and win-win. Many people have the perception that negotiation means one party is at a disadvantage while the other takes full advantage of timing and circumstances to press the other side into negotiating. In fact, this understanding is wrong. To put it simply, negotiation isn't about which side has all the advantages; rather, it's about achieving a more beneficial outcome. This method promotes mutual benefit. So, what should we pay attention to in business negotiations? Besides eloquence, what else should you be mindful of? In terms of verbal etiquette techniques in business negotiations, there are mainly the following key points. Here, the Elocution Training Network will share these with everyone:

1. The language used in negotiations should be highly targeted. In business negotiations, each side uses language to express their own wishes and demands. Therefore, the language used in negotiations must be highly targeted and aimed specifically at the issues at hand. Vague or verbose language can confuse the other party, cause resentment, reduce one's credibility, and become an obstacle in negotiations. For different products, negotiation content, negotiation settings, and negotiation counterparts, language should be used appropriately and effectively to ensure the success of the negotiation. For example, when dealing with a straightforward and impatient negotiator, using concise and clear language may be welcomed. With a slow-paced counterpart, having a heartfelt, lengthy conversation may yield better results. During negotiations, fully consider the differences in the negotiation counterpart's personality, emotions, habits, culture, and needs, and use targeted language appropriately.

2. Expressions in negotiations should be tactful. During negotiations, every effort should be made to use gentle and tactful language, making it easier for the other party to accept. For instance, when rejecting the other party's request, you could say: "What you said makes some sense, but the actual situation is slightly different," then subtly introduce your own perspective. This way, you won't damage the other party's dignity, and they will listen to your opinions calmly and attentively. Skilled negotiators often try to disguise their opinions as those of the other party, enhancing persuasiveness. Before presenting your opinion, ask the opponent how they would solve the problem. If their solution aligns with your opinion, make them believe it was their idea. In such cases, the negotiation counterpart feels respected and believes opposing this plan is like opposing themselves, thus making it easier to reach an agreement and achieve negotiation success.

3. Be flexible in negotiations. The changes in negotiation situations are unpredictable, and unexpected embarrassing situations often arise. This requires negotiators to have flexible linguistic adaptability, linked with emergency measures, to cleverly extricate themselves from difficult situations. When the opponent presses you to make an immediate choice, if you say things like "Let me think about it" or "It's hard to decide temporarily," the other party might perceive you as indecisive, placing you at a psychological disadvantage. At this moment, you can look at your watch and politely inform the other party: "I'm really sorry, it's 9 o'clock. I need to step out and make a phone call to a friend I've scheduled to meet. Please wait five minutes." Thus, you gracefully gain five minutes of thinking time.

4. Appropriately use non-verbal language. In business negotiations, negotiators use non-verbal languages such as posture, gestures, eye contact, and expressions through non-vocal organs, which often play important roles during the negotiation process. In some special environments, silence may be necessary, and appropriate silence can yield unexpectedly good results.
