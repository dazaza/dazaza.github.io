Political Policy Outlook for 2012

by ikonfx123456 on 2012-02-27 13:56:53

Looking forward to the year 2012, the fate of Hong Kong stocks will not be determined by themselves, but rather the key lies in politics and policy. Major countries around the world will hold general elections next year, during which the predictability of policies will be relatively low. Among these, several permanent members of the United Nations Security Council, including the United States, Russia, and France, will all hold presidential elections, and the results of these elections will sway the global situation. The outcome of the French election will bring a certain amount of uncertainty to the Euro debt issue; if the newly appointed French president does not coordinate well with the German Chancellor, it could hinder progress in resolving the Euro debt. The result of the U.S. presidential election will play a crucial role in future debt reduction. According to a report from China International Capital Corporation (CICC), if Obama fails to secure re-election or wins only narrowly, the possibility of the U.S. economy slipping into recession in 2013 will increase. If Obama is re-elected with a clear majority, some economic stimulus measures might be introduced to alleviate the pressure on economic growth caused by deficit reduction. If next year's presidential election results in a deadlock, uncertainties will remain high, and enterprises holding large amounts of cash may take a wait-and-see approach, significantly affecting employment and investment, thereby also bringing certain pressure to economic growth in the second half of 2012.

On the policy front, China, the U.S., and Europe are at the core. In terms of China, the main expectation is that the economic growth rate faces significant downward pressure, necessitating the introduction of policies to stabilize growth, ensuring that GDP does not fall below 8%. Therefore, if inflationary pressures ease, gradually relaxing the tight monetary policy and enhancing fiscal policy is the clear direction. Policy-wise, the focus will primarily be on structural adjustments, and long-planned emerging industry strategies will be implemented more clearly, thus aiding stable economic growth. Under the political cycle, investment growth has always shown a marked rebound in the first two years of each five-year plan. Currently, many provincial local governments have completed their leadership transitions, signaling the start of the political cycle. The current 27.5% growth rate in local project investments indicates that local investment momentum remains strong. On the other hand, both the Central Economic Work Conference and the People's Bank of China's monetary policy implementation report emphasized the need to ensure investment in ongoing and continuing projects next year. As of November, the scale of new and ongoing projects remains enormous, forming substantial follow-up investment for next year's foreign exchange market [http://www.ikonplc.com].
