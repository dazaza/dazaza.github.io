A Brief Talk on the Three Stages of Erectile Dysfunction in Men's Lives

In life, men have three stages of erectile dysfunction. "Women cannot say 'randomly,' and men cannot say 'no.'" Of course, this is a joke, but it implies men's fear of "impotence." This "impotence" is medically known as erectile dysfunction (ED). Experts point out that there are mainly three phases in a man's life when they are at risk of developing ED. If these phases are successfully navigated, sexual happiness can be extended to the age of 80 or even longer.

The three dangerous periods mentioned here are mainly due to psychological issues causing ED, excluding cases of organic lesions. Dr. Huang Xiaojun, deputy director of the Department of Andrology at the Second Affiliated Hospital of Zhejiang Chinese Medical University, pointed out that clinically, psychogenic ED accounts for 50% to 70%. Psychological issues leading to erectile dysfunction mostly occur in three phases: the period of lack of sexual knowledge, the phase of decreased sexual interest, and the phase of reduced libido.

Around one or two years before and after marriage:

During this stage, there is relatively less sexual knowledge, making it easy to engage in sexual activities under conditions of tension, fear, and anxiety, which can lead to initial sexual experience disorders such as insufficient erection and premature ejaculation. Subconsciously, this may plant potential hazards, like the fear of failure in sexual activities, always worrying about not being able to react normally as in the past. Some men lack understanding of certain physiological phenomena during sex, leading to unnecessary worries. For example, some men experience delayed urination and a burning sensation in the urethra after intercourse, thus believing that sexual activity has worsened their health condition, causing fear and worry, and even making them reluctant to have sex again. In fact, this is just a physiological phenomenon that can be restored with rest.

Around ten years after marriage:

After ten years of marriage, it is inevitable to feel bored and experience "aesthetic fatigue," leading to a decrease in sexual interest. There is a famous experiment in sexology called the "Rhesus Monkey Experiment," where two monkeys were kept together and made to mate repeatedly. Over time, the male monkey lost interest in the female monkey and hoped to find a new one, which is an important reason for "aesthetic fatigue." After marriage, due to the wife's hard work for the family and children, her figure may change, her skin may become loose, and her charm may fade. In such a situation, the husband may easily feel that life is dull and lose "sexual" interest. At this point, couples should learn to use innovative adjustment methods to enrich their marital sex life and rediscover the feelings of love, such as women frequently changing clothes styles to maintain a fresh image and trying perfumes that their husbands like.

Male Menopause:

After entering menopause, men's temperaments may become irritable and anxious, and some men may experience decreased libido and erectile ability, leading them to believe they have lost sexual capability. In such situations, changes should first be made in lifestyle. People in their forties and fifties cannot possibly work as hard as they did in their twenties and thirties, working day and night. As physical strength declines, work content or life goals should be adjusted. Additionally, healthy eating habits and moderate exercise should be maintained. Studies show that exercise can increase testosterone levels. In daily life, maintaining good communication with one’s spouse and keeping close relationships are essential. Facing the empty-nest period, couples should re-plan their two-person world.
