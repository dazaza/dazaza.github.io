A Brief Discussion on the Application of Image Recognition in Motor Testing Systems

Publication Date: February 16, 2012, 9:40:38 AM

To control the error in test results, it is necessary to capture images of the probe position during the testing process in real-time. These images are then recognized and calculated, and when necessary, the movement of the probe is controlled via a stepper motor to ensure the accuracy of the square test structure used in four-probe measurements.

The grayscale histogram is one of the simplest and most effective tools in digital image processing. It is a function of grayscale levels, describing the number of pixels in an image that have a specific grayscale value. The x-axis represents the grayscale level, while the y-axis represents the frequency (or number of pixels) of that grayscale level. If an image consists of two unlinked regions, and the histograms of each region are known, then the histogram of the entire image is the sum of the histograms of these two regions. This means that histograms have an additive property. Based on this principle, we separately acquire the histogram of a pure substrate image and the histogram after loading the probe (with the needle tip tilted).

By analyzing the above histograms, we find that the histogram after loading the probe has a new peak at lower grayscale levels. Since we use a reflective imaging system, the reflection effect of the probe is worse than that of the substrate, resulting in a lower grayscale level for the probe's image compared to the substrate. Therefore, the substrate produces the right peak in the histogram, while the probe produces the left peak. There are relatively fewer pixels between the two peaks, creating a valley between them. By selecting the valley as the threshold, we can reasonably distinguish the probe image from the substrate image. We can set the pixel threshold to 117, judging the grayscale values of points in the image; those greater than this value belong to the substrate, and those less than it belong to the probe, allowing us to identify the probe area in the image.

First, we need to make a coarse adjustment to the probe so that its axis aligns with the centerline of the entire image, as shown in Figure 5. Since the probe's needle tip is elliptical and tilted, it cannot be assumed that the first edge point along the probe's axis is the needle tip, nor can various centroid algorithms be used to determine the needle tip's location based on the centroid. After multiple experiments, we start from the center of the entire image and scan in four directions—up, down, left, and right—with a certain pixel width (each pixel corresponds to a physical distance of 0.955 mm). If the grayscale values of pixels within a scanned area are all less than the selected threshold, we consider the center of this scanned area to be the position of the probe's needle tip.

Let the length and width of the probe positioning image be m and n, respectively. Starting from the () point, we scan in four directions with the width of the solid line (7 pixels). Taking the identification of the upper-left probe #1 as an example, we scan upwards. When yy1, if the grayscale values of the pixels in the range indicated by the dashed line do not all meet the requirement of being less than our predefined grayscale threshold, we consider it part of the substrate rather than the probe. Once we scan to y = y1, where all points within the corresponding scanning width fall within our predefined threshold range, we define (y1) as the current position of probe #1. The positioning of other probes follows a similar method and will not be elaborated further here.
