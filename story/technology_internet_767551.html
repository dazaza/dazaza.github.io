The Story Hidden Beneath the iPhone 5s Home Button

by anonymous on 2013-10-22 19:22:30

The Touch ID sensor used in the iPhone 5s brought fingerprint recognition to a new level. This technology came from AuthenTec, a fingerprint sensor manufacturer that Apple acquired a year prior.

"AuthenTec's fingerprint sensors smaller than 8 mm are the soul of Touch ID." On Tuesday evening at an event hosted by North Carolina State University's Industrial and Systems Engineering College, Scott Moody, co-founder of AuthenTec, told the students present.

Of course, the first generation of AuthenTec fingerprint sensors was not as elegant as it is now. The prototype for Touch ID was called FingerLoc — a box much larger than an iPhone packaging, with a long tail trailing behind connecting to an even bigger box where all the fingerprint data processing and matching took place.

After creating the initial sensor prototype, Moody visited investors with this device, "incessantly" describing his grand vision: shrinking the sensor and embedding it into a phone. In early demonstrations, a small hole was cut into a table where AuthenTec's sensor device was placed underneath. Moody jokingly referred to it as "the largest PC we've ever seen."

Even so, the sensors back then had numerous issues. During a product demonstration for IBM engineers, Moody’s demonstration device malfunctioned. The CTO of IBM personally tried out AuthenTec's fingerprint sensor but was mistakenly identified as Dale Setlak, the co-founder of AuthenTec. An awkward moment ensued.

"I joked that all CTOs' fingerprints look alike," the witty Moody attempted to ease the tension with humor but ultimately failed to secure IBM as a client. "They didn't buy."

"We had to convince people that the chip responsible for signal processing could be made small enough. We eventually reduced its size by 80%," said Moody resolutely.

The metal ring surrounding the Home button on the iPhone 5s is actually not much different from the early products. Essentially a capacitor, the metal ring can scan live skin through the outer layer of dead skin on a finger. This is the result of optimization done in collaboration with many dermatologists — also inspired by earlier challenges and breakthroughs.

As the technology matured, the sensor size continued to shrink. More and more devices began using AuthenTec's fingerprint sensors. Behind the brilliant achievements were numerous undisclosed attempts: trials, mistakes, failures, and restarts, over and over again, testing the technical team.

The final product was finally released, attracting attention from many manufacturers, including Motorola, Fujitsu, LG, Samsung, Nokia... and Apple. Among these companies, Apple showed the most enthusiasm and eventually acquired AuthenTec for $356 million in July of last year.

The acquisition negotiations between the two parties were filled with entanglements and strategies. The exposed proxy statement showed that Larry Ciaccia, CEO of AuthenTec, and Fred Jorgenson, Vice President, had been in contact with Apple as early as February last year. In March and April, both parties hammered out contract details and intellectual property issues. However, the negotiations subsequently hit a deadlock — Apple wanted to acquire AuthenTec's technical team, but AuthenTec did not agree.

In May, during renewed negotiations, Apple agreed to acquire the entire company and offered a price of $7 per share. However, AuthenTec was not satisfied. As the negotiations were about to hit another deadlock, Apple presented arguments that changed the negotiation dynamics. Apple stated that no buyer would offer a higher price than Apple, and another potential benefit of accepting Apple's acquisition was developing unique products for Apple, which would be more valuable for new technologies. Apple then emphasized the hope that AuthenTec would consider the progress of product plans and engineering development to expedite the negotiation process.

However, subsequent negotiations remained tense. Apple once considered abandoning the acquisition and instead establishing a partnership with AuthenTec. But in early July, Apple changed its mind, insisting on acquiring AuthenTec, and made concessions on the price — raising the offer from $7 per share to $8. AuthenTec subsequently accepted this offer, and both parties entered the acquisition phase.

Looking at the entire negotiation process, AuthenTec's confidence stemmed from its strong position: at the time, AuthenTec shipped as many as 100 million fingerprint scanner-related products annually. Moreover, it held 200 patents covering fingerprint recognition chips, chip packaging, and software among others.

Back to the beginning of the story. At North Carolina State University, Moody couldn’t help but marvel: fundamentally, the technical team was the soul of AuthenTec. "We have a great team of engineers — I think this is why Apple has kept our technical team intact until now."

For Moody, AuthenTec's goal was to make people's lives more convenient, a goal that did not change before or after the acquisition.

"I don't care at all about the government's standards for fingerprint scanners. What I care about is affordability, and how to help users identify themselves economically and accurately. What gets me hooked isn't the technology itself, but what the technology can do for you."
