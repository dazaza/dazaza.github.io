The Dark Food Chain Behind Official Microblog Promotion

by anonymous on 2013-10-11 19:11:41

This is an era of chaos belonging to the "media", and a wretched world crowded with "new media".

Why do I use quotation marks? I think this needs no explanation, as we already have our answers. Let's first briefly review the background:

The rise of Weibo brought about the first wave of new media trends. Enterprises, regardless of size or industry, rushed in en masse, leading to the birth of a new business category: official Weibo outsourcing.

Soon after, the emergence of WeChat public platforms triggered the second wave of new media trends, resulting in the self-media promotion model. Again, enterprises, irrespective of scale or industry, stormed in chaotically, giving rise to another new business category: official WeChat management.

Due to its rapid and intense development, this industry has experienced explosive growth intertwined with shocking dark food chains.

90%, the "risk-free profit" of intermediaries

To describe official Weibo outsourcing in one sentence: followers are abundant, but results are meager.

Despite the seemingly bustling and highly active official Weibo cases, very few truly stand out or convey the enterprise's own values and philosophy. Even from the perspectives of activity and exposure, only a few cases like Durex's official Weibo account manage to capture attention. Moreover, Durex can only be considered a successful example of official Weibo outsourcing.

How profitable is official Weibo outsourcing? Consider an actual case shared by a friend working for a well-known PR company. A prominent domestic enterprise spends exorbitantly on various market promotion activities each year. For official Weibo promotion alone, they pay an extremely high monthly fee of N hundred thousand yuan. According to the enterprise's logic, N hundred thousand yuan is equivalent to less than a month's advertising placement on vertical network media or a few print media ad pages. Dividing the budget and allocating it to the official Weibo account not only yields impressive numbers and flashy reports but also provides a good justification for innovation-focused executives.

This calculation seems reasonable. Outsourcing, or subcontracting and re-outsourcing, is common knowledge in the PR industry. However, compared to regular PR promotions, the profits from official Weibo promotion are significantly higher. Typically, regular PR promotions yield low profits, with some PR companies outsourcing promotional services while only earning a 10% margin, all while taking risks of hiding information from clients. Additionally, most crucial media maintenance and promotion tasks are handled by PR companies themselves.

Official Weibo outsourcing is different. With a monthly fee of N hundred thousand yuan, the PR company that secures the contract simply removes a zero and subcontracts the execution to another company, effectively pocketing 90% of the profit. The remaining 10% given to the executing company still leaves room for substantial profit. This is practically a risk-free and highly profitable deal. Why is this so?

Who is the ultimate loser in the food chain?

A media friend of mine quit his job two years ago to start a company specializing in Weibo promotion, and surprisingly, he had plenty of business. Originally a journalist in the IT circle, his clients ranged widely, including local branches of state-owned enterprises and local stations of internet companies. The monthly fees from each client were not particularly high, ranging from 20,000 to 30,000 yuan, which is a relatively standard price for small outsourcing companies at the bottom of this food chain.

However, for his small company, graphic designers, planners, and copywriters could be shared across multiple projects. As he put it, "Official Weibo, despite seeming diverse in industries, is actually a standardized product. Similar activities can be pushed to every client, and similar copywriting can be slightly modified and sent to many official Weibos." Therefore, even though his company was at the bottom of the food chain, their profits were not insignificant.

Notably, all of his clients came from large PR companies or advertising agencies. Clearly, the biggest slice of the pie had already been taken, yet numerous smaller Weibo outsourcing companies like his still made substantial profits. They were the winners in this food chain.

Ultimately, in the food chain of official Weibo outsourcing, the companies spending money became the biggest losers, while the PR companies and outsourcing companies profiting from them became parasitic winners. Beneath the harmonious facade, the key determinant of a company's failure lies in its mindset. In essence, the herd mentality and the superficial marketing environment forced companies down this path.

Who is more suitable to be the driving force behind this?

Returning to the original topic, in the face of the allure of new media promotion, no company can remain indifferent or unconcerned. New media promotion indeed enables secondary brand communication and can enhance a company's influence within its industry to a certain extent.

The question is, who is more suitable to take on this role? Currently, there are three prevalent approaches in the industry:

The first approach involves the company handling everything itself. The advantage is that only the company knows itself best. The disadvantage is that professionals may not necessarily understand the intricacies of communication, leading to amateurs handling internal matters. Additionally, gradual promotion increases time costs and leads to higher human resource costs. A famous company like IBM falls into this category, and its cautious, step-by-step style aligns with its traditionally stable corporate characteristics.

The second approach, and the most common one, is outsourcing to PR companies or larger Weibo promotion agencies that have grown significantly. The advantages include strong planning capabilities, understanding the nuances of communication, achieving optimal results in the shortest time, producing attractive reports, and showing more significant effects in the short term compared to companies handling it themselves. However, the disadvantages are also evident: insufficient understanding of the company's product lines, values, and core concepts limits the promotion to merely capturing attention and peripheral aspects. Among such companies, Durex's outsourcing effect is the best. But most others remain at a general level, often becoming tragic figures at the top of the food chain.

The third approach is gaining popularity, where companies achieve Weibo promotion through commercial media outsourcing. Managing official Weibo accounts through media represents a compromise between the first two approaches. Media understands both the company and promotion strategies. However, the disadvantages are obvious; media does not know the company as well as the company itself, and media is not a professional promotion company. After balancing these two aspects, the results fall somewhere between the first two methods. Within the food chain, the media's position is not dominant. Some media-managed official Weibo accounts may not contribute to operational performance and might serve as by-products of advertisements and promotions.

Does outsourcing equate to deception?

The final critical issue revolves around the main source of nourishment outside the food chain: users.

Does the outsourcing of official Weibo and official WeChat mean deception? The biggest difference between official Weibo/WeChat and traditional media promotion is that it is not a third-party outlet. Users naturally assume that the information from this new media platform comes directly from the enterprise itself. Users also naturally consider any interaction or replies from official Weibo/WeChat as direct responses from the company.

Many friends outside the circle have provided feedback to the author regarding issues like: "When asking about certain activities or events via official Weibo, the responses are always delayed and often unclear." This situation is indeed one of the reasons for the rapid decline in Weibo's influence. The primary function of Weibo is interaction, followed by content distribution. When official Weibo loses its main communication function, how much value does its content distribution retain? This is worth pondering.

In conclusion, as a crucial part of corporate promotion, official Weibo and official WeChat can justify some losses, even significant financial expenditures, during the initial stages to offset the human resources and time costs associated with building an in-house new media promotion team. There's nothing wrong with this. However, if these platforms are part of the company's long-term overall image and brand strategy, no matter how reluctant, the company must eventually take charge. Standing at the top of the food chain means realizing maximum value.
