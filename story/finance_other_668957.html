Home industry transformation, red wood furniture may welcome an unprecedented "golden period"

by mumu321 on 2012-02-18 17:13:40

Without the financial crisis triggered by US debt, there might not have been the "trade-in" program for home appliances; without the deepening of the financial crisis due to European debt, there might not have been the furniture "trade-in" program. In the wake of these dual crises, redwood furniture - a traditional industry that has long been semi-moribund - has welcomed an unprecedented golden era. As the saying goes, "Misfortune may be an actual blessing, while a blessing may hide a potential misfortune." This is indeed true, so we should view things from both sides and avoid being overly pessimistic.

The uncompromising regulation of the real estate market has made the home furnishings industry feel desperate. At the beginning of the Dragon Year, Beijing proposed a furniture "trade-in" program following the success of the home appliance program. Although this plan is still in its infancy and only applies to one city, the remarkable effects of stimulating consumption in the home appliance and automobile industries have filled the entire national furniture industry with anticipation. Who would have thought that the wild lily would also have its spring? On one hand, the "trade-in" program has brought unprecedented popularity to the furniture industry, encouraging more families to purchase or afford furniture, leading to significant development in the home furnishings industry. On the other hand, the rise of redwood furniture has brought great prosperity to the furniture industry. Redwood furniture has become an art collectible, grouped with porcelain and calligraphy as the "three treasures of the collection world." The elevation of the furniture industry holds revolutionary significance, akin to introducing high-end and Western design into the real estate market. The once uniform "Tang-style buildings" now appear elevated in their visual appeal. Even Guangzhou's affordable housing and low-cost housing resemble villas.

Despite complaints about extravagant seafood consumption costing nearly ten thousand yuan at a single meal, and hotel stays requiring a 1000-yuan key fee, is Sanya attempting to become China's Dubai in terms of charges? Stripping away the speculative bubbles surrounding redwood furniture, we see that redwood furniture, much like wine, has achieved capitalization. The furniture industry is following a development path similar to that of the wine industry. On one hand, the industry structure becomes more rational, and its form more robust. On the other hand, the concepts, culture, marketing, and value of wine have impacted the liquor industry. Private customization accounts for 40% of the redwood furniture market, and the concept of "selling culture" in redwood furniture leads to continuous innovation in "white wood" furniture. Similarly, Western classical furniture forms an investment market akin to wine, with a mature pricing system. Given time, redwood furniture will not fall short of Western classical furniture and will join the ranks of luxury goods, standing out uniquely.

With redwood as a benchmark, the furniture industry has seen the emergence of new Chinese-style furniture represented by antique redwood furniture and antique white wood furniture, as well as "pan-redwood" furniture represented by nanmu wood. Chinese-style furniture has undergone significant improvements in design. Even "pseudo-redwood" furniture, which can almost pass for genuine, represents a form of progress. If these counterfeit products are reformed and legitimized, they could become excellent furniture pieces and part of the home furnishings industry, just as fake antiques, if no longer faked, can become fine crafts like paintings and ceramics.

We can expect a significant improvement in the overall quality of China's furniture industry, leading to a complete transformation, much like the qualitative leap in China's home appliance and automobile industries, increasingly reaching international standards.

The furniture industry faces the challenges of "going grassroots," "changing style," and "reforming writing style." "Going grassroots" means popularization, targeting a broad consumer base and secondary markets. "Changing style" requires the Chinese furniture industry to move towards high-end production, with redwood furniture taking on this role. "Reforming writing style" demands innovation in design, concepts, and marketing. However, the popularization and elevation of the furniture industry do not have a so-called dialectical relationship; they cannot be forcibly linked. Public sports activities and winning Olympic gold medals are two different matters.

Red wine has created a red haze, and redwood has also created a red haze. It has been extremely popular, even excessively so. It is easy to see through worldly affairs, but difficult to understand redwood thoroughly. Whether it's going up to Foshan or down to Wuhu, the new real estate policies seem like a dream, and it's time to let go of such hopes. The continuation of "Dream of the Red Chamber" is hard, but the dream of redwood is promising. (Editor: Tian Tian)
