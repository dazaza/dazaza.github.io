Impressions of the Jiangsu Prison Delegation's European Tour (Part One)

by lk0fwcc on 2009-07-24 04:14:37

From March 7th to March 21st, 2001, with the approval of the Jiangsu Provincial People's Government, a 14-member delegation from Jiangsu Prisons, led by Comrade Yu Airing, visited Europe. Our main task during this trip was to examine the prison systems and management models of Italy and Switzerland. During the inspection, we exchanged views with the prison administration department of the Italian Ministry of Justice and visited Rebibbia Nuovo Prison in Rome. We also examined Sandrone Prison and Vaud Prison in Switzerland. Everywhere we went, we were warmly received by our foreign counterparts. The prison management systems of the two countries, their excellent hardware facilities, the absolute financial security provided by the state for prisons, the rigorous and efficient working style of prison administrators, the strict protection of prisoners' rights, and the effective use of social educational resources by prisons all left a deep impression on us.

One of the impressions: The rule-following Nuovo Prison in Italy

Italy, the peninsular country located in southern Europe, was our first visiting country. Although the former glory of the Roman Empire can be seen everywhere, Italy is an economically relatively backward country in Europe. Despite being able to see the shadow of the decline of this ancient empire everywhere, as prison administrators from the world's largest developing country, we still felt joy and excitement about being able to visit Italian prisons. Director General Giovanni Tamburino of the Ministry of Justice and other officials warmly received us and introduced us to detailed situations regarding prison management. On behalf of the delegation, Yu Airing expressed gratitude for the thorough arrangements made by the Italian side. He said that as a historically rich and culturally diverse country, the prison management model of your country, especially the application of modern technology and methods for prisoner rehabilitation, are what we want to learn about.

Mr. Paulo, the head of the Correctional Administration Department of the Prison Administration Bureau, and other officials introduced the organizational structure of the Italian Ministry of Justice: Our Bureau of Prisons under the Ministry of Justice has six departments: Personnel Management Department, Prison Staff Training Department, Supervision Department, Correctional Administration Department, Social Services Management Department, and International Exchange Department. There are also 16 regional prison bureaus subordinate to the Ministry of Justice nationwide. The organizational structures and functional divisions of prison organizations in various countries are generally similar, but Italian prisons have their own characteristics in classification and functions. Italian detention centers are managed by the Prison Bureau and are part of the prison system. Prisons are divided into many categories. Besides dividing based on the age of inmates into adult and juvenile prisons, they also classify based on the legal status of detainees and individualized treatment requirements. Adult prisons are further divided into preventive detention centers, penal execution centers, security measure execution centers, and observation stations. Penal execution centers include detention centers for executing short-term imprisonment and centers for executing long-term imprisonment. Security measure execution centers include farms, labor sites, medical detention centers, and judicial psychiatric hospitals. Observation stations can either be independent prisons or separate sections within a prison. Similarly, prisons for disabled individuals can either be special prisons or special sections within a prison.

Moreover, Italy has its unique approach in managing the classification of prisoners: In addition to adopting the universal rules used worldwide such as classifying prisoners by gender (male vs female) and age group (adult vs juvenile), among the same category of detainees, we mainly classify them according to their different levels of danger. Categories mainly include: general, dangerous, and highly dangerous. Prisoners in the general category can have a relatively relaxed range of activities; those in the dangerous category have fewer activities, and those in the most dangerous category have the smallest range of activities. Prisoners in the same category can interact with each other, but those in different categories absolutely cannot. In Italy, the most dangerous prisoners are undoubtedly members of organized mafia groups. For them, we always implement confinement and management according to the highest level of danger. Even when they go outside for fresh air, they are always under the cover of barbed wire."

If these situations mentioned above were still within our expectations, then the high ratio of police force allocation in Italian prisons greatly exceeded our imagination: Italy has 200 prisons, housing 54,000 prisoners and employing over 50,000 prison staff, resulting in a nearly 1:1 ratio of prison staff to prisoners. Among them, there are 43,000 armed prison guards specifically responsible for guarding and escorting tasks, while approximately 7,000 staff members manage the prisoners. Additionally, there are 40,000 social volunteers nationwide who serve as non-regular prison staff. They mainly engage in cultural and technical re-education of prisoners and assist in the correction work within prisons.

Italian prisons also emphasize the reform of prisoners and it is written into the country’s highest law - the Constitution, though it is referred to as "re-education" instead. According to our constitution, from the day a prisoner is sent to prison, the prison has the responsibility to provide them with re-education aimed at changing their thought and behavior, increasing their educational level, cultivating their vocational skills, enabling them to repent and become new people, and reintegrate back into society. Prisons organize compulsory education courses and vocational education courses and carry out cultural and professional training activities through appropriate means. Based on the progress specified in the prison education system, high school courses can be offered inside the prison, arranged by the National Public Education Department according to the needs of the prison authorities in the form of setting up high school-level branch schools in some prisons. They place particular emphasis on young prisoners under the age of 25 participating in cultural and vocational training. They encourage prisoners to take correspondence courses and radio/TV university courses.

Italian prisons value the realization of prisoners' right to labor and write labor as an important means of re-education into the law, mandating prisoners to participate in labor. This aspect of their penal system is somewhat similar to ours. However, due to limitations in labor projects, only one-fifth of the prisoners are able to participate in labor. Moreover, the implementation of this legal provision faces challenges: To achieve the goal of re-educating prisoners, the law stipulates that prisons must organize labor for prisoners. But in practice, we encounter significant resistance. The main reasons include: poor product quality. Most prisoners are idle without any labor skills; lack of corporate orders; excessive environmental and condition restrictions. Prisons lack factory space because building factories costs too much, and labor costs in prisons are higher than in society, so private enterprises are unwilling to set up factories in prisons. Products lack competitiveness and have little survival space. However, the state recently passed a law exempting prison products from all taxes and not requiring the payment of social welfare funds.

We had many more questions to ask, but the time for our scheduled prison visit arrived, so we had to get up and bid farewell. After leaving the Ministry of Justice, accompanied by Ms. Luisa Gandy, Director of the International Relations Department of the Ministry of Justice, we proceeded to visit Rebibbia Nuovo Prison. On the way, Ms. Luisa further introduced us to the specific situation of prison management in Italy and the basic profile of Nuovo Prison.
