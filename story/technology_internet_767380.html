Intel International College Student Challenge: Entrepreneurship Changes Society and Life

by anonymous on 2013-10-13 14:13:27

This year's grand prize winner of the Intel International Challenge was a project from Chile that monitors health indicators for miners.

Chen Jiake (second from right) from Tsinghua University’s sewage treatment project and Professor Zhang Wei (third from right) from the Tsinghua School of Economics and Management introduce Tsinghua's entrepreneurial projects.

Silicon Valley entrepreneurship mentor Steve Blank gives a lecture on entrepreneurship to students from various countries.

At just over twenty years old, Chen Jiake sits calmly in front of the media in a brand-new, well-fitted black suit, speaking confidently; despite still having traces of acne on his face, it is clear that this young man from Hunan exudes a maturity that isn’t feigned but rather an inner confidence that seems slightly incongruous with his age. In fact, though only a first-year graduate student in the Department of Materials at Tsinghua University, he has another identity—that of CEO of Beijing Ruoshui Wujie Environmental Technology Co., Ltd.

However, there was still a black price tag hanging off his perfectly pressed suit sleeve. "Is this a newly bought suit?" He paused for a moment, looked down at the tag, casually removed it, and nodded, saying "yes." There was no sign of embarrassment on his face. The suit was specifically prepared for the Intel International Entrepreneurship Challenge (IGC). This young Tsinghua student already possesses unique water pollution treatment technology, using chemical reagents to help domestic factories treat and recycle heavy metal industrial wastewater; resolving China's water pollution problems while simultaneously generating income and profit for his company.

Every autumn, amidst the falling leaves, Berkeley University in the Bay Area welcomes dozens of students from around the world, as part of the Intel International Challenge co-hosted by Intel and Berkeley University. This year marks the ninth edition. Chinese student teams have consistently excelled in this competition, with Tsinghua students making appearances almost every year in recent times.

As a primary symbol of Silicon Valley, Intel was built on continuous technological innovation, and education remains the driving force behind further development. Among major tech companies, Intel’s investment in education surpasses others, whether during their peak or challenging periods. Every year, Intel invests $100 million globally in the education sector, not only for university research and development but also for teacher training in middle schools and elementary schools. To encourage student innovation, Intel sponsors two annual competitions: the International Science and Engineering Fair (ISEF) for high school students and the International Challenge for higher education institutions.

Although the theme of the competition is technological innovation, the judges' focus is not solely on high-tech. How innovation can be used to address specific issues within individual countries or globally is the main point of interest for Intel's entrepreneurship and innovation competition. Last year's grand prize winner was a novel rural stove project from India, which, although seemingly low in technological content, effectively resolved fuel safety issues for hundreds of millions of rural residents in India. According to Intel, in the nine years since the establishment of the international challenge, they have collaborated with 300 universities worldwide, spawning over 125,000 startup projects through various entrepreneurship promotion initiatives.

The entrepreneurial projects that make it to Berkeley are among the best of the best. This year's international challenge received a total of 18,000 applications from 60 countries, but only 28 project teams from 20 countries made it to California, including three teams from mainland China, making it the country with the most participants. Besides Chen Jiake's sewage treatment project, there were indoor positioning projects from Sichuan University and smart school and nursing home projects from Shandong University. The latter deserves mention: they offer free installation of cameras and monitoring equipment in schools or nursing homes, allowing parents and children to monitor real-time activities of their children and elderly relatives via mobile phones, tablets, or computers. This appears to be a win-win project, potentially sharing revenue with schools and nursing homes in the future, while schools equipped with this system gain more trust from parents. Behind this innovative model lies video acceleration and encoding technology developed by these students. Currently, out of 1,400 primary and secondary schools in Zaozhuang, 95 have expressed clear intentions to collaborate.

However, the disappointment for the Chinese team was that none of the three projects reached the final podium this year. The grand prize winner receiving $50,000 was a mining worker health data project from Chilean students. They developed portable sensors to measure specific health indicators of workers in industries such as mining, transmitting the data to the cloud for analysis to assess health risks for industrial workers. Compared to last year's Indian stove project, this year's winning Chilean project similarly addresses social issues in emerging countries but incorporates the currently popular wearable device element, making it richer in technological innovation.

For the global university students participating, the rewards of this Silicon Valley journey far outweigh the final itself. Many are visiting the U.S. for the first time, touring prestigious institutions like Stanford and Berkeley University under Intel's organization, attending lectures, visiting Plug & Play, a Silicon Valley international incubator and accelerator center, as well as Intel headquarters, listening to entrepreneurs' experiences, and exchanging ideas with students from other countries. Here, they can humbly seek advice from successful entrepreneurs and come into contact with numerous Silicon Valley venture capitalists.

Many participating students mentioned that regardless of the competition results, this five-day trip to California was filled with gains. Zhang Yuguang from Sichuan University remarked that this Silicon Valley innovation experience brought many insights to their entrepreneurial path. After attending a lecture at Stanford University by one of LinkedIn's co-founders, he realized that they should simplify their business model and focus on key developments.

In reality, the competition results aren't the focal point. Tsinghua University's deputy secretary of the Party Committee, Shi Zongkai, specially traveled from Beijing to Berkeley, disregarding jet lag and fatigue to attend the finals. In his view, whether Tsinghua students win awards isn't important; learning from foreign prestigious universities' experiences in fostering an entrepreneurial atmosphere is the purpose of his trip. He even directly proposed to Intel executives responsible for the event whether this competition could be co-hosted with Tsinghua University in the future.

Of course, Intel chose Berkeley as the partner institution for the international challenge for various reasons. Both Berkeley and Stanford are the two major academic innovation hubs of Silicon Valley, with top-tier faculty and students globally; Silicon Valley gathers over 60% of America's venture capital funds and houses tech giants like Intel, Google, and Apple. This intangible comprehensive entrepreneurial atmosphere draws global entrepreneurs to chase their dreams in this small bay area of San Francisco. Shi Zongkai also acknowledges that although Tsinghua might have the best entrepreneurial environment in China, it still lags behind Berkeley and Stanford.

Regarding student entrepreneurship competitions, Shi Zongkai has more thoughts. Many participating entrepreneurial projects are still in the very early stages of a commercial plan, and even some previously award-winning projects have quietly faded away. Many students, when faced with the choice between graduation and employment, abandon their original entrepreneurial ideas and opt for more practical conventional career paths instead.

In the current wave of entrepreneurship in China, Shi Zongkai has reflected thoughtfully. Resources available to Chinese students for entrepreneurship are far less than those in Silicon Valley, and they face more non-technical difficulties when starting a business. Simply encouraging college students to start businesses with impatience and superficiality may not hold much significance. Therefore, he isn't too optimistic about the prospects of undergraduate students starting businesses but hopes that Tsinghua students will settle down and focus on academic research before embarking on more solid entrepreneurial ventures after acquiring technical advantages. Shi Zongkai mentioned that Tsinghua University aims to foster entrepreneurship through various forms of support and encouragement based on a strong foundation in academic research.

Talking about student entrepreneurship, Associate Professor Zhang Wei from the Tsinghua School of Economics and Management also has his own perspective. Tsinghua boasts China's best academic advantages and the strongest angel and venture capital funds through the Tsinghua Entrepreneurs Association. Zhang Wei discussed the subsequent development of previous competition projects, stating that Tsinghua encourages student entrepreneurship but focuses more on the future; perhaps some students later choose work over entrepreneurship, but this entrepreneurial education will benefit them for life, possibly leading them to start again at the right opportunity in the future.

Planting seeds of innovation, cultivating an entrepreneurial spirit, using innovation to change society and the world, and solving real-world problems—this is perhaps also the main purpose of Intel founding the International Challenge.

At the award ceremony dinner, Chen Jiake didn't wear that new suit again; dressed casually, he sat quietly watching the Chilean students go up to receive the award.

"Disappointed?" He neither nodded nor shook his head, still expressionless. "It doesn't matter. My sewage treatment project is already operational and generating revenue, and it solves China's most pressing pollution problems. That's more important."

"So, do you consider going abroad?" "No, I will continue working on this project," said Chen Jiake calmly.
