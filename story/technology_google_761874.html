Android's New Chief Denies the Merge of Android and Chrome OS

by geekzhang on 2013-05-14 08:55:41

Sundar Pichai now oversees Google's two most important departments: Android and Chrome. Since he began managing Android, people have been speculating whether Google will merge Android with Chrome OS. With Google I/O just around the corner, will we see any signs of this during the conference? Additionally, what new strategies does Sundar Pichai have for Android's development? Wired conducted an exclusive interview with him.

Android and Chrome will not merge in the short term

Sundar Pichai said that Andy Rubin's departure was sudden, and he found out about Andy's decision quite late. However, he is excited about his new role because of Android's immense scale, which allows him to make a significant impact. This does not mean that his original views have changed; both Android and Chrome are large, open platforms that grow rapidly. Users won't be confused by Google having two operating systems because users care about apps and services, not operating systems. Developers also want choices. Therefore, we embrace both and will continue investing in both. There won’t be any changes in the short term. In the long run, the evolution of computing itself will determine the direction of change. Perhaps in one or two years from now, things might be different, but in the short term, we have Android, we have Chrome, and we won't change direction.

The opportunities and challenges faced by Android

Sundar Pichai said that many regions around the world still lack internet access, presenting a massive opportunity. As an open system, Android can meet needs at various levels, enabling widespread adoption of low-cost computing devices in developing countries, which holds special significance for me. At the same time, Android faces challenges: how to improve the global end-user experience without compromising its open nature?

Android is a very, very open system and highly customizable. Google does not intend to change this, but they also want to ensure through some means that users have a good experience.

We are not focused on Facebook Home and Kindle Fire

So, what should Sundar Pichai think about Facebook Home's approach to changing user experiences? He said that Google will not block such behavior. We must come up with reasonable ways to do things and implement them in a way that feels fair to users and developers. There will always be a balance. It’s the same as when Facebook makes decisions about its own platform. But currently, we don’t plan to make any changes. We welcome Facebook’s innovations. Sundar Pichai said that for Mark, people are at the center of everything. I take a slightly different approach. I believe life is multifaceted. People are a big part of it, but not the center of everything.

Compared to Facebook Home, Kindle Fire goes further, being a fork of Android. Regarding this, Sundar Pichai said that from a licensing perspective, Amazon has the right to do so. Overall, those of us at Google welcome people developing their own versions of Android because I think it benefits everyone. This isn’t something we’re trying to prevent. Our focus isn’t on Facebook Home or Kindle Fire. The computer industry is undergoing a major transformation. Our opportunity is to ensure that this benefits people and solves important problems for them.

Samsung is an excellent collaborator

Will Samsung's dominant position in the Android ecosystem harm Google's interests? On this, Sundar Pichai said that Samsung is an outstanding partner, and Google collaborates with it on all key products. The GS4 in his hand is proof. Although he hasn’t used the eye-tracking technology on it, you see, Samsung plays a crucial role in helping Android succeed. To deliver great experiences, you need a combination of hardware and software. Whether from daily collaboration or strategic perspectives, our relationship is very solid. Therefore, I am not worried. Look at Microsoft and Intel. They depend heavily on each other, but it benefits both parties.

What about Motorola Mobility? For the sake of the Android ecosystem, Motorola is just another collaborator.

The Android update process

Sundar Pichai said they are thinking about how to make the Android update process smoother. We are talking to partners. We need time to figure out the mechanisms, but for me and the team, this is definitely an area of focus.

(We don’t know if Sundar Pichai can achieve this, given the failure of the Android Update Alliance)

What surprises will this year's I/O bring?

Sundar Pichai said that this year's I/O will be different. It won’t focus on new products and systems but rather on serving developers. The focus of this I/O on both Android and Chrome will be on everything we do for developers to help them create great applications. We will showcase the impressive performance of Google services on these two platforms.

In the final part of the interview, Wired asked what instructions Larry Page had given him as the head of the Android department. Sundar Pichai said that Larry wants them to drive innovation and serve users and developers. His instruction is, please do something at Google's scale.

How does he handle the stressful workload? I have a secret project that adds four more hours to our 24-hour day. It somewhat involves time travel.
