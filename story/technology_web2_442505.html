Introduction: Li Zheng, the Publisher of IT Manager World magazine, and He Zhigang, the Executive Editor-in-Chief, visited Sina.com's guest chatroom to discuss the cover story "C Era" which was planned for half a year by IT Manager World.

The following is the interview transcript:

Host Yin Jun: Hello, dear netizens. Welcome to Sina.com's guest chatroom. This morning, we will talk about the arrival of the C Era. Let me introduce our two guests. The first one is an old friend of Sina Tech Channel, Li Zheng, the Publisher of IT Manager World magazine. Welcome, Mr. Li.

Li Zheng: Good morning, everyone!

Host Yin Jun: The second guest is He Zhigang, the Executive Editor-in-Chief of IT Manager World magazine. Hello, Mr. He.

He Zhigang: Good morning, everyone!

Host Yin Jun: Today, we invited Mr. Li and Mr. He to Sina.com's live broadcast room mainly because this topic is closely related to IT Manager World. In the latest issue of IT Manager World, the cover story talks about the arrival of the C Era and makes a large-scale report on mobile internet. How did IT Manager World come up with such a topic? Please introduce it, Mr. Li.

Li Zheng: We have been thinking about this topic for quite some time, since last year. From basic phenomena, we judged that a new wave of technology is changing the world. For example, at many gatherings last year, many friends spent most of their time using their fingers to swipe on their phone screens. This phenomenon was not present one or two years ago.

Also, when communicating with people from various industries, not just IT professionals but also managers of traditional enterprises, we found that they were very interested in social networks, e-commerce, the Internet of Things, cloud computing, etc. And this interest went beyond traditional industry CEOs; many enterprise CEOs, marketing vice presidents, financial vice presidents, etc., were all paying attention to these topics.

Our performance doesn't need to be mentioned, whether it's the IT industry or the mobile internet industry, or traditional solution providers, they all proposed corresponding new ideas, new products, and new applications for this new era. At that time, we had a vague feeling that this era was quietly approaching.

Everyone has their own feelings and experiences living in this era, but the understanding of this era is not so clear. This era has already arrived, and everyone is already in it. What exactly is this era, and how will it change in the future? Everyone is exploring in their respective fields. As media, should we take a closer look at the arrival of this era and understand what is happening? Therefore, we started planning from the end of last year, and it took about half a year from conception to execution. It involved three months of preparation and three months of operation to achieve the current result. This is the most extensive coverage and the largest number of participants in IT Manager World's history. Specifically, Mr. He has more authority to comment on the execution process.

Host Yin Jun: Mr. He, what insights do you have during the execution process? And how do you view the arrival of the C Era?

He Zhigang: Indeed, the operation of this topic took a long time, and we invested a lot of reporters and editors. Talking about this topic reminds me of over ten years ago when the internet was just emerging globally. Intuitively, it felt similar then, as if websites' advertisements were everywhere, and everyone was excitedly starting businesses in the internet field. It was a relatively restless and exciting era. This is just a similarity that made us feel the arrival of a new wave of technology. As for how to define this wave, how to perceive it, and how to analyze it, that is probably the responsibility of media people.

But looking at it from another angle, if we only consider it from the perspective of mobile internet, I feel that the analysis might only stay on the surface because this wave of technology, like the previous wave of the internet, changes the entire form of society and industries. We thought about whether our magazine could name an era or give a label to an era. This matter may go beyond the level of industrial analysis. On this level, we had multiple dynamic communications with the editors and reporters involved in this topic. Every week, we would discuss this issue.

In such an era, having a little sense of mission is quite interesting. We are a small team, a magazine, identifying an era and seeing if we have the ability to do so. Everyone vaguely feels that this thing is not just a simple concept of mobile internet. When things are unclear, we think the best method is to conduct interviews, discuss with industry friends, and communicate with entrepreneurs of all sizes. Through communication, our confidence gradually strengthened. Initially, everyone discussed mobile internet, like what I use my phone for, but when we mentioned the C Era, everyone got excited, feeling that there was a new concept or a new era entering, but no one defined it or gave it a clear classification. This is perhaps an opportunity for our magazine.

Then, we continuously interviewed. For a period, we conducted group-style interviews where several people might go together. Everyone chatted and interviewed together, creating a small brainstorming atmosphere. In this process, many interesting concepts emerged. The more we discussed, the more we realized that this topic was not just about mobile internet. More and more traditional enterprises quickly accepted the impact of mobile internet. During this process, we continuously deepened the structure and format of our articles. Later, when operating specifically, since we felt we articulated the concept in everyone's mind, we decided to explain it more thoroughly. This issue's cover story spans nearly 50 pages, making it quite large in scale. We hope to articulate it relatively clearly at this moment, but actually, the C Era, as discussed in the article, is a high-entropy era, relatively chaotic and complex, hard to explain fully in one article. There might be some shortcomings here, and this matter requires continuous discussion with readers and interaction with the industry. Many of our upcoming reports will continue to delve deeper into the C Era.

Li Zheng: I want to add a bit more. When naming this era, we had many ideas. Why did we ultimately choose the word "C"? It is relatively straightforward. Since we are in an era where everyone is connected online 24 hours a day, we simply used "C," which is also quite simple. Describing the state where everyone is online 24 hours a day is a typical characteristic of this era, so we finally chose this word to label the era.

Host Yin Jun: When you wrote about mobile internet, you used the term "era," rather than saying the popularization of a new technology or a technological revolution, but used "era." Does this mean that mobile internet is somewhat akin to the birth of the internet, another technological revolution that might bring changes to productivity? How does Publisher Li view this?

Li Zheng: This question was also addressed earlier. Looking domestically, from the mid-1990s to 2005, the internet basically entered a phase of development from its infancy to excitement, then to enthusiasm, entrepreneurship, and relative maturity, taking about ten years. In these ten years, it not only spurred the emergence of a large internet industry but also changed the IT industry and partially altered people's lives and the operations and management of traditional enterprises. However, the first wave of internet development primarily impacted the industry itself, leading to partial societal changes. But starting from 2010, the technical or application drivers behind the C Era we see now can roughly be categorized into a few types: the development of mobile internet, the growth of e-commerce, the expansion of social networks, and the rise of internet cloud computing. These new technologies and applications basically erupted and were introduced to society in 2009 and 2010. When examining this round of technological or application development, we generally believe that what it fundamentally changes can be summarized in one sentence: it connects all people with each other, connects people with objects, connects objects with each other, and connects everything visible on Earth.

Within this round of technological development, everything can become a source of information, a disseminator of information, and a receiver of information. Intuitively, this round of IT technology or internet technology and application development will, over decades, connect all visible and invisible people and objects on Earth. From this perspective, the fully digitized life originally anticipated can truly be realized in this round of development. Based on this judgment, the profound changes it brings to traditional commercial eras, human lifestyles, and the governance and management of society will, to a certain extent, be entirely structured within a network of ubiquitous connections, truly achieving global digitalization. This round of change is profound, and its impact is far-reaching.

Returning to your previous topic, this is also what we considered when constructing the overall framework of the C Era.

Host Yin Jun: In this report, I saw a concept called the "High Entropy Effect." What does entropy mean? Afterward, I learned that it is a unit of physics, which might be unfamiliar to many netizens. Could Publisher Li introduce what the "High Entropy Effect" is, how to understand this concept, and why this term or concept was included in this report?

Li Zheng: The term entropy is indeed relatively mysterious, but in fact, it is a concept from physics two hundred years ago. The term entropy is not new; it is ancient, marking the breakthrough from agricultural times to industrial society. It originally referred to the transfer of energy from high-temperature objects to low-temperature ones, and this transfer is irreversible. Temperature and energy cannot be transferred from low-temperature objects to high-temperature ones. Later, the second law of thermodynamics evolved to signify the disorder or randomness of individual molecules in a physical system, serving as a measurement unit.

In a closed system, the more microscopic individuals participating in collisions and the more complex the collisions, the higher the entropy value of the system. Over time, this concept has been applied in various fields, including information science, economics, and sociology. For over a century, many scientists, including humanities scholars and sociologists, have done significant work on this concept. Now, we use the "High Entropy Effect" to explain the fundamental laws of the C Era, building on a basic foundation.

The C Era completes the connection of all people and objects. In scientific history, commerce is used to explain the evolution of human civilization. For example, in primitive societies, interactions between people were minimal, with no commercial transactions or knowledge exchanges. In primitive societies, the entropy value was very low, with little information and interaction. As society developed, reaching agricultural civilization increased entropy, and industrial civilization further increased it. Now, we are in an explosive era. Psychologically, as human civilization progresses towards modern and contemporary civilizations, we feel time moving faster, with daily tasks piling up, increasing interactions with society and the surrounding environment. Our perception of time differs significantly from the past, unlike the routine of rising with the sun and setting with it. Each person’s entropy value increases, though without using the term entropy to describe it.

Now, the acceleration of entropy is rapidly increasing, and the increase in entropy is not uniform. As human civilization progresses, the entropy value grows larger. In the C Era, the acceleration of entropy reaches near escape velocity. If we consider the Earth as a closed system, the number of individuals participating in collisions within this large closed system is rapidly increasing because we are all connected through the web. People are connected with each other, and people are connected with objects, whereas previously they were isolated. For instance, we didn’t interact with people from the U.S. or Africa, but now through Facebook, we can establish connections. From this perspective, the number of microscopic individuals participating in collisions is unprecedented, reaching the largest state in the history of human civilization. Especially with the development of mobile internet, currently, mobile networks cover 60% of the global population, something unattainable in traditional internet.

Secondly, the increase in the amount of information exchanged between people is also unprecedented. Through microblogs and social networks, the volume of information exchanged by each person, including the number of interactions with others, increases exponentially. Within the entire Earth system, entropy significantly increases, and the speed of entropy increase greatly accelerates. This is a characteristic of the era. The drivers behind this increase are new technologies of the C Era, such as social networks and mobile internet. Mobile internet enables connections without time and space restrictions. Social networks make virtual-world connections even tighter than in the real world. E-commerce fully connects people with objects. Using entropy to explain or capture the features of this era provides a physical and civilizational basis.

Now, let’s see why it’s called the High Entropy Phenomenon. New application platform operators, such as familiar names like Facebook and the Apple model, precisely establish their own subsystems within a large system. Within these subsystems, the more individuals participate in collisions, like the six hundred million users on Facebook, whose daily collisions are non-structured, completely illogical, and disordered.

Facebook’s entire platform has particularly high entropy values, and it controls the rate of entropy increase and growing value through this platform. It implements a system to achieve commercial value within a high-entropy large system, aiming for relative orderliness. By analyzing collision behaviors, for example, if we discuss baby issues, diapers might be recommended. It shares information with relevant developers, pushing diaper orders to you. Through high-entropy subsystems and data analysis, it understands collisions, turning seemingly unordered information into relative orderliness and maximizing commercial value. From this perspective, the "High Entropy Effect" explains the new business models of the current internet innovators. Facebook initially lacked a business model and still lacks a clear way to make money solely through advertising or e-commerce. Yet, in reality, every possible business model exists on its platform, and each realizes commercial value while sharing profits, achieving maximum value.

Using the "High Entropy Effect" to explain the basic level of the C Era and its business models might be a logical approach but requires continuous critique and discussion. Perhaps this is incorrect or needs better development. We propose it now for criticism and promotion by industry elites and friends to collectively drive the fundamental commercial development of this era.

Host Yin Jun: After listening to Publisher Li's introduction, I have a comprehensive understanding of the "High Entropy Effect." The content of the "High Entropy Effect" is vast and could be the subject of a course. Not knowing the "High Entropy Effect," how do you view it, Mr. He, who also participated in content control and has understanding and recognition? What's your opinion?

He Zhigang: First, from the reader acceptance perspective, it is a relatively professional term, although it has existed for a long time. Additionally, as a physics concept, it has its own theoretical system.

Li Zheng: It has overturned the heat agent. According to the second law of thermodynamics, the entire universe will eventually reach entropy equilibrium, with entropy diffusing to a certain extent until the entire capacity becomes orderly, potentially leading to the destruction of the universe, reaching thermal death. However, after this, new dissipation theories explain that the universe cannot reach a completely balanced state, and new systems emerge far from balance, forming new structures.

He Zhigang: Let's not get stuck in the details of the concept, as that discussion may have its own system in physics. From another angle, observing enterprises reveals that current companies change rapidly. For example, consider a bank. From a bank's perspective, the interaction with customers is now coexisting. Traditionally, you might visit the bank once a month to pay your credit card bill and possibly some utility fees. You might interact with the bank four or five times a month, especially in today's China, where banks often have long queues, making you reluctant to wait. However, now, many banks are promoting their online banking, iPhone apps, and Android platform apps. Once you install these, the frequency of interaction between customers and the bank significantly increases. Whenever you buy something, you can use their payment service, increasing your interaction with the bank. Of course, you might not physically go to the bank branch anymore.

We discussed this issue in our article. Leading domestic commercial banks, such as China Merchants Bank, Bank of Communications, and international banks like Standard Chartered, are promoting these services. They discovered that the interaction between terminals and customers goes beyond mere financial transactions, involving much more information, such as consumption-related activities like where to watch movies, where to eat, and where to buy clothes. Banks find that the information exchange with customers has completely changed, with the entire interface and information undergoing significant transformations. So, we can say that traditional banks are low-entropy enterprises, with stable structures, strict hierarchies, and orderly communication with customers, typically interacting only a few times a year. However, in this stage, they become high-entropy enterprises, with constant communication with customers, always online, allowing instant information exchange. This rapid transformation affects traditional banking institutions.

Working on this topic, we communicated extensively with banking professionals. We initially thought banks were steady enterprises, but insiders felt uneasy about facing the mobile internet era. This situation is unclear. In the internet era, especially in electronic commerce payment processes, banks started late. Due to their late start, Alipay met user needs, forming a massive payment platform. This platform, as we know, is clear to everyone—banks both love and hate it. They love the substantial funds it provides to serve customers but hate that the platform isn't in their hands. In the mobile internet era, banks are highly vigilant, realizing they missed the previous opportunity and mustn't miss this one. They discuss how to respond to technological changes, industrial changes, and customer demands.

With the arrival of the high-entropy era and the popularization of mobile internet, traditional enterprises, such as the banks you mentioned, face new challenges and opportunities.
