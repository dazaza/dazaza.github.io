A entrepreneur's narrative: How I sell fish online

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 17:59:16

　　In fact, when I first started selling fish online, many friends around me thought I was crazy.

　　When everyone's eyes are focused on real estate, technology, mobile phones, and cars, I chose to sell fish. It was indeed a tough decision, but it felt solid.

　　How can O2O be done in a creative way? Should we abandon WeChat? Who is more awesome between Lei Jun and J.Wong? Can BlackBerry make a comeback? Can Cook take over from Master Jobs? I don't care about these things because they are far from us. Dreams are big, but reality is small; people should live a little more mundanely.

　　YouCai failed, and its demise was very reluctant. However, I still have no doubt about the enormous potential of fresh produce in e-commerce.

　　In 2012, I began trying B2C online fish sales. YouCai failed, but I survived. Of course, this might be related to the fact that my company is a small-scale start-up with low operating costs and easy execution. However, some experiences I've gathered along the way are still valuable, and I am willing to share them with you all. Perhaps they can offer some inspiration to those who are starting businesses online selling fish, vegetables, or fruits.

　　1. Regarding product positioning

　　My product positioning is: never do daily products at low prices, only focus on mid-range products. In the initial stage, I will not do vegetables and meat, only frozen seafood.

　　Let me explain. I believe your biggest competitor in e-commerce for fresh produce is the local agricultural market. Compared to them, I think I am at a disadvantage. First, the traditional group of shoppers is concentrated among middle-aged and elderly people, most of whom would never have the habit of buying fish online. Second, you could argue that selling fish online saves tens of thousands of yuan in stall fees compared to physical stalls, but the harsh reality is: relative to vendors who restock every early morning and almost maintain zero inventory, your storage, labor, and delivery costs throughout the year would far exceed theirs.

　　YouCai mainly failed here. Observing its product structure, there were almost no mid-to-high-end products, mostly common agricultural by-products found in farm markets, directly facing the impact of local farm markets. Wanting to change the purchasing habits of traditional aunts is as unrealistic as wanting to suddenly make cows stop eating grass and start eating meat.

　　Compared to supermarkets, I found my advantages quite evident: shorter procurement cycles and fresher products (referring to bulk goods cycles), nearly 40% cheaper prices (don’t think supermarkets are cheap), and a younger customer base (my observation shows that most supermarket shoppers are aged 25-40), which fits my requirements well.

　　Seafood can be classified into several categories, mainly live seafood, chilled seafood, and frozen seafood (I won’t explain each one here; those interested can research it themselves). Live seafood has the highest storage requirements, and the price difference between a live fish and a dead fish can reach around 50%, making it unsuitable for small-cost operators like me. On the other hand, preprocessed frozen seafood is the easiest to preserve, with minimal loss, generally lasting about one and a half to two years if the temperature is maintained (Ding Jingtao said that YouCai’s fresh produce loss rate was 5%; I simply don’t believe it).

　　Thus, I positioned my competitors as supermarkets. I selected certain raw material products used in buffets, Western restaurants, mid-to-high-end Chinese restaurants, and Japanese cuisine establishments. High-profit products that aren’t easily found on the market and are easier to preserve allowed me to avoid the impact of farm markets while enhancing the product’s value through its own class.

　　2. Regarding price positioning

　　Unlike phones, cars, and houses—luxury items—this market is extremely sensitive to price.

　　For example, for the same pound of cabbage or salmon, if your price is more than 5 yuan higher than the market price, customers won’t be interested in you. You can only attract them by offering lower prices than the market. But the profit from a pound of cabbage versus a salmon, well, I don’t need to say it—you know the difference.

　　Regarding pricing, Ding Jingtao of YouCai once said: “Even for tomatoes, prices may vary 2 to 5 times. The quality of vegetables on the market varies greatly, and their nutritional safety also differs. YouCai tries to select high-quality goods during procurement. For instance, cucumbers can cost 1.2 yuan or 1.5 yuan, and we choose the 1.5-yuan ones.”

　　“So, the vegetable prices on YouCai are slightly higher than in the market,” Ding Jingtao emphasized, adding that if the quality is the same, YouCai is definitely cheaper.

　　To these statements, I’d say: Mr. Ding must have never sold vegetables himself.

　　90% of the aunts absolutely wouldn’t buy cabbage priced higher than the market rate, no matter how much you exaggerate its qualities—even if you claim it’s green, organic, super-incredible cabbage. Aunts are very practical; whoever offers lower prices and acceptable quality gets their business.

　　Since the product positioning is agricultural by-products, even the slightest price fluctuation significantly affects product sales. If Ding’s approach is followed, only agricultural by-products with barely passing quality but much cheaper than the market could be offered, running a high volume strategy. This allows capturing a large market share with low profit margins. However, for a start-up company without market experience or brand recognition, aiming to capture the market right away is very dangerous.

　　Without sufficient gross margins, it’s hard to even conduct market promotions. Unfortunately, Ding’s mindset aimed to create premium agricultural products, expecting aunts to accept prices higher than the market, without being able to inspect the goods on-site. This undoubtedly boxed him into a dead end.

　　So, I priced my products realistically—they had to be below market rates but still highly profitable. I don’t care about market share; I only care about whether the gross margin is high enough.

　　Earning one-tenth of a yuan from 1,000 people versus earning one yuan from 100 people—I can still do the math.

　　3. Regarding channel costs

　　A personal suggestion: in any industry, you must have your own special channels. These channels don’t necessarily mean monopolizing the market but ensuring fewer competitors, giving your products a certain uniqueness and scarcity.

　　When I was doing wholesale, I pondered this problem, and solving it was quite simple. First, try to avoid fiercely competitive first-tier cities—it’s impossible to compete against someone driving a car when you’re barefoot. Second, choose less popular origins. My product origins concentrate in three places: South America, Africa, and Northeast Asia. The weaknesses of giants (including other large aquatic companies) lie in their inability to adapt quickly due to their size. All I need to do is observe the market, select the right products, and make decisions myself. By the time they get approval level by level, my product channels will already be firmly established.

　　YouCai positioned its channels domestically, which set the bar too low. The principle of wholesale is similar to group-buying—it’s all about volume. So today you can negotiate with farmers, and tomorrow they can just as easily do the same. Today you can create a YouCai, and tomorrow they can replicate it as YouCaiHua.

　　4. Regarding marketing models

　　This is something we must discuss in detail.

　　YouCai positioned itself as "delivering vegetables like milk," attempting to build a closed ecosystem similar to iOS where customers could enjoy doorstep delivery services without leaving home. This involves two issues: 1. How to attract potential customers to your platform? 2. Why should customers stay? (Which Ding referred to as increasing customer stickiness)

　　I first noticed YouCai due to a report on Sina. Because the products weren’t significantly different, I clicked out of curiosity to check YouCai, but opening its UI disappointed me greatly. An outdated interface with no design sense, merely listing product types and prices with no other relevant information, insufficient variety yet including unrelated products like disinfectant and toothpaste…

　　I really couldn’t understand what YouCai could rely on to retain customers. Opening its webpage felt like walking into a roadside variety store, even forgetting that I originally came to look at "vegetables."

　　Relying solely on an uninnovative milk-style sales model of order-delivery-after-sales service to attract customers was undoubtedly YouCai’s biggest blunder.

　　Therefore, before I started, I had my products turned into various dishes. Just for one type of crab, I made it into stews, soups, stir-fries, braises, etc. Then I hired professional photographers to artistically process the dishes, making them appear as appetizing as possible.

　　On this point, I actually borrowed from IKEA’s concept—enticing customers with finished products but only selling semi-finished ones, allowing customers to fully experience the joy of accomplishment, which is far stronger than handing them a finished product.

　　Later, when many customers exchanged ideas on QQ, I simply talked to them about how to use my ingredients to cook, rather than discussing how delicious the fish or crabs were. Taobao doesn’t sell products; it sells pictures, and fresh produce is the same. Eventually, I stopped promoting products and only chatted with foodies about how to eat the best way possible.

　　Actually, targeting a young customer base, many people love to eat but don’t know how. They really want to eat, so you need to teach them how to make it taste good. (Sometimes I even feel Dianping is more suitable for fresh produce).

　　Later, more and more customers gave positive feedback. I observed that 80% of them were foodies, and 20% came due to referrals from friends. Therefore, I specifically created several QQ groups where, as long as it didn’t violate national laws, members freely exchanged experiences about food, comparing other seafood brands. Don’t fear competition; the more competition, the more it highlights the uniqueness and price advantage of your products.

　　In terms of models, YouCai tried to implement an electronic + commerce model, but I personally believe this was also a failure. There are two paths: One is to become a platform-focused model like Taobao, establishing relationships with offline wholesalers and letting the platform handle the commerce. The second is to focus solely on commerce, rapidly building various regional distribution platforms, with options including SNS, regional BBSs, microblog marketing, WeChat marketing, etc., concentrating on channel quality and price.

　　I prefer the latter because it allows direct contact with customers.

　　My current model focuses on offline wholesale, mainly supplying hotels, buffets, and restaurants. Online, I focus on retail, with slightly higher prices than offline. But I don’t build my own platform; I only affiliate with several popular local BBS communities, leveraging their traffic to attract customers. If customers respond positively to the product, it spreads virally.

　　Sometimes, customers even upload photos of their own cooked dishes to "show off." Generally, I reward them with small extras, creating a situation where hardly anyone leaves the QQ group over time. Exchanges increase, and by the second half of last year, I almost didn’t need to promote online anymore since customers helped evaluate the products.

　　Another benefit of this approach is resolving trust crises. Doing online fresh produce in China faces challenges beyond standardization difficulties (which I’ll discuss later); another issue is the lack of trust in the hygiene of online fresh produce. Simply put, after consuming tainted milk and Sudan red eggs, the public has lost significant trust in merchants. However, if word-of-mouth promotion is relied upon, this issue can be resolved.

　　In terms of profitability, I adopt a small-batch retail model. My minimum standard is five pounds per package, avoiding one or two-pound retail sales, though the prices are attractive, and the quantities increase accordingly.

　　5. Regarding warehousing and logistics

　　I dare say, besides procurement costs, warehousing and logistics are another major expense. This includes labor + transportation + warehousing costs + losses.

　　YouCai adopted the idea of milk boxes, delivering door-to-door at fixed times daily. I see a major issue here: Suppose I ordered a pound of cabbage, two pounds of pork belly, and half a pound of ginger and garlic on YouCai just before finishing work, but when I got home, I found that what I saw online differed significantly from what I received in reality, so I decided not to keep it. YouCai's customer service was great and agreed to unconditional returns after communication.

　　My question is: What do I do about dinner tonight?

　　If I have such an experience once, I probably won’t buy agricultural products on YouCai again just to save a few yuan, because I’ll go hungry...

　　And YouCai loses not only jump orders and customers but also faces losses (some products degrade overnight or after being out of the warehouse for a period), plus double the labor and transportation costs.

　　This is the biggest problem e-commerce faces in fresh produce: how to solve product standardization.

　　Frankly speaking, I currently have no good solution to this problem. I’ve heard complaints like "it doesn’t look the same as in the online picture," and all I can do is compensate with service—if they're not satisfied, I’ll refund them.

　　Because my company is small, I can only cut costs. Based on the number of regional orders, I deliver at fixed times and inspect goods face-to-face. This way, I can explain things to customers on the spot and eliminate misunderstandings (some are caused by customers not understanding the product well enough), and I can avoid the repeated losses associated with secondary pickups.
