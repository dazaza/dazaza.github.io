Motorola CEO: Moto G will be a game-changer for the low-end smartphone market

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 20:06:00

From this year onwards, the high-end smartphone market is moving towards saturation, and the overall smartphone market is beginning to shift from high-end to mid-to-low-end markets. The mid-to-low-end smartphone market is showing astonishing growth, with emerging markets becoming the primary focus. On the other hand, the update cycle for smartphones is getting shorter, and the contract periods between operators and users are also becoming shorter.

The trend of smartphones moving towards the mid-to-low-end range is forcing international traditional mobile phone giants to change their pace. The Moto G, released last night, is a typical representative of this trend—it has a similar appearance to the Moto X, excellent specifications, and an almost stock Android experience, all for just $179.

This price in the Chinese market is referred to as a "thousand-yuan smartphone," but the products behind them have never been as inspiring as the Moto G.

"The entire industry has abandoned 5 billion people on this planet; they would never spend $600 on a phone."

In an interview with the media last week, Motorola CEO Dennis Woodside explained the motivation behind launching the Moto G.

Dennis Woodside is very optimistic about the market prospects for low-end smartphones. Although he refused to disclose the expected sales volume for the Moto G, he anticipates that approximately 500 million people will purchase affordable smartphones this year. Most of these individuals live outside the U.S., primarily in emerging markets like Brazil.

This time, Motorola chose Brazil as its launch market. With an average annual income of $11,000 per person, high-end smartphones like the iPhone cannot fully dominate this demographic.

Dennis Woodside mentioned that in the low-end smartphone market, some Android phones are indeed very cheap, even below $70, but their user experience is not good. They either run outdated versions of Android that are incompatible with the latest applications or compromise on hardware, such as lacking a front-facing camera.

Moto G by Motorola strikes a good balance between product quality and price. It features a 4.5-inch 720p LCD display, a 1.2 GHz Qualcomm Snapdragon 400 processor, 1GB of RAM, 8GB/16GB internal storage, a 1.3-megapixel front-facing camera, a 5-megapixel rear-facing camera, and a 2070 mAh battery. More importantly, it runs nearly stock Android 4.3, with an upgrade to KitKat coming within weeks. At $179, it aligns with the level of thousand-yuan smartphones in the Chinese market.

"Outside the U.S., it will be a product that people dream of owning," Woodside said, indicating that the company plans to release more low-end products in the future. "There are many letters before G in the alphabet."
