Europe is walking into a historical dead end

by koeniamr0805 on 2012-02-29 09:14:06

The UK's Financial Times on February 22 published an article by British historian Andrew Roberts titled "Europe Walks Into a Historical Dead End," with the main content as follows: Nothing is inevitable. The entire history of humanity has proven one point: many human societies seem to be heading towards a seemingly certain goal, like Nike ACG, only to deviate and enter a dead end, sometimes for decades, sometimes even lasting several centuries. Given this, why do we still consider it inevitable that the EU (EU) economy will eventually recover significantly? The Greek economy contracted by 7% last quarter, and despite the brave efforts of British Chancellor George Osborne (George Osborne), Moody's (Moody’s) still threatened to lower Britain's credit rating. These news items should prompt us to focus on the ideas of Jeremy Bentham (Jeremy Bentham), John Stuart Mill (John Stuart Mill), Karl Marx (Karl Marx), and Antonio Gramsci (Antonio Gramsci). Because it was these major thinkers of the Whig Party and Marxist historical determinism school who instilled in humanity the notion that we are "progressing" in a certain direction, heading towards a positive predetermined goal in the future. In economics, this concept is embodied in the assumption that economic growth is a constant, almost a natural right. We often take economic growth for granted as part of the human condition, seeing it as the norm rather than the exception. If Thomas Macaulay (Thomas Macaulay), Friedrich Engels (Friedrich Engels), and other historical determinists were correct, would we have endured such a painful 20th century? The past two-thirds of a century has not been representative in this world, with rare conditions highly favorable to growth. There has never been a period in history where no two major powers have been at war for such a long time. The United States was the engine of innovation and leadership, competing against a resurgent, confident Japan. Exchange rates were largely uninterfered with. China was still biding its time. By historical standards, food and energy prices were quite cheap. Overall, trade and markets (in the West) were freer than ever before. The total population grew but remained within controllable limits. Interest rates encouraged lending, and competition between and within European countries created the wealth that Adam Smith (Adam Smith) had once predicted. Today, peace still prevails among the major powers - the Iraq and Afghanistan wars, by any historical standard, were small-scale, ranking about 20th in post-war conflicts in terms of casualties - but the other prerequisites for economic growth no longer exist in the same form. So why do politicians, business leaders, media editors, and other decision-makers still take it for granted that we can still grasp the 'Holy Grail' of growth? Why are we still controlled by irrational Whig optimism? Isn't it more likely that we've actually entered a typical historical dead end? Like the Ottoman Empire from 1683 to 1923 (which also faced Greece's current problems), the Holy Roman Empire in the 17th century, the Spanish Empire in the 17th to 18th centuries, or the short-lived Austro-Hungarian Empire. These empires all experienced sluggish growth that often lasted for decades, much like another failed empire - the former Soviet Union - did for most of its existence. Even the Roman Empire experienced 250 years without territorial expansion or economic growth, merely stagnating, and no one could compare today's EU with the Roman Empire. As hymns tell us, "the most glorious empires on earth must inevitably perish." But if there is no obvious external threat, the interval between rise and fall can be very long. In the phase of stagnant growth, the above political entities (and many other countries) all exhibited a lack of leadership, and any real sense of purpose beyond mere survival disappeared, like women's Nike Dunk Highs. Whether the Dark Ages between the fall of the Roman Empire in 410 AD and the Renaissance in the 15th century belong to this stage is still debatable. After the failure of the siege of Vienna in 1683, no Ottoman Sultan launched another attack; the Holy Roman Empire lost its former glory after Charles V (Charles V); Bourbon-ruled Spain was stagnant; although romantic under Franz Joseph (Franz Josef), Austria was already riddled with problems. Herman Van Rompuy (Herman Van Rompuy) and Manuel Barroso (Manuel Barroso) are overshadowed by historical shadows. However, it wasn't until Napoleon (Napoleon) rose that the Holy Roman Empire completely disintegrated, and it was also Napoleon who (briefly) ended the Spanish Bourbon dynasty. It wasn't until their defeat in World War I that the Ottoman Empire and the Austro-Hungarian Empire ultimately collapsed. Fortunately, there is no such catastrophic threat visible in Europe now. On the contrary, German Chancellor Angela Merkel (Angela Merkel) and French President Nicolas Sarkozy (Nicholas Sarkozy) have begun arrogantly trying to use the current crisis to deepen and strengthen the EU's control over member states. But apart from blind optimism, they have not provided any theoretical basis showing that the EU has prospects for future growth, the only reason possibly being the growth of China, the United States, India, and Brazil in the future. Optimism is written into America's genetic code, evident from the second sentence of the Declaration of Independence, and Americans have already shown signs of impatience with this round of economic downturn. China's rapid development is plain to see, and while the long-term growth prospects of these two countries are not guaranteed, at least they are not lacking in motivation. In contrast, the EU now exudes a historical aura, akin to an empire past its prime, about to enter a long period of relative stagnation. Fortunately, Europe faces no external threats, thanks to the previous generation in the West during the Cold War, defense spending, bond prices, welfare spending compared to wealth creation - all these data used by historians to measure the fate of empires show: Europe's time is up.
