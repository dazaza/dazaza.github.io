Free Books: Book Valley Website Explores Third-party Payment

by vottot on 2008-01-02 07:53:55

Free book purchase? Recently, a book website called "Book Valley" is trying to tell readers that there can be such a thing as a free lunch.

In addition to traditional online book sales, Book Valley has created a precise interactive sales model of "exchanging information for free books." Under this model, Book Valley builds an interactive platform between readers, publishers, and advertisers — readers provide personal information, advertisers offer embedded sponsorship ads based on the information, and publishers sell books.

"We are not just a bookstore; we don't simply sell books but build a platform that benefits all parties," Liu Meng, Chief Operating Officer of Shanghai Darui Culture Communication Company and owner of Book Valley, told the *China Business Daily*.

Third-party payment

Liu Meng is one of the three founders of Book Valley. Before starting Book Valley, Liu Meng was in the digital printing business and had always been looking for a way to combine personalized printing with the internet.

Starting two years ago, Liu Meng and another founder of Book Valley, Shen Bo, President of Darui Culture, established a digital printing website. In fact, this on-demand publishing model has already become popular abroad, but its biggest drawback lies in the excessively high cost, which limits its widespread dissemination.

The practice of the digital printing website prompted Liu Meng to consider whether it's possible to insert a third party that can pay within a general buyer-seller relationship. Therefore, the three founders focused their attention on books and advertisers.

"Like attracts like, people are divided by books." In Liu Meng's view, the attributes of books are very clear, and when readers choose books, they are also releasing personal demand information. "For example, choosing a beauty book shows interest or need in this area."

On the other hand, for advertisers, understanding customer needs and creating value for their products is key. In the past, few media outlets could provide targeted information to advertisers before ad releases.

In September last year, Liu Meng and Shen Bo put their gradually matured concept into practice, and Book Valley was officially launched.

On Book Valley, readers can go through several steps to "exchange" books for free: first, they need to register and leave their real information. After receiving the confirmation code from the website, they select the books they need and then, according to their preferences, choose to insert advertisements from sponsors listed on the website into positions such as the back cover, front and rear flaps of the book. Each insertion reduces the cost of the book accordingly until the price drops to 0 yuan.

In fact, this is a form of targeted marketing where readers are the owners of the information, choosing to exchange information for books, while advertisers sponsoring the books are targeting ads at readers with specific needs. Through book sales, it helps publishers solve inventory problems. Meanwhile, Book Valley encourages readers to comment on the ads and books, gifting these comments to advertisers and publishers for free. "The internet becomes a platform that integrates the resources of all three parties, creating value for each side," Liu Meng said.

Currently, the domestic advertising industry market scale reaches 200 billion yuan, while the book market scale is several hundred billion yuan. Liu Meng believes that having advertisers pay for books is a very feasible approach.

Operational launch

Book Valley has been in operation for more than three months, and its operating conditions have exceeded Liu Meng's initial expectations.

"At the beginning, I still had concerns. When negotiating with advertisers, they said they were willing to sponsor, but the question was whether the books could actually be given away?" Liu Meng said.

The actual operational results showed that the number of orders for sending books every day was so large that they couldn’t keep up. On the busiest day, Book Valley received 15,000 orders, and due to the scale limitations during the early stages of website operation, Book Valley currently controls the daily order volume to around 2,000.

"We currently have a total of 90,000 copies and nearly 4,000 varieties of books, and over 200 advertisers, 60% of whom are in the trial phase, and the remaining 40% are paying customers," Liu Meng said.

According to Liu Meng, Book Valley’s advertisers adopt a pay-per-effect method, and the fees are divided into two parts: one part offsets the price of the reader's book, and the other part is paid to the online platform, with both parties sharing 50% each.

"Currently, advertisers still maintain a relatively cautious attitude towards this emerging medium, but overall, they are optimistic," Liu Meng said.

In the next step of Book Valley's plan, the interaction among readers, publishers, and advertisers will further extend, expanding from the current precise interactive marketing to vertical portal functions, eventually forming a social networking community.

Meanwhile, regarding advertising sponsorship, some advertisers have already become dissatisfied with the purely reader-selected insertion method and hope for more targeted ways. For example, a well-known cosmetics company proposed that after their brand sponsors the reader's book, the readers would pick up the book at the brand's counters located in Watsons stores across different regions. This is equivalent to bringing the advertising audience even closer to the advertiser. Currently, Book Valley has signed a contract with the brand.

In addition, Book Valley has applied for patent protection for its marketing model. "Book Valley's model is a complete innovation. In fact, the supporting force behind such an operational model is also complex, making it difficult for others to easily imitate. With today's intense market competition, we believe many advertisers and publishers are willing to choose this innovative breakthrough model," Liu Meng said.
