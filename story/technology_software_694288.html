Chemistry and Physics - Food and Medicine:

Let's consider what nutrients dogs get from whole animal carcasses versus canned mush or dry packaged food. Those artificial dog foods called "food" have no medicinal properties.

Carnivores' ideal nutrients for energy, growth, and tissue repair come from animals. However, the quality and quantity of these nutrients must be correct to ensure the entire system functions optimally, which is the essence of health - all systems functioning well and in harmony under no stress. Thus, the right diet is like a magic potion.

Nature also gives food its physical shape and texture. The proteins, fats, minerals, vitamins, and trace elements that carnivores need should come from tough, chewy raw food.

Carnivores tear, pull, and gnaw on tough, chewy carcasses or large raw meaty bones, cleaning and polishing their teeth and gums in the process. Humans maintain oral hygiene by brushing and flossing after meals, while carnivores clean their teeth and gums by chewing on meat and bones during meals. Clean teeth and gums are symbols of health.

Most dogs fed processed food have dirty teeth covered in tartar, with decaying gums bleeding and abscessing. Bacteria in tartar cause bad breath known as "doggy breath," which is very unpleasant. Worse still, oral diseases often affect other bodily systems. Studies show that animals with periodontal disease also have affected livers, kidneys, and immune systems, leading to deteriorating health.

Tearing meat off bones takes time and effort, benefiting carnivores by exercising their cheek, neck, and chest muscles, stimulating digestive secretions, exciting the nervous and immune systems. Gnawing and tearing stimulate the brain to produce chemicals that enhance the function of the endocrine and immune systems, maintaining their healthy and joyful existence.

Digestive enzymes in the gut digest food with normal physical and chemical compositions. There are large amounts of bacteria in the large intestine of healthy carnivores, which are indispensable in the final stages of digestion. When the normal physical and chemical composition balance is disrupted, unwanted bacteria proliferate in the large intestine, causing foul-smelling stools, diarrhea, or worse consequences.

Notice that dogs need protein and fat but not carbohydrates. Carbohydrates come from the starch in grains and the sugar in fruits; fiber in plants is another type of carbohydrate, forming the main component of herbivore feces. Occasionally enjoying a piece of bread or leftovers won't harm dogs.

Long-term feeding of grain-based commercial dog food harms dogs' health because these foods have unsuitable physical and chemical compositions. These foods are hard to digest, resulting in large amounts of loose stools, making parks and yards smelly. Dogs fed whole animals or raw meaty bones have small amounts of stool with little odor, turning chalky white under sunlight.

Whole Animals

Dogs evolved from wolves, and for them, the best food is whole animals, which is why zoos use whole animals to feed captive wolves and wild dogs.

During feeding times at the zoo, staff load carts with frozen whole chickens, rabbits, fish, and large chunks of raw meaty bones. These animal carcasses are intact, complete with internal organs, fur, or scales. In the wild, wolves also hunt deer, elk, and other large animals. Therefore, if zoos can obtain deer, the wolves there can eat deer, though most of the food is livestock - cattle and sheep.

“Extreme dining” describes the aftermath where almost nothing remains except some hooves, large leg bones, or the contents of a sheep's rumen (tripe) and large intestine. If given free choice, wild wolves and dogs know what to eat and what not to eat.

If the prey is small, such as chickens and rabbits, wild wolves and dogs often consume the internal organs and their contents. Most of the contents in chicken and rabbit intestines are undigested wild grass, a small amount of fruit, and seeds. However, for wild wolves and dogs, fruits and vegetables constitute only a small portion of their diet.

When talking about what wild wolves and dogs like to eat, don’t forget that these carnivores also eat the feces of their prey, using bare ground as their dining table, swallowing dirt and debris attached to the carcasses.
