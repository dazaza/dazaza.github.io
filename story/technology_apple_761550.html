Rumor has it that iOS 7 adopts a flat design: subverting the skeuomorphic concept

by geekzhang on 2013-05-03 17:09:01

According to a report by the US tech blog 9to5Mac on April 30 Beijing time, people who have seen or are generally familiar with the next generation iOS7 operating system revealed that this system will redesign the user interface. While it may attract new iOS users, it could make it difficult for older users accustomed to the old interface to adapt.Flat DesignOne of the informed individuals said that the new interface is very flat. Another person disclosed that the new system has completely lost the glossy elements of the old system and has thoroughly abandoned the skeuomorphic style prevalent in the old system. Another individual stated that the new system, to a certain extent, imitates the flat design concept of Microsoft's latest Windows Phone Metro interface.Flat design is based on the philosophy of simplicity, discarding textures and real-world mimicry found in skeuomorphic interfaces. Flattening also means that the entire system will adopt a more streamlined interface, standing the test of time. For example, the current yellow notepad used in the iOS Notes app and the leather-bound visual effect used in the Calendar app may not resonate with younger generations of iOS users.Current iOS icon set: Calendar, Reminders, ContactsiOS has always been known for its ease of use and intuitiveness, which Apple emphasizes on its iOS webpage. With its large user base and market appeal, Apple obviously does not want to increase the difficulty of use during the software redesign process.Although the new version may surprise some people, reports indicate that iOS7 is no harder to use than previous versions. The process of adapting to the new system might be similar to adapting to a colored iPod. Although the iOS7 interface will undergo adjustments, its core applications and system foundations (such as the lock screen interface and home screen interface) will still maintain the current operational mode.Three informed individuals revealed that the development code for iOS7 is Innsbruck. This redesign will adjust all of Apple's native application icons and redesign the entire system's toolbars, tab bars, and other basic interface functions. Reports suggest that devices running this system will employ polarizing filters to reduce viewing angles and prevent bystanders from prying.In addition to eliminating the complex interfaces previously used in iOS, Apple is also discussing and testing various methods to add more scannable information and system option panels to the software, such as the Notification Center. Although it is still uncertain what mode Apple will adopt, an early idea was to bring up new panels by swiping inward from the left and right sides of the software interface. This might resemble the gesture used on Mac trackpads to invoke the Notification Center, but the specific gestures that the new iOS system will adopt are still uncertain.Apple conducts many tests before announcing and launching a new version of iOS. For instance, the company once considered adopting a tiled multi-tasking interface in iOS4 but eventually chose the mode that users were already accustomed to: arranging apps in a straight line at the bottom of the screen.Changing ConceptsThe reason Apple is redesigning the iOS user experience is because Jony Ive, the company's senior vice president of industrial design, now also oversees software interface design. After Scott Forstall, the former senior vice president of iOS, left last year, the software design department was handed over to Ive, who has always been responsible for the hardware design of products like the iPad, iPhone, iPod, and Mac computers.In an interview with the UK's Daily Telegraph last year, Ive addressed the issue of disconnect between software and hardware design. At the mention of mimicking stitching design, Ive's facial muscles twitched, seemingly out of sympathy rather than dislike. He refused to directly answer the question and instead gave a polished response: "My focus is working with other teams to plan product ideas and then develop hardware. That's our focus and responsibility. As for the issues you mentioned, they really don't concern me."In line with the flat design of iOS, a previous report by the business magazine Fast Company also indicated that Ive opposes overly decorative skeuomorphic designs: Conflicts regarding this issue have brewed within Apple for many years. It is said that Forstall strongly advocated for skeuomorphic design, whereas Ive and other Apple executives opposed it. You can even judge the opulence of the interface to determine whose handiwork it is, said someone familiar with Apple's design process.Under the leadership of Ive and CEO Tim Cook, Apple's interface philosophy has strayed far from what it was when Steve Jobs was alive. According to the Fast Company article, Jobs, like Forstall, also supported skeuomorphic interfaces: But some say that the true encourager of skeuomorphic styles before Forstall was Jobs himself. The leather-stitched interface of iCal actually originated from a texture on his Gulfstream jet. An Apple senior interface designer said, "There are many internal documents circulating among Apple's interface designers that embarrass them, saying the effects are terrible."While one informed individual said that the iOS7 redesign might win some praise but also surprise others, Cook seems confident. During the first fiscal quarter earnings call of 2013, Cook responded to questions about 2013 software updates, saying: "We are very optimistic about the upcoming software updates."Based on Cook's past comments and actions to further integrate Apple's products and internal culture, it seems he chose to let Ive oversee software design due to the emphasis on a fully integrated experience. In an exclusive interview with BusinessWeek earlier this year, Cook stated that Forstall's departure and Ive's taking charge of software design would further unify Apple's software and hardware.Cook said: "I think Jony Ive is the most tasteful person in the world with the best design skills. He is now in charge of human interface operations. Look at our products" (Cook tapped his iPhone), "its face is the software, right? The face of the iPad is also the software. Ive has achieved extraordinary success leading hardware design, and now we've entrusted him with software design too. He doesn't handle the underlying technology of the software but focuses on appearance and user experience."Prior HintsOver the past few months, Apple has seemed to hint at changes in its software design philosophy.This image shows the comparison before (left) and after (right) the redesign of Apple's Podcasts app.Earlier this year, Apple upgraded the Podcasts app for iPhone and iPad, attempting to simplify some design elements (see the above image). Before this upgrade, the app contained many skeuomorphic design elements, but almost all of them disappeared after the redesign.This is the logo for WWDC2013.Additionally, the logo for Apple's 2013 Worldwide Developers Conference (WWDC) hints at the trend towards flat design. Apple will unveil the iOS7 operating system at this conference. In fact, one informed individual stated that Apple's Game Center icon and interface elements will resemble the colorful style of the WWDC2013 logo. Due to its casino-like interface, Apple's Game Center app has been widely criticized by designers who favor flat design.Moreover, Apple prominently featured several iPad and iPhone apps with flat interface designs on its homepage. For example, the homepage prominently promoted "Letterpress," a game developed by former Apple employee Loren Brichter. "Letterpress" has been widely praised in the industry for its excellent usability and flat design.Due to the vibrant app development community on the iOS platform, the prospects of iOS redesign go beyond just apps and core interface features. Within the third-party iOS app development community, there are concerns that Apple's interface redesign might also affect apps on the App Store, which have always tried to maintain consistency with Apple's style. Once iOS is redesigned, the app interfaces may feel outdated.Jordan Satok, the developer of AppHero, has a comprehensive understanding of the App Store ecosystem and believes that iOS interface adjustments may pose challenges to all developers."When we started developing apps for iOS five years ago, most apps looked the same. Apple provided an amazing UI toolkit that ensured consistent user experiences across different apps," he said. "As the App Store grew, apps evolved, and designers and developers explored new interface styles and concepts."Since many apps have unique interface designs, Apple's adjustments to the core system may not significantly impact these developers. However, once iOS7 is released, developers following the old iOS design style must quickly improve their apps to align with Apple's redesign.Apple's new OSX operating system will also undergo some redesigns, but not as significantly as iOS.
