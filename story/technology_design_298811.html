Old-age problems trouble tens of millions of Chinese families

by 6zns086q7e on 2009-12-08 11:23:49

Family-based care for the elderly has been the primary means of eldercare in China. However, the family structure resulting from the one-child policy - often described as "4-2-1" (four grandparents, two parents, and one child) - has made it clear that this method will not be sustainable. After getting married, the post-80s generation faces the challenge of two people having to support four elderly individuals, or even more. The tension and pressure of this situation have become increasingly evident. China has now entered an aging society.

As the fastest-aging country with the largest elderly population in the world, eldercare has become one of the challenges that young people in China must face. Moreover, China has encountered the issue of "getting old before getting rich," which implies limited resources available from society and insufficient preparation in various aspects.

Family-based eldercare has traditionally been the main approach in China. However, the "4-2-1" family structure caused by the one-child policy has determined that this method cannot continue indefinitely. After marriage, the post-80s generation is faced with the responsibility of two people supporting four elderly individuals, or even more. The stress and pressure of this situation have become increasingly apparent. Combined with the changes in thinking brought about by social transformation, this has resulted in inevitable differences in attitudes and behaviors between children and their elderly parents regarding eldercare.

Our newspaper has been paying attention to the topic of eldercare. This report focuses on individual cases to conduct an in-depth investigation, aiming to reveal the contradictions and conflicts in current eldercare issues, as well as the joys and helplessness experienced by people, in hopes of drawing wider attention from all sectors of society and jointly seeking solutions to the eldercare problem.

Surrounded by mountains and green trees, a group of elderly individuals reside in log cabins styled like resort villas in the northern suburbs of Beijing.

The elderly residents are not accustomed to appreciating the beautiful scenery. One elderly woman sleeps curled up like a five or six-year-old child, her thin blanket rising and falling slightly, breathing through her mouth with a nasal cannula inserted. On her bedside table is a red "Teletubbies" stuffed toy, a gift from her grandchildren.

An elderly man spends the entire morning focused on the television, where static covers the screen, occasionally revealing human figures. Another elderly man, completely unaware, leaves the door wide open, sitting naked on a specially designed commode, blankly gazing out the window.

This nursing home, named "Phoenix," specializes in end-of-life care for mostly incapacitated (those who have lost their ability to take care of themselves) seniors. According to statistics from the Ministry of Civil Affairs, currently, there are over 149 million people aged 60 and above in the country, with over 30 million elderly requiring varying degrees of family-based care. Among them, the number of incapacitated elderly has reached 9.4 million.

Although institutional eldercare is no longer a novelty in China, in a country that believes in "raising sons to support oneself in old age," sending incapacitated elderly to nursing homes remains a reluctant choice for many families.

Who will accompany you through the final stage of life?

Despite avoiding the term "end-of-life," referring to it instead as "Phoenix" or "nursing home," the fact is that most of the elderly sent here have entered the last phase of their lives.

Families choose this place because "compared to most of the other nursing homes we've investigated, this one is professional, clean, and the scenery is beautiful." However, the most beautiful scenery in the hearts of the elderly is being able to enjoy the happiness of family life.

Resident Cheng Fan, an engineer, is clear-minded, optimistic, and generous: "I'm old, my legs have some problems, so I'm temporarily recuperating here. In half a month, I'll go home." He gestures vividly, detailing the address and arrangement of items in his home. Home seems just within reach. He tells his visitors, "Remember my phone number, in a while I'll be home."

The elderly tend to prefer believing that the nursing home is not the endpoint of life. However, nurses privately indicate that according to the gentleman's condition, not only will he not return home in half a month, but possibly never again. To stabilize the gentleman's emotions, his children have fabricated a kind lie.

Professor Wu Ming from the School of Public Health at Peking University Medical Department once wrote that for severely incapacitated elderly individuals, the cost of home-based care is higher than institutional care, and institutional care can provide 24-hour service. From a purely caregiving perspective, institutional care is the optimal choice.

Wu Ming's survey of 317 elderly individuals showed that 82.56% of the elderly prefer to age at home with their loved ones; among those choosing institutional care, 42.45% do so because they "do not want to burden their families," and 15.49% because "their families are too busy and have no time."

However, the sole issue with institutional care is the lack of familial affection. This means that elderly individuals leaving their families must adapt to the lonely and standardized collective life in nursing institutions.

"At first, the elderly don't get used to it, just like when children start kindergarten," said Tian Zhongfan, the director of "Phoenix." The newly admitted elderly "make a lot of noise."

Residing in a south-facing single room, Grandma Mi Yanmin has a loud voice, repeatedly calling for caregivers throughout the morning: "Come here, I'm in pain, I'm in pain." When the caregiver arrives, she requests help taking out her mobile phone to make a call.

"This is an elderly person suffering from brain atrophy and severe osteoarthritis," Tian Zhongfan introduces, saying her children are very filial, but they are all over 50 years old and unable to provide care at home. The family has equipped the elderly with all necessary medications and snacks, preparing everything meticulously.

However, for Grandma Mi, who sometimes gets confused, these things aren't enough. Her top priority is making phone calls. Before the call connects, she yells, "Ping'er, is that Ping'er? Come see me, come quickly."

After shouting a few times, the call finally connects. A female voice asks, "What's wrong, Mom? What happened?"

Apart from "come, come," Grandma Mi cannot articulate a "proper reason," holding the conversation for two minutes before hanging up her daughter's call and dialing another number: "My younger son is good, he cares for me."

"Doo...," in the quiet room, Grandma Mi dials three times until hearing "the number you dialed is temporarily unavailable," after which she finally calms down, her brand-new phone slipping into the hands of the caregiver.

The nurse says that actually, the previous day, the son had visited and stayed with her for three or four hours. "With time, she'll adapt."

If families were to bear the responsibility of caring for these elderly individuals, the situation might be even more difficult.

Another resident, Huo Wei, was also once attached to his home. On the weekend before his passing, Huo Shurong and her sister managed to bring their father home for a bath. The elderly man pleaded like a child, saying, "Let me stay at home, don't let me leave, okay?"

"Who wouldn't want to be with their own family?" Huo Shurong said that if the elderly could take care of themselves, they would definitely age at home. Sending their father to a nursing home was an unavoidable decision.

A few years ago, Huo Wei developed Alzheimer's disease and became irritable, needing someone around him at all times. Even if the children took turns and gave up all work to care for him at home, it was still challenging. Sometimes when family members left briefly to buy food, issues arose upon returning. After several falls at home, the five siblings decided that having someone care for their father around the clock in a nursing home might allow him to better spend his later years.

Thus, on that weekend, the family, seeing how much the elderly man wanted to go home, decided to let him stay in the nursing home temporarily, bringing him home every week for a bath to keep him happy.

In an environment firmly believing in "raising sons to support oneself in old age," some people find it hard to understand such choices. Some say that spending thousands of yuan per month on a nursing home is not as good as hiring a nanny and letting the elderly stay at home. However, for cases like Huo Wei's or elderly individuals with mental illnesses, hiring a nanny to help care for them is impractical.

The daughter of an elderly individual with a mental illness expressed her difficulties: "Nowadays, it's hard to hire a nanny. Nannies, upon seeing the elderly taking psychiatric medication, refuse to come. And even if they do come, they may not necessarily be professional or responsible. Taking medication is as important as eating for the elderly, and it cannot be missed even once, with strict timing and dosage requirements. How can you trust just anyone?"

"Even if you hire a nanny, it's easy for the elderly and the nanny to clash. Once the nanny resigns, I have to put everything aside and find a new nanny. Going through this hassle frequently makes it impossible to work."

The family members caring for incapacitated elderly individuals endure unspoken hardships. Some elderly individuals appear logically sound on the surface but are actually confused. When relatives visit and ask routinely, "What did you eat for lunch?" some elderly individuals, despite having just finished eating, will say, "They didn't give me any food."

Family-based care for incapacitated elderly individuals often involves hardship, complexity, and misunderstandings. That lady mentioned that she almost suffered a mental breakdown while caring for her mother with a mental illness.

Nursing homes may be the most suitable places to receive incapacitated elderly individuals. However, finding a nursing home that meets the requirements is not easy. Some places have excellent conditions but do not accept elderly individuals with mental disorders or those who cannot take care of themselves; some places accept elderly individuals but charge exorbitant monthly fees; others, whose conditions and prices are acceptable to the family, have practical issues: nursing homes only provide daily care and lack medical care, making it difficult to meet the elderly's treatment needs.

Huo Shurong said that all her siblings had run around trying to find a place for their father's eldercare. Eventually, she selected a relatively well-known nursing home with good conditions, reasonable prices, and close proximity to her residence. Initially, she was quite pleased: "Sometimes, I visited him two or three times a day, which was similar to being at home."

After visiting frequently, Huo Shurong realized that to truly make the elderly comfortable, apart from hardware facilities, nursing care was also crucial.

She noticed that some caregivers had to look after three or four elderly individuals, often leaving the elderly on toilet chairs while attending to other tasks, returning only God knows when. "It's not their fault; working at the nursing home pays little, yet the workload is heavy. Expecting them to care for the elderly like family members is unrealistic."

Nevertheless, the father constantly complained about the caregivers: "She always hits me" or "When I wet the bed, they beat me." Although Huo Shurong understood that her father's confusion meant his words might not be true, "Even if cared for at home, occasional accidents are unavoidable." Still, she hired a "reliable" male caregiver at the nursing home and secretly gave him an additional 100 yuan per month, hoping he would treat her father better.

Not long after bathing at home, 79-year-old Huo Wei suffered cardiac arrest upon waking up after living in the nursing home for 24 days. With the deceased gone, what worried her more was the future: "I originally planned to move into a nursing home with friends, but after this incident, I am a bit afraid. However, I only have one son, and it feels cruel to let him take care of me in the future."

The Nursing Home Faces Dilemmas

Huo Shurong only hopes that in the future, if the state could implement a policy to train and grade all caregivers, she would feel much more reassured.

In fact, for nursing homes, finding caregivers is also a headache. The general manager of "Phoenix," Yuan Yanzheng, pointed to a large photo and told the reporter, "They've all left except one." The photo showed seven young female caregivers in white uniforms standing in the snow half a year ago. The shortest duration one of them stayed at "Phoenix" was less than a month.

Due to its remote location, low wages of only a thousand or so yuan, and the task of serving incapacitated elderly individuals, it's almost impossible to recruit workers in Beijing. "Beijing's youth wouldn't even glance at such a job, and parents would rather support their children doing nothing than let them do this work," Yuan Yanzheng said. "Phoenix" learned the hard way that caregivers must be recruited from poorer areas. Currently, all the caregivers come from rural areas in Gansu, Hunan, and Sichuan provinces.

The question is, can they do the job well once they arrive? Tian Zhongfan said that caregivers in end-of-life care need utmost kindness; other skills can be trained, but a bad temper is insurmountable. "At this stage, situations are special. Without patience, it's impossible to treat the elderly well." Under the shortage of caregivers, "Phoenix" once dismissed someone: the caregiver washed the elderly's feet but only cleaned the tops, resulting in a foul odor between the toes.

Another issue is, can they be retained once they arrive? These young women imagine Beijing differently from a nursing institution. A caregiver who stayed the longest said that in a year at "Phoenix," she hadn't even stepped into downtown Beijing, and the nursing home was no different from the mountainous area where she lived, with hardly anyone to talk to, leading to unbearable loneliness. More importantly, earning little money here is intolerable. "We can endure any hardship, tolerate any dirtiness, but earning little money makes it hard to justify to our families."

According to reports, a caregiver initially earned only 800 yuan at "Phoenix," which increased to a little over 1000 yuan after some time. During the interview, there was even a delay in salary payments due to "Phoenix's" financial crisis.

For Yuan Yanzheng, running "Phoenix" has been extremely difficult. He even doubts his initial judgment and whether it's worth continuing. According to Yuan's accounts, "Phoenix" incurs a monthly rent of 80,000 yuan, plus 35,000 yuan for personnel costs and over 70,000 yuan for utilities, totaling at least 125,000 yuan. This requires 50 elderly residents to break even. However, "Phoenix" has never reached this number, sometimes having only a dozen or twenty-some residents. "Every day, opening your eyes means losing several thousand yuan, which is indeed a terrifying thing." He once hoped the government could provide some subsidies.

To cut costs, "Phoenix" once adjusted the meals. However, this was soon discovered by the families. "Eating stir-fried cucumbers five times a week, the elderly were afraid to eat, which is really unacceptable," even Tian Zhongfan couldn't bear it. Families confronted Yuan Yanzheng. Yuan retorted, "This isn't a five-star hotel!" For the families, although "Phoenix" doesn't need to be a five-star hotel, they cannot tolerate the elderly not eating well. The result was that six elderly individuals moved out, and "Phoenix's" meals improved.

This was a significant blow to the already struggling "Phoenix." According to reports, according to Beijing's latest eldercare plan, by 2020, 90% of the elderly in Beijing will enjoy home-based eldercare services, 6% will rely on community services purchased by the government, and 4% will reside in centralized eldercare institutions. Deputy Mayor Ding Xiangyang stated that Beijing is accelerating the construction of public welfare eldercare facilities, increasing 15,000 beds annually, while piloting socially invested operational eldercare facilities.

However, the winter ahead for the privately owned "Phoenix" does not seem optimistic. Looking at the ceiling, Yuan Yanzheng said, "At most, we can hold on until next spring. If there's no improvement, there won't be more money to invest."

This news is also terrible for the families and the elderly. It means that even this less-than-perfect place might disappear. They will have to search for the next "refuge," and that won't be an easy journey.
