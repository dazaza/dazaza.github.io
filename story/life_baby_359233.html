Many people mistakenly believe that a baby's skin is fully developed at birth, but scientific research shows that a baby's skin continues to develop for 12 months after birth. This shows that a baby's skin is delicate, and the barrier function that resists epidermal water loss is not yet perfect, requiring more careful attention.

The stratum corneum of a baby's skin continues to develop after birth. The stratum corneum is the outermost layer of the epidermis, with tightly packed stratum corneum cells acting as a barrier to protect the skin and underlying tissues. An adult has 25 layers of stratum corneum, while a newborn has only 15 layers due to incomplete development, which quickly develops after birth.

A baby's elastic protein is relatively scarce and continues to develop after birth. A baby's elastic protein development is incomplete, with a looser mesh structure compared to adults, weaker mechanical strength, and incomplete cross-linking between collagen and elastic proteins.

The bonding ability between a baby's epidermis and dermis is weak, making it prone to damage under external forces. A baby's skin is thinner than an adult's, with a loose structure and incomplete development. The "brick wall theory" in medicine vividly illustrates the characteristics of infant skin. We can compare "stratum corneum cells" to "bricks", skin secreted sebum to "mortar", and the network connection of elastic proteins to "reinforcing bars". In infants, the "brick wall" is thin with large gaps and less "mortar", leading to a loose and unstable structure. Therefore, a baby's skin is much thinner than an adult's and requires specialized baby care products. Only products that match the characteristics of a baby's skin can protect their delicate skin.
