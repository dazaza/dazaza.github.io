【Qing Dynasty Culture】The "Three Nos" of Emperor Kangxi During His Eastern Tour

As the emperor during the prosperous period of "Kang-Qian", Emperor Kangxi conducted six famous southern tours during his reign, and three of these tours included stops in Shandong Province to visit Mount Tai and Qufu. During this time, he did many good deeds that benefited the country and its people. At the same time, there were three things he firmly refused to do, which also reflected his progressive thinking...

Only sacrificing to the mountain, not Fengshan (a grand ceremony)

In the deep autumn of Kangxi's 23rd year (1684), Emperor Kangxi made his first eastern tour and arrived at Mount Tai. As soon as he arrived in Taian City at the foot of the mountain, his attendants suggested that he should emulate the ancient emperors who had received their mandate from heaven and changed their surnames to become kings by holding a grand Fengshan ceremony on Mount Tai.

Fengshan is a grand sacrificial ceremony, more grand than the coronation ceremonies of emperors throughout history. Only emperors with outstanding political achievements have the qualifications to hold such a ceremony. Throughout history, from the Qin and Han dynasties to the Tang and Song dynasties, many capable emperors such as Qin Shi Huang, Emperor Wu of Han, Emperor Taizong of Tang, Emperor Xuanzong of Tang, and Emperor Zhenzong of Song have all specially come to Mount Tai for Fengshan ceremonies. As an emperor with great achievements, Kangxi certainly had the qualifications to hold this ceremony.

However, Kangxi rejected the ministers' suggestions. In his view, the emperors throughout history spent huge amounts of money to satisfy their own pride and luxury, but they did not receive blessings from heaven as a result. Such labor-intensive and financially draining actions were not worth it. Moreover, Fengshan was not recorded in classic texts and was almost absurd. He warned the officials:

"I have always admired practical governance. The ancients valued gold mud and jade seals (referring to Fengshan), which only wasted manpower and resources and were meaningless. Therefore, this trip is only for investigating social problems, observing the grievances of the people, empathizing with the hardships of the common people, asking about customs and observing the winds, in order to seek better governance. I will absolutely not follow the previous people's practice of inscribing merits and virtues and reporting them to heaven."

From these words, we can see that Kangxi's refusal to perform Fengshan on Mount Tai was not due to humility as expressed in his "Southern Tour Notes":

"My virtue is shallow, and I do not have great achievements worthy of showing to future generations. How dare I follow the example of my predecessors and inscribe my merits and virtues," but rather because Fengshan would be labor-intensive and financially draining, being meaningless and placing a heavy burden on the people, which went against his purpose of "investigating social problems, observing the grievances of the people, empathizing with the hardships of the common people, asking about customs and observing the winds, in order to seek better governance." His opposition to Fengshan was out of love for the people, considering the welfare of the commoners.

Although Kangxi did not perform Fengshan, he still held great respect for Mount Tai, known as the "Respect of the Five Peaks." He knew well that "Mountains are the leaders of the five directions, giving birth to all things," so after arriving at Mount Tai, he also ceremoniously held a sacrifice to the Mountain God of Mount Tai. His ultimate goal was still "to personally offer sacrifices and pray for the happiness of all people."

Not looking at the Cliff of Self-Sacrifice

When Emperor Kangxi and his ministers climbed to the top of Mount Tai, some ministers suggested that he take a look at the Cliff of Self-Sacrifice. This cliff is located south of the Day观 Peak on Mount Tai, with steep walls on three sides and a deep abyss below, making it extremely perilous. There was a local custom where, in order to cure their parents' diseases, some people jumped off this cliff to commit suicide, demonstrating their sincerity and seeking divine protection for their parents. When Emperor Kangxi heard about this situation, he was extremely angry and severely reprimanded the officials: "Ignorant commoners are deceived by absurd superstitions, mistaking self-sacrifice for filial piety. Ignorant commoners do not know that their bodies and hair are gifts from their parents and cannot be damaged. Confucius once said 'Parents worry only about their children's illnesses.' When children are sick, parents are extremely worried; when children sacrifice themselves and seek death, they cannot support their parents, which is the greatest act of unfilial behavior. What is the point of looking at such a place! It is appropriate to inform the common people not to be misled by ignorant nonsense, and local officials should strictly prohibit any further incidents of self-sacrifice..." From then on, local officials strictly enforced Emperor Kangxi's instructions, rebuilding the fence around the Cliff of Self-Sacrifice and assigning watchmen to guard it, significantly reducing the number of people coming to the Cliff of Self-Sacrifice to commit suicide. Emperor Kangxi's clear opposition to and prevention of this life-harming custom further demonstrated his broad compassion for the common people.

Only bowing to teachers, not kings

In November of Qing Kangxi's 23rd year (1684), Emperor Kangxi returned from his southern tour to Qufu, specifically to pay respects to Confucius. On the 17th day of the eleventh month, accompanied by civil and military officials, Emperor Kangxi visited the Confucius Temple, performing the grand ritual of three kneels and nine bows before the statue of Confucius. In the eulogy, he praised Confucius: "Revered teacher, your wisdom matches the primordial transformation, gathering the essence of sages. You have opened up thousands of years of civilization, setting the standard for hundreds of kings; forever shining brightly, none fail to admire and revere you."

After leaving the Confucius Temple, Emperor Kangxi went to the Confucius Cemetery to pay respects at Confucius's tomb. He should have performed another one kneel and three bows ritual, but when he reached Confucius's tomb, he stood there for a long time without kneeling. At this moment, Kong Shangren, the official guiding the emperor (a playwright and author of "The Peach Blossom Fan"), noticed the emperor's intention and quickly came up with a plan. He led Emperor Kangxi aside to rest for a moment, then brought him back to Confucius's tomb, where he respectfully performed the one kneel and three bows ritual. In response, the officials present were greatly puzzled, and later Kong Yuqi, the Duke Yansheng, also asked Kong Shangren about it. Kong Shangren explained: Initially, Emperor Kangxi did not bow because he saw the tombstone inscribed with "Great Accomplished Sage King of Literature" written in seal script by the famous calligrapher Huang Yangzheng in the eighth year of the Ming Zhengtong era. Emperor Kangxi believed that there is no second sun in the sky, and no second king in the country. As the ruler of a nation, he could not bow to another king. He came to Qufu to learn from the teacher, not to bow to the king. For this reason, he let Emperor Kangxi rest aside while he hurriedly covered the "king" character on the tombstone with yellow silk. When Emperor Kangxi came to the tomb again, he no longer saw the "king" character, so he paid his respects to the revered teacher.

Emperor Kangxi, as the Son of Heaven, showed such respect for teachers and valued the way of learning. The subtleties behind this are quite thought-provoking...
