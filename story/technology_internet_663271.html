The vast majority of chemical reactions belong to oxidation-reduction reactions, and thus ozone can react with almost all substances, especially various bacteria, viruses, etc. The principle of ozone disinfection in water is that ozone produces an oxidation-reduction reaction in water. This process generates highly oxidative single-atom oxygen (O) and hydroxyl radicals (OH). Microorganisms in the water not only directly react with dissolved ozone but also indirectly react with the hydroxyl radicals (OH) generated by the decomposition of ozone. Hydroxyl radicals (OH) are strong oxidants and catalysts that can cause organic matter to undergo chain reactions. These reactions are extremely rapid and have a very strong killing effect on various pathogenic microorganisms. Single-atom oxygen (O) also has strong oxidative capabilities and exhibits powerful destructive force against resilient microorganisms such as viruses and spores.

The mechanism of ozone sterilization involves destroying the structure of microbial membranes through oxidative action. Ozone first acts on the cell membrane, causing damage to its components and leading to metabolic disorders. As ozone continues to penetrate, it crosses the membrane and destroys lipoproteins and lipopolysaccharides inside the membrane, altering the permeability of the cell and eventually causing the cell to dissolve and die. In the case of viruses, ozone is believed to directly destroy their ribonucleic acid (RNA) or DNA through oxidative action, which is a bacteriolytic sterilization process and is quite thorough.

In space, ozone gas can eliminate mold and odors from the air and items, degrade harmful substances, and decompose formaldehyde, benzene compounds, sulfides, carbon monoxide, ethylene, etc., oxidizing them into harmless substances. The final products of all ozonation chemical reactions are H2O, CO2, and O2. Additionally, as a transient substance (a state that requires external energy to exist), ozone will decompose and revert to naturally occurring oxygen after a certain period (half-life) when the external electric field stops supplying energy. This makes it a disinfection method without secondary contamination.
