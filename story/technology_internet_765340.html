What! Why doesn't Amazon acquire Pinterest yet!

by anonymous on 2013-08-12 21:58:47

Bezos spent $250 million to acquire The Washington Post. Prior to this, Amazon also invested $56 million in LivingSocial, and further back in 2009, Amazon acquired Zappos for $847 million. Of course, these aren't all of Amazon's acquisitions. But one thing that has puzzled many is: why hasn't Amazon acquired Pinterest?

If you've been following the latest news, you might say that Amazon just launched a Pinterest-like collection feature called Collections, right? Similar to Pinterest, Collections allows users to "pin" and categorize images found online, adding them into various lists. However, the question remains: can it really become the next Pinterest? I think it's basically impossible.

What gives Pinterest the confidence to say "Yes!" to Amazon?

In terms of user numbers, it ranks 15th nationwide as a website, with a monthly user base reaching 48.7 million.

It holds a 40% share in mobile social media. Among them, Facebook's mobile business occupies over 50% of consumer time, while Pinterest's share reaches 48%.

In terms of user demographics, the majority of Pinterest users are between 30 and 49 years old, an age group that typically possesses substantial financial resources. Additionally, its users are predominantly female, and merchants know that women usually control household spending on clothing, home goods, and gifts—precisely the categories Pinterest focuses on most.

Pinterest currently relatively weakens shopping monetization. It does not wish to commercialize itself too quickly, which could lead to some anti-commercial users leaving. More importantly, although Pinterest is already quite large, it still needs to accumulate a great deal of information at this stage to build its own interest graph.

Amazon's Fatal Attraction to Pinterest

1. Amazon is the leader in U.S. e-commerce retail. If it could combine with Amazon, it would undoubtedly better leverage its advantages, making its monetization process smoother and more reasonable.

2. Amazon's excellence in recommendations is well-known. Entering Amazon's embrace would certainly benefit from Amazon's strong technical capabilities, enabling more attractive related recommendations to users.

3. Amazon often maintains the independence of the companies it acquires, such as Zappos, IMDb, Alexa, etc. This would be highly attractive to Pinterest's founders and investors.

Borrowing the Path of Pinterest to Secure the Throne of Online Retail Leader

Similarly, Pinterest undoubtedly represents a super-chance for Amazon to consolidate its position as the leading player in online retail. Not only could it gain massive traffic (Pinterest is currently ranked 15th in the U.S.), but for an e-commerce site, this level of traffic is quite considerable.

The most important point is that Pinterest has a vast user interest graph. Amazon's recommendation system is indeed excellent, but it is still largely based on behavior patterns within its own platform, making it difficult to help discover and dig deeper into more preferences and products for Amazon's users. If Amazon could integrate with Pinterest's data, it would undoubtedly be able to offer more attractive recommendations to users, and also optimize its product offerings more reasonably using Pinterest’s data.

At the beginning of this year, Pinterest completed a $200 million round of financing, valuing the company at $2.5 billion. Of course, money is absolutely not an issue for Amazon. The only conceivable obstacle might be Rakuten, the Japanese company that invested $100 million in Pinterest. As a competitor, Rakuten obviously wouldn't want to hand Pinterest over to Amazon willingly. But precisely because of this, Amazon should recognize the importance of Pinterest and move quickly to bring it under its wing.

Otherwise, one day if eBay suddenly announces it has acquired Pinterest, Amazon and Bezos will undoubtedly regret it deeply.
