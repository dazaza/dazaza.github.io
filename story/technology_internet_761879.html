Mass layoffs at Tencent's North American game studios, GREE closes China office

by geekzhang on 2013-05-14 09:16:06

Our sources indicate: the latest round of layoffs at Tencent's North American office has been largely completed, with about 20 employees laid off, accounting for 40% of Tencent's total workforce in North America. This round of layoffs mainly focused on the product department, specifically Tencent’s North American game studios. Currently, Tencent in North America only retains its local business development (BD) team. This means that Tencent's rapid and aggressive acquisitions of a series of game studios in the North American region over the past few years have ended in failure.And just tonight, we learned that Gree, Japan's renowned social gaming company, has decided to close its office in China and lay off all employees. Several games developed by the Chinese team will be handed over to headquarters for continued development and operation. Meanwhile, Gree, which aggressively acquired 17 companies including OpenFeint, Pokelabo, and Funzio to penetrate markets in North America, China, and other regions, has also begun a series of layoffs in the U.S.Ironically, Gree's strategic partner in China is precisely Tencent.The simultaneous layoffs by these two Asian companies, both possessing social and gaming DNA, in their important overseas markets, has cast a cold splash of water on the overheated illusion of the current gaming market. However, upon closer examination, each company has its own reasons for the layoffs and office closures, as well as some common lessons.Tencent's aggressive game acquisitions in North America over the previous years were primarily concentrated in the traditional client-based gaming (PC gaming) sector, almost becoming the main acquisition direction for Tencent's North American investment and M&A department. Similar to its approach in China, Tencent hoped that these American client-based game studios would bring a large number of high-quality games and revenue to Tencent's platform. However, this strategy never proved effective. It wasn't until two years ago that Tencent began gradually acquiring some Silicon Valley startups related to social communication.It is reported that over the past few months, Tencent's investment and M&A department has been quite dissatisfied with the studios acquired in North America and the game product department there. Coupled with the intense competition and shrinking market for client-based games, and the unstoppable growth momentum of the smartphone gaming market, Tencent no longer holds the same advantage in mobile gaming. Pony Ma himself admitted that the high cost of game development has made Tencent less inclined to develop games on its own, instead shifting towards accepting third-party developers through Mobile QQ and WeChat's future open platform.In this situation, teams engaged in traditional client-based game development are inevitably the primary targets for mass layoffs. Moving forward, Tencent's North American team will no longer undertake any product development functions but will fully transition to business development and investment M&A, including expanding partners in North America across both QQ and WeChat lines, as well as investing in early-stage start-ups related to social and communication fields.GREE's current decline is closely tied to the loss of its competitive edge in the smartphone gaming domain. Amir Anvarzadeh, securities sales manager for Asia at private equity firm BGC Partners, believes that GREE and DeNA will be greatly affected by the rise in smartphone coverage. The smartphone gaming market is currently overcrowded. In this scenario, GREE is attempting to enhance the diversity of its game products by acquiring more small development companies.However, this is exactly where the crux of the problem lies for both Tencent and GREE. Games, especially mobile games, are areas that change extremely rapidly. Teams expanded through acquisitions often struggle to perform well in creating new themes and content development. On the contrary, due to the time and resources consumed by internal integration, global interventions and adjustments, along with the rapid changes in the market and the inherent issue of sustainability in producing high-quality game products within the gaming industry, they usually miss out on opportunities to produce even better games, and sometimes can't even maintain the current level of activity and market interest in their existing games.
