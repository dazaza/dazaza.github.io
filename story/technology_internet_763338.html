An analysis of Cook's word choice shows that Apple is on the decline.

by anonymous on 2013-07-30 11:49:52

On July 30, according to Beijing time, I am an Apple fan, and perhaps no one is more of a "fan" than me. My first Apple computer was the best at the time, with 16K RAM, and programs had to be loaded from cassette tapes. When Macs came with floppy disks, I immediately bought one. I have witnessed Apple's resurgence.

Unfortunately, I want to report something. According to analysis, the world's best brand is fading, its luster is disappearing, and no one is adding more brilliance to Apple’s “magic.” If there is someone, it certainly isn't CEO Tim Cook.

This analytical report is based on a technique called "word mapping," which links leaders' word choices with other terms to reveal their worldview and behavioral principles. I've taught "word mapping" courses to several groups, and the feedback has been excellent; everyone says this course should reach more people.

An analysis of Steve Jobs' word choices reveals that he focused on "paying tribute to greatness," emphasizing "things that haven't changed," bringing Apple "back to its core," and returning to "core values." Jobs believed the world "wants to know what Apple is" and "what it stands for." Jobs concentrated on these things while letting go of others: features, boxes (equivalent to computer cases), speed. These "word mappings" revealed his true vision. Jobs also liked to quickly categorize people as "geniuses" or "idiots" and referred to creativity as "gold." He enjoyed using words that start with "S."

There are some people who can define "what Apple is," and they were appreciated by Jobs. People who ignored details or were overwhelmed by the impossible were eliminated. Judging from Jobs' word choices, he was difficult to work with. He relied on certain individuals who pushed things forward in the real world. Some criticized Jobs, saying he didn't allow others to think like him. Not true, he depended on the best supply chain manager: Cook.

When Cook became Apple's CEO, the question became: "Can he Think Different?" Can he think with vision? After several years as CEO, the answer is: not at all.

An analysis of Cook's word choices reveals that he is completely opposite to Jobs. He emphasizes market share, customer satisfaction, numerous new features, better speed, increased battery life, paying developers more money, and more growth. In a recent earnings call, he said, "We're not just standing still... we have many innovations coming out." His word mapping shows that innovation isn't produced; innovation is something used, like a rare resource.

Looking at Cook's statements, there are no guiding principles that could create something from nothing or imagine the unimaginable. The analysis might also criticize other innovative Apple executives, such as design chief Jonathan Ive. At Apple, there might be a team (about 20-150 people) doing Jobs-style work.

The analysis draws another unfortunate conclusion: it seems that when Apple is criticized for losing its original style, its executives effectively become "immune." Philip Schiller's word mapping shows that he employs a "defense mechanism" to deal with criticism, steering himself and others away from the real issues. He and other Apple executives might say, "Look how cool our new stuff is! If it's not innovation, I don't know what is."

That's the problem. Apple once brought themed, transformative innovation, but now its theme supply has run dry.

It looks like Apple still has a few years of magic left. It will use this scarce resource to make existing products better, faster, cheaper, with longer battery life, supporting more displays, etc. It will launch new products based on existing ideas.

However, the Apple that could imagine the unimaginable is gone. The Apple led by core values, bridging technology and art by doing things no one else thought to do, is gone. That Apple has become a thing of the past.

For Apple and its competitors, I have no financial interest, but I have invested emotion into this company. I hope Apple seriously examines itself, prioritizes the future, and begins reshaping Apple. Perhaps, this way, another once-in-a-lifetime leader will emerge, and this oddball will challenge Apple.

(The author of this article, Dave Logan, is a faculty member at the University of Southern California, a management consultant, and a best-selling author. He has written books such as "Tribal Leadership" and "The Three Laws of Performance." He is a partner and co-founder of the management consulting firm CultureSync.)
