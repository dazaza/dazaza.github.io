Amazon Originals: From Underdog to Threat

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 20:54:29

On January 15, Amazon Studios, the film and television studio that Amazon invested in three years ago, aimed to change the monopolistic situation of Hollywood. As a retailer, CEO Jeff Bezos would sacrifice profit to establish business. As someone once said, no matter what field Amazon enters, competitors should be afraid.

The summary of the full article is as follows:

In April this year, a large group of comedians gathered in Portland, Oregon for the Bridgetown Comedy Festival. One morning, most of the actors were still groggy from the previous night's revelry, but the three-person comedy team Grawlix from Denver was an exception. They got up early, turned on their computers, and watched the pilot episode of their comedy "Those Who Can't" broadcast on Amazon.com. Grawlix had never performed on such a broad stage before; during the airing of the show, thousands of viewers made real-time evaluations. Their phones began ringing non-stop with requests for interviews.

This was the kind of success that Amazon Studios hoped to see when it was founded three years ago. The purpose of the studio was to discover film and TV projects outside of Hollywood and let Amazon users decide whether or not to produce them. This idea helped talented people while also collecting valuable data on what content Amazon users liked to watch.

Although "Those Who Can't" received higher user ratings than any previous Amazon pilots, it was far from enough for Amazon Studios. Its ultimate goal was to challenge Netflix, disrupt traditional film and TV production, and convince more people to sign up for the $79 annual Amazing Prime service. Grawlix’s influence was far from enough. Therefore, Amazon invested at the same level as heavyweight film companies like HBO, inviting big-name stars to shoot series. "Alpha House" was Amazon's first fully produced series, a political comedy premiering on Friday starring John Goodman, with Stephen Colbert and Bill Murray making guest appearances.

As one talent scout said, Amazon's entry into the film industry has the potential to make its competitors fear.

An Idealistic Beginning

Roy Price founded Amazon Studios in November 2010, positioning it as "the film studio of the future." At present, the organization and decision-making in the film industry are all concentrated in Hollywood. Amazon Studios hoped to find unique voices.

Anyone could participate; Price invited users to submit scripts and offer suggestions for revisions. Good scripts could be handed over to Warner Brothers Pictures for filming. If the film premiered in theaters, the creator could receive $200,000. If the box office reached $60 million, the creator could receive an additional $400,000 bonus. However, Warner Brothers Pictures has yet to receive a decent script.

In addition, although Amazon obtained some data, such as the fact that most popular scripts tend to be comedies, the premise of relying on website users was incorrect. Users of a shopping site cannot be equated with audiences. People who both enjoy online shopping and reading scripts are even fewer.

Industry insiders pointed out that although Amazon users have a large base, they do not represent the tastes of audiences. Many people don’t know what they want until they see it. AMC's "Breaking Bad" is an example. A story about a middle-aged high school chemistry teacher who turns to drug manufacturing after being diagnosed with cancer—would Amazon users like this story if they read the script first?

Lagging Behind Netflix

In early 2012, Amazon Studios lagged behind Netflix in original content. Netflix spent more than $100 million, surpassing HBO, to secure the broadcasting rights for two seasons of "House of Cards." Netflix's first original TV series, "Lilyhammer," premiered in February. Netflix CEO Reed Hastings told investors that original shows accounted for 15% of the company's content budget, three times that of Amazon Studios. Hastings also remarked that Amazon's service was "confusingly chaotic."

Although Price did not specify why Amazon changed its strategy, Amazon Studios clearly became much more professional thereafter. Many of the new recruits Price brought in had experience working at places like 20th Century Fox and Sony Pictures.

Price stated: "We decided to implement a dual approach of traditional production processes and receiving ideas online, recruiting top talents and producers. In terms of TV, we aim to create the best TV series and open up the production process."

Data-Driven Business

Despite spending less than Netflix, for instance, Netflix spent $100 million on "House of Cards," Amazon can defeat Netflix in data utilization. Amazon can obtain data from Amazon.com, understanding what movies and TV shows users like to purchase. Additionally, IMDB.com, which Amazon acquired in 1998, can also provide it with data.

During a keynote speech in October, Price mentioned that since Amazon users like "Breaking Bad" and "Downton Abbey," Amazon might produce a TV series about aristocratic drug dealers in Surrey.

Therefore, Amazon deeply mined these data. In April this year, Amazon launched 14 pilot episodes for users to watch and rate. Amazon used this to decide whether to produce corresponding full-season series.

At the Core, a Retailer

The biggest difference between Amazon and its competitors lies in its identity. At its core, Amazon is a retailer. It is well known that CEO Bezos would sacrifice profit to establish business. If Amazon decides to enter an industry, the big players in that industry will also worry.

Amazon Studio's long-term strategy will rely on Prime, which has already become Amazon's strongest weapon. Analysts estimate that Amazon Prime users spend 2-4 times more than non-Prime users. Prime users can watch Amazon's original series for free, attracting new users.

In addition, Amazon allows everyone to watch the first three episodes of a series for free. If they wish to continue watching, they must register as Prime users.

Next, Amazon aims to become a true competitor to Netflix by producing avant-garde series. Netflix has already gained prominence in Hollywood through this method. "House of Cards" won Netflix three Emmy Awards. Amazon also hopes to gain such honors. (Keshan)
