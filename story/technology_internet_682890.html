Does the industry you choose for entrepreneurship necessarily have to have a high profit margin?

[Return on Equity = Profit Margin × Asset Turnover Rate]. To start a business, you must understand this formula, otherwise, you'll die without knowing why.

[Net Assets] is your total assets minus liabilities. Making 100 million yuan a year might seem like an astronomical figure to you, making you ecstatic, but if you were the CEO of General Electric, the board would berate you until you bled. Regardless of how much you earn, first look at how much capital you used to generate these profits. Entrepreneurs typically have limited net assets and need to significantly increase their annual return rate. How can we improve it?

Route one: Profit Margin. We all love selling high-profit items, and so do our competitors, who always sell them cheaper than us, forcing us to lower prices further. Hence, profit margins aren't easily increased.

This leads us to Route two [Asset Turnover Rate]. This is calculated by dividing turnover by assets. Everyone understands turnover, but what are "assets"? Let's look at an example:

Suppose I bought a pair of shoes at Beijing Yansha Friendship Mall, a British brand costing over 3,000 yuan. Entrepreneur A obtained the agency rights for this brand in mainland China, while Entrepreneur B chose to sell shoe polish. Both started with 500,000 yuan, but differently, B first spent 400,000 yuan buying a small shoe polish factory, producing mid-to-high-end shoe polish, then rented a counter in a high-end mall to sell.

A sells British shoes, which have high costs. Ordering a batch of shoes from Britain cost him 400,000 yuan. Each shoe has an average purchase price of 1,500 yuan and a retail price of 3,000 yuan, selling three pairs per day. The daily counter rent is 1,000 yuan, so the daily profit is 9,000 - 4,500 - 1,000 = 3,500 yuan. The profit margin is thus 3,500/9,000, approximately 39% (ignoring labor costs).

B sells 100 tubes of shoe polish daily, each costing 5 yuan to produce and retailing for 20 yuan. The daily counter rent is 600 yuan, yielding a daily profit of around 900 yuan. B's shoe polish factory incurs daily expenses of about 800 yuan for electricity, water, and factory costs, leaving a daily net profit of 100 yuan, with a profit margin of about 5%.

How does B's 5% compare to A's 39%? But is B really that pitiful?

A initially used 400,000 yuan to buy goods, getting approximately 267 pairs of shoes. That's not many because even a men's shoe counter needs at least a dozen styles, with several sizes for each style! These 267 pairs of shoes are barely enough to sell in one mall. So, how long will it take A to earn the 400,000 yuan needed for sales in a second mall? 400,000 / (3,500 × 30 days) ≈ 4 months.

Now let's look at B. B invested the 400,000 yuan in the shoe polish factory and bears its daily operating costs. How much inventory of shoe polish does one mall counter need? Assuming it needs 500 tubes, (500 tubes × 5 yuan cost) / 100 yuan daily net profit = 25 days, meaning B's profit from one counter is enough to open a new counter in less than a month.

This case illustrates that we can use a high turnover rate to offset a low profit margin.

Now let's enter the "entrepreneurship battlefield."

In my neighborhood, there's a small convenience store. If I'm cooking eggs and find I'm out of scallions, I call, and five minutes later, a 50-cent scallion is delivered to my door. No salt or eggs? Quick delivery. Basically, I've become lazy enough that if I want some fruit in the evening, I just call and sometimes don't even care what kind of fruit I get, asking, "What fruits did you stock today? Send me whatever you have."

You might say, "Surely the 50 cents from selling a few bundles of scallions doesn't make much money, right? The unit price and profit margin are too low! Even with a higher turnover rate, it seems far from reaching the goal of 10 million yuan?"

In fact, the core strategy of this store is to "make customers lazy." Scallions and toilet paper might have thin profit margins, but other items have high ones! For instance, wine—this small convenience store has two walls full of imported wines ranging from 200 to 600 yuan, mostly everyday table wines. There are many foreigners in this neighborhood, and for middle-class Europeans and Americans, wine is a daily necessity. The profit from a single bottle of wine far exceeds that from a truckload of leeks, right?

Besides wine, there are various kinds of cheese and ready-to-eat foods.

This is the "pressure principle" of entrepreneurship—though small and not very powerful, with limited customers, the served group is extremely precise, creating immense "pressure."

In fact, the owner of this small store has already opened several branches, all located in the most upscale residential communities in Beijing. Both profit and turnover rates are high!

The boss is highly dedicated and shrewd. Even though these stores carry different goods, that's because the residents of different housing developments are clearly grouped. Some developments have more Europeans, others more Japanese, and still others are practically Korean neighborhoods. Targeting different consumption characteristics, she constantly adjusts her product categories, firmly grasping the spending habits of each community. By adopting the strategy of "making customers lazy" through services like delivering a single scallion (theoretically, delivering just one scallion is a loss-making transaction), she gradually locks in the bulk daily consumption of the housing development's residents.

Narrow and precise target customers, willing to conduct a few loss-making transactions to lock in customers, excellent service, slightly higher pricing (since target customers are insensitive to prices), this is the kind of business everyone wants to do. Sure enough, another convenience store using a similar strategy moved into the building below mine!

This new convenience store engaged in price competition! You sell a scallion for 50 cents, they sell it for 39 cents. Although consumers in this neighborhood are not sensitive to price, everyone prefers cheaper options, especially for standardized products like Nongfu Spring water, Coca-Cola, and Kleenex tissues. Indeed, the old saying holds true: "No loyalty can withstand a five-cent discount."

Low barriers mean competitors can quickly follow and copy. Plainly speaking, if a three-year-old finds a gold ingot, who wouldn't want to grab it? If Mike Tyson holds a gold ingot, try grabbing it from him! Or Liu Xiang holding one, try catching up with him! So the small convenience store faces annoying price competition. Either go the way of Tyson, competing on price and fighting to the death (so-called "hurting the enemy a thousand while losing eight hundred"), or go the way of Liu Xiang, jumping away where the opponent can't even find you.

The convenience store owner decided to adopt the Liu Xiang strategy—the capacity enhancement strategy. She realized that her existing customer base, especially her core customers, were mostly high-income, highly educated individuals aged between thirty and fifty. These people have a significant characteristic—they're afraid of dying! More so than the average person. Thus, the convenience store began promoting the "green" label, such as green vegetables. Additionally, this store had an advantage in wine sales that the other convenience store couldn't match yet! Selling wine requires knowledgeable staff. Training a wine promoter usually takes several months of intensive training: origin? Vintage? Style? Blackberry flavor or chocolate notes? Characteristics of grape varieties?... These things the other convenience store can't handle immediately.

See, this is where capability shows itself—by extending and creating new opportunities, bringing new capabilities.

Moreover, the store owner frequently chats with customers, discovering that some customers have had broken light bulbs for half a month without time to replace them; others haven't had time to pay phone bills or recharge gas cards... The target customers are all "rich but busy" people! This is quite normal. At this point, the convenience store decided to launch new services:

Replacing light bulbs for customers, paying phone bills, recharging gas cards... Through the communication skills and trust cultivated by selling wine, and without additional charges (of course, the cost of the bulb is paid by the customer), the convenience store expanded the meaning of "convenience" from goods to services. This stage, although the services themselves don't make money, significantly increases "transaction opportunities," at least boosting the sales of original items like toilet paper and dish soap. When picking up your electric card, you notice you're out of dish soap, and when returning the charged card in the evening, the store "incidentally" brings you a bottle of dish soap...

Do you see the trick? The convenience store becomes like Ctrip. Booking hotels through Ctrip is cheaper than going directly to the hotel, and Ctrip also earns commissions from the hotel. When the convenience store has "bulk orders," it can indeed squeeze the laundry shops in the neighborhood... and pass a bit of the profit to the consumer.

Through this mindset, the convenience store suddenly opens up its financial channels: hourly cleaning services, shoe maintenance... Having customer resources can be converted into profit.

We can also call the convenience store's approach the "occupying the telephone number strategy." Because most of its business begins with a phone number. Whoever occupies that number monopolizes customer resources and gains strong bargaining power: taking half the profits from laundry shops and housekeeping services! The laundry shop owner has to swallow his anger! Look at the relationship between Gome and appliance manufacturers, and you'll understand.

But why didn't the competitors learn these tricks at the beginning? Note, when the situation isn't clear, competitors don't copy everything blindly! Following strategies only work perfectly if they're flawless.

When competitors fully understand, the "first-mover advantage" often forms a barrier. Competitors trying to copy are too late. Mimicking any single link is unrealistic because it's useless, and simulating the entire value chain is even less realistic because the simulator needs to start from a similar position and have similar resources as the simulated party, right?

Do you believe large companies have developed perfectly, without error, and rapidly reached today's success?! Give yourself a big slap to wake up! In the early stages of entrepreneurship, the worst thing is being completely immersed in the logical beauty of "strategic planning." Is strategy merely something you "think" and "see"? Strategy is the result of proper pre-planning, learning while doing, and summarizing afterward to improve! If the transformation from a convenience store to a personal service assistant is a valid strategic hypothesis, every step, whether big or small, is part of the process of making the strategy more specific.

Making 1 million yuan might only require seizing one or two opportunities. But truly wanting to make 10 million yuan relies solely on one or two "ideas" isn't reliable. The corporate strategy and financial knowledge we've discussed, along with the development path of the convenience store, are laying the groundwork for achieving 10 million yuan. Deeply understanding the commercial logic behind all this, is 10 million yuan still far away from you?
