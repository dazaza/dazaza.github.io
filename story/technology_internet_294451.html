Market conditions are brewing structural changes＿Stock Forum

by fj0953210 on 2009-12-05 22:14:58

First, the phenomenon of polarization in the market is relatively serious, which is also quite evident in the stock index. Although the Shanghai Composite Index has returned to near the 3300-point major level, the SME Board Index has already entered a strong pressure zone around 5500 points. Therefore, regarding the divergence in indices, it is very likely that future funds will flow into blue-chip stocks with relatively low prices.

Secondly, on the news front, Shenzhen Stock Exchange has started to strictly control the excessive speculation of small and medium-sized market capitalization stocks using Jifeng Agricultural Machinery as an example, causing panic in the market. The emergence of this news not only restrains excessive operations by speculative funds but can also reduce the involvement of follower funds.

Finally, from the perspective of valuation, industries that have recovered first due to national preferential policies may face risks when these policies are phased out. Meanwhile, sectors like home appliances and automobiles, although unscathed in this sudden crisis, experienced a policy-induced premature consumption boom, making it difficult to sustain future profits effectively. The corresponding stock prices of such sectors should already be in a risky area. However, there are still some undervalued stocks worth exploring, mostly concentrated among weighted blue-chip stocks. Thus, the market trend is expected to shift towards a "20-80" pattern dominated by these stocks.

In conclusion, structural changes have occurred in the overall development of the market. There is a high probability that the market will transform into one led by mutual fund heavyweights and undervalued blue-chip stocks. Therefore, investors are advised to change their previous strategies and shift focus to undervalued blue-chip stocks with potential for catch-up growth.

The procedures for buying and selling stocks are as follows: When trading stocks, you must specify whether you're buying or selling, along with the name (or code), quantity, and price of the stock. Additionally, any order is only valid for the day it is placed. The content of the order includes the abbreviation (code) of the stock you want to trade, the quantity, and the price at which you wish to buy or sell. Stock abbreviations typically consist of three to four Chinese characters, while stock codes are six digits long. The stock code and abbreviation must match during the transaction process. Moreover, there are specific regulations regarding the quantity of stocks traded: the quantity for purchasing stocks must be a whole multiple of 100, but the quantity for selling does not need to follow this rule.

There are four methods of placing orders: counter submission, telephone automatic order, computer automatic order, and remote terminal order.
