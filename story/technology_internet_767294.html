Should Apple give developers the option to charge for upgrades?

by anonymous on 2013-10-11 19:20:49

When iOS 7 was first introduced, many applications underwent redesigns specifically for iOS 7. However, a controversial issue arose as many of these apps no longer offered free upgrades to existing users; instead, they had to repurchase the app.

A prime example of this is the To-Do application Clear. When its new version was released, the company informed users on its website that the old version would no longer receive updates and was subsequently removed from the App Store.

This led to a discussion surrounding whether consumers should pay for app upgrades.

Weeks later, due to strong public feedback, the Clear development team decided to compromise: the previously removed old version of Clear was re-listed under its original name with all features of the new version; the so-called "new version" was renamed Clear+, differing mainly in that it was a universal version supporting both iPad and iPhone.

In previous discussions, some readers believed that while Clear's actions were somewhat unethical, developers have a tough job too, as users enjoy long-term free maintenance and updates after a one-time payment — something unimaginable on PCs.

Thus, some people suggested: wouldn't it be a great solution if Apple gave developers the option to charge for upgrades?

Let’s look at a set of data from the analytics company Distimo in March this year. It showed that IAP (in-app purchases) accounted for 71% of total revenue in the U.S. App Store, compared to around 50% a year ago. In Hong Kong, mainland China, South Korea, Japan, and other Asian countries, IAP makes up over 90% of the App Store's total revenue.

Some believe that paid apps are dead.

Returning to the question of whether Apple should allow developers to decide whether upgrades should be charged for, because the "living Lei Feng" phenomenon tells us (many applications are not expensive but bear the long-term obligation of updates), charging apps may increasingly struggle to maintain developer income.

If developers' long-term interests are not sustained, it is detrimental to the ecosystem.

Some paid apps have been consistently offering free updates since their debut on the App Store (such as the radio application TuneIn Radio that I personally admire). While this wins them good reputations, it undoubtedly stagnates their income.

However, if an app stops updating, it essentially hands over its market share to emerging competitors. This is a dilemma.

The "cutting off retreat" style of upgrade adopted by Clear is a roundabout way of charging for upgrades used by many paid applications, but its effectiveness is clearly not good.

Actually, paid upgrades are quite common on PCs. The advantage of paid upgrades is that developers' interests are protected, and with a reward mechanism in place, they are more willing to invest greater effort into app updates. Meanwhile, users who purchased the previous version can get a relatively discounted price for upgrading to the new version. Otherwise, a paid app promising lifetime updates is unsustainable for developers. Such negative emotions will eventually reflect in the quality of the app, and situations like Clear's, where users get confused and force the old app to be taken down, could be avoided.

Currently, we see a trend: if paid apps on the App Store continue to use the current fixed-price model, we will likely see more and more free + in-app purchase apps in the future.

But can such a model maintain the overall quality of applications? That's questionable.
