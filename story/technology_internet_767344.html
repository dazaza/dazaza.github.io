Microsoft's Headache: How Should Win8 Take Over XP's Territory

by anonymous on 2013-10-12 19:20:48

By Zhou Pu

Not long ago, I advised a friend who was about to go crazy from being tormented by Windows XP: "Look, your computer keeps blue-screening and freezing, and it takes ten minutes just to send an email. Why don't you switch to a new system?" Unexpectedly, she remained quite open-minded: "It's too much trouble, and I think XP is still fine and very usable!" It's strange, as she was complaining the second before, but when it came to changing systems, she couldn't bring herself to do it. This seems to be a common mindset among many XP users. According to statistics from mobile management company Fiberlink, half of the world's laptops are still running on XP. This decade-old system, why does it still have so many loyal fans? Why don't these people switch to Win8 for a better experience? Is it simply because they're afraid of the hassle?

Current Situation: Windows XP Dominates Second, Third, and Fourth-Tier Cities

During the National Day holiday, while visiting relatives in a southern county, I discovered that my family was still using the XP system. When I asked her why she didn't upgrade, she said: "There's no need. I just browse Taobao, watch videos, and chat on QQ. XP is enough." "Don't you play games?" "I only play QQ games, like Landlord, and all my friends do the same." (Note: Her browser is 360 Browser)

Afterwards, I wandered around the county and found that big and small internet cafes, photo sticker shops, restaurants, photography studios... were indeed all using the XP system. For those users who don't play large games, XP already meets their basic office, communication, and entertainment needs. Therefore, many novice users in the county feel no need to upgrade to Win7 or Win8.

In addition, compared to Win7 which was released in 2009, XP which hit the market in 2003 is more suitable for older computers. XP caught the wave of Chinese buying computers, and for many Chinese, their first computer had the XP system. To this day, those old machines might struggle to run Win7, but XP runs smoothly. This issue is similar to the recent iOS7 problem: upgrading iPhone4 to iOS7 would make it extremely slow, staying at iOS5 would be more appropriate.

According to the IT field's "Andy-Bill Law": Software and games continuously generate demand, hardware digests these demands through technological innovation, this process stimulates users to keep consuming on computers, thus making the computer industry flourish... However, if software products cannot generate demand, what kind of scenario will emerge? As one can imagine, users' satisfaction with XP naturally leads to Win7 and Win8 not being very attractive to users, causing many people to stubbornly believe: "XP is good enough!"

Clarification: People Prefer Spending Five Thousand Yuan on Phones Rather Than Two Hundred Yuan on System Upgrades?

No matter which industry, it's always "vendors want to make money, users want to save money." The term "saving money" doesn't mean having no money, but rather spending it where it's "worthwhile," like my relative who refuses to change systems. She believes mobile devices are important, so she has been using an iPhone and now wants to buy the latest Samsung large-screen phone. She also owns two iPads.

The development pattern of the IT industry is as follows: If predecessors lack something, we provide it; if predecessors have it, we improve it; if predecessors improve it, we make it more human-centric. Due to the development of mobile internet technology, the extended needs of computers in terms of human-centric design have actually been met by mobile devices. When most people get used to playing with their phones before sleeping, mobile devices have already replaced some functions of computers with their natural portability and privacy. This is the bottleneck faced by desktop internet and the reason for the prosperity of mobile devices.

Let's return to the small county: precisely because of the thriving local mobile phone industry, the functional needs of ordinary users for computers are easily satisfied, and their sensitivity to the operation experience of computers is far less than before. "If XP can barely work, then let it be, anyway, there are phones and tablets!" said the handsome guy from the neighboring barber shop.

The sorrow of XP system upgrades isn't because Win7 and Win8 aren't excellent enough, but because the product form of computers can no longer fully meet users' online needs. When users' focus in the digital domain shifts, how could they possibly invest as much cost in computers as they did before?

Insight: The Dying XP is Still Lucrative - Microsoft's Upgrade Policy Needs Caution

Of course, the XP system won't continue to dominate forever. Because the continuous development of mobile devices will gradually change people's usage habits. Once new technology transforms quantitatively into qualitative changes, that's the time when the old computer usage habits are completely altered, and also when the entire computer industry is overturned. Microsoft obviously can't sit idly by waiting for its fate to be reshuffled, so it must push for the popularization of new systems as much as possible, ensuring its products align with the development trends of user habits.

Starting from April 8th next year, Microsoft will no longer release patches or security updates for the Windows XP system, at which point the XP system will face significant risks. Clearly, Microsoft has chosen to bid farewell to XP using "security," the most fundamental and eternal topic. But on the other hand, will 360 Security Guard allow its vast number of users to fall into irreversible ruin?

Risk One of XP Iteration: If Completely Abandoned, Third-Party Companies Will Take Over Updates

In the small county, accompanying every XP computer is inevitably the free 360 Security Guard. It's no exaggeration to say that free security software has already saved and continues to support all pirated Windows users. With this basic guarantee, most pirated users are indifferent to the issue of operating system updates. If Microsoft decides to completely abandon XP, it must handle public relations well with third-party software developers. Otherwise, the originally intended warning effect of "stopping updates" will not function.

Risk Two of XP Iteration: If Too Aggressive, It Will Create Opportunities for Open Source Systems

Not long ago, the German government provided XP users with free Ubuntu system CDs. If Microsoft becomes too aggressive and negatively impacts user experience, the vacuum period after the withdrawal of the XP system will create opportunities for other competitors.

I believe that Microsoft must adopt a combination of soft and hard approaches regarding the replacement of XP. A policy of sheer rigidity will only result in wasted effort without gaining favor.

Speculation: Microsoft's Five Strategies to Reclaim the XP Territory with Win8

Making Win8 replace XP isn't a simple problem that can be solved by merely enhancing software functionality and performance. From my observations, XP users generally use computers to satisfy basic office and entertainment needs, while higher-level personalized experiences are handled by mobile devices. This fragmented usage habit is different from the situation in 2003. Therefore, emphasizing that Win8 is "smoother and has more features" alone cannot impress non-enthusiast users. To ensure Win8 successfully takes over, Microsoft should focus on the following directions:

1. Simplify the Upgrade Method

To prevent piracy, Microsoft hasn't launched a CD version of Win8 in China. However, most XP users still perceive "reinstalling the system" as "buying a CD." If Microsoft wants to reach those inexperienced XP users, it must strengthen the promotion of USB installation and online upgrade methods. Otherwise, it cannot form a closed loop of "hearing—purchasing—installing—using."

2. Launch a Lite Version of Win8

The latest Windows 8.1 requires approximately 15GB of space just for system files, which is not a small number for XP users. I believe Microsoft could try launching a lite version of Windows 8, allowing XP users to try an incomplete version of the new interface and partial new features. Users who enjoy the experience may then develop interest in replacing old equipment.

3. Collaborate with Third-Party Companies

Previously, to eliminate outdated IE6 browsers, Microsoft conducted extensive publicity campaigns. Many third-party websites responded to Microsoft's call and collectively decided not to optimize for IE6 browsers, thereby "forcing" netizens to upgrade to IE8 or switch to other browsers.

Analogously, regarding the XP issue, Microsoft needs to collaborate closely with other software vendors such as browser companies, security software firms, and game companies to jointly phase out XP.

4. More Robust Channel Strategy

Although electronic digital cities have become desolate in first-tier cities, they remain the preferred places for many families in second, third, and fourth-tier towns when they consider changing systems or buying computers. Most novice users are willing to listen to the recommendations and guidance of digital city clerks. In a sense, the salespeople in digital cities are "opinion leaders," and this channel still holds considerable value. Thus, convincing the salespeople in second and third-tier city digital cities to promote Win8 is the first step in winning over XP users.

5. Multi-platform Linked Cloud Services

Selling software solely through heavy advertising is outdated. What can replace XP is not just the standalone Win8 system, but a comprehensive cloud service solution. Only the multi-screen synchronized "cloud" mode can enable users to feel the charm of new technology, thereby stimulating new consumer desires.

Microsoft has also realized this point. Judging from its recent acquisition of Nokia, this software company has targeted the active consumer domain of "mobile phones." Microsoft will open up the user market through Nokia's mid-to-low-end phones, promoting Windows services via mobile terminals, driving Office series software, IE browser, Skydrive, Bing search to become full-platform products. Then, through the extension of these services, it will encourage users to upgrade to new computers.

Fortunately, most XP users have a high brand recognition for Nokia. Microsoft's strategy of "phones pushing services, services promoting upgrades" has a chance to succeed.

I believe that the final adoption rate of Windows 8 will be directly proportional to the sales of Windows Phone smartphones and Xbox gaming consoles. Therefore, the previously best-selling low-end WP phone, Lumia520, bears a heavy responsibility.
