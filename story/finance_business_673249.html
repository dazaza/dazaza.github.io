iPad trademark Lin Shuhao another war of domain name registration

by xwkj0983 on 2012-02-23 14:55:25

The dispute over the iPad trademark between Apple and Shenzhen Proview is escalating. Although the focus is on the trademark registration dispute, the issues reflected go beyond just the battle for trademark squatting. After an investigation, the Yangcheng Evening News learned that with the rapid development of e-commerce, domain name squatting has also become a trend, and even a new way to make money. If this chaos is not curbed, it may become another kind of "trademark dispute."

The cost of registering a domain name is not high. When the reporter queried trademarks on China's Trademark website, entering "iPad" as the trademark name resulted in 47 entries. Among these, there were 2 entries from Proview Technology (Shenzhen) Co., Ltd., while Apple had 16 entries covering 9 major categories including computer products, sports goods, leather, etc. In addition, other companies have also registered the iPad trademark. Records show that Proview applied for the iPad trademark on January 10, 2000, with exclusive rights lasting until June 20, 2021.

Yang Rongshan, chairman of Proview Technology, told the Yangcheng Evening News on the afternoon of January 16 that Proview began developing personal computers similar to tablet computers as early as 1998. Later, they launched a computer named iPad, which was once sold in Europe, South America, and other countries and regions, but production was later discontinued. However, the trademark has been retained. According to the trademark network data, Apple also registered and filed the iPad trademark, first using the trademark in July 2009, but did not complete the transfer until July 2011. Legal experts said that due to the continuous emergence of electronic products in recent years, it has also triggered a wave of trademark squatting in China.

In fact, besides trademarks, the internet domain name squatting is also in full swing. According to the latest statistics from the international statistical agency "webhosting.info", by January this year, the total number of ".com" domain registrations exceeded 100 million, with China ranking second globally in terms of domain registration volume. The reporter found on China Wanwang that the fees for English domain names range from 88 yuan to 500 yuan per year, while the fee for Chinese domain names ranges from 280 yuan/year to 480 yuan/year.

Among the 17 English domains, the familiar ".com" and ".cn" are the most popular domains in China, with fees at 139 yuan/year and 99 yuan/year respectively. The highest fee is for the ".tv" media domain, which will be the mainstream domain in the broadband era, costing 500 yuan per year.

"The cost of domain registration is not high," said a relevant person in charge of Wanwang. The renewal fee for the second year is the same as the first year, and there may also be three-year or five-year bundled purchases, which would be cheaper. This person also revealed that once a domain name is registered, it is protected within the time limit, even if no operations are performed on the domain name.

Even the name "Lin Shuhao" has been squatted. In 2009, PPLive acquired the domain name "pptv.com" for a high price of 1.8 million yuan, while the original owner only paid 3800 dollars to obtain it, resulting in a nearly 70-fold increase in price upon resale. It is understood that angel investor Cai Wensheng has handled countless domain sales over the years, from "g.cn", "520.com" to his self-founded "265.com". Although prices have never been disclosed, rumors suggest that "265.com" alone cashed out at least 20 million US dollars. Last year, the domain name "jingdong.com", which has the same pinyin as JD.com, was purchased by JD Mall for 3 million yuan. Last year, it was rumored that Apple acquired the domain name "icloud.com" for 4.5 million US dollars.

As Apple is embroiled in a trademark dispute, Facebook, which has yet to enter the Chinese market and does not have an official Chinese name, has already registered dozens of various category trademarks in mainland China. According to records on China's Trademark Network, Facebook has registered a total of 61 trademarks, with registration times ranging from March 2006 to April 2011. The trademark names include THEFACEBOOK, FACEBOOK, F logo, and possible phonetic Chinese names such as Fei Si Bo, Fei Si Bo Ke, Fei Si Bu Ke, etc. From the international classification numbers, they cover community networks, electronic magazines, software, search engines, games, photo sharing, and even men's and women's clothing categories.

Even NBA star Lin Shuhao is pursued in the domain registration field. It is reported that domain registrations related to Lin Shuhao include his name domains linshuhao.com/.cn/.com.cn/.net/.org, shuhaolin.com/.net, all of which have been registered. Interestingly, there are also homophonic domains like shuhowlin.com/.cn, shu-how.com, which have also been privately acquired, and the Chinese domain 林书豪.com has also been squatted. As expected, the JeremySHL trademark has also been registered.

Domain squatting is not necessarily profitable. Recently, Taobao Mall suddenly changed its name to "Tianmao", surprising the industry. But Taobao had already preemptively acquired the phonetic domain names "tianmao.com/.com.cn/.cn". The "tianmao.com" domain was transferred by Zhuang Liangji from Xiamen, who sold it last November for 120,000 yuan. Another double-phonetic domain he transferred, "huoxing.com" (Mars), fetched a transfer fee of 5 million yuan, netting him more than 4 million yuan in profit. According to relevant personnel, the value of a domain name is basically determined by how it is used and disseminated, as well as its commercial value and what webpage it is attached to.

An insider revealed that domain investment involves speculative elements, with significant bubbles. After multiple resales, the price may be driven up, but the final buyer may not be able to sell it at a good price, and might even lose everything. Qi Lin, assistant director of the China Internet Network Information Center (CNNIC), once expressed to the media that he firmly opposes and prohibits malicious trademark squatting. Even if malicious registrants bypass strict reviews, enterprises can still seek fair resolutions through arbitration and other legal means when their legitimate civil rights are infringed upon.

Legal experts pointed out that investing in trademarks and domain names requires investors to have foresight and strategic vision. Registering innovative vocabulary domain names may bring legitimate returns. Malicious squatting, however, hopes to gain improper benefits by registering domain names similar or identical to others' trademarks or business names, selling them to related civil rights owners or competitors, or even counterfeiting brand products associated with civil rights owners. Such practices should be stopped.
