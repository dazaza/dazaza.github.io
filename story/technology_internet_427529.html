Surnames are also social characters

Most Chinese people are only familiar with "Hundred Family Surnames," but the related concept of "panda surnames" has quietly become popular on the Internet and has entered online encyclopedic entries. The latest edition of "The Great Dictionary of Chinese Surnames" includes a total of 23,813 Chinese character surnames, among which the longest surname has 10 characters. Directions (East, West, South, North), positions (Up, Down, Left, Right), and daily necessities (Firewood, Rice, Oil, Salt, Soy Sauce, Vinegar, Tea) can all be used as surnames. Currently, over 7,000 surnames are still in use.

At the most recent press conference on language work by the Ministry of Education, Li Yuming, Deputy Director of the National Language Commission, expressed his views on citizens' name selection. He said that characters used in personal names include two parts: one part is for surnames, and the other part is for given names. Due to their historical traditionality, surnames represent a bloodline relationship. Therefore, as long as there is such a surname and at least two generations are using it, the continued use of this surname should be guaranteed. Characters used in personal names are both individual characters and social characters. Individual characters express the wishes parents have for their children and to avoid duplicate names, many obscure characters are used. They are considered social characters because many people need to read and write these names. If the characters used in personal names are real characters with clear pronunciations and meanings, and are not artificially created characters, then they should be realized, primarily through computer technology.

It is reported that during the recent population census registration work, various extremely rare surnames came into the view of census takers, such as "Ah," "Oh," "Ge," "Shi," "Bai," "Qian," etc. There are also surnames named after animals, such as "He" (Crane), "Hou" (Monkey), "Hu" (Tiger), "Huang" (Phoenix), "Que" (Sparrow), etc., http://www.vdisk.cn/down/index/7710062A3268, as well as surnames with martial characteristics, such as "Gun" (Stick), "Bian" (Whip), "Bing" (Soldier), "Ji" (Halberd), etc. These little-known surnames are referred to by netizens as "panda surnames." According to some local census takers, there are hundreds of extremely rare "panda surnames" similar to "Xie" (Some), "Jiao" (Exchange), "Xian" (Immortal), "Chuan" (String), "Bie" (Different), "Ku" (Cry), "Kan" (Watch), etc.

"What is your honorable surname?" "What is your name?" These originally commonplace greetings among the Chinese have now become "profound and unfathomable" for some people. The recent national population census discovered that some people's surnames are as rare as giant pandas, while others choose extremely obscure Chinese characters or artificially created characters for their names. Relevant officials from the National Language Commission recently stated that the determination of citizens' names should follow historical traditions and be convenient for computer input operations.

Using obscure characters in naming causes many troubles

In many banking systems currently, some obscure characters in names cannot be typed out, and some people even use symbols to represent their names, causing inconvenience in daily life. So, how to solve this societal problem? Li Yuming said that if a person's name is very difficult to read and write, it will greatly increase societal burdens. "Therefore, we still hope that young parents, when naming their children, do not choose characters that no one can read, as this will also cause trouble for the child's development. Regarding the situation of characters used in personal names and place names, it is an important part of our social life, especially in an information-based society. The National Language Commission and relevant technical departments will seriously address and solve this issue." By reporter Wang Wei
