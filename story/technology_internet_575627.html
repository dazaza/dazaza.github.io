Do you have bad breath? Are you eating the right foods?

by zangao42 on 2011-11-21 17:00:42

Apart from the well-known "air conditioning sickness", wind-heat, prickly heat and other symptoms, there is another condition that is common in social interactions - bad breath (commonly known as "halitosis"). Firstly, one should eat less "fire-inducing" foods such as sweets, fried foods, ginger, chili peppers, garlic, mutton, dog meat, and lychees.

Liver heat type mainly occurs due to overwork and staying up late which excessively depletes the body, leading to internal heat, commonly seen in people with high work pressure and frequent overtime work. The treatment involves clearing liver fire. A daily dose is recommended. In terms of causation, diseases like nasopharyngitis account for the majority of halitosis cases, and these are not within the scope of dental practice.

Firstly, nutrition should be balanced, eating more vegetables and fruits rich in chlorophyll, such as lettuce and rape; avoiding strong-smelling, stimulating, and hard-to-digest foods. Chinese medicine believes that the occurrence of halitosis is most closely related to the spleen and stomach, often caused by irregular dieting, excessive consumption of spicy or greasy foods, long-term over-fatigue, or staying up late.

To treat effectively, three types of oral malodor need to be distinguished: how to treat halitosis, Dr. Wang suggests first identifying the cause. These foods not only stimulate the gastrointestinal mucosa to produce excessive acid but also easily cause oral malodor due to poor digestion. Gastrointestinal and visceral function damage and endocrine disorders are the main causes of halitosis in most people.

Lung heat type can occur when external "heat" enters through the mouth and nose into the lungs, causing internal heat, commonly seen in people with upper respiratory infections, excessive fluid loss, or those who consume alcohol frequently. Treatment involves clearing lung heat.

According to reports, besides surface-level "halitosis," it may also be a signal of other potential diseases, such as poor oral hygiene, dysfunction of the upper digestive system, oral inflammation, tonsillitis, pharyngitis, rhinitis, sinusitis, smoking, drinking, and greasy diets.

Dr. Wang suggests that if citizens have persistent bad breath, they should go to the hospital for a check-up rather than blindly using toothpaste advertised to eliminate odors on the market.

Clear medicinal teas can help reduce and treat halitosis. Drinking refreshing teas regularly benefits greatly in alleviating and treating halitosis, such as natural herbal teas and mint tea, which are excellent natural remedies.

The actual causes of oral malodor vary widely, but neglecting daily living habits increases the likelihood of its occurrence and persistence. Some cases of halitosis are caused by oral environment or oral diseases, such as poor oral hygiene, plaque, tartar, and calculus accumulation, which are direct causes of bad breath.

In summer, many people enjoy cold drinks, but sugary beverages consumed in excess can lead to oral diseases, another cause of halitosis. Those who consume sweet and spicy foods frequently are prone to bad breath. Although sugar is necessary for the human body, moderate control of sweet food intake is advisable.

If caused by internal organ damage, the condition will worsen and cannot self-heal. Prolonged lack of treatment might lead to severe diseases, and general halitosis products cannot cure this type of halitosis. Traditional Chinese medicine and health teas can only play an adjusting role.

Gastric heat type occurs due to overeating, indigestion, and gastric qi rising, leading to bad breath, commonly seen in people with strong appetites and meat lovers. Treatment focuses on aiding digestion.

Dr. Wang's biggest concern is that many citizens do not realize that oral malodor could be a warning sign of other diseases in the body. They often ignore it or try to solve it by brushing teeth or chewing gum, but this only addresses the symptoms without eliminating the root cause.

For example, patients with chronic gingivitis and periodontitis have swollen gums, bleeding, pus discharge from periodontal pockets, poor oral hygiene, and bacterial action on accumulated plaque, calculus, and tartar. This leads to fermentation and decomposition producing foul-smelling substances like indole sulfur hydrides and amines.

Statistically, 75% of halitosis patients suffer from rhinitis, sinusitis, pharyngitis, and tonsillitis. If these underlying conditions are not promptly identified, latent diseases within the body may take advantage of the situation.

People with damp-heat constitution should consume more cooling and diuretic foods in summer, such as mung beans and cucumbers. Medicinal teas like single-flavor tea, osmanthus tea, chrysanthemum tea, jasmine-mint tea, pomegranate tea, loquat tea, water chestnut tea, bamboo leaf tea, mint tea, hoelen tea, licorice tea, and tangerine peel-ginger tea are beneficial in treating different types of halitosis.

Patients with oral, digestive, and respiratory system diseases that might cause halitosis should seek appropriate treatments actively. Halitosis is often due to anaerobic or facultative anaerobic bacterial infections in the oral cavity or digestive tract, so medications like metronidazole or tinidazole (not exceeding 7 days, contraindicated in mentally ill individuals, pregnant women, breastfeeding women, and those with liver dysfunction) can be used. For halitosis caused by Helicobacter pylori infection, drugs targeting H. pylori eradication can be effective.

Few people visit the hospital solely because of oral malodor, even fewer come for unrelated oral discomforts. Regular check-ups via ENT endoscopic examination systems can effectively detect diseases in the nasal cavity, nasopharynx, soft palate, throat, etc., thereby addressing possible causes of halitosis.

Dr. Wang emphasizes that many patients with nasopharyngeal diseases experience varying degrees of bad breath, and once these underlying issues are resolved, the problem of halitosis is fundamentally addressed.
