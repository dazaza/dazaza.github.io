To investigate the reasons for Apple's success, there may be many aspects, such as Apple's innovation, Apple's appearance design, Apple's hunger marketing model, and even Apple's non-open source policy can be greatly written about.

From Apple's success, we can see a trend, that is, under the increasingly obvious trend of mobile Internet today, the difference and competition of hardware have become secondary competition, while the quality and quantity of mobile applications that can be realized on hardware will determine more about the success or failure of a product. Like iPad, to be honest, I don't think iPad itself is much better than the netbooks that were popular a while ago. But the problem is that the applications available on iPad are too rich. After connecting to the Internet, you can easily pick by clicking an icon. Almost any application you want is available. It's hard for iPad not to be popular.

At the iPhone stage, Apple followed the same pattern. With the launch of iPhone, the App Store was also launched simultaneously. Similarly, the software was pre-installed in the iPhone. Since selection, purchase, and installation can all be conveniently done using the iPhone, and these applications are specifically designed for the iPhone, soon many developers created almost any kind of software. Now there are over ten thousand applications in the App Store. To quote from a recent TV ad for iPhone, "almost anything can be done." And these applications that can do almost anything, on the contrary, stimulated the hot sales of the iPhone. Who wouldn't want to buy a phone that can do almost anything? On the iPhone, Apple almost perfectly combined software and hardware, creating a new myth.
