The history of human enterprise operation is no less than a thousand years, but who has ever seen a company with a thousand-year history? Even a hundred-year-old store is extremely rare. According to reports by the American magazine Fortune, approximately 62% of American companies do not survive beyond 5 years, only 2% of companies last up to 50 years, small and medium-sized enterprises have an average lifespan of less than 7 years, large enterprises have an average lifespan of less than 40 years; some multinational corporations have an average lifespan of 10-12 years; Fortune Global 500 companies have an average lifespan of 40-42 years, and the top 1000 companies have an average lifespan of 30 years.

The success or failure of a company can be attributed to internal and external factors. Externally, this refers to social and governmental environments, where government decisions, policies, lawful administration, and governmental integrity play a life-and-death role for businesses; internally, it refers to product development, personnel management, finance, organizational management capabilities, and marketing. Although such analysis holds true universally, such broad-brush analysis offers no practical meaning for business management, as it neither provides lessons for currently operating enterprises nor prevents them from repeating past mistakes.

After sorting through hundreds of related reports in domestic media over the past few decades, especially studying cases involving private enterprises, we discovered some commonalities. The reasons for the downfall or demise of Chinese private enterprises mainly fall into ten categories. Some are fundamental, having accompanied the growth history of Chinese private enterprises since their inception and continue to severely corrupt their structure today. Others are newly emerging, appearing only in recent years, and some of these new pathogens, like the SARS virus, have strong replicability and expansibility, warranting particular attention from Chinese private entrepreneurs.

**First Death of Chinese Private Enterprises: Death by Smooth Sailing**

Anyone who drives knows that the smoother and wider the road, the more likely problems occur while driving. Because the road is too smooth, the view too open, people tend to become complacent. Therefore, experienced designers intentionally incorporate curves when designing highways, deliberately creating bends. Similarly, in life, if a person's journey is too smooth, they may become overconfident and arrogant. If this person happens to be an entrepreneur, trouble ensues, as their company is not far from disaster. Thus, some say that doing business is not afraid of not making money, but rather of making money right from the start, and big money at that. Companies that don't make money initially, if they endure and head in the right direction, will eventually make profits; whereas those that make money right away, and big money at that, will eventually lose all their earnings, essentially working for others and safeguarding their assets. The reason lies in "smooth sailing." When things go too smoothly, people tend to disregard rules and treat them lightly.

As the old saying goes, "Born in adversity, die in comfort." We can see the shadow of "smooth sailing" in the failures of many private entrepreneurs. Shi Yuzhu is one example, Huai Hanxin another; Wu Bingxin is one example, Hu Zhbiao another. In the past, Li Jiachen made Huya successful in every project he undertook. He earned 180,000 yuan from a wood grain processing technique; 6 million yuan from an artificial marble technique; and in just three months from a synthetic fuel technique, earned 3 million yuan solely from entrance fees for visitors learning his techniques and another 20 million yuan from transfer fees. Consequently, Li Jiachen thought himself capable of anything, expanding his business to inventions, real estate, and even pharmaceuticals. However, a real estate venture cost him several hundred thousand yuan, and a "foot spray" venture cost him several million yuan. Years of savings were depleted within a year or two. Li Jiachen couldn't understand why, despite his intelligence and success in almost everything he did, he failed in real estate and pharmaceuticals! Eventually, he had no choice but to sell his company and use all his savings to repay bank loans. Later, Li Jiachen attended Peking University, and after some time, finally understood. During an interview with a journalist, he said he was job hunting: "I am asking for an annual salary of 120,000 yuan. But if the company has a promising future, I could also work as a driver or assistant for 3,000 to 4,000 yuan a month, because working is also a form of learning." In the short twenty-some years of China's market economy, there have been countless spectacular companies and heroes. Where are these companies and heroes now? Scientific Investment believes that excessive "smooth sailing" is a curse rather than a blessing for companies and entrepreneurs, being the greatest enemy of Chinese private enterprises and entrepreneurs.

**Second Death of Chinese Private Enterprises: Death by Sentimentality**

Humans are emotional beings, whether it be father-son affection, mother-son affection, brotherly love, sibling love, spousal love, or friendship... No matter which kind of emotion, they are all precious. When you start your business, often you need the help of family and friends tied together by emotions. However, if handled improperly, these familial and friendly ties can become burdens for your business. "In this world, there is only eternal interest, no eternal friend." This phrase may sound cold, but it is often the truth. Entrepreneurs should always keep this in mind.

Private enterprises have intricate relationships, and some say one of the reasons they fail is due to excessive sentimentality. Therefore, Chinese private enterprises must be especially careful not to be trapped by emotions. Remember, the flood of emotions can destroy the dam of rationality, and once an entrepreneur loses rationality, their company is not far from ruin.

**Third Death of Chinese Private Enterprises: Death by Politicalization**

China is a politically-oriented country, but here we refer to politics not in the conventional sense, not ideologies. By politicalization of enterprises, we mean a company bearing political significance or carrying the political future of officials, often leading to the rapid demise of the company. Officials first need achievements. Where is it easiest to achieve visible results? Road construction and building. Therefore, accompanying the promotion of officials' achievements and positions, companies in these areas suffer the most setbacks.

There are too many examples of companies failing due to officials' vanity projects. Some entrepreneurs try to沾“political” light, rushing to offer "achievements," ultimately ruining their reputations. A company is a company, and an entrepreneur's mission is profit-making, not engaging in "politics." Learn from the past to avoid repeating it.

**Fourth Death of Chinese Private Enterprises: Death by Psychological Imbalance**

Entrepreneurs must maintain a good mindset, as thoughts determine actions. A wrong mindset leads to wrong behavior, eventually destroying oneself and others. However, maintaining a positive mindset is difficult, especially when it comes to benefit patterns and benefit distribution.

**Fifth Death of Chinese Private Enterprises: Death by Rashness**

Shi Yuzhu later summarized his experience, stating his biggest mistake was not understanding finance and losing control of risks. Wu Bingxin once told Shi Yuzhu: The world is covered in gold, but who can claim it all? One must learn to control one's greed. An entrepreneur's rashness may not entirely stem from greed, but risk control, especially financial risk control, should be a basic skill for entrepreneurs. Companies often die at their most glorious moments due to rashness, making it particularly regrettable.

**Sixth Death of Chinese Private Enterprises: Death by Not Following Rules**

A market economy boils down to a credit economy, where everyone follows the rules. China's market economy is still developing, and during this transformation period, following rules and maintaining credibility becomes especially important. Some entrepreneurs treat rules as trivialities, doing whatever they want, seeking only temporary satisfaction without regard for others' feelings, ultimately facing market retaliation and self-inflicted consequences.

An old saying goes, "Without rules, nothing can be accomplished." Merchants must follow rules. Clever tricks might work temporarily but cannot sustain long-term success. On the other hand, Scientific Investment believes that a person's character correlates positively with their career. People with elegant character may not necessarily achieve great success, but merchants with poor conduct will never succeed. Trying to achieve greatness with minimal virtue is something we have never seen.

**Seventh Death of Chinese Private Enterprises: Death by Brokers**

Brokers are people who earn a living by talking. Their characteristic is making promises easily but rarely fulfilling them. They resemble Su Qin and Zhang Yi of the Warring States period, traveling around persuading various states with their eloquence, but unlike Su Qin and Zhang Yi who helped others achieve greatness, these brokers only destroy others and cannot help them succeed. They harm others and cannot assist in accomplishing tasks. However, in this era of "capital operations," these people are highly favored and increasingly so.

If one day you meet someone who speaks eloquently and tells you how effortlessly they can turn your 100,000 yuan into 1 million, 10 million, or even 1 billion, or how easily they can package your company for listing, or how they have connections with various individuals and aspects to help you acquire and merge companies effortlessly, turning others' or even state-owned companies into yours overnight, making you rich instantly, don't believe them. Such people are most likely brokers! And brokers are most likely immoral, deceiving and swindling whenever possible.

**Eighth Death of Chinese Private Enterprises: Death by Media**

China's media industry is a peculiar phenomenon. On one hand, it represents the government, serving as its mouthpiece and reflecting social responsibility and conscience; on the other hand, with deepening reforms, more and more media outlets are thrown into the market, needing to cover their own costs, thus possessing market attributes. Faced with social conscience, government mouthpiece roles, and market interests, they often find themselves torn between conflicting priorities. In this awkward survival situation of straddling the fence, many strange events occur, combined with some entrepreneurs' immature mindsets, causing significant harm to enterprises. Over the years, numerous companies and entrepreneurs have been either overly praised or harshly criticized by the media.

For companies, the media is a double-edged sword. Used well, it can greatly assist in business growth; used poorly, it offers no help and may even harm itself. In summary, media in the process of marketization needs insiders; entrepreneurs dealing with the media need rationality.

**Other Ten Ways Chinese Private Enterprises Die**

Over the past twenty years of reform and opening-up, private enterprises have continuously risen, grown, and strengthened, becoming an indispensable part of the national economy. Despite this, many private enterprises are quickly eliminated in fierce market competition, disappearing completely. Amid chaos and upheaval, the rise and fall of private enterprises have become commonplace. Although many study the success of private enterprises before analyzing their failures, few conduct systematic analyses or provide simple yet profound insights into how they "die." Careful reflection reveals that summarizing these experiences can be highly beneficial for both oneself and others.

Like humans, companies require careful nurturing. Left unchecked, they will inevitably encounter problems. If left untreated, they become incurable. Therefore, the first way companies die is through illness. These companies either lack internal mechanisms or suffer from outdated systems—such as seniority-based promotions, difficulty sharing honor and disgrace, disputes over reputation, and unfair power distribution—or experience high-level splits where leaders prefer being chicken heads to phoenix tails, resulting in easy defeat by competitors. Overall, this is due to internal systemic issues that are not promptly and effectively addressed, leading to prolonged illness and eventual death.

In a market economy, survival of the fittest prevails. In fierce competition, companies are often squeezed from all sides, with decreasing market share, dwindling talent, and declining benefits. Hence, the second way companies die is by being "squeezed out." Due to vastly different strengths, under the dual pressure of state-owned capital and foreign capital, companies are forced to exit certain industries, being "squeezed out."

The third way companies die is by being dragged down. Some companies, despite reaching a certain scale, fail to establish modern enterprise systems due to various reasons. Poor management leads to rising costs; unfair distribution lowers morale; lack of innovation causes product accumulation; and mismanagement by professional managers leads to significant asset loss. Here’s a story: A friend of mine opened a music store and hired two professional managers. Although they had no shares, they could expense freely. What did they discuss? If the store made money, the boss would naturally be happy, but they wouldn’t get a cut. So, why not increase operating costs? They decided to buy motorcycles, mobile phones, install telephones at home, and spend freely on expenses. Money came in, but disappeared just as quickly. The boss made no profit, and the business couldn’t continue. Another national group company had most of its subsidiary CEOs as professional managers. These people were skilled; once they took over, they locked investors in, earning substantial profits while tightly holding onto customer resources. Whatever they needed, you had to invest. They controlled the resources, and you couldn’t easily replace them. Otherwise, your losses would be greater. When your evaluation system standards were not properly established and your profit targets were set too high, they didn’t admit they couldn’t meet them, finding ways to delay you. Delaying month by month, you wouldn’t easily replace someone for millions or tens of millions. When they couldn’t continue, they wouldn’t tell you, as telling you would mean they couldn’t work anymore. Playing until a certain point, they’d find excuses, anyway earning their high salaries and annual bonuses. Long-term, the company would be dragged down. Investors became liable for unlimited responsibility, endlessly dragged along, while also bearing moral, investment, and social responsibilities—this is where private enterprises falter. Once investments are made, I expect returns. If the manager can’t continue, I must bite the bullet and cut my losses. But investors generally can’t do this; private enterprises mostly operate emotionally, making it hard to act decisively. Considering social impact and brotherly emotions, people feel pressured to continue funding. Ultimately, both the investor and the company are dragged down.

The fourth way companies die is called “finding death.” The main reason is decision-making errors. When a company makes some fundamental mistakes in project decisions, it is essentially committing suicide. For example, choosing projects that don’t match the talent structure, capabilities, or culture of the company, especially repeating previously made mistakes or making basic errors that shouldn’t be made. No one forces you to do this; no one orders you to take on this project. It’s considered a very elementary mistake—not being able to judge projects or oneself, thinking one can do everything. This is called “finding death.” For instance, a famous company, just gaining traction in a familiar industry without forming the necessary talent structure, hastily rushed into the health supplement market. Misled by false market signals, they were temporarily blinded by success and heavily invested in the real estate industry. This caused their capital needs to multiply several times, far exceeding their capabilities. As a result, they lost their original foothold, the health supplement business collapsed, and the real estate ventures left behind only gaping holes.

The fifth way companies die is called being “crushed.” In private enterprises, there are many such instances—blind expansion, greed for size, neglecting foundational construction, lacking internal strength, chaotic internal management, knowing low efficiency but still taking large loans, even high-interest loans, claiming profit equals loan minus cost. Finding themselves burdened with massive debts, they are crushed under the weight of repayment. Unknowingly, they are crushed to death by their own debt. In the end, they harm both others and themselves.

Cash flow is a crucial indicator in modern corporate management philosophy. In a sense, cash flow is more important than profit. Abroad, especially in capital markets, cash flow is highly valued. However, a considerable portion of domestic private enterprises often neglect this, leading to business interruption due to cash flow stoppage, even when external operations appear normal, internal finances struggle to continue. As the saying goes, "A penny can confound a hero." This is called being "strangled" to death. In the course of business operations, you may focus on asset quality, inventory, or many other aspects, but often overlook cash flow. Like playing Go, having two breaths keeps the piece alive, one breath kills it. Cash flow status is another crucial factor in determining whether a company can continue to grow. Even if you have assets and inventory, once cash flow stops, you’ll eventually declare bankruptcy. This is called being strangled to death.

The seventh way companies die is called sudden death. Due to excessive reliance on individual ability and concentrated available resources, once individual judgment errors occur or unexpected events happen, the company becomes highly dysfunctional, suffering heavy blows, leading to sudden collapse. This is called sudden death, akin to putting all eggs in one basket. A mature company, considering investment risk or responsibility to employees and society, should not bear the risk of total destruction.

The eighth way companies die is called wrongful death. There are two scenarios: one involves policy adjustments rendering the business unviable due to strong policy influence. Once national policy shifts unfavorably, it leads to company demise. For example, a private enterprise wanted to participate in a credit union, a good project initially, but upon entry, encountered policy changes. Local governments reclaimed the credit unions, converting them into commercial banks. For years, no dividends or interest were distributed, shares couldn’t be refunded, and equity pledges for loans weren’t allowed. You’re doomed. Another scenario involves overly close government-enterprise relations. Some private enterprises thrive on colluding with certain government officials for gray transactions. It’s not about capability or project feasibility but excessive intimacy with certain officials and insider trading. Due to complex personnel disputes, they become sacrifices in political struggles. There are indeed many such cases. A high school friend of mine from Jiangxi took on a construction project. Due to an official's investigation, he was implicated, arrested, and although later released innocent, his company collapsed. Private enterprises must pay special attention to this issue. We advocate shaking hands in the sunlight to avoid wrongful deaths.

The ninth way companies die is called "aging death." Many companies, due to the low quality and narrow vision of their leaders, settle for small gains, lack lofty goals and long-term vision, and fail to adjust industrial and talent structures according to market changes. These companies have a short lifespan. Their characteristic is slow death, young in age but already aging. This is called aging death. Those most prone to this are the first and second batches of entrepreneurs who went into business. They made money, but most have a short history, exhausting their abilities. They never aimed to be among the Fortune Global 500. Due to inherent limitations, their lifespans are destined to be short—a lamentable, pitiful, and unfortunate fate.

The tenth way companies die is being stabbed in the back. Due to non-standardized management, widespread law violations, numerous flaws, and inverted priorities, once exposed, they either endure humiliation silently or face lawsuits everywhere, eventually collapsing after repeated legal battles. Such companies, despite grand ambitions and lofty ideals, cannot escape premature death due to their own non-compliance.
