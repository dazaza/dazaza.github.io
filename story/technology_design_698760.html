Design strategies for garden landscapes

by atijvnzd04 on 2012-03-09 11:05:13

To: This paper discusses the issues that landscape architects should pay attention to in practical creative research from five aspects: spatial order, appropriate scale, visual artistry, environmental ecology, and place inclusiveness. Through the interpretation of these strategies, it provides a broad ideological platform for landscape designers, broadens their thinking horizons, so they can more clearly and accurately intervene in all aspects that need to be considered in design, and conduct targeted landscape creation research.

Keywords: space; scale; vision; environment; site; garden

From the magnificent temple of Prince Solomon in the West to the bizarre Dutch landscape oil paintings, from China's "enclosure" and "garden" to the mountains and rivers gardens, the charming term "garden landscape" flashes in the river of human civilization, fresh and brilliant. It seems that all beautiful things have the possibility of being modified by it. Entering contemporary society, the single design perspective of the past can no longer meet the increasingly complex social needs. The author discusses the design strategies of garden landscapes from a broader and more diversified perspective. The scope of the discussion mainly includes the following five aspects: the orderliness of space, the appropriateness of scale, the artistry of vision, the ecological nature of the environment, and the inclusiveness of the place.

I. Spatial Order

(I) Defining the landscape axis

John Simonds talks about axes in the section on visible landscapes in his book "Landscape Design - Site Planning and Design Manual." He said: "Essentially, an axis is a linear planning element connecting two or more points. It is always seen as a connecting element." [1] Therefore, defining the landscape axis is necessary. Because the introduction of the axis makes the landscape system directional and orderly. However, garden landscapes are different from other types of landscapes, as they pay more attention to the artistic conception created by the landscape. So sometimes the form of the axis is straight, sometimes it may be curved, but the purpose of defining the axis is to establish the logical order of spatial organization, so as to fit the functional requirements of the landscape and create the atmosphere of the landscape place. The winding axis of Suzhou private gardens allows us to experience the infinite changes of the landscape space and perceive the infinite charm brought by the landscape space.

(II) Combing through spatial connotations

If the axis is the central nervous system of the landscape system, then the spatial connotations covered by the landscape are the media attached to the central nervous system that communicate with people. Each purpose is a need, each need means a behavior, and each behavior necessarily determines a spatial model. Combing through the spatial connotations is based on the principle of designing according to the content carried by the landscape. Only by clarifying the content covered by the landscape will our spatial organization be targeted. When designing garden landscapes, we must clarify the content that the landscape intends to cover, and then assign it the most suitable spatial model according to various contents. To arrange spaces that overlap or can be unified, thus forming clear relationships of spatial model combinations.

(III) Distinguishing spatial hierarchy

When we comb through the spatial connotations, we find that this is a large list of landscape purposes. It is sometimes unrealistic to accommodate so much content within the site. Therefore, we must clarify various landscape needs and determine their priorities, i.e., determine the hierarchy of the landscape space. The purpose of this principle is to enable us to make scientific choices among many issues in the creation of garden landscapes. After clarifying the logical relationship of the spatial hierarchy, we will clearly use the site, reasonably organize the space, and even sacrifice one need when necessary, thereby preserving the overall logical relationship of the landscape system. After determining the hierarchy of the space, we also need to clarify the spatial model, categorize them into individual sets, and then consider their relationships. After careful deliberation, we can argue whether they have intersections, unions, or disjointed relationships.

(IV) Determining the spatial sequence

Stephen Labord and Hans Royd believe: "The spatial sequence is a series of continuous, independent spatial places connected by passages; people can only perceive one space at a time." [2] The principle of determining the spatial sequence is that after we clarify the spatial hierarchy, through the organization of the landscape sequence, give the landscape space rhythm changes, rhythmic beauty, and elastic experiences. Determining the spatial sequence is the final link in the principle of spatial order, its purpose is to deepen the order of space, and at the same time, endow the landscape space with formal beauty on the basis of correct order, and even trigger philosophical reflections through the perception of space. Landscape space not only needs climax landscape axes and rich landscape content, but more importantly, through the handling of the spatial sequence, reasonably layout the subsystem landscape space, just like the excellent and ingenious editing process in a good movie, making people experience the time changes, intensity alternations, and interweaving of scenarios in the landscape, thereby obtaining mentally pleasant experiences. Handling the spatial sequence is the art of solving the relationship between space and space. Spaces need to be connected, and connection means opportunity. The artistic handling of the spatial sequence will inevitably increase the charm of the landscape, making the landscape harmonious and beautiful with good logical relationships.

II. Appropriateness of Scale

(I) Weakening artificial pressure

In the book "Cultural Characteristics and Architectural Design," Amos Rapoport wrote: "People often start with large-scale elements when choosing and stating scales." [3] Our discussion of the principle of appropriate scale also follows such logic, from large garden landscape scales to individual human scales. How to coordinate the relationship between gardens and people is a problem that must be faced in creation. In practice, avoidance and weakening are our responses to garden landscapes. This approach also respects the existing natural environment and respects the organic order of development. Regarding the pressure of oversized scales, we adopt a humble attitude and compliant, weakened landscape creation methods. Appropriately coordinating the harmonious relationship between scales, with humility adjusting the pressure of scales on garden landscapes.

(II) Following site functions

The author has discussed the connotations of the landscape space and made them concrete, so as to grasp them more easily in practice. At the same time, it is necessary to clarify the relationship between space and scale: scale is the degree of quantity, while space needs to be perceived, based more on human experience. Therefore, it can be said that the space in the landscape needs both the dimension of quantitative scale and the experience of spatial perception. Based on the relationship between scale and space, here we introduce the constant scale and harmonizing scale in the landscape. The constant scale in the landscape refers to the fixed scale required by hard-useful landscape requirements, while the harmonizing scale plays a role in transition and harmony, following the functions of the landscape. The harmonizing scale landscape serves as the connecting medium between constant scale landscapes. Only by limiting the harmonizing scale landscape well will the landscape system operate smoothly and meet the external demands of the landscape.

(III) Coordinating garden volume

The landscape organization is compact and flexible, adhering to the scale that the garden imposes on the landscape. In design practice, it is essential to carefully handle the vertical interfaces and landscape structures, paying particular attention to their own height, volume, and impact on the garden environment. It is also important to pay attention to the rhythm organization of the landscape space, and to be modest regarding the landscape nodes and spaces in the garden. The side interface of the landscape space should be controlled appropriately, and the intensity of the space should also be appropriate, which is conducive to the organic continuation of the garden's natural environment. The overall landscape system should pay attention to modifying the overall spatial scale of the garden with relatively soft interfaces, ultimately making the garden's natural environment harmoniously coexist with the landscape.

(IV) Suitable for human scale

Regardless of discussing the scale of gardens, landscapes, or facilities, our perspective is ultimately based on human scale. Different people perceive the same thing differently, but sometimes the results of perception are similar. It can be considered that in the relationship between scale and "people", the concept of "people" should be universal, referring to the social group of people. E.T. Hall proposed four distance scales for human interaction:

(1) Intimate distance: the distance between people is less than 0.5 m, mainly relying on smell and touch, vision is not important;

(2) Personal distance: the distance between people is 0.5～1 m, touch and smell play a certain role, vision plays a leading role;

(3) Social distance: the distance between people is 1～2.5 m (smaller), 2.5～5 m (larger), cognition mainly relies on vision and hearing;

(4) Public distance: the distance between people is 5～10 m, this limit varies depending on cultural, societal, or personal factors.

Human visual and auditory information acquisition accounts for 90% of the total information received, plus olfactory senses and social space among other factors. Within the range of a cubic landscape space with sides of 20～25 m, people can obtain better visual perception for social interaction. Beyond this range, the intensity of perception decreases sharply. This volumetric range of space also provides scientific evidence for the principle of suitable human scale proposed by the author.

III. Artistic Nature of Vision

(I) Quoting natural beauty

Quoting natural beauty has two meanings: borrowing scenery from the beauty of natural mountains and waters; borrowing the essence of natural beauty. Borrowing scenery from mountains and waters is for the purpose of creating landscape layers, introducing beautiful mountains and rivers as the distant view of the landscape layers, stretching the visual beauty to a deeper level. While borrowing the essence of natural beauty is to create landscapes using the aesthetic advantages of natural elements themselves. Liu Xiaoguang divided beauty into three levels in his doctoral thesis "Research on Landscape Symbolism Theory": formal beauty, concrete beauty, and profound beauty. [4] The principle of borrowing scenery from nature and drawing from nature proposed by the author aims to achieve the visual experience of beautiful natural landscapes. Quoting natural beauty focuses on the meaning of beauty and pays more attention to the aesthetic tendency of getting close to nature. Quoting natural beauty is also about exploring natural scenic resources, assigning a familiar sense of beauty to the landscape, and connecting large-scale natural landscapes with man-made ones through the method of borrowing scenery.

(II) Creating interface beauty

Interfaces arise because of space and are elements that define space. Interface beauty is the core of visual formal beauty in design practice. The vertical interfaces in the landscape often determine the character of the landscape space, while the top interface is relatively open. The formal beauty of the bottom interface relates to the overall beauty of the landscape space, and small areas of the bottom interface can also affect the visual sensation of users. However, the formal beauty of the vertical interface concerns the majority of the public's experience of visual beauty, because compared to the single bottom interface and the open top interface, the side interface is full of infinite morphological changes and rich texture expressions. Can Clive Bell's "meaningful form" let us think about the deep connection between "meaning" and "art"? Is this connection the beautiful vibration in the hearts of the public towards the interface beauty in our practice? Chernyshevsky said, Shakespeare cannot replace today's art. Indeed, our interface beauty belongs to the kind people living on this land, and we should tailor it for them.

(III) Perceiving the beauty of space

The beauty of space lies in the emotional waves that the charm of the space itself gives us, and also in the harmonious scale relationship of the space bringing us relaxed and smooth behavioral patterns. In garden landscapes, the principle of perceiving the beauty of space is abstract but indeed exists, abstract in the essence of space and existing in people's hearts. The beauty of space in garden landscapes lies in everything it encompasses, more so in whether the space itself can awaken people's emotional awareness of this intangible concept. Perceiving the beauty of space is a design principle and also the goal we pursue, but it is lagging behind. Only when the public enjoy life and recreation in the landscape does our beauty of space truly become beautiful in its proper place.

(IV) Metaphor for the beauty of humanity

On the road to interpreting the principle of landscape beauty, the ultimate destination is undoubtedly the beauty of humanity. Beauty ultimately needs to be perceived by the public, and the metaphor for the beauty of humanity is the core thread and dominant aesthetic significance of all landscape creations governed by the principle of visual artistry. The beauty of humanity is pure and good. The beauty of humanity interprets the highest level of artistic pursuit, not beautiful forms, nor long-lasting meanings, but touching the heart. The principle of metaphor for the beauty of humanity is the happy endpoint we anticipate in our landscape visual creation.

IV. Ecological Nature of Environment

(I) Respecting ecological value

The establishment of ecological values is the core of the principle of environmental ecology. In garden landscape design, the value of ecology is the concept we must respect in our design, and it should be equally important as human social needs, art, and the charm of aesthetics. From the conception of the scheme to the detailed elaboration, this value concept must always be kept in mind. Responding to the harmonious coexistence of humans and nature with this concept; supporting ecological landscape design with this concept; influencing designers and the public who enjoy the landscape with this concept, respecting the meaning of life that nature brings us in design and life. Respecting ecological value is the formation of a concept, and it cannot solve actual problems in the landscape solely through the concept. It is more like a governing principle, allowing us to always maintain an attitude of respecting environmental conditions and understanding nature.

(II) Accepting ecological substrates

The wonderful garden ecological substrate forces us to actively accept it and make it a clue that runs throughout our landscape design. There are many concepts of ecological substrates, blue belts, gray belts, etc., in modern landscape design at large geographical scales. These large-scale landscapes interpret the perfect concept of the landscape design environment. From the climatic angle of large-scale landscapes, from the physiological perception angle of water systems, from the landscape advantage angle of ecological substrates, we must consider carefully. Tailor our landscape to connect the beautiful natural environment with our landscape patches better, thus integrating them.

(III) Correcting the site environment

The objective existence of the external environment determines the micro-ecological environment of the landscape, where noise, dust, buildings, seasonal winds, and adverse airflows all adversely affect the landscape environment. Correcting the landscape environment is the design principle proposed based on the above adverse factors, that is, mediating the ecological environment within the site through the landscape interface as a medium, such as: blocking noise and bad air through constructing tall tree clusters; guiding adverse airflows through the creation of landscape interfaces; lowering the site temperature by introducing a large amount of green plants, etc. This principle is observed critically, and whether the criticism is accurate still depends on accurate discovery and continuous practice in practice to verify.

(IV) Exploring local resources

Adapting measures to local conditions, using local materials, and emerging timely. Ecology is relative to the environment, and fitting into the locality is about exploring the environment itself to investigate the essence of environmental harmony. Fitting into the locality is not merely a consciousness orientation at the conceptual level, nor is it a general ecological design principle. Its essence lies in interpreting the local environment and rooting it in landscape design. The significance of fitting into the locality is far-reaching, not only in the creation of ecological environments, but also extending to economic, cultural, and aesthetic aspects. Fitting into the locality is about creating a beautiful garden landscape environment for the public, letting people truly experience the realm of "There is true meaning in this, but I have forgotten how to put it into words."

V. Inclusiveness of Place

(I) Including a Broad Group

Place is the spatial energy that spreads emotions, and we hope this energy can be maximized for the public. In practice, we must consistently adhere to the principle of including a broad group, meticulously delving into humane landscape interfaces, barrier-free transportation systems, vibrant leisure scenery full of greenery, and efficient and comfortable landscapes. Place is a collection of emotions, and among emotions, we must focus more on inclusion. All the purposes of landscape creation aim primarily at the quality of use by the public. Including a broad group is not only a guiding principle for practice but also the value orientation of landscape creation.

(II) Drawing on Local Culture

It can be said that culture is the first driving force for the aggregation of places and also the starting point for various behaviors. Landscape design is precisely based on the internal cohesion of "cultural drive," converting regional, ethnic, historical, and cultural accumulations and lifestyle models of people in life into design materials for gardens and spatial organization bases for landscape design. Whether it is direct visual experience, behavior and habits, material composition familiarity, or spiritual experience similarity, the recognition of place sense mainly stems from the recognition of local culture. Therefore, the depth of extraction and breadth of expansion of local culture determine the quality of culturally distinctive landscapes and whether they can gain public recognition.

(III) Responding to Psychological Needs

Actually, each of us yearns to get close to nature, greenery, relaxation, and sports. When we present several garden landscape works for people to freely choose, the most votes often go to works that focus on psychological needs, because these works encompass the essence of humanity's inherent closeness to nature and self-release. In design practice, aligning with higher psychological needs is undoubtedly the best way to identify with a place. Whether it is due to the psychology of getting close to nature, recreational needs, or even the desire to take a breath.

(IV) Interpreting Landscape Spirit

The interpretation of the spirit of garden landscapes relies on the public's participation in nature rather than being packaged in vain in texts or speeches. Only when the public enjoys life in the landscape does that scene truly interpret the ordinary, real, and vivid spirit of the garden. The creation of scenes requires characters, venues, and plots, and the good creation of venues and plots is the original intention of proposing this principle. In practice, interpreting the spirit of the landscape means enriching the emotions of the landscape. Many abstract connotations of the landscape need to be realized through the materialized form of the landscape, and more need to be interpreted through the landscape interface besides the necessary facilities and venues. Materialized forms do not only stay at the level of creating perceptions of spirit but also need to focus on the good connection between the depth of landscape expression and the acceptance level of the public, fully considering the depth of the public's cultural accumulation.

VI. Conclusion

Through the analysis of design elements such as space, scale, vision, environment, and place, it emphasizes the factors that need to be considered in the practice and creation of garden landscape design, enabling designers to clearly understand how to efficiently and effectively carry out garden landscape design, thereby creating better and more suitable works for the public demand.
