Wireless Communication, Space Division Multiple Access, Adaptive Antenna, Application

Abstract: The smart antenna that forms directional beams using digital signal processing technology has attracted great attention from wireless communication engineers. Based on the traditional understanding of antennas, the author elaborates on the basic concepts, characteristics, implementation methods, and application prospects of smart antennas.

1. Overview

Antennas are essential devices for realizing electromagnetic wave propagation: at the signal transmission end, antennas are used to radiate electromagnetic waves; at the receiving end, antennas are used to sense electromagnetic waves. Therefore, no matter what kind of communication system, as long as it uses wireless transmission, antennas must be used, regardless of the working frequency or frequency band of the system, or the multiple access or modulation technology adopted.

With the development of communication and technological progress, the requirements for components and parts have become increasingly higher. Smart antennas are a new concept that has emerged to meet the needs of communication development - they play an important role in wireless access systems, satellite communication systems, mobile communication systems (whether in public or dedicated communication networks) as well as military communications, bringing advantages such as enhanced interference resistance and improved frequency utilization.

Despite still serving the functions of radiation and sensing of electromagnetic waves, smart antennas represent a completely new concept.

2. Conventional Antennas vs. Smart Antennas

According to different classification methods, conventional antennas (hereafter referred to as antennas) can be classified into various types. For example, if classified by element shape, antennas can be divided into linear and planar antennas: the latter includes parabolic antennas, Cassegrain shaped antennas, etc., which are commonly used in the microwave frequency band; the former includes Brown antennas, J-shaped antennas, folded dipole antennas, Yagi antennas, whip antennas, helical antennas, rhombic antennas, etc., which are often used in UHF, VHF, and shortwave frequency bands. If classified by directivity pattern, antennas can be further divided into non-directional antennas (i.e., omnidirectional antennas) and directional antennas: the former, such as exposed dipole antennas and coaxial antennas, have the characteristic that when used for signal transmission, the transmitted energy is uniformly distributed 360 degrees regardless of the location of the receiving user; examples of directional antennas include corner reflector antennas and angular reflector antennas. These antennas form signal transmission or reception in certain directions with high efficiency. If classified by material, there are metal antennas and dielectric antennas. If classified by electric field direction, there are vertically polarized and horizontally polarized antennas. Of course, antennas also have other classification methods, but we will not list them all here. However, no matter how you look at it, the structure of communication antennas is relatively simple, even if the feeder lines connected to the transmitter and receiver are included, forming the "antenna-feeder line system," it remains a simple system.

Smart antennas, however, are complex systems, and as performance requirements increase, smart antennas become more complex. It can be said that smart antennas evolved from adaptive antennas, but there are significant differences between the two: adaptive antennas are mainly used for interference cancellation in radar systems, especially in situations where interference signal strength is extremely large and the number of interferences is small. In wireless communication systems, interference is primarily based on multipath propagation, generally with smaller amplitudes but often in large quantities, especially when radio waves propagate in urban areas. These differences lead to unique characteristics in the formation of directivity patterns or in signal processing. Since smart antennas evolved from adaptive antennas, their structures are similar to those of adaptive antennas. The structure of a smart antenna used for signal reception is shown in Figure 1. That is, a smart antenna is a system jointly constituted by an antenna array and a set of beamforming networks (also called clustering networks). Therefore, from a hardware perspective, calling a smart antenna a "smart antenna system" is understandable.

The structures of smart antennas used on both the receiving and transmitting sides are similar, and their working principles are consistent. The antenna array of the smart antenna is formed by arranging multiple (for example, 5, 6, or even more) unit antennas in a specific form, with common forms including planar, circular, and linear shapes. From an engineering perspective, these unit antennas usually have non-directional directivity diagrams, and their mutual spacing must meet certain requirements. For example, in mobile communications, the spacing between each unit antenna is often taken as λ/2 (λ being the operating wavelength).

The function of the smart antenna's beamforming network is to form a series of extremely narrow signal transmission channels—spatial channels, also known as beams (Beam)—under the support of the antenna array, i.e., forming an extremely narrow signal channel between the transmitting and receiving ends. For example, when a smart antenna is used in a wireless access system, an extremely narrow radio beam channel can be formed between the base station (or central station, relay station) and the user. When a smart antenna is used in mobile communication and personal communication, this extremely narrow beam can move along with the user's movement. Clearly, the application of an extremely narrow beam can enhance the effectiveness of the transmitted power and improve the signal-to-interference ratio during signal transmission. Or, under the condition of ensuring the unchanged signal-to-interference ratio at the receiving end, the transmitted power at the transmitting end can be significantly reduced.

The practical use of this extremely narrow beam also forms the fourth concept of multiple access technology—Space Division Multiple Access (SDMA), and this SDMA can be combined with other multiple access technologies and their hybrid combinations. That is, after adopting a smart antenna, the system can distinguish users according to different spatial beams—spatial propagation paths under the same time slot, same frequency, and same address code conditions.

It is worth reiterating that the formation of a certain directional spatial beam is the joint action of many non-directional antennas and the beamforming network, and the direction of the spatial beam is determined by the different spatial propagation directions of the users. This directional spatial beam is equivalent to cables such as optical fibers and coaxial cables in wired signal transmission.

Smart antennas can be applied to wireless communication systems, whether they are public networks or private networks, and regardless of the technical standards adopted by the system. Smart antennas can be suitable for almost all mobile communication protocols and standards, see Table 1. Some protocols or standards may not yet have officially formed products, such as cdma2000, TD-SCDMA, indicating that the application scope of smart antennas is very wide.

The practical use of SDMA also promotes the improvement of system frequency utilization rate and reduces the difficulty of frequency management and frequency allocation. For example, in mobile communications, the same carrier frequency within the same base station range can be reused multiple times without considering co-channel interference (which greatly reduces the difficulty of frequency allocation). Moreover, the narrower the beams of different directions, the greater the co-channel reuse factor can be, the higher the system frequency utilization rate, and the larger the system capacity. When two mobile phone users occupy the same channel in the same cell simultaneously, the directivity diagram formed by the smart antenna is shown in Figure 2. In the figure, the two main beams formed by the smart antenna are respectively aimed at these two users (without causing co-channel interference), while the gain in other directions is very small, ensuring that the main beam gain can be made very high, and the impact of surrounding interference (including co-channel interference, adjacent channel interference, near-end to far-end ratio interference, etc.) and noise can be reduced to a minimum.

After adopting smart antennas, the same wireless area can not only arrange adjacent or nearby frequencies, but even realize co-frequency reuse, greatly reducing the difficulty for radio management departments in frequency allocation and interference management. Therefore, the National Radio Regulation Committee strongly advocates the early use of smart antennas.

The application of smart antennas can greatly enhance the competitiveness of equipment suppliers, and smart antennas are not restricted by modulation methods and air interface protocols, adapting to existing air interface methods. The core technology of smart antennas is beamforming, mainly realized by beamforming networks.

When a smart antenna serves a specific user, it uses the antenna array to transmit or receive radio waves, and some parts of the beamforming network form an extremely narrow beam pointing towards the user, while in other directions, the smart antenna can adaptively control its directivity diagram to zero. This performance is also called the adaptive nulling function. By using this function, the amplitude of the sidelobe or zero signal area (also called the null region) of the smart antenna can be basically suppressed, resulting in excellent anti-interference performance of the smart antenna.

As long as the main beam can be made extremely fine, the number of main beams of the same base station (or central station) can be sufficiently increased, and the sidelobes can be completely suppressed, then theoretically, the application of smart antennas solves the problem of insufficient wireless communication frequency resources. Therefore, whether in the third-generation mobile communication scheme W-CDMA proposed by Europe and Japan, or in China's proposed third-generation mobile communication scheme TD-SCDMA, smart antennas are described as feature technologies, which makes sense. Even in dedicated communication networks, this feature has significant importance. Taking the 815~821 MHz (mobile station transmission) and 860~866 MHz (base station transmission) cluster system dedicated frequency band as an example to illustrate this issue. Although this frequency band can be divided into 240 two-way channels, due to the superior performance of the cluster system, especially its powerful dispatching function, this system is particularly popular in dedicated communication networks. Many systems such as power, civil defense, transportation, ports, and civil aviation want to develop this system, leading to spectrum tension. However, once the cluster system adopts smart antennas, the problem of spectrum tension will be solved effortlessly.

3. Composition of the Smart Antenna System

The reason why smart antennas possess these excellent performances is related to their system composition, especially the beamforming network. The beamforming network is complex and can roughly be divided into two parts: the network processing system and the network control system. According to the working principles and structures of network processing and network control, smart antennas can be divided into two types: beam switching and adaptive arrays.

Beam switching refers to the fact that the number of spatial beams - spatial channels that a smart antenna can form is predetermined, including both the beam direction and quantity. Specifically, the number of beams for this type of antenna is limited. When a smart antenna serves a user, the system can automatically select one or a combination of several beams from the limited beams to serve the user, regardless of whether the maximum direction of the selected beam points to the user, meaning that although the user is within the coverage of the selected beam, they may not be on the maximum direction. Moreover, when the user moves, the beam remains fixed, and the system switches from one beam to another when the user moves to another beam. For the same reason, the other beam does not guarantee that its maximum direction always points to the user, which constitutes the disadvantages of this type of smart antenna. However, this type of antenna has a simple structure.

Adaptive array-type smart antennas can form an infinite number of beams and ensure that users always receive the maximum beam direction. When the user moves, the beam can also adaptively change. Clearly, this type of smart antenna performs best, but its network control system is quite complex and requires good real-time performance. That is, the processing network needs fast-converging, high-precision adaptive algorithms in software to quickly adjust the complex weighting parameters W of the beam.

Currently, the algorithms used in smart antenna network systems include minimum, maximum signal ratio, minimum deviation, etc. They each have their own characteristics, so in actual systems, they often need to be used together to complement each other, especially in mobile communication and personal communication. This is because in these two communication systems, radio wave propagation is mainly on the ground, and the ground radio wave propagation environment is very harsh. Since the performance of smart antennas largely depends on the characteristics of the network system software, smart antennas are also called "software antennas."

In early smart antennas, the beamforming network used analog circuits, but they were difficult to debug, had poor performance stability and reliability, so currently, digital circuits are advocated. A more consistent opinion is to use high-speed digital signal processing chips to implement them. The main chips actually used are mainly two types: one is the general DSP chip, such as the TMS320 series, etc. The other is the application-specific integrated circuit (ASIC device), the most typical of which is the gate array circuit FPGA capable of large-scale parallel processing, with the C6x mod processor-based DSP system shown in Figure 3. The beamforming network platform should provide sufficient modules to support multiple C6x and adopt a highly efficient I/O structure.

The miniaturization of antennas and the use of microstrip antennas simplify the structure of the antenna array. A notable development in software is the adaptive digital beamforming algorithm based on eigenvalue decomposition, which is particularly valued because this algorithm can be unified with high-resolution direction-finding algorithms and overcome numerous factors causing beam errors. However, the computational load of this algorithm is large.

4. Application of Smart Antennas in Wireless Communications

Smart antennas can be used in many types of wireless communication systems to improve system performance. Future dedicated mobile communication networks will develop towards public mobile communication networks, or the relationship between the two will become closer. It should also be noted that mobile communication cellular areas are developing towards miniaturization and intelligence, with shorter station distances, wider distribution, and more intelligent and real-time beam tracking. Base station configurations will also become more flexible, and the beamforming technology of smart antennas will play an important role in improving the quality of terrestrial radio wave propagation and reducing costs. Due to the use of smart antennas, whether in dedicated mobile communication systems, such as cluster systems, wireless local loops, or in public cellular systems, the emission method of the control channel has changed - from full-cell (or full-sector or full-wireless zone) range radiation to tracking narrow-beam radiation, allowing co-frequency reuse across the entire area, thus forming the new concept of intelligent wireless zones (intelligent cells, intelligent sectors). Because smart antennas inherently have tracking capabilities, there is no need for communication systems to set up additional "location functions", which leads to the emergence of another new concept—"intelligent handover"—in mobile communication systems and personal communication systems using smart antennas. Moreover, the application of smart antennas also reduces costs. Currently, in public mobile communication systems, although high-performance single-polarization omnidirectional antennas ANTEL BCD-87010, single-polarization directional antennas ANTEL RWA-87027, dual-polarization antennas DPS60-16 RSX, and advanced remote-controlled electronic tilt antennas MTPA890-D4-RXY-Z are used, especially the latter provides convenience for daily mobile communication network optimization, people can conveniently adjust the antenna tilt angle according to needs to improve coverage and interference, but they cannot compare with smart antennas. When smart antennas are used in mobile communication systems, they are mainly used for base station transmission and reception.

It should be acknowledged that the application of smart antennas in mobile communication and personal communication is challenging, as the dynamic capture and tracking of signals are difficult due to factors such as multi-user mobility and multipath propagation of radio waves. Therefore, the application of smart antennas in mobile communication and personal communication is relatively late, while fixed wireless access systems, especially fixed wireless access systems, apply earlier. When smart antennas work in TDD duplex mode wireless access systems, the weighting coefficients of the uplink and downlink links can be unified. However, when the uplink and downlink frequencies are different, i.e., FDD duplex mode is used, the weighting coefficients of the downlink link need to be appropriately processed based on the weighting coefficients of the uplink link. Smart antennas are expected to be used in mobile municipal telephone systems to improve the difficulty of frequency configuration and increase network capacity, as well as enhance the network's interference resistance.

Smart antennas can also be used in cordless telephone systems with DECT, PHS, PACS, CDCT, etc., improving their system performance.

Smart antennas can also be used in satellite mobile communication systems, such as L-band satellite mobile systems. The smart antenna used in this system consists of a 16-element, ring-distributed microstrip antenna array and a beamforming network, using left-hand circular polarization. The beamforming network uses 10 FPGA chips, with 2 used for beam selection, control, and interface, and 8 used for quasi-coherent detection and fast Fourier transform of the antenna array.

Smart antennas are also used in various dedicated communication networks and military communication wireless communication systems to improve system performance. Due to the important application value of smart antennas, many universities, research institutes, and communication companies at home and abroad have invested heavily in research and have already achieved significant results.

Conclusion

Smart antennas have great potential in increasing the capacity, interference resistance, communication quality, and enabling frequency sharing among various dedicated networks in both dedicated and public communication systems. In recent years, they have received much attention. However, due to the difficulty of adaptive beamforming and the many influencing factors, smart antennas have been successfully applied in fixed wireless access, satellite communication, and military communication systems, with significant benefits. But there are still challenges in applying them in mobile communication and personal communication. Recently, optimistic news has come. For example, a foreign company conducted practical tests of smart antennas in Shanghai's mobile communication network in June 2000. The type of antenna used was a beam-switching type. The test results showed that the overall network performance was indeed improved. According to reports from Guangdong Province, the province's mobile communication network will introduce smart antennas based on sufficient testing to significantly improve network service quality levels and meet the sharp increase in user numbers.
