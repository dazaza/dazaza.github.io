Viewing the Differences in Entrepreneurship between China and Korea through Coupang, the Korean Version of Amazon

by anonymous on 2013-08-26 14:08:08

Coupang Founder and CEO Kim Bong (leftmost, Tencent Technology photo)

Kim Bong, who dropped out of Harvard to return home and start a business, turned Coupang into Korea's Amazon within two years and is preparing for an IPO in the United States. How did he achieve this?

Dropping out of Harvard to return home and start a business

Kim Bong grew up in Seoul and at the age of 13 went to study at Deerfield Academy, a prestigious school in Massachusetts, USA.

During his time at Harvard Business School, Kim Bong founded the non-profit magazine "Current," which mainly covered current events. Newsweek acquired it in 2001. In 2006, he received $4 million from David-Bradley of Atlanta Media Company and founded "02138," a magazine primarily aimed at Harvard alumni, but the magazine ceased publication in 2008.

Afterward, he was attracted by Groupon’s group-buying model and Gilt-Groupe’s flash sale model. He believed these models had greater potential in the Korean market and thought they could be replicated in Korea. Kim Bong told the media: “The biggest constraint in e-commerce is nothing more than the time it takes for goods to reach you.” In Korea, over fifty million people live on land equivalent to the size of Indiana in the United States, so delivery times are not a big issue.

At the same time, Koreans enjoy the fastest broadband services in the world, with two-thirds of the population owning smartphones, making online shopping a national pastime. With these conveniences, in 2010, Kim Bong secured $2 million from American investors, dropped out of school, and returned to Korea to start a business.

Korean traditional culture emphasizes avoiding risks and fearing failure, which influences the birth of new tech companies. Perhaps because Korea lacks an entrepreneurial spirit, Coupang easily won in the domestic market.

When looking for office space, Kim Bong experienced the influence of Korean traditional culture. The real estate agent asked him, “You speak English, why don't you get a job at a big company?” Kim Bong said that in Korea, people only consider starting a business when they can't find their ideal job.

However, in China, entrepreneurship does not encounter the same traditional resistance as in Korea. Wang Xing, who has the same experience of studying in the U.S. as Kim Bong, also founded Meituan under the inspiration of Groupon's group-buying model in the same year. This was another well-known website after Wang Xing's previous ventures, Xiaonei and Fanfou, and survived through fierce competition during the “Thousand Group Battle.”

Moving away from Groupon towards the Amazon model

The name “Coupang” was creatively inspired by Groupon and means “fun” in Korean.

In the beginning, the website offered discounted group-buying coupons for restaurants, SPA massages, and other merchants, similar to Groupon. In the first winter, Kim Bong expanded the product range to include tourism and fresh produce.

In 2011, Groupon entered the Korean market but lagged far behind the local powerhouse Coupang. However, Coupang's focus became increasingly hard to define; they would sell almost anything, such as a device that removes belly hair recently. Kim Bong explained that the category design was a compromise. Recently, he discovered through user surveys that users liked purchasing quirky and unconventional products.

Today, Coupang sells everything. From fresh cherries to Samsung phones, from underwear to second-hand cars, the website offers free two-day delivery for all products.

After two years of operation, last year the website's revenue reached $6.5 billion (estimated profit of $1.3 billion). In the United States, Groupon mainly relied on half-price discounts from local merchants to achieve rapid growth, but its business model proved unsustainable (the founder Mason was fired at the beginning of the year, and Groupon's stock price plummeted by ninety percent from its peak in 2011).

Kim Bong learned from this lesson and quickly moved into the online sales of physical goods in Korea, which made Coupang's fate significantly different from Groupon's.

At just 34 years old, Kim Bong wants to surpass Groupon's founder Mason and learn from Amazon and Bezos in terms of business models. Therefore, Coupang is also investing in warehousing, flash sales, and other businesses, continuously striving to shorten delivery times. Kim Bong said: “Our business model is very different from Groupon's and closer to Amazon's.” It is reported that local sales account for only ten percent of total revenue, while in Groupon, this proportion is as high as sixty percent.

Unlike Coupang, Wotuo Group in China, which also started with group-buying, developed more toward a platform direction. Life service merchants can open stores in Wotuo Mall, while Wotuo itself does not handle warehousing and logistics goods but focuses on transactions and pre-transaction processes.

Future plans for an IPO in the U.S., unafraid of Amazon

Looking ahead, Coupang's revenue will come more from mobile devices. At the end of 2011, they launched their first mobile app, and the performance on mobile devices was enough to make all American e-commerce companies sweat—mobile apps contributed seventy percent of traffic and half of the revenue. Kim Bong hopes Coupang will go public in the United States next year. Why not list in Korea? He said that American investors understand consumer tech companies better than Asians, and he hopes the company receives a more ideal valuation.

Is Amazon a concern? Amazon hasn't entered the Korean market but generates billions of dollars in revenue in neighboring Japan. Kim Bong said he is not afraid of Amazon: “If Amazon sees the cost of taking our customers, I think Amazon will realize that the Korean market is not very attractive.”

Perceiving differences in entrepreneurship between China and Korea

It is not difficult to see that both Chinese and Korean entrepreneurs have a firm belief in pursuing dreams and dare to practice them. Most have experience in serial entrepreneurship. These experiences make them more focused in their next entrepreneurial projects.

Compared to Korea's traditional culture of avoiding failure, China's entrepreneurial environment has seen noticeable improvements in recent years: policy support, diversification and flattening of financing channels; at the same time, entrepreneurs' mindsets are more relaxed, and the concept of failure being commonplace is becoming increasingly common, but this will not hinder the steps of entrepreneurs. Moreover, users who once pursued full-category offerings on group-buying or e-commerce platforms now prefer small, personalized products, which inspires entrepreneurs to find more niche markets.
