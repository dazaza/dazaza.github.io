Is breaking up China Mobile a good choice?

by mylovehisa on 2008-03-13 10:55:49

On the first interview on Friday, we invited Professor Kan Kaili. In fact, I have quite a lot of conflicts with Mr. Kan in terms of viewpoints, especially our attitudes towards 3G, which are completely opposite. Over the years, in the telecommunications industry, we have done a lot of cooperation. I remember the first time we did an interview on a website was when we were both guests on Sina.com. Over the years in the industry, I have written many articles criticizing Mr. Kan's views, and he has also given me a lot of phone calls to teach me lessons. Of course, viewpoints are one thing, and friendship is another. When I asked him to come for an interview, he didn't hesitate. It's always interesting to talk about the industry together because we understand each other well, and the effect is usually very good. After the chat, I showed Mr. Kan around our office. When I saw him off, he asked about my children and family, and shook my hand tightly. That moment moved me deeply. It's tough for me, but with your support and help, I will face it positively.

This time, the main topic with Mr. Kan was about the telecom restructuring. There is a big issue here: for a long time, there has been no mainstream official opinion on why the telecom industry needs restructuring, and there hasn't been sufficient discussion at this level. Interestingly, even these influential experts in the industry can only speculate about the purpose of the restructuring. Mr. Kan has an interesting proposition: if the main purpose of the telecom restructuring is to establish market competition entities with balanced strength to compete in the market, his suggestion is to split China Mobile and China Unicom into two parts each, forming one company with South Mobile and North Unicom, and another company with South Unicom and North Mobile, encouraging them to enter each other's markets. This way, there will be fewer issues with cultural integration and less technical involvement.

Few people have heard of this plan, and it might sound surprising at first, but upon careful consideration, it does make sense. Do you think this is a valuable option?
