Exclusive disclosure of Lenovo's attitude towards BlackBerry: better to acquire talents than the company

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 19:50:24

A launch event for Lenovo's Yoga tablet saw BlackBerry, which had no relation to the event, become a supporting player. By analyzing the statements of key executives at Lenovo, it becomes clear that the much-discussed relationship between Lenovo and BlackBerry can now be considered over.

Lenovo Strategy: Acquiring talent is better than acquiring companies

At the routine media communication meeting that day, Lenovo executives clearly reiterated their consistent position to the dozens of journalists present: 1) Acquisitions are a core competency of Lenovo. 2) Lenovo maintains an open attitude towards acquisitions. 3) Regarding BlackBerry, there is no information available for public disclosure.

However, during follow-up discussions after the meeting, a core executive from Lenovo Group shared substantial information about Lenovo's interest in BlackBerry. Below are the key points I've compiled:

1) Lenovo has indeed studied BlackBerry. The main issue now is whether BlackBerry's operating system can survive. From a timeline perspective, BlackBerry's value has been continuously declining.

2) Can BlackBerry's technology be integrated into the Android platform? BlackBerry has its own operating system, but Lenovo primarily uses Android. If BlackBerry's technology can be adapted for Android, then BlackBerry's value would increase. This is still under consideration.

3) From an economic standpoint, hiring people is more cost-effective than acquiring a company. The most economical method would be to hire former BlackBerry employees into Lenovo Group, which would be far more practical than purchasing BlackBerry outright.

Feasibility Analysis

Based on the above three core pieces of information, I believe that hiring talent rather than acquiring a company is the most reliable option. In fact, Lenovo already has precedent in this area and knows how to execute such a strategy effectively.

In 2012, when Motorola China announced a reduction of 500 staff at its Nanjing R&D center, Lenovo unexpectedly became the biggest winner of this layoff. According to my rough estimates, Lenovo participated in the talent competition and hired approximately 100 individuals.

Yang Yuanqing revealed that the best window for Lenovo to enter the European and American markets will be around 2014-2015, meaning Lenovo has about a year to complete its talent reserve.

Final Thoughts:

Of course, this does not mean that Lenovo will not consider acquiring BlackBerry. Not long ago, Lenovo began seeking a financing plan worth up to $1.5 billion. It should be noted that Lenovo also holds several billion dollars in cash reserves, leading to speculation that Lenovo may have significant moves planned. Of course, mobile phone business is just one among many businesses for Lenovo; others include servers.
