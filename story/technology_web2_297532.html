On November 29, a report titled "Killing Kennedy Again" appeared on the US Global Strategy website. Here, "Kennedy" refers to the aircraft carrier "Kennedy" which was decommissioned in March 2007. Now, this giant ship that has served for 39 years is quietly moored at the naval vessel storage site in Philadelphia. What kind of fate awaits it? Turning it into a museum would cost a lot of money. The report said that the US Navy plans to provide the "Kennedy" to local governments or non-governmental organizations to be used as a maritime museum. If this plan can really be realized, it will be the largest warship museum in history. Currently, Boston, where the Kennedy family rose to prominence, has shown strong interest in taking in this aircraft carrier. However, whoever takes in this behemoth with a displacement of over 80,000 tons, money is an issue that cannot be ignored - transforming the aircraft carrier into a museum and maintaining it well requires substantial funding. The US Navy has always been willing to turn decommissioned warships into museums, but this is not a free lunch. The Navy often sets strict additional conditions when giving away warships. The most important one is requiring the decommissioned warship to always maintain a "dignified" appearance, meaning good maintenance to prevent rusting. The "Intrepid" aircraft carrier, which served during World War II and is now moored in New York, is such a maritime museum. Two years ago, the "Intrepid" was towed into a dry dock, and more than 100 square meters of rusted hull were replaced, costing $120 million for maintenance. In comparison, its sister ship, the "Yorktown", hasn't been so lucky. After being decommissioned and moored in Charleston, South Carolina, it now needs funds for maintenance, but with the economic crisis sweeping across the United States, it's very difficult to get people to spend large sums of money to maintain warships, and the "Yorktown" is therefore unlikely to escape complete scrapping. The other two possible outcomes are quite tragic. The Global Strategy website also discussed the other two possible outcomes for the "Kennedy": either being dismantled as scrap or being used as a target ship for weapons testing. These two outcomes are obviously more "tragic" than becoming a museum. Let's first look at the method of directly scrapping old ships. For a long time, most retired US Navy ships have been dismantled in this way. The "Enterprise" aircraft carrier, which had distinguished itself in battle during World War II, was quickly sold to a company for scrap metal after being decommissioned in 1947. However, the traditional dismantling process is now facing criticism from environmentalists, who believe it causes environmental damage; if a relatively environmentally friendly method is used for dismantling, the cost is too high. Next, let's examine the method of using old ships for weapons testing. The sister ship of the "Kennedy", the "America", served its final purpose in this way. In May 2005, the US Navy tested the anti-destruction capabilities of aircraft carriers by sinking the decommissioned "America" with various weapons, gaining valuable insights into the effects of modern weapons attacking aircraft carriers through real combat experiments. The report believes that using the "Kennedy" as a target ship could allow the US Navy to further test the anti-destruction capabilities of modern large aircraft carriers, undoubtedly a precious opportunity. In summary, if there isn't enough funding for renovation, the "Kennedy" will probably face the fate of being dismantled or sunk. Why doesn't the US sell old aircraft carriers? Looking at the experiences of several major naval powers in handling decommissioned aircraft carriers, it's not hard to find that the UK, France, and Russia all have practices of selling old carriers to other countries. In contrast, although the US is the main global exporter of decommissioned warships, its export list rarely includes aircraft carriers. Earlier this year, some Indian news websites claimed that the US might give the "Constellation" aircraft carrier to India to counterbalance the Chinese Navy, but both the US and Indian officials denied this claim. Why doesn't the US export decommissioned aircraft carriers to other countries? There are mainly two reasons: First, the US side considers technical blockades and confidentiality. According to relevant regulations, after US aircraft carriers are decommissioned, they undergo strict security processing, meaning the removal of all important combat systems to prevent other countries from obtaining military secrets. However, due to the maturity and leadership of the US in shipbuilding technology and engineering, even a decommissioned aircraft carrier that has undergone security processing can still teach many things to countries that cannot independently build aircraft carriers, which is something the highly secretive US Navy does not want to see. Second, even if the US agrees to sell, for countries wanting to purchase decommissioned US aircraft carriers, simply buying an old ship is quite different from actually forming combat capabilities. Taking the "Kennedy" aircraft carrier as an example, purchasing this large aircraft carrier may not cost much money, but restoring its original combat and command systems, equipping it with corresponding carrier-based aircraft (which can only be American-made), and training the crew members, the total cost is extremely high, and navies of general medium and small countries cannot afford it. (Source: Xinhua News Agency, Author: Guowei Liu)
