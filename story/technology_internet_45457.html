The Current State of Korean Blogs, Part Two

by impark on 2008-01-28 08:50:32

Today, at the request of the Blog Alliance, I will once again talk to everyone about the current state of blogs in our neighboring country, South Korea. For the last update on the status of South Korean blogs, you can refer to the previous article. Our neighboring country, South Korea, is an IT powerhouse with a very high普及 rate of computer usage. With a population of about 50 million, it is said that the普及 rate of computers is around 70%. When it comes to BSP (Blog Service Providers), it originally started as blog services provided by major portal sites, such as Naver, which ranks first in South Korea, along with other websites like Daum, Empas, and Paran. Among these sites, it goes without saying that the blog service provided by Naver ranks first in BSPs. However, later on, Daum placed great emphasis on the blog market, initially investing in Tistory, and eventually acquiring the site outright. To briefly introduce Naver and Daum: Naver is South Korea's top search engine provider, and its knowledge Q&A service is extremely popular, similar to Baidu Knows. It reportedly holds 60%~70% of South Korea's search service market share. Daum initially focused on providing quality email services but has since fallen behind Naver. Currently, Daum's focus is on the blog market and video market, or what they call the UCC (User-Created Content) market. In terms of market share, Tistory and the blog services provided by Daum combined should now surpass Naver to rank first among BSPs. The main content of South Korean blogs revolves around technology, politics, and entertainment. Every day, reading their blog posts, one can't help but marvel at how free the discourse is. Before the presidential election in December last year, on allblog, which ranks first among South Korean blog aggregation sites, there were always many political articles visible. They could comment on any politician, criticize the current president, discuss candidates, and more. Regarding technology, they also have a strong interest in Apple products and new IT news. Compared to Chinese blogs, political discourse is relatively freer. From a technological perspective, they are not particularly interested in services like Facebook or Feedburner but show significant interest in Google. They frequently discuss domestic technological services as well. Another feature is their active use of Trackback; if they see an article similar to their own, they generally send a Trackback to inform the author of their interest, thereby fostering communication. When it comes to corporate blogs, some people support them while others oppose them. However, it seems that there isn't yet a particularly popular corporate blog. To give examples, the second-largest car manufacturer in South Korea, Kia, launched an English blog built on WordPress, and LG Electronics opened a blog using Tistory, South Korea's top BSP, called Xcanvasblog. Of course, everyone knows that the ultimate purpose of a company setting up a blog is still for promotion—not in the form of a numb website, but utilizing the latest popular Web 2.0 concept. This way, direct communication with users can be achieved to attain certain effects. In South Korea, most corporate blogs are built on TISTORY because this blogging platform has many features and is very easy to use. South Korea has quite a number of well-known bloggers.
