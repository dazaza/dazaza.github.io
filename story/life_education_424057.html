Five Tricks to Help You Master SAT Reading

Reading comprehension is the core part of the entire SAT exam and also the most troubling part for many candidates, as it not only requires a solid vocabulary foundation but also corresponding test-taking skills. Some people say, "In the SAT battle, the one who masters reading wins the world." Given the importance and complexity of the reading comprehension section, the training teachers at Zhongliu Education have summarized seven strategies based on their teaching experience to share with all SAT candidates.

First Trick: Break It Down and Work Faster

"Breaking it down" means dividing longer articles into several parts for understanding. The most common articles in the SAT reading comprehension section are about 850 words long. If we read these eighty-plus lines and then quickly answer the questions, it has no practical significance. This is because the human brain cannot absorb so much information at once. Therefore, we should divide the article according to its flow into several parts, i.e., break it down, and then focus our efforts on analyzing and answering questions related to a specific part of the article. This effectively prevents many people from the problem of "reading the latter part and forgetting the former."

In most cases, we divide the article according to its genre habitually. Generally speaking, the italicized part and the first paragraph of a long reading passage would be considered as the first major section of the article. Then we can continue to divide the sections based on our understanding of the article. Of course, there is no absolute standard for dividing sections, nor is there a specific correct answer. As long as we understand the general idea of the article, we can divide the sections based on our understanding. For example, an expository article might be divided into three parts: presenting the phenomenon, several guesses or solutions, and summarizing.

Second Trick: Grasp the Main Idea for Accuracy

"Grasping the main idea" requires us to clearly understand and master the main point of the article. The benefit of grasping the main idea is first reflected in our ability to answer main idea questions in reading comprehension. For example: What is the main idea of the passage? What is the best title for the passage? With which of the following statements will the author most likely agree/disagree? These are typical ways main idea questions are phrased in SAT reading comprehension. Secondly, understanding the main idea helps us comprehend the article as a whole, preventing the occurrence of "seeing the trees but missing the forest." Since SAT reading tests the candidate's reasoning ability, which differs greatly from TOEFL or IELTS reading that stays at the language understanding level, identifying the main idea of the article has become an important prerequisite for ensuring the accuracy of answers. In class, I often compare identifying the main idea of an article to the direction of sailing in the sea; without knowing the direction, we can never reach our expected goal correctly.

So, where exactly do we find the main idea of an SAT long reading passage? This is the most crucial point in understanding the article and answering questions correctly. The articles in the SAT reading comprehension section are excerpts from academic or literary materials published in books, newspapers, etc. Test-makers often select suitable segments from a large article for testing candidates, then compress and summarize the deleted parts into the italicized introduction and the first paragraph; they also summarize the deleted parts at the end into the last paragraph. Here, we must highly value a very special feature of SAT reading comprehension: the italicized part at the beginning of the article. This part provides a large amount of background information related to the article, which is actually the test-makers' effort to prevent the selected segment from being too isolated and difficult to infer the answers. Therefore, to find the main idea of an SAT long reading passage (except for novels), we should look at the italicized part at the beginning, the first paragraph, and the last paragraph.

Third Trick: Line Numbering for Faster Reading

After observing and studying the SAT reading comprehension section, it's not hard to find that 90% of the questions after the article contain line numbers for reference. This characteristic of the questions greatly helps improve our reading speed. Previously, many candidates complained that when doing SAT reading, they either didn't have time to answer the questions after reading the article or didn't have time to fully understand the article while answering the questions. In fact, candidates don't need to worry about this "read or answer" dilemma, as even American high school students can't completely understand the full text before answering the questions. These questions also don't require us to fully understand the original text before answering them. Therefore, obtaining as much information as possible in the shortest time is the key factor to succeeding in SAT reading.

Fourth Trick: Tackle Easier Questions First

For the entire SAT reading comprehension section, the questions mainly fall into three levels: The first level consists of detail questions like REFERENCE LINE and SIDEBAR mentioned above. The second level includes inference questions within a small scope. These questions are marked by words like INFER, SUGGEST, IMPLY in the question stem. These questions require us to think broadly and combine what we've read with our feelings. That is, we need to derive conclusions beyond the content of the article based on our understanding. The third level includes the main idea questions mentioned earlier.

We cannot simply tackle the questions sequentially from start to finish. We should selectively start with detail questions, then move on to small-scope inference questions, and finally tackle the main idea questions covering the entire article. This way, we can progressively obtain relevant clues from smaller-scope questions to help with larger-scope questions. Thus, as we answer more questions, we will feel a growing sense of clarity.

However, we shouldn't arbitrarily skip inference and main idea questions, as many failed experiences show that skipping questions comes with responsibility. In the 2009 exam, several candidates forgot the skipped questions and made mistakes on the answer sheet, leading to losing the entire SECTION. Therefore, I suggest marking the questions we plan to skip with a pencil to avoid forgetting. This way, even if we don't have time to answer these questions, it won't affect the normal scoring of other questions.

Fifth Trick: "Adapt to the Situation" for Precise Inference

If the main idea of the article helps us grasp the overall direction, then the author's attitude and emotions in the text serve as the small direction guiding us to the right answers. Moreover, discerning the author's attitude and emotions helps us answer emotion-related questions, such as "What is the author’s attitude in paragraph 3?" Even if the test-maker doesn't ask about the author's attitude in a particular part, we should still fully appreciate the author's thoughts and feelings while reading. Only by doing so can we "adapt to the situation," find the test-maker's original starting point, and confidently select our inferred answers.

For Chinese candidates, it's not only difficult to truly understand the author's attitude but also challenging to match each emotional attitude in the answer options with the correct English expression. Therefore, during our regular vocabulary accumulation, we must pay special attention to these emotional attitude words to avoid situations where we know the meaning but can't identify the correct answer during the test.
