Silicone breast implants are not permanent.

by nianhan8644 on 2012-02-09 02:09:52

Silicone breast implant insertion is not forever. The farthest report from the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) says, "Be aware that breast implants are not eternal." Due to some rare complications, many men with silicone implants will need to adjust or remove the implants via surgery within about 10 years. This is an important and noteworthy warning, especially for women aged between their 20s to 30s, as this is precisely the age when a small minority of women undergo breast implant surgery. For those women who have undergone implant surgery for aesthetic purposes and those who have had it due to mastectomy, the durability of silicone implants varies. The FDA states that 20% to 40% of women who have had implants for aesthetic reasons need to have them removed, while 40% to 70% of women who had implants after mastectomy also need to have them removed. Damaged implants generally fall outside the quality guarantee range of regular manufacturers. According to a report by MSNBC.com, despite continuous complications arising from silicone implants, the FDA still considers them relatively safe. Preliminary analysis studies did not find evidence indicating that silicone implants increase the probability of cancer, reproductive abnormalities, or other issues. More women prefer silicone implants over saline ones because silicone looks and feels more natural. This is good news for them. These findings come from some investigative studies conducted by Mentor Corporation and Allergan Inc. (both breast implant manufacturers). Silicone implants were banned in the U.S. market starting in 1992 and were only approved to return to the market in 2006. However, behind this approval lies a massive decade-long research effort by many manufacturers. The FDA's report is the first disclosure of these research results. The FDA report shows that Mentor Corporation collected three-year data on one-fifth of the women who received implants, while Allergan Corporation gathered two-year data on three-fifths of the women who received implants. To involve more participants in the survey, the FDA collaborates with these companies to jointly analyze the data. Dr. Jeffrey Shuren, head of the FDA’s Center for Medical Health, recommends that women with implants undergo regular checks and be vigilant for any changes or symptoms. Since early detection is very important, women considering implant surgery must maintain contact. The FDA has yet to reach a definitive conclusion on silicone implants. Do not forget to consult certified plastic surgeons.
