Facebook registers 61 related trademarks in China to eliminate possible disputes

by ming1980k on 2012-02-19 13:17:56

Trademark Bureau Query Results - Sina Technology News, February 16th afternoon news: As Apple's battle with Shenzhen Proview over the iPad trademark is heating up, Facebook has already registered 61 trademarks in mainland China, including "Facebook" and its Chinese name "LianShu" (脸书). This move has effectively secured almost all potential trademarks related to Facebook, minimizing the possibility of future trademark squatting as Facebook plans to enter the Chinese market.

According to the query results from the Trademark Office of the State Administration for Industry and Commerce, Facebook Inc. has registered a total of 61 trademarks in mainland China. All these trademarks are registered under "FACEBOOK, INC." and cover seven different categories. The trademarks include names such as "FacebooK," "LianPu" (脸谱), "LianShu" (脸书) which is commonly used in Hong Kong, Macao, and Taiwan regions, along with "THEFACEBOOK," "FeiSiBoKe" (菲丝博克), and the letter "F," among other phonetic translations and associated trademarks.

Facebook’s foresight in securing these trademarks significantly reduces the likelihood of future trademark disputes when entering the Chinese market. However, the query results do not indicate when Facebook registered these trademarks. As early as November 2007, Facebook had registered a domain name with a .cn suffix, but there was no further news on this front until February this year, when Facebook listed Tencent, Sina, and others as competitors in its IPO application submitted to the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission, leading outsiders to speculate that Facebook may soon take new actions to enter the Chinese market.

The news of Facebook registering 61 trademarks has been widely shared on Weibo, as it forms a stark contrast with the ongoing trademark dispute involving Apple's iPad. The query results from the Trademark Office of the State Administration for Industry and Commerce show that trademarks related to "Pad," from "APad" to "ZPad," have already been squatted. This means that if Apple wants to retain the "Pad" logo, it would be very difficult to choose a prefix from the 26 letters of the alphabet. (Yun Fang)
