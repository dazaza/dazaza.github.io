Amazon's New Kindle Fire Bets Big on Business Users

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 10:56:06

New Kindle Fire Released by Amazon

It’s not difficult to predict when Amazon will release a new generation of its Kindle Fire tablet: the company seems to only stop promoting the previous version on its homepage—one of the most valuable places in the internet world—around this time. Just days before Amazon CEO Jeff Bezos announced the new Kindle Fire on September 25, this situation occurred again. This is Bezos's most serious challenge yet to other tablets like the iPad, but seriousness doesn't always guarantee success.

Over two years, Amazon has been experimenting in the rapidly evolving tablet market and has gained a certain market position. As the world's largest online retailer and producer of the Kindle e-reader, Amazon provides consumers with easy access to its virtual store and offers streaming music and video services. The Kindle Fire business was launched in 2011 with an initial 7-inch product, followed by two sizes with HD screens the next year.

Although Amazon has shaped some essential elements of the tablet market, it is far from being considered victorious: according to U.S. market research firm IDC, the company held only a 4.6% share of the U.S. tablet market in the second quarter, significantly lower than the iPad’s 50.7% and Samsung’s 18.5%. Amazon's previous tablets were criticized for underperforming compared to the iPad and lacking signature features. "They found that the Kindle Fire is popular among Amazon fans. But beyond that, it didn't perform well in the market," said Sarah Rotman Epps, an analyst at Forrester Research.

The new generation of Kindle Fire tablets offers stronger processing power and continues Amazon's consistent low-price policy, with the entry-level HD model starting at $139 and the HDX model at $229. All products are lighter and faster than previous Kindle Fires, and they can automatically adjust screen brightness based on ambient light. Additionally, these devices come equipped with a feature called Mayday, which focuses more on people than technology. Through this feature, users can call Amazon customer service personnel who provide after-sales service via online video. "When testers discovered and used this function, their jaws dropped to the table," Bezos said.

The old Kindle Fire lowered the price floor for tablets and attracted many imitators, including even the iPad mini. However, the Kindle Fire never perfectly integrated into users' lives. It uses a heavily customized version of Google's Android operating system, leading Google to be reluctant to develop popular applications such as Gmail, Google Maps, and Chrome for it. Instead, Amazon provided its own email program and Silk browser, using Nokia's map application. Bezos refused to comment on whether Google apps would be available on the Kindle Fire, emphasizing instead the speed and progress of his own software.

The Kindle Fire was also delisted by Target and Walmart due to competitive reasons. Bezos pointed out that the Kindle Fire still has over 20,000 retail channels globally, with growing annual sales, though he did not disclose specific figures.

Of course, Bezos personally spent $250 million in August to acquire The Washington Post. Speaking about this acquisition, he said that tablets are "a major opportunity for newspapers." He promised that the two companies would operate independently. "There is an opportunity for them to help each other, but it will happen in normal ways," Bezos said.

Tablets may be important for newspapers, but they are even more crucial for Amazon. Since users must pay a commission when purchasing Amazon content through Apple and Google services, Amazon hopes to sell its own hardware to all users. For this reason, Bezos has spent a long time developing digital services. This summer, he traveled from Seattle headquarters to Silicon Valley, where Amazon's hardware subsidiary Lab126 is located. He focused on "areas where things change fast, and allow me to integrate various departments of my company together."

Bezos's biggest challenge is to make Amazon's hardware as attractive as Apple's products. Currently, he continues to add extra services and innovations as much as possible. Amazon Prime members can download videos onto the new Kindle Fire, freeing themselves from wireless networks. Bezos also emphasized the enterprise tools of the new Kindle Fire, including data encryption functions. "The biggest surprise with the second-generation Kindle Fire is the usage and purchase volume by enterprise users," he said, "So, even if it isn't flashy, we will double down on all productivity elements."

"Amazon is doing what they should do in the tablet market and isn’t resting on their laurels," Epps said. During the launch of the new Kindle Fire, promotional information was already displayed on Amazon's homepage. This information will remain there throughout the entire Christmas shopping season, even if the company releases a set-top box similar to Apple TV (Bezos declined to comment on product release plans outside of Kindle Fire). Bezos said that the lessons learned from the previous generation of Kindle Fire are: "People don't want to buy two tablets separately for entertainment and work; they just want one." But the question remains, will that one come from Amazon?
