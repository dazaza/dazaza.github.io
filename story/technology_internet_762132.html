7 Reasons Why You Need To Work For A Big Company

The author of this article, Mark Stephen, is the founder and CEO of IDR Solutions.

Large companies are often seen as sluggish and lacking in excitement, while startups are viewed as flexible, efficient, and full of fun. I am currently running a startup, but to be honest, I am glad that I spent some memorable time working for a large company. Below are the reasons why I think working for a big company can be great:

1. Witnessing Excellence and Absurdity

You will see both the best and the worst in one place. You will come across brilliant insights about source code control and also experience long-winded talks that make people disengage. Large companies may not be as dynamic, but they have their own reasons for growing and thriving. Even the most ordinary institutions have hidden talents. Understanding the logic behind how large companies operate and identifying employees who possess both technical skills and political acumen will be a valuable asset for you.

2. Working with Talented People

In some departments of large companies, you will find clusters of smart individuals. In my opinion, working alongside talented people is an incredible experience, as if the air around you radiates intelligence. These smart people choose to work for large companies mainly due to family and responsibility considerations, as they are unwilling to take too many risks by joining startups. Experienced core employees can teach you a lot, whereas in startups, your colleagues are often around your age and less experienced.

3. Leveraging Strong Networks

Twelve years ago, I worked for a large company. To this day, I still stay in touch with my former colleagues and frequently run into them in various places. This connection is very precious. In today's era of social media, it remains crucial for people to know who you are and what you can do. Your employment history at a large company will be documented, and when you need to conduct business with large companies, this background will provide you with a green lane.

4. Enjoying Excellent Benefits

I miss the cafeteria and gym of the large company I used to work for, and that summer trip to Amsterdam paid for by the company... Oracle training courses funded by the company, fully reimbursed MBA programs, just thinking about it feels great! However, in startups, getting your book expenses reimbursed is already considered generous. Would you rather spend someone else's money to gain knowledge while working at a large company or work 100 hours a week at a startup in pursuit of a distant future?

5. Learning Political Skills

In large companies, you learn not only technical skills but also "diplomatic" and "political" skills. When the day comes that you transition from being an employee to a partner, these skills will come in handy. Large companies like to buy things and spend money generously, understanding their operational rules will help you better conduct business and tap into more potential clients through them.

6. Slower Pace, Better Time

Startups have a fast-paced rhythm like meteors racing against the moon, whereas large companies often move at a slower pace. Although it lacks some of the excitement, it provides more leisure time for learning, recharging, and self-reflection. Sometimes, this "slower pace" can have unexpected benefits. I won't tell you how a major media company avoided spending millions on a ridiculous internet project simply because decision-making was too slow, which coincided with the arrival of the dot-com bubble...

7. Sticking to Principles for Your Dreams

When you first enter the workforce, you may feel bold and fearless, but after experiencing the ups and downs of working for a large company, you will understand what is worth doing and what is not, realizing that some things are important while others are trivial. You will also identify your lifelong dreams and unbreakable principles. Defining your professional creed can help you perform better.

Above are the reasons why I enjoy working for large companies. What are your reasons?
