Dianping: The Bone That Giants Can't Crack; Living Outside the Comfort Zone

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 17:55:44

Zhang Tao, CEO of Dianping

Written by Yuan Yin, edited by Yang Jing, photographed by Xiao Nan

Zhang Tao, the CEO of Dianping, has once again postponed the company's IPO.

In 2011, after receiving a $100 million Series C funding round from Sequoia Capital and others, Dianping's valuation reached $1 billion. According to convention, it should have chosen an appropriate time for its IPO within the following one to two years, but it didn't. Instead, in 2012, it raised another $60 million in Series D funding.

With Dianping's scale and development momentum, going public wouldn't be difficult at all. However, Zhang Tao feels that listing now would be too costly. He breaks down the large internet (PC + mobile) into four elements: people, information, goods, and services. Each connection point offers the opportunity to produce giants. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, and Alibaba connects people with goods. The space between people and services is where the fourth giant could emerge. Since 2003, Dianping has been diligently accumulating data, finally waiting for the explosion of mobile internet, which brings together "location" and "service." Following Zhang Tao's logic, no company seems more fitting for the imagination of the fourth giant than Dianping.

But the outside world doesn't think so. In recent months, with the frequent acquisition strategies of BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent), Dianping has become a hypothetical acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition candidate; whichever family name of BAT it takes on would help them achieve great success early. On the other hand, Dianping's size pales in comparison to BAT's, and it's still premature to declare that it will become a giant. Moreover, whether it's Tencent WeChat's public platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after investing in AutoNavi and Meituan, they are all targeting Dianping's territory — local life services focused on food, drink, entertainment, and leisure.

Before proving the following two things, Dianping going public would be equivalent to "selling at a discount." First, Zhang Tao needs some time to prove that what Dianping is doing now cannot be achieved by BAT. Second, Dianping is about to engage in the ultimate battle of group buying. If it secures the top spot, its valuation could increase several times over.

2013 is Dianping's "year of decisive battles." Regarding speculations and rumors, Zhang Tao pays them no heed. He is very clear: "The internet has indeed entered an era of great integration, but Dianping will not be acquired."

Zhang Tao categorizes companies that might be acquired into three types. The first type has platform attributes but is more suitable to develop within the ecosystem of giants, such as maps and email. These products can easily attract a large number of users but struggle to find an independent business model. Others, like browsers, while profitable, earn far less through navigation services than their strategic value to large companies. "Giants are willing to spend money maintaining an email product because it helps with branding and user stickiness, but I haven't heard of any standalone email company succeeding." The second type involves industry consolidation, like in the video industry, using acquisitions to expand market share. The third type involves companies facing financial difficulties selling themselves for survival.

Now Meituan has already been brought under Alibaba's wing. Theoretically, Dianping should also quickly find a "backer" to avoid being defeated due to rapid expansion funded by competitors. Zhang Tao, however, says, "The giants' assistance to us is limited. We do need users, but execution is more important. Mergers and acquisitions would reduce our execution capabilities and could even marginalize us within a large company." Furthermore, he aims to keep Dianping at the most precise "distance" from the giants. Getting too close to any one of them would be akin to "taking sides," making Dianping an enemy to the others.

Thus, logically, the acquisition of Dianping becomes a dead end. Zhang Tao counters, "Why would we take their money? We don't lack funds; what we need are resources. If a giant is willing to make a small investment and provide us with many resources, I could consider it. But would they? Would a giant give you resources if they only hold a small stake?"

Weapon One: Dirty and Hard Work

In mobile internet, opportunities for companies solely focusing on pure internet products are becoming fewer. New opportunities arise from those combining with traditional industries. Zhang Tao believes that life services mainly revolve around food, drink, entertainment, and leisure. Other segmented markets, such as ride-hailing, can form decent business models, but it's hard for them to grow into giants.

For food, drink, entertainment, and leisure, user needs are simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is the core of life services, which is exactly where Dianping started. Whether in content, member accumulation, or brand, newcomers find it hard to surpass Dianping.

The most representative product form of discounts is group buying. Now group buying has entered the championship race, and Dianping plans to battle Meituan in 2013. This is a winner-takes-all market; as long as there are two strong players, the industry's gross margin will be pulled down, preventing stable profitability.

Another foreseeable future is reservations. Reservations can form a true online-to-offline closed loop, but this must be built on the foundation of Dianping having absolute advantages in both information and discounts. Otherwise, building a closed loop would be difficult. In 2012, Dianping began trying out reservations. Zhang Tao said, "Currently, both user and merchant feedback is good, but if we were to focus solely on reservations, it would be hard to solve the chicken-or-egg problem."

Of course, life services offer some even "heavier" opportunities. For example, food delivery is a rigid demand for users and can increase sales for merchants. In the past couple of years, food delivery companies like "Daojia Meishi Hui" have flourished. However, the core of food delivery is distribution, meaning delivering within a short time frame, dealing with soup spillage issues, and requiring logistics standards higher than fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang Tao said, "If executed well, food delivery does have opportunities, but it's harder dirty work. What Dianping is already doing is tough enough."

If judged by age, Dianping is already an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has painstakingly accumulated merchant data and user reviews, which Zhang Tao calls "dirty and hard work." This primitive growth model once restricted its speed, but the pain of the past has become Dianping's core barrier today. It can be said that currently, besides Meituan, Dianping has no real rivals in the life services sector.

However, he still needs to be wary of BAT's "clutches." Baidu's involvement is the most direct. With over 100 million users, Baidu Maps allows users to search for nearby dining and other life services. Tencent, considered to have secured a ticket to mobile internet, launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest physical goods trading platform, expanding into services naturally makes sense, especially since it has incorporated Meituan and AutoNavi into its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there hasn't been an explosive growth trend in reality or data." Zhang Tao said, "Brand positioning is crucial; the key is which product users turn to first when they want to eat, drink, play, or relax."

Moreover, in the vast field of life services, competition among giants will inevitably cross over, making complete monopolization impossible. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained on Weibo and WeChat. Zhang Tao feels that this makes Dianping safer instead. "In segmented fields like car rentals and business travel, the massive user base directed by giants could easily overwhelm existing companies."

As for Alibaba, which has invested in AutoNavi, invading Dianping's territory isn't that simple either. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, no matter how peculiar, which can still be categorized. A user's experience of a meal depends on various factors like the day's environment, waitstaff, and chefs. Managing merchants cannot rely solely on systems and tools like Alibaba uses; a 'ground force' must be established, managed by 'people.'

"All three giants want to go down this path, and there is room for imagination, but this isn't a highway; it's a rugged mountain road." Zhang Tao believes that first, providing life services requires ground teams deployed at various levels, which BAT lacks the genes and ability to manage offline teams. Second, they lack the willingness; life services require dedicated efforts, not just assigning a small team to accomplish it, and profits come slowly. "A company finds it hard to excel in two major tasks simultaneously, let alone needing to consolidate their advantages in their original fields."

Weapon Two: Small Boats Are Easier to Turn

When Dianping raised $100 million in 2011, Zhang Tao told the public that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, times change fast, and before Dianping went public, his analogous counterparts fell from grace.

He wasn't overly worried about it. "They failed to become big companies because they lacked offline teams; now they want to be America's Dianping."

The current path is something Zhang Tao has gradually figured out step by step. When he felt that selling keyword ads and coupons was outdated, the emergence of group buying caught his attention. Initially, like Americans, he understood group buying as a marketing platform, one deal per day. However, as orders increased, he realized that group buying had characteristics of a trading platform. Thus, he saw the future of Dianping as a life services trading platform. The two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were precisely used for Dianping's transformation from an information platform to a trading platform.

Without this "genetic modification," Zhang Tao might not have had the confidence today to compete for the fourth giant's position. But genetic modification sounds simple, yet in reality, it is equivalent to Dianping's second entrepreneurship, an extremely painful process.

Firstly, compared to selling ads separately, trading platforms have lower gross margins and require stricter cost control and efficiency. Secondly, trading platforms demand higher inter-departmental communication skills.

To maintain Dianping's impartiality and independence as an information platform, the content team and the sales team were completely separated. Zhang Tao even deliberately established a "firewall" between departments — even for the most important clients, sales couldn't modify platform information for sales purposes, which once led to some mutual hostility between content and sales teams. Now, as a trading platform, the editorial and sales teams need deep communication to create good marketing plans. Besides planning, editor cooperation is needed. He said, "Maintaining information quality remains important, but there's no need to reinforce animosity anymore; firewalls need to be dismantled."

Though editors can logically accept this, individual behaviors and values have inertia, and when combined across the entire company, the inertia becomes greater. To address this, Zhang Tao specially held several meetings to promote more communication between departments. Months later, an editor manager casually told a sales manager, "I used to think you were my enemy, but now I see you as a colleague."

The sales department also needs genetic modification. To promote conversion, Zhang Tao cleverly gave the offline team dual roles. First, for merchants, salespeople should become marketing consultants, not just selling products but helping merchants design marketing plans. Second, for the company, marketing consultants are the company's buyers. Dianping is like a supermarket; not only must the product quality be good, but the variety must also be rich. Marketing consultants should aim to help users purchase group-buying products. Of course, dual roles will inevitably generate a series of complex problems. For frontline personnel, this is quite headache-inducing; they must balance the scales themselves.

Substantially speaking, Dianping's "dual role" is very similar to Alibaba's division into 25 business units. By decentralizing decision-making power to the frontlines, the company gains faster response speeds but increases management difficulty. Similarly, compared to frontline employees, Dianping's management team suffers more after genetic modification.

Every year, Zhang Tao decomposes Dianping's company goals into various departments, then each executive further breaks them down layer by layer, but decomposition isn't simple addition or subtraction. Li Jing, co-founder of Dianping, responsible for the sales team, sometimes finds unresolved merchant demands due to sales issues affecting performance. Thus, Li Jing broke down a new position, setting up communication specialists who collect information daily from marketing consultants, submit it to other departments, and track feedback. He often tells his team, "Being good at embracing change doesn't mean liking change; no one likes change. When changes occur, everyone feels uncomfortable. All employees must go through discomfort zones, including me."

Digital figures reflect the "heavy" nature of Dianping's execution team. Out of the current 3000+ employees, more than 2000 work offline. The ground forces directly communicate with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group buys, and combination marketing plans.

For this core team resisting BAT's claws, Dianping has a unique management and evaluation system.

They are not called "sales"; from the first day, their business cards read "Marketing Planning Consultant." Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the phase of watching news and chatting on QQ, at least three to five years behind Taobao merchants in terms of internet understanding. Marketing planning consultants need to tell merchants what online users' needs are and how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing said, "Even for a high-end restaurant, marketing advisors wouldn't suggest group-buying abalone or shark fins, possibly replacing them with other dishes, because online users and offline users have different demands."

An outstanding marketing advisor must be a versatile player. They need to explain to restaurant owners and hair salon owners the essential differences between discounts and group buys; which kind of discount suits their positioning better. If facing a hotpot restaurant owner, the marketing advisor should know whether it's the off-season or peak season, which product costs rise during certain seasons and which fall, proposing a group-buying menu that both merchants and users prefer.

This places high demands on marketing advisors, requiring them not only to be internet marketing experts but also industry experts. Li Jing said, "I must admit, it's impossible for every advisor to be a double expert, so we've set star ratings for advisors." Like hotels, marketing advisors range from one-star to five-star, with the best being like supermen, called Super Stars.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline employees aren't tech-savvy graduates from prestigious universities, making it difficult for them to understand internet products initially. Dianping has established a knowledge base for marketing advisors, containing knowledge points and relevant cases, accessible to both new and old employees. If encountering new problems, employees can ask questions within the knowledge base, and thousands of consultants nationwide will help answer them.

A large number of knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees based on practical experiences. Dianping has set up a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, rewarding employees who help others the most each quarter. Every quarter, Dianping conducts skill assessments for employees, directly linking results to promotions.

Industry knowledge is harder to acquire than internet knowledge. Some experiences are summarized by traditional merchants after years of practice in the industry. A Super Star marketing advisor told Li Jing that hair salons should avoid offering shampoo-cut-blow-dry group-buy packages because men lack motivation to buy them, and women mostly blow dry without needing the full package. Li Jing was astonished, "We men would never think of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing advisors are not only divided by cities but also by industries such as catering, beauty salons, and leisure. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is further subdivided into hotpot, Western cuisine, and buffets, with one marketing advisor responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing advisors can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, making merchants less resistant when selling products," he said.

Each marketing group holds its own case-sharing sessions: what challenges a particular type of merchant faces, what marketing needs they have, which products and solutions are used, and the outcomes. Outstanding cases are reported upwards, and Dianping officially shares one case per week.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition called "Entrepreneurial Winner." Within the first three months of joining, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products but listening to merchants sharing experiences. If they're responsible for hotpot restaurants, after three months, they must submit a business plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hotpot Restaurant Owner." Like graduation, employees present their plans on-site, and industry supervisors evaluate the winners, rewarding them accordingly.

Strategically speaking, whether it's Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, their goals are quite similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O are common knowledge among people familiar with the industry, and their spoken words are almost identical. "The era where storytelling and concept creation could lead to success is over. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model lies in management and execution, not strategy," Zhang Tao said.
