The Dilemma of Appliance Distribution to the Countryside

by jkldas52 on 2010-04-08 14:15:18

The "Appliance Trade-in Program" has unexpectedly caused some second-hand appliance markets in Guangzhou to boom. Many citizens who wish to buy new appliances first purchase inexpensive used appliances from the second-hand market, using them as trade-ins to claim government subsidies, thereby earning the difference. Some second-hand shop owners have taken advantage of the lack of strict supervision at various stages of the "trade-in program," selling dismantled and empty shell old appliances at high prices, creating a cycle that results in wasted government fiscal subsidies.

Even empty shell TVs are sold out

Shop owner: If you need a large quantity, I can help organize the supply.

An undercover investigation revealed that the second-hand home appliance market thrived due to the "trade-in program," with very tight market conditions.

Suddenly hot-selling warehouse waste TVs

Xintao Street Electronics and Electrical Appliances City is a well-known second-hand electrical trading market within the city. At a store with a full range of second-hand TVs, when the reporter inquired about the price of second-hand TVs, the shop owner discreetly said that there was now less stock available, and if one wanted something better, they would have to place an order in advance. The shop owner explained that old TVs were originally unsold inventory but suddenly became highly sought after at the beginning of last year. "I even threw some away, not knowing that suddenly every day people came to buy them." He added that cheap TVs priced at just a few dozen yuan had already been sold out. If someone wanted something affordable, 100-yuan TVs were still available for purchase. The shop owner also mentioned that if a larger quantity was needed, he could help "organize supplies," ensuring delivery within a day, with negotiable pricing.

"Everyone buys them for trade-ins"

The reporter then visited Shop B06 on Xintao Street, where many old TVs were piled up outside, mostly liquid crystal or plasma flat-screen TVs. When asked if there were any old TVs for sale, the boss's wife replied that their main business was repairing and dismantling circuit boards and cathode-ray tubes, so they rarely sold functioning TVs. However, when the reporter mentioned buying an old TV solely for the purpose of trade-ins without concern for viewing quality, the boss's wife quickly changed her tone: "People have bought them before, even empty TV shells." "Can an empty shell work?" the reporter asked. "There were two people who bought (two) empty shells after we removed all useful parts inside," she responded. Pointing to a dismantled "empty shell," she added, "Many people take them like this; we remove the usable parts, no problem." When asked how much an empty shell cost, the boss's wife said around 100 yuan. After the reporter expressed that it was too expensive, she argued, "You can exchange it for 200-300 yuan, so our price isn't high."

At A80B on the second floor of Xintao Street, the boss's wife firmly stated that the cheapest old TV costs 100 yuan. When the reporter inquired about possible discounts, she replied, "No, we don't have enough stock." Since there wasn't much physical inventory at the stall, she offered to fetch some from the warehouse, stating that the condition of the TV didn't matter. However, when the reporter expressed concern about whether non-functional TVs could be traded in, she confidently asserted, "No need to turn it on! For trade-ins, there's no need to turn it on!" Seeing the reporter still hesitating, she promised again, "Guaranteed you'll get the trade-in, or you can return it!" She added that small old TVs sell well, with many people willing to pay 100 yuan, saying, "Everyone is buying to trade for new ones; who still buys broken TVs to watch?"

Undismantled old appliances back into the market?

The state policy mandates that old appliances collected through the trade-in program must be handed over to designated dismantling enterprises for mandatory scrapping and cannot enter the second-hand market. However, reporters discovered that some recycling personnel still risked breaking the rules by swapping customers' valuable old appliances with broken ones from the second-hand market to meet quotas, re-selling the swapped appliances back into the second-hand market for profit.

Additionally, according to relevant policies, citizens who hand in old appliances through designated recycling companies should receive a receipt detailing consumer information, the brand and product number of the old appliance, and the recycling price. However, investigations found that due to lax inspections, recycling personnel often filled out these forms carelessly.

Some citizens worry that if second-hand appliances are not properly handled after being recycled and instead flow back into the second-hand market through certain channels, repeatedly claiming fiscal subsidies, wouldn't the state incur significant losses?

In response, publicity personnel from Guangzhou Gome and Suning both indicated that large enterprises operate more standardly, strictly following the trade-in operation procedures, "Dismantling is done by state-bid dismantling enterprises; we transport the collected old appliances to large warehouses and then send them to Huizhou for dismantling."

After calculating, it was found that generally speaking, in Guangzhou, a color TV that can still be watched sells for around 200 yuan, while non-functional old TVs only require 50-100 yuan. With the highest trade-in subsidy reaching 400 yuan, this means that fiscal subsidies spend seven times more than normal second-hand appliance transactions to purchase unwatchable scrap iron TVs.
