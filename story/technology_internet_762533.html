Dianping.com: The Bone That Giants Can't Crack, Living in the Zone of Discomfort

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 15:57:44

CEO of Dianping.com, Zhang Tao

Written by Yuan Yin, edited by Yang Jing, photography by Xiao Nan

Zhang Tao, CEO of Dianping.com, has once again postponed the company's IPO timeline.

In 2011, after securing a $100 million Series C funding round from Sequoia Capital and others, Dianping's valuation reached $1 billion. Following standard practice, it should have chosen to go public within the subsequent one to two years, but it did not. Instead, in 2012, it conducted another $60 million Series D funding round.

Given Dianping's scale and development trajectory, going public wouldn't be difficult. However, Zhang Tao feels that an IPO now would be too costly. He breaks down the big internet (PC + mobile) into four elements—people, information, goods, and services—and each connection point offers the opportunity for giants to emerge. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, Alibaba connects people with goods, and the space between people and services represents the seat of the fourth giant. Since 2003, Dianping has been diligently accumulating data, finally waiting for the explosion of mobile internet, where smartphones merge "location" and "services." According to Zhang Tao's logic, no company seems more fitting than Dianping to occupy the imagination of the fourth giant.

However, outsiders don't see it this way. In recent months, with frequent acquisitions by BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent), Dianping has become a hypothetical acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition candidate; whether branded under any of the BAT initials, it could help them achieve great success sooner. On the other hand, Dianping's size is far smaller compared to BAT, and it's still premature to declare it as a future giant. Moreover, whether it’s Tencent WeChat's public platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after investing in AutoNavi and Meituan, all are directly targeting Dianping's territory—the local life services centered around dining, entertainment, and leisure.

Before proving the following two things, a Dianping IPO would equal a "discounted sale." First, Zhang Tao needs time to prove that what Dianping is doing now, BAT cannot do. Second, Dianping is about to engage in the ultimate battle of group buying. If it secures the top spot, its valuation could increase multiple times over.

2013 is Dianping's "decisive year." Ignoring speculations and rumors, Zhang Tao is clear: "The internet is indeed entering an era of great integration, but Dianping will not be acquired."

Zhang Tao categorizes companies that might be acquired into three types. The first type possesses platform attributes but is better suited to develop within a giant's system, such as maps and email. These products can easily attract large numbers of users but struggle to find independent business models. Others, like browsers, while able to generate revenue, earn far less through navigation services than their strategic value to large companies. "Giants are willing to spend money nurturing email products because they help with branding and user stickiness, but there hasn’t been a successful standalone email company heard of," he says. The second type involves industry consolidation, like in the video industry, using acquisitions to expand market share. The third type includes companies facing financial difficulties selling themselves for survival.

Now, Meituan has already been brought into Alibaba's fold. In theory, Dianping should quickly seek a "backer" to avoid being defeated due to competitors acquiring capital and expanding rapidly. Zhang Tao, however, says, "The help from giants is limited. We indeed need users, but execution is more important. Acquisitions would reduce our execution capabilities and we might even get marginalized by a large company." Not only that, he must ensure Dianping maintains the most precise "distance" from the giants—if it gets too close to any one of them, it would be equivalent to "taking sides," making it an enemy to the others.

Thus, logically speaking, the acquisition of Dianping becomes a dead knot. Zhang Tao counters, "Why should we take their money? We're not short on cash; what we need are resources. If a giant is willing to make a small investment and give us many resources, I can consider it. But would they? Would a giant give you resources if they own only a small stake?"

Weapon One: Dirty and Hard Work

In mobile internet, opportunities for companies solely focused on pure internet products are becoming fewer. New opportunities arise from those combining points with traditional industries. Zhang Tao believes that the essence of life services mainly revolves around dining, drinking, and entertainment. Other niche markets, such as ride-hailing, can form decent business models, but it's hard to envision them growing into giants.

For dining, drinking, and entertainment, user demands are simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is the core of life services, which is precisely where Dianping started. Whether in content, member accumulation, or brand, newcomers find it difficult to surpass.

The most representative product form of discounts is group buying. Group buying has now entered the championship contention phase, and in 2013, Dianping will face off against Meituan. This is a winner-takes-all market; as long as two strong players coexist, the industry's gross margin will be suppressed, preventing stable profitability.

A foreseeable future lies in reservations. Reservations can create a true online-to-offline closed loop, but they must be built upon Dianping having absolute advantages in both information and discounts. Otherwise, creating a closed loop would be challenging. In 2012, Dianping began experimenting with reservations. Zhang Tao said, "Currently, feedback from users and merchants is positive, but if reservations were done separately, it would be hard to solve the chicken-or-egg problem."

Of course, life services offer some heavier opportunities. For example, food delivery satisfies users' rigid demands, helps merchants increase sales, and has seen companies like "To Home Gourmet" thrive in the past few years. However, the core of food delivery is delivery itself—it must be delivered within a very short time frame, and issues like soup spilling must be addressed. The logistics requirements are even higher than those for fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang Tao said, "If food delivery is executed well, it still holds opportunities, but it's harder dirty work, and Dianping is already doing enough dirty and hard work."

If judged by age, Dianping is already an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has painstakingly accumulated merchant profiles and user reviews, which Zhang Tao refers to as "dirty and hard work." This primitive growth model once restricted its speed, but past pains have become today's core barriers for Dianping. It can be said that currently, apart from Meituan, Dianping has no real rivals in the life services sector.

Still, he must remain vigilant against BAT's "clutches." Baidu's intervention is the most direct; Baidu Maps already has over 100 million users who can use the map to search for nearby dining and other life services. Tencent, considered to have secured its place in mobile internet, launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest goods trading platform, and expanding goods to services is a natural progression, especially since it has also included Meituan and AutoNavi in its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there are no signs of explosive growth in terms of reality and data," Zhang Tao said. Brand positioning is crucial; the key is which product users turn to first when they want to dine, drink, or entertain themselves.

Moreover, in the vast field of life services, competition among giants is bound to overlap, making it impossible for anyone to completely monopolize the domain. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained via Weibo and WeChat. Zhang Tao feels that in this way, Dianping is safer. "In niche areas like car rentals and business travel, if giants redirect their massive user base, it could easily overwhelm existing companies."

As for Alibaba, which has invested in AutoNavi, invading Dianping's territory isn't so simple. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods where even the strangest items can be categorized. A diner's experience depends on numerous factors such as the day's environment, waitstaff, and chef. Managing merchants cannot rely solely on systems and tools like Alibaba does; it must establish 'ground forces' managed by people.

"All three giants want to follow this path, and it offers imaginative space, but it's not a highway; it's a rugged mountain road." Zhang Tao believes that first, providing life services requires ground teams deployed at various levels, something BAT lacks the genetic makeup and capability to manage offline teams. Second, they lack the willingness; life services require dedicated focus, not just assigning a small team, and the profits come slowly. "A company finds it hard to excel in two major tasks simultaneously, let alone needing to consolidate its advantage in its original field."

Weapon Two: Small Ships Are Easier to Turn

In 2011, when Dianping received $100 million in funding, Zhang Tao told the world that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, the world changes fast, and before Dianping went public, his analogous objects had all fallen from grace.

He didn't seem overly worried about it. "They failed to become big companies because they lacked offline teams; now they all want to be the U.S.'s Dianping."

The current path is one Zhang Tao has gradually charted himself. When he felt that selling keyword ads and coupons was too outdated, the emergence of group buying caught his attention. Initially, like the Americans, he viewed group buying as a marketing platform, offering one deal per day. But as orders increased, he realized that group buying possessed transactional platform characteristics. Thus, he foresaw Dianping's future as a life service transaction platform. The two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were exactly used to transition Dianping from an information platform to a transaction platform.

Without this "genetic transformation," Zhang Tao might not have had the confidence to compete for the fourth giant's position today. But talking about genetic transformation sounds simple; in reality, it's equivalent to Dianping's second entrepreneurship, a process filled with extreme pain.

First, compared to selling ads alone, transaction platforms lower gross margins and demand higher cost control and efficiency. Second, transaction platforms require greater cross-departmental communication skills.

To maintain Dianping's impartiality and independence as an information platform, the content and sales teams were entirely separate. Zhang Tao even deliberately established a "firewall" between departments—important clients couldn't alter platform information for sales purposes, leading to some mutual hostility between the content and sales teams. Now, as a transaction platform, the editorial and sales teams need deep communication to craft good marketing plans, requiring editorial cooperation beyond mere planning. He said, "Maintaining information quality remains crucial, but there's no need to reinforce animosity; we need to dismantle the firewall."

Though editors may logically accept this, individual behaviors and values have inertia, and when combined across the entire company, that inertia grows larger. To address this, Zhang Tao specifically held several meetings to encourage more communication between departments. Months later, an editor responsible for sales casually remarked, "I used to think you were my enemy, but now I realize you're my colleague."

The sales department also needed genetic transformation. To promote conversion, Zhang Tao cleverly gave the offline team dual identities. First, for merchants, sales personnel must transform into marketing consultants, not just selling products but helping merchants devise marketing strategies. Second, for the company, marketing consultants act as corporate procurement agents. Dianping is like a supermarket—not only must the product quality be good, but the variety must also be rich. Marketing consultants should aim to help users procure group-buying products. Naturally, this dual identity creates a series of complex issues. Frontline personnel find this quite challenging, as they must weigh the balance themselves.

From a substantive perspective, Dianping's "dual identity" is quite similar to how Alibaba split into 25 divisions. By decentralizing decision-making power to the front lines, the company gains faster response speeds but increases management difficulty. Likewise, compared to frontline employees, Dianping's management team suffers more after the genetic transformation.

Every year, Zhang Tao decomposes Dianping's company goals into various departments, then executives further break them down layer by layer. However, decomposition is not a simple matter of addition and subtraction. Co-founder Li Jing, responsible for the sales team, sometimes finds that unresolved merchant demands stem from sales issues but affect sales. Therefore, Li Jing created a new position, setting up communication specialists who gather information daily from marketing consultants, submit it to other departments, and track feedback. He often tells his team, "Being good at embracing change doesn't mean liking change; no one likes change, and everyone feels uncomfortable when faced with it. All employees must undergo discomfort zones, including myself."

Numbers reflect the heaviness of Dianping's execution team. Currently, out of more than 3000 employees, over 2000 work offline. The ground force is responsible for direct communication with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group buying, and bundled marketing solutions.

For this core team capable of resisting BAT's clutches, Dianping has a unique management evaluation system.

They are not called "sales," but from the first day, their business cards bear the title "Marketing Planning Consultant." Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the stage of watching news and chatting on QQ, lagging behind Taobao merchants by at least three to five years in understanding the internet. Marketing planning consultants must inform merchants about online users' needs and how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing said, "Even a high-end restaurant wouldn't be advised by a marketing consultant to do abalone or shark fin group buying, perhaps choosing other dishes instead, because online users and offline users have different demands."

An outstanding marketing consultant must be an all-around player. They must explain to restaurant owners and hair salon owners the essential differences between discounts and group buying and which discount aligns better with their positioning. If dealing with a hot pot restaurant owner, the marketing consultant should know whether it's the off-season or peak season, which products have rising costs during certain seasons, and which have falling costs, proposing a group-buying menu that both merchants and users prefer.

This imposes high demands on marketing consultants, who must not only be experts in internet marketing but also in the specific industry. Li Jing said, "I must admit, it's impossible for every consultant to be a double expert, so we've set star ratings for consultants." Similar to hotels, marketing consultants range from one-star to five-star, with the best being superstars called Super Stars.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline staff are not elite technical men from prestigious universities, making it challenging for them to understand internet products. Dianping established a knowledge base for marketing consultants, containing knowledge points and relevant cases accessible to both new and experienced employees. If encountering a new issue, employees can ask questions in the knowledge base, where thousands of consultants nationwide will assist in answering.

Most of the knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees based on practical experience. Dianping set up a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, rewarding employees who help others the most each quarter. Each quarter, Dianping conducts skill assessments for employees, directly linking results to promotions.

Industry knowledge is harder to acquire than internet knowledge. Some experiences are only gained after years of trial and error in the traditional merchant industry. A Super Star marketing consultant told Li Jing that hair salons should avoid wash-cut-blow package deals, as men have no motivation to buy, and women mostly blow dry their hair without washing or cutting. Li Jing was taken aback, "We men would never think of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing consultants are not only divided by city but also by industries such as catering, hairdressing, and leisure. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is further subdivided into hot pot, Western cuisine, and buffets, with one marketing consultant responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing consultants can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, avoiding resistance when selling products," he said.

Each marketing group must conduct its own case-sharing sessions: what difficulties a particular type of merchant encounters, what marketing demands they have, what product combinations the company offers, and the outcomes. Outstanding cases are reported upwards, and each week Dianping officially shares one case.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition called "Startup Winner." Within the first three months of joining, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products but listening to merchants sharing their experiences. If they are responsible for hot pot restaurants, after three months, they must submit a business plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hot Pot Restaurant Owner." Like graduation, employees present their defense, and industry supervisors evaluate the winners, with the company providing certain rewards.

Strategically, whether it's Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, what they need to do is quite similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O, spoken by anyone with some understanding of the industry, sound alike. "The era where success depended on storytelling and concept creation has passed. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model lies in management and execution, not strategy," Zhang Tao said.
