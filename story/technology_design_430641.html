The Laughter and Tears of Thai Ladyboys

When the plane arrived in Bangkok, Thailand at midnight, Thailand implements visa on arrival. In the Suvarnabhumi Airport terminal, people of all races queued up for visas - black, white, and yellow. Bangkok truly deserves its reputation as an international metropolis. Among these crowds, not only were the skin tones varied, but also the clothing - representing all seasons, though summer attire was most common. We had prepared for the hot climate by removing our down jackets and changing into sweaters, but we still felt a bit bulky compared to the locals wearing tank tops, shorts, and flip-flops with sun-tanned skin. We clearly stood out like ducks among chickens.

After completing the visa process and exiting the terminal to board our tour bus, Thai ladies greeted us by placing long garlands of fresh flowers around our necks and offering the traditional wai greeting (SOWALICA), leaving a positive first impression. The Thai government's support and emphasis on tourism was evident from this gesture.

On the bus, a male and female tour guide joined us. They humorously referred to the men in our group as "Lao Mama" or "PP", "Big P", while the women were called "Crystal Crystal", "Watery Watery", and "Dry Dry" based on age. They exchanged Chinese yuan to Thai baht at a rate of 1:3.9, though we later discovered that yuan was widely accepted at a better rate of 1:4.3 by both large stores and street vendors. The male guide called the young women "Crystal Crystal", so we nicknamed him "Wang Pipi".

Even though we hadn't left Asia yet, Southeast Asia is part of the Sinic cultural sphere, but everything here was different from home - plants, birds, street vendors. Truly, we had arrived in a foreign land where language and script had changed. If it weren't for the many other Chinese tour groups around, one might feel quite disoriented. The dense coconut groves, oddly shaped and pungent durians, dark-skinned faces, worm-like scripts resembling Arabic, and crowds of Western tourists all indicated this was a different place. Even the Buddhist statues seemed thinner, with tall pointed crowns, some noses curling like elephants'. Except for the saffron robes being golden yellow, most other details differed, proving the philosopher's saying: gods are created by humans, reflecting human nature.

Thailand is a country steeped in Buddhist culture. On the streets, one frequently sees altars about 1.5 meters high and 1 meter wide used for offerings, not just in public places like hotels and shops, but even more so in front of schools and government buildings. According to the guide, this is both custom and law. As common as these altars are, so too are portraits of the current King Rama IX. Royal authority and religious power hold special status here, especially Buddhism, which influences all levels of society from the ruling class to commoners. Rather than the Eastern handshake or bow, Thais greet each other with the wai, similar to monks. Just as during China's Jin Dynasty when everyone greeted each other with "Amitabha", all Thai men must serve in the military once, but also enter the monkhood once, for as short as 7-10 days or as long as life. Afterward, they can enter or leave the monkhood freely, drink alcohol, eat meat, and visit bars unless reported or caught by police. If caught, they must leave the monkhood permanently and cannot re-enter. Despite this, there are still 300,000 monks in Thailand.

Thailand is a country with 60 million people and over 700 years of history, ruled by Khmer for over 200 years without its own writing system. There have been nine kings in total, with the fifth king being the most influential. Having studied in Europe, he absorbed advanced democratic ideas and, like Sun Yat-sen in China, aimed to benefit the people and reform his country. He abolished slavery and did much for the common people, earning their deep respect. Commoners burn three incense sticks when worshipping Buddha, but five when worshipping the fifth king. The current living king is Rama IX, who has reigned for 60 years with three daughters and one son. However, the crown prince has not been appointed due to his bad habits of drinking, gambling, womanizing, etc. It's rumored he forcibly assaulted a famous female singer, making him unpopular among the Thai people. Rama IX dislikes him as well. Among his three daughters, the eldest and youngest are married, while the second daughter is capable and diligent, beloved by the people. Rama IX intended to pass the throne to her, but the crown prince found out and sent hitmen to assassinate her, who was saved by bodyguards. Now Rama IX plans to pass the throne to his grandson when he turns three years and eight months old.

When we visited, Thaksin had just stepped down and domestic tourists had just been allowed to visit Thailand again. However, due to political instability, the guide repeatedly warned against going out alone at night due to potential robberies. According to the guide, on the second day of the coup, there was a soldier every five steps and a tank every ten steps, with very strict security. Thaksin was a talented politician who made many contributions early on, but later became corrupt, selling the national telecommunications rights to Singapore and embezzling billions. Rama IX had wanted to replace him but couldn't act easily, so took the opportunity when Thaksin was abroad to launch the coup.

Thailand is rich in natural resources, with seven provinces, four of which produce red, white, and blue gemstones, especially rubies. Therefore, the walls of the Grand Palace are inlaid with rubies. Initially skeptical of the guide's claims, upon seeing the magnificent and unparalleled palace with my own eyes, touching the walls covered in gold powder, glass, and gemstones, and seeing the large jade Buddha, I truly believed it all. I believe no matter how splendid any building in the world is, none can match the grandeur and rarity of Thailand's Grand Palace. No wonder there are so many European tourists of all races. Rarity attracts people, just as flowers attract bees, foul smells attract flies, and honey attracts children. People are drawn by the rare, aren't they?

What attracts numerous tourists isn't just the Grand Palace, but also the ladyboys. In terms of global recognition, Thailand's ladyboys may be even more famous than the Grand Palace, stemming from human curiosity. However, Thailand, the world's only producer of ladyboys, is indeed a peculiar country. If ladyboys appeared in a decadent, open-minded country, it wouldn't be surprising, like homosexuality in America. But they exist in a supposedly ascetic Buddhist nation, combining abstinence with indulgence, conservatism with eccentricity - such a unique combination exists only in Thailand. Such contrasting elements coexisting in one place is a great spectacle, illustrating that extremes lead to their opposites. When those stunningly beautiful ladyboys, considered the epitome of femininity, offer you the Buddhist wai, you don't know if they're demons turned Buddhas or saints turned licentious. This can be summarized by a local saying: "Visit temples by day, watch spirits by night." And there's a folk joke: "In Thailand, implement the 'three clean' policy: strip naked, spend all your bullets, go home and get slapped by your wife." This shows Thailand's openness; in a supposedly ascetic Buddhist country, people's minds are actually more open and casual.

Thailand's nightlife is extraordinarily rich and diverse, perhaps unmatched in the world. Some places, though prohibited by the Thai government, were still visited under the guidance of our tour. Initially reluctant, standing on the street for two hours would have been even more boring, so I went along. Upon entering, it was like stepping into a den of demons, a bizarre world of monsters and ghosts. Though the Moulin Rouge falls into the same category, I haven't seen it. Compared to Thailand's ladyboys, perhaps the lack of regulation makes it more raw and open here. Once Adam and Eve's fig leaves are torn off, everything is exposed - Adam's lower body can beat drums or blow up balloons, Eve's body can keep goldfish or shoot darts. Viewing it as a performance, it seems like a special skill show; judging from a moral perspective, it feels immoral, boring, and uncomfortable. Nevertheless, things done in the light are healthy and lasting, while those in the dark are sinister and hidden. Experiencing the darkness makes one appreciate the value of light even more.

We use the Chinese term "ladyboy" to refer to men transformed into women, and the character "妖" (yao) is indeed most appropriate. During the day, seeing these charming, angelic ladyboys, we like them. But at night, seeing those on stage with female upper bodies and male lower bodies, one becomes disoriented, unsure of time and place. When unable to determine if someone is male or female, what else could they be called but "yao"? This character is truly brilliant.

There are two types of ladyboys in Thailand: those who, except for lacking a uterus and thus unable to bear children, have all physiological features altered to female, with fully transformed psychology; and those who are only partially transformed, half-male and half-female. Regardless of type, they are man-made, not natural. They usually start receiving estrogen injections from ages eleven or twelve, and as adults undergo surgeries like breast augmentation and genital modifications. Their lifespans are short, health issues arising after forty requiring medication to sustain life. Their belief is: life may be short, but should be lived without regret. Although their income is considerable locally, they are abnormal variants, not natural or normal people, suffering physical harm and mental distress from being looked down upon and treated as curiosities. Yet, there are not few but rather 100,000 such boys in Thailand, a significant population. Why? Perhaps monks become monks for faith, but these individuals do so out of poverty, for money. What does this group of 100,000 bring to Thailand? We'll discuss this later. Due to their prevalence, tall and beautiful women in Thailand are often mistakenly identified as ladyboys, leading to many jokes.

Out of a total population of 60 million, 100,000 in Thailand are ladyboys and 300,000 are monks. This disrupts gender balance, so polygamy is practiced in Thailand. Our two guides, one male and one female, exemplify this. The female guide, in her forties, has two husbands, being the senior wife who works outside and reportedly massages her husband's back at home. The male guide, only twenty-five, also has two wives, one working outside and one managing the household and children, a common arrangement. Despite this, due to openness, many men still seek relationships outside, requiring both wives to jointly monitor their husband. Being born a woman in China is good, with monogamy and higher social status. The female guide envied us this way. Unbeknownst to her, in the Sichuan-Tibet border region of China, with a male-to-female ratio of 5:1, polyandry is common. Thus, imbalance either way is problematic.

Both guides come from the Golden Triangle area near Yunnan in northern Thailand, third-generation descendants of remnants of the Nationalist army. Only these Golden Triangle descendants can speak both Chinese and Thai. Abandoned by the Nationalists, like motherless children, they couldn't return home and had no place to stand abroad, surviving by leasing land in the unregulated Golden Triangle to grow drugs. Children couldn't attend school, adults lacked food and clothing, even shelter, enduring primitive conditions. Visiting the Golden Triangle, seeing stone-age tools and emaciated children beside shacks brought tears to our eyes. A couplet at the exhibition read: "No home everywhere, hard times year after year." To gain recognition and Thai citizenship, they abandoned drug cultivation, sending teenagers to fight for the Thai army, earning citizenship and land through bloodshed. After decades of development, today's Golden Triangle is a bustling town, mostly inhabited by Chinese.

Thailand is rich in resources, boasting treasures in the mountains (gems), snakes on the ground (venomous snakes), and fish in the rivers (crocodiles). This is evident in the gem-studded walls of the Grand Palace. Thailand's superman, having acquired abilities to fly, swim, scale walls, see in the dark, and understand animal languages, confidently prepared to meet Rose. Beforehand, to lighten his suspiciously brown skin, he soaked himself in vinegar overnight, whitening slightly, then perfumed himself, donning a cloak made of white clouds, looking like a wandering knight. Crocodiles, seen stacked densely in the Rachachew Zoo's river, outnumbered pigs in pigpens, prompting our purchase of many crocodile wallets and belts. Snakes, unseen and feared in the wild, were seen abundantly in the snake house. According to the guide, regular venomous snake bites kill within days, Australian tiger snakes within three minutes, and Thai bamboo vipers within two minutes. Thus, Thai snake venom is several times more valuable than gold, treating diseases via a detoxifying principle, effective for stopping bleeding, detoxification, and beauty. Snake bile improves liver function and vision. If bitten, if the wound has flat teeth marks, it's harmless; if two fang marks, it's venomous. Immediate action includes cutting a cross, burning with a lighter, or tying a cloth band not too tight nor loose, loosened every twenty minutes, then rushing to the hospital. Venomous snakes have strong vitality, surviving without food for two to three years, mating for 36 hours and producing eggs for two years afterward, leading many to believe snake penis has aphrodisiac properties.

During our days in Thailand, traffic jams were frequent, necessitating early departures everywhere. The main reasons for congestion include Bangkok being both the capital and a tourist city with many outsiders, compounded by their lifestyle habits. Thai people have a slow pace, starting work at 10:30 AM and finishing at 3 PM with a half-hour snack break, making them poor but with excellent welfare. Education and healthcare are free, medical care and schooling cost nothing, and elderly can live in nursing homes with a monthly allowance of one thousand baht. In seven days in Thailand, we saw neither beggars nor homeless people, creating a favorable impression. I believe that if people are eggs, society should ensure a protective cushion regardless of egg size or equality. Without proper social security, many small eggs would be crushed in the bottom layers of society, creating a grim sight and no benefit to society. Ensuring eggs aren't crushed is more important than ensuring equality. A harmonious society means the rich are joyfully rich, the poor contentedly poor, the rich don't bully the poor, and the poor don't resent the rich. Society shouldn't fear wealth disparity but the existence of starvation. Providing survival guarantees to the disadvantaged prevents many desperate acts.

Despite Thailand's limited resources, it provides such security. Where does all the welfare funding come from? All originates from tourism, whose prosperity brings temple offerings, allowing temples to fund many public services and build many schools. The thriving tourism owes much to the ladyboys, who sacrifice themselves for the benefit of the country, especially the elderly and majority of the young. Thus, Thailand's ladyboys thrive, with their existence having positive significance.
