Dianping: The Bone Giants Can't Chew, Living in the Zone of Discomfort

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 16:07:33

Dianping CEO Zhang Tao

Written by Yuan Yin, edited by Yang Jing, photographed by Xiao Nan

Dianping CEO Zhang Tao has postponed the company's IPO once again.

In 2011, Dianping secured $100 million in Series C funding from Sequoia Capital and others, valuing the company at $1 billion. According to convention, it should have gone public within one to two years afterward, but it did not. Instead, in 2012, it raised another $60 million in Series D funding.

Given Dianping's scale and development trajectory, going public would not be difficult. However, Zhang Tao feels that an IPO now would be too costly. He deconstructs the big internet (PC + mobile) into four elements: people, information, goods, and services. Each connection point offers the opportunity for a giant to emerge. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, and Alibaba connects people with goods. The space between people and services is the seat for the fourth giant. Since 2003, Dianping has been diligently accumulating data, finally waiting for the explosion of mobile internet, where phones merge "location" and "service." Following Zhang Tao’s logic, no company seems more fitting than Dianping for the image of the fourth giant.

However, outsiders don’t see it this way. In recent months, with frequent acquisition moves by BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent), Dianping has become a hypothetical acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition candidate; whichever surname from BAT it adopts could help them achieve great success sooner. On the other hand, Dianping’s size pales in comparison to BAT, and it is premature to conclude that it can become a giant. Moreover, whether it's Tencent WeChat’s public platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after acquiring Gaode and Meituan, they all directly target Dianping's territory—local life services focused on eating, drinking, and entertainment.

Before proving the following two things, a Dianping IPO would equate to a "discounted sale." First, Zhang Tao needs time to demonstrate that what Dianping does now, BAT cannot replicate. Second, Dianping is about to engage in the ultimate battle of group buying. If it secures the top spot, its valuation could increase multiple times over.

2013 is Dianping's "decisive year." Regarding speculation and rumors, Zhang Tao pays them no heed. He is clear: "The internet has indeed entered an era of great integration, but Dianping will not be acquired."

Zhang Tao categorizes companies that might be acquired into three types. The first type possesses platform attributes but is better suited to develop within the ecosystem of a giant, such as maps and email. These products can easily attract large numbers of users but struggle to find independent business models. Others, like browsers, may generate revenue, but the money earned through navigation services is far less than their strategic value to large companies. "Giants are willing to spend money maintaining an email product because it helps with branding and user retention, but there hasn't been any successful standalone email company." The second type involves industry consolidation, such as in the video industry, where acquisitions expand market share. The third type occurs when a company faces financial difficulties and sells itself to survive.

Now, Meituan has been brought under Alibaba's wing. In theory, Dianping should quickly seek a "backer" to avoid being defeated by its rival gaining capital and rapidly expanding. However, Zhang Tao says, "The giants offer us limited help. We do need users, but execution is even more important. Mergers and acquisitions would reduce our execution capabilities and could marginalize us within a larger company." Furthermore, he aims to keep Dianping at the most precise "distance" from the giants. Getting too close to any one of them would be akin to "taking sides," making Dianping an enemy to the others.

Thus, logically, the acquisition of Dianping becomes a dead end. Zhang Tao counters, "Why should we take their money? We don’t lack funds; what we need are resources. If a giant were willing to make a small investment and give us many resources, I would consider it. But would they? Would a giant give you resources if they only hold a small stake?"

Weapon One: Dirty Work

In mobile internet, opportunities for pure internet product companies are shrinking. New opportunities arise from those that combine with traditional industries. Zhang Tao believes that lifestyle services mainly revolve around eating, drinking, and entertainment. Other细分 markets, like ride-hailing, can form decent business models, but it's hard to imagine them growing into giants.

For eating, drinking, and entertainment, user demands are simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is the core of lifestyle services, which is precisely where Dianping started. Whether in content, member accumulation, or brand, later entrants find it difficult to surpass Dianping.

The most representative product form of discounts is group buying. Group buying has now entered the championship race, and Dianping plans to battle Meituan in 2013. This is a winner-takes-all market. As long as two strong players coexist, the industry's gross profit margin will be lowered, making stable profitability impossible.

Another foreseeable future lies in reservations. Reservations can create a true online-to-offline loop, but they must be built on the foundation of Dianping having absolute advantages in both information and discounts. Otherwise, creating a closed loop would be difficult. In 2012, Dianping began experimenting with reservations. Zhang Tao said, "Currently, feedback from users and merchants is positive, but if we focus solely on reservations, it would be hard to solve the chicken-or-egg problem."

Of course, lifestyle services also present some heavier opportunities. For instance, food delivery is a rigid demand for users and can help merchants increase sales. In the past couple of years, food delivery companies like Daojia Meishihui have thrived. However, the core of food delivery is delivery logistics, meaning items must be delivered within a short period, along with resolving issues like soup spillage. The logistics requirements exceed even those of fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang Tao said, "If executed well, food delivery holds potential, but it's harder dirty work. What Dianping is doing now is already dirty and exhausting enough."

If judged by age, Dianping is already an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has painstakingly accumulated merchant information and user reviews, which Zhang Tao calls "dirty work." This primitive growth model once limited its speed, but the past pain has become Dianping's core barrier today. It can be said that currently, apart from Meituan, Dianping has no real competitors in the lifestyle services sector.

Nevertheless, he still needs to beware of BAT's "clutches." Baidu's involvement is the most direct. Baidu Maps already has over 100 million users, who can use the map to search for nearby dining and other lifestyle services. Tencent, believed to have secured its ticket to mobile internet, launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest physical goods trading platform, and expanding its offerings to services is natural. Besides, it has included Meituan and Gaode in its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there are no signs of explosive growth in reality and data," Zhang Tao said. Brand positioning is crucial; the key is which product users turn to first when they want to eat, drink, or entertain themselves.

Moreover, in the vast field of lifestyle services, competition among giants will inevitably overlap, and no one can completely monopolize the sector. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained on Weibo and WeChat. Zhang Tao believes that in this case, Dianping is safer instead. "In niche areas like car rental and business travel, the massive user base directed by giants could easily overwhelm existing companies."

As for Alibaba, which has invested in Gaode, invading Dianping's territory isn't so simple either. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, which, no matter how peculiar, can still be categorized. A user's experience of a meal depends on factors like the environment on that day, the waitstaff, and the chef. Managing merchants cannot rely on Alibaba-style systems and tools; a 'ground force' must be established, managed by humans.

"All three giants want to take this path, and there is room for imagination, but this is not a highway; it is a rugged mountain road," Zhang Tao believes. First, providing lifestyle services requires ground teams deployed at various levels, something BAT lacks the genes and capability to manage offline. Second, they lack the willingness. Lifestyle services require dedicated effort, not something a small team can accomplish, and profits come slowly. "A company finds it hard to excel at two important things simultaneously, let alone needing to consolidate its advantages in its original field."

Weapon Two: Small Ships Turn Easily

When Dianping secured $100 million in financing in 2011, Zhang Tao told the world that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, times change quickly, and before Dianping went public, his analogies had fallen from grace.

He didn't seem anxious about it. "They failed to become big companies because they lacked offline teams. Now, they all want to be America's Dianping."

The current path was also gradually explored by Zhang Tao himself. When he felt that selling keyword ads and discount coupons was outdated, the emergence of group buying brightened his eyes. Initially, like the Americans, he viewed group buying as a marketing platform, one deal per day. But as orders increased, he realized that group buying possessed the attributes of a trading platform. Thus, he envisioned Dianping's future as a lifestyle services trading platform. The two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were exactly used to transition Dianping from an information platform to a trading platform.

Without this "genetic modification," Zhang Tao might not have had the confidence to compete for the fourth seat among giants today. However, genetic modification sounds simple but is equivalent to Dianping's second entrepreneurship, an extremely painful process.

First, compared to selling advertisements alone, the gross margin of a trading platform decreases, requiring higher cost control and efficiency. Second, a trading platform demands stronger cross-departmental communication skills.

Previously, to maintain Dianping's impartiality and independence as an information platform, the content and sales teams were entirely separate. Zhang Tao even deliberately established a "firewall" between departments — even for important clients, sales couldn't modify platform information for sales purposes, leading to some mutual hostility between the content and sales teams. Now, as a trading platform, editors and sales teams need deep communication to craft good marketing plans. Planning aside, editorial cooperation is essential. He said, "Maintaining information quality remains important, but there's no need to reinforce enmity anymore; we need to dismantle the firewall."

Though editors can logically accept this, individual behaviors and values have inertia, and when aggregated across the entire company, that inertia becomes even greater. Zhang Tao specifically held several meetings to encourage more communication between departments. Months later, an editorial manager casually chatted with a sales manager saying, "I used to think you were my enemy, but now I realize you're my colleague."

The sales department also needed genetic modification. To promote conversion, Zhang Tao cleverly gave the offline team dual identities. First, for merchants, salespeople should become marketing consultants, not just selling products but helping merchants devise marketing strategies. Second, for the company, marketing consultants act as the company's procurement officers. Dianping is like a supermarket; not only must the product quality be good, but the variety must be rich. Marketing consultants should aim to help users procure group-buying products. Of course, dual identities will undoubtedly bring a series of complex problems. Frontline personnel find it quite challenging, as they must balance the scales themselves.

Substantially, Dianping's "dual identity" is very similar to Alibaba splitting into 25 business units. Decentralizing decision-making to the front lines allows for faster response times but increases management difficulty. Similarly, compared to frontline employees, Dianping's management team suffers more during the transformation.

Every year, Zhang Tao breaks down Dianping's company goals into various departments, then each senior executive further decomposes them layer by layer, but this decomposition is not a simple arithmetic operation. Li Jing, co-founder of Dianping responsible for the sales team, sometimes finds that unmet merchant demands stem from sales issues, affecting performance. Therefore, Li Jing created a new position, setting up communication specialists who collect information daily from marketing consultants, submit it to other departments, and track feedback. He often tells his team, "Being adept at embracing change doesn't mean liking change. No one likes change; everyone feels uncomfortable when facing it. All employees must go through the discomfort zone, including myself."

Numbers can reflect the "heaviness" of Dianping's execution team. Out of more than 3,000 employees, over 2,000 work offline. The ground force is responsible for direct communication with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group buying, and combined marketing solutions.

For this core team that resists BAT's influence, Dianping has developed a unique management evaluation system.

They are not called "salespeople"; from the first day, their business cards read "Marketing Planning Consultant." Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the stage of watching news and chatting on QQ, lagging behind Taobao merchants by at least three to five years in their understanding of the internet. Marketing planning consultants must inform merchants of online users' demands and how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing said, "Even for a high-end restaurant, marketing consultants wouldn't recommend group buying of abalone or shark fin, possibly opting for other dishes, because online and offline users have different needs."

An outstanding marketing consultant must be a versatile player. They must explain to restaurant owners and barbershop owners the fundamental differences between discounts and group buying and which discount aligns better with their positioning. If dealing with a hot pot restaurant owner, the marketing consultant must know whether it's the off-season or peak season, which product costs rise during this season, and which costs decrease, proposing a group-buying menu that both merchants and users prefer.

This imposes high requirements on marketing consultants, who must not only be experts in internet marketing but also industry experts. Li Jing said, "I must admit, it's impossible for every consultant to be a double expert, so we've set star ratings for consultants." Like hotels, marketing consultants range from one-star to five-star, with the best being called Super Star.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline employees aren't tech-savvy graduates from prestigious universities, making it challenging for them to understand internet products. Dianping has established a knowledge base for marketing consultants, containing knowledge points and related cases, accessible to both new and old employees. If encountering new problems, employees can ask questions within the knowledge base, and thousands of consultants nationwide will assist in answering them.

A large number of knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees based on practical experience. Dianping has set up a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, with employees who help others the most each quarter receiving rewards. Every quarter, Dianping conducts skill assessments for employees, with results directly linked to promotions.

Industry knowledge is harder to obtain than internet knowledge. Some experiences are summarized by traditional merchants after years of industry practice. A Super Star marketing consultant told Li Jing that hair salons should avoid offering wash-cut-blow package deals because men lack motivation to buy them, and women mostly blow dry their hair without washing or cutting. Li Jing was astonished, "We men would never think of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing consultants are divided not only by city but also by industries such as catering, hairdressing, and leisure. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is further subdivided into hot pot, Western cuisine, and buffets, with a marketing consultant responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing consultants can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, reducing resistance when selling products," he said.

Each marketing team holds its own case-sharing session: what difficulties a certain type of merchant encounters, what marketing needs they have, what product combinations the company uses, and what effects they achieve. Outstanding cases are reported upward, and Dianping officially shares one case each week.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition called "Entrepreneurial Winner." In the first three months of employment, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products but listening to merchants sharing experiences. If responsible for a hot pot restaurant, after three months, they must submit a business plan PPT titled "If I Were the Hot Pot Restaurant Owner." Like graduation, employees defend their projects on-site, with industry supervisors evaluating winners, and the company providing certain rewards.

From a strategic perspective, regardless of Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, their tasks are quite similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O are familiar to anyone with basic industry knowledge, and the words spoken sound alike. "The era where storytelling and concept creation alone could lead to success is over. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model is management and execution, not strategy," Zhang Tao stated.
