Twitter's Dilemma: Advertising is a Double-Edged Sword, Loved and Hated

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 19:48:23

The social networking site Twitter recently adjusted the way images and videos are displayed, making browsing tweets on a computer or mobile device both surprising and infuriating. This adjustment by Twitter highlights its dilemma between building a service that everyone loves and pleasing Wall Street.

Just last week, days before Twitter announced its IPO price range, the social network tweaked its text-based format to emphasize visual media content in microblogs.

By default settings, Twitter pictures and Vine video previews will now appear below users' tweets. That is, all tweets containing images and videos can be previewed by default and will be reflected in the user's timeline.

Previously, such multimedia content would not be displayed in the main information feed. This adjustment by Twitter increases the similarity between it and another popular social network, Facebook.

S&P Capital IQ analyst Scott Kessler said, "In fact, Twitter is now following Facebook's old path. They believe that timelines are more beneficial for media content, enhancing interactivity and sensory experience."

Ironically, just a few months ago, Facebook emulated Twitter by adding hashtag functionality to better connect its social network with breaking news and current events.

Twitter's Facebook-like elements are significant. Twitter still has a long way to go before it becomes profitable. In the first nine months of this year, Twitter generated $422 million in revenue but incurred a net loss of $132 million. Clearly, if Twitter wants to become profitable, it must first address some issues, one of which is getting Internet users and advertisers to embrace a site and its unique conventions that can sometimes seem confusing.

Familiar photo and video streams can indeed make people adapt to Twitter's model and bring more advertising influence. However, Facebook-style visual additions may alienate Twitter users who appreciate its uniqueness.

Twitter's new image preview structure also reflects another characteristic of Facebook: it is now impossible to avoid image-based ads. Worse still, Twitter ads now resemble banner ads, popping up directly in the information flow on desktops and mobile devices instead of appearing on the right side of the webpage.

However, brand appeal lies in the ability of a company to pay to promote a tweet to a larger audience and increase its impact with an embedded image. For example, Victoria's Secret recently launched a promoted tweet with a picture featuring a scantily clad model. Previously, users could not see the picture unless they clicked to view it, but now these pictures are blatantly displayed on the timeline.

Altimeter Group media analyst Rebecca Lieb said, "This perfectly illustrates how an ad can be both loved and hated. It's not just about intrusive large-format mode, it's also about the subject matter, which might disturb users who dislike ads appearing on their work computers, putting them in embarrassing situations."

To increase revenue, ads won't be the last unwelcome guest on Twitter's timeline. Twitter may argue that the negative effects of visual ads can be offset by the other benefits of embedding images into the information flow. For instance, countless Halloween pictures have made the web a place for many users to enjoy visual feasts. Moreover, if users don't want to see other content, they can choose to turn off this feature.

A service that users love and meeting Wall Street's expectations isn't just a headache for Twitter; Facebook is even more accustomed to it. Facebook, being more than five times the size of Twitter, seems to have found a temporary balance point.

Twitter assesses the value of its service by calculating advertising revenue per thousand microblog timeline views. Now, not only is Twitter losing money, but its active user growth is also slowing down. What's even more worrying for the company is that the total number of timeline views per user seems to be decreasing.

Kessler wrote in his pre-IPO report on Twitter, "In 2013, Twitter's timeline view growth dropped significantly quarter-over-quarter, from 92% in Q1, to 69% in Q2, and then to 50% in Q3. Additionally, we find it noteworthy that the per-user timeline view growth slowed quarter-over-quarter in Q2 and Q3 of 2012, and actually decreased in Q3 of 2013."

To boost timeline view numbers, Twitter needs to make the information feed more attractive to draw in more users. Kessler said that Twitter needs broader general appeal, which is more meaningful for the company, even if it means continuing to emulate Facebook and bearing the risks associated with annoying ads.

Simply put, Twitter's users and advertisers hold the final say. If Twitter wants to do as it pleases, then let those with the final say decide how images and videos should be displayed.
