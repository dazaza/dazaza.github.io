Smartphone screens: Do we really need them any bigger?

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 14:39:58

English Original: Smartphones: When is 'big' too big? (Smartphones Unlocked)

We must admit that nowadays, "large" phones are more popular than "small" ones. In recent years, the size of smartphone screens has been growing just as fast as the clock speed of processors. A 5-inch screen has become mainstream, while super-large screen phones like Samsung Galaxy Mega (6.3 inches), Huawei Ascend Mate (6.1 inches), and Sony Xperia Z Ultra (6.44 inches) further push the limits of what people can handle. Meanwhile, HTC, Nokia, and other manufacturers seem ready to follow suit. So, do we really need bigger screens?

Back to the early days of touch-screen smartphones, you would never have guessed who was the first pioneer of large-screen phones—it was Apple. The 3.5-inch screen of the first-generation iPhone was indeed the largest at the time, offering users their first experience of a spacious virtual keyboard and stunning multimedia effects. During the Android 1.x era, most devices still had 3.2-inch screens until the appearance of the Dell Streak in 2010. As the first 5-inch smartphone, the Dell Streak was undoubtedly an outlier at the time, and its functional deficiencies quickly caused it to be forgotten.

Afterward, things likely unfolded as everyone already knows. The success of the Samsung Galaxy Note sparked a trend for larger screens. Nowadays, 4.5 inches has basically become the entry-level screen size for Android phones, while models with screens of 5.5 inches or more continue to test the limits of human hand sizes and blur the line between smartphones and tablets.

Advantages of Large-Screen Phones

If you like large-screen phones, there are obviously many reasons to buy one. Below are their main selling points:

· More impressive display performance: Since smartphone usage has shifted from voice calls to multimedia and internet applications, the superior viewing experience provided by a larger screen is self-evident.

· Pixel density: 6-inch smartphones with resolutions of 720P or even 1080P clearly have better pixel density than tablets with the same resolution but larger screen sizes.

· Hardware: Large-screen phones often come with faster processors and larger battery capacities, while their designs emphasize thinness, providing a certain level of portability.

The stylus is also an additional accessory for large-screen devices

· Cost factors: Large-screen phones that can serve two purposes cost less than buying both a tablet and a smartphone.

· Increased operability with a stylus: Large-screen phones like the Samsung Galaxy Note come with advanced electromagnetic pens that enable precise input operations, offering a note-taking-like user experience.

· Relevant high-tech accessories: Accessories like the HTC mini reduce the inconvenience of using large-screen phones to some extent. Similarly, phone manufacturers pay great attention to software optimization, bringing features such as single-handed keyboards and split-screen operations.

Who is buying large-screen phones?

Interestingly, the demographic purchasing large-screen phones is quite broad and not limited to a single area. Generally speaking, the visual impact brought by super-large screens is something all users around the world enjoy. Models with styluses are more popular in Asian markets, and even the idea of "making faces look smaller" has become a selling point for promoting large-screen phones among female users. At the same time, ultra-large-screen, high-specification models remain symbols of status in some markets.

Just an Android trend? Not for long

Perhaps due to the high degree of openness of the Android platform, most phablets are currently concentrated on Android. Apple still sticks to two tablet sizes (7 inches and 10 inches), and the screen size growth of the iPhone remains very slow.

In the Windows Phone field, only a few models like the Nokia Lumia 625 and HTC Titan II currently feature 4.7-inch screens, but it is expected that with system upgrades, larger-screen models will soon arrive, such as the rumored Nokia Lumia 1520 with a 6-inch screen.

Super-large-screen phones are mainly concentrated in the Android space

Future Trends: Still popular but won't become mainstream

Although the screen size of smartphones is increasing, it's clear that super-large-screen phones won't become mainstream. The main reason lies in the fact that super-large screens limit the portability of phones, especially before flexible screen technology matures and becomes widespread. It will be difficult for super-large-screen phones to make significant breakthroughs in terms of body length and width.

The 6.44-inch Sony Xperia Z Ultra is already the extreme size for large-screen phones

Secondly, small-sized tablets are another factor to consider. Usually, you wouldn't need both a 6-inch smartphone and a 7-inch tablet at the same time, and the latter clearly offers a lower price. Therefore, the increase in smartphone screen sizes will slow down, and models with 4.5 to 5-inch screens will be people's preferred choice. Of course, as a specific product type, "phablets" won't disappear quickly.
