Oracle President Mark Hurd: Why CEOs Must Be Customer Experience Evangelists?

by anonymous on 2013-10-15 21:01:16

The following is a blog post written by Oracle President Mark Hurd for LinkedIn:

Recently, conventional wisdom has been increasingly challenged by modern technology. And the generation that grew up with social media and smartphones has turned many traditional business strategies into irrelevant nonsense.

For example, according to classic business school thinking, if customer satisfaction has already reached 98% or even 95%, then striving for more is purely wasteful. Because some customers are just difficult, and no matter what, their hostility cannot be eliminated; this situation is beyond remedy. Therefore, business schools teach us to let it go.

This used to work because sellers controlled everything from what customers could buy, how products were delivered, to whether various options were provided. However, today, this model's viability is about the same as that of video rental stores.

In today's globalized market, buyers have gained autonomy through new technologies and now fully control the buyer-seller relationship. Consumers control the buying leverage, not the seller. With this new mechanism in place, the traditional business philosophies that CEOs have relied on for decades need a major overhaul.

Dissatisfied customers - even if they only make up 2% of the customer base - now have a voice and can cause significant damage to manufacturers they believe have failed to meet standards in product selection, availability, delivery channels, or after-sales service and support.

Social media and mobile computing provide consumers with powerful platforms through which they can influence the purchasing decisions of tens, hundreds, or even thousands of potential buyers.

Now there is some good news: CEOs and most other executives have fully realized that this is a situation where challenges and opportunities coexist. In a market survey, 97% of participating executives said that providing a good customer experience is an essential element for their success.

However, there is also bad news: only 63% of companies have implemented appropriate customer experience systems and processes. Therefore, about one-third of companies are merely paying lip service when it comes to providing excellent customer experiences. How can such companies compete or survive?

Clearly, social media has become an indispensable platform for fostering close connections between customers and businesses and nurturing lasting mutually beneficial relationships. However, once again, we find a striking discrepancy between what companies say they should do regarding social media and what they actually do: 81% of participating executives said they recognize that actively building social media processes and culture is an essential element for success, yet only 65% of companies provide social media channels for sales and services!

So what causes this disconnect? Participating executives attribute it to three aspects: inflexible technology unable to handle new social tools; siloed corporate structures unable to adapt to rapidly changing external conditions; and insufficient funding.

In my view, it is evident that these three problems - core technology, corporate structure, and budget - cannot be solved by an active social team, an energetic sales leader, or even a strong CFO. These are obstacles to becoming a truly social enterprise and providing excellent customer experiences, and only the CEO can remove them.

This is why, when I meet with clients across the United States and around the world, I tell CEOs that they must become evangelists for the customer experience. Simply supporting plans advocated by others or asking the CFO to study whether funds can be reallocated to initiate customer experience projects is far from enough for a CEO.

CEOs must make the customer experience the top priority for the entire company and make it the core goal of transformation efforts aimed at removing the aforementioned three obstacles. These obstacles may seem inconvenient today, but tomorrow they will become dangerous and catastrophic threats.

Let's first consider what I believe is the most serious of these issues: rigid and outdated technology. In many companies I've visited, CEOs have told me that their companies lack flexibility and cannot provide the right information at the right time to those who need it. The CEOs told me that customer satisfaction is declining, and they are spending almost all of their IT budgets on old systems in the basement that don't provide real value and don't help address these new, urgent challenges.

After hearing these situations, I would ask: "How long ago were your core enterprise applications deployed?" The answer is often 15 or even 20 years. This means that the software underlying the critical business processes managed by these foundational enterprise applications should now be considered prehistoric because they were written before the widespread popularity of the Web; before consumer-facing search engines; before smartphones; and certainly before social media, social support, and social business.

The result is a significant lag in technology development, specifically manifested as asynchronous information flow and missed opportunities, inability to fully understand the situation and lack of internal coordination within the company, as well as low morale and under-equipped employees. Actually, these employees may be fully committed to succeeding in the market, but they simply lack the necessary tools to win.

The applications deployed 15 or 20 years ago were never intended for use in today's entirely different environment, which is akin to riding a tricycle in a Formula One race.

Therefore, the task of the CEO is to completely change their approach to core applications and redesign the core applications that run core business processes - from procurement to customer experience. The environment in which companies operate has undergone tremendous changes: today, customer relationships are no longer determined by the company’s technology; consumers dominate the market, freely using powerful new technologies, and consumers of all ages are adopting purchasing habits that are entirely different from the past. Moreover, this is happening not locally but globally.

For CEOs, simply catching up is not enough because the pace of change and innovation has not slowed down; in fact, it is accelerating. What changes will occur in mobile experiences, online payments, and web commerce in the next two or three years? How can CEOs ensure that their companies prepare in advance so that these changes do not hinder their growth?

It is certain that these are very complex questions, but I believe that the best approach CEOs can take is to start by focusing on customers, paying attention to what they want and how they buy, and considering how their needs and purchasing methods may change in the coming years. CEOs must establish corresponding institutions, cultures, and processes to ensure that their companies move as quickly as their customers, allowing their companies to maintain close contact with customers through any channel or combination of channels chosen by the customers.

This is why CEOs must become single-minded evangelists for the customer experience. The journey of CEO's customer experience evangelism may not be smooth, but embarking on this journey is definitely worthwhile.
