QQ's imitation of Facebook, I think it won't work_Logs of In-depth Mining

by geekzhang on 2010-12-03 10:22:58

The unbearable public opinion pressure on Tencent has also opened the door to its API openness, despite being an absolute dominant IM monster in the Chinese market! On November 29th, according to foreign media reports, a Tencent executive revealed that Tencent will emulate some functions of Facebook and for the first time provide users with links to third-party websites and components of externally developed applications. I found a blog link, those who are interested can take a look: "Tencent to Copy Features of Facebook to China!" However, everyone should note that there is a word in this news, the Chinese character "将" (which means "will"), and its English translation "Ongoing", so the extent of openness is still unknown! Currently, some platforms have undergone internal testing, it is hoped that the internal testing period won't be too long, and it is also hoped that the results of the internal testing will be limited to just a few websites that have cooperative relationships with Tencent, such as Discuz (which has already been acquired by Tencent)! The least desirable outcome would be if Tencent resorts to its usual tactics and restricts cooperation access with platforms that conflict with its interests, such as Phpwind (owned by Alibaba)! After all, in the C2C field, Pony Ma's Paipai and Jack Ma's Taobao are not on the same level; and these two forum systems are also the major figures controlling the left and right sides within the circle of individual webmasters in China. According to Tencent's consistent style, whether they will adopt an open approach towards these systems remains uncertain!

One very interesting point is that QQ, this closed empire, its international website IMQQ, is actually leveraging the leading open platform, Facebook, to promote its own products. On the IMQQ website, there is a prominent "Follow Us on Facebook" image link. It is unclear whether this is a satire against itself or a deceitful ignorance towards the Chinese people. After all, Facebook is blocked in China! It is unknown whether Tencent will allow 360, its arch rival, to add another copied product similar to Facebook's "Like" button on its website when the time comes? If it really does allow it, my attitude towards QQ will shift from insignificant contempt to insignificant admiration!

However, this so-called openness seems unfeasible in China at least under the current circumstances! Openness implies sharing! Speaking of sharing, does anyone remember the booming period of Del.icio.us? This bookmarking collection represents the most basic prototype of sharing, which then evolved into the rapid rise of Digg's instant news sharing! It seems that every time a new foreign website becomes popular, it ignites the excitement and passion of domestic so-called entrepreneurs! But times have changed, Del.icio.us remains popular, becoming an essential tool under Yahoo's umbrella, indispensable in netizens' favorites and must-add tools for website layouts! Although Digg has started to decline due to the impact of Twitter and Facebook and other open information and social platforms, a dead camel is still bigger than a horse! However, among the domestic sites that imitated it back then, how many are still struggling to survive today? Most have already been completely forgotten, although webmasters are still looking for bookmark button plugins and such features everywhere, but it seems that the users are only limited to themselves!

Why doesn't sharing work in China? When discussing this principle on a large scale, it can involve cultural traditions and ways of thinking, but on a small scale, it is indeed caused by the network environment that you and I, as common people, have personally experienced. For more details, you can read one of my previous articles: "2010 China Internet Conference - Web 2.0 Websites Collectively Silent!" To summarize in one sentence: In China's Internet, you have no say! Based on personal speculation, Tencent's move (the character "将" meaning "will") is bound to cause a stir like the initial popularity of concepts such as online bookmarks and digg, but its development is destined to repeat the same route of the extinction of once-popular concepts in China: the content shared through the Like button will always be those red and specialized harmonious content. Where is the novelty in sharing? What is the potential for viral spread? What is the basis for triggering netizen discussions and spectating? In the end, it will become another piece of chicken rib, tasteless to eat but a pity to discard! However, for the sake of the name of openness, the Like button will eventually remain, only adding to the heavy workload of Tencent employees daily deleting content!

The above is just my radical personal view. Actually, this move by QQ is still commendable! However, what we need to imitate is not the popular concepts abroad, but their advanced network management systems! Only by fundamentally solving the problem from the system can fresh and popular blood like Facebook and Twitter be brought to China's Internet!
