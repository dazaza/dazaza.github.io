Changing the way of communication. After entering the 21st century, with the acceleration of the globalization process and the increase in cultural exchanges between countries, this method of exchange has become increasingly important. Nowadays, films and TV works have become an indispensable part of people's lives in modern society. Appreciating foreign films and TV works can promote understanding and communication between different cultures and has become an important window and means for people to understand foreign cultures. With the popularity of foreign films and TV works in recent years and the large-scale introduction of foreign films and TV works in our country, film and television translation has gradually emerged in recent years. Compared with other forms of translation, the history of film and television translation is still very short, let alone systematic and comprehensive theoretical research on it. Relative to the huge impact of film and television works on society, the relevant research on film and television translation can be said to be quite insufficient. Professor Qian Shaochang once pointed out: "The audience (viewers) of dubbed films far exceeds the audience (readers) of translated literary works, but on the contrary, the attention paid by the translation circle to film and television translation is far less than that of literary translation." (2000:61) Currently, the research methods and theories in the field of film and television translation are still unclear, and most of the research work is still at the empiricist stage. The research of scholars in the translation circles at home and abroad mainly manifests in the following aspects: research on subtitle translation related to multimedia translation, research on strategies of subtitle translation, research on quality control of subtitle translation, and research on standardization of subtitle translation. Due to its special linguistic landscape, Europe has conducted relatively extensive and in-depth theoretical research, forming a certain consensus and norms in terms of translation strategies, translation norms, and quality control. In contrast, China lags behind in the research of film and television translation, lacking systematic research, and there are few relevant research articles.

1. Insufficiencies in Film and Television Translation Research

Due to various restrictions, film and television translation usually places high demands on translators, making the research relatively difficult. The insufficiencies in film and television translation research are mainly related to the following factors:

Firstly, movies and TV as emerging media have only existed for over a hundred years, especially in China where they have been popular for just about three decades. Compared to mainstream literary translation research, the history of film and television translation is too short to receive sufficient academic attention and widespread recognition. Moreover, within academia, the debate over whether film and television translation belongs to translation or adaptation has never ceased, leading some scholars to avoid theoretical research in this area altogether. Western scholar Whitman-Linsen emphasized in his 1992 article "Through the Dubbing Glass" that "to dispel the disdain of literary intelligentsia, who seem to dismiss film translating and the degree of difficulty involved in it as not worthy of their attention". (1992:17)

Secondly, in the practice of film and television translation, many scholars find that most translation concepts and theories are not entirely applicable to film and television translation. This objectively leads some scholars to focus solely on the practice of film and television translation without further researching or establishing new translation theories specifically suited to film and television translation. Due to the lack of relevant theoretical research, it is unsurprising that film and television translation has long held a marginal academic status. Historically speaking, although translation has played an extremely important role in cross-cultural exchanges, the history of academic research on translation theory is not long either, let alone film and television translation, which holds a relatively low position in the field of translation, making the lack of systematic research understandable.

Thirdly, film and television works themselves are multi-symbol carriers composed of images, sounds, picture colors, etc., and are not simple textual texts. Film and television translation is also not a single process of converting from the source language to the target language, involving the handling of numerous extra-textual factors, which increases the difficulty of theoretical research in film and television translation.

Fourthly, compared to other types of translation, obtaining source language materials for film and television translation is relatively difficult. This objectively poses a certain obstacle to the promotion of related research on film and television translation. For example, only movie studios and dubbing departments generally have easy access to the original scripts and dialogues, while the original translations and translated versions are usually not publicly issued. Originals found online are often uploaded by enthusiastic netizens rather than released by film companies, and their accuracy cannot be guaranteed; even the originals released by some film companies are often pre-edited versions that differ significantly from the final versions shown in theaters, making it difficult for researchers to obtain first-hand, convincing research materials. If the original script or dialogue cannot be obtained, studying film and television translation would require transcribing dialogues and lines from the original soundtrack, a task whose workload and difficulty are evident. Of course, with the recent popularization of DVDs and the improvement in subtitle accuracy on the internet, this situation will gradually improve.

Fifthly, among the vast number of translators, those professionally engaged in the practice of film and television translation are relatively few, mainly concentrated in large dubbing factories or television stations, as well as some senior insiders from other institutions. This group of professionals is small in number, and most rely on experience to engage in translation practices, with few conducting theoretical research. Even professionals do not pay much attention to the theory of film and television translation, while external translators usually do not have the opportunity to translate film and television works, making it hard to talk about the development of theoretical research in film and television translation. Taking China as an example, due to the limited number of imported films each year, a large amount of film and television translation remains semi-underground and amateurish. For instance, subtitle groups that have become popular on the internet in recent years consist mainly of enthusiasts from society who voluntarily form these groups to translate subtitles for popular movies and TV series. These individuals mostly rely on personal experience and enthusiasm, without theoretical guidance, let alone research on theories. Since they are not responsible for the accuracy of their translated works, the quality of translations from different subtitle groups varies greatly. They often prioritize speed over accuracy and lack unified standards. Under such circumstances, these amateur subtitle translators tend to focus on translation practices and show little interest in the theoretical study of film and television translation.

2. Western Film and Television Translation Research

Research in the field of film and television translation began earliest in the West, and currently active experts in this field are mainly Western scholars, particularly European researchers. The rapid development of film and television translation and its related theories in Europe is inseparable from Europe's advanced technological level. Movies and TV were first popularized in Europe, and the needs of the broad audience and the relative emphasis by the academic community have promoted the vigorous development of film and television translation theory in Europe. Factors such as the extremely close ties between European countries, the high correlation of different languages, cultural diversity, and commonality have all contributed to the advancement of research in European film and television translation theory.

2.1 Early Development

Western scholars' research on film and television translation from a translation perspective can be traced back to the late 1950s to early 1960s. Articles like "Traduction et Cinéma" published in the journal Le linguiste/De taalkundige in 1956 and the special edition "Cinéma et traduction" of the magazine Babel in 1960 are considered the earliest studies of film and television translation theory in Europe.

In 1974, Dollerup's article "On Subtitles in Television Programmes" published in Babel was the first independent study of subtitle translation. The article meticulously analyzed various types of errors encountered when English TV programs were translated into Danish. Dollerup's greatest contribution was proposing the importance of subtitles in teaching, suggesting that film subtitles could help with foreign language learning.

Istvan Fodor is a milestone figure in the field of voice-over dubbing research. His studies laid the foundation for Western film and television translation theory. In his 1976 English book "Film Dubbing: Phonetic, Semiotic, Esthetic and Psychological Aspects," he comprehensively studied the dubbing of films from multiple angles including linguistics, semiotics, psychology, and aesthetics. In the book, he first proposed the idea that film dubbing should achieve "synchronization" in three aspects: phonetics, character personality, and content. He also discussed the different meanings of gestures in different languages and cultural systems, as well as the different visual effects caused by camera angles on the perception of characters' speech. He believed that each country's language and culture have their uniqueness and irreplaceability, making it impossible to achieve perfect "synchronization" in all aspects during film and television translation. (Fodor, 1976:82)

Marleau in his article "Les sous-titres... un mal nécessaire" summarized the problems existing in film and television translation from four perspectives: technology, psychology, linguistics, and art aesthetics, and categorized them accordingly. In the book, he constructively proposed suggestions for the layout of film and television subtitle translation and introduced economic factors into film and television translation theory beyond the literary level for the first time, analyzing the operation process of the film industry and pointing out that film and television translation involves the collaboration of various personnel rather than just translators.

In the same year, Titford published "Subtitling: Constrained Translation." In the article, he first proposed the concept of "constrained translation." Titford believed that the main problem faced by film and television translators stems from the inherent characteristics of this unique medium. "derive essentially from the constraints imposed on the translator by the medium itself" (1982:113). Based on Titford's theory, scholars like Mayoral Asensio, Kelly, and Gallardo (1988) applied the concept of "constrained translation" to the practice of film and television translation in their subsequent research.

In 1987, the European Broadcasting Union (EBU: European Broadcasting Union) hosted the Dubbing and Subtitling Conference in Stockholm. This was the first conference held in Europe focusing on dubbing and subtitling. Following this conference, a series of related meetings were held, and a large number of publications related to film and television translation emerged. One significant achievement of the conference was the establishment of non-mandatory guidelines aimed at promoting the exchange and dissemination of TV programs with translated subtitles across European countries. Carroll (1998:160).

2.2 The "Golden Age"

In the 1990s, Western film and television translation research entered its "Golden Age," achieving breakthrough developments in theory. Two important books were published in the early 1990s. In 1991, under the sponsorship of the European Institute for the Media (EIM), Luyken collaborated with four European film and television translation experts to publish "Overcoming Language Barriers in Television: Dubbing and Subtitling for the European Audience." The book analyzes different translation modes for language conversion in film and television works from a professional perspective and provides detailed data on the quantity of translated European film and television works, personnel costs, and audience preferences. Subsequently, in 1992, Swedish subtitle translator Ivarsson published "Subtitling for the Media: A Handbook of an Art." This can be considered the first comprehensive study of subtitle translation in Europe. In the book, he details the technical development history of subtitle translation. His extensive front-line translation experience makes the book superior in translation techniques, combining both theoretical and practical aspects. In 1998, Ivarsson, with Carroll's assistance, published the second edition of the book, incorporating many new strategies and techniques for film and television translation under new technologies like digital technology, renaming the book more neutrally as "Subtitling." These two books laid the foundation for the future development of European film and television translation research.

Since 1995, Western film and television translation research has entered a period of rapid development, with a noticeable increase in related theoretical research, especially significant achievements by European scholars. Notable researchers include Finland's Gambier, Denmark's Gottlieb, Greece's Karamitroglou, Sweden's Ivarsson, Spain's Chaume and Diaz-Cintas, among others. Among them, Gambier is undoubtedly the leading figure in the field of film and television translation. His research covers a wide range of topics, including classification of different modes of film and television translation, strategies and techniques of film and television translation, and audiences of film and television translation, demonstrating profound expertise. At the same time, he has participated in almost all conferences in the field of film and television translation, edited a large number of journals and monographs, and presided over international conferences in the field of film and television translation.

Gottlieb can be considered the scholar with the most systematic theoretical research in the field of film and television translation so far. He has conducted detailed research on film and television subtitle translation from a linguistic perspective, including how idioms are translated in film and television works and has insights into the teaching of subtitle translation. His research also includes how the Danish language is influenced by the popularity of English-dubbed and subtitled films.

3. International Conferences and Academic Organizations

Overall, film and television translation-related research in Europe has received a certain level of attention in recent years. Academic conferences on film and television translation have become increasingly frequent, with many conferences focusing on subtitle translation research due to the prevalence of subtitle translation in Northern Europe. In February 2004, an international symposium titled "Language Transfer on Screen" was held in London, where over a hundred scholars exchanged papers; in October 2005, the University of Bologna in Italy hosted an international conference titled "Between Text and Image: Recent Studies in Audiovisual Translation"; the biennial "Language and Media" international conference gathers numerous scholars and professionals in the field of film and television translation in the West. Some related academic institutions and organizations have also flourished. The International Federation of Translators (FIT) is arguably the earliest academic organization to recognize the academic status of film and television translation, having established a Media Translators and Interpreters committee as early as 1987 in its FIT World Congress to discuss and study issues related to media translation. The academic organization - European Association for Studies in Screen Translations (ESIST) founded in 1995 is the largest academic organization in the field of European film and television translation research. Initially established by 16 university teachers from 10 European countries who have long been concerned with or engaged in film and television translation, they recognized that film and television translation research had not received sufficient academic attention and spontaneously formed this association to strengthen and promote exchanges among researchers in the field, film and television translators, and faculty and students in universities, thereby improving the quality of film and television translation teaching and practice. Today, the association has numerous members from around the world, including relevant researchers, teachers, and professional translators. Many of its members are renowned scholars in this field who actively participate in international academic conferences and publications.

The European Union has provided substantial support for European film and television translation research. In 1995, the European Council held a forum on audiovisual communication and language transfer research to celebrate the 100th anniversary of the birth of cinema in France. Since then, annual international conferences in Europe have included lectures and exchanges in this field, gradually enhancing the academic status of film and television translation research.

3. Chinese Film and Television Translation

Western film and television translation research started earlier and has formed a certain scale and system, with research objects and perspectives showing diversified trends. The forms of film and television translation vary by country, such as dubbing being predominant in France, Germany, Italy, and Spain, while subtitling is more common in Belgium, Denmark, Finland, the Netherlands, Greece, and Nordic countries. In contrast, China's research on film and television translation started later, with relatively lower levels of theoretical research and almost no systematic theories yet developed, primarily focusing on practical exploration, with dubbing being the mainstream form of film and television translation.

3.1 Film and Television Translation Before the Founding of the People's Republic of China

Before the founding of the People's Republic of China, there was virtually no systematic film and television translation practice in China. Movies were not widely popular, with only a few major cinemas in large cities screening imported foreign films, and the number of films was scarce, making the audience a niche group. Additionally, there was no television in China before the founding of the PRC, let alone imported foreign TV programs or so-called TV translation. Therefore, film translation was essentially the only form of film and television translation in China before the founding of the PRC. China's market-driven film translation for the general public roughly began after 1910. Early foreign films screened were silent films, and these silent films typically had foreign-language subtitles, creating the initial demand for film translation. At the time, the commonly used method was the film synopsis, which included plot descriptions and other relevant information about the film. However, the initial foreign film synopses often lacked Chinese versions, leading to a popular translation method known as "live synopsis" or on-site oral interpretation. However, the translations by on-site interpreters were not rigorous, merely providing a summary and introduction of the film for the audience, often omitting plots, adding or deleting words, and mistranslating or missing parts.

In 1921, China's domestic film industry began to develop, and the number of imported foreign films increased, with sound films also entering the Chinese market in large numbers. A new form of translation, subtitle translation, emerged accordingly. At that time, subtitle translation had already become popular in Europe, and the person who first introduced subtitle translation to China was Cheng Shuren, a Chinese student who went abroad to study film (Shanghai Film Chronicle: 597). By the late 1920s, the method of translating and adding Chinese subtitles had basically become the mainstream method of film translation. The extensive use of Chinese subtitles in foreign films even influenced foreign filmmakers, and around 1936, it was a golden period for the importation of foreign films into China. Some foreign film producers even directly added Chinese subtitles to the copies and exported them to China. However, there were many problems with subtitle translation at the time, such as inaccurate translations, inappropriate calligraphy, conflicts between Chinese subtitles and the original foreign subtitles affecting viewing and understanding, etc.

About a decade after the popularity of subtitle translation, in 1939, film translation saw a significant improvement with the emergence of "Yi Yifeng." Originally named Earphone, the equipment was somewhat similar to today's simultaneous interpretation, with headphones installed behind the seats in cinemas. Audiences listened to the "Yi Yifeng Miss" translate the foreign language in the film into Chinese through the headphones while watching the movie. Later, with the progress of the market and the increasing demands of the audience, Yi Yifeng evolved from initially summarizing the gist of the film to portraying characters' personalities and emotions based on the plot, and even explaining specific cinematic language, which can be considered an early form of film dubbing. However, Yi Yifeng ultimately had only one "Yi Yifeng Miss," unable to cater to each character as dubbing could, and the translation difficulty was greater, making it monotonous for the audience to listen to. Around 1946, dubbed films began to gain popularity. Although dubbing was still a novelty at the time, it had its own set of problems, such as mismatched lip movements and loss of the original dialogue's nuances during translation.

3.2 Film and Television Translation After the Founding of the PRC

The true popularization of movies in China can be said to have occurred after the founding of the PRC. At that time, film translation was mainly done through dubbing. From a certain perspective, watching dubbed movies was indeed more relaxing than watching subtitled ones. Additionally, given the overall low level of foreign language proficiency among the masses in China at the time, and the need to accommodate the illiterate or semi-literate population and ethnic minorities, as well as the dominance and protection of the national language, dubbing had significant advantages over subtitling. Changchun Film Studio was the first institution in New China to begin dubbing foreign films. As early as 1949, Changchun Film Studio produced the first dubbed film of New China, "An Ordinary Soldier." In 1950, Shanghai Film Studio established a dubbing department, and its first dubbing work was the Soviet film "Little Hero." Shanghai Film Studio played an important role in the history of dubbed films in New China. Before the Cultural Revolution, three-quarters of the foreign films shown in China were translated by Shanghai Film Studio. Before the reform and opening up, due to political reasons, the number of imported foreign films in China was limited. Although China had television stations since 1950, there were no foreign language programs before the 1980s. The real popularity of movie and TV translation came after the reform and opening up, especially in the last ten years, with a large number of foreign films and TV shows being introduced into China, leading to a flourishing development of both dubbing and subtitle translation.

From 1949 to 1965, over seventeen years, China translated 775 foreign films, averaging about 45 per year, with hundreds of millions of viewers annually. In the 1950s and 60s, China's economic construction was just starting, and the material and cultural living standards of the people were still relatively low. There were very few ways to enjoy cultural and entertainment activities, and watching movies became the most popular and widely accepted form of cultural entertainment. In cities, cinemas were the busiest public places, where families brought their elderly and children, or friends gathered together, rushing to watch movies. The entrances of cinemas were often crowded, and the halls were filled to capacity. In rural areas and factories, outdoor movies were loved by everyone.
