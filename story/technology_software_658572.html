Your legend was no fluke.

by wzyei596 on 2012-02-04 10:27:47

China Radio Network, Beijing, January 30th - According to reports from Economic Voice's "The World of Companies," it is learned that Facebook is about to go public through an Initial Public Offering (IPO). It is expected that Facebook will file its IPO application as early as this coming Wednesday. Commentator Siyuan Wang from "The World of Companies" has made remarks on this matter.

Siyuan Wang: Opening today’s news, all major websites are filled with the news that the American social networking site Facebook is going to be listed. According to foreign media disclosures, the IPO for this social giant with 800 million global users is around $10 billion, and the company valuation will reach $100 billion. If it indeed raises $10 billion, then Facebook will set a new record in Silicon Valley.

Unlike many companies which undergo complicated procedures to go public, Facebook's path to the stock market appears more elegant and refined. Not only does it make Wall Street and Silicon Valley wait eagerly, but even the qualification to be the main underwriter has led Morgan Stanley and Goldman Sachs into fierce competition. According to disclosures, Facebook will begin its IPO process next week, and it is expected to officially enter the stock market between April and June. With the news of Facebook going public, the prices of social concept technology stocks have generally risen, including Chinese concepts such as Renren.com and Sina.com.

The re-examination by investors of the theme of social concept stocks also makes the new wave of IPOs in Silicon Valley imminent. Reviewing Facebook's history, you will find that legends are not accidental. In a previous episode of "Big Talk Company," I mentioned a topic called "Leader's Personality Determines Corporate Character." On Facebook, there is no lack of elements reflecting Mark Zuckerberg's youthful maturity: vitality, passion, diligence, never satisfied, and most importantly, firm commercial beliefs. It achieved fame at a young age but remains calm; it once resisted the capital temptation of giants like Microsoft, eventually bursting forth after accumulating strength and becoming famous at once; even while owning the industry landscape, it has never stopped self-correcting. Western media writers have written such evaluations: "Users often complain about adjustments made during each update by Facebook. We usually refer to this practice as 'Who moved my furniture'. But just as sharks must move to survive, Facebook's updates and innovations not only allow the company to survive, but also enable itself to grow stronger. Facebook has now become a global social giant, devouring one group of new users after another." In fact, behind the fall of the former social giant MySpace was Facebook, this constantly moving, young and powerful shark.

Now, with $10 billion in cash, obviously, the dowry left for "Facebook" is too meager. People online are speculating about Zuckerberg's next moves, and naturally, some troublemakers are offering advice. The first is "unchanged to deal with all changes," maintaining the original structure and efficient business model of the company, using surplus capital to perfect details, and steadily advancing. At the same time, the company's shareholders and investors enjoy satisfactory equity returns, making everyone happy. The second is "expanding territory," accelerating global expansion, and extending the brand and product line to more places by taking advantage of the situation. The third is "acquisition strategy reserve," acquiring valuable small companies, intellectual property patents, and accumulating professional talents to prepare for building a larger stage. Regardless of which choice, Facebook will not slow down because Google+ and Twitter are chasing from behind.

Currently, the problems and performances faced by Chinese social networking sites include: 1. Decreased user stickiness 2. Low level of social interaction, platform value cannot be demonstrated, many friends may already know each other offline, without expanding the social circle. And among users met online, it is difficult to establish direct dialogue and discuss interests and preferences. 3. Decline in celebrity effect. However, in fact, I do not think the environment for Chinese social networking sites is very bad. In such a large and broad field, there is no occurrence of a large number of bubbles dragging down the industry, as seen in the group-buying and e-commerce sectors.

As the concept and awareness of social networking sites develop, professional domain divisions are becoming increasingly clear: comprehensive social networking, dating social networking, travel and food, information sharing, and various types of social networking sites, whose models are gradually becoming clearer. Comprehensive social networking sites like Kaixin and Renren are also attempting open platforms, establishing an ecosystem to meet more user needs. Next, Chinese social networking needs to focus on products and profit models: in this aspect, the industry as a whole has been too eager for quick success and instant benefits. I remember reading a material once, saying that the famous American social gaming company Zynga, from game development to launch, and then tracking users and improving, could leave up to 30,000 reports. This is why investors would value Zynga at $7 billion. Therefore, we must see the gap between Chinese social networking sites and the entire internet compared to America, besides the innovation protection environment that the industry always complains about, there is also the patience and focus of practitioners. Without acknowledging this point, China will find it hard to produce a Facebook.
