The Ministry of Transport, the Ministry of Human Resources and Social Security, and the All-China Federation of Trade Unions request that starting in March, taxi companies nationwide will promote an employee-operated model, replacing the contracting and affiliation system. In the future, the "quota fee" for taxis will be collectively negotiated. Rest day quota fees will be exempted, ensuring that taxi drivers get one day off per week (see A2 of this newspaper).

The so-called contracting and affiliation system originally hoped to ensure service quality in the public service industry of taxis through an industry entry method. This approach limited the operation of taxis to a few enterprises, making it easier to manage due to its narrow scope. However, because the number of affiliated enterprises available to practitioners was limited, this industry was monopolistic to a certain extent. Whatever amount the enterprise set, the driver had to pay, becoming a fixed cost that all taxi drivers must bear.

This "cost" is fixed, but the business situation of taxi drivers is influenced by various complex factors such as road conditions and weather. To ensure profitability, they have to work tirelessly every day on the road, choosing routes and times most favorable to their earnings. However, passengers' needs are diverse. Thus, the "cost" imposed on drivers by the contracting and affiliation system ultimately results in the reality of difficulty in hailing a taxi. Even at the expense of convenience for those who need rides, taxi drivers do not earn money easily. Without rest, working from dawn till dusk, enduring hunger and thirst, in contrast, the affiliated "management" enterprises collect their fees effortlessly, regardless of circumstances. Moreover, considering the reality of difficulties in hailing taxis in cities, these enterprises collecting "quota fees" are indeed prone to criticism regarding the management and service quality of taxis.

It is evident that allowing collective negotiation of taxi quota fees is necessary. This concerns not only the service quality of the public service industry of taxis in major cities but also the labor dignity of all practitioners in this industry. In fact, it's not just taxi drivers; relevant departments should strive to provide all workers across industries with the space to earn money comfortably. The realities of being unable to afford illness or take vacations wear down not only the enthusiasm of workers but also the quality of a happy life.

So-called comfortable earning means having normal holidays and rest time outside of an 8-hour workday, living a normal life where one can sit down with family for a meal, or even when feeling tired, being able to afford the time and material costs to put down work and go out for a stroll. Otherwise, if the strings of workers are pulled too tight, either the price is paid in terms of health or even life—such as the frequent occurrences of "overwork deaths." Just the other day, there was a report about Du Juan, a female worker in Shenzhen, who continued working despite a head injury for the sake of a 600-yuan full-attendance bonus, resulting in her death from cerebral hemorrhage. Or it could mean not earning money even when it’s available—for example, the current difficulty in hailing taxis, which is merely the result of taxi drivers weighing various pros and cons and making the choice most beneficial to themselves, thereby harming the public interest.

Deputy Minister of Transport Feng Zhenglin said that many mass incidents in recent years were related to unguaranteed rights. We cannot always wait until multiple mass incidents occur in an industry before thinking about the rights protection of practitioners in that industry. Some issues really need to be considered and addressed for the workers beforehand. Otherwise, whether it's the frequent occurrences of overwork deaths or mass incidents, they will be operational costs our society cannot afford.
