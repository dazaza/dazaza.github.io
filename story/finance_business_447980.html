This brings a grand vision for the diversification of marketing work.

by tmakefz8 on 2011-06-18 10:41:45

If there is no innovation, the enterprise will have to be eliminated! In the pharmaceutical and health products market of Jiangsu, Zhejiang, and Shanghai, a well-known brand has been making waves for many years, with steadily growing performance, especially in anti-fatigue products where it firmly occupies a leading position. The employees below are proud of this, gradually fostering a sense of "it's cool under a big tree," but in my view, how long can this situation last? Remember, past glories do not necessarily mean smooth future growth; what was once a source of pride may soon turn bitter. Currently, a certain brand’s product line has shown signs of fatigue, lacking momentum. Ultimately, the key issue is the lack of innovation.

Huawei CEO Ren Zhengfei once wrote in "Huawei's Winter": "The Titanic also launched amidst cheers, but what was its ultimate fate?" In today's ever-evolving market, filled with new concepts and business models, a certain brand should awaken. Renowned strategy master and Harvard Business School professor Michael Porter strongly advocated that companies should create sustainable competitive advantages; otherwise, their future would remain uncertain.

Nowadays, marketing driven by brands and services is gradually replacing marketing dominated by advertisements and hype. "Terminal triumph" has become a traditional marketing approach familiar to many pharmaceutical and health product enterprises, and this brand is no exception. Its greatest advantage lies in strategically recognizing the importance of terminals for expanding the health product market and tactically implementing terminal competition. One specific manifestation is the annual updates and changes in packaging, color, and form. Then, large sums of money are spent on buying out shelves and securing prominent positions in key regions nationwide. Promotions, blocking competitors, and during holidays, grand performances such as "plaza shows," "dance shows," and "costume shows" are organized along with product giveaways to achieve widespread sales. As for other aspects, apart from these tactics, there is little innovation. Salespeople below merely rely on physical labor for terminal displays and regular settlements, lacking any space for subjective initiative. To put it bluntly, they lack marketing concepts and have become ascetics of terminal work. Once separated from the company, the vast majority would struggle to adapt to new corporate methods. It seems that most so-called sales elites cultivated by this brand are simple-minded individuals with strong limbs.

There is no denying that terminal work is important—it is the largest carrier for achieving the risky jump from product to commodity. However, with the continuous emergence of homogeneous competing products and fierce competition, if terminal promotion relies solely on spending power, what is the point?

Consumer needs are both diversified and personalized, requiring companies to continuously innovate products to maximize market satisfaction while focusing on service reputation through segmentation strategies. This brings great prospects for diverse marketing efforts.

In recent years, non-traditional marketing approaches based on personalized service have sparked market waves, yet this particular brand appears to remain asleep. They still rely on heavy advertising and channel expansion, finding balance and psychological comfort in shelf dominance and funding expenditure, often taking pride in occupying prime shelf space. It seems they believe marketing is purely about numerical-style promotion, forgetting that health products are not ordinary consumer goods—they require reputation and service. If they continue to indulge in past successes, how long can such good times last?

Former president of Yaohan, Kazuo Wada, earnestly stated when facing failure: market development is a process of continuous innovation and elimination. Only entrepreneurs and enterprises that always maintain vitality and continuous innovation can survive healthily. Without brands, enterprises have no future! In the past thirty years, the ups and downs of the health industry cannot help but make one marvel at the courage of those who ventured into this field under the lure of high profits. Of course, for this adventurous and fortunate concept, they have paid tuition fees repeatedly.

Pharmaceutical health products are unique. They can often gain a certain market share with a unique claim or advertising creativity. However, to achieve long-term market dominance, they must possess convincing brand loyalty, which requires time to prove. Regrettably, our enterprises and brands often fade away before being proven.

Renowned brand marketing expert Mr. Yu Fei believes that for the health industry to mature and grow steadily and positively, creating a long-lasting brand is key.

Recalling the past, China's health industry has seen too many stormy heroes. We call them stormy because they attempted to use irrational advertising bombardments to create an irrational market space, hoping to convert society's resources into personal wealth overnight. Such impatience for quick market gains only fosters fleeting bubbles. Regardless of the market, to achieve long-term market dominance, one must possess convincing brand loyalty, which takes time to prove. Therefore, the health industry without brands cannot become significant.

Brand asset accumulation is a prolonged and continuous process. It cannot cling to old ways or blindly follow trends. It must integrate its own resource advantages in the product market to accumulate and consolidate brand assets. Hype and following trends are short-term market behaviors; excessive involvement only diminishes consumers' expectations of products and loses brand integrity.

Brands are centered around products, but their goal is the broad consumer base. The purpose of brands is to create benefits, and the link between brands and consumers is countless distributors. Building a brand involves finding the intersection between the brand and the market. Observing many failed enterprises, they often did not grasp the psychological needs of distributors and consumers, leaving them struggling in the gap between the enterprise and the industry. The slogan "people-oriented" applies more to marketing. Product development should avoid blind conformity. Only by meeting consumers, meeting the market, and actively cultivating stable distributors can a brand's integrity be established, and consumer loyalty and dependence confirmed. Without all this, "brand" will forever remain a mirage.

Market competition is intense, and establishing a brand requires differentiation.

In today's increasingly homogenized products, only by standing on innovation, creating a humanistic advantage in operations, optimizing the combination of business, products, and prices, and pioneering a new market pattern can we stay ahead in fierce competition and promote brand establishment. Continuously launching distinctive new products, tapping potential consumer needs, and seeking new customer growth points are essential.

Renowned brand marketing expert Mr. Yu Fei believes that only by cultivating consumer-trusted brands can we further promote the orderly development of the health industry.

The market leadership law states: "First" beats "better." Creating a new product and being first in people's minds is easier than trying to convince them that you can offer a better product than the original creator. Based on individual experience, the first brand that enters people's minds typically has twice the market share of the second brand. Brand positioning aims to find this "first" and create differences between brands.

A phrase reveals the true secret of health industry advertising and marketing: "Adapt to your product's potential customers' perceptions and emotions, and assault the mind!" Of course, the prerequisite is that the product must be good, thus involving the question of "how a product or even an enterprise presents itself in the market and how to find the support point facing the consumer group." In fact, every enterprise pays a heavy price to establish a brand. They do not want their brands, into which they have poured their "life's work," to vanish and exit the historical stage. They all hope their brands will live forever, remaining youthful. However, every day, we see so many brands flash by like meteors.

We cannot help but wonder: where is the secret to a brand's eternal youth?

But from the perspective of a brand's long-term development and planning, how can we extend a product's life cycle and avoid the historical tragedy of "each leading for only two or three years"?

This requires us to use developmental and strategic perspectives to make efforts in accumulating and expanding brand assets.

Renowned brand marketing expert and CEO of Blue Ocean Wisdom International Marketing Advisory Institution, Mr. Yu Fei, pointed out that for enterprises to achieve sustainable development, they must combine various factors such as resource advantages, product advantages, and market advantages to accumulate and integrate brand assets. During the process of accumulating brand assets, establishing core ideas such as concern, confidence, honesty, and love is very important. These should align with the product's functional positioning to establish a good integrity relationship between the product and the consumer.

Looking back at today's pharmaceutical and health product markets, they have entered a relatively balanced period. The consumer market has entered a mature and stable phase, and the need for innovative marketing models is becoming increasingly urgent. Successful product cases abound—this is an extraordinary era for health products. In this extraordinary era, how to assess the situation, escape real-world confusion, reduce risks, avoid dangers, and choose the right path to success is something current pharmaceutical and health product enterprises need to consider.

Professor Philip Kotler said: "A brand's most enduring meaning should be its value, culture, and personality, which determine the foundation of the brand."

In recent years, "brands are more important than sales" has gained increasing recognition among many entrepreneurs. Thus, more and more enterprises are beginning to focus on their brand building. Some have started to proclaim the "entry of health products into the brand marketing era." Reviewing the history of pharmaceutical and health product development, over the past 20 years, the rise and fall of health products and star enterprises and star products have been frequent. Therefore, we can conclude: the pharmaceutical and health product industries excel in marketing but are not proficient in true brand marketing.

Why say this?

Renowned brand marketing expert Mr. Yu Fei pointed out that marketing and brand marketing are two different concepts. The former overly emphasizes methods and skills, sometimes to the point of scheming, emphasizing immediate results in short-term operations; the latter focuses more on long-term planning and sustained accumulation. The reason for this phenomenon lies in the industry's initial emphasis on product strength and promotional power, so health product promotions are often concept-driven. Many companies exaggerate effects to cater to individual consumer psychology. Once consumers find the effects are not obvious or inconsistent with the mechanism, naturally, doubt arises. This is the deep-seated cause of the credibility crisis.

Recall the past ten years: how many well-known health products are familiar to the general public? But how many truly famous brands exist? If an enterprise overemphasizes marketing techniques and skills, it might benefit temporarily, but it cannot gain long-term consumer loyalty and trust; it can cultivate products with high fame but cannot build solid brands.

One, or many successful brands, are the pillars of any market, and the health industry is no exception. In the past, we have seen too much short-sightedness in the industry, witnessing many bubble enterprises that rose and fell overnight. A successful brand survives the trials of the market, gaining market recognition and representing the industry's banner. Without a true brand, no matter how prosperous an industry may seem, it is just gilded on the outside.

Although many of our enterprises have experienced "overnight riches," impatience and short-sightedness have become common ailments. However, we have reasons to believe that as long as Chinese enterprises develop solidly, one day, there will be a "global brand" in the health industry.
