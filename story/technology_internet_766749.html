Microsoft's Dilemma: Embrace Change to Match Google and Apple

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 20:50:42

In the past, Microsoft made money by selling software. However, in July of this year, Microsoft underwent a significant internal reorganization. During last week's financial release, the company's CFO, Amy Hood, detailed how their business will now fully pivot towards devices, advertising, and cloud services. Isn't this exactly what Apple and Google are doing? Yes, this is precisely what Microsoft plans to do going forward.

The transformation in July was an 8-to-4 consolidation. All previous product departments were merged into four major divisions: a hardware department consisting of Surface and Xbox, an OS department encompassing desktops, mobile systems, and Xbox, an application service department, and a cloud computing and enterprise department.

This 8-to-4 consolidation is for three key areas.

Hood stated that this transformation aims to bring more economic value to Microsoft. The company has simplified its focus areas to three main categories: devices, enterprise services, and personal services. The acquisition of Nokia completed Microsoft's mobile strategy with Xbox and Surface.

The best example of successful integration of hardware and software is undoubtedly the Apple model. Products like iPod, iPhone, and iPad have all been immensely profitable. In a recent interview, Captain Cook (Tim Cook) openly stated: "Everyone is borrowing our strategies."

Microsoft's old business model was centered on PCs, serving both enterprises and individuals; moving forward, they aim to create multiple revenue streams through enterprise services, personal services, and devices.

The second half of the mobile device era

After Microsoft dominated the PC era, smartphones and tablets emerged as new forms of computing. At this time, companies like Apple, Samsung, and Qualcomm held positions similar to the once-dominant Microsoft+Intel combination. However, Microsoft believes this transition is not over yet, and the company wants to participate in the second half of this market's development.

But the hardware profits from Xbox are not high, and Surface hasn't gained much traction so far. The launch of new products post-Nokia acquisition (Surface 2, WP tablet phones) will be a critical focus.

Can advertising and services compete with Google?

Beyond hardware, Microsoft will also focus on two other businesses: advertising and services. These are, without a doubt, Google's strong suits. Office 365 Premium and Xbox Live have solid user bases, and Microsoft has worked hard on SkyDrive and Skype.

Microsoft's services include approximately 400 million Outlook.com users, some pre-installed apps on Windows 8 already carry advertisements, and the company is experimenting with ad placements within Xbox Live. However, currently, advertising revenue remains a small fraction compared to search business earnings. Microsoft has invested significant effort into optimizing Bing commercially, such as integrating it with Siri in iOS 7, as well as incorporating it into Windows 8.1 and Windows Phone.

Both of these businesses are aggressive and directly compete with Google. Ultimately, the company's past excessive focus on desktop business allowed Google to seize the first wave of opportunities in internet services.

Enterprise services remain a traditional strength

More than 55% of Microsoft's current revenue comes from enterprise customers. Their plan is to transition large clients from custom services to cloud solutions while continuing to offer traditional software licensing.

Regarding this restructuring, Ballmer may not see significant results during his tenure. However, he believes this new model will elevate the company to new heights within 4-5 years.
