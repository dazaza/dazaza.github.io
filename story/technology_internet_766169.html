From "Shall we talk?" to "Coffee table massacre" - the behind-the-scenes story of Microsoft's acquisition of Nokia

by anonymous on 2013-09-08 19:32:05

“Let’s talk.”

This was Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer's opening line in a late January phone call to Nokia Chairman Risto Siilasmaa. It was morning at Microsoft headquarters in Redmond and evening in Finland, where Nokia is based. The conversation lasted only five minutes before both parties agreed to meet face-to-face at the upcoming Mobile World Congress in Barcelona.

Brief as it was, the call carried deep significance.

Despite their strong partnership, many individuals on both sides, including Ballmer, were growing increasingly concerned about the slow adoption of Windows Phone. In fact, both Microsoft and Nokia had invested substantial resources into developing their respective phone brands and applications. However, their engineering teams struggled to collaborate fully, often duplicating efforts in certain areas. As a result, Windows Phone captured only single-digit market share, marginalizing Microsoft in the mobile market dominated by Google's Android and Apple's iOS.

Even more pressing, Nokia's future as an independent entity was being questioned due to its declining sales performance. Its stock and financial health were already showing signs of trouble. Investors were wondering if Nokia would continue to decline faster than expected. Meanwhile, Microsoft feared that Nokia might shift towards Android, which would be unacceptable for the future of Windows Phone. It was time to reassess the relationship between these two giants.

After the deal was finalized, according to sources familiar with the matter, the acquisition process took eight months and involved countless high-level meetings across the globe. The negotiations were not always smooth, nearly breaking down multiple times. Ultimately, persistence, inclusive personalities, and an unexpected interjection from a glass coffee table helped secure the deal.

The Rey Juan Carlos Hotel in Barcelona

As the Mobile World Congress in Barcelona approached, both teams sought to evaluate their partnership and explore further solutions. At the Rey Juan Carlos Hotel in Barcelona, Ballmer and Siilasmaa engaged in a one-hour meeting discussing various options ranging from minor adjustments to their existing partnership, deeper commercial collaborations, or even a merger.

Following this meeting, Siilasmaa introduced several Nokia executives to Ballmer who, in turn, introduced key Microsoft personnel to further explore potential options. Realizing that the talks might lead to acquisition discussions, Siilasmaa and Nokia's board began reviewing all possible choices, including non-Microsoft acquisition scenarios.

This led to prolonged back-and-forth negotiations involving senior executives from both companies. According to people closely tied to both enterprises, the final deal announced on Monday could be traced back to the Barcelona meeting and three subsequent gatherings.

The acquisition, codenamed Project Gold Medal internally at Microsoft (and named after Edwin Moses, a former U.S. Olympic gold medalist runner), was referred to by Nokia using the codename Paavo Johannes Nurmi (a nine-time gold medal-winning sprinter known as "The Flying Finn").

However, reaching the deal proved to be much more challenging than a short race with hurdles.

On April 22, Nokia and Microsoft met at the New York office of Nokia's outside legal firm, Skadden Arps Slate Meagher & Flom. Representing Nokia were Chairman Siilasmaa, CEO Stephen Elop (formerly a senior Microsoft executive), internal chief legal counsel Louise Pentland, and CFO Timo Ihamuotila.

Microsoft's core representatives included CEO Steven Ballmer, Windows Phone division director Terry Myerson, then-CFO Peter Klein (later succeeded by CFO Amy Hood), and longtime legal advisor Brad Smith.

During the April 22 meeting, Smith was delayed due to providing testimony for congressional immigration reform in Washington D.C. He hoped to arrive at the start of the dialogue but the hearing ran longer than expected. Ballmer had to communicate with him via phone, while Nokia's legal counsel Pentland contacted Smith through text messages.

By the time Smith finally arrived, the negotiation was nearing deadlock. Microsoft had made an offer, effectively signaling an acquisition intent, and Nokia was preparing its response. Before reconvening, Smith had only 30 seconds to confer with the Microsoft team.

The meeting resumed, and Siilasmaa calmly and politely spent ten minutes explaining the vast disparity in valuation. Ballmer acknowledged understanding Nokia's position.

Subsequently, both parties decided that continuing the meeting was unnecessary. After another ten minutes of discussion in the conference room, Smith and his Microsoft colleagues headed to the airport. This was perhaps the shortest New York trip Smith had ever experienced.

Despite the impasse, Smith suggested his colleagues revisit the negotiation content the next day. Within Nokia, some believed the gap between the two parties might not be as large as it seemed. Perhaps discussing price too early caused the deadlock, especially since Microsoft may not have fully understood many aspects of Nokia's business, leading to an inappropriate valuation.

The following day, April 23, Siilasmaa sent a text message to CEO Steve Ballmer discussing whether there were still points worth negotiating. Smith and Pentland also decided to meet to clarify the seemingly insurmountable differences.

According to reliable sources, a series of teleconferences brought both parties back to the negotiating table. They decided to meet again on May 24, this time at the London office of Microsoft's external law firm Simpson, Thacher & Bartlett.

On May 24, both parties met and negotiated, showing signs of reaching consensus in some areas but remained deadlocked on others. By evening, the building housed only Microsoft and Nokia personnel. Each side occupied one end of the floor, carefully studying the other's proposals.

As Ballmer and Smith walked back into the conference room, Ballmer had been walking on Smith's left. Suddenly, accompanied by a loud scream, he disappeared. That powerful scream could only come from the booming-voiced Microsoft leader. The scream startled the Nokia team, thinking one of their suggestions had angered Ballmer. Meanwhile, Microsoft employees were confused. Then they heard footsteps rushing around, slightly unsettling.

It turned out Ballmer tripped over an invisible glass coffee table, cutting a large gash above his eyebrow. Two Microsoft security guards ran to fetch a first aid kit. Eventually, Myerson sent a text message informing the Nokia team what had happened. While being bandaged, Ballmer conversed with Sillasmaa and Elop, who came out to check on his condition.

Later, both teams dined together, though Ballmer now sported a large bandage on his face. By the time both parties returned to the conference room the next morning, the "culprit" coffee table had been moved from the center of the hallway to under a windowsill. By afternoon, it was completely removed.

But the negotiation remained difficult. One sticking point was Nokia's mapping business. Siilasmaa considered this crucial for Nokia's continued existence as a company. Microsoft, however, firmly believed that controlling location technology was essential to succeeding in the mobile market.

To break the deadlock, Smith and Ballmer personally flew to Finland, arriving Friday afternoon, June 14, at a residence owned by Nokia in Batvik, a town 30 kilometers west of Nokia's headquarters. Previously owned by the Russian military, it featured a sauna, swimming pool, hunting lodge, and an old train carriage house. It was close to a famous winter-swimming beach.

The Microsoft team communicated with Siilasmaa and Elop for hours about the negotiation's difficulties. No conclusion was reached, but both sides agreed to continue discussions. During a traditional Finnish banquet, conversations shifted from the partnership to the NSA and the global-shaking Prism scandal. At 4:30 PM, Ballmer and Smith boarded their flight back to the United States. At least Smith stayed longer in Finland than his previous brief New York stop.

As July approached, both parties decided it was time to assess whether the deal could be finalized. If not, they would consider other options. Pentland contacted Smith suggesting a meeting, but both sides were too busy. Microsoft's leadership was focused on MGX, the annual sales summit held every second week of July. Meanwhile, Nokia was busy finalizing its acquisition of Siemens' network equipment division.

They eventually agreed to meet on July 20, back in New York. Simultaneously, they decided the meeting would involve only eight key individuals — Ballmer, Myerson, Smith, and Hood from Microsoft, and Siilasmaa, Elop, Pentland, and Ihamuotila from Nokia. A week before the meeting, Microsoft's leadership studied Nokia's conditions while fulfilling MGX responsibilities. The four-person Nokia acquisition team communicated via teleconference — Smith and Hood stationed at Microsoft headquarters, while Ballmer and Myerson were in Atlanta.

When they reconvened in New York, the map issue remained unresolved. Ballmer felt very frustrated about not finding a solution. He likened Microsoft's apps to oil barrels and Nokia's map data to crude oil — neither could exist without the other. And Microsoft wasn't the only barrel needing crude oil. Nokia wanted to provide map technology services to a broader range of collaborators, including competing phone manufacturers, car makers, and other device producers. Microsoft, however, wanted to apply the map business solely to its phones, tablets, computers, and web pages.

The eventual resolution indicated that perhaps they didn't need such a split. Since the map business was software, both parties could share code. Nokia could retain intellectual property rights, while Microsoft could gain "ownership-equivalent" rights. Essentially, Microsoft could not only use Nokia's map data but also make necessary modifications to the code. Ballmer and Myerson called Qi Lu, Microsoft's online business director, via phone to confirm feasibility.

He confirmed it was doable.

In New York, upon hearing the news, Siilasmaa consulted Michael Halbherr, Nokia's map business director, receiving similar confirmation. At the conclusion of the New York meeting, Ballmer and Siilasmaa shook hands amicably, although at that moment they had only agreed on some principles outlined in slides. Over the following weeks, legal and business departments worked hard to translate the slide concepts into detailed terms. Both sides strived to reach a decisive agreement by September 3rd.

To achieve this goal, both teams met daily to decide which issues needed escalation to the four-person committee comprising Hood, Smith, Pentland, and Ihamuotila. The final outcome wasn't just a simple agreement but a series of patent agreements, trademarks, the sale of the handset business, and the contentious map business agreement reached after heated debates.

Amidst all this, Microsoft faced another significant change. Following a series of complex events, Ballmer decided to retire as Microsoft CEO. Just before the public announcement, he informed Siilasmaa and Elop by phone and gained their approval.

In the final days leading up to the agreement, communication between both parties intensified. Both sides tried to use the time lag during document exchanges to catch up on rest, knowing they would need to review feedback early the next morning.

On Sunday, September 1st, Ballmer was already on a flight to Finland. Upon his arrival in Finland on Monday, everything was prepared for the final signing of the acquisition agreement.
