Correctly Identifying the Misunderstandings of Tieguanyin

by meitaocha123 on 2012-02-24 12:52:09

After operating for a period of time, I found that many friends coming to my store often told me their understanding of Tieguanyin: 1. "I've had Tieguanyin [http://www.teaguest.com/], it's very fragrant. 2. My friend gave me Tieguanyin in small bags, labeled as 'Guan Yin Wang'. 3. I don't like Tieguanyin, I prefer Oolong tea. 4. I know Tieguanyin, it's sweet. 5. Some Tieguanyin has stems..." and so on. There are quite a few questions like these, but I hope the text I provide today will be helpful to those who favor Tieguanyin.

Misconception: The more fragrant the Tieguanyin, the better

Beginners buying Tieguanyin are often influenced by some tea merchants' salespeople with wrong concepts, among which the salespeople mostly confuse consumers with the aroma type, making them think that the higher the fragrance, the better the Tieguanyin. Tieguanyin indeed values its aroma, but not necessarily the stronger the better. The most enchanting feature of Tieguanyin is its high and elegant orchid fragrance. The orchid fragrance we refer to is actually a special tea fragrance similar to orchid, giving people a deep impression. However, not all Tieguanyin teas have an orchid fragrance; only a few high-quality products made successfully will show a clear and rich orchid fragrance, also known as "Guan Yin韵" (Guan Yin charm). The above discussion about tea aroma refers to the aroma after brewing, not the dry tea's fragrance. Generally speaking, fresh, well-preserved or newly fired dry tea is more likely to have a clear and high tea aroma, but this is not particularly important because many Tieguanyin teas, though not strongly fragrant when dry, leave a deep impression after brewing; and some teas with strong orchid fragrance when dry become weaker after brewing - therefore, the evaluation of whether the orchid fragrance of Tieguanyin is pure and high should be based on the time of brewing. Similarly, drinking Tieguanyin should focus on tasting rather than just looking at the product's outer packaging.

Misconception: Tea with stems is better

Tieguanyin can be divided into raw tea (semi-finished tea) and ripe tea (finished tea). The former is raw material tea, without removing stems and unbaked, containing higher levels of tea polyphenols, tannins, and chlorogenic acid. Therefore, some raw teas may have a slight astringent taste, which is normal. The way to avoid this taste is either to remove the stems from the tea, then bake it slowly instead of quickly. Another method is to store newly purchased tea for about a month before drinking.

Raw tea with stems carries a slightly astringent taste, affecting the tea's taste.

Misconception: Tieguanyin belongs to green tea, not Oolong tea

Strictly speaking, Tieguanyin belongs to green tea. According to the "Great Dictionary of Chinese Tea Culture", it records: "[① Green tea, also known as Oolong tea, is one of the six major types of tea. The finished tea has green leaves with red edges, with a fragrant and unique aroma, being the best of Oolong teas. Tieguanyin was originally the name of a tea tree variety, because it is suitable for making Oolong tea, its finished Oolong tea is also named Tieguanyin]."

Misconception: Adding ginseng to Tieguanyin tea

Drinking Tieguanyin mainly focuses on freshness, fragrance, and charm. If ginseng is added to Tieguanyin tea, it's like adding licorice or ginseng to Oolong tea, resulting in no distinct flavor. This way of drinking tea ruins the Tieguanyin.

Misconception: Brewing Tieguanyin tea with a purple clay pot

Tieguanyin is a good tea, but if there isn't a corresponding teapot for brewing, it would be a pity. Tea is the father of water, and the pot is the mother of water. Tea leaves and the pot are like a couple forming a tea art family, where good cooperation between the couple brings happiness and fulfillment to the home. When tea leaves and the pot are well matched, the brewed tea soup will certainly be sweet and perfect. Tieguanyin tea belongs to semi-fermented tea, emphasizing aroma and taste, thus a pot with higher hardness should be chosen. High hardness refers to a high firing temperature, such as glass pots, white porcelain cups, and ceramic pots, which have higher hardness compared to Yixing clay pots. Therefore, choosing a ceramic pot to brew Tieguanyin tea is the best option. The method to distinguish the level of hardness is generally to lightly tap the pot body with a metal rod; if the sound is sharp, it indicates high hardness, and if the sound is low, it indicates lower hardness.
