"The Unification Path of the Three Kingdoms Returning to Sui" echoes through the ages of the Southern and Northern Dynasties.

by jqrxass on 2009-10-03 16:50:15

A pair of well-matched opponents, an emperor controversial for a thousand years, a great turmoil difficult to distinguish right from wrong, and a great era showing the true colors of heroes in times of chaos. From darkness to the Tang Dynasty, the entire Chinese nation was reborn in the fire. "The Long Journey of Unification: The Three Kingdoms Return to Sui" in "The Eternal Song of Southern and Northern Dynasties" takes you into ancient China in the 6th century, to taste a historical tragedy full of grudges and emotions.

From the year 386 when Emperor Daowu of Northern Wei, Tuoba Gui, ascended the throne of Daiwang, until the year 589 when Emperor Wen of Sui, Yang Jian, conquered Chen in the south, spanning two hundred years, it is the era of the Southern and Northern Dynasties in Chinese history. This great dynasty connects the Wei-Jin period before and the Sui-Tang period after. The entire Chinese world has almost undergone earth-shaking changes. It is the era of great migration and integration of ethnic groups, as well as the era of great turning points in thought. Some changes that occurred during this period have even influenced our thoughts in various aspects today. This book inherits the writing style of "The Sixteen Kingdoms", telling the historical chapters of division and integration during the Southern and Northern Dynasties.

"The Eternal Song of Southern and Northern Dynasties" - Part One of the Absolute Twin Stars

In "Enlightened Ruler and Foolish Monarch", we mentioned that before his death, Yuan Hong originally intended to entrust all political affairs to his younger brother, Prince Pengcheng Yuan Xia. Yuan Xia was the one among Yuan Hong's brothers who had the highest level of Han culture and understood reason the most. He deeply realized the complexity of the officialdom, the high power overshadowing the master, and it would be difficult to retire safely in the future, so he refused to take over this hot potato. Helpless, Emperor Xiaowen appointed Prince Beihai Yuan Xiang as Sikong, General Wang Su as Shangshu Ling, Grand General Guangyang Wang Yuan Jia as Zuo Pushe, Shangshu Song Bian as Libu Shangshu, plus Grand Marshal Xianyang Wang Yuan Xi and Shangshu You Pushe Rencheng Wang Yuan Cheng, six people as co-regents.

"The Eternal Song of Southern and Northern Dynasties" - Part Two of the Absolute Twin Stars

Killing her own son was not the best choice for Empress Hu. To maintain the legitimacy of her rule, she must protect Emperor Xiaoming's imperial position. Emperor Xiaoming did not have any biological offspring, and once he passed away, there would be big trouble with the succession of the imperial throne, which might make the situation uncontrollable. Emperor Xiaoming wanted to seize her power, which she was worried about, but the people she was more worried about were obviously the favored ministers like Zheng Yan and Xu Ge who were hated by Emperor Xiaoming. If seizing power succeeded, Zheng Yan and Xu Ge's lives would be at risk, but as long as Emperor Xiaoming was still alive, she, as his biological mother, would not end up too badly. At least from Empress Hu's perspective, she should not be the only main culprit. Wei Shou's "Wei Shu" tends to support the theory of Zheng Yan being the main plotter. "Wei Shu; Biography of the Empress" says: "Emperor Suzong's death happened suddenly, public opinion generally believed it was Zheng Yan and Xu Ge's plan." "Biography of Zheng Yan" also holds this view. "Tianxiangzhi" further states: "Zheng Yan and others were frightened, so they persuaded the Empress to poison the Emperor." The word "persuade" emphasizes Zheng Yan's leading role, making people feel that Empress Hu was just the hired gun.

"The Eternal Song of Southern and Northern Dynasties" - Part One of Hou Jing's Rebellion

Regarding Emperor Wu of Liang's devotion to Buddhism, the records in the official history are very detailed. "History of Liang" says that he "deeply believed in the righteous Dharma, excelled in Buddhist scriptures, wrote commentaries on 'Nirvana', 'Great Perfection', 'Vimalakirti', 'Three Wisdoms' and other scriptures, numbering hundreds of volumes. In his spare time from listening and viewing, he lectured at Chongyun Palace and Tongtai Temple. Famous monks and scholars, listeners from the four categories, often numbered more than ten thousand people." The Buddhist scriptures he presided over the compilation and translation of numbered hundreds of volumes. Nearly three thousand Buddhist temples were built and supported within his territory. "Temple" was originally the name of an ancient administrative institution, such as Guanglu Temple and Dali Temple. One of the earliest titles for eunuchs was also "Siren". The Buddhist temple culture of the Southern and Northern Dynasties era took root in the hearts of the people, so much so that many people today first associate "temple" with Buddhist monks rather than Baozheng of Dali Temple. Du Mu's "Four Hundred and Eighty Temples of the Southern Dynasties" could only be conservative in numbers, never exaggerated. The temples and pagodas (Buddhist towers) of the Southern Dynasties, together with the grottoes and Buddha statues of the Northern Dynasties, shine brilliantly as a pair of rare flowers in the ancient relics of Chinese Buddhist culture.

"The Eternal Song of Southern and Northern Dynasties" - Part Two of Hou Jing's Rebellion

Hou Jing starved Emperor Wu of Liang to death and enthroned Crown Prince Xiao Gang, who became Emperor Jianwen of Liang. The temporary emperor Xiao Zhengde, who was initially established, was deposed by Hou Jing as a counselor-in-ordinary and grand marshal on the day Taicheng fell. Xiao Zhengde was both angry and aggrieved, feeling that he had worked hard front and back, only to be sold out by Hou Jing. He sent a secret letter to Prince Poyang Xiao Fan requesting troops to enter the city. The secret letter was intercepted by Hou Jing, and without hesitation, a rope sent Xiao Zhengde to the Western Heaven. (Xiao Zhengde thought that highway robbery and murder would make him tough, but he didn't know that he couldn't even catch up with a hair of Hou Jing.) Hou Jing had married Xiao Zhengde's daughter as a concubine, but now he no longer needed to consider these things. Moreover, Emperor Jianwen married Princess Linyang to Hou Jing as his principal wife. Princess Linyang was only fourteen years old, in the prime of her youth, and was thus defiled by the ugly and lame old villain Hou Jing. The emperor was the legitimate crown prince who ascended the throne, and then became the son-in-law, giving Hou Jing a sense of being Cao Cao of his time.

"The Eternal Song of Southern and Northern Dynasties" - The Division Returns to Sui

Cao Cao was the grandson of a eunuch and did not belong to the aristocracy. A series of political reforms he promoted were largely aimed at suppressing the aristocracy. When Cao Cao rose to power, from Yuan Shao in the north to Liu Biao in the south, they were not only militarily hostile to Cao Cao but also politically and culturally excluded him, because the provinces controlled by Yuan Shao and Liu Biao were all ruled by the aristocracy. Although Cao Cao defeated Yuan Shao and Liu Biao, he ultimately failed to completely conquer Jiangnan, blocked by the Sun-Liu coalition. Ironically, Sun Quan and Liu Bei, who stopped him, were also not from the aristocracy. Liu Bei need not be said, bearing the golden brand of "Uncle of the Emperor," was actually a "weaving mat and selling shoes" commoner; even Sun Quan, through three generations of father and son, was not part of the aristocracy of Jiangdong. In terms of actual ruling territory, Sun and Liu families were still considered "outsiders." Their suppression of the local aristocratic forces under their control, whether soft or hard, never ceased.
