What Pregnant Women with Multiples Need to Know

by lk0lifed on 2009-07-22 14:23:22

In today's society, which advocates for fewer but healthier births and implements the family planning policy, families are becoming smaller and smaller, and there are even quite a few "DINK" (double income, no kids) families. However, for those who like children, having twins (preferably a boy and a girl) is a rather attractive idea. But you should know that multiple pregnancies can bring quite a few troubles.

Causes of Multiple Births

There are two types of multiples: identical multiples, which have a lower probability of occurrence; and fraternal multiples, which have a slightly higher probability. Identical multiples occur when a fertilized egg splits during division into two or more independent embryo cells or cell clusters, each developing into different individuals. Since they all come from the same fertilized egg, the babies born look very similar. Fraternal multiples, on the other hand, are different. There are two or more follicles that release mature eggs simultaneously or successively, and these eggs become fertilized at the same time or one after another. Because the embryos come from different eggs and sperm, the babies born look different, and their genders may also be different (i.e., boy-girl twins). In the United States, there was even such a real case: a white woman gave birth to a pair of twins, one being white and the other black. The reason lies in the fact that this woman had relations with two men of different skin colors during her ovulation period, and she ended up getting pregnant twice.

In the past 20 years, the number of twins and multiples has been increasing sharply. According to statistics, from 1980 to 2002, the number of twins in the United States increased by 52%, and the number of triplets or more increased by more than four times. The main reasons are two: one is the increase in childbearing age, and the chance of multiple pregnancies increases several times for older women; the second is the improvement in reproductive technology, especially the use of ovulation-inducing hormones and the implementation of artificial insemination methods. Some couples cannot naturally conceive due to certain reasons, so doctors either inject ovulation-inducing hormones into the woman (resulting in the ovaries releasing multiple mature eggs at the same time), or implant multiple fertilized eggs into her. If selective reduction technology is not implemented afterward, it will inevitably lead to multiple births.

The Dangers of Multiple Births

For pregnant women carrying multiples, the biggest danger is premature birth. A normal pregnancy lasts 40 weeks, but nearly half of twin pregnancies end before 38 weeks, and the chances of premature birth are even greater for triplets or more. This is because the human uterus is designed for only one fetus. When two or more fetuses develop in the same uterus, they must compete and influence each other. The stronger fetus will secure more nutrients, which is why some fetuses in multiple pregnancies are larger, while others are smaller, underdeveloped, or even unable to survive. Due to insufficient nutrition and incomplete development, multiple fetuses face various health threats, such as jaundice, anemia, polio, etc. Their resistance is weak, making them susceptible to bacterial infections; their lungs are underdeveloped, leading to breathing difficulties; their eyes develop abnormally, making them prone to myopia later in life; and they also run the risk of developing other chronic diseases. Therefore, multiple premature infants require special care, and parents need to seek detailed consultations and make corresponding preparations.

If artificial insemination technology is used, doctors often implant more than four fertilized eggs into the uterus of the recipient to ensure the success of the surgery. If it is found during examination that more than three fertilized eggs have successfully implanted and developed, it is best to take selective reduction measures and retain only the best fetuses. This technology is quite controversial in the West.
