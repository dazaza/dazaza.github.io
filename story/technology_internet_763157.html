Tied to a table: 8 examples of Apple's extreme culture of secrecy

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 16:21:09

Apple has always been renowned for its strict culture of confidentiality. When it comes to new product releases, Apple consistently achieves an impressive level of secrecy. How does Apple manage to accomplish this?

Recently, the U.S. tech website Business Insider listed eight extreme examples of Apple's confidentiality measures:

1) Apple once asked an employee’s wife to “forget everything.”

A woman named Kim Scheinberg recently mentioned Apple's culture of secrecy on Quora. She stated that because her husband was involved in the development of Apple's Mac laptops, Apple had requested that she forget everything she knew. Moreover, Apple required her to reconfigure their home to meet Apple's security standards.

2) Regarding Steve Jobs' death, Apple remained tight-lipped.

Apple co-founder Steve Jobs battled pancreatic cancer for many years before his passing and also underwent a liver transplant. For a period, Apple spokespeople refused to comment on Jobs' death. Clearly, this was a sensitive topic but also one that affected shareholders' interests. The New York Times remarked at the time: "The way Apple handled news of Jobs’ death was quite rare."

3) To ensure the secrecy of the iPad development project, Apple required developers to tether the device to their desks.

An iPad developer told the media in 2011: "Apple’s requirement was, give you a room, but with no windows." Furthermore, Apple changed the keys to the room.

Only four developers were allowed access to this room, and Apple required registration of the names and social security numbers of those entering the room.

Additionally, Apple drilled a hole in the table and used a bicycle chain to tether the iPad to the desk. Apple created a special frame to lock the iPad inside so that developers could not see its appearance. Apple only provided developers with a connector and screen, allowing them to write code for the iPad and operate on the screen to test their applications.

Subsequently, Apple took photos of the wooden frames around each iPad. This was done so that if any photo leaked, Apple could trace it back to the specific desk.

Apple also required developers not to disclose iPad testing information to anyone, including company executives or their spouses.

4) Apple enhanced the security measures at its Foxconn factory in Longhua, Guangdong, China.

In 2010, Reuters described Apple's Foxconn factory as an "industrial fortress."

Employees entering the Foxconn factory needed to swipe cards, and security guards confirmed each employee's identity through fingerprint scanners at the entrance.

A Foxconn employee told the media: "They use metal detectors to search us, and if any metal objects are found on your person when you leave, they will immediately call the police."

Moreover, two security guards attacked a Reuters journalist who attempted to investigate the Foxconn factory.

5) Apple uses multiple manufacturers to prevent product information leaks.

To ensure that Apple product information is not leaked, Apple employs several manufacturers to provide different components for the same product. Additionally, Apple assigns different products to contract manufacturers so that if any leak occurs, Apple knows which manufacturer is responsible.

6) Each Apple development team operates independently.

According to media reports, during discussions, Apple development teams must ensure that everyone in the room is an "insider," meaning all have signed some form of confidentiality agreement.

7) For major projects, Apple rearranges its offices.

According to the book *Inside Apple*, if craftsmen appear in Apple's office, employees understand that the company is working on an important project. New walls are erected immediately, followed by doors, and new security protocols are implemented. Previously transparent windows are blocked. Some rooms do not even have windows; these are called "cages." No information can enter or exit freely here.

8) Apple has a powerful product tracking system.

An Apple employee wrote on Quora that all product models are marked with serial codes to be tracked by a central tracking system (called iTrack). At the same time, physical security measures are highly optimized. When devices are not in use, they are locked up. Apple's requirements for the use of device models are very strict. Even your collaborating colleagues within Apple may not know what you're doing.

Access to certain areas within Apple is also extremely restricted. The most sensitive areas (such as industrial design studios) are equipped with receptionists and external cameras to monitor visitors. Inside these sensitive areas, there are no restrictions on discussing product information or using product models, but this is limited to personnel within the area.
