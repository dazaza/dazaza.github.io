Professional marriage lawyer and analyst builds a divorce club to heal the wounds of "love losers"

by kouhong on 2009-02-10 22:34:12

At the end of 2007, a "Divorce Club" specializing in marital legal services related to emotional issues appeared in Nanjing. The founder of the club said that its purpose was to help divorced individuals regain confidence in life and love. "Many people endure painful struggles before deciding to get a divorce. Once they finally make up their minds and get divorced, they fall into another abyss of pain, finding it difficult to escape the shadow of negative emotions. They are in dire need of psychological counseling, communication, and care," said Chen Siyu, one of the founders of the club and an analyst at Nanjing Weiqing Marital Legal Services Company. She collaborated with Sun Tao, the chief lawyer of Jiangsu Marital Lawyers Network, and Yan Jun, the manager of Nanjing Haojun Investigation Company, to establish the "Happy Divorce Club" on November 11, 2007, also known as "Singles' Day." The club used QQ chat software to build a group as the main platform for communication and regularly organized gatherings, trips, and other group activities, with expenses shared equally among members under the AA system. In just over a month, the club's membership had grown to two or three hundred people.

The American Academy of Matrimonial Lawyers found that the proportion of electronic data used as evidence in divorce cases has increased over the past five years. Family law expert Moses even said that using emails to expose affairs makes the work of lawyers much easier.

On April 14th, USA Today reported that a survey by the American Academy of Matrimonial Lawyers found that 88% of member lawyers revealed that the use of electronic data as evidence in divorce cases over the past five years had significantly increased.

Family law expert Moses, who has been practicing for twenty-seven years, believes that electronic evidence such as emails may not lead to more divorce cases, but uncovering affairs through various forms of electronic evidence "makes the work of lawyers much easier." Over 20% have snooped on emails.

American Academy of Matrimonial Lawyers President Hannah Hart told USA Today that people generally do not leave evidence of extramarital affairs lying around on the dining table, but they often inadvertently leave them on their computers, so "it is very easy to be caught." Don't think that snooping on others' emails is an unusual behavior; the report cited a latest Google survey showing that 27% of women and 21% of men admitted to "having snooped on others' emails."

Marriage attorneys not only search through email accounts to verify extramarital affairs but also scrutinize social networking sites like MySpace and Facebook, electronic calendars, and computer data as evidence in divorce cases. Some companies even offer software to monitor spouses' computer usage.

A woman in our city graduated from university and always wanted to go abroad; to achieve this indirectly, she hastily married a Hong Kong man, causing herself trouble.

She failed to go abroad and was burdened with being "married." According to reports, after graduating from university, this female student wanted to use marriage as a stepping stone to eventually go abroad indirectly. However, marrying a Hong Kong man did not fulfill her goal. Later, when she tried to remarry, she could not find her "ex-husband" and thus could not get a divorce, eventually resolving the issue through legal channels.

Origin: Wanting to Go Abroad but Lacking Opportunity

A girl named Xiaowen (pseudonym) from Dalian graduated in 2004 and couldn't find suitable employment. She wanted to develop overseas but lacked opportunities.

In February 2005, Xiaowen met Zhao (pseudonym) from Hong Kong by chance. They got along well, and Xiaowen suddenly thought that if she couldn’t go abroad directly, going to Hong Kong might be a good alternative, and perhaps from there, it would be easier to develop in countries like the United States. Thus, she decided to marry Zhao, intending to go to Hong Kong first and then transfer to the U.S. when the opportunity arose.

Dream Broken: Unable to Enter Hong Kong

During their conversation, Xiaowen boldly expressed her idea, and although Zhao was initially shocked, he agreed to help her. A few days later, Xiaowen and Zhao went to the civil affairs department to obtain a marriage certificate. On the day of their "wedding," Zhao left Dalian according to his original plan.

In June 2005, Xiaowen processed the necessary documents as the spouse of a local resident and eventually boarded a plane to Hong Kong.

However, when Xiaowen passed through immigration control, she was detained by the immigration officers. They believed that her identity could not be confirmed, and her marriage certificate was not recognized. Moreover, Zhao could not be traced, making her marriage invalid. Xiaowen ultimately failed to enter Hong Kong and had no choice but to return directly to Dalian.

Interlude: Wanting to Remarry But Still Married

In April 2006, Xiaowen started dating a new boyfriend. When they planned to get married, Xiaowen suddenly remembered her "married" status and informed her boyfriend about it. Her boyfriend understood and urged her to process the divorce.

Xiaowen consulted the civil affairs department on how to dissolve her marriage with Zhao. The staff explained that both parties must be present in person to process the divorce. However, she could not find her "strange husband," meaning she could not divorce Zhao and therefore could not marry her boyfriend.

Conclusion: Court Restores Her Freedom

In September 2007, Xiaowen sought help from lawyer Liu Chunying of Dalian Marriage Lawyer Network. Lawyer Liu suggested that Xiaowen quickly file for divorce through the court.

After investigation, the court found Xiaowen's claims to be true and subsequently issued an announcement, requiring Zhao to appear in court within a specified time. Under these circumstances, Zhao remained silent and did not show up. Therefore, the court made an absent judgment and declared the dissolution of the marriage between Xiaowen and Zhao.

Recently, Xiaowen successfully dissolved her marriage with Zhao through litigation, finally shedding her "married" status. She can now legally marry her boyfriend.

Implication: Do Not Use Marriage as a Stepping Stone

Lawyer Liu Chunying particularly warned that dissolving cross-border and Hong Kong-Macao-Taiwan marriages requires relatively complex procedures. Once a party wants to dissolve such a marriage, communication difficulties due to geographical separation or even inability to contact the other party create many obstacles for processing the divorce. Therefore, the newspaper reminds those who want to go abroad to handle the procedures through formal channels, avoid taking shortcuts to change fate, or use marriage as a means to achieve the purpose of going abroad.
