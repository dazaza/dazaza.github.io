Indian National Congress's decisive神奇trio

by foxbank on 2009-05-25 16:21:50

The Indian National Congress achieved an overwhelming victory in the lower house election, thanks to a resounding trio consisting of a mature politician, a person hailed as the "father of India's economic reform," and a star campaigner.

On May 17, the official results of India's marathon five-phase lower house election were announced: the United Progressive Alliance led by the Indian National Congress won 261 seats, with the Congress alone securing 205 seats. Meanwhile, its main rival, the National Democratic Alliance led by the Bharatiya Janata Party, garnered 159 seats, with the Bharatiya Janata Party winning 116 seats.

Behind the Indian National Congress' overwhelming victory was a miraculous "trio" (trio) that no one could have imagined. They are a 62-year-old Italian-born widow who once detested politics – Sonia Gandhi, her 38-year-old, refined son – Rahul Gandhi, and a 76-year-old prime minister renowned for delivering sleep-inducing speeches and being rather wooden in demeanor – Manmohan Singh.

Eleven years ago, when Sonia Gandhi took over as the chairperson of the Indian National Congress, the country's oldest political party, she was considered the most unlikely politician in India – a foreigner with poor Hindi skills, so reclusive that it seemed transcendent, a wife who repeatedly discouraged her husband from entering politics and lost him in an assassination, and a shy mother who stayed at home. Her entry into politics was extremely reluctant, and she joined the Indian National Congress quite late. In Indian political alliances, loyalty and friendship are fleeting commodities. She has proven herself more than ready for this bloody arena. Today, the Indian National Congress under her leadership has achieved remarkable political success, achieving the party's best performance in the parliamentary elections in 25 years. The United Progressive Alliance led by her is only 11 seats short of the absolute majority needed to form a government.

It can be said that the current success of the Congress Party stems from the humility and graceful retirement of its chairperson, Sonia Gandhi, five years ago. In 2004, under her leadership, the Indian National Congress unexpectedly reversed the situation and won the general election. It was natural for her to become the prime minister, but she listened to her "inner voice" and rejected this temptation, instead handing over the prime ministership to the mild-mannered economist Singh. This move consolidated her power and successfully made her the chairperson of the Indian National Congress for a record-breaking continuous ten years, transforming her from a noblewoman with no intention of entering politics into a mature politician.

Singh became the prime minister, but Sonia is undoubtedly the "kingmaker" (KingMaker), becoming the most powerful politician in contemporary India. Now, in India, no one talks about her "foreign bloodline" anymore, and her position as a leader in Indian politics is at its zenith. Indian-born economist and British peer Meghnad Desai even compared her to Catherine the Great, the powerful Russian empress born in Germany.

Although Prime Minister Singh has long been seen as Mrs. Gandhi's "puppet," Singh is by no means an ordinary person. He is known for his extraordinary knowledge and honesty. When he served as India's finance minister in the early 1990s, he carried out unprecedented economic reforms, putting the Indian economy on a path of rapid development, earning him the title of the "father of India's economic reform." During his first term, India's economic growth rate reached as high as 8%-9%. Not only did he increase the budgets for health and education, but he also launched ambitious public works projects in rural areas, provided debt relief for farmers, and implemented rural employment guarantee plans and affordable food programs. Since the majority of Indian voters live in rural areas and they care more about education than security issues, the Bharatiya Janata Party's "security card" and "anti-terrorism card" failed. As a result, Singh will become the first prime minister in the country in 40 years to be re-elected after completing a full term. The last member of the miraculous "trio" is Rahul Gandhi, the key strategist and indefatigable campaigner of the Indian National Congress and Sonia's son. He is a Cambridge-educated, handsome, bookish member of parliament whose friends are in the information technology and financial worlds, yet his speeches represent the rural poor. As the latest embodiment of the Gandhi dynasty, he is like a rock star; wherever he goes, voters flock to him. He attended 120 campaign rallies, three times more than his mother, the party chairperson, or Prime Minister Singh. Symbolically, Rahul drank tea with people of low caste, which was once considered a taboo in India. He insists on secularism and strives to regain the support of the poor, Muslims, and high-caste Brahmins, the traditional supporters of the party. Due to concerns about the backlash caused by the rise of Hindu right-wing and radicals, the Indian National Congress, known as a "big tent" party (a “big tent” party), continues to gain momentum. Rahul's contributions are undeniable.
