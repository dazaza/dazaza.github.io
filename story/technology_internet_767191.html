4 Major Tech Trends from the Perspective of Apple's Product Design Patriarch

by anonymous on 2013-10-10 18:30:43

　　The Godfather of Apple Design Spots 4 Looming Tech Trends

　　Hartmut Esslinger is the founder of design consultancy Frog Design, who has designed classic products for many global companies such as Apple, Microsoft, Samsung, Sony, LV, and Adidas. Hartmut Esslinger was called "the most influential industrial designer in America since 1930" by BusinessWeek.

　　In 1982, Jobs collaborated with Hartmut Esslinger, who designed white, simple computers for Apple that were drastically different from the monotonous beige designs of computers at the time, creating a new design language for Apple that revolutionized the entire PC industry. Esslinger's collaboration with Jobs laid the foundation for Apple's "user-centered, design-driven" values.

　　Esslinger recounts his collaboration with Jobs in his upcoming book, *Keep It Simple: The Early Design of Apple*. In the book, he mentions: "At the time, I knew clearly that what we were striving for was not just to help Jobs create a visual design language. Apple needed a new system that could lead trends so that Jobs could convert his vision into marketable products. What we helped Apple build was such a product—a revolution, one that went far beyond just changing the fate of Apple."

　　Apple's resurgence in that era was the result of multiple factors working together. A human-centered design philosophy, advanced manufacturing technology, and excellent supply chain management have always been the keys to Apple's success. Back then, Jobs positioned Apple as a company centered around designers. The design language Esslinger created for Apple in the 1980s not only led the trend for the next generation of PCs but also laid the groundwork for Apple's later touch-screen tablets and even wearable products like smartwatches.

　　Just as the world eagerly awaits Apple to create entirely new products again, Esslinger has already begun focusing on other design fields. "What we see today are ideas that were conceived long ago. Now we must plan, design, experiment, and prototype for future products." Esslinger still vividly remembers when his mentor in Germany showed him a very practical product model during his studies. "We all know that the future is accelerating. The product development trajectory of the past 40 to 50 years will condense into the development patterns of the next ten years, following these patterns allows us to grasp the future."

　　When discussing future product innovations, Esslinger points out four areas worth paying attention to:

　　Softer Hardware

　　As everyone continues to hotly debate wearable devices, Esslinger has already turned his gaze toward products made using flexible materials. As early as 2004, Frog Design conceptualized a low-cost educational notebook called the Gelfrog Smart Notebook. This product featured a flexible shell, softer than typical computer shells, making it more user-friendly and durable. Esslinger stated that the Gelfrog's shell resembles a sneaker sole, being highly resistant to drops. This product shares similarities with the latest tablet released by OLPC.

　　Since the emergence of the Gelfrog concept product, flexible materials have opened up infinite possibilities in the tech product field, with researchers proposing ideas like "bandage-style processors" and "e-reader displays that can be rolled up like newspapers" incorporating elements of flexible materials. Esslinger said that nowadays, almost everything can be bent.

　　More Human Devices

　　The second area with significant development potential is the interactive experience between users and their devices. Esslinger believes that devices being merely functional is far from enough. Future devices should come equipped with personal assistant-like applications, allowing them to truly become companions for users—managing data flows while understanding users' personalities, tempers, and habits.

　　Esslinger says that this is akin to how after decades of marriage, he and his wife need only exchange a glance to understand each other and know who should be the main speaker at a gathering. Esslinger thinks that users don't want to keep issuing the same commands. Future devices will become more humanized based on familiarity with users, with smarter and sometimes entertaining applications. Just imagine, even a relatively intelligent Siri telling an old joke can make users laugh heartily, so this area might indeed have great development potential.

　　Smarter Software

　　"Hardware production processes are complex, with vast supply chains. Manufacturers improve hardware products, which can make new products drastically different from the previous generation. Software, however, is different—it’s more like a creature that can never be killed." In Esslinger's view, outdated software versions, drivers, codes, and compatibility issues can significantly slow down system performance, waste resources, and delay information mining.

　　He believes that software development should draw inspiration from hardware production methods, forming and implementing synchronized development programs alongside hardware. "There is still much room for improvement in the integration of software and hardware development."

　　3D Interfaces

　　"3D is an inevitable trend because we live in a 3D world. However, current device interfaces remain stuck in 2D." In recent years, from Kinect's motion control design, to Leap Motion's gesture controller, to iOS 7's 3D visual effects, the development of 3D interfaces has been rapid.

　　Of course, developing practical and reasonable 3D interfaces remains a challenge we face. For decades, users have been accustomed to 2D interfaces, but the current wave of hardware innovation seems to be leading users towards a shift to 3D interfaces. "I think 3D will be a field full of opportunities because today's computing devices already have the capability to support 3D interfaces. Historically, the biggest bottleneck to achieving 3D was computational power, but now devices have strong computational capabilities, and they will only get stronger in the future."
