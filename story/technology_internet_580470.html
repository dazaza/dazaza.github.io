Zurblog Interviews Dave McClure: Sharing Investment Insights

by diglog on 2011-11-23 12:10:39

Before becoming an angel investor, Dave McClure worked as a technology consultant for Microsoft and Intel, and then served as the marketing director at PayPal. After leaving PayPal, McClure began investing in internet startups. In 2011, he founded 500Startups, a business incubator and investment company. With over 20 years of experience in Silicon Valley, Dave McClure has accumulated many profound insights into investment strategies. Recently, the Zurblog website interviewed Dave McClure about his years of investment experience.

Numbers: Success and Failure

Dave summarizes the results of his investments into four categories:

1. Companies run out of money (a truly bad result)

2. Companies are unable to generate returns for a period of time (a better result)

3. Companies can generate small-scale returns (a very good result)

4. Companies can generate large-scale returns (an amazing result)

When asked about his own investment results, he said:

50%-75% of companies will run out of money and not provide any real return

20%-30% of companies will not run out of money but won't provide substantial returns

10%-15% of companies do well and are typically acquired, providing returns of $5 million to $15 million

5% of companies perform incredibly well, providing returns of over $100 million

How to Avoid Failure

Dave McClure believes that for startups to succeed, they need to create products that people need and are willing to pay for. Many times, entrepreneurs run out of money because they create products that people might want but don't actually need. To ensure that a company's product is reliable, he usually requires them to present some prototypes (Prototype), "For many consumer or small business problems and services, with persistent effort, it is possible to build a basic framework that people are interested in within weeks or months." Additionally, he discussed areas where caution is needed in investing:

"We typically do not invest in many ad-driven business models, which usually require scale. We also typically do not invest in many companies that focus solely on growth without a clear business model."

Failure Case

When asked about failed cases from 500startups' investments, Dave McClure mentioned one specific example: 500startups invested $100,000 in a company targeting a small-scale enterprise market, offering SMS services in the mobile field. They found that the cost of acquiring customers was high, and the costs of training and educating corporate departments were more than they had anticipated. Their business model was upside down, and they have now decided to change their business model and are still working hard to find profitability.
