Amazon is said to launch a 10-inch Kindle Fire, accelerating the competition for mobile entry points

by geekzhang on 2013-05-06 12:09:06

According to Cnet, Amazon is speeding up its layout in the tablet market and will launch the largest ever Kindle Fire in the second half of this year, reaching 10 inches, the same as mainstream large tablets. Although it will still be inexpensive, the configuration is not low. It is reported that the Kindle Fire 10 will adopt a resolution similar to Samsung's Nexus 10 at 2560x1600, with a PPI even higher than the iPad 4.

This mirrors Microsoft's move into the small tablet market; both sides are filling out their product lines to cater to more niche groups and fully accelerating to capture the tablet market. Previously, IDC predicted that in 2013, 52% of the tablet market would be dominated by small devices, meaning that large and small devices would each take about half of the market share, making both sides equally important.

At the same time, Amazon, starting from its initial 7-inch model and then last year's 8.9-inch version, can to some extent avoid the sharp edge of iPad in the 10-inch field, while smaller sizes can also bring competitive low prices. However, after the introduction of the iPad mini, this misaligned competition no longer exists, and Amazon and Apple have begun direct competition, testing Amazon's cost control and product management.

IDC data shows that in Q1 2013, Amazon sold 1.8 million tablets, accounting for a 3.7% market share, ranking fourth. Compared to Apple and Samsung, there is a significant gap, but compared to other manufacturers, Amazon performs much better.

The Kindle Fire series has always been considered a fairly successful tablet device, mainly due to its low price and relatively well-established content ecosystem. By integrating its online content store with Kindle Fire and adding the Amazon Appstore, along with its closed custom Android system, Amazon controls the entire ecosystem on its own.

By launching a 10-inch Kindle Fire, Amazon further advances its grand vision — attempting to branch out beyond the Android system and build its own ecosystem. Tablets have already proven to have super potential in e-commerce, video, and gaming, and their commercial value is not inferior to smartphones, making them another key entry point. IDC predicts that tablet sales will reach 190 million units this year.

Through nearly zero-profit terminals, providing channels for content, this has become a model established by the Kindle series, a model worth learning from by more manufacturers with advantageous content. But behind this lies the test of control over content, supply chain, production, and user experience, requiring companies to have broad perspectives and multi-faceted layouts, reflecting the internal strength of enterprises. However, Kindle Fire is facing fierce competition, on one hand finding it hard to match the hardware advantages of Apple and Samsung, and on the other hand, its price advantage is gradually being narrowed. How to compete next will be crucial.
