Tesla, More Than Just the Apple of the Auto Industry

by geekzhang on 2013-05-13 18:15:14

Elon Musk is undoubtedly one of the most forward-thinking individuals in Silicon Valley. He was the founder of X.com, which became PayPal, the world's largest online payment system. In 2002, he sold PayPal to eBay for $1.5 billion, making a significant splash. That same year, he founded the space travel company SpaceX and became the main investor and chairman of the board for the photovoltaic company SolarCity. Now, the tech company he founded, Tesla Motors, is shining brightly in the automotive industry. Its new car, the Model S, has even been hailed by some as the best car currently available.

Tesla Motors' new electric vehicle, the Model S, recently received high praise from Consumer Reports magazine, which gave it an extremely high score of 99 points. This indicates that the Model S is just one step away from perfection. The Model S can also be regarded as a milestone in the history of electric vehicles.

You could say that Apple is a phone company, a computer company, or a software company, and all of these statements would be correct but incomplete. Similarly, Tesla Motors is a car company but not just a car company; calling it a tech company might be more accurate. From the start, some have likened Tesla Motors to the Apple of the automotive world. Frankly speaking, there are indeed many similarities between the two.

Both companies only offer premium products and have followers who are religiously devoted. At the same time, they both provide excellent service to their customers. If your Tesla breaks down outside, Tesla can even locate you and provide a free replacement vehicle. Moreover, Elon Musk, the founder of Tesla, much like Steve Jobs, exudes personal charm, is enigmatic, and is aggressively obsessive.

Another commonality between the two companies is offering high-quality products at mainstream prices. There’s no doubt that Apple's iPod, iPhone, and iPad are top-notch products in their industries, and their prices are highly competitive. Take the iPad, for example, it not only redefined the tablet computer but also reset its pricing.

Tesla cars are the same. Their new Model S is priced at $70,000, which, while seemingly expensive, is far more affordable compared to its predecessor, the Roadster, which was nearly $110,000. The Model S has already sold 5,000 units in the first quarter of this year, and future sales will be even higher. As a result, the company has become profitable for the first time. Furthermore, Tesla plans to launch a $30,000 electric vehicle within three to four years while maintaining a gross profit margin of 25%, which is already very competitive in the automotive industry.

Like smartphones, the biggest bottleneck for electric vehicles lies in range. In this regard, the Model S is at the forefront of the industry but still needs improvement.

Being the Apple of the automotive world isn't a bad thing because Apple's current status is enough to be immortalized in history. However, Elon Musk's ambition clearly goes beyond this. His role models seem to include another giant: Google.

Just like with smartphones, the biggest issue constraining the electric vehicle industry is the weak battery endurance and the scarcity of charging stations. Tesla is trying to solve this problem, but it would be much easier if Tesla could unite the entire automotive industry.

Google united smartphone manufacturers into an alliance through Android licensing, and this alliance now dominates the majority of the smartphone market, leaving competitors far behind alongside Apple. Similarly, Tesla could license its advanced technology to competitors to convince General Motors, Ford, Toyota, Mercedes-Benz, BMW, and other major automotive giants, as well as other startup electric vehicle companies, to form a common platform.

This is precisely what Tesla is doing. Next year, Mercedes-Benz will launch a B-Class car based on Tesla's powertrain system, capable of traveling 115 miles on a single charge. This performance far surpasses that of other electric vehicles. The Toyota RAV4 EV, also based on Tesla's powertrain technology, performs well too.

Tesla is willing to sell its batteries because it recognizes that the electric vehicle industry will benefit from a unified platform. Without Android and Android apps, many phones would just be precision-engineered components assembled for making calls. The same principle applies to the electric vehicle industry.

Because Tesla's battery technology leads competitors by 20-30%, it holds a pioneering position in the industry. Even so, its batteries are still not good enough. Today's electric vehicles generally have a range of 100 to 250 miles, which does not fully meet consumer needs. Therefore, fast-charging stations similar to traditional gas stations are necessary. Tesla has already built such supercharging stations, where a 30-minute charge provides enough power for 150 miles of driving. Unconfirmed reports suggest that Mercedes-Benz's new electric vehicle will support this type of charging station. With this, a virtuous cycle begins: as big players follow suit, charging stations will become more widespread, making electric vehicles more appealing, which in turn will increase Tesla's battery sales. Due to economies of scale, the cost of batteries and electric vehicles will decrease.

Elon Musk clearly realizes that great tech companies cannot be achieved solely through product innovation; establishing large platforms through core technology licensing is the way forward.

This is why Tesla mimics Apple in product strategy but aligns more closely with Google in its core and development direction. And Apple's closed ecosystem is simply too difficult to replicate.

Can Tesla, under Elon Musk's leadership, lead the electric vehicle industry into spring? We'll wait and see.
