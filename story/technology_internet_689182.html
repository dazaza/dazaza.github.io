Film Shots

A film shot refers to the continuous footage captured by a movie camera from the moment it is turned on until it is turned off. It is the basic unit that constitutes a film, commonly referred to simply as a "shot."

The composition of a shot includes the following elements:

1. Image: This may include one or several different images.

2. Shot scale: This includes long shots, full shots, medium shots, close-ups, and extreme close-ups.

3. Shooting angle: This includes level, upward, downward, frontal, reverse, and side angles.

4. Camera movement: This refers to the movement of the camera, including panning, dolly-in, dolly-out, tracking, following, ascending, descending, and zooming. Sometimes, several methods can be combined.

5. Shot length.

6. Sound in the shot: This includes sounds within and outside the frame.

Editing shots together forms the structure of a film, also known as montage. Editing generally involves two parts: cutting and combining. It follows the requirements of content, the development of the plot, and the logical psychology of the audience.

In cinematography, the lens is composed of a series of glass or transparent materials. Its function is to gather light rays towards a focal plane, making the film photosensitive. The degree and effect of exposure are determined by the shutter speed and aperture size. Typically, the shutter speed of a camera is fixed at 1/50th of a second, with the film moving mechanically at 24 frames per second through the aperture for exposure. In front of the aperture, there is a rotating metal leaf that appropriately covers it after each frame is exposed, allowing the next frame to reach the correct exposure position.

Lenses can be classified based on their focal length (the distance between the lens surface and the film) into wide-angle, standard, and telephoto lenses. Wide-angle lenses have short focal lengths, offering a broad angle of view and a deep depth of field, suitable for shooting environmental ranges in narrow spaces but prone to distorting object lines and depth. Standard lenses capture images most closely resembling what the naked eye sees, making them the most commonly used general-purpose lenses. Telephoto lenses, also called long-focus lenses, magnify distant objects, having a narrow angle of view and a short depth of field, compressing foreground and background onto the same plane, losing three-dimensional depth perception.

Based on the characteristics of various lenses and factors controlling the passage of light through the lens (aperture and shutter), photographers can predict the effects after exposure, achieving aesthetic purposes.

An individual shot often contains one or several different images, with each image composed of many identical or different frames. The aspect ratio of a shot's frame is horizontal rectangular. For ordinary screen films, the width-to-length ratio is 1:1.375; for widescreen films, it is 1:2.35; and for 70mm films, it is 1:2.2. Special methods such as masking can produce special frames like circles, triangles, verticals, and multiple images.

Shot Scale:

Shot scale refers to the different ranges of images captured by a camera at varying distances from the subject or using a zoom lens. They can be divided into the following categories:

- Long shot: shows distant characters and a vast spatial environment.

- Full shot: shows the entire body of a character and the surrounding environment.

- Medium shot: shows the part of a character from the knees up.

- Close-up: shows the part of a character from the chest up.

- Extreme close-up: shows the details of a person, object, or environment.

Other shot scale names used in cinematography include "grand long shot," "full panoramic," "medium panoramic," "human panoramic," "half-body," "medium close-up," "near extreme close-up," "large extreme close-up," etc., which are more detailed divisions of the above five categories.

Different shot scales are used to give appropriate representations according to the unique ways of cinematic art, based on the size and distance of the subjects and the importance of the content, achieving accurate narration and artistic description. Each shot scale has its own functions. For example, long shots and full shots can comprehensively display character actions, vast environments, and their mutual relationships, excelling in showing crowd scenes, sports events, and depicting atmospheres and moods. Extreme close-ups and close-ups present single, concentrated content, enlarging the subject not only to depict it clearly and finely but also to highlight and emphasize it. Medium shots excel in displaying exchanges and relationships between characters and between characters and the environment, playing an important role in narrating content.

Shooting Angle:

This refers to the angle between the camera and the subject during filming. Generally, it can be divided into level, upward, downward, frontal, side, and reverse angles. These can be used individually or in combination, offering diverse variations. Angles are important factors in the composition of shot images, possessing rich artistic expressiveness. For instance, upward shots make the subject appear tall and majestic, while downward shots make them seem small and open. Frontal shots create solemn and stable images, whereas side shots create dynamic compositions.

Camera Movement:

In the early stages of cinema development, shots were all fixed. The extensive use of mobile cinematography is a recent phenomenon. Camera movement methods can be summarized as follows:

- Panning: maintaining a central position while panning in various directions longitudinally and transversely.

- Dolly-in and Dolly-out: using a dolly or cameraman movement to approach or move away from the subject.

- Zooming: adjusting a zoom lens to capture images from far to near or vice versa, producing different results in perspective compared to dolly-in and dolly-out.

- Tracking: not fixedly following a specific subject, moving longitudinally and transversely.

- Following: continuously filming one or more moving subjects.

- Ascending and Descending: filming while ascending or descending on a crane.

These methods can be used individually or in combination. Shots filmed this way are respectively called panning shots, following shots, ascending/descending shots, etc., collectively known as moving shots. Combining camera movements with the motion of the subject forms cinematic mise-en-scène. They make images more vivid and rich, enhance visual dynamics, and help form expressive artistic rhythms and atmospheres.

Shot Length:

During the silent film era, the normal speed of cameras and projectors was one foot per second, i.e., 16 frames. For sound films, it is one and a half feet per second, i.e., 24 frames. Individual shots, for special artistic effects, can be filmed at higher or lower speeds, commonly known as high-speed or low-frame-rate shots. The length of a movie shot depends on the content being filmed. Short ones may consist of just a few frames, lasting less than a second; long ones can reach hundreds of feet, lasting several minutes. A ninety-minute sound film usually consists of 400 to 800 shots of varying lengths, with silent films and documentaries having even more shots.

Sound in Shots:

Sound is an organic component of film shots, combining with the moving imagery on the screen to form audio-visual combinations. Based on content and artistic treatment requirements, sound can be counterpointed with visuals, juxtaposed with visuals, realistic, or virtual, offering diverse forms of expression.

Editing Shots Together:

Editing shots together, also known as montage, is the fundamental method of constructing and expressing films uniquely. Editing is divided into two parts: cutting and combining. It selects various compositional elements of shots based on content requirements, plot development, and the attention and interest of the audience. It logically and rhythmically edits them together to produce continuity, contrast, association, emphasis, and suspense, creating a smooth, lively, and understandable film that reflects life and expresses ideas fully.

Basis for Editing:

The basis for dividing and combining the subjects being filmed is human living logic and aesthetic principles. Editing must follow human habits and ways of recognizing life and observing things, conforming to people's logical thinking, clearly, orderly, and accurately narrating the content being presented. This is the basic requirement. Editing should also utilize various expressive means, leveraging the strengths of cinematic art, narrating content vividly, profoundly, and with artistic beauty and感染力. This is the high-standard requirement.

Functions of Editing:

When presenting content, editing possesses artistic functions of processing and re-creating it.

1. Selection and Omission: Through the use of shot scales, angles, and editing, it is possible to select and highlight the main and essential parts of the subject being filmed, omitting unnecessary and complicated content. Content can be chosen, emphasized, or de-emphasized.

2. Concentration and Generalization: Through editing, some content can be concentrated and strengthened, allowing for contrast and generalization, endowing each shot with meanings they do not possess individually.

3. Attracting Audience Attention: It allows the audience to focus on one image at a time, understanding the content in the order and logic of the editing, thus guiding and standardizing the audience's attention, dominating their thoughts and emotions.

4. Creating Cinematic Time and Space: Editing can create spaces similar yet different from real life, even spaces that do not exist in reality, enabling leaps across different spaces. Editing can also represent real time similarly, extended, shortened, stopped, or repeated.

5. Forming Different Artistic Rhythms: Editing is an important factor in forming the rhythm of a film. Generally speaking, the combination of short shots creates a tense and urgent rhythm, while the connection of long shots gives a sense of stability and calmness. The methods of forming artistic rhythms through shot editing are varied and have great potential.

Techniques of Editing:

There are three basic techniques for editing shots:

1. Maintaining the Unity of the Lens Axis: The axis refers to the directionality of moving characters, objects, and characters with exchangeable objects. The route of the subject's movement and the relationship line between objects are the axes. Only by paying attention to the axis in shot editing and unifying the axes of mutually connected different shots can the integrity and correctness of the space on the screen be maintained. Shooting across the axis is allowed but must ensure that future screen space does not become confused or incorrect.

2. Selecting the Cutting Point of the Lens: The connection between lenses must pay attention to accurately selecting the cutting point, especially when editing continuous motion lenses of the same subject, more attention must be paid to maintaining the consistency and continuity of actions and positions. Thus, the post-edited screen image becomes smooth and complete.

3. Paying Attention to the Connection and Transition of Lenses: During editing, appropriate methods such as cut-in/cut-out, dissolve-in/dissolve-out, fade-in/fade-out, wipe-in/wipe-out, circle-in/circle-out should be used. Additionally, the method of "throwing" can be used, suddenly "throwing" the lens to or away from the subject, leaving the dynamic "throw" for the audience to see. This type of editing has strong rhythm and dynamism.

1. Establishing Shot

(establishing shot) As its name suggests, it is a wide-angle shot through which the audience can know where a set of events occurs. During the editing process, it is usually the first shot adopted. The audience is thereby brought into the narrative or action. This technique is commonly used to indicate transitions between a series of locations. For example, in the documentary "The Days of Huang Jing's Mother," we can see a large wide-angle shot before transitioning from the printing department to the autopsy department, indicating where the following story takes place. Thus, the audience has a directional expectation of subsequent events, entering the narrative.

2. Master Shot

(the master shot) It is a set of wide-angle shots that record major, central events from the perspective of an objective observer over a long period, such as seeing the speaker on stage making a speech in a large meeting. However, a set of long-duration shots focused on a single subject can be monotonous, so the master shot will change perspectives intermittently to give the audience a different perspective. Meanwhile, during the editing process, other images can be inserted to compensate for the shortcomings of the master shot itself. If carefully planned, the master shot can address issues of continuity in sound or visuals.

3. Shot Transitions

(shot transitions) It is a way of constructing narratives by moving and combining individual shots selected by editors or directors, usually related to the predetermined style of the program. Current digital technology makes this aspect of program production very easy.

Straight Cut:

(straight cut) This is the most commonly used type of shot in film production, such as cityscape montages, conversations at the dining table, parallel events. As a dynamic narrative method, it is acceptable; however, if improperly used in a film, it can create a jarring sensation.

Fade Shots:

(fades) Can be divided into fade-ins (fade ups) and fade-outs (fade down). Fade-in refers to the transition from a black screen to a color screen, while fade-out refers to the transition from a color screen to a black screen. Depending on the director's intention and aspects like time compression and scene changes, it can be either a fast or slow shot. Black is not the only color used. In feature films, a quick shot from color suddenly turning white is often used to express fear, surprise, and sorrow.

Dissolve:

(dissolves) Refers to when one image gradually disappears while another simultaneously appears. It can make the transition between shots smoother, softening abrupt changes. This means that when we compress time or shift locations, we can connect and blend shots. For example, it can be applied to montages of many things, such as dance, musicals, fragments of dreams, or transitions from reality to memories.

Classification of Camera Techniques:

(1) Pull-back Shot:

Its function is to let the audience recognize characters and environments, from points to surfaces, understanding the relationship between parts and wholes. Pull-back shots evoke feelings of breadth and expansiveness. For example, in the story film "Bitter Flower," the first shot shows a close-up of a blooming bitter flower, then a small hand reaching into the frame to pick it, but the audience cannot see the person or whose hand it is. As the shot pulls back, the audience sees Manzi happily digging out the bitter flower. This shooting method is called a pull-back shot.

(2) Follow Shot:

As the name suggests, the lens always follows a moving subject to continuously and detailedly show their activity, actions, or expressions.

(3) Pan Shot:

This refers to placing the camera in a fixed position, looking left and right, panning across a panoramic view, or following the subject's movement. It is often used to introduce the environment or highlight the significance and purpose of character actions.

(4) Push-in Shot:

Refers to keeping the subject stationary while moving the camera closer to create a close-up or extreme close-up. The same lens content, slowly pushing in, gives a feeling of composure, expansiveness, and detail. Fast pushes create tension, urgency, and confusion. Push-in shots can guide the audience to feel the character's inner activities more deeply, enhancing atmosphere.

(5) Subjective Shot:

This treats the film lens as the eyes of the characters in the drama to observe and express objective matters, simulating sensations and rendering atmospheres. Subjective shots are commonly used to show characters' mental states under special circumstances or reflect hallucinations and imaginations, frequently appearing in films. They also play a significant role in portraying character traits. For example, in the beginning of the story film "Little Flower," there is a segment of Little Flower recalling her search for her brother. It starts with running shots leading to young Little Flower and returns to reality with the sound of calling for her brother, naturally flowing and producing strong artistic effects.

(6) Empty Shot:

This kind of shot uses specific visual images (i.e., the frame) to indicate a certain time and place without characters or dialogue, only representing natural scenery and atmosphere with certain implications. Such as mountains, water, sea, pine trees, flowers, clouds, moon, birds, etc. It is an important means of making films poetic, creating broad and profound artistic conceptions.

(7) Up-and-down Shots:

Up-and-down shots can be divided into downward and upward shots. Downward shots, besides bird's-eye panoramic views, can express feelings of gloom and oppression, generally serving a derogatory function. Upward shots provide a sense of admiration, serving a laudatory function emotionally.

(8) Elevating Shots:

Elevating shots are generally used for large-scale scenes. They can change the lens perspective and screen space, helping to render dramatic atmosphere and effects. For example, in the story film "Red Flowers of Tianshan," when Oyiguli got out of the jeep and walked into the crowd welcoming her, it was filmed from a high altitude by a helicopter. This shooting method rendered the atmosphere and showcased a vast space.

(9) Composite Shots:

Composite shots refer to the varied motions of the lens. Sometimes, to more fully and prominently express a particular plot point, push, pull, elevate, descend, pan, and track shots are combined in a single film shot. It creates various lens angles such as front, side, upward, downward, and equal angles, capable of showing both the overall environment and specific close-ups of particular characters, as well as the relationships between characters, making films more expressive. Therefore, film lenses can be used separately or combined and interwoven. This kind of lens is also called a long shot.

(10) Zoom Lens:

It refers to keeping the camera position unchanged and changing the focal length of the zoom lens installed in the camera to accelerate or uniformly pull away or push closer to the subject without changing the distance between the subject and the camera, creating a certain rhythm.
