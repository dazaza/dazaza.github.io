Analysis: Microsoft Still Can't Make Products That Are Easy to Use

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 18:33:37

The New York Times website published an article today stating that in recent years, Microsoft has been striving to shake off its reputation of emphasizing product features over simplicity, but it still hasn't completely rid itself of old habits. Although it successfully created intuitive and clever products like the Xbox console and Windows Phone 7, most of the time it failed to meet the needs of modern consumers, with the Surface tablet being the most typical example.

Below is the main content of the article:

In March 2006, a parody video asked, "What would happen if Microsoft made the iPod?" The video started by showing the actual packaging image of the iPod - a white box familiar to people, with only the picture of the music player on the surface. Then, it asked, what would happen if Microsoft were to make the iPod?

After three minutes of the video, the elegant and compact music player was renamed to "iPod Pro 2005 XP Human Ear Professional Edition With Subscription". The originally clean packaging was now plastered with stickers and printed with terms listing almost all the technical features of the device. A design inspired by modernist architecture (imagine the Guggenheim Museum) unfortunately turned into a gaudy billboard (imagine a jewelry store in Times Square).

In recent years, Microsoft has been striving to shake off its reputation of emphasizing product features over simplicity, but it still hasn't completely rid itself of old habits. Of course, Microsoft also launched intuitive and clever products like the Xbox console and Windows Phone 7. But most of the time, the company was creating products for modern consumers with an information technology mindset.

The Impact of Disconnection

This disconnection goes beyond just being parodied in videos. This month, Microsoft announced that it would write down $900 million for unsold inventory of the Surface RT tablet, which had been on the market for less than a year.

Just thinking about the Microsoft Surface tablet can give you a headache. The company launched two versions: Surface RT and Surface Pro. One came with a touch pen, both had USB ports, microSDXC card slots, HDMI outputs, and kickstands that flip back, but they had different screen resolutions and ran different versions of Windows.

There are surely many people who would be thoroughly confused by these specifications. Most tech industry insiders could understand those features, but for regular consumers, they were incomprehensible.

"For Microsoft, Windows is like a hammer, and everything else is like a nail," said Ryan Block, former editor of Engadget and co-founder of Gdgt. "Look at the Surface; it's the quintessential example. They built this tablet around Windows and a Windows experience, and it was a complete failure that left most people utterly baffled."

Some Microsoft supporters and critics defended the company last week, saying that the lackluster sales of Surface were because the iPad entered the market a few years earlier and had a significant first-mover advantage.

Maybe. But I have a different opinion: the Surface failed because consumers who didn’t want to think about which one was RT and which one was Pro, or which version of Windows their new device would run, were thoroughly confused by Microsoft.

User Needs

"There’s a fundamental misunderstanding among Microsoft people about what users want. Users don't want speed and feeds," said J.P. Gownder, vice president and principal analyst at Forrester Research. "Speed and feeds" is a term used in old-style business magazines to describe the technical specifications of new PCs. Fifteen years ago, even a small improvement in speed and memory compared to the previous generation was a remarkable achievement.

Times have changed. Consumers now want easy-to-use products, and their needs are met by products like the iPad.

One major flaw in the argument that Microsoft tablets didn't catch on because the iPad had a first-mover advantage is that Microsoft wasn't late to the concept of tablets. In 2000, Bill Gates demonstrated a tablet product at the COMDEX trade show in Las Vegas, helping popularize the concept of "tablet computers."

But ten years later, the company that figured out how to simplify the tablet and bring it to mainstream consumers wasn't Microsoft, it was Apple.

Microsoft says one reason it offers all those features and feels the need to explain gigahertz and gigabytes is that it has to sell its products to small businesses and IT professionals, who are still fascinated by "more memory, better performance, and faster speeds."

Cultural Clash

So, when technical culture meets consumer culture, a clash occurs.

Frank X. Shaw, Microsoft's vice president of corporate communications, said the company has been working hard to solve this problem. He pointed out that Microsoft has made great progress in instilling a more balanced design, marketing, and engineering culture. The company's executives also said that the restructuring plan announced this month will lead to more consumer-friendly products.

Microsoft said that its eight product divisions will be streamlined into four new departments based on specific themes. "To effectively execute our strategy, we must turn multiple Microsofts into one Microsoft," Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer said in an interview.

"Are we still a culture focused on engineering? Yes, and that's good. Fundamentally, many excellent tech companies have a strong preference for engineering," Frank Shaw said. "One of our goals is to ensure that we have a more unified vision for the products we bring to market."

A few months after that satirical iPod video gained attention online, Microsoft admitted that the video was made by itself. The company originally intended to circulate the video internally to inform employees of the urgent need to change the company culture.

When asked about the video, Frank Shaw said that Microsoft's culture has evolved from an isolated technical culture to a more consumer-aware culture. "I think we've improved a lot," he said, while also acknowledging that the company still has much work to do. "The question is, how do you reflect the engineering elements with equally strong design and marketing skills? If you're too dominant in one area, strange things happen."

He's right. That results in strange products like the confusing Surface.
