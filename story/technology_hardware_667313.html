Teaching Methods of Human Anatomy for Secondary School Students

*2006 Journal, Henan Medical College for Staff and Workers, Vol. 243.*

The shape of the heart can be summarized as having two sides, one edge, and three grooves at its apex. The cerebral hemisphere's shape can be summarized into three sulci and five lobes. For more complex details, students often find it hard to remember, leading to boredom. However, by providing principle-based teaching that integrates coherent content, the difficulty does not diminish but becomes easier for students to recall.

In the teaching of human anatomy, four methods of enlightenment can be applied. Combining these with students' practical experiences and a progressive approach, some challenging issues can be addressed to enlighten students' thinking and develop their intelligence. For example, in explaining the portal circulation pathway, we design a suspenseful question: Intra-abdominal unpaired organ veins flow into the portal vein, which then enters the liver and returns via the hepatic vein into the inferior vena cava. If liver parenchymal lesions block the blood return pathway, can the blood from the portal vein still return to the heart?

By posing questions, combining textbooks and wall charts, organize students to discuss the following two issues: (1) Can alternative pathways achieve reflux? (2) What are the consequences if successful reflux cannot occur? Allow students to express their ideas, and the teacher summarizes their responses to provide a correct answer. This leads to an understanding of the three return channels when the portal vein is blocked, along with clinical manifestations such as portal vein obstruction, abdominal organ bleeding, and splenomegaly. Timely logical revelation of knowledge makes it easier to open students' minds, stimulating their inquiry and thinking about knowledge. It helps students develop skills in analyzing and solving problems, guiding them to transform knowledge into ability.

The purpose of teaching human anatomy combined with experiential methods is to lay a foundation for clinical application. Teaching should not merely focus on rote memorization of concepts but also teach students how to apply them. Besides allowing students to observe cadavers, specimens, and models through practical activities, each student is a living specimen. The surface projection and function of each organ can be found and experienced within their own bodies. For instance, important body surface landmarks require students to accurately locate these common signs within their own bodies and use these landmarks to determine the location and surface projection of organs. Examples include using the sternal angle to count ribs and locate the apex position, the costal arch to find the lower edge of the liver and gallbladder, and the anterior superior iliac spine to find the surface projection point of the appendix.

To help students grasp the functions of organs, experiential methods can be employed. For example, in studying skeletal muscles of the movement system, students are required to understand the function of certain muscles. During lectures, ask students to first locate a specific muscle in their own bodies, feel its tension during contraction and relaxation, and then perform different actions based on muscle function. For instance, while experiencing the deltoid muscle’s function, move the upper extremity outward and inward, touching the deltoid to feel changes in tension.

When studying the role of the same organ under sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous systems, allow students to touch their pulse strength and rate before and after exercise. Through analysis of the results of the students' experiences, they can understand that the vagus nerve weakens the heartbeat and slows it down during quiet states, leading to weaker and slower pulses; whereas the sympathetic nerve strengthens the heartbeat and accelerates it, thus strengthening and accelerating the pulse.

Combining theory with practice stimulates students' interest, consolidates learning content, and lays a solid foundation for subsequent lessons. Although there are established teaching methods, they are not fixed. Each colleague in human anatomy teaching has their own tricks up their sleeves. The five methods of human anatomy teaching mentioned here are just small experiences gained from teaching practice, akin to a drop in the ocean of human anatomy teaching methods. Any inappropriate aspects are open to feedback from colleagues.

Health science is a basic discipline of preventive medicine and clinical fields. Due to disciplinary limitations, health teaching has almost become a blank slate in clinical teaching. By the end of medical school, students' knowledge of health-related topics is very limited, with over 90% of students believing that health knowledge is insufficient.
