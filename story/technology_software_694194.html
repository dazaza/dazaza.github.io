Buffett reveals his stock pool, which A-shares are blessed by the stock god

by df1iaiw7 on 2012-03-06 22:58:57

On the evening of February 25, Warren Buffett, known as the "Oracle of Omaha," released his annual letter to shareholders on Berkshire Hathaway's website. The letter summarized the company's performance in 2011, its achievements and shortcomings. The entire 21-page document was personally written by Buffett. Each year, Buffett writes a letter to shareholders in Berkshire Hathaway's annual report, a practice that has continued since 1970 and is highly anticipated by global investors.

Buffett's investment portfolio shows significant increases in technology stocks such as IBM and Intel, as well as shares in Wells Fargo and preferred stock in Bank of America. He also reduced holdings in consumer stocks like Johnson & Johnson and Kraft Foods. However, domestic market analysts believe that Buffett's purchasing behavior in the U.S. stock market does not constitute a sufficient condition for buying stocks in the A-share market, and caution against participating in this concept-driven speculation.

Continuing to advocate for equity investments, Buffett began his letter with good news: in 2011, the company's per-share book value of both Class A and Class B stocks increased by 4.6%. Over the past 47 years, under the current management, the book value has grown from $19 per share to $99,860, with an annual compound growth rate of 19.8%.

The letter stated that last year's main business performances were excellent. The five largest non-insurance companies under Berkshire Hathaway—BNSF, Iscar, Lubrizol, Marmon, and MidAmerican Energy—all set new records in operational profits. In 2011, their combined pre-tax profit exceeded $9 billion. Seven years ago, Berkshire Hathaway only owned one of these five companies, MidAmerican Energy, which at the time had an annual pre-tax profit of $393 million. Unless the economy declines in 2012, Buffett expects these five outstanding companies to achieve new heights, with total profits easily surpassing $10 billion.

Buffett mentioned owning substantial stakes in four very excellent companies, including IBM (5.5%). He views these shares as partnership interests in great companies rather than listed securities bought or sold based on short-term prospects.

In addition to adhering to value investing, Buffett continues to emphasize the benefits of investing in equity assets, advocating that investing in stocks is far better than bonds, gold, and other products. This reflects Buffett's preference for buying opportunities during bear markets.

Buffett believes that volatility is not the best measure of risk; the purpose of investment is to ensure safe assets and achieve stable returns above inflation levels. Holding cash, government bonds, or bank deposits may seem safe but inevitably carries risks of inflation and currency depreciation. Currency depreciation is essentially a form of invisible taxation. Analysts suggest that stock investment is the only asset capable of achieving an annualized return higher than CPI, reflecting the essence of Buffett's advice.

However, Buffett's theories clearly cannot be directly applied to the A-share market. Cao Ling, General Manager of the Securities Investment Department at Anzhou Investment in Guangzhou, analyzed that from an environmental perspective, the A-share market is still immature, with significant fluctuations in economic and policy changes impacting the stock market. From the perspective of investor demographics, the U.S. market is dominated by institutional investors, while the domestic market is mainly composed of retail investors, leading to significant differences in operational methods. Additionally, disclosure norms in U.S. listed companies are more standardized, making it difficult for individual investors in China to conduct long-term value investments. Moreover, the volatility in the A-share market is much greater than in the U.S. market, making it hard for both retail investors and funds to endure. Dividend ratios of listed companies and other factors are all key elements affecting value investment, conditions that the A-share market still lacks. Therefore, learning from Buffett's long-term investment philosophy requires combining the realities of the A-share market, integrating value investment with trend investment.

Buffett also admitted to misjudging the timing of the U.S. real estate recovery. Several years ago, he spent about $2 billion purchasing various bonds issued by Electric Future Holdings, a power company serving parts of Texas. This proved to be a major mistake. Broadly speaking, the company's prospects were tied to natural gas prices, which fell shortly after the bond purchase and remained low. Despite receiving approximately $102 million in interest annually since the purchase, unless gas prices rise significantly, the company's ability to make payments will soon be depleted. Buffett noted that in 2010, he wrote down $1 billion on this investment, followed by another write-down of $390 million last year.

By the end of last year, Buffett valued these bonds at their market value of $878 million. If gas prices remain at current levels, further losses may occur. Conversely, if gas prices rebound significantly, some or all of the write-downs may be recovered. Regardless of the outcome, Buffett acknowledged miscalculating the probability of gains/losses when purchasing the bonds.

Another mistake pertained to real estate. Buffett admitted, "Last year, I told you that the real estate recovery would likely begin within the next year or so. I was way off. You should be confident that the real estate market will recover. Over time, the number of houses will match the number of households (considering the usual vacancy rate beforehand). However, in the years leading up to 2008, the U.S. built more houses than there were households. This ultimately led to us building too many houses, and the bursting of the bubble impacted the entire economy."

This conservative estimate by Buffett regarding the real estate recovery aligns with market sentiment. At around 2:00 AM Beijing time on February 25, St. Louis Federal Reserve President James Bullard stated last Friday that the recovery of the U.S. real estate market still requires time because U.S. homeowners are burdened with excessive debt.

It is well-known that Buffett has always been averse to technology stocks. However, last November, during an interview with CNBC, he surprisingly announced holding 5.51% of IBM's shares, causing a stir in the market. Since then, Buffett has opened a window into the technology sector. In the fourth quarter of last year, Buffett also purchased over 5.16 million additional shares of Intel, bringing his total holdings to over 11.49 million shares, worth approximately $3 billion.

Each move Buffett makes overseas stirs the A-share market. Last November, the news of Buffett becoming IBM's largest shareholder caused Yitong Information, which established a joint communication solutions center with IBM, to immediately hit the daily limit. It achieved two consecutive limits despite a weak market. The other two stocks known as the "Buffett Three Musketeers"—Egong Communication and Changcheng Computer—also performed admirably, demonstrating the strong market appeal of the "Buffett Concept."

On February 14, Berkshire Hathaway's disclosed investment portfolio showed that besides increasing holdings in IBM and Intel, it also added 46.6 million shares of Wells Fargo.

Data revealed that Buffett increased his holdings of Wells Fargo by over 22.33 million shares in the fourth quarter of last year, bringing his total holdings to 383 million shares, accounting for 7.3% of Wells Fargo's equity, valued at approximately $11.7 billion, making it his third-largest holding. Not only did Buffett increase his holdings of bank stocks in the secondary market, but he also acquired 6% preferred stock in Bank of America for $5 billion, along with warrants to purchase 700 million shares of Bank of America common stock at $7.14 per share before September 2, 2021.

In the regulatory filings submitted by Berkshire Hathaway, the company also disclosed new purchases of Liberty Media Corp and DaVita, a San Francisco-based kidney dialysis treatment equipment provider. Furthermore, it increased holdings in CVS Caremark, the largest pharmaceutical retailer in the U.S., satellite TV company DirecTV Group, data analytics company Verisk Analytics, and Visa.

Due to the lack of clear corresponding stocks, these moves by Buffett did not cause ripples in the A-share market, though A-share media stocks have remained active due to domestic policy support.

From the investment portfolio, it is evident that Buffett has clearly abandoned some stocks. Besides selling all his ExxonMobil shares, he also reduced holdings in some consumer stocks, such as reducing his stake in Johnson & Johnson to 29 million shares (from 37.4 million shares last year) and continuing to reduce his holdings in Kraft Foods to 87 million shares (previously holding 138.3 million shares two years ago).

Cao Ling believes that Buffett's purchasing behavior in the U.S. stock market does not constitute a sufficient condition for buying stocks in the A-share market. The previous rise in technology stocks and other concept stocks was based on low valuations and small increases. The "Buffett Concept" finds it difficult to drive the rise in bank stocks because domestic bank stocks already experienced a rebound. Therefore, investors are advised to rationally view stocks related to Buffett.

Link: Buffett has selected a successor. In his annual letter, Buffett stated that the board has chosen a successor to replace him as the next CEO but did not provide further details, mentioning only two backup candidates. Buffett also expressed confidence in the newly hired investment managers Todd Combs and Ted Weschler, who are capable of managing Berkshire Hathaway's investment business.

On December 11 last year, during an interview, Buffett announced selecting his eldest son Howard as his successor. Previously, David Sokol was considered a potential successor, but he resigned amid suspicions of insider trading.
