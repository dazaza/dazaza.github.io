Developing children's social skills

by lmxbbwudi23 on 2012-02-27 17:36:08

After babies develop a certain level of self-awareness, the development of their social skills begins. Social skills mainly include: communication skills, the ability to collaborate with peers to solve problems, and emotions such as caring for and sympathizing with others. At this point, the baby's living space expands beyond the family, and they start interacting with more people and engaging with a broader living environment. To help the baby step out of the family circle, parents can accompany the baby in playing some games at home that will help refine the baby’s social skills.

**Guess Who**

1. Mom and Dad sit with the baby and ask the baby to guess first, for example: Who is the person who gets up earliest every day at home? Who is the child in kindergarten who likes Teddy Bear the most?

2. The baby can also come up with questions for Mom and Dad to guess. The whole family can guess relatives, friends, the baby's playmates, kindergarten teachers, etc., and it can be expanded to household items and community facilities such as hospitals and police stations.

【Expert Tip】 Parents can often play this little game with children over 3 years old. This consciously helps the baby consolidate their understanding of family members and the living environment, gradually expanding the content of the game to prompt the baby to pay attention to surrounding playmates, others, and the environment, generating interest in interacting with people and objects around them.

**Three Legs Walking Together**

1. Mom and Dad demonstrate to the baby by standing in a row, tying two adjacent legs together, and then walking in coordination to fetch items from the house. For example, Mom and Dad can play a ball-throwing game with the baby, giving the baby enough time to observe how Mom and Dad cooperate.

2. Mom or Dad can encourage the baby to try walking with three legs, experiencing the feeling of cooperation through moving and walking together.

3. You can invite the baby's kindergarten friends to participate in the game and hold a walking competition, allowing the baby to experience the joy of cooperating with others.

**The Secret in the Box**

1. Mom, Dad, the baby, and other family members or playmates form a circle, with 4-5 people.

2. Everyone passes a small box to the next person in turn. Two rules must be followed: First, pass the box while music is playing, and only the person holding the box when the music stops can open it to see the gift; Second, when the music starts again, the opened box must be closed and continue to be passed.

3. After one round of passing, everyone who follows the rules will receive a small box, and the gifts inside the small boxes will encourage those who follow the rules.

【Expert Tip】 For a baby to cooperate well with others, they need not only to understand the meaning of cooperation but also know that the premise of cooperation is to jointly abide by established rules with others, which is precisely the purpose of this game.

**Caring for Small Pets**

1. Mom and Dad can get a small rabbit or bird for the baby aged 4-5 years, or take the baby to visit families with small pets.

2. In the process of observing small animals, encourage the baby to care about their eating, drinking, and excretion, and read fairy tale storybooks about small animals with the baby, stimulating the baby's love for small animals and imitating how Mom takes care of the baby to take care of small animals. Let the baby know that small animals should be treated kindly and not roughly. Praise the baby's gentle behavior.

【Expert Tip】 Babies around 3 years old have a very strong concept of "self-centeredness," which is a normal stage in child psychological development. Through paying attention to small animals, they can experience "caring" and "sympathy," which aligns with the baby's interests and is an important way to achieve emotional socialization.

**Principles for Parents Guiding Babies in Resolving Conflicts with Peers**

Most 3-4-year-old babies resolve conflicts with their peers in no more than two ways: either retreating to avoid or resorting to violence. Parents need to help babies realize that there are multiple ways to resolve conflicts so they can learn to get along amicably and cooperate with each other.

(1) Help the baby understand the various rules that limit behavior and the reasons for establishing these rules.

(2) Help the baby recognize the cause of the conflict and imagine alternative behaviors and their outcomes. For example, when the baby wants to play with a peer's toy, instead of using violence to snatch it, they can negotiate with the peer to exchange toys or discuss the time to play with the toy.

(3) Guide the baby to understand the meaning of cooperation. 3-4-year-old babies need direct observation of two or more people working together to solve problems or complete tasks, giving them opportunities to imitate.

(4) Encourage the baby to help others. For example, help the neighbor uncles and aunts carry newspapers, and encourage the baby to press the elevator floor button for others when taking the elevator.

(5) When the baby has a conflict with a peer, let the baby try to resolve it themselves as much as possible.

(6) In daily life, parents are role models for the baby's interpersonal interactions. To better play with peers, parents can teach the baby some kind and interesting gestures and frequently smile at the baby. A baby with a sense of humor and a sunny disposition will have good interpersonal relationships.
