Private capital in Zhejiang掀起another PE craze

by zjsr2010 on 2010-07-01 10:27:57

As investment in resource sectors such as coal is restricted, and the real estate and stock markets continue to be sluggish, Zhejiang's vast amount of private capital is urgently seeking new outlets, sparking a new round of PE fever.

The local government is taking advantage of this trend and wants to channel these funds into "beneficial to the economy" investment areas.

On June 26th, Wenzhou established a private capital investment service center that provides services for the connection between private capital and project operations. The local government aims to guide private capital to rationally invest in the transformation projects of traditional industries, as well as sunrise industries such as electronics information and biomedicine.

Prior to this, Wenzhou also established an equity operation center, meaning that shares of a large number of unlisted companies can now undergo property rights transactions, providing a new investment venue for the abundant local private capital.

But will investors buy into the government's well-intentioned efforts?

Forsaking Real Estate for PE

In mid-May, Wenzhou held a "Private Capital Development Summit," where over 1,000 seats were filled to capacity, with even the aisles packed with people.

"When everyone meets, they ask if there are any investment opportunities," an investor named Zhang Yumin, who attended the forum, told the First Financial Daily reporter.

Zhang Yumin told the reporter that after the new real estate policies, in the foreseeable future, replicating last year’s rapid price increase momentum is no longer realistic, and the doors to investment sectors like coal mines are gradually closing.

Since November 2009, Zhang Yumin has sold off at least 30 properties, recovering at least 40 million yuan in funds, preparing to enter the equity investment field.

As a veteran member of Wenzhou's real estate speculation group, since 1998, Zhang Yumin has acquired properties in Hangzhou, Shanghai, Wuxi, and Wenzhou, including residential and commercial properties, and is even the second-largest shareholder in a real estate company in Xinjiang. "This money isn't all mine; at least six or seven other people are following me. Relatives and friends trust me, so they give their money to me," he told the reporter.

Zhang Yumin believes: "The era of super profits in the real estate market has ended. From 1999 to 2009, during these 10 years, housing prices in most domestic cities have risen by 5 to 8 times, and it will be hard to replicate this growth rate from 2009 to 2019."

Because of this, he started laying out plans for equity investments in 2007. "This industry may seem profound, but the principles are quite simple, it's just about making profits from equity transactions." Zhang Yumin believes that in mature capital markets like the United States, the asset price gap between the primary and secondary markets is not significant, but in China, the price difference between the primary and secondary markets can reach 2 to 4 times. Therefore, even if the growth potential of the invested company is average, one can still make money through an IPO exit.

In fact, in Wenzhou, it is already very evident that many individuals like Zhang Yumin are redirecting their recovered funds towards the VC and PE sectors. Relevant data shows that currently, no less than 20 billion yuan of Wenzhou's funds have been invested in various forms in the equity investment field.

Still Prefer Group Investment

The rich private capital in Wenzhou makes many PE and VC firms envious. But Zhejiang Business Venture Capital President Hua Yeayu told reporters that many eventually find out, "It's hard to raise funds domestically, and even harder to raise funds in Wenzhou." These institutional leaders come to Wenzhou for speeches or project promotions, but mostly end up doing "teaching, guiding, and solving doubts" work.

In April this year, Zhejiang Business Venture Capital initiated China's first private equity fund focused on low-carbon concepts - the Noah Low-Carbon Fund - which promoted projects in Wenzhou, raising a total of 220 million yuan. Although this exceeded the original demand, investments from Wenzhou only accounted for about 10%.

"Wenzhou businessmen prefer group investments, and introductions and demonstrations from locals make it easier to attract their investment interests. To transfer tens of millions or even hundreds of millions of yuan to an unfamiliar corporate account, no matter how famous the investment institution is, the vast majority of Wenzhou people dare not do so," Hua Yeayu told reporters. They like to ask, "What if the project is fake, or if he's a scammer who runs away with the money?"

Their concerns are not unfounded. On May 12th, Huang Hao, former executive partner of Dehou Capital, was prosecuted in Shanghai for suspected fundraising fraud, a case known as "China's First PE Case," causing quite a stir in the industry.

Zhang Yumin and a few friends engaged in equity investments are still operating within the old model. He told reporters: "Currently, I am at least a shareholder in three planned listed companies, with a current value of approximately 80 million yuan, but my shares are held by others, and at least half of my funds come from relatives and friends who have entrusted them to me. I cannot say whether there are legal loopholes in this model, but I know that many people in Wenzhou operate this way."

In fact, not only individual "casual" investors like Zhang Yumin, but also some famous private enterprises in Zhejiang are transitioning into this field.

President of the Wenzhou Small and Medium Enterprises Promotion Association, Zhou Dewen, stated that there are more than 100 local venture capital or investment companies of various types in Wenzhou. During interviews, it was discovered that the original shareholders of Zhejiang venture capital include companies like Xilinmen Group, Chuanhua Group, and Binjiang Group. Traditional large enterprises in Zhejiang such as Wanxiang Group, Hengdian Group, and Chuanhua Group have all set up investment companies.

Too Much Money, Too Few Projects

Good projects are hard to find, a common situation for many newcomers in the PE field. In a market supply-demand relationship characterized by "relative surplus of funds," it is normal for several institutions to compete for a single project.

"Currently in China, it's not that there's no money, but rather a lack of investment products. If there were good investment products, housing prices wouldn't be so high because there would be other places to make money," said Cai Hua, Vice President and Secretary-General of the Zhejiang Business Capital Investment Promotion Association.

Hua Yeayu admitted that there is too much capital wanting to enter the private equity fund sector, but there are only a limited number of projects with investment potential, inevitably leading to competition among institutions for projects, which might result in joint investments.

To increase appeal, Zhejiang Business Venture Capital gave its sixth fund the label of "low-carbon." Hua Yeayu explained that whether from the perspective of energy crisis or environmental crisis, "low-carbon" has strong development needs and potential, making it a big treasure trove worth deep exploration.

After half a year of extensive collection, investigation, and argumentation, the company has locked onto nearly 20 low-carbon industry projects. "We've selected a smart grid project, with an investment of tens of millions of yuan, and it will soon pass the board meeting," Hua Yeayu told reporters.

The Wenzhou Private Capital Investment Service Center currently has more than 200 projects included in the project database, with another 454 projects awaiting review, all excluding real estate. Currently, the center has registered and verified 55 capital investors and institutions, with operable capital reaching billions of yuan.

How Far Can Government Pushes Go?

It is not hard to see that an important driving force behind this round of Zhejiang's PE fever is the local government.

As early as February this year, the Wenzhou municipal finance provided 6 million yuan in support fees, co-founded with the China Risk Investment Research Institute and Zhejiang Industrial and Trade Vocational and Technical College to establish the Wenzhou Risk Investment Research Institute. Its main task is to propose policy suggestions in the field of equity investment in Wenzhou and attract dozens of well-known domestic risk investment institutions to settle in Wenzhou.

Ningbo took the lead in 2008 by issuing "Several Opinions on Encouraging the Development of Equity Investment Enterprises," clearly stipulating the specific management methods for equity investment enterprises and equity investment management enterprises established in the form of limited partnerships.

Hangzhou also proposed the goal of building a "low-carbon city" that year and established a start-up investment service center with the aim of "bridging connections, helping projects find capital, and helping capital find projects." Fund managers also have motivation to cooperate with local governments, leveraging local government power to seek financing from local enterprises and introduce investment projects.

However, Hua Yeayu believed that although the government recommended many projects, not many were truly successful. There may appear to be many projects, but those that meet investment requirements are few. Especially for entrepreneurial projects by college students, core competitiveness is not obvious, scale is relatively small, and success rates are lower.

A local government official told reporters that the true function of venture capital is to help entrepreneurs start businesses and thereby profit. Investment institutions should pay more attention to early to mid-stage projects; otherwise, their true functions cannot be realized.
