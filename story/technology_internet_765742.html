Never be afraid of losing your phone? Try out the Android Device Manager.

by anonymous on 2013-08-20 14:51:33

Google recently released a brand-new Android Device Manager, which allows users to locate their phones and remotely erase data on the device. Although these features have been available for some time, it seemed that they were not usable in mainland China when they were first released. Whether this was due to the author's RP issue or a common occurrence is unclear. However, after another test today, it was suddenly discovered that it could be used normally without any modifications.

To use this feature, your Android phone must have Google Play Services installed (if not, please flash manually). If your Android device meets the requirements, you will find a dark green "Google Settings" icon. After entering, find "Android Device Manager," click to enter the next level of options, and select according to your needs.

If you check the option for remote factory reset, you will see a notification in the pull-down notification bar saying "Activate Device Manager." Click and activate it.

After everything is set up, go to the following URL: https://www.google.com/android/devicemanager. With good luck, you can connect to your device at once. If not, try refreshing multiple times to connect. The author personally tested and found it usable.

As shown in the image above, the current location of the phone can indeed be located on the web interface. However, the accuracy of the positioning is not ideal, differing by an entire street. It can be said to be usable but not perfect.

In addition to the positioning function, have you noticed the ring button in the image above? When you click this button, your Android device will ring at maximum volume for five minutes. To its right is the "Erase Device" option, which allows remote locking or erasure of the device.

The original design intention of this feature by Google is naturally good, but after testing, it seems that its usefulness is not as great as imagined.

The inaccurate positioning alone creates many difficulties in finding a lost phone. Moreover, the premise for the positioning function to work is that the phone must be turned on and connected to the internet. Assuming no one else picks it up (ideal situation), you need sufficient battery power to determine the position of your phone based on the ringing sound.

Losing a phone happens either by carelessness or because it was stolen by a thief. A normal-thinking thief would likely find a place where no one is paying attention, turn off the phone, remove the battery, or take out the SIM card, then sell it to second-hand phone dealers. Trying to locate it... forget about it. Remote factory reset... also impossible.

This is not a new feature developed by Google. Apple previously had a service called "Find My iPhone," and many security software programs offer similar functions, even taking a picture of the thief while they operate the phone and sending it to your designated email. But when you take the picture of the thief to the police, do you think there is a high possibility of filing a case?

To recover a lost phone, either the phone you use is so bad that the thief returns it to you; or you have an unexpected stroke of luck; or you encounter a well-meaning person who returns lost items.
