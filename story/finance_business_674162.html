On February 21, the National Development and Reform Commission convened a meeting with the participation of 45 departments to arrange and implement the detailed rules for "Encouraging and Guiding the Development of Private Investment" to be issued in the first half of this year. Two points are noteworthy: one is that all relevant departments are required to formulate targeted, operable implementation details; the second is to develop work plans and reverse schedule timelines according to the completion deadlines requested by the State Council, ensuring timely task completion. The implementation details issued by relevant departments must be promptly announced to the public. This move is equivalent to making each department sign a military order, similar to real estate regulation. On the same day, the Supreme People's Court announced the "Guiding Opinions of the People's Courts on Providing Judicial Safeguards for Preventing and Resolving Financial Risks and Promoting Financial Reforms," providing five measures to ensure the coordinated development of financial markets, the first being ensuring the standard and healthy development of the credit market. Behind these opinions lies the effort to establish a civilian market order. Bypassing private lending to develop China's market economy is no longer possible. The current dilemma of tight investment funds and lack of investment channels for private capital shows extremely low social capital utilization efficiency. At this time, liberalizing private finance is not charity for private finance but a necessity for the development of China's market economy. According to estimates by CITIC Securities in October 2011, the balance of China's private lending has reached 3.8 trillion yuan, accounting for about 33% of China's shadow banking loan total (CITIC estimate), equivalent to 7% of total bank loans. This is a relatively conservative estimate, as most small and medium-sized enterprises in Wenzhou and other places have participated in private lending. However, with the rupture of industrial capital chains, the private lending credit system is nearing collapse. To reform and standardize the private lending market and channel private capital into various industries, the most important thing is to demonstrate reform determination to all sectors of the market. Technical means must be based on market concepts, supplemented by management and interest balancing mechanisms, to take root and flourish. Simply wanting to introduce private capital without allowing it to participate in management, losing dual supervision of state-owned and private assets, is not a wise strategy. Introducing private capital into monopolistic industries is an opportunity to establish a market-oriented management mechanism. Private capital often fails when it "accidentally" enters monopolistic industries. The reason is simple: having equity but no management or profit-sharing rights, easily getting into trouble, it’s better to stay away. Due to disputes over interests, veto rights, etc., private capital gradually withdrew from the Quchang Railway, while the Luoqin Railway raised questions about its complex financing channels. Private capital invested in railways cannot obtain railway rule-making rights or dispatching rights, but establishing a board of directors responsible system within the project company and holding shareholders accountable can achieve two goals: preventing the tragedy of private capital building railways in the late Qing Dynasty where funds flowed to the Shanghai stock market, and more importantly, preventing the new tragedy where state-owned capital uses private capital and then "burns the bridge." In the financial sector, there are currently micro-lending companies and guarantee companies, where private forces appear very strong on the surface but are actually quite weak. Once micro-lending companies grow large and want to become village banks, control shifts to the hands of major banks. Without growing large, they lack deposit-taking rights, so micro-lending companies advancing into areas like usury becomes understandable. Taking away private capital's control is equivalent to depriving it of property rights, leaving private capital to survive grayly in the cracks. This time, the NDRC and other departments implementing the thirty-six measures for non-public economies is essentially requiring each department to perform surgery to eliminate monopolistic interests, which is no easy task. If progress can be made, such as whether partial privatization of crude oil import rights in the petrochemical sector can be piloted to observe if it promotes China's energy security, it could open a door to a market-oriented future.
