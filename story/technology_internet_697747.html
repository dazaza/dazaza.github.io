(China Net Story at the Two Sessions) The "Safety" Expectation from 50 Million Weibo Posts

— Three "Safety Locks" in Dialogue Between Netizens and Delegates

Xinhua News Agency, Beijing, March 4th (reporters Zhu Wei, Zhou Ning, Qin Yazhou, and Zhou Rui of "China Web Stories") — "Avoid taking EMUs or high-speed trains on rainy days," "Don't leave home without a safety helmet," "Passwords as cryptic as hieroglyphs can still be stolen; let's all become 'hackers' then..." A rough estimate shows that by March 1st, Weibo posts reflecting on safety issues had reached nearly 50 million.

In response to this, reporters from "China Web Stories" have identified three highly concerning issues among netizens: public safety, environmental safety, and cybersecurity. They discussed with delegates and committee members attending the National People's Congress and the Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference how to create these "locks."

(Safety Lock One) Accountability Measures to Ensure Public Safety

From the Beijing subway escalator accident to the collapse of residential buildings in Harbin; from the burning of buses on the Jingzhu Expressway to the EMU train collision... In 2011, numerous major public safety accidents made many netizens more vigilant about safety.

[Weibo Excerpt] Netizen "Observer" said: "We cannot only think about the lagging safety standards after an accident has occurred. Can China's safety technology standards also take first place while it pursues being number one in quantity?"

[Suggestions] "These are tragedies that should not have happened." Said national people's congress representative Wang Xia. Once a public safety accident occurs, it easily causes public psychological panic. Relevant departments must quickly fill safety supervision loopholes and bravely take responsibility to answer public doubts. Moreover, the accountability "sword" should be drawn.

National People's Congress representative Tu Jianhua said that China is currently in a stage of rapid urbanization, facing severe situations in urban public safety such as weak awareness education, lack of mainstream information leading to rumors, emphasis on emergency response over prevention, and scattered management. Let public facilities construction, especially engineering quality and safety management, keep up with the fast pace of urbanization. Establishing and perfecting an urban public safety system is a crucial task that needs immediate attention.

(Safety Lock Two) Improving Legal Systems to Solve Environmental Safety Problems

Recently, tap water provided by a water plant in Wuhan developed an unpleasant smell. Due to concerns over drinking water safety, many citizens rushed to supermarkets to stock up on mineral water. This was the third water pollution incident this year following the Longjiang River pollution in Guangxi and the Zhenjiang water pollution in Jiangsu.

[Netizen Comments] Netizen "Jie Mai Qingqing" said, "The aftermath of the Bohai oil spill hasn't subsided yet, and now there's cadmium pollution. These incidents should force us to find solutions to break the environmental protection deadlock!" Netizen "Zi Ning" said, "Blind worship of GDP has led to reckless pollution. When will ordinary people no longer live in fear for their environment? Blue skies and white clouds shouldn't just be childhood memories."

[Representative Suggestions] National People's Congress representative and member of the Central Committee of the Democratic League Chen Wanzhi said that environmental pollution accidents reveal the lag in China's environmental protection laws and highlight the serious mismatch between corporate interests and responsibilities. This encourages companies to blindly pursue profits while neglecting environmental protection. For example, many oil, mining, and pharmaceutical companies in high-pollution industries simply pay fines of tens of thousands of yuan for discharging pollutants directly into rivers, lakes, and seas instead of spending millions on environmental protection. China's environmental protection laws inadequately compensate for private environmental damages and rarely address public environmental damages.

"At present, China's environmental safety regulations are relatively lagging behind. We must formulate targeted and suitable environmental safety regulations according to our national conditions." Chen Wanzhi suggested that environmental safety should be included in the national security strategy system, establishing a decision-making mechanism and organizational coordination system for national environmental safety, and achieving routine management of environmental pollution emergency institutions.

(Safety Lock Three) Scientific Crackdown to Ensure Cybersecurity

"One day you wake up and check your account, only to find a million yuan deposit gone," "Online banking transactions lead to account passwords being stolen," "CSDN 'password leak,' six million netizens lose their passwords"... According to a report by the China Internet Network Information Center, in just the first half of 2011, 217 million internet users experienced virus or Trojan attacks, accounting for 44.7% of all internet users. 121 million users reported having their accounts or passwords stolen. Additionally, 8% of internet users encountered online consumer fraud within the last six months. Each cybersecurity incident frequently raises red flags on the web.

[Weibo Excerpt] Netizen "Gegaitou23" said, "What exactly is happening with internet security? Many netizens habitually use the same password across different websites, so if one password is stolen, all accounts are compromised." Netizens like "Shiwo Shiling" questioned, "Even network operators themselves brazenly believe that 'cybersecurity is the netizens' own problem.' How can we expect them to invest manpower, material resources, and finances into cybersecurity technology research?"

[Suggestions] National Political Consultative Conference member Liu Minghua said that although relevant departments continuously strengthen internet security regulation and legislation, netizens' complex passwords and accounts are still easily stolen, making cybersecurity a long and arduous journey. "I haven't seen any website include 'information leakage' incidents within their scope of responsibility. Even if netizens sue and hold them accountable for infringement, obtaining evidence is quite difficult." Relevant departments should join forces to increase the crackdown on profit-driven "hackers," using high technology to counteract "high technology," ensuring that they stay one step ahead.

"The legal construction targeting cybersecurity is urgently needed," said National People's Congress representative Wang Xia. More than 50 countries and regions worldwide have already enacted personal information protection laws and regulations, but China's related laws are overly broad, lack operability, and are rather scattered. Formulating a law to protect online personal information has become an urgent necessity.
