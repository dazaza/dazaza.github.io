Samsung Convenes Rare Analyst Meeting to Rebuild Investor Confidence

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 19:48:33

Samsung Electronics has always been thorough in its confidentiality work. This tight defensive strategy makes it difficult for Samsung's competitors to guess its next move, and also helps this South Korean tech giant maintain a leading position in the smartphone sector.

Share Price Decline

However, this summer, due to unstable company strategic deployment and questions about how Samsung will use its $50 billion cash reserve, investors suddenly lost confidence in Samsung, dealing a heavy blow to the company. From early June to mid-July, Samsung's share price fell by 20%.

Although Samsung's stock prices have rebounded since then, the company is still striving to reduce investor doubts about itself. Last Wednesday, Samsung invited 350 analysts and institutional investors to Seoul to attend a rare special meeting with senior management. The meeting was rare because it was the first "analyst meeting" held by Samsung since 2005 and only the second of its kind in Samsung Electronics' history.

Analyst Byun Han-joon from KB Investment & Securities in Seoul said, "Previously, they thought that just explaining increasing performance was enough. Now, they realize that they need to maintain better relations with investors."

Senior executives, including Samsung Electronics CEO Kwon Oh-Hyun, will attend the meeting to deliver speeches, disclose the company's financial status, and introduce their outlook on mobile phone business, memory chip business, display business, and other product businesses.

Samsung stated in its announcement, "Through this opportunity, investors will be able to understand Samsung better and discuss its latest strategies and outlook for 2020."

In fact, most investors are most concerned about the future of Samsung Electronics' smartphone business, as nearly two-thirds of Samsung Electronics' revenue comes from this business. This summer, the reason for the sell-off of Samsung shares was that investors believed smartphones would become 'commoditized.' Due to diminishing design and technological differences between various brands, companies like Samsung and Apple might lose their pricing advantage in high-end phones. Not only Samsung, but Apple also faces similar problems.

After the summer, Samsung reassured shareholders with two quarters of strong performance, causing its share price to rebound and now almost reaching its highest point.

Increasing Proportion of Memory Chip Business

Although the profit margin of the smartphone business remains high, Samsung's good performance can also be attributed to the recent rise in memory chip prices.

Samsung is the world's largest memory chip manufacturer, accounting for one-third of global production. Additionally, Samsung produces displays and other key smartphone components. This distinguishes Samsung from Apple, which outsources smartphone production, purchases components externally, and assembles them. Other phone manufacturers such as BlackBerry, Nokia, and HTC are similar to Apple.

Analyst Sundeep Bajikar from Jefferies believes, "There are differences in the Samsung model and how Samsung maintains profitability after other manufacturers faced setbacks."

Many analysts believe that Samsung will not face a 'collapse.' Due to the increasing proportion of semiconductor business in Samsung's profits, the company will continue to achieve high profits. Now, investors want to know how Samsung will use its cash reserves.

Large Cash Reserves

Analyst Mark Newman from Sanford C. Bernstein in Hong Kong estimates that by next year, Samsung will hold $77 billion in cash, reaching $160 billion by 2017. Apple currently holds $147 billion in cash reserves.

Although Samsung holds large cash reserves, investors haven't seen much benefit. Newman wrote in an investor report that the ratio of total dividends paid by Samsung to the company's net profit has been continuously declining, dropping from 50% in 2004 to 5% in 2012.

Newman believes this is one of the reasons for the decline in Samsung Electronics' share price. Moreover, in terms of stock price-to-earnings ratio, Samsung lags far behind Apple, despite having higher revenue than Apple, its market value is less than half of Apple's. According to Newman's investigation, Samsung's investors hope the company will reward them through several possible methods, such as increasing dividends, conducting one-time stock buybacks, or multiple stock buybacks. Newman believes that such measures could significantly boost Samsung's stock price.

However, some people believe that Samsung should retain cash reserves for investment in semiconductors and other potential growth areas. Bajikar said, "I think Samsung should use its cash to block competition."

Listing Location

Some analysts believe another reason for Samsung's falling stock price is that the company is listed in Korea, where Samsung Electronics accounts for nearly 20% of the KOSPI main board market. Unlike other large international technology companies such as Sony, Panasonic, and LG Display, Samsung does not trade in the U.S. via American Depositary Receipts.

Listing in the U.S. would make it more convenient for individual investors, such as Galaxy phone users, to purchase Samsung's stock. However, it would also bring new regulatory and disclosure requirements. If Samsung were to list in the U.S., it might face stricter scrutiny from activist investors like Carl C. Icahn.

Analysts said they do not expect Samsung to make major announcements at this analyst meeting. Samsung executives repeatedly emphasized the company's long-term strategy in interviews. They stated that the company does not focus on short-term fluctuations in stock prices. However, Samsung admitted that confidentiality clauses also have limitations. In its statement, the company said, "Samsung will continue to explore various methods to provide shareholders with more information."
