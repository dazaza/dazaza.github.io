IBM accuses Twitter of infringing at least three of its patents

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 19:59:54

Twitter filed an updated IPO prospectus with the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) on Monday, significantly raising its initial public offering price range from $17 to $20 per share to $23 to $25 per share, reflecting the optimistic sentiment of market analysts towards this social media company.

However, for Twitter, which is set to debut on the New York Stock Exchange this week, the news in the document is not all positive. The filing revealed that the company has received a legal letter from IBM, accusing Twitter of infringing on "at least three U.S. patents" held by IBM, and has "invited us to engage in commercial negotiations to resolve these allegations." Despite this accusation from IBM, the significant increase in the IPO price range indicates that Twitter remains confident about its IPO.

Industry insiders pointed out that the updated IPO filing suggests that although IBM is accusing Twitter of patent infringement and seeking a settlement, it appears that Twitter is prepared to defend itself.

The filing states: "We believe that while we cannot guarantee our ability to successfully dismiss these claims or reach a commercially satisfactory resolution, the defense measures we have taken against IBM's allegations are commendable."

IBM accused Twitter of infringing on three patents: U.S. Patent No. 6957224, concerning efficient retrieval of uniform resource locators; U.S. Patent No. 7072849, regarding methods of displaying advertisements on interactive services; and U.S. Patent No. 7099862, involving programmatic discovery of mutual contacts.

The footnote provided in Twitter’s updated filing sends a warning signal that patents, copyrights, trademarks, and trade secrets frequently become subjects of legal disputes. Twitter admitted that the company is not well-versed in matters related to this field.

The filing states: "In this industry, many companies possess much larger patent and intellectual property portfolios than ours, and many of these companies are our competitors. This could make us a target for litigation, and we may not be able to counter-sue those who bring patent or other intellectual property infringement claims against us. Additionally, 'non-practicing entities' that own patents and other intellectual property often attempt to initiate claims in order to extract value from technology companies."

Industry insiders pointed out that despite Twitter's efforts to become a "developer-friendly" company in the patent domain, accusations from companies like IBM will test Twitter's commitment in this area. Especially as Twitter continues to expand its services into new areas, resistance from these companies will likely increase.

Twitter also mentioned this point in the filing and added that its partners and customers might also face charges as a result.

The filing points out: "We may occasionally launch new products and services in areas where we currently do not operate, increasing the likelihood of being sued for patent and other intellectual property infringements by competitors and non-practicing entities. Furthermore, while standard contract terms for Promoted Products and the public API do not provide indemnification to advertisers or platform partners regarding intellectual property claims, under certain agreements with advertisers, platform partners, and data partners, we are required to provide indemnification for specific intellectual property claims. This could lead to substantial costs in defending against such claims and potentially require large payouts if rulings are unfavorable."
