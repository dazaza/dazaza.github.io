Be wary of the deeper psychology behind the "salt rush"

by tyc9999n6aa on 2011-03-19 14:08:21

In response to the "salt rush" that spread throughout the country a few days ago, most comments criticized the public for lacking in popular science knowledge and rationality. Some even mocked with the couplet: "Japan is truly a great nuclear nation, while China is indeed the offspring of salt shortages." This couplet exposes the netizens' sharpness and preference for satire. It actually laments Japan's orderliness in the face of disaster, as opposed to China's chaos when faced with rumors. In my humble opinion, the editorial view of Southern Metropolis Daily seems no different from the previous discourse.

The lack of popular science knowledge and rationality among ordinary people can indeed explain the "salt rush." Media criticism is based on the premise that "salt has no effect in preventing radiation," but after the disaster, did public media ever tell us what could effectively prevent radiation? Did experts explain in layman's terms why we wouldn't be affected by nuclear radiation? After the explosion in Japan, the experts did appear. For instance, TV commentators first said that Japan's nuclear facilities were safe and would not explode; then they said that even if there was an explosion, it wouldn't have much impact on the surrounding area in terms of radiation. These statements were made with such firmness and rationality, but later facts proved them to be wishful thinking. Perhaps experts themselves may not fully understand uncertain phenomena, but when their confident assertions turn out to be contrary to the facts, how are ordinary people supposed to remain rational?

Perhaps behind the "salt rush," there were unscrupulous merchants fanning the flames, but to completely attribute the blame to them is an even more irresponsible act. In fact, this is not the first time there has been such a rush. During SARS, we hoarded vinegar; during the quack doctor craze, we hoarded mung beans; now, we're hoarding salt. The lack of credibility in authoritative information sources and public media is a significant reason. Ordinary people do not distrust the news because they lack rationality and common sense.

Faced with rumors, the public adheres to the mentality of "better safe than sorry," and many public incidents have proven those who naively thought otherwise wrong. With the same psychology, when the public uses it to question administrative actions, it is widely praised by the media; but when they use it to protect themselves, they are harshly criticized by the media. The double standards of the media contribute to the mistrust of the media itself, which is also a tragedy for a great nation. □ Tian Jia
