Android Development Experience Sharing

by ybcz0519 on 2010-09-22 16:21:42

From the release of the G1 up to now, at least 30 Android smartphones have appeared on the market. Why do some users still complain that Android is difficult to use? What truly matters in related development, and what is the core of development? Why do mobile applications need to pay special attention to user experience? This article will attempt to answer these questions as thoroughly as possible.

The original meaning of "Android" refers to "robot," and it is also the name of the open-source mobile operating system based on Linux that Google announced on November 5, 2007. This platform consists of an operating system, middleware, user interface, and applications, making it the first truly open and complete mobile platform specifically designed for mobile terminals. On September 22, 2008, American carrier T-Mobile USA officially released the first Google phone, the T-Mobile G1, marking the true arrival of the Android era.

From Android 1.0 to the present, there have been multiple version updates, with significant changes occurring in versions 1.5, 2.0, and 2.2. Other updates were relatively less important. Additionally, since each update modifies underlying modules including Dalvik and involves SDKs, this has necessitated changes in some programs according to the Android version. For more conservative developers, rapid version updates increasingly impose limitations. In such a situation, the significance of Android being open-source diminishes somewhat.

Nevertheless, due to Android's tight integration with Google services, this operating system enjoys unique advantages. With strong support from Google, many things become much simpler on Android. It’s also worth noting that Android is an operating system based on the Internet. When connected to the Internet, an Android phone can perform more functions than other phones. However, without network access, Android phones are not particularly outstanding, especially in terms of entertainment compared to the iPhone, where they lag significantly behind.

As developers, after learning and deeply understanding Android, one should leverage its strengths in their own software while addressing its deficiencies or shortcomings through various means. Let's take a look at the characteristics of Android:

- Very convenient interaction with hardware, including cameras, GPS, etc., which can be easily operated.

- Possesses its own runtime and virtual machine with excellent memory management capabilities.

- Provides rich interface controls for developers to use, allowing visual development and ensuring consistency in application interfaces under the Android platform.

- Offers lightweight inter-process communication mechanisms.

- Supports background service applications without a user interface.

- Supports efficient and fast data access methods.

With these features, developing an application on Android is not overly difficult. In fact, any developer with some Java experience can quickly get started with Android development. The core of development has always revolved around several major features of Android phones, including touchscreens, cameras, GPS modules, internet functionality, voice input, and Google accounts. It’s worth mentioning that if a J2ME engineer wants to switch to Android development, the cost will be higher compared to J2SE or J2EE engineers. After all, Android supports a nearly complete subset of J2SE, making J2ME seem far weaker by comparison.

Besides Java, many languages support Android development, with Scala being one of the more well-known ones. As the underlying language of Android itself, the role of C/C++ cannot be ignored either. Currently, within the open-source community, there are already some experts attempting to enable more languages to develop Android applications. A representative example might be Koushik Dutta, who has solved the problem of Dalvik calling Mono code under the Mono platform. Perhaps in the near future, all languages under .NET may run on Android via Mono, which is something worth looking forward to.

Language is no longer an issue, so what else could be a problem? Many people would say "experience." Indeed, experience determines whether a developer can quickly and smoothly complete development work, as well as the robustness of the software, the number, level, and rework required to fix bugs. However, I believe these are not important. Even a newcomer to the Android industry can finish a project by referring to documentation while developing, albeit with low efficiency. There is almost no technical bottleneck in Android development. So where is the bottleneck? In fact, after using many pieces of software, one realizes that many of them are not user-friendly. Many users refuse to use certain software not because it lacks technical depth or fails to meet needs, but simply because it is not easy to use.

User experience transcends technology. Excellent user experience can achieve twice the result with half the effort. Among a group of similar software, the one with the most downloads is undoubtedly the one that feels most comfortable to use, even if its features are not better than others, or even slightly worse. I've seen many developers who view technology as their primary responsibility, focusing solely on technical research, believing that technically superior software will receive good reviews, or adding various dazzling 3D effects in a mobile game. While these are all fine, actual users may not like them. In mobile applications, simplicity and clarity are what users hope to see. Imagine a user playing an RPG game on their phone, filled with dazzling 3D effects. They will find it impossible to proceed with the next action. Although stunning graphics are indeed attractive, they can directly lead both users and developers into a dead-end abyss, making it impossible to return. Ultimately, users will completely abandon the game, and developers or operators will earn nothing.

In the process of mobile platform development, user experience has become the top priority. Only by focusing on user design is there a possibility of being accepted by users. Let's look at some typical examples.

The image above shows the classic Windows Mobile 6.1 interface, which has been promoted as content-rich, containing entrances to all commonly used functions, and very much aligned with actual user needs since the launch of Windows Mobile. Perhaps at the time, such an interface could meet certain demands, but now, this design can only be described as far from user-friendly. Each item is too small, requiring the use of a stylus or fingernail to click. The three icons in the lower-right corner may be hard to reach even with fingernails, and using a stylus occupies one hand, drastically reducing the user experience. In an era where users want to free even their ears, occupying one hand is a big deal. This means that when commuting, users won't be able to hold onto handrails or carry their bags. Moreover, having a free hand during phone operation allows for more opportunities to handle emergencies. Occupying both hands is simply unacceptable. It can be said that the user experience of Windows Mobile is very poor. Fortunately, Microsoft made significant improvements to the interface in the new Windows Phone 7, avoiding past mistakes.

Now let's see how Android handles it. This interface looks simple and clear. Compared to the Windows Mobile shown above, it might seem unremarkable, and even ugly to some. But it is actually quite user-friendly. The icons are large, and the spacing between them is also large. This means users can use the pads of their fingers to click, with a larger range, reducing the chances of clicking the wrong icon.

Although screen-clicking methods are somewhat related to screen materials (resistive screens require a stylus or fingernail, while capacitive screens allow finger pads and some support multi-touch), for ordinary users, using the finger pad is more common. The reason is simple: if the icon is large, users will instinctively use their finger pads to click; if the icon is small, they will bend their fingers and use their nails to tap the screen. The action of "bending fingers" cannot be accepted by most users. Therefore, capacitive screens will gradually become popular, while resistive screens will gradually fade away. This is entirely based on user experience, a natural selection process that perfectly aligns with evolutionary theory.

User experience is not just about the interface. For a phone, every feature can be part of the user experience that deserves exploration, such as whether it has a keyboard or supports multi-touch. Phones with keyboards and those without will naturally involve different gestures when held. One focuses on the lower part of the device, i.e., the keyboard, while the other focuses on the entire screen, meaning fingers may move anywhere on the screen. Designing applications based on the specific device is also necessary. Currently, the framework Google has designed for Android to automatically switch layouts based on screen size is very useful. It changes the previous situation where separate versions needed to be compiled for each device during program design, or where simple stretching was done for different screens. Additionally, in design, practical operational experiences must be considered, such as whether to enlarge an image using a zoom button or multi-touch.
