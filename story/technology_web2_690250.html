Art collection should not be burdened by worldly dust.

by anonymous on 2012-03-05 09:08:55

Art collection should not be burdened by worldly concerns. Collection should inherently be an elegant spiritual pursuit, a culture of self-cultivation. Collectors should be dual pursuers and beneficiaries of both spirit and material. However, this is not the case in today's collections.

Nowadays, whether it be high-end or low-end collections, most collectors are toiling and becoming anxious: those attending auctions have to study dozens of catalogs before the preview, searching for their desired collections, but also fearing the auction company's "no guarantee of authenticity" market rule. They repeatedly research and identify items, sitting at the auction site for several, even ten or more hours, but may not necessarily make any gains; those who visit "ghost markets" weave through crowds, searching among dazzling stalls and antiques, and once they determine their target, verbal battles ensue, but the majority of what they find is not real treasure; those visiting galleries face works with diverse artistic forms and authors they do not fully understand. Although the environment is elegant, and the gallery manager provides detailed recommendations, their minds remain bewildered, and what they end up purchasing is often mass-produced artwork; those inheriting family collections travel around with their pieces seeking expert appraisals of their value, but mostly receive certificates based on age... all these activities consume energy, time, and money, but often result in nothing.

Despite chaotic scenes, the market performance remains impressive. Recent statistical data released by the French media Art Market Information Network shows that China's cultural relics and art auction market total turnover has rapidly surpassed the UK and USA, jumping to the global first place! As a developing country without question, leading in the art auction market raises questions about whether one should celebrate or worry.

From the current state of China's cultural relics and art market, its health condition is far from optimistic. In a collection market, if 30% are investors and 70% are collectors; 30% are high-priced artworks and 70% are average-priced artworks, then the market is in a relatively healthy and stable state. But in China, these proportions are reversed, with investors making up more than 80%! Many of these newcomers with strong economic backing are non-professional institutions, guided step-by-step by experts, becoming new forces in the collection world, creating one "astronomical price" after another worldwide.

Excessive profit-driven speculative behavior makes the elegant act of collecting more perilous than gambling. To buy low and sell high, all sorts of methods of overheating are used, causing artworks to change hands at unprecedented speeds. Friends in the collection world blindly follow trends at unprecedented rates. Investment and collection are inverted, prices and values are inverted... All of this not only disrupts China's original collection order but also disturbs the international auction market, destroying China's traditional collection culture.

Some envision the transformation of China's investment-based collection into Western consumption-based collection. I believe that what China's collection needs is not just rational consumption. Traditional Chinese collection is indeed research-oriented. Besides cultivating personal temperament, collection should more importantly reflect respect for history, protection of heritage, and organization of culture. Li Qingzhao described her and her husband Zhao Mingcheng's experience studying their collections in the "Postscript" of the "Jinshi Lu": "Whenever we obtained a book, we would jointly examine and organize it, sign it, and label it. Upon obtaining calligraphy, paintings, bronze vessels, we would handle and unroll them, pointing out flaws, taking the burning of one candle as a standard... When encountering books, histories, and characters that were not worn or erroneous, we would buy them, storing them as backup copies... Our desks and chairs were covered with items, our pillows and beds filled with books, our intentions shared and plans discussed, eyes meeting and spirits connecting..." Should the revival of Chinese collection have more of this grand path and less of this vulgarity?

Xu Hongmei
