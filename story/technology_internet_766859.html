Google: Using algorithms and big data to "eliminate" linguists

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 22:18:31

Can you imagine a project team dedicated to breaking language barriers without a single linguist? But this is exactly what Google has done. The company plans to rely on algorithms and big data to eventually "eliminate" the need for linguists.

Google is an extremely algorithm-focused company with a strong engineer culture, but you might still be surprised by their approach — they believe that translation is fundamentally a mathematical and statistical problem. Recently, the Google Translate team at Google's headquarters has been rapidly expanding, hiring several German computer scientists while not recruiting a single linguist.

Franz Josef Och, the head of the Google Translate department, is also a German computer scientist by background. He is not only not proficient in linguistics but also not particularly skilled at learning languages. He believes that the key to machine translation lies in expertise in mathematics, statistics, and programming.

The Google Translate team does not attempt to mimic human translators, which is why they have not developed dictionaries or defined grammatical structures and rules. In fact, grammar rules remain difficult for current computers to handle. Google focuses more on using big data and statistical methods. The translation system continuously adjusts the relevance of its translations and self-learns how to process billions of words. Through this method, computers can ultimately continue to optimize their translation results.

One advantage of using big data for translation is that the translation system improves as more data accumulates. Google Translate currently supports mutual translation between 71 languages, and last year users accessed it 200 million times. Moreover, Google, which indexes web pages globally, can improve its translation system by relying on existing translated content available on the internet.

Despite this, algorithm-based translation systems are still far from achieving the precision of human translation. Syntax, tone, and ambiguity are all problems that automatic translation software finds very challenging to handle. The results from Google Translate can only help people roughly understand unfamiliar languages, and sometimes the translations are difficult to read smoothly.

Furthermore, the quality of translations between different language families varies greatly. For example, the quality of translations between English and Spanish is good, while translations between English and Japanese are not so great, and translations between English and German are very poor.

Nevertheless, machine translation has one advantage that human translation finds hard to match — it allows more people to access more information. Imagine being able to communicate with anyone in any language just by holding your phone; that would be an incredible experience.
