Recruitment

There was a time when my company urgently needed a batch of assistant staff. First, the company was implementing the ERP system project and required a group of assistants to help input computer materials. Second, some department assistants had resigned and needed to be replaced urgently. However, during that period, the recruited assistants were extremely unstable; they came and went, leaving the human resources department overwhelmed and the department managers very anxious. To promote the ERP system, the company also hired two high-quality female university graduates, but they didn't even last three months and left immediately after the training ended. This greatly worried the company's HR department and the boss: the inability to recruit people affected departmental work and even impacted the promotion of the newly expensive ERP system.

In this situation, the boss instructed me to "take a look" and be responsible for the final review of the recruitment process.

Actually, for these assistant positions, our requirements were not high: female, high school graduate (middle school graduate is also acceptable), able to operate a computer, with or without work experience, and no age limit. The treatment, compared to the surrounding areas, was neither particularly high nor low, as it was just a relatively junior position. But why was it so difficult to recruit and retain people? I decided to investigate further.

On this day, three girls came for an interview, and two were selected after the initial screening for a statistical assistant position in the production department. These two girls had similar basic conditions, but there were differences. The first girl was a college graduate who had worked in a sales company in the city. She looked good and was local. The second one was married, not too old, a high school graduate, and from another place due to her husband's engineering team being stationed here. The HR director favored the first one, thinking she was young, educated, and attractive.

I conducted the final interviews with both of them: their computer skills, demeanor, and response speed were indeed good, making it hard to choose between them. Later, I asked them why they wanted to join our company. The first girl said she wanted to find a company to learn more. She felt that our company was larger in scale locally and managed more systematically, so she decided to try.

The second girl was very honest, stating that her husband was working on an engineering project here, and she needed to take care of him. When I further inquired about how long the project would last, she said it might be two to three years.

After careful consideration, I chose the second girl.

Explanation

Both the HR director and the production department manager were very puzzled and asked me the reason.

I slowly analyzed: when the qualifications of two candidates are similar, we need to further examine the motivation of the job seeker. Motivation is sometimes the key factor in deciding whether to hire someone, regardless of whether the position is important or critical!

Among the theories of motivation, the most famous one is probably McClelland's Three Needs Theory. He believed that there are generally three important motivations in a work context: the need for achievement (striving for success and wanting to do the best), the need for power (influencing or controlling others while not being controlled by others), and the need for affiliation (establishing friendly close interpersonal relationships).

For the assistant position, it is a relatively junior role. It does not require a high level of education or much work experience; the only requirement is patience and meticulousness. And given our current situation, what we need most is someone who can patiently continue doing the job. Of course, this position has a fatigue period; if there’s no opportunity for advancement within two to three years, people tend to leave. Our company has been around for a long time, and such opportunities for promotion are not abundant. Another reason is that many people don’t stay long enough before leaving.

This position is unlikely to produce significant achievements or generate a sense of power. More importantly, it requires a suitable environment where she can work as long as possible, reducing the company's recruitment and training costs."

The first girl's motivation was a typical need for achievement, and given her higher education, she was unlikely to settle for this position. Additionally, she previously worked in a sales company, which is vastly different from a factory setting. Plus, being local, she doesn't feel unfamiliar and could leave at any time.

The second girl, however, was different. Her motivation was purely to be with her husband, indicating a strong need for affiliation. She didn't have any particular pursuit of power or achievement; she just wanted to do her job well. Moreover, when her husband's project ends, her career fatigue period will begin, which coincides with the time we need to replace her.

They listened half-heartedly, but since I made the decision, they couldn't say much.

Later, it turned out that this girl performed quite well and left after a year and a half when her husband's project ended. By then, our company no longer needed this position.

Capitalizing on Success

Next, we moved on to recruiting assistants for the ERP system. This was a position the company urgently needed, and it was something the department managers were quite concerned about: fearing that the recruited person would leave right after the training! I simplified the process; if the basic conditions were met, I directly asked about their job-seeking motivation, which proved to be quite effective.

The first girl was from Shenzhen to Huizhou. I call this type "passenger-style," meaning they're not from nearby but just visiting relatives or friends casually, making them less stable. However, upon asking why she came from Shenzhen to here, I decided to hire her. She said all her relatives, including her parents and sister, were here, and her parents had long wanted her to come over, fearing she might find a boyfriend locally. Relatives coming to visit are usually quite stable. Indeed, this girl is still working in our company on ERP-related tasks, performing quite excellently.

The second girl was from Dongguan, and her reasons were simple: classmates were nearby, and her boyfriend was also close by. Without hesitation, I hired her. She worked for over a year until the company downsized, and she left.

The last one had just graduated from high school, and her cousin worked in our company. Leaving home for the first time, she had no choice but to seek refuge with her cousin. This was one of the most stable types. I hired her, and she is still with the company today.

For the other various reasons, if her motivation couldn't keep her in the position for a longer period, I basically didn't consider hiring her. For lower-level positions, when the basic conditions are similar, motivation is the key factor in deciding to hire. In China, a country with a five-thousand-year tradition, the need for family ties is a very important driving force. Of course, for higher-level positions, the need for power or achievement becomes significantly more important.

Successfully completing the task, the "high turnover rate of assistants" issue in the company was resolved smoothly.

Reflection

For some people, finding a job is merely about money. But what about things beyond money? These differences are the key points in decision-making. Human resource management personnel, when recruiting and hiring employees, should consider more: eliminating identical conditions and focusing on the specific needs of the company. Only then is it possible to recruit suitable talents who will stay longer. The competency model talks about exactly this: the ultimate factor determining a person's work situation is the deepest level cause - motivation. I hope everyone takes the opportunity to study and practice more, improving the accuracy and efficiency of recruitment.
