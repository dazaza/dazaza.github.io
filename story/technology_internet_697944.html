1. Simple Goiter

Simple goiter, also known as non-toxic goiter, is caused by iodine deficiency, excessive iodine intake, goitrogenic substances, and congenital iodine deficiency. These factors lead to obstacles in the production of thyroid hormones or an increased demand, resulting in relatively insufficient thyroid hormone levels. This causes the pituitary gland to secrete more TSH, leading to compensatory enlargement of the thyroid. However, it does not come with abnormal thyroid function. It can be divided into endemic and sporadic goiters.

- Endemic goiters often occur in inland mountainous areas far from the sea, showing regional distribution. They can affect any age group. Sporadic goiters can occur in non-iodine-deficient areas or high-iodine coastal regions, with females being more susceptible. They commonly occur during puberty, pregnancy, or lactation, potentially worsening hypothyroidism.

- The thyroid may swell mildly to moderately. In the early stages, it presents as diffuse swelling with a smooth surface and soft texture. As the condition progresses, the swelling becomes more pronounced, eventually forming nodular hyperplasia with a harder texture. Local compression symptoms may accompany this.

2. Thyroid Nodules and Tumors

Functionally normal single or multiple thyroid nodules are quite common. Common causes include nodular goiter, thyroid adenoma, and degenerative cysts. The incidence increases with age. Other causes include chronic lymphocytic thyroiditis and thyroid cancer. It's crucial to differentiate between benign and malignant nodules for appropriate treatment.

Most nodular enlargements develop insidiously over years and may go unnoticed until discovered during physical examinations or thyroid ultrasound tests. The primary focus is differentiating between benign and malignant nodules.

- Individuals with a history of head and neck radiation have an increased risk of malignant transformation of thyroid nodules.

- Male or pediatric patients with solitary nodules should be vigilant about thyroid cancer.

- Malignant nodules typically grow faster but can progress slowly; thyroid cancer may present with metastatic lesions.

- Thyroid cancer usually feels hard, has an uneven surface, adheres to surrounding tissues, and may cause enlarged cheek or neck lymph nodes and hoarseness.
