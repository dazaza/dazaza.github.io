Phone cameras and cameras are not a choice.

by geekzhang on 2013-05-13 20:35:48

Nokia has made a big fuss about the camera functions, which makes fans very happy on one hand, but is regarded as a struggle within the confines of Windows Phone on the other. Struggle? I don't think so, at least not entirely.

We can first look back at those products from the past. Regardless of whether the widely acknowledged first color screen phone was NTT DoCoMo D502i, Ericsson T68, or Infotm i108, the year 2000 marked the beginning of color screen phones. Initially, color screen phones were expensive, but they eventually became standard. More recently, the iPod brought music stores, the iPhone introduced app stores, and the iPad initiated the so-called tablet era.

After the PureView 808, Nokia focused all its efforts on photography. Although the historical significance of being the first to introduce optical stabilization in mobile phones wasn't as significant as that of the first camera phone, Sharp J-SH04, it indeed promoted one thing: the professionalization of mobile phone cameras. Of course, the professionalism mentioned here is relative. After PureView, larger sensors, higher sensitivity, and lossless zoom have all become realities.

So, are we going back to the discussion between multifunctional devices and professional devices? I think this isn't a choice question. Just like the arrival of the mobile internet era, the history of browsing web pages with laptops is gone forever, tablets and smartphones are the mainstream choices. However, desktop computers themselves will not be eliminated; they just transfer simple functions to tablets. Although I cannot estimate the exact number, there will always be a market for desktop computers with rigid demand.

Similarly, the development of camera phones is not for replacement. The development of a field cannot set goals and directions in advance; the development itself determines the direction and outcome. The purpose of having more advanced camera functions on phones is actually to give us the confidence to record our lives at any time. The more advanced and professional areas do not belong to phones, and probably never will.

Currently, apart from Nokia, HTC is trying to improve photo quality by reducing pixel density, while Sony is winning through traditional methods of increasing pixel numbers, combined with algorithms and in-camera processing. Under such trends, more and more manufacturers will focus their R&D on camera functions, making the camera another focal point of competition after processors and screens.

What results will this bring? I can definitely say that low-end consumer digital cameras (DC) will be severely affected this time. In such a form factor, what was originally not a choice question becomes very sharp. Choosing a camera phone is clearly more rational than choosing a consumer DC. However, beyond this, from so-called professional DCs, to mirrorless cameras and DSLRs, these are not in the realm of phones.

Finally, let's talk about Nokia. Is the reason why Nokia is so dedicated to photography because the space for Windows Phone to play is too small, and it deliberately creates differentiated products? I think the possibility of making such a decision is small. During the Symbian era when Nokia had no rivals, cameras were also a key focus for Nokia. Even if Nokia hadn't chosen Windows Phone, cameras would still be a key area of development.
