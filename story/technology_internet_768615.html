Ding Lei on a certain WeChat feature: "It's like a brothel downstairs in a five-star hotel"

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 20:06:30

The Internet is one of the few industries in China that has undergone full market competition with little reliance on public power and local protectionism. The winners of the Internet era generally possess a rare quality: they believe in technology and hope to change themselves and the world through it. This is particularly evident in Ding Lei, who has a technical background. However, in his pursuit of a better world, he firmly believes in the hand of fate: life and death are predetermined, wealth and honor are heaven-sent, and everything seems to be written in advance.

Every penny I've earned is clean

Mr. Ding Lei walked onto the stage as one of the first generation of Chinese Internet entrepreneurs, having been absent from the public eye for a long time. On August 19th, NetEase launched its latest instant messaging product "Yixin," which is similar in function to Tencent's WeChat. At the launch event, "anti-monopoly" and "creating a fully competitive market for users" were missions repeatedly emphasized by Ding Lei.

Yixin needs to compete against the usage habits of 400 million WeChat users and a platform with a scale close to 500 million. An unnamed internet observer predicted: "If Yixin can capture 10%-15% of the market, that would already be quite good." He paused for a moment: "This is probably a product destined to fail."

Ding Lei's greatest advantage is his cooperation with China Telecom. Coincidentally, on the day Yixin was launched, WeChat experienced a half-hour network failure. The official explanation from WeChat was "a network hardware failure"—the WeChat system is housed in China Telecom's data center.

This time, even the usually proud NetEase has "relied on a big tree." In response to such ridicule from the outside world, Ding Lei does not internally recognize this set of values. From being an internet enthusiast 20 years ago to becoming the boss of a listed company worth billions, he relies on freedom, fairness, and belief in technology. "Even now, I dare to say that every penny I've earned is clean," Ding Lei told the reporter from People Magazine.

In December 2004, Ding Lei served as the deputy director of the Wireless Information Committee. At that time, gaming and SMS services saved Ding Lei, making him the Forbes richest person in 2003-2004.

Doing what should be done, not doing what shouldn't

In mid-September, at a private gathering, Ding Lei talked about a certain feature in WeChat, evaluating it as an immoral design, akin to "a brothel opened downstairs from a five-star hotel." "How are you supposed to let kids use it?" he angrily asked while throwing his phone on the table.

Ding Lei once angrily printed out a revealing picture of a beauty and posted it on the wall of the editor-in-chief of the portal channel. "If anyone posts such pictures again, I'll print out the photo and send it to their parents." When the online dating market became popular, NetEase launched local dating. Upon discovering a large influx of prostitutes, Ding Lei shut it down: "Some people complained about us, but I don't have to bear the blame for that."

For respect, Ding Lei has a strong demand. Talking about his former title as the richest man, he showed a strong sense of disdain: "Only bosses think being the richest is amazing." From 2003 to 2004, he consecutively topped the Forbes list of the richest people, but he declined all interviews citing overseas vacations.

Some employees believe that Ding is not an excellent product manager, being too idealistic. Impressionist by NetEase is a product where users upload pictures to customize gifts. "Chinese people can't even afford houses, who would have the heart to appreciate their own pictures?" "Actually, it doesn't lose much money. It's a small amount. Moreover, since I've made so much money today, I need to take responsibility for some products," Ding Lei explained.

"If I believe in anything, it's technology." He said to the reporter.

It's hard to tell if Ma Huateng ignited Ding Lei's fighting spirit or if YiXin reignited his passion for developing NetEase's email service back in the day, but this aligns with the expectations outsiders have for him. Among the top 5 most influential figures in China's Internet industry, Ding Lei certainly holds a place. However, this prodigy who rose to fame at a young age once disappointed many, with the most common criticism being: over these past 5 years, he has transformed from an industry pioneer into a cautious conservative.

Ding's strong sense of self-respect also leads him to consider too much before each new product launch. He cannot accept any judgment failures on his part. This makes NetEase appear too slow, especially when mobile terminals arrived, prompting some executives to choose to start their own businesses.

The word 'leader' is strange

People in different positions will make completely opposite evaluations of Ding Lei: some think he is very stingy. In the early days in Beijing, there were two types of taxi models, Jetta and Xia Li. Ding stipulated that only high-ranking executives could reimburse the cost of riding in a Jetta taxi, but no one dared to do so because Ding often took Xia Li taxis; when Ding saw an expensive coffee machine placed in the Shanghai office, he became very angry. Upon learning it was sponsored by a brand, his anger quickly turned to joy.

Others, however, praise Ding's generosity. When senior executive Li Yong left, Ding tried to retain him with stock options worth 1 million yuan, but failed. Still, he insisted on giving him the options as a token of gratitude. In 1999, to raise funds, NetEase moved from Guangzhou to Beijing. Surprisingly, Ding chose the then most expensive office building in the city, Kerry Center, instead of Zhongguancun, "Back then, Zhongguancun was a street full of scammers, filled with utilitarianism rather than innovation and revolutionaries."

Unlike bosses skilled at creating corporate culture, Ding Lei shows no interest in ideology. "'Leader' is a strange term," he furrowed his brow, "Do we really have to rank people out of a hundred? I think everyone has their shining points."

Ding Lei enjoys recounting the development process of "NetEase Open Courses." An employee he didn't know walked into his office and said, "Boss, I see there are great open courses abroad. Give me 100,000 yuan, and I'll do it." To date, NetEase Open Courses have produced 12,000 free episodes, with an annual investment of tens of millions of yuan.

True villains, not hypocrites

Ding Lei is 42 years old, achieving success at a young age thanks to the Internet. His work at the telecommunications bureau helped him gain access to Internet technology earlier than most people. Seizing the opportunity when the Internet was burgeoning in China, he became one of the first Internet entrepreneurs through email and personal web pages. At 26, he founded NetEase, and with great luck, went public on NASDAQ during the peak of capital enthusiasm. Benefiting from the exponential growth of Chinese Internet users, he weathered the Internet economic winter around 2001 through gaming and text messages. Since then, his path has been smooth, becoming China's richest person at the age of 32.

Ding Lei was born into an ordinary intellectual family in Fenghua, Zhejiang Province, which is also Chiang Kai-shek's hometown, preserving traces of traditional gentry culture and Neo-Confucian spirit. In Ding's memory, his parents often said, "Not pleased by external gains, not saddened by personal losses," "A gentleman is open-minded, while a petty person is always worried."

Apart from opposing Ding Lei's departure from the system, his parents almost never interfered in other aspects, allowing him to immerse himself in network technology with a very pure mindset—he loved technology and was among the earliest engineers in China proficient in UNIX and LINUX systems, and was also one of the first 100 users of Beijing Telecom.

In an interview with People Magazine, Ding Lei's friend and financial writer Wu Xiaobo described Ding as "an Internet believer, someone who works for interest."

During the interview, Xue Manzi's prostitution scandal was being widely discussed, and Ding Lei evaluated Xue as a "hypocritical person," whose actions in the past and present cannot withstand scrutiny. "He was convicted of securities fraud in the US around 2000. Many people don't know this, yet they are proud to receive investments from Xue Manzi."

In three interviews with People Magazine, he couldn't help but comment multiple times, "XX earns cabbage money but has the heart of selling white powder," "XX is a hypocrite." He reminded the reporter to turn off the recorder without revealing names: "I prefer true villains."

Ding Lei analyzed himself: "I am straightforward and lack tolerance, so I can only label myself as a true villain, not a true gentleman." He sank into the sofa, relaxed and comfortable, "But being open-minded is very important."

Too chaotic

Ding Lei often resides in Hangzhou. According to a friend of his, he deliberately stays away from controversies and avoids meetings with relevant departments. In 2011, there was a report stating that he attended a "Seminar on Network Cultural Construction" organized by the National Internet Information Office along with Jack Ma, Charles Zhang, and Robin Li, focusing on jointly building and sharing a healthy network culture. The title of the report was "Trained Internet Leaders."

Zhang Peng, editor-in-chief of Business Value, said: "Jack Ma is a very polished person, you won't see anything you dislike in him. He is very positive, doesn't attack others, and always talks about major issues concerning the country; Pony Ma is actually very introverted, but later realized he had to change and started communicating; Ding Lei doesn't seem like a businessman, occasionally saying things that proper businessmen wouldn't say."

On the other hand, in the Internet industry, Ding Lei collaborates most with telecom departments. Many of NetEase's projects have received support from the governments of Guangdong and Zhejiang provinces. Financial writer Wu Xiaobo believes that Ding Lei cannot be defined as someone who avoids the government, "However, he has a grasp of propriety and maintains an independent stance. He is not someone who associates with rent-seekers."

Freedom and Slackness

In late summer of August in Beijing, on a weekend night, the reporter from People Magazine met Ding Lei for the first time. Sitting in a common three-wheeled bouncing cart on the streets of Beijing, Ding Lei skillfully jumped out of the car and then leisurely strolled through the bars-lined Sanlitun street.

To this day, Ding Lei has no bodyguards, drivers, or secretaries. Even during his wedding, he did not follow his wife's suggestion to wear formal attire. His most expensive outfit was a pair of leather shoes and a Rolex watch purchased temporarily for a roadshow in the U.S. in 2000.

Ding Lei's personality is both free-spirited and laid-back. "Sometimes, he says: 'You're doing well, I'll give you a car at the end of the year.' We know the boss has had too much to drink," NetEase PR department's He Kuan told the reporter: "Once during an event, Ding Lei said he would come. He rarely attends press conferences, so we notified many media outlets. The night before, he suddenly said he wouldn't come. I said: 'Boss, this is important, we've already notified the media.' He replied: 'Help me solve it, I can't leave Hangzhou.'” The press conference was “forced” to ask questions via video link.

In 2003, during the Spring Festival, Fang Xingdong and Ding Lei drank tea at Lou Wa Lou in Hangzhou. Fang hoped Ding Lei would carry the banner of China's Internet—back then, the title of richest person put Ding Lei at the center of social attention. "He didn't react much," Fang recalled, chatting about some trivial matters before dispersing, "I enthusiastically sought him out and left feeling disheartened." Not long after, Fang learned that the first thing Ding Lei did after becoming the richest person was to change his car because the previous one consumed too much fuel.

What goes around comes around

Ding Lei's greatest hobby is driving himself, and his most recent trip left him somewhat unhappy. Standing in a northwestern town with a population of only 200,000, he saw the bank's electronic promotional board scrolling with "4.65% interest rate for 103 days of deposit." "I suddenly remembered that this city doesn't have a single bookstore or newsstand. What do you think this country lacks?" "Calcium," he asked and answered himself.

"Do you have faith?" he suddenly asked the reporter while eating in the NetEase cafeteria. "No." "You will have it in the future."

"Do you?" the reporter pressed.

"I don't know what my faith is, but I only know a line from Infernal Affairs: 'What goes around comes around.'"

This simple causal theory also forms his entire fatalistic view—"life determined by destiny, followed by fortune, feng shui, accumulated virtue, and education." The intertwining of chance and necessity in his experiences makes him remember a phrase: "Life and death are predetermined, wealth and honor are heaven-sent."

Considering that he has "far exceeded the imagination of his youth," Ding Lei says he feels lucky. "Doing what I like, no need to punch a clock at work, making money, financially free, and not doing what I don't want to do."

In mid-September, Ding Lei specially came from Hangzhou to attend the wedding banquet of a newlywed couple. During the gathering, the atmosphere was harmonious, candlelight reflecting the rare starry sky of a Beijing night. Long-time friends called him "Dingding," and several girls teased his curly hair with "gangsters get haircuts, no one can stop them."

When it was Ding Lei's turn to toast, he raised his glass of red wine and said to the groom: "To love someone, you must give her freedom." The man blushed shyly. Ding thought for a few seconds and meaningfully added: "Marriage is, not pleased by external gains, not saddened by personal losses."
