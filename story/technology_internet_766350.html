If a computer has always been called a computer, why are phones, watches, and glasses now being called smart XX?

by anonymous on 2013-09-11 14:07:03

"If knowledge or thought is to have an object, then there must be something unchanging besides the perceivable things" ------ Aristotle, *Metaphysics*.

Tool Intelligence

Samsung couldn't bear the silence and launched Galaxy Gear; Xiaomi finally moved its system onto the TV. If Plato were still alive, he would certainly jump out and use the example of "the essential bed" to humiliate these two guys. Since 1985, when Microsoft introduced the Windows computer system that didn’t require typing code for interaction, and back in 1983 when Motorola released the first portable phone, no one at that time could have imagined that these two things could merge together, nor could they imagine that phones could also have operating systems. By 2002, Microsoft had already started promoting the XP system and incidentally developed a mobile phone-based operating system —— Microsoft Windows Mobile for Pocket PC 2002 phone edition. Nokia also released SymbianOS. Because of the existence of these systems, they were called smartphones, just to distinguish them from previous phones and other phones without operating systems. Unfortunately, until 2007, neither the mobile phone industry nor the world underwent significant changes because of the addition of such systems, and phones remained essentially "phones" —— communication tools.

Subsequently came smartwatches, smart glasses, smart TVs, etc. But do these products, which deviate from Plato's "essence" through technological advancement, truly have the potential to change people's lives like smartphones? To answer this question, we need to start by examining why smartphones brought about such a profound transformation.

Open Cognitive Awareness vs. Tool-Based Cognitive Awareness

No matter how powerful the hardware or software of a computer is, it won't be called a "smart computer," just as Superman being incredibly powerful wouldn't astonish anyone —— the emergence of these entities shares a common characteristic: human cognition towards them remains open, let's call it "open cognitive awareness." In simpler terms, the appearance of such entities isn't aimed at solving any specific demand but instead provides some kind of service. Phones, on the other hand, are different. Phones appeared as tools —— fulfilling communication needs, much like Wang Xiaochuan’s recent "tool theory," but here this theory is expanded. Let's call it "tool-based cognitive awareness." This type of cognition limits the depth and breadth of early human understanding of phones. The breakthrough in depth manifests in finding various ways to communicate with others, such as progressing from making calls to sending text messages. In terms of breadth, features like built-in cameras and games can be added. The development of phones during the initial feature phone era verifies this point. Regardless of how far-reaching the advancements were, they couldn't escape the boundaries set by this cognition. No matter how dazzling the models became, how good the camera quality was, or how enjoyable the gaming experience got, all these were merely "added values" of this tool. Naturally, depth brought about the expected changes, while breadth did not significantly impact the world or people's lives.

The transplantation of operating systems from computers to phones, spurred by the internet, played a crucial role. When the internet became widespread, people realized that effective communication could be achieved through computers and the internet, naturally leading them to think of phones. At this point, human cognition regarding phones began to shift, gradually transforming into the aforementioned "open cognitive awareness" —— marking the arrival of the smart era.

Therefore, we have the current era.

Some transformations might be difficult

Phones were lucky; their physical nature, functional attributes, and relatively short existence determined their ability to transition from "tool-based cognitive awareness" to "open cognitive awareness," with the internet acting as a catalyst. However, most things in daily life are products of "tool-based cognitive awareness," such as watches and glasses (we'll discuss TVs later). These more tool-centric products cannot be deeply explored much, focusing more on expanding their breadth. Depth-wise, watches are meant for telling time, and glasses are designed to address certain vision problems, and this cognition has existed for a very long time, much longer than the advent of phones. A key feature of tools is that they are used only when facing specific needs, and the interaction process tends to be simple. For instance, watches were invented to make it convenient for people to check the time, and their interaction process is brief, with time presentation methods kept as simple and clear as possible. Even if their decorative attributes are considered, it doesn't alter their limitations in depth. Changing the cognitive attributes of these items poses some difficulty. All that can be done is to leverage technology to make them smarter, thereby expanding their recognized breadth, much like adding camera and game functions to phones during the feature phone era. Watches can now monitor heartbeats and connect with phones, and glasses can take photos and access social networks, etc. However, since their "tool-based cognitive awareness" hasn't transformed into "open cognitive awareness," these products probably won't achieve the same status as smartphones.

Finally, let's talk about TVs. The appearance of TVs wasn't entirely based on "tool-based cognitive awareness." After all, TV programs only emerged after the invention of TVs, cultivating this particular demand. Since this demand is cultivated postnatally, it can continue to be explored and shaped, such as playing games or watching DVDs, akin to discovering someone's immense strength initially used for pushing carts, and eventually realizing they can also carry heavy objects. Thus, TVs possess a certain degree of "open cognitive awareness," but a significant drawback is their unidirectional nature, limiting their complete transition to "open cognitive awareness." With the advent of the internet and computers, this transformation becomes even harder. Given this, intelligent TVs aiming to bring about changes may need to address this root issue.

I wonder, what will be the next thing to transition from "tool-based cognitive awareness" to "open cognitive awareness"? Because that will surely change the world again.
