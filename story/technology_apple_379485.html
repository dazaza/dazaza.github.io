Stirring the Murky Waters of HTML5 Video (5) - The Future

by diglog on 2011-01-23 04:25:20

If video websites were to completely switch to using WebM, they could save on patent fees. However, "completely switching to WebM" is not practical: a large number of devices (especially mobile devices) only support H.264, so for quite a long time, there will be at least a need to simultaneously support both H.264 and WebM. This means that the storage costs for video sites would instantly double. The Content Delivery Network (CDN) used by video sites would also see its costs double due to the need to cache videos in two formats. For the same raw video, it needs to be encoded into both H.264 and WebM formats, causing the transcoding processing cost to double as well. Additionally, existing video resources would all need to be re-encoded into WebM. Compared to these doubled costs, the patent fees paid for using H.264 technology are negligible. Google has the financial resources to bear this cost, but it's hard to say whether other video sites can do the same.

Even if we ignore the cost issues for video sites, can WebM and H.264 coexist peacefully on the client side? On desktop systems, Chrome and Firefox can play H.264 through the Flash plugin with little conflict with native WebM support. However, mobile devices require hardware decoding chips for support. Given cost and space limitations, it’s hard to imagine rational mobile device manufacturers including two decoding chips to support both WebM and H.264. Another possibility is that in the future, a hardware decoding chip might appear that can handle both WebM and H.264, but currently, there is no news about this.

A more realistic scenario is that even if Chrome, Firefox, Opera, and others don't natively support H.264, most video sites may just maintain the status quo: playing H.264 videos via Flash plugins for desktop systems, and using HTML5 video tags to play H.264 videos for mobile devices that don’t support Flash.
