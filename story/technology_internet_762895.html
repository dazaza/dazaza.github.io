"The Inevitable 'Death of Yougou': Belle's E-commerce Has Many Fatal Flaws"

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 17:56:25

Recently, Yougou.com, which has been suffering from a severe loss of senior executives, received another blow. On July 17th, it was confirmed by an insider from Yougou.com that Zhang Xiaojun, the COO of Yougou.com, will be leaving and is currently processing his resignation. As one of the founding members of Yougou.com, Zhang Xiaojun's departure is indicative of serious crises faced by Yougou.com.

Let’s first review the e-commerce journey of Belle International, to which Yougou.com belongs:

Belle quietly experimented with e-commerce several years ago, developing steadily through three channels: Taobao distribution, Tmall, and B2C distribution (B2C distribution was completed via Taoxiu.com). Led by veteran group member Xie Yunli, the not-so-luxurious e-commerce team initially started by selling products under Belle's own brands, subsequently launching exclusive online versions, followed by new online brands. This development path became a successful case study before 2011, considered more solid than Li-Ning's approach for traditional brands entering the e-commerce market.

Following the over $100 million financing and rapid growth of Letao.com and Haolemai in 2010, Belle Group felt the pressure. On one hand, they were concerned about being controlled by distribution channels again; on the other hand, after evaluation, they realized their significant advantages in the e-commerce sector, including:

1. A multitude of brands: Besides Belle's own major women's shoe brands, the agency sales of internationally renowned sports brands like Adidas and Nike have seen astonishing growth online;

2. Easier category expansion: For Belle Group, known for its women's shoes and agency of famous sports brands, how to achieve absolute market dominance and status through multiple brands and categories has been a key consideration. Expanding offline stores to increase brand and category presence is too slow, while the ongoing e-commerce business can easily expand brands but struggles to quickly add categories;

3. Financial advantage: In cooperation with e-commerce enterprises, Belle discovered that e-commerce is capital-intensive. With financial strength, Belle could develop without relying on external capital, thereby defeating many vertical e-commerce companies dependent on capital investment, aiming to become the "last man standing";

4. Talent advantage: Through years of e-commerce practice, Belle had built a highly professional and efficient e-commerce team. The talent cultivation could support the scale-up of e-commerce operations or even beyond;

5. Supply chain advantage: This made Belle particularly excited and was the core reason attracting top executives from brand e-commerce or platform e-commerce. After all, supply chains always intrigue those in e-commerce as they seem to decide life and death aside from funding. Supply chains include product research and development, production, warehouse management, delivery, and after-sales service — a series of "business" contents. In traditional enterprises emphasizing "commerce" over "e-commerce," this is considered the competitive edge against e-commerce enterprises.

With these advantages, Belle e-commerce decided to make a big move, starting by purchasing the double-pinyin domain yougou.com,

ditching the somewhat copycat “topshoes.cn” Taoshou website for redirection, halting supplies to all distribution sites, while still maintaining direct-operated third-party shops like Taobao and Tmall but reducing investments and focus. They recruited high-profile executives from JD.com and Vancl to serve as CMO and COO respectively, while sending veteran group leaders to act as CEO, forming a golden triangle. They aimed to create a full-category vertical B2C mall, implementing multi-brand strategies while expanding into clothing and other categories, realizing the dream of a department store empire impossible offline!

Everything sounded perfect, but dreams need to meet the harsh reality of the backbone. The actual situation was:

1. Higher technical requirements for independent B2C: Belle in Shenzhen headquarters couldn't recruit more excellent technical talents, and outsourcing tech services were scarce in Shenzhen. However, technology determines user experience, backend management efficiency, and reliable data. Independent B2C involves dynamic technology, different from the relatively static ERP technology. Continuous changes remain a hurdle many traditional enterprises cannot overcome;

2. Increased management difficulty with category expansion: Someone once said that doing well in women's shoes doesn't necessarily translate to men's shoes, something Belle likely felt deeply when expanding into men's shoes. Expanding into women's clothing and accessories within a year must have caused immense challenges at the backend. For traditional brand enterprises accustomed to meticulous calculations, such a rapid strategy worked only in special periods for Fanke, understandable within Fanke and JD.com's systems. Although Belle hired Fanke's COO and JD.com's CMO, whether they could understand and execute with "exaggerated costs" remains questionable!

3. Corporate structure: Many people struggle to grasp this unless they’ve bridged both e-commerce and traditional enterprises. Simply put, departments (organizational structures), approvals, finance and budgets, reporting methods, corporate culture, communication styles, execution methods, risk controls, processes, and regulations constitute a company's so-called "mechanism." This conflict results in e-commerce trying to persuade traditional teams to accept e-commerce's "structure," while traditional teams try but fail to fully comprehend e-commerce's "structure," leading to inefficiency and instability within the execution team. Ultimately, the chairman or board might say, "E-commerce and traditional enterprises each have strengths and weaknesses; the future lies in integration, so you must also integrate..." If such "integration without harmony" occurs, it's called managerial innovation, leading to the bizarre "CEO rotation system" actually appearing in a company like Belle!

Initially, it seemed that Yougou.com from Belle's e-commerce division was far ahead, with top executives waiting for the IPO to enjoy success and wealth, setting examples for countless traditional enterprises' successes in the e-commerce era. Instead, they found themselves entangled in these hard issues.

After all, whether it's the veterans who grew with Belle or the recruited e-commerce professionals, it’s clear they didn’t leave due to lack of ability. When JD.com’s VP returned to JD.com, they continued to perform exceptionally, and when Fanke’s VP had the courage to start their own business. Are these hard issues the three mentioned above? Or are these not just Belle's hard issues but also common ones for many traditional enterprises attempting to enter e-commerce?
