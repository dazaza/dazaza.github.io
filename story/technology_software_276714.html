Keyword interpretation: The reproductive tract is the cavity from the external genitalia opening (also known as the vaginal opening) to the uterus, its largest physiological function being to provide sexual pleasure and childbirth. So-called reproductive tract infections refer to infections caused by bacteria, viruses, parasites, and other pathogenic microorganisms in this cavity, almost every adult woman and some teenage girls have experienced such infections, including vaginitis, cervicitis, adnexitis, etc., all of which fall under this category of diseases. Reasons for listing: Did you know? The initial origin of many gynecological diseases is reproductive tract infections, including the most common uterine fibroids and cervical cancer. This is not an exaggeration; at least 80% of adult women have misconceptions about reproductive tract infections, including many medical personnel in healthcare institutions, and these misconceptions directly affect their bodies, even their lives. For health and happiness, we have no reason not to pay attention to - reproductive tract infections. Misconception one: All reproductive tract infections are related to sexual contact × Incorrect question: Most cases of vaginal inflammation in women are caused by the partner having a long foreskin, because there are a lot of invisible bacteria hidden inside his foreskin; sexually transmitted diseases also belong to reproductive tract infections, and their mode of transmission is sexual contact, so it seems that all reproductive tract infections are related to sexual contact. Is this true? If so, does avoiding sexual contact mean there is no possibility of infection? √ Correct statement: Currently, reproductive tract infections include three major categories: endogenous infections, iatrogenic infections, and sexually transmitted infections. Endogenous infections refer to the overgrowth of microorganisms normally present in the reproductive tract (such as the vagina) due to the influence of certain factors, breaking the original balance of the bacterial flora, thereby causing symptoms of infection. Commonly seen fungal vaginitis and bacterial vaginitis belong to this type of infection. Iatrogenic infections refer to infections caused by unhygienic or improperly disinfected medical procedures, such as intrauterine device insertion/removal or artificial abortion leading to endometritis or pelvic inflammatory disease. Sexually transmitted infections refer to infections spread through sexual behavior, such as gonorrhea, genital warts, syphilis, etc., all of which fall under this category of diseases. Therefore, reproductive tract infections are not necessarily related to sexual contact. Preventive measures: 1. Maintain hygiene during intimate activities, wash private areas before intercourse, and if your partner has a long foreskin, make sure to clean underneath it when washing. 2. Keep your private area dry; the best way is to change underwear frequently. From a medical perspective, well-ventilated, loose cotton underwear is much better than the currently popular tight-fitting and body-shaping underwear. 3. Do a good job with contraception, reduce the chances of pregnancy, and avoid the infection and damage to the reproductive tract caused by instruments during artificial abortion. Misconception two: As long as good personal hygiene is maintained, there will be no infection × Incorrect question: My sisters and I are very mindful of personal hygiene, cleaning our private parts multiple times a day, and even using douching devices to rinse the inside of the vagina. Before and after intimacy with our partners, we also wash our external genitalia, but during our annual routine physical examinations, we are still found to have fungal vaginitis, bacterial vaginitis, and even adnexitis. It's really puzzling why maintaining good personal hygiene can still result in reproductive tract infections? √ Correct statement: In fact, so far, the relationship between personal hygiene habits and reproductive tract infections is not entirely clear. However, one thing is certain: maintaining good cleanliness of the external genitalia cannot protect women from reproductive tract infections. Believing that thoroughly washing private parts before and after sex can prevent disease is completely wrong. On the contrary, excessive washing, especially vaginal douching, can disrupt the natural protective mechanisms of the vagina, causing microbial imbalances, making one more susceptible to fungal infections, bacterial vaginosis, and even pelvic inflammatory disease. In the United States, vaginal douching has been recognized as a harmful practice and abandoned; the World Health Organization has also repeatedly urged in recent years to reduce drug-based cleaning of private areas to avoid reproductive tract infections. But currently, many women are still practicing vaginal douching and consider it a form of self-care. Preventive measures: 1. Healthy women need only clean their private parts once a day with clear water. 2. When being intimate with a partner, it is acceptable to clean the private organs beforehand, but unnecessary afterward, because women naturally secrete antibacterial substances during intimacy. 3. Cleaning does not require the use of medication or soap, and vaginal douching should be avoided altogether.
