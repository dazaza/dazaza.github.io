Post-90s job seekers prefer stability to high salary

by qingian007 on 2012-02-29 10:29:10

I have been tracking this year's graduates since last year with my colleagues, and we found that their job-hunting criteria are mostly ranked by stability, brand, and salary. Their first choice is to take the civil service examination to become a government official, then enter state-owned enterprises or public institutions, and finally consider foreign-invested enterprises and private companies. Some students are even willing to accept very low salaries in order to work for stable units. Generally speaking, this year’s graduates seem to be less idealistic and more realistic.

Employment guidance experts say that the parents of this batch of post-90s graduates caught the good times of the reform and opening up, and their family conditions are more superior compared to the post-80s generation. These children came into contact with the Internet relatively early and are more mature. This is reflected in their job hunting as they have a stronger self-awareness regarding career development. In terms of treatment, the salary requirements of this first batch of post-90s are about 1000 yuan higher than the last batch of post-80s graduates last year, and they also place more emphasis on the length of vacation time.

Netizens: Recruitment is really a painful thing. Every day, I face a group of post-90s kids who can't meet high expectations but also don't settle for low ones. When looking for a job, it's actually better to have a normal heart. Don't demand salaries of five thousand or eight thousand yuan right away.

Another netizen summarized the reasons why they couldn't find jobs: no experience, recent graduates, people don't want us! Or maybe our post-90s generation gives off an impression of not being reliable! Or it might be my own fault, being picky about everything: the salary is too low! The environment isn't good! The working hours are too long! It's too hard! Etc. Sigh! Maybe I am really too picky?

Other netizens noticed that post-90s boys and girls have a serious "soy-sauce-beating" (not taking things seriously) attitude when job hunting.

One netizen shared an interesting story from the canteen today: Two post-90s girls came to the canteen to interview for waitress positions and were hiding at the stairwell discussing. A said, "I don't want to be a cashier because I'd have to count money, which is tiring. Being a dish-serving staff is simpler; you just stand there and serve dishes without moving around." B responded, "Serving dishes would get oil stains on my clothes, and I don't want to wash them. Let's just go back!"

Unknowingly, the post-90s generation, who we used to see as spoiled and rebellious, has started lining up on the job-hunting path.

Recently, Cheng Yating, a post-90s college student from Wuhan, willingly became a sewer cleaner, becoming a hot topic of discussion. Some praised her for her hardworking spirit and positive attitude, others criticized her for wasting years of education and accused her of putting on a show, while her classmates envied her stable job. When Cheng Yating was unafraid of dirt and cleaned the sewers, it made people reflect deeply on the job-hunting views of the post-90s generation.

Compared to post-80s job seekers who care about "how much one earns per month," post-90s job seekers are more concerned about "how many days off one gets per month." Have these young people, who are accused of treating job hunting like "buying soy sauce" (doing things half-heartedly), prepared mentally to take on responsibilities? Can they prove their growth step by step like the post-80s generation did, despite public criticism?
