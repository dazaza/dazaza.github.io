Cancer patients cause cerebrovascular disease

by jiankangyi201006 on 2010-06-23 08:33:18

 

　

Can cancer cause cerebrovascular disease? The answer is affirmative. According to a report on 3426 cancer autopsies from 1970 to 1981, there were 500 cases of cerebrovascular disease, accounting for 14%, among which 255 cases had clinical symptoms, accounting for 7.4% of the total number of autopsied cases. It can be seen that cancer is also one of the common causes of concurrent cerebrovascular disease.

Ischemic cerebrovascular diseases caused by cancer are mainly two types: cerebral embolism and cerebral thrombosis. Cerebral embolism is mostly caused by non-bacterial thrombotic endocarditis. Adenocarcinoma, especially lung and gastrointestinal adenocarcinoma, is prone to endocarditis, forming thrombotic vegetations in the heart. After detachment, if they block the cerebral vessels, it forms cerebral embolism. However, larger tumor fragments directly entering the cerebral arteries are relatively rare. But indeed, cancer emboli entering the cerebral vessels, causing cancerous cerebral embolism, clinically smaller tumor emboli-induced cerebral embolism mainly manifests as epilepsy and reversible cerebral ischemic attacks.

Cerebrovascular diseases caused by cancer are mostly cerebral infarctions, accounting for 54.1%, followed by cerebral hemorrhages, accounting for 45.8%.

 

　

　Cerebral thrombosis caused by cancer mainly has three reasons:

 

　Cancer easily causes coagulation function disorders and increased blood viscosity.

 

　Due to chemotherapy, consumption, etc., leading to decreased body resistance, making infection easy, resulting in infectious vasculitis. On this basis, it's easy to continue with thrombus formation.

 

　In late-stage cancer patients, due to extreme exhaustion and dehydration, blood viscosity increases, also easily leading to thrombus formation.

 

Hemorrhagic cerebrovascular diseases caused by brain tumors are more commonly seen in metastatic brain tumors, followed by primary brain tumors. Metastatic brain tumors mainly transfer via blood source, implantation, and direct spread. They are mostly caused by lung cancer, stomach cancer, and choriocarcinoma, etc.; while hemorrhagic cerebrovascular diseases caused by primary brain tumors are more commonly seen in brain gliomas and meningiomas.

 

　The main reasons for hemorrhagic cerebrovascular diseases caused by brain tumors:

 

　As the tumor grows outward, it erodes nearby brain tissue, causing necrosis,

 

　Necrosis at the center of the tumor, or rupture of new blood vessels and infiltration of tumor cells into blood vessels, causing them to rupture and bleed.

 

　Due to bone marrow infiltration of the tumor and side effects of chemotherapy, etc., the bone marrow hematopoietic function is destroyed, platelet production decreases, and normal coagulation and hemostasis functions cannot proceed. For example, cerebrovascular diseases caused by leukemia. According to reports, the incidence of cerebrovascular diseases (cerebral hemorrhage) in leukemia accounts for 15.2%. Cerebrovascular diseases caused by leukemia often manifest as intracranial hematomas. Their formation is related to the accumulation of white blood cells in the central nervous system and white blood cell nodules in the brain substance. White blood cell nodules infiltrating blood vessels can cause vascular rupture and bleeding. In addition, increased blood viscosity and hypoxia are also reasons for small vessel dilation and rupture, leading to bleeding.

 

　In short, tumors can also cause cerebrovascular diseases. Some have manifestations of cerebrovascular disease before the primary lesion is discovered, so for those with unexplained cerebrovascular disease, the possibility of cancerous stroke should be considered.
