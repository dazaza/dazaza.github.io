Progression techniques for complex terrain in wilderness survival training

by boe868 on 2012-03-07 18:52:16

When traveling in mountainous areas, to avoid getting lost, conserve energy, and increase speed, one should aim to use roads instead of cutting through forests or climbing mountains, and prefer larger roads over smaller ones. If there are no roads available, it is best to travel along longitudinal ridges, spines, mid-slopes, riverbanks, or areas with tall trees, sparse forests, large gaps, and low, sparse grasslands. Generally, one should avoid deep ravines and lush vegetation intertwined with vines and bamboo, aiming to follow ridges rather than valleys, and move longitudinally rather than laterally. Traveling should adhere to the principle of taking large strides, which applies even in mountainous terrain. By increasing stride length and combining three steps into two, one can save many steps and conserve energy over dozens of kilometers. As the saying goes: "It's not about being slow, but about stopping." When tired, it's better to rest by walking slowly rather than stopping completely. Standing still for a minute could mean losing out on several dozen meters of progress while walking slowly.

Mountain travel often involves various rocky slopes and steep walls. Therefore, rock climbing is a primary skill for mountaineering. Before climbing rocks, detailed observation is necessary to carefully identify the quality and weathering degree of the rocks, then determine the direction and route of ascent. The most basic method of rock climbing is the "three-point fixation" technique, which requires climbers to coordinate their hands and feet well; after fixing two hands and one foot or two feet and one hand, the remaining point can be moved to gradually elevate the body's center of gravity. When using this method, avoid jumping or making sudden advances, ensure that points are not moved simultaneously, and always maintain stability, lightness, and speed. Choose the most suitable distance and secure support points based on individual conditions, avoiding overly large strides or reaching too far.

Grassy slopes and scree slopes are the most widely distributed terrains in mountainous areas. Below an altitude of 3000 meters, aside from cliffs, most terrains consist of grassy and scree slopes. Climbing slopes with an incline less than 30 degrees can be done in a straight line. Slightly lean forward, place the entire foot on the ground, bend both knees slightly, and keep the feet in an outward V-shape. Avoid taking overly large or quick steps. When the slope exceeds 30 degrees, ascending in a straight line becomes difficult due to strain on the ankles and potential fatigue. Additionally, steeper slopes cause loose rocks to roll easily, increasing the risk of slipping. In such cases, the "zigzag" ascent method is generally used, where climbers advance diagonally following a zigzag pattern. While climbing, slightly bend the legs, lean forward, point the inner foot forward, place the entire foot on the ground, and slightly angle the outer foot outward. When traversing grassy slopes, avoid grabbing trees or pulling on grasses to prevent falling. On scree slopes, tread lightly to avoid dislodging stones. If accidentally slipping, immediately face the slope, spread arms wide, extend legs (toe up), and shift the body’s center of gravity upward to reduce sliding speed. Look for handholds or footholds during the slide. Never fall facing outward, as this can increase sliding speed and lead to rolling on steep slopes.

During rainy seasons, avoid low-lying areas like valleys and streams to prevent flash floods and landslides. During thunderstorms, seek shelter in low-lying areas or dense shrubbery rather than under tall trees, which attract lightning. During storms, temporarily store metal items in an accessible location or find a lower elevation spot to lie down. In severe weather conditions like snowstorms, heavy fog, or strong winds, stop moving and take shelter under cliffs or in caves until conditions improve.

Do not overestimate your physical capabilities when traveling in mountainous areas. Rest appropriately when fatigued. Avoid waiting until you're nearly exhausted before resting, as recovery will be harder. A good strategy is to alternate between brisk walking and slower-paced relaxation walks, or take short breaks to adjust breathing. When standing to rest, do not remove backpacks or gear; instead, prop them up with a stick to lighten the load. Avoid sitting directly on cold rocks, as they can quickly absorb body heat.

Climbing glaciers and snowy slopes requires extra caution. Glaciers have many crevasses, with icefall zones and edge crevasses posing the greatest danger, especially those hidden under snow. Traverse crevasses in groups connected by ropes, maintaining a distance of 10-12 meters between adjacent individuals. The lead climber should frequently test the firmness of the ground. Subsequent climbers should follow in the footsteps of the leader for safety. When crossing ice bridges, crawl to minimize pressure. On snowy slopes, watch for crevasses and avoid collapsing snow beneath you. At glacier and snow boundary intersections, travel in groups. Test snow bridge stability before crossing. If snow is very soft but unavoidable, crawl across. When climbing steep snowy slopes, ensure both feet are stable before moving. Take small steps, creating platforms with the front of your feet before moving the rear foot. If you slip, immediately lie prone to prevent sliding. Avoid areas with visible cracks when climbing icy and snowy slopes. On hard-packed snow, walk on firmer sections. Do not eat snow to quench thirst, as swallowing snow can cause throat inflammation. If extremely thirsty, rinse your mouth with melted snow but avoid swallowing it to prevent additional strain on circulation and preserve energy.

When walking long distances on soft snow, take large strides to minimize time spent on snow. Begin each step by slightly retracting the foot, then lifting it high and stepping forward. Retracting the foot creates space for movement, and the forward motion helps clear clinging snow. On steep slopes, use the inside edges of snowshoes to ascend, minimizing leaning towards the outside edges. If snow is frozen solid, consider removing snowshoes and walking barefoot. When traversing valleys, stay close to the centerline to avoid rolling rocks from slopes. Avoid snow cornices and never walk underneath them to prevent triggering avalanches. Cornices extend downward on leeward sides of ridgelines or cliffs. Gentle slopes on the windward side are more likely to form cornices, while steep slopes around 45 degrees are less so. Pay special attention in areas with unpredictable winds and varying ridge-side slopes.

In high-altitude areas, avalanches pose significant dangers. Avalanches are triggered by sound, vibrations, rolling rocks or snowballs, and wind effects. They typically occur on slopes where small snowballs roll, where snow has visible cracks, under cornices, on treeless slopes inclined at 36-42 degrees, long slopes, convex slopes, south- and southwest-facing slopes, and where new snow overlays old snow or temperatures rise causing snow to soften. Generally, avalanches occur the morning after a fresh snowfall, particularly between 9 and 10 AM.

When passing through avalanche-prone areas:

1. Loosen backpack straps to quickly discard large packs and other equipment if needed, ensuring freedom of movement.

2. Remove any caps that obstruct vision or hearing to detect early signs of avalanches.

3. Cross dangerous snow slopes horizontally.

4. Avoid actions that produce sounds or vibrations, such as shooting.

5. Avoid movements that impact the snow surface, like falling.

If caught in an avalanche, repeatedly perform swimming motions in the moving snow to try to float to the surface. Once the snow settles, it will be difficult to move, so maximize efforts to surface while the snow is still moving. If buried, allow saliva to flow from your mouth to determine the direction and whether you are upside down, then attempt self-rescue accordingly.
