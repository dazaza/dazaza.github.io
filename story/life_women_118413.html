Why do spots appear?

The formation of spots needs to be explained from several perspectives:

Classification of Spots:

1. Active Spots: Caused by active tyrosinase, making the nature unstable and affected by external light and endocrine factors, causing changes in color depth, can be removed, common types include melasma, sunspots, pregnancy spots, and black spots.

2. Fixed Spots: Nature is stable, not influenced by external factors, once removed, the original part will not grow back, common types include genetic spots, freckles, age spots, and pigmented moles.

Characteristics of Spots:

1. Freckles: Related to genetics, 3% related to autosomes and blood, after sun exposure, the color intensifies, distributed as isolated points with a diameter not exceeding 0.5 cm, commonly found on cheeks, hands, and shoulders, appearing around ages 5-7, cannot be completely eliminated, only lightened.

2. Melasma: Also known as butterfly spots or liver spots, appears as light brown, patchy, varying sizes, clear boundaries, irregular shapes, does not protrude from the skin, commonly seen on the face, symmetrical on both cheeks, mainly caused by endocrine and female hormone imbalance.

3. Pregnancy and Sunspots: Acquired factors, caused by ultraviolet rays, irregular in size.

4. Black Spots: Metal spots, rebound spots, acquired and related to cosmetics, long-term use of makeup or heavy metal cosmetics containing lead and mercury, can be rooted out.

5. Age Spots: Natural aging, physiological decline, slow metabolism, mostly protruding from the skin, commonly found on the face and hands.

Five Major Causes of Spots:

1. External Factors: Ultraviolet radiation, use of poor-quality cosmetics, external stimulation leading to abrasions.

2. Internal Factors: Endocrine imbalance:

A. Tyrosinase activity during pregnancy leading to melanin deposition.

B. Acid-base imbalance with an acidic constitution leads to slow metabolism, causing pigment deposition.

C. Malnutrition, lack of vitamins, slow metabolism, resulting in spots.

3. High stress, excessive fatigue, nervous system disorders, reduced adrenal cortex function, leading to tyrosinase activity, resulting in pigment deposition.

4. Photochemical reactions:

A. Antibiotics and contraceptives easily absorb ultraviolet rays.

B. Foods: Shellfish, seaweed.

C. Plants: Citrus family, mulberry family easily absorb ultraviolet rays.

5. Diseases:

A. Liver and kidney dysfunction, poor hematopoietic function, insufficient qi and blood, slow blood circulation, poor metabolism.

B. Diabetes causes overall fine flow function to decline, also slowing metabolism.

C. Excessive thyroid secretion, hyperthyroidism leads to tyrosinase activity, increasing pigmentation.

D. Skin diseases or acne, pimples cause pigment deposition.

E. Aging leads to reduced metabolic function.

Eye Wrinkles and Spots: Low immunity in the thyroid.

Spots under the eyes: Female hormone imbalance, uterine disease, excessive abortions or use of contraceptives.

Cheek Spots: Weak liver, UV exposure, insomnia.

Mouth Surrounding Spots: Uterus and ovarian gynecological diseases.

Neck: Perfume allergy.

Hairline: Internal issues or hair dyeing, poor liver function.

Characteristics of Eye Skin:

1. Thin skin, only one-quarter the thickness of facial skin, no sebaceous glands or sweat glands, prone to dryness due to oil deficiency.

2. Dermis composed of loose connective tissue: Easily sensitive to external stimuli, prone to poor blood circulation.

3. Rich in microvessels and lymphatic nerve tissues: Easily sensitive to external stimuli, prone to poor blood circulation.

4. Daily blinking frequency 1-20,000 times, easily causing eye fatigue, leading to wrinkles.

Causes of Eye Problems:

1. Formation of Dark Circles:

A. Genetic factors: Easy pigment deposition.

B. Poor lymphatic and blood circulation in the eyes, leading to blood stasis.

C. Due to prolonged sleep deprivation or allergic constitution, emotional instability.

D. Anemia during menstruation, liver disease.

E. Daily habits: Sleeping face down or covering the head tightly puts pressure on the eye tissues.

F. Use of inferior cosmetics, causing chronic poisoning, i.e., lead and mercury poisoning.

2. Formation of Bags Under the Eyes:

A. Edematous bags: Acquired.

B. Fat-induced bags: Inherited genetically.

C. Lymphatic circulation: Poor metabolism, water retention.

D. Aging eyelids, degeneration, relaxation, lower eyelid fat displacement and sagging.

E. Daily habits: Consuming high-salt foods or using cosmetics with high oil content, drinking alcohol, smoking, staying up late.

Wrinkles:

A. False wrinkles (fine lines): Rich facial expressions, dehydration, oil deficiency, collagen protein deficiency.

B. True wrinkles (deep lines): Collagen fibers harden and break after false wrinkles form.

Improvement Methods for the Eyes:

1. Guiding and activating eye circulation, improving poor blood circulation, avoiding excessive eye fatigue, and metabolizing accumulated water (i.e., eye massage).

2. Nourishment: Timely replenishment of nutrients needed for the eyes, promoting collagen synthesis.

3. Ensuring adequate sleep and maintaining a pleasant mood.

4. Diet: Balanced nutrition, eating more vitamin-rich foods.

How to Define Freckles?

Cause of Freckles?

"Freckles" are caused by genetic factors. "Freckle genes" increase tyrosinase activity in the basal layer under UV radiation, forming melanin, also called genetic spots, which are inherent from the fetal stage. Hereditary freckles are divided into visible spots and invisible spots. Visible spots usually start forming between ages 6-12, reaching their peak around age 18; invisible spots mostly appear on the face after pregnancy. This is why some people categorize freckles as congenital or acquired. However, hormonal changes during pregnancy stimulate hidden freckles to erupt, not implying that they are acquired later in life. All freckles are caused by genetic factors. Freckles have the characteristic of skipping generations, explaining why some children develop freckles even though neither parent has them. Although one or both parents may not show freckles, they carry the genetic factor and pass it on to their child.

Definition of Freckles

Freckles refer to scattered dark-brown spots on the face. According to traditional Chinese medicine, this condition is often associated with fire stagnation in the blood vessels or wind-heat in the lung meridian. Treatments include applying Yung Rong Powder mixed with water or white Poria powder to the affected area.

Why are they called freckles? Is it related to sparrows? Yes, the name is indeed related to sparrows. Specifically, it's related to sparrow eggs. People who have seen sparrow eggs should know why facial spots are called freckles. To be precise, freckles are referred to as "sparrow marks," originating from the phrase "facial appearance like scattered sesame seeds, similar to the color of sparrow eggs." Hence, they are called freckles. There are many colloquial names for freckles, such as "fly droppings," "soil spots," "silkworm sand" (Shanxi), "face-covering sand" (Henan), and "flea spots" (Suzhou-Zhejiang region).

Definition of Sunspots

What are Sunspots?

Hormonal imbalances—women most commonly experience chloasma. The formation of spots is primarily linked to internal health conditions and glandular functions, especially those involving organs like the ovaries, breasts, and pituitary gland, all of which secrete estrogen. When these organs malfunction, hormone levels become unbalanced, manifesting as spots on the face. This is more common in women who take contraceptives long-term or suffer from pelvic inflammatory disease or uterine fibroids.

Skincare products with metallic components—some people use skincare products rich in lead, mercury, or other chemical metals, or those containing fragrances. These chemicals absorb light and easily cause pigmentation. Additionally, some individuals store these products in hot environments, causing them to degrade. Using degraded products can irritate the skin, leading to itching and increased susceptibility to sunspots upon exposure to light.

Excessive chemical peeling—some people pursue whitening through chemical peels, believing more frequent treatments yield better results. However, too-frequent peeling makes the skin more sensitive to light, dries it out, and increases the likelihood of red rashes upon sun exposure. This can lead to increased pigmentation, worsening the condition.

Definition of Pregnancy Spots

Pregnancy-related "fetal spots," also known as chloasma, butterfly spots, or pigmentation, are caused by hormonal changes during pregnancy. Most pregnant women experience darker pigmentation of the nipples, areolas, midline of the abdomen, and genital areas, with varying degrees of intensity. Existing moles may also darken. Some pregnant women develop brownish spots on their faces after four months of pregnancy, typically distributed across the nose bridge and cheeks, resembling a butterfly shape, known as "pregnancy spots." However, sufficient skin oil secretion, balanced pH, and smooth metabolism can prevent the occurrence of spots during pregnancy.

Reasons for Pregnancy Spots in Pregnant Women

This type of pigmentation occurs due to increased secretion of melanocyte-stimulating hormone by the pituitary gland during pregnancy, along with elevated progesterone and estrogen levels, enhancing the function of melanocytes. This is a physiological change during pregnancy and does not require concern or treatment. Sun exposure can worsen pregnancy spots, so wearing a hat to avoid direct sunlight is advisable. Several months postpartum, the pigmentation fades and eventually disappears, although sometimes it leaves behind a faint brown trace.

Definition of Age Spots

What are Age Spots?

Age spots, medically termed "senile pigmented spots," are brownish pigment patches that appear on elderly skin, commonly seen in older individuals, also referred to as "longevity spots." Modern medical research indicates that this term is misleading, as it does not signify longevity. Studies show that age spots are not universal among the elderly, affecting only 27%.

Cause of Age Spots

After entering the 60-70 age range, with advancing age, the number of age spots gradually increases, and their area expands. Currently, there are three theories regarding the cause of age spots:

1. After entering old age, cellular metabolic function declines, and fats in the body oxidize, producing senescent pigments. These pigments cannot be expelled from the body and thus accumulate in the cells, forming age spots.

2. As people age, metabolism slows, cell function deteriorates, blood circulation becomes sluggish, and dietary changes and imbalances in animal and plant fat intake contribute to the accumulation of lipofuscin, tiny brown particles, in the basal layer of the skin. Lipofuscin is a product of lipid peroxidation reactions. Aging tissue cells lose their ability to decompose and expel these particles, leading to excessive accumulation within local cell basal layers, forming age spots.

3. In old age, the activity of superoxide dismutase, which counteracts oxidation, decreases, increasing free radicals. Free radicals and their induced peroxidation reactions chronically harm biological organisms. During metabolism, a substance called "free radicals" or lipofuscin accumulates on the skin surface, forming age spots.

In youth, natural antioxidants and antioxidant enzymes in the body neutralize free radicals into inert compounds, preventing the formation of peroxidized lipids and avoiding cellular damage. However, with aging, the body's antioxidant function declines, and free radicals begin to cause damage in old age. Generally, age spots are considered precursors to tissue aging, indicating that cells have entered the aging phase. Lipofuscin not only accumulates on the skin but also invades internal tissues. If it deposits on vessel walls, it can cause fibrous lesions, leading to arteriosclerosis, hypertension, and myocardial infarction. When it accumulates in brain cells, it affects brain function, accelerating brain aging, and may cause memory and cognitive impairments, depression, or even dementia. Accumulation of this substance within cells hinders normal metabolism, leading to systemic aging and eventual death.
