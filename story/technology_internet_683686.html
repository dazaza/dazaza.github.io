According to relevant census data, hemorrhoids are the most common disease of the anal and rectal region. Anyone can get this disease, and the older you get, the higher the probability of getting it. However, due to many people not having a clear understanding of hemorrhoids, they often neglect treatment, leading to worsening conditions and even complications.

So why do hemorrhoids develop? Experts from the Yangzhou Armed Police Hospital's Anorectal Center explain that there are various reasons for the development of hemorrhoids, and the majority are related to poor living habits. The main causes can be summarized in eight points:

1. Dietary reasons: As the saying goes, "disease enters through the mouth." Poor dietary habits are often the cause of diseases. The quality of food (coarse or fine), changes in vegetable types and quantities, the content of proteins, fats, starches, fiber, water intake, all directly affect stool composition, leading to anorectal diseases.

2. Physiological reasons: The colon and cecum are the primary organs responsible for transporting food residue and storing feces. After food is broken down and absorbed within the body, the residues often contain large amounts of harmful substances. Long-term retention of these residues in the colon and cecum can lead to tumors.

3. Bad defecation habits: Many people like to read newspapers or books while using the toilet, which leads to prolonged squatting and extended defecation times, causing blood stasis in the anus and cecum, thereby triggering disease. Some people strain too hard during defecation, blindly exerting force regardless of the intensity of the urge to defecate, increasing unnecessary strain on the cecum, anus, and pelvic floor muscles, as well as local blood stasis, leading to the occurrence and progression of the disease.

4. Abnormal bowel movements: Diarrhea and constipation are the main causes of hemorrhoids. Long-term retention of toxic substances in the cecum can not only lead to cecal cancer but also affect blood circulation due to accumulated feces.

5. Chronic diseases: Long-term malnutrition, physical weakness, leading to relaxation of the anal sphincter muscle. Chronic bronchitis, emphysema, coughing, and increased abdominal pressure result in pelvic blood stasis. Chronic hepatitis, cirrhosis, diarrhea, colitis, etc., are all inducements for anorectal diseases.

6. Anatomical reasons: The venous system of the anus and the venous system of the cavity have many venous plexuses and anastomotic branches at the lower end of the cecum. The vein walls are weak, reducing resistance to pressure. The loose tissue under the cecal mucosa facilitates venous dilation and varicose deformation, making it easy for hemorrhoids to form.

7. Embryonic developmental abnormalities: Developmental abnormalities during the embryonic stage can lead to many congenital anorectal diseases in the anal and cecum areas, such as congenital anal atresia, congenital cecal-vaginal fistula, congenital megacolon, etc.

8. Genetic reasons.
