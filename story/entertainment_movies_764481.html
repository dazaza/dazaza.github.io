Behind-the-scenes production:

From Children's Picture Book to Film

Forty-five years ago, Harper & Row published a children's picture book titled "Where the Wild Things Are." This 10-sentence, 338-word children's book quickly topped various sales charts and won the prestigious Caldecott Medal, often referred to as the Oscar of illustrated books. Its author, Maurice Sendak, became an instant favorite in the world of illustrated literature. In the same year of its publication, animators from the United States and Czechoslovakia adapted the story into a 7-minute animated short. Ever since then, Maurice Sendak had harbored the dream of bringing this story to life as a feature-length film. In the 1990s, he approached Spike Jonze with the hope that he would adapt the story into a movie. However, after reading the script, Jonze didn't show any immediate eagerness to direct this classic; instead, he casually said, "I'll think about it."

This contemplation lasted for six years during which time he directed the critically acclaimed films "Being John Malkovich" and "Adaptation." After watching these films, Maurice Sendak became even more convinced that Spike Jonze was the most suitable director for this project. He remarked, "When I saw 'Being John Malkovich,' I was astounded. It was a movie that existed within all of us. I wanted my book to be directed by Jonze without a doubt, and he was clearly the best choice." Despite having identified the ideal director, there was still a major challenge ahead—writing the screenplay.

In an interview, the usually reserved Jonze admitted that the biggest difficulty with the entire film was the script. He explained, "The original work is a very simple fairy tale consisting of just ten sentences. Since it's intended for children, it can't be too complicated or difficult. However, turning it into a standard-length movie poses a significant challenge. I spent a great deal of time expanding the storyline. This is a classic story, so it couldn't be adapted using overly trendy techniques or conventional methods to stretch its length. What I needed to do was find a good screenwriter and collaborate with him on how to write this script." Jonze partnered with screenwriter Dave Eggers, who at the time only had one screenplay credit to his name—Sam Mendes's "Away We Go." After discussions, they decided to focus the movie's narrative on Max's journey of growth, ultimately transforming the story into an 111-page screenplay. Jonze commented, "This isn't an action movie or a science fiction movie. I consider it a road movie. A child runs away from home and encounters not people like himself but various kinds-hearted monsters. After becoming the king of the wild things, he realizes that the world isn't as simple as he imagined, and thus he needs to grow and mature. At this point, the movie is no longer solely for children. Adults can find their lost childhood and memories of being young, seeing how they evolved from naivety to maturity. Perhaps growing up makes us lose some things, but that's the price we pay."

Painstaking Filming and Diligent Post-production

Maurice Sendak, known as the Picasso of fairy tales, created a variety of unique creatures in his book. Thus, depicting these animals in the film became the second biggest challenge after the script. Spike Jonze remarked, "On one hand, these creatures are dangerous; on the other, they're cute and irresistible to touch. I imagine them existing in our world alongside Max, making his surroundings filled with both danger and humor."

To realistically portray these creatures, Spike Jonze enlisted the help of tall local actors in Melbourne, who wore specially designed costumes to play the beasts. Since some of the creatures in the novel were over three meters tall, the actors had to wear stilts and have frames inside their costumes to compensate for their height. Due to the heaviness of the costumes, the actors had to take breaks every half hour to remove their headgear and cool off with air conditioning for 15 minutes. Unfortunately, stunt performers didn't have such luxuries—they had to endure wearing the costumes for an hour and couldn't use air conditioning to preserve the integrity of the fur. Jonze commented, "This was the toughest and strangest part of filming. We envisioned a world where humans and animals coexist. Sometimes on set, I felt disoriented, as if the world of 'Where the Wild Things Are' truly existed. And the young actor I found was truly exceptional. I can't imagine anyone else playing Max. He belongs to a rare type of child—profound, thoughtful, adorable, sincere, wild, imaginative, and empathetic. He poured all of these qualities into the movie."

During post-production, another challenge arose: how to give the beasts realistic expressions and emotions. The Jim Henson Company, which has collaborated with Spike Jonze multiple times, took on this task. Jim Henson, originally a renowned puppeteer and now the company's owner, stated, "Although we didn't use any new technology, our approach was entirely new. Previously, adding expressions to characters like these mostly involved computer-generated imagery, with technicians referencing their own expressions. This time, however, we hired stand-up comedians from bars to make various facial expressions, which we photographed and captured using motion tracking systems. These data weren't from just one person but from a group of individuals. Therefore, the expression data represents an average value. We then compiled and analyzed this data before synthesizing it onto the animals. As a result, audiences can see incredibly lifelike and detailed expressions on the monsters' faces on IMAX screens."

Fun Facts:

- The film is adapted from a best-selling children's picture book of the same name published in 1963. The original author, Maurice Sendak, is a celebrated American children's novelist. In 1964, the book won the prestigious Caldecott Medal, often called the Oscars of picture books, and its manuscript was permanently archived in the U.S. National Library.

- Maurice Sendak's book was previously adapted into a 7-minute animated short in 1973.

- Eric Goldberg, the director of "Fantasia 2000," was once chosen to direct this film.

- To find the right actor to play Max, the production team held auditions for thousands of boys across several countries over several months.

- As early as the 1990s, Maurice Sendak approached Spike Jonze, hoping he would adapt the novel into a movie. However, Sendak understood that finding the right director and screenwriter wasn't easy, so the plan was shelved until 2005.

- During the scriptwriting process, Spike Jonze and Dave Eggers sent daily updates of the script and creature models to Maurice Sendak for review.

- Upon learning that "Where the Wild Things Are" was one of President Barack Obama's favorite novels, Maurice Sendak sent signed copies to Obama's two daughters. Soon after, he received a handwritten letter from Obama simply saying "Thank you. Obama."

- To bring the beasts' expressions to life, the special effects team collected facial data from dozens of actors and synthesized these expressions onto the beasts using computers.

- Spike Jonze initially wanted to make this film as an animated movie.

- The film was originally produced by Universal Pictures. However, due to disagreements between Spike Jonze and the studio executives, he took the film to Warner Bros. Unsatisfied with the final product, Warner executives even considered reinvesting $75 million to reshoot the entire film in early 2008. Later, the studio gave Spike Jonze more time and budget to revise the film, increasing the total budget to $90 million.

- Filming began in 2005. Warner Bros. initially planned to release the film in May 2008, then postponed it to October 2008. Due to dissatisfaction with the film's quality, the release date was delayed by a full year.

- This is the first film ever shot entirely on IMAX format film.
