Speaking frankly about domestic and foreign affairs, calling for the rise of heroes; breaking the Western blockade, aiming to be a superpower!

In 1996, the book "China Can Say No" made a stunning appearance, calmly analyzing the relations between China and the West, which at that time triggered discussions and controversies worldwide. From "China Can Say No" to "China Is Not Happy," twelve years have passed, during which China has transformed from a country that "only wants to lead itself" into one that is "capable of leading the world." It clearly contains fewer emotional colors compared to "China Can Say No" from twelve years ago, being a "revival declaration" that calls for "addressing domestic grievances," "invoking a noble group," and "becoming a heroic nation." In this sense, "China Is Not Happy" can indeed be considered an upgraded version of "China Can Say No."

The book severely criticizes "popular elites" such as Long Yongtu, Li Yining, Lin Yifu, Fan Gang, Ding Xueliang, Cui Weiping, Zhu Xueqin, Yu Shicun, Song Hongbing, Ma Licheng, Wang Meng, Wang Shuo, Li Yinhé, and Zhou Xiaozheng, focusing on criticizing "defeatism," "know-it-alls," and "cultural sadism with cleverness," hoping to positively alert and promote the construction of morality and culture within the country.

This book represents the mainstream intellectual interpretation of "nationalism" and also looks forward to the thoughts of the younger generation represented by the "May Youth" of 2008 who hope to actively participate in social life.

"China Is Not Happy" Related Information

Our China needs a group of heroes, a true hero group. I don't know how many people are needed, but there must be quite a number; I don't believe that one or two individuals can solve problems. We need such a hero group to lead our nation to complete the task of managing and utilizing more resources in the world, as well as eliminating evil and restoring peace. We need institutional construction and cultural construction. Cultural construction refers to what I call the martial spirit.

"China Is Not Happy" Part One

The relationship between China and the West may have been somewhat vague before, what scholars refer to as structural contradictions. All these contradictions began to become clear in 2008. The incident of the overseas relay of the Olympic torch before the Wenchuan earthquake attracted attention from many levels both domestically and internationally. The most uncertain aspect was that no one expected the performance of Chinese youth in 2008 to be so bold and decisive, showing action and strength. Knowledgeable and cultured young people could respond so quickly. What made them burst with instant cohesive power? This question is very worthy of attention.

"China Is Not Happy" Part Two

The economic crisis of 2008 should say that everyone was a victim, and now it seems that everyone is pinning their hopes on China. However, as time goes by, China's own economic problems are becoming increasingly evident. What is the essence of this problem? Is it that we lack a "big goal" or that the "big goal" is wrong? This is a question that cannot be avoided.

"China Is Not Happy" Part Three

A website editor wanted me to comment on Obama's new book "We Believe in Change." He sent me the book, and I could only flip through it casually, writing a review somehow. Fortunately, although this book appears thick, it doesn't have many words, and you can finish flipping through it quickly. Frankly speaking, this is a book full of empty talk, boasting nonsense, and difficult to read. Its only value lies in providing us with a window to understand the most powerful person in the world.

"China Is Not Happy" Part Four

Our China needs a group of heroes, a true hero group. I don't know how many people are needed, but there must be quite a number; I don't believe that one or two individuals can solve problems. We need such a hero group to lead our nation to complete the task of managing and utilizing more resources in the world, as well as eliminating evil and restoring peace. We need institutional construction and cultural construction. Cultural construction refers to what I call the martial spirit. Unfortunately, in recent years, my idea has almost been completely erased from the vision of China's elite class. Now, only some military enthusiasts still possess such a vision, but military enthusiasts do not belong to the elite class. As soon as you mention this, either they say you're an idiot, or they say you're very evil.
