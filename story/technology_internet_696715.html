The Seven Unbreakable Rules of the Luxury Goods Industry: The Historical DNA, LOGO, and Exclusivity of Luxury Brands.

by liwuwang2012 on 2012-03-08 10:10:58

One day before the Spring Festival, Mark Ritson, who teaches at London Business School, was inexplicably taken to an old house in the suburbs of Paris by a luxury brand company.

After the driver in a black suit left him in that house, he had no choice but to walk around and study the house. Mark read books, looked at portraits, and visited museums inside.

[Before we talk about luxury brands and sales figures, we must understand the essence of this brand—what its roots are, from which person, period, or place it originates. Once you clearly see its history, then you can move forward. This is also the reason why that world-famous luxury brand company took me to that old house back then.] Mark emphasized while gesturing continuously at the CEIBS Top Brand Summit Forum.

Mark Ritson, currently an associate professor of marketing at Melbourne Business School, agrees with the notion that [there are no rules in the business world]. However, in the mysterious land of luxury goods, the management of luxury brands differs from that of regular brands. Mark believes: [The seven commandments of the luxury industry cannot be broken, and they hold practical significance whether it's four or five hundred years ago or in the future.]

Commandment One: [Remember your roots].

When discussing brand management, professors and entrepreneurs often believe that brand building requires brand equity, differentiation, customer association, etc. [But these don't apply to luxury goods], Mark points out, [luxury brands have long histories, and their roots lie in the continuation of that history. To move forward, one must first look back.]

Commandment Two: [Understand your DNA].

Analysis: [DNA determines the behavior of luxury brands.] [Uncovering the DNA of a brand is a pragmatic process; it requires returning to the place, time, and founder of its invention. Luxury brands must adhere to these DNAs and must not conform to what customers hope you will become, otherwise, you will face doom.] Mark said.

Commandment Three: [A brand is more than just a logo].

[Logos can work like magic to enhance brand effects. Some luxury brands make the worst mistakes by wasting money on ads that fail to show what brand they represent. In this regard, Hermes has been very successful. The elements in their advertisements resonate with the logo, conveying the brand's genetic makeup.] Mark said, [Logos can also convey the legacy between the past and future of a brand, as well as a sense of freshness that keeps up with the times.]

Commandment Four: [Embody renewal].

Many luxury brands have a history spanning over 100 years, but how can products loved by the grandparents' generation also appeal to today’s consumers? This requires luxury brands to constantly change and embody renewal. Mark believes: [The most important challenge for luxury brands is consistency. The greatest paradox of luxury is that it needs constant change to remain loyal to its DNA. Although DNA cannot be repositioned, renewal can still be embodied.]

Commandment Five: [Creativity].

In Mark's view, managing luxury brands is very challenging because it's not about managing a consumer good but rather managing a creative challenge. [Managers of luxury brands need creativity to truly manage a luxury brand well. They need the creativity to make the elephant dance, but we must admit that managers and artists are different. So if it's a collaborative model, managers should choose the right creative director and support them. In front of them, luxury executives need to learn to keep quiet and let the artists talk freely. Jack Welch's management philosophy does not work in luxury brand management.] Mark said.

Commandment Six: [Exclusivity].

Luxury brands serve only a niche audience, perhaps just 5% or even 2% of the population, and others can be ignored. A luxury brand cannot try to do everything—targeting both young and old consumers, both fat and thin consumers—otherwise, it becomes a vanilla ice cream without any distinct brand identity.

[Luxury brands must have exclusivity, rejecting the majority of people and preventing most people from buying the product. Although this is not easy, it is the key to the success of luxury goods.]

Commandment Seven: [Break the rules].

[Luxury brands must continuously break the rules and let others follow them.] Mark believes, [luxury brands have no categories or rules. For example, what is Hublot? Is it a watch? No, it's a belief, a kind of energy.]
