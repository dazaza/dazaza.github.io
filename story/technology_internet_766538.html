Cloud smartphones will pose a threat to the Android and iPhone ecosystems

In 2005, Nokia was still the world's largest mobile phone manufacturer, enjoying its peak period. However, today it has fallen to the point of being broken up and sold off. Although based on the current situation, it seems no manufacturer can replace Samsung and Apple's leadership in the smartphone sector, but life is unpredictable, and no one dares to guarantee that they won't repeat Nokia's history.

Dr. Ronald Klingebiel, a professor at Warwick Business School, stated that neither Samsung nor Apple’s positions are secure. A new wave of cloud-based smartphone operating systems is beginning to emerge, each demonstrating its own competitive advantages.

Professor Klingebiel said that the advantages of these cloud-based smartphone operating systems mainly lie in two aspects: first, the accompanying application resources are abundant. Developers can write software for the cloud rather than for a specific model of a phone, much like cloud applications on the PC platform can be compatible with numerous systems such as Windows, Mac, or Linux. The second advantage is the low cost of the phones. Since applications run in the cloud, the demand for processors and memory is very low, thus reducing the cost of the phone.

Currently, Samsung’s Tizen, Firefox OS, Ubuntu Edge, and Sailfish OS seem to have not yet gained significant traction, but Professor Klingebiel said that they will soon seize a large share of the market.

Professor Klingebiel gave an interview to the well-known tech media outlet Business Insider, excerpts of which follow:

BI: There are already quite a few alternative mobile operating systems on the market. Which ones do you think have the best chance of succeeding? Why?

Klingebiel: These four new mobile operating systems each have their strengths and all have opportunities to succeed, depending on how ambitious and capable the companies behind them are. These mobile operating systems enter the market from different angles: Tizen leverages Samsung’s overall advantages; Firefox focuses on the low-end market; Ubuntu aims to establish itself within the Linux PC community, unifying phones and computing devices; Sailfish concentrates on the tech enthusiast market, who particularly admire Nokia and MeeGo's high-quality solutions.

In general, they will soon gain a foothold in the market, and it won’t be a small one.

BI: Which part of the smartphone market will they threaten? High-end, low-end, or feature phones? Why?

Klingebiel: These operating systems are all smartphone operating systems. But soon, smartphones may shift towards new phones that can access smart clouds, where apps on the phone are just links pointing to software in the cloud. Using this kind of cloud smartphone does not require a high level of technical expertise. If this solution becomes an industry standard, it will affect all areas of the current smartphone market.

BI: When do you think these new phones will begin to show their influence on the market?

Klingebiel: These new mobile operating systems are still in the early stages of development, and no one can guarantee their success. But these new systems have the potential to grow rapidly. If only one manufacturer, such as Lenovo, commits to supporting one of these new operating systems, it could drive the overall development of the new operating system. Mobile operators also hope to change the status quo. Larger app developers like Facebook might insist on writing applications once, leaving Apple and Google with no choice but to open their app stores.
