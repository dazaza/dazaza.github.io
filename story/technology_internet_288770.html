Wenzhou property speculation groups in the desert

by 3rtq101s9z on 2009-12-02 10:13:23

Among the 150,000 Chinese in Dubai, businessmen from Zhejiang account for more than 20,000, a large portion of whom are from Wenzhou. They once became addicted to a "hot potato" real estate game in the desert here, but now that the bubble has burst, many of them have no intention of retreating. "Those who took over the properties in Dubai before the crisis will definitely be stuck," said Wang Weisheng, chairman of Huaxing Group and a native of Wenzhou, on November 30, while watching shocking reports about Dubai on TV. His tone was not anxious, even a bit relaxed, despite admitting that he still holds a certain amount of Dubai real estate.

Last weekend, a storm originating from Dubai shook the global financial markets. With the Dubai government holding company declaring a six-month suspension of debt repayment for "Dubai World," a desert real estate myth built on palm islands, seven-star hotels, and the world's tallest building collapsed. It is currently unclear how many Wenzhounese capitalists are buried in the ruins of this collapse.

Among the 150,000 Chinese in Dubai, businessmen from Zhejiang account for more than 20,000, a large portion of whom are from Wenzhou. They once became addicted to a "hot potato" real estate game in the desert here, driving astonishing bubbles towards explosion amidst 100% profit madness. Now, the myth has shattered, but many of them have no intention of retreating.

"The streets of Dubai are now empty, unlike what they used to be," Chen Wendu, a Wenzhounese businessman who has been in the clothing business in Dubai for eight years, told reporters on November 30. "Despite the depression, I do not intend to leave."

At this moment, the Wenzhou merchants seem to recall their initial purpose of coming here - using this largest trade free port in the Middle East to distribute "Made in China" globally. This solid ground after the bubble bursts can help them regain a sense of security.

The "pass-the-flower" game in the desert

The property speculation methods of Wenzhou merchants in Dubai interpret the evolution of the Dubai crisis from one perspective.

"Among the thousands of Wenzhounese in Dubai, basically none of them did not invest in the local real estate market," said Chen Zhiyuan, president of the Wenzhou Chamber of Commerce in the UAE. Currently, he knows of more than 20 Wenzhounese who have abandoned their real estate investments in Dubai, selling off already purchased or partially paid-for properties worth hundreds of thousands of US dollars.

Hu Bin, chairman of Shanghai Zhongzhou Group, is rumored to be one of the more severely affected Wenzhounese merchants in this incident.

Since January 2007, Dubai's average housing prices have doubled. By October last year, local housing prices had exceeded $4,300 per square meter. Under this wave of skyrocketing housing prices, Hu Bin arrived in Dubai.

In October 2007, Hu Bin spent $28 million to purchase "Shanghai Island" in Dubai's World Islands, planning to invest 1.2 billion yuan to develop 58 villas on the island covering a total area of 111 acres. It is said that at the signing site, 12 of these villas, which had yet to lay a single brick, were pre-sold.

Starting from September 2008, Dubai's housing prices plummeted sharply. Morgan Stanley reported in its report earlier this month that the decline reached 25%.

"As a result, Hu Bin only completed the 'shell' of Shanghai Island and got stuck," revealed someone closely acquainted with Hu Bin. "This is a failed project."

However, there are other rumors within the circle stating that Hu Bin's Zhongzhou Group only paid a 15% deposit, equivalent to over 20 million RMB, to the developer Nakheel Group in November 2007, and thereafter did not pay another cent.

As of press time, the reporter could not contact Hu Bin himself to verify these two statements. However, the latter statement aligns with the investment model commonly practiced in the Dubai real estate market.

"Whether as local developers or individual investors, the group of investors from Wenzhou generally adopted a similar model," explained Wang Weisheng. Investments were divided into long-term and short-term categories; residential units were sold upon completion for quick profits, while entertainment centers, health centers, and hotels were considered long-term investments, managed by others after construction for sustained returns.

Among individual investors, Wenzhou merchants in Dubai preferred short-term trading due to the freedom of transactions in Dubai.

Wenzhou businessman Mr. Jin, engaged in real estate development in Dubai, said that Dubai's real estate transactions do not require a pre-sale license. Purchases can be made based on blueprints alone. Generally, investors need only pay a 10% down payment to the developer, followed by an additional 5% to 10% payment three to five months later. After the second payment, few Wenzhounese investors continued to hold onto the "properties," often reselling immediately. In this process, a property valued at 3 million could yield 100% profit.

"We previously invested in over 900 plots of land. Payments can be made in stages: 10% upfront, another 10% after six months, and then another 10% six months later. Once the land is purchased and plans and models are ready, it can be sold, allowing for rapid capital recovery," Mr. Jin said.

Due to the existence of extremely high leverage, Wang Weisheng believes that the actual losses incurred by Wenzhounese merchants in this round of Dubai's real estate crisis may not be as large as imagined externally.

Under the support of the "property speculation" craze, Dubai's real estate market soared continuously since it opened to foreign individuals in 2003. Good locations rose from originally around 400-500 dirhams per square foot to around 1,800 dirhams, equivalent to over 20,000 RMB per square meter. Before the financial crisis, prices peaked at three to four thousand US dollars per square foot.

Mr. Jin's investment in Dubai's real estate amounted to over 20 billion RMB. Before the crisis, the rents on his properties increased dramatically each year. Now, blow after blow has battered his capital chain, forcing some of his developments to halt.

The ups and downs of a Wenzhou-funded television station

Wang Weisheng, who once acted as a bridge connecting Wenzhou capital with Dubai's real estate, originally engaged in commodity trade alongside thousands of other Wenzhounese merchants in Dubai. In 2006, he underwent a transformation, partnering with others to acquire 100% equity in Al Deyar Satellite TV, later renamed Arab·Asia Business TV (hereafter referred to as ABTV).

When ABTV officially launched on August 1, 2006, Wang Weisheng initiated a completely different profit model from its predecessor—shifting ABTV's target audience to the most active regions of Chinese capital, the Yangtze River Delta and Pearl River Delta. Taking advantage of frequent overseas investments by Chinese enterprises, ABTV organized business travel inspections to lead Chinese merchants in examining real estate development, tourism investment, manufacturing, and civil aviation in Ras Al Khaimah, UAE, facilitating local investment attraction. While promoting Dubai, it expanded the Middle Eastern market through co-hosting programs with government agencies in Guangdong and Zhejiang provinces.

At this time, surging oil prices pushed the "Dubai model" into a sweet spot, with massive inflows of private Chinese funds into Dubai's real estate sector. The business inspections organized by ABTV once set a record of signing over 7 billion yuan worth of real estate development projects in a single month. ABTV's profit prospects were also optimistic, attracting three Wenzhounese shareholders from traditional manufacturing sectors in 2006, who collectively held 20% of Asia Business TV.

Wang Weisheng acquired the shares of a founding partner who withdrew due to immigration reasons, increasing his stake in ABTV to 80%. Consequently, his funding requirements multiplied. To address this, ABTV initiated a new round of capital operations in July 2008 to introduce strategic investors.

Originally, several domestic institutions actively approached Wang Weisheng for discussions, valuing ABTV at up to 500 million yuan.

Just then, the financial tsunami extinguished the flames. The collapse of the Dubai model further exacerbated the dire situation.

After the financial tsunami, the Dubai model began to decline, "Chinese enterprises scaled back product promotions in the Middle East, causing a significant reduction in ABTV's previously thriving advertising business," Wang Weisheng said. Meanwhile, the number of investment inspections from various parts of China to the Middle East also decreased. Both major profit points of ABTV were impacted.

Also affected were the bloodlines of the TV station—ABTV's shareholders all came from heavily hit sectors such as manufacturing and overseas trade, including Wang Weisheng himself.

"Global liquidity tightening, reduced international credit ratings for Dubai, and external environments significantly impacted ABTV's capital strategy." Since the beginning of the year, Wang Weisheng has frequently traveled in and out of the country, shuttling between Hangzhou, Wenzhou, and Shanghai, staying nearly half a month in Wenzhou this time. But the fundraising negotiations remain stalled.

Nevertheless, Wang Weisheng optimistically believes that "difficulties are temporary," still viewing ABTV's future positively.

"The Middle East, with its lagging manufacturing sector, remains an important market for Chinese exports. In fact, during this round of crises, China's trade with the Middle East suffered the least impact, far less than trade with Europe and America," Wang Weisheng said. Capable Chinese enterprises can strengthen brand promotion in the Middle East during this low period to lay foundations for future development. All these require media platforms to play a role.

Bottom fishing in the free trade port

"From an operating cost perspective, entering Dubai right now is indeed an opportunity," judged Qiu Xuefan on November 30.

Qiu Xuefan, a Wenzhounese merchant who started in inflatable toys, established an office in Dubai in 2007 but was forced to close it after half a year of operation. "The soaring housing prices at the time were our main reason for withdrawing from Dubai."

Qiu Xuefan compared Dubai with Shanghai, China's benchmark city for high housing prices, finding that even relatively remote areas in Dubai had rental prices two to three times higher than those in Gubei New District, the most concentrated area for Japanese and Korean merchants in Shanghai. Employees needed to earn over 20,000 RMB per month just to cover living costs like rent.

"The current depression in Dubai's market should be short-term," said Qiu Xuefan, who was both repelled by Dubai's high housing prices and captivated by its trade freedom. "We will return to Dubai, we're just waiting for the best timing."

Many aspects of Dubai excite traders from Wenzhou, the manufacturing hub, such as allowing foreign businesses to establish wholly-owned enterprises, enjoying zero income tax and business tax, and unrestricted remittance allowing 100% profit repatriation. Dubai has developed a series of distinctive free zones, where thousands of companies have gathered in recent years.

As one of the seven emirates of the UAE, resource-poor Dubai once determined to transform itself into a pure free port, enabling Arab wealth and global hot money to transit and redistribute here.

Similar to Qiu Xuefan, what kept Chen Wen stationed in Dubai was also its trade freedom. "Most transactions here are cash-based, very straightforward."
