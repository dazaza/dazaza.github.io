by hartyqekb on 2009-12-02 17:27:30

The workplace is like a big melting pot. It is not just about earning a living, but more importantly, it is about learning the principles of being a good person. If you don't even understand the most basic principles, you will not only fail to gain others' respect, but also lose more development opportunities. Because in China, interpersonal relationships are also a form of productivity. There is an ancient Chinese saying: "Those who follow the righteous path will receive much assistance; those who stray from it will find themselves isolated." I have seen many people who, despite having worked for many years, do not even understand the most basic common sense about being a person. The principles of being a person are myriad, but among them, the following three are the most important:

1. Be strict with yourself and tolerant towards others. The workplace is different from life. Although the relationships between colleagues at work may seem good on the surface, you always feel something is missing. A very obvious point is that everyone is using each other, and conflicts inevitably arise due to interest relationships. At this time, if you nitpick, it will surely cause unpleasantness among colleagues. In such situations, to avoid disputes and win others' respect, you need to learn to be more generous and tolerant. When problems occur, first consider whether there is an issue with yourself rather than immediately blaming others (if you continue like this, your mindset will become unbalanced, making it difficult for you to tolerate others). Being strict with yourself and tolerant towards others is necessary. Over time, you will accumulate a lot of connections and gain everyone's respect; when you need help, they will be willing to lend a hand.

2. Learn to listen. Listening demonstrates a person's professional素养 anytime. When conversing with others, remember to wait until the other person finishes speaking before expressing your opinion. Do not interrupt or cut them off. Through this method, everyone will be willing to interact with you, which will also help establish your image in their minds.

3. Honesty and integrity. Although the workplace is filled with mutual exploitation and the pursuit of interests, honesty and integrity remain traditional virtues. Integrity can also bring benefits. As long as you are honest and trustworthy, over time, everyone will form a good impression of you and be willing to interact with you. Those who are two-faced and act one way in front of others and another behind their backs may gain small advantages, but to achieve great success, the basic principle of honesty and integrity must never be abandoned.

Firstly, learn to abide by the rules of the game.

Why did I remind the "angry youth" or "pseudo-angry youth" not to read this article at the beginning? Because they are mostly unrestrained and unwilling to follow the rules! Many people feel that in the workplace, not only are there various constraints, but unfair phenomena abound everywhere. For example, if you are late to work, your boss will deduct your salary, but when you work overtime, you never receive overtime pay. This is a very common phenomenon, and other unfair phenomena are also prevalent. I won't elaborate here since everyone has experienced it.

In this world, do not expect fairness. That is merely a utopian ideal that does not exist in real society. The relationship between employees and bosses is like a game. Every game has its own set of rules; otherwise, the game cannot proceed. But who sets the rules? Of course, it's the boss, and all the benefits lean towards the boss! Initially, you might find this phenomenon disagreeable, which is normal because you are new to the workplace and essentially still pure-hearted. But you must learn to gradually accept these game rules. Don't try to change the rules because you have little say in front of the boss. To put it bluntly, you are just an employee, working for food. It's already good that you are given food to eat; are you complaining that it's cold? If you don't want to work, you can leave. Remember this: "China lacks everything, but it does not lack people."

Remember this sentence: "If you are not the maker of the game rules, you have only two choices: either submit or leave!"

Secondly, opportunities favor prepared minds.

No matter how much you complain or how unhappy you are, you still need to eat, sleep, and work. Future matters always belong to oneself. Whether it's buying a house or getting married, the pressure rests solely on your shoulders. The boss won't worry about any of it. Instead of spending time complaining, it's better to focus and improve your basic skills, prove your abilities through action, and make others look at you differently. Do not ruin your future by being overly idealistic and becoming an "angry youth."

Of course, your preparation involves multiple aspects. Besides professional skills, other auxiliary skills are also necessary, such as communication ability, interpersonal relationship handling, and management skills. More importantly, you should plan for your future, clearly knowing your goals and how to achieve them. It’s appropriate to recharge your knowledge from time to time. Don't hesitate to spend half a month's salary on learning; it's an investment in your future. What's there to hesitate about? If you don't learn to "give up" now, you will never "gain" anything in the future.

Sometimes, the reason you aren't developing well isn't because there are no opportunities, but because you aren't prepared, causing opportunities to pass you by. Society is like this. No matter how unreasonable or unfair it may seem, development opportunities are always present; it depends on whether you are ready. If you really cultivate extraordinary skills, someone might invite you to be the president of a world 500 company tomorrow.

The above is just the accumulation of my experience after many years in HR. I hope it helps everyone. As someone who has been through it, I sincerely hope that working friends and soon-to-be-employed college students can view this article with an "empty cup mentality." Sometimes, the experience of those who have been through it is very valuable; it's their hard-earned lessons from time and money. Some people refuse to believe this, thinking that only personal experiences can truly resonate with them, and others' experiences are irrelevant to them. If you think like this, there's nothing I can do. I must also have a broad-minded attitude and calmly view those "people" who "won't shed tears until they see the coffin and won't turn back until they hit the southern wall," right?
