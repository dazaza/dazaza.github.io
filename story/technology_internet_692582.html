How to be innovative in your work and overcome complacency

by qingian007 on 2012-03-06 10:29:44

The great Greek philosopher Heraclitus once said, "A man cannot step into the same river twice," meaning that everything is constantly changing. To maintain enthusiasm for your career beyond just a sense of responsibility, you must resist the monotonous 'brick-laying' mentality and continuously innovate and progress.

Sighing daily from nine to five, facing similar files and reports, one may feel it's tasteless! Completing an article today while thinking about tomorrow's piece, dreaming about writing articles, not knowing when this will end, wanting to leave but unsure what else can be done. Many people have encountered or experienced such workplace burnout emotions. This occurs because the duration of the job is too long, repetitive, dull, leading to increasing frustration over time. How then can one readjust their mindset and overcome the psychological obstacle of 'workplace burnout'?

After two months of training, A, B, and C entered a bank as savings clerks simultaneously. Initially, all three found the work fresh, especially handling piles of cash every day, which excited them and filled them with passion. However, over time, the initial freshness was replaced by monotony and busyness. A became annoyed, thinking that no matter how much money he handled daily, none of it was his own; he felt like a machine counting other people's money, seeing no future in it. Thus, he started treating work passively, dragging things out, doing less whenever possible. Due to his negative attitude, he became irritable, made more accounting errors, leading to even more irritation, trapped in a vicious cycle.

B also found the savings job monotonous after some time and had thoughts of switching jobs. However, he realized that in today's society, any job could become tedious with time due to detailed divisions of labor. He decided to patiently learn various aspects of the savings business and practice different skills. Although occasionally feeling a bit weary, his efforts earned him recognition and a sense of accomplishment, which naturally diminished his feelings of fatigue.

C was a person who loved pondering problems. After becoming familiar with the savings business, he discovered several areas that needed improvement. He carefully thought through each one. Soon, he devised a new method for counting bills, significantly increasing the speed of counting. Later, he designed an 'automatic coin sorter', where placing coins into the machine would automatically sort them by denomination. Eventually, he collaborated with others to invent portable bill counters and hydraulic bundling machines.

Eight years passed. A remained an ordinary savings clerk, his mood even lower, lacking energy daily, with no improvement in his skills, merely living day by day; B became a highly accomplished business supervisor in the department; C left the bank and opened a company specializing in researching and producing various tools required for banking and postal savings businesses, achieving remarkable success.

As the saying goes, "You reap what you sow." In the workplace, it's the same: what you sow determines what you harvest. When someone approaches work with indifference, laziness, and apathy, work can only reward them with emptiness, boredom, disappointment, frustration, and mediocrity. Conversely, when someone treats their work with full passion and invests all their intelligence and talent into it, work will never fail them. They are sure to achieve excellence and success.

"Workplace burnout" is like high and low notes on a musical staff, lurking in work moods, ready to strike. Facing unchanging environments and repetitive tasks daily, one might fall into a state of gloom. Just like eating your favorite dish every day, eventually, you'll grow tired of it. To avoid being bogged down by fatigue, or escape the swamp of professional burnout, or break the dead silence without hope, injecting fresh blood is crucial, and the best way is through innovation.

Doing the same job for too long may seem easy, but in reality, it feels like chewing leftover food. Whether it's leftovers or fresh dishes, the key is adjusting one's 'taste'. By innovating and varying the patterns, one can effectively overcome 'professional burnout'.
