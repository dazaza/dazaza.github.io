Shanghai releases Weibo to settle in Sina Weibo, making Weibo governance closer to people's livelihood

by edivista on 2011-11-29 16:09:54

Click to enter the Shanghai Fabu Weibo

On November 28, the official Weibo account of the Shanghai Municipal Government News Office officially launched on Sina. This Weibo account, named "Shanghai Fabu," gained over 140,000 followers on its first day online. On its debut day, Shanghai Fabu posted a total of 14 tweets, a number almost equivalent to the number of tweets posted by the Beijing Municipal Government News Office in a week.

The content of Shanghai Fabu focuses mainly on people's livelihoods and includes practical information such as air quality forecasts, traffic conditions, and weekly cultural performance schedules.

Shanghai Fabu, Chengdu Fabu, and Nanjing Fabu are becoming synonymous with the official Weibo accounts of some local government news offices and propaganda departments.

One of the reasons for the collective appearance of local government official Weibo accounts is the central government's top-down policy requirements. On November 28, Wang Chen, Director of the Central External Propaganda Office and the National Internet Information Office, published a signed article titled "Actively Carry Out Weibo Public Opinion Guidance Work" on the front page of the 16th edition of the People's Daily. (Click on the image to enter the Shanghai Fabu Weibo)

Local governments are also experimenting with social governance innovations through Weibo consultations.

Data provided by Sina shows that by early November 2011, more than 18,500 government agencies and officials from various fields had been verified through Sina Weibo, including 9,960 government agency accounts and 8,628 personal official accounts. Nationwide, 15 government agencies and 9 officials have over one million followers on their official Sina Weibo accounts.

The launch of Shanghai Fabu means that the news offices of all four direct-administered municipalities have gathered on Sina Weibo. It also signifies that the Chinese government has entered a new era of using new media for publicity and opened up a new path for socialized government management, said a person from the Sina Public Relations Department.

Dr. Zheng Lei of Fudan University's School of International Relations and Public Affairs and Digital Governance and Mobile Government Laboratory believes that Weibo consultations can technically provide a platform for government-citizen interaction and offer opportunities for social governance innovation. However, whether it can bring about reform remains a hypothesis, depending on whether the government can keep up in terms of attitude, management, and organization to truly achieve the effects of government-citizen interaction and management innovation. If it merely becomes formalistic, some places' Weibos may easily turn into performance projects.

From Local to National

Dr. Zheng Lei has participated in training and research for some regional government Weibos.

According to Zheng Lei, since the beginning of this year, many departments and bureaus in Shanghai have chosen to open Weibo accounts on local platforms like Oriental Net and Xinmin Net first.

On one hand, they complete the task of opening Weibo accounts, while on the other hand, they hide themselves on a relatively safe and controllable platform. Oriental Net and Xinmin Net are both under the supervision of Shanghai's propaganda system, whereas Sina is a company headquartered in Beijing and thus relatively less controllable, said Zheng Lei.

After testing the waters on local website platforms for several months, the official Weibo accounts of Shanghai Metro and Shanghai Tourism Department were then opened on the national Sina Weibo platform.

This time, the launch of Shanghai Fabu was a comprehensive deployment by the Shanghai Municipal Government. Two weeks before the launch of Shanghai Fabu, Shanghai Party Secretary Yu Zhengsheng also extensively discussed Weibo public opinion in a public speech.

Yu Zhengsheng pointed out that microblogs, blogs, and other network new media have increasingly become important sources for public opinion dissemination and hotspots gathering. The frequent occurrence of public opinion hotspots has become the norm. It must be clearly understood that avoidance and perfunctory actions are like ostriches burying their heads in the sand, which cannot solve problems.

According to Zheng Lei, under the mature consideration and explicit requirements of Shanghai's online publicity system, from the end of this year to the first quarter of next year, more real-name certified government institutions will move into Sina Weibo and appear on the Shanghai Fabu platform. Now, whether you like it or not, you have to swim on the national platform.

The excellent operation team of the Shanghai Metro official Weibo, which moved into Sina Weibo earlier, has become a model for visits and learning by various departments and bureaus. The official Weibo of Shanghai Metro is a highlight in Shanghai, with over one million followers. During the handling of the Shanghai Metro Line 10 accident, the response was very successful, fully leveraging the advantages of the Weibo platform, said Zheng Lei.

The Shanghai Metro official Weibo has accumulated much experience during its operations. When an event occurs, even if the media is summoned, the speed can never match that of Weibo. With Weibo, a self-media platform is created, effectively avoiding misinterpretation by the media. Also, the government cannot hold press conferences around the clock, but government Weibos can clarify issues and rumors at any time.

After various departments and bureaus collectively move into Weibo, the business collaboration capabilities between departments are expected to improve, which will become a highlight of Shanghai's Weibo consultations. Currently, there are not many governments that have opened large-scale Weibos overall, said Zheng Lei.

Inevitable Trend

Zheng Lei, who studies e-government, shifted his research focus to Weibo consultations at the end of last year. Two years ago, he noticed that when the Obama administration began using new media like Twitter and YouTube, Weibo was still a novelty for the Chinese government, and local governments were relatively cautious about Weibo, always observing and waiting.

But at the end of last year, the situation began to change.

On one hand, Weibo itself developed rapidly and became a platform for expressing public sentiment; on the other hand, senior officials began to realize that with the increasing influence of Weibo, if the government does not actively use it, it would marginalize itself in the Weibo era.

Zheng Lei believes this is because the credibility of party newspapers is declining, and guiding public opinion without effect would turn into self-entertainment. It is necessary to find where public sentiment lies and where the voices of the people are. Otherwise, when you want to speak, no one will listen to you.

According to Zheng Lei, many departments and bureaus in Shanghai are still reluctant to uniformly move into Sina Weibo, a national platform. On one hand, they feel it adds burdens; on the other hand, it increases risks, as being on Weibo exposes them to criticism and nitpicking.

The online publicity system from central to local levels has basically reached a consensus, especially after Wang Chen's press conference in September. The思路is now relatively clear, said Zheng Lei.

Yu Zhengsheng also emphasized in his speech two weeks ago that the correct attitude and method is to improve governance methods, enhance governance efficiency, accelerate the construction of informatization and networking, strive for breakthroughs in entering and using the internet, see results in cultivating and managing the internet, and strive to be pioneers who dare and know how to use the internet.

Yu Zhengsheng believes that one should bravely face issues that the public cares about. Anything that concerns the public and may affect their interests should be responded to as much as possible in a timely manner.

The firm attitude of Shanghai's leadership makes Weibo consultations an inevitable trend that cannot be avoided.

Publishing and Listening

It shouldn't just be about publishing; it should be more about listening. A netizen replied to the first official tweet from the Shanghai Municipal Government News Office. Netizens have also expressed in various ways their desire for interaction rather than mere publication.

Zheng Lei believes that compared to traditional media, the biggest difference with Weibo is its interactive platform function. Publishing gives the government strong control, carefully choosing words. Although controllable, the interactive effect is poor, and responses are often formal and perfunctory.

This is also related to technical limitations. Websites are good for publishing but not for interaction because interaction requires netizens to continuously revisit the portal site, which is troublesome. The advantage of Weibo is that once you follow Shanghai Fabu, you don't need to revisit repeatedly to receive information published by the Shanghai Municipal Government News Office. Zheng Lei believes that web 2.0 has technically solved the problem of government-citizen interaction. Now, the main issue is whether there is an interactive attitude and whether the technical platform can play its role. This is not a technical problem but an attitude problem and a management problem.

During his research, Zheng Lei found that some local governments have not fully utilized the speed and interactivity features of Weibo. After opening government Weibos, they do not forward, reply, or interact. We call this the 'government website Weibo-ization,' which means moving the government website to Weibo, said Zheng Lei.

However, Zheng Lei also discovered many good examples, such as @PingAnBeijing and @PingAnZhaoqing, whose characteristics include lowering their stance and using lively language.

Taking Zhang Endi, Vice District Chief of Pudong New Area, as an example, he opened his own Weibo account, and his speaking style is completely different from his work speeches, showcasing the characteristics of new media.

Wang Xingya, an official from Jiangsu Province's General Office, also studied the Weibo craze and Weibo consultations. He believed that in the citizens' square of Weibo, the party committees and governments could understand netizens' opinions, promote local work, express attitudes, clarify facts, guide online public opinion, ease netizens' emotions, and lead image dissemination.

Wang Xingya said that through Weibo, a cost-effective, fast, and direct channel for public opinion interaction and citizen consultation could be built.

After the launch of local government official Weibos, they may encounter thorny issues such as netizens posting petition information. Zheng Lei pointed out that currently, the departments within the government that open government Weibos are the news office and the online publicity office, which do not have the function of solving petition problems unless future integration of the petition department and the Weibo operation department occurs. Some local governments explicitly state that Weibo cannot be used as a petition channel.

Now, the petition department still dares not to enter Weibo, not yet ready to face it. Better-performing Weibo teams will collect public sentiment data and provide it to specific business departments.

Regarding how to face criticism and complaints, Zheng Lei believed that the key lies in whether the leadership values it, provides resources, has professionals to respond, explain, and clarify, and has staffing and budget. Some civil servants are highly motivated, even handing over operations to the Youth League, but not even providing mobile data subsidies.

A person from Sina's government Weibo team believed that local governments should be given exercise and time to improve their interactive capabilities and learn to deal with doubts. If they are overwhelmed right away, they might stop altogether.
