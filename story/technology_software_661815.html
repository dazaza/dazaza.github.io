Xing Rong / Yan Rong Jushi

To the utmost sincerity, I prostrate to all Buddhas and Bodhisattvas of the ten directions and three periods of time.

To the utmost sincerity, I prostrate to the Buddhas and Bodhisattvas at the Prajñā Boat Dharma Assembly.

To the utmost sincerity, I bow deeply to all deities and dharma protectors.

To the utmost sincerity, I prostrate to all karmic creditors, benefactor teachers, parents, friends, relatives, and all beings with whom I have a connection.

To the utmost sincerity, I prostrate to my revered teacher, Amitabha Buddha, the compassionate father, Venerable Chang Shan.

To the utmost sincerity, I prostrate to all dharma protectors and fellow practitioners of every practice center.

Dear venerable masters, noble ones:

Namo Amitabha Buddha!

In mid-January 2012, after burying my father in my hometown, I stayed in the old house waiting idly for the New Year to arrive. Suddenly, I received a message from Xiao Xiong Jushi (a lay practitioner in Ye Cheng) who had returned to lay life but still practiced diligently: He said there was a temple in Handan where they practiced non-stop meditation on Buddha for three days and three nights without sleep. It was said that this practice equaled decades of cultivation in merit. He added if I could endure it, I should come.

My heart was restless; I wanted to give it a try immediately. After calculating the possible schedule, I felt it was quite feasible, and I had no hesitation or attachment. Booking tickets and the journey were smooth, and when I arrived in Wuan, Handan, a car from the temple came to pick me up, so everything went very smoothly as I ascended the mountain.

The altitude of Yunyan Temple is quite high. The geographical conditions are unique, and the winter mountain peaks are barren with almost no vegetation. However, after a snowfall, the beauty was breathtaking—it was so quiet, beautiful, dignified, and pure, filled with the grandeur and majesty of the mountains. There wasn't much time to take it all in before starting the practice life (if I had more time, I would have enjoyed it thoroughly). Or perhaps because we arrived at night, there was little visibility. The lighting wasn’t as bright as in the city, and my eyes hadn't adjusted yet. Then I heard that Master Yan Yuan had come to greet us, so I "saw" him briefly. After a simple greeting, I was led into the temple, climbed several steps into a room with thick curtains, and saw something like a heater. Everything was dark, and my eyes hadn't adjusted yet, but then someone announced: “The master has arrived.” Oh! The master had come. Before I even realized it, my body instinctively knelt down (it was truly an inner intuition). In the dim light of the heater, I vaguely "saw" the master. He seemed to be just an ordinary master, and the temple also appeared quite ordinary and not very large. I doubted somewhat: Can this place really do it?

I drank the ginger tea offered by the master, thinking it was part of the daily kitchen offerings. Later, I learned it was specially prepared for the master due to his cold—very precious and not common. Venerable Hui Zhi kindly served me a meal—three sweet pancakes. Afterward, I thought, is this even considered a meal? Usually, I imagined meals to include many dishes and soups, but I didn’t say anything and reluctantly ate one. At that moment, I thought, the conditions here are indeed simple. Even if given a bowl of noodles, which I usually dislike, I would consider it a delicacy now. I finished eating hastily. Later, I learned that this was already considered a very polite reception. The toilet was even more terrifying—it smelled terrible, and the excrement was frozen solid. I was almost scared out of my wits and wondered how such foul-smelling toilets still existed (later during the retreat, after a few days, I couldn’t smell it anymore). In Guangzhou, whether in office buildings, malls, or homes, the toilets are always clean. Some five-star toilets even have air conditioning, hand soap, dryers, disposable seat covers, baby changing tables, hand towels, and toilet paper—all fully equipped, dry, and odorless. Despite this, I overcame myself and continued forward. Soon, I followed everyone into the chanting hall. Upon entering, I smelled a peculiar, "bitter" scent (I thought it might be due to the cold weather making ventilation difficult). Looking around, people walked strangely, almost like zombies from Hong Kong movies, looking very exhausted. I didn’t understand why they walked like that. I tried walking but felt it shouldn’t be too unusual, so I mimicked everyone else’s movements. That night passed without much feeling. But Master Yan Yuan kept praising and encouraging me, giving me confidence. With anticipation, I began the seven-day, eight-night retreat.

The next day, the Dharma master explained the rules and arranged for me to sit in front of him. Seeing his smiling face, I thought he looked pleasant, so I listened lying down. Gradually, I was drawn in by the master's words and smile, beginning to feel joy. I roughly understood the methods and precautions of the Prajñā practice, though still unclear in detail.

On the following day, I was assigned to sweep snow, and I was thrilled. As someone from the south, I rarely see snow and never get the chance to sweep it. I thought it would be a romantic task! However, it turned out I had little time to enjoy the scenery. I had to focus intently to sweep the snow cleanly and work hard to keep up with everyone, barely managing not to fall behind. My body quickly grew tired, and I sweated profusely. Feeling somewhat uncomfortable, I worried about my physical state for the upcoming retreat. In daily life, I almost never engage in labor, my biggest physical activity being chasing public transportation. I eat three meals a day outside, indulging in whatever satisfies me. Clothes, even socks, are washed by machine. Shopping, internet browsing, fine dining, traveling, enjoying luxuries—all these are what we are most familiar with. Daily bathing, changing clothes, different facial cleansers morning and night, imported toothpaste and toothbrushes, the best skincare products, toners, and essential oils applied all over the body. My body rarely experiences hard work and has long been "civilized and degenerated." But now, amidst the mountain winds, I was sweating profusely, feeling both hot and miserable. I thought, if only I could take a hot shower, it would be wonderful. Fortunately, just before the retreat started, I had the opportunity to go down the mountain to take a bath, so I rushed off and enjoyed a rare, refreshing shower. It was truly comfortable...

On New Year's Eve, I entered the retreat room, which was relatively clean, offering some comfort to my heavy body consciousness (being very particular about cleanliness). Who knew that on the first day of the retreat, my feet started hurting. Feeling uncomfortable, I reported to Master Yan Yuan. The next day, the pain worsened, and I, along with Teacher Li, suffered so much that we consoled each other through our labor. I joked with Master Yan Yuan, saying he had lured me to enjoy blessings during the New Year, and now my feet hurt! He even felt embarrassed. Normally, I walk little, and even shopping for extended periods makes my feet uncomfortable. Now, walking continuously without knowing when it would end, I told Master Yan Yuan my goal: to endure until the end would be success. In my heart, I spoke to my karmic creditors: "If you want to collect your debts, it's fine as long as it doesn't stop me from dedicating merits to you. Otherwise, I won't dedicate them, and you can do whatever you want. If necessary, we can continue another time."

The master and fellow practitioners said, regardless of the pain, just think about it. How could that be possible? Pain is a very real sensation. I am afraid of pain. I remember in middle school, when my father took me to get an injection for a cold, I cried. My body is very sensitive and delicate, and I fear pain greatly. What to do? Unable to walk, I dragged my feet (writing this now, I can't help but laugh—I never imagined I'd be in such a situation). Seven days and eight nights felt like an endless night...

Who knew that after darkness fell, as I walked, I entered Samadhi. My body completely lost sensation, and I only followed the crowd chanting the Buddha's name. Hmm, it felt great—no pain in my feet, and I was even stepping forcefully. Oddly strange! Only without pain, everything feels good. But I clearly knew this wasn't my own accomplishment—it felt like there was a force supporting me! Thus, I began to understand what the master had said—that the pain in the body is false and empty. I started learning to ignore the pain and even challenge my limits. At noon, I jumped on one foot, and sure enough, the pain disappeared. The approving looks from others gave me more courage. But soon, the pain returned with a vengeance, spreading throughout my body. My feet felt nearly "injured," unable to be touched. How could I dare to jump now? Yet there was nothing I could do but chant the Buddha's name. Chanting, I suddenly became a small child, chanting continuously. Various beautiful sounds and rhythms of chanting emerged, continuing even in dreams. The child, apart from knowing how to chant the Buddha's name, sees people simply and directly. After circling the master twice and bowing deeply, I inwardly knew the master was a "good person." Later, seeing children in the arms of Amitabha Buddha, I felt it was myself. Suddenly, I wanted to go home—to the Pure Land of the East—and realized wandering for so long had been painful. Whenever I saw the words, "Children, come home, I've been standing here waiting for you..." I was deeply moved. Seeing the Buddha was like seeing my parents—I just wanted to go home! Not seeing the master, I wanted to find "Dad"... After coming out of such realms, I realized I wasn't inherently bad; inside, I was a simple child with deep roots of goodness, originally having such a profound connection with the Buddha. I had always thought I was very bad, but the root cause was incorrect, and this thought gave me great confidence and courage. I began to cultivate according to the master's instructions with a simple and obedient heart. Gradually, I found ways to deal with the pain in my feet: accept it happily, sincerely repent, continue chanting the Buddha's name, and ignore bodily sensations. The pain slowly subsided. Master Yan Yuan said, "Don't get too confident—the hardest test, 'thought demons seizing your mind,' hasn't come yet!" My heart tightened again.

Time passed day by day. I told myself not to think about which day it was, just to sincerely practice and cherish each day's experience. Later, the continuous emergence of realms made me realize my usual chanting efforts were insufficient; I understood that Amitabha Buddha truly is my parent, coming to take me home; I realized that everything in this world is illusory and unreal—if no thoughts arise, then no-thought and thought are both Amitabha Buddha. That state was extremely tranquil, solemn, free, and joyful—our original nature is complete in itself... This gave me immense energy and deep faith—originally, I too am a good child of Amitabha Buddha. Regardless of what I've experienced, in His eyes, I am a good child. I began chanting the Buddha's name, finding various ways to chant. As I did, tests came one after another, requiring my full attention to overcome, feeling like passing through five gates and slaying six generals. Busy! So busy!! I didn't know what was happening in the outside world, to the point where Venerable Hui Zhi criticized me as "isolated," seeing me chanting the Buddha's name even in the bathroom (in samadhi) and accusing me of not understanding the rules. Others saw me hugging a Buddha statue and chanting, thinking I was abnormal, even placing me in solitary confinement. Various rumors about me spread... I laughed and cried, thinking in my heart, "If you don't understand, that's fine. I will continue chanting the Buddha's name." Regardless of what tricks Mara used to test me, I just chanted the Buddha's name, ignoring everything else, free from distress, uninterrupted... Whether chanting loudly, softly, evenly, or slowly, watching my thoughts, I advanced step by step, voice by voice. I truly realized that aside from chanting the Buddha's name, everything else is false! At this moment, the master was my greatest support and source of faith. I only saw the master, ignored others, avoided gossip, and focused solely on chanting and practicing the Dharma... Gradually, I inwardly realized the master was not an "ordinary person," and the practice center was not an ordinary place. Truly, it is a superior place for achieving Buddhahood! I also understood what is most important in life—each step I took, each recitation of the Buddha's name brought me closer to the Buddha!

Time flew by, and it was soon the seventh day of the New Year. Before the retreat ended, I regretfully found the master wasn't present for the dedication ceremony. On the day of rest, I habitually returned to the chanting hall to practice mindfulness and circumambulate. That day, feeling relaxed, I gradually sensed a sense of lightness in my legs, able to walk endlessly without fatigue. Not only that, my mind was calm and joyful, and my face involuntarily broke into a sweet smile. I felt I could continue walking indefinitely, my body adjusting automatically, changing pace and rhythm. I enjoyed the bliss of practicing the Buddha-recitation method. I wished to continue forever, realizing how enjoyable it was to chant the Buddha's name!

Eating became unimportant, and sleep wasn't necessary. This was unimaginable before. Normally, if I had nothing to do, I wouldn't sleep at night, staying up late and enjoying the warmth of blankets. I ate a lot, indulged in snacks, fruits, sugary drinks, and medicinal supplements. But in the practice center, I lost my appetite or ate less, even leaving food untouched twice initially. My spirit grew better, and I didn't nap. Occasionally, if drowsiness came, I observed and chanted the Buddha's name to counteract it. Through the practice, I truly realized that stomach pain disappears when toxins are expelled. My previously pampered body became strong, my legs especially powerful. Returning home, I didn't care about seating arrangements since I could walk continuously for seven days and eight nights. A mere 20-hour journey was nothing to me, especially since I had mastered special skills: sleeping while standing or walking. I no longer feared lacking seats—traveling from Handan to Guangzhou without a seat was effortless! On the train, I marched in place listening to chants, slept standing, and felt incredible!

Not washing my face, feet, combing my hair, or taking a shower, I found I could accept it after letting go completely. Returning to Handan and boarding the train, I realized how "frightening" my appearance must have been, yet I remained unbothered! Continuously chanting the Buddha's name freely. Of course, upon returning home, I immediately cleaned myself thoroughly. Ha ha...

Summarizing this rare and precious experience of the Prajñā retreat, I share some insights for the benefit of all:

1. Fools learn Buddhism best—simple, obedient, letting go of preconceived notions, listening and following leads to great achievements.

2. Every game has its rules—understand the rules beforehand to avoid unnecessary detours, reducing troubles for yourself and others. For example, in the Prajñā practice, don't care for others, ignore pain, and just chant. Step firmly and chant aloud, facing any realm without reaction, just chanting the Buddha's name. Maintain distance from others, avoiding receiving care from the opposite sex. No bathing, including washing face and feet, brushing teeth. Avoid seeking coolness and prioritize keeping warm. Eating less can bring clarity and freedom from distracting thoughts. When karmic obstacles appear, sincerely repent. Always maintain gratitude. Don't listen to or spread gossip. Have a heart to help all sentient beings achieve enlightenment, etc.

3. Chanting the Buddha's name is true benefit—nothing can be taken at death except the Buddha's name and karma. Those who truly seek peace and Nirvana need to let go of everything. Cultivation is about repaying debts, not accumulating them.

4. Firmly believe in the blessings of the Buddhas and Bodhisattvas. Each recitation of the Buddha's name can resonate with the Buddha within and receive the Buddha's radiant light. The Buddhas and Bodhisattvas are truly present at all times, their names not false! Believe that vow power surpasses karma! One must make the firm resolve to achieve personal success and enlightenment!

5. Respect all people, all things, all sentient beings, as they are either future or present Buddhas! Deeply grateful to Venerable Chang Shan for his compassionate contributions to this Bodhi path. Do not view people and things with worldly eyes. Regard the master as the Buddha! View fellow practitioners as Bodhisattvas.

6. The Dharma is beyond conceptualization—one falls into error upon thinking. Non-attachment to appearances brings freedom. Practicing Buddhism should not turn one into a "Buddhist fool." Practicing the Dharma is not about judging others but examining one's own motives and cultivation.

7. Cherish gratitude, diligence, perseverance, and a heart of achievement. Ignore distractions and just chant the Buddha's name. Amidst any obstacle, pain, confusion, realms, favorable or adverse conditions, emotions, and even extreme physical suffering, continue practicing!

Through countless eons of cyclic existence,

Enduring unbearable pain, burning body and mind,

Prajñā is like a cooling liquid,

Pouring wisdom into the mind like nectar,

One day returning home to dwell,

Realizing all virtues inherent in our nature,

Bodhi wisdom naturally possessed,

Why search elsewhere when all is within?

True sweetness and joy lie in purity,

Treasures abound in one's own home,

Hurry back and meet the compassionate father!

With utmost sincerity, I thank all Buddhas and Bodhisattvas of the ten directions and three periods of time.

With utmost sincerity, I thank all Buddhas and Bodhisattvas at the Prajñā Dharma Assembly.

With utmost sincerity, I thank all benevolent deities and dharma protectors.

With utmost sincerity, I thank all karmic creditors, benefactor teachers, parents, friends, relatives, and all beings with whom I have a connection.

With utmost sincerity, I thank my revered teacher, Amitabha Buddha, the compassionate father, Venerable Chang Shan.

With utmost sincerity, I thank all dharma protectors and fellow practitioners of every practice center.

With utmost sincerity, I thank my own body and mind for achieving the path.

Returning to the familiar and beloved Guangzhou, with its warm climate and blossoms everywhere, it is a prosperous city with blessings. Even the BRT bus stations have escalators. The floors in subways, office buildings, and malls shine like mirrors. People travel daily by car, indulging in food, drink, and entertainment. They live information-rich, luxurious lives, bustling endlessly. Friends warmly asked where I had been, and I smiled faintly, thanking them for their concern. Some joked that I lacked compassion, but my heart was warm! Yet this pales in comparison to timely practice and joy. Everywhere I go, I wear earphones, listening to Dharma teachings and chanting the Buddha's name, feeling free and happy. Truly, I have realized there is no concept of space or time, remaining in the energy field of the practice center and the master. Handling affairs, walking, eating, and sleeping are unplanned, all entrusted to the Buddhas and Bodhisattvas. May I truly let go of all attachments to the Saha world, leave behind form, sensation, perception, volition, and consciousness, release all false thoughts, stay away from the five desires and impurities, and draw close to good spiritual friends and death. Awaiting further diligent practice! Early completion of the path, return to the Pure Land, and realization of the body's fruition! Attain Buddhahood and liberate sentient beings!!!

May all sentient beings practice diligently, bravely, and without regression, with sufficient good conditions. Let the compassionate wishes of the master, the father, be fulfilled early. Together with all beings, may we be reborn in the Pure Land! Return to the Buddha's land! Abide in the bliss of Nirvana!!!

Written on February 3, 2012, in Guangzhou by Yan Rong
