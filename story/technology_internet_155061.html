What can we learn from Gmail? | Ray is thinking

by nemo on 2009-06-01 23:17:08

Gmail, the greatest Internet application in my mind, is a single-product site with traffic surpassing YouTube. It’s a product that has redefined the word "email."

What can we learn from Gmail's success? It's like *Dream of the Red Chamber*, where "Confucian scholars see the Book of Changes, moralists see licentiousness, talented youths see romance, revolutionaries see anti-Manchu sentiments, and rumor mongers see palace secrets." Too abstract? Keep reading!

1. **Unconventional Launch**

On April 1, 2004, Google announced the launch of Gmail on April Fools' Day. Both the media and users didn't believe it, treating it as a joke. But remember, what spreads fastest? Rumors! Even though no one believed it, everyone spread the news. From the very beginning, it was rapidly disseminated.

2. **4MB vs. 1GB**

In 2004, most email service providers like Yahoo! and Hotmail offered only 4MB of storage space. In China, even 263 had started charging for email services. Gmail announced a 1GB capacity, which completely stunned people. The birth of every great product always leaves people amazed, just like the iPhone. Revolutionary products don’t just outdo their competitors by a small margin; they utterly crush them.

And then step on them...

3. **Who Can Give Me a Gmail Invitation?**

The April Fools' Day launch and the 1GB capacity were already attractive enough, but what made people itch was that Gmail wasn’t open for registration. You could only sign up via an invitation (Was this invitation system pioneered by Gmail?). As a result, there were websites dedicated to finding invitations, and on eBay, invitations were auctioned off for nearly a thousand dollars each. Successful products always tantalize users in the early stages without revealing everything at once. Love comes fast and goes fast; what you can't get is often the best. This golden rule applies not only to romantic relationships but also to users and products.

4. **Do “Bad Things” with Sincerity**

One of the biggest controversies surrounding Gmail was the inclusion of ads in emails. Some senators even cried out: "This is an unprecedented invasion of the sanctity of personal communication privacy!" Technology is always a double-edged sword, and there's no such thing as a free lunch. What Gmail did was simply make public what other email service providers were secretly doing, telling users: "I provide you with free service, but I will use your privacy to make money. Of course, you have the option not to use it." This sincere attitude is far better than those who pretend to be righteous in words while secretly selling personal information behind the scenes! Don't deceive users; you can't fool everyone all the time.

5. **Openness**

Yahoo Mail is really unlucky. From the day Gmail was born, it has been constantly compared to highlight its greatness, but Yahoo Mail hasn't been competitive. Until now, Yahoo Mail still doesn't allow free users to use POP to retrieve emails—only paying users can. On the contrary, Gmail not only supports POP and IMAP but also allows gadgets to be added freely. No matter how big the company or how advanced the technology, it's impossible to implement all features alone. Openness isn't just about releasing a few documents, setting up an open platform, and shouting "I'm open!" It's a mindset. Those who are always thinking about how to undermine their opponents can never create truly open products.

6. **Innovation**

Google's nine principles of innovation were perfectly embodied in Gmail's growth. Over five years, Gmail has evolved with embedded GTalk, Labs integration, theme appearance, and more. A product is rarely perfect from the start. Innovation isn't about enduring hardship and then releasing everything at once. The release of a product is precisely the beginning of innovation.

7. **Details Are King**

There are too many examples of this. From button adjustments to loading progress bars, to the Recent Activity Record in the footer, and even the title display, you can feel the dedication of the Gmail team everywhere. Internet products are highly homogenized. Besides operation, good products rely on details to win.

8. **Personalization and User Experience**

We always use various methods to observe, understand, and study user needs, but no matter how deeply we research, it's impossible to meet all user demands because each user is a unique individual, and differences always exist. Gmail Labs is a very smart approach. There's no need to argue in meetings about whether to place buttons on the left or right, or whether to use radio buttons or dropdown menus. Gmail puts all these functions into Labs and lets users choose themselves.

9. **The Power of Focus**

Gmail didn't do portals, didn't do downloads, and didn't even do IM (GTalk is embedded). It focused solely on email, trying to increase loading speed by 0.1 seconds and striving for maximum stability. It concentrated on making one product great instead of copying here and there and ending up with dozens of mediocre products.

10. **Always in Beta**

Even after entering the top ten global websites, Gmail still has the word "Beta" next to its logo. These four letters make countless competitors and peers blush. Great products don't boast about themselves or constantly belittle their rivals to elevate themselves. Humility is a virtue.

In today's rapidly developing Internet industry, who will be the next great Internet application? Who will become the next Gmail?
