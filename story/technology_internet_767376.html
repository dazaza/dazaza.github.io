Why Alibaba Gave Up on Hong Kong for Its IPO

by anonymous on 2013-10-13 14:12:47

The haze surrounding the rumors of Alibaba's intended Hong Kong IPO has recently cleared up. The Group's Chief Executive Officer, Lu Zhaoxi, stated that the group had decided not to choose Hong Kong as its listing location.

Why did the win-win plan for Alibaba and the Hong Kong market ultimately fail? Does the announcement from the Alibaba Group mean a complete farewell to the Hong Kong capital market, or will there be another opportunity? The reporter attempts to unravel the clues from various sources to explore the future direction of the event.

Jack Ma and his "Chinese partners"

In the first public statement made by the management team regarding the IPO issue, Lu Zhaoxi concluded this matter on the 10th: "Today's Hong Kong market still needs time to study and digest the governance structure innovation of emerging enterprises, so we have decided not to list in Hong Kong."

The "governance structure innovation" of emerging enterprises mentioned by Lu Zhaoxi is precisely the focus of the disagreement between both parties, which originates from Jack Ma and his "partnership system".

According to internal emails disclosed by Ma to employees, starting from 2010, the Alibaba Group began piloting the "partnership system" within its management team. Every year, new partners are selected to join. They must meet the following criteria: "Worked at Alibaba for more than 5 years, possess excellent leadership skills, highly identify with the company culture, and make positive contributions to the company's development, willing to spare no effort for the inheritance of the company culture and mission." After three years of experimentation, Alibaba currently has 28 partners.

"The partnership system established by Alibaba is not an interest group, nor is it for better control of the power institutions of this company," said Ma.

Ma believes that as operators of the company, builders of business, inheritors of culture, and shareholders, partners are most likely to adhere to the company's mission and long-term interests, creating long-term value for customers, employees, and shareholders.

However, this failed to alleviate concerns about decision-making transparency under such a system. The focus of concern lies in the control of the board of directors by the "partners" after Alibaba's listing.

Public data shows that the "partners", including Jack Ma, currently hold only about 10% of the shares, while Yahoo! USA and Japan's SoftBank own approximately 24% and 36% respectively. Industry analysts indicated that under such an equity structure, Ma and others designed the "partnership system" to maintain their control over the company.

Although neither side disclosed the details of their negotiation, Cai Chongxin, the executive vice chairman of Alibaba's board of directors, revealed in his article "Why Alibaba Launched the Partnership System" on the 26th last month: "Our proposal fully protects the important rights of shareholders, including the unrestricted right to elect independent directors, voting rights on major transactions and related party transactions, etc."

According to Hong Kong media reports on the IPO plan, the Alibaba partners have the right to nominate a majority of the directors on the board.

Senior financial and investment banker Wen Tianna told Xinhua News Agency that the partnership system is quite special and unprecedented in the Hong Kong market, "For Hong Kong regulators, this is truly a dilemma between corporate governance innovation and market standards."

Alibaba's adherence to the "one share, one vote" principle in Hong Kong

On the 10th, Alibaba stated that the group had conducted several contacts and informal communications with Hong Kong market regulatory agencies. Alibaba did not submit any formal applications, did not request a dual-class share structure, and did not challenge the "one share, one vote" standard upheld by the Hong Kong market.

Regarding this, the reporter attempted to interview the Securities and Futures Commission (SFC) of Hong Kong and the Hong Kong Exchanges and Clearing Limited (HKEX), but both declined to be interviewed, citing the inconvenience of commenting on individual companies' IPO matters. In the email response to the reporter, the HKEX spokesperson also stated, "We are very willing to listen and learn from the opinions of all parties in the market, but whether to amend relevant listing regulations requires a joint decision by the Listing Committee and the Hong Kong SFC."

Just recently, Secretary for Financial Services and the Treasury of the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region, Chan Ka-keung, publicly stated in the Legislative Council that there was no intention to change the listing review function of the HKEX, nor were there plans to conduct a public consultation on adopting different equity systems for new stock listings.

Chan Ka-keung also said that Hong Kong's listing rules prohibit dual-class equity structures. To protect investors' rights, it is necessary to uphold the "one share, one vote" principle, "Share capital rights should be consistent with the economic value of the share capital." He also cited overseas examples, pointing out that allowing listed companies to adopt dual-class equity structures is not mainstream.

Wen Tianna pointed out that there is no denying that Alibaba is a company with very high corporate governance standards, but Alibaba's success is also inseparable from Jack Ma's personal charisma, "No one can guarantee that future partners will be as far-sighted as Ma." Moreover, if an exception is made for Alibaba, similar requests from other companies will challenge Hong Kong's long-standing fairness principle.

Where is the road to Alibaba's IPO?

On the 10th, Alibaba simultaneously indicated that it has yet to make a final decision on the listing location. The industry generally predicts that if it is confirmed that Alibaba will not list in Hong Kong, then the United States seems to be the best choice for Alibaba, but it will also face numerous issues.

"The biggest attraction of the U.S. market lies in its guarantee of control rights. It allows dual-class share structures, supporting Ma and his management team to dominate. Like NASDAQ, it has long-term mature and stable technology investors," said Wen Tianna.

However, he also indicated that U.S. market regulation is stricter than Hong Kong, and the U.S. has a long history of class-action lawsuits. If choosing to list in the U.S., Alibaba will face risks regarding some financial issues and information disclosure in the future. "In addition, previous events such as the Alipay transfer incident and the privatization incident of Alibaba's subsidiary in 2012 have cast doubts on the confidence of professional investors in the U.S. towards Alibaba."

More importantly, although the U.S. allows dual-class share structures, Alibaba's "partnership system" is unprecedented in the U.S. as well, and they are not entirely the same. Both sides still need some time to negotiate, and the outcome of the negotiations is uncertain.

As Cai Chongxin stated in his article, "As a company whose main business is in China, Hong Kong naturally becomes our top choice for listing."

If Alibaba truly abandons its Hong Kong listing, it will lose the familiar market environment, numerous individual and institutional investors from mainland China and Hong Kong, as well as a higher valuation. Changing the listing location involves redesigning the listing preparation, involving high tax, auditing, and time costs.

For Hong Kong, losing Alibaba not only means giving up what could possibly be the world's third-largest internet company by market value but also poses a challenge to Hong Kong's efforts to establish itself as a Chinese technology fundraising center.

"Alibaba has developed very rapidly in the past decade. They followed market trends, seized opportunities, and became one of the most innovative companies," Wen Tianna suggested that Alibaba should continue to seek appropriate ways with relevant Hong Kong institutions to resolve the current deadlock for this company with great potential.
