"The Inevitable 'Death of Yougou': Belle's E-commerce Has Many Fatal Flaws"

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 16:57:23

Recently, Yougou.com, which has been suffering from a serious loss of senior executives, was hit again. On July 17th, it was reported that according to insiders at Yougou.com, the company's COO, Zhang Xiaojun, is set to leave and is currently processing his resignation. As one of the founding members of Yougou.com, Zhang Xiaojun's departure is particularly significant, indicating that Yougou.com is indeed facing a severe crisis.

Let’s first review the e-commerce journey of Belle International, to which Yougou.com belongs:

A few years ago, Belle cautiously ventured into e-commerce through three stable channels: Taobao distribution, Tmall, and B2C distribution (B2C distribution was completed through Taixiu.com). Led by veteran group member Xie Yunli, an initially modest e-commerce team started by selling products under Belle's own brands. They then launched exclusive online models and new network brands. This development path was considered a successful case of a traditional brand entering the e-commerce sector more solidly than Li-Ning before 2011.

As companies like Letao.com and Haolemai received over $100 million in financing and rapidly developed in 2010, Belle Group became restless. On one hand, they were concerned about being constrained by distribution channels; on the other hand, they realized their huge potential for development in the e-commerce field. These advantages included:

1. Numerous brands: In addition to its several women's shoe brands, the agency for internationally renowned sports brands such as Adidas and Nike saw astonishing growth in online sales.

2. Easier category expansion: For Belle Group, which owns women's shoes and distributes famous sports brands, how to achieve absolute market advantage and status through multi-brand and multi-category strategies has been a major focus. Expanding offline stores to add brands and categories is clearly too slow, while existing e-commerce operations can easily expand brands but not quickly enough in categories.

3. Financial advantage: During cooperation with e-commerce enterprises, Belle found that e-commerce is capital-intensive. However, Belle Group's financial strength allows it to develop without relying on external capital, thereby defeating many vertical e-commerce companies that depend on capital, becoming one of the "survivors."

4. Talent advantage: Through years of e-commerce practice, Belle already possesses a highly professional and efficient e-commerce team. The talent cultivation can meet the scale development of e-commerce or even higher levels.

5. Supply chain advantage: This excited Belle internally and is the core reason attracting high-level executives from brand e-commerce or platform e-commerce. After all, supply chains always fascinate in e-commerce because they seem to determine life and death aside from funding. The supply chain includes product research and development, production, warehouse management, delivery after-sales service, etc. In traditional enterprises emphasizing business over e-commerce, this is considered the most competitive advantage over e-commerce enterprises.

With these advantages, Belle's e-commerce decided to make a big move, first purchasing the double-character domain name yougou.com,

abandoning the slightly imitative “topshoes.cn” Taixiu website for redirection, stopping supplies to all distribution websites, while still retaining direct third-party online stores such as Taobao and Tmall Mall but reducing investment and attention. High salaries lured JD VP and Vancl VP to serve as CMO and COO respectively, and dispatched senior veterans as CEO, forming a golden triangle. They aimed to create a full-category vertical B2C mall, implementing a multi-brand strategy on one hand, and expanding into clothing and other categories on the other, realizing the dream of a department store empire impossible offline!

Everything sounded wonderful, but every dream needs to face the harsh reality. The reality was:

1. Higher technical requirements for independent B2C: Being headquartered in Shenzhen, Belle could not recruit more excellent technical talents, and technical outsourcing services were scarce in Shenzhen. Technology determines user experience, backend management efficiency, and reliable data. Independent B2C belongs to dynamic technology, different from the relatively static ERP technology, and continuous changes remain a hurdle for many traditional enterprises;

2. Increased management difficulty due to category expansion: Someone once said that doing well in women's shoes doesn't necessarily mean doing well in men's shoes, something Belle must have deeply felt when expanding into men's shoes. It's easy to imagine the difficulties encountered in the backend within a year of expanding into women's clothing and accessories. For traditional brand enterprises accustomed to meticulous calculations, this rapid approach worked for Vancl during special times, understandable within Vancl and JD's systems. Although Belle had Vancl's COO and JD's CMO, whether they could be understood and executed with "exaggerated costs" remains questionable!

3. System: Many people outside of e-commerce and traditional enterprises struggle to understand this. Simply put, department settings (organizational structure), approvals, finance and budget, reporting methods, corporate culture, communication styles, execution methods, risk control, process systems, etc., constitute what is called the "mechanism" of an enterprise. The result of this conflict is that e-commerce attempts to convince and accept the "system" of e-commerce, while the traditional team tries to understand but never truly understands the "system" of e-commerce, leading to inefficiency and instability in the execution team. Ultimately, the chairman or board might say, "E-commerce and traditional enterprises each have advantages and disadvantages, the future will certainly be a fusion, so you must also fuse in these areas...". If this so-called "fusion without harmony" appears, it is called management innovation, and thus the "CEO rotation system" actually appeared in a company like Belle!

It was originally thought that Yougou.com, part of Belle's e-commerce, was far ahead. The top executives were waiting for a spin-off IPO to enjoy success and wealth, setting examples of countless traditional enterprises succeeding in the e-commerce era, rather than struggling with these hard issues.

After all, whether it’s the old-timers who grew up with Belle or the recruited e-commerce VPs, it is clear that they are not leaving because of lack of ability. When the VP from JD returns to JD, they continue to perform exceptionally well, and when the VP from Vancl has the courage to start their own business. Are these three points the hard issues? Or are these not just Belle's hard issues but also the hard issues for many traditional enterprises trying to enter e-commerce?
