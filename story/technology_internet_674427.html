Weibo records of Wang Zhengnian: holding on to his daughter Shulina who suffers from severe thalassemia

by edivista on 2012-02-24 13:46:19

It was still dark, and the only light in the house came from a dim bulb. Wang Zhengnian's 24-year-old wife, Peng Shuangqin, unfolded a pink cotton jacket, carefully placed it by the brazier to dry out the dampness, and then dressed their daughter who was less than half a year old. Her husband, Wang Zhengnian, squatted by the door, slurping a bowl of clear soup noodles.

This was the morning of the 15th day of the first lunar month. It was the last day of the Lunar New Year, but this family in Shiqian County, Tongren City, Guizhou Province, had no festive atmosphere. Wang Zhengnian placed his sleeping daughter in a baby carrier and covered her with a floral blanket. Then he took out the last remaining money in the house—four hundred-yuan notes—from the bedroom, carefully smoothed them out, and put them into his worn-out black wallet.

Wang Zhengnian’s youngest daughter is named Shulin. Not long after she was born, she was diagnosed with severe thalassemia. If she does not receive effective treatment, she may die prematurely in childhood.

This disease, which can only be cured with hematopoietic stem cell transplantation, has completely shattered this fragile migrant worker family. In order to possibly get more reimbursement from medical insurance, Wang Zhengnian and his wife returned to their mountainous hometown from Zhaoqing with their daughter. The cost of stem cell transplantation is astronomical, so for now, Shulin can only temporarily alleviate her condition through blood transfusions. Each month, two or three blood transfusions consume over a thousand yuan, quickly draining the family’s resources, as if washed away by water.

With the collapse of their financial situation, so too have crumbled Wang Zhengnian's aspirations. Since graduating from primary school at the age of 16, he left his village and entered the city to work. This post-80s individual now knows how to send Weibo posts on his mobile phone, speaks fluent Mandarin, and can say "Bye-bye" in English when parting ways with others. He once dreamed of saving up money to buy his own car, open a furniture factory, and never have to work for others again.

But currently, Wang Zhengnian realizes that he is getting further and further away from his dreams. The family may just be stuck in this village where even cars cannot reach, living day by day. At 8 o'clock in the morning, the couple set off with their daughter on their backs. The mountain path runs along the edge of a cliff, some places are only as wide as two feet, and the entire way is steep inclines. After crossing the rocky riverbank and passing through the Longchuan River, they could finally squeeze onto a small public bus that comes every half hour, eventually reaching the county hospital.

The winter rain had fallen all night, and their shoes were soon caked with yellow mud. On the mountain road, there were only the splashing footsteps of the two people.

A new year without slaughtering pigs

Wang's family has lived in the mountains for generations. Looking up from the foot of the mountain, people cannot see the village hidden in the folds of the large mountain. But looking down from the Wang family's yard, layer upon layer of slopes can be seen, and the broad highway at the foot of the mountain looks like a white silk thread hanging at the end.

Wang Zhengnian and his wife live together with their two daughters and parents who are over 60 years old. That is an old house built in the 1980s, with blue-black brick walls filling a wooden frame. Now, no one builds such houses anymore; everyone wants to build brick houses, Wang Zhengnian said while standing in the courtyard. His hair was messy, and the gray mud on his deep blue suit jacket couldn't be brushed off.

At the end of October last year, Shulin was diagnosed with severe thalassemia. Because they had to frequently take their child to the hospital, Wang Zhengnian could no longer find a stable job in the county town, which was about 10 kilometers away from their village. And if they only relied on farming, the crops' yield could barely maintain their meager daily life.

This young man who excels at making furniture began to sell his strength.

The highway from Guiyang to Shiqian is under construction not far below, meaning the original journey of over 8 hours could be significantly reduced. Sometimes, before dawn, Wang Zhengnian would meet up with relatives at the village entrance to do odd jobs on the highway. He learned how to build drainage ditches or lift stones weighing over a thousand pounds with others.

"All manual labor, extremely tiring," said this man who is only 1.6 meters tall. However, working for around 10 hours a day earns him 80 yuan. Occasionally, small decoration teams from the county town hire him as a temporary worker, allowing him to earn 75 yuan a day.

But such work opportunities don’t come every day. When idle, Wang Zhengnian goes with his mother into the terraced fields. The land grows radishes, napa cabbage, and pea shoots. They place thirty to forty jin (about 15-20 kg) of vegetables into a bamboo basket, squeeze onto a small public bus at 6 o'clock in the morning, lay out a small stall in the farmers’ market, and sell them at 1.5 yuan per jin.

However, whenever their daughter falls ill, the money earned from these hard efforts quickly flows into the blood bags and is transfused into the critically ill Shulin.

This family is becoming increasingly impoverished. Shanghai reporter Liu Hangzhe, who has visited Wang Zhengnian's home multiple times, still remembers that last Spring Festival, the couple rushed back to their hometown from Zhaoqing. Before they even climbed the mountain, they received a call from their elderly mother: "The water is already boiled, you slaughter the pig as soon as you return."

Slaughtering the New Year pig was something they eagerly anticipated during their journey home, and it was a joyful occasion in the countryside.

But this year, to treat Shulin, the pig was sold before the New Year. The only New Year goods this year were a fish and a few pieces of cured meat sent by slightly better-off relatives.

Prior to this, they hadn't tasted meat for a long time. Ever since Shulin returned to the village with her illness, the entire family felt as if they had been emptied. On a regular day, they could only boil water on the pitch-black stove, blanch pea shoots, and add a small spoonful of lard and chili to make a meal.

In the twelfth lunar month, on January 8th early in the morning, Uncle Man from the same village slaughtered a pig. Wang Zhengnian brought his 3-year-old elder daughter Lan Yu to help. Wearing a red hat, Lan Yu stood nearby staring intently and asked, "Dad, when will we slaughter a pig for the New Year?"

The young father didn't know how to answer his daughter. Back home, he took out an old mobile phone given by someone else and posted a Weibo message: "When will I be able to give my dad, mom, and kids such happiness? I am also looking forward to that day! But why doesn't it come?"

Before the birth of their daughter, many believed she was a lucky child

On February 6th, the 15th day of the first lunar month, at around 9 in the morning, Wang Zhengnian managed to register at the county hospital. The pediatric examination room was crowded with crying babies and anxious parents. At this moment, Shulin, held in Peng Shuangqin's arms, was rather quiet. She looked around with wide eyes until a doctor in a white coat approached, causing her to suddenly burst into tears.

Peng Shuangqin knew her daughter was terrified of people in white clothes. Every month, they had to draw her blood for testing and provide her with blood transfusions for treatment. During the six to seven hours of transfusion, the needle had to be inserted into the forehead of this chubby baby. Without a bed to rest on, Peng Shuangqin and her husband could only sit on chairs in the corridor, taking turns holding her and soothing her gently.

It was precisely the birth of this daughter that plunged the entire family into despair. However, before Shulin was born, many believed she was a fortunate child, and she had even become an unseen child star in a documentary.

Just before last Spring Festival, a production team from the Shanghai-based Oriental Morning Post prepared to film a documentary about migrant workers returning home on motorcycles.

Commentators once described this arduous journey home as a deeply moving long-distance trek. Over 100,000 migrant workers, carrying their wives, children, and New Year goods, set off from afar, riding motorcycles back to their hometowns. On National Highway 321 within Zhaoqing, more than 7,000 motorcyclists pass by each day.

Wang Zhengnian, who departed from Zhaoqing on January 24, 2011, was just one of these 100,000 ordinary individuals. In the past year and a bit, he mixed concrete at construction sites, while Peng Shuangqin worked in a shoe factory workshop. Together with two other families from their hometown, they planned to ride three motorcycles home for the New Year.

This would be a journey spanning 1,350 kilometers.

Only after the documentary was completed did Wang Zhengnian reveal to the journalists following him that what he was bringing home wasn't just his wife, over 7,000 yuan saved over a year, and a bulging red travel bag, but also the tiny life growing inside his wife for just over two months.

This young couple remembered clearly that the unexpected discovery of pregnancy did not alter their original plan to go home. The reality before them did not allow them the option of a safer journey: if they took a long-distance bus, it would cost them over 1,000 yuan; whereas riding a motorcycle home would cost less than 300 yuan.

Besides the child in her womb, they also had a 2-year-old elder daughter Lan Yu back home.

Lan Yu was born in Zhaoqing but was sent back to Shiqian when she was seven months old. There was no telephone at home, so twice a week, the 60-something grandmother would carry her granddaughter up to a cousin's house on a higher slope, waiting for Wang Zhengnian to call here for a brief chat of a few minutes.

"I miss her very much," the young father choked up, hoping that when he returns, she will have grown taller and will call out 'Mommy' and 'Daddy.' He smiled with anticipation, yet tears welled up in his eyes.

Yet, they did not regret their choice. If they had kept Lan Yu in Zhaoqing, it would mean that Shuangqin would have to give up her job at the shoe factory and a modest income. They harbored many long-term plans: to try and earn more money, first paying off the 40,000 yuan debt incurred from wedding banquets and dowry, living frugally, and eventually saving enough money to bring Lan Yu to Guangdong for better education. More education would make it easier for girls to work and live in the future, Wang Zhengnian said.

During their absence, Lan Yu once wandered out of her grandmother's sight. That time, a scorching hot poker fell, burning the little girl's face. A brown scar remains today, like an indelible mark, on Lan Yu's right cheek.

To earn more money, they didn't return home the previous Spring Festival and never saw the child.

However, 2010 was a good year; they paid off their debts. "I was particularly happy, being able to see our parents and children," Wang Zhengnian happily laughed when talking about his hometown in the documentary.

Carrying all their savings and a small life still inside his wife, they were finally going home.

A perilous journey in the womb

This was an extremely dangerous journey. They would ride from daylight to darkness, from the affluent regions of this country to the poor mountainous areas, from flat highways to snow-covered icy mountain roads.

Wang Zhengnian understood better than anyone the dangers of the journey. In the documentary titled "1350km," he smiled at the camera: "Family members just look forward to your departure today and expect you to arrive home the day after tomorrow. But I myself don't even know if I'll make it home by then."

Among the 100,000 people returning home, some unfortunately died on this road, and others lost 13 pounds by the time they got home. Wang Zhengnian, carrying his wife and unborn daughter, knew he had to be extra careful.

Before departure, his nephew gave him a subwoofer and an MP3 player. Wang Zhengnian enthusiastically downloaded several energizing songs, such as "Wealth God Arrives" and "Shanghai Bund." However, this thunderous music nearly drove the pregnant Shuangqin crazy, almost bursting her ears.

Nevertheless, the noisy songs didn't accompany the journey for long. As the temperature dropped lower, ice hung from both sides of the tree branches, and even the helmets were covered with frost. Wang Zhengnian continuously wiped snot off his hands. On the icy roads, they constantly crashed their motorcycles. The speaker was soon smashed beyond sound, followed by the newly bought clothes for the New Year being torn.

Nowadays, it's hard to tell from the documentary that Peng Shuangqin, bundled up tightly in a down jacket, was pregnant. But every time they crashed, Wang Zhengnian would momentarily worry. Along the way, the pregnant Shuangqin had lost count of how many times she had fallen on the icy ground.

When he noticed his wife leaning against his back and falling asleep, Wang Zhengnian worriedly stopped the motorcycle.

"Don't sleep, hey?" He turned back and tapped his wife's helmet, "How are you feeling? Are you hungry?"

Peng Shuangqin didn't respond, but she opened her eyes. The motorcycle continued its journey.

Journalist Lu Haitao, who followed them closely in a car, remembered that the three motorcycles ahead kept getting lost endlessly. They had no GPS navigation, not even a map. All they had was a notebook, with one page folded at the edges. On that paper, the route from Zhaoqing to Shiqian was recorded, mostly filled with misspelled words.

As they reached Yangchang Town, only 90 kilometers from home, the snow started falling, and the temperature dropped below zero degrees Celsius. The motorcycles could hardly move, and the wives stretched their feet, scraping the ground to add resistance to the vehicles. But this road was too dangerous. A fellow cousin sent his wife on a bus costing 60 yuan per person. However, Peng Shuangqin refused to take the bus, saying the road was tough, and it was comforting to stay by his side.

The originally planned trip of three days and two nights was extended to five days and four nights due to constant detours and crashes.

Before arriving home, Wang Zhengnian tore off the dirty cloth wrapped around his shoes. In the county market, he bought two pounds of beautifully packaged candy. Then, he struggled to push the motorcycle up the bumpy mountain road.

Their daughter Lan Yu, whom they hadn't seen for over a year, was pulled by her grandmother, standing on the muddy road waiting for her parents. She shyly looked at Peng Shuangqin's outstretched hands and walked away.

After returning home, Wang Zhengnian feared upsetting his mother and never mentioned the hardships and dangers of the journey. He cheerfully told his mother, "I was very happy driving the motorcycle, not tired at all, and I could see the world everywhere." But secretly, he cried.

Back then, the future seemed simple; whatever they wanted, they believed they would have.

To welcome the birth of their second daughter, Shulin, Wang Zhengnian and Peng Shuangqin decided to stay in their hometown temporarily.

This was not an unavoidable choice. Despite being away from home for only over a year, Wang Zhengnian was surprised to find himself almost losing his sense of direction.

The highway often mentioned by his parents in phone calls was under intense construction. What used to seem impossible—a mountain highway—now felt like it would be completed soon. Wooden houses on Bayi Street had all been replaced by brick houses, and even buildings of fifteen to twenty stories existed in the county town.
