A Vocational School Girl's Battle in Shenzhen's Workplace -- China's Workplace

by njbrtt94 on 2011-12-08 10:40:39

In March 2003, despite the original boss's reluctance to let me go, I resolutely left that private enterprise because I wanted to seek a broader space for growth.

Firstly, I would like to clarify. I am a 22-year-old girl with a secondary vocational school diploma. Including this job I resigned from, I have worked in three jobs in total. The first job was as a regular employee in a large listed company; the second job was as a secretary in a property management company; the third job was as an internal affairs supervisor in a private enterprise. Sounds good as an internal affairs supervisor, but in reality, I was just a housekeeper who took care of everything, and it was exhausting. Although the boss trusted me very much, that small company with only twenty or so people could not contain my soaring ambition. I wanted to stay in Shenzhen, so I had to find a way forward for my career.

However, girls like me can be found everywhere on the streets of Shenzhen. What qualifications do I have to continue living well in this fiercely competitive city? At school, I studied electrical engineering, and I only have a secondary vocational school diploma. Many job advertisements clearly state that they require at least a junior college degree, and a bachelor’s degree is even more common. Just this point alone is enough to make me feel inadequate. If I want to do management, besides lacking a diploma, I also lack sufficient experience and presence. As for technical work, it’s obviously impossible. Those are my biggest headaches, so I could only focus on secretarial work, which is suitable for young girls.

Once, in the bustling talent market, I mustered up the courage to stand in front of the recruitment booth of a certain company to apply for a clerk position. In front of me was a girl who looked about my age, with a tired expression. She respectfully handed her resume to the examiner. I clearly remember that the recruiter was a woman in her thirties. She took the resume, glanced at it, then looked at the girl, and said in a contemptuous tone: "You're 26 years old and you still dare to apply for a clerical position? And you only have a secondary vocational school diploma?" I saw the girl's face turn red immediately, her eyes flickering with委屈 (grievance), helplessness, and perhaps anger. After grabbing her resume, she quickly disappeared into the dense crowd.

Afterward, I also left the talent market filled with intense anger and enormous psychological pressure. This experience made me realize that diplomas will always be a hindrance to my development. Moreover, as a girl, being a secretary is definitely not a long-term solution. Even if I couldn't currently find a more suitable job, at least I needed to find a large, vibrant, and growth-potential company as a platform for future leaps. Only in such a company could my fate possibly change.

Three days later, I successfully passed the interview for the position of department secretary in the engineering department of a certain group in Shenzhen, relying on my solid foundation in writing, English, and good appearance. The manager of the department told me to start next week. Unexpectedly, the next day, another company informed me to come for an interview. I felt very curious because I didn’t apply to this company at all. Thinking about it, staying at home was just idling away time anyway, so going out to gain some experience might be good. After arriving at the company, a female manager in her thirties greeted me. She was beautiful and looked competent. After a brief self-introduction, she magically pulled out the resume I filled out when applying to the engineering department of a certain group, and said to me: "You must be wondering why I have this resume. To be honest, the manager who interviewed you yesterday is my husband. I saw your resume at his place and became particularly interested in you. Can you explain the phrase 'I have individual writing skills' on your resume?"

Finally, after submitting three different types of articles for her review, I was hired by the company and assigned to work as an assistant to the principal under the market department she supervised (later I found out she was the vice president of the company). Unexpectedly, on the first day of reporting, the HR manager of the company gave me a huge humiliation, which still makes me feel as if needles were pricking my back when I think about it now.
