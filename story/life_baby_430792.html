Many parents think that the more expensive the toy, the better it is for the child's intellectual development. Is this really true? In fact, for children, toys are not just commodities. The value of a toy has nothing to do with its price. What matters to children is whether the toy can attract their attention, which is the standard for evaluating whether a toy is good or not. Toys that allow children to fully use their imagination, transform in various ways according to their wishes, and can be freely disassembled and reassembled, as well as simple, natural, raw materials or semi-finished products, are more precious to children. If a toy can only be demonstrated by adults and does not allow children to participate physically, children will not be interested. For example, if you buy a remote control airplane for your child, once the novelty wears off, they might prefer riding a small chair pretending to drive a car.

Suggestions for choosing and buying toys for children:

1. Choose toys suitable for the child's age and developmental characteristics.

Children of different ages have different preferences for toys. For example, children under 1 year old are more interested in colorful, simply shaped blow-molded toys, small animals made of different materials, music boxes that produce pleasant sounds, and small rattles. These toys can promote the sensory cognitive development of children. Children aged 2 to 3 develop motor skills quickly and need push-pull cars (duck cars, ride-on model cars, etc.) and large balls that they can chase and hug. After the age of 3, building blocks, plastic construction sets, dolls, dollhouse materials, and entertainment toys can all become their favorites.

2. Choose toys that offer multiple variations and new patterns.

Such toys allow children to use both their hands and feet, developing their attention, imagination, and observational skills while playing games that engage both brain and body. For example, balls, building blocks, sand piles, and sticks have endless ways to play and contain infinite wisdom, making children never tire of them and constantly come up with new ways to play. Can such toys fail to stimulate their intelligence?

3. Choose toys closely related to the child's life.

Toys closely related to a child's life, capable of evoking pleasant feelings, and with simple shapes tend to attract children more, such as various dolls, animals, simple mechanical toys, and modeling toys.

Children need toys just as adults need work. Toys are the best mentors and friends in a child's life and an indispensable spiritual nourishment in their growth process. However, children's selection and understanding of toys differ from those of adults. They focus more on whether the toy in front of them can be played with and whether it is fun, rather than how much it costs or how to play according to the instructions. Therefore, from the perspective of the significant importance of toys to children, the value of a toy depends on how much the child likes it, not on its price.
