Barcelona's alleged "escort" championship ignites a bombshell: Opponent: We'll give you 70 points, we'll compete for the second place.

by qinglai888 on 2009-12-16 13:09:37

Barcelona Accused of Being Favorited for Title, Sparking Outrage: Opponents Claim "Give You 70 Points, We'll Compete for Second"

Sina Sports - Last weekend's Barcelona derby left Espanyol feeling hard done by, while Barcelona felt their victory lacked dignity. All this can be blamed on referee Iturralde. He "mysteriously" awarded Barcelona a penalty, helping the home team to a 1-0 win over their fierce local rivals Espanyol. Subsequently, in the players' tunnel after the match, he red-carded Spanish player Baena, who had consistently questioned his decision on the penalty.

In all honesty, this penalty seemed somewhat deliberately aimed at harming Espanyol. Interestingly, under the shadow of one penalty, two completely different versions of the truth emerged from the Barcelona and Espanyol camps.

If Brualla is considered a "Real Madrid affiliated" referee, then the official for this game — Iturralde, one of Spain's most famous referees — belongs to the "Barcelona faction." Previously, whenever he officiated at the Nou Camp, Barcelona never lost. This season, he refereed only one Barcelona game, an away 2-0 win against Getafe. Another telling fact is that in the past six games Iturralde officiated involving Barcelona, five penalties were awarded, all benefiting Barcelona. According to Marca, four of these penalties were questionable, with suspicions of "gifting penalties."

The controversial moment occurred in the 39th minute of the match against Espanyol when Ibrahimovic crossed from the left edge of the box, and Xavi suddenly fell while jostling for position. Iturralde penalized Baena for pulling Xavi’s shirt during his retreat and awarded a penalty. This decision immediately caused strong dissatisfaction among the Espanyol side. Young coach Pochettino said cryptically after the game: “I didn’t see clearly what happened in the box; maybe the referee saw it. Even after watching the replay, I can't confirm it was a penalty. My players told me at halftime that nothing happened, so in my view, this penalty does not exist.” Espanyol player Luis Garcia complained bitterly that Iturralde's unfairness hurt them: “If we use this standard, we could also get a penalty in Barcelona's box. Yes, Barcelona is very powerful, but they shouldn't score points through such penalties.”

The reaction within the Barcelona camp was exactly the opposite. The testimony from the involved party Xavi was: “This is an obvious penalty, didn't you see? In the box, my shirt was grabbed, Baena pulled my shirt and prevented me from jumping. There is no doubt, it's a penalty; he pulled me, because I couldn't exert force with my legs, I could only fall down.” After Xavi fell, Guardiola innocently spread his hands from outside the field, indicating support for his player's claim of a penalty. However, when discussing this decision post-match, he admitted he actually wasn't sure if it was a penalty, merely stating, “Xavi told me Baena pulled him, and I trust Xavi.”

Clearly, Espanyol manager Planes didn’t believe Xavi: “How can Xavi say that? By Iturralde's standards, there could have been 12 penalties in this Barcelona derby! I insist that there was no physical contact between Baena and Xavi. Even if there was, it wasn't enough to cause him to fall.” CEO Collet added, “Why do we always suffer in every derby? Last time it was a penalty in the 93rd minute, and there was Messi's handball. This time again... If our opponent was Real Madrid, someone would have surely called for Franco's overthrow long ago. The federation should consider this issue. If it always goes like this, why don't they just give Barcelona 70 points and let the rest of us fight for second place?”

What others say doesn't matter. Regarding the penalty controversy, Baena, the involved party, remained tight-lipped, refusing to divulge anything until two days later, when the Espanyol player, who came from Barcelona's youth academy, appeared on Catalan Radio to recount the event. “Yes, I did pull Xavi,” this statement confirmed the possibility of the penalty, though either Xavi's act of falling was too exaggerated or Iturralde made an incorrect call, so he continued, “But it definitely wasn't a penalty. My pull on Xavi wasn't enough to make him lose balance and fall. Xavi's position was very forward, I reached back to sense his position, that's all. I blocked Xavi once, the force wasn't enough to make him fall, which can be seen on TV replays. Honestly, I found it hard to believe it was called a penalty, because before turning around, I was thinking Xavi might shoot.”

Reports also claimed that Baena confronted Iturralde in the players' tunnel after the game seeking justice and was subsequently shown a red card and sent off. What did Baena say to infuriate Iturralde? “I simply told him, go check the TV replay! But I definitely didn't use any aggressive tone, it was just like that.” What upset Baena further was Guardiola, as the young Barcelona coach mentioned in the post-game press conference, “Xavi told me that Espanyol's player admitted it was a penalty.” “I really don't know how they can say that, I never told Xavi it was a penalty. If we ever had a conversation, I would definitely tell him it wasn't a penalty.”

This controversy gave Madrid media something to sensationalize about, and the media strongly criticized this controversial decision. Both Marca and AS targeted Iturralde, believing he protected Barcelona to secure three points; otherwise, Real Madrid, having played one fewer game, would only trail Barcelona by two points. Conversely, the Catalan media, although Espanyol is also a Barcelona team, wouldn't speak up for this club whose badge carries the Spanish royal family emblem. To counter the capital media, the Sport newspaper specifically studied the game footage and provided screenshots showing Baena indeed pulling Xavi. Deputy editor Carazo of the Sport stated, “Clearly, it was a penalty.” More interestingly, the paper also produced a screenshot from Real Madrid's game, where Benzema was possibly offside when receiving the ball leading to Real Madrid's first goal against Valencia. The headline was provocatively titled: “Real Madrid's first goal was offside, alright, what's there to argue about?”

Fortunately, the point of contention involving Barcelona, Xavi, and Guardiola has now moved away from the center of the storm in Spain as they have started their Club World Cup journey in Abu Dhabi. In their eyes, winning this championship is far more meaningful than debating whether it was a penalty or not. (Liu Hui)
