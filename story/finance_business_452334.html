According to a popular statistic, office workers nowadays spend as much as 3.4 hours of their 8-hour workday on "boring internet time" aside from normal internet searches, browsing, and chatting. I can't help but ask: what exactly are people doing during this long period? The answer is self-evident - they are handling personal matters. Therefore, in the face of employees increasingly "hidden" practice of handling personal matters at work, companies have to seriously consider how high their office efficiency really is.

During economic downturns, SOHO (Small Office/Home Office) working has become an attractive option - allowing employees to work from home partially aims to cut down on office costs. However, SOHO also requires "investment," so is the return on investment worth it?

Although there are many issues regarding SOHO working - SOHO is becoming a major growing trend in the modern labor market. Statistics show that in the United States, 30 million people work remotely from home, accounting for 16-19% of the U.S. workforce. An annual survey by Olsten Corporation revealed that 56% of North American companies permanently or experimentally allow their employees to work from home, with 82% of high-tech companies, media, and publishing industries adopting this working arrangement. Insurance companies follow closely behind at 67%, while service and wholesale/retail companies come next at 62%.

In China, especially in large and medium-sized cities, more and more young and middle-aged individuals with excellent educational backgrounds and ambitious aspirations are joining the SOHO group. In Shanghai, there are 700,000 remote workers, and in Beijing, this number has exceeded 1 million.

I believe that the developed internet era has made this possible. We are now in the information age where a vast internet network can connect every corner of the globe instantly, thus redefining the concept of "going to work." In the past, one had to physically go to the office, but now as long as the work gets done and can be submitted promptly via email to the boss, it can generally be considered as being at work. Previously, only marketing personnel working out of the office were considered normal, but now we often see editors, journalists, consultants, web designers, etc., who mostly adopt flexible attendance methods. As long as the work is submitted regularly and on time, no one cares where you choose to work.

However, while fully enjoying the "cross-regional convenience" brought by the internet, problems do arise accordingly. Your employees are dispersed to work from home, all becoming SOHOs. So, how do you manage them remotely outside the office? In this regard, Teacher Tan Xiaofang shares two cases with you:

Cisco Systems is a networking company in Silicon Valley, California, USA, often referred to as a "company running on the internet." Cisco adopts a flexible approach to employee working hours and locations. Normally, Cisco provides broadband internet access and a wireless local area network for all employees, allowing about 25% of them to work remotely. In Microsoft and HP's office areas, employees carrying laptops can freely move between different office points. However, this must be based on a sound system.

In this aspect, HP's experience is that the company establishes an evaluation system for SOHO employees, setting clear and measurable goals for each SOHO employee and assessing these goals. HP found that due to the long-term implementation of goal management plans, managers easily accept the method of working from home.

Finally, a reminder to all SOHOs - according to Gartner Group's research analysis, by the year 2002, 20% of global office workers will choose to work from home.
