Discussion on the Application and Operation Relationship of Indicators

by gpgp6486 on 2009-11-22 12:08:58

Discussion on the Application and Operation of Indicators: The market is accustomed to using indicators for analysis because they are the simplest tools to identify, and also due to their ease of use. However, it's precisely because they are too simple that the market tends to learn and apply these simple tools. Often, when applying them, it seems like the results are not ideal or satisfactory. Yet, when used retrospectively, they appear particularly accurate. So where exactly does the problem lie?

The main difference lies in the usage during trading hours versus after the market closes. During trading hours, the situation is still ongoing, so it often looks good but can change with a single fluctuation. This ultimately leads to a time lag in the application of indicator settings.

Is it possible to use them effectively by changing this time lag? This is indeed possible, as the application of time lag revolves around "initial brewing, completion of brewing, launch period, and finally completion." All these stages have specific time relationships.

More importantly, indicators are often formed due to price relationships, so ultimately it’s the changes in price that affect the final relationship values of the indicators. This is the most crucial part. The participation volume, whether large or small, along with interference in price, often affects the final outcome. Therefore, if too many participants use the same indicator conditions, it will also alter the original pattern.

For example, during the initial brewing stage, someone is often quietly accumulating shares. At this point, the trading volume is generally not large, so a gradual buying phenomenon can be observed. After the time is completed and enters the completion of brewing, since the accumulation is finished, any increase in volume will lead to an upward trend. At this point, the trading volume is related to the turnover rate. As long as the trading volume is within the controlled range, the original brewer can steadily control the stock, and the price increase will continue.

The question is, how to initiate the move? When to initiate? What is the target? For instance, the current market is filled with many short-term operation models: buy today and sell tomorrow, aiming only to make a 1% profit. Because the pursuit is daily profits, forming rolling profits, this leads to immediate adjustment after a high volume day, unwilling to operate even one extra day. Encountering such an operational model, the market might be at a loss.

To complete the operational goal for each stock, in the end, it still depends on the mindset of the interveners. If their mindset is steady and pursuing long-term benefits, then you won't experience deliberate washouts or fluctuations after following them. Instead, there will be a steady rise, which is considered the best operational method. However, such interveners are rare.

Therefore, in the end, the market tends to resort to short-term operations, leading to increasingly shorter operational modes. Consequently, various operational tools emerge accordingly. In fact, any tool used for technical analysis has certain limitations. They usually rely on closing prices for calculations, so they only provide statistical probabilities. Generally, the highest probability is slightly above 50%, or up to 60%.

It's already very difficult to exceed such ratios, as there has never been an indicator that can achieve an operational success rate of over 80%. This is mainly because stock prices are dynamic and change according to market demand, not remaining constant. Thus, when assuming a condition remains unchanged while the market changes, it proves that human nature is diverse and variable. Human nature is the most complex and hard to predict, and therefore, it cannot be analyzed through a single indicator to understand psychological factors.
