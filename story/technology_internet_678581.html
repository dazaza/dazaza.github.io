Profit and Loss Analysis of Airports within the Country: 70% of Airports in Central and Western Regions are Profitable

by abcfofas on 2012-02-27 15:29:20

[Problems should not be considered solely from the input-output aspect of the airport itself, but more importantly from the comprehensive functions and effects of the airport to promote its establishment and development.] Li Jiaxiang said.

*Lookout Western Weekly* | Zhou Fancai | Guangzhou Report

At around 12 noon on November 26, 2011, flight MU2331 from Hefei to Urumqi took off from Hefei Luogang Airport. A Ms. Xie on this flight received flowers and a free ticket from the airport - she was the 4 millionth passenger transported by the airport that year.

For Luogang Airport, this was a memorable moment. According to several industry insiders interviewed by our magazine, based on civil aviation airport operating experience, an annual throughput of about 1 million passengers allows an airport to achieve profitability.

Hefei Luogang Airport, built in 1977, started relocation planning in 2006. The new airport was named "Hefei Xinzhai Domestic Airport" and officially began construction a year later. According to the plan, after completion, Hefei Xinzhai Domestic Airport will have the current top-tier aviation zone level 4E in China, large enough to rank among the top ten nationwide, and is expected to open for operations in the second half of 2012.

Statistical data shows that in 2010, the total fixed asset investment in the entire civil aviation industry reached 64 billion yuan, with a significant portion used for the 11 new trunk airports and the renovation and expansion of 60 airports completed that year. In 2011, the entire civil aviation industry invested approximately 70 billion yuan, with airport construction accounting for about 55 billion yuan.

According to publicly available materials from the Civil Aviation Administration of China (CAAC), during the "Eleventh Five-Year Plan" period, there were over 140 airport construction projects nationwide. According to the "Twelfth Five-Year Plan for the Development of Chinese Civil Aviation" announced by the CAAC in early April 2011, based on the existing 175 airports in China at the end of 2010, the number of transport airports will reach more than 230.

For at least the next five years, the enthusiasm for airport construction in China will not diminish.

Airport construction in central and western regions will become a focus.

On the same day that Ms. Xie became the 4 millionth passenger at Hefei Luogang Airport, the Yunnan Provincial Government held another on-site office meeting at the Kunming New Airport construction site.

As provincial capitals, Kunming's speed and confidence are somewhat more prominent. The existing Kunming Wujia Dam International Airport, built in 1922, is the second civilian airport in Chinese history. Located less than 7 kilometers from the center of Kunming, after three rounds of renovations and expansions, it has a designed capacity of 8 million passengers, while the annual passenger throughput in 2010 exceeded 20 million passengers. Further expansion is no longer feasible, making the relocation of Kunming's airport inevitable.

According to established plans, the new Kunming Changshui International Airport, located far from the suburbs, will relocate operations in the first half of 2012. At that time, Kunming will become the fourth city, following Guangzhou, Guangzhou, and Guangzhou, to have a national large gateway hub airport.

Similarly, on November 26, it was also the first day of the calibration flight for the new runway at Xi'an Xianyang International Airport. In the previous year of 2010, the airport's passenger throughput broke through 18 million people, far exceeding the planned capacity of 7.5 million passengers set for that year. Consequently, starting Phase II expansion to add a runway became the choice for airport expansion.

Our magazine reviewed public materials and found that provincial capitals such as Changsha, Shijiazhuang, Zhengzhou, and Chengdu have already initiated or completed airport renovation and expansion plans.

Beyond these, airport construction in central and western cities, especially trunk airports, will be the focus of future airport construction in China. Regarding this, Li Jiaxiang, Director of the Civil Aviation Administration of China (CAAC), introduced that during the "Twelfth Five-Year Plan" period, infrastructure construction in China's civil aviation industry will reach 425 billion yuan. Among this, 56 new airports will be constructed, 16 airports relocated, and 91 airports renovated or expanded, with most projects located in central and western regions.

The expansion project of Handan Airport will be completed in June 2012. According to Handan Mayor Zheng Xuebi, efforts will be made to transform it into a domestic airport by the end of the "Twelfth Five-Year Plan." On November 22, 2011, the Kashgar Municipal Government established a leadership group for the Kashgar Airport renovation and expansion project, aiming to create the second hub airport in Xinjiang after Urumqi.

Recently, the CAAC approved the site selection for the Xinjiang Shache Civil Airport. According to the "Twelfth Five-Year Plan" for civil aviation, Xinjiang will construct the Shache Airport, relocate the Fuyun, Qiemo, and Shihezi airports, renovate and expand the Urumqi, Hami, Korla, and Hotan airports, and initiate preliminary research for the construction of Tumushuke, Loulan, and Taizhong airports. Among them, the site selection work for the Fuyun, Qiemo, Taizhong, Loulan, and Shache airports has been completed.

Yunnan Province, apart from positioning Kunming Airport as a regional hub airport, currently has 11 trunk airports in operation. According to the "Twelfth Five-Year Plan," by 2015, Yunnan will form a layout structure consisting of one large hub, six medium-sized airports, and 12 small trunk airports, with a total of 19 airports in the province.

And then there's Inner Mongolia. Our magazine reviewed the "Twelfth Five-Year Plan for the Development of Civil Aviation in the Inner Mongolia Autonomous Region" and found that Inner Mongolia plans to add five new trunk transportation airports: Zhalantun, Ulanqab, Linxi, Zhenglanchi, and Dongwuqi, and seven general aviation airports: Wulatezhongqi, Keshketengqi, Wushenqi, Zhungeerqi, Etokeqi, MorDaogha, and Molidawa. By the end of 2015, the total number of civil aviation transport airports (including general aviation airports) in the region will reach 20.

"There has been too much historical debt." Gao Jinhua, a professor at the Civil Aviation University of China who has long been engaged in airport planning research, told our magazine that from the founding of New China to before the reform and opening-up, the number of civil aviation airports in China did not increase significantly.

The overall benefits brought by airports

While the "airport construction boom" remains difficult to cool down, the losses incurred after airport construction cannot be ignored either.

In February 2011, Li Jiaxiang, Director of the Civil Aviation Administration of China (CAAC), introduced at a press conference held by the State Council Information Office that among the 175 airports in operation in China in 2010, 130 were in deficit, accumulating a total loss of 1.68 billion yuan. According to materials we reviewed, in recent years, those that could make profits were mostly large airports in places like Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, and Shenzhen, and some medium-sized airports, while more than 70% of airports in central and western regions were in deficit. However, due to the relatively small scale of the aforementioned airports, the losses were not prominent, and overall, China's airports were still profitable.

In 2002, the State Council approved the "Reform Plan for the Civil Aviation System," and civil aviation airports began implementing localized management. As a result, local governments' enthusiasm for promoting airport construction further increased. "We can't look at airports alone," said Gao Jinhua, believing that building an airport means driving the local economy.

According to internal materials obtained by our magazine from the Civil Aviation Administration, according to authoritative domestic institutions' calculations, the input-output ratio of civil aviation is 1:8. From the perspective of the airport industry, the domestic airport association (ACI) research believes that for every one million air passengers handled by an airport, it can generate a total economic benefit of 130 million US dollars and create 2500 related jobs. According to domestic research and analysis, for every one million air passengers handled by China’s airports, it can generate a total economic benefit of 1.81 billion yuan and create more than 5300 related jobs.

That is to say, even if the airport's operation itself incurs losses, the overall benefits brought about by the airport's construction cannot be ignored.

Through reviewing Director Li Jiaxiang's public and internal speeches in recent years, we found that when discussing the significance of airport construction, two airport cases were mentioned most frequently by Li Jiaxiang:

In 2009, after the Tengchong Airport in Yunnan Province opened, it had originally planned to reach a throughput of 200,000 passengers by 2020. However, within just two years of operation, it reached 240,000 passengers, thereby driving the local economy in tourism, commerce, logistics, etc., increasing by over fifty percent;

In 2008, after the Mohe Airport in Heilongjiang Province, the northernmost county in China, opened, it radiated within a 70-kilometer radius. Before the airport was built, the annual income of nearby farmers was about 3,000 yuan. After it opened, local residents earned 3,000 yuan in one day through agritourism.

Gao Jinhua also mentioned to our magazine that after the Pudong International Airport was built, there was initially a plan to close Hongqiao Airport. Suzhou, where manufacturing is extremely developed, is only one hour away from Hongqiao Airport by car, but the distance to Pudong is much farther. "If Hongqiao is abandoned, Suzhou would insist on building an airport." He explained that later, relevant national authorities considered the overall situation, and Hongqiao Airport continued to operate, even retaining part of the domestic flights originally planned to be entirely relocated to Pudong International Airport.

Clarifying the public infrastructure positioning of airports

On November 22, 2011, a piece of news about a civil aviation airport became a hot topic in public discourse.

That day, the National Environmental Protection Department issued a notice stating that the Chaozhou-Shantou Airport in Jieyang, Guangdong, which was originally planned to be put into use by the end of the year, had started construction without approval after changes occurred in the environmental impact assessment approval process, and was ordered to halt construction.

Jieyang Airport is positioned as a domestic trunk airport, planned to meet international standards of level 4E in the long term, and will become the largest civil airport in eastern Guangdong after its completion.

Jieying Airport is not an isolated case. On February 14, 2011, the National Audit Office released the "Audit Investigation Results of Partial Airport Construction Status in 10 Provinces and Regions". Among the 31 airports investigated, 5 airports started construction without the environmental impact assessment report being approved.

Internal information from the Civil Aviation Administration indicates that after the report was released, the State Council leaders instructed to rectify according to the audit requirements.

The concerns regarding operational efficiency and environmental impact assessments have not dampened local governments' enthusiasm for airport construction.

Li Jiaxiang has repeatedly hinted in various occasions that in 2010, 53 provincial leaders from 29 provincial-level municipalities visited the Civil Aviation Administration to discuss development matters. On April 2011, at the "National Civil Aviation Planning and Airport Work Conference" held in Guiyang, Li Jiaxiang said that on March 8, 2010 alone, leaders from 10 provinces, cities, and autonomous regions visited the Civil Aviation Administration.

"Problems should not be considered solely from the input-output aspect of the airport itself, but more importantly from the comprehensive functions and effects of the airport to promote its establishment and development." Li Jiaxiang said.

Regarding the reality of deficits, the Civil Aviation Administration has taken multiple measures through subsidies. In 2008, the administration implemented the "Interim Measures for the Management of Subsidies for Small and Medium-sized Civil Airports." Over the following years, through policies such as interest subsidies for infrastructure loans and central airport usage fees, most airports in central and western regions received subsidies. Additionally, airlines operating routes to underdeveloped areas were granted a three-year protection period for exclusive operation rights.

In terms of investment, airport construction requires less investment compared to high-speed rail construction and yields more immediate benefits. According to internal materials obtained from the Financial Department of the Civil Aviation Administration, "Building an airport with a 3-kilometer runway typically takes about two years to become operational, with an investment of approximately 300 million to 500 million yuan. In plain areas, constructing a 3-kilometer highway generally costs over 300 million yuan; building a 3-kilometer high-speed railway costs about 450 million yuan (higher in plateau areas). Clearly, the investment in building highways or high-speed railways is roughly equivalent to building an airport."

On July 1, 2009, the "Regulations on the Management of Civil Airports" issued by the State Council officially came into effect. This regulation, for the first time, clarified the public infrastructure positioning of civil airports, meaning that civil airports need to balance more public welfare responsibilities. "This is the first time in Chinese history that this nature has been clearly defined," Gao Jinhua said, suggesting that this might help airport construction move beyond the debate over whether it generates losses or profits. Additionally, civil airport construction, especially trunk airport construction in central and western regions, plays a more prominent role in defense, disaster relief, and other general transportation functions.
