The eight most common mistakes entrepreneurs make: overestimating experience and underestimating personality.

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 11:58:51

Introduction: Today, the American tech blog Readwrite published an article stating that failure in the entrepreneurial process can sometimes be catastrophic. However, if entrepreneurs can promptly correct their mistakes and learn from them, even disasters can become valuable assets. The article interviewed eight successful entrepreneurs from the "Young Entrepreneur Council" (YEC) in the U.S., summarizing the eight most common mistakes people make during the entrepreneurial journey.

Here is the full text of the article:

In the entrepreneurial process, some failures may be catastrophic. But for those entrepreneurs who can timely correct their mistakes and dare to step out of the shadow of failure, disaster can also be a treasure. Going through a more difficult entrepreneurial process might result in better processes, more outstanding employees, and more loyal customers.

Eight successful entrepreneurs from the American Young Entrepreneur Council discussed their views on how to handle failure during the entrepreneurial process and shared the experiences and lessons they learned from their failures.

Assuming what customers need

—Lee McNiel, Founder of ReviewPush

I made a mistake in my entrepreneurial process by spending a lot of time and effort developing a new feature that customers did not like. I assumed that customers would prefer RRS subscriptions over email notifications. Soon, I found out that almost none of my customers used RSS readers, which shocked me greatly. Besides this drawback, the added RRS subscription entry also cluttered up the user interface.

After realizing the error, I decided to hide this new feature, not promoting it as a main service but only offering RRS subscription services to those customers who actively requested it. Since then, we no longer develop new features based on assumptions unless some of our customers find it truly valuable.

Misjudging the team's capabilities

—David Ehrenberg, Founder of Early Growth Financial Services

In the early stages of my entrepreneurship, I tried to make my CFO (Chief Financial Officer) also an active business development person. I was very eager to strengthen the company's business development capabilities and thought that having a group of professionals assisting would be very helpful.

It turned out I was wrong. My CFO was a knowledgeable financial talent but not a good salesperson. From that mistake, I realized that I cannot ask employees to do things they are unfamiliar with. Instead, I had to take other methods to expand the business.

Not truly understanding your market

—Jay Wu, Founder of Best Drug Rehabilitation

The biggest mistake I made when starting my business was not correctly understanding the target market. There were significant differences among my target customers in different locations. During the expansion process, I did not make corresponding adjustments but simply copied the same model, hoping to achieve equally good sales revenue and profits. However, I soon encountered trouble because we found that the sales of the same product varied greatly across different stores — a bestseller in one store became completely ignored in another.

After figuring out the root cause of the error, I had to record each unsold item in the underperforming stores and clear them out through discounts. This mistake taught me that when entering a new market, you need to understand the real needs of local users and not blindly copy past successful experiences. Always know your market — this is the invaluable experience I gained from this costly lesson.

Overestimating experience, underestimating personality

—John Berkowitz, Founder of Yodle

In the early stages of my entrepreneurship, I followed the advice of a seasoned veteran in the industry and changed the principle of hiring employees from valuing personality and ability to focusing on skills and experience. This caused immense waste in my human resources, hindered the formation of corporate culture, and slowed down the company's development.

When I realized the problem in the recruitment process, I combined the advantages of both approaches and hired suitable talents for different positions in the company.

Hiring unsuitable employees

—Michael Costigan, Youth Leadership Specialist

This is a fairly common mistake, and it is hard to find a company that has never hired the wrong people. After establishing my personal brand and having the hope of stable income, I felt it was time to hire someone to handle my daily affairs. Although I thought I provided sufficient training, the person I hired could not integrate into my product or match my level of enthusiasm. Eventually, due to communication issues and style problems, he strained my relationship with partners.

Looking back, I should have invested more effort in the recruitment process, such as setting more scenarios to test the candidates' abilities and responses, to deeply judge whether the candidate could meet my requirements.

Choosing cheap options

—Alexis Wolfer, Founder of The Beauty Bean

When I started designing the TheBeautyBean.com website, to save money, I chose cheap developers and designers. It turned out to be a huge mistake! Later, I had to completely redo their design. Indeed, saving money and pursuing cost-effectiveness is crucial for startups, but you must ensure that today's penny-pinching does not affect your future development.

Hiring talent based solely on work experience

—Ziver Birg, Founder of ZIVELO

When I started my business, I hired a senior Chief Operating Officer (COO) with over 8 years of experience, having worked in companies ranging from 10 to 600 people. The results later showed that I might as well have spent less money hiring a less experienced but capable COO. Perhaps a less experienced COO would be more motivated to prove themselves and be more passionate about the work. If you let employees grow with your company, they will become the pillars of your company and absolutely loyal.

Choosing the wrong partner

—Andy Karuza, Founder of brandbuddee

When starting a business, seeking partners is normal. However, my experience shows that I would rather not have chosen some partners, even though I did encounter some great ones. In other words, I set the threshold too low, allowing many people to easily join my company as partners, which planted numerous hidden dangers for the company's future development.

I sincerely urge entrepreneurs to carefully evaluate the value of potential partners and ensure that their expectations for the company align with others (not too extreme). At any time, the company should prioritize its business. If a partner's behavior affects the company's business development, they either need to change or leave.
