US Stops Selling Infant Cold Medicine

by tonzhuanyin0y on 2012-03-01 01:17:40

On October 11, the Consumer Healthcare Products Association, which represents American pharmaceutical companies, announced that it would voluntarily stop selling 14 over-the-counter cough and cold medicines for children under two years old in the United States. If used improperly, these drugs could pose a threat to children's health. The main ingredients of the discontinued drugs include acetaminophen (paracetamol), decongestants (pseudoephedrine), dextromethorphan, antihistamines, etc. The U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention has announced that in 2004 and 2005, at least 1,500 children under two experienced seizures and cardiovascular, respiratory, and nervous system side effects after taking the medication. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) also reported that from 1969 to 2006, there were 54 cases of patients who died after taking decongestants, and 69 cases of death after taking antihistamines, most of them infants under two years old. Four children in Baltimore alone died after taking these drugs. As a result, the Health Commissioner of Baltimore initiated a petition to the FDA. This health commissioner said: "The FDA must take more action to protect children; these drugs are obviously ineffective and unsafe." For this reason, the FDA will hold a hearing to review the safety of these drugs.

However, major pharmaceutical companies have different opinions on this matter. They claim that as long as they are taken appropriately, these drugs are safe and effective. The discontinuation of these drugs is due to concerns about parents giving their children an overdose, leading to complications.

Once the news broke in the U.S., many Chinese media outlets reported on the matter, and many readers asked Life Times: "Paracetamol is a common drug, can we still give cold and cough medicine to infants under two years old?" For this, our newspaper interviewed Professor Sun Zhongshi, Director of Pharmacy at the Naval General Hospital, who has been closely following this issue. "This time, the U.S. suspension mainly targets pseudoephedrine, dextromethorphan, and antihistamines, not paracetamol," said Sun Zhongshi. These three types of drugs can alleviate symptoms such as nasal congestion and coughing during a cold, so they are often mixed with paracetamol to form compound cold medicine. Studies have shown that these three types of drugs indeed tend to cause adverse reactions in infants. However, if the dosage specified in the instructions is strictly followed when given to infants, serious adverse reactions generally do not occur. However, many parents hope to cure their children quickly, so they arbitrarily increase the dosage or frequency of administration. "Since infants' development is incomplete and their metabolic functions are not fully developed, many adverse reactions occur," Sun Zhongshi added.
