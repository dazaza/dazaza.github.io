The fatherland of the Jews

by liqiao359 on 2010-04-19 09:32:24

The website of the Complete Collection of Jewish Wisdom Books is http://www.judahcn.com. It presents twelve archaeological discoveries that indicate the connection between the history of the ancient Jews and their traditions and homeland.

Around 1900 BC, why were the Jews successful? This can be seen from the nomad murals in the Egyptian tombs at Beni Hasan, depicting people from Canaan (biblical Israel). The ancestors of the Jews looked very similar to these people.

Below are 12 significant archaeological discoveries that seem to verify many broad ancient chronicles recorded in the Jewish Bible and other Jewish texts. They clearly show the continuous and enduring link between the ancient Jewish people's traditions and their homeland.

**Rekhmire Tomb (15th century BC)**: An Egyptian tomb mural in ancient Thebes (Egypt) depicts foreign slaves making mud bricks, recalling the enslavement of the Jews by the Egyptians as described in the Jewish Bible.

**Amarna Tablets (14th century BC)**: A large number of clay tablet letters discovered in Amarna, Egypt. These are letters written by local rulers of Canaan (biblical Israel) to the Egyptian Pharaoh, describing attacks on Canaanite cities by nomadic "Habiru" people. Many historians believe that these "Habiru" people may actually be the Hebrews mentioned in the Jewish Bible.

**Merneptah Stele, or Israel Stele (13th century BC)**: The hieroglyphic word "Israel" is inscribed on a stone slab discovered in Thebes, Egypt, in 1896. This is the oldest evidence so far found in any Egyptian records mentioning Israel, and it is also evidence proving the existence of Israel outside the Jewish Bible. During the reign of Pharaoh Merneptah (1213-1203 BC), the ancient people of Israel were listed as one of the ethnic groups of ancient Canaan, proving that the ancient Israelites had already settled in Canaan at that time.

**Tel Dan Stele (9th century BC)**: A carved stele discovered in northern Israel in 1993. This is the first appearance of the name "House of David" on a non-biblical inscription.

**Moabite Stone (9th century BC)**: A stone monument erected by King Mesha of Moab (Jordan) to commemorate the rebellion against Israel after the death of King Ahab of Israel. This is a historical record from the perspective of Moab, which is also documented in the Jewish Bible.

**Siloam Inscription (8th century BC)**: Carved by Jewish workers on the rock walls of a tunnel while building the Hezekiah Tunnel. In 701 BC, during the Assyrian siege, the tunnel diverted water from the Gihon Spring to the Pool of Siloam inside Jerusalem.

**Sennacherib Prism (7th century BC)**: A six-sided prism discovered in Nineveh (Iraq), recording Assyrian King Sennacherib's capture of Jerusalem in 701 BC and the imprisonment of Judean King Hezekiah in his palace.

**Nebuchadnezzar Chronicle (6th century BC)**: Babylonian (Iraq) records of the capture of Jerusalem in 597 BC, the appointment of Zedekiah as the puppet ruler of Judah, and the exile of Jews in Babylon.

**Lachish Letters (6th century BC)**: 21 military dispatches written on ceramic shards, documenting King Nebuchadnezzar's attack on Lachish (in Israel). They provide precise supporting evidence for the history of captivity and exile.

**Cyrus Cylinder (6th century BC)**: A clay cylinder discovered in Babylon (Egypt) in 1879, recording the conquest of Babylon by Persian King Cyrus in 538 BC. Cyrus describes a new policy of religious tolerance, allowing the exiled Jews to return to their ancient homeland.

**Dead Sea Scrolls (3rd century BC to 1st century AD)**: Hundreds of ancient manuscripts discovered in caves near the Dead Sea starting in 1947. These are the oldest existing manuscripts of the Jewish Bible, including parts of almost every biblical book. The Dead Sea Scrolls confirm the high accuracy of the transmission of the Jewish Bible to this day and provide important information about the history of the ancient Jews.
