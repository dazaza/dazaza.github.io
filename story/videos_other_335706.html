Definition, Characteristics, and Function of Tempering in Tempering Furnaces

Tempering is one type of metal heat treatment process, also known as "peen hardening." It is typically used to reduce or eliminate internal stresses in quenched steel parts, or to decrease their hardness and strength in order to improve ductility or toughness. Tempering involves reheating a quenched workpiece to an appropriate temperature below the lower critical temperature, holding it for a period of time, and then cooling it in air, water, oil, or other media. Alternatively, the quenched alloy workpiece can be heated to an appropriate temperature, held for a certain period of time, and then cooled slowly or quickly. Depending on different requirements, low-temperature tempering, medium-temperature tempering, or high-temperature tempering can be employed. Generally, as the tempering temperature increases, hardness and strength decrease, while ductility or toughness gradually increase.

Steel workpieces after quenching have the following characteristics:

1. They contain unstable (i.e., metastable) structures such as martensite, bainite, and residual austenite.

2. There are significant internal stresses present.

3. The mechanical properties do not meet the required standards. Therefore, steel workpieces generally undergo tempering after quenching.

Functions of tempering:

1. Enhance structural stability so that the workpiece does not undergo structural changes during use, ensuring stable geometric dimensions and performance.

2. Eliminate internal stresses to improve the workpiece's service properties and stabilize its geometric dimensions.

3. Adjust the mechanical properties of steel to meet usage requirements.

The reasons tempering has these effects are due to the increased atomic activity when the temperature rises, allowing iron, carbon, and other alloy element atoms in steel to diffuse more rapidly and reorganize, transforming unstable structures into stable ones. The elimination of internal stresses is also related to the reduction in metal strength at elevated temperatures. During general steel tempering, hardness and strength decrease while plasticity improves. The higher the tempering temperature, the greater the changes in these mechanical properties. Some alloy steels with higher alloy element content may precipitate fine metallic compounds within a certain temperature range during tempering, increasing strength and hardness. This phenomenon is called secondary hardening.

Workpieces intended for different purposes should be tempered at different temperatures to meet specific usage requirements.

1. Tools, bearings, carburized quenched parts, and surface quenched parts usually undergo low-temperature tempering below 250°C. After low-temperature tempering, there is little change in hardness, reduced internal stress, and slightly improved toughness.

2. Springs are tempered at medium temperatures between 350°C and 500°C to achieve high elasticity and necessary toughness.

3. Parts made from medium-carbon structural steel are typically tempered at high temperatures between 500°C and 600°C to achieve a good balance of suitable strength and toughness. The heat treatment process of quenching followed by high-temperature tempering is collectively referred to as normalizing.

When steel is tempered around 300°C, it often becomes brittle, a phenomenon known as first-type temper brittleness. Tempering should generally be avoided within this temperature range. Some medium-carbon alloy structural steels become brittle if they are cooled slowly to room temperature after high-temperature tempering. This phenomenon is known as second-type temper brittleness. Adding molybdenum to steel or cooling it in oil or water during tempering can prevent second-type temper brittleness. Reheating second-type temper-brittle steel to the original tempering temperature can eliminate this brittleness.
