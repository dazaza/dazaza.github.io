Translate 37signals' *Rework* into Cantonese and Shanghainese.

by diglog on 2010-12-19 13:51:26

Recently, there has been a discussion on the Chinese Twitter circle about the merits of two Chinese translations of *Rework* (the version translated by Li Yuci published by CITIC Press titled *Revisit*, and the online version jointly translated by Livid and Olivia). As for which one is better, I'll leave that judgment to you. However, this little book by 37signals is simple and easy to read, yet it poses a challenge to translators—especially in today's world where not many people are adept at writing short Chinese sentences. In my free time, I attempted a translation in Cantonese for your amusement. The original text of this section, "Ignore the Real World," can be found here.

"呢啲諗法響現實社會冇用架。" Whenever someone brings up new ideas, they often hear such remarks. This so-called "real world" sounds really discouraging. New ideas, unconventional ways of doing things, and unheard-of concepts are all ignored. Only the things everyone talks about endlessly and practices get done—even if these things have already been proven wrong.

In fact, upon closer inspection, you'll see that the citizens of the "real world" are pessimistic and hopeless. They expect new ideas to fail. They think society cannot possibly accept change.

But it gets worse—they drag you down with them. If you have big ambitions, they will tell you outright that your ideas are impossible. They'll say you're wasting your time.

Whatever you do, don't believe them. Even if their world is real, you don't need to live in it.

Why do we say this? Because our company, no matter how you look at it, shouldn't be able to survive in this "real world." In the real world, you can't have employees scattered across eight cities in two continents; in the real world, without sales or advertising, it's impossible to attract millions of customers; in the real world, when you grow big, you shouldn't share your insights with others. But we've done all these things, and the company is still thriving.

The "real world" is just an excuse—it doesn't exist in this world. People who slack off all day naturally need some justification for themselves. The "real world" has nothing to do with you. (Translated by Li Ruyi)

[1]: "Hea" in Cantonese is a sound without a written character, used to describe a state of "being idle all day but still feeling happy." A widely accepted representation currently is the character created by Hong Kong lyricist Wong Ka Kui: "走之底加一个『喜』字" (a walking radical combined with the character for happiness). The Shanghai dialect version provided by @k1oe:

"嫡厄想法來該現實社會肯定是勿來塞厄。" Whenever someone mentions any new ideas, they often receive such answers.

This kind of "real world" sounds utterly disheartening. No one adopts new ideas, unconventional methods, or foreign concepts. Only the already verified methods, even if universally flawed, are implemented.

In fact, people living in this "real world" are often pessimistic and desperate. They expect new ideas to fail. They feel that society isn't ready for change.

Even worse, they try to pull you down with them. Regardless of your full confidence and ambition, they will criticize you harshly and shoot you down.

Whatever you do, don't believe them. Even if this is their world, you don't necessarily have to live in it.

Why do we say this? Because our company couldn't possibly survive in this "real world" under any circumstances. In the real world, you can't have employees working across eight cities in two continents. In the real world, it's impossible to attract millions of customers without advertisements or salespeople. In the real world, sharing success experiences is unrealistic. Yet, despite doing all these things, our business is still booming.

The "real world" is merely an excuse—it doesn't exist. It's just an excuse for those who don't want to try. Keep far away from the "real world."
