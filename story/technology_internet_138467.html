The city accelerates the renovation of old areas to solve the housing difficulties of the masses. Evening Post reporter Guoqiang Ren, on-site pictures.

Evening Post reporter Dong Yang reported:

There is one date that Mr. Jiang will never forget for his entire life—

That was May 18th last year. On this day, the signing rate for the pilot project of the Nanquan plot in the "Tangyi Tang'er" old area renovation and relocation reached 75.5%, which meant that Mr. Jiang could finally bid farewell to the old house where he had lived for nearly 20 years. Before the Spring Festival this year, on the day Mr. Jiang officially moved out of "Tangyi Tang'er," he said he walked out of the gate smiling.

Old area renovation concerns people's livelihoods and benefits the masses. To tackle the tough task of "Tangyi Tang'er"—the largest old area renovation region in the Lujiazui area of Pudong—successive Shanghai Municipal Party Committees and Governments have devoted a great deal of effort and thought into how to make these 1,290 households of old city residents move comfortably and how to avoid unnecessary trouble during the demolition process. This has been a persistent challenge.

"The current demolition system mainly relies on administrative measures to initiate and implement demolitions. In deciding whether or not to demolish, there is a lack of pre-decision communication and negotiation mechanisms with the public. Often, it is the government saying 'I want you to demolish,' rather than the masses saying 'I want to demolish.' Why not listen more to the residents' wishes regarding the old area renovations, listen more to public opinion, and encourage more residents to actively support and promote the old area demolition?"

Facing a deadlock, facing challenges, starting from 2007, relevant municipal departments actively explored new mechanisms for old area renovations, aiming to break through the problems of Shanghai’s old area renovations and forge a new path for promoting urban old area renovations—the pre-consultation system for old area renovations.

Yesterday, at the "Shanghai Old Area Renovation News Briefing" organized by the municipal government, it was announced that Shanghai will expand the pilot work of the pre-consultation system for old area renovations.

Once talked about old areas with dread

On January 10, 2009, when Mr. Jiang and over 50 households of residents from the Nanquan plot moved out of the "Tangyi Tang'er" old area, the neighborhood committee specially prepared a signature memorial wall for the families moving out en masse, naming it "Laughing Goodbye to Humu."

"Before that day, none of us could smile." Recalling the past years in "Tangyi Tang'er," Mr. Jiang, who just turned sixty this year, sighed deeply.

The "Tangyi Tang'er" plot is located near the Nanpu Bridge, in the central urban area of Pudong New District, involving two neighborhood committees, Nanquan and Humu, covering an area of 31,854 square meters, with 28 residential buildings, 73% being non-standard housing, involving a total of 1,290 certificate holders. The first phase (Xujia Lane) includes 261 households, and the second phase (Humu) includes 1,029 households.

However, what are the actual conditions behind these numbers?

When talking about "Xujia Lane," Zhang Weiguo, the party secretary and director of the Tangqiao Community (Street) Party Committee and Office, left a deep impression. When Zhang Weiguo took office in the autumn of 2006, before coming to "Tangyi Tang'er," he had heard that almost no staff member in the street office didn't turn pale when mentioning "Tangyi Tang'er." Despite having mental preparation, what he saw still shocked him—

The old-style shanty town built in the 1950s had extensive access with a total of 11 exits, without even a single wall; over 1,200 households all had chamber pots outside their doors; to prevent sewage from entering their homes, many people even built "water barriers" over 30 centimeters high in front of their houses; the neighborhood had old and haphazardly connected wires; narrow alleys with winding passages; mosquitoes breeding in summer leading to a terrible environment; over two-thirds of the population were renters, and gambling and other irregularities were rampant despite repeated bans...

Faced with such chaos, more than just Zhang Weiguo drew in a cold breath. "Not only is the housing structure chaotic here, but the terrain is also low-lying, making it prone to waterlogging when it rains," Shen Hong, the secretary of the Humu Neighborhood Committee, recalled vividly. One summer, Shanghai experienced a typhoon, which was the most severe flooding incident in the "Tangyi Tang'er" area. Six fire trucks worked nonstop for 24 hours, taking two days to drain all the accumulated water in the neighborhood.

Mr. Jiang also remembered that flood clearly. "At that time, the water was up to our knees!" When Mr. Jiang first moved to "Tangyi Tang'er" in 1989, his child was in the fourth grade, and now the child is at the age of marriage talks. Talking about the old "Xujia Lane," Mr. Jiang felt more sorrow. "In the middle of Xujia Lane, there was a latrine station that smelled foul in the summer, but it was a must-pass route to work, so we always had to take a detour."

Since it was an old shanty town, illegal constructions were everywhere. One household even privately built a 4-square-meter iron house. In his own words, living in such an "iron shed" was like living in a morgue in winter and a sauna in summer.
