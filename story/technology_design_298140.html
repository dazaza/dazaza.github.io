Eight criteria for evaluating "entrepreneurial opportunities"

by ki582ganxi on 2009-12-07 18:11:54

How to Evaluate Entrepreneurial Opportunities

All entrepreneurial activities stem from excellent entrepreneurial opportunities, and both the entrepreneurial team and investors have extremely high expectations for the entrepreneurial prospects. Entrepreneurs are even more confident in the substantial profits that these opportunities will bring in the future.

However, we all know that nearly 90% of entrepreneurial dreams end up unfulfilled. In fact, the probability of a new venture achieving high success is less than 1%.

Between success and failure, apart from uncontrollable factors such as luck, it is evident that many entrepreneurial opportunities are destined to fail from the start. Entrepreneurship itself is a high-risk activity characterized by learning through doing, and failure can also serve as the foundation for success in the next venture.

Nevertheless, if entrepreneurs could first evaluate these inherently flawed ideas, poorly timed market entries, or those with fatal flaws in a more objective manner, many tragic outcomes could be avoided, and the chances of entrepreneurial success could significantly increase.

Below, we present a set of evaluation criteria for assessing the market and effectiveness aspects of entrepreneurial opportunities, explaining the content of each criterion factor to provide entrepreneurs with decision-making references for evaluating whether to invest in developing an opportunity.

Market Evaluation Criteria:

1. Market Positioning: A good entrepreneurial opportunity must have a specific market positioning, focusing on meeting customer needs while adding value for customers. Therefore, when evaluating entrepreneurial opportunities, one can judge the potential market value created by the opportunity based on whether the market positioning is clear, whether the customer demand analysis is precise, whether the customer contact channels are smooth, and whether the products continue to evolve. The higher the value entrepreneurship brings to customers, the greater the chance of entrepreneurial success.

2. Market Structure: Analyze the market structure of the entrepreneurial opportunity through five aspects: entry barriers, bargaining power of suppliers, customers, and distributors, the threat of substitute competitive products, and the intensity of internal market competition. Through market structure analysis, one can understand the future position of the new enterprise in the market and the extent of possible counterattacks from competitors.

3. Market Size: The size and growth rate of the market also play an important role in determining the success or failure of a new enterprise. Generally speaking, larger markets tend to have lower entry barriers, and the level of market competition may slightly decrease. If entering a highly mature market, despite its large scale, since it no longer grows, profit margins will inevitably be small, making this new enterprise unworthy of further investment. Conversely, a growing market is often full of business opportunities, and as long as the timing of entry is correct, there will always be room for profit.

4. Market Penetration: For an entrepreneurial opportunity with significant market potential, evaluating market penetration (the process of realizing market opportunities) will be a very important influencing factor. Smart entrepreneurs know to enter the market at the optimal time, which is when market demand is about to grow dramatically, and they are already prepared to receive orders.

5. Market Share: The expected market share goal from an entrepreneurial opportunity can indicate the future market competitiveness of this new company. Generally speaking, to become a market leader, one needs to have at least 20% market share. However, if the market share is below 5%, although the market competitiveness of this new enterprise is not high, it will naturally affect the future value of the company's listing. Especially in high-tech industries with winner-takes-all characteristics, new enterprises need to have the ability to be among the top few in the market to have more investment value.

6. Product Cost Structure: The cost structure of a product can also reflect the bright prospects of a new enterprise. For example, judging the degree of added value creation and future profit potential of a company can be done by analyzing the proportion of material and labor costs, the ratio of variable costs to fixed costs, and the size of economies of scale production.

Effectiveness Evaluation Criteria

1. Reasonable Post-tax Net Profit: Generally speaking, an attractive entrepreneurial opportunity should at least create a post-tax net profit of over 15%. If the expected post-tax net profit from entrepreneurship is below 5%, then it is not a good investment opportunity.

2. Time Required to Reach Break-even Point: A reasonable break-even point should be reached within two years. If it takes more than three years, it might not be a worthwhile entrepreneurial opportunity. However, some entrepreneurial opportunities indeed require a longer cultivation period, where these upfront investments create entry barriers, ensuring sustained profitability later on. In such cases, the upfront investments can be considered as an investment, allowing for a longer break-even period.

3. Return on Investment: Considering the various risks involved in entrepreneurship, a reasonable return on investment should be above 25%. Generally speaking, an investment return rate below 15% is not worth considering as an entrepreneurial opportunity.

4. Capital Requirements: Entrepreneurial opportunities with lower capital requirements are generally more favored by investors. In fact, many case studies show that excessive capital does not favor successful entrepreneurship and sometimes even has the negative effect of diluting investment returns. Typically, the more knowledge-intensive the entrepreneurial opportunity, the lower the capital requirement, and the higher the investment return. Therefore, at the beginning of entrepreneurship, one should not raise too much capital but rather create funds through accumulated profits. A relatively low capital amount will help increase earnings per share and further enhance the future listing price.

5. Gross Margin: Entrepreneurial opportunities with high gross margins have relatively lower risks and are easier to achieve break-even points. On the contrary, entrepreneurial opportunities with low gross margins carry higher risks, and companies are easily affected by decision-making errors or significant changes in the market. Generally speaking, the ideal gross margin is 40%. When the gross margin falls below 20%, the entrepreneurial opportunity is no longer worth considering. Software industry gross margins are usually high, so as long as sufficient business volume can be found, the financial risk of engaging in software entrepreneurship is relatively low.

6. Strategic Value: Whether a new enterprise can create strategic value in the market is also an important evaluation indicator. Generally speaking, strategic value is closely related to the scale of the industrial network, the benefit mechanism, and the level of competition. The value-added effect that the entrepreneurial opportunity can create for the industrial value chain is also closely related to the operating strategies and models it adopts.

7. Capital Market Vitality: When a new enterprise is in a highly active capital market, its profit recovery opportunities are relatively higher. However, capital markets change greatly; investing at market peaks results in lower capital costs and relatively easier fundraising. At market lows, the incentive to invest in new enterprise development is lower, and good entrepreneurial opportunities are relatively fewer. However, for investors, the cost at market lows is lower, and sometimes the investment return can be higher. Generally speaking, new enterprises in active capital markets are more likely to create added value, so capital market vitality is also an external environmental indicator that can be used to evaluate entrepreneurial opportunities.

8. Exit Mechanism and Strategy: The purpose of all investments is recovery, so the exit mechanism and strategy become an important indicator for evaluating entrepreneurial opportunities. The value of a company is generally determined by a trading market with objective appraisal capabilities, and the completeness of this trading mechanism affects the flexibility of the new enterprise's exit mechanism. Since the difficulty of exiting is generally higher than entering, an attractive entrepreneurial opportunity should consider the exit mechanism and strategy planning for all investors.
