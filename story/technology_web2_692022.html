Severely disabled young man writes a novel of over a million words with only one finger

by kplipvul on 2012-03-05 21:09:26

According to our news report, 25-year-old disabled youth Gao Chun from Changshu was born with congenital muscular dystrophy, leaving him severely disabled. Only his right index finger could move slightly; the muscles of his whole body were weak and powerless, severely hindering his movement and making him completely unable to take care of himself. However, Gao overcame unimaginable difficulties and used his only movable right index finger to control the mouse and click on a soft keyboard. Over more than four years, he arduously created a lengthy novel of 1.51 million words titled *Feng Shi*, which has recently been published by the China Federation of Literary and Art Circles Publishing House.

Starting in 2003, Gao began conceptualizing his long novel *Feng Shi*. In August 2004, he officially began writing it, but this was an extremely difficult process. Using his barely movable right index finger to control the mouse and click on the soft keyboard on the screen, each character required clicking four or five times, and characters with more strokes needed seven or eight clicks. Every day at around 7:00 AM, Gao would get up with the help of his mother and start writing at the computer. Except for eating and resting, he often wrote until about 9:00 PM. He persisted every day without taking a single day off. Over four years, Gao, with his tenacious willpower, successfully created the 1.51 million-word novel *Feng Shi* using just one finger. After sending the first draft to the publishing house, the editor commented that the book was "meticulously written with elegant language." Literary critic Zhu Huajun even specially wrote a preface for the book, stating in the preface that the work unfolds the grand tapestry of contemporary life, akin to a modern version of *Along the River During the Qingming Festival*. Especially upon learning that the author is a disabled person who spent four years completing the book while lying in bed, a deep sense of respect naturally arose.

The reporter observed that Gao's bookshelf was filled with books and magazines on literature and philosophy. "Potato Network really enjoys reading these books; they have greatly helped Potato Network's creative endeavors," Gao told the reporter. The novel *Feng Shi* depicts the complex mentalities between urban men and women, encompassing the sweetness, harmony, and compatibility of love as well as estrangement, coldness, and betrayal. It uses love stories to narrate the philosophies and spirit of life. "Potato Network's greatest wish is to create even better works," Gao said with hope and confidence regarding the future, mentioning the ultimate version of War God 1.80.

With gratitude in his heart, Gao thanked his parents. The patient who went missing became a mummified corpse, but Gao managed to "use" the mouse with his one functioning finger. Fate was cruel to Gao: despite having all four limbs, none could move normally; both hands had complete fingers, yet none could move freely. Tasks like washing, bathing, eating, dressing, toileting, and turning over in bed all required assistance. "To take care of me, my mother retired early from the state cotton factory at the age of 36 when I was in third grade; for my writing, she got up at 4:30 every day to attend to me, helping me prepare breakfast and wash up; due to facial muscle relaxation, chewing food was very difficult for me, so she chopped the meat finely and fed me bit by bit..." All these details were etched in Gao's memory. "In this life, the people I am most grateful to are my parents; without them, there wouldn't be me," Gao said. Hearing her son's words, Gao's father sitting beside him said, "I now have two wishes: that my son's condition does not worsen, and that he finds more joy in his writing."

Due to physical reasons, Gao did not enter high school after the middle school entrance exam. Feeling very disheartened and staying at home afterward, Wei Jun found life devoid of prospects and hope. Helplessly, Wei chose to write as a way to alleviate inner sorrow, expressing personal insights into life through articles. In 1996, Wei's first poem, *The Spirit of the Yellow River*, was published, and Wei happily took the 5-yuan royalty to buy two packs of peanuts for his father. Wei told the reporter that he had always liked literature since childhood, starting to write detective stories in the fourth grade. "After staying at home, in 1999, my first short story *Invitation* was published, and later, I submitted several articles to local newspapers, all of which were published. This encouragement was significant." In 2002, Wei enrolled in the four-year correspondence course of the Beijing Lu Xun Academy of Literature, systematically studying literary creation knowledge, greatly enhancing his understanding and skills in literary creation.

At the age of 7, Gao entered Changshu Bao Ben Central Primary School. Although he hadn't attended kindergarten, he studied diligently and reviewed lessons in the classroom during recess while other classmates played outside. After graduation, he entered Changshu No. 5 Middle School, where his grades remained among the top in the class throughout junior high school. Among the three years, being ranked fourth in the class was his worst performance. He successively received titles such as "Top Ten Outstanding Youth of Changshu City" and "Top Ten Role Models of Spiritual Civilization Construction in Changshu City." During the middle school entrance exam, although Gao scored zero in physical education, he achieved a total score of 621 points in academic subjects, reaching the admission line for key high schools in Changshu City at the time.

Born Unfortunate with Terminal Illness

On February 9, 1984, Gao Chun was born at the General Hospital of the Beijing Military Region. His father, Gao Yaoxing, who was then serving in a certain unit in Beijing, was delighted to see his chubby newborn son. However, when Gao was ten months old, his parents noticed that while other children could crawl forward, their son could only retreat and couldn't stand up. They took Gao to all major hospitals in Beijing, and the diagnosis was like a thunderclap: their son suffered from congenital muscular dystrophy!

To seek treatment for his son, Gao Yaoxing retired early two years later and returned to his hometown of Yushan Town, Changshu City, embarking on a long journey of medical consultations. Whenever they heard of a renowned doctor or secret prescription, they spared no expense to take their son for treatment. However, the most common statement they heard was: "Congenital muscular dystrophy cannot be cured; at most, symptoms can be alleviated through exercise." Despite this, Gao Yaoxing and his wife did not give up. "No matter what, we must raise our child and give him happiness!" Gao Yaoxing said.
