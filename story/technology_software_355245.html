Four major factors driving up CPI

by splinta24 on 2010-05-14 18:48:15

Currently, China's agricultural production has been affected by disasters, and the international bulk commodity prices are soaring under the accelerated depreciation of US dollar. The inflationary pressure will remain high. In view of the further decline in export growth, it is unlikely that the government will adopt a one-off appreciation measure. Therefore, it is impossible to rely on a single policy to deal with inflationary pressure. Instead, it will continue to adopt a combination of tight money and loose fiscal policies. Administrative price limits will continue. Subsidies for agricultural production and urban low-income groups will be increased, and RMB will also appreciate faster. Given the current high inflationary pressure and a large amount of central bank bills and treasury repo due in March, the central bank may raise interest rates and increase the statutory deposit reserve ratio this month. The inflationary pressure will continue for a period of time, and a single tool cannot work. The government will continue to adopt a combination of measures: Can the government quickly curb the rise in prices? This depends on which factors driving the rise in prices can be controlled by the government. The current inflationary pressure mainly comes from four aspects: First, short-term snow disaster factors. Besides causing transportation disruptions and material shortages, the snow disaster has also had a great impact on agricultural production, which may increase this year's food inflationary pressure because the rise in China's agricultural product prices has always been caused by supply shocks. The demand growth has always been very stable, but the supply fluctuates greatly. Once there is a reduction in production, it will cause price increases. Second, the factor of dollar depreciation pushing up international bulk commodity prices: The current round of inflation since 2007 has global characteristics, and ultimately lies in: Dollar depreciation inputs inflation globally. This round of inflation in bulk commodities and agricultural products began completely in sync with the dollar depreciation. In the past five years, the nominal effective exchange rate of the dollar depreciated by 35%, while during the same period, the CRB price index reflecting international bulk commodity prices rose by 100% (including energy) and 118% (excluding energy), and the S&P GSCI Agricultural Index reflecting global agricultural / grain prices rose by 152%. The inflation pressure brought by dollar depreciation to emerging markets is most obvious. All major developing countries including China have seen rising inflationary pressures recently. This is because the Engel coefficient of emerging markets and developing countries is relatively high (a large part of consumption expenditure is food), and the weight of food in their CPI is relatively high. Moreover, emerging markets generally have large construction investment needs. Therefore, the inflationary pressure brought by agricultural product inflation and the rise in international raw material prices on emerging markets is significantly greater than its impact on developed countries. This input-type inflationary pressure will not disappear before the dollar rebounds. Third, the cost-push inflationary pressure brought about by the rise in factor prices such as labor, land, and resources in China itself. Fourth, the lack of flexibility in RMB exchange rate has become the biggest shackle of monetary policy, resulting in a continuous expansion of money and excess liquidity for many years. Among the above four factors, the government can only control the exchange rate and factor prices, but cannot control the impact of snow disasters on agricultural production and the input-type inflationary pressure brought by dollar depreciation. Therefore, the inflationary pressure will remain high for a period of time.
