A Week of Driving Tesla Electric Cars by China's Tall, Rich, and Handsome Men

by anonymous on 2013-08-26 13:57:12

This article is from Duan Yongping's blog. It was written intermittently over several days, showing how his opinions were continuously revised and supplemented as he tested the Tesla. His final judgment is that, from an investment perspective, Tesla still has the potential to be a game changer; however, "after driving this car for a few days, I realized I didn't fully understand Tesla, so I decided to sell my shares first."

WE?(love) TSLA is the license plate number I registered for my Tesla. Cool, right? The question mark actually represents a heart.

On August 8th, I finally got the car.

The car is top-of-the-line, except for the panoramic roof (which costs an extra $1500), everything else available was purchased. The total cost, including taxes, was about $127,500. There should be a $10,000 tax rebate in the future.

So far, I've driven about 400 kilometers. Here's a brief summary of my experience so far:

One, all the known strengths of Tesla are present:

The appearance is great;

Acceleration is fast, comparable to the Mercedes-Benz AMG S65, possibly even slightly better for short distances (personal feeling);

Google Maps on the large screen is very convenient;

Both front and rear trunks are cool and practical;

The design of the car key and door handles is stylish (the key is somewhat functional but has some minor issues, not as convenient as the S65);

Driving it turns heads;

No more need to refuel in the future (more on mileage later);

I'll add more as I discover them...

Two, areas for improvement:

Tesla's advantages are still advantages, easily visible to everyone, so I won't elaborate much here. Let me point out a few unexpected dissatisfactions.

An additional cool feature: Tesla doesn't have a keyhole. Once you're inside, just put it into drive or reverse and you're good to go. This changes old driving habits, though I haven't found any issues with it yet.

Considering all the above advantages, I've recommended to many friends that they consider Tesla for their next car. At least five to six friends have already ordered the Model S or X.

However, after seeing my own car, I noticed some things I hadn't paid attention to before, so I want to specially remind everyone to seriously test drive the car before making a final decision.

I originally planned to sell my S65 once I got the Tesla, and had even found a buyer, but fortunately, I didn't finalize the deal.

There are a few things I don't like, though I'm not sure why:

After driving for a long time, there's a sense of motion sickness (mentioning dizziness might cause misunderstanding). Interestingly, I didn't notice this during the test drive, probably because it was short (only about 10 minutes) and I was excited, so I overlooked it. If you plan to drive this car every day, you should spend more time test driving, ideally with your family.

I'm not sure what causes the motion sickness, but it's definitely not due to the fast acceleration. For those who are curious, check out my blog's photo album. I suspect the most likely reason is the "energy recovery system" (don't know the official name, but it's the one that slows down when you don't press the accelerator), though I use the non-recovery mode, so the deceleration effect should be similar to regular cars?

The smell shouldn't be the cause, although the new car smell isn't pleasant, it's not abnormal either.

Then there's the "floating" issue. A while back, I met a friend who mentioned Tesla being "floaty" when we talked about it. I thought he was going to start talking about "Rhett Butler and Scarlett O'Hara," but instead, he started discussing the "suspension system." I don't know much about this, so you can research it further.

Also, driving Tesla does give a slight "floating" sensation, which reminds me of driving a Chery QQ years ago. The difference is that the QQ could only reach speeds of 80 km/h before it would "float away," whereas Tesla is much stronger than that.

Tesla's interior is hard to comment on. Yesterday, I saw a friend's Mercedes costing around three million RMB, and its interior was much better than Tesla's. I'm not sure why such a mature product is made this way by Tesla, yet they spent so much effort developing a feature where two children can sit in the trunk.

Driving my sports car these past few days, I found some interior function designs less convenient than the S65, suddenly realizing maybe Tesla Model S's thinking is still on sports cars? But the Tesla Model S consistently tries to appear as a sedan, evidenced by the child seats in the trunk, which isn't easy to pass safety certification. Is it a matter of skill or culture?

Three, let's talk about the process of buying Tesla:

I remember originally ordering a dark red car because that's what showed up on the website, but when I picked it up, it turned out to be bright red, quite disappointing. Checking the website afterward, I found they'd changed the color, leaving me without evidence. Then I asked if I could switch to a different color, and they said yes, but it would cost an extra $2000+.

I remember being told the car would be delivered to my doorstep since it was an online order, but when picking it up, I was told I had to go to a specific location, with no prior notice or apology.

A few days before the scheduled pick-up, I suddenly received an email saying the pick-up time was delayed by a week, with no reason given and no apology. Sending an email asking why it was delayed, I was told it was due to Tesla's delivery schedule being postponed, still with no apology.

It was said that they would send someone to install the charger, but when the time came, they said all local contracted electricians were busy, and we had to find someone ourselves.

Is it a cultural problem? Or a cultural problem? Or a cultural problem?

Thinking about the busy Musk and the Musk who mocked BMW, I gradually became less certain about Tesla, despite initially being very optimistic.

Of course, Tesla's battery technology advantage remains, but technological advantages rarely serve as moats. The culture behind the technology does.

My thoughts on investing in Tesla and "imagination and assumptions":

Firstly, I want to emphasize that writing about my experiences is mainly for those who bought Tesla based on my earlier recommendations. What I want to tell everyone is that my reasons for recommending this car remain valid, but there are some things I initially overlooked, so people considering buying should take more time to experience it themselves. Secondly, deciding whether to invest in Tesla based solely on my personal experience is absurd. Investments must be decided by oneself, and one should avoid things they don't understand.

I started paying attention to Tesla entirely because of its good features, from acceleration to appearance to the large screen map and convenient iPhone connection, plus the fact that electric vehicles save fuel (though I'm still unsure about the environmental aspect, especially after hearing about toxic rice).

I used to imagine that Tesla's cars combined all these advantages on top of existing cars. Based on this assumption, I once imagined Tesla becoming the largest car manufacturer in 15 to 20 years, with annual profits exceeding $2 billion. If that happens, the current price would still be reasonable.

After driving the Tesla, my feeling is that Tesla seems unprepared.

I am just an ordinary user, and my experience with Tesla only represents a part of people. After driving this car for a few days, I feel I don't really understand Tesla, so I decided to sell first. After all, many experts say this car is excellent, and truly knowledgeable people will make their own decisions.

Further supplement: Tesla's large screen connects very conveniently with smartphones, making phone calls and listening to music on the iPhone while driving very good.

Under normal circumstances, I only charge to 90%, otherwise it affects the lifespan. The mileage shown on the car is under ideal conditions, and in actual situations, it's about 70-80%. So, with a maximum range of 260 miles, I usually charge to 230 miles, which allows me to drive 170-190 miles, approximately equivalent to 300 kilometers.

Further supplement: Children all love the acceleration feeling, and there hasn't been any motion sickness for short trips, but I'm not sure about long trips yet.

It's now the morning of August 16th. As of yesterday, I've been driving the Tesla Model S for a week, averaging about 100 kilometers per day.

The dissatisfaction seems to be fading slowly, but the satisfaction remains strong.

Yesterday, I specifically drove to Tesla's Gilroy supercharger station to recharge, and since it was peak time, I had to queue.

Seeing that my electricity was enough to get back home, I just chatted briefly with a few users waiting to charge.

Each Tesla user seemed extremely satisfied with their car, and I didn't hear any complaints, even the slightest ones—amazing!

To summarize:

1. Tesla's strengths are indeed extremely powerful;

2. Weaknesses may be due to rushing development, and time might solve most problems. For example, interiors, services (if it's not a cultural issue).

3. Motion sickness might be an ongoing issue for some people, but after driving for a week, it doesn't seem as noticeable. Not sure if it's caused by the smell. People considering buying should try driving for longer periods.

4. From an investment perspective, Tesla still has the potential to be a game changer, and time will tell everything.

Reflection:

After driving the Model S for over a week, I think this car is indeed good. The initial dissatisfaction mainly came from comparing Tesla's features with the S65. After all, the S65 is a $200,000 car, so having some aspects better than the Model S doesn't mean Tesla isn't good.

I'm restoring my recommendation for people with the means to consider buying a Tesla. It's very practical, at least in North America.

As a user choice: this red Model S isn't for sale anymore, and I'll keep the S65 too, as I still prefer it for long trips.

In my opinion, Tesla can't replace gasoline cars, and probably won't be able to for a long time.

What I mean is that Tesla probably won't replace high-quality gasoline cars for a long time.

Today, I discovered a godlike feature of Tesla: it can show the car's real-time position via the iPhone app, which was unexpected.

Additionally, Tesla can unlock doors and turn on the air conditioning remotely. Remote air conditioning isn't very useful in Northern California, but people currently in hot climates will certainly appreciate this feature.

Tesla allows locking the keys inside the car and then unlocking it with a phone. Cool, right?!
