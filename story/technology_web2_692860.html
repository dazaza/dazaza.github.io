China's Moon Culture

● Interpretation of Moon Culture

Moon culture is a collection of mythological stories, historical legends, folk customs, scenic spots and historical sites, literature and art that use the moon as a medium and carrier.

● The Moon: An Emotional Code for Chinese People

In the hearts of Chinese people, the moon has always been a symbol of reunion, gentleness, and brightness, embodying their aspirations. In ancient Chinese philosophy, the moon holds profound significance. Ancient sages once said, "Nothing in nature surpasses the sun and the moon," meaning that besides heaven and earth, the most prominent natural images are the sun and the moon. Many myths and legends about the moon have circulated among the Chinese populace, such as Chang'e flying to the moon, Wu Gang chopping the osmanthus tree, and Zhu Yuanzhang's rebellion against the Yuan dynasty. Traces of these moon-related myths can also be found in ancient texts like the "Classic of Mountains and Seas", "Chu Ci", and "Huainanzi".

The earliest records about the moon appear in the mythology of Emperor Jun. The "Classic of Mountains and Seas • Da Huang Xi Jing" records, "Emperor Jun’s wife Changxi gave birth to twelve moons, which were the first to bathe." This indicates that more than a thousand years before the Common Era, the ancestors of the Chinese nation had already paid attention to the moon and attempted to interpret it, using it as a time unit closely related to themselves. In the "Huainanzi • Lanming Xun", written under the supervision of Prince Liu An of Huainan during the Western Han Dynasty, there is a record of the "Chang'e Flying to the Moon" myth: "Yi asked the Queen Mother of the West for the elixir of immortality, but Chang'e stole it and flew to the moon, feeling melancholy and sorrowful with no way to continue." Another version states that Chang'e swallowed the elixir herself to prevent Peng Meng from taking it (as recorded in the "Huainanzi • Wai Baipian"). Whether Chang'e became immortal by stealing the elixir or was forced to eat it, she longs for her husband in the moon palace and desires to reunite with him. The Chinese nation often uses "Chang'e Flying to the Moon" to express aspirations for eternal life and freedom, as well as the desire for family reunions. Even today, we still regard the moon as a symbol of reunion,寄托相思, and admire it together on the night of the 15th day of the eighth lunar month every year.

In the popular moon legends, the ancients, unwilling to see Chang'e lonely in the moon palace, imagined the Jade Rabbit and the Toad for her, expressing their kind and beautiful aspirations. The miraculous ability of the elixir that Chang'e swallowed and the osmanthus tree that Wu Gang chopped, which regenerates instantly after being cut, hint at an immortal life spirit. The waxing and waning cycle of the moon not only created the Chinese lunar calendar but also influenced the pursuit of endless vitality and the serene wisdom in Chinese philosophy.

● The Moon: A Source of Inspiration for Chinese Arts and Literature

Since ancient times, humans have explored the moon. Over thousands of years of cultural inheritance, the moon has been endowed with rich connotations and holds a prominent place in Chinese literature and art. China's moon culture is characterized by poetic imagery as its soul, conveyed through the pens of literati, reflecting timeless emotions, just like Chinese landscape paintings, which are subtle, elegant, distant, and tranquil. Poets' curiosity about the moon's mysterious unknown aspects sparked inspiration, while its changes in phases evoked various emotions. The enigmatic moon inspired countless poets and lyricists, leaving behind timeless verses: "What virtue does the moon possess that it dies and then revives? What advantage does it gain by nurturing a rabbit in its belly?" Qu Yuan questioned the moon in "Heavenly Questions": "What characteristic does the moon possess that allows it to die and regenerate? What benefit does it gain by nurturing a rabbit in its belly?" "If the osmanthus tree in the moon were cut down, the light would surely become brighter," Du Fu used romanticism to express his longing for his wife and boldly speculated: If the osmanthus tree in the moon palace were cut down, the moonlight would surely become brighter!

A full moon shining brightly over a vast distance creates a profound and deep atmosphere. The moon's phases provide rich imagery, achieving deeper meanings beyond words. For this reason, poets have written countless poems inspired by the moon, reaching a peak during the Tang Dynasty. According to scholars' statistics, the word "moon" appears more than ten thousand times in the more than fifty thousand poems of the "Complete Tang Poems"; Li Bai wrote one poem with the moon imagery for every three poems. "Bright moonlight before my bed, I wonder if it's frost on the ground. Raising my head to look at the bright moon, lowering it to think of my old home." Li Bai's "Thoughts in Still Night" has been passed down for generations, creating a profound atmosphere and sketching a vivid "moonlit homesickness map."

Using the moon to describe scenery highlights vivid and moving images that evoke aesthetic feelings. Du Fu's "Stars hang low over the vast plains, the moon surges along the great river," Meng Haoran's "Vast fields make the sky lower than trees, clear rivers make the moon closer to people," Wang Wei's "Bright moon shines between pines, clear spring flows over stones"—these verses with "moon" are timeless classics, known even by children.

Expressing emotions through the moon, whether familial affection, homesickness, friendship, or love, can be expressed through the moon. "The bright moon rises over the sea, shared by all at this moment. Lovers lament the long night, thinking of each other throughout the night" (Zhang Jiuling, "Looking at the Moon and Missing Distant Ones"), "Tonight everyone looks at the bright moon, but who knows where the autumn thoughts will fall" (Wang Jian, "Looking at the Moon on the Fifteenth Night"), "Dew turns white from tonight, the moon shines brightest over my hometown" (Du Fu, "Recalling My Younger Brother on a Moonlit Night")—feelings of longing overflow. Ouyang Xiu of the Song Dynasty's "When the moon rises above the willow tips, lovers meet after dusk" cleverly expresses the joy of a lover's rendezvous under the moonlight. "People experience joys and sorrows, partings and reunions; the moon waxes and wanes, brightens and dims—a truth difficult to achieve perfectly. But may we live long and share the beauty of the moon, even if miles apart." Su Shi's "Tune: Prelude to Water Melody" expresses the universal sentiment of valuing kinship. The moon can inspire associations, carry yearning, and incorporate philosophy. Wang Yangming's "Mountain House Concealing the Moon" is an example: "The mountain seems near, the moon far, making the moon appear small; thus, one might say the mountain is larger than the moon. If a person's eyes were as vast as the heavens, they would see the mountain towering yet the moon broader still." This poem reveals how one's perspective and breadth of vision influence their understanding of things and the world. In Li Bai's "Drinking Wine While Questioning the Moon," "The moon seen by people today is not the same as the moon seen by ancients, yet this moon has shone upon the ancients," not only reflects the insight of limited human life versus infinite time but also implies that whether ancients, moderns, or future generations, all are subject to the same scrutiny and witnessing of the moon, embodying the historical view and worldview of "life's insignificance and history's eternity."

Beautiful phrases and verses about the moon are not only found in classical poetry but also abound in modern poetry, prose, and songs. The moon brings endless inspiration, and during moon appreciation, people create numerous exquisite couplets, such as "The moon shines brightly every month, but the moon in the eighth month shines exceptionally bright; mountains are beautiful everywhere, but the mountains in Wushan are exceptionally beautiful," "On Mid-Autumn Festival, the moon in the sky is round, but the moon on earth is half; wanderers miss their hometowns, finding hardship away from home but sweetness in their native land," "The moon in the sky is full, the moon on earth is half, every month the full moon meets the half moon; tonight marks the end of the year, tomorrow the beginning, year after year the end meets the beginning." These are ingeniously constructed and filled with philosophy. Additionally, throughout history, many beautiful names for the moon have been coined: silver hook, jade hook for the crescent moon; silver plate, jade plate, golden mirror, jade mirror for the full moon; silver rabbit, jade rabbit, golden toad, silver toad, toad palace for the mythical creatures in the moon; osmanthus moon, osmanthus wheel, osmanthus palace for the mythical osmanthus tree; Guanghan Palace, Qingxu Palace for the two mythical palaces in the moon.

Worldwide Moon Cultures

Over long periods, the moon has been closely linked to humanity. Since humans appeared on Earth, they have observed the moon with curiosity, attempting to explain this mysterious celestial body closest to our planet.

In the Bible's Genesis, it says God initially created the heavens and the earth, then separated light from darkness, creating day and night. Later, God placed lights in the sky to separate day from night, creating two great lights—one large governing the day, the other smaller governing the night. The large light is the sun, and the smaller light is the moon. Together, they take turns illuminating the earth, marking days, seasons, and years. The light of the sun and moon differs in intensity, with the moon being more gentle, approachable, and endearing compared to the sun.

This biblical explanation of the sun and moon carries strong mythological elements. In real life, the sun and moon alternate daily: during the day, the sun radiates energy, providing life-sustaining power to all living beings; at night, the moon shines brightly, its silver glow quietly guarding all creatures. All things depend on the sun and the moon. Though different, both are indispensable. Under the sunlight, plants and animals accumulate strength for growth, and humans strive towards their ideals. Under the moonlight, all things quiet down, allowing all creatures to enjoy leisure time. The moonlight creates a different mood from sunlight, its soft glow enhancing the serenity of the night. In such an environment, people tend to temporarily set aside their responsibilities and enjoy a relaxed life. The tranquility of moonlit nights makes it easier for people to remove their masks, face their true selves, open their hearts, and express their inner feelings.

Moon cultures exist in civilizations worldwide. China has numerous myths about the moon, and Western mythology includes tales of moon goddesses. In Greek mythology, there are three moon goddesses: Phoebe representing the new moon, Selene representing the full moon, and Artemis representing the crescent moon. Artemis is one of the Twelve Olympian Gods, so most people know her. In Roman mythology, Artemis is called Diana.

In Greek mythology, Artemis is depicted as noble and beautiful, excelling among the goddesses on Mount Olympus. She has a graceful figure, long legs, and a slender waist. Her skin is smooth and radiant, glowing with the holy light of the moon. A luminous moon adorns her forehead, her brown curls reach her waist, flowing in the wind. Her eyes are the most beautiful among the goddesses, clear and lively like moonlight. In essence, her appearance represents all the nobility and beauty of the moon.

According to Roman mythology, Diana is not only beautiful but also the protector of virgins, making her name synonymous with "chaste virgin." Legends say she had many suitors but refused to marry, loving only freedom. Thus, Diana symbolizes beauty, chastity, and the love and aspiration for freedom.

Greek mythology also contains touching love stories about the moon goddess. Artemis is the daughter of Zeus and Leto and the twin sister of Apollo, the sun god. Artemis oversees the moon and loves hunting, excelling in archery. Later, she fell in love with Orion, the son of Poseidon, but Apollo opposed their relationship and tricked Artemis into killing Orion. Grief-stricken, Artemis vowed never to see Apollo again, explaining why the sun and moon do not appear together in the sky in Greek mythology.
