My spiritual forbears did not take kindly to living under theocrats who embraced religious liberty for themselves but vetoed it to others. "Forced worship stinks in God's nostrils," thundered the dissenter Roger Williams as he was exiled from Massachusetts for denying Puritan legislature over his conscience. Baptists there were a "wretched insignificant minority" but they were agitators for freedom and accordingly denounced as "incendiaries of the commonwealth" for holding to their belief in that great democracy of faith—the priesthood of all believers. For refusing to pay tribute to the state religion, they were fined, flogged, and outlawed. In 1651, the Baptist Obadiah Holmes was given thirty stripes with a three-corded lash after he violated the law and took forbidden communion with other Baptists in Lynn, Massachusetts. His friends attempted to pay his fine for his release, but he refused. They offered him liquor to anesthetize the pain of the flogging. Again he refused. It is the love of freedom, he said, "that have to free the soul."
