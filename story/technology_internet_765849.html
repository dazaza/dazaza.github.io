New Ranking Algorithm for Apple App Store: User Reviews May Be Introduced

by anonymous on 2013-08-26 14:10:30

Apple may be trying to change the ranking algorithm of its iTunes App Store, incorporating app ratings and some other new factors into the app rankings in the Apple Store. It is currently unknown whether these changes will bring revolutionary changes to the app rankings or are just experimental in nature. However, these changes will cause many apps to rise and fall in rank regardless of any changes in their download volumes.

Boston-based Fiksu is a startup company mainly focused on app sales business. At the end of July, the company was the first to notice this changing trend in the Apple App Store. They noticed that some apps with four-star ratings or above suddenly had a significant boost in rank and maintained it throughout August. Meanwhile, apps with fewer than three stars dropped in rank and showed no rebound trend even if their download volume remained stable. Apps with moderate ratings of three or four stars were not affected.

Previously, Apple mentioned in its report that download volume and speed were the two main factors determining app rankings, but now user evaluations also have an impact.

There are also signs indicating that the latest version of an app has more influence on its ranking than previous versions. For example, if a publisher has an app that has always been well-rated and ranked high, but the subsequently released patch update is unpopular and receives many negative reviews, the decline in the app's ranking will be greater than before, forcing publishers to carefully adjust their upgrade strategies.

Another significant change discovered by Fiksu is the update cycle of app rankings. In the past, the Apple App Store updated rankings every 15 minutes, allowing developers to constantly monitor the direction of their products. Recently, users only see updates to app rankings every three hours.

This change can effectively prevent developers from using automated methods to manipulate the rankings in the App Store. Moreover, extending the update cycle for the rankings might give Apple time to handle situations where there is a sudden surge in download traffic. According to Fiksu, such surges in download traffic often correlate with automatic downloads and other improper methods aimed at increasing download volumes. Theoretically, extending the update cycle could provide Apple with time to identify abnormal behaviors in download patterns, preventing the artificial boosting of app rankings through illicit transactions.

Notably, almost at the same time as the change in the update cycle, an app called Glide topped the social app rankings and ranked 16th overall, despite many users reporting that they received spam emails from the app seeking to boost its downloads, a method that is no longer novel (Glide’s user interface defaults to inviting contacts to use the app, requiring users to manually uncheck it, and also employs similar tactics, surprising and angering users).

Many apps resort to such underhanded tactics to climb the rankings, and Glide is not alone. New changes in the algorithm can help prevent such tactics to some extent. Once angry users give negative reviews and one-star ratings to such apps in the App Store, the new algorithm may directly exclude them from the top 20. Fiksu indicated that it is unclear whether existing reviews (in terms of quantity or validity) are considered, but it would make sense to do so.

According to Ouriel Ohayon, CEO of app discovery and marketing service Appsfire, some app developers have also noticed that Apple is considering refunds and separately calculating revenue data for free games. He particularly noted the changes in the profitability rankings, where Pandora and Rdio saw significant increases in their rankings (currently Pandora is at the top).

However, Ohayon cautioned that these changes do not mean Apple has completely prevented app publishers from manipulating the rankings. He added, "Many tricks regarding ratings are still prevalent, and rankings can still be boosted through various ad networks, even though our update cycle has been extended to three hours."

Meanwhile, MobileDevHQ focuses more on app search results rather than app rankings. Ian Sefferman, the company's CEO, stated that he has also noticed some recent changes in app searches. Searching for iPhone apps on an iPad yields different results compared to searching on an iPhone. Combined with Fisku's data, this seems to indicate that Apple is improving app discovery comprehensively, not just making minor adjustments to app rankings.

In general, as Niren Hiro of SearchMan pointed out, they have been paying attention to individual reports from developers in the US and Japan, mentioning that newly added user engagement factors (usage time, number of times opened the next day, number of times opened thereafter, etc.) are increasingly influencing category rankings, which will be rolled out globally on August 5th.

As with previous algorithm updates in the Apple App Store, developers will find new tricks to prevent their apps from falling off the rankings, and Apple will continue to deal with these. Regarding ratings, developers can seemingly use third-party tools to divert bad reviews left by users, leaving only positive reviews on the App Store.

Single individual reports are not enough; tracking user engagement will bring about better improvements. If user engagement becomes a factor in rankings, the tricks used to control rankings will become less effective. At that point, the best way to rise in the rankings will be to improve the quality of the app, which is the legitimate path anyway.
