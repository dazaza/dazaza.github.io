[Repost] Criticism is actually a kind of benevolent encouragement

by koucai4472 on 2012-03-06 17:10:04

Since ancient times, there has been a saying in China: "Criticism makes one progress." Many people have continuously improved themselves through criticism, growing amidst the doubts of others and gradually becoming successful. In fact, many people are afraid of being criticized, which is understandable as no one really wants to be criticized. However, in life, it's impossible not to make mistakes. When you're wrong, you should be criticized; otherwise, how would you know about your own shortcomings? In fact, we should have learned this from high school politics classes — when looking at things, we should use a dialectical perspective. Any matter has two different effects, depending on how you understand it. Everyone views problems from different angles, so the meaning you assign to it also differs, thus its significance changes accordingly.

For example, being criticized can have two different meanings. Optimistic people think that others criticize them to remind them where they need improvement, and that the method of reminder might differ, but once they improve, naturally, no one will criticize them anymore. Pessimistic people, on the other hand, believe the other party is just causing trouble, thinking they've done well and couldn't possibly have so many issues. This leads to a gradual estrangement in interpersonal relationships. If within a team, this person's team communication skills would be utterly terrible. In reality, it all depends on how you interpret it. As long as you're willing to think positively, the outcome will naturally be good, and vice versa. Today, the Oral Talent Training Network will share with everyone the purpose and significance of criticism.

1. No Gold is Pure, No Man Perfect

An ancient saying goes: "No gold is pure, no man perfect." Who in this world can avoid making mistakes? There is nothing truly perfect in the world, so everyone will inevitably make errors. The Oral Talent Training Network believes that if one acknowledges their faults without covering them up, even though they may be self-aware, their analysis and reflection on the nature, harm, and root causes of their mistakes will never be as clear as those of numerous observers. We need sincere praise, but also kind criticism. Praise is encouragement, criticism is urging; praise is like sunshine, criticism is like dew, both indispensable. When you understand this, you will naturally comprehend the significance of criticism.

2. "Good Medicine" Does Not Necessarily Taste Bitter

The phrases "good medicine tastes bitter" and "sincere advice is unpleasant to hear" are often used to warn people to humbly accept criticism and not care about the way it is delivered. The way it is delivered should not be considered by the criticized, but the criticizer should research and study it. Medical science has developed to the point where many "good medicines" are coated with sugar or honey, and no longer taste bitter. Language science has developed to the point where paying attention to the methods and language art of criticism can achieve "sincere advice without unpleasantness," appealing to all ages. How can this be achieved? Criticism must be benevolent, respecting, understanding, and trusting the criticized, focusing on matters rather than individuals, convincing with reason. Focus on matters, merely their flaws and mistakes, and not seize on one point while ignoring the rest, leading to the negation of someone's entire work and history. Further analyze their motives and effects. If the motive is good but the effect is poor, first affirm their good intentions, then criticize inappropriate areas, and finally teach the correct method. Avoid losing temper or pointing fingers before the situation is thoroughly investigated, and more importantly, avoid sarcasm, ridicule, mockery, exposing past grievances, settling accounts, or personal attacks. Because these actions only cause or exacerbate adversarial emotions, leading to stubborn resistance, or superficial compliance without genuine acceptance, resulting in fake confessions where thoughts remain untouched and behaviors unchanged after the fact. Such criticism appears highly confrontational, but its actual effect is minimal.

3. Clever Use of Humor

Generally speaking, when receiving criticism, the criticized often experience tension and suppression, especially when superiors criticize subordinates or elders criticize juniors. These feelings may manifest as anxiety, fear, opposition, resistance, depression, or discouragement... These abnormal psychological states become psychological barriers to mutual emotional exchange, significantly reducing the practical effectiveness of criticism. If humor is cleverly employed, the critic can discuss truths and principles with a smile, and the criticized can blush slightly amid laughter, feeling touched rather than stimulated from deep within their hearts, happily accepting education. Isn't this beautiful? Humor differs from satire, although both have the effect of making people laugh. Satirical laughter is sharp and stimulating, embarrassing people, while humorous laughter is light-hearted and warm, subtle and profound, some even containing profound wisdom and philosophy, prompting deep thought and introspection.

Cleverly employing humor in criticism often involves presenting the issue in a semi-joking, semi-serious manner, breaking the deadlock before addressing substantive issues. Even if the other party cannot immediately accept it, it won't hurt feelings or embarrass them. Therefore, well-intentioned humorous criticism differs from harsh satire or mockery, determined by the critic's starting point and attitude. Humorous criticism avoids vulgarity, rigidity, maintains healthy emotions, uses vivid and lively language, and is easy to understand, determined by the critic's ideological and cultural cultivation. Chen Yi, when serving as the first mayor of Shanghai in the new China, often used humorous criticism to correctly handle various contradictions inside and outside the Party, strengthening the unity of the revolutionary ranks and the unity of all levels of the people, promoting the development of the revolutionary cause.

Humorous criticism in social activities, after all, differs from telling jokes or performing crosstalk. Although it contains elements of humor, it remains serious and earnest. The atmosphere can be relaxed and lively, but must not be frivolous, otherwise it will dilute the necessary seriousness of criticism, affecting its effectiveness. Similarly, you must be aware of etiquette. Thus, exaggeration, contrast, puns, and humor should all be measured. The critic can smile, but should not burst out laughing, and certainly should not wave their hands around or gesticulate wildly.
