What are the classifications of diabetic foot?

Experts introduce that diabetic foot disease is divided into wet gangrene, dry gangrene, and mixed gangrene.

(1) Wet gangrene is a relatively common phenomenon of foot necrosis in diabetic foot disease. Due to the decomposition of proteins by putrefactive bacteria producing indole, skatole, etc., diabetic foot patients often emit a foul odor.

(2) Dry gangrene is the result of coagulative necrosis plus the evaporation of moisture from the necrotic tissue, most commonly seen in the extremities. Infection by putrefactive bacteria in dry gangrene is generally milder.

(3) Mixed gangrene occurs in some special groups of people. These patients will simultaneously experience purulent infections and dry gangrene. Mixed gangrene patients are commonly found among type 2 diabetic patients. Generally, patients with mixed gangrene have more severe conditions, and when the infection is heavy, they may experience general discomfort, increased body temperature and white blood cells, toxemia, and sepsis.
