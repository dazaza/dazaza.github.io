I buried you, but I'm still living in the past.

by mimi456 on 2012-01-28 22:05:04

I buried you, but I am still living in the past.

After a hundred years, you lie quietly in a cold box, having forgotten the future you once promised.

Are you asleep?

At exactly 8:40, I received a call from mom. The vibration of the phone numbed the cold table as I stared at that familiar number. Suddenly, I remembered the request I made to mom before leaving home: if grandpa didn't make it, she had to call me immediately. After answering the phone, mom's gloomy voice came from the other end, urging me to ask for leave right away.

Leaving the office, I found a thin piece of paper in my hand, reading "8:43." My heart felt heavy, hanging in my chest cavity while my legs began trembling uncontrollably. I was afraid.

Standing at the last corner before home, I saw many people downstairs with red eyes and sorrowful expressions. Entering the dimly lit store on the ground floor, the atmosphere became even more depressing. The air was filled with the greasy smell of cooking oil mixed with the scent of medicine.

My gaze passed over countless falling tears until it finally rested where you were. You lay quietly on a small bed, half-covered by the soft blanket grandma prepared for you. Still wearing your usual blue shirt, but I could no longer hear the sound of your breathing through the oxygen mask. You lay there silently, just like you were sleeping.

I turned my head away, not daring to look at you any longer because I feared I would cry, which would make grandma even sadder. Suddenly, my heart tightened as tears involuntarily filled my eyes, spreading across my entire eyeball. Stepping out of the room where you lay, I faced the pale sky, just like dad did that day, looking up to prevent the tears from falling. My emotions were tangled like a mess of threads, impossible to cut or untangle. Memories of our shared past flooded my mind - those sorrows, those joys, I lost myself.

Returning to the room where you lay, I found you already seated in a chair surrounded by them, preparing to dress you in your final set of new clothes. Your hands hung lifelessly by your side, your body, drained of nutrients by cancer cells, resembled the bare branches left after autumn leaves return to their roots. The flesh that should have tightly wrapped around your bones now dangled painfully under a layer of wrinkled yellow skin. That wise head, accumulated with countless years of knowledge, was held tightly in the arms of Uncle Biao. The deep grooves carved by seventy-three years of time flowed like rivers, your eyes tightly shut, refusing to open again.

You sat there, sitting silently. An eleven-layered long robe, seven-layered pants, seventy-three waistbands, and a tall hat – I thought you looked like an ancient scholar who had passed the imperial exams. But if you could wake up, I wouldn't care if you were a scholar or even a commoner in a blue robe.

Ice coffin, red pillow, old clothes, incense candles, paper money – my mind went blank. While setting up the mourning hall, dad instructed me to stay by your side, fearing someone might maliciously throw things into your box. So, I took a bench and sat beside you, guarding you tirelessly to prevent anyone from harming you. Those debates about possible outcomes we've had so many times ultimately ended with you enduring this too early. As you said, facing severe pain and the threat of death, you remained calm. Yet, I suddenly felt sad. The overwhelming heartache suppressed deep within me could no longer be restrained by external factors. I bowed my head deeply, letting the tears freely flow down my cheeks, leaving long tear marks. All the little moments from the eighteen years we shared surged forth vividly. The small scale you made for me, the personal income tax you "paid" me every time we played mahjong, every game of chess we played, every birthday present you bought me on the second day of the first lunar month... Suddenly, I remembered a poem that gazed upon us, transforming into our story. Years of affection have become a cold coffin, with you inside and me outside... Then, I realized how much of our past has faded beyond recall, and how my tears have overflowed without restraint.

It must be very cold inside the ice coffin, right?

At noon, I went upstairs and ate lunch. Closing the bedroom door, I stopped crying and instead lay down, trying hard to calm myself. Then, I silently repeated in my heart: you're just sleeping. Closing my eyes, I emptied my mind and drifted off to sleep. When I woke up, you'd still be lying there waiting for me to come see you, right?

It felt like being trapped in a protruding three-dimensional space, floating aimlessly without a place to land. Noisy sounds brought me back from my wandering thoughts. After hastily washing up, I went downstairs, expecting to still hear the sound of your breathing in the empty shop. But there was none. The repetitive blessings echoed endlessly inside. You weren't on the bed; you were in the ice coffin.

The deep blue rectangular screen continuously changed numbers, showing 20.5. How I wished you could crawl out of the ice coffin and tell me it was too cold, that you didn't like it.

I returned to school, sitting on a wooden stool, but my mind wasn't in the classroom. Those cold words couldn't bind my heart, which kept thinking of you.

How many times I walked around your box, hoping you weren't inside.

My chess master, where are you?

Sitting in the mourning hall, I tuned out the voices around me and suddenly remembered the two games of chess we played a month ago. Through thirteen years of ups and downs, on the battlefield of the Chu River, I encountered countless enemies, but I always fell under your feet. However, that day, I took advantage of your declining physical and mental state. If only I had known your departure would be so sudden, I should have followed dad's advice and continued to lose under your feet. Now, besides regret, what else is there?

The chessboard you made, the wooden boards you personally cut, the nails you personally hammered, the grids you personally drew, the Han boundary and Chu river you personally wrote – they aren't here. They're in the last foreign home where you stayed. I decided to treasure them and keep them with me until I grow old.

I want to play another game with you, but the person sitting opposite me is gone.

If crying could bring you back,

On the eve of saying goodbye to you, many people came. We stood in front of your altar in mourning attire. Your friends and colleagues read thick piles of manuscripts, revealing the person you were in their hearts. I didn't cry, but deep down, I admired you even more. I read your over twenty-thousand-word memoir three times, each time gaining something new. The bridges, reservoirs, and power stations you directed the construction of earned admiration from everyone who read your memoir.

When dad delivered his thank-you speech, I had no reason to hold back my tears anymore. The faint father-son bond that was never openly expressed, the belated call carried too much regret. Dad's trembling voice, interspersed with sobs and tears, made many people cry, unable to let go.

Mom held your portrait and cried until her face was distorted, recounting the love you showed her, treating her better than your own daughter. Yes, for many years, our family loved each other dearly. Any sadness we experienced was overshadowed by endless joy. And you always appeared before us as a gentle, kind, and hardworking person, calmly supporting us as a strong pillar and spiritual backbone.

Suddenly, you disappeared, and no matter how much we cried, we couldn't bring you back.

I send you off to an unknown future

At four o'clock in the morning, they carried you out and placed you in the car. Dressed in mourning attire, fifteen cars sandwiched you in the middle, bidding you farewell and taking you away from here. I randomly got into one of the cars, leaning against the backrest of the seat, gradually dozing off amidst the swaying turns.

When I woke up, we had already reached our destination. At the only crematorium's entrance, many cars had arrived before us. We got out of the car and went to the viewing room for one last look at you. You lay on the crematorium's stretcher, covered by the quilt dad had prepared for you a month ago. Your eyes were tightly closed, a thin layer of moisture covering your face. In the blink of an eye, you were wheeled away by the crematorium staff. Crowding around the television displaying live images, I caught one last glimpse of you, suddenly feeling a great fear. Could it be true that you were gone just like that?

Sitting on a plastic chair outside the crematorium for an hour and a half, you finally emerged. But now, you were a pile of scattered ashes. Dad carefully wrapped you in a red cloth, holding you as he walked out of the crematorium, got into the car, and called me to ride with you in the same vehicle.
