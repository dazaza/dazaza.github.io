Microsoft Shifts Its Thinking: Begins to Bow to Apple

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 17:53:06

Julie Larson-Green, the current head of Windows engineering, said in an interview with the media that Microsoft should develop applications for Apple's iOS and Google's Android systems to deal with the situation where these two operating systems dominate the mobile device market.

For years, some Windows executives at Microsoft were reluctant to even mention the word "Apple" in interviews, with only one person referring to the tech giant as the "fruit company." However, Microsoft has now changed its stance.

It is reported that Microsoft has developed around 15 to 20 applications each for the iPad and iPhone, and about 10 applications for the Android system. Although Microsoft had already provided the note-taking program OneNote and the search application Bing on the iTunes Store, it was not until June this year that the company launched Word, PowerPoint, and a limited version of Excel exclusively for the iPhone. These applications do not allow users to use specific features; users must pay to subscribe to Microsoft's cloud-based Office suite to access all functions. Nonetheless, this marks a significant step forward for Microsoft in providing complete products for Apple mobile devices.

In July, Microsoft launched Outlook for the iPhone and iPad. After Apple's new operating system, iOS 7, was released this fall, mobile users' questions to Siri are typically answered by Microsoft's Bing search engine rather than Google.

Data from market research firm IDC shows that in the first quarter of this year, Microsoft Surface tablet shipments accounted for only 3.7% of global tablet shipments, while smartphones with the Windows Phone system accounted for just 3.2% of global smartphone shipments. Last week, Microsoft announced its financial results for the fourth quarter of fiscal year 2013, with net profits falling short of market expectations by the largest margin in nearly a decade. Additionally, Microsoft recorded a $900 million write-down for unsold Surface tablets in that quarter.

Microsoft spokesperson Frank Shaw, speaking about the shift in strategy, said that as the market changes, the company must also adapt. As a high-value service company, you must consider how to respond to customer needs and make changes accordingly. Sometimes it will be on the company's own mini-devices, and sometimes it will be on larger screens that don't belong to the company.
