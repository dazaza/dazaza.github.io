Dianping.com: A Bone the Giants Can't Chew, Living in the Zone of Discomfort

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 16:56:43

Zhang Tao, CEO of Dianping.com

By Yuan Yin, edited by Yang Jing, photographed by Xiao Nan

Zhang Tao, CEO of Dianping.com, has postponed the company's IPO once again.

In 2011, Dianping raised $100 million in its Series C round from Sequoia Capital and others, valuing the company at $1 billion. According to convention, it should have chosen an opportune time for its IPO within the following one to two years, but it didn't. Instead, in 2012, it raised another $60 million in its Series D round.

Given Dianping's scale and growth trend, going public wouldn't be difficult. However, Zhang Tao feels that an IPO now would be too costly. He breaks down the large internet (PC + mobile) into four elements - people, information, goods, and services - each connection point presenting an opportunity for a giant to emerge. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, and Alibaba connects people with goods. The space between people and services represents the fourth giant's position. Since 2003, Dianping has been working hard to accumulate data for this very reason, finally waiting for the explosion of mobile internet when smartphones bridged "location" and "services." Following Zhang Tao's logic, no company seems more fitting than Dianping for the image of the fourth giant.

But outsiders don't see it that way. In recent months, with BAT's frequent acquisition strategies, Dianping has become a presumed acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition candidate; under any of BAT's brands, it could help them achieve great success quickly. On the other hand, Dianping's size pales in comparison to BAT's, and it's still too early to predict whether it can become a giant. Moreover, whether it's Tencent WeChat's public platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after investing in AutoNavi and Meituan, all are directly targeting Dianping's territory - local life services primarily focused on dining, drinking, entertainment, etc.

Before proving the following two points, going public for Dianping would equal a "discounted sale." First, Zhang Tao needs some time to demonstrate that what Dianping is doing now, BAT cannot do. Second, Dianping is about to enter the ultimate battle of group buying. If it secures the top spot, its valuation could increase several times over.

2013 is Dianping's "decisive year." Regarding speculation and rumors, Zhang Tao remains indifferent. He is clear: "The internet has indeed entered an era of great integration, but Dianping will not be acquired."

Zhang Tao categorizes companies that might be acquired into three types. The first type possesses platform attributes but is better suited to develop within a giant's system, such as maps and emails. These products can easily attract a large number of users but struggle to find independent business models. Others, like browsers, can make money, but the revenue from navigation services is far less than their strategic value to large companies. "Giants are willing to spend money to maintain email products because they help with brand and user retention, but I've never heard of a standalone email company succeeding." The second type involves industry consolidation, like in the video industry, where acquisitions expand market share. The third type includes companies facing financial difficulties who sell out for survival.

Now Meituan has been brought into Alibaba's fold. Theoretically, Dianping should also quickly seek a "backer" to avoid being defeated due to competitors gaining capital and rapidly expanding. Zhang Tao says, "The giants' assistance to us is limited. We do need users, but execution is more important. Mergers and acquisitions would reduce our execution ability and there's even a possibility of being marginalized by a large company." Furthermore, he aims to keep Dianping at the most precise "distance" from the giants. Getting too close to any one of them would be akin to "choosing sides," making Dianping an enemy of the others.

Thus, logically speaking, acquiring Dianping has reached a dead end. Zhang Tao counters, "Why should we take their money? We're not short of funds; what we need are resources. If a giant is willing to take a small stake and provide us with many resources, I could consider it. But would they? If a giant only holds a small percentage of shares, would they give you resources?"

Weapon One: Dirty and Hard Work

In mobile internet, opportunities for companies doing pure internet products are becoming fewer. New opportunities arise from those combining with traditional industries. Zhang Tao believes that lifestyle services mainly revolve around dining, drinking, and entertainment. Other segmented markets, such as ride-hailing, can form good business models, but it's difficult to imagine any growing into a giant business opportunity.

For dining, drinking, and entertainment, user demands are simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is the core of lifestyle services, which is precisely where Dianping started. Whether in content, member accumulation, or branding, newcomers will find it hard to surpass.

The most representative product form of discounts is group buying. Group buying has now entered the championship race, and Dianping plans to face off against Meituan in 2013. This is a winner-takes-all market. As long as there are two strong players, the industry's gross margin will be lowered, and stable profitability will be hard to achieve.

Another foreseeable future lies in reservations. Reservations can form a true online-to-offline closed loop, but this must be built on the foundation of Dianping having absolute advantages in both information and discounts. Otherwise, creating a closed loop would be difficult. In 2012, Dianping began trying out reservations. Zhang Tao said, "Currently, both users and merchants provide positive feedback, but if reservations were done separately, it would be hard to solve the chicken-or-egg problem."

Of course, lifestyle services present some even "heavier" opportunities. For example, food delivery meets users' rigid demand, helps merchants increase sales, and has seen rapid development in the last couple of years with companies like "To Home Gourmet." However, the core of food delivery is distribution - delivering within a very short timeframe while dealing with issues like soup spillage, requiring higher logistics standards than fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang Tao said, "If food delivery is executed well, there is potential, but it's harder dirty and exhausting work, and Dianping already does enough of that."

If judged by age, Dianping is an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has diligently accumulated merchant profiles and user reviews, what Zhang Tao calls "dirty and hard work." This primitive growth model once restricted its speed, but past struggles have become Dianping's core barrier today. Currently, apart from Meituan, Dianping has no real rivals in the lifestyle services sector.

Nevertheless, he still needs to be wary of BAT's "claws." Baidu's involvement is the most direct. Baidu Maps has over 100 million users, allowing users to search for nearby dining and other lifestyle services. Tencent, considered to have secured its ticket to mobile internet, launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest physical goods trading platform, and expanding its offerings to services is natural. Moreover, it has incorporated Meituan and AutoNavi into its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there are no signs of explosive growth in reality and data," Zhang Tao said. Brand positioning is crucial; the key is which product users choose first when they want to dine, drink, or entertain themselves.

Moreover, in the vast field of lifestyle services, competition among giants is bound to overlap, and no one can completely monopolize the area. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained on platforms like Weibo and WeChat. Zhang Tao feels that this makes Dianping safer instead, "In细分 fields like car rental and business travel, the massive user base directed by the giants could easily overwhelm existing companies."

As for Alibaba, which invested in AutoNavi, invading Dianping's territory isn't so simple either. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods which can be categorized regardless of how peculiar they are. A diner's experience depends on various factors like the day's environment, waitstaff, and chef. Managing merchants can't rely solely on systems and tools like Alibaba uses; it requires building a 'ground force' to manage with 'people.'

"The three giants all want to go down this path, and there's room for imagination, but it's not a highway; it's a rugged mountain road," Zhang Tao believes. First, providing lifestyle services requires ground teams deployed at various levels, something BAT lacks the genes and ability to manage. Second, they lack the willingness; lifestyle services require focus and aren't something a small team can accomplish easily, and profits come slowly. "It's hard for a company to excel in two important areas simultaneously, especially when they also need to consolidate their advantages in their original fields."

Weapon Two: Small Boats Are Easier to Maneuver

When Dianping raised $100 million in 2011, Zhang Tao told the outside world that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, the times change quickly, and before Dianping went public, his comparison targets had fallen from their pedestals.

He wasn't particularly troubled by this. "They failed to become big companies because they lacked offline teams, and now they all want to be America's Dianping."

The current path is one Zhang Tao has painstakingly explored step by step. When he felt that selling keyword ads and discount coupons was too outdated, the emergence of group buying caught his eye. Initially, like the Americans, he saw group buying as a marketing platform, offering one deal per day. But as orders increased, he realized that group buying had the characteristics of a trading platform. Thus, he envisioned Dianping's future as a lifestyle service trading platform. The two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were used precisely to transition Dianping from an information platform to a trading platform.

Without this "genetic transformation," Zhang Tao might not have had the confidence to compete for the fourth giant's seat today. But genetic transformation sounds easy, yet in practice, it's equivalent to Dianping's second entrepreneurship, a process filled with extreme pain.

First, compared to selling advertisements alone, the gross margin of a trading platform decreases, demanding stricter cost control and efficiency. Second, a trading platform requires higher cross-departmental communication skills.

In the past, to maintain Dianping's impartiality and independence as an information platform, the content team and the sales team were entirely separate. Zhang Tao even deliberately established a "firewall" between departments – even for important clients, sales couldn't alter information on the platform, which once led to mutual hostility between the content and sales teams. Now, as a trading platform, the editorial and sales teams need deep communication to create a good marketing plan, which requires editorial cooperation beyond just planning. He said, "Maintaining information quality is still important, but there's no need to reinforce animosity anymore; we need to dismantle the firewall."

Though editors may logically accept this, individual behaviors and values have inertia, and when combined across the entire company, the inertia becomes even greater. To address this, Zhang Tao specifically held several meetings to encourage more communication between departments. After a few months, an editorial supervisor casually mentioned to a sales supervisor, "I used to think you were my enemy, but now I realize you're my colleague."

The sales department also required a genetic transformation. To promote conversion, Zhang Tao cleverly gave the offline team a dual identity. First, for merchants, salespeople should become marketing consultants, not just selling products but helping merchants devise marketing strategies. Second, for the company, these marketing consultants act as procurement officers. Dianping is like a supermarket; not only must the quality of the products be good, but the variety must also be rich. Marketing consultants should aim to help users purchase group-buying products. Naturally, this dual identity would bring a series of complex problems. It's quite challenging for frontline personnel to balance the scales.

In essence, Dianping's "dual identity" is similar to Alibaba's division into 25 business units. Decentralizing decision-making to the frontlines allows the company to react faster but increases management difficulty. Similarly, compared to frontline employees, Dianping's management team suffered more after the genetic transformation.

Every year, Zhang Tao breaks down Dianping's corporate goals into various departments, then executives further break them down layer by layer, but this breakdown isn't simple arithmetic. Li Jing, co-founder of Dianping, responsible for the sales team, sometimes finds that unresolved merchant demands stem from sales issues, affecting performance. Thus, Li Jing created a new position, setting up communication specialists who collect information daily from marketing consultants, submit it to other departments, and track feedback. He often tells his team, "Being good at embracing change doesn't mean liking change. No one likes change; everyone feels uncomfortable when encountering it. All employees must go through discomfort zones, including me."

The numbers reflect the weight of Dianping's execution team. Out of the current 3000+ employees, over 2000 work offline. The ground force communicates directly with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group buys, and integrated marketing solutions.

Dianping has a unique management and evaluation system for this core team that resists BAT's influence.

They are not called "salespeople"; from the first day, their business cards read "Marketing Planning Consultant." Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the phase of watching news and chatting on QQ, lagging at least three to five years behind Taobao merchants in terms of internet understanding. Marketing planning consultants need to tell merchants what online users want and how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing said, "Even for a high-end restaurant, the marketing consultant wouldn't suggest a groupon for abalone or shark fin, possibly suggesting other dishes instead, as online and offline user demands differ."

An excellent marketing consultant must be a versatile player. They need to explain to restaurant owners and barbershop owners the essential differences between discounts and group buys; which discount aligns better with their positioning. If faced with a hot pot restaurant owner, the marketing consultant should know whether it's the off-season or peak season, which products' costs rise during certain seasons, and which decrease, proposing a group buy menu that both merchants and users love.

This places high demands on marketing consultants, who must not only be internet marketing experts but also industry experts. Li Jing said, "I must admit, it's impossible for every consultant to be a double expert, so we set star ratings for consultants." Similar to hotels, marketing consultants range from one-star to five-star, with the best being called Super Stars.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline employees aren't tech-savvy graduates from prestigious universities, making it challenging for them to understand internet products. Dianping has established a knowledge base for marketing consultants, containing knowledge points and relevant cases that both new and experienced employees can browse. If encountering a new problem, employees can ask questions within the knowledge base, and thousands of consultants nationwide will help answer.

A large amount of knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees based on practical experience. Dianping has set up a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, with employees who help others the most each quarter receiving rewards. Each quarter, Dianping conducts skill assessments for employees, directly linking the results to promotions.

Compared to internet knowledge, industry knowledge is harder to acquire. Some experiences are summarized by traditional merchants after decades of trial and error in the industry. A Super Star marketing consultant told Li Jing that hair salons should avoid offering wash-cut-blow dry group buys because men have little motivation to buy them, and women prefer blow-drying without washing and cutting. Li Jing was astonished, "We men would never think of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing consultants are not only divided by cities but also by industries such as catering, hairdressing, and leisure. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is further subdivided into hot pot, Western cuisine, and buffets, with one marketing consultant responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing consultants can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, avoiding resistance when selling products," he said.

Each marketing group holds its own case-sharing sessions: what challenges a specific type of merchant encounters, what marketing needs they have, what combination of company products they use, and what the results are. Outstanding cases are reported upward, and Dianping officially shares one case each week.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition - Entrepreneur Winner. In the first three months of employment, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products but listening to merchants sharing experiences. If they are responsible for hot pot restaurants, after three months, they must submit a commercial plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hot Pot Restaurant Owner." Like graduation, employees defend their presentations on-site, and industry supervisors evaluate the winners, rewarding them accordingly.

From a strategic perspective, whether it's Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, what they need to do is quite similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O are familiar to anyone with some industry knowledge, and the words they say are almost identical. "The era where success depended on storytelling and concept creation has passed. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model is management and execution, not strategy," Zhang Tao said.
