SAT Reading Methods and Common Question Types

by thatseed123 on 2011-05-03 21:57:58

In response to the common difficulties faced by Chinese test-takers, extensive reading article training and method explanations are integrated into the practical teaching of SAT classes. In short, there are some methods for reading as follows:

1. When encountering an article for the first time, skim it roughly to identify the core idea of the author's writing and get a general sense of the article’s structure.

2. Read the questions after the article. If some questions seem difficult to answer on-site, skip them first and return to solve these tough problems after answering other questions.

3. For more complex questions, go back to the original text to find the article's initial description of the queried items.

4. Try to describe what you believe to be the correct answer in your own words.

5. Finally, look at the five options under the question stem and select the correct one.

Of course, while reading the article, test-takers also need to follow some basic principles:

☆ Pay attention to the brief introduction about the author and the article at the beginning. Since the SAT exam requires candidates to understand the author's way of thinking and writing style during the examination reading, candidates should focus their attention on the provided information about the author and the article from the start of reading. By reading this content, grasp the basis for the article's layout and the author's writing purpose.

☆ Do not overly dwell on a specific detail of the article. Details in each article are merely used to explain the author's thoughts and descriptions. Therefore, when reading details in the article, candidates should adopt a quick-reading method. Many candidates often hope to precisely understand all the contents of the article within the short time of the exam, which is unfeasible both theoretically and practically, and is actually unnecessary.

☆ Carefully read the first third of the article, as important elements are often presented here. These "important elements" refer to the theme of the article. Generally, SAT reading articles already state the theme within the first third of the article; usually, the author's writing intention and attitude towards the subject matter are clearly revealed.

☆ Pay attention to the main idea of each paragraph and mentally describe the structure of the article. Essentially, paragraphs are the basic components of an article. Therefore, when reading SAT articles, candidates should focus on the theme of each paragraph and sketch out the overall framework of the article. Candidates can ask themselves questions like "What is the main point of this paragraph?" "How does the content of this paragraph connect with the previous and following ones?" "What role does this paragraph play in the article?" The better the understanding of these questions, the faster the candidate's problem-solving speed will be, and the higher the accuracy of solving problems will become.

☆ Summarize the content of the article in a timely manner. After the candidate finishes reading the entire article, it is best to take a little time to think about the structure and overall layout of the article and abstractly summarize the central idea of the article in their own language.

Based on past SAT exam questions, the reading section generally has the following types of questions:

(1) Main Idea Question Type. This type of question relates to the whole article, and the question stem may inquire about the primary idea or basic purpose of the article. Among the answers to this type of question, the correct answer explicitly summarizes the main content of the article, while incorrect distractors often have the following forms:

- Irrelevant to the main idea of the article;

- Clearly contradictory to what the article says;

- Substituting details of the article for the main theme;

- Exceeding the scope of the article, being too broad;

- Too extreme, exceeding the narrative scope of the article.

(2) Detail Question Type. This type of question often asks about specific content in a certain part of the article. When answering such questions, candidates generally must return to the relevant parts of the original text to find the source of the correct answer. The distractors for detail questions usually have the following forms:

- Confusing with narratives from other parts of the article;

- Using vocabulary similar to the original text but distorting the meaning of the original text;

- Contradicting the narration of the original text;

- Stating content that is not expressed in the original text and cannot be logically deduced from it;

- Using exaggerated narratives.

(3) Inference Question Type. This type of question can examine either the content at the level of the article's theme or at the level of its details. It mainly requires candidates to infer options that are not explicitly stated in the article but are logically correct based on the information provided in the article.

(4) Vocabulary Question. Vocabulary questions in reading primarily assess the specific meaning of a word in the context of the article. What candidates need to do is deduce the specific meaning of the tested word from the context. It is worth noting that even if the word is familiar to the candidate, the final correct answer in the reading article is often not the well-known meaning of the word, but rather a more obscure meaning. This situation is commonly referred to as "familiar words with rare meanings."

A thorough understanding of the various characteristics and specific question types of the SAT reading exam lays a solid foundation for candidates to achieve high scores on the SAT. As long as the above points are truly followed, candidates will no longer passively and ineffectively read SAT articles but will actively and positively engage in reading. Through further detailed explanations in class and extensive article practice, candidates will surely conquer SAT reading, smoothly obtain high scores, and realize their dream of studying at top American universities.
