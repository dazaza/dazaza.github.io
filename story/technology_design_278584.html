I. Overview:

Acne is a common chronic, inflammatory, and disfiguring skin disease among young men and women. It is also known as "pimples" or "teenage pimples." It mainly occurs on the face, chest, and back. Common symptoms include blackheads, small rashes, pustules, nodules, cysts, and scars. Acne is a common skin condition during puberty, affecting about 90% of teenagers to varying degrees, and usually gradually subsides after the age of 25. Recurrent blackheads, papules, pustules on the face, and even cysts and scars can severely affect appearance and mental health. The occurrence of acne is related to various factors, and treatment must consider all aspects of the disease to choose the best plan to achieve the goal of cure and beauty.

II. Pathogenesis:

Acne can be divided into common acne, conglobate acne, scar acne, erythematous acne, necrotic acne, premenstrual acne, and juvenile acne. Its mechanism is due to excessive keratinization at the hair follicle opening, blockage of the hair follicle opening, which causes the accumulation of keratin and sebum forming blackheads (sebaceous plugs). Inflammation around the blackheads forms small rashes (papules), pustules, nodules, and deeper damage forms scars.

A. Main Factors:

Endocrine factors, such as increased androgens leading to hyperfunction of sebaceous glands resulting in increased sebum secretion, abnormal keratinization of hair follicle-sebaceous gland ducts, narrowing of the duct diameter. When the epithelial cells shed from the hair follicle wall mix with sebum, they plug the hair follicle opening, forming blackheads. Microbial action within the hair follicle, such as Propionibacterium acnes, Malassezia ovale, and Staphylococcus aureus (Demodex mites are not the main cause of acne but are conditional pathogenic bacteria).

B. Related Factors:

Genetics, diet (high fat, sugar, cocoa, etc.), improper use of cosmetics and hormone-based topical medications, emotional stress, and certain chemical factors, such as mineral oil, iodine, bromine, lithium, and some drugs like isoniazid, can all induce and worsen acne.

III. Clinical Features:

A. Common Acne:

Lesions primarily consist of papules, blackheads, pustules, and dark red nodules. The damage is polymorphic and can both induce and worsen acne.

B. Conglobate Acne:

In addition to blackheads, papules, and pustules, there are also nodules, cysts, fistulas, and scars. This is one of the most important types of acne. If mishandled, these types of acne can leave punctate atrophic scars.

IV. Treatment:

Principle: Actively prevent, combine internal and external treatments, and comprehensively treat.

A. General Therapy:

1. Diet: Avoid spicy, sweet, greasy foods, and alcohol. Drink more water, eat more fresh vegetables and fruits, and maintain regular bowel movements (as constipation can worsen acne).

2. Facial Cleansing: Wash your face twice daily with warm water (about 40°C) or mild acidic soap. Sulfur medicated soap is even better. This helps dissolve and reduce sebum secretion, clear excess oil from the skin surface, remove pore blockages, and ensure smooth external flow of sebum.

3. Cosmetics: Avoid using oily cosmetics (oil-in-water cream agents) and avoid long-term use of corticosteroid hormone-based medications. Opt for gel-based or water-soluble cosmetics containing cooling substances.

4. Adjust emotions, maintain a regular lifestyle, and avoid excessive stress in work and study.

5. Avoid squeezing blackheads and papules with your hands.

B. Internal Therapy:

Use anti-inflammatory, antibacterial methods, counteract androgen effects, and correct hair follicle opening keratinization. For example, drugs that inhibit microorganisms within hair follicles such as clindamycin, sulfonamides, dapsone, etc., are effective in treating acne. A significant issue with antibiotic treatment is the emergence and increasing prevalence of resistant bacterial strains. Another issue is the long course of treatment, typically requiring 3-6 months; shorter courses may affect efficacy. Drugs that counteract androgens, such as spironolactone, cimetidine, ethinyl estradiol, etc., can easily lead to endocrine disorders, feminization in males, and irregular menstruation in females. These should not be used routinely and should only be administered under medical supervision.
