LV in the Eyes of Overseas Students

by wanglina on 2010-04-19 16:00:22

Chen Yang (a pseudonym) was once a student at a well-known preparatory school in Australia and now studies at the University of Sydney. Before returning to Hangzhou for his summer vacation (it is summer in Australia), he contacted his classmates in China on QQ and said that the life of overseas preparatory students was hard to imagine when he was in China. These truths shocked him greatly, and very few parents know about them. In this school where more than half of the international students are from China, from "old flowers" to "checkered patterns", LVs are everywhere, including expensive limited editions (the vast majority of which are genuine). Chen Yang recognized the classic "four-petaled leaf" pattern, which represents luxury goods in China and is worth more than 10,000 yuan. In Australia, LV bags are obviously cheaper than in China, with mid-range ones costing 1500 Australian dollars (equivalent to 9000 Chinese yuan). However, it is still a symbol of luxury goods.

One time, a classmate carried an LV bag through Sydney Customs. The female customs officer asked how much it cost, and upon hearing it was 1800 Australian dollars, she joked, "You're so rich, I want to marry you." A while ago, the Australian dollar exchange rate plummeted from 6.5 to over 4. The price of LV didn't change, so I accompanied several girls on a shopping spree, and each bought three or four bags. It's no exaggeration to say that LV is our school bag, and using Gucci would be considered lowering one's status!

Two backgrounds are worth noting: first, most people who can send their children abroad are either officials or wealthy; second, even families of commoners, fearing their children will suffer hardships, always try their best to provide financial support. Symbols of wealth are not just LVs. I have a classmate who bought a Mercedes-Benz in the first year and switched to a BMW in the second year, also opting for Balenciaga. Among my friends, Hummers are quite common, and there are even those who drive Bentleys. A bunch of luxury cars parked at the school gate, with everyone chatting and laughing inside.

Girls all wear makeup for class, and smoky eyes aren't considered exaggerated - plain faces are rarely seen. In the vast empty malls in Australia, there's always a group of Chinese students selecting cosmetics, including Lamer (higher-end than Lancome, with a bottle of cream costing over 10,000 yuan). Many girls buy these products despite the strain, either by borrowing against future dowries or by having a boyfriend who provides for their living expenses. In the eyes of Australians, these preparatory students are "tycoons," dressed in fine clothes and acting ostentatiously.

Circles, circles—when talking about life at the preparatory school, Chen Yang repeatedly mentioned this word. "Because you're lonely, you need friends, and you can't possibly know just one person, so you end up entering a circle." Circles are divided based on academic performance, whether they own luxury cars, use designer brands, or are homosexual.

It's said that Chen Yang's preparatory school originally had only one floor, but after improving relations with Chinese intermediaries, students were continuously sent in, filling up all four floors. Now, more than half of the international students in the school are Chinese. Some even stay in Australia after graduation to open preparatory schools.

If we stretch the analogy further, circles are like the "gangs" of junior high and high school boys. If one circle dislikes someone from another circle, opposing circles will form. "When problems arise between circles, they may even call on associations to intervene," Chen Yang said. The association refers to organized crime. He personally knows a few members of these associations. Chen Yang said that he adheres to his own principles of friendship: no prostitution, no gambling, and no drugs.
