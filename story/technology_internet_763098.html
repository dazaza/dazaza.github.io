New York Times: Could Microsoft Go the Way of Detroit?

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 15:29:25

 

On July 20, according to foreign media reports, Beijing time, the New York Times website recently published an article stating that "Motor City" Detroit had filed for bankruptcy protection, becoming the largest city in the U.S. to file for bankruptcy protection.

On the same day, Microsoft also released disappointing earnings results. The decline of the automotive industry paralleled the maturation of the PC industry. Now, the PC industry is gradually declining, and Microsoft, which has close ties to PCs, inevitably faces development bottlenecks.

The following is the main content of the New York Times article:

This Thursday, a former industry icon faced its final judgment day: Detroit filed for bankruptcy. On the same day Detroit became the largest U.S. city to file for bankruptcy protection, Microsoft reported one of its worst quarterly performances ever.

The timing seemed quite fitting. For years, we have witnessed the gradual decline of the automotive industry alongside the maturation of the PC industry. As we transition into the post-PC era, this contrast becomes even more apparent. Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer is from Detroit, and his father served as a long-term manager at Ford. Ballmer also consulted with Ford CEO Alan Mulally on Microsoft management issues. However, the comparison between the automotive and PC industries hasn't become any less clear because of this.

Pessimism was so severe that investors began heavily selling off Microsoft shares. Over the past decade, investors have generally been bearish on Microsoft, but earlier this year, they saw some positive signs, indicating that Microsoft might be finding ways to transition toward mobile, cloud computing, and other mainstream tech trends. This year, Microsoft's stock price has risen by over 30%. Now, however, Wall Street is questioning Microsoft again. On Friday, investors severely punished Microsoft's stock, causing it to plummet by more than 11%.

In an investment report released on Friday, Nomura Equity Research analyst and veteran Microsoft observer Rick Sherlund pointed out that Microsoft's performance shocked Wall Street. "It happened much faster than expected," Sherlund said, "Reviewing the entire financial report and seeing that every department fell short of expectations is truly frustrating."

Sherlund also mentioned that Microsoft's disappointing performance could lead to calls for reform from activist shareholders, possibly starting with seeking a seat on the board. Additionally, one of Microsoft's biggest problems is that the sluggish sales of the Surface RT tablet forced the company to spend nearly $1 billion in subsidies.

The Surface tablet is Microsoft's first self-branded computer hardware device after learning from Apple. Microsoft decided that the relatively cheaper Surface RT would use ARM chips instead of the more powerful and expensive Intel chips. Microsoft also adapted Windows to run on the ARM chip architecture because without the Windows system, Microsoft's tablet would seem out of place. ARM chips are used in most smartphones and tablets worldwide because they are better suited for battery-powered devices.

However, the Surface RT devices sold poorly, forcing Microsoft to cut their price by $150, reducing it to $349. Another version of the Surface tablet uses an Intel chip and can run traditional PC applications. But various reports show that the sales of this tablet were also unsatisfactory.

Microsoft isn’t giving up. Unlike Detroit, Microsoft isn’t short of money. With over $77 billion in cash and cash equivalents, it has several years to find a new successful business model. Last week, Microsoft announced a corporate restructuring that may help accelerate product updates.

But the entire process will take time. In an interview on Thursday, Microsoft CFO Amy Hood clearly stated that the company does not expect its situation in mobile devices to change suddenly. Hood said, “I think this is a journey, and we need to continue making incremental progress.”
