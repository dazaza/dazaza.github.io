Dianping: A Bone the Giants Can't Chew, Living in the Discomfort Zone

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 14:19:23

CEO of Dianping, Zhang Tao

Written by Yuan Yin, edited by Yang Jing, photographed by Xiao Nan

Zhang Tao, CEO of Dianping, has once again postponed the company's IPO timeline.

In 2011, Dianping secured a $100 million Series C funding round from Sequoia Capital and others, valuing the company at $1 billion. According to convention, it should have chosen an appropriate time for its IPO within the following one to two years, but it did not. Instead, in 2012, Dianping raised another $60 million in a Series D funding round.

Given Dianping’s scale and development trajectory, going public would not be difficult. However, Zhang Tao believes that listing now would be too costly. He breaks down the large internet (PC + mobile) into four elements: people, information, goods, and services. Each connection point offers opportunities for giants to emerge. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, and Alibaba connects people with goods. The space between people and services is the fourth giant's seat. Since 2003, Dianping has been diligently accumulating data, finally waiting for the explosion of mobile internet, where smartphones bridge "location" and "service." Following Zhang Tao's logic, no company seems more fitting than Dianping to occupy the position of the fourth giant.

However, outsiders don’t think so. In recent months, with BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent) frequently acquiring companies and expanding their strategic layouts, Dianping has become a hypothetical acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition target; whichever family name of BAT it bears, it could help them achieve great success earlier. On the other hand, Dianping's size is much smaller compared to BAT, and it is still premature to conclude that it will become a giant. Moreover, whether it is Tencent WeChat's public platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after investing in AutoNavi and Meituan, all are directly targeting Dianping's territory - local life services focused on dining, drinking, entertainment, and leisure.

Before proving the following two points, Dianping going public would be equivalent to a "discounted sale." First, Zhang Tao needs some time to prove that what Dianping is doing now cannot be done by BAT. Second, Dianping is about to engage in the ultimate battle of group buying. If it secures the top spot, its valuation could increase multiple times.

2013 is Dianping's "decisive year." Regarding speculations and rumors, Zhang Tao pays no attention, stating clearly: "The internet has indeed entered an era of great integration, but Dianping will never be acquired."

Zhang Tao categorizes companies that might be acquired into three types. The first type possesses platform attributes but is more suitable for development within the ecosystem of a giant, such as maps and emails. These products can easily attract a large number of users but find it hard to establish independent business models. Some, like browsers, can make money, but the revenue generated through navigation services is far less than their strategic value to large companies. "Giants are willing to invest in maintaining email products because they help brand and user stickiness, but I've never heard of any independent email company succeeding." The second type involves industry consolidation, like in the video industry, using acquisitions to expand market share. The third type is when a company faces financial difficulties and sells itself for survival.

Now Meituan has already joined Alibaba's ranks. In theory, Dianping should quickly seek a "backer" to avoid being defeated due to the opponent securing capital and rapidly expanding. However, Zhang Tao says, "Giants can offer us limited help. We do need users, but execution is more important. Acquisitions would reduce our execution capabilities and there is also the risk of being marginalized by a large company." Furthermore, he aims to keep Dianping at the most precise "distance" from the giants. If Dianping gets too close to any one of them, it would be equivalent to "choosing sides," making it an enemy to the others.

Thus, logically, the acquisition of Dianping ends up in a deadlock. Zhang Tao counters, "Why would we take their money? We're not short of funds; what we need are resources. If a giant is willing to invest a small amount and provide us with many resources, I might consider it. But would they? Would a giant give you resources if they only hold a small stake?"

Weapon One: Dirty Work

In the mobile internet era, opportunities for companies solely focusing on pure internet products are becoming fewer. New opportunities arise from those combining with traditional industries. Zhang Tao believes that the core of life services mainly revolves around eating, drinking, entertainment, and leisure. Other细分 markets like ride-hailing can form decent business models, but it's difficult for them to grow into giants.

For eating, drinking, entertainment, and leisure, user needs are simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is the core of life services, which is precisely where Dianping started. Whether in content, member accumulation, or brand, later entrants find it hard to surpass.

The most representative product form of discounts is group buying. Now, group buying has entered the championship contention stage, and Dianping plans to battle Meituan in 2013. This is a winner-takes-all market; as long as two strong players coexist, the industry's gross profit margin will be pulled down, making it impossible to form stable profits.

A foreseeable future includes reservations. Reservations can create a true online-to-offline closed loop, but this must be based on Dianping having an absolute advantage in both information and discounts. Otherwise, building a closed loop is challenging. In 2012, Dianping began experimenting with reservations. Zhang Tao said, "Currently, feedback from users and merchants is good, but if reservations were done separately, it would be difficult to solve the chicken-or-egg problem."

Of course, life services offer some even "heavier" opportunities. For example, food delivery is a rigid demand for users and helps merchants increase sales. In the past couple of years, food delivery companies like Daojia Meishi Hui have thrived. However, the core of food delivery is distribution, meaning delivering within a very short time frame while addressing issues like soups spilling. The logistics requirements are higher than those of fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang Tao said, "If executed well, food delivery still has potential, but it's harder dirty work, and Dianping already has enough dirty and tiring work to do."

If judged by age, Dianping is already an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has painstakingly accumulated merchant profiles and user reviews, which Zhang Tao refers to as "dirty work." This primitive growth model once limited its speed, but the pain of the past has become today's core barrier for Dianping. Currently, apart from Meituan, Dianping has no real competitors in the life services sector.

Still, he must remain vigilant against BAT's "claws." Baidu's involvement is the most direct. Baidu Maps already has over 100 million users, allowing users to search for nearby dining and other life services via the map. Tencent, considered to have secured a ticket to mobile internet, has launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest physical goods trading platform, and expanding goods to services is a natural progression, especially since it has incorporated Meituan and AutoNavi into its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there are no signs of explosive growth in reality or data," Zhang Tao said. Brand positioning is crucial; the key is which product users turn to first when they want to eat, drink, entertain, or relax.

Besides, in the vast field of life services, competition among giants will inevitably cross over, and no one can completely monopolize the field. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained on Weibo and WeChat. Zhang Tao feels that in this case, Dianping is safer. "In niche areas like car rental and business travel, if giants direct their massive user base, they might easily overwhelm the original companies."

As for Alibaba, which has invested in AutoNavi, it won't be easy for them to encroach on Dianping's territory. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, which can be categorized regardless of how peculiar. A diner's experience depends on factors like the day's environment, waitstaff, and chef. Managing merchants cannot rely solely on systems and tools like Alibaba does; it requires a 'ground force' managed by people.

"All three giants want to take this path, and there is room for imagination, but this is not a highway; it's a rugged mountain road," Zhang Tao believes. First, providing life services requires ground teams deployed at various levels, which BAT lacks the gene and ability to manage offline teams. Second, they lack the willingness; life services require dedicated effort and cannot be completed by just dispatching a small team, and making money is slow. "It's hard for a company to excel at two important things simultaneously, let alone consolidating its advantages in its original field."

Weapon Two: Small Boats Turn Easily

When Dianping secured $100 million in financing in 2011, Zhang Tao told the outside world that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, trends change quickly, and before Dianping went public, his comparison objects fell from grace one after another.

He wasn't overly worried about this. "They didn't become big companies because they lacked offline teams; now they all want to be America's Dianping."

The current path is something Zhang Tao has gradually explored step by step. When he felt that selling keyword ads and coupons was outdated, the emergence of group buying caught his eye. Initially, he understood group buying as a marketing platform, similar to the American model, with one deal per day. However, as orders increased, he realized that group buying had attributes of a trading platform. Thus, he envisioned Dianping's future as a life services trading platform. The two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were exactly used for Dianping's transformation from an information platform to a trading platform.

Without this "gene transfer," Zhang Tao might not have had the confidence to compete for the fourth major player's position today. But gene transfer sounds simple, yet it's actually equivalent to Dianping's second entrepreneurship, an extremely painful process.

Firstly, compared to selling advertisements individually, trading platforms have lower gross margins and require stricter cost control and efficiency. Secondly, trading platforms demand higher inter-departmental communication skills.

Previously, to maintain the fairness and independence of Dianping as an information platform, the content team and the sales team were completely separated. Zhang Tao even deliberately established a "firewall" between departments – even for important clients, sales couldn't modify information on the platform, leading to some mutual hostility between the content and sales teams. Now, as a trading platform, the editorial and sales teams need deep communication to create good marketing plans. Besides planning, editorial cooperation is essential. He said, "Maintaining information quality remains important, but there's no need to reinforce animosity anymore; the firewall needs to be dismantled."

Though editors can logically accept this, individual behaviors and values have inertia, and when combined across the entire company, the inertia becomes even greater. Zhang Tao specifically held several meetings to encourage more communication between departments. Months later, an editor manager casually chatting with a sales manager said, "I used to think you were an enemy, but now I realize you're a colleague."

The sales department also needed gene transfer. To promote conversion, Zhang Tao cleverly gave the offline team a dual identity. First, for merchants, salespeople should transform into marketing consultants, not just selling products but helping merchants formulate marketing strategies. Second, for the company, marketing consultants act as procurement officers. Dianping is like a supermarket; not only must the product quality be good, but the variety must also be rich. Marketing consultants should aim to help users purchase group-buying products. Of course, this dual identity will inevitably lead to a series of complex issues. It's quite headache-inducing for frontline personnel who have to balance the scales themselves.

Substantially, Dianping's "dual identity" is very similar to Alibaba's division into 25 business units. Decentralizing decision-making to the front lines allows the company to react faster but increases management difficulty. Similarly, compared to frontline employees, Dianping's management team suffered more during the gene transfer.

Every year, Zhang Tao breaks down Dianping's company goals into various departments, which are then further broken down by executives layer by layer. However, breaking down isn't simple arithmetic. Li Jing, co-founder of Dianping, who oversees the sales team, sometimes finds that unresolved merchant demands are sales issues, but they affect sales. So, Li Jing created a new position, setting up communication specialists who gather information daily from marketing consultants and submit it to other departments while tracking feedback. He often tells his team, "Being good at embracing change doesn't mean liking change; no one likes change, and everyone feels uncomfortable when faced with change. All employees must go through a period of discomfort, including myself."

The numbers reflect the weight of Dianping's execution team. Out of the current 3,000+ employees, over 2,000 work offline. The ground forces are responsible for communicating directly with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group buying, and combined marketing solutions.

Dianping has a unique management evaluation system for this core team that can resist BAT's influence.

They are not called "sales"; from the very beginning, their business cards read "Marketing Planning Consultant." Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the phase of watching news and chatting on QQ, lagging behind Taobao merchants by at least three to five years in their understanding of the internet. Marketing planning consultants must inform merchants about the demands of online users and how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing said, "Even for a high-end restaurant, marketing advisors wouldn't suggest group-buying abalone or shark fin, perhaps suggesting other dishes instead, because online users have different demands than offline users."

An excellent marketing advisor must be a versatile player. They need to explain to restaurant owners or barbershop owners the fundamental differences between discounts and group buying; among different discounts, which one better aligns with their own positioning. If facing a hot pot restaurant owner, the marketing advisor must know whether it's the off-season or peak season, which products have rising costs in this season, and which have falling costs, proposing a group-buying menu that both merchants and users love.

This places high demands on marketing advisors, requiring them not only to be experts in internet marketing but also in the industry. Li Jing said, "I must admit, it's impossible for every advisor to be a double expert, so we set star ratings for advisors." Similar to hotels, marketing advisors range from one-star to five-star, with the best being like superheroes, called Super Stars.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline employees aren't tech-savvy graduates from prestigious universities, making it challenging for them to understand internet products. Dianping established a knowledge base for marketing advisors, containing knowledge points and related cases, accessible to both new and old employees. If encountering a new issue, employees can ask questions in the knowledge base, and thousands of consultants nationwide will help answer.

Most knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees based on practical experiences. Dianping set up a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, rewarding employees who help others the most each quarter. Every quarter, Dianping also conducts skill assessments for employees, directly linking the results to promotions.

Industry knowledge is harder to acquire than internet knowledge. Some experiences are summarized by traditional merchants after years of practice in the industry. A Super Star marketing advisor told Li Jing that hair salons should avoid offering shampoo-cut-blowout package deals, as men lack motivation to buy them, and women prefer blowouts without needing the whole package. Li Jing was amazed, "We men would never think of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing advisors are divided not only by city but also by industry sectors such as catering, hairdressing, and leisure. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is further subdivided into hotpot, Western cuisine, and buffets, with each marketing advisor responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing advisors can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, making merchants less resistant when selling products," he said.

Each marketing group holds its own case-sharing sessions: what difficulties a certain type of merchant encounters, what marketing needs they have, what solutions are provided by the company's products, and what effects they achieve. Outstanding cases are reported upwards, and Dianping officially shares one case each week.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition called "Entrepreneurial Winner." Within the first three months of employment, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products, but listening to merchants sharing experiences. If they are responsible for hotpot restaurants, after three months, they must submit a business plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hotpot Restaurant Owner." Like graduation, employees present their plans on-site, and industry supervisors evaluate and reward the winners.

Strategically speaking, whether it's Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, the tasks to be accomplished are very similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O are familiar to anyone with a basic understanding of the industry, and their statements sound alike. "The era when storytelling and concept creation could lead to success is gone. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model lies in management and execution, not strategy," Zhang Tao said.
