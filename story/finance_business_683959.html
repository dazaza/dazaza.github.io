How does TV affect children's health?

by yangliu435sh on 2012-02-29 16:53:50

The profound impact of television on children has both positive and negative aspects. People are more familiar with the positive aspects, but many parents lack sufficient understanding of the negative effects. The negative influence of television on the intellectual development of children is manifested in the following ways:

1. Weakening of left brain function.

Modern brain science research shows that the two hemispheres of the human brain have high collaborative complementarity in function, as well as high specialized division of labor. The left brain is mainly responsible for abstract thinking, while the right brain is mainly responsible for imagistic thinking. When reading, the left brain is primarily used to receive textual information, whereas when watching TV or listening, the right brain is mainly used to receive image information. Although TV viewing can also hear language, the most meaningful part for viewers is the image rather than the language. Therefore, if TV viewing continues for several centuries, the function of the left hemisphere of the brain will be weakened, and the population with a more developed right hemisphere function will increase significantly. Because TV viewing plays a negative role in the analytical thinking process necessary for reading, it reduces reading ability.

2. Making children passive.

Compared with book and periodical reading, TV viewing can be considered "passive," while reading is "active." Since images are easier to understand than words and do not require much mental effort, the "passivity" formed by excessive TV viewing will be detrimental to the cultivation and improvement of children's thinking abilities. For children who are heavily dependent on TV, their language skills, abstract thinking abilities, especially their ability to master mathematical symbol systems, will inevitably be severely weakened.

3. Impact of vicarious experience.

Experimental evidence shows that children who learn about rabbits entirely from TV compared to those who learn about them in reality have a significant gap in knowledge and understanding of rabbits. The former has a more one-sided understanding of rabbits, while the latter has a more comprehensive understanding. This experimental result indicates that judgments made by children through vicarious experiences are often one-sided.

4. Children's "TV syndrome."

Children who love watching TV sometimes feel restless and are in a state of hyperactivity. Social psychologists call this state "TV syndrome." Children with TV syndrome cannot continuously maintain attention; they either sit blankly or show restlessness and impulsiveness. If these children watch less or no TV, they return to normal.

To better develop children's intelligence, parents should guide children in watching TV so that they absorb more positive information and overcome negative influences.

First, limit the time children spend watching TV. Do not use TV as a babysitter. Limit the time children watch TV each time to preferably no more than 1 hour. Suitable content can be watched a bit more, otherwise, it should be watched less or not at all. It is essential to ensure time for children to practice language expression, reading, writing, and numerical calculation. Children must also read aloud and develop a habit of reading.

Second, talk more with children about the content of TV programs. To achieve better results, it is best to watch TV with your child and understand their views on TV programs. This way, you can explain characters, things, and scenes that appear in TV programs, or pose questions for the child to think about and answer, thus cultivating their thinking and language expression abilities. Program content should be appropriately selected based on the child's age, psychological characteristics, and comprehension ability. If no selection is made and TV is used merely to placate children, some adverse consequences are bound to occur.

Third, expand real-life experiences. For children, games like tangram puzzles, building blocks, paper crafts, and drawing that stimulate creativity and imagination are very important. Physical exercise should be appropriately arranged in children's lives, paying attention to cultivating their manual dexterity.

A new study shows that if children watch too much TV, their risk of developing heart disease, hypertension, and diabetes increases in adulthood.

Researchers found that among 6-7-year-old children who spend most of their time watching TV, the arteries behind their eyeballs are narrower. As these children grow into adults, their risks of developing heart disease, hypertension, and diabetes increase.

Researchers from the University of Sydney surveyed 1500 children aged 6-7 years from 34 primary schools in Sydney. On average, these children watched TV for 1.9 hours per day, but only spent 36 minutes participating in organized sports activities.

The survey results showed that children who engaged in physical activity for over an hour each day had significantly wider retinal arteries compared to those who exercised for less than half an hour daily.

The study also found that children who participated in the most physical activities had healthier microvessels compared to those who participated in the least. This suggests that unhealthy lifestyles may affect the microcirculation in childhood, increasing the risk of heart disease and hypertension in later life.

Researchers pointed out that many people have the habit of turning on the TV as soon as they get home and sitting down for hours, which is harmful to heart health and overall health. Therefore, free play activities should be promoted among children, schools should mandate two hours of weekly physical activity, and parents should encourage children to get up from the couch and engage in more physical exercise.

According to the latest survey by the British Dietetic Association, British teenagers spend an average of 20% of their day watching TV or playing computer games. The association calls for attention to the health of young people, encouraging them to participate more in outdoor fitness activities and reduce time spent watching TV or playing computers.

Obesity can lead to heart disease, diabetes, and joint diseases, etc. Therefore, experts suggest that young people should exercise more and reduce time spent watching TV or playing computers.
