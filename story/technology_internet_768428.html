10 Key Differences Between Twitter and Facebook IPOs

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 18:16:20

On November 8, according to Forbes, Twitter ensured that its IPO did not experience the same issues as Facebook's at the time of its public listing. On Thursday, Twitter issued 70 million shares at $26 each, and everything went smoothly—a stark contrast to Facebook’s IPO in May 2012. Specifically, there are ten key differences between Twitter's IPO and Facebook's:

1. No need for bank support. Twitter's stock did not require large-scale bank support to keep the price above the offering price and closed much higher on the first day of trading. In May 2012, Morgan Stanley had to buy a large amount of Facebook shares to ensure the price stayed above $38.

2. Same city, different venues. Facebook listed on NASDAQ with Mark Zuckerberg ringing the IPO bell in Menlo Park, but Twitter broke tradition by choosing the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE). On the morning of the NYSE opening, Twitter CEO Dick Costolo appeared with the company's co-founders in downtown Manhattan.

3. No market glitches. There were no issues when Twitter's stock began trading on Thursday morning. In contrast, Facebook's debut was delayed by 30 minutes due to programming issues at NASDAQ, leading to confusion among traders. NASDAQ eventually paid a $10 million fine and compensated brokers with tens of millions of dollars.

4. Smaller scale. Facebook increased its IPO size, raising a total of $16 billion. However, Twitter only raised the IPO price without expanding the scale. Twitter raised approximately $1.8 billion through its IPO, valuing the company at $14.4 billion or $18 billion if restricted shares are included. Facebook's valuation at the time of its IPO exceeded $100 billion.

5. No insider selling. All funds from Twitter's IPO will go directly to the company for general corporate purposes, capital expenditures, or emergency reserves. Meanwhile, over half of the funds raised during Facebook's IPO went into the pockets of early shareholders such as venture capitalists and hedge funds.

6. Different lead underwriter. Goldman Sachs served as the lead underwriter for Twitter's IPO. For years, Morgan Stanley has dominated Silicon Valley's large IPOs, including Facebook's. Goldman Sachs often sells shares to large funds that tend to hold long positions rather than retail investors or hedge funds that prefer shorting stocks.

7. No profits. Facebook was profitable, earning $1 billion in the year before its IPO. However, Twitter reported a loss of $133.9 million in the first nine months of 2013.

8. Fewer users. Twitter has 230 million active users, about one-fifth of Facebook's current user base. At the time of its IPO, Facebook had 900 million users.

9. Fewer lock-up shares. Facebook had more than 1 billion shares unlocked within the first year after its IPO. Twitter will have significantly fewer shares unlocked in the next year.

10. Strong performance on IPO day. Twitter's stock closed at $44.90, representing a 72.69% increase from its IPO price.
