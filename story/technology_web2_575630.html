Bad breath: Are you eating the right foods?

by zangao88 on 2011-11-21 17:01:02

In addition to the well-known "air conditioning sickness", wind-heat, prickly heat and other symptoms, there is another condition that is widespread in social interactions - oral malodor (commonly known as "halitosis"). One should avoid eating "heat-generating" foods such as sweets, fried food, ginger, chili, garlic, mutton, dog meat, and lychees. Liver heat type mainly occurs due to overwork, staying up late, which causes excessive physical strain, leading to internal heat. It often happens to people with high work pressure who frequently work overtime. The treatment involves clearing liver fire. A daily dose is recommended. In terms of causation, diseases like nasopharyngitis account for the vast majority of halitosis cases, and these are not within the scope of dental practice.

Firstly, nutrition should be balanced, and one should consume more vegetables and fruits rich in chlorophyll, such as lettuce and Chinese cabbage; avoiding strong-smelling, stimulating, and hard-to-digest foods. Traditional Chinese medicine believes that the occurrence of halitosis is most closely related to the spleen and stomach, often caused by irregular dieting like indulging in spicy or greasy foods, or long-term excessive fatigue like late sleeping.

To treat halitosis effectively, Dr. Wang suggests finding the root cause first. These foods not only stimulate the gastrointestinal mucosa to produce excessive acid but also easily cause oral malodor due to their indigestibility. Damage to the gastrointestinal and visceral functions, as well as endocrine disorders, are the main reasons for most people's halitosis. Lung heat type can occur when external "heat" enters through the mouth and nose, causing internal heat. This often happens to people with upper respiratory tract infections, excessive consumption of body fluids, or those who drink alcohol frequently. Treatment involves clearing lung heat.

It is reported that besides the surface-level "halitosis," it might also be a signal of other potential diseases, such as poor oral hygiene, dysfunctions in the upper digestive system, oral inflammation, tonsillitis, pharyngitis, rhinitis, sinusitis, smoking, drinking, and greasy diets.

Dr. Wang from the Otolaryngology Department of Qingyuan Central Hospital told reporters that patients specifically visiting for halitosis are relatively few, "mainly due to insufficient awareness of treatment, failing to attract the patient's attention." If the condition is caused by internal organ damage, the disease will become increasingly severe, unable to heal on its own, and may lead to other serious illnesses if untreated for a long time. Regular halitosis products cannot treat this kind of halitosis, and Chinese medicine and health teas can only play an adjusting role.

Actually, many factors can cause oral malodor, but neglecting daily living habits increases the production and persistence of such odors. A small portion of halitosis is caused by oral environment or oral diseases; the accumulation of plaque, tartar, and dental calculus is the direct cause of halitosis. Nowadays, during hot summer days, many people like to drink chilled beverages to quench thirst, but sugary drinks on the market, when consumed excessively, can lead to oral diseases, which is another cause of halitosis.

This situation is not unique to summer, but it becomes particularly uncomfortable and embarrassing during summer conversations. People who prefer sweet or spicy foods are prone to halitosis. In terms of diet, consuming large amounts of spicy foods or barbecues in summer can easily accumulate damp-heat inside the body, preventing the heat toxins from being expelled, thus triggering conditions like eczema or pimples.

However, carbohydrates are essential for the human body and should be moderately controlled in daily life, avoiding frequent intake of sugary drinks. Halitosis is categorized into three types: stomach heat, liver heat, and lung heat.

The most concerning issue for Dr. Wang is that many citizens do not realize that oral malodor could be a signal of other diseases in the body, usually ignoring it or trying to solve it by brushing teeth or chewing gum, "but this only treats the symptoms, the root cause remains unresolved."

If decayed teeth, residual roots, crowns, or improper restorations exist in the mouth without removal, they easily accumulate food residues and dirt, promoting bacterial growth, especially anaerobic bacteria, which decompose and produce sulfur compounds, emitting a putrid smell and causing halitosis. If these diseases are not promptly detected, latent diseases in the body may take advantage of the situation.

For chronic gingivitis and periodontal disease patients, due to swollen gums, bleeding, pus discharge from periodontal pockets, poor oral hygiene, and the accumulation of plaque, tartar, and calculus, under the action of bacteria and microorganisms, fermentation and decomposition produce substances like indole, hydrogen sulfide, and amine classes, generating unpleasant odors. Statistics show that patients with halitosis caused by rhinitis, sinusitis, pharyngitis, and tonsillitis account for more than 75% of all halitosis patients. Dr. Wang suggests that people with damp-heat constitution should eat more heat-clearing and dampness-removing foods in summer, such as green beans and cucumbers.

Citizens often neglect treatment, "few people visit the hospital due to oral malodor, even if they do, it's usually because of other discomforts in the mouth." For diseases in the oral cavity, digestive system, respiratory system, etc., that may cause halitosis, corresponding treatments should be actively pursued, "because halitosis is often caused by infections of anaerobic bacteria or facultative anaerobic bacteria in the oral cavity or digestive tract, so metronidazole or tinidazole (not exceeding 7 days, contraindicated for mentally ill patients, pregnant women, breastfeeding women, and those with liver dysfunction) can be taken; for halitosis caused by Helicobacter pylori infection, drugs that eradicate Helicobacter pylori can be used."

According to reports, patients with halitosis can effectively detect diseases in the nasal cavity, deep nasopharynx, soft palate, throat, etc., through ENT endoscopic examination and diagnostic systems, thereby taking effective corresponding treatments to eliminate possible causes of halitosis. Dr. Wang currently treats many patients with rhinitis, pharyngitis, and tonsillitis who have varying degrees of breath problems, and after treating their nasopharyngeal diseases, their halitosis is fundamentally resolved.

It is suggested that herbal teas can be consumed regularly, such as wolfberry, jujube, hawthorn, poria, licorice, lingzhi, white atractylodes, and astragalus boiled for 30 minutes, which can clear liver fire.
