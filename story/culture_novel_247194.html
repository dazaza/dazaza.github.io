"Next Dawn" The Growing Pains of an Eighteen-Year-Old Girl

by jqrxass on 2009-10-13 20:17:45

The most breakthrough work of Chinese youth literature, presented by the talented poet Zuo Xiaoci.

Her words are calm enough to make one feel suffocated, as if observing the changes of the vast sea with a pair of wise eyes.

Her language is composed enough to command respect, as if listening to a high monk chanting sutras on a high platform.

Her stories are so beautiful that they bring tears, like following a philosopher contemplating life.

"The Next Dawn" - eleven novels about memories of youth. With independent and distinctive subtitles forming clear clues and threads of the novel, and a poetic and concise writing style showcasing the colors inherent in youth. The story revolves around a girl named "Raochen," whose family is a mystery, and her background is a puzzle. In the shadow of grief, she drops out of school and stays at home, suffering, lost, and struggling. Faced with continued familial love, romance, and friendship, what should she do? All sorts of tangled emotions and growing pains quietly unfold around an eighteen-year-old girl...

I started grabbing that stubby pencil.

One day, the girl who passed by my window told me seven dreams. She said you can write them down, I lost my pen.

When the name Jiang Xiao suddenly appeared in the air, they both felt a sense of bewilderment. Especially Raochen, she always thought that as long as Jiang Xiao's name wasn't mentioned, Jiang Xiao was still by her side. She was afraid that shouting for him would push him further away.

"The Next Dawn" - Chapter Three: I Have Also Come

In the car, Manqiu took out two jade pieces she had just bought. She said, give this white one to your daughter, and the green one to your wife, hoping they will both be safe and happy, may my Buddha bless them. After saying that, she closed her eyes devoutly and made a wish.

Sang Nong placed the white jade in his palm, examining it under the sunlight from the car window; it was truly translucent and crystalline. He said, thank you, Manqiu, I'll take this, Raochen will definitely like it.

"The Next Dawn" - Chapter Four: The Rise and Fall of the Bluebird

When Raochen was little, she asked Sang Nong what happiness was. Sang Nong told her, happiness is a beautiful flower, born because of your kindness and diligence. Even though he knew those words were lies, how could you bear to reveal the cruel face of life to a child of seven or eight years old and tell her that happiness is nothing but an illusory dream? Little Raochen didn't interpret it that way; she said, Daddy, happiness is when one day my mom can hug me. Upon hearing this, Sang Nong tightly embraced Raochen and said, Daddy will hug Raochen more for Mommy.
