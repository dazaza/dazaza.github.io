Twitter's data business is already profitable: it has spawned a social data analysis industry

by anonymous on 2013-10-10 18:38:20

In the IPO (Initial Public Offering) documents disclosed last Thursday, Twitter revealed revenue from an lesser-known non-core business: in 2012, the company earned $47.5 million by selling its data to some rapidly growing companies that analyze Twitter's data to gain insights into news events and development trends.

Compared to the revenue generated from advertising, this business contributes little income, but it signifies that Twitter's data business has already caused "ripples" across the entire economy.

On this social network, the continuous expression of user experiences, opinions, and emotions has created a vast commercial ecosystem, providing product developers, Hollywood film companies, large retailers, as well as hedge funds and other investors with excellent insight into consumer habits and psychology. With substantial venture capital support, hundreds of "social listening" companies have emerged.

"Twitter's impact on the economy is much greater than what the company outlined in its (IPO) documents," said Rob Bailey, CEO of London-based social data analysis company Data Sift.

Social data companies focus on tracking trends that would take ordinary people a long time to observe. The United Nations is using algorithms derived from Twitter to precisely detect hotspots of social unrest. Satellite TV provider DirecTV is also utilizing Twitter's data as an early warning system, identifying power outages based on user complaints. Meanwhile, human resources departments analyze data from Twitter to evaluate job applicants.

For data analytics companies, Wall Street may also represent an opportunity. Dataminr raised $30 million just this year and now providing services to traders has become its core business.

Dataminr uses algorithms similar to those used by hedge funds for high-frequency trading. Five minutes before the congressional shooting was reported on television, Dataminr users received alerts and took swift action; five minutes later, the television news caused the S&P 500 index to drop by 20 points, allowing Dataminr users to gain an advantage in stock trading due to their early actions.

Dataminr's algorithm only detected messages posted by eyewitnesses of the shooting on Twitter and inferred an emergency based on the timing of the posts and the influence and location of the posters. Other trading algorithms use "sentiment scores" to track investor sentiment towards specific stocks, driving investment decisions on whether to go long or short on a particular stock.

These social data companies are just part of the burgeoning data industry, where practitioners collect, distribute, and analyze all the information accumulated by companies or left online by users.

While it's difficult to estimate the market value of the social data industry, according to estimates by market research firm IDC, the growth rate of the "big data" market is seven times that of the entire IT industry. IDC predicts that within two years, the total size of the big data market could reach $16.9 billion.

Most social data companies acquire data from a variety of sources, including Yahoo, Facebook-owned photo-sharing platform Instagram, and consumer review site Yelp. However, Twitter, with its public real-time data stream, is the giant in this industry. Now, even the Library of Congress is cataloging Twitter messages.

On the other hand, Facebook, Twitter's competitor in the social networking market, has largely avoided getting involved in the data business. In fact, that's because Facebook's products were not developed for public conversations but rather for private sharing among friends.

However, after seeing Twitter's success in leading public conversations about real-time events, Facebook has recently partnered with several major media companies to provide aggregated consumer conversation data on specific topics.

Each social data company claims to have unique data mining tools, not just basic keyword search functions. Some social data companies can focus on a specific demographic, such as women in a particular postal code area, and monitor phrases expressing emotions. Then, these companies can create a heat map or sentiment score to measure how this specific group feels about a particular topic.

These companies have trained their natural language processing algorithms to understand slang and ungrammatical language and to prioritize Twitter messages containing words like "breaking news."

"We don't just count the quantity of these trends, that would be too naive," said Nova Spivack, CEO of Los Angeles-based social data analytics company Bottlenose. He pointed out that the company focuses on the momentum behind the trends.

"For many years, businesses have been trying to understand the past," Spivack said. "And Twitter has found a way to measure the present."

Of course, data from Twitter is not a perfect "crystal ball" that allows clear foresight into the future. The most active U.S. users of this social network service do not fully represent the U.S. population. According to reports published by the Pew Internet Project, although three-quarters of American adults share information on social networking sites, Twitter users tend to be relatively young, with 30% under the age of 30. Meanwhile, college students and relatively affluent Americans also have higher rates of Twitter usage, though the gap is narrowing.

Companies believe it's still too early to judge the long-term value of Twitter data. One risk mentioned in Twitter's IPO prospectus is that the company's growth could be hindered by spam and fake Twitter accounts. Another risk is that Twitter accounts can be hacked, and hackers might post incorrect information using stolen accounts.

A spokesperson for Twitter declined to comment.

Meanwhile, companies are still trying to understand how social conversations on Twitter relate to the real world. Darrell Jursa, from Fleishman-Hillard, a public relations firm specializing in crisis management, pointed out that most companies have not abandoned survey reports focusing on specific demographics or users for social media monitoring activities. He also noted that "80% to 90% of conversations still occur offline, so you need to connect social data with everything else for analysis."

In addition, companies using Twitter data are trying to figure out whether a Twitter "crisis" resonates with all Americans or only affects a portion of them.

The restaurant chain operator Five Guys uses New Brand Analytics, a data analysis company based in Washington, to compare burger quality across different regions in the U.S., enabling them to identify issues in certain areas. However, Molly Catalano, a spokesperson for Five Guys, stated that New Brand warned the company not to overreact to customer complaints. New Brand found that while some complaints were unpleasant, overall, Five Guys is quite popular among Twitter users.

Twitter is still trying to find ways to extract the maximum value from its data business. According to the IPO prospectus filed with the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC), this business grew by 53% in the past year. Twitter has consistently restricted the amount of data external companies can access freely from its system.

In this context, many smaller data analytics startups are turning to the four companies Twitter refers to as "Certified Data Resellers": Gnip, Data Sift, Topsy, and Japanese company NTT Data, which account for the majority of Twitter's data revenue. Last year, these four companies paid approximately $35.6 million to Twitter.

Twitter's exponential growth means that the influence of this social network has extended far beyond marketing and crisis public relations, with non-profit organizations, human resource managers, and politicians finding Twitter data useful. During Hurricane Sandy in 2011, the Red Cross began analyzing Twitter data. Anne Marie Borregard, a spokesperson for the Red Cross, said the organization used heat maps generated from social media to guide rescue efforts.

Even Twitter itself has analyzed chat messages on its website to predict the results of last year's U.S. presidential election.

However, with Twitter planning its IPO, some small companies worry that the company will further restrict their access to its data. Kristin Muhlner, CEO of New Brand, pointed out: "Investors are not interested in free."
