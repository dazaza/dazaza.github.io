Project Management in Construction Engineering

by lsxzcty on 2012-02-28 15:57:34

[Abstract] In the fierce market competition, how to manage projects scientifically and quantitatively, ultimately achieving profitability, has become the top priority for all engineering contractors. This article discusses some views on cost control, schedule control, quality control, and safety control in engineering projects for your reference.

[Keywords] Construction Engineering, Project Management, Cost, Schedule, Quality, Safety

The four basic control objectives in construction engineering management are: cost control, schedule control, quality control, and safety control. Below, I will share some insights from these aspects, hoping to resonate with peers and improve overall project management together.

1. Develop Construction Quotas to Guide Bidding and Tendering

For a company to survive, it must be profitable. Price competition is an extremely important component of this process. If one does not deeply understand the government-regulated construction quotas but instead blindly applies them without considering the ever-changing market realities and deviating from the principle of separating [quantity] and [price], they will inevitably fail to accurately grasp the actual cost of the project, unable to meet market competition needs, fall behind competitors, and lose advantageous opportunities. The survival of the fittest is inevitable. Corporate construction quotas are also a necessary product of market price competition. Without them, there would be no complete price competition system to meet market demands. Currently, China's existing pricing system has undergone significant changes. Rapid advancements in science and technology have created differences between old and new processes; fluctuations in the building materials market at different times have caused gaps between information prices and actual material transaction prices; the influx of migrant labor has impacted artificial prices in the construction market; varying levels of proficiency within companies result in differing actual profit margins for different projects under the same quota; applying different quotas to the same work content can cause variations in profitability. All these factors may lead to deviations in the budgeted cost of the project due to differences in the qualifications of the pricing personnel. If one does not emphasize investigation, analysis, and information accumulation, or focus solely on improving settlement, change orders, and signature reviews without enhancing the overall quality of construction enterprise personnel, the results will be less than ideal, even counterproductive. Any project can gain the upper hand in price negotiations if its key sub-project costs are well understood. Ignoring these situations during the bidding process could significantly misguide the bidding efforts and even cause substantial losses to the company. Using the comprehensive price at the construction location as a basis, supplemented by industry standards from various enterprises, and further developing a comprehensive set of construction quotas as a pricing guideline for enterprises, then promoting specific project and period cost prices to guide contract negotiations is a meaningful yet challenging task. This requires extensive research and consultation with relevant authorities to truly achieve success. Achieving this point allows construction units to be clearer in management and accounting, providing important references for establishing control target costs, technical-economic analysis, and reviewing construction plans. Only through serious investigation, analysis, and research can we truly master the actual cost of a project, systematically formulate suitable construction quotas for the enterprise, continuously enrich and update them in practice, and promote greater development for our enterprise.

2. Refine Progress Plans and Strictly Control Quality Management

The task of progress plan management is to scientifically organize various forces and tasks within the enterprise throughout the planning process, coordinate the relationships among all aspects and links of production and operation activities, prepare and implement according to production activity requirements, fully utilize human, material, and financial resources, ensuring the full or over-fulfillment of planned tasks. For example, before signing the project contract, thorough client credit market investigations and accurate predictions must be conducted, selecting the best departmental personnel combinations, optimizing duration, cost, and project space, choosing appropriate equipment suppliers for sub-projects, and preparing comprehensive project plans and segmented/sectional progress plans. The network flowchart method combines the advantages of both network technology and flow operations, reflecting the time arrangement of the plan while making the scientific nature of the plan and the critical path of project progress clear at a glance. According to the general construction plan, phase-specific construction progress plans are formulated, and the responsibility for supervising plan execution is assigned to specific individuals, facilitating project management guidance and avoiding the shortcomings of overly formalized and insufficiently detailed flow operation plans. Project quality is the lifeline of engineering projects. From construction preparation to delivery, the entire process must combine the characteristics and difficulties of specific projects, formulating and implementing specific, scientific, and highly targeted management measures, conducting strict monitoring, and effectively managing the quality and technology of the project. To successfully manage quality and technology, the following points must be clarified:

1. Establish and enforce hierarchical position responsibilities and position responsibility systems. Engineering risks must be managed by dedicated personnel; otherwise, they may be overlooked, leading to major accidents. Regulations that do not keep up with the cyclical changes and developments of enterprises are common problems among many construction units. Lacking systematic position responsibility systems at each stage can lead to defects and loopholes in management. Therefore, from the enterprise to the project level, the responsibilities and authorities of technical personnel at all levels must be defined, with lower-level technicians obeying the leadership of higher-level technicians, ensuring clear division of power and responsibility, distinct task assignments, and accountability down to the individual, making position responsibility management institutionalized, standardized, and systematic. For instance, within the enterprise, allowing quality engineers and project progress managers to focus on their respective areas of quality and technical management fosters better collaboration and encourages related engineers to play a more proactive role.
