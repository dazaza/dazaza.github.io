Market reports show an unclear future for genetically modified rice.

by ditan360 on 2010-06-22 16:11:03

Low Carbon Network exclusive - The latest market research report published by the international environmental organization Greenpeace on the 21st shows that due to the long-term safety for human health not being clear, the market prospects for genetically modified (GM) food are currently unclear.

The report states that some rice industry giants - COFCO Rice and Yihai Kerry,旺旺 (Want Want), a giant in the rice cake industry, Yanjing Beer, a giant in the beer industry, etc. - have all publicly committed to using non-GM raw materials. Carrefour, Auchan, and other large supermarkets have also taken measures to ensure that the relevant products they sell do not contain GM components.

In April this year, a survey conducted by Xiaokang magazine and Tsinghua University Media Survey Laboratory also showed that nearly two-thirds of respondents tend to buy non-GM food.

This survey shows that more and more food producers have recognized the cautious attitude of Chinese consumers towards GM food and have begun to take action to respond to consumer appeals by joining the non-GM ranks.

"Many food companies have realized that from the perspective of maximizing profits, manufacturing or producing food containing GM ingredients cannot bring business opportunities to the enterprise," the report said, taking COFCO as an example. As China's largest import and export company and food producer for grain and oil, its "Fulinmen" and other small packaged rice are currently sold in 2543 supermarkets in 256 cities across the country, and future sales areas will cover all provinces, autonomous regions, and municipalities in mainland China.

COFCO committed in 2010 that the Zicai brand, Wuhu brand, Fulinmen brand rice produced by its Dalian, Zhangjiagang, and Jiangxi companies will provide non-GM rice ensuring food safety for consumers.

Another dragon in the packaged rice market - Yihai Kerry has also clearly committed that its Golden Dragon Fish brand, Jin Yuanbao brand, Xiangmanyuan brand, and other seven brands of rice will adhere to the sale of non-GM rice.

In addition, other corporate giants from related industries have also expressed their stance, committing to refuse GM rice raw materials and additives and food ingredients made from GM rice. These include Heinz, which holds 41.51% of the baby food market, domestic brand Beingmate with 21.57%, Snowflake Beer with 19.8% of the beer market, Yanjing Beer with 90% market share in the Beijing market, Luzhou Laojiao, a major liquor brand distributed in North China and Central China, Jian Nan Chun which holds 8% of the national high-end liquor market, etc.

The report says that many enterprises have long insisted on providing consumers with safe food and thus refused GM ingredients. For example, Want Want Group, the leader in the rice cake industry, has been committed since 2004 to refusing to use GM rice raw materials and GM additives. In 2010, it still firmly promised consumers that there would be no use of GM ingredients in its beverage and snack brands.

According to the report, due to market regulations in overseas markets such as Japan and the EU, as well as the cautious attitudes of consumers, these overseas markets' rejection of GM foods makes it impossible for export-oriented food enterprises to bear the economic losses and risks of products being returned or rejected by overseas markets with strict restrictions on GM foods.

The report takes Jinyan Company, a famous local vermicelli brand in Dongguan, as an example. This company produces about 10,000 tons of vermicelli annually. In addition to selling locally and domestically, 60% of its products are exported to European and American countries. From 2006 to now, to facilitate smooth exports, the cost of testing whether the rice and vermicelli samples it procures contain GM components has reached over 5 million yuan, firmly providing non-GM vermicelli to foreign consumers.

The cautious attitude of overseas markets towards GM rice further influences rice enterprises around the world to resist GM rice, such as the Thai Rice Exporters Association and the Vietnam Food Association jointly signing a memorandum of understanding, announcing their non-GM rice production policies. The Indian Rice Exporters Association also strongly calls for the prohibition of developing and planting GM rice in major fragrant rice-growing provinces.

According to a latest consumer survey conducted by Ipsos (China) Market Research Consulting Co., Ltd. in Beijing, Shanghai, and Guangzhou, Chinese consumers are cautious about GM food. 65% of respondents clearly choose non-GM products, while 77% of respondents clearly choose non-GM rice.

The report analyzes that under the situation where all parties are suspicious and resistant to GM rice, once GM rice is planted in rice-producing provinces, the economic impact will be long-term.

The report uses the example of LL601 GM rice contamination in the United States. In August 2006, due to the mixing of Bayer CropScience's GM rice LL601 into the U.S. rice supply chain, it caused a sharp decline in U.S. rice prices, immediate cessation of imports of U.S. rice by the EU and Japanese markets, resulting in maximum economic losses of up to $741 million to $1.29 billion for the entire U.S. rice industry.

The report suggests that related enterprises take the opportunity to establish brand awareness and expand market share by committing to rejecting GM rice, uniting terminal retailers and consumers to establish non-GM food principles. It also suggests that relevant government departments suspend any commercialization process of GM rice and investigate illegal products already appearing in the market to avoid larger-scale pollution.
