Hainan toxic long beans and Greek strikes - a lesson in management from Green Tree Inn

by alexsgxu on 2010-02-28 17:47:33

First is the toxic cowpea problem in Hainan Island. Only a small portion of the cowpeas are toxic, but even such a small amount has resulted in people not daring to buy or eat cowpeas from Hainan Island. If vegetables cannot be sold in time, they will quickly rot in the field.

The interests of many law-abiding and hardworking cowpea farmers have been greatly damaged. We cannot distinguish which ones are toxic and which ones are not, so as guests, we cannot tell them apart and directly punish everyone. Is this reasonable? From the consumer's perspective, we have no choice. Even though most cowpea farmers are kind, honest, and hardworking, their cowpeas, grown with hard work, rot in the fields. Their families' livelihoods cannot be secured, and they may not even have food to eat. Why? Because of a few bad apples, one fly has spoiled the whole pot of soup. This is very unfair to our hardworking farmers.

Of course, we also need to study this issue from another angle: how did these toxic pesticides end up in the hands of our farmers? Were some farmers misled? In this way, we can solve the problem at its source and prevent similar pesticides from ending up in the hands of the public. The manufacturers who sell toxic pesticides should also be dealt with promptly.

This issue gives us an important insight into management. To protect everyone's interests, everyone must unite, follow rules, and serve the guests well. If someone intentionally offends guests, those guests will never come back to your hotel. The entire team's income will decrease, and the team might not be able to pay salaries. The company or team could go bankrupt, and everyone will have to find new jobs and new paths. Now, many jobs may still be relatively easy to find, but what if they're not? Will everyone become unemployed?

When encountering such problems, we must solve them from two aspects. First, many employees want to do their work well from the heart, but they lack good working methods or fail to recognize their own issues. If our leaders can train our employees with new methods, similar problems can be avoided.

Of course, there are a few people with serious problems who cannot be trained. Stealing and slacking off have become their habits. In such cases, we must learn to remove these bad elements from the team. The entire team, especially managers, must have keen eyesight to discover these issues promptly and persuade these individuals to leave who cause the team's failure. We absolutely cannot tolerate them destroying the hard work of other employees, the team's integrity, service quality, and future. I believe that if this principle is explained clearly to the entire team, everyone will fully support your actions. You will certainly be a fair leader who protects the entire team's interests.

Some department leaders, for various reasons—whether it's due to poor management skills, lack of exemplary behavior, avoiding trouble, or personal relationships—fail to identify such underperforming employees or simply ignore them. This further encourages bad habits and styles among these individuals. Other employees, seeing this, will inevitably feel it's unfair. This is indeed the least respectful and fairest form of management towards other hardworking employees. It is a highly irresponsible management behavior. Over time, good employees will choose to slowly leave, and those left behind will create more chaos, making the team increasingly disordered. This is a fatal blow to the team. Therefore, as a leader, one must focus on the big picture (placing the entire team's interests in one's heart) rather than small cliques. One must protect the majority's interests and not sacrifice the entire team's interests because of one person's violations.

The second issue is the strike problem in Greece. Greek civil servants are now striking on the streets, paralyzing many tasks that taxpayers fund the government to perform. They oppose national budget cuts, tightening belts, and reducing deficits (which are important conditions for Greece to gain trust and assistance loans from other countries). Don't you think these people are very despicable? These beneficiaries consume most of the country's income, have drained their country's wealth, and their country is about to collapse and go bankrupt. At this critical juncture, everyone needs to tighten their belts and get through the difficulties together. Isn't this reasonable?

When your motherland cannot feed you or satisfy your desires, what should a citizen do? Kill the motherland? Like a group of children who have sucked all the milk from their mother, leaving her on her last breath, but these people still refuse to restrain themselves, suck less, and let the mother recover? I believe a mother's milk is limited, just like a country's wealth and income, contributed by all taxpayers, also has a limit. It cannot be wasted or consumed endlessly. We all rely on this motherland, so why can't we think more about her?

When a country or collective faces a crisis, we must all work together to help our country and organization get through the difficulties. Shouldn't these people's behaviors be despised?
