Why Apple holds a separate launch event in China?

by anonymous on 2013-09-08 19:22:27

By Guoren

On the morning of September 4, I received an invitation from Apple to attend a new product launch event in China on September 11.

I was also quite surprised. Prior to this, domestic portal media outlets were usually invited about a week in advance to report on Apple's new product launches in San Francisco. Although AllthingsD had already confirmed that the release date would be September 10, it was strange that no invitation had been received yet. It turns out Apple had something special planned.

This is undoubtedly the biggest news of the day. Naturally, everyone is curious as to why Apple is holding a separate launch event for its new products in China and what other peculiarities there might be. Here are my opinions and some information I have gathered:

1. At around midnight on the 4th, foreign media also received Apple’s invitation. I received the email from Apple PR staff in China at around 10 AM, which was a 9-hour gap, corresponding to the time difference for the September 11 release. From this point of view, Apple has indeed kept the media informed simultaneously.

2. If calculated according to Beijing time, whether it’s the U.S. or China, it will all be on September 11. Therefore, you can consider this Apple product launch to truly occur on the same day in both China and the U.S.

3. Will Tim Cook come? Definitely not, unless he's superhuman; even flying from the U.S., he wouldn't make it in time.

4. According to reliable sources, the heads of the three major telecom operators will attend Apple's domestic new product launch event. Telecom and Mobile have already confirmed their representatives, but it seems that Mobile's participant is still undecided.

4-2. There is another coincidence here. Since 2010, Apple has started to pay more attention to the Chinese market, often inviting Chinese media to cover important new product launches. I was fortunate enough to report on several of these events. However, one time they did not invite domestic media — the release of the iPhone 4S in 2011, which took place on the morning of October 5 (October 4 in the U.S.). The next day, Steve Jobs passed away. Moreover, that launch was held in a hall at Apple's headquarters, not at the Yerba Buena Center for the Arts.

This time, the iPhone 5S launch did not invite Chinese media to report from the U.S., and the U.S. launch location was again at Apple's headquarters. Every minor upgrade or model change has been launched at headquarters without inviting Chinese media. Is this a coincidence?

5. This is actually not Apple's first time launching a new product in China. In 2009, when the iPhone 3GS was first introduced in mainland China, there was Apple management present, but the main focus was still on China Unicom. Back then, Apple was still very dominant and didn't care much about the Chinese market.

6. Did Tim Cook specifically come to Beijing to finalize the details of the launch event? Despite his low profile, Cook has already visited China at least twice, meeting with top executives of domestic telecom operators. Perhaps the decision to hold the launch in China was already discussed and agreed upon by Cook and the operators' leaders.

7. Looking back at Apple's history of product launches, there has never been a case where a product was launched in the home country and then separately in another country on the same day. So this move is indeed unprecedented.

8. What exactly makes this separate launch in China different? Surely it won't just be a rehash of the content from the San Francisco launch? Definitely not.

In my opinion, the necessity lies in Apple emphasizing more localized elements for the Chinese market. During the release of the Mountain Lion system last year, it demonstrated its importance to the Chinese market. Not only did it invite Chinese media to preview the unreleased Mountain system months before the official release, but it also dedicated time during the launch event to highlight unique features specific to the Chinese market.

Now, considering the possible new products like the iPhone 5C, iPhone 5S, second-generation iPad mini, and the official version of iOS 7… the reasons for Apple holding a separate launch in China could include the following:

One, is the iPhone 5C exclusively for the Chinese market? I think this assumption is highly unreliable. Apple has never released a product targeted solely at one market, and Apple always tries to avoid different versions of the same product. For instance, the WCDMA and CDMA versions used to be different models, but now the hardware is the same, with only baseband restrictions tailored to different markets.

Two, will the Chinese operators sell the new products globally at the same time? This is the most anticipated point. Previously, the regions where Apple products were available in the first week after their release were always limited to nine areas or fewer. If the Chinese market were to be included in the simultaneous release, it would immediately elevate China to the "rich and handsome" tier.

As for some media claiming to have exclusive leaks, once the truth comes out, anyone can say such things. It's just for show; if guessed correctly, some people may think you're great, and if wrong, no one will pursue it further. Talking big comes at no cost.

Three, will a TD-LTE version be released? This is another point worth watching. TD-LTE is the most localized feature of China. If WCDMA, CDMA, and TD-LTE 4G versions are released simultaneously, it would definitely be more appropriate to do so in China. My colleague even believes that the primary reason for Apple's launch in China is to introduce the TD-LTE version.

However, there are issues with hosting the launch in China for these reasons. For example, one of the key reasons why the Chinese market was late to launch products compared to other markets was due to obtaining the network access license from the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology (MIIT). Unless MIIT provides Apple with a fast-track green channel to quickly obtain the certification this time.

Another point is that China's 4G has not yet been commercialized, so whether Apple will introduce support for TD-LTE is questionable. Actually, supporting LTE is quite simple; using Qualcomm chips that cover from 2G to 4G can solve the issue. But this decision would need to be made months or even a year in advance.

Four, another reason is that this time iOS 7 and the Mavericks operating system incorporate more Chinese elements, making them suitable for a launch in China, such as the addition of Tencent Weibo, the nine-grid input keyboard, and improved input methods.

I believe all these reasons play a part. Summing it up, the Chinese market is simply too important. Let's have a separate launch to make everything clearer.

9. Lastly, the scale of this domestic launch should not be too large. The address on the invitation, Phase III of the China World Trade Center Tower 12A, is actually Apple's office in Beijing. It won't be a large-scale event. From what I understand, the invited media will still be similar to those who were previously invited to attend the U.S. launches. Following Apple's usual approach, this includes a few major portals and regional newspapers that perform well domestically.
