If you ask what the difference is between small and medium-sized webmasters and some mature IT professionals, I think it lies in these four words: "user experience." As far as I know, most webmasters don’t really understand them.

(What is user experience?)

1. My content is pseudo-original; isn't it high-quality?

Sometimes when chatting with some webmasters about the issue of website content, they rarely start from the essence of the content but often look at problems from an SEO perspective. When I ask them, is your content high-quality? They answer, yes, very high-quality. Every article I pseudo-originalize, replacing every “的” with “de,” and rewording every reposted news title. Such dialogues are not uncommon. Different ways of thinking — this is the difference between webmasters and many mature IT professionals, and even on this point, they cannot reach a consensus with users. Many webmasters will counterargue by saying, I do SEO because website content only has value if it’s seen. If users don’t see it, how useful is the content? In fact, marketing serves the content; we don't market just for the sake of marketing.

2. Why am I updating so much content every day, creating so many external links, yet still not getting much traffic?

This is a fairly typical way webmasters operate. They don't care about the users of the website but only focus on SEO tutorials, learning that a website needs daily content updates and external link additions to gain traffic from search engines. Thus, these two tasks become the webmaster's required courses. They find a bunch of articles through search engines, then repost them to their own sites, sometimes pseudo-originalizing them. Of course, they might even skip the manual process, using a collection tool to solve everything, all while the software automatically pseudo-originalizes the content for them. They spend all day busy on forums and blogs, with only one purpose: to create a large number of external links. What's the point of working yourself to death like this? Haven't you heard the saying, "The highest realm of SEO is not SEO"? The essence of marketing is exactly this: if you could market your product, but only by smashing all your products with a hammer to let everyone know about it, would you do it? You definitely wouldn't, because you clearly know that even if users come, they won't buy anything.

3. Who are webmasters? Some are pseudo-webmasters!

Don't assume that having a website makes you a webmaster. Websites also differ significantly. For example, although both are websites, Taobao and Sina are not the same thing. Therefore, websites can take many forms. In the webmaster circle, a few people can collaborate to run a site, one person can independently manage a site, or even one person can operate hundreds or thousands of sites. So the nature of different sites varies greatly. A site managed by several people compared to those operated by one person managing hundreds or thousands of sites cannot be compared. Therefore, we don't call the latter group webmasters; they are marketers. Websites are merely their marketing tools. They just use websites to obtain traffic from search engines and quickly convert it into advertising revenue without caring about the user experience of the website. This causes many webmasters to feel confused, as their hard work yields little traffic, while others, who may lack substantial content or even have garbage content, receive tens of thousands of IPs daily.

4. Webmasters are not jacks-of-all-trades

In the past, webmasters were once labeled as "jacks-of-all-trades," and many webmasters were in a state of being praised. In reality, most webmasters are not jacks-of-all-trades. Talking about editing skills, most just repost content, and many webmasters don't even know what they're reposting. Regarding programming skills, most don't possess them at all, and even those with higher technical abilities can only modify source code. As for marketing skills, this is probably the area where webmasters can somewhat hold their heads up, but many webmasters' marketing skills are lacking. They only know how to attract traffic but don't know how to retain it, which is why we say webmasters don't understand "user experience." Webmasters are not synonymous with jacks-of-all-trades. Those who truly meet the standard of being a jack-of-all-trades are extremely rare. More webmasters are specialists (which is already quite good), and most are in a talentless phase. Therefore, webmasters should focus more energy on areas they are interested in and proficient in, leveraging their strengths and avoiding weaknesses. This is something entrepreneurs should recognize, or they should find other partners to form a team and play to each other's strengths to grow bigger and stronger.

Therefore, if the webmaster community wants to continue long-term, they must recognize "user experience" as a way to retain traffic, moving from simple SEO to "UEO + SEO," focusing on long-term sustainable development.
