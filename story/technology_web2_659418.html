Sleeping in the middle

Written by Mumu Xi,

My cousin married a barber from a nearby village. At that time, my cousin was so infatuated that she insisted on following that man. Despite Uncle and Aunt's attempts to dissuade her, they couldn't stop her. They told her that man wouldn't bring her happiness, saying he had relocated from another place, and his family background was unknown. They gave many reasons against the marriage, but none could sway my sister's determination to marry that man. Eventually, Uncle and Aunt gave up their opposition and agreed to the wedding. Thus, my cousin married the man she loved.

When I started remembering things, my cousin already had a child with a nice nickname, Kangkang. I was probably three years old at that time. My mother told me that the little boy was my nephew, and he called me "Auntie," so I should take care of him and not bully him. As time passed, Kangkang and I grew up together. He often played with me, and when he was around eight or nine years old, he listened to me the most, always holding onto my clothes and following behind me, calling out "Auntie" incessantly. I took him to many relatives' and friends' houses and was surprised to discover how sweet-talking he was. Whenever he came to play at my house, he would ask me to take him to my grandmother's house. Then he would snuggle up next to grandma, making her laugh without any hesitation. Grandma said this child would definitely be an excellent boy when he grew up.

At the age of ten, Kangkang started spending more time living and eating at my house. Although I liked having him stay with me and play, I still asked my mom why Kangkang didn't often stay at his own home. Mom quietly told me that his parents frequently argued at home, which wasn't suitable for him to stay there as it would affect his growth. I then asked mom if they knew this, why didn't they stop arguing and fighting. Mom continued to explain that Kangkang's father was wasteful, always gambling and squandering all the family's money. Cousin had high aspirations and ran the barber shop herself, but even that money wasn't enough to cover what his father kept taking out. In the end, mom helplessly said that these were adult matters that children couldn't understand.

Back then, I didn't quite understand the adults' affairs or how much harm these things caused Kangkang. All I knew was that from that point onward, I cared for him even more, taking him everywhere with me and saving food for him. He began relying on me more, telling me stories about his childhood. He said one night he followed his father out to play, and his father lost sight of him. A car passed by him, brushing against his leg, and he rolled into a ditch beside a tree. No one found him, and he climbed out of the ditch by himself. Hearing his story, I felt chills down my spine; he was so young, yet his tender heart showed unusual strength.

He was a unique child, sensible and strong-willed. He didn't like sleeping in the same bed as others, and since there weren't any extra beds at our house for him to sleep in, he combined two small sofas and slept in between them, covering himself with thick blankets. I asked him if sleeping inside would leave him unable to stretch out fully, and he happily replied, "Auntie, you don't know how comfortable it is to sleep there. It's a pity that you've grown taller and can't experience it."

My brother, also Kangkang's uncle, often clashed with him, leading to disagreements. Every time this happened, Kangkang would shout that he wanted to go home, back to his own home. My brother and I would hide by the door, watching him walk away before turning around to secretly peek behind him. Once we showed ourselves, he would quickly hide behind a nearby tree. Each time, my brother and I would pretend not to know where he was hiding and search aimlessly in all directions. When he was happy, he would jump out, grinning mischievously and say, "You two are so stupid, you can't find me," and then follow my brother and me back home.

Later, for some unknown reason, Kangkang was taken back by Uncle. Mom said Kangkang was leaving, and Uncle wanted him to stay a few more days. I didn't understand what "leaving" meant, thinking it was either because of school or that his parents had reconciled and he was going back, so I went about my days as usual. Whenever I missed him very much, I would walk for half an hour to Uncle's house to play with him.

One evening, a car suddenly arrived outside the door, and Kangkang came with Cousin. Excitedly, I asked if Kangkang was coming back to live at my house again. Then there was silence, and no one answered me. I looked at Cousin's weary face and tactfully stepped aside. Cousin brought a set of clean clothes and shoes and had Kangkang sit on the sofa to change into them. She told him to listen to his father and not always be stubborn. Listening to these seemingly irrelevant words, I started feeling sad, as if I understood something. Kangkang just sat silently on the sofa, head down, not speaking. I stood next to him, pulling his hand repeatedly, but he ignored me.

Not long after, Kangkang's father came in amidst Cousin's murmured complaints. After entering, he didn't say much, picking up the things prepared for Kangkang and hugging him to leave. Cousin couldn't control her emotions and burst into tears. My mother, red-eyed, seemed to say something about treating the child well or else facing retribution. I stopped hearing anything, focusing solely on Kangkang's every move. He struggled to stand on the ground and walked toward the door himself. Before stepping out, he finally turned around and looked at me for several seconds before getting into the car and disappearing into the dark night, vanishing without a trace.

For so long, it was the first time I saw his cold expression. He used to smile a lot, talk a lot, and love to jump around. Mom said Kangkang was always like that in front of his father. He actually knew everything, but kept it bottled up inside without ever expressing it. Later, I really never saw Kangkang again. During this period, I heard some scattered rumors. It turned out that Kangkang's father had run off with a married woman from the same village, returning to his hometown in Henan Province. The village he was in highly valued boys over girls, so he came back to take Kangkang, often beating and scolding Cousin. Unable to bear seeing his daughter suffer such torment, Uncle persuaded Cousin to agree to let him take Kangkang away.

News became increasingly scarce, and Sister remarried. I thought Kangkang would thus fade from my life, never to be mentioned again with sadness. That year, while working outside for a long time, I returned to Uncle's house and accidentally saw a letter written by Kangkang's stepmother to Cousin. The content roughly stated that Kangkang was attending junior high school, but due to his strong personality, he wasn't studying well. There were many other details, ultimately implying that they wanted to send Kangkang back. At that time, Sister had just had a baby from her new family, and the letter was sent to Uncle's house. I guessed Uncle must not have told Cousin.

Two more years passed, and Mom told me Kangkang had run away from home in Henan. Kangkang's father hadn't dared to tell Sister, but eventually, he couldn't hide it anymore and disclosed that Kangkang had been gone for more than a year. This news hit me hard. Many nights, I woke up from nightmares, dreaming of him wandering on the streets, with nothing to eat or wear. Every day, I prayed that he would be okay. I told Mom I wanted to find Kangkang, but she said it wouldn't be easy. Kangkang was a painful topic in our large family that no one dared to mention, as it made everyone sad. They had searched for many years without success, unsure whether he had met with misfortune.

I clearly understood Mom's meaning. But as an adult, as his aunt, I believe I have the right to call him back, back to my side. I told Mom I must find him, and even if I can't, I should try my best, right? Grown-up, he will surely become an excellent young man. Though I don't know his current situation, after much deep thought, I sat in front of the computer, tears streaming down my face as I recalled events from over a decade ago, writing this story. I hope everyone can do their part and pay attention to whether there is a runaway child like him nearby, to save an innocent and pure youth. I also hope that Kangkang sees this and contacts me as soon as possible.
