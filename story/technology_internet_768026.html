Tragic: Paid 5288 yuan for a gold iPhone, but it turned out to be a display model

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 17:18:13

Attention, fruit fans. Besides refurbished phones, there are also demo phones.

Recently, the gold iPhone has been very popular. Mr. Ye from Cangqian in Yuhang also wanted to experience it. In early October, he spent 5288 yuan on a gold iPhone 5S Tianyi contract phone. However, after using it for not long, he discovered a large amount of someone else's information on the phone. After several verifications, he learned that he had purchased a demo phone, which left him very upset.

Mr. Ye believed that someone tampered with the sales process and that he was deceived. The business hall responded that the salesperson had mistakenly taken the demo phone as a real one due to haste when retrieving the item. Yesterday, after multiple negotiations, both parties reached an agreement: Mr. Ye would be given a new phone and compensated with 500 yuan worth of phone credit.

The new phone contained a large amount of other people's information; the gold phone turned out to be a demo unit.

Mr. Ye works as a freelancer, is from Fujian, and has been in Hangzhou for five years. On October 14th, he went to a China Telecom business hall on Cangxing Street in Cangqian, Yuhang, and spent 5288 yuan to buy an iPhone 5S Tianyi contract phone. Unexpectedly, before he even got used to his new phone, problems arose: the phone stored a large number of phone numbers, photos, and other people's information, and some software could not be downloaded or installed. "Basic software like QQ and WeChat couldn't be downloaded."

He was worried that the new phone might be a second-hand one, so he consulted a knowledgeable friend, who informed him: the phone indeed had issues—it was a demo unit. "My knowledgeable friend said that these demo units have a D-prefix mark on their packaging boxes. I checked, and sure enough, I found it. Additionally, when my phone was on standby, it displayed a demonstration video that only appears on demo units."

Mr. Ye suspected there were tricks during the sales process—there's a significant price difference between demo units and real ones. He also noticed that the serial number on his receipt did not match the one on the phone. Moreover, demo units cannot sign up for contract packages. He felt that the business hall should refund him and provide compensation.

The business hall explained that they made a mistake—exchanging the demo unit for a new one and compensating with 500 yuan of phone credit.

Regarding the issues Mr. Ye raised, the reporter verified the situation with the China Telecom business hall on Cangxing Street in Cangqian Town. Manager Wang of the business hall stated that the issues Mr. Ye reported were indeed true—the phone was indeed a demo unit. "We promised unconditional returns and exchanges and offered 100 yuan of phone credit as compensation. He disagreed and demanded a one-for-one replacement." As a result, Mr. Ye also reported the situation to the Yuhang Industry and Commerce Department.

Yesterday, after multiple negotiations, both parties finally reached an agreement and signed a contract. The Tianyi business hall gave Mr. Ye a new iPhone 5S and, according to his request, exchanged it for a bare unit while providing 500 yuan of phone credit as compensation. Regarding Mr. Ye's doubts about the business hall, Manager Wang explained: at the time, the goods were retrieved hastily, and the salesperson accidentally took the wrong item.

What is a demo unit? How does it differ from a real one? According to Apple staff, demo units must not be sold publicly but are only used to demonstrate phone operations to customers. There is no difference in hardware compared to real units—they can still make calls and send messages—but there are differences in software—certain functions are restricted. "The system of the demo unit has been modified, limiting functions such as fingerprint recognition, reset function, and downloading/installing applications."

Apple staff recommended that consumers, after purchasing a phone, find the IMEI serial number under 'Settings' and then check the purchase date and warranty period on the Apple official website. Generally, the warranty period and purchase date are not far apart.
