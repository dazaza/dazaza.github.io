Do Chinese websites really need to go international?

As the "number one internet giant", Tencent's traffic, user base, cash flow, and total revenue have always been envied: its total revenue in the second quarter was 1.9 times that of Sina, nearly three times that of Sohu, 2.1 times that of Baidu, and 1.5 times that of NetEase; its abundant net cash flow also makes other companies envious. However, Tencent has not taken the route of internationalization but instead focused on refining its operations in the Chinese market, venturing into various fields such as IM, C2C, and search, without pursuing international development. In contrast, another domestic internet giant, Baidu, has loudly proclaimed its internationalization strategy and even implemented it, with what results? It suffered a setback in its initial attempt in Japan.

Internationalization, a term that stirs excitement among Chinese enterprises, is also one that brings sorrow to many international companies, because many companies not only lose their original capital when going international but also gradually lose their basic domestic markets. Such situations occur not only in China but also in other countries. Everyone knows that internationalization is the future trend and the only way to gain more resources and profits. But how easy is it to truly go international?

Internationalization is not as simple as creating an English version of a website. If it were that simple, the author’s CaiKe Online (www.coko365.com) would have developed an English version long ago. As a member of Chinese websites, it's not that we haven't considered internationalization, but rather we focus on doing well in the present, which means strengthening the Chinese market, focusing on familiar and skilled areas.

Baidu went international but hit a wall. Why? Not just due to cultural differences, but more importantly, because network technology around the world is almost homogenized. Any advanced network technology in China will definitely exist in Japan, and any forward-thinking business model in China will likely soon appear in Japan. Therefore, the success rate of website internationalization is much harder than in traditional industries.

Apart from technical reasons, the internet is a new industry and product, so websites are still relatively unfamiliar in various countries. Even if a foreign website comes to China, the Chinese people themselves are not yet fully familiar or understanding of their own domestic websites, let alone foreign websites, because the cultural concepts and habits of foreign websites do not align well with China's national conditions.

Why does the probability of failure increase once a website goes international? For example, after eBay acquired EachNet, its performance plummeted. With eBay's backing, EachNet had far more technology, resources, and money than before, so why did it fail? Why can’t Google beat Baidu in China? It's not because Google lacks money or has poor technology, but because Google was not created by yellow-skinned people.

If websites can truly achieve internationalization, the author believes that cooperation is the best way, and mere acquisition and merger may not necessarily be good. Only through cooperation can mutual competition and development be achieved!
