Synopsis:

The film tells the story of an idealistic social worker who, while trying to save a 10-year-old girl from domestic violence and abuse, discovers that the situation is far more complex than she initially thought. The little girl may not be as innocent as she appears... A hidden, ominous force lurking in the shadows seeks a way out, ready to unleash its power, stronger than anyone could have imagined. These terrifying omens occur as a social worker attempts to rescue a young girl who refuses to speak for mysterious reasons.

Emily Jenkins (Renée Zellweger), a professional social worker dedicated to family services, has witnessed countless cases of lies, neglect, and abuse within families. She believed she had seen all sorts of horrors related to family life—until she encountered "Case 39." Emily becomes involved with Lily Sullivan (Jodelle Ferland), a 10-year-old girl from a bizarre, frightening, and secretive family. When Lily's parents attempt to harm her during a midnight religious ritual, Emily feels compelled to intervene in their strange lives.

Deeply saddened by Lily’s loneliness and apparent innocence, Emily decides to do something she would never normally allow herself to do: inject personal emotion into her work. Emily hopes to help the introverted and isolated child by providing her with a temporary warm and happy home environment until a suitable adoptive family can be found.

What Emily doesn’t realize is that true horror is just beginning. What drove Lily's family to the point of wanting to kill their only child? Why does everyone who comes into contact with Lily suddenly go mad? As a destructive and mysterious source of fear reveals itself to Emily, how will she survive the overwhelming terror? Upon discovering the truth, Emily must cling to her rational beliefs to face her most terrifying nightmare—especially when she enters a surreal realm where humanity is mercilessly stripped away by evil. Now, Emily's only fear should be the power of fear itself.

Behind-the-Scenes:

[What Constitutes True Fear?]

From "The Omen" to "The Exorcist," from "Rosemary's Baby" to "Demon Seed" and then to "The Others" and "El Orfanato," fear often manifests in seemingly ordinary families… Looking back at movie history, the most memorable horror stories often arise from small cracks in family relationships, revolving around unstable emotions like anxiety, paranoia, or delusions—or uncertainty about what lies hidden beneath. Like "Case 39," it tells the story of Emily Jenkins, a social worker whose job is to save one broken family after another. When she encounters a little girl drowning in despair, she spares no effort to protect this helpless soul. But soon, Emily realizes that taking care of children is not a simple task, leading her to uncover deeper fears—fear of failure, misunderstanding, and losing control.

These sources of fear are enough to drain one's strength and courage, but on top of that, "Case 39" introduces another horrifying possibility released by Lily Sullivan, the girl temporarily adopted by Emily—all forbidden corners of the deepest, darkest subconscious filled with evil. The resulting fear grips your throat because it is not only fatal but also carries a sense of destructive destiny.

Renée Zellweger, who won the Oscar for Best Supporting Actress in "Cold Mountain" in 2003, will showcase her "scream queen" side for the first time in "Case 39," which is also German director Christian Alvart's first American film… In 2005, Alvart gained recognition in Europe for his innovative psychological thriller "Antikörper," a story centered around a serial killer whose violent mindset might infect the interrogating officer through a virus—thanks to its captivating visual style and provocative statements, "Antikörper" made Alvart stand out among emerging filmmakers.

In Christian Alvart's subsequent plans, he hoped his next work would create a radically unconventional horror-thriller cinematic model. When producer Kevin Misher handed him the script of "Case 39" written by Ray Wright, the German filmmaker quickly found himself deeply immersed in the story's chilling atmosphere. Alvart explained: "At that time, I was even considering changing my taste and temporarily stepping away from the peculiar world behind horror films... But after reading 'Case 39,' I immediately realized it contained a sharp, crazy, and unexpected ending to an excellent story that I had to bring to life on screen. The script was very engaging, and as I read the words, vivid images continuously formed in my mind. Wright created several shocking scenes that delighted me as they all successfully triggered unexpected effects in new ways. I could see from the script that Wright kept trying to take the audience away from their comfort zones—everything about it was extraordinarily attractive to me."

Christian Alvart has always been interested in horror films that evoke universal emotions. He believes: "'Case 39' possesses exactly the traits I value most. I particularly like the realism and genuine emotions reflected in the story surrounding dysfunctional families—for me, the core of the story is that even as a parent, you can never truly grasp your child's thoughts. You can try your best, but you can never fully control them... What if someone does everything right like Emily but still fails? And what if someone does something evil but proves it to be the most effective method?"

These questions continually drive Christian Alvart into a profound yet realistic world until a series of sudden events lead the story into a tragic surrealistic environment. Alvart said: "I always wanted 'Case 39' to have a realistic, brave, and resilient appearance."

[From Realism to Surrealism]

For Christian Alvart, pursuing realism doesn't mean abandoning his signature cinematic style, so he still uses a somewhat chaotic visual rhythm to build a mysterious and suspenseful world, creating a chilling influence. Alvart said: "I prefer to tightly control every thread of such films, so every physical element, including every prop, color combination, and every actor's movement, is designed to give the audience a sense of being there—a high-temperature processed realism style that leads people to places they may never know."

It was Christian Alvart's creative filmmaking methods, combined with the excellent structure of the "Case 39" script, that led two well-known producers, Kevin Misher and Steve Golin, to join the project. Misher said: "Alvart always has an uncanny intuition for similar story materials. When Golin and I met him for the first time, we felt an immediate connection. We all liked him and were captivated by his passion, focus, and unique perspective when talking about movies. Although he had never directed an English-language film before, his talent was already evident in 'Antibody.' His desire for topics related to suspense and thrillers could be described as 'greedy.' From the day he received the script, he already knew what kind of lens perspective he wanted to use to tell the entire story."

Before the pre-production preparations were finalized, Christian Alvart had already created a detailed storyboard, outlining a complete form of "Case 39" in his mind and adjusting every detail of each scene. He knew how to handle some exceptionally shocking moments—such as the oven scenes that make the audience extremely tense and sever the good relationship between Emily and Lily; and the unforgettable bathroom scene where countless bees attack Emily's psychologist friend Doug... Every element must achieve a certain balance carefully. Alvart stated: "We hope these scenes are not only scary but also create a disturbing influence beyond measure. On the basis of achieving the original purpose, they should not exceed the scope of a psychological thriller."

During the casting process, Christian Alvart mainly looked for actors who could create mystery, appropriate emotional atmospheres, anxiety, and longing. He said: "If the surreal elements in 'Case 39' are to work effectively, the characters must appear credible. Therefore, the actors I need must establish strong credibility for the bizarre events in the film and deepen the emotional connection with the audience. Fortunately, I found a group of excellent actors led by Renée Zellweger, Jodelle Ferland, and Ian McShane."

Renée Zellweger delivers a real and bold performance in "Case 39." Her previous works have proven her acting ability covers a wide range, including her Oscar-winning role in "Cold Mountain," her energetic criminal role in "Chicago," and her iconic comedy series "Bridget Jones's Diary"... In this film, Zellweger portrays a completely new nurturing "adoptive mother" image, struggling with despair as she realizes the little girl she thought she was saving is actually the source of all mystery, fear, and chaos.

Initially, for the character of Emily, Christian Alvart sought a physically stronger, more serious actress because she needed considerable skill and courage to make her terrifying environment believable. Alvart admitted: "Since I knew the main rhythm shift of the film depended on the actress's grasp of the entire story, I envisioned a woman with true strength and mature thinking, not a youthful beauty. Honestly, getting the assistance of someone as popular in Hollywood as Renée Zellweger was a pleasant surprise for all of us."
