10 Stories to Witness the Growth of Google's Search Business

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 10:57:07

Today, Google celebrates its 15th birthday with the launch of a new search algorithm called Hummingbird. As the cornerstone and foundation of Google's success, how has the search function evolved over the past 15 years? TNW has compiled ten "historical facts" related to Google Search.

Google Goes Live

First and foremost, the most important event in Google's search history is its founding. In 1998, founders Larry Page and Sergey Brin established Google in a garage in Menlo Park, California. The company’s name originates from the word "googol" (10 to the power of 100), symbolizing the infinite information on the web and Google's constructive approach to organizing it, helping users find answers.

Gaining Prominence, Thanks to Yahoo

Prior to 2000, Google was relatively unknown in the mainstream search market. Yahoo, an older search engine founded in 1994, was then the leader. Ironically, it was this leader that brought Google to center stage—Yahoo abandoned Inktomi and partnered with Google instead. Interestingly, times have changed; the former junior partner now holds the top position, and their relationship has soured.

Disputes in various fields were the catalysts for the breakup, including a patent dispute. In 2004, Google gave Yahoo 2.7 million shares of Class A stock in exchange for a patent from its subsidiary Overture, which Google later used to develop Google AdWords.

Making Search Monetizable through DIY Mode

In the early days, search engines like Lycos, Ask Jeeves, and Excite relied on banner ads on web pages for monetization—a practice popular in the late '90s but eventually ignored. This posed a challenge: how to place ads effectively and make them profitable.

In 2000, Google launched AdWords, initially serving 350 clients. This product was revolutionary—it eliminated the need for advertisers to communicate with content publishers, allowing advertisers to DIY, saving time, effort, and money. AdWords' personalization capabilities meant ads no longer appeared randomly but aligned with user searches. Google later increased revenue through an auction system where higher bids placed text ads in better positions. Yahoo and Microsoft quickly followed suit, changing Google's history—its total ad revenue in 2012 reached $42.5 billion.

Microsoft + Yahoo > Google?

Unhappy with Google's dominance in search, Yahoo and Microsoft teamed up in 2010 to counter Google—signing a search alliance agreement where Yahoo became the exclusive media for both companies' global premium ad businesses. How effective was their partnership? We don't know, but Yahoo extended its collaboration with Microsoft (starting April 1, 2012, for 12 months). Of course, after Marissa Mayer took over Yahoo, she stated that the partnership did not deliver the expected market share or revenue, so earlier this year, Yahoo and Microsoft parted ways, turning instead to an advertising partnership with Google.

Why Can’t My Website Rank Higher?

Many companies questioned whether Google used fraudulent means and preferential treatment to manipulate "PageRank." In March 2006, parenting site KinderStart sued Google for manipulating rankings, affecting its business. The company claimed Google violated the U.S. Second Amendment and Sherman Antitrust Act, constituting "religious and political discrimination," but U.S. District Judge Jeremy Fogel dismissed the lawsuit. Other companies also complained about Google allegedly manipulating rankings. In a September 2011 U.S. Senate Antitrust Subcommittee hearing, Yelp CEO Jeremy Stoppelman stated he had to be polite to Google because it was many users' gateway: "Google demands websites provide free content to benefit its competitive products. Then, Google gives its own products preferential treatment." Facing accusations that Google altered algorithms to boost its services’ rankings, Chairman Eric Schmidt publicly declared: "Senator, I assure you we have not tampered with anything."

The Rise of the Knowledge Graph

In today's era dominated by platforms and big data, it seems every major player needs a graph—Facebook has the social graph, Eventbrite has the event graph (Event Graph), LinkedIn is developing the economic graph (Economic Graph), and thus Google has the knowledge graph. Incorporating resources from the CIA World Factbook, Freebase, Wikipedia, and others, Google's knowledge graph resembles Ask Jeeves and Wolfram Alpha. Many believe it helps Google move closer to the semantic web, making it smarter—not just analyzing the literal content in the search box but understanding complex requests. It's estimated the knowledge graph contains over 570 million data points, mostly about people, places, and things, with 18 billion cross-references. Google has integrated this database into many of its services, including Google Now.

Is This the Picture You Were Looking For?

It seems the only way to find content online is by describing it in words, but what if you only have a picture and want more information about it? In 2011, Google introduced image search, allowing you to use your photo as a search query directly.

All you need to do is drag the image into Google's search bar to get relevant results.

Helping Third-Party Websites Make Money

In 2003, Google launched a new project called AdSense, breaking its previously closed ad system and involving third-party websites. Anyone could place these ads on their site to earn money, replacing their inefficient banner ads. Google handled all the details, giving advertisers the chance to appear on high-value sites with better targeting. Google uses AdSense across many services, including content, feeds, search, mobile, domains, and video. In 2012, AdSense generated $2.88 billion in revenue (27% of the company's total).

Monopolistic Google?

No one would be surprised to say Google dominates the search market. The U.S. government almost sued Google. In 2013, the company signed an agreement with the Federal Trade Commission to avoid litigation, ending a two-year investigation into the company's search activities.

Competitors like Microsoft and Yelp petitioned the government for an antitrust lawsuit against Google, but thanks to the aforementioned agreement, Google avoided the trouble Microsoft faced in the 1990s.

According to comScore, in July 2013, Google held a 67% search market share, up 0.3 percentage points month-over-month. Bing from Microsoft trailed behind at 17.9%.

Search in Motion

Among all the new tools, products, devices, and services Google has launched, one fact is crystal clear: all user data enters the company's vast search algorithm, enabling better search results.

Google was one of the first companies to bring search to mobile devices, allowing users to access Google Now on Android devices or Chrome apps. As Senior Vice President Amit Singhal mentioned today, when Google first started, you could only search via desktop. If you were walking and wanted quick information like movie showtimes, maps, or background info, there was no good solution (now there is). Users love Google's mobile search service; according to eMarketer in August, nearly 1/5 of Google's U.S. ad revenue comes from mobile search. In short, mobile services create a win-win-win situation for consumers, advertisers, and Google itself.

Search has evolved from a simple text box into a platform driven by the knowledge graph, integrating Gmail, YouTube, Google Books, G+, Docs, and Maps. Of course, while collecting data, Google mustn't forget its motto: Don't Be Evil.
