Series of Q&A on Earthworm Farming

Q: Methods for collecting earthworms

Answer from the Hunan Earthworm Farming Base of Jindi Agriculture:

Earthworms reproduce very quickly and need to be harvested in time. Earthworms have the habit of not living with their grandchildren, so if adult worms are not harvested in time, they will escape. Raising worms of different sizes together can also lead to inbreeding, causing the worm stock to degenerate. Therefore, when the adult worms grow up and a large number of young worms have hatched, about 20,000 per square meter, timely harvesting should be carried out without delay; otherwise, it will have various impacts on earthworm farming.

There are many methods for harvesting earthworms, and measures should be taken according to the farming method, site, facilities, as well as the earthworm's fear of water and light. Here are some harvesting methods for reference:

1. Earthworm Attraction Method: Earthworms like sour, sweet, and smelly foods. Place rotten tomatoes, apples, watermelon peels, or sprinkle sugar water, boiled sweet potato water mixed with cow dung, or fish washing water on the surface of the breeding base. Earthworms have a keen sense of smell and will crawl out of the soil in large numbers to eat, allowing them to be collected.

2. Direct Collection from Earthworm Beds: This method is suitable for shallow pit farming (pits about 0.5 meters deep). If worms of different sizes are raised together, first separate the upper layer containing eggs, then evenly loosen and move the feed to one side, and the earthworms will drill down. If there are multiple pits arranged together, alternate layers can be processed one by one, greatly improving labor efficiency. If raising worms of different sizes, ensure an appropriate amount of reserve worms, and do a good job of adding feed, watering, and covering. In good weather, harvesting can be done every 20 days. The advantage of this method is that all procedures for extracting earthworms can be completed within the breeding pit; the disadvantage is that it is difficult to take small ones and leave large ones if raising worms of different sizes; this method is best for three-group feeding.

3. Earthworm Box Turning Method (Suitable for indoor farming): Utilize the earthworm's phototaxis by moving the boxes to sunlight or lamp light, making the earthworms completely descend, then remove the boxes, concentrate the earthworms on the ground surface, and quickly collect them.

4. Earthworm Screening Method: Make two identical sieves with different mesh sizes, one 3mm and the other 1mm. Fold them together with hinges, the top sieve having larger holes and the bottom sieve smaller holes. Pour the earthworms, worm castings, and feed into the sieve. Place the sieve under sunlight or light (preferably blue or ultraviolet light, as earthworms fear these most), allowing the earthworms to pass through the sieve holes onto the fine sieve, while small worms fall through the fine sieve holes into the container below. What remains on the sieve is worm castings and soil. This method has low labor intensity and is suitable for indoor use.

5. Earthworm Sieving Method: Pour the earthworms and worm castings from the bed onto a sieve with 3mm holes, vibrate it back and forth, screen the worm castings and eggs into the container below, then harvest the adult worms remaining on the sieve.

6. Earthworm Plow Method: Nail 1-2 inch nails onto a wooden board, slightly larger than the steel brush used on construction sites to remove rust, attach a handle, and use the homemade hand plow to gently loosen the feed, forcing the earthworms to drill downward. Then scrape off the upper layer of worm castings layer by layer until the earthworms remaining at the bottom of the bed can be concentrated and harvested.

7. Water Submersion Method: Earthworms fear being submerged in water. Take advantage of this habit by flooding the breeding pit or rack, filling the lower 2/3 of the feed with water and leaving the top 1/3 exposed. The earthworms will quickly float to the surface of the feed where they can be concentrated and scraped off.

Q: What is the prospect of earthworm farming?

Answer from the Hunan Earthworm Farming Base of Jindi Agriculture:

A paper mill in Japan imported 125 tons of earthworms from the U.S. to clear paper pulp residue. Previously, this factory spent $400,000 annually to clear the residue. After introducing these earthworms, selling the worm castings allowed the factory to recoup the cost of purchasing the earthworms within a year. Japan has a type of breeding box measuring 0.5 square meters and 30-50 cm deep, each capable of housing 1,000 earthworms. After two years, these can proliferate to 1 billion worms daily consuming 50 tons of paper waste and producing 25 tons of worm castings. Japan now has over 200 large and small earthworm farms, with Kyushu and Hokkaido being the most developed. A major earthworm farm in Hyogo Prefecture raises 1 billion earthworms annually, processing 60,000 tons of sludge from food and fiber processing plants. In the U.S., a worm production factory uses crushed household garbage fed into biogas tanks to produce large amounts of biogas for market use. After biogas production, the residue is used to breed earthworms, all of which are exported. Each kilogram sells for $15, and the worm castings are sold to flower companies for $40-100 per ton, truly achieving the goal of turning waste into treasure and generating significant economic and social benefits.

Earthworms can indeed be used in pharmaceuticals, with current international market prices for dry earthworms ranging from 80,000 to 150,000 yuan per ton or even higher. Raising earthworms not only handles animal manure but also processes garbage, purifies the environment, and accumulates fertilizer. Earthworms turn garbage into fertilizer. Countries such as Japan, the U.S., Canada, Myanmar, India, the Philippines, Australia, and West Germany use earthworms to process garbage, purify the environment, eliminate public nuisances, and manufacture organic fertilizers. Some countries have developed earthworm farming to the level of industrial farming and commercial production.

(A reminder: Some breeding projects claim they will buy back products at high prices. 99.9% of these people are scammers. When you try to sell your products to them, they may claim your product is substandard or simply disappear.)

The materials for earthworm farming are very diverse and particularly cheap, and the value of the farmed earthworms increases significantly. The technology is not complicated, and more and more people are seeing the potential of earthworm farming, with a very broad future.
