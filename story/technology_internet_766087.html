Why Nokia Failed: Six Key Lessons to Learn

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 15:02:30

When Microsoft acquired Nokia's mobile phone business for $7.17 billion and Nokia was ready to write a new chapter in mobile networks and maps, there was no shortage of optimism for the future.

Facing the drop from Nokia's market value of 303 billion euros in 2000 to its sale price of $7.17 billion, there is always some curiosity and responsibility that leads people to look back at the process by which Nokia’s mobile phones declined from their peak. Summarizing various reasons why Nokia "the more successful it became, the more it failed," perhaps offers some reference value for other companies.

Below are several lessons identified by Tencent Technology:

1. Slow action, missing the opportunity for smartphones

No company can be a perpetual winner. When transitioning from analog phones to 2G, Nokia surpassed Motorola. With the advent of the 3G smartphone era, Nokia should have been aware of the possibility of being overtaken.

But it didn't.

Nokia, sitting on its position as the global leader in mobile phones, looked down on other manufacturers. In 2007, when the iPhone arrived, Nokia mocked it, saying Steve Jobs first needed to convert brand awareness into market share.

Then Android phones came, Samsung and HTC rose due to Android, and the touch screen era arrived. Nokia still clung to Symbian and physical phone buttons.

As a result, Nokia's market share dropped from over 40% in 2008 to 25% in 2011, subsequently being overtaken by Samsung.

2. Afflicted by the mentality of being the leader, unwilling to ally with new operating system players

To this day, Nokia's alignment with the Windows Phone platform is a foregone conclusion. Previously, there were voices fantasizing about Nokia making Android phones. If Nokia's market share was declining, they could always make a comeback by leveraging Android, given their excellent hardware capabilities.

But they didn’t. Nokia's rationale for choosing an operating system was whether it could become their leader, consistent with their mindset as the industry leader. In Nokia's view, choosing the Android system would mean being Google's largest contract manufacturer at best, working for Google. Choosing Microsoft, however, allowed them to build another mobile ecosystem.

However, from 2011 to 2013, the Windows Phone ecosystem consistently lagged behind other systems, leaving Nokia in a difficult situation.

3. Indecisive strategy, constantly starting from scratch

While being targeted by numerous smartphone manufacturers vying for market share, Nokia should have fully utilized its advantages, found a direction, and firmly followed it.

But it didn't.

Symbian was no longer suitable for the development of the smartphone era. Except for Apple, almost all phone manufacturers turned to the Android platform. Nokia chose to collaborate with Intel to start anew with Meego, only to abandon it shortly after. All previous investments went to waste.

Within a week of launching the N9 based on Meego, Stephen Elop clearly responded that despite the significant impact of the N9, Nokia would still abandon MeeGo and focus entirely on developing Windows Phone.

In early 2011, Nokia formed an alliance with Microsoft and comprehensively transitioned to the Windows Phone platform, essentially starting from scratch again. Even the best foundation can't withstand constant upheaval.

4. Clinging to traditional thinking, closed strategies leading to the failure of mobile internet strategy

In 2007, Nokia was the first globally to launch the Ovi store, a year before Apple's App Store. Subsequently, Google launched Google Play, and the success of Apple and Google's app stores proved that one shouldn't try to dominate the entire industrial chain but instead embrace openness and collaboration.

But Nokia didn't.

Contrary to the open platforms built by Apple and Google, Nokia's transition to the internet was more like a vertical integration aimed at the internet. To launch location-based services, Nokia spent heavily to acquire navigation software companies, mapping companies, and even related operational websites, but this model of dominating the entire industrial chain did not seem to enhance Nokia's competitiveness in the internet world. After spending $15 billion, Nokia's Ovi strategy ended in failure.

5. Always underestimating the market, massive R&D investment failing to convert into productivity

As early as 2004, Nokia internally developed touch technology and even the now-popular 3D technology. Reviewing Nokia's financial reports, Nokia's R&D expenses in 2010 were approximately 5.8 billion euros, more than four times that of Apple. Nokia had the most extensive R&D resources, which should have been converted into weapons on the battlefield.

But it didn't.

They always said, "This market is too small, no one will buy it, it costs too much..." Not until a year after the iPhone was launched did Nokia release its first touch-screen phone. By this time, Nokia had already mastered touch-screen technology for several years.

6. Poor personnel management, American executives managing a European-style company is inherently risky

Nokia is a Finnish company. In September 2010, American executive Stephen Elop took the helm of Nokia, becoming the first non-Finnish "leader," which itself was a glamorous adventure.

In a team full of aristocratic flair, slightly introverted European style, the more passionate and assertive Elop required time to integrate with the team. At the time, some insiders predicted that Elop's networking and software experience would add insult to injury for Nokia.

Indeed.

After he took office, Nokia's market capitalization fell from 30 billion euros in 2010 to 10 billion euros in the spring of this year. Since February 2011, when Nokia announced its partnership with Microsoft, the company's stock price has fallen by more than 50%.

Stephen Elop, Nokia's current CEO, comes from Microsoft. After taking office, he continuously implemented pro-Microsoft strategies, leading to strategic vacillation, suspicions of being a mole, and a lot of distrust within Nokia towards Elop. Some shareholders even initiated Plan B, demanding that the board expel Elop.

Now, after the deal between Nokia and Microsoft is completed, Elop will return to Microsoft, directly reporting to Ballmer. Everyone laments the fate of Nokia, except for Elop, whose smile remains unchanged.
