Apple: Adherence to High-end and Innovation, an Advantage over Android

by anonymous on 2013-09-24 22:19:36

Apple's Three Titans

Introduction: In this week's Bloomberg Businessweek article, it is noted that Apple’s recently launched new iPhones, especially the iPhone 5c, have sparked controversy. Following the product launch, several Apple executives, including CEO Tim Cook and industrial design chief Jonathan Ive, gave interviews discussing the new iPhones as well as Apple's strategies and market share.

The following is the full article:

Sticking to the High-End Route

Apple is not immune to its eventual demise. This is what people have been hearing and seeing lately. Indeed, Apple has had its moments of glory with products like the iPhone and iPad, but all of this is about to come to an abrupt end. If you want proof, look at China: the world's largest smartphone market, flooded with cheap phones and tablets from local manufacturers — products that didn't even exist before Apple launched its first-generation iPhone. You might think these affordable phones and tablets are confined to the Chinese market, but that's not true; China is becoming the epicenter for declining global phone prices. Competition isn't limited to domestic Chinese brands but also includes all manufacturers using Google's Android system. And how is Apple performing? Compared to its peak a year ago, Apple's stock price has dropped by approximately 33%.

This hasn't troubled Apple CEO Tim Cook. In an interview, he said, "I don't get excited when Apple's stock goes up, nor do I get anxious when it goes down. I've experienced many such roller-coaster ups and downs." Regarding the rise of low-cost phone makers, he calmly stated, "Every market I've seen behaves this way. All consumer electronics industries go through this — from digital cameras to PCs, to tablets, and then to phones. Going back even further, there were VCRs and DVDs. I don't know of any consumer electronic product that doesn't go through this."

Cook, aged 52, was seated in a sunny meeting room in Apple's main headquarters building during the interview, wearing a polo shirt on top, black pants below, and frameless glasses. It was just two days after the release of the iPhone 5s and iPhone 5c, and upon entering the meeting room, the first thing he wanted to know was my opinion on these two phones.

Cook believes that competition in the phone market isn't linear but divergent. One path leads to low-end commoditization. Cook remarked, "There's always a large demand for junk in the market, but we don't participate in that business." The other path leads to high-end products, which may be more expensive but offer higher value. Cook said, "There's another segment of the market that wants products that add more value to their lives, and those are the customers I hope to win over. For the performance in other segments of the market, I won't lose sleep over them because they're not our markets. Fortunately, both markets are large, with a significant number of users wanting great user experiences from their phones or tablets, and that's what Apple truly aims to deliver."

For long-time Apple fans, this is a familiar argument. As far back as 2004, Apple co-founder Steve Jobs, responding to similar doubts, said, "Apple's market share is greater than BMW, Mercedes-Benz, and Porsche in the car market. What's wrong with being BMW or Mercedes?" Now, six years after launching the first iPhone, Apple is no longer the sole dominant force in the phone market, facing strong challenges from Samsung, Motorola Mobility (Google), Nokia (Microsoft), Xiaomi, and Indian manufacturer Micromax. Is Apple's commitment to the high-end route a sustainable strategy, or merely a delay tactic?

Internal Collaboration

In the past year, Apple has become unusually active. They added the iPad Mini, released the iPhone 5s extending into the iPhone 5c, and launched iOS 7. Moreover, Apple reshuffled its upper management; Scott Forstall, former senior vice president responsible for iOS software, left and was replaced by Jonathan Ive and Craig Federighi.

The collaboration between Ive and Federighi is crucial for developing the great experience Cook envisions. Ive, aged 46, is a design genius already famous worldwide. His talents have been showcased in the iMac, iPod, iPhone, and iPad. Federighi, aged 44, worked alongside Jobs at NeXT. During the interview, Ive and Federighi sat in a meeting room on the first floor of Apple headquarters. Ive is of medium height with a shaved head. Federighi is taller, with slightly gray hair. Both are articulate, diligent, and carry the DNA that Jobs instilled in Apple.

Their formal collaboration began last autumn, but they had worked together for many years prior. Ive said, "We've never discussed our roles; instead, we've talked about how to take our current collaboration to the next level." Collaboration isn't just about making people feel comfortable. When a company faces external attacks, internal conflicts must be avoided. Ive said, "You might think that successful collaboration means your views are the most valuable and ultimately become the leading direction. But that's not collaboration."

What makes their collaboration run smoothly is that both executives focus on usability and simplicity. Conversations with Samsung's mobile executives might revolve around how their company listens to the market and quickly addresses global needs. In contrast, Ive and Federighi could spend ten minutes discussing how to perfect the blurred background effect in iOS 7. When asked what users expect when using a product, Ive said, "Don't let irrelevant content distract you. We truly focus on how users feel when they care about something."

Currently, critics argue that Apple's pace of innovation has slowed, but neither Ive nor Federighi agrees. These executives not only refute the claim by pointing out new features in their products but also highlight deep integrations between them. For instance, with the fingerprint scanner in the iPhone 5s, Ive said, "There are so many problems that need solving to achieve this feature." Although they didn't mention competitor Samsung directly, both executives believe that some so-called innovations in the market are illusory.

Two Obstacles

Two current trends are hindering Apple's growth: the rise of Android and the decline in product prices. On the Android front, Google's free platform continues to dominate the global mobile operating system market. IDC data shows that nearly 80% of smartphones and almost two-thirds of tablets run on Android. Additionally, starting last year, the unsubsidized average selling price of smartphones fell from $450 to $375, while the iPhone 5s is priced at $650. Analyst Charles Golvin from Forrester Research said, "The current situation is that Apple is not lagging behind in the phone market, but other manufacturers are benefiting significantly in the low-end market."

Regarding Apple's newly launched lower-end iPhone 5c, the focus has been on its price. While no one expects the iPhone 5c to match the prices of low-end smartphones in the Chinese market, it shouldn't be excessively high either; around $300 would be appropriate. Golvin said, "In developing countries, many consumers haven't bought iPhones. If the iPhone 5c is cheap enough, they will likely purchase it enthusiastically."

Unfortunately, the iPhone 5c is not priced at $300 but at $550, only $100 less than the high-end iPhone 5s. Cook explained, "We never intended to sell a cheap phone; our primary goal was to sell a great phone that offers a great user experience. With the iPhone 5c, we simply developed such a product at a lower cost."

In countries like China, price reductions must be noticeable to make an impact. For Apple, the Chinese market cannot be ignored, which is why Apple has already partnered with China Unicom and China Telecom and is currently negotiating with China Mobile. With over 700 million users, China Mobile's subscriber base is more than three times that of AT&T and Verizon Wireless combined. Although Chinese carriers also provide subsidies for the iPhone 5s and iPhone 5c, the subsidized prices remain much higher than local brands. Analyst Benedict Evans from Enders Analysis said, "This raises the question: does Apple really want to become a mass-market brand? Or does it prefer to maintain a 40% share in the U.S. market, a third in Europe, and nothing elsewhere?"

Meanwhile, Android's market share continues to grow as more manufacturers adopt the platform, including high-end products like the Samsung Galaxy S4. Horace Dediu, analyst at Finnish research firm Asymco, said, "What has surprised Apple and everyone else is how fast Android has grown."

The rapid development of Android is significant. Currently, developers tend to create apps first for iOS and then for Android. However, if Android continues to expand, the order of app development could reverse. Data from App Annie shows that in the second quarter of this year, Apple's App Store revenue was 2.3 times that of Google Play, but Google Play had a higher number of app downloads.

Tero Kuittinen, analyst at Alekstra, said, "The real issue in the mobile app market is that its volatile trend changes far exceed expectations. From a certain perspective, growth is more important than the current scale. Therefore, even though Apple currently provides more revenue to developers, if Android grows faster than Apple, developers will shift to Google Play."

On Android's Advantage

However, Cook said, "Compared to before, the trend of two sharing the mobile platform market is now more evident. But if you look at customer satisfaction and usage, you'll find that Android still lags far behind iOS."

Cook continued, "Customers may buy an Android phone, but the back cover of that phone bears Apple's logo." According to NetMarketShare data, nearly 55% of mobile internet activities come from iOS devices, while Android accounts for only 28%. IBM data shows that on last year's "Black Friday," 88% of online shopping traffic from all tablets came from iPads. Cook said, "If devices aren't used, what good is a higher shipment volume? We focus on users using our products. We truly want to enrich people's lives, but putting products in drawers won't accomplish that."

Talking about Android fragmentation, Cook said, "I don't see Android as a single product." Unlike Apple, which has only one iOS system regularly updated, Android has multiple versions. Although Google releases new versions, there's no guarantee users can upgrade to the latest version. Because the Android systems users use have been customized by phone manufacturers or mobile operators, upgrades are often delayed.

Google says that 45% of users are using the latest Android version, "Jelly Bean," but 31% are still using "Gingerbread" introduced at the end of 2010, and another 22% are using "Ice Cream Sandwich" released in October 2011. In comparison, Apple claims that by the end of June this year, 93% of users were using iOS 6 (Apple just released iOS 7 on September 18).

In Cook's view, the incompatibility between different Android versions makes each version seem like a completely different product. He thinks, "Android is often not the latest version when users buy the phone." A recent survey showed that among 25 Android phones sold by AT&T, six did not use the latest system version. Cook said, "When users stop using this phone, the operating system version of the phone has a history of 3 to 4 years. It's like the device in my pocket is still using iOS 3, and I can't imagine that."

Fragmentation complicates the situation, referred to by Cook as a "combination problem." He said, "Problems arise among developers and users who cannot use a particular app. This will also bring information security issues because if you cannot push users to upgrade to the latest version, then you need to take care of previous products and fill their vulnerabilities. Actually, this kind of work cannot be well done." In response, Google spokesperson Gina Scigliano replied via email, "The measures we take are protecting users."

You might think that Apple's approach in the mobile field ignores history, especially the battles between Mac and Windows in the '90s, where Apple was the loser. Currently, Android is equivalent to Windows in the mobile field, used by almost all hardware manufacturers, while iOS only appears on Apple's devices. Microsoft earns income by charging Dell and HP license fees for Windows, while Apple's market share is squeezed. Google provides Android to hardware manufacturers for free, but Google's logic is that if more people access the Internet via mobile devices, more people will search, consume online content, which will drive online advertising business. Since Google is an online advertising giant, such development will help Google's profitability. Cook believes that comparing Android with Windows will mislead: "Microsoft ensures the consistency of Windows, and the fragmentation problem is not obvious. There aren't that many versions of Windows in use simultaneously in the market."

Innovation Reigns Supreme

Ironically, Apple's competitors are imitating the company, integrating hardware and software. In 2011, Google acquired Motorola Mobility and began designing Android phone hardware independently. Earlier this month, Microsoft announced it would acquire Nokia's mobile phone business for about $7.2 billion. Regarding Microsoft's deal, Cook said, "Everyone is trying to adopt Apple's strategy. We don't seek validation of this strategy outside of Apple, but I think it indicates that imitation of this strategy is widespread, and people have realized its importance."

If Nokia's story is on one hand a confirmation, then on the other hand it serves as a warning. When Apple entered the mobile phone industry, it was Nokia's domain. Nokia was then considered magical. Tero Kuittinen of Alekstra recalled, "I remember the era when Nokia had a 25% profit margin, and their leadership position seemed impossible to shake." Cook pointed out, "I think Nokia reminded everyone in the business world that you need to keep innovating, and those who fail to innovate will eventually perish."

"Innovate or perish," this phrase has become more than a slogan for Cook; it is a topic frequently repeated by Apple fans and analysts alike. Since Steve Jobs returned to Apple in 1996, Apple has not only invented new products but also perfected existing ones, creating new markets, thereby achieving rapid growth. Apple did not develop the first music player, nor did it manufacture the first smartphone or tablet, but Apple made these products magical, prompting users to line up overnight to buy them.

Apple is maintaining its own rhythm of development, making it difficult for other companies to keep up. Regardless of whether users like it or not, every time Cook stands on stage to announce a new product that is less impressive, the world, at least Wall Street, responds with disappointment. On the day of the new iPhone launch, Apple's stock price fell by 5%, and since the beginning of the year, it has cumulatively dropped by 10%. Cook said, "Am I happy about it? No, it's impossible. But you need to return to this question: 'Are you doing the right thing?' That's what I focus on, and I won't let anyone else or the market decide my feelings."
