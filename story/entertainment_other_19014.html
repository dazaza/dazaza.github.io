Comic Fans Comment on Spiderman vs Superman [Pic] -- Social News - Oriental Net

by xiaopang on 2007-05-10 13:44:05

Can you imagine Spider-Man battling Superman? One is a mutant who can effortlessly spin webs and swing between the steel and concrete of urban landscapes, while the other is an alien visitor with invincible strength, capable of feats both in the heavens and on earth. If these two were to clash, the spectacle would surely make the heavens themselves pale in comparison.

As the Hollywood blockbuster "Spider-Man 3" is being globally released simultaneously, our newspaper has specially planned this "imaginative news" titled "Spider-Man vs Superman," inviting two long-time enthusiasts of the Spider-Man and Superman series to analyze the various strengths and weaknesses of each superhero in a hypothetical showdown. In this battle of the ages, who will ultimately dominate?

**Round 1: Natural Abilities**

**No.1 Strength: Superman could throw an entire continent into outer space; Spider-Man struggles to save a derailed train.**

Quan Liang (referred to as "Quan"): Superman was born with god-like strength, capable of easily lifting an entire continent and tossing it into outer space. After his mutation, Spider-Man gained extraordinary abilities, but his physical strength remains relatively limited. Recall from the second movie when Spider-Man had to risk his life to save passengers from a derailed train - something trivial for Superman, who could likely handle it with just one finger.

MkGenie (referred to as "M"): Without a doubt, Superman wins here. Let's be honest, he's an alien, far above us mortals, almost akin to a deity. How could our Earth-born Spider-Man possibly compare? As the saying goes, "with great power comes great responsibility." So Superman roams the world as a savior, while Spider-Man at best acts like a neighborhood watchman in New York.

**No.2 Speed: By the time Spider-Man throws out his left web, Superman has already circled the Earth several times.**

Quan: Zipping through the urban jungle of New York, Spider-Man is known for his speed. In "Spider-Man 3," during his fight with Sandman and Venom alongside the Green Goblin, Spider-Man showcases his high-speed agility perfectly. However, compared to Superman, who can circle the Earth multiple times in the blink of an eye, traveling distances measured in light-years, Spider-Man's speed is practically akin to comparing arrows to rockets.

M: Superman's speed is so fast that he can even reverse time. Can Spider-Man do that? Likely not. By the time Spider-Man throws out his left web, Superman might have already circled the Earth several times and returned. Of course, excessive speed isn't always advantageous; many sights along the way are missed, and direct flight lacks aesthetic appeal compared to Spider-Man's nimble movements. Most importantly, Superman must pray not to collide with a Kryptonite wall mid-flight, as stopping in time might prove impossible.

**No.3 Agility: Superman doesn't bother dodging bullets, but in combat, he's no match for Spider-Man's agility.**

Quan: Just think about how deftly spiders catch mosquitoes, and you'll understand Spider-Man's agility. In "Spider-Man 3," his response to Green Goblin's attacks shows his all-around awareness. On the other hand, Superman ignores bullets hitting him directly in the eyes, as long as they're not made of Kryptonite, showing little need for evasive maneuvers.

M: Superman's ability to hear six directions at once has been showcased in "Superman Returns," where he listens for trouble on Earth from outer space before flying to resolve it. Even when Lois Lane whispers behind his back, he hears her more clearly than anyone else. However, in practical combat scenarios, despite his incredible speed and strength, Superman lacks the refined agility of Spider-Man, making him inferior in this aspect.

**No.4 Recovery Ability: Spider-Man has never been hospitalized, whereas Superman spent days recovering.**

Quan: Spider-Man frequently faces new enemies and injuries, which have become routine for him. Fortunately, his recovery abilities are exceptional; whether beaten by Doctor Octopus or struck by Sandman's massive fists, he quickly regains mobility. Superman, on the other hand, has no natural enemies except Kryptonite.

M: Superman's constitution is unrealistically strong. Yet, Spider-Man can proudly claim that despite being relentlessly pursued by opponents, he's never been hospitalized. Conversely, in "Superman Returns," after being stabbed with Kryptonite, Superman had to recuperate in the hospital for several days, perhaps the most embarrassing memory of his life. Both have lost their superpowers in their respective sequels, reverting to normal human form. Peter Parker still managed to rescue a Chinese girl from a burning house without his powers, while Superman was brutally beaten bloody in a bar, an undignified moment indeed.

**Round 2: Weapons and Equipment**

**No.1 Costume: Spider-Man's suit gets torn often, while Superman's design feels outdated.**

Quan: Spider-Man's costume design is more practical. As a superhero, fighting is inevitable, and Spider-Man's suit fits snugly, free of any hindrance, making it perfect for brawls, rescues, and emergencies. Additionally, though his suit often gets torn up in battles, it somehow looks brand new shortly afterward. Given the lack of scenes showing him changing or repairing his suit, it’s speculated either he has multiple suits or the suit itself has self-repairing capabilities. In contrast, Superman's outfit falls short, especially his infamous "underwear outside pants" style, which has long been criticized. His cape has also been mocked as something that could get sucked into airplane engines. It's no wonder that capes belong to the 80s, and Superman needs to modernize.

M: At the end of the day, both wear tight-fitting outfits designed to make women drool. However, Spider-Man was the first superhero to complain about discomfort with his costume. In the second movie, he explicitly tells someone in the elevator that "the crotch area itches!" Overall, Superman's suit seems to be of better quality, rarely getting as tattered as Spider-Man's.

**No.2 Weapons: Superman's whole body is a weapon; Spider-Man's webs seem insignificant.**

Quan: Spider-Man's primary weapon is his webbing, which can form nets or ball-shaped projectiles. Without it, Spider-Man is helpless, as seen in the second movie when his web-shooters malfunctioned during a bank battle with Doctor Octopus, resulting in severe beatings. Superman's body itself is a weapon; his eyes can emit powerful laser beams, and his ears capture information instantly. No matter how advanced weapons others may use, Superman remains largely unaffected. Practically speaking, Spider-Man's webs are small fry compared to Superman's arsenal.

M: Both primarily rely on brute force. Webs, in my opinion, are merely tools.

**Round 3: Fatal Weaknesses**

Children and innocents can disrupt Spider-Man, but only Kryptonite affects Superman.

Quan: Heroes are human too. Spider-Man's weakness lies in his compassionate heart. Instead of engaging in direct combat, villains could target children and the weak, exploiting Spider-Man's distraction to strike decisively. In the second movie, Doctor Octopus drained Spider-Man's energy using a runaway train, capturing him effortlessly. Superman's sole weakness is Kryptonite; even holding Lois Lane and her child hostage wouldn't significantly affect him unless Kryptonite is involved.

M: To defeat Spider-Man, attack his spirit first - as long as Mary Jane lives, he'll protect her like Athena, making him nearly indestructible. For Superman, only Kryptonite works, as dull as Achilles' heel.

**Round 4: Romantic Appeal**

Spider-Man chooses love over glory; Superman sacrifices romance for Earth.

Quan: Spider-Man's love triangle with Mary Jane and Harry Osborn (Green Goblin) is complicated enough, let alone adding Gwen Stacy and Eddie Brock in the third installment. Torn between love and friendship, Spider-Man struggles to decide. He appears quite inexperienced in matters of the heart. Superman handles emotions with unwavering loyalty, remaining steadfast toward Lois Lane for years, even across light-years.

M: Regardless of romantic turmoil, both superheroes remain devoted in their movies. Mary Jane represents everything to Spider-Man; every episode starts with thoughts of her and ends with her rescue. Even kissing someone who looks like his aunt doesn’t deter him, showcasing his unique taste. In the second movie, hearing Mary Jane was getting married, he abandoned his core belief of "with great power comes great responsibility," losing all motivation and quitting as Spider-Man, revealing his deep selfish nature, placing love above the lives of common people. Superman, however, remains gentlemanly, ready to sacrifice love for Earth's salvation, for finding his homeland, or for others' happiness—a realm Spider-Man could never reach, no matter how long he learned.

**Round 5: Career Potential**

Spider-Man earns money through self-taken exclusive photos; Superman is a top employee.

Quan: Setting aside their superhero identities, Peter Parker is a student who takes photographs part-time for the Daily Bugle, while Clark Kent is a renowned journalist for the Daily Planet. Currently, the latter seems less successful than the former, but future potential is hard to gauge.

M: One is a permanent staff member, the other a freelancer—clearly different levels. Clark Kent types incredibly fast, uses his omnidirectional senses to uncover the strongest news leaks, and can reach news sites in seconds, making him limitless potential and a top employee in any boss's eyes. Poor Spider-Man relies on selling photos, constantly exploited by his editor, appearing rather pitiful. Spider-Man's journalistic peak occurred in the third movie after transforming into the Black Spider, becoming so confident he could put his feet on the desk talking to his boss, doubling his asking price, destroying competition, and even flirting with the secretary—truly impressive.

--- **Revealing Secrets ---**

Spider-Man and Superman clashed as early as 1976.

In the early 1970s, screenwriter David Obst suggested creating a "Guan Yu vs Qin Qiong"-style movie, pitting Marvel's Spider-Man against DC's Superman. However, since Warner Bros. was planning the first Superman movie, instead, the two comic companies collaborated on an oversized comic book titled "Superman vs The Amazing Spider-Man: The Greatest Battle of All Time," published in 1976. In the story, due to misunderstandings, the two heroes engaged in fierce combat. Spider-Man's suit was hit by Lex Luthor's special ray gun, neutralizing much of Superman's powers, leading to an evenly matched fight where Spider-Man initially gained the upper hand with his agility and strength. When Superman regained his full power, he broke Spider-Man's arms, forcing his surrender. Only then did they calm down, clear the misunderstanding, and join forces against their old enemies—Lex Luthor and Doctor Octopus—to rescue their kidnapped girlfriends.
