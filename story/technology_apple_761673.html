WeChat, please copy the iPhone!

by geekzhang on 2013-05-06 13:38:51

There is such a saying: an article about Tencent that doesn't mention the word "copycat" is embarrassing. So goes the phrase, "No copycat, no Tencent." One, it means that Tencent is very familiar with this way of doing things; two, it proves to a large extent that copying has achieved great success for Tencent, and the market also accepts this approach. As a giant in China's Internet industry, being labeled as such, the pulse of the times, commercial strategies, and Chinese characteristics are worth savoring. Later, WeChat came along, and everyone said Tencent had finally innovated, finally raising its head and letting out a breath.

However, the newly empowered Tencent is not at ease. Now, the number of WeChat users could have already exceeded 400 million, and the issue of operator charges has been causing quite a stir. Commercialization is imminent, but international expansion remains nowhere on the horizon. What's going on? In one word, chaos. Operators have muddled calculations, third parties are swarming with various schemes, users are easily stirred up and love to join in the commotion, and perhaps internally at Tencent, they've already lost their footing.

Optimists believe that the old order has been disrupted, and a new order awaits establishment. Pessimists think that disorder equals loss of control, which is very dangerous. How should WeChat handle things? A non-copycat Tencent seems to have lost its direction. Why not continue copying? Copying by Tencent is a strategic style, steady and solid; it’s about careful planning, assessing the situation; it's about taking the essence and discarding the dross; it's a winning formula, always effective. The key question is, who should WeChat copy, and how should it copy? That's a technical skill.

Why iPhone?

In the article "WeChat-like APPs, the Third Major Entry Point of Mobile Internet?" a contributor to Huxiu mentioned that WeChat-like apps have the potential to become another entry point of mobile internet after app stores and mobile browsers. Zhang Xiangdong, president of GO Desktop of 3G Portal, also recently wrote an article titled "Facebook Home启示：争夺浅入口" (Facebook Home Enlightenment: Competing for Shallow Entry Points), stating that application stores, browsers, super apps, and mobile desktops are the four major entry points of mobile internet, naming WeChat as potentially becoming an entry point as a super app. It's clear that expectations for WeChat are incredibly high. Tencent shouldn't undervalue itself; it must fully embrace the positioning of WeChat as an entry point.

Since the positioning is an entry point, the object to be copied must necessarily be of this heavyweight class. Counting the few entries or potential entries in mobile internet, the most mature among them is the app store. Among app stores, Apple's App Store far surpasses Amazon's app store and Google Play in revenue generation capabilities. Data from American mobile analytics company Flurry shows that the per-user revenue of Apple's App Store is five times that of Google's Google Play. According to Canalys statistics, in the first quarter, among the four major application platforms (Google Play, Microsoft Windows Phone, BlackBerry World, App Store), Apple accounted for 74% of application revenue. Analysts pointed out that although Google's download volume data looks better, Apple users are more willing to purchase content and spend money on high-end applications. Based on Tencent's previous style of choosing objects to copy, earning capacity is absolutely a key indicator, so the object that WeChat needs to copy has surfaced - it's the iPhone.

As for WeChat versus iPhone, both are based on mobile internet, both start with communication, similar in that the iPhone has an App Store and WeChat has official accounts, and both founders are dedicated and committed. Differences include the iPhone being a combination of software and hardware based on the iOS system, with the founder being the CEO of Apple, while WeChat is an app applicable to any phone system, with the founder being Tencent's vice president responsible for the Guangzhou R&D department. So, how should WeChat copy the iPhone? There are three key points, explained as follows.

Zhang Xiaolong needs to transform into Steve Jobs

Having read some reports about Zhang Xiaolong, he is generally described as a very calm and humble person, vastly different from Steve Jobs' fiery temper. However, calmness and fiery temperament are just appearances; Zhang Xiaolong needs to transform into Steve Jobs in spirit. Former Apple employee Glenn Reid wrote a blog post revealing part of the reason why Jobs liked being CEO - so no one could stop him from focusing on product design details. Clearly, for Jobs, being CEO was merely a path to achieve full control over products; his dedication and commitment to products were the soul and spirit of Jobs. Therefore, the test for Zhang Xiaolong is whether he can firmly grasp the rights to WeChat's product design, even if he isn't in the CEO position. How to convince Pony Ma to continue giving special treatment to the WeChat team, how to handle various demands from internal business units at Tencent, how to balance the urgency between WeChat's product architecture and Tencent's impressive financial reports, etc., etc., Zhang Xiaolong will need to face these challenges one by one and respond accordingly.

Regarding product design, Jobs firmly believed that users don't know what they want. In other words, paying attention to user needs does not mean letting users lead the way. Jobs was indeed domineering, and the full screen of conical icons on the iPhone is a good proof. But precisely because he insisted on being the standard-setter, he was able to redefine smartphones. Zhang Xiaolong also has this talent. If you want to send pure text status updates in Moments, please long-press the camera button; this feature is hidden deep because sending pure text status updates is discouraged. Indeed, under mobile scenarios, pictures are a more natural form of expression, a demand that exists universally but users haven't yet realized. Once such demands are called out, they gradually become habits, something natural. Satisfying known demands and uncovering unknown ones are two realms, the difference between Nokia and the iPhone. One belongs to the past, the other points to the future. Jobs belongs to the latter, and Zhang Xiaolong appears to be the same. Here's a tangential comment: personally, I think the next successful smart product to realize a combination of software and hardware will be a camera, not a TV. Everyone feel that.

Success or failure lies in the ecosystem

Pony Ma mentioned during the Two Sessions that WeChat would be turned into a platform, constructing simple rules, leaving specific business models to partners or individuals. This statement initially sounds like an open-minded attitude, allowing third parties to fully exert their subjective initiative. But from another perspective, it's like wanting to be a hands-off boss! Throughout its journey, Tencent has reaped many benefits when things naturally fell into place. When others set up the stage, Tencent steps in to take over. It's precisely because such situations often occur that Tencent earned the title of "King of Copycats." But this time, the situation has changed. WeChat is leading the way in the mobile internet era. It's like being the first to arrive at virgin land; if you want to harvest, you must cultivate the land yourself. This requires WeChat, or rather Tencent, to shoulder more responsibilities. Not only must they build the stage, they might also have to organize the troupe; from infrastructure construction to user cultivation, compared to doing nothing, they must emphasize action to build a healthy platform ecosystem. In short, WeChat's commercialization cannot just focus on making money for itself; more importantly, it must promote employment and allow third parties to achieve commercialization. This is a mandatory option, not an optional one. Suddenly, I'm reminded of that old saying in the martial arts world: when you come to mix, you must eventually pay back. Tencent has reached the time to repay.

The good news is that if the WeChat ecosystem is built, the biggest beneficiary will undoubtedly still be Tencent. Let's take a look at the powerful blood-making function of the App Store ecosystem created by Apple. Asymco analyst Horace Dediu published a research report stating that Apple's iTunes generated $13.5 billion in revenue last year, becoming Apple's fourth-largest business. Dediu estimated that the revenue of Apple's iTunes Music Store will maintain a 10% annual growth rate, while the revenue of the App Store will maintain a 50% annual growth rate. Additionally, video and other aspects of revenue will maintain a 90% annual growth rate. Isn't that eye-catching? The prospects are absolutely tempting, but the inevitable hardships along the way cannot be avoided. If WeChat resolves to build a similar healthy ecosystem, it needs to do the following:

First, connect payments. The importance of payment for the WeChat ecosystem is akin to water sources for a forest. Connecting payments doesn't simply mean integrating Alipay, Tenpay, or redirecting to bank pages for payment. That only connects with existing payment methods but doesn't truly integrate WeChat payment. By the way, any payment method that makes users switch interfaces twice or more, requiring input of various information each time, with slow verification and likely failed payments, is essentially a scam. True integration of payment means WeChat handles all the hard work, mental and physical labor, so the God-like user only needs to press a button to complete all in-WeChat consumption payments. An ideal way can refer to Taobao's recent virtual credit card. If such payment is integrated, with a large enough base of WeChat users with strong stickiness and numerous applications, there will be no shortage of money.

Second, solve the unchargeable problem. The unchargeable problem actually refers to the unclear business model of mobile internet, mainly manifested in the nascent development of mobile internet advertising models and the lack of domestic user payment awareness. How to solve this problem? In an article titled "Cheng Lingfeng's Nine Judgments on Self-Media," WeChat Official Account Yunkeji stated: One Yunkeji may not sway advertisers, but ten Yunkejis certainly can. Only when the potential pie is big enough, will professional third-party companies establish metrics to measure the influence of self-media, package self-media, and introduce mature advertisers to this area. Once the revenue loop is connected, self-media will sprout like bamboo shoots after rain. So, it's still an ecosystem issue: good platform, good content, can make money, more good content, more participants, finer division of labor, formed industrial chain, mature business model, bigger platform. As for the habit of free lunches for Chinese people, well, where's the good fortune? Those soft advertisements disguised as news reports, those pop-up windows more captivating than the content itself, etc., users are constantly paying with their time and attention, often unknowingly and without choice. The problem now facing WeChat is whether to micro-innovate traditional domestic industries and web-era business models, go further to challenge existing bundled profit mechanisms, advocate content payment, and return choice to consumers. This is a sweet selection question.

Third, do proper planning and improve supporting facilities. The iPhone is first a communication tool and a phone, then a carrier for downloading application software, audiovisuals, and other entertainment content consumption; WeChat is an app starting with communication, later adding Moments and official account designs, becoming a content-carrying platform with immense imagination space. We know Apple has big divisions like iTunes, App Store, and Game Center, but it seems WeChat's official accounts are all clustered together. Clearly, the one-to-many broadcast interaction format of official accounts isn't a universal socket, plug-and-play. For example, celebrities opening official accounts result in fans losing interaction among themselves, missing a big chunk.

So far, a group of independent media figures have mastered WeChat, but another group of Taobao merchants have hit walls. Sometimes, it's not that the path hasn't been smoothed, but rather that this path doesn't exist. Hardly moving the entire e-commerce booth to a customer service platform works. We expect WeChat to create one or two interface designs at the level of WeChat Official Accounts, satisfying various needs, or simply innovate on features like Shake and Scan. In short, all kinds of service applications and content consumption should ideally be planned at the foundational level by WeChat, so third parties can orderly enter.

On April 18th, Tencent Interactive Entertainment Group announced externally that it would transition from a single online game business platform to an interactive entertainment entity encompassing music, games, animation, literature, film, and other related businesses, initiating a pan-entertainment strategy. This is actually a significant move by Tencent at the corporate level to build a pan-entertainment content ecosystem and promote copyright business models, which is a great boon for WeChat. Besides games, the next goldmine should be music. Digital music has already taken off overseas, with domestic and foreign internet giants entering the scene. It's also time for WeChat to push digital music, rescuing domestic music workers from dire straits while achieving mutual benefit. Tencent has already laid out in digital music, like QQ Music and QPlay. If integrated with WeChat, it will be a big cake.

The WeChat team doesn't necessarily have to be responsible for building all the supporting facilities; they can just collect requirements and put them out for bidding. Currently, there's a pressing need for a management application targeting WeChat Official Account content. Evernote just saves articles for subsequent organization, which is completely inadequate and inconvenient. As for the Official Account rankings that many people have mentioned, it seems there's no need for them to appear. Why? WeChat is highly personalized, with each person constructing their own exclusive little world, connecting the real and virtual worlds. Who you are determines what your WeChat world looks like. Instead of saying you discovered something in WeChat, it's better understood as what you placed in WeChat. Rankings have already formed outside the WeChat world; WeChat's job is to provide the optimal experience for realizing these services and content consumption. Therefore, rankings are unnecessary; everyone has their own ranking in their heart.

Either explode within copying or perish being copied

Now, WeChat stands at a crossroads, either successfully copying the iPhone or becoming mediocre amidst a wave of copycats, possibly even being completely replaced.

What does successfully copying the iPhone mean? It means doing better than the iPhone, surpassing and disrupting it, and WeChat has such potential. The breakthrough lies in the fact that WeChat, as an application, has no exclusivity regarding the phone system, highly separated from hardware, capable of replicating Windows' success, thereby disrupting the integrated iPhone. Specifically speaking, the relevant experience differences brought by buying Android phones or iPhones will be replaced by whether the WeChat application is installed (in the domestic market).

This can be seen as an upgrade in the competition for mobile internet entry points. In the mobile internet era, browsers simulate the web era, connecting to the past; app stores are currently the mainstream, representing the present; super apps sit above the phone system layer, being the future of the present. As for the desktop being an entry point, well, whichever app is most suitable to serve as a desktop can make a launcher, and the desktop won't be a standalone entry point. The lukewarm reception of Facebook Home is a proof, as is the launch of launchers by Facebook, Feixin, and South Korean chat app KakaoTalk. Notably, communication apps are indeed suitable to be made into launchers, covering the phone's communication services and standing independently from other apps. One day, when WeChat IDs replace phone numbers as people's primary contact methods, the WeChat platform can meet most entertainment, social, and information needs, and WeChat groups become virtual communities sharing knowledge, perhaps a launcher named WeChatHome will be born.

Another possibility is that WeChat slowly fades away amidst a wave of copycats. Many copycats have already appeared, the most blatant being China Mobile's Feixin. On April 12th, China Mobile Guangdong Company bid for the next round of Feixin operations and maintenance, mentioning public platforms, information push platforms, continued investment in voice and video calls, and plans to launch Feixin launchers and Feixin ROMs. It seems China Mobile wants to turn Feixin into a comprehensive product, not only learning from WeChat but also catching up with the latest wave of launchers. Regarding the WeChat public platform, which has drawn much attention, it has even attracted cross-industry imitation from Sina Weibo and Alibaba. Public platforms are poised to become the next standard for internet products. Although these imitators haven't amounted to much so far, more and more substitute products are appearing, traditional operators are beginning to revolutionize themselves, and various vertical fields are loading public platforms, using communication as the entry point, increasing user stickiness with Moments, plus a lively-looking public platform, doesn't WeChat feel a bit thin-skinned?

Is it accelerating forward to be an innovator or slowing down to let everyone play together? WeChat must make a decision. Tencent has always liked playing it safe, liking to make minor innovations on products and models that have already gained market recognition to better satisfy consumers. But this time is really an exception. Can WeChat continue to be exceptional? We'll wait and see.

Bonus Chapter

Recently, Zhang Xiaolong gave an interview to Tencent Technology talking about his logic behind product creation and WeChat. He mentioned the difficulty of WeChat's internationalization, especially in the European and American markets, believing that product creation should not start from a cultural perspective but from a demand perspective. So let's take a look at the demand situation for WeChat in the European and American markets.

First, is there such a demand, and how big is it? WeChat's initial appeal was free call experiences and magical social functions. Due to the tariff standards provided by operators and the cultural differences between China and the US, it's not as attractive when placed in America. However, the demand for communication apps in the mobile internet era absolutely exists. Like WhatsApp, which thrives on a one-dollar annual fee and game revenue shares, Facebook is also striving to develop its built-in communication functions. Undoubtedly, in the absence of China Mobile, China Telecom, and China Unicom in the U.S., WeChat's mobile communication entry point is destined not to be a killer-level product.

Second, in the U.S., can WeChat grow into WeChat? In China, WeChat has a good father, Tencent. Though the product is genuinely excellent, reaching over three hundred million users in two years is inseparable from favorable timing, geographical advantages, and human harmony. This geographical advantage refers to WeChat being Tencent's WeChat. Because of these three hundred million users, there followed the liveliness of Moments and public platforms, and all sorts of imaginations about WeChat. In the U.S., Tencent is not Tencent, Facebook might be Tencent. Whether WeChat can rely on its basic functions to immediately attract hundreds of millions of users is crucial, everything else comes afterward. In the U.S., where Facebook and Twitter have already occupied the main mountains, there are plenty of small teams like Instagram and Snapchat charging ahead to capture smaller peaks. Either they get absorbed or surrounded and severely weakened. In such a severe revolutionary situation, can WeChat simply shake to become a platform? From the current perspective, it's not optimistic.

Zhang Xiaolong also said in that interview: If we don't make WeChat a platform and don't expand it, then we will die. So, international site selection is important, it must be countries and regions where a platform can be built. In short, product creation doesn't start from internationalization, but from demand.

If WeChat commercialization takes big strides, it will gradually transcend the positioning of a mobile social app, becoming the definer of a one-stop mobile lifestyle app. Grasping the promotion of the WeChat lifestyle at different stages is the top priority. In the mobile internet era, providing users with a more convenient life, enabling social resources to be allocated more reasonably, and further reducing the cost of commercial information communication, aren't these starting points more meaningful and interesting? Perhaps one day Mark in the U.S. will go through great lengths to enjoy WeChat's services, just a joke.

Indeed, historically, Tencent has often won by being better rather than newer. But this time, WeChat is an exception, unprecedented and not yet followed. WeChat gives Tencent perhaps a once-in-a-lifetime opportunity to engage in genuine competition with Western internet giants, and possibly even surpass them. In terms of grasping the pulse of future life, building communication technology, the rationality of interaction logic, updating business models, perceiving humanity, and user experience, WeChat has much to offer, not just creating an international version and conducting overseas business. In summary, WeChat's commercialization is urgent, but international versions should be considered carefully over the long term.
