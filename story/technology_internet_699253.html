The Only Female Village Official in Afghanistan: Can Ride a Motorcycle and Carry a Rifle

by loveh1o7 on 2012-03-09 14:12:28

In Afghanistan, a patriarchal society where women are not allowed to drive alone or appear in public, Salifa Qazi Sada, the only middle-aged woman as the village official, has been commended 18 times by the Afghan government. Her actions stand out remarkably in Afghanistan.

According to reports, Salifa Qazi Sada is originally just an ordinary Afghan woman. She got married at the age of 12 and has already raised 15 children. However, she has some unique qualities: she is the only female village chief in Afghanistan who can drive a tractor and motorcycle — remember, Afghanistan is a country where women are not allowed to drive alone or appear in public. She also carries a Kalashnikov rifle, and her actions stand out remarkably in Afghanistan.

A female riding a motorcycle is rarely seen in Afghanistan

Since the fall of the Taliban regime, Qazi Sada has been dedicated to improving the lives of villagers. She also particularly protects the rights of women. Qazi Sada often rides a motorcycle from house to house to resolve village affairs. She is the only female village chief in Afghanistan. Her village is located in Mazar-i-Sharif in northern Afghanistan. For many villagers, it is rare to see a woman ride a motorcycle in Afghanistan. However, the villagers elected Salifa as the village chief voluntarily.

Qazi Sada said: "People see me bring electricity to the village, build a mosque, and always stand by them despite having no political position; because I helped them, they elected me as the village chief."

Bravely facing male ridicule

High prestige among women

Many men do not like having a woman as their village chief. But Salifa knows how to protect herself, including physically: "When I first started my work, some men ridiculed me. I pushed them around and used force to make them shut up. Now no one dares to ridicule me anymore. Some even tried to kill me, but thank God, I'm still alive."

Among the female villagers, Qazi Sada enjoys extremely high prestige. In the village, women can pray together with men in the mosque. In other parts of Afghanistan, women rarely enter mosques.

Qazi Sada said: "We've had a lot of trouble over the mosque issue. We used to have to take turns praying; after the men prayed, the women would pull a curtain behind them and pray. Now, men and women can pray together."

(Personal Aspiration)

Wishes to marry later and attend university

According to reports, Qazi Sada said that women should live equally with men, so the autonomy of women's lives and their right to education are particularly important. Although Qazi Sada herself only studied until fifth grade, she understands the way modern society operates.

She said: "I got married at the age of 12, and after that, I couldn't go back to school. But I was always the best student in class. Every time there was a village meeting, I couldn't give speeches due to insufficient education, which was a painful experience for me; my heart felt like it was bleeding."

She said that if she had married later, she could have attended university, and she hoped to become a judge. Now, she tries to help other women achieve their dreams.

Qazi Sada can ride a bicycle, drive motorcycles, and tractors. This poses no problem for her: "Once, a tractor got stuck in a ditch, so I made the driver get out and I sat behind the wheel. He shouted behind me that women cannot drive. I only told him to shut up, and then I drove the vehicle out of the ditch myself. He and the other drivers were stunned."

Qazi Sada's work has been highly valued. The Afghan government has praised her 18 times. Salifa said that she will definitely continue working and run for a seat in the national parliament. For her, there is still a long journey ahead.
