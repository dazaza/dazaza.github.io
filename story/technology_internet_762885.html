When the world revolves around Mobile...

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 17:49:47

If we say that the world in which my father's generation, commonly known as the Baby Boomers, grew up was a world revolving around "television," then the world in which my generation, commonly known as Generation Y, grew up is a world revolving around "PC" and "Web."

Various signs indicate that my son's generation, commonly known as Generation Z, should be a world revolving around Mobile – the shipment volume of Smartphones + Tablets officially surpassed that of PCs in 2011, and by 2015, it will widen to five times the gap.

What are the differences between a world revolving around Mobile and the previous world revolving around PC + Web? Bill Gurley of Benchmark Capital and Bubba Murarka of DFJ recently wrote separately about their observations and predictions in "Transitioning To A Mobile Centric World" and "9 Ways A Billion Dollar New Mobile Company Might Be Created".

Combining the points they raised with my own observations, I believe there are the following threads worth entrepreneurs considering:

Saturation? – Although the penetration rate of smartphones has exceeded 50% in many countries, this may indicate that the growth rate might start to slow down. However, from another perspective, there’s still half of the consumer base yet to penetrate, and the penetration rate of tablets still has a long way to go.

Time Share – More importantly, Mobile will increasingly occupy more of consumers' "time." Tablets, in particular, will take away consumers' attention from PCs, televisions, and magazines.

Design – On Mobile, due to the smaller screen space and mainly "touch," an extremely intimate interaction method, the importance of design (not beautification) will only continue to grow.

Android – Now what developers are discussing is no longer whether Android or iOS takes priority, but which versions and models of Android should take priority. This certainly shows the fragmentation of Android, but also its weight.

Apps – In the Web world, the browser is the most important channel for going online, but in the Mobile world, the browser is just one of the apps and occupies a weaker position. Previously, we accessed 200 million websites worldwide through browsers; in the future, we will discover and use millions, tens of millions, or even hundreds of millions of apps globally through the Mobile Desktop, App Store, and Play Market.

Cross-border – Network companies starting from Desktop Web now must consider what apps they need to create to capture users who are migrating to Mobile. Network companies starting from Mobile cannot lack Web either, so the difficulty of developing and operating network companies in the future will inevitably increase.

Search – In the Mobile world, the importance of search has significantly decreased, and Google Search's dominant position faces serious challenges.

SEO – Since there is no unified search engine, the effect of driving traffic through search engine optimization (SEO) will naturally decrease significantly, and thus the importance of SEO will gradually weaken.

Promotion – Promoting apps will continue to be difficult because there lacks a long-term effective Earned Media channel like SEO.

User Value – Leaving a website requires just one mouse click, while uninstalling an app involves several more steps. Adding the high cost of promotion mentioned above, the result is that an app installed on a user's Smartphone/Tablet will have far greater value than a visitor to a website.

Payment and Commerce – On Mobile, convenient payment channels are expensive – both Play and App Store charge a 30% transaction fee, and cheaper payment channels are inconvenient. Therefore, mobile payments and mobile commerce still have a long way to go (my personal estimate is 5-10 years).

The above are some observations about the Mobile world that is arriving, hoping to give you some food for thought.
