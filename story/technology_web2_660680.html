The $4500 Basketball Shoe

In January 2008, a set of classic 1985 Nike Jordan basketball shoes sold online for $4500. These original Jordans were in mint condition, with tags still attached, remaining unworn inside their box, and had never been used. Remarkable? Not really, considering that the Air Jordan shoe is just as popular today as it has ever been.

A Solid Start

Back in 1985, people were actually robbed at gunpoint for their Air Jordans. Before the signature shoe's launch, Nike signed Jordan to an then-uncommon $2.5 billion endorsement deal. The shoe’s bold black and red styling clashed with the NBA’s then-standard color scheme of team colors on white, leading to its ban by officials. Jordan continued to wear the shoe on court and was fined $5000 per game. Nike gladly covered that cost and has been cashing in on the shoe's popularity ever since. On the streets, the shoe was the first ever to cost $100 and was coveted by kids everywhere. Hence, the gunpoint robberies reported in several cities. If you had Jordans, you had status. There were even famous TV commercials directed by and featuring director Spike Lee with the tagline: "It’s Gotta Be the Shoes."

Still Going Strong

The Air Jordan line is currently at the landmark model XXIII (23), matching the now-retired number worn by Jordan during his championship years with the Chicago Bulls. The shoe was released in limited edition at just 23 locations nationwide at a price of $230. Only one month later, pairs of the limited edition XXIII (23) were selling for up to $1500. Over the 23 years since the first launch, the Air Jordan has seen a new release every year. It has also expanded beyond shoes to clothing, fragrances, and jewelry. In recent years, without slowing the line's popularity, Nike has re-issued some Jordan models in limited edition and introduced a "retro" version of Jordans to capitalize on the demand shown by collectors.

Is This the End?

The Jordan brand, with its "Jumpman" logo of a silhouetted Jordan, has now been spun off into its own division of Nike. Some current NBA players are now being signed to the Jordan brand instead of having their own signature lines, which is routine with other manufacturers. Jordan himself is now part-owner of the Charlotte Bobcats, and Nike would not comment on the details of the current agreement with the legend. With the world Jordan line currently at the landmark edition XXIII (23) and still as popular as ever with fans and admirers, neither Nike nor Jordan will confirm or deny rumors that the line might end with edition XXIII (23). As profitable as the line has been for Nike and Jordan, I wouldn't bet on it ending anytime soon. After all, it's business, and who kills a thriving brand? Either way, the shoe will continue to exist even after Nike and Jordan have cashed their checks and moved on, as collectors continue to buy and sell them in the thriving resale market.
