"There's a Girl Named Suzy" is a novel in Yan Geling's Youth Memory series.

by jqrxass on 2009-09-19 15:15:23

Twelve stories form a unique novel: all revolve around the female protagonist Xiao Sui-zi, scattered throughout her life from age 0 to over 20. The number of characters that appear is more than in any other novel, and whether they are main or supporting characters, their personalities are rich, vivid, and interesting. Tragic experiences are written about optimistically and freely in this book, with different natural environments as the backdrop. This book is clearly a Chinese version of "The Sorrows of Young Werther" with historical significance.

This is the novel closest to the youth experiences of novelist and screenwriter Yan Ge-ling herself, a semi-autobiographical novel (the author said "this should be the novel closest to my personal experiences"). Youth, memories, and love in the red era. A beautiful long novel about women's growth, it can also be seen as a collection of stories. Illustrated edition.

Part One of "There's a Girl Named Sui-zi"

Sui-zi was not sure if her grandfather's disability allowance had been stopped from that day on. She wrote letters to her parents that summer, saying she missed them very much, and also said that she had always felt guilty for hurting her mother's feelings. In her correspondence with her parents during this summer vacation, Sui-zi did not mention a word about her grandfather. But her parents still found out that his special food supply had been interrupted.

Sui-zi's parents decided to take their daughter away. They had several private talks with Sui-zi, urging her to understand the greater good, emphasizing that parents' rights over their children are supreme. They said that for a long time they were forced to be separated from their daughter, and both Sui-zi and them had suffered great emotional losses. Now was the time to make up for these losses. Her mother said, "We were too weak, letting our child keep an unrelated old man company. And he's an old man with unclear history!"

Upon hearing "unrelated," Sui-zi looked at her mother confusedly.

Part Two of "There's a Girl Named Sui-zi"

The first question raised about Geng Di was one evening in May when everyone was sitting on the wall watching their fathers carry bricks, occasionally commenting, "My dad's hairstyle looks better than your dad's," "My dad pretends to have changed more convincingly than your dad; look how bent his waist is like a shrimp," "Look at Sui-zi's dad, pretending to be so honest! His face is as trustworthy as a yellow dog's!"

Geng Di sat silently among them, occasionally bursting out laughing. After sitting for a while, someone needed to urinate and jumped down to the other side of the wall. Upon hearing the "shh shh" sound from the other side of the wall, Geng Di slightly pursed her lips and her face turned a bit red. As evening approached, Geng Di swung her long legs like getting off a horizontal bar and jumped down from the wall. Someone asked, "Where are you going, Geng Di?" Geng Di replied, "To the toilet."

Everyone fell silent because they realized that after such a long time of close living together, Geng Di had never urinated with them. Even when they went to the toilet together, Geng Di always waited outside. If asked, "Geng Di, don't you need to go?" Geng Di would sneer disdainfully, "What's it got to do with you? Lack of manners - your dad is still a counter-revolutionary literary giant!"

Part Three of "There's a Girl Named Sui-zi"

The people on the concave-shaped building upstairs began to notice the middle-aged officer coming to see Xiao Gu. Xiao Gu told everyone she met, "Isn't it funny? My cousin has been assigned to the provincial military control committee." People thought it made sense why Yang Mai’s sentence was reduced; usually, "current counter-revolutionaries" would be executed if they were unlucky. Yang Mai's sentence was reduced from life imprisonment to a fixed term, then further reduced to six years of supervised labor reform.

If it weren't for a group of children catching a glimpse on the fourth floor, the people in the concave-shaped building would never have known the real relationship between Xiao Gu and Huang Representative.

On a hot summer night, seven or eight girls climbed onto the railing of the rooftop platform and walked along the one-and-a-half-meter-wide cement railing. One girl pointed at a window on the south side of the third floor and said, "Quick, look! The soldier is hugging Xiao Gu."
