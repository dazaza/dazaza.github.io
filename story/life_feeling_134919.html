How to define love

by wgmking on 2009-03-28 10:39:24

It's easy to understand what it means to have a fleeting attraction or infatuation, but what is love? Under the education system that emphasizes exam results, we don't have much energy to consider this question. After entering society, pressured by reality, we mechanically meet the needs of socialization and reproduction, fulfilling the roles of husband or wife without further contemplation. In fact, love is a subtle dynamic process with different meanings at different stages.

1) Anticipation

Everyone has the capacity to love. Like animals, we are subconsciously driven by the will to life to reproduce with suitable partners. The basic rule is that this person can compensate for the deficiencies in our own genes, ensuring that the next generation is more beautiful, smarter, and healthier, both psychologically and physiologically suited to survive in an unpredictable world. Therefore, everyone has their ideal type, the person they are willing to be with. Unlike animals, our social nature means that only in the eyes of the one who means everything to us does our existence gain justification. Of course, the ability to love varies from person to person; some love those who are overly capable, while others love those who seem incapable.

2) Pursuit

Love often arises quickly because the desire to love precedes the desire to be loved. We first want to love a certain kind of person, and then our longing for love brings about the appearance of our beloved. The allure of perfection emanating from our beloved can lead to feelings of inferiority, which in turn prompts us to pretend to possess traits we do not actually have in order to please them, setting the stage for future emotional crises. Western thought suggests that love is unreciprocated, and the less likely it is to be returned, the stronger the desire grows. Montaigne once said that love is merely a frantic longing for those who flee from us.

3) Mutual Love

Impulsive mutual love stems from a lack of understanding, filling ignorance with desire. Mature love, on the other hand, involves exchanging past experiences and viewpoints before deciding whether to love each other, a decision based on mutual understanding and affirmation. However, as understanding increases, it can either be an attraction or an obstacle – as it may cause conflict between ideals and reality. In marriage, it seems that one party must always sacrifice, either personal interests or species interests, as the well-being of the next generation comes at the cost of the current one.

4) Doubt

We often doubt whether the person we love in our hearts truly exists, or if they are just illusions created by us to prevent depression caused by a lack of love. In the oasis, thirsty people imagine seeing water, palm trees, and shade, not because they have found evidence, but because they need this evidence. Extreme need creates illusions; thirst creates the illusion of water, and the need for love creates the illusion of a perfect partner. Happiness comes from things we can control or obtain after hard work. The difficulty in accepting the happiness brought by love lies in the fact that we cannot control the factors that lead to happiness, hence the doubts about love.

5) Anxiety

The anxiety caused by loving someone stems from our fear that happiness can easily fade away, as the person we love might suddenly lose interest, pass away, or marry someone else. The uncertainty of how love will end threatens every love story, making it as terrifying as its unpredictability. Sometimes love is so beautiful that it makes us fear that what we see before us is all we have ever hoped for, and this moment could be the only life we might live. Due to the anxiety brought by extreme love, we may not be able to endure the uncertainty and risks of love, ending the relationship prematurely rather than waiting for habit, reality, or a third party to do so.

6) Degradation

Loving something you already possess goes against habit. Being together with your beloved brings immense happiness, and your love for them stops you from starting another relationship. But if we truly love them, why do we still find others attractive? Sometimes we satisfy our need for love but fail to fulfill our longing for it. Love changes so quickly that we cannot determine the reasons for loving or not loving someone. If we ignore all this and still firmly believe in love, it might be because the time spent loving each other far exceeds moments of boredom and indifference.

7) Quarrels

The intensity of our accusations and their irrationality indicate that we argue not out of hatred for each other, but out of love. We hate ourselves for loving the other person to this extent. We yell loudly at each other partly to test if the other person can tolerate our yelling. We want to verify each other's limits; only after trying to destroy each other do we know we are safe.

8) Departure

We should forgive those who are heartless towards us after some time. Whenever someone awkwardly expresses their need for space and time, unwillingness to commit, or fear of intimacy, their rational mind may appreciate our qualities, but their will to life acts oppositely and removes their sexual desire in an undeniable way. The tragedy of love lies in its inability to escape the dimension of time. When we are with our current beloved, thinking that all that remains of past lovers is coldness, it is cruel indeed. If our present love defines the meaning of self, does the end of love one day mean the partial extinction of ourselves?

Love is a happy bullet; without anticipation, there is no harm.
