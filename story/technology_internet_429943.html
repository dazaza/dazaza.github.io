Fate

I don't know why my life is so bad. Heaven didn't let me be born in the 1980s, but in the 1970s; since I was born in the 1970s, why wasn't I born in Western Europe or North America in the 1970s, but in China in the 1970s; being born in China, I was also born in Henan Province; being born in Henan, it would have been fine to be born in a city, but I was born in a rural area; being in a rural area, it would have been better to be a boy, but I was a girl. My fate was not only fixed at the bottom of society, but at the very bottom of the bottom.

Since I am a girl from a rural area in Henan, China, I must do physical labor. But God didn't let me grow up strong, instead giving me a willowy figure that looks good, but when it comes to actual work, I'm at a loss. If I had been born in a city, this might have been a good thing, saving me the trouble of dieting and slimming down. But being born in the countryside, it was a bit out of place. I remember once I went on a blind date, I didn't like the family, but they didn't like me either, the reason being: with such a frail body, how could I possibly carry bundles of corn stalks or dig sweet potatoes in the field? Looking back, I was really unlucky, I don't know what bad deeds I did in my past life to be reborn into such an environment. For this, I was extremely distressed, especially when I saw those dirty and tiring farm jobs that only cattle and men can handle. I was terrified. So, I made an early resolution, determined to change my identity no matter what, not wanting to stay home wielding a hoe. If I were to cultivate the earth for my entire life, my life would truly lose its meaning. I couldn't imagine that several years later, I would be a farmer's wife, disheveled, dressed untidily, one hand leading a cow, the other holding my snot-nosed child, shouting loudly at the cow while scolding my crying child who is crying because there's no money to buy pencils. If it were like that, I'd rather die.

Not only do I have high expectations for myself, but my father, a tombstone engraver and former member of the fourth class (a term used during the Cultural Revolution), who barely finished primary school (high-grade primary school, not high school) as a rural intellectual, also had high hopes for me. He instilled in me from a young age the idea that if I didn't attend Qinghua University (Tsinghua University), I should at least go to Dazhuan School (a vocational school). As long as I could get into university, I would be able to eat meat every day and have white bread buns every meal. At the time, I only understood Qinghua and Dazhuan as frogs in the river and bricks on the ground, not knowing their true meanings, but the prospect of eating meat every day was indeed fatally attractive to me. For this reason, I studied quite hard when I was young, always ranking among the top few in elementary school. Unfortunately, being a girl, I couldn't escape the common rut of most girls: a precious gem in elementary school, standing aside in middle school, and completely failing in high school. I don't know why this happens, but my performance in middle school was already less than stellar, even though I still hadn't fully matured emotionally or known about romantic relationships. This may just be a rule, something most people can't avoid. Just like my later attempts to change my rural identity and move to the city, which I couldn't accomplish. Perhaps the pursuit of changing one's fate has always been as difficult as climbing to the heavens, similar to what Christianity says: since God placed you in this place, it was His arrangement and will, just as Jesus was born in a manger, and just as humans cannot escape Earth's gravity. If you insist on going against God's will, aren't you opposing God? Therefore, your heart is tormented, your muscles are strained, your body is starved, and your spirit is drained, yet you still don't know whether fate can ultimately be changed.

I often think, if heaven allows gifts, in my next life, I must give God a generous gift so that He does not place me in such a place again. A seed planted in barren soil, no matter how good the seed, without good soil, it can barely survive, let alone grow into a towering tree, or hope to be transplanted to fertile soil. The hardships and torment involved in this process cannot be described in a single sentence. Boys enduring hardship is one thing, but for a girl, if she doesn't accept her fate, she might very well lose happiness in the pursuit of it. Listen to that overused saying, and you might understand some of the bitterness. That saying is: Behind every successful woman stands a group of men. If you were to choose the statement you dislike the most, or find the most nauseating, I believe I would choose this one, because it is an insult and extreme disrespect to women, as well as discrimination against successful women. Why does a man's success require only one woman behind him, while a woman's success requires a group of men? What does that mean? I believe only a society biased against women would say such things. Moreover, these words also imply that a woman, if unsuccessful, can only submit to the whims of fate, and if successful, must endure others' strange gazes. In short, whether successful or not, if you're a woman with aspirations, you bear much bitterness and helplessness, especially if you're at the bottom of society. Imagine if a high-ranking official's child or a city girl said she wanted to become an artist or scientist, people might believe or doubt her, but if a rural girl stood in the rice paddy, muddy hands planting rice, and said she wanted to become an artist or entrepreneur, probably even you wouldn't believe her.

The concept of male superiority and female inferiority may merely exist ideologically in cities, but in the rural areas of the 1970s and 80s, it wasn't that simple. Its practical effect was that a girl's life was worth less than a blade of grass. Those born after 1980 might not fully grasp this, and city dwellers feel it even less. However, in the countryside, the situation was vastly different. Even today, among abandoned infants, girls still outnumber boys. Have you noticed that characters with negative meanings all have "female" as their radical, like "jian," "pian," "yao," "nu," "xian," etc., none of which have "male" as their radical. In rural areas where historical prejudices are still heavy, how devalued a girl's life is, I'll illustrate using my cousin and neighbor Aunt Yan.

My cousin is the third child. Because there were already two girls before her, when she was born, her parents, who had hoped for a boy, were utterly disappointed. Love is what, but she cried and fussed a lot. Actually, babies cry and fuss, right? Her father, my uncle, got so annoyed he wanted to abandon her. My aunt didn't want to, but couldn't resist my uncle, and eventually, she was taken away and thrown out. Afterward, my aunt cried at home. Luckily, my cousin's grandmother came by, asking why my aunt was crying. Upon learning the baby had been thrown out, the grandmother insisted on retrieving her. My aunt said it had been hours, and with the cold weather outside, the baby might not survive. The grandmother said they should at least check. She found the baby in a small ditch by the road, frozen stiff, unable to cry, with her eyes half-opened. The grandmother took her back, fed her some rice water, and saved her life. During her wedding last year, when the host asked her to thank her parents, my cousin bowed only to my aunt, not to her father. To this day, she still isn't close to her uncle.

Then there's my neighbor Aunt Yan. She was also the third child. When she was born prematurely at seven months due to her mother falling and injuring herself on the way back to her maternal home, if it had been a boy, they would have tried desperately to save her. But seeing it was a girl, they gave up, thinking premature babies wouldn't survive anyway. They wrapped her in old cloth and left her in a corner until she passed away, planning to throw her out afterward. But after a day and night, when they checked the next morning, she was still breathing. Thinking this child had a strong fate, they decided to try feeding her. They cooked some rice water and fed her, and she survived. Recently, Aunt Yan had a baby girl. On the baby's full moon celebration, while Aunt Yan was outside with her baby enjoying the sun, she said, "Even if I have to beg, I won't let my daughter suffer." She added, "I will never be as heartless as my parents were." Her mother was sitting nearby, hearing this, showing no reaction.

Compared to them, I was fortunate to be the eldest and favored by my father, so I didn't face such misfortunes. But fate didn't favor me much more. As a rural girl, avoiding physical labor was impossible, especially for someone like me, without older brothers or sisters, and physically weak and thin, looking like a willow branch.

Although my father always treated me as his pearl, placing high hopes on me, my academic performance was unsatisfactory. During the college entrance exam, the school, pursuing a higher admission rate, allowed only a few students to participate. I didn't even get the chance to take the exam, so I couldn't talk about failing it. After returning home, I felt heavy-hearted and lost, unsure of what to do next.

Let's rewind to when I first left school, to see how my destiny unfolded as someone who refused to bow to fate. As a person insignificant as an ant or a blade of grass, how did I resist going with the flow in the midst of China's drastic social changes, only to ultimately sink? My story lacks the legendary charm of those great figures' biographies, but it is true, representing and reflecting the voices of thousands of blades of grass and countless rural girls. At the time, I didn't realize the significant changes happening in Chinese society, nor did I foresee that the path I chose would be the same one followed by most rural girls who failed their exams in later years. Of course, there were exceptions. The following diary entry records my inner journey shortly after leaving school.

May 2, 1989

Last night, I didn't sleep a wink. Since coming back from school, I've been worrying about my future. This morning, just as I closed my eyes, my mother woke me up. I was so sleepy I couldn't lift my head, but I still got up. There was no longer any reason to stay in bed lazily now that I wasn't going to school anymore.

When we went to the fields, my mother actually asked me to take the sheep along, which was embarrassing. Me, a girl, herding a flock of sheep—how could I ever face my classmates again? Didn't she consider that failing school meant I was worthless enough to end up herding sheep? Oh, my great mother, how thoughtful you are! After being scolded a couple of times, I headed to the fields alone, grumbling under my breath. No matter the scolding or even the beating, I wouldn't herd sheep.

Strictly speaking, today was the first time I did real farm work. If I were still in school, my father wouldn't expect me to work. Thinking about how he hoped I'd attend university, and how I not only failed to enter university but didn't even get to take the college entrance exam due to the school's pursuit of higher admission rates, I felt ashamed.

Now, thinking about those gray, half-inch-long wheat bugs wriggling on the ground between the wheat rows, I still feel scared and disgusted. But then I think about my father's sweat-soaked shirt, tightly sticking to his back, and I couldn't bear to stand idly by.

We don't even have a fan at home. Now, as I write this diary, sweating profusely, the red marks on my arms from the wheat awns sting when soaked with sweat. Alas, life at home is truly unbearable.

May 3, 1992

Yesterday morning, I worked in the fields for half a day, and by afternoon, I was already exhausted. By dusk, there wasn't a comfortable spot on my entire body—my legs hurt, my arms ached, my back was stiff like a board. The blisters on my hands from the hoe handle had burst and were bleeding, and every time I dug a hole with the pickaxe, it rubbed painfully against my hand. But I had to keep going.

Lying in bed last night, I couldn't find a comfortable position for my arms and legs, tossing and turning uncomfortably for half the night before finally falling asleep. Consequently, I didn't manage to write yesterday's diary.

This morning, my mother called me early to go cut wheat in the west field. I opened my eyes groggily, feeling that this was worse than being hanged.

While working in the field, Aunt Juan from the neighboring self-cultivation land heard that I had dropped out of school and said that with my good looks, I could still lead a good life. I replied that good looks were useless; no matter how pretty I was, if I couldn't pass the exam, I would still be bent over, digging in the dirt for food. We peasants, if we don't dig in the dirt, where else can we find sustenance? Farming isn't easy, but there are plenty of people doing it. Once I find a good husband, you'll still live a good life, Aunt Juan said. Hearing this, I quickly declined, but my mother was unhappy. She said since I wasn't going to school anymore, I should find a husband sooner rather than later, or else all the good families would be snapped up by others. It sounded like I was some leftover item needing quick sale. How ridiculous and absurd. I was only a few years old. Even if I were starting a business or career, it would still be too early, let alone getting married. According to her, if I didn't marry, I would have no future, perhaps having a child within two years.

May 4, 1992

Yesterday, I thought threading corn was the most painful work, but today, cutting wheat showed me what real labor is. Only now do I understand why everyone says labor is glorious, and why they say 'face the yellow earth, back to the sky.' Before, I only said it as a phrase, but now, experiencing it firsthand, the taste and feeling are deeply embedded in my bones. Among all kinds of labor, nothing is heavier than farm work. This is not only a physical torture but also a test of one's spirit and perseverance.

Under the blazing sun, bending over, waist curved, our backs exposed entirely to the sun, being scorched and baked, not only does our posture become bent and submissive, but our spirit becomes humble and obedient, as if we want to shrink our heads into our stomachs and our limbs into our bodies, wishing we could find a crack in the ground to crawl into. At such moments, thoughts of those who don't need to toil this way towards the land make us look up to them somewhat. Especially when thinking of my classmates in school, the feelings inside are indescribable. While I was cutting wheat in the afternoon, I saw Li Xiaofeng from the same village riding his bicycle to the city. Suddenly, I envied him—not for anything else, but because his father was the director of the county commerce bureau, and it was rumored that his family would soon move to the county town. Due to having a good father, despite both of us being the same age and unable to continue schooling, he didn't have to endure this kind of suffering. Although he graduated from junior high school, and although deep down I somewhat looked down on him for relying on his father, at this moment, as long as I didn't have to labor under the sun, as long as I didn't have to face the earth and endure the heat, I would be content. Back in school, I never felt happy during those days when I didn't have to work, but now I realize those were truly the happiest times.

May 10, 1992

These days, whenever I come to the edge of the field and see the endless golden wheat shimmering under the sun, I feel fear and dread. I think the composer of the line "wheat waves glistening gold" is absurd, and all talk of joy in harvest and glory in labor are big lies. Because this season of harvest is one of drawing tendons and peeling skin, a season that can exhaust people. Everyone works tirelessly like machines, too tired to feel anything, let alone joy. I believe the person who wrote this song hasn't experienced the terror of these wheat waves, hasn't cut wheat at dawn or twilight, otherwise, they would never sing about the glittering wheat waves. These days, while cutting wheat in large patches of rolling wheat fields, I always think of the word "Jingwei filling the sea." I feel like I am to society as Jingwei is to the sea—so insignificant and unimportant, so powerless that I can't even control my own home. If I could control it, I would rather die than plant this land, starve rather than cut this wheat.

With all this hard work, my mother still thinks about Aunt Juan setting me up with a matchmaker. She says Aunt Juan has mentioned it to her, and the other party is her younger brother from her maiden home. He apparently values me highly to offer me to his own younger brother, otherwise, with their newly built two-story house and being an only son, there would be countless matchmakers knocking on their door. Haha, it seems my value is equivalent to that two-story house. This Aunt Juan is annoying. I've told her I'm not considering this now, so why bring it up again, causing my mother to constantly remind me that once the wheat is cut, she wants me to meet the potential suitor. Isn't this creating conflict? Today, I told my mother: Even if he had a ten-story building, I wouldn't go. My mother, busy kneading noodles, picked up the noodle stick and threatened to hit me, scaring me into running out of the kitchen. Coincidentally, my father returned, asking what happened. I explained to him, and he sighed, trying to persuade my mother not to force me too much. Unexpectedly, this stirred up a hornet's nest, making my mother shout and curse, calling me and my father ignorant. She said since there was no hope for me in school, marriage was my only path forward. Why wouldn't I take it? Do I plan to ascend to heaven?

May 12, 1992

Last night, watching the thresher rotate and roar, swallowing bundle after bundle of wheat, I was afraid, especially remembering how Village Brother Guang's hand was crushed by the machine a few days ago. I lacked the courage to step forward. Seeing my hesitant demeanor, my mother became anxious, saying it would have been better to drop out of school earlier, so I wouldn't be so pathetic and incapable now, making me feel terrible, beyond description.
