Microsoft CEO Search Head: No Puppet of Gates

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 10:48:29

Microsoft CEO recruitment leader John Thompson (John W. Thompson)

Introduction: The online edition of the Wall Street Journal published a commentary article titled "Meet the Man Picking Microsoft's Next CEO" on Thursday. Although many people believe that Microsoft's board of directors is Bill Gates' "rubber stamp," chief independent director John Thompson strongly denies being Gates' "lackey."

Full text of the article:

Not a rubber stamp

Since last week was likely Steve Ballmer's (Steve Ballmer) final analyst meeting as Microsoft CEO, it naturally attracted a lot of attention. However, many people also focused on another individual—John Thompson (John W. Thompson).

As Microsoft's chief independent director, Thompson also leads a special committee specifically tasked with finding Ballmer's successor. Since this process could determine Microsoft's development over the next few years, Thompson's responsibility is immense. Whoever replaces Ballmer will become the first CEO in Microsoft's history not from the early leadership team.

In addition, Thompson needs to rebuild the increasingly estranged relationship between the company and Wall Street—a task that is clearly not easy. Some analysts blame Microsoft's directors for allowing Ballmer to implement many measures that have suppressed the stock price, including the $7 billion acquisition of Nokia's mobile phone business.

Therefore, at 64 years old, Thompson must convince critics that he and the other external directors of Microsoft will not become rubber stamps for Microsoft Chairman Bill Gates (Bill Gates). "I have great respect for Bill," Thompson said in an interview on Wednesday, "but as a member of the board and chief independent director, I will not be Bill's 'lackey'."

Thompson refused to provide detailed information about the new CEO selection process. However, he quickly denied a popular claim: Ballmer's sudden departure was not because the board wanted to prevent attacks from activist investors like ValueAct Capital Management.

"It has nothing to do with that," he said. The real reason is that Ballmer knew that this transformation would take 5 to 7 years, exceeding the time he wanted to stay at Microsoft, and changing CEOs during the transition would be detrimental to the company.

"I appreciate Steve's attitude, who said: 'Look, although we are not in the most advantageous position right now, doing this thing is most beneficial for Microsoft's long-term healthy development,'" Thompson said.

Rich management experience

Insiders say that Thompson himself was a possible candidate for Microsoft CEO, partly because he had previously served as CEO of both Microsoft's competitors and partners. Some people who know him also think that he would be very suitable as Microsoft's CEO. However, he firmly denied this in an email.

Thompson served as CEO of Symantec, a security software and data protection company, for 10 years. Under his leadership, Symantec's revenue increased tenfold, rising from $593 million in fiscal year 1999 to $5.9 billion in fiscal year 2008. During his tenure, he acquired Veritas Software for $13.5 billion in 2004, despite initial opposition from some board members.

After leaving Symantec in April 2009, he largely disappeared from public view. But a year later, he surprisingly became the CEO of Virtual Instruments, a rapidly growing Silicon Valley company that monitors the operation status of enterprise computer systems, in which Thompson himself also invested. "I heard a lot of puzzled voices," he said.

Among the few African Americans who hold high positions in the IT industry, Thompson is one of them. Before joining Symantec, he worked at IBM for 25 years, holding various management positions, including General Manager of IBM Americas.

He also served on a U.S. government committee investigating the causes of the 2008 financial crisis, making him a hot candidate for U.S. Commerce Secretary. As a Democrat, he hosted a fundraising event for Obama's first presidential campaign at his home.

His 4.3-acre estate was purchased for $10 million in 2003 and is adjacent to Larry Ellison's (Larry Ellison), Oracle's CEO, larger mansion. Thompson is fond of red wine, bird hunting, and has various classic cars in his garage, including a 1952 Chevrolet pickup truck.

Strong coordination skills

Friends and colleagues say that Thompson is good at coordinating various conversations, making him an important figure on the board. Insiders say that Microsoft's board often sees heated debates, but Thompson always resolves conflicts and coordinates contradictions with his calm perspective.

Another person familiar with him says that during the selection of Microsoft's new CEO, Thompson will not hesitate to clash with Gates.

"He is a very independent thinker," Enrique Salem (Enrique Salem), who worked with Thompson at Symantec and succeeded him as CEO, said.

The ability of Thompson to coordinate the board and Wall Street relations was fully tested during Symantec's acquisition of Veritas. Since the acquisition aimed to enter the data storage software business, which deviated significantly from the company's PC antivirus software business, the board worried about difficulties in integrating sales teams, and analysts believed such a huge deal would dilute Symantec's profits.

However, Thompson persisted and did not give up. This caused the company's share price to plummet and continued into Salem's term—Salem also left in 2012. Salem said that history would prove Thompson was right, as some businesses previously under Veritas have yielded good returns.

A difficult journey to join

Thompson joined Microsoft's board in February 2012. However, insiders revealed that the time spent recruiting him set a record for Microsoft's director recruitment history.

Thompson said that after news of his impending departure from Symantec spread, he received an invitation from Microsoft in 2008. He declined because he still needed to serve as chairman of Symantec, extending his term from an initial one year to two years.

Thompson said that after officially leaving Symantec, he engaged in many dialogues with Ballmer and other Microsoft directors, including his longtime friend and venture capitalist David Marquardt (David Marquardt), who has long served as a Microsoft director. He said he has always respected Microsoft and believes "that I can add some value here."

In October 2012, Microsoft announced that Reed Hastings (Reed Hastings), then the chief director and CEO of Netflix, would not seek re-election. Thus, Thompson's position on Microsoft's board quickly rose.

Thompson did not anticipate that Microsoft's board would face so many major issues this summer: first, a large-scale restructuring took place, followed by Ballmer's decision to retire. Recently, Microsoft acquired Nokia's mobile phone business, and the board recently agreed to provide ValueAct with a board seat next year. To appease other investors, Microsoft's board recently decided to increase dividends by 22% and initiate a $40 billion stock buyback program. At last week's meeting, one analyst bluntly told Thompson that Microsoft should distribute more cash from its $77 billion reserve to investors.

Thompson politely but firmly rejected this idea. "Why distribute so much dividend and repurchase so many stocks at such a crucial time of selecting a new CEO?" he said.

To select a new CEO, Thompson has to shoulder a lot of work, frequently traveling, meeting with investors like ValueAct, and even working weekends. "At this moment, everything must be given priority," he said, "because this is an extremely important company."
