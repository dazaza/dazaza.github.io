The quality of clothing is all the fault of contract manufacturing.

by shenzhen123v on 2012-02-04 10:53:55

Some people say that OEM (Original Equipment Manufacturing), a production method that once expanded the influence of brands, is now repeatedly causing setbacks for brands. It's truly a case of "success and failure both coming from the same source." But is this really the case?

OEM allows brands to operate more efficiently. People familiar with brand operations all have this impression: "Operating a brand is a money-burning task." Yes, if you want to create and maintain a brand, you need to advertise. Advertising requires money. Taking Vancl as an example, despite avoiding the high-cost TV advertisements and choosing relatively cheaper portal sites and bus stop advertisements, its advertising costs are still astronomical. Public data shows that Vancl's advertising investment in 2009 was 2 billion yuan, and in 2011, it skyrocketed to 10 billion yuan. Some media analyses suggest that every product sold by Vancl includes a 10-yuan advertising and marketing fee. Compared to the dozens of yuan products commonly seen on Vancl's website, this advertising fee can be considered extravagant. Even so, Vancl dares not halt its advertising. As the saying goes, as long as the brand continues, so does the advertising.

Of course, besides advertising, design, production, channels, terminals, logistics, every aspect of clothing brand operations requires money, and none of these aspects can be spared. Branding is indeed a labor-intensive and time-consuming endeavor. Reducing one's own burden has become a necessity that brands must constantly contemplate. Nike adopted the "asset-light operation" model in the 1980s. The so-called "asset-light operation" model outsources product manufacturing and retail distribution businesses, focusing instead on design development and market promotion. This model reduces the company's capital investment, especially large fixed asset investments in the production sector, thereby increasing the return on assets. Nike's "asset-light operation" model takes "horizontal integration" and "supply chain management" to the extreme. Entering the 1990s, the "asset-light operation" model was adopted in many industries, and Nike's competitors also chose the "Nike-style" survival method.

For the "asset-light operation" model, the media gave it high praise, considering it the first great contributor to the sports apparel industry truly becoming a global industry in the 1990s. Regardless of whether other brands have learned its supply chain management knowledge, almost all clothing brands thereafter began to look for OEM factories like Nike to process their branded products.

In fact, OEM also reduces the risks of brand operations to a certain extent. In 2010, cotton prices soared, especially after entering September, the speed of cotton price increases became significantly faster. By September 25th, the China Cotton Price Index had risen from 18,002 yuan/ton at the beginning of September to 20,236 yuan/ton, an increase of 12.41%, setting the highest record in over a decade. Compared to September 2009, within a year, the price of standard cotton per ton rose from 8,000 yuan to over 20,000 yuan, rising 1.5 times.

At that time, the survival states of OEM enterprises and brand enterprises were like two different worlds. "The profits of OEM enterprises are originally very low; a slight rise or fall in raw material costs greatly affects corporate profits," said OEM enterprises. "For well-known clothing brands, the retail price is at least five times all production costs; even for ordinary clothing brands, the retail price is three to four times the production cost. For example, a denim jeans costing three to four hundred yuan, a cost increase of ten or twenty yuan would not greatly affect the enterprise," said brand enterprises. From this, we can see that OEM enterprises greatly share the risk of rising raw material costs for brand enterprises. Moreover, if brand enterprises give orders to OEM enterprises and find them unqualified upon inspection, they can generally cancel the order according to the contract. In this sense, the quality risks of brand products are also shared by OEM factories.
