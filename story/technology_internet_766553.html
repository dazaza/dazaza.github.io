If Twitter can't be king of the social network, it will be king of social TV.

by anonymous on 2013-09-18 21:54:26

The importance of television to Twitter goes without saying; social TV is one of the few areas where Twitter can beat Facebook. Currently, Twitter has become the preferred application for social TV's second screen. Twitter's success in this area is inseparable from a series of shrewd acquisitions, new products, and partnerships.

The Development of Social TV

In the first quarter of 2013, reports showed that 46% of smartphone users and 43% of tablet users would use their mobile devices while watching TV daily. A large part of this activity relates to the content being aired on TV, with 53% of mobile device users mentioning TV content in their mobile social activities.

Looking up information and using social media applications are among the most popular TV-related activities:

Half of tablet users say they look up information related to TV programs.

According to Nielsen data, 21% of tablet users and 18% of smartphone users said they pay attention to discussions about the TV shows they've watched on social media.

Data from NPD shows that 15% of TV viewers who own tablets and smartphones discuss the TV shows they've watched on social networks.

A Very Crowded Second Screen Application Market

The second screen application market for TV is exceptionally crowded. Apps like Zeebo, Viggle, and Shazam have audio recognition or subtitle scanning features that allow them to detect the TV show content you're watching. They all come with social elements.

Shazam has long been a popular app that can identify song titles and artists after listening to a snippet of a song, and it later expanded into the TV domain. Five percent of respondents indicated they used the Shazam app last month.

Many advertising giants launch ad campaigns within these apps, and there are also pure social TV platforms like GetGlue.

Twitter Becomes the King of Social TV

Twitter's actions in the social TV field began with its collaboration with Nielsen, jointly researching the relationship between social TV and ratings. Subsequently, it acquired social TV analytics firms Bluefin Labs and Trendrr, and launched the video-sharing service Vine.

Industry expert David Aufhauser stated, "Twitter's voice is loud and its influence strong; it is becoming the leader in this field (social TV)."

Although Twitter's audience size may not match Facebook's, and its impact on brand advertisers may be smaller, it has consistently emphasized its unique and intimate relationship with TV, backed by actual data.

Tweets have a significant impact on ratings, especially for competitive reality shows:

A Nielsen study on the relationship between ratings and Twitter showed that the ratings of 29% of TV programs are closely related to the number of Tweets.

Besides, Twitter also plays a role in TV ad audience targeting. It analyzes the content of Tweets to determine which programs users are paying attention to, allowing brands advertising during those program time slots to accurately target their audience.

With an IPO already in preparation, Twitter's development in the social TV field might also raise Wall Street's stock price expectations.
