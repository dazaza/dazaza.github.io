[Baby Weekly] Experts clarify 10 misconceptions about childbirth for you - 0

by kangkai08 on 2010-04-12 23:28:49

"Women with bigger butts are better at giving birth?" "Does a larger fetus mean a healthier baby?" As an expectant mother, do you realize that your childbirth beliefs can affect the smoothness of delivery? Below, we will discuss 10 common misconceptions about childbirth among pregnant women to help everyone understand more truths about pregnancy.

1. If the first child was delivered via C-section, must the next one be as well?

90% of expectant mothers mistakenly believe: If the first child was delivered via C-section, the next one must be as well.

If the reason for the first C-section was due to incorrect fetal position, too viscous amniotic fluid, poor fetal heartbeat, or personal factors, then the success rate of natural childbirth for the second child can reach 80-90%. However, if the first C-section was chosen because of a small pelvis or slow labor progression, the success rate of natural childbirth for the second child would be as low as 60-70%. Sometimes, choosing a C-section for the second child is out of fear of uterine rupture.

2. A smaller fetus won't result in premature birth?

10% of expectant mothers mistakenly believe: The fetus is still small, only weighing a few hundred grams, so it won’t lead to premature birth.

If the expectant mother experiences abnormal uterine contractions or insufficient rest, it increases the risk of premature birth.

Some expectant mothers think that since the fetus is still small, around 10-20 weeks, it’s unlikely to result in premature birth. In reality, premature birth can happen at any stage, even at 500 grams. It's not necessary for the fetus to reach a certain size before premature birth occurs; even at a very early stage, there is still a possibility of premature birth. Premature birth should be taken seriously at all stages.

3. Does a larger fetus mean a healthier baby?

40% of expectant mothers mistakenly believe: A larger fetus means a healthier baby.

A heavier fetus increases the risks and difficulties during delivery, raising the likelihood of prolonged labor, fetal hypoxia, meconium aspiration, arm or clavicle fractures. Sometimes vacuum extraction is required, which can cause injury to the fetus, and also increase the chances of maternal pelvic and perineal injuries. Therefore, a larger fetus is not necessarily better; a smooth delivery is what matters most.

After birth, babies who eat well naturally grow well. Maintaining normal weight during pregnancy is sufficient; overeating in the later stages to increase the baby's weight is unnecessary. Babies born with heavier weights do not necessarily grow up to be taller adults.

4. Are larger babies harder to deliver, while smaller ones easier?

25% of expectant mothers mistakenly believe: Larger babies are harder to deliver, while smaller ones are always easier.

Some expectant mothers think that a 3200g baby is definitely deliverable, while a 3800g one isn’t.

In fact, the size of the fetus does not have an absolute relationship with whether it can be delivered. Large fetuses can still be delivered, and sometimes smaller ones cannot. There are many reasons why a fetus might not be easily delivered, including the size of the pelvis, the strength of uterine contractions, the fetus's tolerance to pain, and the effectiveness of pushing. The size of the fetus is just one factor.

5. Do women with larger butts give birth more smoothly?

25% of expectant mothers mistakenly believe: Women with larger butts give birth more smoothly.

There has been a saying that "women with larger butts are good at giving birth."

This originates from the belief that a larger butt means a larger pelvis, making childbirth easier. In reality, whether childbirth is easy depends on the width and slope inside the pelvis, and a wider pelvic outlet. These cannot be judged by appearance. Some women with larger butts or lower bodies may not have a larger pelvis but rather more body fat, leading to higher risks of pregnancy-induced hypertension, difficult labor, and excessive fetal weight. Therefore, having a larger butt or lower body does not necessarily mean easier childbirth.

6. Is Cesarean section or natural birth better?

40% of expectant mothers mistakenly believe: Cesarean section is better or natural birth is better.

Neither method is inherently better; the choice depends on the individual situation of the expectant mother.

Generally, cases such as a large baby, small pelvis, macrosomia, placenta previa, and malpresentation require cesarean sections. If none of these issues exist, under normal circumstances, expectant mothers can opt for natural birth. Doctors generally recommend natural birth unless medical necessity dictates otherwise. Factors not suggested by doctors should be minimized as much as possible.

7. Must older mothers undergo cesarean sections?

40% of expectant mothers mistakenly believe: Older mothers must undergo cesarean sections.

Older mothers do not necessarily need cesarean sections. As long as the pelvis size and uterine contraction strength are normal, many older mothers can still give birth naturally. Sometimes, due to age, the flexibility of pelvic ligaments and muscles may be insufficient, causing longer labor times, but this does not necessarily mean cesarean sections are required. The key is for expectant mothers to exercise regularly, avoid allowing the fetus to grow too large, and cooperate with other conditions to achieve natural childbirth.

8. Is inducing labor harmful? Will the body naturally prepare for birth when the time comes?

50% of expectant mothers mistakenly believe: Inducing labor is harmful, and the body will naturally prepare for birth when the time comes.

Post-term pregnancy creates an unfavorable uterine environment, with insufficient amniotic fluid and poor placental function, making it unsuitable for the fetus to continue growing, posing a health risk. In such cases, inducing labor is a necessary measure and a preventive concept. Generally, waiting until the due date to give birth is ideal, but this excludes dangerous pregnancies. If the baby's head is slightly large after full term, early delivery is recommended to avoid surgery when the baby reaches 3-4 kilograms. Some expectant mothers agree to surgery only after failing to deliver naturally, resulting in stillbirths or fetal hypoxia. Pregnant women with preeclampsia may experience poor placental function, leading to fetal growth restriction and no weight gain, requiring induction. Waiting for the baby to be born naturally without intervention can be risky.

9. Does administering anesthesia during childbirth harm the spine?

70% of expectant mothers mistakenly believe: Administering anesthesia for painless delivery or cesarean sections harms the spine.

Painless delivery procedures are performed by obstetric anesthesiologists, injecting anesthetics into the outer layer of the spine. Anesthesia relaxes the pelvic muscles, reducing labor pain by over 90%, without harming the spine.

The timing for administering painless delivery is after the cervix has dilated two fingers wide and the uterine wall has thinned. Administering it earlier may delay labor. Occasionally, painless delivery can lower the mother's blood pressure, slightly affecting the fetal heartbeat. In such cases, observation lasts 15-20 minutes, and if abnormalities occur, intravenous medication is administered immediately, preventing permanent damage to the fetus.

Most back pain is caused by posture or hormonal changes, not directly related to painless delivery.

10. Is choosing an auspicious time for cesarean delivery based on superstition beneficial?

10% of expectant mothers mistakenly believe: Choosing an auspicious time for cesarean delivery based on superstition is beneficial.

Nowadays, some expectant mothers request cesarean sections to select a better birth time for their babies. However, babies born prematurely often have underdeveloped lungs, leading to breathing difficulties, posing a threat to the fetus's life. For a healthy baby, being born after full term is best.

If after full term, the expectant mother insists on a cesarean at a specific time, it is advisable not to schedule it at night in case of emergencies where manpower support might be insufficient.

Especially in years like this “Olympic year,” some expectant mothers might want to choose an auspicious day for their baby's birth for good luck. However, regarding the baby's birth, it is better not to force anything to prevent adverse effects on the baby.
