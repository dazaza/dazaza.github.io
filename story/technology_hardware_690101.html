Introduction of Ma Huateng

Ma Huateng (born October 29, 1971) is a male native of Chaoyang, Guangdong. He is the Executive Director, Chairman of the Board, and Chief Executive Officer (CEO) of Tencent Company, responsible for the overall strategy planning, positioning, and management of the group. As one of the main founders, he has been employed by the group since 1999. Before his current role, he was in charge of the research and development of Internet paging systems at Shenzhen Runxun Communications Development Co., Ltd., a supplier of telecommunications services and products.

In December 2009, the British Sunday Times published a list of "The World's Top Ten Most Recognizable Chinese People in the Next Decade," which included Ma Huateng, CEO of Tencent. The paper stated that these individuals will bring about a different world and are likely to become as well-known as American IT giants Bill Gates and Warren Buffett in the next decade.

Ma Huateng - Resume

Ma Huateng was born in October 1971 in Chaoyang, Guangdong.

In 1984, he moved with his parents from Hainan to Shenzhen.

From 1989 to 1993, he studied computer science at Shenzhen University.

After graduating from Shenzhen University in 1993, he joined Runxun Communication Development Co., Ltd., progressing from a software engineer specializing in pager software development to the head of the development department.

In 1998, he co-founded Tencent Computer System Co., Ltd.

In 2009, he was elected as one of China's top commercial leaders in the past decade.

In 2010, he ranked 249th in the Forbes billionaires list and 6th among mainland Chinese billionaires.

On May 14, 2010, he ranked 5th on the "2010 New Fortune 500 Rich List" with assets worth 33.62 billion RMB.

On October 21, 2010, he ranked second on the "2010 Hurun IT Rich List" with assets worth 29.3 billion RMB.

Ma Huateng - Professional Experience

Early Entrepreneurship of Ma Huateng

In November 1998, Ma Huateng co-founded Shenzhen Tencent Computer System Co., Ltd. with other founders. By June 2000, Tencent became profitable. In 2001, Ma Huateng licensed the Q-Gen brand to Guangzhou Donglihang for operation, and by 2003, they had over 100 specialty stores. At the end of 2003, Ma Huateng began operating an online game. He successfully transformed Tencent from a small company into one of China's leading Internet service providers. Ma Huateng has consistently led Tencent's development with practical focus and guided the innovation of its products and services. He previously worked at Runxun Communication Company, responsible for the research and development of Internet paging systems. Ma Huateng graduated from Shenzhen University's computer science program in 1992. Back then, he worked in various small companies, considered on the fringes of Shenzhen society. An accidental encounter introduced him to ICQ, an instant messaging tool developed by an Israeli, which Ma Huateng found fascinating.

Ma Huateng believed that ICQ could serve as a PC-based pager. At that time, mobile phones were symbols of great wealth, and having a pager was quite luxurious, making it easy to attract girlfriends. Ma Huateng thought ICQ could provide instant messaging on computers but had a major drawback: there was no Chinese version, making it inconvenient to use. Thus, Ma Huateng wanted to create a Chinese version of ICQ. He gathered a few friends to start a company, mimicking ICQ to create a Chinese version. I admire the keen insight and natural business acumen of people from Chaozhou.

Initially, Tencent faced many difficulties. Often, QQ used underhanded methods to steal bandwidth and space because they couldn't afford servers. However, the Chinese version of ICQ, OICQ, was extremely popular, with many downloads. In the year 2000, it was OICQ's winter and Tencent's winter as well. The first internet bubble swept across all of China's internet sector, affecting Tencent too. They tried to sell out.

A high-ranking Tencent official approached Sina and Chinanet. To them, 1 million RMB seemed like pocket change, but at that time, it felt like an astronomical sum to Tencent, enough to let go of their uncertain future. However, the executives of Sina and Chinanet politely asked the Tencent representative to close the door behind them. Now we are grateful that Sina and Chinanet did not spend 1 million RMB to eliminate this communication tool, QQ. At the time, they thought it was too expensive, just a simple chat box that severely infringed on the rights of ICQ, the Israeli product, demanding 1 million RMB. They decided not to buy it and would rather develop their own, as evidenced by Sina's UC later on.

After Ma Huateng's dream of becoming a beautiful bride faded, QQ realized that everything depended on itself. Ma Huateng almost invested all he had, unsure where the profit point lay. With so many registered users, what could they do? Could QQ only charge a pitiful registration fee to users applying for QQ numbers? (For a period, applying for a QQ number required payment, a testament to QQ's tough times.)

Importantly, venture capitalists saw potential in Tencent. With their investment, Tencent went public in Hong Kong. QQ lacked cash capital at the time; if anything, its capital was the vast number of QQ users, approximately 340 million. From that moment on, Tencent entered a new era—the capital era, which Ma Huateng dreamed of. After going public, Ma Huateng's personal net worth soared to 1.7 billion HKD! It is emphasized that this refers to when Tencent first went public. How much Ma Huateng is worth now is best asked directly of him.

According to reliable sources, Tencent's current market value is 3.3 billion USD, roughly equivalent to 27 billion RMB.

Ma Huateng unintentionally built a vast QQ empire, changing how Chinese people communicate. Phones, telephones, emails, and QQ have become essential tools for most Chinese people. Indeed, many young people can do without phones or emails but cannot live without QQ, where they find many like-minded people and enjoy numerous games. Starting from a certain period, Tencent adopted a diversified strategy, similar to Sina and 163, launching its own portal site qq.com, which quickly became China's fourth-largest portal site. Alibaba's Taobao runs smoothly, so Tencent launched its own paipai.com based on the promising C2C model in China. Chen Tianqiao's Shanda earns substantial profits from online gaming, so Tencent introduced QQ Fantasy and QQ Hall. Google and Baidu excel in search, which is the second-largest battleground in the internet industry, so Tencent developed its own search engine soso.com—Tencent expands its empire online like a network octopus, endlessly expanding. Offline, it does the same; its collaboration with Coca-Cola in promotional activities is a prime example. I advise everyone that in the near future, you may see QQ Tower or QQ Mall in Shenzhen, Guangzhou, Shanghai, or Beijing. This is just one step in the QQ development plan orchestrated by Ma Huateng, a powerful yet low-profile helmsman.

Ma Huateng, a mysterious and low-key person, does not enjoy the limelight like some corporate presidents. New QQ products always appear quietly before our eyes. In reality, every expansion of QQ effectively seals off competitors' throats. For instance, the launch of QQ Games marked the end of Hao Fang's once-unstoppable glory, burying that brilliance in the past. We wonder how many such stories will unfold as QQ continues to expand.

Challenges and Opportunities

Like other startups in the internet industry, funding and technology were the biggest issues for Tencent. "First, there was a lack of funds, and when funds were available, the software couldn't keep up."

In February 1999, Tencent developed the first "Chinese-flavored" ICQ, named Tencent "QQ," which was warmly welcomed by users. The number of registered users skyrocketed, reaching tens of thousands in a very short time. With the increase in users, they had to continuously expand their server capacity, but even the server hosting fees of a couple of thousand yuan were unbearable for the company at that time. "We could only use others' servers wherever we could, starting with a regular PC placed in a broadband-equipped room, then secretly placing the program on others' servers to run."

"At that time, we thought about selling it if we couldn't sustain it," Ma Huateng recalled with relief: "But we encountered trouble when trying to sell QQ. Many content providers (ICPs) wanted exclusive buyout rights." This made Ma Huateng, who originally planned to make money by selling QQ software to multiple companies, very hesitant. The most critical negotiation was with Shenzhen Telecom Data Bureau, who was willing to pay 600,000 RMB, but Ma Huateng insisted on 1 million RMB, and the deal fell through.

Unable to sell the software, but with rapid user growth, the investment required to operate QQ increased significantly. Ma Huateng had to raise funds everywhere. Banks said they hadn't heard of loans secured by "number of registered users"; domestic investors cared more about how many computers and other fixed assets Tencent had. In the second half of 1999, inspired by former internet friend Ding Lei's overseas financing, Ma Huateng started looking for foreign venture capital with a business plan revised six times and spanning over 20 pages, eventually meeting IDG and Pacific Century CyberWorks. "They invested 2.2 million USD, each holding 20% of the company's shares. When QQ reached 20 million users, the money hadn't been fully used."

With this funding, the company purchased a 200,000-yuan IBM server. "When placed on the table, my heart was filled with joy," Ma Huateng recalled, still delighted.

Ma Huateng believes that Chinese internet enterprises need "innovation" to rise. Ma Huateng explains that the internet industry is characterized by high knowledge density, high added value, and high industrial driving capability. Moreover, this industry has low energy consumption and low environmental pollution, "two lows." One can say that the internet service industry is not only a high-end service industry but also an environmentally friendly and green service industry.

In his speech, Ma Huateng points out that the internet industry is very new, changes rapidly, and has a short history.

Ma Huateng believes that the primary reason for the rise of local Chinese internet companies is indigenous innovation. Local companies can fully understand the characteristics and development patterns of Chinese users, developing applications and services truly suitable for Chinese netizens. This is the fundamental factor. In the future, international giants can also perform well, like Google recruiting many excellent talents, but localization is still necessary. In the future, we will see more high-end service industries opening up and entering the capital market. Without reforms in enterprise management mechanisms and talent attraction and incentive systems, it will be impossible to compete with more professional service-oriented international companies.

Building an Open Platform

On June 16, 2011, at Tencent's inaugural partner conference since its establishment 12 years ago, Ma Huateng appeared relaxed and declared, "This is a decision that wasn't difficult at all." He announced that apart from fully opening eight major platforms, the total scale of Tencent's Industry Win-win Fund for investments would double to reach 10 billion yuan. Tencent would also allocate 20 billion yuan, equivalent to its annual revenue at the time, to various third-party partners on the open platform, aiming to "recreate another Tencent." Since December 5 of the previous year, when Tencent announced entering a six-month strategic transformation preparation phase, all eight platforms—Tencent Friends, QQ Space, Tencent Weibo, Tenpay, e-commerce, Soso, Color Bean, and QQ—were fully opened. Tencent's hundreds of millions of users were successively made available to third-party partners, and a 5 billion yuan Tencent Industry Win-win Fund was established to invest in companies within the industry. To date, the Tencent Industry Win-win Fund has already invested over 2 billion yuan, and Tencent's open platform has attracted nearly 20,000 partners. Ma Huateng expressed hope that the number of partners would exceed 100,000 in the future.

When asked about Zhou Hongyi, chairman of Qihoo 360, who accused Tencent two weeks prior of being unwilling to cooperate with Qihoo 360 and thus being "fake open," Ma Huateng responded: "We must stick to our commitment, which is that we will never cooperate with 360. Moreover, 360 is not a developer, so there is no issue of whether they should connect with us. Simply selling ads cannot claim to be an open platform. If an open platform lacks experience and ability in application development, how can it ensure that partners make money?"

Ma Huateng - Overview of Tencent

Tencent was founded in Shenzhen in November 1998 and is one of China's earliest developers of internet instant messaging software. In February 1999, Tencent officially launched its first instant messaging software—"Tencent QQ"—and listed on the main board of the Hong Kong Stock Exchange on June 16, 2004 (stock code 700). Currently, Tencent has developed into one of China's largest providers of internet application services and mobile value-added services. In the future, Tencent will adhere to the spirit of innovation and excellence, with the mission of enhancing human life quality through internet services, gradually realizing its vision of becoming the "most respected internet enterprise." By September 21, 2007, Tencent became the first company in China's internet history to surpass a market capitalization of 10 billion USD.
