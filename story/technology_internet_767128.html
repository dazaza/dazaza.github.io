Microsoft Recycles Inactive Outlook Accounts, Not Stated in Service Agreement

by anonymous on 2013-10-08 18:19:52

Microsoft Recycles Inactive Outlook Accounts

Similar to Yahoo, Microsoft is also recycling email accounts without clearly informing users of this policy in the service agreements for Hotmail, Live, and Outlook.com.

Due to the recycling of unused email accounts, Yahoo faced strong criticism. Yahoo had to reassure users that it would take appropriate measures to prevent privacy breaches when recycled email accounts are reused.

In fact, Microsoft's Hotmail has been implementing a similar recycling policy for years. Recently, Microsoft confirmed to a Dutch media outlet named Webwereld that this policy has been extended to other Microsoft services, such as Live and Outlook.com.

Microsoft’s service agreement does mention that users must “log into their Microsoft account at least once every 270 days” to ensure continued service. Otherwise, Microsoft reserves the right to “cancel your account access” and “permanently delete your data from the servers.”

However, Microsoft did not mention that such email accounts could be reused. The company has since confirmed this possibility. In a statement, Microsoft pointed out that when an account becomes inactive, “the email account automatically enters the server deletion queue. Then, after a total of 360 days, the email account can be reused.”

Despite precautionary measures, some new users of webmail accounts have reported receiving highly sensitive emails intended for the previous user of the account. Recently, InformationWeek reported several such cases: new Yahoo account users received emails originally meant for the previous user, including emails from the latter’s friends and family.

A confused and frustrated user recently complained to Webwereld that his inbox frequently received private emails — these emails were intended for the previous user of the email address. Microsoft did not adequately inform the previous user about its email recycling policy or the potential privacy risks. This former user is considering filing a complaint with the Dutch Data Protection Authority CBP.

Mike Rispoli of Privacy International, based in London, pointed out that such complaints are beneficial as they raise awareness about privacy protection among people and relevant institutions.

Rispoli said, "When Yahoo announced its account recycling policy, experts warned and predicted serious security and privacy issues. Yahoo underestimated the risks of this policy and ignored public criticism, but it now appears that these concerns were not groundless."

Rispoli added, "Microsoft should absolutely make clear mention of this recycling policy in its service agreement so that users are fully aware of it."

International Privacy Organization has always been critical of account recycling policies. Rispoli pointed out, "These companies purely consider problems from a commercial perspective to improve their bottom line and attract new users, ignoring privacy and user rights. It is a serious issue of trust, and that trust has been broken."

Meanwhile, Google has confirmed to Webwereld that it does not recycle unused accounts. Google states on its website that users can delete their accounts, but doing so “will not release your username. Once you delete your Gmail address, you will not be able to use the same username again.”

Microsoft stated, “We carefully listen to user feedback and are constantly considering how to ensure that Outlook.com remains the best email service.”
