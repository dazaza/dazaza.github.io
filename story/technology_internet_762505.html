"The Inevitable 'Death of Yougou': Belle's E-commerce Has Many Fatal Flaws"

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 15:48:33

Recently, Yougou.com, which has been suffering from a serious loss of senior executives, was hit again. On July 17th, it was confirmed by an insider of Yougou that Zhang Xiaojun, the COO of Yougou, would resign and was currently processing his resignation. As one of the founding members of Yougou, Zhang Xiaojun's departure is particularly significant, indicating that Yougou is indeed facing a severe crisis.

Let us first review the e-commerce journey of Belle International, to which Yougou belongs:

A few years ago, Belle cautiously tested the waters in e-commerce, steadily developing through three channels: Taobao distribution, Tmall, and B2C distribution (B2C distribution was completed via Taixiu.com). Led by group veteran Xie Yunli, this relatively modest e-commerce team initially sold products under Belle’s own brands, subsequently launching exclusive online models, followed by new internet-exclusive brands. This development path was considered before 2011 as a more solid example of traditional brand companies successfully entering the e-commerce market compared to Li-Ning.

Following the over $100 million financing and rapid development of Letao and Haolemai in 2010, Belle Group, which supplied these two platforms, felt compelled to act. On one hand, they were worried about being constrained by distribution channels; on the other hand, they assessed their significant advantages in the e-commerce sector, including:

1. Numerous brands: Besides its own several women's shoe brands, Belle also distributes international renowned sports brands such as Adidas and Nike, whose online sales have been growing astonishingly fast;

2. Easier category expansion: For Belle, which specializes in women's shoes and agency for well-known sports brands, achieving absolute market advantage and status through multi-brand and multi-category strategies became the most thought-about issue in recent years. Expanding offline stores to introduce new brands and categories is evidently too slow, while the ongoing e-commerce business can easily expand brands but cannot quickly add categories;

3. Financial advantage: In collaborating with e-commerce enterprises, Belle realized that e-commerce is a capital-intensive business. With its financial strength, Belle does not need to rely on external capital for development, thus inevitably defeating many vertical e-commerce platforms dependent on capital, aiming to become the "last man standing";

4. Talent advantage: After years of practicing e-commerce, Belle has cultivated a highly professional and execution-oriented e-commerce team, capable of handling large-scale e-commerce operations or even more;

5. Supply chain advantage: This is what excites Belle internally and is the core reason for attracting top executives from brand e-commerce or platform e-commerce. After all, supply chain issues always captivate e-commerce operators, as it seems to be the life-and-death determining factor aside from funding. The supply chain includes product R&D, production, warehousing management, delivery, after-sales service, etc., all "business" content. In traditional enterprises emphasizing "commerce" over "electronics," this is regarded as the superior competitive advantage over e-commerce enterprises.

With these advantages in place, Belle's e-commerce decided to make a big move, starting with purchasing the domain name yougou.com,

abandoning the somewhat counterfeit "topshoes.cn" Taixiu website for redirection, stopping supplies to all distribution websites, while still retaining direct-operated third-party online stores like Taobao and Tmall, albeit with reduced investment and attention. High-profile recruitment brought JD.com VP and Vancl VP to serve as CMO and COO respectively, along with a group veteran appointed as CEO, forming a golden triangle. They aimed to build a full-category vertical B2C mall, implementing multi-brand strategies on one hand, and expanding into clothing and other categories on the other, realizing the dream of a department store empire impossible to achieve offline!

All sounds very promising, but every dream needs to face the harsh reality. The realities are:

1. Higher technical requirements for independent B2C: Based in Shenzhen headquarters, Belle could not recruit more excellent technical talents, and outsourcing technical services companies in Shenzhen were scarce. However, technology determines user experience, back-end management efficiency, and reliable data. Independent B2C involves dynamic technology, different from the relatively static ERP technology, and continuous changes remain a hurdle for many traditional enterprises;

2. Increased management difficulty due to category expansion: Someone once said that doing women's shoes well doesn't necessarily mean doing men's shoes well, something Belle probably deeply understands when expanding into men's shoes. It's easy to imagine the challenges at the backend when expanding into women's clothing and accessories within a year. For traditional brand enterprises accustomed to meticulous calculations, this rapid approach worked for Vancl during special periods, understandable within the systems of Vancl and JD.com. Although Belle employed Vancl's COO and JD.com's CMO, whether they can understand and execute with "exaggerated costs" remains questionable;

3. System: Many people don’t understand the conflict between e-commerce and traditional enterprises. Simply put, departmental settings (organizational structure), approvals, finance and budget, reporting methods, corporate culture, communication methods, execution methods, risk control, processes, and systems form an enterprise's so-called "mechanism." This clash results in e-commerce trying to convince traditional teams to accept e-commerce "systems," while traditional teams struggle to understand e-commerce "systems," leading to inefficiency and instability of the execution team. Ultimately, the chairman or board may say: "E-commerce and traditional enterprises each have strengths and weaknesses, the future will definitely involve integration, so you must integrate in these aspects...". If this so-called "integration without harmony" occurs, it leads to so-called management innovation, and even "CEO rotation system" appeared in a company like Belle!

It was originally believed that Yougou of Belle e-commerce had taken the lead, and the senior executives were waiting for a spin-off IPO to enjoy success, honor, and wealth. Instead, they are now entangled in these hard injuries.

After all, whether it is the veterans who grew up with Belle or the recruited e-commerce professionals, it can be seen that they did not leave because of lack of ability. When the VP from JD.com returns to JD, he can continue to deliver remarkable performance, and when the VP from Vancl has the courage to start a business. Are these hard injuries limited to the above three points? Or are these not only Belle's hard injuries but also those of many traditional enterprises attempting to enter the e-commerce field?
