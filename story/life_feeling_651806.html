Those unforgettable pasts, have they been fine since we parted?

by gege on 2012-01-03 00:33:51

Those unforgettable pasts, are they still fine after all these years?

For the insights into the mundane world, they are too real, and I am bewildered. Yet, I will make room in my heart for a pure white background to continue my own tranquility. Thinking of those microscopic creatures floating in the dust, so tiny, living and dying within a day, going through a seasonal cycle. And me, I am ultimately just an ordinary person, trying to live out the rest of my life peacefully in this complicated world, being born naturally and aging naturally.

Day after day, the hustle and bustle of early morning departures and late evening returns, not knowing why I live. Sadness arises at unpredictable moments, spreading bit by bit in my heart. Walking down the streets of the bridge town, under the dazzling neon lights, my shadow is precisely stepped on beneath my feet, caught between the reflection and the body, I hesitate to move forward. A few passers-by glance sideways as they brush past me. I catch the familiar scent of a floral soap from a child's body. Turning around, I see those clear eyes, untouched by the world, their purity moves me suddenly.

While waiting at the bus stop, I see that woman who was wearing red flower-patterned clothes a year ago and appeared mentally unstable. Now she looks completely worn out, her clothes tattered and dirty, her hair messy. She drags her heavy steps along the roadside, occasionally smiling foolishly at passers-by. Speculations about the reasons for her condition have long spread throughout the streets and alleys. I only remember that many years ago, she kindly sold her goods with two children, quietly smiling at the crossroads. The two images in my memory cannot overlap, yet they are inadvertently juxtaposed, like the thickness of a layer of sugar cake, measuring her past and present.

After days of leisure, life amidst the busy scenes becomes calm. After much thought, I board the bus heading to the small town where my friend once took me. During the long journey, we pass vast green fields. At that time, your words echoed in my ears, gradually becoming clearer. This long walk is a kind of reminiscence, thread by thread, like the countless efforts I made to weave a complete scarf, interlocking rings, spreading boundlessly in my heart.

The small town I once visited is a place full of vitality and elegance. People passing by all smile at me, a warmth that cannot be resisted silently penetrates my heart. Walking on the stone pavement, I feel the presence of you who once existed here. At the beginning of this year, you took me, climbing mountains and crossing rivers, to this secluded little town. You said it's called Huo Town, where you were born, your expressions serene. You smiled and recounted your childhood memories, the integration of mountains and waters, a process of happiness and satisfaction, making me repeatedly immerse myself in this town filled with your happy recollections.

Walking silently through this path, I begin to miss with peace. Youth, in the end, must come to its conclusion. I have become accustomed to my unchanging silence and plainness, neither overly joyful nor overly sorrowful. During the day, I hustle and bustle alone, eating alone; at night, I read and write quietly alone. Life passes simply. However, when twilight approaches, my heart feels empty. It has become a habit to enter the fruit shop and buy food that I like at first sight, enjoying the warmth of possessing them, at least so that I am not left with nothing.

It's the autumnal equinox, and I take out the pinkish-purple grapes from the refrigerator to fill my stomach. Their honey-like juice is rich and sweet, the cold taste melting in my lips, a slight coolness, a faint chill. As the years increase, I prefer the light original flavor in food. Life unfolds like a series of paintings, with warmth and coldness known only to oneself.

Faint times leave some marks that cannot be erased. There is beauty, but it is too fleeting, like a sudden flash of brilliance, consumed fold by fold in the depths of pity. In the end, what can remain is only the deep and shallow images in memories. Year after year, doing one thing: forgetting.

In the afternoon, I met a friend I haven't seen for many years on the bus. His appearance hasn't changed much, and I recognized him instantly, calling his name softly. After talking about many people and events, it turns out that we who live in the same city have never had such an unexpected encounter before. Most of our time together was spent in silence. However, when we parted ways, looking at his eyes filled with happiness, I saw the panic hidden deep in my own heart. Under the streetlights in the drizzling rain, I shivered slightly and walked toward the depth.

At 12:10 AM, a song begins and ends with the tapping of fingers. In the early morning, I like to softly sing to myself.

Are those unforgettable pasts still fine after all these years?
