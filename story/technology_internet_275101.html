The Hundred-Year Evolution of Business Districts: Chinese Commerce Over a Century, Rising and Falling with Politics

by 1ejp159z9m on 2009-11-24 10:50:31

The factor of networking can, of course, bring more business opportunities to entrepreneurs. But what is the driving force beyond that? If the social turmoil and regime change before the founding of new China prompted merchants to establish their own organizations for mutual support due to uncertainty, then where does this driving force come from today? The Cambridge History of China says, "In this long history of China, the merchant class is only a supporting role." The commercial trajectory since modern times shows that the power of commerce rises and falls with politics. The changes in business circles reflect the hundred-year history of a country... There are the dim lights of the Qiao Family Compound of the Jin merchants, the red top hats of Hu Xueyan of the Anhui merchants, the ferries trading at Ningbo Zhoushan Port, and the sailing ships of Chao-Shan people going south to Nanyang. Here, is the source of the history of business circles.

At the end of the Ming Dynasty and the beginning of the Qing Dynasty, the establishment of large commercial groups for the first time concentrated merchants on a large scale.

In the following hundred years or so, the business circle was like a running child, enduring many hardships, undergoing thousands of changes in different social environments, even taking wrong turns. Then, continue forward. History always repeats itself. Today, those once most primitive business circles have been repackaged and brought back on stage. This cycle is a memory of the business circle. At the end of the Ming Dynasty and the beginning of the Qing Dynasty: the collective feast of fellow merchants

Representative business circles: Jin merchants, Ningbo group, etc. Once the commodity economy arises in China, civilian forces quickly intervene and take the dominant position. At the end of the Ming Dynasty and the beginning of the Qing Dynasty, people gradually abandoned the idea of prioritizing agriculture over commerce and engaged in business. With the gradual drive for greater commercial interests, large commercial groups were subsequently established. There is an example: during the middle period of the development of the Jin merchants, southern warfare disrupted the tea trade routes. Ordinary grain business made one-tenth profit, but tea business could make three-tenths net profit. Driven by huge profits, the Jin merchants formed different groups to conspire for financial gain. In "Qiao's Compound," Qiao Zhiyong carried the shares of Jin merchants from Qi County and successfully opened up the tea trade route, winning the monopoly position in the tea market for the Jin merchants.

Indeed, under the large-scale operation of commercial groups, the Jin merchants not only monopolized the tea business but also monopolized businesses such as salt and draft notes. Later, the handicraft trade of the Huizhou merchants, the steamship freight and hardware industries of the Ningbo group, and others developed and grew accordingly.

These commercial groups were established through family ties, blood relations, or native-place connections. To increase overall competitiveness, they supported each other, expanded commercial capital, and achieved shared benefits. At that time, there were nine major merchant groups nationwide. They raised funds themselves and established public institutions in various places to facilitate contact among members of their respective groups dispersed across different locations.

At the end of the 19th century, the history of the merchant groups began to be rewritten. At that time, the world's attention began to focus on the narrow Wall Street in New York. Oil tycoon Rockefeller, financial magnate JP Morgan, and the ruler of the steel empire Carnegie quickly rose. In Japan, private modern enterprises rapidly emerged, and a businessman named Yanosuke Iwasaki bought the Nagasaki Shipyard from the government for 1 yen, which later developed into the famous Mitsubishi Corporation. While these giants were crazily developing their domestic economies, they also began dumping goods in China.

After the Opium War, a large amount of foreign capital and goods flooded the Chinese commercial market. Foreign goods were of superior quality and low price, quickly becoming the mainstay of the market. Hu Xueyan, a Huizhou merchant, lost all his capital in the silk trade battle with Westerners. Subsequently, the Jiangsu-Zhejiang silk merchant alliance tied to Hu Xueyan disintegrated and fell one after another; far away in Shanxi, the Qiao Family Compound also became desolate due to its inability to keep up with advanced management systems. Traditional merchant groups ended their collective prosperity because they were stuck in feudal commercial operating methods and did not accurately judge or innovate new commercial forms.

At this time, the modern merchant group represented by Ningbo stepped forward. After the opening of Ningbo port, Ningbo merchants quickly integrated into the vast import and export trade industry. Generations of Ningbo merchants supported each other and successively came to Shanghai. They started their businesses in Shanghai through introductions from relatives and friends within their merchant groups.

By the end of the Qing Dynasty, Ningbo people had already opened 20 hardware shops in Shanghai, accounting for a total of 25 shops at the time; there were 150 shops on Nanjing Road in Shanghai, 50 of which were opened by Ningbo people. By the 1930s, the total tonnage of ships controlled by Ningbo businessman Yu Qiaqing accounted for 13% of the nation. Someone calculated that at the time, there were 1836 well-known figures in the Shanghai business community, 453 of whom were from Ningbo.

Famous figures in the Ningbo group quickly established their own circle in Shanghai —— the "Ningbo Travelers' Association in Shanghai." Among them, famous entrepreneurs include Yan Xinhou, Ye Chengzhong, Yu Qiaqing, Zhu Baosan, Liu Hongsheng, etc.

In 1904, the luxurious business circle mainly led by the Ningbo group —— the Shanghai General Chamber of Commerce (then called the Shanghai Business General Association) was established.
