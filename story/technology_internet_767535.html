After 180 Websites, I’m Ready to Start the Rest of My Life as a Coder

I previously translated Jennifer Dewalt's blog post from day 115. Her post at that time garnered over 2,000 points on Hacker News and sparked heated discussions. In September, I also checked her blog for updates and found she had reached over 160 projects. On September 27th, Jennifer updated her blog, announcing that she had finally completed her goal, feeling extremely happy and excited, almost indescribably so. Her 180th small project was "Hello World," which is essentially a celebratory program asking for likes. After sticking with it for 180 days, she described herself as "completely overwhelmed" and needed some time to catch up on sleep, relax, and consolidate what she had learned. The following content is another blog post she wrote on October 3rd, revealing her thoughts after completing 180 days of coding.

I learned programming by building 180 websites in 180 days, and now I really want a vacation! Over the past six months, I pulled countless all-nighters, often feeling exhausted from failures, but all of this was worth it compared to achieving my goal.

All the details I mentioned can be found in this blog post, but for those who don't have time to read it (TL;DR), here’s the short version: without any prior programming experience, I decided to learn programming. I didn’t enroll in any courses or boot camps; instead, I chose the JFDI method and followed these three rules:

- Build a different website every day for 180 consecutive days.

- Write a blog post for each website you build.

- Make all your code publicly available on GitHub so everyone can see it.

(Translator's note: JFDI = "Just Fucking Do It"; TL;DR = Too Long; Didn’t Read)

Background

I studied art. What attracted me to studying art was that it provided a platform to explore how people communicate and interact. In the past, I explored interpersonal communication through sculpture and painting. But soon, I began considering technical methods.

Technology, especially the internet, has opened up countless amazing ways for us to communicate, allowing us to spread and consume vast amounts of information. In my view, the internet is fantastic, and I became somewhat obsessed with the way it enables communication through computers. However, not everyone shares my passion for the internet. How could I make them aware of its charm? I realized that my paintings couldn’t depict the power of the internet. I wanted to immerse myself in it and personally create these artistic forms of communication.

From CSS to JavaScript, Rails, and Node.js

In the beginning, I used a little CSS and JavaScript knowledge to create simple websites. Soon, I started experimenting with complex CSS animations. By day 37, I began exploring the element. On day 69, I built my first Rails website, allowing users to interact with each other. By day 119, I created my first backend application, though I admit I didn’t fully understand it. Throughout the project, I made games, toys, and tools. I worked with external data, using APIs from companies like Instagram and Twitter, and built real-time communication applications using Node.js.

How Did I Do It?

For me, the most important principle was “Start Small. Keep Building.” When I designed this project, I knew I wanted to learn about dynamic interactive websites, but I also knew I had no idea how to write code. Instead of attempting to build a complex, fully functional social website, which would require an enormous amount of work, I took a small step forward. On the first day, I wrote a bit of HTML and CSS to create the homepage of the project.

I spent an entire day Googling just to find out a little bit about markup language. I could have used books or tutorials, but instead, I only looked up what I needed on sites like StackOverflow, MDN, and CSS Tricks, or blogs and examples throughout the entire project. I also used some excellent online guides, such as the Rails Tutorial, which helped me gradually build complex websites from scratch.

Completing small goals gave me manageable problems to solve, and these problems led to new small goals and even smaller problems. Eventually, after solving many small problems, my perspective on them changed. For example, by day 100, small problems were no longer as daunting as they were on day one. Every step counts, and eventually, I achieved a dynamic website.

What Else Was Essential?

Maintaining a strict one-day minimum deadline helped keep progress rapid. Having to produce something every day forced me not to get stuck trying to understand every detail. As a beginner, it's easy to get bogged down in confusion, obsessing over a concept for hours, trying to understand it. I discovered that learning to code is a bit like doing a jigsaw puzzle. You can study a single piece in detail, but it won't tell you where it fits or what the whole picture looks like. You need to gather several pieces and start putting them together to begin forming the larger picture.

What Was Unnecessary?

At the beginning of the project, I put immense pressure on myself to achieve perfection. I overly focused on whether every detail was correct, and if it didn’t match my vision, I felt like a complete failure. The result was that I nearly drove myself crazy. Displaying all my work on the screen made it look terrible, but I had to learn to accept imperfection. Nothing is perfect, especially when you're learning, but imperfection doesn't mean failure. Throughout the project, I found myself obsessed with adding all possible features, staying up until 3 a.m., trying to implement everything. I even lost sleep over some minor functionalities.

What’s Next?

Keep coding! The end of 180 days and 180 websites marks the beginning of my life as a coder. This project hasn’t given me a comprehensive understanding of software development, but it has provided a solid foundation to continue improving. I plan to research more complex websites that take more than two days to complete. Building larger websites will allow me to fill in gaps from earlier stages. Once I feel I've caught up enough, I’d like to join small teams and work with passionate individuals who are equally enthusiastic about inventing new things. During this time, I will continue writing about my learning progress on my blog and keeping my code public on GitHub.
