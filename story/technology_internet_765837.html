Windows' success has instilled in Microsoft some bad habits that are difficult to overcome.

by anonymous on 2013-08-26 14:08:29

Any big company that once dominated the market can be crushed by the wheels of history. Microsoft, despite still being profitable, has gradually lost its former glory. The success of Windows has instilled many hard-to-overcome bad habits within Microsoft, and these habits have been dragging it down, leading to its gradual decline.

According to foreign media reports, for years, Microsoft observers have always been curious about when Steve Ballmer would step down. Yesterday, we finally got the answer. Ballmer has been at Microsoft for 33 years; he joined in 1980 and became CEO in 2000. Now, he has announced that he will retire within the next 12 months after a successor is appointed.

If you don't pay much attention to Microsoft's stock price, then Ballmer's legacy becomes far more complex and cannot be fully described with a single chart, a Twitter message, or a blog post.

I am not one of Ballmer's harshest critics, partly because I believe that all tech companies' dominance will eventually wane—it’s just a matter of time. The history of tech companies is littered with giants that once defined markets: Lotus, WordPerfect, Netscape, and Palm, among others. In 2013, Microsoft hasn’t fallen so far as to join their ranks yet; on the contrary, it remains highly profitable and competitive in many areas, from general enterprise software to blockbuster video games.

Most importantly, I believe that over these years, even if someone else had been at the helm—Bill Gates himself, perhaps—it wouldn’t have run significantly better than under Ballmer.

However, I find it hard to imagine anyone still believing that Microsoft will regain the same level of dominance in the future personal computing space that it held for the first 40 years. Ballmer doesn’t think so either. Last month, he initiated a major corporate restructuring aimed at transforming Microsoft into a "devices and services" company. These moves suggest that even if Microsoft succeeds in the future, it won’t be the Microsoft we know today.

Just like this restructuring, some of the most notable moments in Microsoft’s recent years have included sudden, epoch-making changes. Ballmer's company did many unexpected things, from giving Windows 8 an entirely new interface to producing its own PC hardware. The extent of these changes made many consumers struggle to adapt, as they value familiarity in major product updates.

If Microsoft’s behavior seems like it’s trying to make up for lost time, it’s because it needs to do exactly that. Microsoft lost out to Apple and Google in defining the future of personal computing for one reason: Windows was too successful for too long. At some point in Microsoft's development, the goose that laid golden eggs daily turned into a heavy burden.

The era of Windows—from Windows 3.0 in 1990 to today’s desktop and laptop computers—has brought billions of dollars in returns to Microsoft and its investors. But it also instilled many hard-to-overcome bad habits in Microsoft.

For example:

1. The success of Windows led to Microsoft's arrogance.

From the very beginning, Microsoft was never a company lacking in confidence. Its stubbornness paid off in defeating competitors in fields such as PC operating systems and office software. However, when the competition was gone, Microsoft failed to take seriously any serious threats that could shake its personal computer dominance. (I don’t know whether Ballmer’s act of snatching an employee's iPhone and posing as if to crush it was playful or genuinely angry, but either way, it's regrettable.) There were also times when the company treated paying customers poorly.

2. The success of Windows made Microsoft narrow-minded.

In its early days, Microsoft had an amazing ability to keenly identify important trends and seize opportunities, from microcomputers to tablets to smartwatches. However, it always viewed them as natural extensions of the Windows empire, leading to poor product design decisions, such as having a mini Start button on the Windows Mobile operating system. After Apple launched the iPhone—a device that looked and functioned nothing like a Mac—Microsoft realized that new products don't have to resemble old ones. This realization ultimately led to the development of the Metro interface for Windows 8 and Windows Phone.

3. The success of Windows led to Microsoft's laziness.

In the past decade, when Bill Gates was still serving as Chief Software Architect and involved in daily operations, Microsoft hit a low point. During this time, it released Windows Vista, which barely addressed the numerous bugs that needed fixing in Windows. It also allowed Internet Explorer 6 to become outdated and useless without taking action.

4. The success of Windows gave Microsoft plenty of funds to waste.

For a long time, Microsoft has been known as a patient company, willing to spend years immersed in the market before making significant developments or becoming profitable. Sometimes, this approach paid off handsomely, as seen with Microsoft Office. However, with enough funds to try various things, Microsoft now faces the challenge of selecting the right battlegrounds and establishing its brand. For instance, Bing was introduced as the only real competitor to Google's search engine. However, it’s difficult to see how much benefit Microsoft has gained from its search business. In most cases, it's just a money-sucking black hole and a distraction.

Of course, Ballmer bears partial or full responsibility for every strategic decision Microsoft made in this century. Therefore, I wouldn’t absolve Ballmer by blaming all of Microsoft's shortcomings on the excessive success of Windows. But I believe that anyone else in charge of Microsoft might have made the same mistakes; besides, some of them might not have been as enthusiastic as Ballmer was in recent years about implementing large and surprising changes.

Looking back, it’s easy to see what Microsoft should have done but didn’t. If Microsoft had spent less time defending and expanding Windows' territory and more time thinking about how to replace Windows with something shinier and more useful for the next generation, the company might have been in a better position. It should have attacked its monopoly status with the same vigor it used against Lotus, WordPerfect, and Netscape. However, Ballmer didn’t do so when he could have, and it’s already too late for his successor to do so now.
