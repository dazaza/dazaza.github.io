**Hong Kong Company FAQs**

**Q: What are the requirements for establishing a limited company in Hong Kong?**

**A:** At least one shareholder aged 18 or above. Mainland Chinese citizens or overseas individuals with a passport or ID card are eligible. A registered address and a statutory secretary for the limited company (usually provided by us) are also required.

**Q: How do I proceed with setting up a Hong Kong company?**

**A:** You just need to fill out a power of attorney form. The three main aspects covered in the power of attorney are:

1. Confirming the Chinese and English names of the company.

2. Determining the registered capital amount, which has a minimum of HKD 10,000 (typically divided into 10,000 shares). The full amount does not need to be paid upfront but can be increased as needed.

3. Submitting shareholder information and a copy of their ID. If there are more than two shareholders, not all of them need to become directors, but there must be at least one director. After receiving these three pieces of information from you, we will prepare the complete legal documents for your signature within the next day. The process is usually completed within 10 working days after signing the documents.

**Q: How long does it take to establish a new company? How long if I buy an existing shell company? Can I change the name of an existing company? How long does that take?**

**A:** Establishing a new Hong Kong company takes about 10 working days after signing all the legal documents. Purchasing an existing company can be completed in 1-2 working days, and expedited services can finish it on the same day. Setting up a branch office takes 3 working days. After purchasing an existing company, you can change its name, which requires 15 working days.

**Q: What is the difference between a shareholder and a director?**

**A:** Shareholders are investors in a joint-stock company, while directors are elected by shareholders and manage the company. The chairman of the board is responsible for the board of directors. Often, shareholders also serve as directors.

**Q: What are the differences between choosing a company name in Hong Kong versus mainland China?**

**A:** Naming a company in Hong Kong is relatively free, and companies that are difficult to register in mainland China can often be registered in Hong Kong as long as the name is not duplicated. Regardless of the size of the registered capital, the Hong Kong government allows the use of words like "international," "group," "holdings," "industries," "investment," "college," "association," "center," "foundation," and "institute" in company names. You can also add preferred place names (e.g., France, USA, Italy, Japan, Hong Kong, Shanghai) or even use your own name or hometown name in the company title, allowing for personalized naming.

**Q: Are there any restrictions on the business scope for Hong Kong limited companies?**

**A:** Hong Kong is one of the freest trading ports in the world, and there are no significant restrictions on business scope. Companies can engage in any type of business, such as finance and investment, healthcare, shipping, import/export trade, real estate, construction, interior decoration, information technology, clothing textiles, tourism, cultural publishing, etc. Many small and medium-sized enterprise owners prefer not to specify the business scope to avoid limiting their operations.

**Q: Where is the registered capital reflected for a Hong Kong company? Is it necessary to pay the full amount upfront?**

**A:** The minimum registered capital for a Hong Kong limited liability company is HKD 10,000, and there is no requirement to pay it upfront (no capital verification needed). The registered capital is only reflected in the company's articles of association. To prove your registered capital, showing the articles of association is sufficient. Alternatively, our accountant can issue a certificate of true copies.

**A:** The services include:

1. Government certificate fees (Company Registration Certificate, Business Registration Certificate)

2. Printed materials (18 copies of the articles of association, 1 share certificate book, 1 shareholder register, 1 set of application documents)

3. Seal costs (1 signature seal, 1 steel seal, 1 alteration seal)

4. One year of registered office address service

5. One year of statutory secretary service for the limited company

6. Miscellaneous fees (document fees, courier fees, travel expenses)

7. Professional accounting service fees

Cash payments can be made at locations such as Hong Kong, Los Angeles, Kuala Lumpur, Shenzhen, Guangzhou, Chengdu, Yiwu, etc. Overseas clients can remit funds to our Hong Kong account, while mainland clients can remit to our Shenzhen account. **Q: After establishing a Hong Kong company, what annual fees are required?**

**A:** There are two main annual fees: Annual review and annual report submission.

1. Tax Bureau annual review and Company Registry annual report fees are mandatory annual payments. Prices may fluctuate based on government adjustments, typically ranging between HKD 3,000 and 5,000. These fees must be paid even if the company has no business activities.

2. Accounting and auditing services are required if the company has commenced operations and generated business transactions. If the company has no business activities, accounting and auditing services are not required, but tax filing is still mandatory.

**Q: Who is responsible for the annual accounting and auditing of a Hong Kong company? What documents are required if you handle it for us? What are the specific charges and how long does it take?**

**A:** Accounting can be done by the company itself or entrusted to a Hong Kong accounting firm. However, auditing must be handled by certified accountants or auditors. If we handle it for you, you need to provide all business records, including ledgers, invoices, receipts, payment vouchers, bank statements, accounts receivable, and accounts payable. Generally, it takes about one month to organize the accounts and 1-2 months to complete the audit.

**Q: What is the difference between statutory secretary services and business secretary services provided by you?**

**A:** According to government regulations, a limited company must have a statutory secretary, who can be either a local individual or entity in Hong Kong. We can arrange a statutory secretary for you. The statutory secretary is a formal secretary required by the Hong Kong government for communication and handling official documents.

Business secretary services are tailored to client needs and include practical business services such as answering calls, relaying messages, sending/receiving mail, handling letters of credit, logistics, etc. The advantage of business secretary services is that they help build your corporate image, making it seem as though you have employees and an office in Hong Kong, along with a full set of company resources. This ensures smooth business operations without missing any opportunities.

**A:** Our business secretary services are specifically designed for this purpose. Please refer to the detailed business secretary service descriptions for more information.

**Q: How does Hong Kong tax businesses?**

**A:** Hong Kong is a low-taxation free port with few tax items. The only annual tax a company must pay is profits tax, which is calculated at 17.5% of the company's net profit. For example, if a company generates HKD 10 million in revenue with a gross profit of HKD 2 million and a net profit of HKD 1 million, the profits tax owed would be HKD 175,000 (1 million x 17.5%). If the company does not generate a profit, no tax is due.

**Q: Are taxes imposed on goods entering or leaving Hong Kong?**

**A:** Except for tobacco and alcohol, goods entering or leaving Hong Kong are generally not taxed. However, all imports and exports must be declared through customs. Customs fees are charged at 0.5% of the actual value of goods up to HKD 470,000 and 0.3% for values exceeding HKD 470,000.

**Q: Are there any taxes when establishing a company?**

**A:** Yes. When establishing a Hong Kong company, you must pay stamp duty on the registered capital. The stamp duty is calculated at 1/1000 of the registered capital. For example, if the registered capital is HKD 1 million, the stamp duty would be HKD 1,000, which is paid once during registration and does not need to be paid again afterward.

**Q: Which department issues invoices and receipts for Hong Kong companies?**

**A:** Invoices and receipts for Hong Kong companies are not issued by the tax authority but are printed and issued by the company's board of directors based on the nature of the business. Invoices, receipts, and other documents become effective once they are stamped with the company seal and signed by the responsible person.

**Q: Can all shareholders of my Hong Kong company be residents of mainland China if I plan to establish a foreign-invested joint venture or wholly-owned enterprise in mainland China?**

**A:** Generally, a Hong Kong company can consist entirely of mainland shareholders. However, if you plan to establish a foreign-invested joint venture or wholly-owned enterprise, the Ministry of Commerce usually requires at least one overseas shareholder to sign the relevant documents. Therefore, having an overseas shareholder in the company is advisable.

**Q: Can our newly established Hong Kong company directly apply for a foreign-invested joint venture or wholly-owned enterprise in mainland China?**

**A:** Generally, foreign investors coming to China must have been in operation for more than one year. Specific policies depend on local regulations.

**Q: Can a company account be opened in a domestic foreign bank if registered in Hong Kong? For example, through a Hong Kong bank’s branch in Shenzhen?**

**A:** Yes. After incorporation, company directors can open an offshore account at a domestic foreign bank using their mainland ID. However, the director must personally visit the bank to open the account.

**Q: How are daily tasks such as tax filings handled for Hong Kong companies? Can we entrust you with these tasks? What are the annual fees?**

**A:** In Hong Kong, tax filings are done annually. The cost depends on your company's specific situation. If you choose our business secretary service, tax filing is included at no extra charge. However, accounting and auditing services require separate payment, which is determined by turnover and the volume of documents. If the company has no business activities, no additional fees are incurred.

**Q: How is the tax rate calculated for Hong Kong companies? What is the deadline for filing taxes?**

**A:** The tax rate in Hong Kong is calculated at 17.5% of actual profits. Hong Kong companies typically use March 31st as the fiscal year-end, and you can file taxes up to one year later.

**Q: What do the terms "CORP" for business nature and "BODY CORPORATE" for legal status mean on the business registration certificate?**

**A:** "CORP" in the business nature section means there are no specific business limitations, and the company can engage in any business allowed under the Companies Ordinance. Alternatively, you can specify industries such as electronics, chemicals, or trade. "BODY CORPORATE" refers to the corporate entity and represents its legal status.

**Q: How can I rent an office in Hong Kong?**

**Q: Can I check the background of a Hong Kong limited company online?**

**A:** Currently, this is not possible. Anyone with questions about a company can visit the Hong Kong Companies Registry in-person at the address in Admiralty.

**Q: After establishing a publishing house, how can I legally publish books?**

**A:** After establishing a publishing house, you are qualified to apply for publishing. However, two additional steps are required: applying for an International Standard Book Number (ISBN) or International Standard Serial Number (ISSN), and registering as a publisher before publishing. Completing these steps will allow you to legally publish books. If you wish to distribute in mainland China, you can apply to the relevant authorities using your Hong Kong publishing house credentials.

**Q: What are ISBN and ISSN? What is the difference between them?**

**A:** ISBN stands for International Standard Book Number, and ISSN stands for International Standard Serial Number. Having these numbers indicates that your publications meet international standards and are registered with the respective international organizations. You can use these numbers to query publication background information. The fundamental difference lies in the target audience: ISBN is for book publishers, while ISSN is for magazine and journal publishers.

**Q: If my business performs poorly, how much do I need to pay annually if I apply for temporary suspension?**

**A:** A Hong Kong company can apply for temporary suspension at any time. Besides paying the annual report fee to the Companies Registry and the annual review fee to the Inland Revenue Department, no other fees are required. If market conditions improve, the company can resume operations immediately, saving considerable costs.

**Q: Can my Hong Kong company be dissolved?**

**A:** Yes. If your company is no longer in use and you do not cancel it, the Hong Kong government will assume that it continues to exist and operates illegally. The government will impose cumulative fines on illegally operating companies, and severe cases may result in imprisonment for the board of directors. Since dissolving a company also incurs fees, we recommend selling the company as the best option.
