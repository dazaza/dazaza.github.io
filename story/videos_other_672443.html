Screen Printing and Pad Printing Knowledge

A: Pad printing is a printing technique that uses a flexible silicone pad as an intermediary to transfer an image from an etched plate onto an object.

B: During pad printing, the depth of the etched plate, the hardness, size, shape of the silicone pad, the characteristics of the ink (such as evaporation speed), the surface features of the object being printed on (smooth or rough, degree of curvature), and the control of the working environment (temperature, color, humidity, dust prevention) must all be properly managed and adjusted for successful printing.

C: Judging the depth of the etched plate for suitability:

1. Use a depth gauge to measure; standard pad printing plates are around 2.4 microns deep. For fine text and lines, 2 microns may suffice. For solid graphics, up to 3 microns is suitable, while halftones at 1.6 microns might be too deep.

2. The actual depth required can vary based on the pattern and ink layer requirements. For example, if the surface to be printed on is rough, a deeper plate may be necessary compared to smooth surfaces. Issues such as ink returning (ink not fully transferring), blurry images, bubbles (pinholes), or dried ink on the pad before printing could indicate problems with plate depth.

D: Selection of the silicone pad:

1. Elasticity

2. Curvature

3. Not infinitely large - pad printing isn't suitable for large area prints but works well for irregular shapes and uneven surfaces.

Currently, pad colors are varied (red, white, yellow, blue) mainly due to the raw materials used in production. From a technical standpoint, the color does not affect the pad's ability to pick up and release ink; instead, the material and manufacturing process are more critical.

E: Ink Characteristics:

Pad printing requires quick-drying inks to ensure proper transfer from the pad to the object. Screen printing requires slower drying inks to prevent clogging of the mesh. This distinction highlights differences between screen and pad printing solvents. However, under certain conditions (e.g., dry, high-temperature environments), screen printing inks can be used for pad printing.

F: Why do air bubbles (pinholes) often appear during printing?

This issue primarily relates to three aspects: the silicone pad, the etched plate, and the ink.

1. Incorrect pad shape, too flat or wrong curvature.

2. The highest point of the pad center aligns directly with the image, trapping air and creating bubbles.

3. Aging pads become sticky, damaged, or dirty, affecting print quality.

4. Insufficient ink thickness on the plate.

5. Ink is too thin or too slow-drying.

6. Ink is too thick or too fast-drying.

G: Pad Printing Work Environment:

Temperature (best at 18-20°C). Humidity (best at 60%-70%). Dust prevention. Light-colored walls and floors.

For beginners, it's recommended to start with single-color machines, small patterns, pointed round pads, and easy-to-print materials like PVC or PS. Light-colored or white backgrounds work best initially. If results aren't satisfactory, adjustments can be made by changing the pad, plate, or ink.

Common Issues in Screen Printing:

1. What are the advantages and disadvantages of UV ink compared to traditional solvent-based inks?

(1) UV ink has better wear resistance.

(2) UV ink doesn't clog screens.

(3) UV ink dries faster.

(4) UV ink has better solvent resistance than traditional inks.

(5) UV ink allows for larger print areas.

(6) UV ink has no solvent evaporation, making it more environmentally friendly.

(7) UV ink is more expensive than traditional inks.

2. Why do some people call 300-mesh silk screens 120T?

The difference arises from unit conversion: 300 mesh per inch vs. 120 mesh per centimeter (1 inch = 2.54 cm).

3. What is the appropriate tension value for a screen?

Screen tension depends on various factors including mesh quality, thread diameter, mesh count, design requirements, object shape, and operator skill. Generally, finer details and more colors require higher tension. Typically, tensions should be above 16N.

4. Why are screens sometimes curved in manual screen printing?

Manual screen printing offers low investment costs and is generally used for flat objects. For curved objects, a curved screen helps adjust the distance manually, improving printability but requiring skilled operators to avoid distortion. Wooden frames are commonly used, though they limit precision for multi-color prints.

5. What causes clogged screens during production?

(1) Too fast-drying ink or mismatched thinner.

(2) Too high mesh count.

(3) Environmental factors like heat, dust, or wind.

(4) Ink particle composition.

(5) Poor attention to detail during production, such as slow printing speeds.

(6) Thin coating of emulsion on the screen.

6. What causes ink return issues in cylindrical screen printing?

Reasons include:

(1) Incorrect off-contact distance.

(2) Loose screen.

(3) Low mesh count or thick emulsion layer.

(4) Squeegee misaligned from the center.

(5) Excessive squeegee pressure.

(6) Mechanical inaccuracies.

(7) Rough or shaped object surfaces.

(8) Misaligned fixtures.

(9) Gear mismatches.

(10) Too soft squeegee.

(11) Too thin ink.

(12) Slow printing speed allowing ink to seep through.

7. Why are there terms like "pad film" and "screen film"? What's the difference?

The difference lies in the side of the film where the graphic resides. Naming conventions help differentiate usage and function. Viewing the film, if the emulsion is on top, it's screen film; if underneath, it's pad film. This placement avoids interference from the film's thickness during exposure.

8. Why do some say using yellow mesh improves stencil-making?

Yellow mesh is primarily used for extremely fine detail work because it absorbs UV light better than white mesh, reducing reflections and improving final print quality. For less detailed work, regular mesh suffices.

9. After printing a circular object, the pattern appears stretched by 1mm. What causes this and how to solve it?

(1) The main reason is the gear ratio used for printing is smaller than the actual circumference of the object. Increasing the number of teeth can resolve the issue.

(2) Another cause could be insufficient screen tension and excessive squeegee pressure. Solutions include increasing screen tension, adjusting squeegee pressure, or modifying the artwork to fit the gear ratio.

10. Why do some prefer yellow mesh for stencil-making?

For non-critical designs, selecting the right mesh count is sufficient without needing yellow mesh. Yellow mesh is reserved for highly detailed work as it better absorbs UV light, preventing reflections and yielding superior print results compared to white mesh.
