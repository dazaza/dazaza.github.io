Why is iOS considered as the Windows of a new era?

by geekzhang on 2013-05-06 09:08:21

May 5, Beijing Time - According to a recent article on the American tech news website AppleInsider, industry insiders have been anticipating the arrival of "the Windows of mobile" for years. Mobile operating systems like PalmOS, Linux, Windows Mobile, and Symbian were all once hailed as potential candidates to become the new era's Windows, but they all eventually failed without exception. However, since the release of the iPhone in 2007, with its excellent products and sticky ecosystem, Apple's iOS has now become the new era's Windows.The following is a summary of the article:In the past six months, Apple has sold 85 million iPhones and 42 million iPads, generating over $98 billion in revenue and achieving $22 billion in profit. Apple's profits in the mobile sector are three times higher than Nokia's peak profits in 2008. Why couldn't Apple's competitors stop their unstoppable rise?A comparison of global smartphone manufacturers' operating profits from 2007 to 2012How did Apple manage to earn the majority of the profits in the entire mobile device industry and continue to dominate the direction of the industry? The answer lies in the combination of three factors: a product that offers clear value to the masses; a sticky platform that can resist erosion by competitors; and the ability to help partners increase the sales of complementary products.The emergence of the new era's WindowsFrom the day the iPhone was introduced, traditional analysts believed that Apple lacked the capability to enter the smartphone market, let alone defeat numerous established players and claim the crown in the mobile device field.For years, industry insiders have been eagerly awaiting the arrival of the new era's Windows. Initially, they praised PalmOS as a potential Windows for the smartphone industry, but soon shifted their focus to Linux, then Windows Mobile, then the Symbian Foundation, and then Windows Phone. Samsung even placed bets on all these smartphone platforms at one point or another.Despite many in the tech industry concluding that Samsung has matched or even surpassed Apple (at least in smartphone sales), this isn't entirely accurate. Asymco analyst Horace Dediu noted that Samsung's profit levels are equivalent to those of the mobile industry five years ago, while Apple’s profits are double Samsung's.If we must compare today's mobile market to the historical PC era, this time Apple plays the role Microsoft once held, and Samsung takes the place of the former Apple: back when Apple was the only early PC maker to survive under the dominance of Windows, other early competitors such as Atari, Commodore, and Acorn had long disappeared. Even Apple, which survived through niche positioning, made far less profit than the larger-scale PC industry leader, Microsoft.iOS is the new era's WindowsEssentially, Apple's iOS has become the Windows of our time. Many analysts tried to link the fate of the iPhone to that of the early Macintosh, predicting that Apple would repeat the same mistakes and end up as a niche player with a smaller market share in the mobile industry. Meanwhile, Android, as a widely licensed free open-source platform, was often seen as playing the role Windows once played.However, the principles that helped Microsoft achieve success in the PC industry during the 1990s apply more to Apple's iOS than to platforms like Android.Although Android has achieved great success in terms of market share, it hasn’t generated enormous profits akin to those of Windows and Intel. Unlike Android, which freely provides its software platform, Apple generates billions of dollars in revenue each quarter primarily from hardware sales.Ironically, just as Samsung now plays the role of Apple in the 1990s (Apple being the lone niche survivor in a PC market dominated by Windows 95), Google's Android plays the role of MacOS. MacOS has many passionate supporters, is almost free to distribute, and has a healthy business model, but it cannot achieve dominant profits comparable to the Windows platform.A product with clear value for the mass marketThis is the primary factor behind Apple's entry into the mobile market and its subsequent domination. When the first iPhone was launched in 2007, Apple captured the attention of virtually all consumers, focusing nearly all the industry's attention on itself, and quickly became a world-renowned household brand.A comparison of the iPhone with electronics from 1993 to 2013The iPhone rapidly became a star-level computing product globally, more akin to the launch of Windows 95 by Microsoft than the introduction of the first Macintosh by Apple in 1984. Windows 95 was initially priced quite high, equivalent to about $5,655 today after adjusting for inflation.By the time Windows 95 was released, it had been ten years since the debut of the Mac. Therefore, Microsoft marketed Windows 95 to existing DOS users as an experience similar to the Mac but at a much lower price. For the mass market, Windows 95 was a product with clear value.Android, however, could not replicate the high-value/low-cost appeal of Windows 95 because users could never purchase a retail version of Android and install it onto existing BlackBerry, Palm Treo, Windows Mobile, or Symbian devices, nor obtain an experience similar to the iPhone. Even if you had hacker-level skills, you couldn't get Android running on these devices. In fact, throughout Android's history, almost all Android devices struggled to receive system updates, and most users didn't even understand that they needed them.I've interviewed various enthusiastic Android fans and regular users, and very few knew what version of Android they were using. No one cared about the Android version number. The only commonality among Android fans was their desire to buy non-Apple products. And many regular users bought Android devices simply because they were cheaper. For the vast majority of regular users, there was nothing on the Android platform to retain them.Most Windows users weren't deliberately avoiding Apple but sought similar products at a lower price. Then these users were locked into the sticky Windows ecosystem. Therefore, Android fans share more similarities with early Mac or Amiga or OS/2 users than with Windows users in the 1990s. Android fans seek more unique and interesting products rather than ones with clear value for the mass market. Regular users exhibit very low loyalty to the Android platform.Leveraging existing market position to transformIt's difficult to disrupt an industry with just a new product that has clear value for the masses. You need to capture attention and scale production capabilities to compete with rivals. If you already hold a strong position in a certain area, this becomes easier because you don't have to live in someone else's shadow.Microsoft successfully transitioned from being an MS-DOS licensing provider to becoming a company offering a graphical desktop computing experience because it provided a product similar to but better in some ways than the Mac (Windows 95 was often described this way). This aspect of Microsoft aligns more closely with Apple's iOS than with Android.Similarly, before launching the iPhone, Apple already had a foothold in the mobile market that facilitated its transformation. In fact, Apple had two products that laid the groundwork for the iPhone: the Mac and the iPod. Google's Android originated from the acquisition of Andy Rubin's Android Linux/Java platform. The difference lies in the fact that MS-DOS, Mac, and iPod were highly profitable businesses with many loyal users; Rubin's Android was initially just a byproduct of the niche Danger phone, and although the Java platform it relied on was widely used, it never generated real profits or cultivated any loyal customers.In the early 1990s, everyone knew that MS-DOS was destined to be replaced by graphical desktop systems. For many years before the release of Windows 95, the PC industry bet on projects like OS/2 from Microsoft and IBM's joint venture, as well as Taligent from IBM and Apple's joint venture. Ultimately, Microsoft leveraged its position as an MS-DOS provider to successfully transition and launch Windows, achieving massive success and driving OS/2 and Taligent into obsolescence.Similarly, around 2005, many predicted that the peak iPod would be eroded by smartphones with built-in MP3 players. But ultimately, Apple leveraged the existing market advantage of the iPod to successfully transform and launch the iPhone and later the iPad, capturing the majority of profits in the mobile market.Years ago, the concept that PCs would be replaced by mobile devices wasn't widely accepted. Back in 2000, a Japanese friend told me that in Japan, almost everyone (especially the younger generation) had stopped using desktop PCs and favored feature-rich mobile phones instead. I found it hard to believe at the time, but a decade later, more and more people spend more time on their smartphones than on desktops or laptops.As a previously popular computing platform, the Mac provided Apple with a huge user base for the iPhone and iPad. Essentially, the iPhone is a fusion of the Mac and iPod: Apple utilized the mobility of the iPod as a multimedia consumption device and the software development and operating system platform of the Mac. The replacement of Windows 95 for MS-DOS technically can't be repeated, but the case of the iPhone replacing the iPod is very similar. The selling points of both products are similar: our new product is better, more affordable, and clearly valuable.For the vast number of users who were never attracted by Microsoft Windows CE or other smartphone platforms, the iPhone immediately gained popularity upon its release.The key to creating such massive demand was Apple's powerful product: the iPhone had a very clear value proposition—it combined the functions of an iPod, a browser device, and a phone. Before the iPhone, many users were already familiar with and loved the iPod. The value of a browser device was self-evident. As for phone functionality, it wasn't just an important feature but also a marketing tool: anyway, you needed to buy a phone, so why not choose one that's smart and combines an iPod and browser functions?If Apple had stopped here, the iPhone might have quickly drowned in a sea of knock-offs or alternatives with similar web browsing and video/music playback features. After all, Apple had previously launched successful products like the LaserWriter laser printer in the mid-80s, introduced the Newton MessagePad to bring tablets into the mainstream in 1994, and released the pioneering QuickTake digital camera in the mid-90s. But ultimately, Apple lost its dominant position in all three product categories.So how did Apple turn the iPod, iPhone, and iPad into long-term businesses and maintain their dominant positions?A sticky platform resistant to competitor erosionThe second factor contributing to iOS's success is the ecosystem Apple built around iTunes. This includes the iTunes library and content store, the simple mechanism for syncing content with the iPod, and Apple's efforts in several key areas, such as iTunes U for education, support for enterprise users, and the carefully nurtured app ecosystem. The iOS app market ensures developers can make money while creating new value for iOS users. Corporate clients can also develop private apps for internal use only.Apple's iTunes U Course StoreBuilding an ecosystem comparable to iOS or Windows in the 90s is far more challenging than developing an operating system. In fact, Apple faced a mass exodus of Mac platform developers to Windows in the 1990s. Apple understands better than anyone the importance of developers to a platform. Developing a completely new platform is difficult, but doing so in the shadow of an already successful platform is even harder.Google, Samsung, Amazon, and Microsoft are all trying to build similar mobile ecosystems for Android, Kindle, TouchWiz/Bada/Tizen, and Windows Phone, respectively, but apart from attracting a few users drawn by low-priced hardware, they have no other advantages. These users aren't highly valuable because they don't attract enough developers to enhance the value of the platform and ecosystem, thus creating a vicious cycle. Moreover, there's already a mature mobile ecosystem in the market: iOS.One reason Apple managed to survive through the 1990s, when Windows dominated the market, was that it had established a niche product image among consumers, allowing some to willingly pay a premium for Apple hardware. While many PC users mocked those who paid high prices for Mac computers, the reality is that the support of these high-end consumers kept the Mac platform alive. Whenever Apple attempted to pursue pure market share by introducing low-end product lines, like the Performa series in the 90s, it started attracting low-value customers who contributed little to enhancing the platform's value.Google's Android and Microsoft's Windows Phone adopted similar strategies, gaining volume and market share by selling large quantities of low-margin, low-end products. However, this strategy doesn't effectively support ecosystem development because these low-end users are less likely to pay for apps and content, making it difficult to sustain the development of new apps and content.Microsoft failed to leverage its dominant position in the Windows PC market to create a sticky and resilient platform. Its attempt with Metro was clearly unsuccessful.Similarly, due to Google's loose management of the Android software platform, the Android app ecosystem lacks stickiness, making it unable to prevent Android users from switching to iOS. For example, even many satisfied Android smartphone users will buy an iPod Touch or iPad to enjoy the benefits of Apple's iOS platform.This erosion mirrors what happened to Apple's Mac platform in the 1990s. Now, Apple is striving to maintain the simplicity, usability, and attractiveness of iOS to prevent users from migrating to other platforms.Besides having an excellent product and a sticky ecosystem, there's a third factor that helps Apple maintain the majority of profits in the mobile industry.The ability to boost partners' complementary product salesWhen Microsoft launched Windows 95, replicating the Mac desktop environment, it leveraged its position as an MS-DOS supplier to establish strong marketing partnerships with Intel and numerous PC hardware manufacturers. For hardware makers aiming to sell computers to compete with Apple's Macintosh, Windows software was the killer application they needed.Microsoft captured the bulk of the PC industry's profits, but it also allowed Intel to sell processors and hardware manufacturers to sell PCs. Intel and PC manufacturers could also profit by selling Unix PCs or DOS PCs, but supporting Microsoft Windows meant more money because many consumers wouldn't buy anything but Windows PCs. Although supporting Microsoft exclusively wasn't in the long-term interest of PC manufacturers—since it essentially handed control of their fate to Microsoft—the strong consumer demand forced Intel and PC manufacturers to support Microsoft.Ultimately, PC manufacturers had no choice but to sell Windows and abandon alternative platforms like OS/2, Linux, and ChromeOS because consumers wanted Windows. Meanwhile, Intel or PC manufacturers rarely criticized Windows' shortcomings or complained about its price because new versions of Windows were often responsible for boosting PC sales. Nowadays, when Windows 8 no longer stimulates PC sales growth, Intel and PC manufacturers begin complaining to Microsoft.Similarly, Apple not only created consumer demand for the iPhone but also stimulated demand for data services. Unlike Microsoft partnering with hardware manufacturers, Apple partnered with mobile carriers. Since many consumers wouldn't buy anything but an iPhone, Apple could leverage this advantageous position to secure favorable terms in collaborations with carriers. AT&T willingly handed over control of key services like app and content sales, software updates, and visual voicemail to Apple because the iPhone boosted sales of AT&T's killer application: expensive data plans.In 1995, Microsoft had a very strong market position, forcing virtually all PC manufacturers to support Windows simultaneously. However, Apple couldn't do the same and had to gradually establish partnerships with carriers worldwide. It wasn't until the fourth year after the iPhone's launch that Apple signed a cooperation agreement with Verizon, the second-largest carrier in the US. Another year later, Apple gained the support of Sprint, the third-largest carrier in the US, and several prepaid carriers. It wasn't until the sixth year after the iPhone's launch that Apple finally signed agreements with the top five US carriers, including T-Mobile and U.S. Cellular.Imagine if Microsoft had only partnered with HP when launching Windows 95, didn't start cooperating with Dell until 1999, and didn't reach agreements with most PC manufacturers until 2001. That's the situation Apple faces today. It may be hard to believe, but Samsung, Nokia, and BlackBerry still have far more mobile carrier partners worldwide than Apple does.In its partnerships with PC manufacturers, Microsoft required joint marketing and exclusive agreements, whereas Apple's terms with carriers were much stricter: Apple demanded control over customers, hardware design, software and service functionalities, and that carriers sign large financial commitments and pay upfront. These requirements were so strict that negotiations with most US carriers took a long time.Apple can impose these conditions because it possesses the first two advantages: a product with clear value for the masses and a sticky platform resistant to competitor erosion. Combined with the strong purchasing power of iPhone users for data plans, Apple has sufficient leverage to make various stringent demands on carriers.Average selling price comparison of major smartphone manufacturers' productsOne fact that fully demonstrates Apple's strong negotiation power is that many carriers initially didn't want to partner with Apple, but they soon realized they had no choice. For carriers, the procurement cost of the iPhone is very high (as shown in Figure 5), similar to how Windows was more expensive than DOS or Linux. However, just like Windows, Apple's iOS attracts so many buyers that it compensates for the cost of signing strict agreements with Apple.At the end of 2008, after failing to cooperate with BlackBerry to launch the Storm smartphone, Verizon bet on Android, attempting to replicate the iPhone's success by attracting buyers and selling data plans. Verizon launched an advertising campaign in 2009 called "iDon't," mocking the iPhone, and heavily criticized the iPhone 4 in Motorola Droid X ads in 2010.However, the Android platform has never been able to match the iPhone in attracting valuable customers. Given this, Verizon began collaborating with Apple to sell the iPad at the end of 2010 and launched the CDMA version of the iPhone 4 in early 2011. In February 2011, Verizon announced that the iPhone 4 was the highest-selling product in its history.Sprint and T-Mobile similarly mentioned in memos to investors that the lack of the iPhone was the main reason their customers switched to competitors. After officially reaching an agreement with Apple, Sprint also announced that the iPhone was the most successful product launch in its history.At the end of 2011, Mary Dillon, CEO of the fifth-largest US carrier U.S. Cellular, publicly stated that the company refused to cooperate with Apple because Apple required an upfront payment of a substantial sum, which was unacceptable from a risk and profit perspective.But just a month later, Ted Carlson, CEO of U.S. Cellular's parent company TDS, explained Dillon's previous comments, saying that U.S. Cellular would initially focus on building its 4G LTE network before attempting to sell iPhones.We will never promise never to sell the iPhone, he said. If the iPhone fits our future direction, we might consider cooperating with Apple.Not long after, Dillon's attitude did a 180-degree turn: We’ve implemented multiple strategies aimed at increasing customer loyalty and attracting new customers, including our collaboration with Apple later this year.According to industry insiders, under the cooperation agreement with Apple, U.S. Cellular committed to purchasing approximately 2 million iPhones worth $1.2 billion over three years.Why did Dillon's attitude change so drastically in such a short period? An Associated Press report revealed that during the 18 months after Dillon refused to cooperate with Apple, U.S. Cellular lost 268,000 customers, equivalent to losing 5% of its most profitable contract plan customers.Is iOS doomed to repeat Windows' fate?Like Microsoft, Apple enjoys significant platform advantages. However, if Apple fails to continuously make the iPhone a product with clear value for the masses and maintain the stickiness of the iOS platform, these advantages may weaken. If Apple's products fail to stimulate the sales of partners' complementary products in the future, iOS may also face risks.As the iPad increasingly erodes the PC market, Microsoft's Windows platform is losing its dominant position. Windows itself is also losing its status as a sticky platform resistant to competitor erosion. Gradually, Windows is losing its ability to boost partners' PC sales. To avoid repeating Microsoft's mistakes, Apple must learn from Windows' lessons over the years.These lessons include ignoring security vulnerabilities in Windows XP; launching major changes like Windows Vista before consumers were ready; and spending billions of dollars imitating competitors (such as Zune, Slate PC, and Surface) instead of finding original new businesses. So far, Apple has avoided these pitfalls, while Google's Android has fallen into every one of them mentioned above.
