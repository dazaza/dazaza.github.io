He is a poplar tree.

He is a Poplar Tree

A poplar tree, that was what he seemed like to me when I first met Xiao Yong. He had a short haircut and smiled gently as he introduced himself: "My surname is Yang, the 'Yang' in 'Poplar'." Because of this, I immediately remembered him. Tall as he was, he resembled a poplar, a tree from the north.

The autumn of 2000 was damp and gloomy. At the time, Xiao Yu and I were still bright-eyed third-year university students. In the school's dance hall, we met Shen Tong, who said, "I'll take you out for hotpot." The allure of hotpot wasn't particularly strong, but Shen Tong, squinting his almond-shaped eyes, looked quite cool with a cigarette in hand, so we followed him. When it came time to pay the bill, however, he claimed he didn't have enough money. Xiao Yu, like a real fish, leapt from her seat and cursed the fraudster. But she was wrong; Shen Tong wasn't a scammer. He made a phone call and called over Yang Xiao Yong.

Shen Tong said Xiao Yong was both his colleague and buddy. Xiao Yong smiled; he didn't resemble Shen Tong at all. Unlike Shen Tong's eloquence, Xiao Yong blushed when I looked at him. Throughout the entire evening, he only spoke three times: once to pay the bill, once to introduce himself, and another time to ask for my phone number.

On the way back to the dormitory, Xiao Yu criticized Shen Tong from head to toe — his behavior, his mannerisms, everything. She warned me sternly, "Don't get involved with a guy like him." I yawned, "Relax, I'm not going to compete with you." She glared at me and added, "And by the way, Yang is a good-for-nothing nice guy. He'd make a decent friend, but as a boyfriend? You'd be wasting your life. Hey, did you hear me?"

Reflecting on past events, I have to admit that Xiao Yu was indeed a perceptive person. But even with her clarity, we were still just twenty-something college girls, with peony cheeks and strawberry lips, dreaming of rose-filled futures. Despite her insight, I, in my naivety, followed the winding, unpredictable emotional lines in my palm and kept moving forward.

With Shen Tong and Xiao Yong, our dorm room phones weren't lonely that winter. It wasn't Shen Tong calling Xiao Yu or Xiao Yong calling me. Shen Tong's record was seventeen calls in a day plus a dozen roses. Xiao Yong, on the other hand, only called once a day.

At six o'clock in the evening, when it was too early to study and dinner had just been finished, during those idle moments, the phone would ring. On the other end, it was always Xiao Yong's smiling voice. Our conversations were mostly dominated by me; he wasn't a chatterbox, but he was an excellent listener. Only once did he interrupt my chatter. He said, "Wait, there seems to be a car accident outside. I'll grab a wallet and go check it out." Later, I asked if one needed a wallet to see accidents, and the voice on the other end paused before answering, "If there are injured people to be sent to the hospital, I can help cover the costs."

You see, I was born and raised in Shanghai, where I had only seen poplars in fashionable oil paintings. But at that moment, I suddenly realized that beneath the rough bark of the poplar must lie a tree heart like a layered cake, fragrant, sweet, soft, and tender. I liked layered cakes, so I thought, I liked him.

February 14, 2001, was our first Valentine's Day. At ten o'clock in the evening, Xiao Yong stood on the steps of the science building, watching me walk toward him. The streetlights illuminated his face, his nose red, holding a box of chocolates and a shy rose hidden behind cellophane, its cheeks also flushed from the cold. Later, Shen Tong told me that Xiao Yong rushed to our school as soon as work ended that day, but to avoid disturbing me while I studied, he smoked half a pack of cigarettes in the wind.

Xiao Yong gave me a box of 24 chocolates, each wrapped beautifully in gold foil. I ate one every day, feeling happy for 24 days. Xiao Yu said I looked like a fool eating the chocolates, but she didn’t know that Xiao Yong was the fool. His kiss tasted of tobacco and was so clumsy. Recalling it now, how could I not laugh like a fool?

To this day, I still preserve the illusion that Ferrero chocolates taste of nicotine. I haven't eaten chocolate for half a year now. They say pregnant women should avoid smoking and drinking, so I’d better be cautious.

Under Your Spell

In 2001, spring in Shanghai was unusually bright, and our love basked in the sunlight, growing wildly like campus animals. But the song says, "Wherever the sky doesn't rain," and so, on a stormy summer night, Xiao Yong told me, "I'm going back to Xi'an."

Oh, Xi'an, he had mentioned it more than once—the Bell Tower, the Stele Forest, the Great Wild Goose Pagoda. But what he talked about most was Hongqing, a small town in the eastern suburbs of Xi'an, where his widowed mother, younger brother, their old house, and three acres of poor farmland were located.

He said, "My mom is sick, and the headquarters in Xi'an needs someone to return."

How thoughtful, how meticulous—loyalty and filial piety combined. But he forgot me, forgot our budding love. I didn't say a word, pushed aside his umbrella, and walked into the rain. He caught up with me and embraced me tightly from behind. But three days later, he still boarded the train heading northwest.

I resolved to forget him, not answer his calls, not read his text messages. At the weekend dance, I laughed like a blooming flower, but when the handsome boy asked for my contact information, I blurted out his phone number instead. The next evening, I waited anxiously by the clock in the dormitory until the hour and minute hands formed a straight line. When the phone rang, I burst into tears. Xiao Yong, you've won—you’ve got me under your spell.

From that day on, we started a long-distance relationship. Xiao Yu said it wouldn't last, and I wasn't optimistic either. I counted the days one by one, hoping to determine a timeline for this relationship. I counted from summer to autumn, then from autumn to winter. The leaves fell off the trees, yet there was no sign of our relationship coming to an end. Sigh, I loved him more and more. On the last day of final exams, I agreed to spend the Spring Festival with him in Xi'an.

Nightmare of the Spring Festival Travel Rush

On January 30, 2002, at the bustling Shanghai railway station, I saw him after being apart for half a year. Tall and thin, he wore a dark green down jacket, truly resembling a poplar tree. Seeing me, he smiled, took big strides, grabbed my backpack, and kissed my cheek.

Our journey began sweetly, but we lacked the fortune to continue the sweetness. The Spring Festival travel rush was almost a nightmare—stale air, crowded compartments, and dirty bathrooms turned my first long trip pale. He kindly handed me a cup of warm water: "This is what the Spring Festival travel rush is like. You'll get used to it."

I looked at Xiao Yong, whose eyes were so black and beautiful, but he didn't understand a girl's heart at all. He actually used such a terrifying word—"habit." Why should I get used to it? How could I? For him, I could do many things—I could skip dances, I could wait by the phone for him, I could squat here breathing foul air. But, Xiao Yong, I could endure temporarily, not forever.

Taking the cup of water, I said nothing, keeping a grim face as we arrived in ancient Xi'an.

After getting off the train, an old bus carried us through stunted pines and yellow earth for over an hour, finally arriving at Xiao Yong's hometown, Hongqing, in the evening. Behind his family’s mud house stood a row of tall poplars, but I was too exhausted to feel any joy seeing them.

Objectively speaking, Xiao Yong's family were all good people—his mother kind, his younger brother clever—but we couldn’t communicate well. Under dim lighting, I held a porcelain cup listening to them converse in a language I couldn't understand. The water in the cup gradually cooled, and warmth quietly slipped away from my palms.

"Xiao Ru," Xiao Yong suddenly called me.

I looked up, and Xiao Yong's mother was smiling at me, her lips moving, her wrinkles blossoming like chrysanthemums. I strained to decipher her words but couldn't grasp any meaning. The rural woman before me had clear features; it was evident that twenty years ago, she must have been pretty, enjoying a youthful prime. But the harsh winds of the northwest and life itself were merciless. Time carved and carved again, leaving only a breathtaking trace.

"Xiao Ru, Mom is talking to you." Xiao Yong's tone carried a hint of reprimand, quickly mixed with embarrassment: "Mom said that when you graduate, come work in Xi'an. She wants us to get married soon."

I stared at him in shock.

Was this Xiao Yong's proposal? No ring, no roses, not even a single "I love you," just "Xi'an," just "Mom," just "Xi'an," delivered in such a casual, matter-of-fact tone.

The double blows of disappointment and loss left me unable to lift my head. In front of Xiao Yong's mother, it was inappropriate to react, so I lowered my head, drank some cold water, which had a strange kerosene smell, and my nose started to sting.

Late at night, lying awake on Xiao Yong's family kang bed, I tossed and turned, unable to sleep. The wind crept in through the cracks in the window, and rats raced across the beams. I missed my home in Shanghai, I missed my tidy little bed, I missed my mother. There was something I never told Xiao Yong—my mother didn't even know about his existence. This trip to Xi'an, I lied to her. I told her I was going to Kunming with Xiao Yu. My mother isn't a domineering woman, but putting myself in her shoes, who would want their only daughter to marry far away and join a poor family?

The New Year was approaching, but I became increasingly silent. Xiao Yong's mother didn't know what to do, so during meals, she piled food onto my plate. Looking at the greasy pork in my bowl, I felt nauseous, dropped my chopsticks, and ran out the door.

In front of the fields near the house, Xiao Yong caught up with me. As soon as he grabbed my arm, my tears flowed freely. Xiao Yong sighed and took me to the poplar forest behind the house. It was afternoon, and the winter wind sang long songs among the treetops. I hung my head, unable to see Xiao Yong's expression, but he gripped my shoulders so tightly, I knew he must be very anxious.

"Xiao Ru, what's wrong with you? Look at me."

I said nothing, nor did I raise my head. I didn't want to look into his eyes—they would soften me, make me give up.

"If you have anything, just tell me. Don't give Mom attitude; it will hurt her. You need to be reasonable," Xiao Yong started lecturing me.

"Hurt? Am I not hurt?" I lifted my head, laughing bitterly in anger: "Do you think I came to Xi'an during the New Year just to learn reason?"

"What's wrong with you? You were fine on the road." He pulled me into his arms, relaxing his tone: "Alright, don't sulk anymore. Mom really likes you. She even said that after we get married, she'll let us use the east house as our new home."

Xiao Yong was truly a fool, knowing full well that when I got angry, I couldn't control my mouth, yet he brought up the topic that would set me off.

I pushed him away and exploded: "Who said I wanted to come to Xi'an? Who said I wanted to marry you? I don't want to eat fatty pork, I don't want to squeeze onto trains every year, I don't want to drink such disgusting water, and I certainly don't want to end up looking old and worn out like your mom!"

A fiery slap landed on my face, stunning me, tears streaming down. Fear gripped my heart—not because Xiao Yong hit me, but because I had spoken such cruel words. Originally, I was even more cowardly than I imagined. Life's burdens hadn't yet fallen on my shoulders, but my fear of them had already corroded my soul.

"I'm sorry," Xiao Yong hugged me.

"It's my fault," I curled up in his arms. God knows, I loved the warmth of his embrace, but I wasn't an angel. I wasn't strong or brave enough. I couldn't pass the test, couldn't endure the hardships. Taking a deep breath, I said, "Xiao Yong, let's break up."

"No," he tightened his grip on me firmly: "Trust me, everything will be fine."

He held me too tightly, it hurt, but it also felt so blissful.

The breakup was left unresolved, but bad luck, like a stalker, once it latched on, persisted relentlessly. I fell ill, a cold making me cry incessantly. Clutching Xiao Yong's collar, I sobbed, "I want to go home."

On the fifth day of the first lunar month, my fever subsided, but my body remained weak, and I missed my mother more and more. So, on the day we were supposed to stay home to welcome wealth and blessings, Xiao Yong woke up early, cooked fragrant porridge, coaxed me to eat, carefully wrapped me in a coat and scarf like a dumpling, and carried me out of the village.

The train back to Shanghai was a night train. Leaning against his chest, I blankly gazed at the endless darkness outside the window. He whispered in my ear, "I've wronged you." A single sentence made my tears flow uncontrollably.

Even at this point, I still liked him. I liked his eyebrows, his hands, the faint tobacco smell on his sweater, the dark green buttons on his down jacket. I wanted to be with him. But this southern flower couldn't adapt to the salty soil of the northwest. And Xiao Yong, he was a northern poplar tree, whose lush canopy sheltered his family home, his younger brother, his mother. He was a responsible tree, and he couldn't let them go. So it was only right that I leave.

When we parted, he kissed me. He wasn't afraid of catching a cold, but I feared his sincere eyes. That was probably our most hasty kiss. I couldn't get into character at all, constantly asking myself how to explain, how to break up with him. But until he left, I couldn't bring myself to say those two words. Watching his dark green figure disappear around the corner, I cried bitterly. Colds are truly detestable things.

A month later, I sent him the last text message. I only typed three words: "Let's break up." Around graduation, my job search was unsuccessful, facing setbacks repeatedly, feeling irritable and almost going crazy. Unable to see the future, to prove my abilities, I had to sever ties with the past.

The Unpressed Eleventh Digit

I vowed to carry the breakup through to the end. To avoid his calls, I moved out of the dormitory and changed my phone number. But breaking up with him didn't solve all my problems. Day after day, I carried my resume, wearing high heels, aimlessly wandering in front of skyscrapers in the city. Written tests, interviews, preliminary rounds, finals—workplace was like a glass house, and I was a fly outside it, seeing clearly but unable to find the entrance. When I was tired, when I felt wronged, I missed him.

On April 14, 2002, Xiao Yu finally became his advocate, saying, "Call Yang Xiao Yong, he's almost driven mad." I remained silent. She listed various reasons—Xiao Yong was smoking two packs a day, his voice was hoarse; Xiao Yong was coming to Shanghai for May Day to see me; Xiao Yong was about to go on a business trip. But no matter what she said, I stayed silent.

That night, I hid under the covers and dialed his phone number repeatedly, stopping only at the first ten digits. I lacked the courage to press the eleventh digit. Neither Xiao Yong nor Xiao Yu knew that in this defensive battle between him and me, I was at a relative disadvantage. If I let my guard down, I would surely lose everything and flee in disarray.

The next morning, I overslept and nearly missed an important interview. Getting out of the taxi, I sprained my ankle, and my left shoe broke instantly. I glanced at my watch—it was 10:40, only five minutes before the scheduled time. Years later, when mentioning my limping yet heroic performance that day, my American boss was still startled. He said, "Could I not hire you? You could scare an elephant to death."

What's the World Record Worth?

That evening, while eating dinner, I absentmindedly watched the evening news. The female anchor enunciated clearly, "Boeing 767 aircraft numbered B2552 crashed at 10:40 this morning in Busan, South Korea." What a coincidence, it happened at the same time my high heels broke, though it wasn't as shocking as the broken heel.

Xiao Yu called after ten o'clock. Her father was watching the late-night news, and I lazily picked up the phone. The weather in April turned chilly when Xiao Yu's sobs chilled me to the bone. She said Xiao Yong was on the crashed flight but wasn't on the survivor list.

In a daze, I inserted my old SIM card. Thanks to China Telecom, I received seven delayed text messages from Xiao Yong. The last one was sent at 8:01 AM on April 15, 2002. He said his file had been transferred back to the Shanghai branch. After returning from Korea, he would come directly to see me. He said he would bring his mother and younger brother to Shanghai, "Starting may be tough, but it will get better. I love you." He concluded like that. Thirty-six minutes later, the plane took off from Beijing Capital Airport. Two hours and thirty-nine minutes later, he disappeared from me in the drizzling mist of Busan. Eleven hours and twenty-seven minutes later, I sobbed uncontrollably into my phone.

The TV continued reporting on the Busan air disaster: "Prior to this, the international airline had maintained a world safety flying record for forty-seven years." These people were incomprehensible. Losing a world record meant nothing compared to losing Xiao Yong.

There's a Hole in My Heart

Later, I used that blue-screened mobile phone for a long time. New models appeared daily in shop windows—color screens, data cables, cameras, external storage cards—but people are like that, extremely fickle yet desperately clinging to cherished memories. Luckily, I had those seven text messages, insulating me from impatience.

Two years and three months after Xiao Yong's passing, I lost that phone. A clumsy coworker dropped it into a coffee cup, and I cried till I was unrecognizable. The guy was terrified and pale. We scoured all the Nokia repair points in Shanghai but couldn't fix it. Later, I told the clumsy guy all about the story of the poplar tree. Eventually, he became my husband.

Now, I live well, using the kindness Xiao Yong taught me to manage my life. But occasionally, Ferrero chocolates, six o'clock phone calls, and layered cakes still make me cry. After all, there's a hole in my heart where a poplar tree once grew. The leaves of time fall too slowly to easily fill the hole.

In July this year, I will become a happy mother. I've discussed with my husband that when our baby grows up a bit, we'll take him to Xi'an to see the Bell Tower, the Great Wild Goose Pagoda. I'll hold the baby's hand, letting him touch the roadside trees, telling him, "This is a poplar, a tree from the north."
