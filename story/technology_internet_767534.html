Intel Bets on Clone Tablet Makers: Hopes to Find the Next Lenovo

by anonymous on 2013-10-22 19:19:38

As an old employee of Intel, Hong Li this time truly felt the enthusiasm of Shenzhen's white-box tablet manufacturers. Around this time last year, when he had just gone to Shenzhen to expand partnerships, it was common for some company bosses to suddenly rush into the meeting room and ask if he was from Intel. After receiving a positive response, these bosses would propose to have a good chat with him.

This was something Hong Li hadn't anticipated, but in reality, it was what he hoped for most. As a chip giant, Intel has not been able to make any significant impact on the rapidly growing mobile market, but Intel is determined to reclaim a leading position in this market, and expanding cooperation with Shenzhen's white-box tablet manufacturers is part of that effort.

**Opportunity**

In the global electronics industry landscape, Shenzhen is a region with significant influence. In this vibrant city, there are numerous electronics manufacturers. To domestic consumers, these manufacturers are simply labeled as counterfeit producers, but it is precisely these lesser-known brands that sell tens of millions of tablets overseas.

According to a survey by the market research company eMedia Asia, China’s white-box tablet manufacturers shipped more than 50 million tablets in 2012, and this growth trend shows no signs of slowing down this year. White-box tablets typically refer to inexpensive tablets that use low-end chips and run the free Android operating system.

Relevant data shows that in just the past quarter, the shipment volume of these types of tablets reached as high as 25 million units. Based on this development trend, the total shipment volume of white-box tablets this year is expected to be between 80 million and 100 million units, accounting for more than 50% of the global Android tablet market share.

For any company providing mobile chips, this is a huge market, and Intel, which is just starting out in the mobile field, naturally wants to enter. Fortunately, Intel has encountered the best opportunity to enter this market.

In the past few years, due to price advantages, the shipment volume of white-box tablets has maintained rapid growth, but in terms of product differentiation and profit margins, they have actually hit a ceiling, with some products' profits dropping to just a few yuan. Under such competitive circumstances, some companies that want to go far and grow bigger have begun seeking paths for differentiation.

"Now (white-box tablet manufacturers) are fighting price wars to the point where they calculate profits using calculators, you can imagine how hard it is," said Hong Li, who serves as Director of Product Line Operations at Intel's Mobile Communications Division in China: "If a way to achieve differentiated development can be found, it will help escape the current bloody red ocean price war."

**Investment**

So, what kind of differentiation can Intel provide to these white-box tablet manufacturers? Besides the difference in Intel chips, Intel has also specifically launched some reference designs for these white-box tablet manufacturers, and these reference designs differ significantly from other manufacturers' solutions, involving aspects such as the system-on-chip used in tablets, circuit boards, and memory.

However, initially, these white-box manufacturers did not fully 'trust' Intel's differentiation strategy. Rmagic Technology is one such manufacturer. This company initially produced a small batch of products using Intel's solution, and only after receiving market feedback did they decide to increase their investment.

Compared to the cautiousness of these manufacturers, Intel's investment has been substantial. Intel has established a dedicated Mobile Communications Division in China to support Chinese partners, participating in product planning, product definition, and later market promotion work.

This is the first time for Intel. "I've been at Intel for 19 years, and this is the first time I've seen such a large investment specifically targeting the domestic industry with dedicated product offerings and team setups, extending all the way from the backend to a support team of hundreds of people," said Hong Li.

The substantial investment has allowed Intel to reap certain rewards. So far, including Rmagic Technology and Teclast Technology among others, 14 manufacturers have already partnered with Intel, and this number is expected to continue to grow.

**Harvest**

In fact, Intel has gained a new experience through its cooperation with white-box manufacturers. Previously, Intel was most familiar with collaborating with traditional PC manufacturers like Lenovo and HP, following established procedures. This time, however, things were quite different.

Stephanie Hallford, Director of Business Development and Customer Marketing at Intel's Mobile Communications Division in China, said: "Through cooperation with these local manufacturers, Intel has learned the most about speed and flexibility."

Previously, Intel took approximately 8 to 12 months to jointly develop, test, and produce a platform with manufacturers. Now, this cycle has been reduced to within 6 months, with some even taking just 2 to 3 months. The product development cycle has been significantly shortened.

Regarding flexibility, Hong Li was even more impressed. He recalled that during the development of a tablet with Rmagic Technology, Rmagic suddenly proposed changing to another type of screen, so both parties quickly selected the screen and replaced it on the product before releasing it. "This level of flexibility is unprecedented for us."

**Challenges**

Setting aside the gains, supporting domestic white-box manufacturers is actually a very labor-intensive endeavor with an uncertain future. Historically, many white-box tablet manufacturers have failed, and many companies have exited or transitioned out of this market.

iSuppli Semiconductor Chief Analyst Gu Wenjun believes that currently, more manufacturers in the white-box tablet market rely on low prices and quick product launches to profit, but they may not pay much attention to long-term development. In his view, Intel should increase support for brand manufacturers like Lenovo and Samsung.

However, regarding this issue, Intel has a different logic. "Back in 1994, I spent my days writing emails to headquarters introducing Lenovo and explaining why we should support them. At the time, Lenovo's annual PC sales were only in the tens of thousands," Hong Li said. "But now, look, Lenovo has become the leader in the PC industry."

Hong Li firmly believes that Intel will reap greater rewards after making this investment. "I believe that during this process, some good manufacturers will emerge, and we will also grow together with them in this process."
