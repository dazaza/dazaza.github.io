Apple: The Shift From Great To Good

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 10:57:47

There were no surprises.

On September 10th, at Apple's headquarters in Cupertino, California, the latest product launch event hosted by Tim Cook could be simply summarized with these four words. The new iPhone 5S and 5C performed largely in line with external expectations. There was no rumored iWatch, no iTV, and no new iPad. There were absolutely no surprises.

Do you remember the scene when the first iPhone was introduced? On January 9, 2007, Jobs humorously but emotionally presented this "revolutionary" product to the world: "I have been looking forward to this day for two and a half years. In every era, there are some revolutionary products that define their time. If one encounters even one of them during their entire career, it is already very fortunate. And Apple is the luckiest of the lucky because we have developed more than one world-changing product. In 1984, the Macintosh computer came out, changing not only Apple but also the computer industry. In 2001, we launched the iPod, which not only changed how we listen to music but also transformed the entire music industry...", "Today, we will redefine the phone."

The continuous release of revolutionary products and constant creation of surprises for the world allowed this company, founded by Jobs in 1976, to earn the reputation of being "great." Of course, it eventually grew into the world's "most profitable" company.

However, just two years after Jobs' passing, this company, known for its slogan "Think Different," remains outstanding but seems to be gradually losing its legendary luster.

Standing behind Steve Jobs, a charismatic leader with a legendary aura, is a "tragedy" for Tim Cook, the current CEO of Apple. Because he will constantly be compared to Jobs. But at the same time, he will, in a sense, "redefine" Jobs.

Because in the future, under Cook's leadership, Apple will reveal a core secret of this mysterious company to the outside world: Was Jobs' greatness about creating a series of revolutionary products or building a company capable of continuous innovation?

Inspiration Buried in the Grave

"Jobs has taken Apple's inspiration with him to the grave." Two weeks after the release of the iPhone 5S, Bai Yongxiang, CEO of Meizu Technology, told this reporter.

He bluntly stated that this time, Apple did not demonstrate the same level of innovativeness as before, "After the iPhone 4S, Apple began to seem 'ordinary'." Notably, the iPhone 4S was also the last iPhone from the Jobs era. It is said that Jobs watched the launch of the iPhone 4S from his hospital bed on the day before his death.

In fact, during the launch of the Meizu MX3 on September 2nd, this Chinese smartphone manufacturer, which has always claimed to take "Apple as its teacher," revealed its intention to surpass. At the launch event, Meizu repeatedly compared the MX3 with the iPhone instead of Samsung or HTC, which are part of the Android camp. This was very rare in previous Meizu product launches. Of course, even today, traces of Apple can still be seen in Meizu's product design. For example, during the launch of the MX3, Meizu downplayed hardware specifications and emphasized the smooth experience of the system and hardware.

Luxia De, chairman of Weishang, also believed that Apple's new product launch had "no surprises." This company, which started with mobile phone accessories, survived the mobile internet wave thanks to the popularity of the iPhone, becoming one of the few companies in Huqiangbei to do so. "Back then, making shells for the iPhone 4 was a one-time effort because the iPhone 4, as a single model, caused a craze where people lined up to buy it. As an accessory manufacturer, we didn't have to worry about inventory buildup, nor did we need to consider product line allocation for other phone models. We just needed to get the molds for the iPhone 4, start production quickly, and establish distribution channels to make money."

Of course, as an accessory manufacturer, while sharing in the massive market benefits brought by Apple, Weishang also bore the risks of Apple's upgrades and technical standard changes. "If Apple makes any disruptive design changes in smartphones, all our accumulated advantages will vanish, and we'll be on the same starting line as many industry newcomers," Lu Dexiao said.

Perhaps for this reason, Lu Dexiao felt that the weakening of Apple's innovation after Jobs' death wasn't entirely bad for him. "I think Apple's new products this year lack novelty. They're exactly the same as the materials we've previously obtained. We just need to follow the routine and produce phone cases and power banks."

Still Financially Outstanding

Despite being "surpassed" by Chinese competitors and criticized by accessory manufacturers, Apple remains, at least financially, the world's "most profitable" company, with performance still commendable. Apple's 2012 financial report showed that the company achieved sales revenue of $156.508 billion and net profit of $41.733 billion, increasing by 44.58% and 60.99%, respectively, compared to 2011, the year Jobs passed away.

iPhone's contribution to Apple's remarkable financial performance was quite significant. In 2012, iPhone and related products and services contributed $80.477 billion in revenue, accounting for over 50% of total revenue.

In a sense, since the release and launch of the first-generation iPhone in 2007, Apple's financial performance has been closely tied to the market performance of this flagship product. Its sales revenue of $156.508 billion in 2012 increased by 317.5% compared to $37.491 billion in 2008.

More importantly, with the popularity of the iPhone, Apple's profit margin continued to increase significantly. From 2008 to 2012, Apple's profit margins were 16.32%, 19.19%, 21.48%, 23.95%, and 26.67%, respectively.

However, in 2013, Apple's growth has stagnated, or even declined. According to Apple's second-quarter report (April to June 2013), the company's sales revenue for the quarter was $35.323 billion, slightly higher than $35.023 billion in the same period in 2012. However, its net profit of $6.9 billion dropped significantly by 21.8% compared to $8.824 billion in the same period in 2012.

This was largely due to the iPhone 5, released in September 2012, which was the first true iPhone of the Cook era, failing to meet market expectations. Although Apple's stock price hit a historical high of $705.07 per share that month.

The weakening of innovation ultimately led to Apple's profit margin declining after 2012. From October 2012 to June 2013, Apple's profit margin was 22.12%. In April to June 2013, Apple's profit margin further dropped to 19.53%.

Where Is the Next iPhone?

In February 2013, Apple held its 2013 shareholders' meeting at its headquarters in Cupertino, California. At that time, Apple's stock price had fallen below $480 per share, down by about one-third compared to its historical high in September 2012.

Under immense pressure, Cook stated at the shareholders' meeting: "Please be patient. Apple focuses on long-term products. In the future, we will still have great products." Seven months later, Apple launched the iPhone 5S and 5C, but the rumored iWatch and iTV did not appear as expected.

We can say that although Jobs has passed away, the market's expectations for Apple remain unchanged — "exceeding expectations." When Apple abandoned its usual "arrogant" style and compromised with the market like other brands, it faced strong disappointment from the market. Apple's stock price remained hovering around $480 per share.

In fact, whether they are Apple's users, shareholders, or partners, they are all asking the management represented by Cook a simple question: Where is the next "revolutionary product" like the iPhone?

Lu Dexiao is one of them. Although his smartphone accessory business has become less dependent on Apple due to the rise of brands like Samsung, he still looks forward to Apple igniting the next market hotspot. He hopes that the accessory business related to iWatch and iTV will further boost his business. However, he frankly admitted that Apple's current performance made him "very disappointed."

In Yang Dongwen's view, president of Skyworth Group and chairman of the Color TV Division, iTV will be Apple's "product to change the world again." "Currently, the standards for smart TVs are not clear yet, and all companies producing smart TVs are 'feeling their way across the river.'" "We have always hoped for a company like Apple to define a product for smart TVs, just as they defined smartphones with the iPhone."

Masonglin, director of the R&D Center of TCL Group and deputy director of TCL Industrial Research Institute, has much lower expectations for iTV. "Apple's innovation research differs from many Chinese enterprises; they usually start R&D preparation ten years or even longer in advance. Therefore, the recent decline in Apple's innovation may stem from misjudging the future direction earlier. In the future, without Jobs, Apple will find it even harder to accurately predict future directions," Masonglin said. "As for the iWatch, Apple's actions are clearly late compared to Android brands like Samsung."

Even if, under Cook's leadership, Apple eventually brings iTV and iWatch to the market, they will still need to withstand the strict scrutiny of the market. The iPhone 5 serves as a cautionary tale.

In Zhu Jizhi's view, executive president of Cotec Core City, from a hardware design perspective, the iPhone 5 has been "over-designed." "The iPhone 5 did not consider user experience; many cutting-edge designs are hard to reflect in ordinary consumers. Jobs' pursuit of perfection continues in the iPhone 5, but within Apple, there is no one else who can perfectly integrate extreme design, user experience, and market response like Jobs did."
