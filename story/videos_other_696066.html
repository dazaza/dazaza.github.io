Uniqlo: The Ultimate Trade

by wgyuvs8a on 2012-03-07 19:52:07

Yanai has turned 61. Between him and his ultimate trade fantasy, there are three obstacles.

In terms of sales volume, the apparel retail brand Uniqlo he founded (which belongs to Fast Retailing that he established) is currently ranked fourth globally on an annual basis. Before him lie ZARA from Spain, GAP from the United States, and H&M from Sweden. Together, these three represent the dominance of Eastern brands in the apparel industry.

Unlike manufacturing, in industries such as clothing, fashion, and entertainment, the Eastern world possesses relative discourse power and the ability to lead trends. Sales volume is just the most superficial manifestation of a brand's influence. Don't forget how GAP accurately conveyed the spirit of American cowboys from the 1950s through buttons and pant lines, how ZARA overturned conservatism with the fast-fashion trend and its high-speed supply chain, and why H&M's crossover design makes global emerging consumers flock to it.

As the only Asian brand to make it into the top five, Uniqlo has already achieved remarkable success. But Yanai is not satisfied. "Fast Retailing's goal is to become the world's number one apparel retailer," Yanai said in a recent interview with Global Entrepreneur.

Uniqlo seems capable of gradually approaching this dream. Just in 2009, Uniqlo's global sales were ranked fifth, but within a year, it surpassed the Dutch competitor C&A. This year, industry conditions continue to change favorably for Yanai. Currently, one of the targets to overtake, H&M, is in the most concerning phase of its performance in the past decade. On June 21, Swedish local time, H&M announced that the sales of its existing stores in May increased by only 2% year-on-year, lower than analysts' conservative estimate of 5.4%. In the second-quarter earnings forecast published almost simultaneously, profits are expected to drop by 16%, marking the third consecutive quarter of profit decline in 10 years.

In contrast, according to statistics from May, Fast Retailing's overseas market operating profit increased by 60% since the beginning of the year. By August, its overseas operating profit is estimated to increase 33 times from 3 billion yen three years ago, reaching 10 billion yen. In its most relied-upon Japanese market, which accounts for nearly eight-tenths of its total sales, same-store sales in April increased by 4.6%, dispelling the gloom of the previous three months of slowing sales growth.

The question is, how did Uniqlo achieve all of this? Normally, in many aspects, Uniqlo appears insufficient to be a rival to Zara and others.

Founded in 1984, Fast Retailing is a "youngster" in the apparel industry, 37 years later than H&M and 9 years younger than ZARA. When it embarked on its globalization journey in 2001, ZARA had already entered Uniqlo's home turf, the Japanese market. After more than 20 years of rapid development, Uniqlo surpassed many well-established apparel manufacturers with over 800 stores, becoming Japan's first brand in apparel retail, and now has more than 2,000 stores in 11 countries and regions. However, Uniqlo has always lacked the distinct fashion-forward characteristics of ZARA and the revolutionary retail management model. Looking at individual items, most belong to the "everyday" style basics, far from the glamorous appeal exuded by European brands.

Each person owns one computer, and so do ZARA and others. In April this year, during the "Ruili·2011 China Apparel Forum," Yanai delivered a keynote speech: "I believe Uniqlo's mission is to pioneer transformative casual wear, improving the quality of life for everyone around the world."

After the 2008 global financial crisis, numerous renowned multinational brands faced difficulties, while Uniqlo gained prominence in the international apparel industry. In 2009, Uniqlo stood out with high sales volume, allowing Yanai to ascend to the richest person in Japan. Last year, the National Retail Federation (NRF) awarded him the International Brand Award, recognizing Uniqlo's efforts in listening to consumer needs during severe economic conditions. In BusinessWeek's 2010 ranking of "The World's 50 Most Innovative Companies," Fast Retailing made its debut. As Sony and Toyota, among other old Japanese benchmark companies, quietly faded into the twilight of their idols, Uniqlo became the best representative of the new generation of Japanese corporate spirit.

However, Yanai deeply understands the necessary path to achieving his goals. Balancing multiple elements in different periods is a significant challenge for any apparel company. This short-statured (possibly only 1.60 meters), gray-haired, serious elder's autobiography is currently popular in mainland China, titled "One Win, Nine Losses." This represents his business attitude over the past decade.
