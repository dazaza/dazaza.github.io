How much longer can Samsung's mobile phone boom last?

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 19:58:01

Apple's newly released IOS7 has received a lot of criticism. In contrast, Samsung's third quarter financial report released at the end of October showed positive data, and its shipment volume has surpassed Apple to become the world's largest smartphone manufacturer.

We can say that Samsung has finally ushered in its own prosperous era, but how long will this prosperity last?

The signal released by financial data: Strengthening the component business

Although Samsung's second and third quarter reports both show that Samsung is still in a growth trend, the driving force maintaining this growth is no longer the mobile phone business. As the world's largest mobile phone manufacturer, Samsung is also the world's largest memory chip, display screen, and television manufacturer. The excellent performance of Samsung's second and third quarter reports mainly owes to memory chips: benefiting from the rise in memory chip prices, the operating profit of this business department increased by 71% year-on-year in the second quarter (the profit margin of the mobile phone business decreased from 19.8% in the first quarter to 17.7% during the same period). Although the component business only accounts for three-tenths of Samsung's total operating profit, this proportion may change in the future.

Samsung's investment budget in chips this year has reached 13 trillion Korean won (approximately 12 billion US dollars), which is already on par with Intel's 11 billion US dollar investment in semiconductors. This move by Samsung sends a signal: In the future, the component business will contribute more profits, while the mobile phone business has already hit the ceiling.

Product strategy: A hybrid of HTC and Nokia

Despite the launch of the S4 drawing collective disapproval from analysts, which once caused a sharp drop in Samsung's stock price, the speed of breaking through ten million units in shipments was 20 times faster than the S3. Samsung has reasons to refute the external accusations about its lack of product innovation. However, it is worth noting that the S4 is no longer just a flagship product, but has evolved into a flagship series: the S4 zoom focusing on photography, the relatively cheaper S4 mini, the S4 LTE-A version supporting 4G, and the S4 active, a ruggedized product focusing on sports.

Today, the S4 is not fighting alone; this seems to remind us of the shadow of HTC ONE. Many people once criticized HTC for having complicated products without a flagship product. In fact, before the launch of the S3, Samsung was the same. After the S4, it might return to the starting point, just like its smartphone series name: galaxy.

In terms of product routes, Samsung follows Nokia's sea of machines strategy. With superlative manufacturing capabilities, the cooperation of its own industrial chain, and substantial financial strength, Samsung adopts a rapid trial-and-error approach in product development. Once a product receives good market feedback, it makes slight improvements and produces it on a large scale, creating a generation of machine kings.

As for the product itself, the gap between today's Samsung and domestic manufacturers has narrowed. Its actual product strength has always been overshadowed by its grand marketing strategies. Whether in manufacturing processes or materials, Samsung is not the most meticulous or value-for-money one.

The Future of Samsung and Apple

Some people say that Apple hiring Burberry's CEO indicates a lack of ideas. But in fact, we discover from IOS7 that what Apple wants is the product temperament that the fashion circle excels at. Although IOS7 is not perfect, its overall style leaves a deep impression. In the future, this will be the "Apple style". No matter how you copy the product shape, the inner soul and temperament cannot be copied.

This is a height that Samsung can never reach. Recently, the aggressive advertisements of Note3 + smartwatch Gear in various media have truly revealed some signs of decline - a new product that changes little compared to its predecessor and an immature product, what kind of chemical reaction can they produce? At least so far, no synergy brought by this combination has been seen.

Meanwhile, domestic mobile phone manufacturers are beginning to covet Samsung's market. With the high openness of the supply chain, Samsung can no longer lead in products. In software, Samsung and other manufacturers are all using Android, so all the common problems of Android apply to it. In hardware, Samsung has never used a metal body. In processors, Samsung's own Exynos chips tested frequently on high-end models are not as outstanding as Qualcomm's flagship products. All these have become possible for domestic manufacturers to seize the market. The only thing that domestic manufacturers cannot match might be the accompanying industrial chain that Samsung owns outside of mobile phones, which supports its overall financial performance.

Previously, there were frequent rumors in the industry that Apple's turning point would soon appear. However, currently, Apple is certainly not a parabola reaching its peak but rather maintaining fluctuations at a high level; the one that should really beware of the turning point might be Samsung.

A fundamental difference: Apple does not need large marketing expenses, naturally having a large number of followers rushing to buy; Samsung, on the contrary, relies heavily on marketing expenses to support its product sales. No one is genuinely waiting and expecting Samsung's products; they must be dressed up in gorgeous attire to win applause. When the market matures and users see through the reality beneath the gorgeous outerwear - the product strength of domestic manufacturers is sufficient to match it, perhaps the prosperous era of Samsung mobile phones will be hard to sustain.
