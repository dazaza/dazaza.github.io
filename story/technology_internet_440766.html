Website sells AIDS test strips with a profit margin of up to 10 times (picture)

by anonymous on 2011-06-01 17:46:12

Screenshot of an HIV test strip sales website. The biggest concern for buyers of test strips is the reliability of the test results. The picture shows HIV self-test strips and blood collection needles sold on the website. Photo by Xu Tao.

□ Southern Daily Reporters Xu Tao, Zhong Kai, Intern Zhang Fangyuan

On April 6, in response to the recent public attention on the "negative AIDS" incident, the Ministry of Health officially stated that "negative AIDS" is merely a symptom of AIDS phobia, with no unknown virus involved, and so-called "AIDS-like illness" has nothing to do with AIDS. A discussion surrounding this "new" type of AIDS has temporarily settled, but the chain reactions it triggered are far from over.

"15-minute result determination," "eliminate inner fear"... Online, HIV self-test strips that had been dormant for some time are once again in high demand, becoming a lifeline for some people to dispel their fear of "AIDS-like illness" and AIDS.

However, behind the clamor lies a fear that no test strip can change. A small test strip may plunge panicked buyers into a new cycle of fear.

The Fear of "AIDS-like Illness" Becomes a Selling Point Online

"A single test strip can free you from panic in 15 minutes." Website advertisements directly target those who suspect they are infected with the HIV virus but dare not speak up.

When searching online for "HIV test strips," numerous websites appear, varying widely in type. Some AIDS websites claim to be "China's first professional platform providing AIDS prevention and testing consultation services," while others call themselves "official AIDS testing portals." The recent fear of "AIDS-like illness" has become a promotional gimmick for many websites, which tailor their messages accordingly: "AIDS-like illness" is just "AIDS phobia," and a single test strip can help you escape panic in 15 minutes.

In the interactive area of a test strip sales website, a netizen named "Qi" said that after engaging in high-risk sexual behavior, he began experiencing throat inflammation, dry coughing, and later developed a fever. He asked anxiously, "Am I showing early signs of AIDS infection? I'm so scared... What should I do? I'm still young... I deeply regret doing that."

Website advertisements tell "AIDS-phobic individuals": "Do not let worry and fear linger in your heart. With simple self-testing, you can get rid of those annoying shadows," targeting those who suspect they are infected with the HIV virus but dare not speak up.

A salesperson from one website told Southern Daily reporters that they had conducted a small-scale survey to understand the psychological characteristics of their target customers. According to the introduction, those with purchasing needs are extremely fearful of AIDS and generally have a tendency to discriminate against AIDS-infected individuals. Secondly, potential customers do not care much about specific prices and will not bargain like when buying other products. "You can test at home, with quick results—only 15 minutes needed, simple operation, and accuracy reaching 99.7%," "Self-test at home, know the results in 15 minutes, confidential and free mailing, face family members calmly!" Many websites prominently advertise quick detection times, convenience, and thorough privacy protection.

Network Monopoly and Tenfold Profits on a Single Test Strip

The purchase price of a single test strip is less than 5 yuan, yet websites make more than tenfold profits. Because these cannot be purchased in physical pharmacies, the internet has inadvertently become the "monopoly" seller in this market.

Journalists found through investigations that websites do not set low prices for test strips. For example, a domestic brand of test strips is priced at 50-60 yuan per piece on most websites as a "fixed price." So-called imported test strips are even more expensive, two to three times the cost of domestic ones. When journalists proposed to "bulk buy" domestic test strips, website salespeople initially refused to lower prices, saying, "Bargaining for this product is something we've never seen before." However, after some negotiation, the salesperson eventually agreed to sell them at 400 yuan per box, containing 40 test strips, or 10 yuan per strip, significantly lower than the initial "fixed price."

According to a salesperson from a certain website, the purchase price of a single test strip is less than 5 yuan, yielding over tenfold profits based on the usual selling price on the website. Such test strips cannot be purchased in ordinary physical pharmacies, making the internet an implicit "monopoly" seller in this market. Journalists found that all major websites have detailed regulations regarding customer privacy protection, aiming to provide "the most professional humanized service."

Through customers who had previously purchased test strips, journalists learned that packages sent by websites have no markings related to HIV test strips from outer packaging to inner packaging, nor any wording related to AIDS testing websites. "There’s no difference from ordinary packages; the outer packaging is a cardboard box, the inner packaging is an aluminum foil bag, sealed, lightproof, radiation-proof, and moisture-proof, making it impossible to see through." Shipping labels from some websites clearly indicate items like "accessories," "office supplies," or "gifts." A website manager told Southern Daily reporters, "Our mailed packages enjoy inspection exemption rights," and delivery personnel will not know the contents of the package. He repeatedly emphasized, "If you choose door-to-door delivery, you don’t need to worry about the delivery person knowing what you bought. Please rest assured." Websites also provide tracking services for shipping numbers, helping buyers monitor the delivery status, or display "recent AIDS test strip shipment numbers" and shipment statuses on the homepage.

Panic Unresolved, Self-Test Results Increase Burden

Many buyers carefully hold their self-test results while remaining internally uneasy, as uncertainty about the results means fear has not been completely eliminated.

Spending tens of yuan for a single test, or hundreds of yuan if using the website-recommended "product combination," multiple tests and repeated confirmations can be very costly. But due to convenient purchasing, easy use, and protected privacy, the website's test strip business is thriving. Website salespeople say that going to hospitals and disease control centers involves many inconveniences, so many people still buy test strips.

However, during the investigation, journalists discovered that self-testing, which should help buyers "escape the shadow," did not actually reduce their psychological burden.

For instance, the "self-test buffer solution" provided with the test strips on the website became a new source of烦恼for some buyers: "Is the self-test buffer solution made from HIV viruses? Will wounds touched by the buffer solution during self-testing cause AIDS infection?" Similar concerns occur among almost all those who have self-tested.

One buyer told Southern Daily reporters that after the test showed no issues, they still worried whether it was a false impression. "I would repeatedly recall the operation process, fearing that the good news I just received was all just a dream," they said. Under such thoughts, they continued to repeatedly buy test strips, "spending a lot of money not only wasted resources but also trapped me further."

Salespeople admitted that many buyers carefully held their self-test results while remaining internally uneasy, as the uncertainty of the results meant fear had not been completely eliminated. Therefore, the majority of website consultations were for repeated confirmation of negative results.

Even more terrifyingly, due to the existence of "false positives" in test results, a few patients with problematic self-test results might not have been infected with the virus, and the self-test result was just a misjudgment. According to data publicly disclosed by some websites, the probability of this situation occurring is 3%.

A self-tester with similar experiences recalled that at the time, they were unaware of the existence of "false positives," so after the first test, they felt as if "the sky had fallen." Later, they went to the hospital for testing and finally received normal results.

Expert Reminder

Any Deviation in Self-Testing Can Affect Results

"We should trust the recognition results of authoritative institutions; the situation grasped by the state is definitely more credible than that of the folk." Regarding the panic caused by so-called "AIDS-like illness" and AIDS, Dr. Hu Yunfeng from the Dermatology and Venereology Department of Guangzhou Overseas Chinese Hospital believes that China's AIDS detection technology has already reached a very high level. "But in clinical practice, we often encounter people coming here for testing who do not believe our results and go to other hospitals for testing," Dr. Hu thinks this is similar to self-testing, where psychological factors play a significant role.

An unnamed doctor from the laboratory department of another top-tier hospital in Guangzhou explicitly stated, "Hospitals with testing qualifications have specialized laboratories, and testing is a complex process." He revealed that under certain specific circumstances, such as blood donation or emergency surgery, a few rapid test strips similar to those sold on websites are used, but these reagents have very strict requirements for temperature and humidity during transportation and storage. Any deviation at any stage could lead to inaccurate results. "The reagents used in hospitals are only for the initial screening steps of AIDS. If there are problems, a series of checks must be conducted to confirm the diagnosis."

This doctor does not recommend self-testing. He stated that since AIDS virus testing requires a high environmental standard, occurrences of "false positives" or "false negatives" can delay treatment or leave a huge psychological shadow. Additionally, improper blood drawing during self-testing can cause other infections.

Journalist's Pursuit

Does the Website Have the Qualifications to Sell?

A staff member of an AIDS test strip sales website told reporters that they directly obtain goods from the manufacturer. The reporter then contacted Beijing Jinhuo Pharmaceutical Co., Ltd., one of the manufacturers. Mr. Huang, the sales manager, introduced that the company's HIV test strips are mainly sold to hospitals, blood stations, and CDCs across the country, with agents everywhere but no physical stores.

Another producer, Mr. Sun, the sales supervisor of Akon Biotechnology Co., Ltd. in Guangzhou region, said that he noticed many websites selling HIV test strips, "Estimating a website sells around 100-200 units per month," he believed selling to general consumers posed risks because ordinary people might not use them correctly at home, leading to misjudgments.

Despite this, the interviewed salespeople from production companies indicated that general sellers or individuals could purchase test strips from manufacturers. "As the possibility of personal self-testing increases, the hidden market demand is growing."

Reporters learned that HIV test strips are "in vitro diagnostic reagents for blood source screening," but are managed as drugs. According to national regulations, to operate such in vitro diagnostic reagents, one must possess both "two certificates": a drug operation license and a medical device operation license. According to relevant regulations, obtaining these two documents is not easy; licensed pharmacists, ambient temperature warehouses, cold storage facilities, etc., are the most basic hardware conditions.

When reporters consulted a website about whether it had the relevant operating licenses, the website personnel avoided the question by claiming they "had to leave for something." In the sales websites found by the reporters, none displayed these required documents on their web pages. (Editor: SN026)
