Legendary Delphi Sold Off Cheap

by nc119 on 2008-05-21 18:01:48

In the field of development tools, Borland has always been a strong challenger to the Microsoft empire. However, in May 2008, Borland sold its IDE business, which had once brought it great success including Delphi, to another company for $23 million. This news did not even attract the attention of mainstream IT media in the United States. So, what will be the future of the well-known development tools like Delphi that have been acquired? Will they disappear from people's sight just like the acquisition news?

A $23 Million Deal

On May 7, 2008, a piece of news appeared on Borland's official website: Borland officially announced the sale of its subsidiary CodeGear to Embarcadero Technologies.

CodeGear was established on November 14, 2006, as a wholly-owned subsidiary of Borland, mainly responsible for Borland's integrated development environment business.

Although it was a young company only two years old, its products held a legendary and revered status for most programmers. These products included JBuilder, Java development tools, Delphi and C++ Builder, as well as newly developed PHP and Ruby IDE development tools.

However, these famous development suites would no longer belong to Borland. An era of legend had come to an end.

Integrated Development Environment

An Integrated Development Environment (IDE) is an application used for program development environments, generally including a code editor, compiler, debugger, and graphical user interface tools. In other words, it integrates functions such as code writing, analysis, compilation, and debugging into a single software package. Any software or software suite with these characteristics can be called an IDE. Examples include Microsoft's Visual Studio series, Borland's C++ Builder and Delphi series, etc.

Actually, as early as two years ago, when CodeGear had not yet separated from Borland, Borland CEO Tod Nielsen began planning to sell the IDE department. The reason was that the IDE department faced fierce competition from Microsoft and free open-source products, especially Eclipse Foundation's Java IDE, causing Borland's IDE revenue share to continuously decline.

Borland at that time hoped that selling the IDE business would allow the company to gain more resources and invest more effort into ALM (Application Lifecycle Management) business, which Borland was newly focused on.

But at the time, Delphi and JBuilder were still shining brightly. Although many buyers were very interested in Borland's IDE business, the prices they offered were unsatisfactory to Borland. After half a year of unsuccessful attempts to find a buyer, Borland decided to spin off the IDE department into a separate subsidiary named CodeGear.

This move itself was still a strategy of waiting for the right price. Because after being spun off, CodeGear’s operations were completely separated from Borland except for financial ties. This meant that at any time, once another company showed interest in purchasing, Borland could cleanly and thoroughly sell off this business.

Two years later today, Turbo C, which once taught countless people how to program, and Delphi, which made countless programmers fascinated by Windows programming, were finally sold off by Borland. For $23 million, CodeGear found its final value - a price that in the US wouldn't even cover the cost of making a second-tier movie. Borland disposed of this IDE business, which had once brought it glory, like throwing away a hot potato.

Borland's Misstep

Selling the IDE business at a low price can be considered a great irony for Borland, or perhaps a failed corporate strategy. Let's review how past glories turned into today's helplessness.

In the 1980s, Borland dominated the computer compiler tool and application software market with products like Turbo C, Turbo Pascal, and SideKick, accumulating huge funds. The sudden success made Borland's management extremely arrogant.

In 1991, Borland spent $400 million to acquire Ashton-Tate, which was already rapidly declining at the time. Despite Wall Street analysts thinking Ashton-Tate wasn't worth that much, Borland hoped to use Ashton-Tate's dBase to fully occupy the PC desktop database market and compete with Bill Gates to become the dominant force in PC software.

However, after being acquired by Borland, Ashton-Tate's nearly ten years of brilliance disappeared. Ashton-Tate originally had a good chance to become today's Oracle and continue to dominate the PC database market, but the emergence of Windows and FoxPro changed everything. At the time, dBase did not have a Windows version, and DOS-based dBase programmers urgently needed a Windows-based dBase development tool. Therefore, when FoxPro for Windows, which was compatible with dBase format, was launched, it quickly attracted many users of dBase III/dBase III Plus.

But Borland's self-satisfaction did not detect the changes in the market until after Microsoft released two versions of FoxPro for Windows. Only then did Borland realize that dBase users were starting to leave.

Although Borland later launched a Windows version of dBase, it was too late to change the market. By this time, the development of the market had changed; the PC database market had begun to enter the era of relational databases, and the desktop database market started to gradually shrink and show signs of decline. It wasn't until March 12, 1999, after dBase had completely fallen, that Borland reluctantly sold off the dBase it had acquired for hundreds of millions of dollars at a cheap price.

Although the success of Delphi and JBuilder helped Borland recover some losses, the launch of Microsoft's .NET disrupted Borland's plans again. It began to hesitate about whether to follow Microsoft into the new .NET platform because Borland wanted to take advantage of the Linux trend and go cross-platform rather than struggle against Microsoft, which had already become a giant, on the Windows platform.

As software profits overall began to decline, Borland had to find ways to maintain the company's growth and develop new product lines. Starting from 2000, Borland introduced and expanded the Kylix product line, entering the Linux programming market. However, as Linux returned to normal development from explosive growth in 2000/2001, Borland soon realized that relying solely on the Java and Linux markets would not generate enough profit.

All renowned information research institutions analyzed that .NET would, along with Java, occupy a significant market share in the future. Only then did Borland realize it could not lose the .NET market. Thus, Borland hastily invested in .NET product development. However, by this point, the world was already dominated by Microsoft alone. These failed actions caused Borland to invest excessive resources, ultimately sowing the fatal seeds for the end of its own legend.

Looking back today, if Borland hadn't made so many mistakes and lost so many valuable opportunities, it might have become another dominant force in the software market, able to rival Microsoft and establish itself as a major software company. But Borland blindly lost its precious chances in arrogance.

Can Delphi Make a Comeback?

The acquiring company, Embarcadero Technologies, was founded in 1993 with its headquarters in San Francisco, USA. It is a company providing professional-level database tools for designing, developing, managing databases, and their data. It currently has over 12,000 enterprise customers worldwide, including more than 90 from the Fortune 100 list. Its flagship database tools include ER/Studio, DBArtisan, Rapid SQL, and Change Manager, generating annual revenues of $60 million.

The benefits of this acquisition for Embarcadero are obvious. First, CodeGear's development tools have loyal developers and technical communities, especially the large number of passionate fans who have followed Delphi, JBuilder, and C++ Builder for many years. Many of these developers work on database-related projects in fields such as finance, telecommunications, manufacturing, healthcare, and government. For Embarcadero, if it can successfully convert these developers into loyal users of its own, the benefits for its database software sales will naturally be evident.

Additionally, due to different applications, there are various database platforms, and many enterprises face increasing problems regarding productivity and resources. If Embarcadero can successfully integrate all of CodeGear's products into its existing product line, it can offer its customers an integrated product suite for designing, developing, managing, and optimizing heterogeneous applications and their databases.

CodeGear's global marketing channels are also well-established, and the acquisition will enable Embarcadero's products to quickly reach the sightlines of millions of software developers, designers, independent software providers, and database professionals worldwide. Analysts have predicted that if the acquisition integration goes smoothly, it will create a global platform-independent software supplier with annual revenues exceeding $100 million, over 15 million users worldwide, and an annual profit growth rate of 10%.

However, the capital forces behind this acquisition are also worth noting, as Embarcadero's parent company, Thoma Cressey Bravo, is a quite well-known private equity firm. This company has extensive practical experience in "industry consolidation" or "development and acquisition." It currently manages nearly $2 billion in equity capital through a series of private equity funds. In the software industry, this company has completed 34 acquisitions, with total annual revenues exceeding $500 million. And this acquisition was completed under the guidance of this company.

Therefore, in this capital game, the future fate of familiar software like Delphi and JBuilder remains unknown. After all, interests and wealth are the results that investment companies focus on most. As for the development of technology, to them, it is no different from any other wealth-creating tool.
