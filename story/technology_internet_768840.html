The Many-Worlds Interpretation in the Framework of Quantum Mechanics and Schrödinger's Cat

by anonymous on 2013-11-25 11:33:09

The Many-Worlds Interpretation (MWI) proposed by American quantum physicist Hugh Everett is considered to belong to the third type of parallel universes. From the perspective of quantum mechanics, every event in this kind of parallel universe generates countless possible universes. In Everett's view, during the measurement process, the universe where the initial state wave function exists "splits" into many mutually unobservable yet equally real branches of the universe. Each measurement only produces one result, and the results in each branch of the universe are obviously different. The first EWG interpretation introduced by Everett was aimed at seeking an intrinsic explanation for the quantum mechanical system, where the observer is an intrinsic component of an isolated system and also the object described by the wave function.

In 2001, Professor Max Tegmark from the University of Pennsylvania published an article commemorating the hundredth anniversary of the discovery of quantum theory. He believed that according to the decoherence theory and the latest experiments, the Many-Worlds Interpretation could replace the orthodox Copenhagen interpretation, representing the latest interpretation of quantum mechanics. The Many-Worlds Interpretation has gone through three stages of development: First, in 1957, Everett published "The Relative State Formulation of Quantum Mechanics"; Second, in 1973, "The Many-Worlds Interpretation of Quantum Mechanics" and in 1999, "Quantum Mechanics and the Mind-World Relation"; Third, the 2007 conference on the Interpretation of Quantum Mechanics 50 Years After Everett held at Oxford University.

The Many-Worlds Interpretation of quantum mechanics (EWG theory) initiated by Everett needs to be formed under the guidance of a mathematical framework, which requires the elimination of classical observers in the EWG theory. To this end, Everett introduced two basic assumptions: the first is the complexity assumption, which posits that the world is complex enough to be decomposed into single systems; the second is pure wave dynamics, which can provide a mathematical model for support. In the Many-Worlds Theory, the observer is regarded as an "interaction system" between an isolated system and other subsystems. The infinite number of "possibility" worlds are not abstract but possess the same reality and objectivity as our own world. DeWitt once evaluated the concept of "world splitting," believing that due to interactions among countless systems, the universe is continuously splitting into surprising "branches," each corresponding to a spacetime extremely similar to our own universe.

From the perspective of cosmology, the Many-Worlds Interpretation explains the wave packet collapse formed during quantum measurements. For this, Everett redefined the relevant states of quantum mechanics and believed that all isolated system evolutions follow the Schrödinger equation, and the wave function does not collapse. In the superposition state of composite systems, each observer perceives a definite result. The collapse of the wave function appears to occur at a subjective level, but the observer only perceives one definite result. In 1970, scientists at Heidelberg University first articulated the theory of decoherence, which explained the destruction of coherence between branches of system wave functions after interaction with measuring instruments.

The term "splitting" mentioned in the Many-Worlds Interpretation is gradually being replaced by parallel and complete worlds. These countless derived parallel universes coexist simultaneously in the same time and space. According to the explanation of the Many-Worlds Theory, experimental observations can form countless parallel universes. For example, in one parallel universe, the experimental subject may still be alive, while in another, it may have died. The most famous case of this is Schrödinger's cat experiment, which reveals the magical side of the quantum mechanics world. Schrödinger linked the possible decay of a radioactive atom to the fate of a cat, resulting in the cat being both dead and alive due to the possibility of the radioactive atom decaying or not.

Everett believed that there are countless possibilities before the wave function is determined, existing in mutual parallelism. The appearance of an observer is not to change the probability of occurrence, but rather to select one from countless possible spaces, similar to countless wave function superpositions forming a very large number of parallel universes, estimated to be about 10^100. Regarding Schrödinger's cat thought experiment, scientists conducted various verifications. In 1996, scientists at the National Institute of Standards and Technology successfully achieved coherent experiments at the single-atom (beryllium) level, setting up two coherent state wave packets to simulate a cat that is both dead and alive. In 2000, a research group at Stony Brook University used a non-dissipative superconducting loop to successfully simulate the superposition state of "dead cat" and "live cat."

Schrödinger's cat, as a micro-macro entangled state, links the decay and non-decay states of microscopic atoms with the life and death states of macroscopic cats, which is crucial for understanding the non-locality of quantum states in quantum mechanics. Additionally, it is very helpful for understanding the instantaneous wave packet collapse in quantum measurements. In real life, the existence of decoherence effects causes quantum physics to revert to classical physics in the transition field between quantum and classical physical media.

In 2007, at the international conference on the Interpretation of Quantum Mechanics 50 Years After Everett, scientists believed that the Many-Worlds Interpretation does not contradict the Copenhagen interpretation but rather complements quantum cosmology from a different angle than the Copenhagen interpretation. In 2007, scientists discovered a massive cosmic cold spot while studying cosmic microwave background radiation (CMB), suggesting the existence of parallel universes beyond our own. According to the latest calculations, parallel universes may exist in other dimensional spacetimes, separated by approximately 1 millimeter.
