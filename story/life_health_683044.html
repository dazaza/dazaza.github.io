Experts inform about the early symptoms of leukemia

by laomao741 on 2012-02-29 11:26:46

Here is some information about leukemia, specifically its early symptoms:

1. Anemia. Anemia is often the first symptom of leukemia, mainly manifested as pallor, weakness, fatigue, and excessive sweating. Whether during activity or at rest, patients feel shortness of breath and an accelerated heartbeat. As time goes on, these symptoms gradually worsen. The more severe the anemia, the more serious the leukemia may be. However, other causes such as hemorrhoids, gastrointestinal bleeding, excessive menstruation, and dietary bias that can lead to nutritional deficiency anemia should be ruled out.

2. Bleeding. Bleeding is a common symptom of leukemia, with nearly 40% of cases presenting bleeding as an early sign. Bleeding can occur in various parts of the body, commonly seen as unexplained bruises on the skin, oral, nasal, gum bleeding, excessive menstruation, etc. Retinal blurring often indicates that the patient has retinal bleeding; severe headache accompanied by nausea and vomiting often suggests intracranial bleeding. Therefore, if any of the above situations occur, the patient should promptly seek medical attention.

3. Fever. More than half of the patients present fever as an early symptom, which can be low-grade (below 38°C) or high-grade (above 39°C or even 40°C). Most fevers are recurrent and irregular, often accompanied by symptoms of respiratory infection such as nasal congestion, runny nose, cough, or urinary tract infections like frequent urination and urgency. This often leads to misdiagnosis. At this point, checking the peripheral blood of the patient will reveal a large number of abnormal cells. It is important not to use antipyretics like Metamizole or Paracetamol arbitrarily to mask the original condition.

4. Sudden onset. Leukemia usually has an acute onset, a short course, and is more common in children and young adults.

5. Lymph nodes. Widespread lymph node enlargement throughout the body is more common in acute lymphocytic leukemia, though less prominent in acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL) compared to chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL).

6. Hepatosplenomegaly. About 50% of leukemia patients experience liver and spleen enlargement, most significantly in acute lymphocytic leukemia.

In addition, leukemia cells can infiltrate the respiratory, digestive, and genitourinary systems, as well as the eye socket, lacrimal gland, and retina. Patients may exhibit diffuse or nodular changes in the lungs, along with pleural effusion, digestive dysfunction, proteinuria, hematuria, amenorrhea or excessive menstruation, exophthalmos, and vision impairment.
