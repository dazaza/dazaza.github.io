Behind the Scenes:

【New Director, New Attempt】

In the fifth year at Hogwarts School of Witchcraft and Wizardry, it is a pivotal year for Harry Potter because he is no longer a child but is learning to face the choices and challenges that adults need to make: Harry not only has to deal with Voldemort's return but also endure the pain of losing his good friend Cedric Diggory. Everything happening to him forces him to be more mentally mature than other people of the same age because he shoulders unforeseen major responsibilities… To find an entrance into the magical world of Harry Potter, director David Yates did a lot of preparatory work before filming, thoroughly analyzing the background of the "Harry Potter" series: "This episode occupies a very important position in the entire novel set, because by this point, our young protagonist is gradually moving towards maturity, and the surrounding matters become increasingly complicated. They begin to discover the bumps in this world and experience the 'growing pains.'"

As the producer of the "Harry Potter" series, [David Heyman] said that it was precisely the unique characteristics of this story that led him to ultimately choose David Yates to direct the film: "Yates is very good at handling such fantastical story themes, and he also demonstrates the ability to handle political issues in a highly entertaining way. Of course, "Harry Potter 5" is not a politically charged film, but it does extensively depict the politics followed in the magical world. One could say that Yates joined with infinite passion, and watching the process from unfamiliarity to trust between him and the children, you will find that Yates is indeed an expert at motivating actors. He understands that the young actors who have grown up with the series know their characters in the films better than anyone else, so Yates often encourages them to boldly express their ideas and inject their real personalities into the roles."

Daniel Radcliffe, who portrayed Harry Potter for the fifth consecutive time, expressed: "I like working with David Yates, especially his polite and gentle personality. However, the fifth installment is the hardest movie I've filmed, partly due to the special nature of this chapter, and partly because of the arrival of the new director Yates. He always makes me analyze the character of Harry more profoundly, and I found this approach to be very necessary. He is truly a talented genius director."

Rupert Grint, who plays Ron Weasley, agrees with this view: "David Yates' directing style differs greatly from the previous directors, but he always achieves great results in the easiest way." And Emma Watson, who portrays Hermione Granger, Harry's ever-loyal friend, added: "The cutest thing about Yates is how he listens carefully when we talk about our characters. For us who have played the same role in five films, Yates sincerely admires this fact. He values the relationship between me, Daniel Radcliffe, and Rupert Grint because it helps enhance the authenticity of the friendship between the characters in the films."

Besides David Yates, screenwriter Michael Goldenberg is also new to the "Harry Potter" series: "When David Heyman approached me, I was so excited that my whole body trembled. I know my words might sound cliché, but being able to join this magical series and bring it to the big screen through your efforts is an indescribable feeling. Although David Yates gave me enough creative space, he also had one requirement for the script, which is that every moment of the story should be as realistic as possible. Clearly, the primary task of the screenplay is to preserve the essence and soul of the original novel. One could say that the fifth installment is the most crucial part of the entire series, where Harry transforms from a child into an adolescent, starting to realize that nothing is absolute: villains have good sides, and good people have evil sides… He discovers that the adult world isn't as ideal as imagined, and it too has flaws—these are the themes we need to highlight in the film."

【The Magical Ministry of Magic】

Before Harry faces the challenge of confronting Voldemort, there are other matters to resolve, such as escaping the trial at the Ministry of Magic… Here, the Ministry of Magic's hall is vividly depicted, where art director Stuart Craig (Stuart Craig) covers an entire wall with a giant poster of Fudge, the Minister of Magic, giving off a strong Soviet vibe. Craig also stated that despite the wizards at the Ministry of Magic flying instead of walking and using paper airplanes instead of telephones, it cannot hide the fact that it is essentially a pure bureaucratic institution: "In the UK, government buildings have architectural features from the Victorian era of the 19th century, with numerous decorative elements. Since the Ministry of Magic is located underground, the first thing we did was visit all the oldest subway stations in London to feel the cold atmosphere and design style. We noticed that many subway stations use large amounts of colorful tiles for decoration—combining this feature, we finally invented an underground world—the Ministry of Magic. After covering each wall with luxurious black tiles, the grandeur immediately became apparent. However, this posed quite a challenge for cinematographer Slawomir Idziak (Slawomir Edzak), who had to consider the reflective elements of the tiles during shooting."

Looking across all the "Harry Potter" series films, the art design of the Ministry of Magic is arguably the largest set piece, over 200 feet long, 120 feet wide, and 30 feet high, with at least 30,000 tiles—all manually glued. Moreover, in the film, this hall will be digitally processed to appear even larger.

Harry enters the Ministry of Magic accompanied by Mr. Weasley through the "Visitor Entrance"—which looks like an ordinary telephone booth on the London streets, Stuart Craig laughed: "I think such a setting makes the Ministry of Magic more interesting, located right below the Muggle government buildings, so we chose a phone booth near the Ministry of Defense… Muggles would be surprised to learn that the Ministry of Magic is actually beneath the British Ministry of Defense." David Yates also remarked: "One of the most interesting story points in the "Harry Potter" series is showcasing how the magical world 'hides' within Muggle life. In fact, these two worlds coexist, it's just that Muggles haven't realized it yet."

【Pink Represents a New Kind of "Dark Force"】

At the start of the new semester, the school welcomes a new Defense Against the Dark Arts teacher—Professor Umbridge, portrayed by Imelda Staunton. From head to toe wrapped in pink, Professor Umbridge possesses a practiced fake smile and a sickeningly sweet voice, but her true nature is entirely the opposite. David Yates stated: "Fudge believes Dumbledore is coveting the position of Minister of Magic, so he sends his most trusted deputy, Umbridge, to investigate the truth at Hogwarts. But Umbridge believes her mission is to clear away all potential threats for Fudge, causing chaos at Hogwarts—she is truly a wolf in sheep's clothing." Staunton agrees: "Many people, like Umbridge, appear charming on the surface but are full of malice inside, making it a challenge I am willing to accept."

Even though it's to remain consistent with the original novel, Imelda Staunton sometimes still finds playing Umbridge a bit annoying: "In the book, she is described as extremely ugly, resembling a loathsome toad, so when someone tells me 'you're the perfect choice for this role,' it doesn't exactly make me comfortable."

Imelda Staunton is also the actor closest to costume designer Jany Temime (Jany Temime), because designing Umbridge's overall image is one of the most challenging tasks in the entire film, Staunton said: "We came up with many interesting solutions for this slightly plump lady, I don't want her clothes to have overly sharp outlines—although her first impression is gentle and kind, her heart is far from it."

To display Professor Umbridge's "gentleness" in appearance, Jany Temime explained: "We stuffed a lot of padding into Imelda Staunton's costumes because she is particularly slim." Temime also used many soft fabrics on the clothes to enhance the sense of gentleness in her character. However, the colors of the clothes were already decided in the novel: pink, even pinker, and the pinkest. Temime continued: "Every time we see her, the pink clothes she wears are different in style, not just color. As she gradually displays her privileges at Hogwarts, the brightness of the colors increases accordingly, becoming very eye-catching, ultimately turning into the darkest cherry pink."

The theme of pink extends to Professor Umbridge's office, decorated with various pink items by art director Stuart Craig and his design team, including frilly velvet-like curtains and various cute little ornaments around the room. The furniture style comes from France, as Craig describes it, "very line-oriented"—suggesting from all aspects that the owner of this office has a very distinct personality. However, the most distinctive feature of this office lies in the 200-plus plates on the walls adorned with kittens, hinting at Umbridge's feline-like, unpredictable character. In contrast, the classroom used by Professor Umbridge appears serious and simple, much like her strict teaching style that limits students—only allowing them to sit in their seats and study the textbooks she assigns. Rupert Grint said: "Students find Professor Umbridge's Defense Against the Dark Arts classes completely foreign, but she firmly believes we must study theory without any practical application, which is absurd in a magic school."

【First Kiss Under the Mistletoe】

When Hogwarts takes a break for the upcoming Christmas holiday, Harry's D.A. study group must interrupt for a while. After the last class, all D.A. members left in pairs, except for Cho Chang. Harry had liked her since last year, but their relationship became somewhat complicated due to the murder of Cedric Diggory. As if aware of their hidden feelings, the all-knowing Room of Requirement decorated with a sprig of mistletoe during the last D.A. meeting, finally bringing the moment all Harry fans eagerly awaited—Harry's first kiss.

Daniel Radcliffe recalled the filming of this scene: "I was a bit uneasy because I noticed Katie Leung seemed nervous. This is not just a kiss; it also hints at the complex relationship between Cho and Harry. After trying a few times, we found it wasn't so scary and was even fun." Katie Leung continued: "I was particularly nervous because it was my first kiss on the big screen, but luckily director David Yates was very professional. He told us what kind of effect he wanted, so we didn't feel this scene was hard to shoot. At first, it was a bit awkward, but Daniel Radcliffe was very good at leading the scene, making the filming simple, and he is a kissing expert."

The director's approach may have alleviated the concerns of the two young actors but overlooked the equally anxious crew nearby because these people have watched Daniel Radcliffe grow up step by step with the "Harry Potter" series films. Producer David Heyman said: "Many of us knew Radcliffe when he was ten years old, and then he grew up right before our eyes... So we care about him and want to protect him. Watching him experience his 'screen first kiss' here feels strange. I kept telling myself not to look, but I couldn't help wanting to watch. Fortunately, this scene was perfectly shot, and I believe the audience will definitely feel the sweetness and beauty of tenderness."

【Giant Brother Grawp】

Knowing that he would eventually be driven out of Hogwarts by Umbridge, gamekeeper Hagrid assigned Harry, Ron, and Hermione a special task: to help take care of his half-brother Grawp—who happens to be a 16-foot-tall giant.

To bring Grawp to life on the big screen, this involved not only the collective wisdom of manual design, motion capture, and computer special effects but also the brilliant performance of an actor named Tony Maudsley (Tony Maudsley). David Heyman said: "In our assumption, Grawp is a very innocent giant, just a bit hyperactive and unable to concentrate for too long. Maudsley and David Yates spent a long time discussing some details of the performance because Grawp is a digital character that needs 'motion capture' technology assistance." Yates praised Maudsley's performance: "He fully immersed himself, which can be seen from every subtle performance. Even though this character is almost entirely generated by digital technology, Maudsley gave Grawp life and soul."

When Grawp is with Hermione, you can see from their relationship that Grawp is not just a monstrous giant. Emma Watson said: "Grawp also has a cute side; in front of Hermione, he is as gentle as a kitten. Hermione is the only one who can calm him down... I know Grawp is a digitally created giant, but the staff have made him as real as possible. He has adorable puppy-like eyes, and I love him so much."

Nick Dudman, the biological special effects artist, mentioned that they also made a full-size head for Grawp to be used in scenes with other actors. Rupert Grint excitedly said: "Filming with Grawp was amazing; there was a huge head and shoulders on set, so realistic that we forgot we were only interacting with a part of his body. My favorite part is when Grawp really likes Hermione and picks her up with his hand. Then Ron gets jealous and tries to make Grawp put Hermione down—he wants to be the hero and keeps hitting the giant... You can probably guess the result—Ron gets flung away by Grawp."

【Five Major Cuts】

Since "Harry Potter and the Order of the Phoenix" is the book with the most pages in the seven-book series—870 pages long—and the page count for the seventh book "Harry Potter and the Deathly Hallows" has been determined to be 784 pages, the screenwriter of the film could only painfully cut out parts of the novel that were "insignificant." Five sections have now been confirmed to have been removed.

1. All Quidditch-related scenes...this is not hard to guess, as Harry spends most of his time in "Harry Potter 5" being detained by Professor Umbridge, not only missing Quidditch training but also several important matches. However, for Rupert Grint, who plays Ron, this is undoubtedly bad news because Ron will participate in the Gryffindor Quidditch team for the first time in this episode. Regarding this, Grint can only regretfully say: "Hope for next year." Since there is no Quidditch match, we won't hear the related song "Weasley Is Our King."

2. Mrs. Weasley and Sirius argue about Harry at the Order of the Phoenix headquarters...cut.

3. House-elf Dobby no longer appears...In "Harry Potter 4," all of Dobby's scenes were replaced by Neville, so it's not hard to guess why this character missed out on the fifth episode. The only one likely to be unhappy about this is Hermione because this practice clearly violates the principles of her founded "Society for the Promotion of Elfish Welfare" (S.P.E.W.).

4. Neville's parents...they were once brave Aurors who went insane after being tortured by Voldemort and are currently recuperating at St. Mungo's Hospital for Magical Maladies and Injuries. This is indeed unfortunate news because as Neville's role becomes increasingly important in each episode, his parents should have received corresponding "respect" rather than being mercilessly cut.

5. Harry and others clean up the Black family home, the Order of the Phoenix headquarters...this is very regrettable because we won't get to see the live appearances of the magical creatures here.
