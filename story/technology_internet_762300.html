The collapse of the monopoly position of the Windows operating system

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 14:47:13

Fifteen years ago, the PC industry was at its peak, and its core technology, the Windows operating system, was unparalleled. Fifteen years later, PCs have no advantage compared to smartphones and tablets, and the dominance of the Windows operating system is no longer.

At the end of the 1990s, there was a U.S. tech company that was undoubtedly leading globally. The company was almost as wealthy as a nation, with formidable technical strength. It not only dominated desktop operating systems but also had growing influence in other software areas. However, being the tallest tree in the forest invites the wind, and the U.S. government dragged this company into a protracted antitrust lawsuit. Yes, this was Microsoft back then.

Eventually, Microsoft lost this lawsuit and had to accept stringent legal constraints. Many people worried that this case would have a significant negative impact on Microsoft, fearing that this iconic tech company would stagnate because of it. But the truth is, over the past 15 years, what ultimately caused Microsoft to lose its dominant position in the global personal computer market wasn't the antitrust lawsuit, but changes in the market itself.

At that time, Microsoft had become the focal point of the global tech world, nearly breaking records to become the world's first trillion-dollar company. In 1999, Microsoft reached its highest historical market value, which according to Standard & Poor’s data, was $620.58 billion. If we consider inflation at the time, Microsoft's total market value on December 30, 1999, would be equivalent to about $850 billion today. However, the changes brought by the booming development of the internet were beyond Microsoft's control. Microsoft was merely a surging and beautiful wave in the global tide of the internet.

The antitrust case did indeed cause many losses for Microsoft, but they were not fatal. Due to the emergence and widespread adoption of new electronic products like cloud computing, smartphones, and tablets, the once-dominant desktop operating system has lost its former glory. As long as an electronic device can access the internet and run relevant programs, it can basically meet user needs, regardless of the operating system it uses.

Through the following three tables, we can objectively understand the challenges faced by tech giants such as Microsoft, HP, Dell, and Intel before the new wave of the internet.

Global Internet Device Shipment Statistics (2000 to 2012)

First, let's look at the global internet device shipment statistics: from 2000 to 2012, although PC shipments remained around 200 million units annually, after the appearance of smartphones and tablets, the market landscape fundamentally changed. Now, PC shipments pale in comparison to those of smartphones and tablets.

Global Major Platform Market Share (Q1 2009 to Q1 2013)

From the table, we can see that the diversification of personal internet devices significantly weakened the dominance of the Windows operating system. This change has only appeared recently; three years ago, 70% of global PCs ran on the Windows operating system. Now, due to the widespread adoption of Google's Android system and Apple's iOS platform, the Windows operating system's global market share has shrunk by almost 30% to 50% compared to before. The most notable change is that Android now holds a larger global market share than Windows.

Finally, let's look at the statistics compiled by Horace Dediu, an analyst at the U.S.-based research firm Asymco. The PC business not only appears "small" in the face of explosive sales of smartphones and tablets, but the PC business itself has shown a clear downward trend. Now, internet devices have become diversified, and although PC technology is already quite mature, PCs are not the most cost-effective or convenient devices for internet use. The status of PCs has been overturned by smartphones and tablets, and personal computers have become specialized office equipment.

Growth Rate of Annual Shipments of PCs Running the Windows Operating System

From the chart above, we can see that before the release of the iPad, PCs running the Windows operating system had impressive shipment growth. However, starting from the end of 2010, the growth rate of PC shipments has been sluggish, and recently, there has even been noticeable negative growth.

In fact, it's not all bad news for Microsoft. Microsoft's performance in transformation is commendable, and its Office suite remains an important source of revenue. Although Google and Apple have launched their own office software, they have not yet posed a fatal threat to Office.

Time flies, and it has been 15 years since Microsoft's antitrust case. The once monopolistic product, the Windows operating system, will never again be sued for monopoly, and the PC industry closely related to the Windows operating system faces a bleak future.
