In general, allergic rhinitis is divided into two types: perennial allergic rhinitis and seasonal allergic rhinitis.

Patients with perennial allergic rhinitis suffer from perennial onset. They have three main clinical manifestations: continuous sneezing, running clear nasal mucus (if secondary infection occurs, it can turn purulent), and nasal obstruction. Seasonal allergic rhinitis often occurs during the flowering season, so it is also called "hay fever". It is characterized by repeated attacks, sudden nasal itching, continuous sneezing, profuse watery nasal discharge, nasal congestion, and the attack time is relatively short.

How to prevent its onset?

Allergic rhinitis must be treated long-term and stably. The purpose of treatment is to eliminate the cause and allergens, achieve desensitization, reduce swelling, and improve breathing. However, there is currently no special curative therapy, only less frequent attacks and reduced symptoms can be achieved. Patients can take anti-allergic drugs, including those with sedative effects and those without; they can also use hormone drugs, such as nasal sprays, prednisone, dexamethasone, etc., but long-term oral intake should be avoided due to their significant side effects. Generally, nasal drops made from these drugs can be used. Cromolyn sodium can be used before the onset of symptoms; patients with severe symptoms are advised to use some drugs to relieve nasal congestion, and patients with excessive nasal secretions can use some drugs to reduce secretion; some herbal teas also have certain effects; additionally, traditional Chinese medicine, laser, microwave, surgery and other methods can be used for treatment. People with perennial symptoms of allergic rhinitis, allergy to multiple allergens, combined with asthma, persistent cough are recommended to undergo desensitization treatment.
