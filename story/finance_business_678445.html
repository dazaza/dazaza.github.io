Xuanhuan Social Media Oscar Frenzy: Twitter Live Streaming, Exclusive Streaming Interviews

by sznstejx3q3 on 2012-02-27 14:28:09

The niche movie nominations and the "politically correct" jury of the 84th Academy Awards注定 will find it hard to break any TV rating records. However, this did not prevent it from becoming the grandest social media carnival night in history: Twitter live broadcasts, microblogging instant reviews, streaming media exclusive interviews...

Why did the American Broadcasting Company (ABC), which could have exclusively enjoyed the "Oscar pie," choose to extend an olive branch to social media? The answer is actually very simple: more and more people are getting used to interactive, compound viewing experiences—staring at the TV screen with one eye while scrolling through their phones with the other. In the Twitter and Facebook realms, real-time comments can range from harsh criticisms to satirical parodies.

We might even pre-design acceptance speeches for future actors and actresses: "Thank you to the Academy, thank you to the director and all staff members, thank you to my friends and fans—especially my Twitter followers..."

Social Media "Live Broadcast"

On the evening of the 26th, ABC will entrust two critics to broadcast backstage trivia via Twitter in real time, including well-known blogger Shira Lazar. Questions posted by netizens on Twitter beforehand will also be posed by red carpet hosts to attending celebrities. The Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences has also collaborated with Facebook to launch a new feature: users can vote for their favorite movies and actors on the website and share their predictions with friends.

Moreover, there will be 20 additional cameras installed in the red carpet area, lobby bar, and backstage. Winners can make lengthy speeches without restraint in front of the "thank-you camera" backstage, without worrying about being labeled as verbose like previous winner Halle Berry. Videos edited from these cameras will not be aired on live television but uploaded to the Oscar.com website and Oscar mobile applications. The buzzers on websites and mobile devices will also provide real-time location updates of "celebrities + cameras," aiming to give netizens the feeling of being present at a "celebrity live show."

According to Karin Guilford, Senior Vice President of Digital Media at ABC, during the live broadcast of the Oscars, a third-party company hired by ABC will search for information and comments on Twitter containing #oscar, #redcarpetqa, #bestdressed hashtags, and post them in real time on the official website. "If a certain moment or shot makes everyone go crazy, then we'll know which video clip to upload. Even mercilessly critical comments can help us gain insights." Just like last year when netizens humorously commented on the golden couple hosting the Oscars—Anne Hathaway and James Franco. Regarding Franco's apparent disengagement and absent-mindedness, host of the TV commentary show "The View," Joy Behar, wrote on Twitter: "Anne Hathaway was running around with James Franco all night, I'm amazed she didn't run out of breath."

Social Media Dominates Film and Television Celebrations

According to digital media analyst Brufen Laboss, last year, social media contributed approximately 966,000 comments to the Oscars. During the live broadcast, 87% of social media comments were related to the Oscars, with the third and fifth most frequently appearing words being "love" and "dress," respectively. The vast majority of these comments came from Twitter rather than Facebook—the reason being simple: comments on the former are publicly visible, whereas status updates on the latter are only available to friends.

The Oscars are not the only ones allying with social media. The recently concluded 2012 Super Bowl garnered 12.2 million comments from Twitter and Facebook, more than quadrupling last year's numbers; the Grammy Awards received a staggering 13 million comments, over 22 times last year's total. Although there is no direct data proving the link between social media and actual ratings, as Albert Cheng, Executive Vice President of Digital Media for Disney/ABC Television Group said, "Social media enhances audience participation in TV programs and extends their interest span," which has become an undeniable fact. For instance, commissioned by Twitter, a group of TV stars and singers, including Eva Longoria from "Desperate Housewives," Randy Jackson from "American Idol," and comedian Whitney Cummings, announced that they would publish real-time comments during the live broadcast, accumulating quite a bit of popularity for the Oscars.

At the same time, as the host of the "Oscar Party," Vanity Fair magazine will also showcase the sights and sounds of "post-Oscars" through Twitter, with complete videos being promptly posted online. This way, it can give readers a sense of being there while not infringing on the privacy of big-name stars, thus "achieving a subtle balance between shared joy and personal enjoyment," as Chris Lovasz, digital editor of Vanity Fair, put it.
