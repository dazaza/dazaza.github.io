Yu Family Stone Village - Phoenix News Auto

by tianxiangyi on 2012-02-11 14:14:10

Shitou Village is located in the mid-west of Jingxing County, Hebei Province. Covering an area of 10 square kilometers, it has more than 400 households and over 1600 residents, among whom more than 95% are surnamed Yu and are true descendants of Yu Qian. The entire village consists of more than 4000 stone houses, over 3700 meters of stone streets, more than 1000 stone wells and cellars, over 2000 stone tools, and more than 200 stone steles. However, most of these were destroyed during the Cultural Revolution, leaving only a dozen or so today.

Recently, while reading "Ming Dynasty Those Things," I came across the story of Yu Qian. Coincidentally, I heard that there was a Shitou Village in Jingxing County, Hebei Province, where all the residents claimed to be descendants of Yu Qian. Soon after, driven by the stories I had read, I visited this real-life descendant village, hoping to bridge history with reality.

A simple Yu family member

Following the Beijing-Shijiazhuang Expressway down to the Xiulin exit, we found a small signpost on Provincial Road 202 leading to Shitou Village. Following increasingly rudimentary signs through several forks in the road and passing ordinary rural areas, we finally arrived at the grand entrance arch of the Yu Family Stone Folklore Village just as the sun was setting. Stone walls and residential buildings were clearly visible on both sides of the road.

The host Mr. Yu, who we had arranged to stay with at Yu Xingshui's homestay, was waiting for us early at the scenic area parking lot. In his forties, with a booming voice, he was a simple and efficient farmer. The path to Mr. Yu's courtyard was truly made of stone, and under the twilight sky, the stone slabs emitted a faint bluish light.

Mr. Yu's courtyard was spotlessly clean. As dinner time approached, we sat in the middle of the courtyard eating freshly picked vegetables, drinking fragrant millet porridge, and enjoying chicken stewed in a large firewood pot prepared by Mr. Yu two hours in advance! After dinner, Mr. Yu took us to the roof. To facilitate drying grain, the roofs of homes in the stone village are all flat. Picking up a peanut and peeling it, the sweet and crispy taste delighted us. Mr. Yu chuckled and said, "All our vegetables and grains are grown ourselves, fertilized naturally, completely pollution-free, much tastier than in the city."

Looking up at the starry sky, it seemed like you could touch the stars with your hand. The dark mountains in the distance stretched endlessly, and the stars seemed to rise from behind them. Occasionally, fireflies flew by, surprising me after living in the city for so long. Mr. Yu said that later on, the fields would be filled with swarms of fireflies, even more beautiful. Talking about the stories of the Yu family village, Mr. Yu laughed humbly, saying he wasn't good at telling them, suggesting we hire a guide the next day: "They tell it better." But he couldn't help feeling proud: "We are all descendants of Yu Qian, with deep cultural roots. Many scholars and officials have come from the Yu family village." For over five hundred years, few people have moved out of Yu family village. It is only in recent years, with improved living conditions, that some have moved to the city or other provinces.

"Is this stone village really left over from hundreds of years ago? Hasn't it been repaired or renovated?"

"Some minor repairs have been done, but basically, it remains as it was hundreds of years ago. Otherwise, how could it be selected as one of the fifty ancient villages in the country?" Mr. Yu pointed at the courtyard wall, saying, "Look at my courtyard wall. Next door is our old courtyard, which is no longer inhabited, but it was indeed left from back then, over three hundred years old. As for repairs, if the gaps between stones get too big, we just fill them with lime. There have been no major changes. Stone houses don't need foundations. During the great earthquake in Xingtai, not a single house or courtyard wall in our Yu family village collapsed!" Before descending from the roof, Mr. Yu said, "Don't rush. Wait until tomorrow morning when you climb the nearby small mountain to see the full view of the stone village!"

An epic written in stone

The next morning, we went again to the roof to watch the sunrise. The orange-red sun slowly rose from behind the mountains, lighting up the stone village. The stone roofs connected into a patchwork, uneven and staggered, covered with golden corn almost hiding the blue roofs. This expanse of gold extended towards the nearby stone mountains, stopping abruptly at their feet.

We climbed the small mountain mentioned by Mr. Yu. Although it was all stone mountains, wherever there was soil on the slopes, crops were planted. The full view of the stone village appeared before our eyes. This beautiful ancient stone village was so peaceful under the morning sun, yet also so magnificent, as if containing a mysterious force that made one respect it involuntarily.

Descending the mountain and strolling through the village, the stone roads shimmered with a pale red glow under the morning sun, entirely different from last night's weathered gray appearance. The entire stone village was full of vitality. We followed the uneven stone roads deeper into the village.

The ancestors of the Yu family had clear norms for housing layouts and street designs. Streets ran east-west, alleys north-south, and dead-ends were called lanes. Allegedly, the village had six streets, seven alleys, and eighteen lanes, totaling nearly 4000 meters. Streets were three to four meters wide, and alleys and lanes were about two to three meters wide. These narrow streets were all paved with blue stones. Materials mined from the surrounding stone mountains provided the main material for houses and courtyards, and the leftover materials became these stone streets. These blue stones varied in size and shape; tracing their history, some were five hundred years old, others three hundred. Over time, with the coming and going of people and animals, each stone was polished smooth and shiny.

One large estate was not built of stone but brick and tile, belonging to a wealthy family that produced twelve scholar-officials during the Ming and Qing dynasties. Thus, they built a brick-and-tile estate to display their noble status. No wonder Mr. Yu said that only wealthy families could afford brick-and-tile houses in the past. Now, brick-and-tile houses are common, while stone houses have become popular attractions for tourists.

In the Yu family village, there were no fewer than a hundred stone quadrangle courtytes. Grandma Huo's grape courtyard had a gnarled grapevine upon entry, shading the entire courtyard. Clusters of grapes hung from the trellis. The rooms in the courtyard were constructed in cave-like structures using large stones. The north room was 300 years old, while the south room, divided into two sections, was 200 years old, both featuring large beds. Grandma Huo, 82 years old, with white hair, neatly dressed, and spirited, leaned against the door, basking in the sunlight.

Another feature of the stone village was its temples of various styles. Located in the center of the stone village, the Zhenwu Temple faces south and was originally built during the Jiajing era. It is a brick-and-stone structure. Inside the temple's main hall stands a gilded statue of Zhenwu. A stone tablet in the courtyard records the founding strategy of the Yu family village, the location of building materials, and other content, serving as a rare "village history." The village also has a Guanyin Pavilion and a Pantheon, each with a stage opposite it. On the first and fifteenth days of each lunar month, or on auspicious days celebrating deities' birthdays or enlightenment, sacrificial ceremonies and temple fairs take place in front of the shrines, featuring performances rich in local customs on the stages. Beside each stage is a well, some abandoned, others still in use. Interestingly, besides basic functions, tour guide Ms. Fan said the wells have a special function: amplifying sound. Performances rely on these wells to spread sound further. However, I cannot figure out the principle of well amplification and wish to hear its effect firsthand.

Unknowingly, we reached the core of the Yu family village, the Yu family ancestral hall, and the celestial instrument. Here, the Yu family has preserved an epic of survival written with a resilient stone spirit over centuries.

An epic of survival written with resilience

The Yu family ancestral hall is also a stone quadrangle courtyard, well-preserved, covering 300 square meters, with 15 rooms dedicated to the veneration of Yu family ancestors and deceased descendants. It also narrates the origin of the Yu family village.

After Yu Qian was unjustly executed, his eldest son was killed by villains, and his second son was protected by the common people and fled to Nanyu Village in Jingxing County beyond the Niangziguan Pass to live in seclusion, having three sons. During the Chenghua period, due to "poverty making survival difficult," the eldest son, Yu Youdao, moved his family to this remote wilderness to live in seclusion. At that time, this place was uninhabited. The Yu family worked hard to create a life, building houses and stacking stones, creating stone tools to cultivate land and grow crops, and crafting all cooking and dining utensils from stone. Through spring winds and autumn rains, amidst the vicissitudes of life, the Yu family thrived here, relying on the abundant stones to develop this piece of land, multiplying from a few families to a stone village. Later named Bai Miao Village due to a white temple on the northwest mountain, it was eventually renamed Yu Family Village. Yu Youdao had five sons, each branch forming a lineage and household. The ancestral hall lists the names of the deceased from each branch. Except for the third son's descendants who soon migrated to Shanxi and lost their records, the genealogies of the other four branches have continued to this day, reaching the twenty-seventh generation of Yu Qian's descendants. At the entrance of the ancestral hall, red notices of joy are posted, announcing the addition of children in each branch, bringing happiness to the entire clan. The long history and customs of the Yu family will continue with life.

In the center of the ancestral hall stands an ancient cypress tree known by three names: the twisted trunk is called the Twisted Cypress, the dragon-like branches with five claws are called the Dragon Cypress, symbolizing the four branches remaining in the Yu family village, while the slightly finer branch represents the third son's branch that migrated away, extending precisely toward Shanxi. More curiously, this branch faces exactly the direction where the third son settled. The Tea Cypress is named for its unique leaves used to brew tea, sweet and fragrant. The ancient tree adds mystery to the Yu family ancestral hall, as if imbued with spiritual energy under the Yu family's spiritual influence.

Passing through the village, far off in the distance stands the towering Cool Pavilion, built on a slope with natural boulders, marking the eastern entrance of the stone village. This is the iconic building of the stone village, representing another manifestation of the Yu family's determination and strength. Also known as the Immortal Pavilion, it served as the ancient villagers' gateway to the stone village, built in 1581 (the ninth year of the Wanli era of the Ming dynasty). Legend has it that it was built solely by Yu Xichun, who possessed immense strength. The wind-sensitive plaque hanging on the second floor, moving with slight winds but staying still with strong ones, was destroyed during the Cultural Revolution. Recently, the villagers attempted to recreate it, trying many methods without success, nearly damaging an ancient stone on the second floor. It is now impossible for later generations to guess how Yu Xichun managed to hang the wind-sensitive plaque alone.

Returning to the starting point of the Yu family stone village, the stone museum, every stone tool displayed there is carefully crafted by the Yu family, permeated with their diligence and wisdom. The museum exhibits two intact stone steles: the "Rectifying Village Rules" stele erected during the Wanli era of the Ming dynasty and the "Willow Pool Prohibition" stele erected during the Qianlong era of the Qing dynasty to regulate water usage among the villagers. The inscriptions reflect the management system and moral norms formed by the Yu family, showcasing their pursuit of a harmonious and benevolent society.

Leaving this ancient and rustic stone village, stepping out of this history filled with misfortune yet imbued with familial affection, and departing from the spirit and customs passed down through generations by the Yu family, I looked back and felt a heavy heart, filled with satisfaction and emotion. Behind me, that beautiful and mysterious village lay asleep in the afternoon sun, quietly recounting its story in its dreams.

Tips:

1. It is recommended to hire a guide. Don't save money by wandering around blindly. All temples and ancestral halls are locked, and only guides have the keys. Guides provide detailed explanations, revealing many unknown histories and customs.

2. There is a small mountain to the east of the village. Climbing it offers a panoramic view of the stone village, but be careful not to damage the locals' crops.
