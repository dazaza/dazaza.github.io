Who is the first governor of the staff:

by william9355 on 2012-02-08 10:54:00

When trainers conduct sessions, they often ask the business owners or distributor bosses in the audience: who is the first person that employees think of? The answers are varied. Some say the employee themselves; some say the boss; others say money, policy, or even habits. These answers make sense within this context but aren't what the author is looking for. Among Chinese people, who do they respect the most? Before an employee starts their job, who is the one guiding them? Who is the first to know when an employee decides to switch jobs, and who provides the greatest support? It's undoubtedly the employee's parents. Often, the boss is the last to know. In many cases, the employee's primary influence is their parents.

For companies, incorporating the employee’s parents into their strategy can be beneficial, yet it is often overlooked or forgotten by most businesses. Parents don’t require a lot of time and effort, but they have a significant "leverage effect" on employees. A private enterprise owner deeply understands the importance of treating employees' parents well. His company has many veteran employees who have worked there for five or ten years. Because of the stability of his workforce, labor productivity is particularly high. There was a couple working at his company from the countryside. When the father fell ill, despite it being the production off-season, the boss still gave the couple a month off to return home and care for their elderly parent, without reducing their pay by a single cent. The couple’s monthly salary amounted to over ten thousand yuan, and not a penny was withheld. Before passing away, the father said to his children: "If you don’t work hard for your boss, I will not rest peacefully in the afterlife!" The impact of these words on the employees is unimaginable. Truly, one word from a parent is worth ten thousand words from a company!

One of the hottest phenomena this year is the "Haidilao phenomenon." The legend of a hot pot restaurant became a nationwide sensation due to a book written by a Peking University professor titled "Haidilao: You Can't Learn It." Haidilao stands out because, in an industry with the lowest skill requirements, no market barriers, no need for connections, fierce competition, and low worker qualifications — the hot pot restaurant business — it has established a threshold that even competitors in the same field and other food service industries find hard to surpass. Haidilao's secret is incredibly simple: treat employees as people, find ways to make employees feel like the company is their home, and they will put their heart into serving customers. Simple, right? Very simple, but very few companies can achieve it, hence the saying "Haidilao: You Can't Learn It."

Haidilao excels in recognizing the importance of the primary influencer of employees — their parents. Haidilao has institutionalized policies concerning employees’ parents. First, Haidilao sends a monthly stipend to the parents of employees who are team leaders or above. Second, every year they organize tours for the parents of outstanding employees. Third, the company conducts annual visits to the homes of employees. These actions represent the company showing filial piety on behalf of the employees, accurately tapping into the psychology of Chinese parents. Not only does this comfort the hearts of the employees' parents, but it also makes the employees feel more valued. Through these measures, Haidilao brings the employees' parents into its fold, aligning them with the company’s goals, making employees willingly committed and loyal!

For smaller enterprises or distributors with fewer employees, it is even more important to leverage the role of the primary influencer — the parents. Since the scale is small and the number of people limited, the team places greater emphasis on human relationships and requires more familial and emotional human touches, which are easier to implement regarding employees' parents. For example, some excellent distributors who understand this concept invite the parents of employees for meals and send them daily necessities (such as firewood, rice, oil, salt, soy sauce, vinegar, tea) directly to their homes during holidays, all of which are great practices involving employees' parents.

If we say that the Chinese have faith, then that faith lies in the concept of "home." If we talk about China's longest-standing culture, the "home" culture deserves that title. From traditional sayings like “Parents are alive, so one should not travel far” or “Among the three types of unfilial behavior, having no heir is the worst,” the shadow of family culture is deeply embedded. Only when employees take care of their own families can they truly care about the company! It's not that the river dries up when the sea runs dry, but rather that when the streams dry up, the river dries too! The primary influencer of good employees — their parents — dear business owners, haven't you started considering this yet?
