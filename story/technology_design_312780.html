Barca being accused of handing over the championship ignites a shockwave; Opponent: We'll give you 70 points, we'll compete for second place.

by qinglai888 on 2009-12-17 17:49:40

Barcelona Accused of Being Gifted the Title, Sparking Controversy: Opponent Remarks - "Give You 70 Points, We'll Compete for Second"

According to reports on Sina Sports, after last weekend's Barcelona Derby match against Espanyol, the Spanish side felt they had been unfairly defeated, while Barcelona felt their victory lacked dignity. The blame was directed at referee Iturralde. Due to his "mysterious" decision to award Barcelona a penalty, it helped the home team secure a 1-0 win over their local rivals Espanyol. Subsequently, Iturralde showed a red card to Espanyol player Baena in the players' tunnel after the game, as Baena continuously questioned the validity of the penalty call.

In all fairness, this penalty seemed deliberately aimed at harming Espanyol. Similarly intriguing is that from one single penalty decision, two entirely different versions of the truth emerged, represented by Barcelona and Espanyol factions.

If Bruel is considered part of the "Real Madrid clique" among referees, then Iturralde, one of Spain's renowned referees, belongs to the "Barcelona faction." In previous matches officiated by Iturralde at Camp Nou, Barcelona has never lost. This season, he only officiated one Barcelona match, where they triumphed 2-0 away against Getafe. Another telling fact is that in Iturralde's past six games involving Barcelona, five penalties were awarded, all benefiting Barcelona. According to *Marca*, four of these penalties were questionable, hinting at the possibility of "gifted penalties."

The controversial moment occurred in the 39th minute of the match against Espanyol when Ibrahimovic crossed from the left edge of the box, and Xavi suddenly fell while contesting for the ball. Iturralde ruled that Baena had pulled Xavi's shirt during his defensive retreat, leading to the penalty decision, which immediately incited strong dissatisfaction from the Espanyol side. After the game, young coach Pochettino remarked cryptically: “I didn't see clearly what happened inside the box. Maybe the referee did. Even after watching the replay, I couldn't be sure if it was a penalty. My players told me at halftime that nothing happened, so in my view, this penalty does not exist.” Espanyol player Luis Garcia expressed his grievances, complaining about Iturralde's unfair judgment harming Espanyol: “If we follow this standard, we could also get a penalty inside Barcelona’s box. Yes, Barcelona is very strong, but they shouldn't score through such penalties.”

The reaction within Barcelona's camp was quite the opposite. Xavi, who was involved in the incident, testified: “This was an obvious penalty, didn't you see? Inside the box, my jersey was grabbed; Baena pulled my shirt, preventing me from jumping. There is no doubt, this was a penalty. He pulled me, and because I couldn't use my legs properly, I fell.” After Xavi fell, Guardiola innocently spread his hands outside the field, indicating support for his player's claim for a penalty. However, when discussing the call post-game, Guardiola admitted he wasn’t sure if it was a penalty either, simply stating, “Xavi told me Baena pulled him, and I believe Xavi.”

Clearly, Espanyol's manager Planes didn’t trust Xavi: “How can Xavi say that? Following Iturralde’s standards, there could have been 12 penalties in this Barcelona derby! I insist that there was no physical contact between Baena and Xavi. If there was, it certainly wouldn’t have caused Xavi to fall.” CEO Collet added, “Why is it always us getting hurt in every derby? Last time it was a penalty in the 93rd minute, and Messi’s handball, this time again... If our opponent was Real Madrid, someone would have surely cried out to overthrow Franco already. The federation should consider this issue. If this keeps happening, why not just give Barcelona 70 points and let the rest of us compete for second place?”

All other comments were irrelevant. Regarding the penalty controversy, Baena, the involved party, remained silent, refusing to speak until two days after the event when he appeared on Catalan Radio to explain the situation. “Yes, I did pull Xavi,” he admitted, confirming the existence of the penalty. However, either Xavi's act of falling was too exaggerated, or Iturralde made an incorrect judgment, so he continued, “But it definitely wasn’t a penalty. My pull on Xavi wasn’t enough to make him lose balance and fall. Xavi's position was very advanced; I reached back to sense his location. I slightly obstructed Xavi, but with a force insufficient to make him fall. This can be reviewed on TV replays. Honestly, I found it hard to believe it would be called a penalty, as I was thinking before turning around that Xavi might shoot.”

There were also reports that Baena confronted Iturralde in the players’ tunnel after the game, demanding justice, resulting in a red card. What exactly did Baena say to provoke Iturralde? “I just told him to look at the TV replay! But I definitely didn’t use any aggressive tone, that’s all.” What upset Baena further was Guardiola, who mentioned in the post-match press conference, “Xavi told me that Espanyol players admitted it was a penalty.” “I really don’t understand how they could say that, I never told Xavi it was a penalty. If we had really discussed it, I would have certainly told him it wasn’t a penalty.”

This controversy provided material for Madrid-based media to stir up. Media opinions on this contentious decision were strong. Both *Marca* and *As* pointed fingers at Iturralde, accusing him of protecting Barcelona and allowing them to take three points, otherwise Real Madrid, having played one fewer game, would only trail Barcelona by two points. Conversely, Catalan media, despite Espanyol being a Barcelona-based team, wouldn’t defend them, especially since their club badge carries the Spanish royal crest. To counteract capital city media, *Sport* carefully studied the match footage and even attached screenshots showing Baena pulling Xavi. Deputy editor Carazo commented, “Clearly, this was a penalty.” More interestingly, the publication also included a screenshot of Real Madrid’s match against Valencia, where Benzema was potentially offside when receiving the ball for Real Madrid’s first goal against Valencia. The headline provocatively read: “Real Madrid’s first goal was offside, alright, what’s there to argue about?”

Fortunately, the parties involved in the penalty controversy—Barcelona, Xavi, and Guardiola—have moved away from the storm center in Spain and started their FIFA Club World Cup journey in Abu Dhabi. In their eyes, winning this title is much more meaningful than debating whether that was a penalty or not. (Liu Hui)
