Hulu CEO Giral: Internet's impact on content industry surpasses television

by bingo123 on 2012-03-08 17:13:57

Hulu CEO Jason Kilar

Sina Technology News, March 3rd - In a recent interview with the media, the CEO of the American professional video website Hulu, Jason Kilar, stated that the internet, like television, was initially met with resistance by content creators but would eventually bring about change. Its impact could even surpass that of television.

The following is an excerpt from the interview:

Q: You believe that the entire content industry will undergo fundamental changes. Please explain.

A: For me, this is similar to the invention of television, which came at a time when theaters and the film industry were flourishing. Initially, television was seen as the embodiment of evil by the content industry. Over time, it has become one of the best channels for content creators. I think the internet is the same. In fact, the internet's influence on content creators may even exceed that of television, because suddenly we can publish content around the clock, not only in every room of our home, but also in other places. The TV screen can be put in your pocket through a smartphone.

Q: What is the current state of pay-TV?

A: Today's pay-TV industry is very healthy and doing well. Using Netflix costs money, watching HBO costs money, viewing Vudu costs money, and so does Hulu Plus. In fact, the only difference between these services is the content you get for your money. For about $85, you can get a big package with hundreds of TV channels, mainly watched in the living room. Through other services, you can spend $7, $8 or $9 to buy some small program packages. The entire industry will remain healthy for a long time.

Q: What about content creators?

A: It's a great time for content creators right now.

Q: That’s what you're working on. You're not just content distributors, but also content creators.

A: We launched our first original series "Battleground" a few weeks ago. We did this for two reasons: First, we believe it tells a meaningful story; Second, we believe it will make services like Hulu stand out.

Q: There's a lot of content on your site that users can see elsewhere. How do you think people will choose?

A: Ultimately, it comes down to ease of use and value, which are the thoughts in people's minds and hearts. This involves many different aspects: how easy is it to use? Is the design good? Is the price reasonable? Do I have a close relationship with this brand? This is also the direction we strive for every day, including reducing search engine latency, aggregating thousands of content providers, and providing a pleasant user experience.

Q: How do you judge the winners?

A: It depends on who provides the most value to users. By the way, I think there will be many winners. This is a very broad industry.

Q: How do you measure the scale?

A: I consider the amount of time global users spend on it. So, first is sleeping, second is working, third is visual, auditory, and dynamic image consumption. Americans basically spend four or five hours a day watching videos, so it's a big deal. It's an important way people spend their leisure time. I think such a large market will definitely attract a lot of people.

Q: You've launched a service called Hulu Ad Swap. What is it?

A: I really like Hulu Ad Swap because one of the important innovations in the history of video advertising was the introduction of color. What we've introduced rivals the invention of colorized video ads. Imagine: you're watching something you like, and suddenly an ad pops up that you don't like. At this point, you can sell that ad via Hulu Ad Swap. So this is the first time users can control their own ad experience. To me, since users can control what they see, it will be a major victory. Advertisers will also benefit because they can improve relevance.

Q: What is the biggest threat you face?

A: I'm most concerned about our culture, which is hard to deal with. I think great companies ultimately come from great cultures. We have invested a lot of effort from the beginning to think about what the ideal culture of the next generation of tech and media companies should be, which is a tricky problem.
