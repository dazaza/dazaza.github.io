A photographer captured the stunning moment of a kingfisher diving to catch fish. According to The Daily Telegraph, Charlie Hamilton James photographed the kingfisher plunging into the water to catch fish, part of a series of photographs published in the November issue of National Geographic magazine.

Kingfishers dive at speeds of up to 25 miles per hour (approximately 40 kilometers), catching fish up to 60 centimeters below the water's surface. Their dives are so fast that typically all you see is a flash of blue as it enters the water. Each kingfisher protects a specific area, which serves as its stable "fishing ground" and a good nesting site.

Whether male or female, kingfishers are solitary animals with a strong sense of territorial protection. Hamilton James said, "They make a lot of noise, as if telling other birds that it has arrived. I think kingfishers are quite arrogant."

Since seeing his first kingfisher near his home in Bristol 20 years ago, the photographer began pursuing and documenting kingfishers. He said, "Few British people who have seen a kingfisher will ever forget where they saw one. I was a child when I saw my first kingfisher, and since then, I've been fascinated by these birds."

Kingfishers are mainly distributed in central and southern England, often appearing near rivers or still waters, feeding on fish and aquatic insects. It is estimated that there are between 4800 to 8000 pairs of kingfishers kept by people in the UK.
