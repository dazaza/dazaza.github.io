Google Reveals Its 9 Principles of Innovation

Wondering what makes Google the holy grail of productivity and creativity? It's certainly not drinking the magical water in Mountain View. Instead, it’s about adhering to the so-called "Nine Principles of Innovation" as advocated by Google's Chief Evangelist for Brand Marketing, Gopi Kallayil. Any business, regardless of size, can learn from Google's culture of innovation.

1. Innovation is everywhere

Innovation can come from anywhere—top-down or bottom-up—even from places you least expect. For example, a doctor treating Google employees pointed out that Google had a responsibility to help people who searched for "how to commit suicide." This led Google to adjust its search results, displaying the phone number of the U.S. National Suicide Prevention Lifeline at the top of the screen. Soon after, calls to the hotline increased by 9%. Later, similar adjustments were made in many countries (note: apparently not in China).

2. Focus on the user

Don't worry about money right away; first, focus on the user, and everything else will fall into place. By offering predictive analysis when users type just a few letters, Google improved search speed with instant search, saving milliseconds for every search conducted by each user. Google's sales team worried this would reduce the time customers spent viewing ads, but the company proceeded, believing it was worth the risk.

What was the result? Thanks to Instant Search, Google calculated that one year after its launch, the time saved amounted to giving humanity back 5,000 years. "Create a great user experience, and revenue will take care of itself," Kallayil said. Additionally, more customers will be attracted by the benefits your product brings.

3. Aim for ten times better

If you only aim for a 10% improvement, you'll only see incremental changes. If you want radical, revolutionary innovation, aim for a 10x improvement, which will force you to think outside the box. For instance, in 2004, Google started the Google Books project, proposing to organize all the world's information and digitize every book ever printed—a massive challenge.

Google's co-founders developed their own book scanner, initially requiring someone to manually turn pages in sync with the scanner. Now, Google has already scanned 30 million of the 130 million books they originally planned to digitize, with dozens of libraries worldwide participating in the project.

4. Bet on technical insight

Every organization has unique insights, and if you bet on them, they can lead to major innovations. The idea for self-driving cars came from Google engineers, not the automotive industry—because they didn’t ignore the millions of traffic deaths caused by human error. Google Maps, Google Earth, Street View cars—all the components needed to develop self-driving cars were already in place. Collaborating with Stanford University's artificial intelligence team, Google engineers have produced experimental self-driving cars capable of driving freely between Lake Tahoe and the Bay Area, enabling blind individuals to drive independently for shopping or errands.

5. Ship and iterate

Don't wait until everything is perfect; ship early and often. Let users help you "upgrade." In 2008, when Chrome was launched, Google released an improved version every six weeks. "Now Chrome has become the No. 1 browser in many countries using this approach," Kallayil said: "Your product may not be perfect, but trust that users will bring perfection back to you."

6. Give employees 20% free time

Allow employees 20% of their work time to pursue projects they are passionate about, even if these projects are not part of the company's core mission or objectives. "They will delight you with their creative thinking," Kallayil assured. At Google, engineers and project managers have one day per week to experiment with ideas they love. Many of these ideas have been transformed into products or product improvements. A good example is an engineer planning a trip to Spain who realized he couldn’t get close-ups of his inn because the street was too narrow for the Street View car. He modified the Street View camera, mounting it on a custom-built Google tricycle to capture images of tourist attractions inaccessible to cars.

7. Default to open processes

Open your processes to all users. Crowdsource ideas and leverage the collective power of your users. When Google created the Android platform, it knew it couldn’t hire all the best developers in the world. Therefore, it adopted a "default open" policy, encouraging developers outside Google to create apps for the billion people using Android devices daily. "That's how ecosystems form," Kallayil said. In marketing, Google also sought user input on how to promote its voice search app, with some children sending clever videos that rivaled those produced by major advertising agencies.

8. Failure is good

Failure should not carry a stigma. If you're not failing much, you're probably not trying hard enough. At Google, any product that cannot reach its full potential is discontinued, but the best features are retained. "In fact, failure is an honor," he said: "Failure is the path to innovation and success. You can be proud to fail."

9. Have a mission

"This is the most important principle," Kallayil said: "Everyone at Google has a strong sense of mission and purpose. We believe our work positively impacts hundreds of millions of people." Everyone should have their own story.
