The consequences of not treating hemorrhoids can be unimaginable.

by s9w78dp7 on 2012-03-05 11:21:24

Hemorrhoids include internal and external hemorrhoids, which respectively refer to the mass formed by the dilation of the upper and lower venous plexus of the rectum above and below the dentate line (an anatomical structure of the anus). The dilated veins may rupture and bleed due to friction during defecation, or form a mass that prolapses, causing great suffering to the patient. Hemorrhoids generally do not pose a serious threat to the health of the body, but their presence can easily lead to misdiagnosis of other diseases. Experts from Beijing Fengyi Proctology Hospital point out that if hemorrhoids are not treated in time, they could potentially trigger rectal cancer, leading to unimaginable consequences. The most prominent similar symptom between hemorrhoids and rectal cancer is bloody stool, and often the blood is fresh. Especially in the early stages of rectal polyps and rectal cancer (in the late stage, tumor bleeding and necrosis may result in purulent bloody stools), there are no other discomforts except for bloody stools, making it easy to mistake them for hemorrhoids. Additionally, rectal polyps or rectal cancer can easily compress the rectal veins, causing obstruction of blood return in the rectal veins and subsequently leading to the occurrence of hemorrhoids.

Rectal cancer refers to malignant tumors occurring in the rectum. Due to its proximity to the anus, it often manifests as dripping blood during defecation. Only when the tumor grows inward into the intestinal cavity and becomes quite large will obstruction symptoms appear. Therefore, in the early stages, it is easy to confuse the symptoms with those of hemorrhoids, making it difficult to distinguish between the two. Many people assume they have hemorrhoids and do not take it seriously. Hence, teaching patients how to differentiate between the two becomes crucial.

Firstly, hemorrhoids usually do not cause difficulty in defecation, especially thinning of the stool. Even if there is prolapse and incarceration of the hemorrhoidal nucleus, it only manifests as short-term reluctance to defecate due to pain during defecation. Once the edema and inflammation subside, normal function is immediately restored. However, patients with rectal cancer may experience difficulty in defecation and thinning of the stool even in the early stages, accompanied by abdominal distension, intermittent abdominal pain, and sometimes even audible bowel sounds. Once these symptoms appear, they generally do not resolve on their own and tend to progressively worsen.

Secondly, in patients with rectal cancer, the tumor often does not shrink but gradually enlarges, presenting as cauliflower-like, pale red masses that bleed easily upon touch. On the other hand, the swelling caused by hemorrhoid edema often appears smooth, dark red or purple, and does not bleed easily upon touch.

Moreover, in the late stages of rectal cancer, symptoms may appear due to tumor invasion of surrounding tissues, such as urinary pain and frequency if the bladder is invaded, severe continuous pain if the sacral nerves are affected, liver enlargement and jaundice if the liver is metastasized, along with anemia and other phenomena. Hemorrhoids do not exhibit these symptoms.

Experts from Beijing Fengyi Proctology Hospital state that discovering rectal cancer clinically is not difficult. Since rectal cancer often occurs in the rectal ampulla below the peritoneal reflection line, it can usually be detected through a digital rectal examination. Adding colonoscopy generally confirms the diagnosis. Therefore, once you notice symptoms different from those of hemorrhoids, you should promptly seek medical attention for relevant examinations. You must not forego these checks due to fear of pain or a history of hemorrhoids, allowing the killer rectal cancer to escape detection. With proper treatment, especially in the early stages, the five-year survival rate for rectal cancer can reach over 80%.

Under current medical conditions, some malignant tumors are not frightening, what is truly terrifying is failing to detect them early. Many malignant tumors occurring in the colon or rectum, if diagnosed and treated early, yield very satisfactory results. Therefore, patients with bloody stools, whether or not they have hemorrhoids, should regularly visit the hospital for doctors to perform rectal or colonoscopic examinations. When symptoms commonly associated with hemorrhoids, such as bloody stools, are found, it is essential to consider excluding other intestinal diseases. During the treatment of hemorrhoids, if symptoms persist without significant improvement or if bloody stools recur, it should be taken seriously. Beijing Fengyi Proctology reminds patients not to let small hemorrhoids delay the diagnosis of diseases like rectal cancer.
