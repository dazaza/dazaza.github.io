A few days ago, during a meeting, I met an old friend whom I hadn't seen for many years. During the break, he hurriedly approached me and said, "Doctor Wang, I really want to have a chat with you." As expected, the next day, he called me. On the phone, he hesitantly said, "Doctor Wang, I will be 60 years old next year. Recently, I've gradually noticed that my sexual function has declined compared to before. I want to ask, at my age, should sexual activity stop? Or should it be moderated? Is the decline in sexual function related to age?"

This question raised by my old friend is not uncommon in daily life. In the clinic, many middle-aged and elderly people also ask me if they should moderate their sexual activities when they get older. Some also ask if the decline in sexual function is a precursor to stopping sexual activity. Some view sexual activities among the elderly as "not good," and even their children don't believe their parents would "do this."

This is actually a misunderstanding of sexuality. Because, from the moment a baby is born until death, sexuality accompanies a person's entire life. We often see toddlers sucking their fingers, which actually demonstrates the initial sexual desire impulse. Thereafter, in various stages of life, there are varying degrees of sexual awareness. Adolescents entering puberty begin to awaken their sexual consciousness, showing affinity and attraction towards the opposite sex. This is the stage where sexual awareness and sexual physiology rapidly develop. After marriage, vigorous sexual desires are released and mature. With increasing age, the onset of menopause in women marks the beginning of changes in sexual function, while men start experiencing a gradual decrease in serum testosterone levels around the age of 60. Naturally, this leads to a decline in sexual interest, reduced sensitivity to sexual stimulation, fewer instances of sexual activity, less frequent penile erection during intercourse, prolonged time to reach orgasm, or no ejaculation or minimal ejaculation during intercourse. Some may experience symptoms such as erectile dysfunction, weak erections, or impotent ejaculations. This is similar to the aging of other organs in the body and is a normal physiological phenomenon.

However, these changes in sexual function due to age only indicate variations in the degree and intensity of sexual function in elderly men and women, and does not equate to the disappearance of sexual function. A Japanese scholar, in order to investigate the sexual lives of the elderly, asked his elderly mother, but she just smiled without answering, continuously stirring the remaining ashes in the fire basin with a small wooden stick. The son finally understood that his mother was telling him that the sexual needs and activities of the elderly, like embers, do not cease until they are completely extinguished.

For a physically and mentally healthy elderly couple, sexual interest can last until the ages of 70 to 90, or even longer. However, due to the influence of traditional concepts and excessive emphasis in some books on the need for elderly men to moderate sexual activity, "abstain from sexual intercourse," "sleep alone," etc., such erroneous and abnormal sexual psychology propaganda has led some elderly men and women to develop feelings of repression and guilt regarding sex. To a large extent, this hinders the normal awakening and expression of their sexual desires and capabilities. Over time, they may truly become sexually indifferent or incapable.

For middle-aged and elderly friends over fifty, on one hand, they need to recognize the changes in their own situation, and on the other hand, they should eliminate the influence of some traditional incorrect teachings from a psychological perspective. Post-menopausal women, due to vaginal dryness, can increase the time of caressing during sexual activity, and the actions should be gentle. Vaginal lubricants can also be used before intercourse to reduce penile friction pressure. For men, first, certain neurological diseases or conditions causing erectile dysfunction must be ruled out. For example, coronary heart disease patients can prepare nitroglycerin before sexual activity, avoid having intercourse when full or fatigued, keep the duration of intercourse short, and avoid overly strenuous movements.
