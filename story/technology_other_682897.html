The Principle and Significance of Ball Mill Grinding Media Grading

by hx515317 on 2012-02-29 10:27:17

The task of the grinding media is to crush the lumps fed into the mill and grind them into fine powder. The particle size of the material fed into the mill is around 20mm, and it ultimately needs to be ground into a fine powder less than 0.08mm (the sieve residue generally should not exceed 15%). For the large chunks of material newly fed into the mill (inside the coarse grinding chamber), the grinding media primarily delivers strong impacts while also providing some grinding action to break the material down. During this process, there are inevitable collisions between the grinding media themselves. The loud noise generated during the mill's operation mainly comes from the coarse grinding chamber. As the material particle size decreases, it moves into the next chamber where the grinding media shifts to primarily grinding, gradually reducing the noise. After being finely ground, the material is discharged from the mill. Different types and specifications of grinding media are used in different grinding chambers.

Principles for ball mill grinding media grading:

1. Match according to the particle size, hardness, grindability of the feed material, and the required fineness of the product.

2. There should be distinctions in steel ball grading between large and small mills, raw material mills, and cement mills.

3. To control the material flow rate and meet fineness requirements, large and small balls are often used together, reducing the void ratio of the steel balls to slow down the material flow rate and extend the material's retention time inside the mill.

4. When grading the grinding media for each chamber, there should generally be fewer large and small balls and more medium-sized balls — that is, smaller at both ends and larger in the middle.

5. Single-chamber ball mills should be fully loaded with steel balls, not steel segments; double-chamber mills use steel balls in the first chamber and steel segments in the second; for mills with three or more chambers, the first two chambers are usually loaded with steel balls, and the rest with steel segments.

6. In closed-circuit mills, since there is return material entering the mill, the impact force of the steel balls is weakened due to buffering effects, so the average diameter of the steel balls should be larger.

7. When the selected liners result in insufficient ball carrying capacity and reduced impact force, the proportion of large balls should be appropriately increased.

8. The total loading amount of the grinding media should not exceed the design-permitted loading amount.

Significance of ball mill grinding media grading:

The size of the steel balls and their mass combination is referred to as the grading of the grinding media. The quality of this grading directly affects the mill's output and the consumption of the grinding media. Grading is mainly determined based on factors such as the physical and chemical properties of the material being ground, the construction of the mill, and the fineness requirements of the product. In the grinding process, the material initially has a larger block size, requiring larger diameter steel balls for impact crushing. As the block size decreases, smaller steel balls are needed to grind the material, increasing the grinding ability. Under constant grinding media loading conditions, reducing the size of the grinding media increases the contact area of the grinding media, enhancing the grinding ability. The selection of steel ball specifications has a certain relationship with the particle size of the material being ground. The larger the material particle size, the larger the average diameter of the steel balls should be. Therefore, using only large-diameter or only small-diameter grinding media inside the mill is unsuitable. It is necessary to ensure both adequate impact ability and sufficient grinding ability to achieve high-quality, high-yield, and low-consumption goals.
