Superficial phlebitis can occur in various parts of the body and usually affects the limbs, followed by the chest and abdominal walls. In some cases, it may present as migratory episodes. The clinical characteristics include: sudden redness, swelling, heat, pain, or tenderness along the superficial veins, with the appearance of cord-like structures or hard lumps. After the acute phase, the cord-like structure becomes hardened, and there is hyperpigmentation of the local skin.

Deep vein thrombophlebitis generally occurs in the lower leg, calf veins, thoracic veins, and femoral iliac veins. The initial symptoms of deep vein thrombophlebitis are not obvious. Only during the middle to late stages does the pain manifest, allowing the patient to feel the effects of the condition. Therefore, compared to superficial phlebitis, deep vein thrombophlebitis poses greater risks.
