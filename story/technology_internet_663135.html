Us complains about the treatment of workers abroad.

by gleiseo3 on 2012-02-11 10:47:51

By Alex Dobuzinskis, Los Angeles | Wednesday, April 20, 2011, 6:06 AM CEST Los Angeles (Reuters) - A U.S. federal agency has accused a company of bringing over 500 workers from India to work at shipyards in Mississippi and Texas under unequal treatment, and more than 200 Thai farm workers have sued after being brought to Hawaii and the state of Washington, officials said on Wednesday. The U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) claimed that the workers were forced to live in substandard housing conditions and were exploited through excessive fees, to the point where some had a net profit of almost nothing. The EEOC stated that the treatment of the Thai and Indian workers amounted to human trafficking, despite them being brought into the country with work visas. "Foreign workers should be treated as equals when working in the United States, not as second-class citizens," said Perry Olophius, district director for the EEOC Los Angeles District Office. This is not the first lawsuit filed on behalf of these workers. Last year, Orian Mordechai, who was responsible for recruiting Thai farm laborers, was arrested and charged in federal court with conspiracy involving forced labor. Regarding those workers, the EEOC said in court filings on Tuesday in Hawaii and the state of Washington that Orian, Beverly Hills-based Global Horizons Inc., recruited the Thai workers to work on six farms in Hawaii and two in the state of Washington between 2003 and 2007. The workers earned about $8.50 to $9.50 per hour harvesting crops ranging from pineapples to coffee beans, but many were forced to pay recruitment fees between $12,000 and $25,000, EEOC officials said. Some of the workers took out high-interest loans or mortgages on their land in Thailand, and they were told that accommodation and food would be provided free of charge, officials stated. "They were nickel-and-dimed to the point where they really weren't able to pay off everything," said Anna Park, regional attorney for the EEOC Los Angeles office. Additionally, workers had their passports taken away and were threatened with deportation if they complained, officials said. Some of them were forced to live in cramped conditions amid rats and insects, according to the EEOC. Workers of other nationalities on the same farms were not subjected to the same kind of treatment, Park could confirm. An attorney for Orian was not available for comment. Orian, an Israeli national, is currently under electronic monitoring as federal criminal proceedings against him advance. EEOC officials said that some of the Thai workers have returned to their home country, and that the total number of affected workers could reach up to 400. In the case of the 500 Indian workers, the EEOC filed a lawsuit on Wednesday in Mississippi, alleging that Gulf Coast Marine Services International LLC subjected the welders and pipefitters to segregated facilities and discriminatory treatment. The Indian men paid recruiters up to $20,000 to come to the United States, and upon arriving at Signal's shipyards at the end of 2006 and beginning of 2007, they were forced to pay rent for overcrowded apartments in squalid labor camps, according to the EEOC. In some cases, 24 men shared a trailer with only two toilets, the EEOC said. A representative from Signal did not return calls. In both cases, involving the Thai and Indian workers, the EEOC has filed lawsuits under the U.S. Civil Rights Act of 1964 and is seeking back pay, compensatory damages, and injunctive relief against future discrimination. Kay Buck, head of the Coalition to Abolish Slavery & Trafficking, was involved in handling the case of the Thai workers and equated their treatment to slavery. "Human trafficking is really the modern-day continuation of slavery," she said. (Editing by Greg McCune)
