How to solve the problem of Twitter feed filtering

by anonymous on 2013-10-08 18:20:22

Twitter's unfiltered information stream submerges users in an ocean of data. This will lead to fewer connections being established between users, hindering Twitter's development. Twitter should introduce button tools to help users conveniently unfollow content and recommend what they truly care about.

The report states that on Twitter, every tweet you post is shown to your followers. This makes Twitter very much like the world's real-time pulsating heartbeat, but this same feature can also impede Twitter's growth. If you follow too many people, you lose the opportunity to discover new content, becoming submerged in a sea of information.

Imagine you have just joined Twitter. You followed some friends, acquaintances, well-known media outlets, or celebrities of interest. The unfiltered feed begins to increase, with updates every minute informing you of what your acquaintances are doing. Although there aren't too many tweets in the feed, you still miss the messages you care most about.

Then you start following more people. You discover more acquaintances and colleagues on Twitter. You subscribe to the feeds of experts in certain fields or find interesting forwarded messages from friends, so you follow the authors. Sometimes, out of politeness, you reciprocally follow others. Gradually, your feed becomes chaotic. Some accounts send dozens of messages daily, completely drowning out others. Twitter is an information stream, not a queue, so you don't have to read every message. But even so, you find yourself missing many interesting updates and links from close friends. This is exactly where Twitter's business problem lies.

You can endure these messages but at the cost of a poor user experience. If you're capable, you could create a list of accounts to follow, but this requires a lot of work. For most people who want to streamline their follow lists, they simply don't know where to start.

Twitter continuously suggests that users follow more people, but it doesn't inform them which accounts they've never interacted with or shouldn't follow. Deleting accounts that follow you back involves multiple steps; removing 10 accounts is already difficult, let alone hundreds.

Following too many people leads to two consequences: users visit less frequently, and they stop following new people.

In the first scenario, users click on fewer ads and retweet fewer messages, resulting in a corresponding reduction in active user numbers.

In the second scenario, your followed friends and acquaintances lose their audience. No one wants to talk to a dead account, and within days, weeks, or months, these people will give up. Twitter has no shortage of zombie accounts.

A report claims that Twitter has over 1 billion registered users, but Twitter officially confirms only 218 million active users. Such a ratio suppresses Twitter's ability to generate profits.

Worse still, those who abandon Twitter or rarely log in may turn to Facebook. Facebook listeners can comment on posts. Users don't need to become "thought leaders" competing for follower counts. They accumulate friends through life anecdotes, making it not a competition to see who has the most fans.

Even so, Facebook's feed has filtering features, limiting the number of friends and pages users connect with and like. Facebook doesn't dump all unfiltered information on users; it only displays the best posts – articles receiving the most likes and comments.

Although Facebook is less real-time than Twitter, it ensures that the information fed to users is valuable. Filtering is automatically completed, and users are given direct control to decide the type of information and how often friend updates appear.

The issue of information filtering is not unique to Twitter. Instagram will eventually face this dilemma too, but it is still young, with its feed full of beautiful photos, making it less susceptible to spam. In short, managing one's own feed is a daunting task, and Twitter needs to innovate in information management methods to ensure sustainable growth.

Of course, unfiltered feeds are a cornerstone of the user experience. Setting aside filtering, we can address the feed problem through other methods.

Twitter hopes to encourage users to establish more connections within the "interest graph," which benefits its ad placements. However, it should make it easy for users to unfollow accounts.

Firstly, an unfollow button can be added. Or more assertively, Twitter can analyze people you've never interacted with and suggest unfriending them via a single button.

Twitter can also provide a feed cleanup button. It can score the accounts you follow based on your engagement level, making it easier to unfollow those you've never interacted with. Moreover, this tool must be mobile-friendly since most Twitter users access the service via mobile devices.

If Twitter does not address these issues, it will encounter growth challenges.
