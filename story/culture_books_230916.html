"China Sketches" is the latest collection of essays by Xiong Zhaozheng, winner of the Mao Dun Literature Prize.

by jqrxass on 2009-08-30 23:25:49

"China in Brief" includes nearly sixty prose and essay works by Xiong Zhaozheng, the author of the long historical novel "Zhang Juzheng" and winner of the Mao Dun Literature Prize. Xiong Zhaozheng focuses on writing thousand-word essays, striving to create a sense of grandeur and lasting beauty within short pieces. This kind of writing first requires substance, then flavor. To have substance, one must see many things; to have flavor, one must express emotions and reason thoroughly. Where to stop, where to leave unsaid, depends on learning and insight. Mr. Dongpo said "deep accumulation, restrained expression," which is the only way to write short articles.

China in Brief First Collection

Historically, Yangzhou, from the Sui Dynasty to the Qing Dynasty for over a thousand years, though frequently ravaged by war, did not lose its prosperous and splendid atmosphere. Generally, a city, like a person, has different fates, some good, some bad. Some are lifelong failures, drinking cool water and still getting stuck teeth; others achieve success at a young age and never suffer misfortune until old age. Yangzhou belongs to the category of "many difficulties for the noble." Compared with Hangzhou and Suzhou, it has suffered the most trampling. But after each trampling, it always manages to recover its vitality.

"The one who survives great calamity will surely be blessed later on," these eight words are also fitting for Yangzhou.

China in Brief Second Collection

In ancient times, the Yellow Crane Tower was a gathering place for literati and poets. In May, climbing the tower, looking at the plum blossoms falling on the snow waves of lotus flowers; in September, leaning on the railing, seeing the willows of the long pavilion, tying boats in the rice market and fishing streets. Those who wield the pen have their own insights about this; some write about worries and troubles; others lament the loss of their hometowns.

Today, during peak season, there are many visitors to the tower. However, few people here indulge in nostalgic thoughts. Of course, one doesn't need to chase after where the graceful yellow cranes of the Han dynasty flew or where the white clouds of the Tang dynasty drifted. Stories of chickens, dogs, mulberry trees, and hemp have long been lost in the clamor of disco music filling the tower. Mixed among the tourists, I have also stood several times in this lofty imitation ancient building, overlooking the vast Han River, gazing back, singing, and shouting.

China in Brief Third Collection

The Miluo River winds along gracefully and has flowed through Chinese poetry history for more than two thousand years.

Poets like us, considering it as waves of anger against society, are not few; those who take a sip from it are even more numerous. Of course, what they drink is not nectar, but rather the bitterness that has become turbid and clear again, clear and turbid again over the long spring and autumn seasons. This bitterness is stronger than autumn tea.

At this moment, I am by the banks of the Miluo River, picking up a handful of muddy yellow river water that disappoints me, holding flowers when the fragrance seeps into my clothes, and scooping water with both hands when the sky reflects in the water. What can this handful of ancient mud, older than worm-eaten thread-bound books, reflect?
