Nokia's Stubbornness and Coolness: A Bright Future After Weathering the Storm

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 16:00:35

Nokia CEO Stephen Elop

Nokia's CEO, Stephen Elop (Stepehen Elop), may not understand Chinese, but he surely believes in the saying "perseverance will be rewarded." However, from an outsider's perspective, his and Nokia's chosen path appears somewhat unreasonably stubborn.

Since resigning from his position as head of Microsoft Office to become Nokia's first foreign CEO, Elop has led this Finnish mobile giant for nearly three years. The most important strategic decision he made at Nokia was joining the Microsoft Windows Phone camp while firmly shutting the door on Android, the market’s largest operating system. Unlike Samsung and HTC, he didn’t adopt a dual strategy ("putting both feet in two boats").

This might be the biggest controversy in the smartphone industry. As Nokia undergoes a painful transformation with declining financial status and stock prices, every piece of related news, whether positive or negative, prompts the same question: What would have happened if Nokia had joined the Android camp initially? Due to his prior work background at Microsoft, Elop has never escaped media scrutiny.

In a sense, this Canadian bears the heaviest pressure within the industry. However, since Nokia allied with Microsoft over two years ago, Elop has never changed his stance: Android is not suitable for Nokia; it wouldn't make Nokia better. Nokia needs to maintain its uniqueness and trust in the future prospects of WP phones. Each time he was asked about Android, Elop's attitude remained consistently resolute.

Nevertheless, despite continuous losses, massive layoffs, and even selling the headquarters building, Nokia's board has steadfastly stood by Elop, adhering to the principle of "trust those you employ, don’t use those you distrust," just as they did three years ago when they chose this former Microsoft executive. In reality, as an "outsider," Elop would never have the final say in decisions like allying with Microsoft or resisting Google—decisions that determine Nokia's future fate must ultimately be decided by Nokia's board. Therefore, they continue to support Elop in pursuing Nokia's own path.

Hence, the question "What would happen if Nokia made Android phones?" has lost its practical significance. As long as Nokia isn’t cornered and there are no major adjustments to the board, they are unlikely to launch Android products. No matter how outstanding Nokia's product design and technical functions are, they won't appear on the Android operating system.

Among the three major manufacturers in the Microsoft WP system, only Nokia is exclusively focused on the WP platform, while Samsung and HTC primarily focus on Android. On one hand, Nokia bears the risk of "putting all eggs in one basket," while on the other hand, it gains full support from Microsoft. Microsoft provides Nokia with $1 billion annually for marketing expenses, and Lumia advertisements now cover almost all advertising platforms in the U.S., a market originally unfamiliar to Nokia.

How important is Nokia to Microsoft? Data from AdDuplex, a WP platform advertising research company, shows that Nokia accounts for as high as 85% of total WP shipments. Since their collaboration with Microsoft two years ago, Nokia has shipped 28 million WP phones, driving WP's market share in the U.S. to 5.6%, and in some European countries, sales even surpass those of the iPhone. It's not an exaggeration to say that Nokia single-handedly supports Microsoft's smartphone strategy.

By stubbornly rejecting the Android platform, Nokia gave up a large volume of potential sales, making its transformation process more arduous. According to the latest published second-quarter results, Nokia's transformation journey continues. With the rapid popularization of smartphones, the traditional feature phone market is rapidly shrinking, causing Nokia's cash cow business to continuously shrink, leading to a constant decline in revenue. In terms of smartphones, while Lumia series sales continue to grow, they temporarily cannot offset the bleeding rate of Nokia's traditional business, which is the main reason for Nokia's substantial losses over several quarters.

However, reviewing Nokia's past performance reveals that although the giant is still undergoing a painful transformation, it has passed through the most difficult period and is now climbing out of the trough. Although Nokia is still incurring losses, these losses have sharply narrowed, and it even achieved profitability in the fourth quarter last year. The loss in the second quarter was mainly due to a $290 million inventory write-down. If Nokia can clear its inventory, this write-down amount could be reduced.

The key point in Nokia's financial report lies in the Lumia business. Over the past few quarters, Nokia's core business, Lumia, has seen shipments of 2.9 million, 4.4 million, 5.6 million, and 7.4 million units, showing a clear upward trend. Following the current growth trend, Lumia sales are expected to reach close to 10 million units in the fourth quarter. Considering the significant impact last year when Microsoft abandoned WP7 users, affecting Nokia's inventory, this growth is particularly valuable. Additionally, the average selling price per smartphone increased from $198 last year to $206.

Quarterly sales reaching the ten-million level indicate that Nokia's Lumia business has basically stabilized. Currently, only five manufacturers (Q1 data) have broken through the ten-million unit mark in quarterly smartphone sales: Samsung, Apple, LG, Huawei, and ZTE. Even Sony and HTC, with the best hardware design and manufacturing processes in the Android camp, haven't entered this top-tier club. The bulk of China's two major manufacturers' shipments are concentrated in the Chinese market, and mostly consist of mid-to-low-priced products.

Elop emphasized during an exclusive interview with Sina Tech that the Android camp is dominated by Samsung, and Nokia had already missed the opportunity. Even if it had entered the Android market, it would have been difficult to develop well. He pointed out that there are also some very outstanding hardware products in the Android camp, yet they haven't resulted in strong sales. Reasonable speculation suggests he was referring to Sony and HTC.

According to Elop, if Nokia launched Android phones, even though the hardware design would be top-notch, its situation wouldn't be much stronger than Sony and HTC, unable to compete with Samsung, which has the entire supply chain advantage. If Nokia could achieve ten-million unit quarterly sales in the WP camp, then Elop indirectly proves: without Android, Nokia, though sacrificing considerable sales, can still survive and do better than in the Android camp.

Considering Nokia's recent product deployments, the rapid growth momentum of Lumia will be further confirmed. Nokia's current Lumia products include models such as 520, 620, 720, 820, 920, 925, and the newly released 1020, covering virtually all price ranges from RMB 800 to 5000. Although the smartphone market is still growing, signs of saturation are appearing in the high-end market, while emerging markets like China, India, and Brazil will become the strongest regions for smartphone growth.

The Asha platform and Lumia 520 model are Nokia's key strategies for the low-end market. Given Nokia's recently launched Asha platform products, Elop's emphasis on the low-end market is evident. According to Deloitte estimates, global smartphone users exceeded 1.2 billion last year, reaching 2 billion this year, and are projected to hit 4.5 billion by 2018. Most of these new users come from emerging markets, where mid-to-low-end smartphones are the primary models. In these markets, Nokia retains brand and channel advantages from its feature phone era, making this a battleground for low-cost competition.

Data shows that the lowest-end 520 is the best-selling phone in the WP camp, accounting for 12% of WP sales. In the sub-RMB 1000 price range, the 520 directly competes with numerous ultra-low-end Android counterfeit phones in China. Elop told Sina Tech, "In the low-end market, Nokia is not afraid of the impact of Chinese counterfeit Android phones. We also have excellent cost control measures, strong brand value, and unique innovations. These Chinese counterfeit Android phones are like the low-end feature phones of the past; once Nokia enters, Chinese counterfeit manufacturers won't be competitors."

While focusing heavily on the low-end market, Nokia hasn't relaxed its efforts in the high-end market either. The new flagship Lumia 1020, unveiled last week in New York, reached the pinnacle of smartphone camera capabilities, even achieving mainstream compact camera imaging standards. Starting from the Lumia 920, Nokia has consistently led the smartphone industry in camera functionality, and the 41-megapixel Lumia 1020 further expanded this lead.

Of course, by choosing the WP platform, Nokia must face WP's greatest flaw: the lack of applications and quality gaps. Compared to over 600,000 apps available on the Apple and Google platforms, the WP platform currently has only 165,000 apps. Quantity isn't the decisive factor; although there are hundreds of thousands of app choices, users typically use only a few thousand apps. As long as Microsoft and Nokia attract these commonly used apps to the WP platform, user needs can be largely met.

The real issue lies in app quality. Due to the enormous difference in user base size, developers prioritize Apple and Google platforms when developing apps, and clearly invest less effort in the WP platform. For the same app, the WP version is usually the last to be launched, and its functional completeness lags behind the Google and Apple versions. As the WP platform's functionality gradually improves, the number and quality of apps will become critical factors determining the future development of WP and Nokia.

A year ago, BlackBerry's sales were nearly twice that of Microsoft's WP, but today WP phone sales have surpassed BlackBerry, establishing its position as the third-largest platform. This growth is mainly driven by Nokia. Since its fate is almost entirely tied to Microsoft, Nokia even actively attracts developers to create more apps for Lumia. Nokia launched an SDK development tool for the Lumia 1020, attracting popular apps like Foursquare, Flipboard, Yelp, and Path to its platform.

By abandoning the Android platform, Nokia undoubtedly lost a large volume of sales, but on the other hand, it made its products more distinctive, avoiding the cutthroat competition of Android's vast array of devices, becoming the sole choice for the third-largest platform, Microsoft WP. In return, Nokia received strong support from Microsoft, something impossible to achieve on the Android platform. The true balance of this strategy's gains and losses might require another one to two years for a more reasonable judgment.

Although Nokia's transformation is not yet complete, and revenue declines caused by the shrinking feature phone market continue, the two core businesses, Lumia and NSN, show obvious growth trends. Nokia has passed through its lowest point and is now in a phase of steady recovery. If we exclude the inevitable exit of feature phones from the market, Nokia's growth speed in the smartphone sector is unstoppable. Perhaps in another two to three quarters, the growth brought by Lumia and NSN will offset the losses from the feature phone business, at which point Nokia will truly stop long-term losses and move onto a path of sustained profitability.

NSN, which once dragged down Nokia's performance, is now a wholly-owned subsidiary of Nokia, contributing nearly half of its revenue and achieving profitability. Meanwhile, Lumia maintains a robust growth momentum. Although Nokia cannot return to its former position as the mobile phone kingpin and lacks the scale and strength of Samsung and Apple, based on the current momentum, Nokia is sufficient to be an excellent niche manufacturer. Further, if Lumia achieves quarterly sales of ten million units, Nokia will re-enter the top tier in terms of performance and become a front-line manufacturer again with WP phones.

At the end of my exclusive interview with Elop last week, I decided to ask about Android one more time: "If Lumia sales don't meet expectations, does Nokia really not consider Android at all, and have no Plan B?" Elop smiled and said, "From the feedback we've received, consumers prefer Lumia over Samsung and Apple. Nokia needs to maintain its uniqueness and offer users different choices." He took out the Lumia 1020 and said, "Hold on, let me take a picture of you." "See, Lumia 1020's photos can clearly display the words on your watch when enlarged."
