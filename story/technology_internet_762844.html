Economist: Chinese returnees face some difficulties in finding jobs

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 17:37:54

The Economist reported that the importance of "sea turtles" in China has declined for many reasons. Sometimes companies hire returnees, but find that after studying abroad, they are not very familiar with the rapidly developing industry situation in China. They adhere to Western concepts and cannot manage local employees. The quality of returned talents also varies greatly, and many real talents stay overseas.

Following The Economist's article, let's look at the current status of returnees in China:

Li Sanqi recalled: "I left China in 1980 with only $3 in my pocket." After the Cultural Revolution, he was among the first group of students allowed to study abroad. Like most successful people, his performance was outstanding. He advanced to a coveted position at the University of Texas and founded several technology companies. Because he had the opportunity to help China establish world-class multinational enterprises, he returned to China. Now, he is a senior executive at Huawei Technologies Co., Ltd., a telecommunications giant (data shows that he serves as the Chief Technology Officer of Huawei's carrier network business).

Li Sanqi is an outstanding representative of the "sea turtles". The term "sea turtle" refers to "returned overseas students". For a long time, all sectors of China have praised the contribution of overseas returnees in introducing advanced technology. In the past, these people were invincible in the job market and enjoyed generous compensation. Unfortunately, those good days are gone. Returnees are no longer highly regarded, the salary gap is gradually narrowing, and some can't even find jobs. Some jokingly call them "seaweed" now. Looking back at the contributions of the "sea turtles", this is a surprising change.

This year marks the 100th anniversary of the establishment of the Chinese Association of Returned Overseas Scholars from Europe and America. Wang Huiyao, a member of the association, believes there have been five waves of returnees. The first occurred in the 19th century, including the builder of China's first railway and the first university president. The second and third waves both occurred before 1949, and many were political leaders. In the 1950s, many went to the Soviet Union for further studies, which was the fourth wave.

The fifth wave began in 1978 and is currently the largest returnee trend. Since then, about 2.6 million Chinese have studied abroad. In recent years, the number of Chinese students studying abroad has expanded to 400,000 per year. Most stay overseas, but the 1.1 million who returned to China have already promoted changes in the country. Wang Huiyao believes that the first three waves prompted revolutionary changes in China, the fourth wave promoted modernization, and the fifth wave promoted globalization.

Returnees helped China's economy connect with the world. They founded heavyweight technology companies like Baidu, and many became senior executives of multinational corporations' Chinese branches. They helped China link with global commerce, politics, and popular culture.

Then, why has their importance decreased? Several studies show that returnees on average take longer to be promoted to relatively low positions, and the wage gap with local employees has also narrowed. One reason is the weak job market for new graduates. Another factor is that with the rise of China's domestic market and the continuous development of industries such as e-commerce, these developments make overseas students who have been abroad for a few years unfamiliar. Gary Rieschel of the venture capital firm Qiming Venture Partners said that ten years ago, investors only invested in people returning from Silicon Valley in the United States. Now, investors support entrepreneurs who graduated from local universities, who are more familiar with local consumption patterns, online gaming habits, and social media platforms like Weibo.

As China's economy prospers and grows rapidly, local managers begin to shed their inferiority complex. A senior official at Tencent, a Chinese social media company, said he still recruits returnees from foreign companies, but finds it difficult for them to manage local engineers. A European investment banker pointed out that returnees often insist on Western concepts such as transparency, elite management, and ethics, which puts them at a disadvantage in China's super Darwinian economy. In China, people care more about what the boss or client wants and do everything possible to achieve the goal.

Even foreign companies in China are becoming more selective when hiring. Yannig Gourmelon of the German management consulting firm Roland Berger said that the profit margins of multinational companies have been significantly compressed, leading to reduced salaries for "gilded sea turtles."

There is another explanation: many newly returned sea turtles have low qualifications. In the past, only top students were allowed to go abroad to pursue degrees, and competition for government scholarships was fierce. However, with rising incomes, many students of medium caliber have the ability to pay for their education abroad, attending universities of questionable quality where graduating doesn't help much with employment. Moreover, with the economic downturn in Western countries, many people return home without any work experience.

There are fewer capable sea turtles, and the top Chinese students still remain overseas. Research funded by the U.S. National Science Foundation (NSF) found that five years after graduation, 92% of Chinese students with Ph.D.s from the U.S. remained in the U.S., compared to 81% of Indian students, 41% of South Korean students, and 32% of Mexican students.

To attract these superstars back, China offers generous subsidies and allowances. But do these measures work? The results seem unpredictable. Despite these policies, returning entrepreneurs still face many problems: rising labor and land costs, rampant intellectual property infringement, serious corruption, and very few top scientists return. In a paper by David Zweig, a scholar at the Hong Kong University of Science and Technology, he wrote: "If China wants to attract top talent back, it must fundamentally reform academic and scientific institutions to break the power of administrative agencies in recruitment and funding."

The troubling fact is the inner conflict of overseas Chinese towards their homeland. Li Sanqi, who works at Huawei, appears to be a typical sea turtle, but his wife and children still live in the United States. In addition to increasing subsidies, China needs to strengthen the rule of law, eradicate corrupt officials, purify the air, clean water resources, and ensure food safety. Once these are achieved, the sea turtles will certainly notice.
