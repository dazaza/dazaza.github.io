Chang Cheh's Three Versions of "One-Armed Swordsman"

The idea for "One-Armed Swordsman", perhaps plagiarized or inspired by Louis Cha's (Jin Yong) "The Return of the Condor Heroes". In "The Return of the Condor Heroes", Yang Guo also has his arm cut off by his capricious younger sister. It could be said that "One-Armed Swordsman" is a tribute to "The Return of the Condor Heroes".

This is normal. For Ni Kuang, who has written over 400 scripts and numerous science fiction novels, there aren't so many original stories. In 1967, upon its release, "One-Armed Swordsman" was groundbreaking. Chang Cheh then continued with two more sequels, namely "King of the One-Armed Swordsman" (1969) and "New One-Armed Swordsman" (1971).

Movies related to "One-Armed Swordsman", aside from the three directed by Chang Cheh, include Stanley Tong's "94 The One-Armed Swordsman" (1994) and Tsui Hark's "Broken Sword" (1999). However, apart from paying homage to their predecessors, these films have little relation to Chang Cheh's style.

The hero losing his arm exudes a sense of tragic grandeur. The broken sword is a symbol, representing perseverance and self-reliance in adversity, starting anew and rebirth. Therefore, in this sense, "One-Armed Swordsman" can even be seen as an uplifting and inspiring motivational film. The one-armed hero uses the broken sword to create a unique one-armed sword technique. The unity of man and sword symbolizes not only the hero's rebirth but also a realm.

In wuxia films, weapons are symbols of character traits and the physical manifestation of a person's spirit. "One-Armed Swordsman" embodies Chang Cheh's "castration" complex, or what might be called "aesthetic of imperfection." Severed arms, disembowelment, gouging out eyes, dismemberment - he even made a movie called "Residual" that gathers all forms of imperfection.

Chang Cheh's three "One-Armed Swordsman" movies became progressively more violent. By the third installment, "New One-Armed Swordsman," David Chiang's character Lei Li even mutilates his own right arm. The climactic fight on the bridge sees Lei Li fighting from the beginning to the end of the bridge without mercy, reminiscent of scenes from action films directed by John Woo, Chang Cheh's disciple, except that in Woo's films, Chow Yun-fat holds twin guns instead.

Perhaps another source of inspiration for Chang Cheh were Japanese samurai films shown in Hong Kong during that period, such as the "Zatoichi" series starring Shintaro Katsu, which also featured themes of imperfection and violence.

Hong Kong's one-armed heroes and Japan's blind swordsman echo each other, shining brilliantly together. In 1971, after leaving Shaw Brothers and Chang Cheh, Wang Yu teamed up with Shintaro Katsu to star in the Affiliated Film Company production "The One-Armed Swordsman vs Zatoichi" (directed by Hsu Chi-Hung).

Chang Cheh's films were even more violent than Japanese ones. However, behind Chang Cheh's violence lay a romanticism of passionate young men. This romanticism wasn't just about external form (graceful and beautiful visual style), but more importantly, it was about inner spirit and sentiment.

Chang Cheh was undoubtedly the author of Hong Kong genre films. Through wuxia films, he expressed his views on the world and conveyed personal sentiments. Fang Gang, the melancholy and lonely hero in "One-Armed Swordsman," mirrored the frustration Chang Cheh felt at the time when several early films at Shaw Brothers didn't perform well ("Butterfly Cup" and "Tiger Knight" both had average results).

"The King of the One-Armed Swordsman" is the sequel to "One-Armed Swordsman." Fang Gang defeats the villain trying to seize the position of leader of the martial arts world in order to repay his teacher's kindness and is acclaimed as the "Sword King." However, Fang Gang abandons this title and retreats to the countryside with his wife, Xiao Man. Chang Cheh uses this to express his stance against violence.

"New One-Armed Swordsman" goes against traditional wuxia conventions, portraying Long Yi, the older generation of martial artists, as a cunning and hypocritical villain, while the younger generation are thousand-gold-promise, sky-high righteousness heroes. This character model resonates with the rebellious tide of the youth of that era who despised tradition and authority.

Long Yi's character somewhat resembles Yue Buqun from "The Smiling, Proud Wanderer." It is unknown whether this "pseudo-hero" image influenced Ni Kuang and Chang Cheh? Although the span of time between these three films is only four years, Chang Cheh's style underwent significant changes.

Firstly, "One-Armed Swordsman" is of great significance, being the pioneering work. Within four years, Chang Cheh rose from a newcomer in the film industry to become a sought-after director at Shaw Brothers. His films also underwent many changes. For example, "New One-Armed Swordsman" features dual male protagonists, with the official debut of Ti Lung and David Chiang, which was refreshingly new and became the best duo in Chang Cheh's films.

After Wang Yu left Chang Cheh, the somber and lonely atmosphere of the heroes in Chang Cheh's wuxia films (Nie Zheng in "The Great Assassin," Yin Peng in "Golden Swallow," and Fang Gang in "One-Armed Swordsman") disappeared. The lone wanderer was replaced by a pair of heroes. Not only did the character relationships change, but also the bright and optimistic emotional tone. For example, in "New One-Armed Swordsman," when Ti Lung appears, the whole tone shifts from somber to passionate. Thus, Chang Cheh began to passionately sing praises of brotherhood among men.

In the three "One-Armed Swordsman" films, women often serve as places of solace for the heroes' hearts, providing them with a bed for rebirth. For instance, in "One-Armed Swordsman," Xiao Man provides Fang Gang with the "Broken Sword Manual," and in "New One-Armed Swordsman," Bajiao secretly brings Lei Li a treasured sword from home.

Notably, in "New One-Armed Swordsman," the brotherly bond among men replaces romantic love, with an increasingly ascetic flavor.
