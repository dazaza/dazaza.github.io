A talent cultivation model based on computer network technology and communication skills - published in the core journal of Computer Science

Abstract: In this article, based on the computer network environment and the traditional classroom teaching and learning environment, two communication skills training models are compared to illustrate the effectiveness and superiority of the language communication skills training model under the computer network environment in overcoming emotional barriers, improving language fluency, and increasing communication success rate.

Keywords: Computer network environment, traditional classroom environment, communication skills, emotional psychological barriers

The crux of the issue lies in the fact that in the traditional face-to-face classroom environment, language communication is a complex, non-instinctive psychological activity. Learners may have limited communication expression abilities due to tension, depression, or lack of security in the atmosphere. This could be because of a lack of confidence or excessive concern for surrounding evaluations, which can easily lead to negative emotional factors such as anxiety, panic, fear, and shyness, thereby hindering normal language communication. To obtain real data from a psychological perspective, we conducted a psychological survey through questionnaires among non-English major students in 2008 and 2009.

Random sampling was performed on 213 students for analysis and evaluation. Table 1 shows the results of the random sample. Based on the theory of language development using computer network technology, experimental class teaching communication skills were developed.

3.1 Identification and basic conditions of the experiment: To ensure universality, different professional categories were selected from 2008 and 2009 as pilot studies for the teaching practice and research of communication skill cultivation using computer network technology, comparing suitable professional categories with traditional teaching models. A total of the pilot classes and comparison classes of students in 2008 and 2009 were analyzed regarding their English language entrance requirements, learning psychology, and personality characteristics shown in the table. Statistical data indicate that there is no significant difference between the students in the experimental class and the control class, meaning they are comparable at the start of teaching. Steps and methods implemented show that many studies indicate an individual's ability to predict success is not primarily determined by IQ but rather emotional intelligence. Therefore, during the first stage of our experimental psychological preparation phase, we introduced communication skills teaching within a low-rationality emotional barrier network environment. Initially, each student's psychological profile, involving family background, learning resume, interests, preferences, psychological traits, and personality types, was comprehensively understood. The final stage involved encouraging internalization and establishing positive, healthy emotions among students, marking the implementation stage of experimental teaching.
