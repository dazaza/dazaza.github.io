The relationship between American Jews and Israeli Jews is strong yet complex. Before the Holocaust, American Jews had a complicated reaction to Zionism and the return of Jews to their ancient homeland. As new immigrants to America, American Jews were more concerned with culturally integrating into American society and avoiding anti-Semitism. These factors influenced how American Jews responded to World War II and the Holocaust. During the war, many American Jews, fearing anti-Semitic sentiment in America, did not actively lobby the U.S. government to save their relatives in Europe until after the war ended. When the war ended and the extent of the Holocaust became widely known, American Jews were shocked by the consequences of their political inaction and subsequently became an overwhelming force in supporting the establishment of the State of Israel.

However, it wasn't until the 1967 Arab-Israeli War, out of concern for Israel's possible destruction by its enemies, that many American Jews openly declared their support for Israel. During that war, American Jews held large-scale political demonstrations in support of Israel, showing solidarity across the United States. Since then, strong support for Israel’s existence has become one of the main characteristics of the American Jewish community.

In recent years, new issues and trends among American Jews have sparked discussions and divisions regarding the nature of the relationship between American Jews and Israeli Jews.

One of the most pressing challenges facing the American Jewish community today is balancing support for Israel's existence while criticizing policies of the Israeli government they disagree with. This divide stems from American Jews' concerns that criticism of Israeli policy might bolster arguments of those who wish to destroy the Jewish state of Israel. This issue remains central and dynamic, closely tied to ongoing political instability in the Middle East.

More importantly, differing demographic trends in the United States and Israel are altering the long-term relationship between American and Israeli Jews. Many American Jews are disturbed and shocked by the fact that the American Jewish population (approximately 5.2 million) is rapidly shrinking due to low birth rates and intermarriage with non-Jews. As a result, many American Jews' core Jewish identity and their connection to Israeli Jews are gradually weakening.

In contrast, the Jewish population in Israel (approximately 5.3 million) is gradually increasing due to higher birth rates and immigration. Consequently, the average age of Israeli Jews is lower than that of American Jews. An even more surprising fact is that Israeli culture, centered on Hebrew and rooted in the ancient Jewish homeland, appears more vibrant and self-sustaining than American Jewish culture.

Therefore, it seems that the traditional role of American Jews as financial and moral supporters of the State of Israel is gradually reversing. It is becoming increasingly clear that Israel is quickly becoming the wealthiest and most vibrant center of Jewish civilization. In the future, American Jews may find themselves needing to rely on Israel for cultural, religious, or economic support.

Regardless of the outcome, the fascinating relationship between American Jews and Israeli Jews will continue to be the central political and cultural axis of the Jewish world.
