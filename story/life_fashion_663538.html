The cultural habits of using perfume in different countries are different from those in their own country.

by xiaomyan2004 on 2012-02-11 22:51:55

Keywords: French perfume, perfume culture, habits, new perfume

The production sites of perfumes are spread all over the world, and people's habits of using perfumes in different places are also not exactly the same.

In 1533, Catherine, the Pope's niece, married King Henry II of France, bringing the habit of using perfume to France (by the way, it is said that this marriage brought not only the habit of using perfume but also the way of making ice cream and the habit of using forks while eating). Still, those two greatest people in French history, Louis XIV, the Sun King, and Napoleon, promoted the vigorous development of France's perfume industry. However, until the early and mid-20th century, using perfume was still a characteristic of refined living. Customers would choose their favorite empty perfume bottles in perfume shops to hold the perfume they wanted to buy. At that time, the entire fashion industry only served a minority of nobles and wealthy families.

Starting from the 1950s and 60s, the middle class in Europe and America gradually grew larger, and with the spread of globalization starting in the 1980s, the once snobbish fashion industry became increasingly larger, bearing more and more commercial marks. In the 1990s, on one hand, large luxury groups and cosmetics companies gradually formed, with the biggest few being in France, such as LVMH, Vendome (Richemont), Pinault-Printemps-Redoute, L'Oreal France, etc.; on the other hand, fashion designers launched second-line brands to capture the market of young people and customers with relatively lower income, extending their brands beyond clothing: perfume, cosmetics, eyewear, home goods, etc., were several key areas, and representatives of brand extension included designers like Ralph Lauren, Calvin Klein (CK), Pierre Cardin, YSL, etc.

During that period, perfume was no longer a specialty for a few people, but became an increasingly mass-market refinement. Industrialization reduced the production cost of perfumes, commercialization expanded the market influence of perfumes, and globalization spread a brand's perfume to all over the world. But at the same time, the increasing number of perfumes led to both the smell and the perfume bottles lacking individuality. Nowadays, perfume has become an essential item in women's lives.

France: France is truly the gathering place of all kinds of perfumes! The perfume culture of the French is also long-standing. But the root of their habit of using perfume is that they are lazy about taking baths, yet they want to look presentable, so they use perfume to cover up odors. Their perfumes, when smelled alone, always seem a bit pungent, but if you mix them on unwashed skin, then smell again, you'll surely find the scent thick and smooth, the initial scent like a fairy, the middle scent like a witch, and the final scent like a young girl. Smelling it throughout the day will surely make you lose yourself.

United Kingdom: There's nothing much to do there except for the rain, so most locals have a musty smell. You don't know how they endure the "plum rains". The perfumes they produce, if you go ahead and smell them, always have a faint camphor ball feeling.

Germany: Their perfumes follow time rules strictly. Once the time is up, no matter how good the perfume is, the smell disappears immediately, and no matter what you do, it won't work. Moreover, their perfumes give off a particularly serious and formal feeling. Going on a date with someone who wears German perfume is like meeting your superior leader.

Canada: The climate there is extremely cold, the population is sparse, and the environment is harsh. Therefore, their country stipulates that the perfume produced by manufacturers must have positioning functions. This way, if you get lost in the vast icy wasteland, just apply some local perfume, and rescue dogs will quickly come to save you.

Peru: Relying on their Mayan and Aztec cultures, the perfumes they produce always have a tomb smell, no matter how you smell them. However, it's still good. It's said that since the movie "Tomb Raider" became popular worldwide, this kind of perfume is deeply loved by the new generation.

Japan: Their daily dishes and meals have a light taste, so they dare not make their perfumes too concentrated, lest it affects appetite. Moreover, Japanese perfumes mostly have a marine smell, and people wearing this perfume might be mistaken for fishermen or fish vendors.

India: The smell of perfume is similar to the incense burned there. If you don't know what incense smells like, you can go to some high-end restrooms. Wearing this kind of perfume, walking seven steps in each direction, feels like being in a deserted place, which could be described as: above the sky and below the earth, I am the only ruler.
