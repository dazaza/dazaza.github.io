Physiological Characteristics and Functions

Hair can be divided from bottom to top into four parts: the hair papilla, hair follicle, hair root, and hair shaft. The physiological characteristics and functions of hair mainly depend on the hair papilla, hair follicle, and sebaceous glands below the scalp epidermis.

The hair follicle is the part of the hair root located within the dermis, composed of the inner hair root sheath, outer hair root sheath, and hair bulb. The inner hair root sheath is the sheath layer adjacent to the hair during the late anagen phase. The inner hair root sheath is a rigid, thick-walled keratinized tube that determines the cross-sectional shape of the growing hair. The lower part of the inner hair sheath consists of three layers: HUXLEY sheath, HENLE sheath, and the outer layer of the inner hair root sheath. Before hair keratinization, the inner hair root sheath grows together with the hair, both originating from proliferating cells at the base of the hair follicle. Near the epidermis, the inner hair root sheath separates from the epidermis and hair follicle.

The hair papilla is the lowest part of the hair follicle, connected to capillaries and nerve endings. At the bottom of the hair follicle, epidermal cells continuously divide and differentiate. These epidermal cells follow different differentiation pathways, forming different components of the hair (such as the cortex, cuticle, and medulla), with the outermost cells forming the inner hair root sheath. At this stage, the cells are soft and non-keratinized.

The function of the sebaceous gland is to secrete sebum, which is squeezed out through the sebaceous duct when the hair passes through it, carrying away the sebum. Sebum provides natural protection for the hair, giving it luster and waterproof properties.

The arrector pili muscle is a small muscular organ connected to the epidermis, its function depending on external physiological conditions. The arrector pili muscle can stretch or contract. A drop in temperature or the action of adrenaline can pull the hair follicle to a higher position, making the hair stand up.

Overview

The nature of hair can be divided into physical and chemical properties.

The hair cortex is the main component of hair, consisting of elongated cells parallel to the hair axis. These cells contain 10？ millimeter tension filaments and inter-filament matrix, which determine the main physicochemical properties of the hair. Filaments are composed of fibrous proteins, with 50% of the proteins exhibiting a helical structure, and the matrix composed of non-helical proteins rich in cystine. These proteins are synthesized at the lower end of the hair follicle, and the final stage involves the conversion of cysteine to cystine.

The physical properties of hair are related to its chemical composition. When immersed in water, hair quickly swells, increasing its weight by about 40% compared to its dry weight before immersion. This swelling phenomenon indicates that hair is almost purely protein, with very little lipid content. Hair has strong resilience due to the alignment of filaments parallel to the hair axis.

Physical Properties

Hair roots are thicker and become finer towards the tips, so the diameter differs, classifying them as normal, coarse, or fine hair.

1. Shape of Hair

It can be categorized into straight, wavy curly, and naturally curly hair. Straight hair has a circular cross-section, wavy curly hair an elliptical cross-section, and naturally curly hair a flat cross-section. The thickness of hair is unrelated to whether it is straight or curly.

2. Water Absorption of Hair

Normal hair typically contains about 10% water.

3. Elasticity and Tension of Hair

Elasticity refers to the ability of hair to stretch to its maximum length and still return to its original state. A single hair can stretch 40-60%, determined by the cortex layer. Tension refers to the force required to stretch the hair to its limit without breaking. A healthy strand of hair can support approximately 100-150 grams of weight.

4. Porosity of Hair

Porosity refers to the hair's ability to absorb moisture, which is relevant to processes like dyeing and perming.

5. Heat Resistance of Hair

Hair's thermal properties are closely related to its nature. Heating to around 100 degrees Celsius causes significant changes, eventually leading to carbonization and dissolution.

Structure

Hair is composed of keratinized epithelial cells. The portion outside the skin is called the hair shaft, the portion inside the skin is called the hair root, the expanded terminal part of the hair root is called the hair bulb, and the concave lower end of the hair bulb is called the hair papilla, containing connective tissue, nerve endings, and capillaries that supply nutrients to the hair bulb. The area near the papilla at the bottom of the hair bulb is called the hair matrix, which is the growth zone for hair and hair follicles, equivalent to the spinous and basal layers of the epidermis, and contains melanocytes. 1. Medulla located in the center of the hair, consisting of 2-3 layers of shrunk cuboidal keratinized cells. Terminal hair and vellus hair lack medulla;

2. Cortex forms the main body of the hair, composed of several layers of spindle-shaped keratinized cells, containing a large number of pigment granules;

Normal Hair Physiology

The normal human body has about 90,000-140,000 hairs. The hair growth cycle is divided into the anagen phase (about 3 years), catagen phase (about 3 weeks), and telogen phase (about 3 months). About 80-90% of mature hair is in the anagen phase, growing at a rate of about 0.27-0.4mm per day for approximately 1000 days, reaching 50-60cm in length. About 10-20% of hair is in the resting phase, lasting about 3 months. This cycle is dynamically balanced. That is, 10 hairs in the anagen phase are always adjacent to 1 hair in the telogen phase. Normally, a person can lose 70-100 hairs per day, with an equal number regenerating. The length of hair in different areas is related to the duration of the growth cycle. Eyebrows and eyelashes have a growth cycle of only 2 months, hence their short length. Hair growth is influenced by genetic, health, nutritional, and hormonal factors.

Changing Evening Hair Washing Habits

Modern life is fast-paced, with busy work, activities, and social engagements leaving little time during the day to wash your hair comfortably in the evening.

If you have enough time to let your hair dry completely before sleeping after washing it in the evening, that’s ideal. But sometimes, it gets too busy, and you don’t wash your hair until after 10 PM. After washing, you might feel too tired to keep your eyes open, leading to going to sleep with wet hair.

Older generations would say this could cause headaches, and modern scientific research suggests there’s some truth to this.

We must understand that during metabolism, the human body constantly generates heat to maintain body temperature. This heat is also conducted to the body surface through blood circulation, radiating, conducting, convecting, and evaporating to dissipate heat to the environment.

For individuals with strong resistance, the body’s temperature regulation mechanism works normally, balancing heat production and dissipation. When the body temperature tends to drop, heat production increases, and heat dissipation decreases, maintaining the body temperature around 37°C.

However, for those with weaker resistance, their ability to regulate body temperature and maintain normal body temperature is poorer. Thus, when the whole body or a part of it gets chilled, they are more prone to catching a cold.

After washing your hair with hot water, due to the warmth, the capillaries in the scalp expand, increasing the radiation of heat. Also, since the hair is wet after washing, a large amount of water evaporates, taking away a lot of heat. Generally, 1 gram of water evaporation removes 500 calories of heat.

Due to increased heat dissipation, the body becomes chilled, causing reflexively the contraction of capillaries in the upper respiratory tract, reducing local blood flow, and lowering the resistance of the upper respiratory tract, allowing viruses or bacteria already present locally to multiply and cause upper respiratory infections, resulting in symptoms of a cold such as runny nose, nasal congestion, headache, or even fever.

If you fall asleep with damp hair, the regulation function of the body's temperature regulation center is low, making it even easier to catch a cold.

Therefore, generally speaking, it's best not to wash your hair right before bedtime. If you must, dry it thoroughly with a towel first, then blow-dry it before sleeping. This way, you won't catch a cold.

Sleeping with wet hair makes you more susceptible to catching a cold.
