Can endometritis cause amenorrhea?

by jlzx9123 on 2012-03-06 16:05:54

Can endometritis cause amenorrhea? For many female friends, endometritis might be a relatively unfamiliar condition. Therefore, after being diagnosed with this illness, many women may not worry about the possibility of experiencing amenorrhea. In fact, when endometritis worsens to a certain extent, it can transform into myometritis. At this point, whether endometritis can lead to amenorrhea becomes a frequently asked question.

**Can endometritis cause amenorrhea?** Taiyuan Zhongshan Reproductive Hospital advises that during acute endometritis, sexual activity should be strictly avoided as it could lead to further spread of the infection. Due to increased vaginal discharge, abdominal pain, lower back soreness, and pelvic heaviness, females may experience a decreased interest in sexual activity. Even if the inflammation is under control, resuming sexual activity too frequently could cause pelvic congestion and weaken resistance, potentially leading to a recurrence of the disease and resulting in amenorrhea.

**Can endometritis cause amenorrhea?** Taiyuan Zhongshan Reproductive Hospital explains that endometritis can be classified into three types: acute, chronic, and senile.

1. **Acute Endometritis**: This is often caused by infections from childbirth, abortion, intrauterine surgery, necrotic endometrial polyps, or submucosal uterine fibroids.

2. **Chronic Endometritis**: Besides tuberculous endometritis, long-standing conditions such as salpingo-oophoritis, intrauterine devices, retained placental tissue after delivery or abortion, can all lead to chronic endometritis.

3. **Senile Endometritis**: In elderly women, due to low levels of estrogen, the endometrium is more susceptible to infection, leading to senile endometritis.

**Can endometritis cause amenorrhea?** Whether acute or chronic, endometritis poses significant risks to everyone's health, making it crucial to take the condition seriously. During the acute phase of endometritis, the endometrium becomes congested, swollen, and infiltrated by inflammatory cells. Severe cases may develop into abscesses. The primary symptoms of acute endometritis include fever, lower abdominal pain, increased vaginal discharge (sometimes bloody or foul-smelling), an enlarged uterus, and uterine tenderness. Chronic endometritis presents with similar symptoms and may also result in heavy menstruation, lower abdominal pain, and noticeable lumbar-sacral discomfort. Acute endometritis can progress to myometritis, salpingitis, and pelvic inflammatory disease, worsening the condition and potentially leading to amenorrhea.
