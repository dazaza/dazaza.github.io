Google is testing a local news service in Google Now.

by anonymous on 2013-08-02 10:32:24

Beijing time, August 1st early morning news, Google is testing local news "cards" in the Google Now intelligent assistant service, attracting users to focus on the most relevant local news for them.

Google Now has been integrated into all phones with the latest Android system installed, and iPhone users can also use Google Now through the Google search app. Industry insiders believe that Google Now is a reasonable carrier of local news because the service can understand the user's location and provide users with "information related to the current environment." Google Now will consider the user's interests, time of day, and the user's location, among other factors.

Google had not previously disclosed tests of its local news service in Google Now, and the current tests are conducted independently by Google. Joanna Wright (Johanna Wright), Vice President of Google Search and Assistant Services, revealed this information in an interview last week.

Wright said: "One of the services we are currently testing is highly regional local news cards. This function is very useful, it can tell me what is happening in my community. For example, I can know that Miss Mexico went to my son's school, the local Chipotle restaurant is running a promotion, and there was a murder in the park near my house. This completely targets you and your interests."

She also mentioned that Google's new local news product is currently just a "trial version," and "we are conducting A/B tests, hoping to understand user feedback in the market."

It is currently unclear whether Google will fully roll out the local news service in Google Now. However, if the initial tests go well, the service is likely to be launched. Currently, the card list in Google Now does not include the local news service.

So far, no internet or media company has found a way to profit from local news services. Previous such services included NBC's EveryBlock, The Guardian's Guardian Local, and AOL's Patch. Therefore, some industry insiders believe that local news services will never make a profit. As such, Google might be the most suitable candidate to try again, as Google is merely an aggregator service and is currently working hard to help users discover local news products more easily.

Compared to establishing a website that provides a large amount of local news information, placing local news in context-related situations may be a better approach. In the latter model, local news will become a push medium because Google Now will decide autonomously when to push which cards to users.
