Character introduction: After 5 years of becoming famous, Walcott is finally a mature player. His next destination is to become the Gunner King.

by qiyouchax74 on 2011-05-05 14:01:38

NetEase Sports, August 30th report:

After Henry's departure, Arsenal fell into a slump. Although Wenger still had Fabregas and Van Persie, the former was always entangled with Barcelona, while the latter had long been labeled as injury-prone. At the start of the new season, both were either absent due to injuries or fatigue. Just as people were worried about how the young Arsenal team would perform at the beginning of the season, Walcott stepped up.

Walcott Becomes a True Talent After 5 Years of Fame

Apart from being slightly raw in the first match against Liverpool, the young tiger scored 4 goals in the next two matches, helping Arsenal secure all 6 points. This wasn't just about him tying with Drogba and Malouda for the top spot on the Premier League goal-scoring chart. Consider that in the past four seasons, the most league goals the Englishman had scored in a single season was 4, and now after only 3 rounds, he has already matched his personal record. Post-match, Wenger boldly stated that he intended to cultivate Walcott into a high-output striker like Henry, implying that the title of Gunner King, which had been vacant for 5 years, would soon have a new owner.

Born on March 16, 1989, in the small village of Compton in Berkshire, Walcott showcased astonishing athletic talent even in elementary school. At the Berkshire regional championships, he ran the 100 meters in 11.5 seconds, breaking the county games record. Initially starting football training with Swindon's under-11 team, he later joined his first club, Southampton. On August 2005, in a game against Wolves, then only 16 years and 143 days old, Walcott made his debut. In October, he started his first game against Leeds and scored his first goal for Southampton. That year, he was named among the top three for the BBC's Young Sports Personality of the Year.

In January 2006, Arsenal spent £5 million to bring him in from Southampton, with the fee potentially rising to a maximum of £12 million based on appearances. Seven months prior, in October 2005, Walcott was an unknown child who had just entered Southampton's first team. By February 2006, he was listed on Arsenal's Champions League roster and sat on the bench during their match against Real Madrid. The events of these few short months were incredible for the 17-year-old star, transitioning from a substitute in a Championship team to a member of the Premier League giant Arsenal.

An even greater surprise followed. Sweden's Eriksson, then managing England, saw Walcott and regarded him as a treasure, bringing him to the 2006 World Cup finals. However, at just 17 years old, Walcott hadn't even secured a stable position in Arsenal's starting lineup, let alone playing in the World Cup. During that exciting yet boring summer in Germany, amateur cameraman Walcott spent his first World Cup experience sitting on the bench.

However, four years later at the South Africa World Cup, theoretically, after four years of honing his skills, the young tiger should have unleashed his full potential. Unfortunately, recurring injuries kept Walcott from having consistent performances after making his Arsenal debut in the 06/07 season. From his shoulder to his knee, every time the young tiger wanted to prove himself, he was struck down by injuries. It was reasonable for Capello to eventually decide not to select Walcott, who had just recovered from injury, especially since he had only started 12 Premier League games for Arsenal in the 09/10 season.

But this setback did not leave Walcott disheartened. The complete recovery of his shoulder injury was his greatest relief. Compared to five years ago, Walcott is now more mature. With Fabregas and Van Persie yet to regain their form, the young tiger saved Arsenal's new season with 4 goals marking his complete transformation. Eventually, Fabregas will leave, and Van Persie may get injured again (he suffered another scare against Blackburn). Wenger was right; if anyone has the qualifications to become the next Henry, it is none other than Walcott.
