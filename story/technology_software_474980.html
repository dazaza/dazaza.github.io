Governance Question 5: What should I do if my business philosophy cannot be fully implemented?

As a business manager, I only want to find my place within the company so that I can fully realize my business philosophy and ultimately achieve added value. However, I don't have this feeling; I always feel like I've been left out, unable to integrate into the overall operations of the company. I don't know what my future goal is. As a result, I have become less talkative and devoted myself to improving my own business skills. I am trying to accelerate my understanding of this industry in order to achieve a business philosophy that aligns more closely with reality. Only I can truly understand my passivity. However, I can still feel elements of exclusion and distrust around me. It's really hard to put into words! What should I do?

Dr. Zheng's Answer:

This case is not a communication case but a governance case. However, it doesn't matter because communication problems are essentially governance problems. A governance communication expert must first be a governance expert.

Interestingly, things like this happen every day in real life, and I myself have encountered a few instances in my professional life. I believe that anyone with over ten years of work experience, especially ambitious managers, will have similar experiences to varying degrees. Therefore, this question also has the typical characteristics of a problem that I am willing to provide an answer to: the problem has universal significance or uniqueness.

Let's first analyze the fundamental issues present in this case. The core problem of this case actually only has two aspects. One is the issue of the enterprise being able to provide employees, especially managers, with a career growth environment and platform. The other is how managers appropriately position themselves and make necessary adjustments and decisions based on whether the situation meets their personal growth needs.

First, let's talk about the first issue. In fact, this primarily stems from potential problems in the enterprise's strategic human capital planning. We often see that enterprises lack long-term consideration when hiring managers, only seeing which positions are currently vacant and quickly recruiting someone to fill them. However, there is a lack of even basic consideration for the growth of these individuals after they join the company, i.e., their career development plans. Moreover, many companies, especially those with relatively strong market positions, tend to hire managers who exceed the qualifications required for their positions. Even if the salary conditions are slightly lower, considering the potential for personal career value appreciation due to the company's future growth and expansion, these managers generally are very willing to join such enterprises.

At the beginning, everyone is quite happy because it is evident that the company indeed has its good qualities, and the recruited managers are fully capable, if not more than competent, in performing their duties. But after half a year to a year, at most two years, problems start to arise. Why? Because there is a latent conflict or misalignment between the lack of long-term planning by the company when hiring and the high expectations of the managers when choosing the company. The seeds of trouble were sown the moment the manager decided to join the company — the manager unknowingly dug a trap for their own career growth within the company.

From the company's side, they hired you just to handle the current managerial position, never guaranteeing any specific promotion in the future. Sometimes, they might even make some incomprehensible internal transfers for the manager. On the other hand, the manager has great ambitions, with abundant energy and abilities, hoping to promptly find a career platform within the company to unleash and express themselves. This would allow them to gain more income while continuously enhancing their status. Thus, the huge disparity between the company’s positioning of the manager and the manager’s own abilities, qualities, and personal career growth requirements gradually becomes exposed.

On one hand, the company, due to structural rigidity, lack of significant breakthroughs in business, or a corporate culture that values seniority, fails to fulfill the encouraging words used during recruitment to motivate the managers. On the other hand, the manager harbors great ambitions, possessing a set of skills but is forced to remain in a low-level or secondary managerial position that cannot satisfy their need to utilize their full potential. Just as in this case, the manager begins to feel "unable to integrate" into the entire management structure. In reality, the company believes he has already integrated into the management structure and that he is perfectly suited for his current managerial role because he undoubtedly excels in his current management responsibilities. The problem lies with the manager himself, as he does not认同 the company's current positioning of him, and the company's management reality clearly tells him that he cannot obtain a more suitable managerial position at the moment. Naturally, he develops a sense of "not being integrated and being left out," which, in fact, is true: part of his abilities are entirely ignored by the company, which only expects and utilizes a small portion of his talents. Although he is extremely competent in his current position within the company, he feels "unable to integrate into the overall operation of the company." The reason is simple: the company never intended nor planned for him to participate more in the company's management.

The occurrence of such situations is a tragedy for both the company and the manager.

For the company, it means bringing in a very capable manager who could contribute more to the enterprise, yet the company never considers or pays attention to this issue, always operating in a routine manner. The result is that they must wait until this person leaves and then mechanically recruit someone else from the talent market to fill the vacancy. The best approach for the company would be to use the manager's excellent abilities to cultivate a qualified successor for the company and then transfer this already seasoned employee to a managerial position where he can better utilize his talents. This is truly a win-win strategy: first, the company does not need to recruit externally, achieving internal cultivation and reducing recruitment costs; second, existing employees receive leadership training, giving old employees valuable growth opportunities; third, higher-level managers use this manager, avoiding the need for external recruitment.

The occurrence of such a situation is a tragedy for both the company and the manager.
