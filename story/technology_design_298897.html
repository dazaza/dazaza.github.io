The professor did not forget the "handshake incident" when the gunner won.

by ka888g68u on 2009-12-08 13:16:08

After the Gunners' victory, the professor still remembered the "handshake scandal". Wenger: I wouldn't shake hands even if I won. Before the game against Stoke City, Arsene Wenger, Arsenal's manager, appeared early near the player tunnel exit, waiting to shake hands and chat with the opposing manager Pulis, in order to wash away the bad name of being disrespectful to his peers that Hughes had given him. However, the ungrateful Pulis failed to come out of the dressing room on time, leaving the professor no choice but to awkwardly walk back to the home team's coaching bench.

Manchester City's manager Hughes criticized Wenger for leaving without shaking hands after the game, mocking the professor as a terrible loser. When this statement was made public, it caused a stir in public opinion. Last Friday, every Premier League manager who attended the pre-game press conference had to face a prepared "mandatory question" from the media: "Was Wenger's action really impolite?" The sports news channel of Sky TV even inserted live footage of different managers responding to the issue during their programs, making the "handshake scandal" even overshadow the World Cup draw ceremony that day.

The first to face the media was the "defendant" Wenger. The professor had initially stated post-game that he would not respond to this matter, but seeing how things were escalating, he had to go back on his word and talk about his attitude: "Saturday will be my 500th league match. I have shaken hands with the opposing manager at least 497 times in the league. Although the etiquette of shaking hands after the game is important, what people value more is the humility and respect for the opponent it embodies. I am not an angel, and I am indeed a terrible loser, but that day, I would have stuck to my decision (not to shake hands with Hughes) even if I had won. There are always some unpredictable incidents that happen on the coaching bench during the game, but I never bring these up at press conferences to point fingers at the opposing manager. I don't want to make a big deal out of it."

The professor's "497" figure is verifiable. Prior to Hughes, Wenger only refused Mourinho and former West Ham manager Pardew. Pardew was because he rushed into the visiting team's technical area to celebrate after a goal, while Mourinho was because he called Wenger a "voyeur." For those who do not respect him, the professor seems uninterested in "repaying grievances with virtue." Instead, the Frenchman prefers to "repay grievances straightforwardly," expressing his feelings frankly by refusing to shake hands after the game. This controversy stemmed from Hughes repeatedly "crossing boundaries" and entering the visiting manager's command area during the game. It was also reported that when Wenger "politely escorted" Hughes out, the Manchester City manager hurled a British national swearword at the Frenchman, which was the real reason why the professor ultimately refused to shake his hand.

Most Premier League managers also showed no interest in getting involved in this controversy. Ancelotti said he had no say since there is no tradition in Italy for coaches to have to shake hands after the game. Bruce and Phil Brown played Tai Chi, assuring the media that they would definitely shake hands with the opposing manager the next day. Only Keane, the Ipswich manager, could be considered a man of passion: "Who among us isn't a terrible loser? I certainly wouldn't nitpick just because the opposing manager didn't shake hands with me after the game."

As the colleagues distanced themselves from the matter one by one, the media began to shift the blame towards themselves. The "plaintiff" Hughes also called off the battle on Friday: "Let's just let this pass. After all, we're all adults. But I did not verbally insult Wenger; these rumors are part of Arsenal's crisis PR conducted by their media department."

And under the watchful eyes of the media, Wenger also promptly greeted Pulis after the game on Saturday to shake hands and greet him. This time, the enthusiastic professor didn't even miss the opportunity to shake hands with the assistant coach of the visiting team. Fans joked after the game that the professor's move seemed like data brushing: "Counting the handshake with the opposing assistant coach, Arsene has already brushed his league handshake data to 499. If he had waited a little longer at the player tunnel entrance before the game, he might have really succeeded in brushing this data to 500 in his 500th game."
