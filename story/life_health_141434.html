Treatment of hydrosalpinx

by mark46 on 2009-04-20 11:14:57

The fimbria of the fallopian tube is composed of serosa, smooth muscle, and mucosa. It is located at the distal end of the ampulla, like an open umbrella covering the surface of the ovary. Its apex is the opening of the fallopian tube into the peritoneal cavity, with a diameter of 1 to 1.5 cm. The muscular fibers are sparse in the fimbria, but the mucosal folds are abundant. The mucosal epithelium consists of ciliated cells and secretory cells. Under normal circumstances, more than 60% of the mucosal epithelial cells are ciliated, and the movement of the cilia is directed towards the uterine cavity. This ciliary beating helps in the transport of the egg. The pickup of the egg is usually achieved through direct contact between the cilia of the fimbria of the fallopian tube and the surface of the ovary. At this time, the ovarian ligament and the fimbria of the fallopian tube must act in coordination with the mesovarium and mesosalpinx, so that the rotation of the ovary and the movement of the fimbria on the ovary are mutually coordinated, allowing the egg to enter the fallopian tube smoothly.

The pregnancy rate after fallopian tube neostomy is relatively low, only about 20%. The main reason is that inflammation of the fallopian tubes may destroy the mucosa and ciliated cells at the fimbrial end. For patients with severe hydrosalpinx at the fimbrial end, even if the neostomy is successful, it may affect its normal oocyte pickup function due to the destruction of the cilia, thereby affecting conception. Another factor is the possibility of re-adhesion and obstruction at the fimbrial end after neostomy. After neostomy, if no contraception is used and pregnancy does not occur within half a year, a repeat hysterosalpingography can be performed to assess the patency of the fallopian tubes. If there is re-obstruction at the fimbrial end, in vitro fertilization (IVF) is necessary. For patients who have undergone fimbrial neostomy and whose fallopian tubes are found to be patent upon review by hysterosalpingography, if infertility persists for up to two years despite excluding ovulation problems, sperm quality issues in the male partner, and immune factors, IVF treatment should also be considered.

The Wang Compound Infusion Therapy overcomes the shortcomings of previous interventional treatments and achieves good results even in cases of distal fallopian tube hydrosalpinx and fimbrial occlusion. Compared with neostomy, it has the advantages of being non-invasive and without surgical incision. After unblocking the fallopian tubes, special anti-adhesion drugs are infused into the tubes, which effectively resolve adhesions and prevent recurrence. This therapy restores the normal physiological function of the fallopian tubes very well, and the postoperative pregnancy rate can reach over 80%, far higher than that of fallopian tube neostomy.
