Especially the progressively advancing supervisory indicators of the CBRC

by loveyan5 on 2012-02-05 10:03:48

To avoid the possible liquidity overflow brought by the government's 4 trillion yuan investment and banks' 10 trillion yuan loan, all financial departments are thinking of ways. The result is that each department issues policies to tighten control one after another, leading to the superposition of macroeconomic policies and triggering concerns about "over-adjustment" of policies, which also triggered the fact of the international market "shorting China". It doesn't stop there. In fact, the previous rigid policy superposition has already caused a lot of trouble for the actual monetary policy. Especially the continuously improving regulatory indicators set by the CBRC have made it difficult for the PBOC to inject currency when international capital flows out.

For example, the hard constraint of the 75% deposit-loan ratio, plus the net reduction of foreign exchange reserves, may cause problems for the PBOC to inject liquidity into the market. Now, according to the data displayed in the third quarter reports of banking industry, the deposit-loan ratios of most listed banks have approached the 75% regulatory red line set by the CBRC. Some banks are only a step away from the 75% regulatory requirement, such as CITIC Bank at 74%, Industrial Bank at 74.6%, and Minsheng Bank has exceeded the red line to 75.5%. Among the five major banks, Bank of China and Bank of Communications have better deposit-loan ratios, but they are still at 72% and 72.8% respectively.

This is the reality. Note that there may be some water content in these data because they are end-of-quarter survey data. We all know that every quarter-end, commercial banks start large-scale deposit gathering, just to meet the deposit-loan ratio requirements set by the regulatory authorities. This means that the actual deposit-loan ratio of commercial banks during normal times may have already exceeded 75%, and they reach the regulatory indicators after large-scale deposit gathering at the end of the quarter.

Why do I particularly mention the issue of "deposit-loan ratio"? Because it relates to the effectiveness of the PBOC's monetary policy.

Historically, the PBOC usually injects liquidity into the market through two "effective" channels: one is through foreign exchange purchases, and the other is through loans. Now, when foreign exchange reserves are net reduced, it indicates that the PBOC can no longer continue to inject currency through foreign exchange purchases, and this window has even become a net withdrawal. To balance the compression effect of foreign exchange purchases on the money supply, the PBOC must inject base money through other channels. What channels? They are nothing more than recovering central bank bills, reverse repurchase, and lowering the statutory reserve requirement ratio.

Now, whether it is through any channel of injection, it can increase the liquidity of commercial banks, which is true. But the problem is, how can commercial banks transmit the PBOC's easing signal to the market? This is troublesome. Because commercial banks dare not inject more loans, because they are constrained by the deposit-loan ratio, increasing loans will break the red line set by the CBRC. At the same time, they also dare not let deposits transfer, because reducing deposits directly means that they must simultaneously reduce the scale of loans, which will affect the performance of banks and threaten the realization of annual business objectives.

Especially since the deposit-loan ratios of all banks have already been very high, the PBOC's ability to influence the market through the loan end and stabilize economic growth has greatly weakened. To put it bluntly: even if the PBOC wants to inject currency to increase loans, due to the rigid constraints of the deposit-loan ratio, no matter how much money there is, it cannot be converted into loans.

If the PBOC rigidly injects currency, or the interest rate market may also undergo abnormal changes. Because the PBOC can only inject currency into the money market, the abundance of funds in the money market will lower interest rates. Can this interest rate affect the interest rate of the deposit-loan market? No. Because commercial banks cannot increase the scale of loans, so they dare not lower the loan interest rate, otherwise their business objectives cannot be achieved; deposits are the same, the PBOC injecting currency will inevitably lead to a good expectation in the capital market, the rise in the stock market will form competition for bank deposits, so banks must retain deposits at high interest rates, while increasing the fund borrowing in the money market.

In this way, the transmission of money market interest rates to the capital market will obviously be blocked.

The transmission of money quantity to loan quantity is blocked, and the transmission of money market price to capital market price is also blocked. Isn't this a serious problem? In fact, before the Central Financial Work Conference, people talked a lot about establishing a "China Financial General Control Mechanism" above the "one bank and three commissions", the reason lies in this. It also shows that the lack of coordination between the PBOC and the CBRC's policies can easily lead to policy superposition and over-adjustment.

So what should we do now? There are ways, but reforms are needed. And the most basic reform idea. China's policy-making often involves short-sighted behavior that loses sight of one thing for another. Policies are formulated during the period of foreign exchange inflow without considering the possibility of future foreign exchange outflow; the compression process spares no effort, but sets up many obstacles for future easing.
