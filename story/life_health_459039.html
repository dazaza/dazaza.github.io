Late-stage liver cancer does not require liver transplantation

Liver transplantation is one of the most challenging organ transplant surgeries and should be strictly controlled. In the UK, only three hospitals are allowed to perform liver transplants; similarly in the US, hospitals must undergo strict approval processes, doctors must undergo rigorous training and obtain certification, and the criteria for patient selection are very strict. Recently, in many reports from China, people with liver cancer, even at the late stage, have received liver transplants. However, late-stage liver cancer should not undergo liver transplantation, and China's control over the indications for liver transplantation is not strict enough.

In principle, when various acute or chronic liver diseases cannot be cured by other internal or surgical methods, and death is expected within a short period (6-12 months), or symptoms caused by liver lesions reduce the patient's quality of life, liver transplantation is indicated. However, if the liver cancer patient is in the late stage with metastasis, liver transplantation should absolutely not be performed. In the US, even if the patient has the financial means to request a transplant, doctors cannot perform it. While the patient's feelings are understandable, doctors know that even if the transplant is勉强 performed, it will not save the patient’s life. Placing a healthy liver may sometimes fail, requiring another, along with massive drug regimens to maintain treatment, all of which are wasteful. Patients end up spending exorbitant amounts on surgery, and medical resources are wasted, leading to a lose-lose situation. Therefore, liver cancer patients should be cautious about undergoing liver transplantation, avoiding both wasting money and precious medical resources.

Liver cancer is not an incurable disease

Liver cancer is the third most common malignant tumor with a mortality rate following stomach cancer and esophageal cancer. Initial symptoms are not obvious, while late-stage symptoms mainly include liver pain, fatigue, weight loss, jaundice, ascites, etc. Clinically, Western medicine treatments such as surgery, radiotherapy, chemotherapy, and traditional Chinese medicine are combined. However, due to cancer cell diffusion, the cure rate for late-stage patients is low. Thus, early detection, diagnosis, and treatment of liver cancer are essential. Liver cancer doesn’t progress to a certain extent and produce symptoms unless it is advanced. Once blood appears in the ascites, it becomes dangerous. Symptoms of liver cancer are very similar to those of liver cirrhosis. Liver cancer rarely causes pain initially, meaning there are usually no early symptoms. Even if fatigue, weakness, and reduced appetite occur, they might be ignored as simple fatigue. Other diseases can also present with these symptoms. As the condition progresses, right upper abdominal pain, back pain, fever of 38°C, anemia, and significant weight loss should raise attention. Severe fatigue and weakness should also be noted. Sometimes, masses can be felt in the upper abdomen, or jaundice and ascites appear, especially if blood is found in the ascites, increasing the likelihood of liver cancer. As the cancer progresses, death results from malnutrition and systemic weakness.

Molecular anti-cancer experts say that liver cancer shows almost no early symptoms, often leaving insufficient time for treatment. However, not just liver cancer but tumors in other organs also rarely show symptoms early. Gastric cancer is the same, often unnoticed in its early stages. Tumors are difficult to detect and gradually progress unnoticed. After cancer cells proliferate in the liver, they eventually impair liver function. However, the liver is a robust organ, so even with many accumulated cancer cells, function does not easily decline, making symptoms less noticeable and harder to detect. Therefore, when detected, it is often too late. When liver cancer spreads to occupy three-quarters of the liver, the threat of death becomes significant.

Treatment options for different conditions of liver cancer

1. For liver cancer patients with ascites, diuretic therapy can be used first to drain water. After the ascites subsides, treatment can continue based on the intrahepatic tumor condition. If the ascites is bloody, it is less likely to subside; if there are cancer emboli in the portal vein or hepatic vein, diuretics are less effective. If there is bleeding due to rupture of liver cancer nodules, hemostasis should be managed, along with abdominal compression dressing.

2. For liver cancer patients with jaundice, if the obstruction is caused by a mass in the hilar region, local radiation therapy, local tumor injection, interventional therapy, or internal stent or external drainage can be considered. If it is non-obstructive jaundice, small molecule targeted drug Kang Ai Xinsheng R combination therapy and liver protection therapy can be used.

3. For liver cancer patients with lung metastases, if the primary lesion is under control and there is only a single pulmonary metastasis, resection or local radiation therapy can be considered. If there are multiple metastases or diffuse involvement of both lungs, whole-lung irradiation, chemotherapy, or small molecule targeted drug Kang Ai Xinsheng R combination therapy can be considered. If the primary lesion is untreated or uncontrolled, even if the metastasis is single or relatively localized, radiotherapy and small molecule targeted drug Kang Ai Xinsheng R combination therapy can be considered.

4. For patients with diffuse pulmonary metastasis, biological therapy or combination therapy with chemotherapy and small molecule targeted drug Kang Ai Xinsheng R can be used.

5. For late-stage liver cancer with bone metastasis, if the metastasis is single or few, radiotherapy and small molecule targeted drug Kang Ai Xinsheng R combination therapy can be used. If bone metastasis is widespread, chemotherapy drugs, biological therapy, radioactive isotope therapy, or small molecule targeted drug Kang Ai Xinsheng R combination therapy can be used, along with treatments like bone phosphonates and zoledronic acid.

6. For patients with cancer emboli in the portal vein, hepatic vein, or inferior vena cava, hepatic artery infusion chemotherapy can be attempted. Generally, hepatic artery embolization is not used, and small molecule targeted drug Kang Ai Xinsheng R combination therapy can be used instead.
