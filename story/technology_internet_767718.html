This is the motivation for Google's acquisition of Motorola

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 13:08:43

Recently, American tech writer Farhad Manjoo wrote that in the long term, Google's acquisition of Motorola could simply be to lower smartphone prices, clearing obstacles for its ultimate mission.

Mission

This summer, I visited Google's headquarters and met with Dennis Woodside, CEO of Motorola Mobility under Google. Woodside showed me the yet-to-be-released Moto X. I felt that although this phone has some innovative features, it was not worth the $12.5 billion Google spent on it.

Last week, Google’s stock price broke through $1000 for the first time. However, the latest earnings report shows that Motorola Mobility lost $248 million in Q3. A year ago, this figure was $192 million. This raises many questions about Google's acquisition of Motorola: What are Google's long-term plans for Motorola? Is it for patents or to get into the hardware business? How much more money will Google invest?

I think that Google's acquisition of Motorola may not be for profit. Google's smartphone business won't directly make money, but Google is trying to lower phone prices to destroy other companies' mobile phone businesses.

Specifically, in the long term, Motorola may lower the smartphone profit margins of Apple and Samsung. The iPhone alone contributes nearly two-thirds of Apple's profits, making it the most profitable product globally so far. Meanwhile, Samsung earns billions in revenue from selling Android smartphones. Samsung is nominally a Google partner, but given Samsung's huge influence in the Android ecosystem, Samsung has gradually become a competitor to Google. In 2012, the combined smartphone revenue of Apple and Samsung reached $53 billion, making phones the most profitable product in the tech industry.

In the long term, Google will not ignore these profits. As a company, Google is unwaveringly focused on a lofty mission. From core products like Android and Chrome to experimental products like driverless cars and smart glasses, everything Google does is aimed at one goal: getting more people to use the internet more frequently.

Clearing Obstacles

The high profits of smartphone manufacturers are an obstacle to this mission. In Google's view, the price of the iPhone is too high. If the iPhone were cheaper, Apple could have sold more phones. Since other manufacturers price their products based on the iPhone, the entire industry could have sold more phones. For Google, more iPhones mean more browsing, which would certainly lead to higher ad revenues. Lower prices would also reduce Apple's profit margin, which would benefit Google.

So how can we force Apple to cut its profits? In 2011, when Android had just started eating into iOS market share, venture capitalist Bill Gurley made a highly prescient observation. Gurley believed that Android and Chrome are not traditional "products." Google invested heavily in their development but didn’t plan to make direct profits from them; instead, Google viewed them as a "moat" defending its "castle" (advertising business). This is Google's true purpose. By forcing competitors like BlackBerry, Nokia, and Apple to compete with products that have no profit motive, Google has designed "the greatest legitimate wealth destroyer in history."

Two years later, Gurley's prophecy has almost come true. Today, Android is the world's most popular smartphone system, having created numerous cheap competitors to iOS devices, significantly slowing down Apple's revenue growth.

Of course, Google will not stop there. Although many manufacturers produce cheap Android phones, most of these phones have low configurations and are very unsuitable for web browsing. Therefore, customers are still willing to buy the more expensive Apple or Samsung phones.
