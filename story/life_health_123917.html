Introduction to ventilators

by kouhong on 2009-02-10 22:24:15

A ventilator is a device that can replace, control, or alter a person's normal physiological breathing, increase lung ventilation, improve respiratory function, reduce the consumption of respiratory work, and conserve cardiac reserve capacity. When infants and young children develop acute respiratory failure, after active conservative treatment proves ineffective, with weakened breathing, excessive and thick phlegm, difficulty in expectoration, airway obstruction or atelectasis occurs, tracheal intubation and the use of a ventilator should be considered.

A ventilator must possess four basic functions: filling the lungs with air, transitioning from inhalation to exhalation, expelling alveolar air, and switching back from exhalation to inhalation, repeating this cycle continuously. Therefore, it must have: 1) a power source that can deliver gas, replacing the work of human respiratory muscles; 2) the ability to generate a certain breathing rhythm, including respiratory rate and inhalation-to-exhalation ratio, to replace the function of the human respiratory central nervous system controlling the breathing rhythm; 3) provide an appropriate tidal volume (VT) or minute ventilation (MV) to meet the needs of respiratory metabolism; 4) supply gas that has been warmed and humidified, replacing the function of the human nasal cavity, and providing more oxygen than atmospheric content to increase the concentration of inhaled oxygen and improve oxygenation.

Power Source: Compressed gas can be used as power (pneumatic) or a motor as power (electric). The respiratory rate and inhalation-to-exhalation ratio can also utilize pneumatic pneumatic control, electric-electric control, or pneumatic-electric control types. The phase switch between inhalation and exhalation often occurs when the predetermined pressure is reached within the respiratory circuit during inhalation, switching to exhalation (fixed-pressure type), or when the predetermined capacity is reached during inhalation, switching to exhalation (fixed-volume type). However, modern ventilators usually have both of these forms.

Therapeutic ventilators are commonly used for patients with more complex and severe conditions, requiring comprehensive functions to perform various breathing modes to adapt to changes in the patient's condition. On the other hand, anesthesia ventilators are mainly used for patients undergoing anesthesia surgery, where most patients do not have significant cardiovascular and pulmonary abnormalities. The required ventilator only needs adjustable ventilation volume, respiratory rate, and inhalation-to-exhalation ratio, capable of performing IPPV, which is basically sufficient for use.

The basic principle of a ventilator: The majority of the more commonly used ones operate through dual-loop circuits inside and outside a bag (or folded bellows). The inner loop gas flow communicates with the patient's airway, while the outer loop gas flow is primarily used to squeeze the breathing bag or bellows, pressing the fresh gas inside the bag (or bellows) towards the patient's alveoli for gas exchange, also known as drive gas. Since it does not communicate with the patient's airway, compressed oxygen or compressed air can be used.

The basic structure of a ventilator: Power system, ventilation source, control system, respiratory circuit, monitoring alarm system.

Ventilator classification:

1. Classification by connection method with the patient:

Invasive ventilator: The ventilator connects to the patient via a mask.

Non-invasive ventilator: The ventilator connects to the patient via a tracheal tube.

2. Classification by purpose (six categories):

Emergency ventilator: Specifically used for on-site emergency care.

Respiratory therapy ventilator: Provides long-term ventilation support and respiratory therapy for patients with respiratory insufficiency.

Anesthesia ventilator: Specifically used for anesthesia respiratory management.

Pediatric ventilator: Specifically used for ventilation support and respiratory therapy for children and newborns.

High-frequency ventilator: Possesses ventilation frequency >60 times/min function.

Non-invasive ventilator: Completes ventilation support via a mask or nasal mask.

3. Classification by drive method (three categories):

Pneumatic pneumatic control ventilator: Both the ventilation source and control system use only oxygen as the power source. Mostly portable emergency ventilators.

For example, the STAR-100 type anesthesia ventilator designed by our hospital uses upper and lower double-folded bellows. The upper bellows connect to the patient's airway, the lower bellows connect to the outer chamber of the upper bellows, and there are holes separating the two chambers. Through the expansion and contraction of the bellows and the opening and closing of magnetic valves above and below, the intermediate partition valve is controlled. When the drive gas flows into the upper chamber, the gas in the lower bellows also flows into the upper chamber due to the Venturi effect, increasing the pressure in the upper chamber and forcing the bellows to move downward, directing fresh gas to the patient's lungs during inhalation until the preset VT value is reached. Afterward, the bellows cannot compress further, and as the pressure in the upper chamber continues to rise, the gas outside the upper chamber bellows transfers to the lower chamber bellows, lifting the bellows and opening the intermediate partition valve attracted by the upper magnet. The gas in the upper chamber flows through the open holes into the lower chamber, and the exhaust port releases the gas into the atmosphere, reducing the pressure in the upper chamber, allowing fresh gas to enter the upper bellows in preparation for the next inhalation. When the upper bellows is pressed down to the preset value, the intermediate partition valve is pressed downward and closed by the lower magnet while the drive gas continues to flow into the upper chamber. The Venturi effect causes the gas in the lower bellows to flow into the upper chamber, causing the lower bellows to move downward without affecting the closure of the intermediate partition valve until the start of the next inhalation phase, when the pressure in the upper chamber becomes high enough for the gas to flow inversely into the lower bellows, lifting the bellows and opening the intermediate partition valve. This process repeats continuously. The size of the drive gas flow and the size of the exhaust opening in the lower chamber can control the frequency and inhalation-to-exhalation ratio, thus basically satisfying the four functions of the ventilator.

Electric-electric control ventilator: Both the ventilation source and control system use electricity as the power source, with internal cylinders, piston pumps, etc., representing simpler-function ventilators. For example, the domestically produced SC-3 type ventilator uses two sets of four-link structures to convert rotational motion into oscillation, thereby driving the up-and-down reciprocating motion of the gas storage bag to produce controlled breathing.

After motor deceleration, the disc M is driven, and then through the connecting rod, the movement is transmitted to the rocker N, causing it to oscillate. Another connecting rod L causes the rocker K to move up and down. During the exhalation phase, the rocker moves upward, increasing the windbox capacity for charging. During inhalation, K moves downward, forcing the gas inside the windbox to flow towards the patient's lungs. The speed of M can change the frequency, and adjusting the connection point of L and K can change the VT. O2 enters from the inlet H, passes through the one-way valve and is stored in the gas storage bag C. During exhalation, the windbox expands, and O2 from C enters the windbox. During inhalation, the one-way valve E closes, and the O2 gas inside the windbox enters the patient's lungs. When the airway pressure >60cmH2O, the pressure-limiting valve G opens, releasing gas to reduce the airway pressure. PEEP is connected to the fish-mouth breathing valve F, and the patient's exhaled gas is discharged through the PEEP valve. ⑷ High-frequency jet ventilator: Its principle involves intermittently injecting high-flow gas from a high-pressure gas source directly into the patient's airway, creating high-frequency jet ventilation. The fundamental principle utilizes a rotary valve, pneumatic valve, or electromagnetic valve to control the jet airflow. The entire respiratory circuit communicates with the atmosphere, and its exhaled gas is directly discharged into the atmosphere. Its flow, pressure, and frequency are adjustable, suitable for cases, conditions, and surgeries with specific requirements.

Pneumatic-electric control ventilator: Uses oxygen as the power source for ventilation and electricity as the power source for the control system. It is the mainstream design of multi-functional ventilators. For example, the Ohmeda 7000 type ventilator is a typical application of a pneumatic-electric dual-loop circuit. Its electronic control system calculates the VT, inhalation time, exhalation time, and inhalation flow rate based on the set values of MV, inhalation-to-exhalation ratio, and respiratory rate, thereby controlling the required drive gas flow. During the inhalation phase, the electronic control unit closes the exhaust valve, and the driver enters the outer box of the windbox. As the drive gas continuously flows into the box, the external pressure rises, compressing the windbox, moving it downward, and forcing the gas inside the box to flow into the anesthesia breathing circuit, entering the patient's lungs. When the total amount of delivered drive gas equals the specified amount, the inhalation phase ends, and the electronic control unit opens the exhaust valve for the drive gas, causing the external drive gas pressure to drop. The mixed gas of fresh gas and the patient's exhaled gas continuously enters the box, causing the windbox to rise. When the exhalation ends, the exhaust valve closes again, and the driver enters the outer box of the windbox, repeating this cycle continuously.

4. Classification by ventilation mode (four categories):

Time-controlled ventilator (time-switching): Completes the transition from exhalation to inhalation according to the preset time.

Volume-controlled ventilator (volume-switching): Completes the transition from exhalation to inhalation according to the preset output gas volume.

Pressure-controlled ventilator (pressure-switching): Completes the transition from exhalation to inhalation according to the preset airway pressure value.

Flow-controlled ventilator (flow-switching): Completes the transition from exhalation to inhalation according to the preset gas flow rate value.

5. Classification by pressure and flow generator (four categories): Mapleson (1959)

Constant-pressure generator: Low ventilation source driving pressure, constant pressure during inhalation, inhalation flow varies with lung pressure.

Non-constant-pressure generator: Low ventilation source driving pressure, regular changes occur during inhalation, inhalation flow is influenced by both driving pressure and lung pressure.

Constant-flow generator: High ventilation source driving pressure, gas flow remains unchanged during inhalation.

Non-constant-flow generator: High ventilation source driving pressure, gas flow undergoes regular changes during inhalation.

Pressure generators are suitable for patients with normal lung function, while flow generators are suitable for patients with poor lung compliance.
