Lenovo responds to why it cancels the "four-leaf-clover" design

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 11:06:47

After Lenovo released the VIBE X, Liu Jun, Senior Vice President of Lenovo and President of the Lenovo business group, Feng Xing, Vice President of Lenovo and General Manager of MIDH business, and Chen Wenhui, Vice President of Lenovo and General Manager of Lenovo Mobile Terminal Division, were interviewed.

During the interview, we raised several questions that we were concerned about and received detailed answers, which we have organized in a Q&A format. These included the sales volume of K900, how to face competition from Xiaomi's Redmi, how to surpass Samsung, Lenovo's channel strategy, the profitability of its mobile phone business, and Lenovo's smart TV business. Liu Jun also directly responded to media inquiries regarding the adjustment of the VIBE, S, and A brand structure and the replacement of Clover with VIBE ROM.

ifanr: Today, when talking about VIBE X moving towards the high-end market, the first thing that comes to mind is K900. Whether Lenovo can successfully move towards the high-end may be judged by the sales volume of K900. Could you introduce the sales volume of this phone?

Feng Xing: K900 was our starting point for entering the high-end market, but it cannot solely be used to judge our confidence and progress in advancing to the mid-to-high end. The sales of K900 are progressing normally, but it indeed did not become a major hit, falling short of our expectations.

ifanr: I noticed that the specifications of VIBE X are not particularly high - MTK MT6589T processor, 1GB memory, 2000mAh battery (later confirmed as 2GB memory, twice that of Redmi), with a slightly better screen than Redmi. However, Redmi’s price is 799 yuan, which is quite aggressive. How do you plan to consolidate your position in the 1000-2000 yuan range and avoid being impacted by products like Redmi?

Liu Jun: Compared to two years ago, the smartphone market has changed significantly. Two years ago, everyone knew that hardware configuration was an important marker of a smartphone's class, but today, this situation has changed greatly. Two years ago, smartphones were single-core, 3.5-inch screens, with frequencies below 1G. But after two years of rapid development, smartphones have evolved to quad-core processors and screens larger than five inches. In my personal opinion, the difference between high-end and low-end products is no longer mainly in the hardware platform, because the current hardware platforms are already sufficient. More differences lie in user experience and design, including materials and craftsmanship.

To put it simply, if we compare purely based on hardware, I think it would be hard for the iPhone to sell well, as many phones now have dual-core processors, making such comparisons difficult. Our VIBE X product has very high-quality characteristics, with significant differences from lower-end products.

(Liu Jun, Senior Vice President of Lenovo and President of Lenovo Business Group, was previously responsible for Lenovo's MIDH; currently, Feng Xing is responsible for Lenovo's MIDH business.)

ifanr: During the CES interview in January, Chen Xudong mentioned selling phones in PC stores in the future; in May, it was mentioned that Lenovo aims to surpass Samsung within two years. We know Samsung's two key advantages are product update speed and extensive channel coverage. Where does Lenovo's confidence come from?

Liu Jun: Lenovo's first advantage is innovation. When I set up MIDH, I made significant process reorganizations in two mainstream manufacturers. One core business process is the so-called product development management process, which is why Lenovo mobile phones have been leading over the past two years - the first to launch dual-core, the first to launch quad-core, the first to launch 4-inch, 4.5-inch, and 5-inch screens, etc. This leadership is due to our confidence. As a company coming from the PC industry, we are confident that we will not be slower than competitors like Samsung in terms of product updates. This is the product development process.

The second process is the supply chain management process that we rewrote, enabling us to quickly bring products to market and deliver them to users. This is another characteristic of ours.

The third feature, which I believe is increasingly important, is the difference in user experience. Lenovo has invested heavily in software and cloud services from the beginning. As Chen Yu mentioned in today's presentation, tens of millions of users are using our app store, security, synchronization, and茄子快传 applications. In fact, we have full confidence in seeing that our products are accepted by users and used daily, with more than 20 million App downloads per day. This number changes every time we meet because it is growing exponentially. On this point, we aim to create a significant difference, where user experience far exceeds that of competitors, hoping to make it our third distinction.

Chen Wenhui: From the perspective of product design, I support Mr. Liu's view. You mentioned the selection of platforms earlier. When we were doing PCs, people often focused on "wearing underwear outside." However, when it comes to smartphones, including all of you who use them, how much time do you spend thinking about what platform is inside? I guess there isn't much time for that.

Let me give an example. In 2011, when we were selecting platforms, operators gave us a lot of doubts, saying we were using platforms from counterfeit phones. In reality, we didn't consider it from that angle. We evaluated quality and performance comprehensively, considering long-term factors from upstream manufacturers. It turned out that the platform achieved great success, with no quality issues or performance problems related to this. Who ultimately benefits from this? The users. Everyone here knows that new entrants like MTK hope that more vendors join in, allowing final discounts and benefits to reach customers.

Regarding channel strategies, Liu Jun answered in detail during another media question:

Liu Jun: Let me add something about channels. If we divide channels into two categories, one is operator channels, and in this category, we are not at a disadvantage compared to Samsung. Currently, we are the top choice for China Mobile and China Unicom, and overall, we have ranked first and second. Now, the gap between the two companies is very close, so I don't think we have any disadvantages here.

We also feel that open storefronts (the first flagship store is just opened) have their advantages, as we see the integration of channels in the next few years as a big trend. By channel integration, I mean the integration of PC channels and smartphone channels, which is a major trend. Today, Lenovo has announced a comprehensive transformation towards PC+, including accelerating the transition of all PC channels to intelligent interconnection channels. This transformation is ongoing. With tens of thousands of PC specialty stores in China, we will transform in this direction and complete the transformation within two to three years. Once this transformation is completed, I believe our channel strength will be enormous. (In his response, Liu Jun also mentioned that Lenovo manages 30,000 global sales outlets, fewer than Samsung's 65,000).

ifanr: I saw earlier that in late January, it was stated that Lenovo's mobile phones became profitable in the third quarter of the 2013 fiscal year. I want to confirm whether it was Lenovo's mobile phones that became profitable or if it was the MIDH division?

Feng Xing: It was in the mobile phone sector.

ifanr: Has MIDH become profitable?

Liu Jun: Sorry, I can't answer that.

ifanr: Lenovo's PC sales are already the world's first. Do you feel a sense of unease at the top? What's the next step? Because smart devices are also very popular now. Secondly, last year you personally launched the sale of smart TVs under MIDH, but the market only heated up in September this year. Does it feel like you got up early but arrived late?

Liu Jun: First, let's talk about smart TVs. Indeed, the smart TV market has clearly picked up this year. I don't think companies that are prepared when the market heats up are latecomers. I believe we are at a very appropriate juncture, and we are optimistic about the rapid growth of the smart TV market in the future.

The PC market is indeed strong for Lenovo right now. Although we are the new champion, having only taken the top spot for one quarter, we are still quite confident that we should maintain this position going forward. The entire PC market still faces certain challenges, as the PC market is declining faster than expected. We hope for a quicker recovery. We are more concerned about the overall market situation. Moreover, I agree with the view that in the future, more attention will be on the PC+ market, or the SCD (Smart Connected Device) market. An increasing number of consulting firms are categorizing this as a new category. Currently, we hold the third position, which is a challenger's position, so there is absolutely no feeling of unease at the top. We are eager to experience the chill at higher altitudes.

Regarding the brand architecture of VIBE, S, and A, and their replacement of the previous KPSA brands, Liu Jun mentioned that the K and P series will gradually be categorized under the VIBE sub-brand:

In the summary page I provided, I did not mention K. In fact, this summary represents our future direction. For instance, the P series still exists today. In the future, we will mainly unify under these three series (VIBE, S, A). First, the K series is clearly our high-end product line and will continue to be sold. You'll notice its complementarity with X. Our statistics show that 95% of K series buyers are male, making it suitable for men. This VIBE X, I believe, appeals to both genders, especially women. We will continue with the K series, but in the long term, from a branding perspective, we will gradually unify our high-end series under VIBE. This is our direction.

About Lenovo finally replacing the previous four-leaf clover design with the square-icon VIBE ROM, Liu Jun gave a direct response:

In UI design, it's important to strike a balance. Of course, we need to inherit, but we cannot cling to the past rigidly. So today, in the UI discussion, there is a change in whether the icons are round or square. When we designed the layout, the main image was square, but users accustomed to round icons can easily switch to another version.

There are two reasons for the adjustment. First, we asked the team if this is a trend. Since this is an open world, we must continuously attract new users. If new users are already accustomed to square icons, we cannot lose sales just because we dislike round ones. If a standard becomes an industrial trend, we must adapt to such standards rather than insist on being different.

The second reason is practicality. Sometimes, a single icon contains multiple smaller icons, and in such cases, square icons display clearer than round ones. When we initially decided on round icons, we did not yet have the mode of placing sub-menus within a single icon. With the emergence of the new mode, we must adapt to it. We hope to find a balance between maximizing adherence to tradition while adapting to change, as this is a rapidly changing emerging market. We need to quickly adapt while balancing tradition and innovation.
