Everyone shrinks within the school, it's bound to fail. Might as well go with Baidu.

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 13:21:04

In the era of big acquisitions, many people are speculating who will be acquired next? Recently, an insider from Renren revealed that Renren has been confirmed to be acquired by Baidu as a whole package. However, Renren Games, the only profitable business line under Renren, is not included in this package.

What’s the way forward for Renren? Some suggest it should return to campuses, but in reality, Renren has already tried everything possible in the campus market with little effect. Therefore, Renren is eager to expand from the campus market to the national market. Unfortunately, when Renren was just about to transform and start, it encountered the wave of Weibo, which ultimately led to its retreat back to the campus market. The campus market may look promising, but it is a market with little value.

Renren will die if it retreats to campuses

Why did Xiaonei (the original name of Renren) change its name to Renren? Because Renren had long foreseen the limited value space of the campus market, and the name "Xiaonei" was too restrictive. Thus, the first step in its transformation was to rename itself Renren.com. Everyone is familiar with Renren's bitter experiences, so I won't repeat them here. Let's talk about why the campus market has limited value.

Renren's active users are mostly college students and high school students. Most of them use Renren as a social network for leisure and entertainment, sharing interesting things. Few users use Renren for studying. After breaking free from the stress of academic life, would they come to Renren to study? They come to Renren to relax and have fun. If you ask a few Renren users why they use Renren, the answer will definitely not be for studying. Therefore, Renren has no market or value in related training. Moreover, the competition in the training market within schools is extremely fierce, almost like a world war. Renren wants to enter this field through the internet, but lacks fundamental appeal.

The campus life market isn't something Renren can just walk into. Renren has made efforts in the campus market; many school clubs and student unions have Renren public pages, but the results are far from ideal (you can check a few campus pages yourself). The culture of campus student groups determines that these accounts won't be very active. Freshmen might be crazy about clubs and student unions at the beginning, but after one semester, very few people will still participate in these activities.

Besides, the competition in the campus food delivery market is extraordinarily intense. Surrounding shops all do business with students, and now students prefer staying indoors. Therefore, most restaurants offer delivery services, and ordering a meal only requires a phone call. Would anyone bother to order on Renren? Moreover, freshmen often don’t have access to the internet or aren't allowed to bring computers, and the eating habits formed during their freshman year won't change just because Renren offers food delivery.

As for the movie ticket market, it's even more competitive. Schools regulate such commercial services very strictly. Commercial behaviors that gain the school's tacit approval usually involve some connections, and there won't be too many cinemas around the school. Relevant companies will negotiate with the school for cooperation, and the interest relationships involved are quite complex. It's hard for Renren to enter this market, given how many eyes are on it. Moreover, group-buying websites offer a wide selection of movie tickets, leaving no room for Renren. That's why Renren launched an independent group-buying website called Nuomi instead of adding a movie ticket purchasing service on Renren.

Campus recruitment is even less valuable. Both Renren and Tencent's Pengyou have tried it, but both have barely survived. First, graduates have many choices: studying abroad, preparing for postgraduate exams, becoming civil servants, etc., which diverts a large number of people. Among those left looking for jobs, some have family arrangements, some plan to find work back home, some rely on school recommendations, and educational institutions in the location of the school also hold multiple job fairs for recent graduates. There are also professional online recruitment websites like Zhaopin and 51job. What market does Renren have left?

School campus forums have also lost much of their popularity. Nowadays, few students bother to leave messages on their school forums or Tieba. Instead, they chat on social platforms like Weibo, WeChat, Renren, and QQ. They browse communities like Tianya, Mop, D-bar, and Douban. Female students spend most of their time watching TV shows, including American dramas, Korean dramas, Chinese dramas, and variety shows. Male students spend their time playing games like Dota, Warcraft, CF, and LOL, and shouts of "double kill" can often be heard in dormitories. When they're tired, they might go out to play basketball or exercise, and those with extensive social circles frequently go out for meals. Who still goes to the school forum or BBS?

For Renren, there is really no more significant value to be mined from the campus market. Not returning to campuses, Renren could still struggle to maintain itself, but if it publicly declares a return to campuses, then Renren would lose any imagination.

Just give up to Baidu

I’ve long wanted to suggest that Renren give up to Baidu. Especially when Baidu invested in Nuomi, I hoped Baidu would acquire Renren along with it, saving Renren from its dire situation. Renren's current market value is less than $1.5 billion, while Baidu spent $1.9 billion to acquire 91 Wireless, and with acquisition premiums, spending $2 billion to buy Renren wouldn't be difficult for Baidu. But Baidu needs to consider what to do after acquiring Renren.

Just as I was about to write this article, news spread that Baidu was going to acquire Renren as a whole. Alright, it seems Baidu has this intention. In fact, soon after Baidu invested in Nuomi, there were rumors that iQIYI was going to acquire 56.com, which was a sign that Renren and Baidu were getting closer. Originally, Nuomi and 56 were Renren's layouts for group-buying and video services based on its social network. Now that Baidu plans to acquire these two businesses, what's left for Renren? Only the Renren social platform and the slightly profitable Renren Games. But without Nuomi and 56, Renren has basically lost its imagination. Therefore, for Renren, since it has handed Nuomi over to Baidu and is willing to sell 56, it might as well completely join Baidu.

In the context of Sohu teaming up with Tencent, Sina with Alibaba, and NetEase with Telecom, Renren's strength is becoming increasingly weak. Without reliance on a major player, Renren lacks strong backing for future competition. For Baidu, the social domain has always been a weakness. Under the background of Tencent dominating the social market, Alibaba has actively engaged in competition, investing in Sina Weibo, Momo, and aggressively promoting Laiwang. In comparison, Baidu has done almost nothing. Acquiring Renren can quickly make up for its weakness in social networking, and the combination of social + maps + O2O holds more market potential.

It makes little sense for Renren to struggle on alone, especially in the mobile internet era, where Renren doesn't have a single successful product. Baidu can provide Renren with not only ample financial support and technical support, but also strong mobile application distribution channels. Not only that, Baidu has more traffic and advertising clients. With Baidu's support, Renren can more easily complete its transformation from the campus market to the entire social network market.

One party is willing to acquire, and the other is willing to sell; they might as well just get together. It seems Renren Games doesn't want to be sold? But what value does Renren Games have outside of Renren? Baidu doesn't care much about Renren Games' small issues; it can attract game developers with just a call, and there will be many lining up. What does Renren mean? Does it want to separate its social business and sell it to Baidu, then earn money from Baidu through games later? Doing this doesn't make much sense unless Renren Games intends to completely transform into a game developer.

We can't say for sure that the combination of Baidu and Renren will definitely explode, but at least it has the potential for explosive growth. Alone, Baidu is weak in the social market, and Renren has lost its market competitiveness. The combination of the two might achieve a result greater than the sum of their parts. However, we currently don't know if the news of Baidu's full acquisition of Renren is true or false, so we can only wait for further updates.
