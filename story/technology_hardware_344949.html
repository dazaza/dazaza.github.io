Detailed Introduction to the Annealing Process in Heat Treatment

Annealing is a commonly used process in heat treatment technology. The annealing process can be divided into incomplete annealing and complete annealing, with certain differences between the two. Below, Jiangsu Will Furnace Co., Ltd. will provide you with a detailed introduction to the differences between incomplete annealing and complete annealing, the application scope of incomplete annealing; the differences between isothermal annealing and ordinary annealing, the cooling method of isothermal annealing, as well as the methods of spheroidizing annealing.

1. Differences Between Incomplete Annealing and Complete Annealing

(1) Incomplete annealing is also called incomplete crystallization annealing. It involves heating steel to a certain temperature between Ac1 and Ac3 or Ac1 and Acm, holding it at that temperature, and then slowly cooling it down, causing the steel structure to undergo incomplete recrystallization.

(2) Incomplete annealing involves heating the workpiece to semi-austenitization for annealing, while complete annealing involves heating the workpiece to full austenitization for annealing.

(3) Incomplete annealing is generally used for hyper-eutectoid steel but can also be applied to hypo-eutectoid steel. In contrast, complete annealing is typically used only for hypo-eutectoid steel.

(4) Complete annealing usually results in lamellar pearlite, whereas incomplete annealing yields globular pearlite.

2. Application Scope of Incomplete Annealing

Incomplete annealing can be used for both hypo-eutectoid steel and hyper-eutectoid steel. Its characteristic is that after annealing, the cementite in pearlite becomes globular. This type of incomplete annealing is also referred to as spheroidizing annealing.

For hypo-eutectoid steel, incomplete annealing is often employed under the following circumstances:

(1) To improve machinability, this type of annealing is mainly applied to structural components after forging, especially those with higher carbon content. Due to the excessively fine pearlite formed after forging, the hardness is too high, making machining difficult.

(2) Spheroidizing annealing to improve cold deformation properties, which is used for hypo-eutectoid steels requiring cold deformation.

3. Differences Between Isothermal Annealing and Ordinary Annealing

There are certain distinctions in the process and cooling methods between isothermal annealing and ordinary annealing. Ordinary annealing generally involves the slow cooling of the workpiece within the furnace after the insulation process is completed. When the workpiece cools below 500°C, it can be removed from the furnace and air-cooled. In contrast, isothermal annealing involves rapidly cooling the workpiece after heating and holding it at a certain temperature below Ac1 for some time, allowing austenite to decompose isothermally into pearlite. Afterward, the workpiece is cooled quickly (air-cooled) to room temperature. The advantage of isothermal annealing lies in its shorter time and better quality.

4. Cooling Method of Isothermal Annealing

(1) The cooling speed from the annealing temperature to the isothermal temperature can be arbitrary. In production, it is common to use two furnaces for the process.

(2) The isothermal temperature is generally 10-30°C below Ar1, which corresponds to the pearlite transformation temperature.

(3) The cooling after transformation can be arbitrary.

5. Methods of Spheroidizing Annealing

(1) Ordinary Spheroidizing Annealing: Heat the steel piece slightly above Ac1 (usually Ac1 + 20-30°C), hold it at that temperature, and then cool it slowly at a rate of 20-50°C/h to below 500°C, followed by air-cooling outside the furnace.

(2) Isothermal Spheroidizing Annealing: After heating to the same temperature as ordinary spheroidizing annealing and holding, cool the piece inside the furnace to just below Ar1 for isothermal treatment. After isothermal treatment, remove the piece from the furnace and air-cool it.

(3) Periodic Spheroidizing Annealing: This involves alternating heating and cooling above and below Ac1, equivalent to multiple isothermal spheroidizing anneals.
