Windows 8 Gains Significant Market Share in August While XP Declines Sharply

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 14:39:47

According to the latest statistics released by market research firm Net Applications, in August this year, the usage rate of Windows 8 saw a sharp increase, while that of Windows XP plummeted. Additionally, Google Chrome's browser usage began to significantly decline.

As reported by the U.S.-based tech blog ZDNet, on the first day of every month, market research firm Net Applications releases the NetMarketShare statistics, providing a comprehensive overview of the usage rates for desktop and mobile operating systems and browsers globally.

The August 2013 statistics contained several surprising points:

- Since its launch in October last year, the usage rate of Windows 8 has been steadily increasing, albeit not dramatically. However, in August this year, its usage rate surged sharply, rising from 5.42% to 7.65%.

- The usage rate of Windows XP has been steadily declining, but not significantly. In August this year, it dropped sharply from 37.19% to 33.66%.

- Google Chrome's browser usage rate fell sharply in August, dropping from 17.76% to 16%. Meanwhile, the usage rates of other browsers all increased, with Internet Explorer (IE) reaching its highest point in two years at 57.6%.

Given that these operating systems and browsers have a global installed base of 1.5 billion PCs, any change in usage rates represents a shift of millions of users.

What’s going on? Did 40 million people suddenly realize within one month that Microsoft is about to stop supporting Windows XP, prompting them to hurriedly replace their outdated computers with new Windows 8 machines?

In fact, that's not the case.

The real explanation might seem somewhat mundane: Net Applications recently changed the way it measures usage rates. In an undated note titled "Important methodology change," the company explained:

"This month, we removed hidden pages from our usage statistics. Hidden pages are those that have been loaded but were never seen by users, so they should not be included in the usage data. An example of a hidden page is one that loads in the background when opening a browser and is never shown to the user."

This note was added to the company's website in July this year. However, it did not specify when the new statistical method would take effect. The new method aims to measure the actual behavior of PC users rather than the background activities executed by browser code. Previously, the company's statistics also excluded pre-rendered pages, which load in the background when Google users input search queries. In February 2012, pre-rendered pages accounted for 4.8% of Chrome pages. By June 2013, this figure had exceeded 12%.

Net Applications provided details on the proportion of hidden pages across different browsers on its FAQ page. Below are the statistics for June 2013:

IE: 0.70%

Firefox: 6.38%

Safari: 0.15%

Opera: 6.33%

Chrome: 5.60%

If you factor in the changes in the statistical method, then the decline in Firefox and Opera usage rates should have been greater than Chrome's. In reality, the usage rates of all browsers increased except for Chrome, whose usage rate declined. We will need to compare data over several consecutive months to determine whether this represents a trend or just a temporary fluctuation.

The change in the statistical method also sheds light on the sudden significant drop in Windows XP's usage rate. Since Microsoft no longer allows Windows XP users to use the latest version of IE, these users may switch to other browsers, such as Chrome or Firefox, both of which use hidden pages. Conversely, Windows 8 users are more likely to use standards-compliant IE10 (and IE11 in Windows 8.1).

In this month's report, there are a few other noteworthy points:

- Windows 8 (7.65%) now has more users globally than all versions of OS X combined (7.26%). Based on the global installed base of desktop and portable computers, there are now over 100 million Windows 8 computers in use.

- Despite being only in preview, Windows 8.1 achieved a sufficient usage rate (0.24%) in less than 60 days to secure a place on Net Applications' monthly rankings.

- More than two years after its launch, Google Chrome OS still hasn't crossed the 0.1% threshold required for inclusion in Net Applications' monthly rankings. Unfortunately, we cannot compare it directly with Microsoft's Windows RT, as the latter's statistics are already included under Windows 8.

All these numbers must be considered against the backdrop of a changing market. Globally, consumers and businesses are purchasing fewer traditional desktop and portable computers, instead spending more on tablets and other mobile devices. However, browsers and operating systems still have a global installed base of 1.5 billion PCs, which remains a force they can rely on.
