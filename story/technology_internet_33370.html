Insider Shock: China Unicom is Deploying a Large-scale Layoff Plan

by netbeta on 2007-11-05 18:53:21

Today, NetBeta received insider information that China Unicom will carry out a large-scale layoff, and in some provinces, the number of layoffs has reached 30%. However, an industry analyst expressed considerable interest in this rumor when interviewed. "It's not entirely impossible. Currently, China Mobile is putting great pressure on China Unicom."

Xie Linzhen began his career in telecommunications in 1963. After 28 years of teaching and research in communications, he joined the government. From 1994 to 2002, during the most significant growth period of mobile communications, he successively served as the Deputy Director of the Communications and Systems Equipment Department of the Ministry of Electronics Industry and the Deputy Director of the Electronic Information Products Department of the Ministry of Information Industry. He participated in promoting the establishment and listing of China Unicom, as well as the competitive and cooperative relationship between China Mobile and China Unicom. He also promoted the construction and development of the CDMA network and supported the growth and strengthening of a batch of domestic mobile phone enterprises including Bird, TCL, Konka, and CECT.

Xie Linzhen said that from today's perspective, after the separation of postal and telecommunications services, "telecommunications" is undergoing standardized corporate restructuring in accordance with the requirements of establishing a modern enterprise system and independently participating in competition as an equal competitive entity.

At that time, the mobile communications that were about to take off benefited the most. "In 1991, China had only 50,000 mobile users, which increased to 1.5 million in 1994. However, starting from 1998, the development of mobile users basically doubled every year, reaching over 100 million by September 2001. In contrast, the prediction made by China's telecommunications planning department in 1991 estimated only 450,000 mobile users by 2000."

Xie Linzhen emphasized that if the two mobile operators had not formed a healthy competitive relationship, no matter how large the market was, such remarkable results would not have been achieved.

After witnessing the rapid development of mobile operators and mobile phone manufacturers, Xie Linzhen took over the position of Vice President of the China Mobile Communication Federation at the age of 63. The reason was simple. Xie Linzhen said that it was the field to which he had devoted his entire life, and it was also the place where his "mobile communication" emotions could be fully released.

The person believed that the current situation of Unicom is that GSM network users are severely leaking, and the development of the CDMA network is slow. Facing competition, they have no choice but to make the decision to lay off employees to cut costs. "It is estimated that it will mainly involve regular employees, then the leaders will be demoted in sequence. It won't be a nationwide layoff; provinces with lower performance contributions may be hit first."

However, after entering 2002, Unicom's pace clearly slowed down.

"Since 2001, Unicom has carried out a series of arduous and complex construction works. In just seven months, it built a CDMA network with the largest capacity in the world, which amazed the world but also to a great extent restrained Unicom's development."

Xie Linzhen recalled the development situation of Unicom at that time: before Unicom decided to launch the CDMA network, its GSM network had already developed very well, owning approximately 50 million users in 2001, almost half of the total number of mobile phone users. However, after building the C network, Unicom had to focus on the network coverage and user development of the C network.

Nevertheless, it takes time to improve network coverage, and the terminals of the C network are not as diverse as those of the G network. There is a significant difference in the choices of network and terminals that users consider. More critically, Unicom's full investment in the C network further dispersed the development of the G network.

"The government's decision for Unicom to launch CDMA instead greatly weakened its competitiveness, which was not what the supervisory authority hoped to see." Xie Linzhen stated, "CDMA was introduced because it is more advanced than GSM technology, and the Ministry of Information Industry also hoped to give the emerging operator Unicom a better opportunity for development."

"But the facts prove that the development of mobile communications itself cannot be controlled solely by technology, but must comprehensively consider the market, user awareness, and the global mobile communication environment. It is a systematic project with its own development laws."

Xie Linzhen said that Unicom's launch of CDMA sounded the alarm for the telecommunications industry, indicating that the development of a new standard inevitably requires paying a huge cost, or even the sacrifice of some companies.

In addition, the insider elaborated on the relationship between this rumor and the reorganization of operators. "If Unicom carries out layoffs, we do not think there is a direct relationship between layoffs and the reorganization of operators." Recently, relevant leaders of the Ministry of Information Industry indicated that China still does not have a 3G timeline, so it seems that the matter of operator reorganization will be delayed again and again.

Regarding this, Ai Media reporters immediately interviewed relevant personnel from the Comprehensive Department of the Unicom Group, who firmly denied it: "I haven't heard of it, it's not true." A staff member from Guangdong Jinglian Tong stated that they do not know the situation in other provinces, but at least in Guangdong, there are no signs of layoffs yet, and all work is proceeding methodically. Judging from the current situation, the rumors of a large-scale layoff by Unicom seem somewhat baseless, but considering the many unstable factors in China's telecommunications market, it is not completely groundless either.
