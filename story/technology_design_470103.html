After her husband was sentenced for selling fake cigarettes, the wife continued the business, saying that she would stop after making several billion yuan.

by zzf000zxye on 2011-08-03 14:18:06

Our reporter had a dialogue yesterday with Cai Chunrui, the woman "cigarette overlord" who turned herself in.

Two years ago, her husband Fang Haibin, the "cigarette overlord," was sentenced to 15 years in prison and fined 2.7 million yuan for selling counterfeit cigarettes. This was the longest sentence and highest fine in Jiangsu's history of anti-counterfeiting cases. However, despite this heavy blow, Cai Chunrui did not stop but instead took over her husband's "unfinished business" and continued to sell large quantities of fake cigarettes in the Huai'an area. At the end of last year, under continuous pressure from the Huai'an tobacco authorities and police, Cai Chunrui turned herself in. Behind this series of complex experiences, how did a weak rural woman from the Minnan mountains become a female "cigarette overlord"? The reporter learned that Cai Chunrui was sentenced by the Huaiyin District Court before National Day, so yesterday, the following dialogue took place at her detention location:

Reporter: Did you and your husband have a good relationship? Did you sell counterfeit cigarettes before?

Cai: Like most families in Minnan, my family is one where the man works outside and the woman manages the household. I take care of all the big and small chores at home. Sometimes when his business was too busy, I would help him out and occasionally manage the accounts.

Reporter: Was your husband the biggest producer of counterfeit cigarettes in Yunxiao? Did he have any "goals"?

Cai: In the past, there were people who did much larger scales than him, but later they went "legitimate." Before he got into trouble, he was already the biggest one locally. He originally planned to make a few billion yuan and then quit, finding a place to invest in legal businesses.

Reporter: After your husband was sentenced to a heavy prison term, why did you continue to manufacture and sell counterfeit cigarettes?

Cai: Life must go on. My mother-in-law is over seventy and has cancer, my son is only 11 years old, and there are no other male laborers in the family. If we didn't sell counterfeit cigarettes, we wouldn't have any source of income. Anyway, the workers, machines, and network were all ready, so it was natural to continue.

Reporter: Haven't you learned from your husband's mistakes?

Cai: The methods for remittance and transportation have been changed, becoming more complicated and hidden.

Reporter: That's learning lessons the wrong way, which is incorrect. Were you afraid of government crackdowns?

Cai: The underground factory he left behind employed over 200 people. If no one led them, they would disperse. Many people in our area make a living from this and don't think it's a shameful thing, so at first, we weren't very afraid as long as we were careful. But in recent years, the government's anti-counterfeiting efforts have become increasingly severe, and the tobacco inspectors and police from the affected areas often come to arrest people, so I started to feel a bit scared.

Reporter: Is that why you turned yourself in?

Cai: That's one reason. Another is that I didn't want to keep hiding anymore. As my child grows up, I hope to live a peaceful life. Your Huai'an inspectors and police also kept giving me "policy persuasion," so I decided to cooperate.

Communicator: Rao Bo
