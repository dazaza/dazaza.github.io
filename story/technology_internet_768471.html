Google Uses Smartphone Location Data to Track Whether Users Go Shopping

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 18:36:30

On November 10, tech companies of all sizes have been trying to use smartphones to connect consumers' online activities with their real-world actions. Google has told advertisers that it can already achieve this by tracking the geographical location of consumers' smartphones.

According to executives at advertising agencies, Google is testing a service that uses smartphone location data to understand when consumers visit stores and considers this factor when providing mobile search services to consumers, thus enhancing the relevance of search ads.

Google declined to comment on this matter.

For example, if a consumer searches for the keyword "screwdriver" using Google's mobile search service, nearby hardware tool stores may purchase ads targeted at this user. By combining the user's location data with its store database, Google can determine whether the user shopped at the store advertising. The foundation of this service by Google is that users have enabled location services on their smartphones.

Dan Auerbach, an employee of privacy protection organization Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF), said that when consumers choose to enable "location services," they may not realize that they will be tracked continuously, "This type of application does not provide sufficient disclosure to consumers. I believe there is a gap between consumer expectations and the actual 'operations' of the application."

It is easiest for Google to track the geographical location of Android users because Android has built-in location tracking features. Once users enable location services, Google collects the user's geographical location data continuously whenever possible. According to market research company eMarketer, Android is the largest mobile operating system in the U.S., with a market share of 45.9%. 20.3% of the U.S. population uses Android smartphones.

Google can continuously track the location of iPhone users through its Maps app. eMarketer states that iOS holds a 38.3% market share in the U.S., making it the second-largest mobile operating system after Android. Nearly 17% of the U.S. population uses iPhones. Even if an iPhone user is not actively using an app, the app may continue to run in the background. Despite consumers possibly being unaware, these apps keep running. Google's mobile search service continues to collect geographical location information even when running in the background. This information can be used to determine whether a consumer visited a store and whether the shopping was related to searches conducted within the app.

For years, Google has been collecting consumers' geographical location data. Currently, Google uses geographical location data to determine whether consumers shop at a particular store and promotes the effectiveness of its mobile advertisements to advertisers. (Bamboo)
