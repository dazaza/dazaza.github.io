Zhengzhou workers outside the production line

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 16:46:43

　　Human resources are the biggest part of a company’s cost. In order to cut down on labor costs, companies either lay off employees or move their operations to places where wages are lower. Foxconn has done just that, moving from Shenzhen to Zhengzhou, which is gradually becoming a new manufacturing hub. Around the clock, factories in the area churn out international products like the iPhone. Here, young Chinese workers refuse to be treated as worker ants.

　　Near the factory, there is a bar where all sorts of people come at night to dance to loud disco music and let loose. Lian Yulong (音), a 19-year-old inspector who works on iPhone motherboards at Foxconn, is a regular visitor to the bar. "Dancing helps me release my anger and stress. Whenever I'm here, I forget everything else." He uses dancing to forget the monotonous and dull reality of his day-to-day life.

　　An increasing number of recreational spaces attract workers back to the assembly lines. These workers manufacture products that others desire but endure hardships themselves. Because opportunities are rare, their leisure time is precious, and they endure the present for a better future. Every worker looks forward to the weekend, even if some have simple plans. For example, Bai Si Hai (音), a 24-year-old worker, plans to indulge in online gaming with friends and then call his girlfriend who lives far away.

　　Now, the foremen in the factories are beginning to see the benefits of giving breaks. In recent years, "worker suicides" have become an ongoing shadow over companies like Foxconn and Samsung. After several reports of "sweatshops," under external pressure, Foxconn improved worker benefits and promised not to make them work overtime. However, after another suicide incident at the Zhengzhou plant, Foxconn initiated a "silent mode," prohibiting workers from discussing non-work related topics during shifts. Although Foxconn claims to have canceled this rule amid external doubts, workers say that nothing has changed.

　　The environment in domestic factories cannot compare to those abroad. Workers overseas enjoy high allowances and perks such as haircuts, rock climbing walls, table football, etc., while offices are luxurious. Domestic workers wear uniform clothing and listen to routine scolding and shouting from their supervisors. As for dormitories, a room houses eight hard metal bed frames and a combined shower and toilet room, with nothing more.

　　Perhaps because of this, there is a large market in front of the factory gates. When the sun sets, Mandarin pop music drifts out from hair salons, street vendors sell DVDs, sliced watermelons, and roses glisten under silver lights. A flatbed truck loaded with toy bears in different colors catches the attention of young girls. Some boyfriends, at the behest of their girlfriends, immediately take out 10 yuan to buy one. At this market, there are vans adorned with artistic tattoos and large video games. Young workers drink light beer and smoke incessantly.

　　Sometimes, elaborately dressed singers perform on nearby stages. As for the youth, they find sufficient entertainment in liquor. Luo Haojie (音) left the factory in May, quitting his job making iPhone 5 components. He said, "Our factory supervisor is malicious." The food in the factory cafeteria is unpalatable, he told a circle of drinking friends.

　　Ultimately, he will look for another job, but for now, he enjoys his youth, drinking with friends and dating girls. Luo said, "I stayed because of my buddies. Without them, I'd be miserable."

　　Summer is the off-season for Chinese factories, and many workers can enjoy weekends off, sometimes even two days. The variety of entertainment near the factory satisfies their needs. At night, mobile food stalls pop up with names resembling KFC. Acrobats perform, and someone blows fireballs. Workers say that if you came a few months earlier, the acrobatics troupe would have real lions and tigers.

　　On Saturdays, Zhou Pengzheng (音), another iPhone 5 motherboard tester, wearing roller skates worth thousands of yuan, carefully avoids plants and begins spinning. These skates cost one-third of his monthly salary. He says, "It feels like I'm flying." Before the crowd grows, he quickly takes another lap.

　　Quad skates and inline skates are gradually forming a cultural atmosphere among those striving at Foxconn. Several teams have formed, and on weekends, they skate through the city. Fang Tim Ma (音), 17, joined a hundred-person skating team called "Shadows" soon after starting work at Foxconn. However, due to her age, she couldn't work overtime and left the factory. She says, "I used to come to the skating rink twice a week, now I come every night."

　　At 11 PM, the performers have left, and the hourly-rate hotels start getting busy. "We work every day, so why not go on dates?"
