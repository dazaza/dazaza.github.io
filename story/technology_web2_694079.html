Section 1: "The Bucket", "The Spoon", and "Adding Water"

Each of us has an invisible bucket. Depending on what others say and do to us, our buckets are constantly being filled or emptied. When our buckets are full, we are elated. When our buckets are empty, we feel dejected.

Each of us also has an invisible spoon. When we use it to fill other people's buckets—by increasing their positive emotions through our words and actions—we also fill our own buckets. However, when we use this spoon to take water from others' buckets—by reducing their positive emotions through our words and actions—we also empty our own buckets.

Like a cup overflowing with water, a bucket full of water makes us energetic and confident. Every drop of water in the bucket makes us stronger and more optimistic.

But an empty bucket makes us listless, exhausted, and stagnant. That’s why every time someone takes water from our bucket, we are hurt.

Therefore, at every moment, we face a choice: we can either fill each other's buckets or take water from them. This is a crucial choice—it profoundly affects our relationships, work efficiency, health, and happiness.

Section 3: The Authors’ Words

In the early 1950s, my grandfather Don Clifton taught psychology at the University of Nebraska. During his teaching, he discovered an important issue: the focus of psychology at that time was solely on people's problems.

He thought, wouldn't it be more meaningful to study people's strengths?

Over the past 50 years, Don and his colleagues have interviewed millions of people—not about their deficiencies, but about their strengths.

Don found early in his research that everyone's life experience depends on their interactions with others. Whether having deep conversations with good friends or ordering food in a restaurant, each interaction impacts our lives. The results of these interactions are almost always either positive or negative, rarely neutral. Although we may overlook these interactions, they accumulate over time and profoundly influence our lives.

By the 1990s, a new discipline emerged: Positive Psychology, which focuses on people's strengths. Nowadays, many top scientists worldwide are studying the effects of positive emotions.

In 2002, the American Psychological Association fully recognized Don's innovative research, hailing him as the "Father of Strengths Psychology" and the "Grandfather of Success Psychology." In the same year, Don was diagnosed with advanced cancer that had spread throughout his body. As expected by family and friends, knowing his days were numbered, he spent the last few months of his life doing what he did best: helping others identify and leverage their strengths.

Despite having already published several specialized books, including the bestseller "Now, Discover Your Strengths," Don still invited me to co-author one final book with him, one centered on the theory he developed in the 1960s. For decades, due to the widespread recognition of this theory, many people urged Don to write this book. Over the past 40 years, more than 5,000 organizations and over a million people have applied this theory. Moreover, people have recommended it to their friends, colleagues, and families.

Don's theory uses a simple metaphor of a "spoon" and a "bucket" to clearly interpret his lifetime of research findings. In the final months of Don's life, he and I worked day and night to compile the most important discoveries from his half-century of research. Even though Don was undergoing chemotherapy and radiation therapy, as long as he could hold out, we were racing against time to write.

In his study, we would spend hours carefully organizing impactful research, data, and stories. As Don's condition gradually worsened, I had to read materials to him and then record his feedback. He meticulously reviewed every word of the manuscript to ensure that each story and its implications resonated with you.

For me, collaborating with Don on this book was an honor. He was my mentor, role model, and friend. Our relationship was exceptionally close, and we shared unforgettable good times. His foresight inspired and motivated me. Don knew that this theory had deeply influenced my entire life. As described in Chapter Four, applying Don's "spoon and bucket" theory helped me overcome cancer myself.

Looking back, I believe writing this book injected vitality into Don, helping him fight cancer one last time. He devoted his entire life to helping individuals, one by one, to make the world a better place. He knew writing this book would be highly beneficial. A few weeks after we completed the book, he passed away in September 2003.

Throughout Don's 79-year career, through his writings, lectures, and the multinational company he founded, he impacted tens of millions of people. Don viewed helping individuals and organizations identify and leverage their strengths as his lifelong mission, becoming a master teacher.

We hope that as you read this book, you will discover how important it is to fill your bucket for your entire life.

Tom Rath

Section 4: A Birthday Gift

As you read this book, you might wonder: "Is a person's mindset—positive or negative—genetically determined and therefore difficult to change?" The short answer is yes. We've all met people who seem (and probably are) born pessimists. And you’ve undoubtedly encountered people who are naturally very positive.

Scientific opinion has been divided on this question. Some studies suggest that positivity or negativity is primarily innate, while others claim it is shaped by external influences. Currently, the most popular theory holds that both nature and nurture play important—and possibly equal—roles.

Renowned psychologist Ed Diener points out that we have a baseline capacity for experiencing joy, much like our weight. Some people are naturally thin no matter what they eat. Similarly, some people are naturally happier than others. However, depending on the events in our lives, our positive emotions can fluctuate significantly. If most of us paid as much attention to increasing positive emotions as we do to our diets, decreasing negative ones, it would likely benefit us.

Regardless of one's natural disposition, consistently filling the bucket increases positive emotions. To illustrate its long-term effects, I'd like to share a personal story.

A Birthday Gift

Not long after Tom Rath and I began collaborating on the book, it was approaching my birthday. Given that we were co-writing a book about positive emotions, I decided to write him a letter describing how important filling the bucket had been in my life. I thought such a birthday gift would be more meaningful than a typical present. I also felt it was a good time to express my gratitude, given that he was battling cancer.

I remember him saying when I was young that we should gather to praise a person’s achievements while they're still alive. He came to this conclusion because, at many funerals he attended, he sadly realized that many people waited until their loved ones had passed away before generously filling their buckets. Don questioned, "Why not do it while they're alive?"

Thus, on his 79th birthday, I shared my own story with him. He was moved to tears when he read my letter. A few days later, Don asked if I would include this story in the book. He felt it was a great example of continuously filling the bucket. I agreed.

Below is the story I told Don on his birthday. It describes how continuously filling the bucket influenced my life trajectory during my youth.

Section 5: Searching for Signs of Talent

In our large family, I was the first child of the third generation, benefiting from a unique parenting method that went against the conventional wisdom of the time. From the day I was born, every family member was determined to help me focus on my strengths. To achieve this, they provided continuous support and encouragement.

When I was four, my mother and grandmother discovered my love for reading and spent several hours a day teaching me to read. Their instruction, nurturing, and attention benefited me greatly.

Whenever relatives visited, they would ask me what books I was reading or what activities I enjoyed. Now I understand that they were searching for clues about my natural interests and talents. Every time the family noticed a strong interest in a particular area, they encouraged me to explore it further. They never hesitated to give praise. Whenever they saw me achieve even small accomplishments, they promptly praised me.

At eight or nine, those around me who were filling my bucket noticed an entrepreneurial spirit and a desire to lead among my peers. So, at ten, my grandfather Don suggested I start my own company. I liked the idea and decided to open a snack shop. As usual, the entire family enthusiastically brainstormed ideas and helped me launch my new venture.

A few months later, my "kid company" was thriving. As our business expanded, we stopped buying from local wholesalers and instead partnered with a large candy distributor who agreed to give us wholesale prices and deliver directly. Our operations eventually extended beyond snacks to include clothing and small goods. By the time I was twelve, the company employed over twenty of my classmates, generating thousands of dollars in profit that we shared. Several years after the company's founding, it made the front page of the local newspaper and received national media coverage.

All this care and genuine effort to fill my bucket had a profound impact on my life. My bucket was always full, allowing me to focus on filling others' buckets. At the end of each month, I gave awards and bonuses based on each person's sales. As my bucket was filled, my friends, family, and every young colleague were inspired, bringing me immense joy.

Throughout my schooling, my family continued to give me positive encouragement. My parents asked me daily about my favorite classes and extracurricular activities. Though I didn't excel in music or art classes, they never took water from my bucket but instead encouraged me to spend more time on subjects I enjoyed.

They noticed that I was analytical and interested in data and current events, so they suggested I spend more time studying math and social sciences. Despite already earning A’s in these subjects, my family believed I would gain more from my education by focusing on areas I enjoyed.

Unlike the teachers and parents of my friends, my parents didn’t expect me to be well-rounded. Knowing I lacked a sense of rhythm, they understood forcing me to take music lessons would be futile—I’d only reach mediocrity. There's a saying in our hometown: "Don't teach pigs to sing. You waste your time and annoy the pig." As a young student, I found this liberating. No one pressured me to excel in everything. Instead, I could focus on excelling in areas where I had natural talent.

Section 6: A Safe and Warm Home

I remember visiting a friend's house in elementary school, where the atmosphere was starkly different from my own home. After coming home from school, we would excitedly walk in, but his mom would always greet us with these questions:

"Did I tell you to bring friends home?"

"Did you cause trouble at school again?"

"Don't fail your exams!"

Perhaps she sometimes had good reason to say these things, but what struck me was how her first words were always negative. Another classmate would find a handwritten note on his bed every day saying things like, "You need to improve your attitude." This was not a warm environment, whereas at my house, I was accustomed to hearing:

"How was school today?"

"What do you want to do this afternoon?"

"Do you want me to see your homework?"

"Did you play basketball with your classmates in gym class today?" (Our favorite sport)

At first, I thought these friends' homes must be abnormal. But as time went on, I realized this was quite common. Looking back, I think I now understand why my childhood friends often came to my house. At my home, positive support and encouragement continually filled our buckets. My house was like a kind of "base," fueling us with positive emotions to help us deal with the real world filled with negative emotions.

Facing a Serious Challenge

My life continued positively and optimistically—until I was 16. Around that time, my vision in my left eye suddenly deteriorated, and I encountered the first major obstacle of my life.

Doctors found several tumors in my eye and performed multiple major surgeries on me. A year later, I completely lost my vision in my left eye—and permanently. Moreover, my condition indicated that I had a "genetic abnormality" that would periodically cause tumors to appear throughout my body. After a DNA test, doctors confirmed that I had a rare disease: von Hippel-Lindau disease. As a result, tumors could grow unpredictably in my pancreas, kidneys, eardrums, adrenal glands, brain, and spine.

When I heard this diagnosis, I was both shocked and nervous. However, on some level, what surprised me was that I wasn’t depressed. From that day forward, my family helped me focus on what I could do to overcome the disease rather than succumbing to its uncontrollable negative aspects. Despite my fear, I never fell into depression. At a critical moment, the genuine care and encouragement from my loved ones had a magical effect on me.

Within a week of my diagnosis, I started learning about how to control and manage my condition. When friends asked about my blindness in my left eye, I would immediately respond that my right eye had 20/10 vision, far surpassing the average person’s.

Looking back, the key was not viewing my diagnosis as some curse or death sentence. Instead, I saw it as an opportunity to act and maintain optimal health.

By continuously learning about this rare disease, I discovered that most potential tumors in my body could be controlled through early detection and treatment. I decided to measure my progress through regular check-ups.

Meanwhile, my life continued as usual. Whether socially, athletically, or academically, my daily routine remained unchanged. In the following years, I only thought about my condition during my 6-12 month check-ups. Needless to say, I would be anxious waiting for each check-up result. However, I managed to keep these feelings within controllable limits. In many ways, my confidence and spirit were stronger than ever.

My secret was to face these challenges head-on. I wouldn’t say my attitude was entirely conscious, but I never let these issues overwhelm me. Ten years later, my close friends admitted how scared and worried they were at the time. However, they also recalled how my positive attitude baffled them. While they knew I wasn’t panicked, they couldn’t believe I wasn’t deeply concerned about my condition. In hindsight, it seemed I possessed some incomprehensible mental immunity that others couldn’t understand.

But there was nothing miraculous or mysterious about it. The drops of water added to my bucket daily by friends and family stored enough water to help me get through tough times.

Section 7: An Abundance of Positive Emotions

During my middle school years, my family continued to point out the strengths they observed, helping me set priorities and establish ideals. By junior high, I had decided to study psychology in college. I enjoyed researching and understanding the motivations behind human behavior. Thus, I began looking up and applying to leading psychology programs nationwide. Needless to say, my family was extraordinarily supportive of my decision to pursue higher education. They helped me apply and even accompanied me on visits to several institutions.

The family's positive support helped me quickly adapt to the new university environment. Even though they were thousands of miles away, they still regularly contributed to filling my bucket. Everything progressed smoothly during my first three years of university.

Unfortunately, new challenges awaited me.

In my senior year, a check-up revealed a tumor on my adrenal gland. Five years later, doctors discovered malignant tumors on my kidneys. At the time of writing this book, scans showed new tumors on my pancreas, adrenal glands, and spine.

Each time the results came in, I experienced fear and frustration, but finding these tumors before they spread to other organs brought relief. My vigilance had paid off. Each lesion could be surgically controlled. Thus, I read extensively on various surgical options and their risks to fully understand them. All my energy was focused on what I could do, not on what had already happened or was beyond my control.

To this day, I have never paused along my journey to ask, "Why is all this happening to me?" Honestly, while I have felt frustrated, I have never resented fate, and these are two very different things.

From a personal perspective, there is no reason to throw my hands up and sit idly, as it would serve no purpose. Furthermore, sinking into despair would only harm my emotional and physical health.

Although various cancers threaten me daily, I have no choice but to focus on what I can do next and stay ahead of the disease. Honestly, maintaining this daily mindset is not difficult.

Why? Simply put: for nearly thirty years, my family and friends have been adding water to my bucket at every moment.

Section 8: We All Need Full Buckets

My story is undoubtedly an extreme example of filling the bucket. If I had read it for the first time, I might have thought it exaggerated. But I assure you, every word is true. Given that I am still fighting the disease, this intense filling of the bucket is literally saving my life.

We all encounter serious challenges on our life journeys. During these times, we often feel unlucky or that the world is unfair. However, we don't have to be constrained by difficulties. Our reactions to adversity and our psychological states are far more important. Enhancing our inherent strengths can help us avoid being overwhelmed by negative emotions. Understanding our strengths in the face of adversity not only helps us survive but also helps us grow.

Section 9: Five Strategies to Increase Positive Emotions (Part 1)

Praising Strengths

Every interaction we have with others is an opportunity to praise their strengths and fill their buckets.

A friend of ours recently discovered the power of focusing on strengths. She was dissatisfied with her marriage and relentlessly pushed her husband to change his behavior. But he avoided her whenever she approached, and during her complaints, he fiercely defended himself. This led her to criticize even more, hoping to draw his attention. Contrary to her expectations, the situation only worsened.

She realized that constantly telling her husband how disappointed she was in him was ineffective. So, she tried a different approach: she started focusing on the things he did well and the qualities she admired in him. Although she lacked confidence in this method, she decided to give it a try. Guess what happened? A few days later, her husband came home in a better mood and was more willing to engage with her. As a result, her positive attitude toward him filled his bucket, and in turn, his care for her began to fill hers.

What she least expected was that by focusing on his strengths rather than dwelling on his weaknesses, her own mood improved significantly. This made her more positive in her interactions with others. Within weeks, the couple began passing on their newly gained vitality to their friends and colleagues.

We must not underestimate the long-term effects of filling others' buckets. Barbara Fredrickson says that positive emotions create "chains of interpersonal connections," producing lasting effects that we may not even see.

Each time you fill a bucket, it creates a chain reaction.

Imagine this: if you fill two buckets a day, and those two buckets go on to fill two new buckets, within ten days, over a thousand buckets will have been filled. And if each of these people fills five buckets instead of two each day, in just ten days, 19 million buckets will have been filled!

So, let's continue this chain: if someone fills your bucket, welcome it—don't ignore it or belittle their kindness. By saying "thank you," you show the other person that you value their recognition and praise. Simultaneously, you become more willing to share your increased positive emotions with others.

Section 10: Five Strategies to Increase Positive Emotions (Part 2)

Reversing the Golden Rule

The golden rule—"Treat others as you want to be treated"—does not apply to filling buckets. Instead, we suggest modifying it to "Treat others as they want to be treated." We devoted an entire chapter to explaining this point, but we want to reiterate: the key to making your bucket-filling actions meaningful and impactful lies in tailoring them to the individual. Therefore, when you fill someone else's bucket, be sure to reverse—or at least redefine—the golden rule.

We illustrated this point with the story of customer service representative Matt receiving a picture of his daughter, demonstrating that everyone is unique, and it is precisely these unique aspects that determine what will fill their buckets—and vice versa. The same action means different things to different people. Some people prefer material rewards or gifts. Others prefer verbal praise and recognition. Some enjoy public acknowledgment. Others prefer private praise or congratulations from people they admire and respect.

Another important aspect of personalization is that recognizing what others value will help them gain self-awareness and shape their future achievements. For this reason, bucket-filling must be tailored to the individual.

If you don’t know where to start, ask a few simple questions. We’ve provided some questions for you to consider. Try them out with your friends. If you’re a manager, ask your subordinates and see if it works—then take action accordingly. There’s a pocket-sized questionnaire in the back of this book. If you want to learn more, visit our website.

Bucket-Filling Interviews

1. What name do you prefer people call you?

2. What are your "hot buttons"—what are your favorite topics and interests to talk about?

3. What most increases your positive emotions or "fills your bucket"?

4. Whose recognition and praise do you value the most?

5. What type of recognition and praise do you prefer? Do you prefer public, private, written, verbal, or another form of praise?

6. What type of praise motivates you the most? Do you prefer gifts, certificates, titles from winning competitions, meaningful letters or emails, or something else?

7. What was the most memorable praise you’ve ever received?

To enhance the effectiveness, praise must not only be personalized but also specific. Writing down the praise or sending it via email is a good method. Written praise is especially cherished because it lasts—recipients can revisit it repeatedly.
