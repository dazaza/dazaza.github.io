Why is "I" capitalized in English?

by erczrgut on 2012-03-04 14:15:47

Have you ever wondered why the singular first-person pronoun "I" is always capitalized, regardless of its position in a sentence when preparing for the College English Test Band 4 [http://www.52jiwai.com]? Why aren't other pronouns like "you," "we," "they," "he," and "she" capitalized? Professor Ku Zhengkun from the Department of English at Peking University provided an interpretation on this topic during his lecture on CCTV's "100 Schools Forum" series ["When Tea Meets Coffee"] under the theme "Comparison of Chinese and Western Languages, Characters, and Cultures":

"Within our human culture, including both Chinese and Western cultures, sometimes what is easily expressed in spoken language can be clarified with just one word. For example, in English, regarding pronouns, 'you,' 'we,' 'they,' 'he,' 'she' are all written in lowercase, but only one word is capitalized—'I.' Why is 'I' capitalized? Conversely, in China, it’s quite the opposite. The same term for 'me' is expressed differently. How do the Chinese refer to themselves? They use terms like 'this humble one' or 'your servant,' even emperors refer to themselves as 'this solitary person' or 'this unworthy one,' all demonstrating respect for others and humility. This contrasts sharply with the concept of 'I' in Western culture. Just through the use of one pronoun, we can see that these two cultures have diverged in many aspects, often being polar opposites."

Professor Ku Zhengkun is a linguist I deeply respect, but I must respectfully disagree with him on this specific issue. The capitalization of the singular first-person pronoun "I" has a linguistic consensus. "The Barnhart Dictionary of Etymology" (W.H.W. Wilson, 1988) explains:

"The pronoun 'I' developed from the unstressed form of Old English (circa 725) 'ic,' the singular pronoun of the first person (nominative case). Modern and Middle English 'I' evolved from the earlier 'i' in stressed positions. 'I' began to be written with a capital letter, making it a distinct word and avoiding misreading handwritten manuscripts. In the northern and midland dialects of England, the capitalized form 'I' appeared around 1250." [http://www.52jiwai.com]

In other words, in Old English, the word for "I" was originally represented by "ic," which gradually evolved into a single letter "i." Once it became a single letter, a problem arose: the lowercase "i" was easily confused with preceding or following words in handwritten texts. Therefore, starting around 1250, the capitalized "I" began to be used in the northern and midland dialects of England. "Morris Dictionary of Word and Phrase Origins" also points out that capitalizing "I" has nothing to do with ego but is simply to avoid confusion and error.

Moreover, some linguists argue that "I" as a personal pronoun differs from "you," "they," "he," and "she" because it represents the writer's self-reference. For instance, if the author's name is John Wilson, writing his own name would naturally be capitalized; therefore, when "I" represents John Wilson, it should also be capitalized.

Thus, the capitalization of the singular first-person pronoun "I" in English does not stem from a Western mindset of individualism or egocentrism. Regarding logic, there are also inaccuracies in Professor Ku's aforementioned statement. The capitalization of "I" in English is merely a grammatical rule of the English language and does not imply that the singular first-person pronouns in all European languages must be capitalized. For example, in French, "je" (I) is not capitalized within a sentence. How did Professor Ku generalize from a simple English word to all Western languages and conclude that Western culture has "diverged" from Chinese culture?

Of course, my questioning of the cultural implications of capitalizing "I" merely suggests that this example may not be the most appropriate. It does not deny that differences between English and Chinese vocabulary indeed reflect distinctions between Eastern and Western cultures. Professor Roger T. Ames, a sinologist and philosopher at the University of Hawaii, offered another example:

For instance, saying in English, "[Everybody please stand up]," we use terms like "everybody" (each body) or "everyone" (each person), whereas in Chinese, we say "[Everyone please stand up]." The Chinese term "everyone" ("大家") differs in meaning from the English "everybody."

In Professor Ames' view, this example illustrates the differences between Chinese and Western cultures. Chinese culture emphasizes interpersonal relationships, highlighting mutual dependence and interconnectedness among humans, and understanding an individual requires examining different social relationships.
