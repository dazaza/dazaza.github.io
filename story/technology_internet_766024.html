Nokia crosses over into the automotive industry.

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 14:10:49

Not only Google, Apple and Microsoft, but Nokia is also entering the automotive industry.

In fact, Nokia's HERE Maps have already gained a good reputation. Currently, 80% of global in-car navigation systems use HERE’s map data services. However, as the automotive industry gets closer to technology, Nokia wants to do more. It recently announced its plans: to launch a driving solution based on mobile internet, including embedded in-car navigation systems and infotainment platforms.

Michael Halbherr, Executive Vice President of Nokia HERE, recently discussed Nokia's in-car plan, with the ultimate goal even pointing towards autonomous driving and integrating cars into the smart city network of the future. HERE Auto will be Nokia's first step in this area.

Nokia obviously also wants to enter the rapidly developing market for internet-based in-car services. Its breakthrough will lie in the integration of cloud and core smartphone applications. Nokia's entire solution includes three parts: the embedded in-car navigation service HERE Auto, the cloud data service HERE Auto Cloud, and the complementary mobile application HERE Auto Companion.

According to Floris Van-De-Klashorst, Vice President of HERE Automotive Connectivity, HERE Auto aims to replicate the experience from smartphones and tablets onto the car control panel. Therefore, Nokia's HERE maps and navigation system will be at the core of HERE Auto. Just like the application on Windows Phone, it includes functions such as 3D maps, real-time traffic information, points of interest, street view images, and local business service searches.

However, unlike other embedded navigation systems, HERE Auto is not closed. It can synchronize with smartphones or web services through HERE Auto Cloud. All your favorite destinations, commonly used routes, contacts, and preferences are stored in the cloud and can be synchronized across different devices. If you mark a route on your phone or PC, as long as the car is connected to the network, the same route will appear in your car. Even if the car cannot connect to the internet, the most frequently used routes and settings can still be stored locally.

Besides information synchronization, Auto Cloud can also provide multiple real-time dynamic services, including road conditions such as road closures and restricted areas, allowing car owners to access various location-based services such as dining, parking, charging, and refueling.

Nokia also wants to build an infotainment application platform on top of HERE Auto. It acts like a virtual application layer that can be mounted on the embedded operating systems of car manufacturers. Although Nokia and Microsoft have collaborated extensively in the smartphone field, in the automotive sector, apart from Microsoft's Windows Embedded system, Nokia tends to choose more partners, such as BlackBerry's QNX system.

Moreover, Nokia has launched an SDK for developers and opened a series of API interfaces. Application developers can integrate their applications with HERE Auto. These applications can be standalone entertainment applications, such as internet radio services, or they can be combined with HERE's core map functions. For example, Foursquare could design a version that displays your friends' check-in locations on the HERE map, and you can even set a route to drive to the latest check-in place of a friend.

Combining this point, Nokia has launched a mobile application called HERE Auto Companion, which supports iOS, Android, Windows Phone, and web versions, and can be used in conjunction with the in-car system. It will include core HERE maps and other applications developed by car manufacturers, enabling features such as navigation, in-car communication, remote unlocking, remote car starting, and checking tire pressure and fuel levels via the phone.

Giagoam author Kevin Fitchard saw a DEMO display of this system at Nokia HERE's headquarters in Chicago.

According to his description, he set a route on an iPad and saved it to his frequently used routes. Soon, this route appeared on the control panel inside the car.

HERE Auto guided them to the center of Nordstrom and found a parking spot. It automatically saved the parking spot's location on the map and synchronized it to the HERE map on the Lumia phone. The indoor map on the phone provided walking navigation to the designated store and finally guided them back to the car.

Although this system bears distinct Nokia marks, Van-De-Klashorst said that was not their intention. They will treat this as an independent business. Car manufacturers can customize this platform, including the applications and user interface on the platform, promote their own developer programs, choose their preferred third-party applications to integrate into the dashboard, and price the system themselves.

It indeed looks great, but for Nokia, the key lies in whether it can gain support from car manufacturers. Most major manufacturers already have their own embedded systems, and companies like Ford and GM are also collaborating with developers to build their application platforms. Although HERE has been discussing cooperation possibilities with some manufacturers, it currently seems that this move is a bit late.
