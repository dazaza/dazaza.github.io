Microsoft reassures employees: Ballmer is gone, and the reorganization will continue

by anonymous on 2013-08-29 16:29:04

Microsoft informed its employees that the restructuring plan formulated by CEO Steve Ballmer, who is set to step down, will continue to be implemented. This move aims to reassure senior managers worried that the search for a successor might throw the restructuring into disarray.

An anonymous source familiar with the situation said that some members of Microsoft's executive team sent an email to employees last Friday, reaffirming their commitment to executing Ballmer's vision and restructuring plan.

Previously, there were concerns among employees that the new CEO would not continue with the large-scale restructuring plan announced by Ballmer last month — the biggest in over a decade. Ballmer hopes to transform Microsoft into a device and services company, shielding it from the effects of the declining PC industry and enabling it to better compete with Google, Facebook, and Apple.

"In the short term, there will be more confusion among Microsoft employees," said Sid Parakh, an analyst at McAdams Wright Ragen. "The question now is how the new CEO will view these changes. From the perspective of employees, they will be thinking, 'Alright, what are we supposed to do here?'"

A Microsoft spokesperson, Tony Imperati, declined to comment on this matter.

The source indicated that Microsoft's leadership team also held a meeting as scheduled last Friday. The board stated that the new CEO will continue to follow Ballmer’s strategy.

Microsoft has already fallen far behind its rivals in the mobile space, with poor sales of new products and declining core revenue from the Windows system. Therefore, it cannot afford the consequences of a new CEO changing Ballmer’s plans. During Ballmer’s tenure as CEO, which began in January 2000, the company’s stock price dropped by 37%. Additionally, the quarterly revenue and profit figures released by Microsoft last month both fell short of analysts' expectations.

Now, while searching for a new CEO, Microsoft will also need to work hard to retain its employees and alleviate their anxiety. Some employees have been feeling uneasy since the announcement of the major restructuring plan in July.

The source noted that according to the restructuring plan, Microsoft executives and staff are entering new departments and roles. They worry that after the new leadership takes over, their positions or responsibilities may change again; some executives dissatisfied with their new roles may leave the company after receiving equity rewards and bonuses at the end of August. The source also revealed that since the restructuring, Microsoft executives have received more external job offers, a situation expected to worsen until the new CEO is selected.

Pat Becker, portfolio manager at Becker Capital Management, pointed out that the Microsoft board should replace the CEO within four to six months rather than a whole year to minimize adverse impacts on the company. Doing so could also allow the new CEO to launch better products before the 2014 holiday shopping season.
