Diagnosis and Treatment of Mammary Duct Fistula (Report of 23 Cases)

**Organizations**: Completely open the fistula, scrape the inflammatory granulation tissue within the fistula using a dilatation catheter and rat-tooth forceps to remove dyed scar tissue. After hemostasis with gauze, bandage the area. However, we encountered difficulties in setting the probe and mosquito clamp for the fistula. We tried using an epidural catheter and later referred to Zhao Guangming's method, utilizing a circular needle as the probe. The tail of the circular needle was inserted from the fistula crossing over to wear clothing from the nipple, then cutting the top of the tissue. None of the patients in this group were stitched. Postoperatively, oral antibiotics were prescribed, and dressings were changed every 2-3 days. The wound gradually healed.

**Results**: There were 23 cases. All patients were treated by dressing changes, with durations ranging from 10 to 30 days in this group. Only 10 patients were followed up for more than one year regarding dressing changes, but no recurrence was observed. Additionally, there was no papillary necrosis.

**Discussion**:

2.1 **Clinical Manifestations and Diagnosis**: Mammary duct fistula is more common in young women, with varying lengths of course, mostly unilateral. In this group of patients, only one case had bilateral fistulas. The lactiferous duct showed pot-like enlargement at the base of the nipple. Narrow at both ends, bacteria can cause infection of the mammary duct and poor drainage along the expansion of the catheter retrograde. This leads to the formation of an areola abscess, where pus cannot be self-drained through the nipple but instead ruptures next to the areola, forming a fistula. Among the 23 patients in this group, 18 cases had Taiwan-like nipple sagging, suggesting that nipple subsidence may be related to mammary duct fistula. Additionally, chronic inflammation leads to fibrous tissue contraction, pulling the nipple, leading to nipple subsidence and ramp trap, which could be reasons for milk duct atrophy in patients with nipple subsidence. Milk duct fistula patients generally have mild systemic symptoms; acute phase manifestations include peripapillary masses, local swelling, and stroke pain. When the abscess ruptures, symptoms slow down with the sinus having an external opening, showing pseudo-healing and recurrent Tsui. Delayed healing manifests as chronic recurrent areolar abscesses. Physical examination reveals limitations of the areola or adjacent uplift, palpable cord-like masses, tenderness, and squeezing of the areola lump shows pus coming out from the nipple or adjacent rupture. The diagnosis of this disease should pay attention to differentiating it from mammary tuberculosis. According to typical history, a difficult diagnosis requires pathological examination for difficult cases.

2.2 **Treatment**: The special treatment method for mammary duct fistula is still surgical treatment combined with antibiotics. Sinus incision, drainage, and curettage surgery were performed on all patients. None of the patients underwent en bloc resection of the fistula and the involved catheter in Phase I, and all patients underwent thorough scraping after blue staining, followed by removal of methylene blue-stained inflammatory tissue, ensuring adequate drainage. Three patients underwent two surgeries. One was transferred outside the hospital in an inverted manner, and two cases due to this disease remain unknown to us.

In short, completely opening the fistula, thoroughly removing the inflammatory tissue, and ensuring adequate drainage is still the mainstay of treating mammary duct fistula. As long as there is an understanding of the disease and the method, satisfactory treatment can be achieved.
