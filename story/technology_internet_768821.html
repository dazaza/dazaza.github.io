Apple Completes Acquisition of PrimeSense? The Latter "Denies" It

by anonymous on 2013-11-25 11:13:39

According to the Israeli media Calcalist, Apple has completed the acquisition of PrimeSense for $345 million, thereby gaining key technology in the indoor entertainment field. Previously, this media had also disclosed that in July, Apple had held acquisition talks with PrimeSense, but it may have failed due to price issues.

However, shortly after this news was released, PrimeSense issued a statement to multiple media outlets calling it "recycled rumors" and refused to comment, which could be seen as a denial to some extent. This likely indicates that the two parties are in contact, but have not yet reached an agreement.

In theory, Apple should acquire a gesture recognition technology company, as this is the future of interaction methods. PrimeSense's technology can be applied to home gaming consoles, smart TVs, and even smartphones and tablets. Given Apple's massive user base in the consumer market, integrating PrimeSense’s technology could enhance the software experience of these devices while opening up the home entertainment market.

PrimeSense's technology focuses on algorithms and chips. As a B2B business, its name is not well-known, but you surely know the famous Kinect, whose motion tracking device uses PrimeSense's technology. The company also provides open-source gesture recognition software downloads, which have been applied to tracking customer behavior in supermarkets, mobile radar for robots, and security markets.

The consumer market is clearly much larger. Originally, partnering with Microsoft to sell large quantities of Kinects would have provided comfort, but Microsoft abandoned PrimeSense in the latest Kinect 2.0, opting instead for their own technology. This significantly reduced PrimeSense's application scope in the consumer sector, making acquisition by a giant a viable path forward.

If the acquisition succeeds, theoretically, this chip could be applied to Apple's smartphones, tablets, and Macs. However, Apple clearly places more emphasis on investing in the future; PrimeSense will be a key technology for Apple's future devices. The living room is the next market Apple aims to disrupt, and gesture control is essential. Apple has long desired the living room market, but previous releases of Apple TV have been lukewarm, selling only a couple of million units per quarter.

But Apple hasn't given up, continuously strengthening product features through acquisitions. In August, it acquired Matcha.tv, an iOS app providing comprehensive previews of TV programs. Through Matcha.tv, users can centrally manage videos they intend to watch, including content from traditional cable TV providers as well as Netflix, Hulu, Amazon, and iTunes.

For today's living room market, poor set-top box sales are related to the lack of application scenarios. Microsoft's success with Xbox360 + Kinect entering the living room market can serve as a reference for Apple. Gesture games naturally fit well in the living room, greatly enhancing practicality. Games can serve as a driving factor to promote Apple's living room strategy.

Moreover, from an interaction perspective, the remote control has been criticized for many years. Combining gesture operations and voice commands can change this situation, and even Apple's iWatch can participate as a control node.

Notably, Apple has recently made several strategic investments and acquisitions. Not long ago, it invested $578 million to collaborate with GTTA to build a factory, ensuring the supply of sapphire for the next 5-6 years. These all indicate that Apple will soon release new products unlike any before.
