Revitalizing bicycle low-carbon transportation is a kind of awakening.

by ditan360 on 2010-07-07 11:41:22

Exclusive to Low Carbon Network: It was reported that at the beginning of the new year, the mayor of Guangzhou received a gift. However, this gift was not given for free. The people who gave the gift also made a request: "I gave the mayor a bicycle, and I ask the mayor for a parking lot. I hope the mayor can personally experience the embarrassment of riding a bike in Guangzhou and provide a better environment for citizens to travel by bike."

The people who gave the bicycle were a group of young people in Guangzhou who called themselves "Bike·Guangzhou." Bike is the phonetic translation of the English word "Bike," and "Bike·Guangzhou" is an organization aimed at creating better conditions for the citizens of Guangzhou to travel by bicycle. It is composed of some young people who are enthusiastic about public welfare.

However, for today's Guangzhou, it is not easy to realize these suggestions. Like all large and medium-sized cities, the number of motor vehicles has increased rapidly, and the survival space for bicycles has been continuously squeezed. There are fewer and fewer places to park bicycles. Meanwhile, urban traffic is becoming increasingly congested, and air pollution is getting worse. This is the current situation in Guangzhou.

In fact, there are many groups similar to "Bike·Guangzhou" in China. They hope that their city will have a good living environment, and they ultimately still pin their hopes on the most widely used means of transportation in China - the bicycle.

It should be said that the choice of ordinary people is wise, and even more visionary than the government authorities. Because the most important thing in the world today is to ensure that humans have the most basic living environment, so it is necessary to reduce carbon dioxide emissions, which is the issue that the Copenhagen Climate Summit aims to solve. It can be seen that ordinary people still have a high perspective and a long-term vision.

Actually, reviving bicycle transportation has long been valued by many countries around the world. Fifteen years ago, the Netherlands elevated bicycle transportation to the core of urban transportation, with other modes of transportation serving bicycle transportation. Therefore, the Netherlands became the first country in the world where the number of bicycles exceeded the population, and the first country to build a bicycle highway. The government's overall plan for bicycles clearly proposed that for trips within 5 kilometers, people should try to abandon motor vehicles and switch to bicycles. From home to the train station, bicycles are the most suitable mode of transportation. The Netherlands currently has over 30,000 kilometers of dedicated bicycle roads, accounting for 30.6% of the total road length in the Netherlands, ranking first in the world.

Since it is a national policy, Dutch leaders lead by example. Today, ministers of the Dutch government, mayors, and other government officials ride bicycles to work. Dutch civil servants use bicycles and public transportation for 70% of their work-related travel.

Under the guidance of the idea of "zero carbon emissions," Germany passed a new road traffic law last April, stipulating that in the future, more bicycle lanes will be combined with car lanes. In the future, there will be fewer cycling signs on dedicated bicycle lanes in German cities, and more bicycle lanes will be set up on car lanes, of course, these roads will have markings allowing cycling. Cars are also allowed on these roads, but the speed is limited to less than 30 kilometers per hour. Since 2001, Denmark has reduced taxes for cyclists, and one-third of the residents of the capital, Copenhagen, mainly rely on bicycles as their means of transportation.

Bicycle transportation is considered an effective way to achieve energy conservation and emission reduction, and this has been accepted by more and more countries. Europe is the pioneer in reviving bicycle transportation. More than 40 European countries have re-established bicycle transportation policies and were the first to advocate "Car-Free Day." Then, many countries launched free bicycle travel services. In the United States, known as the "Kingdom of Cars," more than 20 cities have re-planned bicycle transportation and allocated special funds to build dedicated bicycle lanes.

In the South Korean capital Seoul, three years ago, a 6-kilometer-long elevated road was converted into a bicycle lane, pedestrian path, and park. The goal for 2010 is to increase the share of transportation handled by bicycles from 1.8% in 1998 to more than 10%, saving 2 trillion won in gasoline costs annually.

In Colombia in South America, under the leadership of former Mayor Enrique Peñalosa of Bogotá, the following reforms were carried out: during peak traffic hours in this city of 7 million people, restrictions were placed on cars. In the past five years, the proportion of people riding bicycles in the city has risen from 0.1% to 5%. Ultimately, this city was transformed, with a significant reduction in car exhaust and a marked improvement in air quality.

It should be said that revitalizing bicycle transportation is a kind of awakening, a reflection on the incorrect traffic policies implemented in the past. According to calculations, for every 10,000 additional bicycles on the road, at least 10,000 people can be replaced daily in public transportation, directly saving 40,000 liters of fuel, and reducing carbon emissions by more than 1 million tons.

In choosing the model of urban transportation, those who are forward-thinking first consider that global warming will bring catastrophic disasters to humanity. One of the main culprits of global warming is the massive emission of industrial waste gases. Undoubtedly, cars are among the main contributors to the symphony of exhaust gas emissions from means of transportation. Therefore, some countries in Europe and America do not hesitate to use legislative means to stop the growth of this "tumor" and firmly support "zero carbon emissions" in bicycle transportation.

The charm of a city lies not only in its beautiful living environment, modern office buildings, and high-end hardware facilities, but also in its pleasant traffic space, meaning that people can fully enjoy the beauty of greenery during their travels. Therefore, it can be said that revitalizing bicycle transportation means that humanity is taking practical action to "make amends," taking a gratifying step toward preventing global warming.
