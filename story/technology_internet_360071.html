McDonald's claims McNuggets are normal, consumers are not satisfied

by hpreal3470 on 2010-07-07 00:27:50

According to recent reports in the United States, McDonald's McNuggets contain two chemical components: one is "polydimethylsiloxane," which is also found in modeling clay, and the other is "tert-butylhydroquinone (tBHQ)," an extract from petroleum.

Yesterday, McDonald's China responded via a letter stating that the content of these two substances complies with the current national hygiene standards for food additive usage. The Beijing Health Supervision Institute and the City Food Safety Monitoring Center expressed they are closely monitoring this matter. It is understood that the aforementioned substances do not fall under the routine testing items for food additives.

Event Background

American media exposes McNuggets containing clay and petroleum components

According to recent media reports, research by CNN found that McDonald's McNuggets sold in the U.S. contain polydimethylsiloxane, an antifoaming agent used in modeling clay, as well as the chemical component tBHQ. Each nugget contains 0.02% tBHQ, which is derived from petroleum and used as a preservative for plant oils and animal fats. Ingesting 1 gram of this substance can cause nausea, ringing in the ears, vomiting, and even feelings of suffocation and faintness as side effects.

McDonald's USA explained that polydimethylsiloxane was added to McNuggets for safety reasons, to prevent the cooking oil from foaming during frying. According to World Health Organization animal tests, this trace amount is harmless to humans. It is understood that McDonald's adds these chemicals to maintain the texture and block shape of the McNuggets.

Department Statement

Rubber-like chemical substances are not part of routine testing projects

Yesterday, Guo Zixia, deputy director of the Beijing Health Supervision Institute, stated that according to the requirements of the Food Safety Law and the deployment of Beijing's food safety measures, McDonald's, as a restaurant, falls within the scope of daily supervision by health authorities for all foods produced on-site, including McNuggets. Currently, the routine supervision method for the food safety of fried fast foods like McNuggets mainly involves random inspections of semi-finished products, edible oils, and finished products, including checking the hygiene status of the oil, the safety of food additives used in the production process, and whether they exceed allowable limits.

Regarding the exposure of chemical components in McNuggets, Guo Zixia said that based on the limited information currently available, rubber-like chemical substances "are not part of the routine testing projects for food additives." However, the health supervision department will continue to closely monitor the safety of all food additives in fried fast foods like McNuggets. If the situation develops further, both the State Food and Drug Administration responsible for catering food safety and the Beijing Health Supervision Department will promptly investigate.

The Beijing Food Safety Monitoring Center also detected the incident involving chemical components in American McNuggets yesterday. A relevant official stated: "We are paying attention to this matter and actively organizing research."

The Beijing Food Office also confirmed to reporters that polydimethylsiloxane and tBHQ are indeed legally permitted food additives under national regulations. Based on McDonald's current response, their use appears legal. As for whether McDonald's use of these additives in McNuggets exceeds allowable limits, this awaits inspection by relevant functional departments.

Corporate Response: McDonald's did not disclose the specific content of additives

In response to claims from the U.S. regarding chemical components in McNuggets, McDonald's China responded via email yesterday afternoon.

"Content fully meets standards, poses no harm to consumer health"

McDonald's China stated that in China, "the content of polydimethylsiloxane and tBHQ in McNuggets fully complies with the current national hygiene standards for food additive usage" and poses no harm to consumer health.

They also emphasized that McDonald's has always maintained a strict attitude toward consumers, implementing rigorous quality control procedures for the food they provide, fully complying with food safety and hygiene standards related to various countries.

"Chinese McNuggets differ little from those sold abroad"

However, when asked about the specific content of polydimethylsiloxane and tBHQ in Chinese McNuggets and whether McDonald's had conducted authoritative testing, relevant personnel from McDonald's China did not directly respond. They only replied that "McNuggets sold in China differ little from those sold abroad," and that the content of additives in McNuggets strictly adheres to China's current national food additive standards—GB2760 Hygiene Standards for Food Additive Usage—and that the content of both additives is far below the upper limit set by national standards. Regarding whether these additives are used in other McDonald's products like Spicy Chicken Wings, the individual indicated that they would need to consult McDonald's headquarters to answer.

It is also understood that KFC’s Colonel Sanders Chicken Nuggets are a product similar to McDonald's McNuggets. Yesterday, when contacted by reporters, the public relations representative of KFC Beijing only stated that "KFC China places high importance on food safety and strictly operates according to national standards," without explicitly stating whether Colonel Sanders Chicken Nuggets contain these two food additives.

Consumer Reaction: "Hope food supervision departments intervene soon"

Yesterday, reporters visited various McDonald's restaurants at Wangfujing, Xinyoukou, and Xizhimen Halls, where McNuggets were still being sold normally and had not been affected by the incident.

Some McDonald's staff told reporters that they had not heard any news about problems with McNuggets and had received no notifications from superiors. Sales have not been significantly impacted so far.

However, consumers who learned about the "McNuggets incident" online yesterday began to have some doubts about McNuggets. "I definitely won't eat them anytime soon," said Ms. Lin, a consumer, adding that McNuggets have always been her favorite food. But if it is not clarified whether there is really a problem, she will stop buying this food. She believes that under these circumstances, McDonald's should give consumers clearer explanations and answers to eliminate concerns.

More consumers, upon hearing that McNuggets contain clay and petroleum components, were shocked. "I wonder if there are problems with other McDonald's foods? Will KFC foods also be implicated?" Many people raised several questions in succession. They hope that food supervision departments will quickly intervene in this matter, carry out testing and investigations, and promptly inform the public.

Expert Opinion: No unified, easy-to-implement national detection methods

Nutritionist Liu Qingchun from the General Hospital of the Armed Police Force: In an interview yesterday, he stated that tert-butylhydroquinone has certain toxicity and carcinogenicity and should absolutely not be overused. Additionally, since there is currently no unified, easy-to-implement national detection method for this substance, its usage monitoring is not very ideal.

"Allowed for limited use but must not be overused"

Professor He Jiguo from the College of Food Science and Nutritional Engineering at China Agricultural University: Tert-butylhydroquinone is an "antioxidant" for oil and can also be called a preservative. This substance is allowed for limited use in China's food additive usage hygiene standards, must not be overused, but there is currently no proof of harm after overuse. Generally speaking, using this substance is safer than not using it, especially in some edible oils, which would spoil quickly without it. He analyzed that if this component is present in McNuggets, it is likely introduced through the edible oil used to fry the chicken. Polydimethylsiloxane is also commonly used in the food processing industry. Most food additives are chemically synthesized, and those listed in the national hygiene standards as permissible food additives have been proven safe through extensive experiments.

"Both are legal food additives"

Biochemistry PhD Fang Zhouzi: Polydimethylsiloxane and tert-butylhydroquinone are both legal food additives internationally and domestically, and are included in the list of food additives released by the World Health Organization and the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations. There has been no discovery of harm to human health during normal use thus far.

Fang Zhouzi noted that many artificial synthetic additives currently in use are extracted from petroleum, coal, tar, and other substances and are widely applied in the food processing process. Naturally extracted additives, due to their high cost, are not used as widely.

Links

Tert-Butylhydroquinone

An internationally recognized food antioxidant, widely used in the oil and oil-containing food industries in dozens of countries and regions. Within the range of application, it inhibits molds harmful to human health such as aflatoxin. China's GB2760 Food Additive Usage Hygiene Standard stipulates that it can be used in edible oils, fried foods, etc., with a maximum usage of 0.2g/kg and a recommended usage of 0.01% to 0.02% of the total oil quantity.

Polydimethylsiloxane

Contains rubber chemical components, a chemical substance used as an antifoaming agent, paint, and daily chemical additive, and can also be used as a food-grade antifoaming agent. The current national food additive usage hygiene standard GB2760 specifies that the content standard of polydimethylsiloxane in food is 0.1 grams per kilogram.
