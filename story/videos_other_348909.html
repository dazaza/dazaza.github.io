I just wanted to eat him up at that moment.

by pgvn053hc8 on 2010-04-20 14:06:17

Around October 2006, during a chat with a fellow car enthusiast (a Hunanese woman from the same hometown as ZX, whom ZX has also met), she mentioned playing Rome Revival on the QQ platform. This was my first time learning that these single-player games could be played online in multiplayer matches! Encouraged by her, I downloaded Rome Revival to play online. Unexpectedly, upon logging onto the QQ platform, I discovered a Blitzkrieg room! Although compared to thousands of players in games like CS, Warcraft, and Age of Empires, Blitzkrieg's two or three hundred players seemed rather meager. But at that moment, it felt like discovering a goldmine. Beautiful memories about Blitzkrieg surged back instantly, and I yearned for the thrill of online battles.

I searched everywhere for websites where I could download the online version of Blitzkrieg, finally succeeding on APRM's website in 2003. Reading technical posts, game introductions, and online guides... Thus began my journey into online Blitzkrieg. The road to online gaming was extraordinarily arduous. Without the safety net of unlimited saves, there were only fierce, no-holds-barred battles. Moreover, it wasn't just one person fighting; teamwork was essential. Although having some experience from single-player mode prevented me from being completely clueless, knowing how to use shortcuts and positioning, I lacked any understanding of combined arms tactics, team coordination, and overall awareness. I was thoroughly crushed. Fortunately, I wasn't too dumb. After more than ten days of chaotic battles, my performance slowly improved. From consistently losing many tanks to only losing a few, and eventually winning occasionally, joy gradually blossomed. My skills started to improve, and I no longer dragged my teammates down, securing numerous victories.

Just as I was beginning to get the hang of things, I encountered the most important person in my Blitzkrieg career—866. At that time, 866 was at his peak, unstoppable and awe-inspiring. I remember the first time I met him in a match, we were playing Manover. His planes kept bombing above my head without showing even a shadow, leaving my troops scattered and broken. By the time I saw a shadow, it was too late! His infantry came charging across the battlefield, followed by an endless sea of tanks. Imagine the scene where you're crushing rookies: you lose only a little infantry and some trucks, reconnaissance vehicles, while your opponent leaves nothing behind but corpses and tank wreckage. Yes, that's exactly what happened then, and this is how 866 used to crush me! However, 866 didn't leave me with any psychological scars because he soon recognized my rare talent (just a joke) and recruited me into 503, making him my teammate. But he left me with another kind of dependency. After that, whenever I played alongside 866, I felt extremely confident and secure. Following him, I reaped many benefits. 866 took the lead in chewing through the tough parts for the enemy, and his general would have already opened up the vision. His infantry advanced well, and his reconnaissance vehicles and aircraft were all in place. All I had to do was concentrate my tanks and attack fiercely. This left a legacy problem: my level of combined arms tactics improved very slowly, and it took a long time for me to effectively utilize armored cars and reconnaissance aircraft. But these issues aside, I started paying attention to the use of infantry and armored vehicles early on because it directly affected the effectiveness of attacks. What was most fatal was that I never worried about vision when cooperating with this group of teammates, so I long lacked the habit of using generals to open vision. It wasn't until I faced multiple rookies in independent combat and suffered significant losses that I developed the habit of using generals and officers.

In the early stages after joining 503, as a rookie, I was marginalized. Except for 866, no one paid attention to me. Even though I felt close to any player wearing the 503 emblem back then, enthusiastic greetings often met with cold faces. Later, I realized that almost every team had a tendency to discriminate against rookie teammates. Blitzkrieg is a realistic representation of cruel warfare. In the circles of Blitzkrieg, strength equals voice. If your skill level is far inferior to the main members of the team, it's hard to integrate into the mainstream circle. This remains a harsh reality even today. There's no right or wrong here. If the gap in strength is too large, it's difficult to cooperate, and naturally, there will be less interaction and communication. Being unfamiliar, how can others feel passionate towards you? So at that time, I understood this principle and had no complaints. Mostly, I focused on wild matches. It was during those days that I met ZX, GOOD, WANWAN, and others.

The friendships formed during our inexperienced times are always unforgettable. Therefore, the people I met during my early days of Blitzkrieg—866, ZX, GOOD, and WANWAN—always gave me a different feeling. To this day, seeing them fills me with a sense of closeness, and I instinctively consider them my best friends. 866 goes without saying; he was my guide who taught me a lot. ZX transitioned from the internet to real life. He visited Beijing several times, and we gathered happily each time. Our gatherings were not only those of two middle-aged Blitzkrieg enthusiasts but also two old handsome men. ZX is a warm and outgoing person. I imagine many people in matches have enjoyed his warm greetings. ZX is highly mobile in matches, loves commanding, and dares to take responsibility. Later, ZX, GOOD, and WANWAN founded GB and developed it into the strongest HS2 team in 2008. ZX visited Beijing three times, from our initial meeting at Quan Ju De in Yuquan Road, to our long talks at a restaurant in Changchun Street, and finally our participation in a car club hiking trip to Phoenix Ridge. We progressed from strangers to acquaintances, and then to mutual admiration. Coincidentally, his profession is somewhat related to mine, and he also enjoys history and current affairs, making our topics of conversation very broad, almost covering everything. Recently, his work has been busy, and it's hard to see him. In his work, he's a formidable figure, leading a team that created sales exceeding hundreds of millions.

GOOD is not only one of my earliest Blitzkrieg friends, but later I learned that his hometown is also in northern Sichuan, only about three hours away from mine by car. This relationship of brotherhood and being from the same hometown undoubtedly brought us closer. Other fellow townspeople I met in Blitzkrieg include KUGUA from 503 and 98K from GB, both remarkable figures. KUGUA's aviation skills are highly accomplished, following the teachings of 866, surpassing his mentor. KUGUA is usually quiet, but when he speaks, he shakes the entire 503. In the autumn of 2008, he left 503, which still pains me deeply. Due to his departure, he also harbored some resentment towards me, leaving me helpless. 98K loves building maps and focuses on formations, demonstrating composure under pressure. His layer-by-layer advancement is very resilient. GB also has a fellow townsman named NAIHA, who unfortunately disappeared long ago, known for his fiery temperament and equally fiery gameplay. GOOD's combat style differs from these fellow townspeople. He plays steadily, always keeping his infantry, ATs, and 88 cannons moving closely with the main force, displaying meticulousness. He likes attacking the opponent's 88 cannons and excels in using them to erode opponents. You cannot expect him to shoulder the heaviest burdens like TTT or DADAYANG, but neither do you need to worry about him causing overall passivity. He is a stable and reliable force.

My friendship with WANWAN did not grow tenderly in a greenhouse but rolled forward amidst arguments. Years spent on political forums honed my sharp democratic thoughts. Meanwhile, WANWAN leans towards supporting communism. We had many arguments in QQ groups. Blitzkrieg players love war, and war is the most extreme form of political expression, so most Blitzkrieg enthusiasts inherently carry political elements. Because of democratic and pro-communist political topics, countless debates erupted in the Blitzkrieg community. Such topics are marginalized in the Blitzkrieg world but still sparked various grudges and friendships. Luckily, except for my falling out with HIPHOP over such topics, my arguments with WANWAN brought us closer. Arguments with the NEARMILITARY team passed quickly, leaving no trace. Happily, I found SDK as an ally. NEARMILITARY is one of the commanders of 117, possessing excellent strategic thinking and a humble mindset, skilled in leading relatively weaker teammates to defeat stronger teams. The most memorable impression is that when working with NEARMILITARY, he often says, "You don't need to worry about here, I'll handle it." This certainly gives extra confidence and courage to teammates. SDK is adept at both offense and defense, particularly excelling in defense. In one Berlin map match, I annihilated most of his tanks by 8 o'clock. He was left with two Type 4 tanks and a few AT guns, while I still had several Type 4s and one Panther tank. Thinking I could finish him off easily, I rushed in aggressively, ignoring the fact that I had no infantry left. Unexpectedly, despite firing at his Type 4s for a long time without inflicting damage, he managed a perfect counterattack using AT guns and infantry, leaving me utterly frustrated. Later, KIR summarized that the defensive formations of SDK and ABC in HS2 left the deepest impression on him, and I couldn't agree more. WANWAN has always been a core member of GB. However, I cooperated with him more often than I competed against him. I remember once playing Bagration, seeing his tanks standing still without AT guns or 88 cannons nearby, I eagerly charged in. As I approached firing range, his tanks remained motionless, filling me with joy. Suddenly, hordes of infantry poured out from between his tanks. Oh no, I pressed the E key, and all the tank fire was drawn to the infantry, while my own infantry failed to distract the enemy. While adjusting my tank positions, WANWAN's tanks destroyed much of my armor forces. Helplessly, I had to retreat hastily. Similar deceptive tactics have only caused me pain in OYZ and Rommel.

The truth is, if you don't join a team while playing Blitzkreich, you can still enjoy yourself. But if you want to improve your skills faster, joining a team is the wisest choice. So I must thank 866.

As the novice period of Blitzkrieg gradually passed, my battle records became increasingly impressive, and I started to integrate into the 503 team. In 2006 and 2007, 503 was strong and full of talented individuals. Although B52 was already gone by the time I joined, his heroic deeds were legendary, legends only. However, at that time, 503 still had two pillars, HUANGGUA and 866, along with outstanding commanders like SSYY and GUDE, and core members like DAIFEICHONG, LAIBU, BERLIN, KUGUA, FORTRESS, STEEL, WATCHMAN, XIAOAI, PORKRIBS, and others. HEIZHEI, FEILANG, and I joined around the same period. HEIZHEI's growth speed was incredibly fast, quickly advancing to become a mainstay of the team. HEIZHEI excelled at using Allied forces, was young, and full of energy, a teammate I liked very much. In 2008, GB also saw a 'HEIZHEI phenomenon,' with KIR emerging suddenly like a dark horse, shouldering heavy responsibilities. Frankly speaking, in three years of online gaming, I've only seen these two new talents rapidly enhance their combat capabilities. Many mainstays of teams are gradually promoted through hard work. Generally speaking, there are several levels of skill in Blitzkrieg: 1. The leaders of each team, such as OYZ from 117, HUANGGUA and 866 from 503, TTT and DAYANG from GB, etc., represent the highest competitive standards of HS2 during certain periods. There are only a few players at this level. 2. The mainstays of each team. Their skills are comparable. Generally, they possess a certain level of micro-management, combat awareness, and coordination awareness. Some mainstays excel in specific areas. For example, KUGUA's aviation, ROMMEL's training soldiers, SSYY's map creation, are almost universally known stories. There aren't many players at this level either. 3. Players who join teams but remain substitutes or on the fringes. They've shed the marks of rookies but generally lack tactical skills. There are quite a number of players at this level. 4. Long-term guerrilla players. There are many of them. A few have high skills, but overall, their level is relatively low. 5. Newbies. Every day, there are new players trying the game. However, the game's high difficulty weeds out a large portion. They are in this phase, either eliminated by the game or eliminating the game or integrating into it. Of course, such classifications are not absolute. Today's guerrillas may become tomorrow's team mainstays, and today's team mainstays may become tomorrow's renowned team leaders. But it's clear how arduous the process is to grow from a newbie to a team mainstay.

During that period, there were indeed many teammates in 503 qualified to be mainstays. HUANGGUA was undoubtedly the leader then. He was talkative, and his magnetic voice constantly resonated on TS: "Brothers, hurry up, let's take him down!" HUANGGUA's tank army was famous worldwide. But what made him most commendable was his readiness to take responsibility. Each charge, he would say, "Brothers, I'll go first." On the ever-changing battlefield, it was often his offense that first tore through the enemy lines. Later, he retired, and then 866 disappeared for a while. The withdrawal of these soul figures is why 503 hasn't fully recovered. At that time, LAIBU was closest to me. This guy played fiercely, swinging three axes wildly first. If he killed, great; if not, he would sacrifice himself. He pursued a thrilling style of play, loved and hated by all. Loved for his bravery, often carving a bloody path for you, but also despised for his stubbornness. This is due to his Northeastern Han character, haha. He has a fellow townsman named JACK, a good friend of mine, known for years. Although they are both Northeastern Han, often drinking together, JACK's style of play is entirely different. JACK moves cautiously, excels at forming arrays. However, his temper is exceptionally explosive, and I've seen him rage in matches many times. DAIFEICHONG rarely appeared at that time, but it was known that he was also very skilled with aircraft. XIAOHEI once said that our 503's proudest tradition is excelling at flying. Indeed, apart from 866, KUGUA, and DAIFEICHONG, these famous aircraft experts, XIAOHEI and BERLIN also had excellent flying skills. Later, when 503 fell into difficulties, DAIFEICHONG appeared appropriately and took on the responsibility of reviving the team. When a person has a strong sense of responsibility, their career progresses rapidly. CHONG accepted the mission in a crisis, and his level improved drastically, showing signs of becoming a top-tier expert. However, several teammates have recently been lured by the Brothers in Arms online game, including FED'R, who joined 503 in 2008, indicating that 503's revival path is still long.

FED'R's complex HS2 history rivals SDK's, both having transferred between multiple teams. One thing they share is that, although their skills are widely praised, they haven't found the ideal team. We only see the transfers but fail to understand their heartaches. The feeling of unfulfilled ambition isn't pleasant. FED'R and I cooperated seamlessly, forging a deep bond of combat friendship. His kill ratio was often terrifying, thoroughly understanding and utilizing various weapons. I firmly believe that if given a larger stage, he would impress many. BERLIN found a big stage, fairly speaking, he has put in a lot of effort in HS2 station construction and developed rapidly. But being young, he failed to manage some relationships well, resulting in some setbacks. He plays with great vigor and enthusiasm, excels in micro-management. Unfortunately, he is busy with studies and plays games less frequently.

In the process of integrating into 503, I met so many comrades-in-arms, broadening my horizons immensely. Teammates also helped me participate in many matches, gradually improving my micro-management and awareness. However, in important official competitions, I could only play a substitute role. At that time, 503's lineup was too complete. Only occasionally did I get a chance to substitute. But participating in one official competition could excite me for many days. I remember that in 2007, HS2 organized a large-scale competition (it might have been Operation Barbarossa, though I'm not sure). Although I only participated in two matches, losing one to 117 (when HUANGGUA wasn't there), I was already very satisfied. The only regret was that after losing to 117, 866 and GUDE had a heated argument, and I got involved. Looking back, at that time, my participation in official competitions could only be considered mediocre, not dragging the team down nor making major contributions, merely completing my tasks.

I should say that I am quite happy. As I gradually became a veteran of 503, I met more and more experienced players. Players whose names I had heard before appeared in my field of view, on TS, and as teammates in my matches. I remember that several major players from 117 were well-known, including now rarely seen GUOGUO and TATA, ROMMEL, NEARMILITARY, OYZ, and others whose names were thunderous. Although 503 and 117 had competitive relations and sometimes intense conflicts, the actual degree of integration between the two teams was quite deep. I remember that happy time when some members of the two teams frequently collaborated in matches. From 117, there were ABC, BLACKINKFISH, UFO, NEARMILITARY, ROMMEL, occasionally OYZ; from 503, there were DAIFEICHONG, KUGUA, LAIBU, me, XIAOHEI, 866, and others; they regularly teamed up. Honestly, it wasn't that the two teams were naturally ambiguous. Simply put, neither could gather a complete team, so they had to band together for warmth. Together, they swore, cursed, joked, and tormented enemies. Spending so much time together erased the barriers between the teams. Some members' relationships became intimate. For example, I became close to ABC and BLACKINKFISH, and XIAOHEI flirted with ALIEN.

This intimacy has its core. ABC and BLACKINKFISH, like me, were newcomers to 117. Sometimes ABC inevitably received criticism from NEARMILITARY and ALIEN during matches, and I occasionally faced accusations from teammates. Haha, perhaps we all had some shortcomings, growing at unsatisfactory speeds. Now, sometimes seeing TTT criticizing THEONE brings back those slightly sour memories, yet I also understand TTT's mentality. ABC and I both liked purple. He is a gentle and dignified person. He has now grown into one of 117's mainstays and contributes his energy to forum construction and management. ABC has one distinct feature: if you see a very neat and efficient purple artillery array, it's generally ABC's masterpiece. As for BLACKINKFISH's tactical features, I've forgotten, but I remember his unique voice. It's been months since he last appeared. I miss him a lot. Actually, I have a good friend, XIAOAI, who has been missing for a long time. This guy used to waver between GB and 503. Unexpectedly, after getting married last year, he disappeared, dedicating all his attention to his small family life.

ALIEN entered my line of sight, and the first glance made me very upset. Unidentified Flying Objects are my favorite, and I've been reading "Flying Saucer Exploration" for nearly twenty years. He sat on the golden signboard of UFO, leaving no chance for me. But surprisingly, when using Soviet troops, ALIEN really has that elusive quality. ALIEN's German troops flow smoothly, unlike most players who favor equipment from one country; he is genuinely strong in both. And ROMMEL almost exclusively uses German troops. He must train soldiers in matches, becoming his signature move. ROMMEL trains soldiers professionally. In a few matches, while I trained soldiers to 600 experience points, his soldiers had already reached nearly 800. Some veterans familiar with his methods often send bombers to sneak attack his training grounds. After blood and flesh fly, you only hear ROMMEL cursing—"CNM." ROMMEL has super-strong attack power, partly due to high-experience drivers and partly because of his unusual formation. When attacking, his formation doesn't spread out like most people's but stays compact, focusing on micro-management. Many times, I've seen his tank formation in clusters, which theoretically is disadvantageous, but in ROMMEL's hands, it becomes a lethal weapon to burst the opponent. This may be because his tanks have high shooting accuracy, and there may be other secrets. I've never seen his recorded matches, so I can't explore further. ROMMEL often says, "Do you know why OYZ is so strong? One, I provided him with armor. Two, I gave him high-experience drivers." This statement is true, as it is precisely the team strength of 117 that achieved OYZ's HS2 myth in some aspects. The strongest player, the top expert, is the honor many people give to OYZ. If you want to practice micro-management, OYZ is your best model to imitate. Unfortunately, I don't know him well, nor do I often see his recorded matches, so I can't delve deeper, but I know he is truly a super-expert.
