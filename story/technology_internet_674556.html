The design of extruded thermoplastic elastomers includes the geometric parameter design (such as wall thickness, ribs, radii, hollow sections, and hinges), while also considering their impact on product processing and performance. Below is a list of general guidelines for extrusion design.

Wall Thickness

A uniform or nearly uniform cross-sectional thickness will be easier to process, reduce costs, improve error control, provide better surface finish, and allow for more complex shapes. The minimum wall thickness is 0.5 mm (0.02"), while the maximum wall thickness is 9.5 mm (0.375"). Thinner walls are possible but would require the use of the Santoprene 8000 series of thermoplastic elastomers. Variations in wall thickness should be smooth and as small as possible, as this will help balance the mold during stamping.

Ribs

During changes in wall thickness, if the thickness changes too abruptly or drastically, issues may arise in balancing the flow field. The thickness of the ribs should be 50% of the nominal wall thickness, and the radius should be designed accordingly.

Radii

Sharp corners should be replaced with fillets at areas of abrupt change. The minimum radius for an extruded part is 0.20mm (0.007").

Hollow Sections

There may be hollow sections within the cross-section. The extrusion die might initially have the shape of the hollow section, and during cooling, compressed air can be used inside the hollow section to maintain its shape. Alternatively, vacuum can be applied externally on the extruder to help maintain the shape of the hollow section. More hollow sections make mold design more complex and maintaining the profile shape becomes more difficult. Hollow sections should be minimized or avoided unless required by design.

Blowing air inward during extrusion is a method for cooling the inner walls of the component. This requires airflow along the cutting lines or punching direction.

Foam Extrusion

Thermoplastic vulcanizates (TPVs) can be foamed through chemical and mechanical methods. For chemical foaming, foaming agents such as azodicarbonamide can be used. Achievable foam densities range from 0.97 (typical unfoamed TPV) to 0.70. Lower densities are patent-protected. Foaming agents degrade between 180°C to 190°C, whereas most TPVs are processed at temperatures between 195°C to 215°C.

For the mechanical method, water acts as the medium. A patented technology named "water foaming" is used here. Specialized equipment is needed to achieve consistent foam structure and density. Densities can be reduced from 0.97 to 0.20. Densities within this range can be achieved by controlling the processing conditions. Reduction in density affects mechanical properties, so it is considered part of the shape design in applications.
