As a high-risk, high-reward investment venue, the futures market is far more likely to make investors "fail miserably" compared to the stock market. The use of leverage greatly amplifies the fear and greed inherent in human nature; one misstep, and you could face margin calls that force you out of the game. Futures investment is a long-term process. Some people joke that if an investor can survive in this market for four to five years without being eliminated, then they might have a chance to start making profits. During this process, investor education becomes particularly important. Looking back on my five-year investment journey, I am especially grateful to the second futures company where I opened an account. It was thanks to their patient and meticulous guidance that I truly began to understand this market. For new investors, investor education is indeed an indispensable step. From this perspective, investor education is invaluable.

It was also around this time that I started systematically studying futures trading knowledge. At the time, the China Financial Futures Exchange had just been established and carried out many beneficial attempts in investor education, especially recommending many books that were very helpful to investors. One book, *A Decade Dream: A Confession of a Futures Trader*, helped me immensely. After carefully studying this book, I gained a deeper understanding of the risks in the futures market and began to develop my own investment philosophy.

Five years ago, when I entered the futures market to seek gold, I was fortunate enough to narrowly avoid becoming part of the group referred to as "meat." Experienced traders often say it's terrifying for newcomers to start making money right away because it makes them overconfident. And once you become overconfident, bad luck will follow like a shadow. That’s exactly what happened to me! In 2006, during the end of the first major upwave of the commodities bull market, I started going long on rubber. In just one week, I made a 50% profit. Instead of making me cautious, such high returns made me even greedier. So I increased my position size and started planning how to spend my doubled assets...

The newly opened futures company was relatively responsible. When opening the account, they systematically warned about the risks and provided a lot of futures-related materials for learning and reference. They also offered learning suggestions to help investors grow.

It wasn't until 2009, after a long period of study and preparation, that I seemed to find a stable profit-making model, with my capital curve starting to steadily climb.

At this point, I truly realized how ignorant I was in the face of this market. The initial futures company didn’t fully explain the huge risks I would face before I entered the market. I found this company unreliable, so after a period of silence, I closed my account and found a well-known large futures company domestically, ready to re-enter the market.

The subsequent process was not smooth sailing. Even after a lot of study and knowledge accumulation, once you enter the market and start trading, the red and green candlesticks still affect your mindset, causing you to chase gains and cut losses impulsively, leading to random trades. Of course, during this process, the account funds continued to shrink.

But when countless people enter this market with dreams of getting rich overnight, they rarely consider the eternal theme of market risk. They are like unarmed soldiers, entering the battlefield without any risk warnings or investor education. The result is predictable. Experienced traders in the futures market often refer to newcomers as "meat," because only with fresh "meat" entering can the old hands make money.

In late May 2006, the price of Shanghai rubber peaked and then began to plummet. Faced with this situation, lacking basic risk control knowledge, I didn't choose to take profits and close my position but instead started adding against the trend. As a result, my profits gradually disappeared, followed by principal losses. By the time I finally decided to cut my losses, I had already lost most of my principal.
