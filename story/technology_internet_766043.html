TIME: How Microsoft Almost Made the iPhone

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 14:39:16

Introduction: Last Saturday, the online edition of the American magazine *Time* published a commentary titled "Yes, Microsoft Could Have Invented the iPhone. Here’s How." The article argued that the Surface coffee table computer, which was showcased before the iPhone, shared a similar vision for the future of computing interfaces as the iPhone. However, due to miscalculations in device size, pricing, and target audience, Microsoft missed this crucial developmental opportunity.

The following is the full text of the article:

The Surface Coffee Table Computer

It has been over a week since Steve Ballmer announced his retirement plans, yet the entire blogosphere continues to discuss his performance during his tenure as CEO. While commenting on Horace Dediu's post from Asymco, John Gruber of Daring Fireball made the following statement:

"In theory, Microsoft could have been the first to create the iPhone—not the actual iPhone, but a disruptive device that would lay the foundation for the future of mobile computing, making mobile computing the dominant platform where most people spend their time. This wouldn't have disrupted Microsoft's highly profitable existing business—at least not in the short term."

In my previous blog post, I mentioned that I believe Microsoft couldn’t dominate mobile technology simply because companies leading the old paradigm rarely continue to lead in the new era. However, Gruber's comment about Microsoft inventing the iPhone sparked some thoughts in me.

At first glance, this seems absurd. Given all aspects of Microsoft's characteristics, claiming they almost created the iPhone might sound ridiculous, akin to designing the plot of *Pulp Fiction* with Disney directors.

However, I soon recalled that under Ballmer's leadership, Microsoft did indeed develop a device that was similar to the iPhone in many ways and was introduced even earlier. Many of its top-notch technologies were developed by Microsoft itself.

Developed Concurrently with the iPhone

There was one key difference between Microsoft's invention and the iPhone: it was enormous, too large to fit in your pocket. It was called Surface—not the tablet Microsoft later developed, but a coffee table-style computing system that Ballmer showcased at the 2007 D Conference.

This Surface coffee table computer had a sleek multi-touch interface. Although it ran on Windows Vista, it completely abandoned the traditional Windows interface in favor of a more modern, direct, and interactive touch interface. The applications demonstrated by Microsoft shared many similarities with the beloved features of the iPhone, including viewing pictures and playing games.

If Microsoft had released the Surface a year after Steve Jobs unveiled the iPhone, it might have been ridiculed as a copycat product. However, it’s worth noting that both products owe much to researchers like Jeff Han, whose demonstrations of multi-touch technology predated both the iPhone and Surface.

Microsoft's development of Surface occurred almost simultaneously with the iPhone. They first revealed glimpses of this secret technology to journalists at CES (Consumer Electronics Show) in January 2007, slightly before Jobs unveiled the iPhone. (When I first saw the iPhone, my immediate reaction was: "This is just a pocket-sized version of Surface!")

Development Went Astray

Internally, the Surface included a Windows PC, a DLP projector, and multiple cameras used to capture various gestures on the touchscreen. This product was expensive, which might explain why Microsoft initially targeted it for commercial environments such as hotels, casinos, and stores. When the product was launched at the D Conference, I wrote in the electronic magazine *Slate* that it seemed like an underutilization of such a clever idea.

This product, named Surface, which was released six years ago, still exists today, but I think it’s fair to say it has been disappointing. (I'm not sure if I've seen this device elsewhere, but I did see one at Microsoft headquarters during the Xbox One launch event earlier this year.) I know that when a company reuses a product name for a sexier new product, it often signifies a lack of confidence in the older product—Surface is a good example of this. Microsoft renamed their newer tablet as Surface and the original coffee table computer as PixelSense.

At a cocktail party at the 2007 D Conference, Bill Gates told attendees that he believed Surface-style computing devices would eventually end up on everyone's desks, though it might take a long time. He might be right—in essence, products like Lenovo Horizon are consumer versions of Surface, priced affordably for regular consumers. However, Lenovo may need to adjust the interface of this product—Windows 8 wasn't designed for such applications.

Missed Key Opportunity

Regardless, when trying to imagine how Microsoft could have leapfrogged into the mobile computing market ahead of the iPhone, the most obvious approach seems to involve evaluating the then-current state of Windows Mobile. This system featured a small Start button and a dated interface, showing a severe lack of imagination, but Microsoft appeared content with it.

However, Surface proved that Microsoft already had a clear understanding of what future user interfaces would look like; they only misjudged the device's size, target audience, and price. The true destiny of this concept wasn't to place it on everyone's desk but rather inside a significant portion of people's phones, and this trend would have started immediately.

Thus, imagining what it would be like if Microsoft had invented the iPhone isn't difficult. Just picture putting into your mind the ideas Microsoft researchers presented to Ballmer during the early stages of Surface development and ask yourself: what would the tech industry look like today if Ballmer had said, "That's fantastic—can we make it fit into our pockets and sell it with a profit margin of hundreds of dollars per unit?" (Shuyu)
