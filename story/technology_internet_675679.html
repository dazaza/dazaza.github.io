My father is a retired soldier who told me that Lei Feng was an ordinary soldier in peacetime, never went to war, and did not do earth-shattering things. After receiving such an answer, I felt somewhat disappointed. But this spring, as a journalist, I came to Fushun where Lei Feng worked and fought during his lifetime. Here, I saw roads, stadiums, schools, and even gas stations named after Lei Feng, and the people here take "Lei Feng" as an honor.

In nearly a week of interviews, I understood: Lei Feng's greatness lies in his ordinariness. He had the characteristics of a young person; he also cared about beauty and dressing up. He wouldn't even drink a 3-cent bottle of soda, but he was willing to donate 100 yuan to disaster areas. He was willing to help others, diligent in research, dedicated to his job, and loved life. In fact, each of us can become Lei Feng, or even do better than him. However, Lei Feng persisted throughout his life, which is the greatness of Lei Feng.

I am Chen Tan, born in 1983. As a member of the post-80s generation, I have heard many stories about Lei Feng, but frankly speaking, I find it hard to resonate and feel moved, and even think Lei Feng is far away from us. He is just a story, an uncle wearing a cotton cap with a simple smile.

In February this year, I came to Wangcheng District, Changsha City, Lei Feng's birthplace, with curiosity and inquiry. Here, I had long conversations with his elementary school classmates and listened to the old curator of the Lei Feng Memorial Museum talk about him... Through the narratives of one interviewee after another, Lei Feng was restored in front of me into a flesh-and-blood good person, caring for others, dedicating himself, seeing justice and helping those in need... Suddenly, I felt like I understood Lei Feng. His spirit and qualities will not disappear due to changes in the times. Therefore, when I, a post-80s journalist, truly understood the reasons for Lei Feng's persistence in faith and belief, I found my own spiritual support.

Starting today, our newspaper will successively publish reports sent back by special correspondents Wang Qipeng and Chen Tan from Liaoning and Hunan provinces.

Lei Feng's love for beauty can be seen from his hairstyle. Before joining the army, Lei Feng had bangs, with his forehead hair neatly combed. After enlistment, Lei Feng not only cut his bangs but also calculated how to save money every time he got a haircut.

In February 1960, shortly after enlisting, Lei Feng followed his unit to Fushun to participate in the expansion construction project of Fushun Special Steel. Across the road from the military barracks, there was a "Steel Plant Barber Shop," where the price of a haircut was half that of other barber shops, so Lei Feng often came here for haircuts. On July 31, 1962, Lei Feng had his last haircut here in his life.

At the time, Ms. Xue Ruilan, aged 76, was a barber at the "Steel Plant Barber Shop." Speaking of events more than fifty years ago, the elderly woman was emotionally excited, gesturing with her hands, as if the scenes of those days were once again vividly before her eyes.

"Lei Feng's first visit was when my husband gave him a haircut." Lei Feng's first visit to the "Steel Plant Barber Shop" left a deep impression on everyone. The barbers were very curious about this small soldier who was not tall, asking him what his name was and where his hometown was. This young soldier then told everyone: "My name is Lei Feng, my hometown is in Hunan, and I just joined the army." Lei Feng also talked about his tragic past, "He showed us the three knife scars on his hand, telling us how his parents and younger brother died, my husband shed tears at the time." Ms. Xue Ruilan recalled.

Besides the workers of the special steel plant, soldiers participating in the steel plant construction also came to the "Steel Plant Barber Shop" for haircuts. Lei Feng came here for haircuts because it was close and cheap. In his letter to the LiaoYang Municipal Party Committee, Lei Feng wrote that his monthly allowance was six yuan, except for the monthly cost of haircuts, all the rest was saved. Ms. Xue Ruilan recalled that popular barber shops people often visited included "Dazhong" and "Meidu", slightly higher-end, but each haircut cost five jiao. The "Steel Plant Barber Shop" belonged to the steel plant's welfare, with general conditions, hence the price was cheaper than other places. "Including cutting and washing is twenty-six fen, without washing is twenty-three fen."
