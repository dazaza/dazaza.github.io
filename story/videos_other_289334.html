1 Grapes

1.1 Fresh Grapes: The ripe fruit harvested from vineyards.

1.2 Wine-making Grapes: Fresh grapes mainly used for winemaking based on their characteristics.

2 Grape Juice

2.1 Interrupted Fermentation Grape Juice: Grape juice where alcohol fermentation is inhibited by the following methods: sulfur dioxide treatment, carbon dioxide gas suppression, sorbic acid treatment; it allows a small amount of spontaneous alcohol in the juice but should not exceed 1% (v/v, volume percentage of alcohol at 20°C).

2.2 Concentrated Grape Juice: Grape juice preserved by partial dehydration using methods approved by the International Organization of Vine and Wine (hereinafter referred to as O.I.V.), with a density no less than 124g/ml at 20°C.

2.3 Caramelized Grape Juice: Unfermented grape juice directly heated using permitted methods for partial dehydration and preservation, with a density no less than 1.3g/ml at 20°C.

3 Wine

Wine refers only to beverage alcohol made entirely or partially from the fermentation of fresh grapes or grape juice, with an alcohol content no less than 7% (v/v).

3.1 Sugar Content in Wine:

Dry Wine: Contains sugar (calculated as glucose) less than or equal to 4g/L. Or when the difference between total sugar and total acid (calculated as tartaric acid) is less than or equal to 2g/L, the wine's sugar content can be up to 9g/L.

Semi-dry Wine: Contains more sugar than dry wine, up to a maximum of 12g/L. Or when the difference between total sugar and total acid is determined by the method for dry wine, the wine's sugar content can be up to 18g/L.

Semi-sweet Wine: Contains more sugar than semi-dry wine, up to a maximum of 45g/L.

Sweet Wine: Contains sugar greater than 45g/L.

3.2 Carbon Dioxide Content in Wine:

Still Wine: Wine with carbon dioxide pressure below 0.05Mpa at 20°C.

Sparkling Wine: Wine with carbon dioxide pressure equal to or greater than 0.05Mpa at 20°C.

Low Sparkling Wine (or Grape Soda): Wine with carbon dioxide pressure between 0.05-0.25Mpa at 20°C.

High Sparkling Wine: Wine with carbon dioxide pressure equal to or greater than 0.35Mpa at 20°C (for bottles smaller than 250ml, pressure equal to or greater than 0.3Mpa).

4 Specialty Wines

Specialty wines refer to wines made from fresh grapes or grape juice using specific methods during harvest or winemaking processes.

4.1 Liquor Wine: Final alcohol content between 15%-22% (v/v).

4.1.1 Alcohol-added Liquor Wine: Liquor wine processed from wine with a total alcohol content generated from grapes over 12% (v/v). Can add grape brandy, edible distilled alcohol, or wine spirit. The alcohol content generated from the original sugar contained in the grapes should not be less than 4% (v/v).

4.1.2 Sweet Liquor Wine: Liquor wine processed from wine with a total alcohol content generated from grapes at least over 12% (v/v). Can add brandy, edible distilled alcohol, concentrated grape juice, caramelized grape juice, or white granulated sugar. The alcohol content generated from the original sugar contained in the grapes should not be less than 4% (v/v).

4.2 High Sparkling Wine: Made from grapes, grape juice, or according to O.I.V. permitted technology. Depending on different winemaking techniques, high sparkling wines should have the following characteristics:

? Carbon dioxide produced in the bottle;

? Carbon dioxide produced in a closed wine tank.

Divided by sugar content:

Natural Wine: High sparkling wine with sugar content less than or equal to 12g/L.

Extra Dry Wine: High sparkling wine with sugar content greater than natural wine, up to a maximum of 17g/L.

Dry Wine: High sparkling wine with sugar content greater than extra dry wine, up to a maximum of 32g/L.

Semi-dry Wine: High sparkling wine with sugar content greater than dry wine, up to a maximum of 50g/L.

Sweet Wine: High sparkling wine with sugar content greater than 50g/L.

4.3 Grape Soda: Low sparkling wine processed from wine made according to O.I.V. permitted technology, having similar physical characteristics to high sparkling wine, but with part or all of the carbon dioxide artificially added.

4.4 Ice Wine: Wine made from grape juice obtained by harvesting and pressing grapes that have been left on the vine until the temperature drops below -7℃, causing them to freeze.

4.5 Noble Rot Wine: Wine made from grapes that have been infected by Botrytis cinerea during the late ripening stage, causing significant changes in the composition of the fruit.

4.6 Film-producing Wine: After complete alcohol fermentation of grape juice, a typical yeast film forms on the free surface of the wine. Adding grape brandy, wine spirit, or edible distilled alcohol results in wine with an alcohol content equal to or higher than 15% (v/v).

4.7 Aromatic Wine: Wine made from grape base wine, infused with aromatic plants or with extracts (or distillates) of aromatic plants.

4.8 Low-alcohol Wine: Beverage alcohol made from fresh grapes or grape juice through full or partial fermentation and special processing techniques, with an alcohol content of 1%-7% (v/v).

4.9 Non-alcoholic Wine: Beverage alcohol made from fresh grapes or grape juice through full or partial fermentation and special de-alcoholization techniques, with an alcohol content not exceeding 1% (v/v).

4.10 Mountain Wine: Beverage alcohol made from fresh mountain grapes or mountain grape juice through full or partial fermentation.

5 Grape Distilled Spirits: Distillate obtained from distilling wine or fermented grape pomace.

6 Grape Base Wine: Wine entering the storage phase after the completion of alcohol fermentation is called grape base wine.

Part Two: Wine-making Grapes

1 Grape Varieties

1.1 Famous Grape Varieties

White Grape Varieties: Chardonnay, Gewürztraminer, Riesling, Sauvignon Blanc, Muscat Blanc, Pinot Gris, Pinot Blanc, Müller-Thurgau, Chenin Blanc, Semillon, Sylvaner, Italian Riesling.

Red Grape Varieties: Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, Pinot Noir, Syrah, Cabernet Franc, Gamay, Petit Verdot, Ruby Cabernet, Cinsault, Grenache, Misho, Sangiovese, Carignan.

1.2 Other Grape Varieties: Longyan, Hamburg Musk ( "Rose Musk"), Rkatsiteli, Carignane, Ugni Blanc, Yan73, Yan74, Rose Honey, Red Musk ("Red Rose"), Late Red Honey, Bacchus, etc.

Suggestion:

a) Based on ecological conditions and varietal characteristics, famous varieties should be selected for planting under the same conditions after conducting wine-making trials.

b) According to international wine development practices, European grape varieties are preferable for wine-making.

2 Grape Cultivation

Grapes should be cultivated in a pollution-free environment.

2.1 Fertilization

Determine the required amount of fertilizer based on soil fertility analysis, prioritizing organic fertilizers with chemical fertilizers as supplementary.

2.2 Pest and Disease Control

Integrated pest management should be the main principle for controlling grape pests and diseases. No irrigation one month before harvest. No insecticides one month before harvest. No fungicides within ten days before harvest. The use of grape pesticides should comply with the "Pesticide Safety Use Standard" (GB4285).

2.3 Prohibition of Using Ripening Agents and Coloring Agents in Grape Cultivation

3 Grape Yield

The yield of grapes for making high-quality wine should not exceed 15,000 kg per hectare, while the yield of grapes for making regular wine should not exceed 22,500 kg per hectare.

4 Grape Sugar Content

The sugar content of grapes for making regular wine should not be less than 150 g/L (titratable sugar), while the sugar content of grapes for making high-quality wine should not be less than 170 g/L.

Part Three: Wine Making

1 Sorting

Definition: Selecting grape bunches, removing unripe, damaged, or rotten grapes. Classifying according to grape variety and maturity.

Purpose: Selecting quality fruits.

2 Crushing

Definition: Breaking the skin so the grape pulp juice flows out.

Purpose:

a) Allow natural yeast on the fruit surface to come into contact with grape juice, which is beneficial for yeast proliferation.

b) In traditional maceration wine-making, allow soluble substances in the pomace to diffuse well into the grape juice.

Regulation:

a) Crushing should be done as soon as possible after picking.

b) Prevent crushing seeds and stems.

When making white wine, prevent the grape juice from being in contact with the solid parts of the grape for too long (except in cases of macerating skins).

3 Destemming

Definition: Separating the grape berries from the stems and removing the stems.

Purpose: Reduce wine loss; reduce tannin content and astringency; reduce stem flavor.

4 Pressing

Definition: Pressing grapes or grape pomace to separate the liquid portion.

Purpose:

a) Separate the grape pulp juice to make grape juice or brew wine without grape solid matter (i.e., making white wine).

b) Separate press wine from the pomace after skin fermentation.

Regulation:

a) If fresh grapes, press as soon as possible after picking; if crushed grapes, press as soon as possible after crushing.

b) Press slowly and continuously without breaking or crushing the solid parts of the grape.

5 Grape Juice

5.1 Free-run Juice

Definition: Juice naturally flowing out after crushing.

Purpose: Obtain grape juice containing a small amount of stems, skins, and seeds.

5.2 Sulfur Dioxide Treatment of Grape Juice

Definition: Adding gaseous sulfur dioxide, sulfurous acid solution, or potassium metabisulfite to crushed grapes or grape juice.

Purpose:

a) Prevent harmful microbial proliferation.

b) Anti-oxidation.

c) Favorable yeast selection.

d) Aid in product clarification.

e) Strengthen dissolution and maceration effects.

f) Regulate and control fermentation.

g) Make semi-fermented grape juice.

Regulation:

a) Add during or immediately after crushing.

b) Distribute sulfur dioxide evenly throughout the crushed grapes and grape juice.

c) Refer to the regulations in the "International Codex of Oenological Practices" established by the International Organization of Vine and Wine for the sulfur dioxide used.

5.3 Clarification of Grape Juice

Definition: Removing suspended solid matter from grape juice before fermentation.

Purpose:

a) Remove dust particles.

b) Remove organic particles to reduce phenolic oxidase activity.

c) Reduce harmful microorganisms.

d) Reduce pectin content and lower turbidity.

Regulation:

a) Clarification can be achieved by the following methods:

? Static clarification: Low-temperature static clarification method

Pectinase method

Bentonite (soap earth) method

? Mechanical clarification: Centrifugal clarification method

b) In high-temperature regions, grape juice should be cooled beforehand and/or sulfited.

5.3.1 Low-Temperature Method

Definition: Lower the temperature of grape juice to below 10°C, usually around 5°C, and maintain for a certain period to clarify.

Purpose: Facilitate the settling of suspended matter in grape juice.

Regulation: Usually combined with sulfur dioxide and bentonite (soap earth) treatment.

5.3.2 Pectinase Method

Definition: Add pectinase to crushed grapes or grape juice.

Purpose:

a) Facilitate the clarification and filtration of grape juice.

b) Help extract pigments or aromatic substances.

c) Improve pressing efficiency.

Regulation: Refer to the regulations in the "International Codex of Oenological Practices" for the pectinase used.

5.3.3 Bentonite (Soap Earth) Method

Definition: Add bentonite (soap earth) to grape juice.

Purpose: Facilitate the clarification of grape juice.

Regulation: Refer to the regulations in the "International Codex of Oenological Practices" for the bentonite (soap earth) used.
