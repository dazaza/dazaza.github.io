Three-pronged approach effectively prevents hand, foot and mouth disease

by kk2040 on 2008-05-10 14:58:54

Hand-foot-mouth disease (HFMD) spreads through many channels, and infants and children are generally susceptible. Ensuring personal hygiene of children, as well as the cleanliness of homes and daycare centers is key to preventing infection.

Recently, HFMD has spread across the country, with varying degrees of outbreaks in different regions. Is HFMD as severe as SARS? Many parents with young children are very concerned these days, especially as the epidemic continues to spread. The question is how to protect your child. In fact, HFMD is not uncommon; it has occurred from time to time in the past. The key lies in how well preventive measures are implemented. Besides maintaining personal hygiene, environmental cleanliness should not be overlooked.

HFMD spreads through many channels, and infants and children are generally susceptible. Ensuring personal hygiene of children, as well as the cleanliness of homes and daycare centers is key to preventing infection.

(I) Personal Preventive Measures

1. Wash hands with soap or hand wash after meals, after using the toilet, and after going out. Do not let children drink unboiled water or eat raw and cold food, and avoid contact with infected children.

2. Caregivers should wash their hands before touching children, after changing diapers for toddlers, and after handling feces, and properly dispose of contaminated materials.

3. Baby bottles and nipples used by infants should be thoroughly cleaned before and after use.

4. During the peak period of this disease, it is not advisable to take children to crowded places with poor air circulation. Maintain family environmental hygiene, ventilate living quarters regularly, and frequently air bedding.

5. If a child shows related symptoms, seek medical attention promptly. Children receiving home treatment should not come into contact with other children. Parents should promptly air or disinfect the child's clothes and properly handle the child's feces for disinfection. Mild cases do not require hospitalization; home-based treatment and rest are recommended to reduce cross-infections.

(II) Preventive Control Measures for Daycare Centers and Elementary Schools

1. Ensure good ventilation in classrooms and dormitories during the peak season of this disease.

2. Clean and disinfect toys, personal hygiene items, and tableware daily.

3. Staff should wear gloves when cleaning or disinfecting (especially cleaning toilets). After cleaning, they should wash their hands immediately.

4. Wipe and disinfect door handles, stair railings, and tabletops daily.

5. Educate and guide children to develop the habit of proper handwashing.

6. Conduct morning inspections daily. If suspicious cases are found, isolate the child and send them home for rest. Disinfect items used by the child immediately.

7. If the number of cases increases, report promptly to health and education departments. Depending on the need to control the epidemic, education and health departments may decide to close daycare centers or elementary schools.

(III) Preventive Control Measures for Medical Institutions

1. During the peak period of the disease, hospitals should implement triage and designate specific clinics (counters) to receive suspected HFMD patients. Guide febrile exanthematous children to designated clinics (counters). Increase the frequency of cleaning and disinfection in waiting and examination areas. Use wet cleaning methods during indoor cleaning.

2. Medical staff should wash their hands or disinfect their hands after treating each patient.

3. Non-disposable instruments and items used in diagnosis and care should be wiped and disinfected.

4. Other non-enterovirus-infected children should not be admitted to the same ward. Severely ill children should be isolated for treatment.

5. Beds, chairs, and other facilities and items used by hospitalized children must be disinfected before reuse.

6. Respiratory secretions and feces of patients and contaminated items should be disinfected.

7. If medical institutions find an increase in HFMD patients or enterovirus-related deaths, they should immediately report to local health authorities and disease control agencies.

Correct Handwashing Method

The correct handwashing method is one of the important aspects of personal good hygiene habits and can effectively prevent the transmission of enteroviruses. Wash your hands promptly after contacting patients, before touching eyes, mouth, and nose, after sneezing or coughing, after using the toilet, before wearing a mask and after removing a mask, after contacting public facilities such as handrails, door handles, elevator buttons, and public telephones, and after returning home from outside. A recommended five-step handwashing method: Wet, Lather, Scrub, Rinse, Dry.

I. Wet

Wet your hands under running water and apply soap or handwash.

II. Lather

Rub palms, backs of hands, between fingers, and fingertips for at least 20 seconds:

(1) Rub palms together with fingers interlaced;

(2) Palm-to-back rubbing along the finger creases, alternating hands;

(3) Palms together with fingers interlaced, alternating hands;

(4) Rotate and rub the thumb of one hand with the other, alternating hands;

(5) Bend the joints of each finger and rotate and rub them in the palm of the other hand, alternating hands;

(6) Rub the wrists, alternating hands.

III. Rinse

Rinse hands thoroughly with clean water.

IV. Turn off the tap with a clean hand.

Use clean water to rinse the faucet clean, then turn off the tap.

V. Dry

Dry hands with a clean towel/paper towel or use a hand dryer.

I. Personal Hygiene

1. Staff at daycare centers should wash their hands frequently. Staff responsible for morning inspections should use 5000mg/L (0.5%) iodine solution to disinfect their hands after discovering suspicious cases. Staff should wash their hands correctly with handwash and running water before touching children and their belongings, after changing diapers, and after handling feces.

2. Children should wash their hands correctly with handwash and running water before and after playing, before meals, and after using the toilet.

3. Do not let children drink unboiled water or eat raw and cold food, and avoid contact with sick children.

4. Educate children not to rub their eyes, pick their noses, or suck their fingers indiscriminately.

5. When sneezing or coughing, cover your mouth and nose with a tissue. Dispose of used tissues in a covered trash can to avoid droplet contamination of others. Wash your hands immediately after coming into contact with respiratory secretions (such as after sneezing).

II. Environmental and Item Cleaning and Disinfection

1. Regularly wash children's toys and expose them to sunlight for at least 2 hours, or wipe them with a chlorine-containing disinfectant solution at an effective concentration of 500mg/L for 30 minutes. Toys exposed to ultraviolet light for more than 60 minutes can also be used. Avoid exchanging toys among children.

2. Boil children's utensils and drinking cups in boiling water for 30 minutes daily or disinfect them in a sterilizer cabinet, or soak them in a chlorine-containing disinfectant solution at an effective concentration of 250mg/L for 30 minutes and then rinse with clean water. Kitchen staff at daycare centers should also pay attention to food hygiene and personal hygiene.

3. Wash and air bedding frequently. If there are patients, their clothes should be washed separately and can be sun-dried for more than 2 hours, boiled for 20 minutes, or soaked in a chlorine-containing disinfectant solution at an effective concentration of 500mg/L for 30 minutes.

4. Clean and disinfect the toilets at daycare centers daily with a chlorine-containing disinfectant solution at an effective concentration of 500mg/L by spraying or wiping. After use, cleaning supplies can also be soaked in a chlorine-containing disinfectant solution at an effective concentration of 500mg/L for 30 minutes for disinfection and should be kept as dry as possible. Urinals used by children should be cleaned promptly and soaked in a chlorine-containing disinfectant solution at an effective concentration of 500mg/L for at least 30 minutes. Staff should wear masks and gloves when handling secretions or excretions from patients and when performing cleaning or disinfection work, and clean their hands promptly after handling.

5. Wipe and disinfect surfaces of objects in daycare centers, such as floors, tables, door handles, and stair railings (especially places where children can touch), daily with a chlorine-containing disinfectant solution at an effective concentration of 500mg/L for 30 minutes.

6. Water play pools in daycare centers should replenish fresh water daily during use, maintain cleanliness without odor, and disinfect the pool water daily, keeping the free residual chlorine concentration at 0.3-0.5 mg/L to ensure good water quality. Once a HFMD outbreak occurs in the daycare center, stop using the water play pool and conduct a thorough cleaning and disinfection.

7. Daycare centers should pay attention to opening windows for ventilation to keep the air circulating. Ventilate at least three times a day for no less than 30 minutes each time. Air conditioners should have dust-proof filters cleaned regularly, which can also be wiped with a chlorine-containing disinfectant solution at an effective concentration of 500mg/L. Ultraviolet lamps can be used indoors for irradiation, at least once a day for 60 minutes.

8. Daycare centers should carry out environmental cleaning and pest control focusing on fly and cockroach extermination. If the fly density is high, use deltamethrin and cypermethrin at 25mg ai/m² for residual spraying in kitchens and surrounding environments to kill flies. Kitchens, children's rest, and activity areas can use commercially available cockroach bait with pesticide registration to kill cockroaches.
