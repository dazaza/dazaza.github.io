Are Windows really that bad?

by geekzhang on 2013-05-03 19:04:47

When asked the question of what exactly about Windows drives you up the wall, what would your answer be? Is it the system's tendency to crash, its less-than-stunning interface design, or simply the fact that it wasn't a masterpiece by Steve Jobs? In most cases, these three points are enough to satisfy those who oppose Windows. However, it's clear that Microsoft still has many staunch supporters in this world, and Balaji Viswanathan is one of them.

Balaji was once a developer in Microsoft’s core operating system department. In response to external doubts about Windows, he boldly stood up on the social Q&A site Quora and cited the words of his department head to counter the slanderous remarks. Windows is an essential component for activating nuclear reactors, maintaining central banks, and ensuring the smooth operation of large corporations. No matter what you do in the future, remember not to mess it up.

Perhaps, as you read this article, you might be one of those haters. Every day, after turning on your computer, you may habitually complain about your dissatisfaction and hatred for this system. But after all these years, when Windows has accompanied you through tough days and tried its best to help you finish your work tasks, and even when you consider that Microsoft engineers are also losing sleep over your complaints, have you ever thought about their feelings? Sometimes, the slowness and crashes of the Windows system aren't even Microsoft's fault:

End-to-end tracking: Windows needs to support every type of hardware. When different original equipment manufacturers release products with varying models, engineers must immediately ensure compatibility. Whether these devices are $200 netbooks or expensive high-end ultrabooks, Microsoft engineers need to track the entire process to ensure Windows' compatibility on these devices, whereas Apple never has to worry about such issues.

Rigorous attitude: When Microsoft engineers design system features, they must ensure that software running on the Windows system supports backward compatibility. Channel control: Microsoft will use every trick in the book to sell its products to PC manufacturers, but after granting the usage rights of these products, Microsoft's position among original equipment manufacturers diminishes. This means that even if companies like HP, Dell, Lenovo, or Best Buy add bloatware to the original system, Microsoft can do little about it.

Fighting against itself: Every web developer detests the IE6 browser, and so does Microsoft itself. However, when IE6 was first launched, it wasn't that bad. But as the world moved forward, Microsoft chose to stand still. Although IE10 corrected these mistakes and flaws, people still despise the IE browser. While this isn't Windows' fault, it still gets blamed by everyone.

Every day, Microsoft engineers tirelessly analyze millions of fault dumps (the window that pops up after a Windows system crash, with text suggesting to send an error report). Even though users repeatedly report the same problems, which cause headaches, these engineers never complain.

Additionally, if you're part of Microsoft and need to support the global computing infrastructure while aiming to produce your product with the lowest possible unified standards, your options are extremely limited. No other company in the world worries as much about compatibility issues as Microsoft does.

Although Microsoft's development in the mobile industry hasn't been satisfactory, in the PC industry, it remains a globally leading power. Even though many former followers of Microsoft have stopped supporting it, there are still people in impoverished and remote areas of India or Brazil, in the American Midwest, and in Europe, who take pride in owning this technology.
