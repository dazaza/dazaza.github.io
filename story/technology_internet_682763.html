Breaking into the Indian gaming market

by bestmen1988 on 2012-02-29 08:56:38

Gaming isn't quite in India the way people expect it to be. It's one of those regions that's notoriously hard to crack, like Korea or China; industries don't know what works and what doesn't. What's different is that publishers have been able to make some headway in these two countries. Free-to-play transaction-based online games are popular in the Republic of China, where Blizzard Activision's World of Warcraft has enjoyed moderate success as well. Similarly, countless Korean MMORPG publishers, most notably Gravity Co with their game Ragnarok Online, have made headway into the Indian market through distribution and promotion by local publishers. Blizzard Activision's "Starcraft" franchise also enjoys a dedicated following nationwide, while Nintendo's Wii and DS have made more reasonable progress and managed to penetrate as well. In fact, the mere fact that there exists a "Nintendo Korea" speaks volumes. Piracy is just as much, if not more, of a problem in India as it is in Korea and China. So, what makes India such a tough nut to crack specifically? Partly, it's cultural. Indians didn't grow up gaming. Gaming franchises Joe is familiar with on both hands, you can count: Mario, Tetris, Street Fighter, Tomb Raider, Duke Nukem, Need for Speed, Contra, Command & Conquer, Counter-Strike. Any attempts at making something else, genres wide apart in meaning, unknown. As if that wasn't enough, they all had something in common, some magical ingredient that made them resonate with Indian youth. Mario and Contra were very popular because back in the day there was a significant number of pirated NESes and SNESes in India. Cartridges loaded with several different games are still, commonly, how many Indian players were introduced to gaming primarily. Much like elsewhere, playing your games when you're young tends to leave the biggest impression. Tetris falls under the same category. Street Fighter was popular because malls in India enjoyed a period of relative success. I personally remember spending hours on a Wonder Momo with Capcom arcade machines in south India, when I was in my teens. The SNES version of Street Fighter II is also something many remember fondly. Once PC gaming took off though in the 90s in India, technically superior games started catching up quickly. Tomb Raider among the first graphically and technically impressive 3D computer games allowed free roaming and strategic, personalized approaches to combat. Need for Speed and Road Rash, both popular 3D racing games together almost synergized. In fact, I think Electronic Arts might be the most well known game publisher in India. Command & Conquer among the first strategy games enjoyed widespread popularity nationwide, while Counter-Strike took off thanks to its competitive nature. When it received a 3D reboot during the PS2 era, Toronto admired it for letting you drive around anywhere you wanted and blow stuff up. As you can see, every game on this list is popular for different reasons. Sometimes the same reasons people like them in the US, other times it's because people simply don't know any better games exist. For example, while Tomb Raider did well, the traditional series of Kain and Soul Reaver never did. Keep in mind that this popularity doesn't necessarily translate into big sales. Piracy has always been an issue in India and will be for the foreseeable future. School kids are used to borrowing each others' games and burning their own copies. Similarly, office workers are used to downloading ISO seeds and loading them onto external hard drives to bring home. Even retailers used to sell pirated items, only recently have anti-piracy campaigns, ineffectual as they may be, sprung up like mushrooms through ads and public information spots during breaks in movies. As for PC gamers, who comprise the majority of the Indian gaming population, care about games as entertaining distractions, and while fun, aren't worth spending money on. Money is spent on equipment like iPods or private housing development plans... or cool cell phones. Today's situation is slightly different from how it was in the 90s. In fact, it's almost like a step backwards. Despite the fact that today gamers in India are more educated about the gaming industry generally than they were in the 90s (definitely more so than "casual working professional gamers"), tastes have narrowed. While IGN and Gamespot get regular visitors from India who want to know all about upcoming graphically impressive explosions that'll make their video cards beg for mercy, this is exactly the problem. If you want to determine what games will be popular in India again, and thus what does NOT translate into "economic success", just pick out the top three most graphically impressive upcoming games, there's your answer. Add two points if they're first person shooters. Ubisoft's "Assassin's Creed" and Far Cry 2. Crysis from EA. Call of Duty: Modern Warfare. These were last year's big hits. Final Fantasy XIII will be popular too, thanks to how amazing it looks, but with console games and RPGs, it won't be in the second thought of most of India's gaming population when it finally releases. Even though it'll be technically free for them. My last job was at a video game art outsourcing studio up in Noida. For two solid years, I worked with people helping develop art under the supervision of various publishers equally big domains. While I can't name specific titles, I'd say some of them are among the most anticipated games of 2010 and 2011. Regardless, whenever interesting game related conversations came up, the same names repeated among my colleagues. GTAIV, Modern Warfare, Far Cry 2 and Fallout 3. So I say tastes have narrowed. RPGs are seen as a waste of time because they require too much thought and customization. Strategy games, while they suffer from the same "flaws", are excused based on competitiveness. No one likes adventure games because all you do is walk around and click things. Ironically enough, though this is true in India as well, nostalgia doesn't hold a candle to high-tech, visually impressive titles. For example, no one cares about the Nintendo DS even though they've all heard New Super Mario Bros is out because it just doesn't seem "cool" enough. Instead, they all want the PSP. When you ask them what games they plan on buying, you might hear something PC related, the same thing. Toronto, maybe God of War. Now keep in mind, it's easier to pirate DS games at this point than PSP ones. But this time, it's not a factor in the decision making process (though, the fact that you CAN pirate games on the PSP). No, even people in India have "standards". The DS doesn't make big explosions, it doesn't have fancy visuals, it doesn't have widescreen. Therefore, it sucks and probably won't have any good games. End of story. This one time, I found a colleague at work playing Call of Duty: World at War on the DS. I like portable shooters immensely, and the fact that this one has online, a huge step from Modern Warfare, was a massive plus. I told him how to control it and even got him to take an online spin where he played a couple of other people. At the end of it all, I asked him what he thought of it. "Is this also on the PSP?" he asked. "No, you wouldn't have touchscreen controls so what you did would be harder to pull off on the PSP..." "Damn. This is definitely something I want. The screen's too small. And there's no blood." "Oh, I thought you were enjoying it..." "Yeah, it's all right. If it was on the PSP, you could see blood and explosions." And that's a couple of growth diaries. World at War on the DS didn't get a second look. I tried showing New Super Mario Bros to another colleague later that week. "Is this also on the PSP?" he asked. "Nintendo doesn't develop games for Sony. They're competitors." "Damn. It would look better on the PSP." And there goes New Super Mario Bros as well! Can you imagine my helplessness, towards a game I usually write about. Unfortunately, I gave up long ago trying to convince the majority of the gaming audience in India that there's a whole world outside their FPSes waiting for you to discover. They'll even download three different versions of a 4 GB game, despite the fact that high speed internet at home is very rare. Whatever it takes, just as long as it costs no money. Despite the popularity of the PlayStation 2, Xbox 360 and PS3 because of "chips", it's seen as a viable alternative for people unable to afford upgrading their PCs to match new games. However, even the Xbox 360 suffers rampant piracy. To put things in perspective, this means Xbox Live isn't incentive enough to keep everything legal and legit. How, as a publisher, do you try breaking into the Indian gaming market? I'd say the first logical step to combating piracy or incentivizing purchase is the same as it is elsewhere - offering rewards for buying games. A comic, an art book. Posters. Anything else you can think of that would work. Take a good look at what distributors are doing in India. Stripping out manuals, stripping out large box cases, and selling things for as low a price point as possible. A good deal, India's average consumer is all for it. There doesn't exist here the concept of splurging on non-essentials. They can always figure out how to play games online somehow, or calculate the information themselves. Also, tutorials in games are passe anyway these days. My third suggestion is, don't bother getting Indian game development studios to create what you think are "games suited to Indian culture." As I just said, people here want the best graphics, the best technology, the best visuals, often a highly competitive multiplayer portion. Offering a game that doesn't provide any of those opportunities isn't going to stand out. Not only do Indian game development studios lack experience competing with Western or Japanese publishers, they also don't know the first thing about marketing and PR. Get people who already know the industry. Good public relations, of course, is as important in India as it is elsewhere. Having a representative in India speak on behalf of your organization and its games and interact with the gaming community, educating them on what you have to offer, is beneficial. People think cultural differences, namely the prevalence of Bollywood culture, constitute a problem. While I agree cultural differences are certainly a factor hindering the progress of the gaming industry in India, I'd say they aren't the biggest factor. After all, like all other Asian nations, India is easily westernizing and doing so at a rapid pace. What's "Indian" about a WWII shooter? It feels like games haven't taken off here the most because publishers haven't put in the effort required to talk and educate consumers. If you're willing to take this step, you have a chance of making progress in the long run, even if it may take some time to get there. Ishaan Sahdev
