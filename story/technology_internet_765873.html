Google TV Stick Bans Local Playback, Unfriendly to Third Parties

by anonymous on 2013-08-26 14:36:48

After the release of Google's TV stick Chromecast, while its low price was impressive, several functional defects were also quite regrettable, including the inability to play photos and videos saved on the user's phone or tablet. Recently, third-party developers managed to hack the system and developed an app for playing local content, but it was mercilessly banned by Google.

This banned app for playing local video content is called AllCast (another name is "AirCast"). Tech media pointed out that from the banning of this app, it can be seen that Google does not want third-party developers meddling with the TV stick Chromecast, meaning Chromecast may not be the all-encompassing Android ecosystem.

Besides being able to play videos saved on the phone, AllCast can also access cloud-stored videos, such as those in Dropbox or Google's Drive. The developer of this app is a skilled individual named Dutta (Koushik Dutta), who cracked the Chromecast system through reverse engineering.

Dutta released multiple test versions of the aforementioned app and planned to upload the software to Google Play. However, unexpectedly, Google recently upgraded the Chromecast system, and Dutta found that his app could no longer run. He personally believed that this was a "deliberate" action taken by Google.

Dutta told the media that Google appears to have a very strict policy on third-party development for Chromecast, allowing only approved video content to be played through the TV stick. "Chromecast looks like it’s not a developer-friendly device," he said.

Currently, the officially supported video content that can be streamed to TVs via Chromecast includes videos from the Play Store, Netflix, and Google's own YouTube. There were previous reports indicating that two other American online video providers, HBO and Hulu, might gradually support playback through Chromecast. Media points out that as more video partners join, Google's control over the Chromecast user interface will be affected.

Regarding the ban on AllCast, a relevant Google official, Chandra (Rishi-Chandra), explained to the media. He stated that if third-party developers follow Google's Chromecast model, which involves streaming videos stored in the cloud, Google is willing to collaborate with them. Google cannot arbitrarily allow any third party to privately stream video content through Chromecast.

However, this senior official also mentioned that if users are streaming online videos through the Chrome browser rather than dedicated apps, Google will not impose restrictions.

Industry analysts have already pointed out that supporting the ability to play photos and videos stored on phones and tablets on TVs is already a common and user-needed function. Google's Chromecast currently needs to urgently address this weakness. Additionally, besides the two major video sources Netflix and YouTube, Chromecast also needs to introduce more movie and TV content providers. Only then can Chromecast compete in content with cable TV, satellite TV, and other internet set-top boxes (like Apple).
