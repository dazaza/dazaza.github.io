AOL Bids 30 Million Dollars to Acquire TechCrunch: Reports _ Internet _ Tech Era _ Sina.com

by cacard on 2010-09-29 11:18:49

Sina Technology News, early on the morning of September 29 Beijing time, according to foreign media reports, AOL has announced that it reached an acquisition agreement with the technology blog TechCrunch. Including incentive plans, AOL will spend a total of $30 million.

AOL CEO Tim Armstrong once stated that he hoped to create a "build-first" corporate culture within AOL, promoting AOL's self-growth. However, this deal does not align with AOL's strategy.

This transaction echoes AOL's acquisition of WebLogs in 2005, which at the time owned well-known blogs such as Engadget and Joystiq. In addition to TechCrunch, AOL also announced on Tuesday the acquisition of online video startup 5Min. There are also reports that AOL is in contact with blogs such as Mashable and Howcast.

Industry insiders believe there are five reasons why AOL acquired TechCrunch:

-TechCrunch has a large number of high-quality readers.

TechCrunch stated that its website has over 9.2 million readers monthly, with page views reaching 30 million. comScore data shows that TechCrunch attracts approximately 7 million global readers each month. Clearly, this is a group of readers with extremely high value. Unlike Engadget, TechCrunch's readers include angel investors, venture capitalists, corporate executives, and people with a strong interest in tech news. AOL sales director Jeff Levick said: "TechCrunch and Engadget have almost no overlap." In summary, most of TechCrunch's readers are industry insiders, allowing AOL to sell ads on TechCrunch at higher prices.

-TechCrunch also has regular readers.

Even without partnering with AOL, TechCrunch can attract consumer product advertisers such as Cadillac and Mercedes-Benz. Armstrong said: "You will get two types of readers; we think this is a two-in-one deal."

-TechCrunch has conference organizing capabilities.

TechCrunch holds the TechCrunch Disrupt conference every six months, which is highly profitable. If tech startups wish to participate in this conference, they need to pay for booths to attract investor attention. Some large tech companies also attend this conference to identify potential acquisition targets.

-TechCrunch may already be profitable or close to profitability.

TechCrunch is a private company and does not disclose its financial data. TechCrunch blogger Michael Arrington stated that the company is already profitable, with annual profits of about $10 million. Considering the size of the company's workforce and its conference organizing abilities, this figure should be roughly accurate.

-Startups within AOL are full of problems.

AOL's best media assets, such as Engadget and Joystiq, were all acquired from external sources. Meanwhile, AOL's internal media websites lack attention. An AOL employee said: "When Armstrong announced continued investment in profitable assets, everyone knew what that meant and the impact on existing assets."

However, acquiring TechCrunch also brings potential issues to AOL. The role that Arrington plays in TechCrunch could cause trouble for AOL and Armstrong, but readers love Arrington's style. Moreover, TechCrunch's success is closely tied to Arrington himself, raising questions about whether the site can continue to succeed if Arrington leaves. This issue exists for many well-known tech blogs, such as Business Insider, PaidContent, GigaOm, Mashable, Venturebeat, and ReadWriteWeb.

Armstrong might not be worried about this point. AOL has asked Arrington to serve for three years, while Armstrong hopes that Arrington will work for AOL for life. He said: "Many of these types of companies are founder-driven, and we hope that Arrington and the TechCrunch brand will continue to develop in the future." (Li Hui)
