HSBC Hong Kong Business

The Hongkong and Shanghai Banking Corporation (HSBC) started its business in Hong Kong with the registration of companies in Hong Kong as its foundation. When it opened, it used the Victor Building on D'Aguilar Street (now known as Bank Street) in Central, which was owned by one of its shareholders at the time, Sassoon & Co., as its headquarters and obtained the right to issue banknotes. The original location of the headquarters was close to its current location, and in 1886, it purchased and renovated it to become the second-generation head office site. In the 1880s, after the Oriental Bank Corporation went out of business due to poor coffee harvests in Ceylon, HSBC took over the Hong Kong government's current account originally held by the Oriental Bank Corporation, becoming the quasi-central bank of Hong Kong. In 1872, the British Hong Kong government authorized HSBC to issue one-dollar banknotes to supplement the severe shortage of one-dollar silver coins. After World War II, HSBC took on the responsibility of rebuilding Hong Kong's economy. During the banking crises of the 1960s and 1980s, HSBC acted as the ultimate rescuer. On another note, HSBC built a headquarters in Kowloon to handle accounts and services for the Kowloon and New Territories areas. Located at 673 Nathan Road in Mong Kok, Kowloon, the Mong Kok HSBC Tower served as its second headquarters. Moreover, in 1999, HSBC moved into the HSBC Centre in Tai Kok Tsui. The HSBC Centre consists of three buildings and is part of the first phase of the MTR Olympic Station property development plan. The original site of the HSBC Centre was the Tai Kok Tsui Pier Bus Terminus. In 1998, before the building was named, the developer, Sino Group, sold the office building to HSBC for 4 billion Hong Kong dollars to be used as a back-office headquarters for human resources and to relocate some of the operations originally from the Mong Kok branch, thus naming it the HSBC Centre.

HSBC is a market leader in Hong Kong. In terms of deposit market share, HSBC Hong Kong holds 24%, while its market share in credit card balances is 29%. It also holds approximately 17% in the mortgage market and 20% in the life insurance market.

Hong Kong Dollar Banknotes: Since its establishment, HSBC has been issuing Hong Kong dollar banknotes. However, initially, they were mainly used for commercial transactions. Until 1935, when the Hong Kong colonial government passed the Currency Ordinance, HSBC was authorized to issue Hong Kong dollar banknotes above 5 dollars (the issuance of 5-dollar notes ceased immediately after the release of the 5-dollar note in 1975). Starting in 1994, the government issued 10-dollar coins, and HSBC ceased issuing 10-dollar banknotes. Currently, HSBC's issuance of Hong Kong dollar banknotes accounts for more than 63% of all banknotes in circulation in Hong Kong.
