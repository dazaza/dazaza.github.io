Baby can read early learning system

by phonics6168 on 2012-02-10 02:15:21

Curious about your baby being able to read? What does it take to learn it and how exactly does it work? If you spend any time watching television or late night television shopping channels, you may have seen advertisements for the Your Baby Can Read early literacy program. The program claims to be able to teach infants at a higher level of literacy than simply recognizing basic words, but actually having preschoolers read chapter books. If you're like most parents, you probably had some skepticism going on while viewing these ads. Understanding how the program works may help alleviate some of your concerns. The Baby Can Read program doesn't use teaching phonics, but instead uses word recognition. Babies are taught to associate images or actions that represent the meaning of words in view. This is similar to the way they learn oral language. When mother teaches her child to say "juice," she'll say "Do you want some juice?" and then point to the child's bottle or cup. Soon, the child understands that "juice" means his favorite drink. Even before he can say the word, he's made the association. If mother says, "Do you want some juice?" he'll also point to the milk or shake his head "no" if he doesn't want any. With the early literacy program, children start by watching short DVD programs that show video clips or pictures with words shown on screen. Children are encouraged to see and hear the media speak and gesture so it takes the most stubborn. The program also includes books and teaching cards that parents can underline and review what is being taught with their own children. The creators of the Baby Can Read program suggest that parents review the DVDs along with their children. This isn't nanny time. It's teaching time. If you sit down with your child and encourage them to interact with the program, you'll find it is more reinforcing. Since the program works at the child's own pace and puts no pressure on the child, it can be very effective. Parents need to review materials to ensure they're using the appropriate DVDs. Overall, don't try to force young ones to learn from these DVDs. When the time is right, the child will learn with the right encouragement. So, will your Baby Can Read program turn your child into a genius? Probably not, although he may seem like one when he's reading books before his third birthday. However, studies have proven that children who develop early literacy skills do better in school when traditional reading instruction begins. That's a bonus for anyone concerned with their child's education. Plus, kids who are reading are rarely bored. So, opening the door to early age literacy for your child gives him two excellent gifts.
