Herniated cervical disc is a relatively acute cervical and lumbar disease, which is induced by trauma and slight sprain. The symptoms of herniated cervical disc vary depending on the type. So, how many types does the herniated cervical disc divide into and what are their manifestations? The symptoms of each type are different. What are the symptoms of each type? Cervical spondylosis is a general term for cervical osteoarthritis, proliferative cervical spondylitis, cervical radiculopathy syndrome, and cervical disc prolapse. It is a disease based on degenerative pathological changes. Mainly due to long-term fatigue damage of the cervical vertebrae, bone hyperplasia, or intervertebral disc prolapse, ligament thickening, resulting in compression of the cervical spinal cord, nerve roots, or vertebral arteries, leading to a series of functional disorders clinical syndromes.

The central type of herniated cervical disc has symptoms mainly appearing on the side of the cervical vertebrae, in front of the spinal cord, which can compress the anterior part of both sides of the spinal cord and produce bilateral compression symptoms of the spinal cord. Different degrees of weakness in the limbs, with the lower limbs often more severe than the upper limbs, manifesting as unstable walking; serious cases may result in incomplete or complete paralysis of the four limbs; dysfunction of urination and defecation, manifested as urinary retention and difficulty in defecation. Different degrees of decreased muscle strength in the limbs; sensory abnormalities, both superficial and deep sensations may be affected, depending on the level of sensory abnormalities caused by the intervertebral disc protrusion; increased muscle tone in the limbs; hyperactive tendon reflexes, positive patellar clonus and ankle clonus, positive pathological signs such as Hoffmann and Openheim.

The lateral type of herniated cervical disc has symptoms where some protrusions occur outside the posterior longitudinal ligament and inside the uncovertebral joint, with the protruding intervertebral disc compressing the cervical spinal nerve root passing through this area, producing root compression symptoms. Neck pain, stiffness, limited mobility, similar to "stiff neck"; severe pain when the neck is extended backward, which can radiate to the scapula or occipital region; one upper limb has pain or numbness, but rarely both sides occur simultaneously. The neck is in a stiff position; tenderness at the vertebral segment on the diseased side, percussion tenderness, tenderness between the spinous processes of the lower cervical vertebrae and the inner side of the scapula; positive nerve root tension test and Spurling test; sensory, motor, and reflex changes in the distribution area of the affected nerve root, with atrophy and muscle strength reduction in the innervated muscles.

The paracentral type of herniated cervical disc has symptoms where the protruding part is biased to one side between the cervical spinal nerve root and the spinal cord, compressing the unilateral nerve root and spinal cord. In addition to the lateral type manifestations, there are also varying degrees of unilateral spinal cord compression symptoms, manifested as an atypical Brown-Sequard syndrome. This type often masks the spinal cord compression due to intense root pain, and once spinal cord compression occurs, the condition is usually severe.

Experts kindly remind: Herniated cervical disc can cause great harm to people and requires treatment. Experts advise regular hospital check-ups during treatment to monitor the development of the condition. Preventing cervical spondylosis is particularly important. The basic prevention of cervical spondylosis is to improve bad habits in life and work. Many people like to lie on the sofa and read books, stay in a low head posture for a long time, or sleep with their heads raised while taking public transportation, all of which are bad habits. During work, get up every half hour, rotate your neck, and exercise for a while. Air conditioning should not blow directly on the neck; you can prepare a collar coat in the office to keep the neck and shoulder warm. In addition, pay attention to sleeping posture, with supine being the best. Side-lying or prone sleeping, pressing the arms under the head, will increase the burden on the cervical vertebrae.
