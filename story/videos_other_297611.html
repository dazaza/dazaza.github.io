by bjseeprof on 2009-12-07 11:32:20

Today's headlines: "Jiangsu Xuzhou legislates to ban human flesh search engines with maximum fines of 5000 yuan" Yesterday, the "Xuzhou Computer Information System Security Protection Regulations" was passed by the seventh meeting of the provincial eleventh People's Congress Standing Committee and will come into effect on June 1, 2009. The regulations make detailed provisions for computer security level management, protective measures, prohibited behaviors, and legal responsibilities, especially for the recently widely watched "human flesh search engines," the regulations clearly say "no." "China News Service: Anti-corruption through human flesh searches fits China's national conditions" The latest achievement of human flesh searches is the downfall of Zhou Jiugeng, the former director of the Jiangning District Housing Bureau in Nanjing. Zhou Jiugeng angered netizens by claiming that selling houses below cost would be investigated, it was found out that he smoked high-end cigarettes worth a thousand yuan each and wore a ten-thousand-yuan brand-name watch. Just before the arrival of 2009, the Jiangning District Committee announced that his statements were inappropriate and that he had engaged in luxurious consumption by purchasing high-end cigarettes with public funds, deciding to dismiss him from his position as housing bureau director. Investigations are ongoing regarding other issues raised by netizens... I have always been a respectful and life-loving youth. From the perspective of personal privacy, morally I would definitely advocate not intentionally exposing others' privacy. However, the introduction of this legislation banning human flesh searches in Xuzhou is really poorly timed. Recently, numerous incidents such as customs officials molesting children and directors smoking ultra-high-end cigarettes have been exposed online, all thanks to human flesh searches. The introduction of this policy at this time inevitably raises suspicions of suppressing public discourse. Indeed, any matter involving the interests of the people usually takes five to ten years to resolve, and the results are often unpopular. On the other hand, policies and laws involving high-ranking government officials are always implemented extremely swiftly. On the surface, these laws seem reasonable and justified, but in practice, they are applied differently depending on the situation. Human flesh searches involving local officials are naturally responded to quickly and dealt with severely according to the law. This is the reason. Looking at recent events, one can deeply compare various equal phenomena under different societies: Those who embezzle tens of millions or hundreds of millions often receive suspended sentences because they confess voluntarily and show good repentance attitudes. In contrast, someone who exploits a bank system error to withdraw over a hundred thousand yuan receives a life sentence because they acted with criminal intent and the circumstances were particularly severe. Some satirical rhymes circulating online vividly illustrate the current harmonious situation, such as those about our banking industry: 1. Withdrawing counterfeit money from an ATM - > Bank has no responsibility 2. Online banking theft - > Account holder's responsibility 3. Bank gives too much money - > Account holder's obligation to return 4. Bank gives too little money - > Not responsible after leaving the counter 5. ATM malfunctions and gives too little money - > User's responsibility 6. ATM malfunctions and gives too much money - > User is charged with theft and sentenced to life imprisonment 7. A bank manager in Kaiping, Guangdong embezzles 400 million yuan - > Sentenced to 12 years 8. An ATM mistakenly gives Xu Ting, a common citizen, 170,000 yuan - > Sentenced to life imprisonment The double standards in political rhetoric versus action are well-known to the Chinese public. Once actions are taken, they often invite criticism. It is evident that the supervision of rule-makers' power is still just lip service to democracy and public welfare, while behind closed doors, there is continuous retaliation. The "gag order" from Xuzhou, Jiangsu fully demonstrates the foresight of Xuzhou's powerful officials in preparing preventive measures in advance. This pioneering move also shows the spirit of innovation in reform frontline efforts, establishing exemplary practices for other provinces and cities to emulate, enhancing workplace sentiments, and simultaneously preventing potential crises where Xuzhou citizens might conduct a "human flesh search" on their own local officials. It seems that their hands aren't very clean.
