Wallpaper Agent Historical and Cultural Development of Wallpaper

by wallpaper5 on 2011-08-25 16:42:13

Today's wallpaper bears little resemblance to its earliest origins, which date back to the first cave dwellers who painted simple but realistic depictions of their daily lives on the walls of their caves. Fast forward to ancient Egypt, Greece, and Rome, where more examples of wall decorations can be found. These decorations were more intricate and elaborate, with more paintings and patterns, all directly painted onto the walls. It wasn't until 200 BC that wallpaper appeared in China. And it wasn't until the 8th century when the Arabs introduced paper-making techniques to Europe that other records of wallpaper began to appear in historical texts. However, the use of paper as wallpaper did not immediately take off, as wealthy Europeans preferred to decorate their walls with textiles and leather. From both an aesthetic and practical standpoint, leather and textiles provided better protection against moisture and cold in homes that lacked sufficient heating at the time.

Throughout the 15th and 16th centuries, tapestries, leather, wood paneling, and tiles dominated the walls of the wealthy, although there is evidence that some handmade painted wallpapers were beginning to be used. In the 17th century, the availability of large quantities of commercial-grade paper expanded the functionality of wallpaper, giving rise to the wallpaper industry. In 1839, Britain's Imperial Decorating Company Ltd. was the first to use machine-printed wallpaper, marking the beginning of modern wallpaper production.

It is no surprise that the first mass-produced wallpapers imitated textiles, with flocked paper becoming a substitute for luxurious Italian brocade and velvet. A century later, hand-painted scenic panels emerged in France, originally painted on wooden panel walls, but only affordable by the nobility. Some designs were so complex that producing scenic wallpapers required up to 3,000 woodcut blocks to recreate popular town scenes, classical ruins, and mythological themes. Printed on durable linen rag paper, some of these wallpapers have survived to this day.

A revolutionary change in the wallpaper industry occurred in the mid-19th century with the advent of mechanical paper printing technology, making wallpaper production faster, cheaper, and more widely available. While production increased, design standards declined, and the wallpapers sold were no longer as beautiful or detailed as hand-painted ones.

It wasn't until the early 20th century that the reformed wallpaper industry revived. In the 1930s, silk-screen processing became popular. Modern wallpaper manufacturers, using technology, computers, and advanced dyeing techniques, combined with developments in pigments and materials, brought wallpaper design and manufacturing to new heights, improving quality and making wallpaper easier to apply.

As early as the Tang Dynasty in China, people began drawing on paper to decorate walls. In the mid-18th century, the British Morris began mass-producing printed wallpapers, marking the advent of modern wallpaper. As times changed, the development of wallpaper evolved alongside the global economy and culture, progressing through various stages: from plain paper, hand-painted paper, foamed paper, printed paper, registered embossed paper, to special process paper.

Like other decorative materials, wallpaper has continuously developed and changed with the global economic and cultural progress. The use of wallpaper in different periods reflects the local economic development level, advances in new materials science, and prevailing consumer psychology.

The earliest wallpapers were created by painting or printing various patterns on paper, offering some decorative effect but limited to use in royal courts and other high-end venues for partial decoration. It wasn't until the late 1970s and early 1980s that wallpaper truly entered common households alongside other decorative materials. Throughout the 1980s, foamed wallpaper was prevalent. Foamed wallpaper, also known as screen-printed embossed foam, involves adding foaming agents to raw materials and applying high temperatures during production to create a fermentation-like process. This results in wallpaper with a textured feel and soft touch. The advantage of such wallpaper is its strong three-dimensional effect, enhancing the spatial perception of a room. However, the disadvantages are obvious: it is not wear-resistant, easily scratched, and prone to dirt. Currently, foamed wallpaper is gradually being phased out but can still be seen on ceilings in some rooms.

By the late 1980s, with advancements in the plastic industry, vinyl-coated wallpaper emerged as a replacement for foamed wallpaper. This type of wallpaper does not foam, making it firmer and significantly improving upon the drawbacks of foamed wallpaper. It is waterproof, moisture-resistant, durable, features fine prints, excellent embossed textures, and can display a variety of patterns and designs. Its color representation and practicality opened a new chapter in wallpaper development. Currently, vinyl-coated wallpaper accounts for about 70% of global usage.

When vinyl-coated wallpaper was first introduced, satin-finished wallpaper, which exudes a glossy texture, gained widespread popularity. Later, as trends in home decoration shifted, matte-finished wallpaper (fabric-textured wallpaper) began leading the trend. Wallpaper's ability to express color and patterns became increasingly appealing, gaining widespread consumer acceptance. Manufacturers engaged in fierce competition as a result.

Currently, the penetration rate of wallpaper in Japan and Russia approaches 100%, while in European and American countries, it ranges between 50%-65%. In China, however, the penetration rate is less than 1%! With China's economic development and continuous improvement in living standards, wallpaper, as a necessity of life, will surely enter ordinary households!

Wallpaper, as a decorative material capable of beautifying environments, is suitable for many settings:

Home use: living rooms, bedrooms, dining rooms, children's rooms, studies, entertainment rooms, etc.

Commercial spaces: hotels, restaurants, department stores, shopping malls, exhibition halls, etc.

Administrative spaces: office buildings, government agencies, schools, hospitals, etc.

Entertainment spaces: restaurants, karaoke bars, bars, nightclubs, teahouses, coffee shops, etc.
