George Chu, Vice President of Yahoo! Northern Research: Yahoo! Northern Research is more like a start-up company.

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 18:33:06

Introduction: At the age of 12, George Chu moved to the United States where he was influenced by the entrepreneurial stories of Steve Jobs and Bill Gates, aspiring to enter the IT industry. After working at Microsoft for 13 years and being involved in various Visual Studio development tools, George decided to return to China for further development. He felt that Yahoo's Beijing R&D Center resembled a startup company, so two years after returning to China, he joined Yahoo. As Vice President of Yahoo's Beijing Global R&D Center, George Chu was interviewed by CSDN's "Dialogue with CTO" column, sharing experiences about the culture, team, research and development, and talent management at Yahoo's Beijing R&D Center.

Vice President of Yahoo's Beijing Global R&D Center, George Chu

The following is the interview transcript:

"To me, Yahoo's Beijing R&D feels more like a startup company."

CSDN: Please talk about your professional experience and your impressions of Yahoo's Beijing Global R&D Center (hereinafter referred to as "Yahoo North").

George Chu: I moved to the U.S. at the age of 12, inspired by the entrepreneurial spirit of Steve Jobs and Bill Gates, determined to pursue a career in computing. Before joining Yahoo, I worked at Microsoft for 13 years, focusing on the development of Visual Studio tools, including work on the first versions of C# and .NET, compilers, debuggers, IDEs, parallel computing, and Windows Azure.

Four years ago, I decided to return to China for new opportunities. My interest lies in the internet, and China's internet sector is developing rapidly, presenting an opportunity for me. Although I grew up in the U.S., I have Chinese heritage, and both my family and I have always hoped to come back to China to personally experience its rapid growth.

Even though Microsoft is one of the world's top software companies and a great organization, if I wanted to learn about internet commerce and technology, I needed to find companies that were disrupting the world, like startups. Two years ago, I joined Yahoo's Beijing Global R&D Center.

In terms of business and technology, Yahoo is undoubtedly one of the best in the industry, and here I can learn what I want to learn.

Simultaneously, to me, Yahoo North feels more like a startup. Indeed, it started from nothing and has grown to its current size in just four short years. During these four years, we've moved offices three times, expanding from 10 people to over 350. The company continues to grow rapidly, and even though I haven't experienced all these changes firsthand, I can still feel the entrepreneurial passion within the company. People don't work just for the sake of working or innovate just for innovation; everyone enjoys thinking and sharing their opinions to make things better. This is the passion of a startup company. Unlike many other mature enterprises, we don't have strict regulations, rigid hierarchies, or complex processes; everyone can develop freely and communicate equally.

Besides the entrepreneurial mindset, what impressed me most about Yahoo North is its emphasis on technical talent. As a research and development institution, R&D technicians are undoubtedly the lifeblood of Yahoo North. Beyond generous benefits and a pleasant working environment, Yahoo North constantly reinforces the sense of ownership among R&D personnel, offering them broader and more diverse development opportunities. Yahoo North also has a relatively complete training system, allowing junior R&D personnel to quickly gain experience and grow through internal resources.

Personally, I also love the delicious free desserts and coffee provided daily at Yahoo North.

CSDN: In your career, what aspects of different roles do you find most interesting? What aspects are the most challenging?

George Chu: Throughout my career, I have indeed taken on many different roles, such as programmer, development manager, and team manager. Among these roles, I am most interested in those that encourage brainstorming and create something entirely new. For me, transforming initial ideas into excellent ones is the most difficult but also the most valuable task.

CSDN: During your career, is there anything you would handle differently if given another chance?

George Chu: Yes, I would say paying more attention to family. Regardless of the position held, people in the software development industry are very busy, often working around the clock. If I could go back and choose to handle some things differently, I think I would spend more time with my family.

Team: Yahoo Beijing Global R&D Center holds a crucial strategic position

CSDN: What is the current scale and structure of Yahoo North's technical team?

George Chu: Yahoo North was established four years ago, and currently, we have about 350 engineers, all top talents. In my impression, they are mostly young, passionate, creative, and proactive.

Currently, Yahoo North's business structure can be divided into three major R&D directions: science-driven personalization and advertising, mobile platforms and applications, and cloud computing.

CSDN: What is the mission of Yahoo North, and what is the R&D model? What advantages does it have, and what challenges does it face?

George Chu: "Core strength · Global impact" is both our development strategy and our mission. Today, Yahoo North has become a key driver of innovation for Yahoo, playing a significant role in Yahoo's revival and transformation into a technology company. I must ensure that Yahoo's vision in product and technology is realized, and we must be responsible for providing the best user experience to over 700 million users globally.

In terms of development models, Yahoo North does not have particularly unique practices; agile development is common. The key lies in the way of thinking during development. Many young developers tend to experiment first, then think, then modify, which may work in computational science experiments but might not be ideal in engineering. This traditional method is somewhat akin to sculpting—taking a block of stone, chiseling away at it until the final piece emerges. However, engineering development should be more like injection molding—knowing exactly what the final product should look like beforehand and preparing the mold accordingly, making the process smoother. Therefore, I require them to think first, from macro to detail, clearly identifying what adds the most value to users before proceeding, aiming for a one-time success. For most developers, this is a reverse thinking mode, and gradually establishing this awareness and putting it into practice is often our biggest challenge.

Actually, some advantages have already been mentioned, such as the aforementioned thinking approach, which represents our advantage in development philosophy. Emphasis on talent is our management advantage, and our three major R&D directions—science-driven personalization and advertising, mobile platforms and applications, and cloud computing—are also areas where our technological strengths lie. We have contributed numerous products and technologies to Yahoo in these fields. The reason we can achieve this is because we adhere to "science-driven engineering development." The close integration of scientific research and engineering development is embedded in the DNA of Yahoo Beijing Global R&D Center. Scientific research is Yahoo's secret weapon for providing unique, relevant, and personalized mobile and internet experiences. Through scientific research, we aim to provide richer user interactions, more relevant ad recommendations and content recommendations, and seamless cross-device user experiences, thereby encouraging advertisers to promote their products and services on Yahoo's internet platforms through display ads and search ads.

CSDN: What achievements has Yahoo North made in the past two years? Given its important strategic position within Yahoo, how do you think Yahoo North should perform to match its status?

George Chu: I believe Yahoo North has already achieved this status, and the key indication is that many R&D institutions in China are still involved in peripheral product development and localization for multinational companies, whereas our tasks mainly involve Yahoo's core products and technologies, which directly affect the experience of over 700 million global users.

One of the most important projects currently is OnePush, which is already applied in key Yahoo products like Flickr, Yahoo Mail, and Yahoo Cricket, and will gradually support all Yahoo desktop, mobile, and web applications in the future.

Previous major achievements include the mobile client development of heavyweight applications like Yahoo! Messenger, Flickr, and mobile search; the development of intelligent search technology and the optimization of search and display advertising platforms; the development of IDEs, packaging technologies, and runtime libraries related to Yahoo's cocktail platform; and the development of cloud computing platforms supporting SMS interaction and ad systems.

CSDN: Yahoo North has been established for four years now. How do you see its future direction? Or, what kind of Yahoo North would you like to build with the over 300 technical professionals?

George Chu: Yahoo North has always been dedicated to providing innovative technologies, top-notch products, and outstanding development platforms for Yahoo's global business. We will continue to do so in the future. Our culture embodies the dual genes of Silicon Valley and startups, offering a relaxed environment, many talented individuals, and a strong learning atmosphere, promoting rapid growth.

From the perspective of Yahoo North's own development, we have always aimed for a "triple-win" vision: First, Yahoo Beijing Global R&D Center provides employees with opportunities to work on the company's global core projects and offers a relaxed working environment while actively collaborating with local research institutions and technology communities, letting talent "win." Second, with the help of Chinese wisdom, Yahoo Beijing Global R&D Center helps Yahoo Company "win." Finally, by bringing world-class core projects to China, we enhance the country's innovation capabilities, making China "win." The triple win will remain our consistent development and effort direction.

R&D: Yahoo is essentially open-source

CSDN: During the development process, how do Yahoo's engineers maintain project transparency and avoid duplication?

George Chu: On a large scale, for possible overlaps between projects, we identify commonalities through reviews and formulate solutions. Internally, teams use various tools to prevent repetitive work, including VersionOne and Rally, among others. Each team uses the same tools. Transparency is very important at Yahoo. Knowing what each other is working on fosters suggestions.

CSDN: If engineers encounter difficulties during project development, can engineers from different projects usually help each other?

George Chu: Engineers can collaborate when they have completed their assigned tasks. Product development always encounters difficulties, and teamwork becomes essential at such times. Yahoo values interaction highly. Through these interactions, engineers learn how to leverage others' experiences to expedite product creation. Socrates once said, "I know only that I know nothing." The more capable a person is, the more humble they become, realizing the boundless nature of learning. I hope Yahoo's engineers maintain Socrates' spirit of inquiry.

CSDN: Regarding the amount of data and resources available to Yahoo's engineers for research, testing, and development, how much access do they have?

George Chu: Yahoo is very open-minded. If you believe something is right and beneficial to the company, you can innovate. During the innovation process, you can utilize all of Yahoo's resources, including Hadoop cluster resources, whether for your project or for unrelated matters.

CSDN: Speaking of Hadoop cluster resources, can you discuss Yahoo's performance in open-source contributions?

George Chu: Essentially, Yahoo is an open-source company. Our technology not only comes from the community but also gives back to it. For example, Hadoop—Yahoo currently uses Apache version 0.23, to which we have made significant contributions. Yahoo has a strong reputation and influence in the Apache community, and Yahoo's technology is exceptional. Yahoo's Hadoop clusters are of a very large scale. Typically, the industry follows our large-scale deployment of a particular Hadoop version before adopting it themselves because this version is considered "Yahoo-verified," and thus trustworthy.

Conversely, if we achieve excellent results in any area, we are also willing to give back to the community.

Regarding open-source tools, many of the tools used by Yahoo engineers are open-source.

Talent: Entering Yahoo requires meeting the 3A standards

CSDN: You mentioned earlier that Yahoo attracts many outstanding engineers. What do you think is the key factor to becoming an exceptional engineer?

George Chu: I believe that learning never ends. Who is the smartest person in the world? Socrates. Why? Because Socrates kept learning continuously. Socrates famously said, "I know only that I know nothing." Therefore, maintaining humility and a spirit of learning is an important factor in continuous progress. To become an exceptional engineer, this is necessary.

CSDN: What are the advantages and disadvantages of Chinese technical talent (software engineers, scientists) compared to engineers and scientists from other countries? If there are disadvantages, how does Yahoo North address them?

George Chu: Advantages include Chinese engineers being skilled in teamwork and focused on technology. Disadvantages include English being the preferred communication language in the company, which might cause some discomfort. However, the company provides various trainings to help improve language and communication skills.

CSDN: What type of talent does Yahoo currently need the most? What qualities should these people possess? Do you personally interview programmers being recruited?

George Chu: We need all kinds of good talent. In terms of qualities, I believe learning ability and adaptability are the primary factors. Analyzing from a talent perspective, I think people entering Yahoo should meet three A's:

The first is Aptitude, the ability to do things. Many people can handle familiar tasks easily, but may struggle with unfamiliar areas. This reflects issues with aptitude, or ability. As I mentioned earlier, Yahoo is striving for innovation, requiring employees to adapt and grasp new technical fields.

The second is Altitude, height. Yahoo doesn't just look at how excellent a candidate's past is, but also considers their future long-term development potential. Whether an employee can continuously learn, grow, take on increasing responsibility, plan their career, and maintain stability are all factors we focus on.

The third is Attitude, attitude. There are no smooth sailing paths in work, and we encounter challenges when collaborating with others. Can employees persevere and overcome these obstacles?

Among these three A's, I believe the last A is the most important.

During the interview process, I personally interview all candidates. Yahoo ensures fair evaluation for all applicants.

CSDN: You mentioned the 3A standard for talent. How does Yahoo internally cultivate employee competencies?

George Chu: Cultivating talent is more important than finding it.

In terms of cultivation methods, we believe not only in self-learning but also understanding how others learn. Everyone shares their experiences with each other, collectively improving. In this aspect, Yahoo has been very successful. Yahoo has created a good platform for engineers to interact and learn from each other. Yahoo regularly holds Tech Pulse events, which are great opportunities for employees to exchange knowledge. Additionally, Yahoo emphasizes project transparency, ensuring transparency between projects. As the Chief Architect of the Beijing Global R&D Center, I am involved in every project, so I can share my views on each project with many colleagues, fostering mutual exchanges. Through project transparency, everyone gains insights into each other's projects, enabling interaction and exchange, and promoting mutual learning among engineers.

In terms of cultivation directions, Yahoo aims to nurture three types of "strength" in engineers:

The first is hard strength, referring to technical competence. When working on projects, all engineers rely on their technical abilities to deliver results.

The second is soft strength. Hard strength refers to doing things, while soft strength refers to interpersonal skills. When several brilliant engineers collaborate, differing perspectives can lead to debates and disagreements. At such times, engineers need to use their soft skills to consolidate ideas and create solutions acceptable to everyone.

The last point is that if engineers possess both hard and soft strengths, they can achieve the third type of strength, clever strength. Yahoo nurtures talents who not only focus on executing projects but also effectively communicate externally, making others aware of and trust in their products.
