Six Kinds of Advanced Workplace Eloquence

by 304885068 on 2009-02-12 09:47:01

1. Pouring-out style:

This is the most intense form of emotional and intellectual exchange, which is based on maximum dependence on the listener. It involves telling the other person all about your joys, troubles, grievances, as well as your plans and grand visions, allowing them to help, judge, and choose.

2. Silent-listening style:

When you're unsure about the direction of the other person's thoughts, silent listening can help buy time and clarify things. While silently listening, you should respond with nods, smiles, gestures, or meaningful facial expressions that align with the speaker’s emotions, drawing their attention and guiding the conversation. The speaker can find comfort and strength in these simple cues, embodying the idea that "silence speaks louder than words," often leading to a productive dialogue.

3. Interactive style:

During pauses in the other person's speech, interjecting appropriately to express your views can promote the exchange of ideas and feelings. However, when interjecting, it's important to do so at the right time and in moderation; avoid rudely interrupting or making irresponsible comments. Through interactive conversation, the other party may feel a sense of resonance with you, gaining psychological satisfaction and thus feeling pleased.

4.启发式:

For those who are not good at expressing themselves, patient guidance from various angles can encourage them to open up and gain new insights through conversation, motivating themselves to improve. When using this approach, choose words that are gentle and tactful, either offering an initial thought for others to build upon or providing thoughtful prompts, without being impatient or overly direct.

5. Jumping style:

Sometimes, conversations happen without prior arrangement, during casual gatherings, or unexpected encounters where opportunities are rare. In such cases, it's important to skillfully shift topics. By making frequent transitions, guide the conversation towards the intended subject. Maintain a gentle and friendly attitude to make the other person feel at ease, avoiding forcing the conversation in a certain direction.

6. Interval style:

After long periods of conversation, both physical fatigue and mental exhaustion may set in. At such times, take a short break, perhaps by listening to music or playing chess, before resuming the conversation.
