Listening Class Reflection

Yesterday, I attended five Chinese language classes at the sixth annual Chinese teaching skills competition held in Tai'an City, North Shi Xiao School. All five lessons were based on the same topic, the 26th lesson from the Grade 5 lower semester Chinese S Edition textbook, "The Young Run Tu". This text was written by Mr. Lu Xun, with lengthy content that includes many semi-classical oral expressions and sentences, making it challenging for students who are encountering it for the first time. The teaching styles of the five teachers varied, as did their approaches to the article and their instructional designs, each having unique aspects worthy of my learning. In particular, the teacher who taught the first class gave me the most inspiration, delivering an excellent lecture.

This teacher’s analysis of the text was very accurate and well-placed. They grasped the main content of the article as a whole, clarified its key points and difficulties, and established a clearly defined teaching process with closely linked steps that remained orderly throughout. The teaching consistently revolved around one central theme, progressively deepening until the students fully immersed themselves in the text and understood it. The teacher’s introduction was highly relevant to the students' actual situation because the competition required the teacher not to be familiar with the students beforehand but to prepare only one day in advance with the given topic. Students might have just learned what they would study right before class. Therefore, the teacher began the class by proposing three different levels of requirements: students who hadn't finished reading the text should continue reading; those who had finished could re-read sections of interest; and after finishing the text, they should think about what events were narrated between "me" and Run Tu. Subsequently, the teacher guided the students to divide the text into three parts: acquaintance, interaction, and farewell. After a transition phrase—"In this short month of interaction, Run Tu made me feel"—the teacher presented the sentence "Ah! Run Tu's heart contains countless strange stories," which became the main thread leading the classroom discussion. Focusing on "strange stories," the teacher guided the students back into the text, asking them to find and read dialogues where Run Tu told me interesting stories. Then, the students summarized these stories into four subheadings. Although there were only four summaries, the teacher emphasized prompting students to be sensitive to the words: "Did Run Tu only tell these four stories? How many?" The students quickly realized that beyond the explicit text, there were "countless strange stories." Next, the teacher posed an enlightening question: "If you were me, which story would interest you the most? Which one would you want to try?" This stirred up the students’ emotions, prompting them to speak up. Following this, the teacher shifted the topic again, guiding the students back into the text: "If time could rewind, which story would the 'me' in the text choose?" The students entered the text and found the dialogue "watching melons and spearing badgers." The teacher then guided silent reading practice. A great deal of effort was put into this part, with the dialogue being read at least three times, each time drawing the readers deeper into the characters' inner world. First came free reading to appreciate the interestingness of the event, followed by individual readings where the teacher provided hints for specific parts—"Do we guard against thieves?" guiding the students to read with a tone of curiosity; "Doesn't it bite people?" guiding the students to express my concern. Next, the teacher displayed a picture of "me" interacting with Run Tu, guiding the students to observe the image and describe how the two protagonists spoke and listened. Once again, the students connected emotionally with the characters—"telling vividly" and "listening intently"! The teacher seized the moment, bringing the students back into the text, incorporating their actions and expressions into the dialogue segment for partner performances. One can imagine how much the students enjoyed the Chinese language in this section! Afterwards, the teacher used poetic language to introduce the first natural paragraph of the text, displayed on the computer screen. "This image has accompanied me for ten years, twenty years, never fading away." The students read this passage together, feeling the beauty of the language and understanding the author's memories.

At this point in the teaching, the main content naturally led to the next phase. Using the method of reading instead of lecturing, the teacher covered the remaining three strange stories and once again presented "Ah! Run Tu's heart contains countless strange stories!" guiding the students to understand why the author felt such an emotion. Focusing on the word "Ah," the teacher let the students experience my feelings. With the previous thorough reading and immersion in the text, the students here understood exceptionally well. Subsequently, the teacher showed the sentence "four-cornered sky," creating a context for the students to imagine and contrastingly understand the monotony and lack of interest in my life. At this point, the students truly entered the inner world of the "me" in the text, and the understanding of this difficult sentence became clear like water flowing naturally. "Let us also forever remember this lovely young man!" Run Tu's image was already permanently etched in the students' minds! This is the success of the teacher's understanding and handling of the text, and the uniqueness and subtlety of the teaching design! I couldn't help but deeply admire and feel inferior!

Reflecting on this outstanding Chinese language class, full of the essence of Chinese literature, I realized that as a Chinese teacher, it is crucial to first correctly understand the text, grasp its essence, and conduct detailed textual analysis. Only when the teacher immerses themselves in the text and integrates with the characters can they stand at a higher perspective and a fresher angle to reorganize the text and design good teaching ideas with their own innovations. Additionally, another impression was the teacher's concise, classic, and subtly influential language in the classroom. Poetic transitional phrases, clear and enlightening questions, and appropriate evaluations of the students enriched the entire class with a strong sense of Chinese language and immersed everyone in the artistic atmosphere of classic literature.
