On the one hand, the tragedy of housekeeping clerks being damaged has been performed several times, and the rights of millions of housekeeping service personnel have been plagued by repeated violations. On the other hand, cases of nannies neglecting their duties or stealing, causing loss to the personal safety and property of family members, have become a recurring nightmare for many families. Relevant legal experts believe that all this originates from the overall lack of legal basis in the housekeeping service market.

Regarding this, Professor Yang Dawen from the Law School of Renmin University of China believes that such an understanding is actually artificial and lacks hard legal regulations. From a long-term perspective, the establishment of laws related to protecting the rights of housekeeping workers still needs to converge towards the "Labor Law." He believes that the labor performed by housekeeping workers shares significant commonalities with the labor defined as "laborer" under the law. It is inappropriate to completely exclude them from the scope of the "Labor Law." Many provisions in the law are also applicable to the group of housekeeping workers. Therefore, it can be considered to add a chapter specifically addressing housekeeping services on the basis of the "Labor Law" currently being revised, to achieve the purpose of protecting the rights of housekeeping workers.

President Li Dajing of the Beijing Housekeeping Service Association stated that since the process of waiting for national legislation could be quite lengthy, possibly lasting 5 to 10 years, and the rules and regulations formulated by the association are difficult to popularize in society, there is still a need for the introduction of government-enforced policies.

Currently, the relationship between housekeeping workers and employers is considered an employment relationship and therefore does not fall within the scope of adjustment by the "Labor Law." According to Article 4 of the "Explanations of the Supreme People's Court on Several Issues Concerning the Application of Law in the Trial of Labor Dispute Cases," disputes between families or individuals and housekeeping service personnel do not conform to the provisions of Article 2 of the "Labor Law" regarding employment entities and should be handled according to employment relationships. Moreover, due to the special nature of housekeeping services, such as working hours, welfare benefits, and labor protection, these cannot be regulated by the "Labor Law," which determines why housekeeping services are excluded from the scope of application of the "Labor Law." To date, China does not have any national laws or regulations standardizing the housekeeping service industry, thus determining its status as informal employment.

Faced with the lack of legal protection, how should housekeeping workers whose rights have been violated protect themselves? From a macro perspective, Wu Qing, a representative of the Haidian District People's Congress of Beijing and a professor at Beijing Foreign Studies University, proposed her suggestion: legislating solely for the legitimate rights protection of housekeeping service personnel is relatively narrow. Because among housekeeping workers, migrant farmers make up a large proportion, and these people are highly mobile. Doing housekeeping is often their first choice upon entering the city, but they may soon transfer to other industries. Therefore, a rational approach would be to legislate for the entire population engaged in informal work, expanding the protection coverage to the maximum extent, namely the so-called "Informal Employment Law."
