Singing techniques and methods

Singing Techniques

The interpretation of a song requires performance in many aspects.

Firstly, it's the voice. This is a very professional field of study, including breath control, vocal use, oral resonance, articulation methods, etc. In future articles, I will try to exchange what I know with everyone.

Secondly, it's the emotion. This requires singers to have a strong understanding of lyrics and melody. Many friends focus all their attention on the voice part when singing, neglecting the emotion, which will never result in a good song. The reason for over-focusing on the voice is due to limited singer ability, constantly thinking about where to pay attention to breathing, where to breathe, preparing for high notes, etc., which are unavoidable. Therefore, if you want your singing to be "emotionally rich," practicing is very important. When your vocal practice matures, your attention during singing will shift to emotion. There is a good saying: songs are not sung by the mouth, but by the heart. If your voice reaches a state of complete breath control and perfection, you can more freely inject emotion into your singing, making the performance more infectious.

Thirdly, it's the feeling. Here I emphasize again that musical feeling is innate; you have what you have, and it's hard to make up for it later. Pop songs require pop music feelings, folk songs require folk music feelings. For example, if you ask Yan Weiwén to sing "I Have Nothing," I guarantee it would make a bunch of people laugh! Different feelings among singers lead to different ways of handling songs. Some handle them very plainly, while others handle them in a way that touches the heart. These treatments are very subtle, such as a small breath, an emphasized accent, a long note without breathing or a sudden ending... These can only be understood intuitively.

Fourthly, it's rhythm and pitch accuracy. Have you ever seen a 3-year-old child who can hear a melody and tap out embellished rhythms? Have you ever seen a 3-year-old child singing live with his father playing the accordion, changing the pitch at will, yet still hitting the correct notes? Have you ever seen an 8-year-old child humming the sound of "1" in any pitch like "E-flat" or "C-sharp" without hearing any sound beforehand, and then pressing the keyboard to verify perfect accuracy? This is talent, and singing songs requires this foundation. Although you don't need to be as outstanding as them, you definitely cannot be weak.

Fifthly, it's quality. This is the difference between amateur singers and professional singers. In fact, comparing just in terms of voice, emotion, feeling, rhythm, and pitch accuracy, many amateur singers are not worse than professional singers. The gap lies in quality. Do you know your own posture and expression when singing? You may have a vague image, imagining yourself while fully engaged in singing, and this image is likely to be the original singer of the song you're singing. But these are all your imaginations, and your imagined image is almost perfect. Do you know what others see? Have you ever filmed yourself singing formally? Actually, from your first glance or first word on stage, people can tell whether you're an amateur or professional. The quality mentioned here also includes knowledge of music theory, instruments, music history, and common music knowledge, etc. Of course, many professional singers are not comprehensive either, but personally, I believe that if you want to be a professional singer, you should follow through on these things and deserve the title of "professional."

Understanding Lyrics

Before learning a new song, how many times do you read the lyrics from beginning to end in your mind? How many times have you pondered the thoughts and expressed imagery of the lyricist? Whether a song has lyrics first or music first, both authors carefully consider what the other (lyricist or composer) wants to express. If you completely ignore their painstaking efforts and just sing according to your own feelings, I think it's inappropriate. Some individualistic singers like to express themselves based on their own understanding, and this method isn't bad (and I like it too), but I would first understand the author's intentions before developing further. Moreover, not every song allows for arbitrary development; some lyrics and melodies don't offer better expressions, such as the national anthem.

After deeply understanding the song, your emotional handling will certainly improve a lot. If your perception ability is strong, you will naturally emphasize certain notes, lightly pass over others, and passionately drag out a full long note...

Speech and Singing

Besides melody, rhythm, and breath, are there other differences? Yes, that's the way of articulation. Please randomly read "Let me love enough" in your speaking manner, then passionately sing it out... You'll find that when singing, your mouth shape opens wider on the sides, and needs to be exaggerated. If you feel it's similar, then there's a problem with your articulation. Therefore, singing requires more exaggerated mouth shapes compared to speaking, making articulation clearer, breath smoother, and more conducive to expressing emotions.

When singing Mandarin songs, standard pronunciation is better. For example, the distinctions between "Z, C, S" and "an, ang" and "L, N" are easy to mix up. Hong Kong and Taiwan singers sometimes intentionally pronounce certain words without rolling the "r" or with rolling it, either because their tongues can't keep up, they think it sounds bad, or they simply don't know the correct pronunciation. When we cover their songs, I don't have a definitive stance—imitating without correcting is fine. However, if you want to develop your own style or participate in competitions, then corrections should be made.

Regarding Cantonese songs, I advise you to learn well before singing, otherwise, it's easy to be funny. Like my past experience singing Cantonese songs, I thought I was standard, but now I realize there was a gap. Cantonese also has subtle differences similar to Mandarin's "Z, C, S" and "an, ang," and serious learning is necessary.

Breath Control

Speaking of this topic, it feels a bit vague because it's the hardest thing to describe in words. Before singing, I practiced bel canto for two years, which gave me deep insights, and breath control greatly helped me. Generally, people who haven't studied vocal music produce flat sounds, using the normal speaking position (throat) to emit sound, which we call "white" sound, lacking color and filtering. Below, I will describe the bel canto vocal training method.

The voice should originate from the lower abdomen, imagining the sound passing through the back spine, to the back of the head, to the root of the oral cavity, the entire sound should be vertical and backward. Find this feeling: take a big bite of an apple, showing your upper teeth, and while biting down, make the "um" sound, feeling the sound at the back of the oral cavity and the upper part of the nasal cavity, which is one resonance point of bel canto. Next, sink your breath, expand your lower abdomen, slightly push with your lower abdomen, and make a sound in the previous position, male voices press down the larynx, female voices focus more on the position behind the head, and you will find the sound much louder than before. Regular wall-practicing helps with vocalization, as leaning against the wall allows you to easily find the sensation, and chest resonance can resonate with the wall, helping you find the sensation of chest resonance easier. Start with the "mi～ma" exercise, gradually increasing, which helps with high-note breath control; then practice from high to low, repeating this process until one day you find the sensation. Bel canto emphasizes "transparency," often imagining your voice as vertical rather than flat; it also emphasizes "resonance," where sound resonates through the chest or above, making it smooth and full. To experience resonance, you can close your mouth and make the "um" sound, slightly adding some heartfelt feeling, extending the "um," feeling vibrations in your head and chest, and if your breath control is good, this resonance will add color to your voice. Carefully listen to some tall Americans speaking, and you'll understand what chest resonance is (foreigners' chest structure makes it easier to produce resonance).

Additionally, I must mention lung capacity. Singers need large lung capacities, such as long, non-breathing melodic phrases, so you shouldn't finish singing red-faced and panting. Lung capacity must be maintained through regular physical exercise. Also, mastering certain techniques can compensate for insufficient lung capacity. First, practice breath control; good vocalization methods effectively control the airflow through the throat. For example, when singing "...not afraid of your betrayal...", singers with unstable breath might burst out the "afraid" character explosively, quickly depleting their already insufficient air. Singers with good breath control can manage these explosive sounds, avoiding popping sounds on the microphone. So, I suggest placing a candle in front of your mouth while practicing, trying to keep the candle flame steady! This way, your breath will stay within your body longer. Think about whether continuously exhaling lasts longer or holding your breath does. Of course, singers need to relax, not be nervous; once nervous, your heartbeat quickens, and your breath becomes even harder to control.

Here, I can only explain so much; these things require personal insight. However, I must remind you that vocal exercises are the foundation of singing pop songs, and you should not directly perform pop songs using vocal exercise methods, as that would be overly artificial, like Yan Weiwén singing "I Have Nothing."

Regarding the use of voice in pop songs, my view is that as long as it fits the song's imagery, shouting or breaking the voice is not unacceptable, but these are absolutely forbidden in bel canto. Therefore, we should take the strengths and make up for weaknesses, as we are not practicing bel canto to sing bel canto but to sing pop songs better, just like martial arts practicing horse stances—you don’t fight squatting in a horse stance, right?

Some friends say such explanations are hard to understand, asking me to identify which pop singers have transparent breath control, making it easier to learn by imitation. This is an effective method. Personally, I recommend female singers listen more to "A-Mei," who naturally has transparent breath control; male singers should analyze "Man Wénjūn," who truly sings with breath control. Additionally, Zhang Xueyou has great breath control, but for teaching purposes, Man Wénjūn is more suitable.

High Notes

This is the question friends love to ask most, as they put it, because high notes are "impressive," haha! Let me reiterate, good singing involves many aspects, and it's not true that the higher the high note, the better the singing. Indeed, excellent high notes can enhance the climax of a song, but I advise friends not to only value high notes while ignoring mid and low notes, as full mid and low notes are also very infectious.

When singing high notes, breath control must be rooted in the lower abdomen and dan tian, and Peking opera emphasizes "dan tian qi" referring to this vocal position.

Find the Feeling 1: If you're a girl and suddenly see a rat on your shoe, what would you do? Probably let out a long "ah～" with a "falsetto" vocal position. After calming down, try "ah～" again, and most likely, you won't be able to "ah" as high. Why? At the moment of fright, your lower abdomen tightens (think about the feeling of being startled—isn't your whole body muscle tensed?), precisely because of this foundation, combined with a powerful airflow (which can be understood as singing breath), rushing straight to the throat, it's no surprise the voice goes high. Therefore, you can understand the feeling of reaching high notes as "tension" and "excitement," remember, everything is rooted in the lower abdomen, and to reach high notes, it's not about lifting your head upward, but a rebound force felt by lowering the center of gravity.

Find the Feeling 2: Have you seen a rubber hose? What do you do to make the water spray farther? Pinch the front of the hose tightly, smaller gaps increase pressure, and the water shoots farther. High notes don't necessarily need to be loud; some singers sing high notes with a big sound, which means they are using strength and lung capacity rather than breath control and technique. Imagine the water faucet theory, and you should be able to understand some principles.

Find the Feeling 3: There's a huge rock on the ground, pick it up. You bend down, count in your mouth: "one, two, three, go..." I bet this "go" uses your breath. I won't elaborate further; feel it yourself, look around for something heavy nearby... haha! These are some self-created methods I use to help you feel the breath, not formal, but quite effective.

Also, let me tell you one more thing: the ability to perform high notes is related to your natural vocal conditions. If your voice is naturally high-pitched, it's a significant advantage, like Sun Nan, whose high-pitched voice makes high notes less strenuous. Singers with poor vocal conditions rely heavily on breath control and methods, but regardless of whether the vocal conditions are good or bad, the voice is produced through breath, just like cigarettes passing through a filter, filtered sounds are more beautiful.

Additionally, I'll share a psychological method: when singing high notes, imagine you're facing a vast ocean, singing loudly, the boundless sea and sky, doesn't this scenery deserve a loud shout?

Mid and Low Notes

Mid and low notes are generally best when full and emotional. However, like high notes, many lack mid and low note resonance. Low notes require chest resonance and a broad vocal structure, which is mostly innate. Based on my experience, it's difficult to train a voice that doesn't naturally reach low notes to go much lower; going down a few notes is already good. If you must record a song rich in low notes, record immediately after waking up without brushing your teeth or washing your face, and your voice will go much lower. Therefore, those lacking mid and low notes should avoid such songs, leveraging strengths and avoiding weaknesses. Those with rich mid and low notes should fully utilize their potential; well-sung mid and low notes are very charming!

To sing low notes, keep your chest and throat relaxed, sighing can strengthen some resonance, and at very low parts, adding some breath can create oral resonance, making the sound deeper. Regardless, those with naturally deep voices have more room to play.

To sing mid-range notes, add more color, including articulate articulation and breath changes, which of course relates to singing skills. Mid and low notes listeners should check out "Tsai Chin."

Whole Song and Style

Many singers only focus on the details between sentences, treating the unit of detail as "sentence" rather than the whole song.

Draw a wavy line across the entire song, and you'll know where to be subdued and where to be intense. Broaden your perspective, like position sense on a football field—how can you achieve effective long-pass attacks by focusing on local areas?

Unless you have a certain level of skill, don't talk about style. In the early stages, I support starting with imitation, but don't stick to one style. Sing various styles of singers' songs, and through osmosis, you'll grasp some resonance, tone, and breath control basics, which is a good thing. After maturing, combined with your own vocal exercises and insights, discovering your own style will come naturally.

Singing requires engagement, but engagement should have a limit, not forcing excessive effort, sadness, or joy. On the basis of understanding the lyrics, measured engagement is sufficient. For example, for similar lyrics like "...how could you bear to make me sad...", I've seen people sing in extreme pain, which lacks感染力and instead seems funny. But I've also seen a friend who truly went through a breakup sing this song at karaoke, with a blank face, calm singing, a沧桑voice, and the helplessness and loneliness conveyed through the singing moved everyone present. I think this is just the right measure, no need to cry. But how many people can consistently perform this well? This depends on the singer's musical sensitivity.

Let me give another example: Some friends exaggerate the characteristics of stars when imitating them. For instance, when imitating Jacky Cheung, they overly press their throats to produce a strange thick sound (a feature of Jacky Cheung's early period, which is rarely seen now); when imitating Leslie Cheung, they exaggerate his tongue-tied-like resonance at the back; when imitating Faye Wong, it's an awkward sound like a tied-up tongue. Actually, listening carefully to the original, it's not that exaggerated. I've heard a friend who sounds very like Jacky Cheung singing, but each time he sang Jacky's songs, he didn't grasp the measure, and later I advised him to be more natural, making him sound even more like the original. Sometimes, outsiders see clearly, so seeking opinions from friends can be very beneficial.

Moreover, emotionally rich people tend to interpret songs better than those who are emotionally bland. Resonating with the emotions in the song also requires singers to have rich life experiences. The more experienced, the more insightful.

This is something many friends pursue, haha! Thinking that without vibrato, it's not professional or impressive enough. Little do they know that vibrato is merely one way of expressing music in a song; does not having vibrato mean you can't sing well?

Vibrato, essentially, is the vibration produced when a powerful airflow impacts the back of the oral cavity and nasal cavity, with the pressure from the lower abdomen generating the airflow. I don't advocate that pop music must use such rigid methods to produce vibrato; some stars produce vibrato through larynx shaking (Andy Lau) and throat tightening and loosening (Jeffrey Chao). These are small techniques or styles, but in many climactic parts, they also have powerful airflow vibrato. So, the key phrase remains: breath control is fundamental. Using breath control, airflow impact naturally produces vibrato. For pop songs, some low-key parts don't need every word to be on breath control like folk songs; sometimes it's like whispering, sometimes like a helpless sigh, without powerful airflow supporting your vibrato. At such times, you need to use some small techniques to decorate your endings. I believe that vibrato in pop songs doesn't entirely rely on breath control; sometimes shaking the larynx and controlling the throat sound is acceptable, as long as it doesn't affect the overall atmosphere of the song.

Friends without vibrato, if they are determined to crave vibrato, can start with imitation—imitating ambulance sirens. Funny, isn't it? This is just a self-exploration method when there's no vocal teacher around, not a regular vocal exercise. Emitting "woo woo" cyclic high-low sliding tones (slightly fast) helps you find the sensation of larynx shaking. Gradually change the "woo woo" sound to "umm umm," then slowly to "nee～" and sing some songs ending with "nee"… haha, this is just a way to feel the larynx-shaking vibrato, different from breath-generated sound, guaranteed to make vocal teachers angry! But everything needs gradual progress, slow exploration, so give it a try.

Finally, let me restate my view: if we broaden our horizons, the current emphasis on vibrato in pop singing might not be popular in five years. Why worry about not having vibrato? Vibrato can effectively enhance the atmosphere of a song, deep and penetrating, but I also enjoy simple, pure voices. Therefore, as long as you sing sincerely, you might be even more distinctive.
