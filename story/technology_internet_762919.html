Dianping: A Bone the Giants Can't Chew, Living Outside the Comfort Zone

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 18:05:32

Tao Zhang, CEO of Dianping

By Yuan Yin, edited by Jing Yang, photographed by Nan Xiao

Tao Zhang, CEO of Dianping, has once again postponed the company's IPO.

In 2011, after receiving a $100 million Series C funding round from Sequoia Capital and others, Dianping was valued at $1 billion. According to convention, it should have chosen to go public within the next one to two years, but it didn't. Instead, in 2012, it raised another $60 million in its Series D round.

With Dianping's scale and development trend, going public would not be difficult. However, Tao Zhang believes that listing now would be too costly. He breaks down the large internet (PC + mobile) into four elements—people, information, goods, and services—each connection point presenting an opportunity for giants. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, Alibaba connects people with goods, and the space between people and services is the seat of the fourth giant. Since 2003, Dianping has been working hard to accumulate data, finally waiting for the explosion of mobile internet, where mobile phones merge "location" and "service." Following Zhang's logic, no company seems more fitting than Dianping for the imagination of the fourth giant.

But the outside world doesn’t see it this way. In recent months, with frequent acquisitions by BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent), Dianping has become a hypothetical acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition target; whichever family name from BAT it adopts could help it achieve great success early on. On the other hand, Dianping’s size pales in comparison to BAT, and it is still too early to declare it as a future giant. Moreover, whether it's Tencent WeChat's public platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after investing in AutoNavi and Meituan, all are directly targeting Dianping’s territory—local life services primarily centered around dining, drinking, entertainment, etc.

Before proving these two things, Dianping's IPO would be equivalent to a "discounted sale." First, Zhang needs time to prove that what Dianping does now, BAT cannot do. Second, Dianping is about to enter the ultimate battle of group buying. If it can secure the top position, its valuation will increase several times over.

2013 is Dianping's "decisive year." Regarding speculations and rumors, Zhang pays them no heed. He is very clear: "The internet has indeed entered an era of great integration, but Dianping will not be acquired."

Zhang categorizes companies that might be acquired into three types. The first type has platform attributes but is more suitable for development within the system of a giant, such as maps and emails. These products can easily attract large numbers of users but find it hard to establish independent business models. Some, like browsers, may make money, but the revenue they generate through navigation services is far less than their strategic value to large companies. "Giants are willing to spend money nurturing email products because they help with branding and user retention, but I've never heard of any independent email company succeeding." The second type involves industry consolidation, like in the video industry, where acquisitions expand market share. The third type includes companies facing financial difficulties who sell themselves for survival.

Now, Meituan has already been brought under Alibaba's wing. In theory, Dianping should quickly seek a "backer" to avoid being defeated due to the rapid expansion of its competitors after securing capital. Zhang, however, says, "The help from giants is limited. We indeed need users, but execution is even more important. Mergers and acquisitions would reduce our execution ability, and we might even get marginalized by larger companies." Not only that, he also wants Dianping to maintain the most precise "distance" from the giants. If it gets too close to any one of them, it would be akin to "taking sides," making it an enemy of the others.

Thus, logically speaking, acquiring Dianping has reached a dead end. Zhang counters, "Why would we take their money? We're not short of funds; what we need are resources. If a giant is willing to take a small stake and give us many resources, I might consider it. But would they? Would a giant give you resources if they only hold a small stake?"

Weapon One: Dirty and Hard Work

In mobile internet, opportunities for companies solely focused on pure internet products are becoming fewer. New opportunities come from those combining with traditional industries. Zhang believes that life services mainly revolve around dining, drinking, and entertainment. Other niche markets, such as ride-hailing, can form good business models, but there's little chance for them to grow into giants.

For dining, drinking, and entertainment, user demands are simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is the core of life services, which is exactly where Dianping started. Whether in content, member accumulation, or brand, newcomers find it hard to surpass Dianping.

The most representative product form of discounts is group buying. Group buying has now entered the championship race, and Dianping plans to face off against Meituan in 2013. This is a winner-takes-all market. As long as two strong players coexist, the industry's gross profit margin will be reduced, making stable profits impossible.

Another foreseeable future is reservations. Reservations can create a true online-to-offline closed loop, but they must be built on the foundation of Dianping's absolute advantage in information and discounts. Otherwise, creating a closed loop would be difficult. In 2012, Dianping began experimenting with reservations. Zhang said, "Currently, both users and merchants provide positive feedback, but if we were to focus solely on reservations, it would be hard to solve the chicken-or-egg problem."

Of course, life services present some heavier opportunities. For example, food delivery is a rigid demand for users and helps merchants increase sales. In the past few years, food delivery companies like "To Your Home Gourmet" have flourished. However, the core of food delivery is delivery, meaning it must be delivered within a short period of time, with issues like soup spillage needing resolution. The logistics requirements are higher than even fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang said, "If executed well, food delivery still presents opportunities, but it's harder dirty work, and what Dianping is doing now is already dirty and tiring enough."

If judged by age, Dianping is already an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has painstakingly accumulated merchant profiles and user reviews, which Zhang refers to as "dirty and hard work." This original growth model once restricted its speed, but the past pain has become today's core barrier for Dianping. It can be said that currently, apart from Meituan, Dianping has no real competitor in the field of life services.

However, he still needs to beware of BAT's "clutches." Baidu's involvement is the most direct. Baidu Maps already has over 100 million users, allowing users to search for nearby restaurants and other life services using the map. Tencent, considered to have secured a ticket to mobile internet, launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest goods trading platform, and expanding from physical goods to services is a natural progression, especially since it has incorporated Meituan and AutoNavi into its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there are no signs of explosive growth in reality or data," Zhang said. Brand positioning is crucial; the key is which product users choose first when they want to eat, drink, or play.

Moreover, in the field of life services with immense imagination potential, competition among giants is bound to be cross-sectoral, and no one can completely monopolize the field. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained on platforms like Weibo and WeChat. Zhang feels that this makes Dianping safer instead, "In niche areas like car rental and business travel, the massive user base directed by giants could easily overwhelm existing companies."

As for Alibaba, which invested in AutoNavi, wanting to invade Dianping's territory isn't that simple either. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, which can be categorized no matter how peculiar. A user's experience of a meal depends on factors such as the day's environment, waitstaff, and chef. Managing merchants cannot rely solely on systems and tools like Alibaba uses; it must involve a 'ground force' managed by people.

"All three giants want to take this path, and there is room for imagination, but it's not a highway; it's a rugged mountain road." Zhang believes that first, providing life services requires ground teams deployed at various levels, something BAT lacks the genes and capability for managing offline teams. Second, they lack the willingness; life services require dedicated effort, not something that can be accomplished by dispatching a small team, and earning money is slow. "A company finds it hard to excel in two important tasks simultaneously, let alone having to consolidate its advantages in the original field."

Weapon Two: Small Boats Turn Easily

When Dianping secured $100 million in financing in 2011, Zhang told outsiders that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, the world changed fast, and before Dianping went public, his analogous counterparts fell from grace.

He wasn't particularly troubled by this. "They failed to become big companies because they lacked offline teams, and now they want to be America's Dianping."

The current path is one Zhang has explored step by step. When he felt that selling keyword ads and coupons was outdated, the emergence of group buying caught his eye. Initially, he understood group buying as Americans did—as a marketing platform, one deal per day. But as orders increased, he realized group buying had the characteristics of a trading platform. Thus, he saw the future of Dianping as a life services trading platform. The two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were precisely used for Dianping's transformation from an information platform to a trading platform.

Without this transformation, Zhang might not have had the confidence to compete for the fourth spot among the giants today. But while the transformation sounds simple, it was equivalent to Dianping's second entrepreneurship, an extremely painful process.

First, compared to selling ads alone, the gross margin of a trading platform decreases, requiring stricter cost control and efficiency. Second, a trading platform demands higher inter-departmental communication capabilities.

To maintain Dianping's impartiality and independence as an information platform, the content team and sales team were completely separated. Zhang even deliberately established "firewalls" between departments—important clients couldn't alter information on the platform for sales purposes, leading to some mutual distrust between the content and sales teams. Now, as a trading platform, editors and sales teams need deep communication to achieve good marketing plans, requiring editorial cooperation beyond mere planning. He said, "Maintaining information quality remains crucial, but there's no need to reinforce animosity; we need to dismantle the firewalls."

Though editors may accept this logically, human behavior and values have inertia, and when combined across the entire company, the inertia becomes even greater. Zhang held several meetings specifically to promote inter-departmental communication. Months later, an editor manager casually told a sales manager, "I used to think you were my enemy, but now I realize you're my colleague."

The sales department also needed transformation. To promote conversion, Zhang cleverly gave the offline team dual roles. First, for merchants, salespeople should become marketing consultants, not just selling products but helping merchants develop marketing strategies. Second, for the company, marketing consultants are the company's buyers. Dianping is like a supermarket; not only must product quality be good, but the variety of goods must be rich. Marketing consultants should aim to help users purchase group-buying products. Of course, dual roles inevitably lead to a series of complex problems. Frontline staff may find it challenging to balance the scales themselves.

Substantially, Dianping's "dual role" is similar to Alibaba's division into 25 business units. Decentralizing decision-making to frontline personnel allows the company to react faster, but it increases management difficulty. Similarly, compared to frontline employees, the management team suffers more after Dianping's transformation.

Every year, Zhang decomposes Dianping's company goals into various departments, then executives break them down layer by layer. However, decomposition is not simple addition or subtraction. Li Jing, co-founder of Dianping, responsible for the sales team, sometimes finds unresolved merchant needs due to sales issues affecting sales performance. Therefore, Li Jing created a new position, setting up communication specialists to collect information daily from marketing consultants, submit it to other departments, and track feedback. He often tells his team, embracing change doesn't mean liking change; no one likes change, and everyone feels uncomfortable when faced with change. "All employees must pass through discomfort zones, including me."

Numbers illustrate the "heaviness" of Dianping's execution team. Currently, among the more than 3,000 employees, over 2,000 work offline. The ground force communicates directly with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group-buying products, and comprehensive marketing solutions.

For this core team resisting the claws of BAT, Dianping has a unique management evaluation system.

They aren't called "salespeople"; from day one, their business cards read "Marketing Planning Consultant." Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the phase of watching news and chatting on QQ, lagging at least three to five years behind Taobao merchants in understanding the internet. Marketing planning consultants must tell merchants what online users need and how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing said, "Even a high-end restaurant shouldn't be advised to offer abalone or shark fin group-buying deals; perhaps different dishes should be offered, as online and offline users have different needs."

An excellent marketing consultant must be a versatile player. They need to explain clearly to restaurant owners and hair salon owners the essential differences between discounts and group-buying; among various discounts, which better fits their own positioning. If facing a hot pot restaurant owner, the marketing consultant must know whether it's the off-season or peak season, which products' costs rise in this season, and which costs decrease, proposing a group-buying menu that both merchants and users prefer.

This places high demands on marketing consultants, who must not only be experts in internet marketing but also industry experts. Li Jing said, "I must admit, it's impossible for every consultant to be a double expert, so we rank consultants with stars." Like hotels, marketing consultants range from one star to five stars, with the best being superstars called Super Stars.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline employees are not tech-savvy graduates from prestigious schools, making it challenging for them to understand internet products. Dianping established a knowledge base for marketing consultants, containing knowledge points and relevant cases that both new and old employees can browse. If encountering new problems, employees can ask questions within the knowledge base, and thousands of consultants nationwide will help answer them.

A large number of knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees based on practical experience. Dianping set up a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, rewarding employees who help others the most each quarter. Every quarter, Dianping also conducts skills assessments for employees, linking assessment results directly to promotions.

Compared to internet knowledge, industry knowledge is harder to acquire. Some experiences are summarized by traditional merchants after years of industry practice. A Super Star marketing consultant told Li Jing that the hairdressing industry should avoid offering shampoo-cut-blow-dry package deals because men lack motivation to buy them, and women often blow-dry their hair without needing the full service. Li Jing was astonished, "We men would never think of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing consultants are not only divided by city but also by industry, such as catering, hairdressing, leisure, etc. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is further subdivided into hot pot, Western cuisine, buffets, etc., with one marketing consultant responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing consultants can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, making merchants less resistant to selling products," he said.

Each marketing team holds its own case-sharing sessions: what difficulties a certain type of merchant encountered, what marketing needs they have, which products were used in the solution, and how effective it was. Outstanding cases are reported upwards, and Dianping officially shares one case each week.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition—Startup Winner. Within the first three months of employment, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products but listening to merchants sharing their experiences. If they are responsible for hot pot restaurants, after three months, they must submit a business plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hot Pot Restaurant Owner." Like graduation, employees present their defense, and industry supervisors evaluate the winners, rewarding them appropriately.

Strategically speaking, regardless of Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, what they need to do is quite similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O are common knowledge for anyone familiar with the industry. "The era when success depended on storytelling and concept creation has passed. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model lies in management and execution, not strategy," Zhang said.
