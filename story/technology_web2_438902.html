Centipedes are oviparous animals. They usually mate on sunny days after rain from March to June. Every spring and early summer, the eggs gradually develop and mature. From mid-June, they start laying eggs. The peak egg-laying period is in the first and second ten-day periods of June and July, continuing until early August. Before laying eggs, the mother's body becomes bloated with her abdomen close to the ground, moving slowly. For mothers showing signs of egg-laying, about 3-4 days prior, use a bottomless canister approximately 10 cm in diameter to cover each one individually. Cover the canister opening with fiberboard or glass. This effectively utilizes the breeding area, avoiding mutual interference and predation, facilitating smooth egg-laying in a quiet environment, and making it easier to separate young centipedes from the mother (or into different pools).

Generally, each mother lays 20-60 eggs, and the smooth egg-laying process takes about 2-3 hours. The eggs stick together, resembling the size of peanuts, elliptical in shape. The mother centipede guards the eggs for around 43 days. During this period, if disturbed or subjected to other external stimuli, they may become agitated and aggressive, potentially eating all the hatching egg masses, leading to hatching failure. Therefore, it is crucial to maintain absolute silence in the hatching pool environment. Especially note that feeding should be stopped a few days before the centipede lays eggs. During the 40-50 days of the hatching process, feeding must be strictly prohibited; otherwise, the centipede might eat food that gets stuck on the egg mass or embryo, consuming the egg mass or embryo as well. The hatching process is relatively slow; after 15-16 days, the eggs form kidney shapes and begin to crack open, marking the first molting stage. After 20 days, the second molting occurs, forming a crescent shape, initially resembling a larva. Around 35-40 days, the third molting begins, allowing the young to crawl up and down within the mother’s embrace. After another 5-7 days, the young centipede's exoskeleton thickens, turning yellow-brown. Under the mother's protection, they live independently after about a month, seeking their own food.

Three, Precautions

When breeding centipedes, attention should be paid to possible viral infections affecting the centipedes themselves. Additionally, breeding personnel should prevent being stung and poisoned by centipedes.

1. Preventing disease in centipedes: Due to the threat from poultry, rats, geckos, etc., especially ants which are the main natural enemies of centipedes, it is necessary to regularly observe whether there are any harmful insects inside and outside the centipede farm to prevent these predators from invading and attacking the centipedes. To prevent ants from entering the pool, you can draw several circles around the pool with cockroach and ant powder pens, or mix one part camphor balls and one part plant oil with five parts sawdust, stirring evenly. Then frequently sprinkle this powder on the outer walls of the breeding pool.
