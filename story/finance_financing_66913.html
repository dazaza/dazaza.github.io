Will the Family SNS Model Work in China?

by fenqu on 2008-05-05 14:28:18

Is a Family-Based SNS Model Viable in China?

China is a country that places great emphasis on familial blood ties. Currently, the SNS (Social Networking Service) model is popular worldwide, and China has launched similar platforms to Facebook, such as Xiaonei (Renren) and Hainei, along with some peripheral SNS websites like Roline and Lianjia. So, is a family-based SNS website viable in China?

Based on the author's observations, there are nearly a dozen family-based SNS websites in China, such as "My Home My Show" (www.5j5x.com) and "Family Connect" (www.famioo.cn). However, not many of them have distinct features. "My Home My Show" focuses on bloodline family connections and tracking, with its core product being the family tree. On the other hand, "Family Connect" emphasizes social connections between families. Both "My Home My Show" and "Family Connect" are still in their infancy in China, but both websites boast impressive technology. The key will be how well they execute their market operations in the future.

Is China a suitable land for the development of family-based SNS models? Why?

1. China is a country that highly values family bloodlines. Historically, there has been a tradition of compiling genealogies. For instance, in the Confucius family, each dynasty provided government subsidies to maintain their family records, a practice that has continued for over 2000 years—a world wonder. Given this excellent traditional virtue, why can't the traditional compilation of genealogies and the maintenance of bloodline relationships be moved online? It just requires some adjustments to better leverage network tools for maintaining family bloodline connections.

2. The principle of reciprocity can more easily promote the development of family-based SNS websites. In China, reciprocity is highly emphasized. In real life, it is a form of etiquette, but what about online? Making reciprocity more convenient aligns with the fast-paced lifestyle and widespread use of the internet. More people are choosing to stay in touch with friends and family through the internet or text messages. Family-based SNS websites provide a convenient service platform for this kind of interaction.

3. Lifestyle patterns require the services of family-based SNS websites. In large cities like Shanghai and Beijing, people live in closed-off buildings, and interactions among relatives and friends are decreasing. Most communication happens via QQ, MSN, email, or text messages. While these tools play a certain role, they are not centralized and cannot effectively manage relatives and friends who share blood ties. Currently, China's business SNS and campus SNS platforms are built around business acquaintances or schoolmates, but there is no SNS platform centered on family blood ties. This represents an untapped market.

Although China has fertile ground for the survival and development of family-based SNS websites, how promising is their future? With China's large population, naturally, there are also many families. However, currently, 80% of China's population resides in rural areas, so the prospects for family-based SNS websites are not optimistic. Additionally, the profit model is quite unclear. Even China's relatively successful campus SNS website, Xiaonei (Renren), lacks a clear profit model. 51.COM is also still exploring ways to generate profits.

If China launches an SNS website specifically for family networking, would you register and use it? Why would you register? Is it because your relatives' families have already registered? How can family relationships be maintained through a network platform? These are all questions that family-based SNS websites need to consider.
