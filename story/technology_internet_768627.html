Amazon's Most Powerful Weapon: Bezos's Obsession

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 20:15:30

Let me ask you to do something scary: Imagine you're Jeff Bezos, and you've just spent the morning reviewing your retail empire. How do you feel? Confident? Or filled with a deep, abiding paranoia that your competitors are already at the gates?

I bet it's the latter delusion. In fact, I suspect your delusions explain Amazon.com Inc.'s recent spate of crazy-expansionary moves, from launching Sunday delivery to getting into groceries to offering live, face-to-face tech support on the Kindle Fire.

What else could Bezos possibly be paranoid about? Impermanence. In the retail business Bezos inhabits, all innovations are quickly noticed and copied, profit margins are often driven to zero (in part thanks to Bezos), and customer loyalties shift with every new fad or fancy. The Internet gave Amazon an enormous advantage, but it also came at a steep price: its business leaves virtually no impression in the minds of its customers.

Hence the fury: Amazon is rushing to embed itself in the fabric of global commerce so deeply that it becomes an unavoidable part of everyone's shopping habits — before its competitors fully understand what it's up to.

The Sunday-delivery gambit shows how. It requires throwing every resource available into building a platform for shopping so magnificent that it rewrites the major features of modern commerce, from server farms to shipping to customer service to marketing. Indeed, consider many of Amazon's recent moves, including its $600 million contract to store data for the CIA, and it begins to seem less like a shopping site and more like a global infrastructure company — Maersk rather than Macy's.

The idea is to make this platform an inevitable part of all your shopping activity, even when you're buying stuff sold by other companies. That's when it will really start making money. The U.S. Postal Service deal is only one small piece of that plan, but it reveals the grand strategy. Over the past two years, Amazon has been rapidly expanding its operations to get products delivered faster. In the past, it had placed large warehouses in low-cost states to ship goods to the rest of the country, avoiding collecting state sales taxes from most of its customers.

Then Bezos changed tactics. He struck deals with some states that offered Amazon temporary tax holidays, dropping his opposition to sales taxes in exchange for building warehouses across the country, including many near big cities. Storing goods closer to customers has allowed Amazon to lower shipping costs while offsetting the higher prices caused by the sales taxes. The new warehouses have also enabled faster shipping, including same-day delivery in some cities.

Amazon continues pushing harder, offering Sunday delivery to customers at no extra cost — a service that might delight them enough to get loyal Amazon users to pass their affection along to friends. That sense of delight is key to the plan. By outspending its rivals, Amazon can offer a service so surprising that it pushes every other competitor in the field to spend similar sums to compete.

Here's a good example: Not long ago, when I tried to return a pair of too-large, $13 gym shorts bought on the site, I received a stunning message: As a valued customer, I didn't need to return the item to receive my refund. In other words, after calculating my value as a customer against the cost to the company of taking back the item, Amazon was willing to absorb the loss in the sale.

The postal-service deal also demonstrates Amazon's cunning attempt to commoditize the express-shipping industry. By helping prop up a struggling U.S. Postal Service, Amazon creates the possibility that FedEx Corp. and United Parcel Service Inc. will compete with the Postal Service. If Sunday delivery proves popular enough among other retailers, these private giants may be forced to offer it as well.

But that's only part one. Amazon's larger goal is to embed these shippers within its own shopping platform so deeply that Amazon — not FedEx or UPS — becomes the first brand that merchants think of when shipping to consumers. Consumers might not notice, but many of the items sold on Amazon aren't actually sold by Amazon at all; they're sold by other companies that have struck agreements with Amazon to handle marketing, storage, packaging and shipping. In other words, instead of dealing separately with the shippers, these smaller retailers deal directly with Amazon.

By some estimates, third-party goods now account for more than half of Amazon's merchandise sales. Because Amazon takes a commission on third-party sales and because such sales carry low marginal costs (since it's using infrastructure it's already built), it makes more money selling other people's goods than it does selling its own.

For Amazon, this creates a nice network effect: The availability of third-party goods expands the selection available through the platform, attracting more customers, which in turn makes Amazon more attractive to other third-party sellers. Meanwhile, as Amazon gains more third-party sellers, more customers and more sales, it gets greater flexibility in choosing among shippers such as UPS and FedEx, further driving down its infrastructure costs and again boosting sales. Imagine a point where third-party sales become Amazon's main business, with its own-brand goods representing only a small fraction of the company's total business, and where the company focuses primarily on e-commerce infrastructure rather than e-commerce itself, and you begin to see how this isn't such a crazy proposition.

None of this addresses the key question haunting the company: Will Amazon ever make money off this plan, and if so, how much? Amazon-watchers debate fiercely, but the real answer is unknowable. If you're inclined to shop, though, you'll benefit from Bezos' paranoia.
