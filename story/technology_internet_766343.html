Interview with Facebook's Sandberg: Mobile Internet Balances Work and Life

by anonymous on 2013-09-11 14:05:52

The first 3 chapters of "Lean In": http://news.cnblogs.com/n/180997/

Women often find themselves at a disadvantage in the workplace, which is why female leaders in business or politics often become advocates for women's rights. In the tech world, Sheryl Sandberg, the current Chief Operating Officer of Facebook and also the first female member of Facebook's board of directors, is undoubtedly an outstanding female leader.

Before joining Facebook, Sandberg served as the Vice President of Global Online Sales and Operations at Google and was also the Chief of Staff to the U.S. Treasury Secretary during the Clinton administration. As a woman who has had successful professional experiences in both the political and business worlds, Sandberg has consistently worked to fight for women's rights.

Yesterday evening, Sandberg arrived in China to begin promoting her book "Lean In," and Tencent Technology conducted an exclusive interview with her at the earliest opportunity. Sandberg stated that the core message of "Lean In" is to ensure that women's voices are heard and to encourage women to take their place at the table and speak up confidently.

"Currently, there are more male leaders, so many decisions made tend to favor men. Therefore, it is crucial to allow women to voice their opinions and give due importance to women's power and ideas," she said.

Sandberg prepared Chinese business cards specifically for this trip to China. Although her main purpose for visiting is to promote her new book, Sandberg will also meet with senior executives from some Chinese internet companies. Despite Facebook not yet being available in China, the company certainly does not want to miss out on this enormous market.

Making full use of mobile internet

As Facebook’s Chief Operating Officer, Sandberg maintains a good balance between her life and work. To spend more time with her family, she and her husband have agreed to return home every weekday to have dinner with their children, and if they must work overtime, it will be scheduled after dinner.

However, the advent of mobile internet has begun to blur the boundaries between work and personal life.

Sandberg once hid in the restroom during her child's sports game to reply to work emails using her phone. Despite this, Sandberg still holds a supportive attitude towards mobile internet.

She believes that mobile internet allows people to work anytime and anywhere, but it also makes people feel like they're always in a working state. Her solution is to focus fully on work when working, and to concentrate entirely on her children when spending time with them.

In fact, Sandberg believes that for working mothers, while mobile internet helps them work better, it also enables them to communicate better with their children. She mentioned that when she travels for business, she uses tools like Skype or Facetime to stay in touch with her family and conduct video calls; whereas when she was a child, parents traveling could only call their children, and the cost of phone calls was high, so parents away on trips often wouldn't call home frequently.

Mobile internet has made connections between people tighter, making communication cheaper, easier, and faster. "This may be even more important for Asian women because Asians often work longer hours than those in Europe and America, so it's necessary to use mobile internet more effectively to balance family and work," she said.

Moreover, mobile internet has diversified people's social interactions. Sandberg noted that the most critical aspect of mobile internet is that it connects us better and facilitates sharing more conveniently.

"The thing that surprised me the most over the past ten years is how quickly people have moved to mobile internet. In China, more people have already connected to the world through mobile internet. And I think this is a good thing," she said, "People want to connect, and in China, people's social needs are very diverse. China has many products in the social networking space, including QQ, WeChat, Renren, etc., all of which meet people's communication and social needs."

Sandberg highly appreciates the achievements of China's internet companies, but she believes that if they want to compete with Silicon Valley tech companies, an important point is to create global products.

"The best tech companies in the world make products that benefit all people globally," she said. In this regard, China's internet companies perform best in gaming, but there is still much work to be done in other areas.

Letting women's voices be heard

Although Sandberg believes that women can do anything, she also acknowledges that there are differences in gender roles in societal division. From a natural disposition standpoint, some jobs are inherently suited for men, while others are suited for women. However, she believes the most important thing is to let women's voices be heard and give women adequate respect in their careers.

Often, when a woman takes on a leadership role, people may consider her "bossy," whereas when a man leads, he is simply seen as a leader. "Therefore, I tell the people around me that when your daughter takes on a leadership role, introduce her as a corporate executive rather than using the word 'bossy,'" Sandberg said.

In the workplace, women tend to be less confident in themselves, while men are more self-assured. For example, when doing the same task, women often feel they haven’t done well enough, while men believe they’ve done exceptionally well — yet in reality, they've performed equally well.

In such cases, Sandberg believes people need to adjust their expectations: women should express their thoughts more confidently and take on corresponding responsibilities; managers should realize that women's silence doesn’t mean they can't do a good job.

However, as more and more people recognize that women can excel just as men do in many jobs, women's rights are improving globally. From the perspective of female leadership, Sandberg introduced that 5% of large enterprise leaders in China are women, but among small and medium-sized enterprises, about 20% of leaders are women.

But the challenges faced by female leaders are still greater than those faced by men. Sandberg was once interrupted during a very formal speech, with someone even using their hand to block her mouth to stop her.

"The challenges I encounter are actually experienced by all female leaders worldwide," she said, "Recognizing that we all face difficulties, but having the courage to do it anyway, to take our place at the table, is very important."

Balance between family and work

For women, balancing family and work seems to be an eternal challenge. Sandberg believes that solving this problem requires the joint efforts of both husbands and wives.

Sandberg frankly admitted that there are times when she feels extremely exhausted. She pointed out that globally, women bear far more responsibility than men. In the U.S., women undertake 30-40% more household chores than men, this figure in China might be double, and in South Korea and Japan, it could be five times as much. Therefore, husbands should take on more household duties.

Sandberg indicated that survey results show that when husbands take on more household responsibilities, families become more harmonious; when husbands spend more time taking care of their children, it benefits the children's growth.

"I often tell my male friends, if you want to make your wife happy, don't send flowers, do the housework instead," Sandberg said.

And for women, the most important thing is to let go and let their husbands handle the housework without giving too many instructions. Sandberg believes that if wives constantly nag about how their husbands should do specific tasks, the husbands may end up doing nothing. "You can put the diaper on the baby's head and let your husband change it. Believe me, without your teaching, your husband will learn how to change diapers," she said.

Regarding the balance between work and family, Sandberg believes that the most important thing is for women to believe in themselves, trust that they can do anything, believe that they can choose between family and work, and simultaneously believe that they can excel in their work while maintaining a fulfilling family life.
