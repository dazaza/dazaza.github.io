This is how the new Yahoo logo was born...

by anonymous on 2013-09-08 19:43:25

Friends familiar with the Premier League will all know that after Russian billionaire Roman Abramovich took over Chelsea FC, he immediately redesigned the club's historically rich emblem. Following this, clubs like Liverpool and Everton also redesigned their emblems, making them appear younger and more modern. The tech world has also seen a wave of logo changes. Not long ago, Motorola adopted a new logo. Under Marissa Mayer’s radical reforms, Yahoo! also decided to change its logo that had been in use for nearly 20 years. After a "30 days of change" logo trial period, Yahoo! finally unveiled its new logo, interestingly, this new logo was not among those showcased during the past 30 days.

The new logo, created by Yahoo!'s internal brand design team and product designers, retains most of the original logo elements (purple, exclamation mark, varying letter sizes representing yodel sound waves), but makes it appear more modern while incorporating classic aesthetics.

Kathy Savitt, CMO who joined the company last September, believes: "This design represents a significant evolution of the logo." She described the team's fine-tuning of the original logo's characteristics: the purple is now deeper and richer, the exclamation mark is thinner and rounder, and animation effects have been added so that when refreshing the Yahoo! page, the exclamation mark dances around the latter part of the logo. This design of the animation effect is similar to Google's doodles, but Yahoo!'s is dynamic. Kathy Savitt said: "You can think of the exclamation mark as bouncing on a pogo stick, or swinging back and forth like Tarzan; we want to express our respect for the whimsical ideas that users love."

The new logo uses an entirely new font, which isn't surprising for those who have been following Yahoo! over the past 30 days. The reviews of these trial logos were that they merely changed the font style, making them seem even less interesting compared to Google's periodic doodles.

Designers' opinions

"To be honest, (these trial logos) some used random fonts, from a design perspective, there wasn’t much principle behind these logos, you could tell different designers were involved in their creation process," said Kevin Farnham, CEO of the design firm Method. Kevin, along with Geoff Katz, co-designed Yahoo!'s original logo when they worked at Organic in San Francisco as Art Director and Executive Creative Director respectively.

Luke Wroblewski, Yahoo!'s former Chief Design Architect who changed the Yahoo! logo from red to purple in 2009, agreed with Kevin's viewpoint, finding most of these logos unappealing. However, both believed it was time for Yahoo! to change its logo.

Although Kathy Savitt considered updating the Yahoo! logo after joining the company, the real impetus came from Yahoo!'s redesign of its homepage in February this year. Yahoo! users subsequently reflected that the original logo didn't match the new products, and Yahoo! surveyed its more than 12,000 employees, with 87% believing Yahoo! should change its logo. Of course, this doesn't mean people will necessarily like the new logo. Luke Wroblewski conducted a voting survey on users' preferences between the trial logos and the original logo. The results showed that only the trial logo on the tenth day surpassed the original logo in terms of preference. "Each typeface that appeared during the event represented one aspect of the design principles we tested, our initial design concept" was to redesign it without completely abandoning the original logo, Kathey Savitt said.

Trial Logo on the Tenth Day

Yahoo!'s proprietary font

The new logo uses Yahoo!'s exclusive font (it is said that this new font hasn't been named yet). Kathy Savitt explained: "We hope to develop our own proprietary font, they will become intellectual property from Yahoo!, from our design team."

Despite the influence of Apple's iOS 7, the design world is currently trending towards flat design, but this time Yahoo! went against the trend and opted for a more classic design. This type of font would more likely appear on the banners of knights during the Renaissance or on football club emblems. This aligns with Marissa Mayer's idea of reviving Yahoo!, emphasizing the company's confidence in restoring its past glory. Hunter Tura, President of Bruce Mau Design, believes: "If you want to establish a stable image for your company, you tend to adopt a more classic design style." Kathy Savitt said: "The new logo is the foundation of our brand building, product development, and user experience enhancement."

Yahoo! has gone to great lengths for the new logo, and now awaits feedback from consumers, advertisers, and shareholders regarding the new logo.
