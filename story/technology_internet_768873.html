Nokia's shareholders meeting is held tonight, to approve the transfer of mobile phone business to Microsoft

by anonymous on 2013-11-28 11:09:52

At 8 PM Beijing time on November 19th, Nokia will hold a shareholders' meeting. Despite encountering dissatisfaction from a small number of shareholders, the media reports that if there are no surprises, the meeting will approve Nokia's transfer of its mobile phone business to Microsoft.

In September, Nokia and Microsoft reached an agreement for Nokia to transfer its mobile phone business to Microsoft for a price of 5.44 billion euros, approximately 7.4 billion US dollars. Due to years of failed efforts to revitalize its mobile phone business, Nokia's management ultimately chose to shed the burden and move forward lightly.

The shareholders' meeting on Tuesday will be held at the "Ice-Hall" conference hall in Helsinki, Finland, at 2 PM local time, which is 8 PM Beijing time. It is estimated that shareholders dissatisfied with the transfer transaction will use the meeting to vent their discontent; however, it will be difficult for them to reverse the decision to transfer the mobile phone business to Microsoft.

At Nokia's previous shareholders' meeting, many shareholders criticized CEO Stephen Elop's strategy, especially his decision in 2011 to abandon both Nokia's own Symbian operating system and the popular Google Android system in favor of Microsoft's WP system, which had almost no market presence. Elop was previously a senior executive at Microsoft.

Finnish tabloids have mocked Elop as a "Trojan horse," but some analysts have expressed sympathy for Elop's decisions because when Elop took over Nokia in 2010, the company was already retreating in the smartphone market. The Symbian system was too immature and development progress was too slow, making it unable to compete with Apple's iOS and Google's Android. Moreover, Nokia, as an established large company, was unwilling to adopt the "lacking personality" Android system.

The transaction to transfer the mobile phone business is expected to be completed in the first quarter of next year. By the third quarter of next year, Nokia's cash holdings will increase from the current 2 billion euros to 8 billion euros. It is expected that the company will distribute the benefits of the transfer transaction to shareholders in the form of a special dividend.

Since the announcement of the acquisition plan, Nokia's share price has doubled, closing at 6 euros per share on Monday. Last year, Nokia's share price fell to 1.33 euros, the lowest level in 20 years. Investors were concerned that Nokia might run out of cash and go bankrupt before catching up with Samsung Electronics and Apple.

Billionaire and technology investment tycoon Dan Loeb said in October that he had purchased a large stake in Nokia and expected shareholders to receive substantial returns from the Microsoft acquisition deal.

Nokia is a symbol of Finland's rise and occupies a position in Finland's economy similar to that of Samsung Electronics in South Korea. The transfer of its mobile phone business to the United States has left some Finns feeling disappointed.

A Finnish businessman who once served as a manager at Nokia established an organization called "Nokita" (which means "higher bidder" in Finnish) hoping to rally other companies to bid for Nokia. However, on Monday, this person stated that he did not receive sufficient investor support.

After transferring its mobile phone business, the new Nokia company will derive 90% of its revenue from mobile communication equipment, following Ericsson's transformation path. Whether Nokia can achieve breakthroughs outside the telecommunications industry through network maps and so-called "high-tech businesses" remains highly questionable.
