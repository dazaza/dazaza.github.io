Exported refined oil prices reported over half cheaper than domestic sales

by flying0663 on 2009-11-27 16:47:55

According to the report reprinted by China Chemical Network (http://www.63w.cn), the current international oil price level remains below $80, while the domestic refined oil price in China has reached a historical high. The price of No. 93 gasoline in Beijing has hit a record high of 6.66 yuan per liter. Recently, a post quickly spread online and sparked controversy, stating that domestic oil companies were exporting refined oil at 2.4 yuan per liter, much lower than the domestic refined oil price.

"The export volume of refined oil from January to August was 14.96 million tons, with an export value of $6.65 billion, averaging $444.5 per ton," the post analyzed. It said that $444.5 is approximately equivalent to 3036 RMB, but as this figure does not include the ratio of gasoline to diesel, the volume-to-weight ratios of gasoline and diesel were calculated separately, and a simple arithmetic average was taken. The density of gasoline is 0.739 and that of diesel is 0.86, so one ton of refined oil is approximately 1258 liters. Calculating accordingly, the average export price is: 3036/1258 = 2.4 yuan per liter. Meanwhile, the domestic selling price of Sinopec during the same period was about 5.5 yuan, making the domestic price more than twice the international price.

Subsequently, the reporter from China Chemical Network checked the website of the General Administration of Customs. The data showed that the export volume of refined oil from January to August was 14.96 million tons, with an export value of $6.65 billion, averaging $444.5 per ton, which slightly differs from the $444.2 per ton mentioned in the post. According to the exchange rate of RMB to USD at 6.8 and the approximate equivalence of one ton of refined oil to 1258 liters as mentioned in the post, the price per liter was calculated to be 2.41 yuan.

If the result obtained from this calculation matches the actual situation, domestic consumers would have been "enjoying" this huge price difference throughout the year.

In response, Sinopec only stated yesterday that the domestic refined oil price is determined by the government, and the company respects the government's pricing mechanism.

The algorithm used in the online post is "unscientific"

Yesterday, Niu Li, an energy expert at the National Information Center, told the reporter from China Chemical Network, "Oil companies definitely won't do business at a loss. The key issue is that refined oil includes many different varieties, which are very complex. For example, there is a significant price difference between No. 90 gasoline and No. 93 gasoline, and between No. 0 diesel and No. -20 diesel, so it cannot be calculated so simply."

"There is also a difference between whether the price is the free on board (FOB) price or the cost, insurance, and freight (CIF) price. Regardless of what the price is, customs duties and VAT must be added when the product finally reaches overseas markets, potentially doubling the final price. After such calculations, if the domestic consumption tax of 1 yuan per liter is removed, the price gap should not be this large," Niu Li indicated.

Niu Li believed that comparing this price with the domestic tax-inclusive sales price is not scientific.

"Besides whether it is tax-inclusive, exported gasoline is usually sold in bulk transactions of tens of thousands of tons, so its price will naturally be cheaper." Liao Kaishun, an analyst for the refined oil industry at Entrade Consulting, said that this method of comparison is inappropriate.

It is understood that the current ex-factory price of No. 93 gasoline at Sinopec refineries is 7314 yuan per ton, but the average wholesale price in various regions is 6904 yuan per ton. Liao Kaishun stated that the current domestic refined oil wholesale price being lower than the ex-factory price reflects the awkwardness of the current pricing mechanism. "Although the oil price has increased, the oil cannot be sold, so the wholesale price remains low. The wholesale price of refined oil best reflects the domestic supply and demand issues."

Regarding oil companies "cheaply" exporting refined oil abroad, Liao Kaishun said, "Most of China's refined oil exports are processing trade, where only some fees for processing on behalf of others are charged. However, since the beginning of this year, domestic refined oil sales have not been strong, and the profit from selling abroad is clearly lower than domestically. Oil enterprises actively export to relieve the pressure of high inventory."
