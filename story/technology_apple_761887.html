Why Apple Is Losing the Smartphone Wars: Hardware Matters Less and Less

by geekzhang on 2013-05-14 16:15:23

Forbes website published a column article today discussing the waning momentum Apple has shown in the mobile sector recently. The article lists three reasons why this iPhone manufacturer is gradually losing ground in the smartphone battle.Below is an excerpt from the Forbes article titled "Why Apple Is Losing the Smartphone War":Like everyone else, I was one of the first to buy when Apple launched its first-generation iPhone smartphone in 2007. This device was unlike anything I had ever seen: it was a phone that seemed capable of doing anything.I lived in Kyiv, Ukraine at the time, and the iPhone was very popular there. There wasn’t an official carrier authorized to sell iPhones, so all the products on the market were jailbroken. Shops on the street offered a $20 unlocking service. Even the president owned one.This demonstrated the groundbreaking nature of the iPhone at the time. Those who didn't have one wanted to buy one, and there were no other alternatives available on the market.However, last week, something made me realize that the era of iPhone dominating the market has passed. My Google search app pushed a notification inviting me to try Google Now, the new personal assistant software introduced by this search engine company, powered by artificial intelligence technology.This app doesn’t know me well yet, but it's already recommending restaurants, estimating travel times for dates, and even telling me if there will be traffic jams. Although it’s a bit scary that Google knows so much about me, this app is indeed very practical and addictive.Moreover, Google Now can run completely independent of the platform. If I switch to a Samsung or LG phone, it can still work properly and retain the information it has gathered in the past. This is a big problem for Apple.In my view, it is clear that Apple is losing its leading position in the mobile field for three reasons:1. Hardware is becoming less important: It may sound strange now, but as early as ten years ago, there was a trend where hardware was dead and software reigned supreme. In the 1990s, Microsoft was the world's most valuable company, while personal computer manufacturers gradually became appendages. Apple revived the hardware industry with products like the iPhone.The cycle has repeated itself, and we seem to have entered a new phase of this cycle. In this phase, software is powerful again. Over the next decade, most of the value people derive from their devices will come from services provided by the cloud rather than built-in features.Besides, any phone picked randomly from various manufacturers can rival the iPhone. Fierce competition and homogenization mean shrinking profit margins and declining market share, which applies to Apple as well.2. Apple can no longer control the functionality of the iPhone: When Apple released its bug-ridden Maps app, I waited months before upgrading my iOS system because I didn’t want to replace Google Maps with a subpar product. I only upgraded after Google Maps became available again in the Apple App Store.I still prefer the iPhone, but honestly, I can’t explain why. Everything I do on my phone is provided by others. I use Google for browsing, searching, and finding locations, Zite and Hootsuite provide content, Brewster manages contacts, and Skype handles calls to friends overseas. I suspect that Square will eventually handle payments.Although I won’t switch to the Android platform, the amount of Google-related stuff on my phone is increasing and surpassing Apple.3. The rise of artificial intelligence: As I've mentioned before, although Apple was the first company to let consumers experience the charm of artificial intelligence (AI) on their phones with Siri, Apple has fallen behind in this area and shows no signs of progress.In the future, switching software platforms isn't just about learning a new interface. Switching systems will be like moving to a new town where people need to get to know you before they can provide the services you depend on for daily life.Currently, Google Now is clearly leading in the AI-powered mobile application sector, and others may join in. Microsoft has the technology, but progress is slow currently (though this could change quickly through collaboration with some excellent OEMs). IBM certainly has the capability if they choose to pursue it. Amazon is probably preparing as well.Companies like Facebook and Nuance (the company providing core technology for Apple's Siri) also have strong AI development capabilities, and many companies already possess or are developing exciting technologies. It seems strange that Apple's management, who are both smart and capable, are taking a hands-off approach in this field.Perhaps, as many Apple fans firmly believe, the company might have a secret plan that will make the world go crazy again. However, whether they can achieve this is questionable. It is extremely difficult to quietly acquire specialized expertise, and Apple's tradition of secrecy does not fit well with acquisitions. If no one knows you're involved in this area, it's hard to conduct acquisitions.This doesn't mean Apple can't develop or acquire AI technology (a powerful company like Apple can do anything it decides to), but there are currently no signs that they are doing so, and they continue to lag behind in the machine learning field.Thus, my iPhone is gradually turning into a Google phone.
