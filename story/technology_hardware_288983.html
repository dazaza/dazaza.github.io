In China, the Zhou people "burned wood to produce incense," creating smoke for the purpose of sacrificing to heaven, which was called "Yin" or "Yinsi." By the time of Emperor Wu of Han, incense came to symbolize the meaning of being passed down from generation to generation, embodying the spirit of reverence for heaven and respect for ancestors. After the Sui and Tang dynasties, the use of incense became more widespread and secular. Song dynasty poet Chen Yuyi wrote in his poem "Burning Incense": "Infinite intentions are embodied in this wisp of smoke," reflecting how the joys and sorrows of life, as well as metaphysical questions and pursuits, found their outlet here. Incense had already completely permeated and internalized within people's spirits. Interestingly, not only do Chinese people burn incense, but many nations and countries around the world also do so, such as the ancient Egyptians, Babylonians, Indians, Greeks, Romans, and Israelites...
