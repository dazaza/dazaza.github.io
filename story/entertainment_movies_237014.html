Chang Cheh's Three Shots of The One-Armed Swordsman

The concept for "The One-Armed Swordsman", perhaps the screenwriter Ni Kuang/Chang Cheh borrowed or referenced Jin Yong's "The Return of the Condor Heroes" story. In "The Return of the Condor Heroes," Yang Guo also had his arm chopped off by his willful younger sister. It could also be said that "The One-Armed Swordsman" is a tribute to "The Return of the Condor Heroes."

This is normal; for Ni Kuang, who has written over 400 scripts and numerous science fiction novels, there aren't that many original stories. In 1967, upon its release, "The One-Armed Swordsman" was groundbreaking. Chang Cheh then continued with two more sequels, namely "The One-Armed Swordsman King" (1969) and "New One-Armed Swordsman" (1971).

Movies related to "The One-Armed Swordsman", apart from the three directed by Chang Cheh, also include Mui Yee-fung's 1994 "94 One-Armed Swordsman: Love" and Tsui Hark's 1999 "Broken Sword". However, aside from paying homage to their predecessors, these films have little connection to Chang Cheh's style.

A hero losing an arm exudes a sense of tragic grandeur. The broken sword is a symbol, representing perseverance and self-reliance in adversity, starting anew, and rebirth like a phoenix rising from the ashes. Therefore, in this sense, "The One-Armed Swordsman" can even be seen as an uplifting and inspiring motivational film. The one-armed hero uses the broken sword to create an exclusive one-armed sword technique. The unity of man and sword symbolizes not only the hero's rebirth but also a realm.

Weapons in martial arts films are symbols of characters' personalities, materializations of their spiritual essence. "The One-Armed Swordsman" embodies Chang Cheh's "castration" complex. Or what could be called "aesthetic of imperfection." Severed arms, disembowelings, gouged eyes, dismemberment, he even made a movie that collected all sorts of imperfections called "Imperfect."

Chang Cheh's three "One-Armed Swordsman" films became increasingly violent with each installment. By the third part, "New One-Armed Swordsman," David Chiang's Lei Li even mutilates his own right arm. The climactic fight on the bridge sees Lei Li fighting from the beginning to the end of the bridge without hesitation. This brings to mind the blood-soaked scenes in the heroic films shot by Chang Cheh's disciple John Woo, except that Chow Yun-fat wields twin guns instead.

Perhaps the inspiration for Chang Cheh came from Japanese samurai films shown in Hong Kong at that time. For example, the "Zatoichi" series about the blind swordsman, starring Shintaro Katsu, which were also filled with violence and imperfection.

Hong Kong's one-armed heroes and Japan's blind swordsman echo each other beautifully. So much so that in 1971, after leaving Shaw Brothers and Chang Cheh, Wang Yu and Shintaro Katsu teamed up to star in the Golden Harvest production "One-Armed Swordsman vs Blind Swordsman" (directed by Xu Jihong).

Chang Cheh's films were even more violent than Japanese ones. However, behind Chang Cheh's violence lay the romance of passionate young men. This romance was not just an external form (an elegant and beautiful visual style), but more importantly, the internal spirit and sentiment.

Chang Cheh was undoubtedly the author of Hong Kong genre films. He used martial arts films to express his views on the world and to convey personal sentiments. Fang Gang, the lonely and melancholy figure in "The One-Armed Swordsman," was a reflection of his frustrated mood during his early days at Shaw Brothers when several movies ("Butterfly Cup," "Tiger Knight") didn't perform well.

"The One-Armed Swordsman King" is a sequel to "The One-Armed Swordsman." Fang Gang, to repay his master's kindness, defeats the villain trying to steal the position of leader of the martial arts world and is hailed as the "Sword King" by the masses. But Fang Gang abandons it all and retreats to the countryside with his wife, Xiao Man. Chang Cheh uses this to express his stance against violence.

"New One-Armed Swordsman" goes against traditional martial arts norms, portraying the older generation's Long Yi as a cunning and hypocritical villain, while the younger generation are the true knights who keep their promises and value righteousness above all else. This character model resonates with the rebellious trend of young people in that era who despised tradition and authority.

The character Long Yi somewhat resembles Yue Buqun from "The Smiling, Proud Wanderer." Whether this "false knight" image influenced Ni Kuang and Chang Cheh is unknown. Despite a four-year span between these three films, Chang Cheh's style underwent significant changes.

First, "The One-Armed Swordsman" was of great significance, being a pioneering work. Within four years, Chang Cheh rose from a newcomer in the film industry to a famous director at Shaw Brothers. His films also underwent many changes. For instance, "New One-Armed Swordsman" featured dual male leads, with Ti Lung and David Chiang forming a new and refreshing duo, becoming the best pairing in Chang Cheh's films.

After Wang Yu left Chang Cheh, the melancholic and solitary air of the knights in Chang Cheh's martial arts films (Nie Zheng in "The Great Assassin," Yin Peng in "Golden Swallow," and Fang Gang in "The One-Armed Swordsman") disappeared. The dual knights replaced the lone wanderer. Not only did the character relationships change, but also the bright and optimistic emotional tone. For example, in "New One-Armed Swordsman," when Ti Lung appears, the entire film's atmosphere shifts from melancholy to exhilarating. Thus, Chang Cheh began passionately singing praises of brotherhood among men.

In the three "One-Armed Swordsman" films, women often serve as the healing place for the knights' hearts, providing them with a bed for rebirth. For instance, in "The One-Armed Swordsman," Xiao Man provides Fang Gang with the "Broken Sword Manual," and in "New One-Armed Swordsman," Banqiao secretly fetches a family treasure sword for Lei Li.

In "Golden Swallow," Zheng Peipei even becomes the reason for Yin Peng's self-abandonment and debauchery, eventually leading to a duel with Luo Lie. Notably, in "New One-Armed Swordsman," the brotherly bond between men replaces romantic relationships, with an increasingly evident ascetic flavor. In "New One-Armed Swordsman," Lei Li is completely passive in his relationship with Banqiao. Thus, when Ti Lung plays the knight Feng Junjie, the neglected Banqiao can only hold Lei Li's empty sleeve.

Hong Kong film critic Lo Ka's commentary is very apt: "Compared to 'The One-Armed Swordsman' four years ago, 'New One-Armed Swordsman' lacks some of the former's sharpness and extremity but gains more worldly wisdom and hidden melancholy. More importantly, it strengthens the life-and-death commitment between men, thus enhancing the tragic feeling. 'New One-Armed Swordsman' may not be as groundbreaking as 'The One-Armed Swordsman,' but it is richer in the intense passion and sorrow between men."

Comparing the three "One-Armed Swordsman" films, from a plot perspective, "The One-Armed Swordsman" is the best. The story is full of twists and turns, delicate and touching, with tremendous dramatic force.

From the perspective of martial arts design, "The One-Armed Swordsman" stands out first. Masters Lau Kar-leung and Tong Ga emphasized the realism of martial arts, combined with the camera's flexible movements, resulting in a spectacular display. Master Lau's innovative weapon design, the "Gold Knife Lock," adds a unique touch.

From the technical aspect of the film, "The One-Armed Swordsman" is also the most innovative. Chang Cheh's signature techniques, such as handheld cinematography, slow-motion, and Western electronic music, are used appropriately. This set a model and standard for subsequent films.

From the film's entertainment value, "New One-Armed Swordsman" takes the lead. With Ti Lung's addition, the life-and-death commitment between men far surpasses the previous focus on the master-disciple relationship in terms of emotional depth. The climax's large-scale battle is exhilarating. Especially, Lei Li's final use of the aerial three-strike technique to kill Long Yi clears all previous frustrations, making the audience feel especially satisfied.
