New iPhone supports domestic 4G, China Mobile's 6-year courtship with Apple comes to an end

by anonymous on 2013-09-11 14:38:15

Apple has just released its new smartphone products, the iPhone 5s and iPhone 5c, at midnight Beijing time. Although there was no mention of whether these two models support TD-LTE networks during the launch event, according to the product descriptions on Apple's Hong Kong website, the model A1529 iPhone 5C and model A1530 iPhone 5S both support TD-LTE.

If this information is accurate, the iPhone 5s and iPhone 5c will be the first iPhones to support China’s self-developed TD-LTE 4G standard. This means that the six-year-long negotiations between China Mobile and Apple regarding the introduction of the iPhone smartphone will finally come to an end.

Introduction as early as November

It is understood that the newly released iPhone 5s and iPhone 5c use Qualcomm multi-band LTE chips, meaning that China Mobile, which has 700 million mobile phone users, will soon offer contract iPhones. Although mainland China will release the new Apple products simultaneously with the rest of the world, due to the fact that China's Ministry of Industry and Information Technology (MIIT) has not yet officially issued 4G licenses, the introduction of the iPhone 5s and iPhone 5c by China Mobile will be slightly delayed.

There are rumors that the TD-LTE version of the iPhone 5s passed the network access test of MIIT's Telecommunications Administration Bureau on August 30th, becoming one of the first five phones to obtain MIIT's network access license. However, because Apple required confidentiality, it did not appear on MIIT's Telecommunications Administration Bureau website.

According to China's regulations for mobile phone market sales, a new mobile phone needs to obtain China's 3C certification, approval from the Radio Administration Bureau, and a network access permit from the Telecommunications Administration Bureau before it can be sold on the market. Previously, after the global release of the iPhone, it often took about two months for it to be available in mainland China, mainly due to the local approval process.

From internal sources, it is learned that during his previous two visits to China, Tim Cook communicated with relevant authorities and reached a consensus. Apple will include mainland China as one of the initial regions for the new iPhone's launch and submit the necessary approval procedures to relevant departments in mainland China before the iPhone's release, aiming to obtain MIIT's network access permit before the iPhone goes on sale. The relevant authorities will keep the approval information confidential.

Currently, according to the latest updates on Apple's Hong Kong website, both the model A1529 iPhone 5c and model A1530 iPhone 5s support TD-LTE. This sufficiently indicates that these two latest products support TD-LTE. The main reason they are not displayed on the mainland's official website is because China's TD-LTE 4G license has not been issued yet, while Hong Kong already has commercial 4G services (both TD-LTE and FDD-LTE).

The source revealed that China Mobile has already completed the relevant preparations for introducing the new iPhones. The final timing of the introduction still depends on when MIIT issues the 4G license. Currently, the possibility of issuing the license in November is the highest, so China Mobile's introduction of the iPhone 5s and iPhone 5c is likely to happen as early as November.

Six years of courtship

The Apple iPhone is currently sold in over 100 countries and regions through more than 240 carriers, but the world's largest mobile operator, China Mobile, was absent until now.

In fact, China Mobile had shown interest in the iPhone early on and was the first domestic carrier to start negotiating cooperation with Apple. As early as November 2007, when the iPhone was launched in the U.S. and became wildly popular, Gao Nianshu, then Director of China Mobile's Data Department, and others visited Apple's headquarters to discuss bringing the iPhone to China.

In January 2008, China Mobile announced that it had suspended negotiations with Apple. However, five months later, at Apple's Worldwide Developers Conference, the then-CEO of Apple stated that the 3G version of the iPhone would enter mainland China within the year, pending regulatory approval. According to informed sources, the turning point in the negotiations came when Apple made concessions and no longer insisted on a revenue-sharing model, allowing the two parties to restart their talks.

By 2009, China had issued 3G licenses. Chang Xiaobing, Chairman of China Unicom, led a negotiation team to the U.S. to negotiate with Apple. On August 28 of the same year, the two parties announced a three-year agreement to sell two 3G versions of the iPhone in mainland China and signed an exclusive distribution agreement, making China Unicom the sole authorized telecommunications operator in mainland China for selling Apple products, with a term of two years. At that time, China Mobile was operating its own innovative 3G standard, TD-SCDMA. Apple indicated that developing a TD standard would take some time but expressed full support for TD-LTE (4G).

In the following years, China Mobile never gave up negotiating with Apple. The main reason the two parties did not reach an agreement earlier was due to differences in specific terms such as revenue distribution.

Additionally, chip compatibility was also an objective factor. Whether China Mobile could introduce the new iPhone largely depended on Qualcomm's chips. Starting with the iPhone 5, Apple changed the iPhone's chips to Qualcomm ones, primarily because Qualcomm's chips supported multiple standards including FDD LTE, WCDMA, CDMA 2000, CDMA, GSM, etc. However, they did not support TD-SCDMA, which was why China Mobile couldn't introduce the iPhone 5. Currently, Qualcomm has resolved this issue by launching multi-mode chips that support TD-LTE and have products already on the market.

Last month, during Apple CEO Tim Cook's visit to China, his first stop among the three major operators was China Mobile, sparking speculation about whether China Mobile would introduce the iPhone. The source revealed that Cook spent the longest time at China Mobile, almost an entire morning. Chairman Xi Guohua of China Mobile personally greeted him, and the two parties reached agreements on many details.

How powerful is the iPhone? After introducing the iPhone, China Unicom's performance in profit indicators has not been ideal. On the surface, it seems like they lost money, but they established a reputation in the 3G domain and boosted the sales of smartphones in the 1000-1500 yuan price range. More importantly, China Unicom used the iPhone to develop social channels such as Suning and Gome for selling phones. Simultaneously, the iPhone helped China Unicom attract many Global Pass users from China Mobile.

In the 3G era, China Mobile could be considered a failure. In the 4G era, having a star product like the iPhone could not only turn things around but also facilitate a rapid transformation into data-driven operations. Despite China Mobile not introducing the iPhone previously, by the end of last year, China Mobile had over 15 million iPhone users, generating significant traffic.

Meanwhile, Apple, due to a lack of innovation, is stepping down from its pedestal, facing challenges to its market share. In the third fiscal quarter ending June 29, Apple's sales in Greater China decreased by 14%, reaching only $4.6 billion. According to market research company Canalys, during this quarter, Apple's market share in China's smartphone market fell to 5%, ranking seventh. Samsung led with an 18% share. Local manufacturers like Huawei and ZTE also outperformed Apple.

From a long-term perspective, collaborating with China Mobile may help Apple reverse its declining trend in China, potentially reigniting the iPhone's sales market in China and once again becoming the top smartphone brand in consumers' minds.
