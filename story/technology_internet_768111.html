Day 23: Release!

【Part 1 – Introduction and the First 2 Days】

Introduction

I've wanted to develop an Android game for a while now, but I never had a good idea. Recently, I've been playing Subway Surfers and found it really fun. Normally, I don't like games without an endpoint because you can never complete them. So, every time I play these kinds of games, I set some arbitrary goals for myself and then play towards them. This time, my goal was to achieve a 30x score multiplier. When I reached this goal, I got some skills and became addicted to the game. This game made me realize that an Android game doesn't need to have a big backstory or complex settings; simplicity works just fine.

Anyway, I think a game where you just point your finger at something and shoot would be great. If there's also a systematic task system with daily and weekly challenges and some weapon upgrades, the game would be even more interesting. My first idea was to create a game where cute little monsters run around in a beautiful scene, hide behind trees, or lurk in old houses, even in clouds. All you have to do is shoot as many of them down as possible. By cute little monsters, I mean things like Gremlins. They could carry huge machines or do other silly things to make you laugh. Of course, as an independent game developer with zero budget, considering the animation complexity I can imagine, I know this will require a lot of artistic design and development time. So, I decided to adopt a different design so I could finish it within a reasonable timeframe on my own.

My current goal is to develop an interesting and playable Android game within a month.

Day 1: Backyard Panic

While browsing animation asset websites, I found some powerful and free zombie animations. There were animations of zombies walking and being shot by guns, so this might be a zombie shooter type of game.

The character would be on their porch, shooting zombies coming from the outside fields. The game would be called Backyard Panic. You could switch characters, like an old grandma, a smelly guy with a shotgun, or a soldier with a long-range rifle. Below is a very rough sketch.

Update: I just played some Zombie vs Humans. The game was interesting, but constantly pressing the shoot button was a bit boring. I plan to design Backyard Panic so that touching a zombie kills it instantly, at least no boss zombies. After careful consideration, the zombie theme seems a bit too dark for me, and this genre is already saturated in the market. So, I'm not sure if the game will end up being this type. Also, this game looks like it would involve 3D graphics, but I'll stick to 2D, at least for my first Android game.

Day 2: Installing Eclipse, Choosing Game Libraries

I downloaded the Android package and installed Eclipse on my Linux system. It runs "Hello World" on my Galaxy S2 phone. Hello Java. I really need to remember all these Java commands since I haven't used them since 2002.

Looking at these 2D/3D OpenGL classes, it seems using toolkits/libraries to build the game would be a good approach. I got some free Unity3D serial numbers from NordicGame2013, but I don't have the permission to publish on Google Play yet. So, I think I'll skip Unity for now. Right now, I'm looking into CoronaSDK and some other libraries.

【Part 2 – Day 3 to Day 5】

Day 3: A New Game Idea

While browsing Android-related forums online for ideas, I saw an ad for "Revenge of the Titans". It's a cool game with retro graphics and nice animations. I can do retro graphics too. So, aliens will land in the backyard from space...

No! The backyard doesn't seem like a good idea. I think I need to come up with a new name for the game.

Aliens attack remote defense stations at the border. They destroy the automatic systems, so you have to manually operate and shoot. The game could be called MANUAL OVERRIDE or EMERGENCY OVERRIDE. I checked online and on Google Play, and it seems no one else has used these names.

The large alien ships land at a docking station, and you must drive them away. They might hide behind oil drums or landed ships, etc. I want to add some destructible objects near the enemies in the game. The game might be called DOCKING BAY or DECK 42.

Do the names sound exciting? Hmm, not really.

Day 4: A Shift

I've been trying to draw the graphics for the docking station, but they look terrible, so I'm abandoning this idea. The battle will definitely take place in space, no change there. Aliens will appear from one end of the screen, and you can shoot them before they reach the other end. There are some destructible objects at the bottom. Aliens might hide behind these objects, and you can destroy them or shoot holes through them. Below is an example with towers, satellite receivers, and other stuff.

My idea is to let players kill as many aliens as possible within a limited time (say, one minute).

Since this game has a space theme, I browsed my font collection and found some cool galactic fonts. Currently, I'll use the AdourGM font to display text in the game, such as points and screen options.

Inspired by Gimp's nebula tutorial, I completed the image above using Gimp. After reading Gimp's nebula tutorial and making some adjustments, I finished the image in a few hours. I hate spending too much time on one thing, but fortunately, this background can be used in most parts of the game.

Day 5: libGDX and Graphic Ideas

I chose the library I will use. It's based on Java and seems quite complete. I built and debugged a test program quickly. However, I'm still confused about whether to use scenes (Scene), actors (Actor), and other features, or build these things myself as usual. The documentation for this library lacks examples and doesn't explain how to combine them. In this case, I guess I'll have to rely on Google. For example, there's no explanation on how to connect actors (Actor) and sprites (Sprite). If you have nearly 20 identical sprites (or similar things), how should you handle them.

For the first project, I think I'll just learn how to use sprites and then build the rest using previous experience. For future projects, I might be smarter and know which things I need are already available. I just hate learning a lot first and then finding out that the framework or library isn't flexible enough to meet some unconventional ideas I might encounter in the future.

Tips: I noticed that some games developed with libGDX usually fix the screen orientation. The game interface won't rotate when you flip the device. I found a simple solution in some fragmented posts and comments on SO (StackOverflow): just replace landscape with sensorLandscape in the AndriodManifest.xml file to allow the screen to automatically rotate with the device.

Day 7: Changing Layout and Screen Orientation

Now you can shoot aliens. Since it's a retro-style graphic, I decided to use large pixels (enlarging the alien images 5 times using interpolation in Gimp) when the aliens explode. Then I wrote a bit of fragment management code to perform intermediate calculations when libGDX randomly emits fragments in all directions from the center.

You may notice there's no graphical representation of yourself on the screen. This is intentional. If you didn't realize that, it's fine because it's a first-person shooter game. Funny, right! At least technically speaking, it is. You don't need to know where you are, just shoot the enemies. In some test versions, I also tried scaling the aliens to add depth, making you feel they're coming from afar. But it's hard to shoot small targets with your fingers on Android devices, so I abandoned this idea.

To make the game more interesting, the laser gun used to shoot aliens needs constant energy replenishment. Each gun can shoot 10 times, then you need to click the energy button in the lower left corner of the screen. I'm not sure about its characteristics yet because it still needs testing.

When I was testing this feature, something bothered me: when shooting aliens, my index finger would cover the entire screen, making it hard to see what's happening. Then I looked at various Android games on my phone and realized that a vertical screen orientation would work better. In vertical mode, the index finger comes from the right side instead of the bottom (of course, you need to get used to using your right hand). In this mode, most of the screen area is still visible. I also changed the direction of the aliens' flight; they now appear from the top of the screen instead of previously appearing from left to right.

Based on the same reason, I also changed the main design of the game. You can keep playing until an alien crosses over you, rather than just playing within a fixed time. Initially, my idea was that you could play for one minute each time, scoring as many points as possible during that minute (similar to the scoring rule in Fruit Ninja). Now my idea is that although you can have 3 lives, the game ends once an alien crosses over you.

Later, I also modified the background nebula image. Previously, I wrote about how to draw a night sky with stars and nebulae using Gimp, but I had to do a lot of experiments to achieve satisfactory results each time. You can subtract two layers and add some inherent noise, then apply a gradient color layer to achieve the effect. The problem is that subtracting two layers always produces some "small" nebulae, but I want the entire screen to be filled with colors. I randomly dotted some points on the drawing paper with a brush, then colored them differently. I divided the stars into three layers: the first layer adds a two-pixel shadow to each point (without compensation), the second layer blurs it, and the third layer keeps it unchanged. This technique can produce excellent star effects, the secret lies in ensuring nearby points have different colors or clarity. This is exactly the characteristic of real stars. Stars that appear close together are actually light-years apart, they just appear close from Earth.

Day 8: Battle in the Starry Sky

I'm trying to come up with a new name for this game. Although I thought of "Battle in the Starry Sky," I still feel it needs further study to see if it fits. I tried some fonts and later decided to use a font called Ruslan Display.

Today, I improved the algorithm for the alien explosion effect. I've been thinking about how to make it seem like the enemies are attacking in waves. First, there are 10 waves of enemies, followed by a boss. After passing a level, the game speeds up a bit, and some new enemies are added. In every 10 levels (10, 20, 30...), there's a new boss. This algorithm repeats until the player loses. If you find that you want to eliminate all the aliens, the game speed will be set to twice the starting speed, requiring a lot more effort from you.

Here's the current idea for power-ups:

Power-ups with time limits, collectable during the game:

Shield (at the bottom of the screen, preventing one alien from passing)

Score multiplier boosters

Rapid fire (just press your finger and move around)

Big bomb (destroys all aliens on the screen)

Superpower (skips ten waves of enemies but scores all points)

Galaxy ninja (slices enemies with a lightsaber - Fruit Ninja style)

Automatic energy replenishment (automatically refills the laser gun's energy)

Multi-fire (three or four laser guns firing simultaneously)

Extra lives (starts the game with four lives)

Permanent power-ups, upgradeable each time you play:

Rapid fire

Shield

Galaxy ninja

Automatic energy replenishment
