Gold T+D, also known as Gold Deferred Trading, simply put, refers to a spot deferred delivery business conducted through margin trading. Both buyers and sellers establish buy/sell contracts with a certain percentage of margin (10% of the total contract amount), similar to gold futures, allowing two-way trading. Buyers and sellers can close their held contracts at any time based on market changes. During the holding period, there will be a daily deferral fee of two ten-thousandths of the total contract amount, and the direction of the deferral fee payment is determined by the settlement declaration situation on that day. Traders can choose to settle the contract on the same day or choose a certain extended settlement time. The profit model is to obtain price differential profits through buying low and selling high, or selling high and buying low.

In October 2008, China’s foreign exchange reserves experienced negative growth for the first time since the currency reform. Subsequently, due to the outbreak of the international financial crisis, overseas hot money decreased, resulting in a not-so-high growth rate of foreign exchange reserves. However, half a year later, with signs of economic recovery, the situation of significant foreign exchange reserve growth reappeared. By the end of June 2009, China’s foreign exchange reserve balance was $2.1316 trillion, breaking through $2 trillion for the first time. By March 2011, China’s foreign exchange reserve balance reached $3.0447 trillion, surpassing $3 trillion for the first time.

However, some changes occurred in September this year. According to data released by the People's Bank of China, China’s foreign exchange reserve balance at the end of September was $3.2017 trillion, a net decrease of $60.8 billion compared to last month, which was the first decline since May 2010.

The reason lies in the sharp strengthening of the dollar against major currencies' exchange rates in that month, leading to book exchange losses for China’s foreign exchange reserves, causing a drop in the reserve balance. However, according to an article by Tao Guan, director of the Balance of Payments Department of the State Administration of Foreign Exchange, the increase in purchase and payment of foreign exchange and the improvement of foreign exchange supply and demand imbalance were also important reasons for the negative growth of foreign exchange reserves. According to data from the State Administration of Foreign Exchange, the bank agency customer purchases of foreign exchange increased by about $1 billion compared to last month, while customer settlements decreased by about $2 billion, reducing the monthly settlement and sales surplus to $2.6 billion, down 31% from last month.
