"Life of Powder and Ink: Tears of the Peking Opera Queen," a dramatic and eventful biography of the "Queen of Peking Opera."

by jqrxass on 2009-08-30 23:18:36

Yan Huizhu was the daughter of Yan Jupeng, a famous founder of Peking Opera Art in China. She first studied under Cheng派 and later followed Master Mei Lanfang, becoming a direct disciple of the Mei school and was publicly called "Female Mei Lanfang". In the 1940s, she was hailed as the "Queen of Peking Opera" and was also a movie star whose fame once reached its peak. However, at the beginning of the Cultural Revolution, she could not bear the persecution and committed suicide.

The author of this book, Yan Qingqing, is the only son of Yan Huizhu. The author narrates his mother's colorful life and recalls Yan Huizhu's exquisite artistic achievements. At the same time, from the perspective of a witness, he provides his own version of the character and emotional entanglements of his stepfather, the famous Peking Opera performing artist Yu Zhenfei.

This book adopts more than two hundred precious historical photos, as well as artistic and life portraits of Yan Huizhu, most of which are published for the first time.

If it weren't for the Cultural Revolution, if it weren't for the 'Gang of Four,' my mother would never have left this world so early, and I wouldn't have lived such a wandering life for all these years after my mother's death, struggling to survive just as Elder Sister Zhang Yihuo wrote.

Nowadays, with prosperity and peace, there are many people over ninety years old. Many of my mother's friends who are now in their eighties or nineties are still living well. If my mother were still alive, how wonderful that would be!

"A child without a mother is like a blade of grass." For more than forty years, from adolescence, through youth, to now being over fifty, I am an orphan.

For more than forty years, how many tears have I shed?

Aunt Li Huirong was my mother's classmate at the Chunming Girls' Middle School in Beijing in 1934. She was a sister with whom my mother could confide her innermost thoughts and also one of my godmothers to whom I was entrusted.

Mother Li once told me that Yu and Yan's relationship wasn't good to begin with. At the start of the Cultural Revolution, Yu tried every means to distance himself from my mother to protect himself. My mother repeatedly said to Mother Li: "Old man (Yu Zhenfei) wants me dead!"

Mother Li was deeply saddened and repeatedly persuaded my mother not to give up hope, saying: "Don't fall for the old man's tricks!"

Without performances, my mother surely had a lot of complaints, which naturally caused some leaders and colleagues around her to have opinions about her. This was where my mother offended people. Was this my mother's fault?

My mother's health wasn't very good to begin with; she had some neurasthenia. But the real cause of her poor health was due to the lack of performances. Even when she did perform, she didn't receive the support she should have, especially after some failed performances (without good partners or excellent musicians). She couldn't sleep at night because she was truly heartbroken.

My mother said: "When I met acquaintances on the road, they didn't know I was in Shanghai. They asked me, 'Where do you work?' When I went shopping, the shop assistants asked me the same thing. How could I answer? I said: 'I'm here, at the Peking Opera Theater, in a corner, covered in mold, I'm rotting away!'"
