Investing in and appreciating wine, whether it's current vintage or futures, is more than just an investment.

by tfbgfnbv on 2009-11-25 17:40:17

The most direct way to invest in wine is to invest in either readily available wine or futures, but investing in either requires a great deal of knowledge about wine. From the place of origin and vintage to the investment boom cycle, purchase, storage, and sale of wine, there are many things to consider.

People who know a little about wine all know the five famous wineries of Bordeaux, France: Lafite, Latour, Margaux, Mouton, and Haut-Brion. The wines produced by these five prestigious wineries are not only gourmet delights favored by elegant people, but also the most recognized investment targets by investors. In particular, when purchasing futures, one must choose wines from the top five wineries; otherwise, there may be no one to take over the investment.

In addition, some second-tier wineries in France currently have significant growth potential, such as Romanée, Rotschild-Séjour, Beaucastel, Ausone-Tourelles, and Rosé. Especially those with good vintages, like 1982, 1990, 1996, and 2000, have little difference in quality compared to the wines from top wineries, but are much cheaper in price. Some can even be up to seventy percent cheaper, making them the first choice for many beginner investors.
