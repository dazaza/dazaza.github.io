The scapegoat and accomplice of the Sanlu milk powder incident

by mygod13 on 2008-09-16 15:01:52

The Sanlu infant kidney stones incident has been resolved. According to news reports, the Shijiazhuang municipal government of Hebei province, where Sanlu Group is located, announced that the "problematic milk powder" produced by Sanlu Group was due to illegal elements adding melamine during the raw milk acquisition process. By 7:00 AM on September 12th, the Shijiazhuang police had summoned 78 suspected individuals. Currently, this case is still under further investigation.

In the company's crisis public relations handling process regulations, once a problematic product appears, the first priority is to "expel external threats and stabilize internally". The search for culprits and scapegoats follows the principle of prioritizing external over internal factors. This is currently the first stage of expelling external threats. Whether or not the so-called dairy farmers, raw milk collectors, and other 78 suspected individuals can be classified as culprits or become scapegoats cannot yet be determined. However, the technical operational difficulty is extremely high. It is estimated that it will soon enter the second stage of searching internally and pushing out the "first perpetrator".

The reason why expelling external threats has great technical difficulty comes from the attributes of powdered milk products. As a solid instant beverage, it is unlikely that prohibited substances like melamine would be added in the fresh milk production and acquisition stages; the reasons are simple: 1) Melamine does not dissolve in milk, so even if dairy farmers and collectors were to add it, there would be obvious floating and sedimentation. It wouldn't pass the manufacturer's visual inspection, let alone the reportedly strict hundreds of inspection procedures; 2) The amount of problem milk powder is seven hundred tons. If it involves the addition of melamine, thousands of dairy farmers and collectors would have participated. Such a large-scale organized action, who gave them technical guidance? Who supplied them with melamine? Through what logistics channels? 3) The price of fresh milk acquisition does not exceed three yuan per kilogram (asked Inner Mongolia Duolun Milk Factory), while the purchase price of melamine is between 11,000 to 12,000 yuan per ton. With such an expensive ingredient, adding it would only increase weight, which is harmful without any benefit—who would do that? 4) If dairy farmers and collectors added melamine, and Sanlu did not detect it (the probability of which is almost zero), then all Sanlu milk powders would be doomed. Why did the problem only appear in the low-priced baby milk powder?

These four questions are the unbreakable iron locks that must be crossed when looking for scapegoats externally. Melamine has the physicochemical characteristics of being directly added to powdered solid products. After excluding the suspicion of external addition, it is estimated that the internal scapegoats within Sanlu will soon come to light.

But even after pulling out these scapegoats, there are still four iron locks that make Sanlu hard to escape blame: 1) What was Sanlu's purpose in adding melamine? Adding melamine worth tens of thousands of yuan without benefits or uses, what use is there in adding it? The answer is already clear—adding melamine is to increase protein and fat content, which means reducing the amount of fresh milk used and cutting the cost of milk powder; profit drives manufacturers to take reckless risks; 2) Whose directive was it? Was it a decision made by the highest leadership level of the company or unauthorized actions by production personnel below? 3) Did the contract with the melamine supplier specify the toxic side effects of the product? What were the other parameters specified in the melamine supply contract, such as quantity, amount, delivery time, breach disputes, etc.? 4) In May 2007, the National Quality Inspection Bureau had already reported cases of melamine being added to fake proteins. Knowing that melamine is toxic, how could Sanlu not know? Unless they had a firm grasp on safety and assurances from the supplying company, how would Sanlu dare to use it recklessly?

Sanlu milk powder is a company with a "precedent." In 2004, among the companies exposed in the Fuyang poisonous milk powder case, Sanlu milk powder was clearly listed. But at the time, Sanlu claimed that the involved milk powder was counterfeit produced by illegal manufacturers, thus escaping the major cleanup of milk powder factories. This time, Sanlu reappears with a sense of invincibility.

Introducing scapegoats does not represent solving the entire problem. Who are the accomplices in the Sanlu problematic milk powder incident?

Does the government bear any responsibility? Years ago, Sanlu was suspected of involvement in the Fuyang poisonous milk powder case. The alarm was lifted not too long ago. Even if Sanlu enjoyed excellent quality and was exempt from inspections, it doesn't mean the relevant authorities could ignore its development. The supervisory responsibilities of relevant departments should not just be written on paper or hung on walls, nor should they only target small enterprises, right?

The higher-ups of Sanlu are not free from blame. It is estimated that the possibility of direct instructions from the higher-ups of Sanlu to use melamine is small. They usually deal with strategic issues, and specific tactical operations are carried out by subordinates. But this does not mean the higher-ups are without responsibility. Fierce market competition forces higher-ups to constantly talk about controlling costs and emphasize performance in meetings. Employees are almost pushed to their limits. Without coercion, who would willingly resort to improper practices?

Has the market coerced good people into wrongdoing? In the distribution channel, wholesalers, dealers, and supermarkets add layers of prices, hoping for higher profits and larger sales volumes with lower wholesale prices. Beyond normal prices, they often demand discounts, promotions, and cost-price dumping from companies. Not long ago, at Baolongcang Supermarket in Hebei, I personally saw a well-known cool tea priced at more than three yuan sold for one yuan, and a big brand of cola openly marked down to 0.99 yuan—a behavior of selling below cost, constituting unfair competition but accepted as normal by the supermarket. If Sanlu wasn't squeezed by the distribution channels, how would it rack its brains to control costs and take reckless risks?

Do product endorsers bear some responsibility? Renowned actress Deng Jie once endorsed Sanlu infant milk powder, repeatedly saying in the advertisement: "Professional production, quality assurance, branded product, trustworthy, economical, I trust Sanlu Huiyou infant milk powder!" Guo Degang, Wen Qing, Ni Ping, Xue Jianing, and the Flowers Band also endorsed Sanlu's products. Now that Sanlu milk powder has caused multiple cases of infant kidney stones, shouldn't these celebrities who received substantial endorsement fees from Sanlu take responsibility for this event?

And the consumers who pay for the product and channel costs, originally a group of completely innocent victims. However, the demand for low-priced products leads distributors and manufacturers to operate on thin profits, resorting to fakes if necessary, ultimately harming the consumers—the pitiful and innocent infants. In China, how many consumers knowingly buy cheap, inferior products despite knowing the risks? How many people can afford the exorbitantly priced imported milk powder? This leads us back to investigating the government's responsibility...

Thus, scapegoats and accomplices are intertwined. Responsibility breaks bones but remains connected. Everything in the world is like this, filled with intricate related transactions. Sanyu fell, Sanlu stopped production, who will be next? Will the problems stop appearing? Treating symptoms instead of root causes, failing to solve problems fundamentally, will result in more people getting hurt.
