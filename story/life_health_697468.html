Children are prone to purulent sinusitis mainly due to their physiological characteristics:

(1) Nasal sinuses primarily start developing after birth. A child’s nasal sinus development is incomplete, with narrow and long passages connecting the sinuses to the nasal cavity, while the sinus openings are relatively large. The nasal and sinus mucosa is delicate and prone to infection, leading to mucosal edema, blocked sinus openings, and poor drainage.

(2) Factors unique to childhood also increase the likelihood of this disease. Children have weaker resistance and adapt poorly to changes in the environment. Changes in weather or improper temperature regulation can easily lead to colds and upper respiratory infections. Poor nutrition and physical weakness make them more susceptible to secondary sinus infections on top of existing illnesses. During childhood, various systems are underdeveloped, weakening the defense system and making them prone to acute infectious diseases like influenza, measles, scarlet fever, whooping cough, and pneumonia, all of which can easily lead to sinusitis.

(3) Allergic reactions are also an important factor in pediatric sinusitis. Children often have congenital immune deficiencies and allergic constitutions. Allergic reactions frequently cause nasal and sinus mucosal edema, blocking the sinus openings and hindering drainage, leading to sinusitis. Additionally, frequent colds and nasal mucosal infections increase the body's sensitivity to allergens.
