Turns out: 9 facts about quantum computing

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 12:41:28

　　Quantum Computer D-Wave

　　English Original: 9 Facts About Quantum Computing That Will Melt Your Mind

　　On Wednesday, the U.S. tech news site SAI published a long article that introduced nine facts about quantum computing in an easy-to-understand language. The article mentioned that quantum computers are not used for browsing the internet, sending and receiving emails, or running conventional software. Instead, the foundation of quantum computers is quantum mechanics. Quantum mechanics is a branch of physics that has been around for about 100 years and challenges traditional perceptions. Quantum physics studies very small objects, such as electrons and photons, and attempts to solve problems that people have previously been unable to resolve.

　　If you claim that quantum computers run like magic, this statement is not far from the truth. When we face these very small objects (imagine something smaller than a single atom), phenomena such as time travel and teleportation described in science fiction seem commonplace. Traditional physical "rules" do not apply here.

　　This undoubtedly opens up some exciting possibilities, especially in the field of optimization within mathematics. Optimization, by definition, is finding the best answer from a large number of potential answers. For this specific mathematical field, it aims to solve real-world problems that can be felt. How do UPS delivery trucks choose their optimal delivery routes? How should airports reasonably schedule flights to keep them from being delayed?

　　In handling some optimization calculations, traditional computers are quite under-equipped. Daniel Lidar, the scientific director of the USC-Lockheed Martin Quantum Computing Center, said that humans spend a lot of time verifying protein folding states, but nature can complete this task in seconds or minutes, while traditional computers would take billions of years to think about solving these problems.

　　To some extent, quantum computing also has the ability to interact with the world like nature does. This might be a profound idea that's hard for people to understand. Even so, this is just the tip of the iceberg when it comes to quantum computers.

　　1. Quantum computers rely on quantum mechanics to operate, and quantum mechanics is very "crazy"

　　Compared to the motion rules of objects we see with our naked eyes, the motion rules of quantum particles are vastly different. For example, quantum particles can exist simultaneously in two places, move forward or backward quickly, and even perform what physicists call "quantum tunneling."

　　These are usually things we see in science fiction. But in the quantum world, these phenomena are extremely common. Scientists cannot fully explain why this happens either.

　　2. No one truly understands what happens inside a quantum computer

　　A well-known concept in quantum mechanics (which also applies to general scientific principles) is that simply observing an activity changes the outcome. We are limited by the accuracy of the tools we use, which is particularly true for scientists who need precise vision. Once a quantum particle is observed or measured in any other way, its properties will change forever.

　　3. Forget about 0s and 1s – quantum computers use "qubits," and this is enough to spark "wild" thoughts

　　At the core of your personal computer, it undoubtedly processes through bits – representing 0 and 1 respectively, and nothing more. However, quantum computers use qubits to achieve their operations. Like bits, qubits can represent 0 or 1, but their true magic lies in their third state, the "superposition" state – they can represent both 0 and 1 simultaneously.

　　This magical ability means that the same set of qubits can simultaneously represent a large number of different things. For example, if you create two qubits, they can maintain four possible values simultaneously: [0,0], [0,1], [1,0] or [1,1].

　　Does this start to get a bit confusing? There's no need to worry. Some smart people who make a living studying quantum mechanics find it equally difficult to understand.

　　4. Quantum computers can solve problems that traditional computers cannot or find difficult to solve

　　Quantum computers shine when solving optimization problems. Since some optimization problems are too complex, if handled by traditional computers, it could take a tremendous amount of time, possibly billions of years.

　　A classic example is the "Traveling Salesman Problem." Imagine you have a map with a bunch of cities, and the distances between them are shown. You're a salesman trying to figure out the shortest route that covers each city. To let a traditional computer solve this problem, the only method is to record the distance of every possible route, compare them, and find the shortest one. However, this isn't a very "elegant" solution.

　　We remember that qubits can simultaneously represent more than one thing. This means that a quantum computer can try countless possible routes at the same time and return the shortest route's answer within seconds, without taking geological time periods.

　　5. Some people believe quantum computers perform computations in parallel universes

　　No one can confirm the mechanism that allows a qubit to represent more than one thing simultaneously. It’s the strange nature of quantum mechanics, which overturns our previous understanding of things. Just because we don’t understand it doesn’t mean it isn’t happening. Scientists have various views on this possibility. The most acceptable one is the multiverse theory, where theoretical physics claims there are multiple (likely infinite) parallel universes.

　　In this model, a quantum computer solving the traveling salesman problem is likely performing computations in parallel universes, tracking potential routes in other universes to significantly reduce the computation time required to solve this problem.

　　6. Your personal computer can do many things that super-expensive quantum computers can do

　　Considering that quantum computers can find optimized solutions to problems, they also depend on some basic mathematical tools found in the computers you use daily. This typically refers to already optimized basic arithmetic. Adding a bunch of numbers isn't much smarter than simply adding them, multiplying numbers isn't much smarter than simply multiplying them. In such cases, your personal computer will be just as efficient as a quantum computer.

　　7. As quantum computers become easier to use and less expensive, they will revolutionize data encryption

　　Besides solving some optimization problems, quantum computers will completely颠覆 our current concepts of encryption and data security. Any communication between two people in the future will use encryption techniques that are practically unbreakable.

　　8. Quantum computers must remain at extreme low temperatures to function properly

　　The Kelvin temperature scale, or absolute zero, is the coldest measurable temperature. According to the kinetic theory, when the temperature reaches absolute zero, the kinetic energy of a single atom is zero; when atoms stop moving, they also stop producing heat. The quantum computers produced by D-Wave in the U.S. maintain an internal temperature of 0.02°K, equivalent to -460°F.

　　9. Quantum computers operate at speeds far beyond your imagination

　　Professor Catherine McGeoch of Amherst University compared the operating speeds of quantum computers and traditional computers. After letting both devices handle the same task, McGeoch concluded that the speed of quantum computers is "thousands of times" faster than traditional computers.
