Twitter's new money-making trick: Discussing American TV series can also be profitable

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 21:29:59

Many users often chat about their favorite TV series on Twitter or discuss various topics related to popular American dramas. As cable TV kicks off the autumn TV promotion season, Twitter aims to increase revenue by leveraging its ability to spark real-time online discussions.

Before going public, Twitter hopes to play a groundbreaking role in the television industry: helping it make money. As U.S. major TV networks begin to promote new American dramas, Twitter hopes to seize this opportunity to generate more income. On Twitter, many users engage in real-time discussions about TV series.

Twitter's "hashtag" feature can track current hot trends, and this function has been used by TV networks as a marketing tool. However, TV networks do not always pay for this, but often use Twitter as a free marketing tool. Twitter is trying to leverage its website influence to create a business model, and attracting other companies to pay for using Twitter services will be a key part of this, especially considering Facebook also aims to become the center of online real-time conversations.

Twitter wants to profit from TV promotions

As TV stations launch advertising campaigns to promote new autumn programs, Twitter wants to earn more advertising revenue by creating a large amount of real-time online public opinion. The trending hashtags on Twitter have become a common marketing tool for TV stations, but they usually take advantage of Twitter's service for free, benefiting from the promotional power of this social network without paying for what they gain.

Twitter is attempting to convert its network influence into a business model. For this 7-year-old company, attracting businesses to pay for Twitter's promotional effects is crucial, especially considering that competitor Facebook also wants to become the preferred platform for real-time conversations.

In recent months, Twitter has launched several more advanced marketing products to pursue TV stations and advertisers. Its senior executives are currently meeting with the media industry at the "Advertising Week" conference in New York, and the company is expected to announce new advertising partnerships this week.

Driving up ratings

Marc DeBevoise, Executive Vice President and General Manager of CBS Interactive Entertainment, News, and Sports, said that CBS wants to use Twitter to build momentum for these TV dramas, driving more people to watch them. Regarding Amplify, he stated: "We are looking at various sponsorship and revenue models around the Twitter ecosystem."

CBS's vague attitude towards this service partly stems from the uncertainty regarding how much return this product can bring to the TV network. DeBevoise said: "We see a correlation between Twitter user activity and user reviews, but the problem is that we cannot determine the causal relationship."

Nielsen released a report in August showing that an increase in Twitter messages led to a statistically significant 29% increase in user reviews for a series of TV dramas studied by the agency. Nielsen plans to launch a Twitter-related review service on September 30, focusing on viewers who check Twitter messages related to a specific TV drama.

However, a large number of Twitter messages surrounding TV dramas does not necessarily translate into higher ratings. In July, Syfy's "Sharknado," owned by Comcast, became a "Twitter phenomenon." There were 5,000 tweets per minute about this movie. But according to Nielsen data, the movie only attracted 1.4 million viewers, consistent with previous movies.

However, Syfy still considers Twitter an important tool for connecting with audiences. Michael Engleman, the channel's Executive Vice President of Marketing, Digital, and Global Strategy, said: "We will pay for 'trends' and will pay for their analytical services."

Facing strong rival Facebook

Twitter will also encounter a formidable rival in its journey to become the small-screen partner of the television industry. Facebook announced earlier this month that it would allow a few select media partners access to the data stream of all public posts on Facebook.

These partners will be able to see Facebook user posts on specific topics and segment them according to demographic statistics such as gender. This service is currently offered for free.

Last Monday, during the first live broadcast of Disney-owned ABC's new season of "Dancing with the Stars," ABC showcased real-time social media conversations from Facebook. This is similar to how Comcast-owned NBC used Twitter during the live broadcast of "The Voice."

Fox Sports has become the first sports TV network to sign up for Facebook's service. Pete Vlastelica, Senior Vice President of Digital Business at the company, said: "I think Twitter is an excellent companion during live broadcasts. But I would say Facebook also has the potential to achieve this."
