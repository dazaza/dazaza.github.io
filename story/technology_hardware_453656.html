Not understanding the investment in customers to do business together

by rlocka69 on 2011-06-29 15:59:24

There is a saying: Leading people requires leading their hearts, but I believe that leading their hearts is not as effective as stealing their hearts - this applies not only at work but also in interpersonal relationships.

The ways to steal someone's heart are numerous and depend on each person's preferences. However, regardless of who you are dealing with, "caring even more than usual" is an essential condition for successfully stealing someone's heart.

At 3:30 AM, my phone suddenly rang.

"Who is it?" I asked half-asleep.

"Manager, I'm about to harm myself." The caller was Mr. E, a male supervisor.

Instantly, I woke up completely and asked anxiously, "Where are you?"

"I'm at home, I'm about to harm myself." Then the call ended, and no matter how many times I dialed, the phone wouldn't connect.

I immediately got out of bed, quickly changed into formal attire, and drove from my home in Tianmu to Mr. E's house in Wugu.

I pressed the doorbell anxiously, and Mr. E's sleepy wife came to open the door.

"Where is your husband?" I asked.

"What happened?" she replied.

"He said he was going to harm himself."

Mr. E's wife looked at the living room and pointed to the sofa. I saw Mr. E wearing only a pair of shorts, sleeping soundly, with empty wine bottles scattered around.

"Should I wake him up?" Mr. E's wife asked.

"No need. Just tell him I've been here and remind him to come to work tomorrow," I said.

The next day, Mr. E appeared at the company on time, and when he saw me, he smiled awkwardly.

Not long after, during a performance competition, Mr. E, who was usually quiet, surprisingly shouted out his target first. Not only that, when other colleagues set low targets, Mr. E would encourage them to raise their goals, which amazed me greatly.

I think that night, Mr. E probably just wanted someone to vent to, but he never expected that I would drive for 40 minutes at 3:30 AM to reach his house. My sincerity and care stole his heart!

If you want to steal someone's heart, then you must genuinely help them with a caring attitude. This way, before long, that person's heart will gradually gravitate towards you.

A broad mind leads to great achievements

Absolutely, absolutely do not argue with customers. There was once a monkey who complained to the Jade Emperor before reincarnation that he wanted to become human. He said, "It's unfair! Monkeys can only jump around in the mountains, while humans can have beautiful lives with comfort and luxury. I want to be human, not a monkey!"

After hearing this, the Jade Emperor asked the monkey, "Are you really sure you want to be human?"

"I am very sure!" the monkey nodded.

"Good, then let's go!" The Jade Emperor said, "Pull all the hair off the monkey."

When the first hair was pulled, the monkey almost jumped up, shouting, "It hurts! It hurts! Don't pull..."

As the second and third hairs were pulled out, the monkey not only cried like a fountain but also rolled on the ground in pain, yelling, "Don't pull anymore! Don't pull anymore!"

"You don't want to pull your hair?" the Jade Emperor asked.

"No, I don't!" the monkey confirmed.

"You have so much hair, and you're greedy, so you cannot become human. You should remain a monkey!" the Jade Emperor said.

A money-loving monkey cannot become human, just as a person with a small mind who doesn't understand investing in clients to do business will find it hard to achieve great success.

In sales, we often encounter customers who want to take advantage of small discounts. What should we do?

Years ago, when I was selling baby formula, the company stipulated that each new mother could only receive one bottle and one towel. If more was given, the salesperson had to pay for it themselves.

However, there was a head nurse in the maternity ward of a hospital who liked to "break the rules" and often requested extra towels.

Other salespeople would tell her, "No, no. If I give more, I'll have to pay for it myself."

My approach was different. When the head nurse said she hoped I could give her a few extra towels, I directly handed over a dozen towels and told her, "I'm not afraid of you taking; I'm only afraid you won't introduce clients to me."

As a result, the head nurse started recommending only the baby formula I sold to the new mothers.

I often give speeches overseas and sometimes meet foreign super sales managers. I found that people with a broad mind are more likely to win others' hearts than those with narrow minds.

For example, if a group of friends goes to a restaurant, the bill is usually split evenly; otherwise, the total cost could be quite high.

However, if you dine with a group of successful sales managers, you'll notice they all compete to pay the bill.

The reason is that in the past, whenever they dined with clients, they always treated them (and some even invited the entire family of the client). After becoming sales managers, they naturally treat their subordinates the same way. (Please note, I am talking about "successful" sales managers. Those with small minds might only become managers but cannot become "successful" managers.)

If one day you become a manager, remember, even if it's a minor managerial position, your capacity must exceed that of your subordinates. Otherwise, it will be difficult to grow your team.

Although we shouldn't haggle too much with clients, we also can't give things away for free. Since clients dare to ask, we can certainly make requests too - clients want goods, and salespeople want results. This is what's known as the "equivalence principle."
