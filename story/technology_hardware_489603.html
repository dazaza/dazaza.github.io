The basic factors affecting furnace slagging are two: one is that the melting point of coal ash is too low, and the other is that the furnace temperature is too high (or the local temperature in the furnace is too high).

(1) Coal Ash Characteristics: An important basis for judging whether slagging occurs during coal combustion is the melting characteristics of ash. The ash from different types of coal has different components and melting characteristics. If the ash melting point is very high (greater than 1350℃), it is difficult for the temperature of the ash layer on the tube wall and the nearby flue gas to exceed the softening temperature of the ash, so it is generally believed that slagging will not occur at this time. If the ash melting point is relatively low (less than 1200℃), ash particles can easily reach a softened state, making slagging more likely. Therefore, the softening temperature of ash is usually used as the main criterion for determining whether slagging will occur.

(2) Furnace Air Dynamics Characteristics: The flue gas temperature inside the furnace, as well as the temperature conditions and medium atmosphere near the water-cooled wall, are closely related to the air dynamics characteristics inside the furnace. Under normal operating conditions, the high-temperature flame center should be located at the geometric center of the furnace cross-section. In actual operation, improper organization of airflow inside the furnace can cause the flame center to shift. For example, with swirl burners arranged on the front wall, when the airflow range is too large, the flame center will move towards the rear wall, causing ash particles to adhere to the water-cooled wall on the rear wall before they have been sufficiently cooled. In the case of direct-flow burners in tangential firing furnaces, when the coal powder torch hits the wall, it can cause high temperatures near the water-cooled wall, with large amounts of ash particles impacting the heated surface of the water-cooled wall. Uneven distribution of wind and powder momentum at the four corners' burners will deform the actual tangential circle, causing the high-temperature flame to deviate from the center, leading to localized water-cooled wall slagging. Additionally, the atmosphere surrounding the molten slag particles is also an important factor affecting water-cooled wall slagging. The reducing atmosphere produced by flames of coarse coal powder or high coal-air ratios hitting the water-cooled wall promotes water-cooled wall slagging, especially when burning coal with high Fe2O3 content. This is because, in a reducing atmosphere, the high-melting-point Fe2O3 in the ash is reduced by CO into low-melting-point FeO, which then forms lower-melting-point eutectics with SiO2, sometimes decreasing the ash melting point by 150~300℃. As a result, the possibility of slagging increases.

(3) Furnace Design Characteristics: The size of the heat intensity of the furnace volume, cross-sectional heat intensity, and burner area wall heat intensity all have certain effects on slagging. When the furnace volume heat intensity is too high, due to the small furnace volume and less heat-exchanging surfaces arranged inside the furnace, the temperature inside the furnace will increase. Practical experience shows that under these conditions, slagging tends to occur on the walls near the burners. If the cross-sectional heat intensity is too high, since the heat released near the burners cannot be sufficiently absorbed by the water-cooled walls, the local temperature in the burner arrangement area becomes excessively high, easily causing slagging on the water-cooled walls near the burners. Conversely, if the cross-sectional heat intensity is too low, the furnace cross-section is large but the height is insufficient, and the flue gas does not get enough cooling before reaching the furnace outlet, leading to slagging on the heat exchange surfaces at the furnace outlet.

(4) Boiler Operating Load: When the boiler load increases, the temperature inside the furnace also correspondingly rises, increasing the likelihood of slagging.
