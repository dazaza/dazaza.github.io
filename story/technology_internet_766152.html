What If Microsoft Had Bought BlackBerry In 2009 Instead?

This week's hottest event in the tech world — not counting any potential iPhone news yet — was undoubtedly Microsoft’s acquisition of Nokia’s devices division for over $7 billion. Actually, I quite support this move. Apple and Samsung have already proven in recent years that only large-scale sales of high-priced hardware can make a company profitable in the smartphone market, with carriers also providing appropriate subsidies for phone retail. Of course, this business model will eventually become outdated, but for now, it still works effectively, and it is worth a try for Microsoft.

Before this week, Microsoft had essentially nothing going for it—mediocre services, constant battles with Android over patent issues, and very little profit. But when it came to anything truly capable of shaking up the entire industry, Microsoft had nothing.

Today’s Microsoft is no longer in decline; a reliable business model has begun to take shape—manufacturing and selling high-end smartphones. As always, the key lies in execution, and Microsoft's history shows that messing things up is all too easy. However, before last week, there was no clear path to success.

So, what if Microsoft had acquired BlackBerry back in 2009?

For example, RIM made hundreds of dollars in profit per BlackBerry sold, including fees from BlackBerry email/Internet services. On the other hand, according to data from Strategy Analytics, Microsoft only makes $8-15 in profit per phone through its WP system.

According to analysis firm projections, RIM could generate $14 billion in profits in the next fiscal year, whereas Microsoft would only make $400 million from WP (assuming 30 million Windows Phone units sold at an average licensing fee of $12 per unit). This is certainly not a small number for Microsoft.

Of course, since 2009, BlackBerry has been on a steady decline—longtime co-CEOs were ousted, its tablet market collapsed, market share and profits plummeted, and few people held much hope for BlackBerry's future.

The main issue with BlackBerry is that it failed to create an operating system that could compete with iOS, Android, or even Windows Phone. For instance, John Gruber reviewed the recently released BlackBerry Z10 as follows: "The hardware is decent, but the software is terrible."

This is why, in 2009, Microsoft should have partnered with BlackBerry—Microsoft had just launched the WP7 operating system, and BlackBerry needed a decent OS. Additionally, their overlapping businesses, RIM's market share, and even the nascent BlackBerry Messenger service could have made such a partnership a win-win situation.

Of course, the cost at the time wouldn't have been small. I wrote then: "RIM's market cap dropped to around $21 billion, and Microsoft would need about $35 billion to complete the acquisition." Today, BlackBerry's market value has shrunk to $5 billion, with most of its value evaporated. By comparison, Apple's profit reached nearly $200 billion. In this industry, only 'going big' allows you to stay relevant.

Indeed, we will never know today whether Microsoft's acquisition of BlackBerry would have been a comedy or a tragedy. Based on various statistics, the latter possibility is significant. However, this could have been one of the most important transitions in computing history, and the best opportunity missed by the world's largest software company. Acquiring BlackBerry four years ago and managing it well might have led to better results.

Alright, let's bring in some data. We've all witnessed the explosive growth of Google's miraculous Android system in recent years, but do you remember when Microsoft led the U.S. smartphone market in 2006?

The glory days are long gone—the second chart showing actual user statistics reveals the truth behind being the first to succeed. Similarly, BlackBerry once topped the charts but couldn't maintain its position. Microsoft failed to seize the moment for reform. Overconfidence too early often leads to premature decline. When Apple amazed the world with its new system, Microsoft's fate was sealed.

Similarly, Nokia didn't solve the problem either, but at least it gave Microsoft a faint possibility—a minuscule chance—for the empire to rise again. And that, is enough.
