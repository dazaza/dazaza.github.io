Liberty Global reportedly mulls acquisition of Intel's pay-TV service

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 19:25:40

According to three people familiar with the matter, Liberty Global, the European cable operator under John Malone, is in talks with Intel to acquire its in-development online pay-TV service.

Bloomberg reported that these anonymous sources revealed Malone would use systems outside the U.S. to expand Liberty Global's business. They added that negotiations between the two parties are still in the early stages and may not lead to a final agreement. Meanwhile, the sources declined to comment on the transaction price being discussed.

Liberty Global is headquartered in London and owns subsidiaries including Virgin Media in the UK. It also operates in markets such as Germany, Belgium, and other countries.

Since at least the beginning of 2012, Intel has been developing an online pay-TV service and had promised to start offering this service from this year. However, the massive investment required for cable programming deterred Intel, prompting the company to begin seeking partners. Last month, a person familiar with the matter revealed that Verizon Communications, which owns a U.S.-based fiber-optic TV service, was in talks with Intel to acquire this project under development.

Intel spokesperson Chuck Mulloy and Liberty Global spokesperson Marcus Smith both declined to comment on this news.

Compared to pay-TV service operators in the U.S. market, European operators have launched network services more quickly. In the UK, BT Group has started offering free access to paid sports content for its broadband service users. Virgin Media, under Liberty Global, announced in September that it would bundle Netflix's streaming subscription service for its cable TV service users.

Strategic Shift

As of yesterday's close this year, Liberty Global's stock price traded in New York has risen by 26%. Through Liberty Media, located in Englewood, Colorado, Malone holds shares in several pay-TV service providers, including Charter Communications, the fourth-largest operator in the U.S.

Intel, the world's largest chipmaker, hired former BBC executive Erik Huggers in January 2011 to oversee its digital media division as the company sought new ways to deliver interactive content powered by its chips.

Huggers said in February this year that the company planned to start offering online pay-TV services from this year. The product Intel is developing, named OnCue, is designed to provide content that can be viewed on televisions, smartphones, and other mobile devices.

Mobile Market

Although Intel had planned to offer both live broadcasts and on-demand video programs, Brian Krzanich, who became CEO in May this year, has shifted the focus to selling more mobile phone chips.

A person familiar with the matter revealed in September that earlier this year, Intel decided to reduce investments related to the pay-TV service project and began looking for partners who own Intel's user base or hold rights to films and TV shows.

Intel CFO Stacy Smith stated in an interview on October 15: "For us, having rich content available to provide an excellent user experience is crucial, so we need the right partners. In the end, (whether the pay-TV service succeeds) is entirely related to whether it can attract users and audiences."
