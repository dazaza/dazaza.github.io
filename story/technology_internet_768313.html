Twitter is red-hot, but the challenges are big

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 20:18:43

Twitter's preparation for its IPO this week is undoubtedly the most watched event in the tech world recently. Compared with other tech companies, Twitter has much greater visibility; it is often a driver of hot events and a platform for public opinion, being a source of many news stories. Since Twitter has proven its value in information dissemination multiple times, it garners much more attention from the general public.

Moreover, yesterday Twitter increased its offering price, highlighting the capital market’s recognition of its value. According to the latest offering price of $23 to $25 per share, Twitter's maximum market value could reach $13.6 billion, potentially raising a total of up to $2 billion, even exceeding Google's initial public offering financing amount, ranking high among tech companies. These funds will support Twitter's future development.

However, the market is always unpredictable, and one must remain calm, especially during periods of frenzy. Although Twitter is extremely popular, not everyone is buying into it, particularly when compared with its biggest rival — Facebook — investors often side with the latter. A survey of investors released on Monday by the Associated Press and financial TV channel CNBC found that many people are skeptical about Twitter.

The survey showed that only 36% of active investors considered this a good deal, while as high as 49% did not approve of Twitter. Among investors earning over $75,000 annually, 56% did not approve of Twitter. In comparison, last year, when Facebook went public, 54% of investors were optimistic.

There are also some unfavorable data points, such as more than half of users not posting messages but instead "lurking," indicating low user engagement. Meanwhile, in Twitter's primary profit model, ads placed in the timeline are not very effective, with more than half of users never noticing promoted content. Only 42% of users have noticed them, and only 31% have clicked on ads. According to Forbes, Twitter's average ad price has been declining sharply, with a drop of 46% in the third quarter. To offset the impact of lower prices, Twitter has had to increase ad placements, which could affect user experience.

This poll comes from 1,000 samples, with respondents being investors who have traded stocks in the past year. The accuracy of the data is debatable, but it provides valuable reference.

The capital market's dual attitude towards Twitter is filled with contradictions. On one hand, Twitter is already large enough, with undeniable market and social values. On the other hand, Twitter indeed faces some thorny issues.

Earlier, BGR analyzed why Twitter initiated its IPO in September, citing reasons such as slowing user growth and an uncertain future, along with a surge in strong competitors.

Twitter announced in December last year that its monthly active users reached 200 million. The company's CEO Dick Costolo earlier this year told employees that active user numbers would reach 400 million by the end of this year.

But currently, Twitter's latest monthly active user count stands at 240 million, meaning that this year Twitter has added less than 500,000 new users per month. If this pace continues, by the end of this year, Twitter's active user number can only reach around 260 million, representing a growth rate of approximately 30%, rather than the 100% expected by Costolo.

Meanwhile, a slew of emerging social applications are competing with Twitter for users. These apps have performed exceptionally well recently, with Pinterest, Snapchat, Instagram, Kik, and others seeing good development, with increasing user numbers and valuations. Pinterest was valued at $2.5 billion in its financing round in February this year, and Snapchat's latest valuation has reached as high as $4 billion.

Slowing user growth is just one of Twitter's problems. Additionally, profitability is another challenge facing Twitter. Twitter has never turned a profit since its inception, with a net loss of $69.3 million in the first half of this year, an increase of 41% year-over-year. In contrast, Facebook's net profit was already as high as $1 billion in the year before its IPO, with a significant gap in revenue per user.

As a company primarily reliant on advertising for income, increasing revenue often seems to contradict enhancing user experience, yet Twitter has still taken this route, beginning in October to emphasize visual effects in ads to improve promotional effectiveness and average ad prices.

Of course, Twitter is indeed seeking more sophisticated advertising methods, such as collaborating with TV programs to create "audio commentary tracks" for TV shows, promoting mutual interaction between both parties, combining topics to attract advertisers, and enhancing the appeal of advertisements.

Regardless, with the backing of the gold-standard product Twitter, its stock remains highly sought after. However, caution should be exercised against excessive market hype to avoid following in Facebook's footsteps of long-term trading below its IPO price. Today, Facebook's stock price has soared to $50 due to successful commercialization and smooth mobile adaptation, but for Twitter, the challenges will be much greater.
