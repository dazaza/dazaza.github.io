Apple's Mishaps Keep Coming: iPhone 5s Accelerometer Issues

by anonymous on 2013-10-22 19:22:00

Users who joyfully purchased the iPhone 5s might recently have noticed that the device's Compass app seems a bit off. According to user feedback on Apple’s support forum, when an iPhone 5s is placed flat on a surface and the Level function within the Compass app is activated, a deviation of 4°-6° is observed.

Gizmodo also conducted tests and found that the compass direction itself has a deviation of approximately 8°-10°. What happens when the level function experiences such a deviation? Applications that rely on the hardware accelerometer may encounter issues. For instance, in the game "Real Racing 3," the car tends to drift automatically to the left, making it difficult to continue playing.

Why does the accelerometer in the iPhone 5s malfunction? Eagle Jones, CEO of RealityCap, identified the cause as being related to hardware design issues.

Based on teardowns by Chipworks and iFixit, Jones discovered that the iPhone 5s uses the Sensortech BMA220 accelerometer model, rather than the STMicroelectronics L3G4200D used in previous Apple products like the iPhone 5, iPhone 4s, and iPad 2. What are the differences between these two products?

Jones explained that there are two key metrics for evaluating the performance of an accelerometer:

STMicroelectronics' Noise Density and Bosch's Output Noise — these two data points influence the precision of the accelerometer measurements. From the specifications, Bosch's accelerometer performs less accurately in this aspect compared to STMicroelectronics'. However, after practical testing, the output noise data of the iPhone 5s was found to be comparable to that of earlier iOS devices. The Zero-G Offset is another critical metric. This value indicates that manufacturers calibrate the sensor first to ensure accurate data output. However, this value can change with physical stress or temperature variations. And here, Jones found an issue: the zero-g offset for STMicroelectronics' accelerometer is +/- 20mg, while Bosch's is +/- 95mg. To put it simply, +/- 20mg corresponds to +/- 1° of deviation, whereas +/- 95mg equates to +/- 5° of deviation.

It cannot be said that Apple chose Bosch's accelerometer to save costs, as the prices of both are roughly equivalent, around $1. Therefore, Apple selected Bosch's product for other reasons — Bosch's accelerometer operates at 1.8V, compared to STMicroelectronics' 2.5V, meaning the former consumes less power.

Now the question arises: since the iPhone 5s has already been released, and for various reasons, not all faulty phones may be replaced, what solutions exist for addressing the accelerometer data deviation issue? Jones proposed that developers could add a calibration factor within their applications to normalize performance.

Apple, known for its perfectionism, has made repeated mistakes in its products lately, a fact that leaves many people disappointed. Regardless of whether the purpose was to save power or something else, such errors in the accelerometer are hard to forgive. Did Apple fail to detect this problem during testing? Or did they consider it minor enough to release the product with the defect? Apple Maps has already become a stain in the history of Apple products; hopefully, the iPhone 5s won't repeat the same mistake.

To be honest, I’d rather Apple be notorious for delays than rush to market problematic products. As an outsider, I can't imagine what's happening internally at Apple, from Apple Maps to the iPhone 5s accelerometer issues. Perhaps Tim Cook really is the problem.
