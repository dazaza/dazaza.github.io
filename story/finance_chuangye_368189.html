How high is the profit margin of a clothing store?

Author: Source: First Entrepreneurship Network Update Time: October 9, 2010

Question   A friend has rented a small shop for doing clothing business in the recent two years. But there is not much cash in hand at the end of each year, even less than the costs invested before the Spring Festival. So I don't know how to calculate the profit of the shop???        

However, from the daily business situation, it seems that basically some money can be earned, or at least break even, sometimes even lose money. But after a month, there should be some profit overall. So why can't we see any cash at the end??        

Profit = total monthly sales - cost of goods - utilities and taxes - rent - other miscellaneous fees        

But there are some things unclear here, which is: every time the proceeds from selling goods are used to buy new goods, so how should this be calculated?]???        

Sometimes we do the total accounting at the end of the year like this: Profit = (cash on hand at the end of the year + value of inventory)   -   cash invested before the Spring Festival   -   miscellaneous expenses of the current year (daily expenditures)        

Is this method feasible? Is it reasonable?        

At first glance, it seems correct, but actually, it overlooks the labor cost as the owner, which is also known as the opportunity cost in accounting terms. In simple terms, you need to estimate your own salary, that is, if you don't open a clothing store, you will have wage income, which is the "opportunity" of doing this instead of that, so it needs to be counted, referring to the average standard wage you could earn based on your actual ability in society. Additionally, there's the interest if the invested funds were deposited in the bank. Ideally: Profit = Income - Investment (including estimated interest) - Salary - Taxes, Business Management Fees - Daily Miscellaneous Expenses.
