Netizens claim that after signing the house viewing agreement, they were threatened by the intermediary when they wanted to buy a house elsewhere

by zzf000zxyr6 on 2011-07-12 13:02:04

Shanghai Morning Post reporter, Zhang Yi

For many home buyers, being asked to sign a "house viewing agreement" is an experience they often encounter. Recently, a netizen posted online about his painful experiences of being threatened by the original real estate agent after he changed agents due to price issues. In this situation, the party involved had signed on the house viewing agreement which caused him quite a bit of trouble. The lawyer stated that one should carefully read through the terms before signing.

Netizen:

This agent showing but another agent closing leads to threats

Netizen "Blue Watch" posted that he liked a house from a certain real estate agency, but neither the price nor the commission could be negotiated to his ideal level. Afterwards, "Blue Watch" found the same house at another agency with a more satisfactory fee and price. When the original agency learned about this matter, they called and threatened "Blue Watch", stating that if he signed with another company, they would sue him. They also mentioned that the house was locked on the real estate information website, implying that even if "Blue Watch" bought it, he wouldn't be able to complete the transfer process.

According to "Blue Watch's" description, he had signed a "house viewing agreement" when he viewed the house with this agency. Based on this, the agency claimed that if "Blue Watch" closed the deal with another agency, it would be considered as a breach of contract. They also threatened that even if "Blue Watch" bought the house, they wouldn't let his family live in peace. Due to this, "Blue Watch" posted online for help.

Lawyer:

Can raise objections to extreme clauses

Lawyer Wu Bin from Shanghai Nuosheng Law Firm said that the house viewing agreement with signatures serves as an agreement between both parties and should be recognized as legally binding unless it violates any laws. If the house purchased by the buyer was indeed provided by the previous intermediary for viewing services, the court can reasonably determine that the buyer should pay the necessary intermediary service fees to the intermediary. Given the current actual situation, viewing services take up a considerable proportion in the entire intermediary service, and the specific standard will be determined by the court according to individual cases to balance the interests of the intermediary company and the home buyer. According to Wu Bin's introduction, in a similar case, the court finally decided that the homebuyer should pay 5000 yuan in service fees to the intermediary.

As for the extreme clause in the house viewing agreement that states "cannot purchase this house through other intermediaries within 6 months or even a year," it is suspected of infringing on the home buyer's right to choose. Wu Bin reminds home buyers that once they sign, it indicates to some extent that they accept the terms of the agreement. Therefore, home buyers should raise objections to unreasonable terms before signing and negotiate to avoid disputes. Regarding the threat from the intermediary locking the property information on the real estate information website and preventing the transfer, Wu Bin clearly stated that "this behavior is not legal."
