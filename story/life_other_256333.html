1. **Xiangyang Bakery**: I think their blueberry cake is pretty good, it's quite tender and looks very juicy. The current Napoleon cake is an improved version, but it's not as exquisite as it was three years ago. The old one tasted better with more layers, and it even had soda crackers. It used to be my favorite, but the current one is far inferior to what it used to be.

2. **Andrewson**: There are so few varieties, after all these years, there hasn't been much new stuff, or just a change of name for the same thing. I think their bread with ham is pretty tasty, and also their pillow cake. Once, I bought pillow cakes from Andrewson, Xiangyang Bakery, and Ousimai to compare, and only Andrewson's cake stood out with its aroma, having a rich dairy fragrance and a moist texture. However, when I tried it a while ago, it seemed to have become ordinary. Another long-time favorite is their carrot toast, especially the thick slices. After being disappointed by the pillow cake, only this maintains its consistently high quality.

3. **Aolian**: Their tuna and walnut offerings, I personally find them too oily and greasy. After trying everything, I only like the $3.50 (used to be $3) Black Forest cake in the freezer. Once, I bought it and on the bus, I couldn't resist peeling off the shell and finished it in no time. It was satisfying and not at all greasy. I absolutely love it!

4. **Te Xiang Bao**: When I was a kid, I always thought Te Xiang Bao was just a big bread, but in fact, it’s actually a bakery. Although they have many stores, the craftsmanship differs. The regular stores generally don't have much to commend them. You could say the sweet ones are cloying and coarse, making you uncomfortable. But, the Te Xiang Bao from the old store in Sibei is really chewy and fragrant, unforgettable! It doesn’t fall short compared to Breaktalk's large bread.

5. **Ousimai**: I once exchanged all my Xiangyang coupons for Ousimai, not for any other reason, just for their daily bread crusts. These hard-to-find bread crusts are both resilient and flavorful. The key is that a full bag costs only 3 yuan, and it's very satisfying to eat. I've tried Aolian's, which is soft and limp, lacking strength. It seems I prefer chewy and non-greasy bread, so I also fell in love with their French baguette. However, the quality of the baguette fluctuates. Besides, their Empress Pastry is also good, though some might find it too sweet.

6. **Shengfuxing**: This name has always made me feel it's a bit rustic. I only like their Japanese-style hot dog (I think that's the name). It's a slice of bread rolled around a sausage, with a small piece of grilled cheese on the outside. The highlight is this piece of cheese; I once tried to fry cheese at home like this, but failed multiple times—either the shape was ugly or it melted apart, never as intact and solid as theirs, salty and fragrant. As for the rest, most lack distinct features or have fancy shapes but mediocre taste.

7. **Aloha**: I like their hard bread, the crust has a delightful crispy flavor after baking. Some are topped with bacon, others with scallions and garlic, which are also nice. Then there's the freshly baked French baguette, which may well be considered one of the best in the world. Their garlic breadsticks are good, but eating too much can get greasy. I haven't been to the store for half a year, so I’ve kind of forgotten. In general, I hate their coconut tarts; I took one bite and threw it straight into the trash, a memory that remains vivid.

8. **BreadTalk**: I don't know why I've never had much affection for it. Four years ago in Xidan, Beijing, during their opening promotion, I remember the cheapest item in the entire store was a 5-yuan savory bread, which seemed novel and fragrant. Mainly because their varieties were all relatively unique. Now with the times progressing, I feel it's nothing special. If it's the same as other places, then it becomes comparable, but since they're all different, isn't saying it's delicious somewhat one-sided? Moreover, some of their staff members are more arrogant than those in Gucci.

9. **Hei Mian Cai**: Selling doughnuts that are both sweet and fattening for 7 yuan each seems a bit unfair. Andrewson's doesn't seem to exceed 3 yuan, right? And for such food, our family tradition is to use it to redeem bread coupons, so we basically don't like it. Last month, while aimlessly wandering in Sibei, I went in and bought a plain one, which was fairly chewy, but how much can you expect from a bread ring?

Additionally, about **Yuanzu**: Xiamen might not have it yet, but in many big cities, it's everywhere. Their birthday cakes are truly remarkable, as are some of their small pastries, occupying much of my thoughts. Also, Rosa's most ordinary lemon cake, that fineness and softness... One time, I brought it back from afar, and after my mom ate it, she said that after eating this cake, Andrewson's seemed so rough...
