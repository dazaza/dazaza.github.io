The two new iPhone models will be released simultaneously in China and the rest of the world.

by anonymous on 2013-08-29 17:00:13

According to information obtained from carrier sources, the iPhone set for release in September will include two models: the "iPhone 5S" and the "iPhone 5C." The iPhone 5C is the long-rumored plastic-cased iPhone, while the next-generation iPhone has been confirmed to be named the iPhone 5S.

This source also revealed that both iPhone models have received certification from the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology and will be simultaneously released by China Unicom and China Telecom. More importantly, Chinese carriers will achieve global synchronization for the first time, with sales of the new iPhone beginning at the end of September. However, the exact pricing remains unclear.

The much-anticipated LTE version of the iPhone does not yet have a definitive update due to licensing issues; it is reported that the packaging of test units lacks the "LTE" label. The introduction of the LTE version of the iPhone will depend on the progress of the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology issuing 4G licenses. If the ministry releases the 4G license in September, China will sell iPhones supporting the TD-LTE 4G network; if the 4G license is issued later, future firmware upgrades will be needed to support LTE networks.

iPhone 5S and iPhone 5C

This year marks the first time since the iPhone's launch in 2007 that Apple will release two iPhone models simultaneously—validating the reports from The Wall Street Journal which began covering rumors of a low-cost iPhone back in 2010.

Details about the configuration of the iPhone 5C are currently mostly speculative and unconfirmed. It is rumored to share similar internal specifications with the iPhone 5S, including an 8-megapixel camera, a 1600mAh battery, and a 326 PPI screen. Differences lie in its use of the A6 chip, f2.4 aperture, and polycarbonate plastic casing. There are claims that the iPhone 5C may not support Siri.

The definition of "C" in iPhone 5C is speculated to stand for color or cheap, but given Apple's style, they likely wouldn't overtly emphasize "cheap," so it might simply mean iPhone 5 Color. It is rumored that the iPhone 5C will come in six colors: black, white, green, blue, pink, and yellow (another version mentions black, white, gray, silver, and gold). The attention on "iPhone 5C" was sparked by the exposure of five-colored plastic shells of the low-cost iPhone by former Apple employee Sonny Dickson on Twitter on July 10 this year.

Despite analysts and Apple's upstream suppliers not considering the iPhone 5C as truly "cheap," it is expected to be priced around $400, positioning it as a mid-range product. Therefore, during the official launch, Apple would prefer not to convey any message of "cheapness." Comparatively, referring to it as a low-cost iPhone would be more objective.

In July, there were reports that the low-cost iPhone had undergone multiple trial productions at Foxconn, with those who handled the plastic-cased iPhone claiming that the plastic feels "very good." The quality can be compared to the shells of Nokia Lumia series phones, though based on numerous leaked shells, the iPhone 5C might use glossy plastic rather than matte or sandblasted materials.

Compared to the iPhone 5C, the annually upgraded iPhone 5S has garnered less attention. Speculated configurations for this flagship model include: a 64-bit A7 chip, 1GB RAM, 8-megapixel camera, 326 PPI screen, f2.0 large aperture, and possibly a 128GB version.

However, the iPhone 5S represents a significant profit source for Apple. For six years prior to the appearance of the low-cost iPhone, the iPhone and iPad constituted Apple's primary profit sources, boosting the company's overall gross margin (as seen in the chart below). After the release of the iPhone 5C, Apple's profit margin will inevitably be squeezed. If the iPhone 5S is overshadowed by the iPhone 5C—a situation already observed with the iPad mini—Apple's gross margin will decline. Ideally, the iPhone 5C will capture the mid-to-low-end market dominated by Android, while the iPhone 5S focuses on the high-end market.

The iPhone event is expected to take place on September 10th ("9·11" being a U.S. memorial day, no event will occur then). Following tradition, pre-orders should be available on September 13th (Friday), with official sales starting on September 20th.

Synchronous Release with Global Carriers

Based on the aforementioned carrier source, Chinese carriers will synchronously release the Apple iPhone globally. According to past practices, Apple specialty stores, Apple Store, will also release the new iPhone simultaneously. This means that Chinese consumers can expect to purchase the iPhone 5S and iPhone 5C around September 20th.

This person revealed that both China Unicom and China Telecom will be included in Apple's first batch of carriers selling the iPhone, introducing both new models. China Telecom's contract phones will also be sold in Apple Stores. However, whether China Mobile will introduce the iPhone remains uncertain.

This year, the inclusion of China Unicom and China Telecom in the first batch of iPhone releases holds symbolic significance. Since China Unicom introduced the iPhone 3GS in 2009 and China Telecom introduced the iPhone 4S in early 2012, Chinese carriers have always been placed after the third batch of carriers, preventing Chinese consumers from obtaining the latest iPhone simultaneously with global consumers. This year marks the first time in history that the two major carriers will globally synchronize the release of the iPhone.

China's market is receiving increased attention from Apple due to its immense potential and the challenges faced by Apple within this market. According to an IDC report released in June, 97 million phones were sold in the Chinese market in the first quarter of this year, representing a 15% increase year-over-year; among these, 78 million were smartphones, showing a 117% year-over-year increase. IDC predicts that China's smartphone market will maintain rapid growth in 2013, reaching an annual shipment of 460 million smartphones by 2017, with a market size of 740.5 billion yuan. Moreover, China's 4G capacity is also substantial, with IDC predicting that over half of the phones sold in 2017 will be 4G phones, with the remaining half being 3G phones. ABI Research forecasts that LTE-Advanced will reach 159 million users in the Asia-Pacific region by 2018, with most being Chinese users.

More importantly, Apple's market share in China has been continuously squeezed by the impact of domestically produced low-cost smartphones. The aforementioned IDC report shows that Apple holds only a 9% share in China, ranking fifth, lagging behind Samsung by 10 percentage points. Samsung's shipments of smartphones priced under $200 in China grew by 47% year-over-year in the first quarter, a price range known as "thousand-yuan smartphones" in China, which has been cultivated by domestic phone manufacturers and Chinese carriers for over two years, achieving maturity. Recently, Xiaomi even reduced prices to 799 yuan with its "Redmi" brand to secure massive shipments. Additionally, the traditional relationship between "ZTE Huawei Coolpad Lenovo" and operators, as well as the expansion of their own channels (such as e-commerce channels), continues to squeeze Apple's market share.

If Apple continues to place Chinese carriers in later batches, it risks missing out on the 2013 feast in the Chinese market, especially regarding the iPhone 5C specifically targeting the Chinese market, which needs to quickly gain market share. Starting from September onwards, many domestic and international manufacturers will host several product launches, including Meizu MX3, Xiaomi 3, Samsung Galaxy Note 3, Sony, OPPO, etc., all primarily targeting the mid-to-low-end market, making them strong competitors for the iPhone 5C.

Of course, placing the Chinese market in the first batch also entails risks, with production capacity being the biggest concern. Previously, The Wall Street Journal reported that Apple's Asian contract manufacturers started producing standard and low-cost iPhones as early as June this year, but whether three months of production can meet the demands of the Chinese market and other global markets remains uncertain. Shortages are expected during the initial launch phase. Once shortages occur, it becomes an opportunity for companies like Meizu, Xiaomi, and Samsung. Therefore, the Chinese market represents a tough challenge for Apple.

iPhone and China Mobile

The possibility of China Mobile introducing the iPhone this year is very high. On July 30, Tim Cook visited China again just six months after his previous visit, starting his trip at China Mobile's headquarters—an arrangement different from previous visits.

If senior management's movements could be considered speculation, objectively speaking, the timing for China Mobile to introduce the iPhone has matured, mainly due to the development of TD-LTE 4G network technology led by China Mobile.

Unlike being constrained by TD-SCDMA in the past, TD-LTE is a widely accepted 4G standard internationally. Currently, there are 17 commercial TD-LTE networks globally with 200,000 TD-LTE base stations. TD-LTE is a parallel 4G standard to LTE-FDD, and Apple's motivation to support this network far exceeds its interest in the uniquely Chinese TD-SCDMA.

After the release of the iPhone in 2007, China Mobile executives traveled to Cupertino in November to negotiate the introduction of the iPhone. However, these discussions stalled in 2008 when Apple launched the App Store due to overlapping business interests with China Mobile's Monternet service—a concern corroborated during my recent interview with NTT Docomo's headquarters in Japan. Subsequently, China Unicom introduced the iPhone in 2009, followed by China Telecom in early 2012. China Mobile's plans to reintroduce the iPhone began after 2011 but failed due to restrictions imposed by the TD-SCDMA 3G network standard and differences in commercial terms.

Currently, the technical standard issue has been resolved, and the Chinese government announced that 4G licenses would be issued in the second half of 2013. Specific arrangements can now be made accordingly. For example, if the 4G license is issued in September ahead of schedule, China Mobile could enter the first batch of carriers; if issued after October, China Mobile would be placed later.

There is another possibility: Given that China Mobile's 4G network is still in its initial construction phase with insufficient coverage, and its TD-SCDMA 3G network already has broad coverage, allowing China Mobile's iPhones to drop to TD-SCDMA or GSM-Edge networks where 4G coverage is unavailable, thus requiring downward compatibility with TD-SCDMA networks. This implies that even without a 4G license, China Mobile could potentially be listed in the first batch and then upgrade via firmware to support TD-LTE. The most optimistic scenario is that all three Chinese carriers could simultaneously introduce the iPhone globally, though I believe this possibility is smaller.

After resolving the technical issues, the only remaining task is negotiating commercial interests. At the press conference for China Mobile's semi-annual financial results on August 15, Chairman Xi Guohua stated that "negotiations with Apple are ongoing positively, both sides desire cooperation, but there remain some commercial and technical issues," highlighting the focal points of discussion. The "technical issues" mentioned here likely refer to downward compatibility with TD-SCDMA, while commercial issues involve committed sales volumes, promotion support, subsidy support, and even revenue sharing from value-added services. These negotiations have been ongoing for four to five years, with the previous chairman Wang Jianzhou taking a hard stance and historical timing not aligning. With Chairman Xi Guohua reportedly having a more conciliatory attitude (due to user losses to other operators in the 3G era as shown in the chart), coupled with the allure of the Chinese market to Apple and signs of Apple's declining momentum, the likelihood of successful negotiations increases.

Even if China Mobile fails to successfully introduce the iPhone this year, the iPhone 5S and iPhone 5C will still have close ties to China Mobile. According to previous reports, the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology will issue 4G licenses based on the TD-LTE network standard first, followed by LTE-FDD licenses. Currently, China Mobile has built 20,000 TD-LTE base stations (planning to build 200,000), while China Telecom and China Unicom are just beginning their investments in TD-LTE networks. This means that after the issuance of TD-LTE licenses, these two operators may need to rent China Mobile's TD-LTE network to provide 4G services to their users (Xi Guohua also mentioned this on August 15). Although in the long term, according to our information, China Telecom and China Unicom lean towards developing LTE-FDD networks.

Finally, considering China Mobile's stringent requirements for chips supporting "five modes and ten frequencies," TD-LTE versions of the iPhone may be priced higher than regular iPhones. If China Mobile's subsidy level is not substantial enough, combined with network coverage issues, the appeal might be limited. However, with its 740 million users, even capturing a small portion would significantly boost Apple.
