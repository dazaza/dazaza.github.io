Learning to negotiate is a great advantage in the workplace.

by qingian007 on 2012-03-09 11:48:54

In the workplace, compromise is often not a good solution and in most cases, it is a method that cannot bring about good results. In order to achieve good mediation, one must master some informal negotiation skills in the workplace.

Mr. Li is the English creative director of a trading company, responsible for packaging products; Xiao Qiang belongs to the sales department, responsible for presenting and selling the company's products to customers. [Many people are repulsed by Xiao Qiang because he frequently encounters problems when communicating with different departments.] Mr. Li said. Xiao Qiang always likes to take the copy provided by the creative department to express dissatisfaction to his superior, such as [these few English expressions are not authentic] or [this concept is completely outdated], but never directly proposes suggestions to the department or individuals who provide the copy.

Everyone is clear about Xiao Qiang's copywriting and creative abilities. Mr. Li said: [Even if he really feels there’s an issue, he can directly discuss with colleagues from the creative department or 'negotiate' with me, the department supervisor, instead of complaining to upper management. This way of cooperation ignores my existence and will affect their evaluation of me.]

Once, Xiao Qiang was rushing to sell a classic product to a customer, Mr. Li said [I no longer want to cooperate with this kind of person, so I just made up an excuse that other sales managers were also rushing for packaging, and told him he had to wait in line. So, I kept delaying. As a result, the deal failed.] Mr. Li said, [Because of this incident, Xiao Qiang was demoted to deputy manager, lost his bonus for the first quarter, and finally resigned unhappily. He couldn't wait for the new copy, so he just took last year's and made random changes.

A [negotiation expert] understands the principles of human nature and adapts accordingly. Depending on the negotiation counterpart, different interests, and relationships, we adopt different negotiation techniques, requiring thorough preparation.

Negotiation is not about winning or losing, only about good or bad results: Workplace negotiation differs from commercial negotiation; it is not warfare. Negotiation aims at better collaboration and is a joint effort by both parties to reach a consensus. Our focus should shift from [commercially defeating the opponent] to [mutual assistance in work]. The result of negotiation only varies between good and bad, not win or lose.

Reasons are not necessarily essential; caring for others' emotions is equally important: During negotiations, one should respect the other party to better understand their perspective and ideas, steering the negotiation outcome towards the expected direction.

Negotiation is flexibility, ultimately aiming for [win-win]: True workplace negotiation may require mutual concessions and compromises, essentially a form of mutual adaptability.

[Negotiation] permeates work and life. When mentioning [negotiation], don’t assume it's a serious term. It's not exclusively a commercial behavior. In fact, even with our closest people, we can learn the techniques and art of [negotiation] to help us resolve interpersonal dilemmas. Negotiation doesn't necessarily mean sitting solemnly around a conference table discussing issues. It can happen anytime and anywhere. Moreover, the society we live in is a vast network of interpersonal relationships. Thus, [negotiation] means learning to integrate resources and find the best balance point.

Whether in work or life, [negotiation] is an art that helps us reach consensus with others and is an effective way to manage interpersonal relationships. In the workplace, when conflicts arise with superiors, colleagues, or even the company, [negotiation] becomes indispensable. Mastering this art is a required course for professionals.
