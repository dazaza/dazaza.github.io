Breeding centipedes requires attention to the potential issues with the centipedes themselves. Generally, each female lays 20-60 eggs, and the smooth egg-laying process takes about 2-3 hours. The eggs stick together, resembling the size of a peanut, and are oval in shape. The female centipede guards the eggs for about 43 days. During this period, if disturbed or stimulated by external factors, the female may become agitated and eat all the hatching eggs, leading to a failed hatch. Therefore, the environment of the hatching pool should be kept quiet absolutely. Especially note that feeding should be stopped a few days before the centipede lays eggs, especially during the hatching process from day 40 to 50, feeding must be strictly prohibited. Otherwise, when the centipede eats, the food may stick to the eggs or embryos and cause them to be eaten as well. The hatching process is relatively slow; after 15-16 days, the eggs form a kidney shape, start splitting in the middle, which is the first molting stage. After 20 days, the second molting forms a crescent shape, initially showing the larval morphology. Around 35-40 days, the larvae enter the third molting phase and can crawl up and down within the mother's embrace. After another 5-7 days, the larval cuticle gradually thickens, turning yellow-brown, living under the protection of the mother. After about a month, they can live independently and search for food on their own.

Centipedes are oviparous animals. They usually mate in clear weather after rain from March to June. Every spring and early summer, the eggs gradually mature. Egg-laying starts from mid-June, with the peak in the first and second ten-day periods of June and July, continuing until early August. Before laying eggs, the female's body becomes bloated, her abdomen close to the ground, moving slowly. For females showing signs of laying eggs, they should be isolated using a bottomless canister of about 10 cm in diameter 3-4 days in advance, covering the opening with fiberboard or glass. This effectively utilizes the breeding area, avoiding mutual interference and predation, facilitating a peaceful environment for successful egg-laying and also making it easier to separate the young centipedes from the mother for separate rearing (or separate pools).

On the other hand, breeding personnel should prevent being stung and poisoned by centipedes.

1. Preventing centipedes from suffering from diseases: Due to poultry, rats, lizards, etc., preying on centipedes, especially ants being the main natural enemy of centipedes, regular observation should be conducted inside and outside the centipede field to check for the appearance of harmful insects, preventing these enemies from invading and attacking the centipedes. To prevent ants from entering the pool, use cockroach and ant powder around the pool or mix camphor balls, plant oil, and sawdust in a five-part ratio, evenly mixing them, and frequently sprinkle this medicinal powder around the outer walls of the breeding pool to form a circle of medicine lines. Especially during molting, egg-laying, or hatching, centipedes have weak resistance and can easily be bitten to death by ants. If ants enter the pool, quickly transfer the centipedes and scald the ants with boiling water.
