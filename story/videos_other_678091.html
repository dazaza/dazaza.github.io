College students planning to use 1 egg for $1000000 (group)

by lidgen7nt on 2012-02-27 11:39:17

The record of the camera star signature cricket eggs for the Aussie Millions journey: from eggs, CDs, cameras to automobiles, star signatures crickets, and African travel packages... all for 1,000,000 Australian dollars. According to reports by Australian media on the 11th day, an egg usually costs only a few cents, however, an ordinary egg in Australia is expected to become worth $1,000,000.

Three Australian college students, in order to raise funds for charity, launched the "One Egg Around the World" plan in November 2008. They intended to use the method of bartering, starting with one egg, aiming to eventually reach $1,000,000 (equivalent to 6,220,000 yuan).

According to the report, these three students — Christopher Wright, Josh Ou Mei, and Andrew Fincher — are engineering students at an Australian university. In November 2008, in order to raise donations for charity, these three students initiated the "One Egg Around the World" plan. They intended to use the method of bartering, exchanging an ordinary egg for other items, gradually transforming it into more valuable goods, ultimately aiming to exchange the egg for $1,000,000.

According to the sponsors of the plan, Wright and Ou Mei, they have been inspired by the sensational story of "Pins for a House" that created a stir a few years ago. In 2006, Canadian youth Kyle MacDonald used the internet and, through bartering, exchanged a red paperclip for a beautiful residence.

Inspired by this story, Wright and Ou Mei, when raising a cat named "Hot Box" in their backyard, suddenly thought — why not follow suit using an egg as "start-up capital", using it to eventually exchange for a $1,000,000 donation to charity?

For the laying hen, they built a website soon after. Wright and Ou Mei pulled another classmate, Andrew Fincher, into the "$1,000,000" program. They published the plan on the social networking site "Facebook" personal space, but this "Facebook" personal space master was not them — it was the hen "Hot Box" who laid the egg! Their rapid actions won a lot of support among their friends. Soon, "One Egg Around the World" became a famous "Facebook" website discussion group, with a large number of users applying to become part of "Hot Box". Later, Wright and the two other undergraduates simply established a special website for the "One Egg Around the World" plan. Wright and Ou Mei said they think the "$1,000,000" exchange program will be a very good fundraising tool.

It has "value-added" to $2,200 really. Immediately after the exposure of the "$1,000,000" creative action, it caused numerous netizens' attention. Soon, the three students achieved their first transaction — the egg was exchanged for the soundtrack of the movie "Coneheads". Then, the soundtrack was exchanged for a series of word games, and the word games were replaced with a travel book. The travel book was then exchanged for an old digital camera, which was then replaced by an alarm clock...

Ou Mei said: "The exchange goods are very small, but the turnover rate is very rapid. The first five transactions occurred within a month." Letting Wright and the three undergraduates surprise, after several twists and turns, they exchanged the alarm clock for a 1988 model old car. Then, they sold the car, earning about $100. After several rounds of exchanges, after nine months, the egg had been replaced by a late famous cricket star Don Bradman's autographed cricket. Then, they sold the cricket to a user for 2,200 Australian dollars, and then used the money to buy a 14-day Kenya two-person travel package.

They plan to donate any raised money. After 15 months of non-stop exchanges, starting with a single egg without a single copper coin, it has now changed into a $2,200 14-day Kenya two-person travel package, and hopefully, in the next exchange process, continue to "value add" geometrically. It is reported that the three students are waiting for people to provide exchange goods to reach the $1,000,000 fundraising goal.

The three university students say that once the goal is realized, they will donate the $1,000,000 obtained from an egg to three Australian charities. When the Canadian youth Kyle Mike Donald learned about the "$1,000,000" plan a few years ago, he said: "I get a lot of fun from this program; they not only made a deal but also contributed charitably, which is a win-win situation result. I sincerely hope that their 'One Egg Around the World' plan can achieve ultimate success."
