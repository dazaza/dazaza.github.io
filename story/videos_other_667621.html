The woman on the anatomy table suddenly opened her eyes! Before I switched careers to become a pharmaceutical sales manager, I was an anatomy lecturer at Taishan Medical College. It wasn’t because I didn’t do well in my field that I made the switch. In fact, my classes were quite remarkable, and if I hadn't quit, I think I might have been promoted to associate professor by now. What drove me away from the university podium was psychological factors—because I hated dead bodies and feared the deceased. It was an unfathomable fear, like a movable cold needle that drilled into the soles of your feet, traveling through your bloodstream, and you never knew when it would reach your heart—it could be in half a year, a month, or even a minute. Similarly, I didn’t know when it would come again, but I felt it wasn’t far from me. It was still somewhere, watching me, waiting to kill me.

This story begins with an anatomy class three years ago. For the students, this might have been the most unforgettable class of their lives because the first full-body dissection always leaves a strong impression. I had emphasized the need for mental preparation, but some students still vomited. In the following three days, very few people went to the cafeteria to buy meat, especially dishes like stir-fried pig liver.

This time, the body was that of a young woman, which was rare in medical colleges because the shortage of cadavers has become a common challenge for all medical schools. Most cadavers are elderly people who died of illness, with organs already failing. Even so, full-body dissection classes are often postponed repeatedly. According to local customs, even if the patient had voluntarily expressed a wish to donate their body to the medical cause during their lifetime, the deceased's children often wouldn't allow it, considering it a desecration of the dead. Therefore, every cadaver was a rare opportunity for practical training, and a young and fresh one was even more precious.

The female corpse lay quietly on the dissection table. Before the class began, the body was covered with a white cloth. I followed the routine and explained the precautions and the importance of dissection in medicine to the students. Finally, I asked them to treat the body with noble respect. The students' eyes were filled with curiosity and a bit of fear, but no one spoke out loud, as if they were waiting for an extremely solemn moment. When the white cloth was lifted, there were a few slight sighs from the students nearby. This was a very young female corpse, probably only twenty-five or twenty-six years old. It was said that she had been a secretary who committed suicide by cutting her wrists due to emotional problems. Her friends found a donation consent form among her belongings, filled out during her student days. Young people generally don't consider such things; why did she have this intention? Perhaps it would forever remain a mystery. She wasn't a particularly beautiful woman, with slightly sunken eye sockets, possibly due to great stress during her lifetime. She had her eyes closed, looking peaceful, as if sound asleep, without the rigid appearance of ordinary corpses. Maybe death was truly a release for her.

I thought about this as I covered her face with a square towel according to procedure. Without seeing her face, her pale body stood out starkly. "Now, let's begin!" I said, indicating for the students to focus their attention on the demonstration dissection table. Cheers erupted around me as I took the scalpel from the tray and placed it against her throat. The white plastic gloves matched the color of the female corpse's skin, creating a suffocating whiteness. Her body was still somewhat soft, with skin retaining elasticity, which felt different from other bodies I had encountered before. Somehow, my scalpel hesitated to cut down, and a terrifying thought crossed my mind: perhaps she wasn't dead yet.

But soon, I laughed at myself for this notion, thinking it might be because the girl's death was too tragic, leading to this illusion. The students were all staring wide-eyed at the scalpel. I focused and finally pushed the blade down forcefully. The sharp scalpel encountered almost no resistance and reached her lower abdomen, as if pulling open a zipper. We could clearly hear the slightly brisk hissing sound of the scalpel cutting through the flesh, due to the pressure inside the cavity. The skin split apart actively, revealing purplish-red muscles, stiffening and shifting to the sides along with the skin, becoming loose. After using clamps to spread the skin and muscles, the internal organs were fully exposed before us.

At this stage, I had forgotten who the person on the table was. Actually, it didn't matter anymore. What mattered was how to make the students remember the structure of the human body tightly, which would have a profound impact on their future medical practice. Internal organs were removed piece by piece, explained in detail to the students, then dissected further and their structures explained. After all the internal organs were removed, the female corpse was left with just a red cavity. The class went smoothly, although several students turned green in the face, nearly all were feeling nauseous, but they endured the test, making it worthwhile.

After the students left, I was alone in the dissection demonstration room. The white lights shone strongly on the dissection table, reflecting blinding brilliance. I began placing the removed organs back into their original positions and stitching the skin layer by layer with thread. The school bell rang five times heavily. I lifted the square towel covering the female corpse's face, and then something extraordinary happened! The female corpse suddenly opened her eyes and stared at me fiercely, scaring me almost to the point of falling over.

I carefully stood up and realized it wasn't a hallucination. Her eyes were wide open, staring at the ceiling. Her expression was no longer peaceful but full of anger. But she was indeed dead. Gathering my courage, I examined her thoroughly and eventually found a reasonable explanation: perhaps it was due to bioelectricity, where the dissection process triggered a bioelectrical nerve reflex. I closed her eyes and covered her with the white cloth again, then left the dissection room.

For the next few days, the female corpse's eyes kept flashing in my mind. I wasn't a believer in the supernatural, but for some reason, those eyes haunted me like ghosts. I kept wondering why she opened her eyes at that moment, and recalling her gaze later, it seemed to convey some kind of information, not entirely like the vacant stare of the dead.

Three days later, I learned that the female corpse had been cremated, and her ashes were taken home by her parents to their hometown in Northeast China. A year passed, and I seemed to have forgotten this incident. During this time, I met a girlfriend. We met on a rainy night. That evening, after attending a meeting at school, I was heading home on Wenshui Road. It was raining heavily, and there was no one on the road. Unable to call a taxi immediately, I walked alone with my umbrella. As I walked, I suddenly noticed someone following me at a steady pace. I became nervous, fearing it might be a robber. So I quickened my steps, and so did the person behind me, maintaining a distance of four or five meters.

After walking for a long stretch, I couldn't bear it any longer and turned around to see what was going on. To my surprise, it was a slender girl in a yellow raincoat. We stopped back-to-back.

"Why were you following me?" I asked her.

"Sorry, I felt scared walking alone," she timidly looked at me.

I sighed in relief and smiled, "How do you know I'm not a bad guy?"

She laughed and said, "Because you look like a teacher, and teachers rarely are bad guys."

"Haha! You guessed right, I am a teacher. Don't worry, I'll walk you home!"

I accompanied her while we walked together, and I sent her home safely.

After that night, we frequently met on our way home and gradually got to know each other. I never dared to tell her what course I taught, so she only knew I was a teacher at the medical college and had no idea about the nature of my work. One day, I finally told her I was a lecturer in human anatomy. She didn't react with surprise or fear as I expected but instead showed a strong sense of curiosity.

"You say, does the body feel pain when the scalpel cuts through it?" she asked seriously, awaiting my response.

"How could it? People stop feeling once they're dead."

"How do you know they don't feel anything?"

"Modern medicine defines death as brain death. Once the brain neurons die, any stimulation to the nerve endings loses its effect, and naturally, there is no sensation."

"That's what we living people think, but reality might be different," she insisted stubbornly.

"Don't overthink it," I laughed.

Later, she asked this question many times. Each time I answered, something in my mind seemed to be hooked by an iron hook but quickly sank back down. However, she kept asking the same question, and I gradually felt an inexplicable sense of fear growing heavier, pressing down on me. I even started avoiding seeing her. On reflection, there was nothing particularly strange, and I guessed it might be due to the psychological pressure from constantly dealing with corpse dissections.

Until one accidental discovery, I realized the seriousness of the issue. One evening, I went to her dormitory to find her, but she wasn't there. The door was slightly ajar, so I sat on the sofa waiting for her. Growing impatient, I stood up and flipped through her desk, intending to find a magazine to pass the time. There weren't any interesting magazines, so I casually picked up an old newspaper. Accidentally, a piece of paper fell out from the layers and landed on the ground. It was an old, slightly yellowed paper, and my nerves tightened instantly. I seemed to have seen this paper somewhere before.

I picked up the paper and turned it over, shocked and wide-eyed. It turned out to be the consent form of the female corpse I dissected a year ago. Before the body was transferred to the dissection room, I had signed it. Yes, my signature was still there, but how did it end up here? I was alarmed and hastily opened an old edition of Taishan Weekly. In the Social Perspective column, there was a social news item titled "White-Collar Beauty Commits Suicide Over Love." The date of the newspaper was the day I dissected the corpse. I felt as if I had fallen into an ice cellar, trembling with cold, sensing an indescribable eerie atmosphere in the room.

At that moment, I heard clear footsteps in the hallway, high heels clicking closer step by step. I didn't know what to do, so I braced myself for her arrival. The footsteps stopped at the door, and though I didn't see anyone, I felt as if she was standing there, staring at me. My legs felt weak, but I dared not move. Soon, the high heels clicked again, getting farther and farther until they disappeared.

In a frenzy, I ran home. After several hours of pondering, my mind raced rapidly. How could this happen? Perhaps she was just a classmate or colleague of that girl, or even a close friend. If so, keeping these items wasn't unusual. Moreover, those footsteps might have come from downstairs, and all this was just my overly sensitive nerves playing tricks on me.

My mind eased a bit, and I called her mobile phone, hoping to uncover the truth. No one answered, and despite my repeated attempts, it only produced long tones. The more she didn't answer, the more frightened I became. Soon, I heard footsteps outside the door, distinct from the ones I heard earlier. The crisp sound of high heels on the cement floor echoed loudly. My heart pounded, and I dared not breathe.

"Knock! Knock! Knock!" Someone was knocking on the door. It was her; she had come to find me!

After much hesitation, I finally convinced myself to open the door.

"It's you!" I said, my throat dry.

"It's me," she replied.

"I came to find you tonight, but you weren't there."

"I went out to run some errands. When I returned, I saw you'd been here."

"Is that so?"

"Why have you been calling my phone nonstop?"

"I... I was worried something might have happened to you."

She smiled and said, "Would it be okay if I stayed with you tonight?"

I wanted her to leave, but I couldn't bring myself to say it. We'd known each other for so long, and she had never allowed me to touch her body. I thought maybe I was being paranoid. Her face bore no resemblance to that girl, so how could there be any connection?

"I'll take a shower first!" she said and headed towards the bathroom.

"Alright!" I stepped aside.

Sitting in the living room, I heard the water running inside, my heart uneasy. But I kept telling myself not to dwell on those strange thoughts; it might just be a coincidence.

She emerged wearing pajamas and sat across from me on the couch. We sat in silence.

"Let me give you a massage," she smiled, moving behind me and kneading my shoulders.

"You say, does the body feel pain when the scalpel cuts through it?" she suddenly asked.

I jumped up from the couch, shouting, "Who exactly are you?"

But a sudden pain in my neck, as if struck by a heavy object, caused me to lose consciousness.

When I woke up, my head was splitting with pain, and I found my limbs tied to the bed. I saw her standing by the bed, glaring angrily at me. That glare! I remembered, it was completely different from the female corpse!

"You... you are..." I was overwhelmed with terror, struggling futilely. I noticed her face was changing, slowly transforming—her eyes, nose, mouth—all shifting position. In a moment, a horrifying face appeared before me. It was her—the female corpse from a year ago!

"You say, does the body feel pain when the scalpel cuts through it?" she asked me again harshly.

"Maybe... maybe it does!" I trembled as I replied.

She slowly undid her pajamas. I had never felt such disgust before. From her neck down, her body was just an empty shell, devoid of internal organs, exposing a red cavity.

"You say, do I feel pain?" she angrily said.

"But you volunteered!" I shouted.

"I regret committing suicide for that man. Just as I was preparing to leave this filthy world, you awakened me! I want you to stay with me forever!" she said.

"You, what do you intend to do?" I asked in horror.

She chuckled stiffly and pulled out a shiny scalpel from her pajama pocket, waving it in front of me before pressing it against my neck.

"I want you to know the pain of being dissected!" she said darkly.

"Don't! Don't! You're dead, and I'm alive!" I cried out.

A sharp pain surged in my throat, as if I was being flayed alive, and I screamed in agony as I sat up. I realized my whole body was drenched in sweat, the moonlight shining through the window onto me. She wasn't in the room. Was I dreaming all night?

I found it hard to believe, but I was relieved, experiencing a sense of joy akin to surviving a close call.

The next day, when I got up, I discovered something that would haunt me forever. Beneath the bed lay a scalpel, its sharp edge gleaming coldly.

That afternoon, I went to her room again, but the door was tightly shut. An old neighbor lady informed me that since that woman mutilated herself, no one had rented the room.

From then on, I dared not touch any corpse, nor could I endure staying in the medical college. I changed careers and became a pharmaceutical distributor. I left Taishan and moved to Jinan, the provincial capital. But was the incident that night real or not
