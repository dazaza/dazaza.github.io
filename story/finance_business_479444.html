2. Bait Feeding

The bait for juvenile centipedes is relatively abundant. Any animals that centipedes consume in the wild can be used as bait during this period. Common varieties include earthworms, maggots, locusts, grasshoppers, frogs, and mealworms. It's important to control the feeding amount, avoiding overfeeding which wastes food and causes pollution, while also preventing constant shortages to avoid cannibalism due to hunger among centipedes.

Some sources suggest adding small pieces of meat or bones to the soil to attract prey for centipedes to eat. We believe this method is not advisable because these scraps not only attract prey but also predators and pests, leading to more harm than good. Using this method indoors will yield no results.

3. Grouping by Size

Frequent grouping should be combined with adjustments in density. Within the same pool, try to keep centipedes of the same age and similar size. Also, note that centipedes from the same nest should ideally be kept together rather than randomly distributed across different pools. From our observations, interference and cannibalism between centipedes often occur between those from different nests, while it happens less within the same nest. During each grouping, only select slower-molting, smaller, weaker centipedes for separate breeding, keeping healthy and fast-growing centipedes in the original pool.

4. Careful Observation

Observation of juvenile centipedes during their growth phase can be done using short-term lighting methods. The best time for observation is around 10 PM when most centipedes emerge from the soil to forage, drink water, and move around on the surface or activity field. At this time, you can observe their activity levels, physical development, health status, and whether they are entering the molting phase. Each observation session should not be too long, and the light should not be too strong. You don't need to observe daily, but if you notice any abnormalities in the centipede group, sudden changes in temperature, or after implementing new measures, timely observation is necessary to address any issues promptly.

If centipedes appear restless and remain out of the soil even after sunrise, consider whether there are any abnormalities with the soil that might require replacement. If centipedes are startled or eating other centipedes during molting, consider whether the density is too high or if there are environmental disturbances. When sick centipedes are found, handle them immediately, checking factors like rapid decline in numbers, correct feeding times, and appropriate feeding amounts.

5. Adjust Temperature and Humidity

Juvenile centipedes aged two years or older have similar temperature and humidity requirements as adult centipedes. Given the long duration of this phase and frequent external climate changes, patience and dedication from the breeder are essential.
