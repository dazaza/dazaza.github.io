The auspicious and inauspicious relationship between graveyard Feng Shui and residential Feng Shui

by bodamn2 on 2009-07-25 09:08:34

The auspicious or inauspicious geomancy of a Yin residence (grave) can be seen from the burial site. The mountains and waters at a thousand chi are called "shi" (trend), while those at a hundred chi are called "xing" (form). When the trend and form are favorable, it is auspicious; when they are adverse, it is inauspicious. If the trend is inauspicious but the form is auspicious, out of a hundred blessings originally present, only one may remain. If the distant trend is auspicious but the nearby form is inauspicious, disasters may strike within days after the burial. A thousand chi of trend, winding and pausing, dissipates without coalescing, unable to gather inner qi externally, causing the qi in the earth to vanish. The classics say: A grave site where the vital energy scatters without accumulation is an ominous site that causes the coffin to decay and brings no blessings to the living. The exhalation of qi disperses the vital energy, hence the need for the Azure Dragon and White Tiger to guard the vital energy of the grave site. If there is a high mound in the middle without an Azure Dragon on the left, a White Tiger on the right, and open space front and back, then the vital energy of this mound will be completely blown away by the wind. The classics say: A deficient site is a place that causes the coffin to rot. External qi (water) is used to stop internal qi, while passing water is used to block the dragon's approach. When the distant trend comes and the nearby form stops, with mountains and waters welcoming in front and support behind, this is a propitious feng shui site.

For the division and combination of auspicious or inauspicious grave site geomancy, leaning against a round mountain behind is called "fen" (division), and having a pointed mountain welcoming in front is called "he" (combination). There must be division behind and combination in front. The classics say: The burial site divides into the mountain itself and the four trends in front, left, and right. Qi surges from eight directions, so the burial site takes the left as the Azure Dragon, the right as the White Tiger, the front as the Vermilion Bird, and the back as the Black Tortoise. It requires the Black Tortoise to bow its head, the Vermilion Bird to dance, the Azure Dragon to undulate, and the White Tiger to crouch submissively. If it is the opposite, the method dictates destruction and death. Therefore, a crouching White Tiger is called "holding the corpse," a sitting Azure Dragon is called "jealous of the master," a non-bowing Black Tortoise is called "rejecting the corpse," and a non-dancing Vermilion Bird is called "flying away." In short, it is not propitious for burial. Use a soil guiyi to determine the orientation of the burial site and a jade ruler to measure distances. Take the land as the Azure Dragon and White Tiger, requiring their rise and fall on high ground to embrace the burial site like arms. When water is taken as the Vermilion Bird, its decline and prosperity are related to the shape of the water. Avoid turbulent water that makes a rustling sound, which seems like sorrowful weeping, making the burial site ominous. The Vermilion Bird water originates from the vital energy, growing from small to large, from few to many. Then it decreases from large to small, from many to few. It gathers at the acupoint and then flows away. When it flows away, it bends and twists, as if lingering around the burial mountain. The classics say: Mountains coming and water returning bring wealth, longevity, and abundance. Mountains imprisoned and water flowing cause the downfall of kings and nobles. The soil of the burial site should be fine and hard, without seepage. A moist burial site with stones is an ominous site.
