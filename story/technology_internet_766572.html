What if Nokia went with Android

by anonymous on 2013-09-18 21:59:50

On September 16th, Beijing time, it was reported that in 2011 Nokia abandoned Symbian and turned to the WP platform. Since then, there has been ongoing discussion about "whether Nokia should develop Android phones."

Adopting Android is an understandable option: Android is the largest smartphone OS platform, accounting for two-thirds of total sales; in emerging markets, Android is gradually eroding Nokia's smartphone business.

Theoretically, if Nokia cannot beat Android, it might as well join it. This is an attractive suggestion: combining the invincible Android operating system with Nokia's design capabilities, brand reputation, and supply chain would be a good choice.

Benefits of adopting Android

Nokia faces an app problem, for example, the experience of opening the WP app store on Nokia phones is quite poor—it’s like a ghost town compared to Google Play. Nokia and Microsoft tell you about their app data, but what matters is having high-quality apps. If Nokia were to join the Android camp, this issue could be resolved.

Adopting the Android platform could also save cash. Android is a free system, and although Microsoft provides Nokia with $250 million per quarter in support, Nokia still has to pay patent fees.

Assuming Nokia combines its design strength with Android, who wouldn’t want its phones? However, Nokia chose the WP platform, and many times Nokia's challenges are not related to hardware. If focused on Android, Nokia would not have to be tied to the “ghost town”-like WP app store, and could instead launch its own apps on the Android platform, such as maps.

Of course, this assumption is overly simplistic. In the Android space, apart from Samsung, other phone manufacturers haven't been very successful. Switching to Android is not a magic bullet for Nokia, especially now that Samsung has already taken a dominant position.

The WP market faces similar issues; aside from Nokia, no other company sells many WP phones. Despite this, LG, Lenovo, and ZTE sell more Android phones than Nokia does WP phones.

It is no longer possible to switch to Android now

Perhaps in 2011, Nokia should have switched to Android. We can't assume how far they could have come in the past two and a half years. But by 2013, the market had changed. Today, regardless of anything, it's best for Nokia to stay with Microsoft.

Without Nokia, Microsoft's mobile business would return to square one, without a strong partner producing devices to support its OS. By acquiring Nokia, Microsoft isn't looking for a partner; it needs a manufacturing business—acquiring BlackBerry would work too.

Simply put, Microsoft has other options.

So, is it possible for Nokia to abandon the WP platform next year and switch to Android? Impossible.

All of Nokia's key efforts in apps, developer coverage, integration, and partnerships are all tied to Microsoft. Developing Android phones wouldn't be difficult for Nokia, but unraveling the Nokia-Microsoft relationship would be hard. Making a device run on one OS isn't difficult, but making an entire enterprise do the same thing is not easy.

Some might say that Nokia could choose a multi-OS strategy; other WP phone manufacturers follow a multi-OS path, producing both WP and Android phones.

This is not a bad idea, but cooperation with Microsoft would cool down.

In 2011, Nokia made a big turn from Symbian to WP. If another big turn were to happen in 2014, shareholders would likely not agree.

In 2011, Symbian's sales were still significant, supporting Nokia while increasing WP shipments without collapsing. Now, relying on WP phones to keep the company afloat while increasing Android phone shipments would be too difficult.

Once, Nokia could have switched to Android and possibly succeeded, but that opportunity has passed.
