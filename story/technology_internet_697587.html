Voluntary Principle of Real Estate Mortgage Loan

by pp70gaugna on 2012-03-08 16:25:32

The principle of voluntariness requires that property mortgage loans [http://www.zhetong.cc/] and guarantee contracts must represent the true intentions of the parties involved. An expression of intent refers to an act where the desire for a civil legal effect is manifested externally. This expression must have an inner intention to produce a civil legal effect, and there must be an action to manifest this inner intention externally. Moreover, the inner intention and external manifestation must be consistent; this is what constitutes a true expression of intent. For example, if a loan guarantor willingly provides a guarantee for the borrower internally and signs a loan guarantee contract with the lender, the former represents the "intent," while the latter is the "expression." A true expression of intent is autonomous, whereas an untrue expression of intent is coerced.

The principle of voluntariness opposes the use of improper means such as coercion, threats, or deception to force others to provide guarantees. If borrowers, lenders, or others use methods like coercion, threats, deception, or extortion to force third parties to provide guarantees, this not only directly infringes upon the mental will of the third party but also potentially violates their property ownership rights. As guarantors, third parties pledge their own property as loan guarantees. The property ownership rights of citizens, legal entities, and other organizations exclude illegal interference from others. Forcing someone to provide a guarantee is an act of illegally interfering with another person's property ownership rights, which is one point. Secondly, when a guarantor provides a guarantee under duress, they may have to bear the responsibility of repaying on behalf of the borrower, thereby potentially infringing upon their legitimate property rights. Thirdly, forcing someone to provide a guarantee results in a behavior where the expression of intent is not genuine. Therefore, any loan guarantee behavior caused by coercive actions is invalid, and the coerced guarantor can negate the guarantee through legal means. Any losses incurred by the lender or guarantor as a result shall be borne by the person who engaged in the coercive behavior. However, if a lender requires the borrower to provide a guarantee and refuses to grant a loan without it, and the borrower does not wish to provide a guarantee but has to do so in order to obtain the loan, the lender's coercive behavior is aimed at securing their own creditor rights and should not be deemed illegal.

In loan guarantee activities, violations of the principle of voluntariness are not uncommon, such as financial department credit officers forcing regular loan applicants to provide guarantees for relatives or friends; borrowers deceiving others' seals or property to use as guarantees for their loans; borrowers using authority or other relationships to coerce or threaten others into providing guarantees for their loans, etc. These acts that violate the principle of voluntariness often cause guarantors to unjustly bear guarantee responsibilities. Simultaneously, this harms the lender because such behaviors often render loan guarantee contracts invalid. If the borrower fails to fulfill the loan contract upon maturity, the lender's claim will be difficult to secure.
