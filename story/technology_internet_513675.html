Son's congenital leg length discrepancy prompts father to sell house for medical treatment; cure rate is less than 30%

by dkbcenter on 2011-10-22 10:26:51

Zhang Juncao, who will be 2 years old in 4 months, has his left leg wrapped in a thick plaster cast. The photo was taken by Nandu reporter Huang Jihao. According to reports by Nandu reporter Xue Bingni and intern Lin Honghai, if it weren't for the plight of his young son, Fujian farmer Zhang Hebao would have never left his small town, let alone consider which Changchun hospital had the best abortion techniques.

In Guangzhou, his nearly 2-year-old son was diagnosed with congenital leg length discrepancy, a disease with an incidence rate of only one in ten thousand. It is reported that under the current domestic medical level, the cure rate is less than 30%, and in the worst case scenario, amputation might be necessary. This impoverished father also suffers from congenital neurofibromatosis, and his wife is deaf. Yesterday, he frankly stated that if his young son could be cured, he would sell their house to seek medical treatment.

With less than 30% cure rate under domestic medical standards

On the orthopedic bed at the Baiyun branch of the Second Guangdong Traditional Chinese Medicine Hospital, Zhang Juncao's right foot, just 4 months away from turning 2 years old, was wrapped in a thick plaster while he played with a baby bottle and a mobile phone. Father Zhang Hebao introduced that the family are farmers from Shibi Village, Shibi Town, Ninghua County, Sanming City, Fujian Province. Four months after the birth of his son, when giving him a bath, he accidentally discovered that his son's legs were of different lengths. Afterwards, after seeking treatment at a local hospital in Fujian, the doctor removed part of the child's bent tibia, but the bone did not continue to grow, making the condition more complex. He was later referred to this hospital in Guangzhou upon a friend's recommendation.

Director Zhang Xiao of the Orthopedics Department at the Baiyun branch of the Second Guangdong Traditional Chinese Medicine Hospital introduced that Zhang Juncao suffers from congenital fibrous dysplasia of the tibia and fibula combined with pseudoarthrosis. Due to the congenital bending of the tibia in the lower leg, a false joint was produced, directly resulting in leg length discrepancy. The incidence rate of this disease is only one in ten thousand, and previously, he had only encountered two or three similar cases. According to the current domestic medical level, the cure rate is still less than 30%.

He said that Zhang Juncao has already completed the first phase of surgery, and further surgeries will depend on the progression of his condition. In the worst-case scenario, amputation would be necessary, meaning he would have to use a prosthetic limb in the future. "The prognosis of the surgery is still not optimistic because the child is in the growth stage, and the entire surgical cycle is relatively long. It may take two or three years to determine if he is fully cured. The total cost of the surgery is estimated to be around 100,000 yuan."

"Planning to sell the house to treat the child"

A 100,000 yuan expense is almost a devastating blow to this farming family. However, this farmer with only a junior high school education still holds strong hope for his son's condition. "If it can be cured, I plan to sell the house and do everything possible to treat my child."

Zhang Hebao told Nandu reporters that he also suffers from congenital neurofibromatosis. His whole body is covered with tumors of varying sizes. He is a farmer, and his wife is also a deaf patient. Since the nineteenth day of the first lunar month this year, he has been taking his child out for treatment, spending half a year already, and now owes over 40,000 yuan. The farmland at home has also been abandoned." He said that apart from the money owed for his son's treatment, he already owed 180,000 yuan due to building their house, and the income he earns from farming each year is only about 4,000 yuan.

Facing an uncertain future for his son, Zhang Hebao sighed, "As long as there is a bit of hope for the child, we will do everything possible to treat him. If the surgery can cure his 'leg length discrepancy,' I will sell the house, and the whole family will rent a place to live."
