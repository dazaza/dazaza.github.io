Is that a fact?

by pcbtiv45 on 2011-06-09 16:21:00

Under the professional background, the competition between enterprises is no longer the competition of products, technology, market, and talents, but the competition of business models. However, this business model based on the integration of industry and finance collapsed at the turning point of drastic environmental changes.

If we break down the word "enterprise" in Chinese into its components, it means "people stop here." Therefore, when discussing systems, we need to separate entrepreneurs from the enterprises themselves and not attribute the current crisis to moral issues of individuals. The crisis we are facing now is a systemic phenomenon. Only with this understanding can we make an objective and positive judgment about our current situation.

"Chicken meat" is still "chicken meat"

What impact does the financial crisis have? A direct example is that last year, ABN AMRO was sold for 24 billion euros, with three bidders vying for it, and eventually they split up this century-old bank. Less than a year later today, all three are simultaneously in trouble, and the bank has been put back on the market. Now, at 8 billion euros, no one is interested. When you see a company suddenly collapse, you realize there was nothing substantial. Looking at the subprime crisis, many people say they understand it - it's the market selling "chicken droppings" as "chicken meat." Is that really the case? Of course not! It's because someone was willing to pay the price of "chicken essence."

So what have we gained from the modern enterprise system? There are two types of enterprises: family businesses and public companies. Family businesses represent the genes of a capitalist - your own business, your own money, your own affairs, trying to pass it down through generations. Public companies, on the other hand, have become a kind of public commodity. When enterprises are viewed as commodities, a trading-oriented enterprise system emerges.

The essential difference between the modern enterprise system and purely family-run businesses is that it no longer belongs to any individual. Thus, professional managers begin to become the true core of enterprises. As a result, we start to see the professionalization of management and the commodification of enterprises happening simultaneously. When we refer to Peter Drucker as the father of modern enterprise management, many people may not realize that those who honor him so highly are Welch and Zhang Ruimin, and they are all professional managers. Thus, under the professional backdrop, the competition between enterprises is no longer about products, technology, market, or talent, but rather about business models.

When we place business models at the center of operations, many talk about the Haier model, the Delong model, the Lenovo model, but these are actually enterprise models, not business models. A business model is something all enterprises must follow under such conditions.

In fact, if we carefully analyze various enterprises' professionalized management, they all converge on one point: converting any production, supply, and sales chain into an investment and financing model under certain conditions. This is the business model of modern enterprises. Such a business model causes changes in corporate behavior and accounting systems.

When we incorporate investment and financing activities into regular business operations, a problem arises. Non-listed companies in an industry operate in the dark; listed companies operate in the light. If one person is in the dark and the other is in the light, who will win? Many would conclude that the one in the dark will win. However, when the mainstream enterprises in an industry become listed companies, private enterprises in that industry almost inevitably face catastrophic consequences. The more transparent a company is, once problems arise, it becomes extremely opaque. In 2007, Wall Street witnessed its largest bonus feast ever, with security guards earning three times Buffett's $100,000 annual salary. Behind this bonus feast, who could have foreseen that Wall Street would collapse so dramatically today? A trading-oriented economy begins to integrate traditional production, supply, and sales activities with modern capital market investment and trading. Corporate operations start to change. When capital markets are defined as markets for trading expectations, any enterprise of this type can be directly defined - it is an enterprise that operates on expectations.

Thus, a standardized business model for enterprises emerges with three pillars: First, you must have cash flow from real economic activities; second, you must have investment income; third, you must have the ability to finance while operating the enterprise.

This also means that the size of operational expectations determines the original real value of the enterprise.

Looking back at what happened in the past decade, due to Greenspan's liquidity-oriented policy, global liquidity overflowed, triggering a global asset premium. As a result, as long as you possessed capital, you enjoyed the profit from asset premium. Once you enjoyed the profit from asset premium, you could amplify the company's financing capacity. With amplified financing capacity, you could operate more real businesses. Even if they didn't make money, you could still use the cash flow from real operations to continue amplifying the company's financing capacity. We then observed a cycle - a cycle of investment, financing, and operation. In this game, "chicken meat" remained "chicken meat," except some treated it as "chicken essence," while others regarded it as "chicken droppings."

Business Model Challenges

The financial industry, which used to be part of organized crime, is now a transparent industry. Because no industry needs to explain itself to the public as frequently as the financial industry does.

However, in this transparent industry, everyone suddenly realizes they've been deceived. Why? You might think it's the sins of the industry, but that's not true. The financial industry initially provided liquidity for the real economy. But later, it became a risk management business because risks from all industries eventually converged in banks. In front of the economic cycle, banks are like "nannies": giving milk during prosperous times and becoming mothers during recessions. So banks need to collect risks. When risks become assets that all financial institutions must face, the modern financial industry turns them into properties through securitization.

Traditional industrial cycles can be seen as periodic overproduction. However, financial crises are accumulations of mistakes. Everyone knows the risks clearly but pretends to be ignorant. Each person only cares about the limited risks they face within their segment of time. Thus, in this market, a game of hot potato appears. Passing it off works perfectly, yielding both profits and risk transfer.

The inevitable outcome of such a game is that financial crises recur every ten years. For example, the 1997 Asian Financial Crisis, the 1987 Black Monday, the 1977 European Currency Crisis leading to the Latin American Debt Crisis, and the 1967 Pound Crisis. Every ten years, eight years for profit, one year for loss, and one year for cleanup.

During this process, many enterprises fail to recognize that this is a challenge to the business model, a change in the foundation upon which the business model relies.

In fact, many enterprises' difficulties do not stem from real industries but from their investment and financing. If the real economy is merely in late autumn, the financial market has already entered winter. Hence, we see sudden, malignant bankruptcies of large enterprises rather than small and medium-sized ones. For instance, the bankruptcies of Hejun Group and Jinwu Group were not due to industry issues but because of reversals in the capital market, causing the sudden collapse of the investment and financing chain.

Under the backdrop of low operational profit, high investment returns, and strong financing drive, when the global asset premium suddenly transforms into asset depreciation due to the financial tsunami, this business model faces challenges. These challenges include investment returns turning into investment losses, financing functions transforming into financing shrinkage, and previously subtle operational advantages becoming disadvantages.

Survival of the Fittest

What do Chinese entrepreneurs need to know? How can we find our way in this game dominated by individuals? We want to survive and grow. What should Chinese enterprises do now?

If we view an enterprise as a tripod business model - balancing investment capability, financing capability, and real operational capability - any enterprise operates under dynamic equilibrium.

At this moment, let's bring accounting into focus: net profit accounting, cash flow accounting, investment-financing accounting. Behind these lies something hidden yet transparent: the capital chain accounting.

To evaluate an enterprise, we look at four aspects: profitable enterprises are smart; positive cash flow enterprises are handsome. However, investment and financing are the skeleton, and the capital chain is the blood. An enterprise can be neither smart nor handsome but cannot lack blood and flesh. So enterprises have two sides, yin and yang. Beautiful appearances don't necessarily mean good survival.

Enterprises seek survival. They're not afraid of making no money, but they fear broken chains. High attention must be paid to the capital chain, which hides behind cash flow and investment-financing. When the cold wave has arrived, enterprises must control their investment and financing chains. In this context, the secret to surviving the winter is eight characters: "reproduce and rest, prolong life." Recognize the situation; survival is king.

All sages are survivors. Confucius is called a sage because the average lifespan at that time was 34 years, and he lived to 73. He survived and became a sage. What are sages in the business world? They are the surviving enterprises. One general's success comes at the cost of countless bones. Any industry's victor rises from the corpses of peers. As long as you survive, you live. Amid gunfire, lie down early. When you open your eyes again, the field is full of corpses. This is the business cycle. At this moment, people must prepare for the winter, adjust their business operations.

Bull market issues stocks, bear market issues bonds

Sitting idly waiting for death is definitely not a way to "survive."

At this stage, operations must continue. For listed companies, issuing stocks in a bull market and bonds in a bear market remains an unchanging principle. Enhance the company's debt financing capabilities, reduce investment risks, and stabilize operational expectations. When walking on three legs, at least two must be steady. Missing one leg, you can still walk. If two legs stop moving in a three-legged walk, the remaining leg cannot act.

Under such circumstances, what sustains evergreen enterprises? The key to successful enterprises is operating countercyclical cash flows within controllable ranges. Treat the cash flow of the real economy and the capital chain of the virtual economy as one chain to manage simultaneously. Only by combining the real and the virtual can you avoid cyclical economic crises. Supplementing the real with the virtual and vice versa, integrating both, this is a successful business model for modern enterprises in today's market environment, a standardized model. Under such a model, any successful entrepreneur understands how to adapt to market operations. Doing well is less important than selling well; performing well is less critical than financing well; adding is less effective than subtracting.

However, everyone knows that addition is easy, subtraction is hard; investing is easy, financing is difficult; doing the real is easy, doing the virtual is challenging. The way of civil and military affairs involves balance. The art of commerce combines reality and illusion. Managing the internal structure of enterprises and their external environments simultaneously, each person must consider what the true cash flow of our enterprise is? What is the relationship between the capital chain and cash flow? Enterprises manage external operational expectations and internal risks. What is risk? It's the dynamic equilibrium between cash flow and the capital chain. In such a transformative era, with abrupt changes in macro-environments, every enterprise must take care of itself, seize the right moments, and promptly adjust its business model. The business model of an enterprise must align closely with its team. That is, when everyone focuses on the virtual, make the real more virtual; when everyone focuses on the real, make the virtual more real. This concept is easy to say but very hard to achieve. It heavily depends on the intuition of frontline decision-makers. Therefore, the entrepreneur's intuition in challenging risks and adjusting business models determines the survival of the enterprise.

Thus, under such a backdrop, urging everyone to reproduce and rest, prolonging life, is because I firmly believe: China will quickly rise using this opportunity. China did not rise in growth but unexpectedly rose in decline. Because our decline is relative to the global recession, our relative position has sharply improved. Therefore, in the future international market, our survival space will relatively increase!
