"The Inevitable 'Death of Yougou': Belle's E-commerce Has Many Hard Injuries" (Note: "硬伤" is translated as "hard injuries" to convey significant flaws or weaknesses in this context.) If you want a more natural English translation: "The Inevitable 'Fall of Yougou': Belle's E-commerce Business Faces Numerous Fundamental Issues."

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 14:59:24

Recently, Yougou.com, which has been suffering from a severe loss of senior executives, was hit again. On July 17, it was reported that according to insiders at Yougou.com, the company’s COO Zhang Xiaojun will be leaving and is currently processing his resignation. As one of the founding members of Yougou.com, Zhang Xiaojun's departure is particularly significant, indicating that Yougou.com is indeed facing a serious crisis.

Let’s first review the e-commerce journey of Belle International, the parent company of Yougou.com:

A few years ago, Belle cautiously tested the waters in e-commerce, developing steadily through three channels: Taobao distribution, Tmall, and B2C distribution (B2C distribution was completed via Taixiu.com). Led by group veteran Xie Yunli, a relatively modest e-commerce team initially focused on selling products from Belle's own brands before gradually introducing exclusive online models and then new network brands. This development path was considered a successful case study for traditional brand companies entering e-commerce more solidly than Li-Ning before 2011.

With the rapid rise of Letao.com and Haolemai after receiving over $100 million in financing in 2010, Belle Group became restless. On one hand, they were concerned about being constrained by distribution channels; on the other, they realized their immense potential in the e-commerce field, with advantages including:

First, a multitude of brands: In addition to Belle's several women's shoe brands, its agency of international sports brands like Adidas and Nike experienced astonishing growth in online sales;

Second, easier expansion of categories: For Belle Group, known for its women's shoes and agent brands, achieving absolute market advantage through multi-brand and multi-category strategies was a key consideration. Opening physical stores to expand brands and categories was too slow, while ongoing e-commerce operations could easily expand brands but struggled to quickly diversify categories;

Third, financial strength: During cooperation with e-commerce enterprises, Belle discovered that e-commerce is capital-intensive. The group's financial strength allowed them to develop without relying on external capital, potentially outlasting many vertical e-commerce platforms dependent on funding;

Fourth, talent advantage: Through years of e-commerce practice, they had built a highly professional and efficient e-commerce team capable of handling large-scale business or more;

Fifth, supply chain advantage: This excited Belle internally and attracted many high-level executives from brand e-commerce or platform e-commerce. After all, for e-commerce, supply chain management is always fascinating, as it seems to determine survival apart from capital. Supply chain includes product development, production, warehouse management, delivery, and after-sales services—all “business” aspects that are seen as superior competitive advantages for traditional enterprises compared to e-commerce firms.

On these strengths, Belle e-commerce decided to make a big push. First, they acquired the domain name "yougou.com,"

abandoning the somewhat copycat "topshoes.cn" Taixiu website for redirection purposes, stopping supplies to all distribution sites while retaining direct third-party stores on Taobao and Tmall but reducing investment and attention. They hired top VPs from JD.com and Vancl at high salaries to serve as CMO and COO respectively, appointing a group veteran as CEO to form a golden triangle. Their goal was to create a full-category vertical B2C mall, implementing multi-brand strategies and expanding into clothing and other categories, aiming to achieve an impossible-in-the-offline retail empire dream!

Everything sounded perfect, but every dream needs to face the harsh reality. The actual situation was:

First, higher technical requirements for independent B2C: Located in Shenzhen headquarters, Belle couldn’t recruit more excellent technical talents, and outsourcing tech services were also scarce in Shenzhen. However, technology determines user experience, backend management efficiency, and reliable data. Independent B2C involves dynamic technology, different from relatively static technologies like ERP, and this continuous change remains a challenge for many traditional enterprises;

Second, increased management difficulty with category expansion: Someone once said that doing well in women's shoes doesn't necessarily mean success in men's shoes, something Belle likely felt during their male shoe category expansion. Expanding to women's clothing and accessories within a year would undoubtedly cause difficulties on the backend. Traditional brand enterprises accustomed to meticulous cost control might find this fast approach effective only under special circumstances, understandable within organizations like Vancl and JD.com. Although Belle employed Vancl’s COO and JD.com’s CMO, whether they can understand and execute with “exaggerated costs” remains questionable!

Third, organizational structure: Many people struggle to grasp this issue unless they have crossed between e-commerce and traditional enterprises. Simply put, department setup (organizational structure), approval processes, finance and budgeting, reporting methods, corporate culture, communication styles, execution approaches, risk control, and procedural systems constitute a company's so-called “mechanism.” The conflict arises when e-commerce tries to persuade traditional teams to accept its system, but the traditional side finds it hard to fully understand the e-commerce system, leading to inefficiency and instability in the execution team. Eventually, the chairman or board may say, “E-commerce and traditional enterprises each have advantages and disadvantages, and the future must involve integration, so you must integrate in these areas…” If this kind of “integration without harmony” occurs, it becomes a supposed “management innovation,” resulting in bizarre practices like a “CEO rotation system” even appearing in Belle!

It was originally thought that Yougou.com, Belle’s e-commerce arm, was far ahead, with its top executives looking forward to enjoying the glory of spin-off IPOs and countless successes in the e-commerce era. Instead, they found themselves entangled and troubled by these critical issues.

After all, whether it’s the veterans who grew up with Belle or the recruited e-commerce VP professionals, it’s clear they didn’t leave due to lack of ability. When the VP from JD.com returns, they still perform exceptionally well, and when the VP from Vancl ventures into entrepreneurship, they do so courageously. Are these three points the real weaknesses? Or are these not just Belle’s weaknesses but also common issues faced by many traditional enterprises attempting to enter e-commerce?
