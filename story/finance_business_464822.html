The harm caused by the extortion should not be considered as a work-related injury.

by zegpyy61 on 2011-07-22 17:13:28

In November 2006, the party Wu Huan used someone else's ID card to join an electrical company’s recruitment interview and started working for the company the next day. At that time, both sides had not signed a written labor contract. A few days later, while working at the electrical company, Wu Huan injured his left eye, which was diagnosed as a penetrating injury of the left cornea and traumatic cataract in the left eye after treatment. In November 2007, Wu Huan filed a work-related injury recognition application with the labor department. After reviewing the materials, the labor department made a decision in March 2008 recognizing Wu Huan's injury as a work-related injury and delivered the "Decision on Work-Related Injury Recognition" to both parties. Dissatisfied, the electrical company applied for administrative reconsideration with the government. Later, the government upheld the labor department's decision regarding work-related injury recognition. Still dissatisfied, the electrical company filed an administrative lawsuit with the court.

During the court hearing, the defendant electrical company argued that the third party, Wu Huan, impersonated someone else using their ID card during the job application process. Therefore, there was no real and valid labor relationship between them. The fraud committed by Wu Huan should not be considered a work-related injury. Thus, they requested the court to revoke the "Decision on Work-Related Injury Recognition" made by the labor department. The labor department countered that according to relevant legal provisions, after investigation, it should be recognized that Wu Huan, an employee of the electrical company, suffered a work-related injury to his left eye while working. Although Wu Huan used someone else's ID card when entering the company, this does not negate the existence of a labor relationship between the company and Wu Huan. Wu Huan's injury was due to work reasons, not so-called "fraud." The principle of recognizing work-related injuries is based on no-fault liability, so the company's reasons do not affect the recognition of the work-related injury. In summary, they requested the court to uphold the decision on work-related injury recognition.

After deliberation, the court believed that according to the relevant provisions of the "Work Injury Insurance Regulations," employees who suffer accidental injuries due to work reasons during working hours and within the workplace can be recognized as having work-related injuries. In this case, Wu Huan suffered an injury to his left eye while working for the defendant. The circumstances of the injury meet the relevant provisions and should be recognized as a work-related injury. Although both parties did not sign a written labor contract, the party has already worked in the designated position according to the requirements of the defendant, so there exists a de facto labor relationship between the defendant and Wu Huan. Although Wu Huan provided someone else's ID card during the interview, the person actually injured was himself, not someone else, and the injury was caused by work reasons. Therefore, the concept proposed by the defendant that the damage caused by Wu Huan's fraudulent actions does not constitute a work-related injury has no other basis to support it. For this reason, the view raised by the electrical company that "Wu Huan impersonated someone else's ID card to enter its unit for work, there is no real and valid labor relationship between him and the defendant, and the damage caused by his fraudulent actions does not constitute a work-related injury" is not supported. After investigation and verification by the labor department, based on relevant evidence, the work-related injury recognition made was appropriate. Ultimately, the Nan Chang Court made a first-instance ruling upholding the labor department's work-related injury recognition. However, the judge also reminded that although the injury suffered by the party in this case should be recognized as a work-related injury, the act of impersonating someone else's ID card during recruitment is not commendable. (Wu Huan is a pseudonym.)

Although Wu Huan's injury should be recognized as a work-related injury, the company sued the labor department in July 2008 at Wuxi Nan Chang Court, arguing that Wu Huan engaged in fraudulent behavior to gain employment, requesting the labor department to revoke its decision on work-related injury recognition.
