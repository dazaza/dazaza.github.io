Start Your iOS/Mac Development Journey: 16 Things You Should Know

by anonymous on 2013-10-09 17:22:23

I once recommended 30 people on Twitter that iOS developers should follow, and received feedback from many developers. Encouraged by this, I asked renowned iOS developers and designers the following question – if you could go back to when you first started developing iOS/Mac apps, what advice would you give your past self from your current perspective?

Matt Gemmell: Every app has been done hundreds of times, but there is still room for better ones.

Founder of Instinctive Code

There is always space for better software. On the iOS platform, every type of app has been done hundreds of times, but there is still room for those that are simpler, more outstanding, and easier to use. Even in completely saturated categories like lists and text editors. If you think your idea is unique and very valuable, then go ahead and develop and release it. If there are features you want, others will want them too.

Twitter: http://www.twitter.com/mattgemmell

Blog: http://mattgemmell.com/2013/09/20/nets/

Ray Wenderlich: Don't expect overnight success

Founder of iPhone development studio Razeware and administrator of Raywenderlich.com.

Don't expect overnight success; keep learning, growing, and developing apps – each one should be better than the last.

Twitter: https://www.twitter.com/rwenderlich

Blog: http://www.raywenderlich.com/

Mattt Thompson: Making an amazing app has never been easier – go for it!

Author of popular open-source projects AFNetworking & NSHipster.

When I started iOS development, I was a programmer with four years of Ruby & Rails experience. I spent a lot of time on language syntax and worked hard to master all the system frameworks. At the time, tutorials for basic tasks were very scarce, and there were almost no open-source projects. I had to rely on Apple's sample code.

Now, I'm glad that iOS developers have access to a large number of excellent resources and open-source project libraries. For tutorials and example code, I recommend Ray Wenderlich's website and NSScreencast. For developers who want to learn more advanced techniques, they can also check out NSHipster, where I write articles about Objective-C and Cocoa every week. In terms of open source, CocoaPods is essential. Not only does CocoaPods allow you to manage dependencies effortlessly, but it's also an amazing tool for discovering new libraries. Since I first used this tool, I've found many changes. Making an amazing app has never been easier, so my advice to those starting or considering iOS development is "go for it!"

Github: https://github.com/mattt

Twitter: https://www.twitter.com/mattt

Blog: http://mattt.me/

Dan Rowinski: Give equal importance to design and development

Mobile editor at ReadWriteWeb.

In the development planning process, give equal importance to design and development. App developers often overly focus on what the app can do, rather than how it looks and how users interact with it. If you create a killer feature that is very difficult to use, users will find it hard to return to your app. On the other hand, if you create an app that looks amazing but keeps crashing or doesn't perform as promised, users will choose to "escape." Look at Path and Instagram; they do a great job balancing design and functionality.

Twitter: http://clicktotweet.com/Vebl9

Jeremy Olson: Don't target only the niche market that can only pay 99 cents

Founder of Tapity.

Don't ignore ideas. We often hear that creativity isn't important, execution is key. I used to firmly believe this, but then something happened. My second app – Languages – made more money in one day than my first app did in two years. What was going on? Both apps were well-executed, Grades even won Apple's Design Award. Both apps were marketed well, both were recommended by Apple and the media, but what was the difference? – Ideas.

Grades was limited to its small niche market – college students who care about their grades (smaller than we imagined). Languages appealed to a broader audience, with most people interested in offline translation apps. Don't misunderstand, as the chart below shows, niche market apps can definitely make money, but the niche market must highly value the app and be willing to pay for it. Unfortunately, a common mistake most apps make is targeting a niche market that can only afford 99 cents, leading to a disappointing business equation.

Twitter: http://www.twitter.com/jerols

Marco Arment: Hire a designer

Born June 11, 1982, Marco Arment is an American iOS and web developer, tech writer, and magazine editor living in Westchester, New York. Marco Arment co-founded Tumblr and developed Instapaper.

Twitter: http://www.twitter.com/marcoarment

Blog: http://www.marco.org/

Peter Steinberger: Don't lose confidence, take more risks.

Founder of PSPDFKit.

In 2009, I wrote a social app that grabbed content from the internet, and I learned many tough lessons. This social app was very successful, and I was fascinated by the platform, but just before attending my first WWDC, Apple shut down the platform. I was devastated, it took me a long time to get over it and gather the courage to quit my regular job and become a freelancer. Later, I also struggled to give up freelancing and start my own thing. It was a hellish experience that brought me to where I am now and gave me a sustainable business.

Twitter: http://clicktotweet.com/b6m6t

Marc Edwards: The best way to learn is to share and discuss

Chief Designer at Bjango.

Collaboration is crucial. Find excellent people and work with them; they can complement your skills. Respect them and learn their techniques so you can speak the same language. For iOS designers, this means getting familiar with Xcode and the naming conventions Apple uses for UI elements. You should also attend developer conferences and meet good developers.

The fastest way to learn is to share and discuss tips and techniques. Writing helps form solid opinions and serves as a great way to check if you're working correctly (if you're not working correctly, make sure someone lets you know). Strategy is important, I like reading Asymco.com, ben-evans.com, and listening to The Critical Path. This wasn't obvious at first, but a deep understanding of industry trends can give you insight into the direction of design development. When things are more predictable, you can also plan accordingly.

Twitter: http://www.twitter.com/marcedwards

Aaron Hillegass: Don't "fall in love" with your ideas, look for ways to prove yourself wrong.

Chief Learning Officer at Big Nerd Ranch

Becoming a developer requires a certain amount of intelligence and confidence. For beginners, these traits can become disadvantages; new developers tend to "fall in love" with their ideas. Experienced programmers, after years of trial and error, are more skeptical of their assumptions. So if you're just starting your programming career, you need to find simple ways to prove your wrong ideas, don't believe everything you think of.

Twitter: http://www.twitter.com/aaronhillegass

Blog: http://www.bignerdranch.com/instructors/hillegass.shtml

Dave Verwer: Identify your users first, then develop an app to meet their needs.

Founder of iOS Dev Weekly, iPhone and iPad developer and trainer.

Twitter: http://www.twitter.com/daveverwer

Daniel Jalkut: What distinguishes successful people from others is persistence.

Founder of Red Sweater Software, Co-organizer of Core Intuition

What distinguishes successful people from others is persistence, even when others give up. Knowledge and intelligence are key factors in achieving goals, but compared to perseverance and an indomitable spirit, knowledge and intelligence pale in comparison.

Twitter: http://www.twitter.com/danielpunkass

Blog: http://www.red-sweater.com/blog/

Robin Raszka: Focus on designing the best experience for real users.

Co-founder of Tapmates and Pttrns.com

Don't try to be the next Jony Ive, focus on designing the best experience for real users. Don't stop learning new things, forget about wireframes and uploading screenshots to Dribbble, instead, learn how to prototype your work and get it running on the device as soon as possible.

Blog: http://lkd.to/robinraszka

Twitter: http://www.twitter.com/robinraszka

David Smith: To make this process enjoyable, we need to enjoy it and like the people we meet along the way.

Founder of FeedWrangler, Host of Developing Perspective

Make sure you understand what success looks like for you. If it's solely related to finances, then it's a harsh market. To make this process enjoyable, we need to enjoy it and like the people we meet along the way.

Blog: http://david-smith.org/

Twitter: http://www.twitter.com/_DavidSmith

Dan Counsell: Sometimes fewer features can be your biggest competitive advantage.

Founder of Realmac Software, Developer of the popular app Clear.

Keep the product focused by removing all unnecessary elements and features. Sometimes fewer features can be your biggest competitive advantage.

Twitter: http://www.twitter.com/dancounsell

Blog: http://dancounsell.com/about

Craig Hockenberry: Don't be afraid to meet others, overcome your introversion.

One of the developers of Twitterific, one of the prettiest iPhone clients for Twitter, and also one of the leaders of the Iconfactory website, author of several development-related books.

When I first developed software for Mac, I was very lucky to work with some genius designers like Jeffrey Zeldman and some people on Iconfactory (http://iconfactory.com/home/staff). Later, I parted ways with Jeffrey, but I still can't imagine doing products without the help of my colleagues at Iconfactory. Having a collaborator during the product development process is very important. Consider further, don't be afraid to meet others, overcome your introversion, and spend time building friendly relationships with designer and developer colleagues. You can learn a lot from others. Before starting a conversation, figure out what you know that others don't.

Twitter: http://www.twitter.com/chockenberry

Blog: http://furbo.org/

Rene Ritchie: Development, design, and marketing are different, but require equally important skills to ensure success.

iMore columnist @MobileNations

Give equal attention to coding and design, product and marketing. Development, design, and marketing are different, but all require equally important skills to ensure success. If you don't know how to code, hire the best programmers. The same goes for design and marketing. Anyone can get lucky, but the smarter you are, the luckier you'll be.

Twitter: http://www.twitter.com/reneritchie
