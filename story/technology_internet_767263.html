What's different about Cook's Apple?

by anonymous on 2013-10-11 19:11:18

Na Li

On October 5, it was the second anniversary of the death of Apple's "godfather" Steve Jobs.

Besides a commemorative video on the official website, Tim Cook, the current CEO of Apple, also sent an internal email to employees last week to honor this founder. Cook said that Jobs left behind a company that only he could have created, and his spirit would be the cornerstone for Apple's continuous progress. The popular movie "Jobs" and the hot-selling new Apple products also paid tribute to this genius in another way.

However, the situation has changed somewhat. Even though Apple has released many new products this year, its innovation ability has been criticized. Few people can clearly explain the direction of Apple under Cook's leadership. After experiencing the roller-coaster fluctuations in Apple's stock price, the sharp questions Cook faced when he took office remain: Can Apple continue to innovate? Is Apple still a leading brand after Jobs?

The Changing Apple

In the two years since Jobs' departure, Cook has been trying to bring some new changes to Apple.

The industry summarized these changes as follows: "In the Jobs era, Apple's business strategy was centered on creating an ultimate user experience, supplemented by marketing strategies to impact users, forming the Apple brand effect, stimulating consumption; whereas in the Cook era, Apple has shifted from product experience to strengthening the dominant position in the industrial chain ecosystem, occupying the dominant discourse power in the industrial chain, but with little innovation in products."

Zhang Yi, CEO of iMedia Research, believes that the reason for the change is due to the judgment of the current competitive rhythm, as well as the need to consider the personality differences of the managers.

"Steve Jobs had a perfectionist spirit, demanding that each product must have earth-shattering innovation. A few successes made people form this illusion and set increasingly high expectations for Apple's products, so much so that even the very excellent iPhone 5 did not meet people's overly high expectations. Moreover, Jobs insisted on releasing only one product per year, giving competitors ample time to innovate and find the most suitable product, which might attract Apple users to abandon waiting for the next generation of iPhones," Zhang Yi told reporters. In this situation, it's not easy for Cook to continue Apple's brilliance.

"Cook comes from the supply chain background, doing what he is good at, increasing the added value of products is the right thing to do." Zhang Yi believes that if the momentum of Apple's rapid growth over the past two years came from the inertia Jobs left behind, then what Cook needs to prove externally now is that he can create new miracles for Apple in this era.

Integrating the supply chain is the primary task. Taking the Chinese market as an example, Cook is more open when facing supply chain partners. Besides gradually publishing the supply chain list, accepting investigations by third-party independent organizations FLA on labor issues in Apple's supply chain, he also frequently visits contract manufacturing areas to inspect product conditions, and appropriately expands product cooperation objects to more flexibly control product launch cycles.

Moreover, Cook pays more attention to product release channels. In March last year, Cook suddenly appeared in high-profile attire at the Apple store in Beijing's Xidan Joy City for inspection. This was the first time in Apple's history that a CEO visited China. This move led the media to comment, "Cook did something Jobs never did." Later, Apple officially launched the iPhone 5S and 5C phones globally, and mainland China became one of the rare regions to have the first release. Apple's online store, retail stores, as well as partners like China Unicom and China Telecom also started selling simultaneously.

In the past two years, Cook has used his outstanding diplomatic skills to win good reputations for Apple in marketing and channel construction. Granite Investment Company analyst Timothy Lesko believes that Cook clearly understands what the company needs and will succeed in defeating critics in the long run. Former Apple sales director David Sobotta described it this way: The biggest difference between Cook and Jobs is that the latter's passion for products is unmatched, while Cook's attitude towards technology is more like a "lightweight fighter".

Different from Jobs' meticulous refinement of products, after the new generation of Macbook and iMac, almost all of Apple's product lines were required to be updated within a few months, and the product release times were shortened. The industry believes that this allows Apple to join the competition of other products in a more flexible manner, and this market approach aligns with Cook's "businessman" style.

Restarting dividends is one of Cook's decisions different from Jobs. After accumulating nearly $100 billion in cash, American Apple finally said that there was no need to hold onto all the money. Under Cook's leadership, this "most valuable" global enterprise announced plans to return profits to shareholders through dividend payments and share repurchases, with expected expenditures of $450 billion in the first three years after the plan starts. The previous lack of dividends was largely due to Jobs' personal will. He believed that the more responsible action for shareholders was to use the money wisely rather than distribute it as dividends. This may be because Jobs had witnessed Apple's decline more than once and remained particularly vigilant about debt. However, Cook is more willing to take on debt and return capital to investors.

The Leader's Embarrassment

Advisory company Interbrand pointed out in an annual report titled "Best Global Brands" that the Apple brand is currently the most valuable brand in the world. Its brand value is $98.3 billion, an increase of 28% over the previous year. The value of the Apple brand has surpassed Coca-Cola, which ranked first in brand value the previous year.

Former Apple CEO John Sculley also publicly praised Cook recently, believing that he is "doing a great job" in his new position. "Apple is developing well now, and I am very optimistic about it. This does not mean that Android's market share will not continue to increase; it already has a high market share. But Apple takes the premium route," Sculley said.

The "premium route" mentioned by Sculley refers to Apple's method of releasing only a few products a year and boosting performance through the huge influence of these products, such as how the iPhone has consistently contributed more than half of Apple's revenue. However, this approach is becoming less favored by Wall Street and consumers.

"Reliance on premium products can be dangerous. Apple faces a competitive environment that is different from before," Zhang Yi told reporters. Whether it's Samsung, Google, or Amazon, none of them want Apple to stay at the top for too long. Now it feels like Cook is spending more energy on increasing Apple product sales through operations, but in terms of product innovation, this company doesn't seem capable of continuing its former glory.

Zhang Yi stated that from the perspective of the product appearance, features, and pricing currently seen, Apple's competitiveness is undoubtedly declining. Except for rumored products like the Apple TV and wearable devices, Apple is still doing "micro-innovations" in consumer electronics, which clearly can no longer satisfy consumer demands. Previously, there was also the "map incident" with the iPhone 5, where Cook had to apologize to consumers, stating that Apple failed to "provide the best consumer experience."

Competitors are also unwilling to be followers.

In September this year, Samsung officially launched the GALAXY Gear smartwatch. This watch, with a 1.63-inch screen, comes with a built-in camera, supports S-Voice functionality, and has a battery life exceeding 25 hours. Lee Young-hee, vice president of Samsung Electronics' wireless division, once stated that Samsung has always been preparing for the development of watch-type smartphones, emphasizing that the key is who can commercialize the product that consumers favor first. In May, Samsung had already applied for patent registration for this product with the South Korean government.

Cook seems to disagree with external criticism, taking the iPhone 5 as an example. He believes its update is the most important since Apple launched its first iPhone in 2007. "Apple focuses more on how to make the best phone and how to perfectly integrate technology into this charming little device, rather than hastily adding the latest functions that appear in the industry. We're not trying to make our product just a list of features," he said.

This may be the latest awkward situation Apple faces. After the release of the new product, the iPhone 5S, the gold version's selling price has been hyped up to over ten thousand yuan. In the U.S. market, it sold out within just three hours of its launch. The reason for the product's popularity is simply "it looks different from the original." For more people, innovation might just be as simple as asking, "Please prove what makes this product better than the previous generation or distinguishes it from competitors."

In this regard, Jobs might understand it better than Cook.
