A Concert in the Midst of War

by nnphotowyl on 2011-08-15 22:53:52

On the evening when CCTV's nightly news had just finished airing, Mr. Wang Huo, an elderly writer from Sichuan province, called me from Chengdu. He told me he had just watched a concert broadcast on the news held in Belgrade's main square to protest against the continuous U.S. and NATO airstrikes on Yugoslavia. With great excitement, he said to me: "That square is where you recited your poem! Now it's engulfed in war, which is extremely infuriating. You should write an article about this!" I had just finished watching the same broadcast. The makeshift stage erected in the square was the very same one where I had once recited a poem, located in the exact same spot, with the same large black speakers. Beside the stage was the bustling commercial pedestrian street, and opposite the stage stood the bronze statue of Mihailovic on his high horse, a national hero who resisted Turkish invasion in the 18th century and became a symbol of Belgrade. This square is called Republic Square, the largest and most famous square in Belgrade, where many gatherings in the city take place.

One and a half years earlier, in October 1997, as part of the Chinese Writers' Delegation, I attended the 34th International Writers' Conference held in Belgrade. Our delegation leader was none other than Mr. Wang Huo. On the first day of the conference, Wang Huo, who had written works that won the Mao Dun Literature Prize, delivered a speech titled "War and Peace." The next day, the organizers arranged a poetry reading at Republic Square, with the theme still revolving around war and peace. Each of the 25 participating countries sent one writer to read their own poems, translated into Serbian by Yugoslav writers, and recited live by an actor from Belgrade. Mr. Wang Huo entrusted me to represent the Chinese Writers' Delegation and read a poem on stage.

That day was bright and sunny, beautiful flowers adorned the periphery of the square, and flocks of pigeons flew gracefully in the sky above. Imagine how incredible it must have been to hear the calls for peace, spoken in 25 different languages but united in a single voice, echoing through the air. Although I am not a poet and cannot write poetry, I read a poem written by my son called "You Tell Me." As I recited it, the entire square fell silent. In front of me were so many warm and sincere eyes of Belgraders, and my voice, amplified by the excellent sound system, resonated clearly across the square, sounding so beautiful, almost like it wasn't my own voice. I was deeply moved by myself and by that day.

Who would have thought that now, all the familiar and beautiful aspects of that place are shrouded in the smoke of war, flames rising high, and sirens wailing? It has only been a year and a half, yet peace can be so brutally trampled upon. War truly is the snake lurking among the flowers, always threatening us. Even more unimaginable is that under such fierce threats of war, there could still be such an inspiring concert, right there in Republic Square where peaceful poetry once echoed, now resounding again with the notes of peace. Without flowers, without pigeons, without sunlight, only artillery fire and smoke, yet the surge of blood and the call for peace remain undeterred. On this land repeatedly scarred by war, wars may abound, but so do poetry and music. Those who have heard the poetry and music reverberating through Republic Square will understand that in this increasingly unstable world, power and force may level everything, but they cannot level the hearts of people. There is nothing more detestable than war; it is the cancer of the world and humanity. And while poetry and music lack the penetrating power of long-range missiles, they are the flowers, doves, and sunlight of our world. Watching the concert aired on TV from Republic Square, even though the footage was fleeting and the specific content of the concert unknown, the scene moved me deeply. I came to believe more firmly that no combination of weapons could match the strength of this unique concert; all aggressors will vanish, but music will endure forever. This concert held amidst warfare will forever be etched in the hearts of all those who love music and peace.

This special concert at Republic Square in Belgrade reminded me of Jascha Heifetz, who during World War II performed over 300 concerts at the front lines, playing Schubert's "Ave Maria" repeatedly with his violin for the soldiers fighting for peace, also amidst the chaos of war. The melody soared like an eagle, carrying peace proudly forward. I also recalled Shostakovich's Symphony No. 8, also known as his famous "Leningrad Symphony." Its premiere on August 9, 1942, in Leningrad, saw the defiant notes flying amidst warfare. Perhaps many things cannot be compared directly, and we often forget our scars once healed. But all forms of aggression and war are equally detestable, reducing us from upright beings back to crawling animals. During that time, war spread like a plague across the globe, with bombs roaring overhead at any moment. Yet didn't Heifetz's concerts continue at the front line? Didn't Shostakovich's concerts proceed despite the German Nazis bombarding Leningrad with hundreds of tons of shells daily? Countless residents of Leningrad braved falling bombs, leaving the ruins behind, gathering around loudspeakers to listen to this rare and extraordinary concert.

What music could be more touching or powerful than this?

Our lives aren't filled with flowers, our world isn't bathed in sunshine, our music isn't solely soft and sentimental nocturnes, and our concerts aren't always held in luxurious concert halls. We have a kind of music, created in Wuhan studios, that carries the anger of war and hegemony in its melodies. We have a kind of concert, born amid the chaos of warfare. Art, including music, doesn't merely indulge in self-pity or chew on personal joys and sorrows or entertain itself. Instead, it is closely intertwined with the fate of humanity and the times, connected by flesh and blood. It has a broad heart and grand themes. Calling for peace and cursing war are among these themes.

I remember the poem my son wrote, which I recited at Republic Square in Belgrade a year and a half ago, with these lines: "You tell me / the sky is golden yellow / green grasslands / white lace carpets stretching from the mountain top to the foot / You tell me / the black smoke from chimneys / are kites shimmering with seven colors / dancing on the purple earth / You tell me / the sun is green / to cool down summer..." This is the plea and longing for peace from our next generation. All music, aiming to make the sun green to cool the summer, to keep the sky golden forever, and to transform the black smoke from chimneys and the smoky fires of war into kites shimmering with seven colors, holds a profoundly moving and lasting power.
