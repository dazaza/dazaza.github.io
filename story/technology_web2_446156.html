Governance of the distance between leaders and employees ≠ simply insisting on the distance between leaders and employees

by ikfagsod on 2011-06-15 11:18:06

Since yesterday when I shared my governance vigilance with everyone, I have continuously received feedback from some friends: there are those who share their feelings; those who participate in discussions; and even those who inquire about information, asking me what exactly I saw on the employees' MSN. However, everyone may have overlooked the other side of the issue: the distance between management leaders and employees ≠ simply maintaining a distance between leaders and employees. As managers, we also need to learn to look at problems dialectically.

In fact, as I mentioned in my shared insights, what matters employees talk about regarding me? How they evaluate me? These are not important. What is important is that we can see the work ecosystem that managers are in:

1. The distance between employees is closer than the distance between them and us, and their communication is more unobstructed than their communication with us.

2. Everyone generally only thinks from their own perspective. Their opinions and judgments are based on their position, values, and the information they possess. We might say this is a weakness of human nature, or more neutrally, it's a characteristic of human nature. But no matter how you say it, this is a reality that we as managers must face.

Therefore, as managers, when communicating with employees, we need to constantly remind ourselves to be mindful of the choice and control of topics, ensuring that the information we convey can be correctly understood by employees. Otherwise, a slight error could lead to significant deviations.

This is the true meaning I wanted to express yesterday.

However, the aforementioned concepts and conclusions are merely one side of the problem. As managers, we must also learn to look at problems dialectically: we need to learn to see the other side of the problem. That is: the relationship between management leaders and subordinates should not only maintain distance but also reduce distance. The only thing we need to seriously consider is what kind of distance to maintain and what kind of distance to reduce.

In fact, all people (including all employees) will earnestly do what you need them to do because they "like" you. A famous American educator, David Aspy, once wrote a book titled "Children Don't Learn From People They Don't Like," which points out that if a student doesn't like a teacher, he will develop contradictory emotions towards the classes taught by that teacher, which may even cause a sharp decline in grades for that subject. I believe everyone who has been a student has had personal experiences or observed such occurrences.

Similarly, as managers, if there is only distance between you and your employees, then you may gain "respect," but you won't gain "liking." And since all of us are rational and emotional beings, "liking" usually has a greater influence. I believe everyone who has experienced a failed romance understands this deeply. TA left you, not because you weren't good enough, but because you weren't "likable" enough, at least in their eyes.

Therefore, what I want to say is that as managers, we also need to see the other side of the coin: reducing the distance with employees. So how to do it? My personal experience is:

1. Let them understand you. Especially understand the real you, your true character. Understand your joys and sorrows, your happiness and troubles. Authenticity is the most convincing and can best reduce the distance between people. Moreover, humans have a unique characteristic: they like helping others. Therefore, if you share some of your life issues with your employees and receive their help, it will further contribute to healthily reducing the distance between each other.

2. Acknowledge your employees more and convey this acknowledgment to them. Whether it’s Peter Drucker or Fredmund Malik, both have discovered that excellent managers are good at identifying employees' strengths and timely conveying their discoveries and joy to the employees. Many companies today implement performance management. However, the vast majority of enterprises turn meetings between supervisors and employees into criticism sessions or trials (i.e., only evaluations), forgetting that it is also a rare opportunity for leaders to acknowledge employees.

3. Spend more time with your employees. Even if it’s just for mutual understanding. No one likes someone they don’t know at all. This is also why I consistently advocate and personally practice having lunch together with my team at noon in my company and team. Of course, if during interactions, leaders can listen more to the voices of employees, the results will be much better.

Of course, the distance between management leaders and employees is a problem that involves both science and art. It is something based on science but practiced through art. It requires every leader to continuously learn and practice, and there is no end to it. In this sense, in the process of growing into an outstanding leader, we are always: on the road.
