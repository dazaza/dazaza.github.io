Introduction to Trip.com

by skyito on 2009-02-03 13:17:55

Ctrip provides information on travel destinations, travel guides, and a travel community. Online booking for rooms and tickets is available.

Ctrip Company Profile:

Ctrip Travel Network was created by Ctrip Computer Technology (Shanghai) Co., Ltd in May 1999 and officially launched in October of the same year. Ctrip Computer Technology (Shanghai) Co., Ltd is a high-tech travel service company established with venture capital from home and abroad, with branches in Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, Shenzhen, and Hong Kong. In July 2000, to better serve members and expand business scope, the website founders invested in establishing Ctrip Business Co., Ltd in Shanghai, participating in the development and construction of Ctrip Travel Network. Within less than a year, Ctrip Travel Network quickly grew and achieved one-stop online services for travel products, covering hotel, air ticket, and travel route bookings as well as business travel information inquiries. In October 2000, Ctrip acquired Beijing Modern Runthrough Business Tourism Service Co., Ltd, one of the largest domestic reservation centers, becoming a large-scale business travel service enterprise and hotel distributor. Ctrip has branches in Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, Shenzhen, and Hong Kong. Our goal is: to use efficient internet technology and advanced electronic information methods to provide fast, flexible, quality, affordable, considerate, personalized travel services for members, thus becoming an excellent business and self-guided travel service institution.

Team: The team has rich experience in management, information technology, tourism, and investment. As the company continues to develop, more senior talents from the travel service industry have joined Ctrip. The perfect combination of resources, management, and experience of the management team and their seamless cooperation ensure the rapid and stable development of the company in all aspects.

Investors: Ctrip's investors include several well-known domestic and overseas venture funds, including Softbank, International Data Group (IDG), Orchid Asia, Carlyle Group, etc. They not only provided sufficient start-up capital for Ctrip but also brought rich management experience and numerous business partners to Ctrip.

Business Philosophy: Ctrip's philosophy is: customer-centric, with seamless cooperation mechanisms between teams, meticulous professional spirit, and genuine integrity in cooperation concepts, to create a "win-win" partner-style cooperation system, thus jointly creating the greatest value.

Ctrip Company Major Events in 2002: In February 2002, Ctrip's Shanghai headquarters moved; In February 2002, registered members exceeded 3 million; In March 2002, monthly room reservations exceeded 150,000 nights; On May 2, 2002, daily room reservations exceeded 8,000 nights; In May 2002, the Member Center was established.

Ctrip Perspective: 1+1=10? Jianzhang Liang: Ctrip's development has actually gone through a continuous process of selection. Ctrip's development has gone through the following stages: In 1999 when Ctrip was founded, we wanted to create Ctrip into an online encyclopedia about tourism. On our website, netizens could look up detailed information about various countries, regions, and domestic tourist attractions. All kinds of tourism needs such as food, clothing, travel, sightseeing, shopping, and entertainment could be met through Ctrip Travel Network. But during the operation of the website, we felt that we had to find ways to make money, and could not be like many websites that just hyped a concept, running completely reliant on risk investment, which obviously was not a long-term strategy.

We evaluated various industries in domestic travel services, such as hotel reservations, air ticket reservations, travel routes, etc., and found that hotel reservations at the time had a series of advantages such as no need for delivery, no warehousing, convenient for guests to pay, etc. Moreover, the profit margin in this industry in the tourism sector was also relatively generous. Therefore, we immediately set Ctrip's 2000 goal, which was to become China's largest travel website while also becoming the largest domestic hotel reservation center. As a quick way to capture the market, we chose Modern Runthrough, which was the largest company in this industry at the time. This company was the first in China to implement hotel reservations using an 800 free phone number, with a peak room night volume of around 20,000 room nights per month. Room night volume is a term in our industry, meaning that in a month, 20,000 people booked different star-rated hotels and guesthouses through this reservation company. If the average price of each room was around 400 RMB, then the monthly turnover of this company would be 8 million RMB.

In September 2000, Ctrip was the first to acquire Modern Runthrough, entering a traditional industry, which caused quite a stir in the internet industry at the time. When many websites were struggling to find ways to make money, Ctrip became a representative of the combination of the internet and traditional industries. Looking back today, this combination can be said to be a very successful case.

Mentioning the transformation, it cannot really be called a complete transformation, but rather a change in our methods: from purely using the internet to now using both the internet and traditional methods simultaneously. We are still doing traditional businesses, but while using traditional methods, we are using the internet and other high technologies to enhance them, which is one of our advantages. We should be stronger than those who only use traditional methods, definitely, because we have the support of high technology.

Culture Counterattack? Jianzhang Liang: After acquiring Modern Runthrough in the traditional industry, in the process of company integration, it is actually similar to some historical phenomena. Ctrip has been learning the operational experience of traditional things, including myself. Of course, our service methods and marketing methods are still different from what they originally did. What they did well, combined with what we do, adds value. For example, they used to do everything manually, but now it is completely standardized, processed through our current reservation system, becoming computerized operations. Doing it online is more efficient. Also, their original marketing might have been traditional, but now this traditional marketing works well too, but with the enhancement of modern technology, our sales analysis, market analysis, including e-commerce, have greatly improved.

Our integration is basically a state of complete fusion, without having a separate company. After they came in, we immediately fully integrated, using a unified marketing system, unified brand, unified management structure. Now there is no trace of Modern Runthrough, it is a complete company.

This is actually also a method and a way, this is the maximization of absorption, the most efficient way. Some companies may be different, they may want to retain their original set of things. But our two companies are doing the same market, have the same positioning, so we can fully integrate.

As for culture and personnel, whether Manchu or Han, websites should learn from traditional things, learn operational skills, but cannot give up the advantages of high technology; and those who originally did traditional business must quickly enter modern marketing methods, using high-tech input methods to do it, so when combined, it will be very tight and highly efficient.

Industrialization Strategy: Jianzhang Liang: Originally, China's tourism industry was not industrialized, they were all workshop-style, providing completely personal service methods. We need to gradually turn each product into a standard, form a network scale, and then win our surplus value, our profit, through improving efficiency and enhancing service.

In fact, online travel services in the United States have long become a very mature e-commerce model. Among them, Expedia, Travlocity, etc., have done very well. When designing our business model, we fully drew on the business models of Priceline, Travlocity, and other travel-related websites. At the same time, we launched many practical services according to local conditions and specific national conditions, including self-service travel services.

Currently, the total market share of travel agencies nationwide is less than 5%, and the remaining 95% are individual travelers. This situation is conducive to us providing corresponding travel services in the e-commerce model. In the operation process, we avoided many restrictions of the mechanism in many aspects. For example, cross-regional operations of travel agencies are limited, but since we are online, there are not too many restrictions, at least absolutely none in hotel reservations, and ticket agents are geographically restricted when delivering tickets, but we generally do not deliver tickets ourselves. After industrialization, we may have hope to become the leader in this industry.

Three Keys to Ctrip Management: It is obvious that Chinese manufacturing enterprises can use huge cost advantages to cover up their disadvantages in management and technology. I once visited some well-known Chinese manufacturing enterprises, whose management and application of information technology lag far behind foreign-funded enterprises, but because their costs are much lower than foreign-funded enterprises, they still have great advantages. However, this is not the case for service-oriented enterprises, because the cost advantage of Chinese enterprises is not very obvious, and the gap in management becomes prominent. McDonald's is an example. McDonald's personnel and raw material costs are similar to others, yet China's fast-food industry is vulnerable, the problem lies in management. McDonald's decades of accumulated experience in chain fast food, as well as its technology and equipment, are something China's fast-food industry cannot catch up with in the short term, so Chinese service enterprises must learn management quickly to occupy a place in fierce market competition. The success of Ctrip lies in using network information technology to quickly accumulate primitive experience in quality service. Ctrip's three keys: standards, concepts, and tools.

Standards refer to having a set of advanced quality service norms, including standard processes and evaluation systems. Concepts refer to a set of quality service corporate cultures, and tools refer to arming quality service with high-tech means such as network information technology. To establish a good service enterprise, these three are indispensable.

Quality Control, Aligning with Manufacturing: Jianzhang Liang: I have a very important concept, which is to try to operate and manage a service company like a manufacturing enterprise. Ctrip has a slogan: "Treat service quality like a manufacturing enterprise." Why say this? Mainly to emphasize the standardization of service quality. We treat service quality like manufacturing enterprises. In manufacturing, a defect rate often below one-thousandth or ten-thousandth is qualified; in the service industry, achieving over 90% customer satisfaction is already very good. Actually, 90% customer satisfaction still indicates a 10% defect rate; even 99% satisfaction means 1% defective products, which is unacceptable. We now advocate zero defects, although the entire customer contact service process has many steps, we still require the overall defect rate to be below 1%. Achieving this is very difficult because services are not easy to standardize. Each process and every step must be fully controllable and measurable; each interaction between service personnel and customers, every sentence spoken; each different requirement from customers, service personnel encounter different situations: meeting these requirements is a very difficult task.

At Ctrip, we standardize and proceduralize every service link, then use computers for quantitative indicator evaluations. After setting strict comprehensive service standards, we also established a real-time, objective, and precise measurement and evaluation system to ensure that quality service standards and norms are implemented.

In fact, every customer interaction is a product, requiring a whole set of indicators for measurement and control; if any item is unqualified, it is considered a defective service. General service enterprises measure by customer satisfaction, but we don't simply evaluate satisfaction rates; instead, we use multiple indicators to test "pass rates." At Ctrip, frontline service personnel have 34 qualitative and quantitative items assessed weekly at management meetings. Every incoming call has classified standard handling procedures for reference, order response speed is monitored by dedicated personnel, order completion times are tracked and improved by designated staff, and all employee calls are recorded, archived, and randomly checked.

Culture - The Soul of Service Enterprises: Jianzhang Liang: I can give an analogy - if standards are the law of quality service, then building a corporate service culture is establishing quality service ethics. Both complement each other and neither can be missing. If every employee in the company is not genuinely creating value for customers, then even the best systems and tools are futile.

Ctrip pays great attention to this aspect; from the beginning, I proposed CTRIP's core concept, summarized as C (Customer), T (Teamwork), R (Respect), I (Integrity), P (Partner).

When you walk into Ctrip, you'll find Ctrip's cultural promotional slogans everywhere: the core concept and business goals are made into employees' screen savers, displayed on every idle computer; core slogans are made into vivid and interesting posters, posted throughout relevant departments, allowing every employee to come into contact with them anytime, anywhere. We have also put considerable effort into training.
