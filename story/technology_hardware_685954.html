New material: walls can breathe too

by szj93742te on 2012-03-01 18:35:48

In the sudden cold wave, a "flower wall" composed of yellow daisies, red poinsettias and green grass brought the breath of spring to the industry fair. This flower wall utilized a technology called "3D flowers", which recycled waste plastics into containers, and used organic waste such as branches and leaves from gardening garbage as soil and fertilizer. The ecological wall inside the theme pavilion of the Expo Park covers a total area of more than 5000 square meters, making it the largest in the world. Inside the exhibition hall, there is also an "electronic water curtain" that changes rhythm according to the music, which is quite eye-catching. Engineers on site introduced that the principle of forming the water curtain is similar to that of an "inkjet printer", controlled point-to-point by more than 600 low-voltage electromagnetic valves, dancing randomly, and can even form patterns. There is also one installed in the Expo Center, which serves both decorative and partitioning functions. Besides using flowers as walls and flowing water as curtains, do you want to know the secret behind the canopy resembling flowing clouds on the "Expo Axis" channel which is about 1 kilometer long? The industry fair also reveals this: the canopy is made up of 69 pieces of white membrane fabric, making it the largest membrane structure at home and abroad so far. Such a huge cable membrane has a thickness of only 1 millimeter, with a design tension force reaching the highest strength in the world, and is unparalleled in architectural history. In the "Expo Pavilion Collection" section of the exhibition hall, the tensile membrane entertainment center tensile membrane is also a collection of new materials: the exterior walls of the Spanish National Pavilion are covered with willow woven products with strong local style; the intelligent LED curtain of the Swiss National Pavilion is made of soybean fiber, which can generate electricity and degrade; the exterior walls of the Japanese National Pavilion are ultra-light membrane structures with solar power generation devices, known as "breathable walls"; the Italian National Pavilion uses "transparent cement", which adds glass-like components to concrete, with different levels of transparency... Domestically developed new materials have also been showcased: smart temperature control glass coating, when applied to building glass, can achieve intelligent cold and heat regulation, ensuring the thermal insulation effect of buildings; self-insulating energy-saving wall materials, which burn silt into bricks, protect the environment while allowing the outer walls of buildings to change with outdoor environmental changes; thermal insulation boards, with durable performance and high thermal resistance, can reduce the emissions of greenhouse gases and other polluting gases... They have all been widely used in Expo buildings. Review by Zheng Shiling (academician of the Chinese Academy of Sciences, professor of Tongji University): The glass-constructed Crystal Palace at the first World Expo in London in 1851 amazed the world, and its shadow can still be seen in today's glass curtain wall buildings; in 1867, at the Paris Expo, gardener Monier exhibited small boats and flowerpots made of reinforced concrete, since then, reinforced concrete has become one of the main characters in the world civil engineering; the Eiffel Tower at the Paris Expo in 1889 revolutionarily used forged iron components... It is very glad to see that this industry fair has displayed the wonderful new materials of the 2010 Shanghai Expo. Among these materials, some may become exemplary examples passed down through generations, more importantly, the energy-saving, low-carbon, and green concepts they embody lead the global new building concepts. (Source: Liberation Daily)
