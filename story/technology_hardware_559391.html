Piracy in Central and Eastern Europe affects iTunes; Apple unwilling to discuss sales performance

by szjqd510 on 2011-11-15 17:31:01

According to foreign media reports, Apple's attempt to sell its movies and music in Eastern and Central Europe, where piracy is rampant, will likely be an uphill battle. Websites in this region that share files offer a wide range of films and TV programs, allowing users to watch for free or at low cost. Additionally, the content provided by Apple does not include subtitles in local languages, reducing its appeal to users.

Apple's iTunes has been operating in Western Europe for many years but only began offering video services in 12 less affluent European countries last month, including the Czech Republic, Poland, and Hungary. It was only in September that Apple started selling music in this region via iTunes.

An Apple spokesperson declined to comment on the company's sales performance in the region so far and would not disclose whether Apple plans to introduce dubbed or subtitled content for these markets.

Apple's iTunes faces competition from websites that provide content for free in every market it enters—these sites usually violate copyright laws. Eastern Europe has long been criticized for its lax enforcement of intellectual property laws and the ease with which pirated materials can be obtained. However, without government efforts to crack down on file-sharing websites, it is unlikely that the situation will change.

Marketa Prchalova, chairwoman of the Czech Anti-Piracy Alliance, said: "Many lawmakers are not very interested in adopting stricter anti-piracy laws."

Apple's approach in this region has been rather low-key so far. Patrick Zandl, editor-in-chief of the Czech tech news site Lupa.cz, said: "Apple is promoting iTunes videos quite discreetly; only the core Apple fans seem to know about their offerings."

Czech graphic designer Josef Svec said he stumbled upon iTunes' services and wouldn't mind paying over $2.70 to rent a movie from the Czech Apple store, but he considers language to be a significant barrier. "I must say, I'm not really interested in the movies on iTunes unless they have Czech subtitles," he noted.

The Prague-based Nodus Technologies operates file-sharing websites in the Czech Republic and Poland, enabling users to anonymously upload and share videos. Movies on Nodus websites typically come in multiple digital formats and often feature local-language subtitles or dubbing.

Nodus claims that it does not violate any laws, and its spokesperson stated that the website’s terms of service explicitly prohibit uploading content that infringes on copyright.

However, anti-piracy groups argue that such file-sharing platforms are wrong and should be strongly regulated under current copyright protection laws.
