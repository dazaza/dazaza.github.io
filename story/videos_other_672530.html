Insiders lament that actors' salaries are rising faster than housing prices; market chaos is the main cause.

by dahslkh6 on 2012-02-22 23:03:11

There is a saying: what has risen fastest in China in recent years? It's not the stock market, not housing prices, nor oil or gold prices. The answer is: actors' pay! Yesterday, a post on Weibo by Mai Te Media Culture President Chen L Lizhi also expressed such sentiments. Prominent real estate developer Ren Zhiqiang then forwarded his Weibo post, agreeing and humorously adding: "It's rising faster than housing prices." Producers and screenwriters complain about the rapid increase in actors' pay. "A certain female actress who was paid 20,000 RMB per episode last year is now at 300,000 RMB per episode. A male actor who was paid 150,000 RMB per episode last year is now at 500,000 RMB per episode. Another male actor is asking for 700,000 RMB per episode. As for films, prices have suddenly skyrocketed, from 800,000 RMB to 3 million RMB, and those who were at 1.2 million RMB one or two years ago are now asking for 6 million RMB." In the entertainment industry, news of exorbitant pay has been persistent. Yesterday, a Weibo post by Mai Te Media Culture President Chen L Lizhi once again expressed frustration with this phenomenon. At the end of the post, Chen L Lizhi also called for: "Whether it's TV series or movies, they haven't developed healthily yet, but have already inflated malignantly, waiting for a reshuffle!" Subsequently, when the reporter called Chen L Lizhi to further understand the situation mentioned in his Weibo post, he declined, citing "not convenient to say much," but he did indicate that the rise in actors' pay has been a significant phenomenon in recent years, a feeling shared by everyone in the circle. Indeed, besides Chen L Lizhi, many producers have also complained about the "skyrocketing" phenomenon of actors' pay. Director Feng Xiaoning once told the media in an interview: "The combined pay of a few main actors is an astronomical figure, accounting for more than 50% of a movie's total cost. But in contrast to the high pay of stars, some stars' performances are increasingly disappointing." He also complained: "Actors' price hikes are too extreme, which counts as an economic crisis for production units." Besides directors and producers, many screenwriters have also voiced their grievances. In one interview, a top-tier screenwriter told the reporter: "Our top-tier screenwriters writing a good script earn only over a million RMB in royalties, while an actor shooting a show can earn nearly ten million RMB. This situation will definitely affect screenwriters' creative enthusiasm."

The madness of price hikes: outrageous pay, normal for prices to change monthly. Has the crazy rise in actors' pay really become a major phenomenon in the industry? Yesterday, the reporter contacted some TV production companies. When talking about actors' pay, almost everyone unanimously stated: "It's outrageously high, especially in the past two years, rising particularly fast."

Mr. Lu, a specialist responsible for negotiating with actors at a TV institution, told the reporter that the surge in star salaries started in 2009. "Previously, shooting an episode of a TV series, second-line actors' salaries were between 30,000 to 50,000 RMB, now the basic quote is around 100,000 RMB. And first-line tail-end, second-line head actors, who were quoted around 100,000 RMB two years ago, are now quoting around 3 to 4 million RMB. First-line actors don't need to be said, prices that were around 150,000 RMB two years ago are now generally reaching 4 to 5 million RMB, some even nearing a million RMB."

Miss Wang from a TV production company gave the reporter some examples. "Two years ago, a second or third-line male actor WT had an episode pay of only 30,000 to 50,000 RMB, now it's around 150,000 RMB; a female actress GTT, whose previous pay was approximately 120,000 RMB per episode, now demands over 300,000 RMB per episode; another male actor WZ, whose pay has increased several times in the past two years, was less than 100,000 RMB per episode two years ago, now it's over 700,000 RMB per episode; and a big-name male actor SHL, whose pay per episode is close to a million RMB..."

Although actors' pay is already astonishingly high, the pace of price hikes hasn't stopped, and it's becoming increasingly absurd. Mr. Lu told the reporter that he is currently negotiating cooperation plans with some actors, and every time he communicates, their pay keeps increasing. "It basically increases every half month, generally by 500,000 to 1 million RMB every half month. This isn't an individual phenomenon, the situation where prices change monthly is everywhere. And the pay of top-tier big names can even increase every few days."

Severe consequences: six to seven tenths of the investment goes to actors' pay. The crazy rise in actors' pay directly leads to an increase in TV drama production costs. "In 2007, we could make a very good piece with 8 million RMB, inviting big-name stars from Hong Kong, Taiwan, and other places, and still ensuring post-production quality. But now, let alone 8 million RMB, even tens of millions might not be enough to cover actors' pay." Mr. Zhang from a TV production company calculated for the reporter, if a piece invites two top-tier stars, at the lower end, these two people would require 800,000 RMB per episode, shooting 30 episodes would mean 24 million RMB just for the two big names' pay. Adding the pay of other actors, it wouldn't stop below 30 to 40 million RMB. "Now shooting a TV series, actors' pay proportion basically takes up six to seven tenths of the overall investment, or even higher." Mr. Zhang indicated that last year, a TV series with an investment exceeding 40 million RMB saw a single actor's pay reach over 10 million RMB.

With actors' pay taking up too large a proportion, it will definitely affect investments in other areas. "Some viewers may notice that the costume craftsmanship in a certain drama is very crude, props are all from Taobao, and post-production seems incomplete... Yes, but there's nothing we can do, all the money went to actors' pay, so we can only save costs in other aspects." Miss Zhao from a TV production company lamented that the phenomenon of robbing Peter to pay Paul is very common in today's TV production. The current uneven quality of TV dramas is somewhat related to the excessively high proportion of actors' pay.

Complicated reasons: chaotic market creates a situation of excessive bargaining. Why has there been such a crazy rise in actors' pay in the past two years? Several insiders analyzed this.

Firstly, the TV drama market is getting larger, with more and more people entering. Especially many big bosses and big companies are now involved in film and television markets, but due to lack of understanding of this market, they simply throw money around, directly leading to the crazy rise in actors' pay. "They have plenty of money, seeing a good script, they insist on finding a certain person to act. If this actor doesn't agree, they directly raise the pay, offering much higher pay than market prices to attract them. And there are only so many well-known actors domestically, one person raises their pay, others follow suit. First-line actors raise their pay, second and third-line actors won't fall behind either."

The influx of large amounts of capital is an important reason for the crazy rise in actors' pay. The unwritten rule of TV stations choosing shows based on actors also gives actors leverage to raise their prices. "Now TV stations choose shows based on actors. One could say, currently actors with pay under 400,000 RMB basically have no influence, TV stations look at whether there are any first-line big names with pay over 400,000 RMB." Mr. Zhang said, this situation of choosing shows based on actors allows these actors the confidence to raise their pay.

Helpless strategies: production companies are at a loss, prefer not to shoot. With actors' pay being outrageously high, is there any way to slow down its rising trend? Regarding this, the insiders interviewed by the reporter were all helpless: "We don't want them to rise so high either, but it's really difficult."

Miss Zhao indicated that current countermeasures are somewhat helpless. "Generally, we think of promoting new talents. But this carries great risks, although new talent pay is low, their market influence is weak, if the production doesn't get watched, it's a loss. So the most common method is using one or two first or second-line big names, and then use new talent for the rest, trying to lower the proportion of pay."

Besides promoting new talent, another method is signing actors themselves. Mr. Lu said: "Now many TV production companies cultivate their own artists, which is cheaper to use. But this carries great risk, generally after these actors become famous, they easily switch jobs. After becoming famous, the market expands, and they have more choices." An even more helpless method is robbing Peter to pay Paul, sacrificing investment in costumes, props, post-production, etc., to reduce overall costs. "This method directly affects the quality of TV dramas, but it's what many production companies adopt now."

Another strategy is joint production of TV series by multiple companies. "Before, one company could easily bear the cost of producing a TV series, now if you want to ensure quality and market, often multiple companies need to jointly produce. But this reduces the producer's profits significantly, so some people don't want to shoot anymore." Miss Wang expressed this view.

During interviews, several insiders all expressed hope for measures within the industry to regulate the phenomenon of excessively high actors' pay. "People often bring it up at industry meetings, but there's never been a good solution. Of course, relying solely on policy won't work, the market must control it."
