The media exposed that some of the Supor stainless steel cookware contained four times the amount of manganese allowed.

by lovei0c1 on 2012-03-06 13:56:25

Clothing, food, shelter, and transportation are the things that ordinary people care about most when it comes to daily living. Recently, the issue of the quality of Supor pots has been on the minds of many consumers. After testing, the Harbin industrial and commercial department determined that 81 specifications across four major categories of stainless steel cookware from the well-known brand Supor were substandard products. However, Supor Company insisted that their products meet the standards. This matter has been debated back and forth, but until now, there has been no authoritative conclusion. What exactly is the background of this incident, and what is the truth?

In October 2011, the media revealed that a large number of products from the famous cookware brand Supor had been found substandard upon inspection. These included four major categories with 81 specifications and 960 items, covering soup pots, steamers, milk pots, and kettles.

The problematic Supor products almost covered all the low-end product series of Supor, with market prices generally under 300 yuan. The reasons for disqualification were excessive manganese content and insufficient nickel content.

As the leader in China's cookware industry, the news that Supor products were found substandard immediately caught the attention of the media and consumers. Many merchants and supermarkets quickly removed the related implicated products from their shelves.

Facing doubts, Supor soon issued an announcement claiming that their products had passed relevant department inspections and were qualified. The reason for the detection of substandard products was mainly due to the different standards referred to by both parties regarding stainless steel materials. It was understood that the Harbin industrial and commercial department based their test on the "Hygiene Standard for Stainless Steel Food Containers" issued by the Ministry of Health in 1988, while Supor followed the stainless steel utensils industry standard issued by the Light Industry Ministry in 1992.

According to information, the "Hygiene Standard for Stainless Steel Food Containers" used as the basis by the Harbin industrial and commercial department was formulated in 1988 in China and falls under the category of national mandatory standards. This standard specifies that three types of austenitic stainless steel can be selected for various food storage containers and food processing machinery.

Why did one side say不合格 (unqualified) and the other say no problem? This requires explaining the raw materials used in pot production. Stainless steel is a high-alloy steel, and its composition greatly affects the material's characteristics. In the manufacturing process, it requires the addition of many alloy elements; adding nickel enhances the corrosion resistance of stainless steel, while adding manganese increases strength. The material problems detected in this case involved excessive manganese content.

Liu Jin Hua, the information director of the Stainless Steel Division, introduced that in the new national cold-rolled steel sheet standard, there are a total of 71 steel grades, specifying that manganese content should be less than or equal to 2%.

According to tests conducted by the Heilongjiang Hardware Electrical Appliances Product Quality Supervision and Inspection Station, the manganese content in these substandard Supor products was significantly higher, far exceeding national standard requirements.

From the test report, some substandard Supor products had manganese content nearly four times higher than the national standard. According to experts, manganese in steel can increase the strength and plasticity of steel and enhance its work-hardening ability and wear resistance. However, if manganese exceeds the national standard limit, it can reduce the corrosion resistance of stainless steel.

Experts also pointed out that the main reason for excessive manganese and insufficient nickel content is that stainless steel companies use manganese instead of nickel during production to reduce costs because manganese is much cheaper than nickel. Nickel costs 130,000 yuan per ton, while manganese costs only 13,000 yuan per ton.

It was learned that enterprises using similar materials with excessive manganese content are not limited to Supor alone. Using manganese to replace nickel has become an unwritten rule in the stainless steel industry.

Regarding the so-called 2% limit on manganese content in stainless steel materials, Supor believed that this was merely an industry-recommended standard, not a national mandatory standard. In other words, companies could choose to follow it or not.

In November 2011, Zhang Liping, public relations supervisor of Supor, stated in a media interview that several types of stainless steel grades specified in the 1988 national standard have been abolished in the stainless steel material standard. Supor has reported the situation to the Ministry of Health and the China Hardware Products Association seeking help. Subsequently, this incident saw new developments. On November 4, 2011, the China Hardware Products Association and Supor held a media briefing where they presented a reply letter from the Ministry of Health titled "Reply on the Applicable Standards for Stainless Steel Food Containers." The reply indicated that the new hygiene standard for stainless steel food containers about to be issued would eliminate specific restrictions on stainless steel models. Based on this, Supor claimed that authoritative government departments had clarified the facts, proving that Supor's products fully comply with the standards and are reliable and trustworthy.

On December 21, 2011, the new national food safety standard for stainless steel products was released and implemented. The new standard eliminated specific restrictions on stainless steel models, which to some extent clarified Supor's material issues.

In February 2012, Supor was exposed by CCTV’s “Focus Talk” program. The focus of the report centered on excessive manganese content in Supor materials and suggested that overconsumption might lead to neurological diseases such as Parkinson's disease. Following this, numerous media outlets followed up with reports, causing widespread consumer concern known as the "Supor Manganese Panic," with many consumers indicating they intended to discard their Supor pots. Public opinion once again pushed Supor into the spotlight.

In response, Supor argued that the manganese content issue does not relate to the food safety and hygiene of stainless steel. Manganese content does not necessarily mean manganese leaching; in other words, even if there is more manganese in the pot, it does not necessarily transfer into the food.

The key point of the controversy over the detection of substandard Supor products lies in the manganese content, yet the newly announced Ministry of Health standard on December 21, 2011, focused on the migration amounts of heavy metals like chromium, nickel, cadmium, and arsenic in different stainless steel models and did not involve manganese migration. Currently, in the physicochemical indicators section of China's hygiene standard for stainless steel food containers, only the leaching amounts of lead, chromium, nickel, cadmium, and arsenic are specified, without any standard or method for detecting manganese leaching.

Besides whether manganese content and manganese leaching are directly proportional, another major point of contention is whether excessive manganese will lead to poisoning. According to the dietary reference intakes for Chinese residents, the appropriate intake of manganese for adults is 3.5 mg per day, with a tolerable upper intake level of 10 mg per day. So, would long-term use of stainless steel utensils with excessive manganese lead to manganese poisoning?

On February 24, 2012, in response to the widely publicized Supor quality scandal, the National Food Safety Risk Assessment Center organized relevant experts to conduct a risk assessment on more than 40 batches of Supor stainless steel cookware and subsequently published preliminary assessment results. The results showed that the average manganese migration amount in Supor stainless steel products was 0.35 mg per kilogram, within a safe and controllable range.

Experts also emphasized that manganese is one of the essential trace elements for the human body, present in most foods and drinking water, with food being the primary source. Manganese, as an essential trace element for the human body, is a component of our reductase and serves as an activator for some metabolic enzymes. A lack of manganese in the body can cause deficiency symptoms, such as abnormalities in protein, sugar, and vitamin metabolism.

If consumed excessively, it can harm the central nervous system, manifesting mildly as symptoms of neurasthenia, such as headaches, dizziness, weakness, insomnia, and memory decline, and may lead to autonomic nervous system disorders. Typical clinical manifestations resemble Parkinson's syndrome.

Experts particularly stressed that manganese poisoning mostly occurs in special industries, such as workers involved in manganese mining and smelting or electric welding, where the main route of exposure is through the respiratory tract. To date, there have been no clinically reported cases of adverse reactions caused by dietary manganese intake among ordinary residents, nor any reports of manganese poisoning caused by food containers.

The results from the National Food Safety Risk Assessment Center, to some extent, mitigated the negative impact of the manganese excess incident and cleared Supor of the "toxic" label. Some netizens commented: This evaluation result only indicates that manganese excess does not lead to manganese poisoning but cannot prove that there are no quality issues with Supor products.

Senior commentator Liu Busu in the home appliance industry believes that there are two problems in this Supor product quality incident: first, does the Food Safety Evaluation Center have the qualifications to conduct quality appraisals of cookware? Second, the stainless steel association has standards, and Supor can argue that their determination is not authoritative. Supor's crisis will continue to escalate because they have not fundamentally resolved consumer confusion: whether there is an excess and whether it affects public food safety. Up to now, we have not seen the most authoritative conclusion.

On the day the evaluation results were announced, Supor again issued an announcement emphasizing that their products pose no risk of manganese poisoning and that there is no issue with excessive manganese content. The next day, the related problematic products were relisted for sale.

Reporters from the "Investigation" program visited several large malls and found that many pots with manganese excess issues reappeared on Supor counters. However, few customers visited the cookware counters, making them unusually quiet.

In fact, since the end of last year, apart from issuing announcements insisting that their products have no problems, Supor has never made an apology statement or return/exchange notice regarding external doubts. The removal of related products from shelves was a unilateral measure by some malls.

In 2007, most shares of Supor were acquired by SEB, one of the world's largest small appliance companies, turning Supor into a company with foreign capital background. However, neither being the largest domestic cookware producer nor having a foreign capital background helped Supor successfully handle this crisis.

Recently, companies besides Supor have also faced suspicion and questioning. For example, Anxin floors, which were found to have excessive formaldehyde content, and Gui Zhen Tang, which practices bile extraction from live bears. In these three crises, Supor, Gui Zhen Tang, and Anxin consistently used various standards or theories to prove their innocence but never considered how they should face the public and consumers. In contrast, Wang Shi, the responsible person of Vanke Group, a real estate enterprise using Anxin floors, gave the most reassuring statement: once product problems are discovered, Vanke will assume full responsibility and protect consumer rights. Even a 1% error means 100% for consumers.

The Supor quality scandal is not just a test of product quality but also a test of relevant departments' ability to handle emergencies. When facing trust crises, companies should primarily think of consumers and safeguard their interests. Similarly, when problems and controversies arise, consumers hope to hear authoritative voices and fair handling from regulatory agencies as soon as possible rather than letting the public remain in panic and doubt, leading to loss of trust.
