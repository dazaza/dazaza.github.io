Index futures - correlation reaches 46%

by zhanladaim on 2012-03-08 10:16:28

⊙ China Securities Futures Liu Jian ○ Editor Yang Gang International energy markets have changed dramatically in the past two weeks. On February 19, after Iran imposed a crude oil embargo on the UK and France, the next day saw a significant rise in the electronic trading price of New York oil. On February 21 last year, which was also a Presidents' Day holiday in the United States, the worsening situation in Libya caused the New York electronic trading price to soar by more than 6%. However, this year's oil market appears slightly weaker, with the strong rally lasting only a week before gradually declining. I believe that the recent easing of the Iranian nuclear crisis, along with the weakness in US consumption and the slowdown in China’s economic growth, will significantly suppress oil prices. It is expected that the strong momentum in the oil market will shift.

The Iranian Nuclear Crisis Eases

In the Iranian nuclear crisis, the hardline confrontation between the EU and Iran has been the focus. To force Iran to abandon its nuclear program, the EU resorted to imposing an oil embargo on Iran to achieve its goal. However, due to the refusal of Asia-Pacific countries to cooperate, the sanctions on Iran have not had any substantial effect, instead making the surge in oil prices the biggest threat to the economy. Therefore, the EU's stance has also begun to change recently. On March 6, the EU agreed to resume nuclear negotiations with Iran, showing a marked softening compared to last year when it demanded that Iran "set no preconditions."

From the current situation in Iran, I believe the possibility of military conflict is extremely small, and diplomatic dialogue and economic sanctions will remain the only means for the West against Iran in the future. In the long term, the Iranian nuclear situation will still be complicated and repetitive; but in the short term, the temporary easing of the situation is likely to occur, and the most important factor supporting the rise in oil prices over the past two weeks will be significantly weakened.

Weakening of US Consumption

Apart from geopolitical factors, due to the EU economy being mired in difficulties, the consumption in the US has become the dominant factor in the oil market. From the data on US energy consumption, although the US economy continues to improve, energy product consumption remains weak. In February, the US refined oil consumption fell to the lowest level in over 10 years, while gasoline consumption dropped to the lowest level in nearly 15 years.

From the current proportion of US petroleum products in personal consumption, although it has declined for three consecutive months, it still remains at around 4%, significantly higher than the recent three-year average of 2.5%. Although US auto sales have continued to grow since the third quarter of last year, the high gasoline prices remain the main constraint. Currently, gasoline prices have risen since the end of last year, with an increase of over 30% so far. In the next one to three months, it is expected that gasoline consumption will remain at a low level.

Therefore, from the perspective of US market consumption, the consumption of petroleum products continues to decrease, and this trend is expected to continue. From the current operation status of US refineries, since mid-February, there have been three consecutive weeks of declines. However, during the same period, supply pressure has not decreased, with rapid growth in imports from Canada. The crude oil inventory at Cushing, the NYMEX delivery location, has reached 33.8 million barrels, an increase of 5.5 million barrels from the beginning of the year. Therefore, I expect that the US market will not show significant improvement in the next one to three months, and inventory pressure will significantly suppress oil prices.

Slowdown in the Chinese Economy

Traditionally, as the world's second-largest oil consumer with a low self-sufficiency rate, China has always been a major buyer in the international energy market. In the past decade, China's crude oil consumption has grown at an average annual rate of nearly 8%, becoming the main driving force behind global crude oil consumption growth. Historically, China's crude oil growth has been strongly correlated with GDP growth, with a correlation of 46%.

On March 5, Premier Wen Jiabao pointed out in the government work report that the GDP growth target for 2012 is set at 7.5%. A slowdown in China's economic growth has become a certainty. For the energy market, the decline in China's consumption will become a major drag in the future. Data shows that 75% of the GDP growth decline will be transmitted to crude oil demand. Based on this calculation, the slowing of GDP growth in 2012 will result in a reduction in crude oil consumption growth of about 1.2%, or approximately 5 million tons (100,000 barrels/day), while crude oil demand growth in 2011 was only 800,000 barrels/day.

In summary, we expect that in the short term, geopolitical factors will ease significantly, and the short-term support factors for the upward movement of crude oil will begin to weaken gradually. After two weeks of consolidation, the US stock market retreated from its highs, and the sharp rebound of the dollar in the past week will all weigh on oil prices. In the medium to long term, the weakness in US consumption and the drag from China's economic slowdown will both significantly affect international crude oil consumption. Therefore, we expect that oil prices will see a noticeable decline from March to the second quarter.
