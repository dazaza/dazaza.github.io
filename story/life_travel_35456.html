Historic and Cultural Famous City - Dali, let me introduce to you my lovely hometown. Dali, a renowned historic and cultural city, is located in Yunnan Province, China. It is a place where natural beauty and cultural heritage blend harmoniously. Surrounded by the majestic Cangshan Mountains and overlooking the picturesque Erhai Lake, Dali offers breathtaking landscapes that have captivated visitors for centuries. The city is known for its rich history, dating back over 1,000 years as the capital of the Nanzhao and Dali Kingdoms. The ancient city of Dali, with its cobblestone streets, traditional Bai ethnic architecture, and well-preserved gates, provides a glimpse into the past. The Three Pagodas of Chongsheng Temple stand as iconic symbols of Dali's historical significance. Besides its history, Dali is also celebrated for its diverse ethnic cultures. The Bai people, who are the main ethnic group in the region, have their unique traditions, festivals, and craftsmanship, such as tie-dyeing and silverware making. These cultural elements add charm and character to the city. In addition to its cultural attractions, Dali boasts stunning natural scenery. Visitors can hike on Cangshan Mountain, enjoy boating on Erhai Lake, or relax in the charming villages like Xizhou and Zhoucheng. The combination of beautiful landscapes, mild climate, and friendly locals makes Dali an ideal destination for travelers seeking tranquility and cultural enrichment. I am proud to call Dali my hometown, and I warmly invite you to visit this enchanting place!

Dali is located in the middle-west of Yunnan Province. To the east lies Erhai Lake, and to the west are the Cangshan Mountains. With a mild climate and fertile soil, Dali boasts picturesque landscapes and is one of the earliest developed areas on the southwestern frontier of China. As early as four thousand years ago, there were activities of primitive residents in the Dali area.

Throughout its long history, Dali has held a prominent position and played an important role. During the Qin and Han dynasties, Dali was a necessary stop on the "Shu-India National Road" (a route starting from Chengdu, Sichuan, passing through Dali and Baoshan in Yunnan, entering Myanmar, and then leading to India). This passage played a crucial role in promoting the connection between the Dali region and inland areas, as well as fostering friendly exchanges and cultural interactions between China and various Southeast Asian countries.

In the second year of Yuany Feng during the Western Han Dynasty (109 BC), Yeyu County was established in the Dali area, and this administrative division remained unchanged until the Northern and Southern Dynasties. At the beginning of the Tang Dynasty, among the "six zhao" around the Erhai area, the Mengshe Zhao's power gradually increased. With the support of the Tang Dynasty, in the 26th year of Kaiyuan (738 AD), it unified the "six zhao" and established the Nanzhao Kingdom. In the 27th year of Kaiyuan (739 AD), the capital of the Nanzhao Kingdom moved from Weishan to Taihe City (near today's Taihe Village at the foot of Cangshan Mountain in Dali), and later relocated to Yangjumie City (in the west area of today's Dali Ancient City). The capitals of the Nanzhao Kingdom and the subsequent three dynasties, as well as the Dali Kingdom, were all located in Yangjumie City. From the 8th to the 13th century over five hundred years during the Tang and Song dynasties (from the establishment of the Nanzhao Kingdom in 738 AD to the fall of the Dali Kingdom in 1253 AD), Dali was always the political, economic, and cultural center of Yunnan, and the location of the capitals of the Nanzhao and Dali Kingdoms.
