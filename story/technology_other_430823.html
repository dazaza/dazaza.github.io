**Breeding Techniques for Mealworms**

The larvae of mealworms contain 51% protein and 29% fat. They also contain various sugars, amino acids, vitamins, hormones, enzymes, and minerals such as phosphorus, iron, potassium, sodium, and calcium, making their nutritional value higher than other bait insects. The so-called "king of animal protein feed," the superworm (or breadworm), has a protein content of 47%, fat content of 23%, and carbohydrate content of 23%, all of which are inferior to the mealworm. Additionally, mealworms are rich in chitin and contain small amounts of astaxanthin, which helps improve the coloration of fish and turtles.

Mealworms prefer dry environments, are strong in vitality, and can withstand hunger and thirst. They can grow and reproduce throughout the year. Their life cycle, from egg to larva to pupa and finally to adult after molting, takes about 100 days. When the temperature is below 10°C, they enter hibernation. The optimal growth and development temperature is between 20°C and 32°C, while temperatures above 35°C can be lethal. The relative humidity of the air should ideally be between 65% and 70%. The outer shell of the mealworm egg has spines around its body. The egg stage lasts 8-10 days. At 30°C, adult mealworms lay the most eggs, with each adult capable of laying up to 1000 eggs. Below 14°C, mating and egg-laying decrease significantly, and below 10°C, there is no mating or egg-laying. Mealworms prefer to cluster together. They start to move and eat at room temperatures of 13°C, and can still grow at temperatures above 5°C. They grow fastest at 25°C, but temperatures above 35°C can cause mass deaths. Both larvae and adults have a tendency to cannibalize smaller individuals, and larvae may sometimes injure pupae. Therefore, it is necessary to separate mealworms of the same age, as well as eggs, pupae, and adults, into different containers for breeding.

Mealworm shells are thinner and more brittle compared to those of superworms, making them easier to digest and less likely to cause overheating when consumed in large quantities. Experiments have shown that feeding various types of fish with mealworms results in a digestion rate of 90%, whereas the skin of superworms is almost indigestible and is excreted whole. Similarly, the shed skins of mealworms can be eaten by larvae, but this is not possible with superworms.

Below is an example of a sawtooth turtle that was exclusively fed superworms. After refusing food and dying, an autopsy revealed undigested superworm shells in the intestines, causing intestinal blockage.

During the growth of mealworm larvae, their body color first appears milky white, turning yellowish-brown after the first molt. Thereafter, they molt every 6-10 days, with a total of 6-10 molts during the larval stage. Around 30 days old, the larvae pupate in the feed. At room temperatures above 20°C, the pupae transform into adults after ten days. Immediately after molting, adult mealworms have soft white wings that harden and turn dark brown within 1-2 days. They are active at night, crawl quickly, and cannot fly. They begin mating and laying eggs about ten days after molting.

A kilogram of mature mealworm larvae contains approximately 700-800 worms, with a male-to-female ratio of 6:4. About 400 females can lay eggs, and if each female lays at least 300 eggs, considering mortality and other factors in farming, a conservative estimate would yield 100-120 kilograms of larvae.

After collecting the mealworm eggs, it is appropriate to add green fodder and fine feed to the adult mealworms, and promptly remove any dead adult mealworms from the waste material. Adult mealworms are active at night, so more feed should be provided during the evening. Green fodder can be directly placed in the breeding container, allowing the mealworms to feed freely.

After some time of breeding mealworms, select individuals over 5 cm long, with smooth bodies and rapid movement. Place them separately in disposable cups. After single rearing for a few days (mealworms do not need to be fed during this period), their bodies will swell. This is not a sign of illness but rather a precursor to molting into pupae. Once they have molted, they can be taken out and put into a new container. Pupae develop faster after molting, initially appearing white and then turning yellow. Finally, they become dark brown, completing the transformation very quickly. At this point, these newly transformed adult mealworms possess the ability to mate and lay eggs.

Place bran and adult mealworms in the container. Adult mealworms also require food, such as green leafy vegetables and carrots.

Adult mealworms will continuously mate and lay eggs. Eggs are usually laid in the bran pile below, avoiding being eaten by the adults.

After 2-3 weeks, the eggs hatch into larvae. At this point, some fine feed like flour or cornmeal can be added, or fruit peels can be placed in the container, where mealworms will gather to suck the juice.

Finally, they grow into our final product: mealworms. It is important to regularly supplement adult mealworms to ensure a continuous supply.
