I. Innovation in hotel design and service are the two main wheels driving the development of the hotel industry.

The hardware and software of the hotel industry have always been in a state of change. New trends in Europe and America include the announcement by the French Louis Vuitton Group on April 8, 2010, to open a wholly-owned subsidiary, Louis Vuitton Hotel Management Group, specializing in the design and operation of high-end hotels; the New York Grand Hyatt introduced New York's fashionable dining establishments into its hotel lobby.

Looking back at history, whenever there is a major transformation in productivity, changes in socio-economic life will accelerate accordingly. This was true during the industrial revolution and it remains so in today’s information revolution, as science and technology along with customer needs determine product types and service methods.

The renowned American futurist Alvin Toffler predicted in his book "The Third Wave" published in the 1980s that the scale operations of the industrial era would be replaced by diversification, multiplicity, and personalization in the information age. Toffler's prophecy later came true in the hotel industry as well. In his hometown, news emerged that chain hotels were losing out to independent hotels. This news appeared in the authoritative magazine "Hotel" in the first issue of 2001, disclosed in the "Standard Historical Trend Report 1994-2000" by the U.S. Smith Travel Research Institute. The data showed that the occupancy rate of chain hotels in mid-range hotels in the United States fell from leading independent hotels by 6.1 percentage points in 1994 to lagging behind by 0.6 percentage points in 1999, and further to lagging behind by 1.1 percentage points in 2000; in terms of average room rates, chain hotels were at $68 while independent hotels were at $90. In economy-class hotels, chain hotels had an occupancy rate 7 percentage points higher than independent hotels in 1994, but now they are on par. In budget hotels, the average occupancy rate of chain hotels has been declining by more than 1 percentage point annually for seven years, shifting from a significant lead to falling behind by more than 1 percentage point. The only category still maintaining a lead is luxury high-end hotels, but over six years, the trend of occupancy rates has been continuously declining, significantly weakening their advantage. It should be noted that these statistics are solely from the United States and may not have broad representativeness. Even regarding the situation in the United States, a systematic analysis combining relevant factors should be conducted. However, we cannot ignore this phenomenon; it should be viewed as a possible trend, studying the intrinsic reasons behind this trend to summarize adaptive strategies and adopt corresponding countermeasures. In fact, some pioneers have already proposed related viewpoints from a positive perspective.

Schragger, who became famous in the 1970s for constructing New York's discotheque "Studio 54", is now the owner and operator of more than a dozen innovative hotels worldwide, including "Mondrian" in Los Angeles, "Sanderson" in London, "Delano" in Miami, and "Hudson" in New York. He frankly believes: "An actual change now is abandoning the mass-market, monotonous hotels that dominated for 70 years, hotels are becoming more original, uniformity is no longer a virtue, and in 25 years, people will not stay in chain hotels."

Cahill, President and Chief Operating Officer of Fairmont Hotels & Resorts, which owns many charming hotels, predicts that travelers will become tired of uniform chain hotels and trendy economy hotels. "Our hotels enjoy unparalleled status in their communities due to their history and location. People stay here to place themselves at the center of all activities."

Alan of Hilton said that continuous technological progress and the emergence of long-stay hotels mean that future hotels will increasingly be designed for guests rather than for the hotel itself.

II. Experience Economy and Cultural Competition

American futurist Alvin Toffler divided the development of social economics into four stages: agricultural economy, industrial economy, information economy, and bio-economy in his book "The Third Wave," and further divided the development of industrial economies into three stages: manufacturing economy, service economy, and experience economy in his book "Future Shock," viewing the experience economy as the future direction of the service industry.

Leisure industries in the United States have become the primary industry, predicted to account for half of the GNP by 2015; Japan's electronic gaming industry alone accounted for 20% of the national economy in 2001, surpassing the automotive industry to become the primary industry; South Korea's gaming industry has also become one of the most profitable sectors, with a production value reaching 20 billion US dollars and an annual growth rate of 30-40%.

In April 1999, when the book "The Experience Economy" co-authored by Joseph Pine II and James Gilmore was first published, it received widespread attention. The book proposed that "work is theater, business is stage," making the experience economy instantly popular.

The experience economy encompasses a relaxed, fashionable culture, human-centered thinking, a pursuit of innovation, emphasizes satisfying psychological needs, provides a humanized environment and service, integrating material enjoyment with spiritual enjoyment, reflecting the essence of postmodernism.

The experience economy is a business model oriented towards customer needs and experiences, where products are almost entirely hidden behind services, reversing the relationship between service and product. Products depend on conditions created by services, as exemplified by Starbucks.

Returning to our hotels, the sprouts of the experience economy actually already exist; we just haven't elevated them to a rational understanding. Wedding banquets are relatively common examples because hotels can provide wedding experiences.

Human needs are the driving force behind economic development, and the need for experiences is one of those needs. Marx predicted that in communist society, labor would become the first need of people's lives, "labor is a pleasure." In Maslow's hierarchy of needs, "self-actualization" is the highest level, and the experience economy is an economic form aimed primarily at satisfying people's emotional needs and self-actualization needs.

In the experience economy, enterprises mainly focus not on providing products but on providing a stage. Experiential elements are embedded in products and services, and consumption is merely the process. Consumers become the product of this process, and after the process ends, the experiential memory will be stored in the consumer's mind for a long time. Consumers willingly pay for the experience because this experience becomes a pressing need when people's living standards reach a certain level. Consumers themselves undergo or participate in this process, and their emotions and psychology are satisfied.

To incorporate experiential elements into traditional hotels, we need to consider both people's regular needs and their spiritual needs. These spiritual needs should be studied from the perspectives of diversity, personalization, and multimedia approaches. We should emphasize transforming culture and knowledge into productive forces; knowledge is profit.

Hotel suppliers should be the main body of hotel room sales. They are the ones controlling the channels and should not let the channels control the hotels. This is why international hotels perform better. It’s not necessarily about having the lowest price website being the best, but the product that brings real benefits to consumers is the best. It’s not about how many rooms third parties sell being the best, but about the hotel's overall revenue truly increasing being the best. And it’s not about doing only direct sales being the best, but about effectively utilizing distribution for supplementation being the best.

We should consider this question: Where does the profit reported in each quarterly financial report of Ctrip and eLong come from? Is it equal to the profit lost by the hotels they contract with?

Everyone understands this issue, so why are improvements not made? I think it might be due to this reason: For example, a hotel with 100 rooms has 20 rooms coming from distributors, ensuring an average occupancy rate of around 80%. If the hotel chooses not to be controlled by distributors, it means losing the sale of 20 rooms, causing the occupancy rate to directly drop to around 60%, possibly turning the hotel from a profitable state to a loss-making one. This is something no manager wants to see. But continuing out of fear means forever being unable to break free from this web and remain controlled, preventing hotel suppliers' profits from ever increasing.

Swift Action = Increased Revenue — Expanding Channels

It is imperative to act and establish your own direct sales network. Establishing direct sales does not mean completely closing distribution. As I mentioned earlier, maintaining distribution when direct sales reach marginal benefit is a very good choice. Moreover, the core purpose of doing so is to increase revenue. Therefore, what hotel operators need to do is adjust the effective ratio of direct and distribution sales, for instance, reducing the 20% rooms in the previous example to within 10%, even within 5%. Due to Ctrip's "Lowest Price Guarantee" causing a storm in the travel distribution industry and hotel industry, some Shanghai hotels cooperating with World Expo marketing were banned on Ctrip, intensifying the pricing power dispute between distribution channels and hotel suppliers. Similar situations have also occurred in the U.S., such as last year's controversy over Expedia and Choice Hotels renewing their agreement, reflecting the sharp contradictions between direct and distribution channels.

Where Did the Profits Go? — Channels Being Controlled

When communicating with many media outlets, I have expressed such a view: Major global hotel groups developed based on highly mature direct sales, leveraging distributors only when marginal benefits could no longer continue to expand channels and ensure maximum revenue. However, the general situation in China's hotel industry is that a large number of hotels transformed from official reception centers have no concept of channels, allowing distribution channels to rapidly develop and monopolize the market, eventually controlling hotels. If hotel professionals do not realize and improve this situation early enough, it will inevitably lead to continued profit loss.
