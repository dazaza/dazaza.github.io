The key spirit of a business

by nrkx3488 on 2011-07-13 17:52:49

An excellent creative strategy can nurture a brand and also allow it to easily stand out in competition. Procter & Gamble has set a great example for us.

Why is the battle of brands essentially a battle of creativity? Because anything related to a brand is invariably tied to creativity. Today, when a brand is born in the market, it doesn't live on the shelves of shopping malls; it lives in the hearts of consumers. When consumers see a brand on the shelf, if they don’t know it, feel indifferent to it, or dislike it, that brand will lack appeal. Therefore, the true key for a brand lies in living in the hearts of consumers. When entrepreneurs develop a product, they must find some unique characteristics within the product itself. Procter & Gamble believes that a product entering the market without distinct traits finds it difficult to break through and become a winner. This is a belief upheld globally by P&G. Thus, P&G products all have their own unique features. Don't think scientifically— even ordinary products, with the help of advertising creativity, can reach new heights and become strong brands. This is an inaccurate notion. Branding is about systematic operation, not just surviving three to five years but thriving for 50 or even 100 years. For manufacturers, from product to brand, everything is indeed related to creativity. Creativity should not be narrowly defined as advertising creativity but includes ideas about packaging, naming, promotions, color schemes, price setting, and more. How to introduce a distinctive product into the market and how to transform it into a distinctive brand are two separate tasks. Both aspects require significant effort to cultivate a successful brand. Don't assume that an ordinary product can succeed solely through advertising. The successful brands that have survived until now do not follow such examples. Do you think Coca-Cola tastes bad? McDonald's food unappetizing? BMW cars hard to drive? Or do you think Tide detergent or Safeguard soap is ineffective? Hence, the product itself and its power are two different things, but both need to be balanced.

Consumers recognize a brand whether through advertising, packaging, or promotions. In any medium, including outdoor billboards, what they see is the brand image. Packaging is the face of the brand. What does Coca-Cola look like? Red. What does McDonald's look like? Golden arches. What do Kodak and Fuji look like? What does Changhong look like? The first thing that comes to mind is the brand. So, a brand lives in the hearts of consumers, not on the shelves. Shelves are merely the stage for display. Packaging is crucial as it defines who you are before forming the brand. If the brand is already in the market, consider: First, does the product itself have good traits? Second, what impression does the product leave on consumers—is it in their hearts or on the shelves?

What exactly is the power of creativity? I believe it ignites a battle in the minds of consumers. This battle, regardless of whether it leads to "undying loyalty" or "changing allegiances," is significant. You currently favor Changhong, but I hope you fall in love with Sony. You currently prefer Sony, but I wish you would switch to Hitachi, Sharp, or Panasonic. You currently use Chongqing Ouni shampoo, but I hope you will switch to Head & Shoulders. This is "changing allegiances." Advertising must create a resonance when it interacts with consumers, sparking a storm in their minds. If consumers feel nothing after seeing the advertisement, it is a waste. All your capital and funds invested in sales would then be squandered. What is the power of creativity? We see "Nongfu Spring," a dark horse in the purified water market, successfully threatening Wahaha, Lebo, and Kangshifu waters with its superior positioning. Nongfu Spring tastes sweet, and its packaging differs from other purified waters. Summarized in one point, the power of many good creative ideas is thinking one step ahead or implementing something earlier than competitors, leaving them wondering why they didn’t think of it first. From this perspective, like when Lebo water entered the market using "27 layers of purification," finding an angle others hadn't considered created an opportunity for the brand to survive and occupy a niche in the market. Ultimately, from the global brand history perspective, good creativity actually creates brand legends. We talk about the "brand kingdom," which means systematic brand management. As Procter & Gamble believes, no brand has a so-called "maturity period." They believe brands continue to grow and thrive through systematic management. Every brand needs confidence and planning from the moment it enters the market to permanently occupy its niche, whether first or second place, and then manage systematically.

In fact, creativity isn't just about finding an agency or a creative mind. The highest guiding principle of creativity is strategy because strategy defines where the brand goes, how long it stays, or what other battlefield it switches to. Through direction guidance, achieving efficiency on a particular path is the power of creativity, which is why it is directly related to branding. Creativity is not narrowly defined as advertising creativity but encompasses all thoughts relevant to brand marketing. Creativity is absolutely not about seeking attention but precision. Therefore, the kind of creativity that sparks storms in the minds of consumers should be precise to have explosive power; otherwise, it cannot achieve good results in a short time. Precision stems from strategic guidance. A product entering the market, which segment to enter, what kind of brand to become, is still related to strategy. Strategy and creativity are inseparable entities. In the past, we thought creativity was creativity and strategy was strategy. In the future concept, there is no high wall between the two. They are two sides of the same coin, both equally important.

From an advertising perspective, creativity talks about effectiveness. BIG IDEA, grand creativity, discusses efficiency. Any script or proposal from an advertising company might be a good creative idea, but the key is its effectiveness. Advertising, brands, products—all marketing activities compete with time and opportunities. Lux soap was China's leading brand for 60 years, but why was it overtaken by Safeguard in recent years? It was due to timing. Safeguard saw consumer dissatisfaction with environmental pollution and concern for health, thus creating a brand legend with antibacterial soap, remaining the market leader to this day. Therefore, the development of a product and the launch of a brand are highly related to timing. A good idea is about creating an effective, successful brand with minimal money in the shortest time.

Procter & Gamble identifies three characteristics in evaluating an effective TV advertisement: 1. Recall. That is, after watching the ad for a while, consumers still remember the brand. 2. Trial potential. All advertisements and promotions aim to make consumers eager to try the product, changing their preferences. Currently drinking "Wahaha" or "Kangshifu," would watching the ad lead you to want to drink "Lebo"? 3. Persuasiveness and uniqueness. These are the three effects that P&G requires its agencies to achieve globally. P&G trusts research; if consumers score highly on these three points in research, P&G considers it an effective advertisement.

To ignite success with creativity, to generate purchasing power or uniqueness, besides the product and brand having traits or uniqueness, most importantly, when defining the advertising message, a key selling point must be identified. This key selling point aims to achieve the marketing goal of the current advertisement. Each advertising campaign must clearly define its goal. By identifying a key selling point and focusing solely on that, it is enough to influence consumers to try the product, known as focused messaging. For example, regarding Head & Shoulders, I believe saying "removes dandruff while maintaining beautiful hair" surpasses saying "cheap price," surpasses saying "smooth combing," surpasses saying "cleans thoroughly." Defining this key selling point achieves the focus of Head & Shoulders in capturing other brands. In fact, a key selling point is a strategy but must be simple. Many ads are too complex, stemming from client insecurity and unclear thinking by advertising agencies. If unsure, research must be conducted; research can reveal deficiencies and provide excellent reference indicators. Since markets change rapidly, you cannot predict the current state of consumers. If you cannot timely understand what consumers are thinking, how can you guide them and seize creative opportunities? Product development, brand research, and advertising research—every P&G product follows this process. The singing and dancing ad for Tide detergent succeeded after extensive research in various markets, achieving high scores on the three mentioned points, leading to its market success. Therefore, defining a key selling point is crucial for a brand to successfully lead the market.

Currently, many brands effectively use price wars, especially in second- and third-tier cities. However, don't forget that price is not the sole value of a brand. Procter & Gamble’s products, like detergents, shampoos, and soaps, are not cheap but remain market-leading brands. Don't think that price determines brand success; low pricing only provides the first opportunity to attract consumers. Low prices may be effective and fast, but branding is a marathon, not a sprint, and relying solely on price won't suffice. "Watsons" introduced lower-priced brands abroad but failed to capture the market. Therefore, the key is whether price offers quality. If the price exceeds the value consumers perceive, they will reject it. In reality, a brand ultimately represents value greater than price.

Brand personality determines brand value. Brand personality refers to the impression a brand leaves on consumers. Changhong and Sony have different personalities but each has its own loyal fan base, worth pondering. The difference in brand personalities is a critical factor in defining a brand's niche. BMW defines "driving pleasure"; Volvo defines "safety zones"; Mercedes-Benz defines "status and position," all defining differences to establish their respective niches.

Procter & Gamble is a successful global enterprise, adhering to four fundamentals: (1) Consumer-centric. Never underestimate consumers, assuming they are incapable. Too many companies ignore consumers when doing advertising and marketing, yet ultimately, consumers are the final arbiters of whether a brand succeeds. This is why P&G spends a large budget annually on research. They seek to understand and gain insights into consumers, knowing the differences between Japanese, Taipei, and local consumers. (2) Creating outstanding products. P&G believes that the market's success or failure depends on the quality of the product. Even a small difference in products is considered differentiation. Market differentiation determines market outcomes, which P&G firmly believes in. Of course, this is just a reference, not the sole standard or answer, but rather the belief of a successful company. (3) Creating unique brands. Each brand embodies a unique personality. "Head & Shoulders," "VS Sassoon," and "Rejoice" define completely different personalities. P&G believes every brand must have its uniqueness, just as advertising agencies must have unique charm to attract business owners. (4) Focusing on the future. P&G invests much time in researching future products, anticipating consumer needs. Developing new products tests the systematic operation of a company. These four points summarize P&G's culture, forming the foundation of global brands.

The real effect of a brand is whether it is perceived as intimate, technological, or caring. It must have a tangible impact, whether rational or emotional. For example, BMW can offer the tangible effect of accelerating dozens of kilometers per hour in one second. Product advantages determine market outcomes, and the brand itself must provide tangible benefits that consumers can touch and feel.

P&G's lifelong corporate goal is to create enduring brands. When a company begins its plan and strategy to create a brand, it should be about systematic operation. Many companies today are fleeting due to the lack of long-term plans and ideas for brand management.

The last point is that the key to a company managing a brand lies in communication—its partnership with advertising agencies. A company's main focus should be on product development. Globally, P&G spends 80% of its advertising budget on partners with over 35 years of collaboration. In a mature market, business owners should maintain close relationships with long-term partners. Domestically, companies tend to change agencies annually, rarely maintaining long-term partnerships. In fact, systematic brand management requires continuous collaboration with advertising partners or distributors to create consistent brand personalities. This is a successful case of P&G, explaining why "Saatchi & Saatchi" has maintained a 50-year partnership with P&G globally.

P&G respects its agencies, believing that good advertising partners can create brand assets and establish long-lasting relationships between brands and consumers. All brands competing in the market revolve around these two points. Domestically, we often talk about "pitching," but what pitching truly compares is not the creative work of the advertising agency but the feelings and confidence between the agency and the company. The works are just demonstrations of the team's abilities during a specific period. True friends are for life. Managing a brand requires a good partner to walk alongside, which is very important.

However, domestically, too many factors prevent business owners from establishing good, professional relationships with agencies, which is regrettable.
