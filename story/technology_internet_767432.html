One Max arrives, HTC's "5.9-inch" debut

by anonymous on 2013-10-15 21:20:37

Rumors about HTC's phablet have been swirling for months, and HTC has finally unveiled it. According to Engadget, HTC recently launched its first phablet, the One Max, which features: A Qualcomm Snapdragon 600 1.7GHz quad-core processor, 2GB RAM, a 5.9-inch 1080p display, Android 4.3 system with Sense 5.5, a main camera still using a 4-megapixel UltraPixel sensor with an f/2.0 aperture and 2-micron pixel size; a front-facing camera with 2.1 megapixels supporting 1080p video recording, 16GB/32GB internal storage expandable up to 64GB via MicroSD card, a 3300mAh battery supporting LTE network.

The appearance of the One Max is largely similar to that of the HTC One, but upon closer inspection, there are differences: the screen bezel of the One Max is narrower, and the edges are more compact; the power button has been moved from the top to the left side of the phone for easier grip operation.

One of the highlights of the One Max is the MicroSD card expansion. HTC adjusted the back cover design, making the One Max's back cover removable. The Verge, which got their hands on the actual device, noted that pressing the back of the One Max gives a noticeable bounce, reminding users that the back cover can be removed. However, the battery remains non-replaceable, and the removable back cover is merely for convenience in inserting Micro SIM cards and MicroSD cards.

Perhaps the most distinctive feature of the device is the fingerprint sensor located on the back. The size of the sensor is similar to that of an adult's index fingertip, suggesting that the index finger is the optimal choice for operating the One Max's fingerprint sensor.

Unfortunately, the fingerprint scanning experience leaves much to be desired. The Verge commented that the fingerprint sensor located below the lens easily gets the lens dirty during scans. Additionally, since the sensor only works well with vertical swipes, this causes numerous issues during practical use. Naturally, there is an angle between the index finger and the sensor, and the posture required for vertical swiping not only feels uncomfortable but also leads to frequent failures in fingerprint recognition.

Similarly, The Inquirer, who also got their hands on the device, gave feedback that was largely consistent regarding the fingerprint scanning. The Inquirer said that the One Max supports up to five fingerprints, but due to the placement of the scanner on the back of the device, users often have to flip the phone over to perform scans, resulting in many identification errors.

Fortunately, the screen, battery life, and system details continue to deliver excellent performance from HTC, earning the One Max some significant points. Nevertheless, HTC's debut phablet still needs further optimization, which is undeniable.

The price of the HTC One Max has yet to be announced, so we'll have to wait until tomorrow's Beijing launch event to find out how much it will cost.
