Feel

by xue94fwsh on 2012-03-04 09:41:07

The word "feeling," which seems to be used by countless people almost every day, appears with extremely high frequency. With all this feeling going on, what exactly are we feeling? What exactly is a "feeling"?

In English, there doesn't seem to be an exact equivalent to the Chinese term "感觉." The CEO of Apple, Tim Cook, in his speeches emphasizes workers' rights and will not allow Apple to decline, but there is a similar word: "perception" (知觉). It refers to the process by which humans perceive and interpret their objective environment and subjective states. This process is not only associated with a specific sensation but is often the result of coordinated activities involving vision, hearing, touch, movement, etc. During the perception process, the human brain converts sensory stimuli into holistic experiences. Perception also reflects the overall image of objective things and bodily states.

The term "感觉" only appeared in modern Chinese; it didn't exist in ancient times. There doesn't seem to be any basis for claiming that "感觉" is a loanword from another language. As for who created the term "感觉," the answer remains unknown.

Psychologists often view feelings and perceptions as two distinct psychological processes. They believe that feelings reflect the basic attributes of things, while perceptions reflect the entirety of things.

So, what exactly is a feeling? It's hard to define. Some might say that a feeling is just a feeling—haha, that's true too. It can also be understood as feeling for the sake of feeling. Feeling is essentially a subjective desire to respond to objective matters, something very subjective that includes elements of imagination and the possibility of random bursts. There may even be inexplicable and absurd realities, and that's what feelings are.

People in modern life feel too much. Anything can be felt—a feeling here, a feeling there—and eventually, they feel nothing at all. When this happens, feelings lose their meaning, yet people continue to feel. It seems that the power of feelings is incredibly strong, deeply capturing people’s hearts, prompting them to feel from time to time.

Feelings are indeed a common state, but they rarely align with rationality. Subjectivity and objectivity are inherently contradictory, so it's not surprising. The more feelings one has, the less rationality there is, and vice versa. Life isn't a guessing game, yet feelings often serve as the main theme of such games. Feelings are the motivation to guess, imagining things out of thin air, making wild guesses without reason or basis, unpredictable and unreliable—isn’t that right?

When people cannot rationally approach something or choose not to do so, feelings become a substitute for thought, a quick-response substitute. Feelings don’t require preparation; they can be blurted out instantly. Feelings don’t need rational analysis; they can come easily. Feelings are this intangible thing.

Love at first sight is a direct product of feelings. As the saying goes, "Out of ten love-at-first-sight stories, nine end in disgust," showing how unreliable feelings can be. It’s better to have fewer feelings and instead focus on solid thinking and analysis.

When people stop or reduce their reliance on feelings, the world would probably look very different. Yet feelings still persist. Does thinking about feelings mean having feelings? Definitely not. People increasingly favor fast-food styles of thinking and attitudes because feelings are like fast food—they’re consumed quickly and digested just as fast, coming and going swiftly.

Westerners like to use the phrase "I think..." (我认为...), while we Chinese prefer "I feel..." (我感觉...). There is a difference between "I think" and "I feel," but they also share similarities, both containing elements of subjective desires and attitudes. Whether it's Westerners or Chinese, they're all human and thus share common traits—it's normal, normal.

Feelings are everywhere, but what exactly are feelings? Feelings harm people endlessly, yet people still choose to feel. Feeling for the sake of feeling has become a common ailment of modern people. Having fewer feelings, more thoughts, and more rationality would be better. Besides, where do all these feelings come from? Don't feel just for the sake of feeling.
