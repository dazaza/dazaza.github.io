No matter when or where, you should try to find a way to express praise, acknowledgment, and encouragement for the person. Acknowledging others' efforts can strengthen the friendly and positive energy within a team; at the same time, it makes you look like a member of the team family, a good leader, and gold that shines wherever it is placed. Our entrepreneurs and managers can ask themselves:

Which form of encouragement from others motivates me?

When I praise someone, have I ever left them in awe?

When was the last time I praised someone at work?

Have I ever noticed someone needed encouragement? If so, did I give it to them?

Is there anyone who might think I only care about encouraging others but ignore them or let them feel low?

Management scholars Whitman and Peters discovered through their research on 60 of the longest-standing and most successful companies in U.S. history that the secret to their enduring success lies in "treating employees as key assets" in their operations. During the process of business management, they consistently create environments more conducive to attracting and retaining talent, striving to meet employees' needs for respect and self-actualization.

William James, a professor at Harvard University, pointed out in his book "Behavioral Management" that through his research on employee motivation, he found that workers paid by piecework only utilize 20%~30% of their abilities; however, when fully motivated, they can unleash 80%~90% of their potential. In other words, the same person can perform three to four times better after being fully motivated compared to before.

Surveys show that the rising employee turnover rate has become the biggest issue troubling corporate growth, forcing many companies to spend significant time and money on rehiring employees. The losses caused by losing talent go far beyond higher recruitment and retraining costs; it even becomes one of the reasons for corporate failure. Although many managers realize there's a problem with the company's incentive mechanism, they attempt to lower the turnover rate by improving benefits, but the results are still limited. How to effectively motivate employees so that they happily work for the company has become a common challenge troubling the entire management field.

In fact, motivating others is quite easy. A simple pat on the back, showing your friendliness, will not cost you anything while benefiting the recipient immensely. Recent study findings indicate that the motivational effects of praising others are comparable to those achieved with monetary incentives. Therefore, our leadership must seriously learn the skills and methods of motivation. Some say the Japanese are "bees who only know how to work," "working like crazy in rabbit holes," and are "addicted to work." This high level of work enthusiasm is closely related to the incentive mechanisms in Japanese companies. Matsushita Konosuke summarized 21 tricks for motivating employees based on his lifetime of management experience: ① Let each individual understand their position and regularly discuss their job performance with them; ② Reward them according to their achievements; ③ Inform employees immediately if there are any changes; ④ Involve employees in decisions and plans related to them personally; ⑤ Trust employees and win their loyalty and trust; ⑥ Connect with employees directly, understanding their interests, habits, and sensitive issues; ⑦ Listen to subordinates' suggestions; ⑧ If someone behaves strangely, investigate the cause clearly; ⑨ Communicate your ideas to everyone as gently as possible; ⑩ Explain the "why" behind actions so employees can perform better; Admit mistakes promptly and apologize; Inform employees of the importance of their roles to give them security; Criticize with reason and find ways to improve; Before criticizing someone, point out their strengths and show that you want to help them; Lead by example; Avoid inconsistency; Seize every opportunity to express pride in your employees, enabling them to reach their full potential; If someone complains, quickly find the reason for their dissatisfaction; Do everything possible to soothe dissatisfied emotions, otherwise, everyone could be affected; Set short-term and long-term goals so people can measure their progress; Support your employees, ensuring their rights and responsibilities are properly balanced.

If used out of sincere, heartfelt care, it's called "skill." However, in some specific management practices, certain motivational methods often prove ineffective or even counterproductive. How can we effectively motivate employees?

1. Assign work content that matches the employee's personality

Each person has their own personality traits, categorized as sanguine, choleric, phlegmatic, melancholic, etc. Simply put, some people are calm, while others are lively; some believe they can control situations, while others think their success mainly depends on external influences; some enjoy high-risk challenging work, while others avoid risks.

Employees have different personalities, and their jobs should vary accordingly. Work that matches the individual can make employees feel energetic and comfortable. For instance, traditional employees who prefer stable, procedural work are suitable for accounting or cashier positions in travel companies, whereas confident and ambitious employees are better suited for roles such as sales managers or liaison managers in travel agencies. In short: assign tasks based on individual talents!

2. Set specific and appropriate goals for each employee

Setting clear work goals for employees generally leads to higher performance.

Goals create pressure, which motivates employees to work harder. When employees achieve阶段性goals, managers should provide feedback to them. Feedback allows employees to understand whether their effort is sufficient and whether they need to work harder, thus helping them further improve their goals after achieving stage goals.

The goals set by managers should be clear. For example, "This month's sales revenue should increase compared to last month" is less motivating than "This month's sales revenue should increase by 10% compared to last month." At the same time, goals should be challenging but achievable. Research shows that regardless of whether goals are objectively achievable, if employees subjectively believe they are unattainable, their effort levels will decrease. Goal setting should be like apples on a tree—impossible to pick from the ground, but reachable with a jump.

3. Reward employees who achieve predetermined goals

Dolphins in the circus get their favorite food every time they complete an action. This is the trick of animal trainers. People are the same—if employees are rewarded for achieving a goal, they will work harder to repeat such behavior in the future. This approach is called behavioral reinforcement. For an employee who has been habitually late by more than 30 minutes, if this time he is only 20 minutes late, the manager should praise him to reinforce his improvement. Some may wonder why someone who is still late by 20 minutes deserves praise? It's simple—he has improved.

For example, a child scores 8 points on a test. Upon returning home, Chinese parents and foreign parents may react differently. Chinese parents might say, "Dear, you're amazing! You actually scored 8 points, Mom is so proud of you!" Then they hug and kiss the child. Many might think foreign parents have shortcomings. In reality, if the child previously scored 6 points and now scores 8 points, improvement deserves praise. If Chinese parents knew the child scored 8 points, they might be shocked and criticize the child, "How can you be so stupid! What a disappointment...!"

Managers should increase the transparency of rewards. For example, publicly announcing the rewarded employees will have a motivational effect among staff. Positive reinforcement represented by rewards is far more effective than negative reinforcement represented by punishment.

4. Provide different rewards for different employees

Human needs include physiological needs, safety needs, social needs, esteem needs, and self-actualization needs across various levels. After one need is satisfied, employees shift to other needs. Since each employee's needs differ, a reward method effective for one person may not work for another.

Managers should provide personalized rewards tailored to employee differences. For example, some employees may desire higher wages, while others may not care about salary but value free vacation time. Also, for high-income employees, increasing their salary may be less attractive than granting them the title of "Outstanding Employee Star," as it makes them feel valued and respected.

5. The reward system must be fair

Employees do not work in a vacuum; they constantly compare themselves to others. If you graduate from university and are offered a job with a monthly salary of 1500 yuan, you might feel satisfied and work hard for the organization. However, if after a month or two, you discover that a classmate who graduated at the same time and has similar age and education earns 2500 yuan per month, how would you feel? You might feel disappointed and no longer work as hard as before. Although 1500 yuan is relatively decent for a university graduate, that's not the issue. The key issue is that you feel it's unfair.

Therefore, managers should design compensation systems that fairly evaluate employees' experience, ability, and effort.

Only a fair reward system can ignite employees' work enthusiasm.

6. Implement flexible management

To establish a people-oriented management philosophy, managers should fully respect employees' labor, protect their rights, and implement emotionally-driven management. Flexible management is a practical form of "people-centered" management, relying on human liberation, equal rights, and democratic management. Through methods such as motivation, inspiration, and guidance, it stimulates employees' intrinsic potential and work initiative from the depths of their hearts, making them work wholeheartedly and joyfully for the company.

Tan Xiaofang believes that since sales and business department personnel have strong autonomy and work separately, it is difficult for management to monitor the entire work process and identify problems. Therefore, adopting flexible management methods can allow employees to voluntarily accomplish what they should do.

7. Build an excellent corporate culture

Building an excellent corporate culture is a fundamental guarantee for adapting to competition and maintaining long-term success. Therefore, companies should enhance corporate culture construction by creating a favorable corporate cultural atmosphere and shaping a positive corporate image, allowing employees to establish shared values and behavioral norms. This creates strong cohesion and centripetal force within the company, enhancing employees' sense of belonging and honor.

Through my long-term work in employee motivation and corporate training, I've found that the most challenging problem for many trainers is not that employees cannot be motivated, but that the motivation lasts too short. Of course, humans are not machines, and we cannot expect employees to run indefinitely after being wound up once like a "perpetual motion machine." The key to maintaining continuous motivation does not lie in constantly pressuring or cheering employees, but in breaking down the company's vision.
