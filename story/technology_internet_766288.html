NY Times: Next Generation iPhone Will Decide Apple's Future Success or Failure

by anonymous on 2013-09-11 13:26:01

Introduction: The New York Times website today published an article titled "Apple's Next Product Could Be a Make-or-Break Moment" (Disruptions: Apple's Next Unveiling Could Make or Break a Business). It states that smartphones like the iPhone have well explained the term 'creative destruction.' The upcoming release of the new iPhone this week will attract significant attention, but so far, there doesn't seem to be any major technological leap. This may determine the future success or failure of Apple.

The following is the full text of the article:

On Tuesday morning, local time in the United States, about 200 people from the media will gather in Cupertino, California, for a presentation by Apple CEO Tim Cook regarding the new iPhone.

According to previous reports, Apple will launch a long-awaited, more affordable new iPhone this week to attract consumers in emerging markets. This phone will come in a variety of bright colors such as yellow, blue, and even pink, better meeting the needs of younger users. On the other hand, it is expected that Apple will also introduce a gold version of a high-end smartphone aimed at customers with stronger purchasing power.

There is no doubt that media coverage of this event by Apple will be extensive. Three groups of people will be paying close attention: Apple fans, entrepreneurs hoping to take full advantage of the new features of the iPhone, and executives of competing companies who may be affected.

The annual iPhone launch has become the "Day of Creative Destruction" in Silicon Valley. Do you remember all those companies developing small digital camcorders? After Apple added video recording capabilities to the iPhone in 2008, very few of these companies still survive. Through features and services gradually introduced by the iPhone, Apple has impacted many companies, including other smartphone manufacturers like Nokia and BlackBerry, as well as gaming console companies like Nintendo.

Economist Joseph Schumpeter coined the term "creative destruction" in 1942 to describe an imperfect way of free market development: the telephone replaced the telegraph, mobile phones replaced landlines, and smartphones are replacing feature phones. Some things are destroyed, and innovation is born on that basis.

In recent years, perhaps no other product has caused as much destruction and creation as the iPhone. The iPhone offers a wide range of functions. Users can download millions of apps to utilize these functions.

Need a calendar, phonebook, or notebook? The iPhone can do all that; forget about paper-based tools. Need to take high-resolution photos, record an interview, or play games? All you need is a phone; you don't need a separate camera or camcorder.

Columbia Business School professor Rita Gunther McGrath, who focuses on innovation in large enterprises, said: "Once Apple creates a storm worldwide and clearly affects competitors, responding becomes too late."

So, what changes will the iPhone and other smartphones bring in the future? Beyond breaking through technology boundaries, think about how smartphones will target consumers' wallets. You can ask consumers if they want a cool car or a cool new smartphone. In most cases, they'll choose the latter, and car manufacturers should learn from this.

Market research company Gartner's automotive industry analyst Thilo Koslowski said: "The iPhone is today's Ford Mustang. These products offer the freedom and social reach that only cars could provide before."

From Nokia's and BlackBerry's perspective, the iPhone is a destroyer, but others see it as a creator. The App Store for the iPhone has driven the growth of tens of thousands of small companies. And some of these companies, like Rovio, the developer of Angry Birds located not far from Nokia's headquarters, have grown significantly.

Harvard Kennedy School internet and policy expert Nicco Mele said: "Competition has become very cheap, which has already affected individuals, such as app developers." Resources that were once necessary for success are now minimal. He pointed out: "The rise of app developers means anyone can compete with big companies from home."

Of course, there's also a terrifying side. Due to the development of the internet, inexpensive smartphone apps, and reduced corporate costs, destruction and innovation in the tech industry are increasing. For example, despite being founded just a few years ago, Facebook has grown from a startup to one of the most powerful companies in Silicon Valley. Meanwhile, Facebook closely monitors future new threats. When unable to defeat competitors, Facebook acquires them, such as the popular photo-sharing service Instagram.

Under the wave of smartphone development, who is safe? Apparently, no company is, and neither is Apple.

Apple is no longer the king of the smartphone market. When the new iPhone is released on Tuesday, it will undoubtedly attract massive attention, and competitors will be eager to imitate its latest features. However, currently, Samsung sells the most smartphones, and emerging companies like Huawei and ZTE are also challenging. Based on current information, the new iPhone won't have any major technological breakthroughs.

This is the concept of "creative destruction": you never know exactly when you'll stop creating and ultimately be destroyed.
