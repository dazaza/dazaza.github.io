Hong Kong Company FAQs

Q: I would like to set up a limited company in Hong Kong, what conditions are required?

A: At least one shareholder aged 18 or above is needed. Mainland citizens or overseas individuals with passports or ID cards are eligible. A registered address and statutory secretary for the limited company (usually provided by us) are also required.

Q: How do I go about setting up a Hong Kong company?

A: You just need to fill out a power of attorney form. The three main aspects covered in this form are:

1. Determining the Chinese and English names of the company;

2. Determining the amount of registered capital. The minimum registered capital is HKD 10,000 (usually divided into 10,000 shares), which does not need to be fully paid but can be increased according to actual needs.

3. Submitting the data and ID copy of one shareholder. If there are more than two shareholders, not all shareholders need to become directors, but at least one director is required. After we receive the three aspects of information you provide, we will prepare complete legal documents for your signature the next day. After signing the documents, it usually takes about 10 working days to complete the process.

Q: How long does it take to set up a new company? How long does it take to buy an existing shell company? Can I purchase an existing company and then change its name? How long does it take to change the name?

A: Setting up a new Hong Kong company takes 10 working days from the date all legal documents are signed.

Purchasing an existing company takes 1-2 working days, expedited service can be completed on the same day.

Setting up a branch office takes 3 working days.

After purchasing the company, you can change the company's name, which requires 15 working days.

Q: What are the differences between shareholders and directors?

A: Shareholders are investors in a joint-stock company. Directors are elected by shareholders and are responsible for managing the company; the chairman of the board is the head of the board of directors. Of course, more often than not, shareholders are also directors.

Q: What are the differences between choosing a company name in Hong Kong compared to mainland China?

A: Naming a company in Hong Kong is relatively free. Some companies that are difficult to register domestically can be registered in Hong Kong as long as they are not duplicated. Regardless of the size of the registered capital, the Hong Kong government allows company names to contain words such as international, group, holding, industry, investment, college, association, center, foundation, research institute, etc. The company name can also have a preferred location added in front, such as France, United States, Italy, Japan, Hong Kong, Shanghai, etc., or use your own name and hometown name before the company name, fully reflecting personalization.

Q: Are there any restrictions on the business scope of a Hong Kong limited company?

A: Hong Kong is one of the freest trading ports in the world, and there are no significant restrictions on the business scope in principle. Companies can engage in any type of business, such as finance and investment, healthcare, shipping, import and export trade, real estate, construction, decoration, information networks, clothing textiles, tourism, cultural publishing, etc. Many small and medium-sized enterprise owners do not clearly define their business scope so that their operations are not restricted to a certain range.

Q: Where does the registered capital of a Hong Kong company appear? Is it necessary to fully pay the registered capital?

A: The minimum registered capital for a Hong Kong limited liability company is HKD 10,000, which does not need to be fully paid (no verification of capital). The registered capital only appears in the company's articles of association. If customers want to prove their registered capital, presenting the articles of association is sufficient proof. Alternatively, our accountant can issue a credit certificate (CERTIFIED TRUE COPIES).

Q: What services are included in setting up a Hong Kong limited company?

A: Establishing a Hong Kong limited company includes the following services:

1. Government certificate fees (company registration certificate, business registration certificate)

2. Printed materials (18 copies of the articles of association, 1 share register, 1 shareholder minutes book, 1 set of application documents)

3. Stamp fees (1 signature stamp, 1 steel seal, 1 amendment stamp)

4. One year of registered address fees

5. One year of statutory secretary service fees for the limited company

6. Miscellaneous fees (document fees, courier fees, travel expenses)

7. Professional accounting service fees

Q: After confirming the establishment of an offshore company, how should payment be made?

A: Payment method: You can pay in cash, transfer, telegraphic transfer, check, or draft to our designated Hong Kong or mainland account. Cash payments can be made at locations such as Hong Kong, LA, KUALA LUMPUR, Shenzhen, Guangzhou, Chengdu, Yiwu, etc. Overseas individuals can remit funds to the Hong Kong account; mainland China individuals can remit funds to the Shenzhen account. Account details are available upon request.

Payment method: After filling out the power of attorney, clients must prepay 70% of the fees, with the remaining 30% paid upon completion of the company setup. The full amount of the stamp duty on the registered capital (1/1000 of the registered capital) must be prepaid. The payment method for agents and clients is the same.

Q: After establishing a Hong Kong company, what annual fees are required?

A: There are two types of annual fees: annual review and financial reporting, and accounting and auditing.

1. The fees for tax bureau annual review and company registry annual report must be paid annually. Prices may adjust according to government policies, typically ranging between HKD 3,000 - 5,000. Even if the company has no business activities, these fees must still be paid.

2. Regarding accounting and auditing, if the company starts operations and conducts business, it must undergo accounting and auditing. If the company has no business activity, it does not need to conduct accounting and auditing. However, tax filing is mandatory.

Q: After establishing a Hong Kong company, who is responsible for the annual accounting and auditing? If you handle the accounting for us, what documents are required? What are the specific charges? How long does it take?

A: Accounting can be done by the company itself or entrusted to a Hong Kong accounting firm. However, auditing must be entrusted to an accountant or auditor. If we handle it for you, you must submit all records of business transactions: ledgers, invoices, receipts, payment vouchers, bank statements, accounts receivable, accounts payable. Generally speaking, it takes about one month to organize the accounts, and 1-2 months for auditing.

Q: What are the differences between the statutory secretary services and business secretary services you provide?

A: According to government regulations, a limited company must have a statutory secretary, who can be either a natural person or a legal entity based in Hong Kong. Generally, we can arrange a statutory secretary for you. A statutory secretary is a formal secretary required by the Hong Kong government for convenience in contacting and handling government-related documents.

A business secretary provides customized business practical services based on client needs, from answering calls, transmitting information, receiving and sending mail, to letters of credit, logistics services, and more. The advantage of a business secretary is that it helps build your corporate image, equivalent to having your own employees and office in Hong Kong, along with a set of company resources. This makes conducting business smooth and efficient without missing any opportunities.

Q: Our company does not have an office or employees in Hong Kong. After establishing a Hong Kong company, how do we manage and operate it?

A: Our business secretary service is specifically designed for this purpose. For more details, please refer to the business secretary service details.

Q: How is taxation handled for Hong Kong enterprises?

A: Hong Kong is a free port with few taxes and low rates. The only tax a company must pay annually is profits tax, which is calculated based on the company's income, i.e., 17.5% of net profit. For example, if a company's annual turnover is HKD 10 million, gross profit HKD 2 million, and net profit HKD 1 million, the profits tax payable to the government is HKD 1 million * 17.5% = HKD 175,000. If the company does not make a profit, it does not need to pay taxes.

Q: Are goods entering and leaving Hong Kong taxed?

A: Except for tobacco and alcohol, general goods entering and leaving Hong Kong are not taxed. However, all imported and exported goods must be declared through customs. Customs fees are charged at 0.5% of the actual value of the goods (up to HKD 47,000) and 0.3% (over HKD 47,000).

Q: Are there any taxes to be paid when setting up a company?

A: Yes. When setting up a Hong Kong company, the stamp duty on registered capital must be paid. The stamp duty is calculated at 1/1000 of the registered capital. For example, if the registered capital is HKD 1 million, the tax payable is HKD 1,000, which is paid once to the government during registration and does not need to be paid again.

Q: Which department issues invoices and receipts for Hong Kong companies?

A: Invoices and receipts for Hong Kong companies are not uniformly printed by the tax bureau but are printed and issued by the company's board of directors based on the nature of the company's business. Invoices, receipts, and other documents become effective after being stamped with the company seal and signed by the responsible person.

Q: I am setting up a Hong Kong company to apply for a foreign-invested joint venture or wholly-owned enterprise in mainland China. Can all the shareholders of the Hong Kong company be residents of mainland China?

A: Generally, a Hong Kong company can consist entirely of mainland shareholders. However, if you wish to establish a foreign-invested joint venture or wholly-owned enterprise, the Ministry of Commerce usually invites at least one overseas shareholder to sign the documents. From this perspective, arranging for an overseas shareholder to join the company is necessary.

Q: Can our newly established Hong Kong company directly apply for a foreign-invested joint venture or wholly-owned enterprise in mainland China?

A: Generally, foreign investors coming to China for investment must have been established for more than one year. Specific situations depend on local policies.

Q: If I register a company in Hong Kong, can I set up the company account in a foreign bank within the country, such as a Hong Kong bank's branch in Shenzhen?

A: Yes. After your company is established, the company directors can open an offshore account with a domestic foreign bank using their mainland ID card. However, a prerequisite is that the directors must personally visit the bank to open the account.

Q: How are various daily tasks such as tax reporting for Hong Kong companies resolved? Can you handle it for us? What are the annual fees?

A: In terms of Hong Kong taxation, tax is reported once a year. The fee structure depends on your company's actual situation. If you choose our business secretary service, tax reporting is included without additional charges. However, accounting and auditing require payment, and the fees are determined by the company's turnover and the size of the bills. If the company has no business activity, there are no related fees.

Q: How is the tax rate for Hong Kong companies calculated? How is the specific tax reporting time defined?

A: Hong Kong's tax rate is calculated at 17.5% of actual profits. Hong Kong companies typically use March 31st each year as the fiscal year-end date, and you can defer tax reporting for one year.

Q: What does the "business nature" column marked as CORP and the "legal status" column marked as BODY CORPORATE mean on the business registration certificate?

A: In the "business nature" column, marking CORP means there is no limitation on a particular business; any business compliant with the Companies Ordinance can be operated. Of course, in this column, you can also specify electronics, chemicals, or trade as the business scope. As for BODY CORPORATE, it refers to a corporate entity, representing legal status.

Q: If I need to rent an office, how should I proceed?

A: Based on different client requirements, we offer company custody, desk rental, single office rental, and entire building rental. Just let us know your needs, and we will immediately find suitable office space for you.

Q: Can I query the background information of a Hong Kong limited company online?

A: Currently, this is not possible. Anyone with questions about a company can visit the Hong Kong Companies Registry in Hong Kong's Golden Bell Road for inquiries.

Q: After establishing a publishing house, how can I legally publish books?

A: After establishing a publishing house, you have obtained the qualification to handle publishing matters. However, there are two tasks you still need to complete: apply for an International Standard Book Number (ISBN) or International Standard Serial Number (ISSN). Secondly, you must apply for publication registration before publishing. After completing these two tasks, you can legally publish books. If you wish to distribute them in mainland China, you can apply to relevant authorities as a Hong Kong publisher.

Q: What do ISBN and ISSN mean? What are the differences between ISBN and ISSN?

A: ISBN stands for International Standard Book Number. ISSN stands for International Standard Serial Number. Having these two numbers indicates that our publications have reached international standards and have been registered with the ISBN and ISSN international institutions. These numbers allow you to query the background information of the publication at any time. The fundamental difference between ISBN and ISSN lies in the objects they are issued to. The former is issued to book publishers; the latter is issued to magazine publishers.

Q: If business is poor, how much must I pay annually to apply for the company to suspend operations?

A: A Hong Kong company can apply for temporary suspension of operations at any time. Besides paying the annual report fees to the Companies Registry and the annual review fees to the Tax Bureau, no other fees are required. If market conditions improve, the company can resume operations at any time, saving many costs.

Q: Can my Hong Kong company be dissolved?

A: Yes. If your company is no longer in use and you do not cancel it, the Hong Kong government will assume that your company continues to exist and engages in illegal operations. The government will impose cumulative fines on companies operating illegally according to Hong Kong law, and serious cases may result in imprisonment for the board of directors. Since dissolving a company also incurs fees, we suggest the best way is to sell the company.
