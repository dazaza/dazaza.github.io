"Artist" Beats "Hugo" to Win 5 Oscars

by dahslkh6 on 2012-02-27 22:22:39

The 84th Academy Awards ceremony came to an end amidst cheers from the French audience. Recalling film history has inexplicably become this year's theme, with both "The Artist" and "Hugo," which pay tribute to the silent film era, receiving the most nominations and a similar number of awards. However, the latter only swept the technical awards related to sound and visuals, while the Best Actor, Best Director, and Best Picture awards were given to non-American films for the first time. The results of the awards lacked originality and failed to stimulate or encourage any color. The Oscar ceremony followed a familiar pattern but had a particularly prominent theme: paying tribute to films, again and again. Whether it was through old movie medleys, allowing filmmakers to individually recount how movies have impacted their lives, or summarizing the work of nominees in various categories—this was a format never seen before. At 84 years old, the Oscars may be old enough to reminisce about the past, but it is not typically a year for summaries. This was clearly inspired by several well-received and commercially successful nominated works. One of them is Michel Hazanavicius' "The Artist" from France, which made its debut at last year's Cannes Film Festival and won over the media with its faithful imitation of silent film production methods, reviving the early beauty of Hollywood movie myths. Although the story is a bit clichéd, it is cheerful and likable. However, it only won Best Actor at the time until it entered the American market and started raking in awards effortlessly. In France, it also beat out the box office champion "Intouchables" and "Les Chevaliers du ciel," winning César Awards for Best Actress, Best Director, Best Cinematography, Best Original Screenplay, Best Score, among others.

There has never been a foreign film in Oscar history that achieved what "The Artist" did, simply because it does not represent a value system that Americans cannot understand, but rather their most influential cultural domain now often mocked by Europeans for lacking culture: movies, specifically Hollywood movies. Regardless of whether Michel Hazanavicius had any speculative motives in making it, the sentiment resonates emotionally, and as for depth and technical height, they can be obscured. Therefore, Terrence Malick's grand yet obscure "The Tree of Life" had no advantage at domestic award ceremonies.

Coincidentally, Martin Scorsese also released "Hugo" last year, paying tribute to the silent film era. It was also his first attempt at full 3D shooting. If both "Hugo" and "The Artist" are flattering old movies, "Hugo" appears more culturally profound. Adapted from a children's book, this movie uses the search for film master Georges Méliès as a clue, taking the audience back to the dawn of cinema. Interestingly, the story of "Hugo" takes place in the birthplace of cinema: France, transforming Paris into a fairy-tale-like place.

Scorsese has long been dedicated to film restoration work, and his preference and understanding for those lost old movies are fully reflected in "Hugo." By combining the latest 3D technology with the cross-era significance of cinema in human cultural history, it shows clever thinking. However, the academy ultimately only acknowledged its performance in technical categories. Interestingly, even in the association's annual awards, "Hugo" didn't even receive a nomination.

Respect for veterans also extends to the acting awards. Meryl Streep winning Best Actress again was not surprising. She was the only flawless selling point in "The Iron Lady," delivering textbook-level performances. To criticize her would be due to the fact that she is the nominee with the most awards, especially compared to Glenn Close, whose qualifications and level are comparable but whose luck in winning awards is quite poor (she has only been nominated twice for the Oscars so far). But looking back at Meryl Streep's seventeen nominations and three wins, it's no wonder she received such high support.

Christopher Plummer, a veteran actor, won Best Supporting Actor. Having acted for nearly fifty years, he became internationally famous through "The Sound of Music," active on both stage and screen, but only received two Oscar nominations. No wonder he muttered on stage holding the golden statue: "Where have you been all these years?" This year's Best Supporting Actor list was also filled with senior actors. Besides Jonah Hill from "Moneyball," Max von Sydow from "Extremely Loud & Incredibly Close," and Nick Nolte from "Warrior" are all legendary actors.

Jean Dujardin won Best Actor for the first time at the Oscars with "The Artist," which could be said to be thanks to the emotional points of the film itself. He has always been popular in France for performing absurd comedies and did not win at the César Awards, but he gained favor with the Americans; George Clooney, Gary Oldman, and even Brad Pitt, who put in real effort to make their dramas look effortless, regardless of their qualifications or popularity, each had their own chances, but ended up losing to this French comedian.

Among the major awards for feature films, perhaps the least controversial result was the Best Foreign Language Film. The Iranian film "A Separation" won the Golden Bear for Best Film at last year's Berlin Film Festival and the Silver Bears for Best Actor and Actress, analyzing a country's complex political and cultural environment through the least political family disputes. It also competed for Best Original Screenplay but lost to Woody Allen's comedy "Midnight in Paris," which also happens to be a tribute to the old literary era.
