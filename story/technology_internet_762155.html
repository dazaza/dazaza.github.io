"The Inevitable 'Death of Yougou': Belle's E-commerce Has Many Fatal Flaws"

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 14:00:30

Recently, Yougou.com, which has been suffering from a severe loss of executives, received another blow. On July 17th, it was confirmed by an insider from Yougou that its COO, Zhang Xiaojun, is leaving and currently processing his resignation. As one of the founding members of Yougou, Zhang Xiaojun's departure holds significant implications, indicating that Yougou is indeed facing serious crises.

Let’s take a look back at the e-commerce journey of Belle International, the parent company of Yougou:

Belle had been quietly testing the waters of e-commerce for several years, developing steadily through Taobao distribution, Tmall, and B2C distribution (B2C distribution was completed via Taoxiu.com). Led by a senior group figure, Xie Yunli, a relatively modest e-commerce team started by selling products under Belle's own brands before gradually launching exclusive online versions and new network brands. This development path became a successful case study of a traditional brand's entry into e-commerce, considered more solid than Li-Ning's approach prior to 2011.

With the rapid rise of Letao.com and HaoLeMai in 2010, after receiving over $100 million in financing, Belle Group, which supplied these platforms, began to feel uneasy. There were concerns about being restricted by distribution channels again, but also evaluations revealed significant advantages in the e-commerce domain. These advantages included:

1. A multitude of brands: In addition to Belle's own women's shoe brands, international sports brands like Adidas and Nike saw astonishing growth in online sales.

2. Easier category expansion: For Belle Group, known for its women's shoes and agency of well-known sports brands, achieving absolute market dominance through multi-brand and multi-category strategies became a top priority. Expanding offline stores was too slow, while the ongoing e-commerce business could easily expand brands but struggled to quickly increase categories.

3. Financial advantage: During collaborations with e-commerce companies, Belle realized that e-commerce is capital-intensive. With financial strength, Belle could develop independently without relying on external capital, thus defeating many vertical e-commerce platforms dependent on funding, becoming the "last man standing."

4. Talent advantage: After several years of e-commerce practice, Belle possessed a highly skilled and professional e-commerce team. The talent cultivation could support the scale development of e-commerce or even higher.

5. Supply chain advantage: This excited Belle internally and was the core reason for attracting high-level executives from brand e-commerce or platform e-commerce. After all, supply chains are always fascinating in e-commerce as they seem to decide life and death beyond just capital. The supply chain includes product research and development, production, warehouse management, delivery, after-sales service, etc., considered superior to pure e-commerce enterprises in traditionally commerce-heavy companies.

Based on these advantages, Belle's e-commerce decided to make a big move, first purchasing the double-barrelled domain name yougou.com,

abandoning the slightly imitation "topshoes.cn" Taixiu website for redirection, stopping supplies to all distribution websites, while retaining direct third-party shops on Taobao and Tmall but reducing investment and focus. High salaries were offered to attract JD.com VPs and Vancl VPs to serve as CMO and COO, respectively, along with a senior member of the group appointed as CEO, forming a golden triangle. They aimed to create a full-category vertical B2C mall, implementing a multi-brand strategy and expanding into apparel and other categories, realizing the dream of a department store empire impossible offline!

Everything sounded perfect, but every dream must face the hard realities. The reality is:

1. Higher technical requirements for independent B2C: Based in Shenzhen headquarters, Belle couldn’t recruit more excellent technical talents, and technical outsourcing services were scarce in Shenzhen. Technology determines user experience, backend management efficiency, and reliable data. Independent B2C involves dynamic technology, different from relatively static technologies like ERP, making continuous changes a hurdle for many traditional enterprises.

2. Increased management difficulty due to category expansion: Someone once said doing women's shoes well doesn't necessarily mean men's shoes can be done well. This must have been deeply felt by Belle when expanding into men's shoes. Expanding into women's clothing and accessories within a year would undoubtedly pose challenges to the backend operations. For traditionally cost-conscious brands, this fast-paced approach might have worked for Fanke during special periods, but whether Belle could understand and execute it with “exaggerated costs” remains questionable!

3. System: Many people don't grasp the differences between e-commerce and traditional enterprises. Simply put, department setup (organizational structure), approvals, finance and budget, reporting methods, corporate culture, communication styles, execution methods, risk control, processes, and systems form what is called a company’s "mechanism." The conflict arises when e-commerce tries to persuade traditional enterprises to accept their system, and vice versa, leading to inefficiency and instability in the execution team. Eventually, the chairman or board might say, “E-commerce and traditional enterprises each have strengths and weaknesses; the future lies in integration. So, you must integrate in these aspects...” If such so-called "integration without harmony" occurs, it leads to supposed management innovation, and surprisingly, "CEO rotation" appeared in a company like Belle!

Originally, it was thought that Yougou from Belle e-commerce had taken the lead, with top executives waiting for spin-off listings to enjoy success, wealth, and honor as countless traditional enterprises' examples of e-commerce success. However, they found themselves entangled in these tough issues.

After all, whether it's the old-timers who grew up with Belle or the poached e-commerce VPs, it’s clear they didn’t leave because of lack of ability. When VPs from JD.com returned, they continued to perform admirably, and those from Vancl had the courage to start their own ventures. Are these three points the real hard injuries? Or are these not only Belle's but also many traditional enterprises' hard injuries when trying to enter e-commerce?
