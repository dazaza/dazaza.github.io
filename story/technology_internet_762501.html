Dianping.com: A Bone Too Big for the Giants to Chew, Living in the Discomfort Zone

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 15:47:53

Tao Zhang, CEO of Dianping.com

Written by Yuan Yin, edited by Jing Yang, photographed by Nan Xiao

Tao Zhang, the CEO of Dianping.com, has postponed the company's IPO once again.

In 2011, Dianping received a $100 million Series C financing round from Sequoia Capital and others, valuing the company at $1 billion. According to convention, it should have gone public within the next one to two years, but it didn't. Instead, in 2012, it conducted another $60 million Series D financing round.

Given Dianping's scale and development momentum, going public wouldn't be difficult. However, Zhang Tao feels that an IPO now would be too costly. He breaks down the large internet (PC + mobile) into four elements—people, information, goods, and services—with each connection point offering the opportunity for giants to emerge. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, and Alibaba connects people with goods. The space between people and services is where the fourth giant could sit. Since 2003, Dianping has been working hard to accumulate data, finally waiting for the explosion of mobile internet, which integrates "location" and "service" through smartphones. According to Zhang Tao's logic, no company seems more fitting than Dianping for the image of the fourth giant.

However, outsiders don't see it this way. In recent months, with frequent acquisitions by BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent), Dianping has become a hypothetical acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition target; regardless of which of BAT's names it bears, it could help them achieve great success early on. On the other hand, Dianping's size is much smaller compared to BAT, and it's still too early to conclude that it will become a giant. Moreover, whether it's Tencent WeChat's official platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after investing in AutoNavi and Meituan, they all directly target Dianping's territory—the local life services focused on dining, drinking, entertainment, etc.

Before proving the following two things, a Dianping IPO would be equivalent to a "discounted sale." First, Zhang Tao needs time to prove that what Dianping is doing now is something BAT cannot do; second, Dianping is about to engage in the ultimate battle of group buying. If it secures the top position, its valuation could increase several times over.

2013 is Dianping's "decisive year." Ignoring speculation and rumors, Zhang Tao is very clear: "The internet has indeed entered a great era of integration, but Dianping cannot be acquired."

Zhang Tao categorizes companies that might be acquired into three types. The first type has platform attributes but is better suited to develop within a giant's system, such as maps and email. These products can easily attract a large number of users but struggle to find independent business models. Some, like browsers, can make money, but the revenue earned through navigation services is far less than their strategic value to large companies. "Giant companies are willing to invest in email products because they help with brand and user stickiness, but there haven't been any successful standalone email companies." The second type involves industry consolidation, like in the video industry, using acquisitions to expand market share. The third type occurs when a company faces financial difficulties and sells itself to survive.

Now Meituan has been brought under Alibaba's wing. Theoretically, Dianping should also quickly seek a "backer" to avoid being defeated by rivals gaining capital and rapidly expanding. However, Zhang Tao says, "The giants' assistance to us is limited. We do need users, but execution is more important. Mergers and acquisitions would reduce our execution capabilities and might even marginalize us within a large company." Furthermore, he aims to keep Dianping at the most precise "distance" from the giants. Getting too close to any one of them would be akin to "choosing sides," making Dianping an enemy to the others.

Thus, logically speaking, acquiring Dianping becomes a dead end. Zhang Tao counters, "Why should we take their money? We're not short on funds; what we need are resources. If a giant is willing to take a small stake and provide us with many resources, I might consider it. But would they? Would a giant give you resources if they only hold a small percentage of shares?"

Weapon One: Dirty and Tiring Work

In the mobile internet era, opportunities for companies that only create pure internet products are decreasing. New opportunities come from those combining with traditional industries. Zhang Tao believes that life services mainly revolve around dining, drinking, and entertainment. Other segmented markets, such as ride-hailing, can form good business models, but it's unlikely any of them will grow into giants.

For dining, drinking, and entertainment, user needs are simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is the core of life services, which is exactly where Dianping started. Whether it's content, member accumulation, or brand, newcomers will find it hard to surpass Dianping.

The most representative product form of discounts is group buying. Group buying has now entered the championship race, and in 2013, Dianping will face off against Meituan. This is a winner-takes-all market; as long as there are two strong players, the industry's gross profit margin will be reduced, preventing stable profitability.

Another foreseeable future is reservations. Reservations can form a true online-to-offline closed loop, but they must be based on Dianping having absolute advantages in both information and discounts. Otherwise, creating a closed loop would be difficult. In 2012, Dianping began experimenting with reservations. Zhang Tao said, "Currently, feedback from users and merchants is positive, but trying to do reservations alone makes it hard to solve the chicken-or-egg problem."

Of course, life services offer some even "heavier" opportunities. For example, food delivery meets users' rigid demands and helps merchants increase sales. In the past couple of years, food delivery companies like "To Home Gourmet" have thrived. However, the core of food delivery is delivery—getting meals delivered within a short time while solving issues like soup spilling. The logistics requirements are even higher than those for fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang Tao said, "If food delivery is executed well, there's still a chance, but it's harder dirty and tiring work, and what Dianping is already doing is dirty and tiring enough."

If judged by age, Dianping is already an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has painstakingly accumulated merchant information and user reviews, which Zhang Tao refers to as "dirty and tiring work." This primitive growth model once restricted its speed, but past struggles have become Dianping's core barrier today. It can be said that currently, apart from Meituan, Dianping has no real competitors in the life services sector.

Nevertheless, he must remain vigilant against BAT's "clutches." Baidu's involvement is the most direct. With over 100 million users, Baidu Maps allows users to search for nearby restaurants and other life services. Tencent, considered to have secured a ticket to the mobile internet era, launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest physical goods trading platform, and expanding into services is logical. Moreover, it has incorporated Meituan and AutoNavi into its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there hasn't been any explosive growth in terms of reality and data," Zhang Tao said. Brand positioning is crucial; the key is which product users turn to first when they want to dine, drink, or entertain themselves.

Moreover, in the vast field of life services, competition among giants is bound to overlap, making it impossible for anyone to completely monopolize the domain. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained on Weibo and WeChat. Zhang Tao feels that in this way, Dianping is actually safer. "In niche areas like car rental and business travel, directing a huge number of users from the giants might flood out existing companies."

As for Alibaba, which invested in AutoNavi, invading Dianping's territory won't be that simple either. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, which can always be categorized no matter how peculiar. A diner's experience depends on various factors such as the day's environment, waitstaff, and chef. Managing merchants can't rely solely on systems and tools like Alibaba does; it requires building a 'ground force' managed by people.

"The three giants all want to take this path, and there is room for imagination, but it's not a highway; it's a rugged mountain road," Zhang Tao believes. First, providing life services requires deploying ground teams across multiple levels, which BAT lacks the genes and ability to manage offline teams. Second, they lack the willingness; life services require dedicated focus, not just assigning a small team, and earning money is slow. "It's hard for a company to excel at two important things simultaneously, especially since they need to consolidate their advantages in their original fields."

Weapon Two: Small Boats Are Easier to Maneuver

When Dianping raised $100 million in 2011, Zhang Tao told the outside world that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, trends change quickly, and before Dianping went public, his analogies fell from grace.

He wasn't anxious about it either. "They failed to become big companies because they lacked offline teams; now they all want to become America's Dianping."

The current path was gradually explored by Zhang Tao himself. When he felt that selling keyword ads and coupons was outdated, the emergence of group buying caught his eye. Initially, he understood group buying as a marketing platform, like the Americans, one deal per day. But as orders increased, he realized group buying had transactional properties. Thus, he envisioned Dianping's future as a life service transaction platform. The two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were precisely used to transform Dianping from an information platform to a transaction platform.

Without this transformation, Zhang Tao might not have had the confidence to compete for the fourth seat among the giants. But transforming is easier said than done; it's equivalent to Dianping's second entrepreneurship, a process filled with immense pain.

First, compared to selling ads separately, transaction platforms have lower gross margins and require stricter cost control and efficiency. Second, transaction platforms demand higher cross-department communication skills.

Previously, to maintain Dianping's impartiality and independence as an information platform, the content and sales teams were entirely separate. Zhang Tao even deliberately established a "firewall" between departments—no matter how important a client, sales couldn't alter platform information for sales purposes, leading to some mutual hostility between content and sales teams. Now, as a transaction platform, editors and sales teams need deep communication to create effective marketing plans, requiring editorial support beyond mere planning. He said, "Maintaining information quality remains crucial, but there's no need to reinforce animosity anymore; we need to dismantle the firewall."

Though editors can logically accept this, individual behaviors and values have inertia, and combined across the entire company, that inertia grows larger. To address this, Zhang Tao specially convened several meetings to encourage more communication between departments. After a few months, an editor manager casually mentioned to a sales manager, "I used to think you were my enemy, but now I realize you're my colleague."

Sales departments also needed transformation. To promote conversion, Zhang Tao cleverly gave the offline team dual roles. First, for merchants, sales personnel should become marketing advisors, not just selling products but helping merchants devise marketing strategies. Second, for the company, marketing advisors act as procurement agents. Dianping is like a supermarket; not only must product quality be good, but the variety must be rich. Marketing advisors should aim to help users procure group-buying products. Naturally, dual roles inevitably bring complex issues. Frontline staff find it challenging, needing to balance scales themselves.

Substantially, Dianping's "dual role" resembles Alibaba's division into 25 business units. Decentralizing decision-making to the frontlines gives the company faster response speeds but increases management difficulty. Similarly, compared to frontline employees, Dianping's management team suffers more during the transformation.

Each year, Zhang Tao breaks down Dianping's company goals into various departments, then executives further decompose them layer by layer, but decomposition isn't simple arithmetic. Li Jing, co-founder of Dianping, responsible for the sales team, sometimes finds unresolved merchant demands due to sales issues affecting performance. Therefore, Li Jing introduced a new role, communication specialist, who collects information daily from marketing advisors, submits it to other departments, and tracks feedback. He often tells his team, "Being good at embracing change doesn't mean liking change; no one likes change, and everyone feels uncomfortable when faced with it. All employees must go through discomfort zones, including myself."

Numbers illustrate the "weight" of Dianping's execution team. Of the current 3,000+ employees, over 2,000 work offline. The ground force communicates directly with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group buying, and bundled marketing solutions.

For this core team resisting BAT's clutches, Dianping has a unique management evaluation system.

They aren't called "sales"; from day one, their business cards read "Marketing Planning Advisor." Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the era of watching news and chatting on QQ, lagging behind Taobao merchants by at least three to five years in understanding the internet. Marketing planning advisors need to tell merchants what online users need and how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing said, "Even for a high-end restaurant, marketing advisors wouldn't suggest shark fin or abalone group buying; perhaps other dishes, because online users differ from offline ones."

An outstanding marketing advisor must be a jack-of-all-trades. They need to explain to restaurant owners and hair salon owners the essential differences between discounts and group buying; which discount suits their positioning better. If dealing with a hot pot restaurant owner, the marketing advisor should know whether it's the off-season or peak season, which products' costs rise in this season, and which costs fall, proposing a group-buying menu appealing to both merchants and users.

This sets high demands on marketing advisors, requiring them to be not only internet marketing experts but also industry experts. Li Jing said, "I must admit, not every advisor can be a double expert, so we've set up star ratings." Similar to hotels, marketing advisors range from one-star to five-star, with the best labeled Super Star.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline employees aren't tech-savvy graduates from prestigious universities, making it challenging for them to understand internet products. Dianping has built a knowledge base for marketing advisors, containing knowledge points and relevant cases accessible to both new and old employees. If encountering new problems, employees can ask questions within the knowledge base, where thousands of consultants nationwide will assist in answering.

Most knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees based on practical experience. Dianping has set up a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, rewarding employees who help others the most each quarter. Every quarter, Dianping conducts skill assessments for employees, directly linking results to promotions.

Industry knowledge is harder to obtain than internet knowledge. Some experiences are summarized by traditional merchants after decades of industry practice. A Super Star marketing advisor told Li Jing that the hairdressing industry shouldn't do shampoo-cut-blow package deals because men lack motivation to buy, and women prefer blow-drying without washing and cutting. Li Jing was astonished, "We men would never think of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing advisors are not only divided by city but also by industries such as catering, hairdressing, leisure, etc. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is even subdivided into hotpot, Western cuisine, buffet, with a marketing advisor responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing advisors can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, ensuring merchants don't resist when selling products," he said.

Each marketing group holds its own case-sharing sessions: what difficulties a certain type of merchant encounters, what marketing needs they have, what solutions are provided using company products, and how effective they are. Outstanding cases are reported upward, and Dianping officially shares one case weekly.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition—Startup Winner. In the first three months of employment, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products, but listening to merchants sharing experiences. If responsible for hotpot stores, after three months, they must submit a business plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hotpot Store Owner." Like graduation, employees present their defense live, with industry supervisors evaluating winners, and the company providing certain rewards.

Strategically, whether it's Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, the tasks they need to accomplish are quite similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O, anyone familiar with the industry can talk about them similarly. "The era where telling stories or fabricating concepts could lead to success has passed. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model lies in management and execution, not strategy," Zhang Tao stated.
