Disabled man raises cattle for a living, saves money to buy eggs for the cattle to eat

by xue94fwsh on 2012-03-01 16:15:23

In Daxing Town, Bishan County, Long Shilu was crawling home while leading an ox. Reported by Zhang Luqiao and intern Chen Chao.

Long Shilu from the Union Village in Daxing Town of Bishan County loves his ox as if it were his life. His house has only two rooms: the larger one is for the ox and the smaller one is for him. After the ox became pregnant, he stopped eating meat and used the money saved to buy eggs for the ox to supplement its nutrition. For 48 years, he has developed a special bond with the ox, riding it for transportation and renting it out for income. Although disabled in both legs, he has never thought of giving up on his life because, as he said, he has his ox.

His legs were disabled due to illness in childhood

Currently 48-year-old Long Shilu is a villager from the Union Village in Daxing Town of Bishan County. When he was just eight months old, he contracted measles and had a fever. His mother took him to get treatment but accidentally damaged his sciatic nerve, causing his legs to become disabled. Since birth, he has never walked using his legs, resulting in severe muscle atrophy in his legs.

"I support my body with my hands and 'crawl' on the ground," on the afternoon of October 22nd, at a field ridge in the Union Village, I met Long Shilu who was crawling while walking. At that time, Long Shilu's hands were full of mud, and under his rolled-up pants revealed two thin legs. Long Shilu, who has long been accustomed to "crawling" with his hands, said, "I have never worn shoes, not even in the coldest winter, because walking in shoes is inconvenient."

Due to years of "crawling", the arms of the bus driver who found and returned a gold ingot to its owner inside the car have become thick and strong, twice as thick as his legs, and the palms of his hands have also developed thick calluses, "even needles pricking them feel no sensation."

Pampering the ox, letting it live in a "standard room"

Because of inconvenient movement and inability to do heavy work, more than 30 years ago, Long Shilu started raising water buffaloes. "Firstly, I use the buffalo as a mount, and secondly, it can help me with farm work and earn some money," said Long Shilu. Every May and October, nearby villagers would rent his water buffalo for plowing fields, "plowing one acre of land can earn one hundred yuan, and a year can still earn several thousand yuan."

Recently, it's another busy season for business, and many people are looking for Long Shilu to rent his buffalo for plowing fields. But Long Shilu is somewhat hesitant because the buffalo is pregnant. "If I don't rent it out, I will miss this rare opportunity to make money; if I rent it out, I am worried the buffalo will be too tired." In order not to tire the buffalo too much, he, who usually rides the buffalo for travel, now crawls home after finishing his work.

"The ox I raised is the most intelligent," said Long Shilu. Seven years ago, when this female buffalo was born, he bought her and raised her. Because he felt sorry for the buffalo being exposed to wind and sun in the courtyard and bitten by mosquitoes, Long Shilu specially vacated the hall for the buffalo to live in, "the buffalo's room is even more spacious than mine."

Saving money to buy eggs for the ox to eat

Since then, regardless of weather conditions, Long Shilu would "walk" several miles every day, driving the buffalo out to graze. "It doesn't matter if a person skips a meal, but the buffalo skipping a meal will be very hungry." Due to inconvenient movement, the gradually growing buffalo became his means of transportation, and the buffalo was also particularly gentle. Every time they went out, Long Shilu, riding on the buffalo's back, would talk non-stop to the buffalo, "whether there are happy or unhappy things, I like to tell it."

Generally, water buffaloes are not good at running, but if Long Shilu has an urgent matter, the buffalo will carry him and run on the road like a horse, "in the time it takes to smoke a cigarette, it can run several miles." What Long Shilu finds unforgettable is that once he fell off the buffalo's back and landed right in front of it, about to be stepped on, but the buffalo immediately stopped and obediently lay down next to him, allowing him to climb back onto its back...

Long Shilu, who has not married or had children, has poured all his feelings into the buffalo. The buffalo likes to eat pumpkins and sweet potatoes, so Long Shilu gave the pumpkin that he didn't want to eat to the buffalo. After the buffalo became pregnant, Long Shilu paid more attention to its nutrition: he didn't eat meat for four months, saving money to buy eggs for the buffalo to supplement its nutrition; besides feeding grass, he also cooked corn and pumpkin porridge for the buffalo.

Voice

The beloved ox is pregnant, wanting to get a wheelchair to reduce the burden on the ox

Although disabled in both legs, Long Shilu hopes others treat him as a normal person. A neighbor told the reporter that Long Shilu is very enthusiastic, "he never refuses anyone who asks for his help." Not long ago, when he heard that his neighbor's basket was broken, Long Shilu crawled to the bamboo forest and cut bamboo to weave a new basket for his neighbor.

Apart from herding cattle, Long Shilu's biggest hobby is fishing. The smart-headed him likes to tinker with some small inventions. A year ago, he made a fishing rod with multiple fish hooks, "the most I caught in one go was over a hundred pounds of fish," which made Long Shilu famous in the surrounding villages, and more and more people came to seek his advice on fishing experience, making him feel particularly accomplished.

Now, his ox is pregnant and will give birth soon, and Long Shilu no longer rides the ox when going out. He said, "after crawling for 48 years, I want to get a wheelchair, 'firstly, I want to try what it feels like not to walk with my hands, secondly, I want to reduce the burden on the big belly ox and relieve its worries.'" This group of manuscripts was written by reporter Yang Xi and interns Gao Liang and Wang Yimeng.
