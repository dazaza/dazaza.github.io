Facebook Design Chief: Why Designers Make Good CEOs

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 17:28:13

Maria Giudice, Facebook's director of product design, firmly believes that company executives can benefit immensely by applying the principles of good design to their management style. In her book "The Rise of the DEO: Leadership by Design," co-authored with fellow designer Christopher Ireland, Giudice offers modern managers a practical handbook. Recently, Fortune magazine interviewed Giudice to share her insights on design, entrepreneurship, and her personal experiences working at the world's largest social network.

Fortune: How do you define design?

Maria Giudice: I know that traditionally, design has always been seen as an expense on the income statement rather than an investment. I want to change that perception. My definition of design is very broad. The typical designer is the shaper of products.

The word "design" itself needs to be redesigned. Designers don't need to focus so much on the medium they use but should place more emphasis on the process of creating something tangible that truly meets people's needs. This process requires facilitation and collaboration. It cannot happen in a vacuum. Design is the collaborative process of working with multidisciplinary teams to create better products.

What qualities does an excellent designer need?

I believe designers should have certain qualities — what I call superpowers. First and foremost is empathy. Learning how to put yourself in someone else's shoes while studying at art school or design college will serve you well in a business environment later on.

Another is the ability to think systemically. Our world is incredibly complex, and technology has always played a balancing role within it. Various systems are highly intricate, so when providing design solutions for issues that require thorough consideration, one cannot see only the trees and not the forest. Designers must delve deeply into understanding systems.

Just as information and access to it were the fundamental challenges of the 20th century, complexity seems to be the fundamental challenge of the 21st century.

That’s absolutely true. So designers love to simplify this complexity. Through pattern recognition, they understand complexity and learn to simplify it. Therefore, people who can think systematically need to be elevated to the CEO level. Companies will also need talent with this kind of thinking ability.

Another (superpower) is being willing to take risks, but in a smart way. Traditionally, CEOs are slaves to the profit and loss statement. It's hard to make bold moves. But the world won't wait for you. You must move quickly, and the only way to do that is to understand how to take calculated risks.

There are indeed some CEOs who dare to take risks. For example, Mark Zuckerberg took a risk. Another example is Autodesk, a software company that is now rethinking what its products can do for the world beyond just being a software provider. That’s taking smart risks.

Are you advocating for promoting Chief Design Officers?

Exactly, having designers become CEOs.

This is really about showcasing those soft skills early on. Whether CEOs like them or not, they must possess these skills.

I’m not saying you have to go to design school to become a Chief Design Officer. It’s about applying the attributes learned in design school. However, theoretically, these attributes could be learned at a younger age instead of focusing solely on math and English. When it comes to creativity, we all follow the same curve when we’re young, but there’s a noticeable decline by fourth grade. This is because we start worrying about exams and shift our focus to math and English. Some people think they lack creativity, but actually, they had it stripped away from them by the time they reached fourth grade.

If companies begin to realize they need to integrate design and creativity more directly into their organizations, how can they learn to do this?

Many traits of creative people aren’t well utilized in a business environment. On one hand, we always say we need creativity. On the other hand, people categorize creativity or group people based on it. How does that inspire creativity? Ultimately, it means hiring specialists — creative individuals — at every level of the company.

How did you become a designer?

My career roadmap is like this: I grew up on Staten Island and started drawing at a young age. At 15, I began my entrepreneurial journey. My mom taught cooking in the basement of our house on the island, and my drawings hung on wooden wall panels. Her students asked if I could draw pictures of their dogs. So, at 15, I started earning money through my art. Doing what I loved while making money — I achieved that early on.

I never thought of myself as a businessman. Later, I worked for designer Richard Saul Wurman. After that, I became a freelancer. As I got busier, I hired people to work for me. My partnership lasted five years before it closed. In 1997, I founded HOT, growing it into a company with 75 employees.

HOT was a completely independent company, debt-free, with strong cash flow. It experienced ups and downs, almost went bankrupt, and had prosperous times too. Six years ago, it opened an office in New York. Over the years, it became increasingly difficult to maintain financial independence, so we started considering exit strategies.

Was this why you joined Facebook?

Yes, it was a "Back to the Future" moment for design studios. These studios are extremely popular. Last October, a colleague from Facebook whom I've known for a long time asked if I could recommend a few designers. Facebook is one of the few tech companies that has consistently valued great design. They treat designers equally with engineers and business professionals. They appreciate and respect the core competencies and strengths of talented individuals. In terms of design, they were understaffed. When we started working with Facebook, everyone felt happy. We became a creative agency that could operate within a product company, which is quite rare. In early November, I went to meet with a client. A Facebook acquaintance leaned over the plate of fried potatoes and said, "I’d like to ask how interested you might be in an acquisition." I had never thought about it.

A week later, I received an email: "Sheryl Sandberg wants to have coffee and chat with you." So I went. Once there, I entered a meeting room to meet with Mark (Zuckerberg) and Sheryl. They asked me many questions. If someone had told me beforehand that I would meet Mark Zuckerberg and Sheryl Sandberg, I might have prepared. But that’s not how things work at Facebook. Their demeanor was simply, "I want to ask you questions and have an honest exchange." Everything felt natural, and it worked effectively.

Twenty minutes later, I walked out. It was mid-November. We had another candid conversation in January, and the news was announced to the public in March.

What is it like working at Facebook?

They don't care too much about titles, which is another thing I like. My title is Director of Product Design. The team I work with is called "Platform," and it handles all experiences related to Facebook.

My goal is: I want to make Facebook's design a benchmark in the industry — helping the entire company build teams where designers and engineers can collaborate more effectively.
