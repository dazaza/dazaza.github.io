Retinal hole

by weixing123 on 2009-09-22 15:15:42

A retinal tear refers to a full-thickness split in the retina, located at the bottom of the human eye. It is the result of degeneration in both the retina and vitreous humor tissues. It commonly occurs in middle-aged and elderly individuals, especially those with high myopia.

Symptoms:

Acute retinal tear: flashes of light in front of the eyes, floaters (web-like or fly-like objects that change position with the movement of the eyeball), sometimes accompanied by blurred vision.

Chronic retinal tear or atrophic retinal tear: often asymptomatic.

Main signs:

Full-thickness defect in the sensory layer of the retina.

Other signs:

Acute retinal tear: pigmented cells may appear in the anterior vitreous, vitreous hemorrhage, posterior vitreous detachment, and a floating cover membrane in the vitreous cavity above the retinal tear.

Chronic retinal tear: pigment rings surround the tear, a clear boundary exists between detached and non-detached retina, and there may be signs of acute retinal tears (but without symptoms).

Preceding damage: lattice degeneration, high myopia, aphakia, pseudophakia, senile retinoschisis, vitreoretinal traction, meridional folds, history of retinal tear or detachment in the other eye.

Examination:

Detailed ophthalmological examination, particularly binocular dilated examination using an indirect ophthalmoscope and scleral depression. If the patient has traumatic anterior chamber bleeding, scleral depression is generally not performed within 2-4 weeks. Examination of retinal tears using a slit lamp combined with a three-mirror lens can be helpful.

Treatment:

Generally, acute tears require laser treatment, cryotherapy, or scleral buckle surgery within 24-72 hours. Postoperatively, traditional Chinese medicine treatment and conditioning are required for both acute and chronic tears. Treatment should be based on the specifics of each case.

(1) Recommended treatments:

1) Acute symptomatic tears, such as horseshoe-shaped tears or covered tears.

2) Acute traumatic tears.

(2) Considered treatments:

1) Large asymptomatic retinal tears (e.g., >1.5mm) or those located on the horizontal meridian or both, especially when there is no posterior vitreous detachment.

2) Asymptomatic retinal tears appearing in aphakic or pseudophakic eyes, highly myopic eyes, or eyes with a history of retinal detachment on the same side or opposite side.

(3) Recommended medication:

Oral administration of 5g of Quyu Mingmu capsules, Tid, after meals; external use of Zhuhong Mingmu eye drops.

Follow-up:

Experts from Beijing Chaoyang Hospital of Integrated Traditional Chinese and Western Medicine suggest: patients with preceding lesions or chronic retinal tears should have a follow-up every 6-12 months. Patients who have received treatment should have follow-ups one week, one month, and three months after treatment, followed by a review every 6-12 months. Patients should be informed about the symptoms of retinal detachment (an increase in floaters or flashes in front of the eyes, or the appearance of curtains, shadows, or bubble-like structures in the field of vision), and they should be advised to seek immediate medical attention if these symptoms occur.
