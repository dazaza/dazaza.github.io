Game of Thrones: Sex and Power Inside Google

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 20:51:33

Introduction: On Thursday, the American tech blog Business Insider published an article titled "Google's Sex and Politics: A Game of Thrones in Mountain View." It highlights that while little is known about the internal sexual and political struggles within Google, many situations resemble scenes described in the novel *A Game of Thrones*.

The following is the full text:

In the global perception, Google’s work environment is as relaxed and pleasant as its logo. From the outside, Google is a company that adheres to the principle of “Don’t be evil,” with leaders who are geeks sitting on bean bags, wearing t-shirts and jeans. In the world's imagination, Google’s leaders are nerdy technological idealists who invest in researching self-driving cars, installing computers on every desk, or launching WiFi-accessible airships over Africa.

This impression of Google is accurate but incomplete. Beneath this facade, at its headquarters in Mountain View, California, Google is also a breeding ground for sexual and political conflicts. It has always been so.

A person who has spent most of their career at Google said this: "Inside Google, it’s similar to 'A Game of Thrones.'" *A Game of Thrones* is a popular novel turned into a TV series by HBO. The plot is complex, but in short, it revolves around several noble families' deadly battles for the throne. Throughout the novel, there is violence, sex, and political intrigue.

Of course, there will be no violence at Google, but what about sex and politics? The answer is yes.

Sex

*A Game of Thrones* frequently depicts sexual relationships between characters, but these characters are actually vying for power. Frankly speaking, from the early days of Google's founding, such relationships have existed between those in power and those seeking power.

Douglas Edwards recounted the early days of Google in his book *I'm Feeling Lucky: The Confessions of Google Employee Number 59*. At the time, Google's human resources director, Heather Cairns, once remarked: "Hormones were flying everywhere, and not everyone remembered to lock the doors."

Edwards narrated a specific incident in the book: "In a windowless lounge, we had a bed for exhausted employees to rest. One afternoon, an employee peeked inside and found two engineers lying on the bed engaged in 'non-computational parallel processing activities.'"

Google eventually did not reprimand the two engineers, setting a precedent. "The company did not issue stern policy reminders. The moral high ground was hard to claim, so unofficial 'user interface experiments' continued, just shifted to nighttime in offices lit up by monitor screens filled with passion."

Since then, sexual and romantic relationships among employees have occurred across all levels at Google. In most cases, these relationships are healthy and normal, without scandal. Two Google employees might date for a while, break up or get married, and it wouldn't attract much attention from others.

Sometimes, however, things escalate into scandals. Recently, the American tech blog AllThingsD reported that Sergey Brin had separated from his wife, who happens to be the sister of a Google executive. Additionally, it was revealed that Brin was dating a young female employee, whose ex-boyfriend was Hugo Barra, the former head of Google's Android division who has since left for Xiaomi, a Chinese smartphone manufacturer. Insiders insist these events were coincidental, but no one can deny the potential complexity behind them.

The truth is, Brin's scandal is far from being an isolated case at Google. There have been rumors of a powerful executive asking his assistant to arrange dates for him. Another rumor claims an executive dated his own assistant. Moreover, two married Google employees secretly had a child together. A few years ago, *The New York Times* interviewed Eric Schmidt's wife regarding rumors of Schmidt's infidelity. Her response was: "I live independently."

In reality, Google allows employees to act this way. An employee stated that Google finds the policy reasonable. This employee, who is also in a relationship with another employee, said: "There are many technical talents and business talents in places like Google, and it's natural for them to attract each other. You can't stop these people from being attracted to each other." He added that the situation is similar at Facebook.

However, this source mentioned that Google's HR department does not ignore such relationships. If a couple becomes a superior-subordinate relationship, the HR department will intervene and suggest that one party adjust their job position to do other work. Sometimes, this suggestion is mandatory.

At Google, although sex is prevalent, it is not a method people use to pursue power or expand their territory within the company. Instead, this is achieved through so-called "geopolitical wars."

Politics

In February this year, Google's global executives gathered at a resort called Carneros Inn located in the mountain vineyards of Napa Valley. These executives were all vice presidents and above, including Susan Wojcicki, head of advertising; Andy Rubin, head of Android; Salar Kamangar, CEO of YouTube; Sundar Pichai, head of Chrome; and Vic Gundotra, head of Google+. Each executive brought along their senior staff.

This meeting was confidential, and the conference room at the resort did not display any Google logos. Most of the resort's employees and guests didn't even know that during these two days, they were staying alongside the entire executive team of one of the world's most important companies. In fact, the attending Google executives themselves didn't know what significant outcomes the two-day meeting would bring.

However, when CEO Larry Page gathered everyone to speak, they understood the problem. Page's speech was both a warning and a mobilization. With his trademark sharp tone, Page told everyone in the room that Google had grand ambitions, but if the people in the room couldn't stop fighting each other, Google would never achieve its goals.

Page declared that from now on, Google would adopt a zero-tolerance attitude towards internal strife. He pointed out that in the early days of Google's development, the company needed its leaders to compete because the problems Google faced back then were "linear" issues. Google had to increase the market share of all its products from zero to a competitive level and ultimately win.

However, now that most of Google's products lead in their industries, the current issues are "exponential" problems. Google needs to grow tenfold, create new markets, and solve problems in unimaginable ways. To solve these "exponential" problems, Page believed that Google's executives should cooperate better with each other.

Finally, Page set the rules: "If you continue attacking each other, we will gladly let you go elsewhere to compete." During Page's speech, one attending executive whispered to a friend: "Is he saying zero tolerance for conflict? I've been here for many years, and what we've always done is fight."

He was right, as another long-time Google executive put it: "If the princes are fighting, it's because the king tolerates them." Google's king has tolerated internal corporate conflicts for a very long time.

Since Page's speech in February, many changes have occurred within Google. The experience of Android chief Andy Rubin serves as the best proof. Rubin joined Google in a 2005 acquisition, helping develop the Android system, which became the world's hottest mobile computing platform. He convinced major phone manufacturers like Samsung to use Android. However, he believed that if Samsung and other phone makers were to use Android, Android should operate relatively independently within Google. Thus, he actively protected Android's independence.

As Android's market share grew, Rubin received everything he wanted from Page. A source said this was because Rubin delivered what was needed. However, Rubin was unsatisfied with his power. If Kamangar was a whistleblower and Gundotra was good at storytelling, then Rubin was the one shouting loudly at meetings. He chose to fight, becoming an obstacle. A senior employee from another Google department said: "Sometimes, negotiating with Apple is easier than negotiating with the Android team."

Ultimately, this confrontational attitude cost Rubin dearly. A few weeks after Page's February speech, Rubin stepped down as head of the Android division. On the surface, Rubin said he would join Google X, Google's research and development lab. But some insiders within Google doubted whether he was still working there.

Page chose another executive, Sundar Pichai, to take over Rubin's role. During his tenure at Google, Pichai successfully persuaded some computer manufacturers to pre-install the Google Toolbar. He excels at making deals and helping parties reach agreements. His promotion may indicate that Google will become more friendly and cooperative.

However, none of this means that the internal struggles within Google have stopped; they have merely changed form. A source who attended Page's February meeting said: "Page's zero-tolerance policy means don't go too far." He believes there is a healthier way to struggle internally at Google. "The healthier way is to wear two hats simultaneously. You can choose either the YouTube or Android hat, but mature Google executives will know when to switch to the bigger Google hat."

Time will tell whether this means the end of Google's internal "Game of Thrones."
