Chen Nian has responded to the previous topic of "Fanke or Louis Vuitton (LV)" with a compiled reply. Chen Nian indicated that the idea of Fanke buying LV was originally meant as a joke, but when Fanke becomes wealthy and has spare time, it would certainly be considered.

In Chen Nian's view, the reason LV can sell at lower prices lies in the market's pursuit of it. Chen Nian stated that in terms of profit, there is not much difference between LV and Fanke. The reason LV sells at a lower price is due to the emerging class chasing after it, which is the main reason for its high price.

Below are the majority of views expressed in Chen Nian's interview:

1. The notion of Fanke buying LV was actually meant as a joke. Because I feel that what LV satisfies is a sense of arrogance, especially the pride of the domestic nouveau riche, while I value something else more.

2. In fact, I also want to merge with LV. If one day the Fanke management team could have some leisure time and a bit of money, we might really buy several such companies and make them sell at the same price as Fanke.

3. The reason I use LV as an example is because it is relatively famous. Other brands at the same level have lower public recognition. If I could really acquire LV, I would definitely sell it at the same price as Fanke products. The reason I say this is because I understand the cost-profit ratio of LV; it is really only that much.

4. If you look into the small groups in Jiangsu-Zhejiang, Yangtze River Delta, which are domestic manufacturing export bases, you will find it quite disappointing. Because here are Fanke's goods, and over there are some luxury brands, all using the same materials, the same cotton, the same workshops, and the same workers. What's the difference?

As for myself, after operating Fanke, I spend less money (on luxuries) and my consumption has decreased significantly. It's hard to convince myself to buy those things anymore. These luxury brands keep talking about their brand culture, or perhaps they call themselves national treasures of their countries. (If that's true), then their own countrymen should support them, not particularly the Chinese people. I see that the officials in their countries don't buy these, only the nouveau riche in emerging markets do. So where does the so-called brand culture come from? Where does the cultural accumulation come from? They're all the same stuff.

5. When it comes to the design philosophy of these brands, I've thought about this issue myself. What are designers originally? Aren't they just tailors? At the beginning, sewing piece by piece, incorporating personal preferences and customer likes, summarizing it isn't that the design philosophy? When opening stores, especially in the modern era when brands expand, maintaining "my style" and having a unique characteristic is the design philosophy.

6. Later on, with substantial profits coming in, there is an effort to "invest" in a designer, to "bet" on a designer. Exactly how powerful a designer is, the profit chain doesn't clarify, nor does the audience. However, it indeed requires financial accumulation to rise. There is such a process.

7. Then, during the 80s and 90s in Europe, the emergence of H&M and ZARA broke the concept of high-end designer philosophy itself. This matter has been concluded in Europe. Yet we still emphasize the design philosophy and imagination abilities of the previous era, isn't that laughable?

8. Just like, because you have a certain skill, you can increase the price of something by a thousand times or ten thousand times. I feel that emphasizing that skill again is shameful, not dignified.

9. In the past, whenever we talked about brands, we looked up to them, admired them. Saying a brand is great because it dares to multiply its price by ten times, a hundred times, which is called brand "premium," multiplying the profit by many times, hundreds or thousands of times, until it becomes LV. But why should it be multiplied that much? It doesn't make sense.

10. As a result, we see that every time these brands multiply their prices, it's supported by nothing but lies. After reading so many years of books, I truly understand how culture shapes a product.
