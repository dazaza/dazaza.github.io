Xiaomi Faces After-sales Fund Pressure, and it may need 15 million yuan to build a Xiaomi Home

by bingo123 on 2012-03-08 17:40:06

"I am extremely dissatisfied with the current state of after-sales service for Xiaomi phones," Mr. Lin, a Xiaomi phone user from Fujian, told the Investors Herald on February 18th. He mentioned that repairing a Xiaomi phone is inconvenient, the after-sales service hotline is difficult to get through, and the entire repair process is very complicated.

Mr. Lin's Xiaomi phone took 10 days to repair, exceeding the "5-day" limit promised by Xiaomi.

What troubles Mr. Lin even more is that different departments within Xiaomi do not communicate well, and the after-sales staff are largely unaware of the results of the phone repairs. Moreover, "the after-sales department never proactively called me during the entire repair process."

According to the Investors Herald, currently, Xiaomi only has Mi Home service stations in seven cities: Beijing, Shanghai, Shenzhen, etc. Users in other cities can only send their phones back via courier for repair. In comparison, Nokia has 33 authorized service centers just in Beijing, and Motorola has over a dozen customer service centers in Beijing as well.

Within six months of its launch, the total sales volume of Xiaomi phones approached one million units. Despite the impressive sales figures, questions about Xiaomi's after-sales service have been surfacing recently. Media reports indicate that many Xiaomi phone users find the after-sales repair process cumbersome and the costs high. Issues such as unavailability of customer service hotlines and excessively long repair times are hard for "Mi Fans" to accept.

Xiaomi has already recognized this problem. On February 8th, during the release of the Xiaomi Telecom Edition, Lei Wanqiang, President of the MIUI mobile operating system project at Xiaomi, revealed to the media that Xiaomi had finalized plans to establish repair centers in 30 cities before the Spring Festival. The company also plans to introduce around 600 third-party repair centers to cover 120 cities.

Following the example of HTC and Samsung, Xiaomi mainly collaborates with larger local repair centers, paying them labor fees.

The cost of establishing 30 Mi Home stores has not been disclosed by Xiaomi Technology, but it can be compared to Meizu, another domestic smartphone manufacturer that has also made significant strides in the smartphone market. Since its official establishment in 2003, Meizu has accumulated over 300 stores across mainland China, including 12 stores in Beijing alone.

On February 22nd, Ms. Liao, the person in charge of the mainland market for Meizu, told the Investors Herald: "Meizu has strict requirements for franchise stores, such as a minimum store area of 60 square meters, street-front locations, and busy areas, all of which are mandatory standards."

Ms. Liao further explained: "The initial cost for a Meizu store is at least 500,000 RMB, including rent, renovation, equipment, etc."

Based on this calculation, if the upcoming "Mi Home" stores are built according to the minimum standard of Meizu stores, the capital cost for just 30 Mi Homes would exceed 15 million RMB. Not to mention the need to establish over 600 third-party after-sales repair centers. It is clear that Xiaomi faces considerable financial pressure in building its after-sales service network.

Geng Yan, an analyst at CCID Consulting, said in an interview with the Investors Herald: "Compared to other phone manufacturers who have spent many years accumulating after-sales service outlets, Xiaomi’s plan to cover 120 cities in such a short time is too fast, making it difficult to ensure quality. And from a purely financial perspective, it is quite challenging."

Claiming to be the "world's fastest" smartphone priced at only 1999 RMB, Xiaomi boasts powerful hardware configuration and low pricing. In addition, Xiaomi plans to adopt a model without physical stores, relying entirely on online sales, which is a first in China's mobile phone sales market. Previously, only Google's Nexus phones followed a completely online sales channel model, but they ultimately failed.

Industry insiders pointed out that the inability for consumers to see physical devices, the lack of distributors leading to insufficient grassroots marketing efforts, and reliance solely on the internet for after-sales services were key reasons for Google's failure.

From the current situation, Xiaomi may have fully considered the reasons for Google's failure and has been using an excellent marketing team to boost Xiaomi's sales performance. However, whether Xiaomi will repeat the same mistakes as Google in terms of after-sales services remains uncertain. ■
