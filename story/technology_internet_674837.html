For patients, the most undesirable situation is the recurrence of a disease after treatment. This not only wastes a lot of money but also causes suffering. However, in reality, some diseases are prone to recurrence, such as anal fistulas.

Therefore, patients often ask about the reasons for the recurrence of anal fistulas. According to Professor Peng Peng from the Yangzhou Armed Police Hospital's Anorectal Center, the reason why anal fistulas easily recur mainly lies in the improper handling of the internal opening of the fistula. This is the most crucial point in treating anal fistulas. Generally, there are five reasons for the recurrence of anal fistulas:

1. The primary source of infection, such as anal sinusitis or anal gland infection, still exists. Contents from the intestinal cavity can continue to enter the fistula through the internal opening, forming new fistulas. This is the key reason for the recurrence of anal fistulas.

For anal fistulas, many non-professional doctors cannot find all the internal openings; they merely remove the surface fistula without addressing the critical internal opening, leading to repeated episodes of anal fistulas.

2. The perianal area cannot rest properly, making it difficult for the abscess cavity to heal. During defecation and urination, due to the contraction of the sphincter muscle or inflammation stimulating the anal sphincter, the sphincter muscle is often in a state of spasm.

3. Feces, intestinal fluid, and gas from the intestinal cavity continue to enter the fistula, stimulating the tube wall, causing the connective tissue of the tube wall to proliferate and thicken, making it difficult for the tube cavity to close.

4. Poor drainage of the abscess cavity, or the external opening becoming smaller, intermittently closing and breaking open again, causing pus to accumulate in the cavity, leading to the recurrence of an abscess which then perforates and forms new branches or fistulas.

5. The fistula passes through the anal sphincter muscle at different heights, and the contraction of the sphincter muscle hinders the discharge of pus, resulting in poor drainage.

In addition, some other diseases can also be causes of anal fistulas, such as tuberculosis, Crohn's disease, and ulcerative colitis.
