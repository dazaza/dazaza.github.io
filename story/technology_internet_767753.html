Apple and Burberry are control freaks: online and off

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 13:20:54

At Burberry's flagship store on London's Regent Street, you'll find attractively displayed clothing, "magic mirrors" showcasing fashion show images, Arabic signage for Gulf visitors, and a top-floor "VVIP" room, all creating an experience akin to entering Willy Wonka's sweet world.

Burberry's Wonka is Christopher Bailey, the creative director who designs everything, including a limited-edition white crocodile leather jacket priced at £70,000. His luxury stage operates at a level several tiers above the Apple store also located on Regent Street, but there is a connection. Last Wednesday, people queued outside the Apple store to buy the iPhone 5s.

This link is proven by Angela Ahrendts, Burberry’s CEO, being appointed as Apple's online and retail stores chief. Ron Johnson, who masterminded Apple's highly successful retail stores, transitioned from computers to fashion, trying to transplant Apple's magic onto JC Penney in the U.S., but failed miserably. Ahrendts’ career move has been in the opposite direction.

I believe her chances of success are higher than Johnson's because Burberry shares more similarities with Apple. The partnership between Ahrendts and Bailey mirrors that of the late Steve Jobs and Apple designer Sir Jonathan Ive; they are both control freaks. Early in their collaboration, they realized the necessity of controlling everything, whether online or offline.

This approach is costly and risky, requiring significant capital investment, and the scale must be large enough to generate appropriate returns. However, as online retail continues to erode physical distribution channels, control is becoming increasingly crucial. The French Parliament passed a law attempting to prevent Amazon from undercutting France's bookstores with discounts and free shipping. Other retailers worldwide must defend their own territories.

When Ahrendts took over in 2006, Burberry had already rapidly expanded under former CEO Rose Marie Bravo, but at a heavy cost. The brand had issued too many licenses, leading to a proliferation of designs, making its signature checkered pattern popular even among football fans, which devalued the brand. Ahrendts invested heavily in repurchasing these licenses, reasserting brand authority.

Today, her power rivals that of Apple CEO Tim Cook. Burberry's staff use iPads to assist customers, storing their shopping histories so purchases can be made at home just as easily as in-store. Online and in-store displays change synchronously each month, with identical pricing.

In reality, Cook has been practicing vertical integration within Apple much like Burberry. He placed Sir Jonathan in charge of both hardware and software, while Ahrendts will oversee both the web and retail operations, unlike Johnson. At least Cook still retains his own job. After Ahrendts' departure, Bailey will become both CEO and Chief Designer of Burberry, wielding extraordinary control.

Jobs was a genius, but Ahrendts isn’t. Other luxury companies implement similar strategies, and the luxury sector expands faster than other parts of retail. It makes more sense for high-end and luxury brands to invest in smart stores and websites compared to discount stores or mid-market chains like JC Penney.

However, retailers such as Inditex, Arcadia, and Uniqlo benefit from the integration of design, production, and retail without adopting this model, whereas brands that don't integrate, like Best Buy and Barnes & Noble, suffer from Amazon's impact. One CEO remarked: “If you don't control your brand, you're in trouble.”

David Marcotte, retail head at Wipro Technologies, discussing luxury companies like Burberry, said: “These companies operate in a closed environment, which is key.” It’s also easy to underestimate the challenges, as seen when Johnson joined JC Penney in 2011. He tried to make JC Penney more like Apple by reducing the number of brands, cutting discounts, and rewarding employees based on customer service rather than sales volume.

The problem was that JC Penney's customers could buy similar items elsewhere, and if they checked prices online, they could find cheaper options, something he couldn’t control. He only managed the prices and products within his stores, but there was another world outside.

Apple and Burberry, on the other hand, are more tightly sealed — 68% of Burberry’s sales come from its own stores, and it’s difficult to find its products at lower prices in department stores. Although Walmart is offering discounts on the iPhone 5, Apple largely controls retail prices.

They strive to prevent customers from finding more attractive options elsewhere and reward those who visit their stores. Their buildings are captivating (Burberry’s Regent Street store was once an old cinema), the staff are friendly, and sometimes there are live music performances, enhancing the immersive experience of trying on products.

Johnson and others have found that this magic is hard to replicate. Other retailers selling products while operating their own digital stores have seen their store revenues decline. Bain & Company partner Marc-André Kamel noted: “The growth of online sales exceeds industry growth, leading to cannibalization.”

Therefore, achieving integration, where the overall performance exceeds the sum of its parts, is critical. Burberry hasn’t fully achieved this yet; it doesn’t separately report online and in-store sales, and some analysts believe its online investments yield relatively meager returns. But Ahrendts and Bailey have extended the boundaries of their world beyond the stores.

Ahrendts' reward is a job at Apple, while Bailey will become Burberry's CEO. Willy Wonka couldn’t ask for more.
