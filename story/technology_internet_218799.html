Why can't I remember my English words?

by lk0cswa on 2009-08-06 06:35:19

I'm already in my third year of college, but my English vocabulary is only about 3000 words. I've passed the CET-4 twice, each time with a score around 40 points. I've consulted many people about methods for learning English, and most have suggested that I memorize more words. So, I spend all day memorizing words, but I find that my vocabulary hasn't increased much. I also plan to take the postgraduate entrance exam, so I must improve my English. Can anyone help me think of some ways to effectively remember more words? Thank you!

1. Choose the right book

1) The selected word book should not be too thick.

People tend to get bored easily with old things. If you keep reading the same book, you may feel annoyed and lose confidence because you don't see any progress or results. When memorizing from a thick word book, people often only manage to memorize the first few pages before giving up. The next time they try again, they start from the first page again. As a result, the first few pages of the word book are memorized fluently, but the latter part remains unknown. Furthermore, it usually takes a long time to finish such a book, so it's easy to forget what was learned earlier while studying later parts. This doesn't achieve the desired effect. If the book has an appropriate thickness, after finishing one book, you will feel a strong sense of accomplishment and satisfaction, which will encourage you to continue studying the second book or even re-read the first one to deepen your impression.

2) Repeatedly reviewing the same book can be very beneficial.

It allows the meaning and usage of the words to firmly root in the learner's mind, making them less likely to forget. Some might ask: Does repeatedly reviewing the same book waste time? In fact, when reviewing a word book for the first time, it may take two or three months, but the second time, since most of the words have been memorized and there are only a few unfamiliar ones, it may only take twenty to thirty days to review. The third time, it may only take seven or eight days. This method is much better than reviewing one book and then abandoning it for another. After all, if you only review once, the impression of the words won't be deep enough, leading to high forgetting rates. It's better to review multiple times, saving time and effort, and feeling more secure.

3) The selected word book should not be too difficult; it should match your English level.

We all know this experience: if you recognize three or four out of ten words in a book, it's relatively easy to memorize; if seven or eight out of ten words need to be remembered for the first time, it's more difficult; if all the words in a word book are unfamiliar to you, you might feel discouraged and give up. Therefore, choosing a vocabulary book that suits you is particularly important. Don't start by buying a very difficult and advanced vocabulary book, hoping to become an expert overnight. You can start with a more basic vocabulary book and gradually advance, which will yield better results.

4) The selected word book should have detailed definitions and example sentences.

For general English learners, knowing the Chinese definition of a word is sufficient. However, there is an important issue: words translated into Chinese can easily cause confusion. Many English words have subtle differences that aren't reflected in Chinese translations. For instance, many English words like "big," "great," "large," "huge," "immense," "giant," "vast," etc., are all translated as "big" in Chinese, but they actually differ. Therefore, corresponding example sentences are needed to help you remember their usage. There don't need to be many example sentences; one or two are enough.

5) Ideally, the book provides easier memory methods.

Memorizing words can be a dull task, but if you can find some interesting memory methods, it can become quite enjoyable.

a. Root-prefix-suffix memory

Example: paleoanthropologist (ancient anthropologist) - "paleo" means ancient, "anthropology" is human studies, "ist" indicates a type of expert.

b. Phonetic memory

Example: foment (instigate) - remember as "making steamed buns."

forge (to forge, counterfeit) - remember as "imitate."

insolent (arrogant and rude) - remember as "should be restrained."

c. Pictographic memory

Example: peep (sneak a peek) - the middle "ee" looks like two eyes, and the sides "p" look like open windows.

d. Contextual association memory

Example: husbandry (cultivation, management) - associate with male farming and female weaving.

wand (stick, baton, magic wand) - associate with the magic wand in round table knight games.

e. Story-based memory

Example: chauvinism (extreme patriotism) - derived from a play's main character Chauvin, who was a soldier of Napoleon, fervently admiring Napoleon and advocating conquering other nations by force.

procrustean (forcing conformity) - derived from Procrustes, a giant in Greek mythology, who would bind captured people to his bed, cutting off limbs of taller individuals and stretching shorter ones to make them the same length as the bed.

f. Unconventional memory

Example: Knowing "fort" means fortress, associate "actress" as female actor, consider "fortress" as mother fortress, thus remembering its meaning (fortress).

ferocious (fierce) - remember as "for meat," meaning carnivorous animals, naturally fierce.

farce (comedy) - break it down as "farmer + dance," imagine a group of farmers dancing wildly.
