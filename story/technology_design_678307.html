Nothing in life is certain... but take it easy.

Unintentional actions and intentional actions... From the perspective of behavioral results, are the differences really that great? Here's an example that isn't particularly interesting: those who intentionally continue reading versus those who unintentionally do so—how different are their experiences? The survey is ongoing... hehe.

1. Waterhead

On a Saturday morning in Shenzhen, it was overcast. Without any particular intention, I set off for the tip of Dapeng Peninsula. My usual route would take me through Dapeng and Nanao, then into Dongchong or Xichong (not Xiyong). On the way, I passed near Waterhead. Because I didn’t like the dining environment there, I stayed on the main road and continued forward, planning to have lunch in Nanao, even though my stomach was starting to feel a bit hungry.

Suddenly, I noticed that the road signs had been recently updated, indicating that one must turn left at the next intersection to reach Dongchong or Xichong. Following the sign, I ended up back at Waterhead, where I had no choice but to have lunch, despite not having planned to. Was this good fortune or bad?

2. Nail

What I disliked most about Waterhead were the seafood vendors and restaurant staff who aggressively solicited customers, obstructing the path. However, after returning, I discovered a suspicious nail embedded in the rear right tire of my car. I suspected it might be the handiwork of a disgruntled young worker from the restaurant outside Waterhead. Fortunately, two days later, while washing the car, I noticed the issue and safely drove to the 4S shop to resolve it. Was this good fortune or bad?

3. Road Repairs

While angrily criticizing the "error" on the road signs, I continued deeper into Nanao. Ahead, the road was under repair. The signs indicated that one must turn around and detour through Waterhead to reach Dongchong or Xichong. I cursed the previous sign, realizing the cost of being overly confident. Was this good fortune or bad?

At the fork leading to Dongchong and Xichong, I unintentionally chose Dongchong. The road was smooth, but soon enough, construction on the mountain road began. To the left, a dirt road; to the right, a sandy base. At that moment, I thought I should have chosen Xichong instead. Was this good fortune or bad?

4. Chinese Surfing Open

After navigating the bumpy roads, passing through the village, we arrived at the seaside. There were parking lots 30 meters to the left and 50 meters to the right. Someone opted for the closer option, only to find that parking and beach access were free. Was this good fortune or bad?

I lazily strolled along the beach and saw a group of black-clad surfers far out in the sea. Upon getting closer, I realized they were surfing enthusiasts, including foreign children and adults. Some were surfing, others drinking on the shore. I initially thought Dongchong had attracted Western tourists who usually resided in Thailand.

The wooden houses by the shore were beautiful (the other buildings were terrifying). Unintentionally, I decided to stay overnight, planning to visit Sanmen Island the next day. The hotel informed me that all the rooms in the wooden houses were fully booked. As I left dejectedly, I suddenly noticed a whiteboard at the entrance clearly stating in English, "2006 Chinese Dongchong Surfing Open." What a surprise! Despite the bumpy journey, it wasn't wasted. Was this good fortune or bad?

5. Sunshine

The wind was strong by the sea, but unlike what I had predicted—a cloudy city with clear skies by the coast—I felt somewhat disappointed. On the way to fetch the kite, it seemed as if heaven heard my earlier confident predictions and began to clear up. The temperature rose, feeling like a warm early summer day. Was this good fortune or bad?

As the kite took off, the wind shifted, and I started to get angry—why was the sun always shining directly into my eyes?

6. Kite

Under the unfavorable sunlight, the kite headed westward, swiftly pulling the reel. Nearly 2000 meters of string was almost fully extended. The orange Batman kite looked like a small dot, occasionally disappearing into the clouds. An eagle approached, observed, and then moved away. Staring blankly at the kite, I pondered the implications hidden within the clouds. Was this good fortune or bad?

Suddenly, I noticed that the Batman kite was unbalanced and had a tendency to spin (later estimated that it might be due to rain in the distant high altitude, wetting the edges of the wings and causing unequal wind-receiving areas on both sides, a similar case happened before). Reeling in the line required skill and was physically demanding—an absolute workout. It was more effective than gym training. Reeling in several thousand meters of line meant cranking the reel thousands of times. Rapid reeling in emergency situations was torturous or perhaps a trial. Was this good fortune or bad?

More troubling than the kite’s imbalance was the sudden drop in wind force. The motion of reeling in had already tortured (or trained) my shoulders, back, deltoids, upper arms, forearms, fingers, and palms to the point of soreness and weakness. Biting my teeth, I continued to rapidly turn the reel, continuing to torture myself (or train myself).

Who says that positive, optimistic stories generally have happy endings? After spinning, the kite fell into the crown of a distant tree. After some futile attempts, just like fishing, when it's time to cut the line, don't struggle uselessly. I only managed to recover over a thousand meters of string, making it the most painful personal case in my history. Goodbye, Batman, who had accompanied me for over three years. Was this good fortune or bad?

Searching for the next kite...
