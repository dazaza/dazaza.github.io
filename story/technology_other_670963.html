Coal grindability and its measurement methods

by hx515317 on 2012-02-21 14:05:50

The grindability of coal refers to the ease or difficulty with which coal can be ground into coal powder. Typically, a certain mining area's easily grindable bituminous coal is used as standard coal, and its grindability is set at 100. The larger the actual measured coal grindability index, the easier it is to crush; conversely, the more difficult it is to crush. Measuring the grindability of coal holds significant importance in some industrial sectors. For example, thermal power plants and cement factories that use powdered coal often need to measure coal grindability when designing and improving pulverizing systems, as well as estimating the production capacity and electricity consumption rate of coal mills. In the coke industry, where non-coking coal is primarily used, coal grindability must also be pre-measured to understand the pulverizability of the coal material, thereby determining the number of stages in the pulverizing system and the type of pulverizing equipment. Moreover, the grindability index of coal is an important datum in coal quality research. Coal grindability is related to factors such as the degree of coalification, coal lithotype composition, moisture content in coal, and the type, quantity, and distribution of minerals.

There are different methods for measuring coal grindability in the laboratory. China’s national standard (GB2565) and the international standard (ISO 5074) specify the Hardgrove method for measurement. The symbol representing Hardgrove grindability is HCI. The theoretical basis is the grinding law: the energy consumed during the grinding of coal powder is proportional to the total surface area of the coal after grinding. In practical measurement, this is indicated by a relative index derived from comparing the coal sample being tested with a standard coal sample.

The key points of the Hardgrove method are as follows: Weigh out 50g of coal sample with particle size ranging from 0.63 to 1.25mm, place it in a Hardgrove grindability tester containing eight steel balls, and let the grinding ring rotate at 20 ± 1 r/min for 3 minutes. Then sieve through a 0.074mm (200 mesh) sieve. The grindability index is calculated using the formula below based on the amount of coal remaining on the sieve:

HGI = 13 + 6.93(m - m1)

Where m is the mass of the coal sample (g), and m1 is the mass of the coal sample remaining on the 0.071 mm sieve after grinding (g).

The relationship between the Hardgrove Grindability Index (HGI) and the degree of coalification shows a parabolic change as the degree of coalification increases, reaching a maximum value when the carbon content on a dry ash-free basis (Cdaf) is 90%, at which point the coal is easiest to grind.

Both the grindability and brittleness of coal characterize the ease or difficulty of pulverizing coal. However, from the experimental methods, it can be seen that coal grindability involves grinding coal into fine powder, making this indicator more suitable for the pulverizing process of non-coking coal; whereas the brittleness of coal, determined by the anti-fracture strength method, has a particle size range closer to that of coking coal, making it more appropriate for evaluating coking coal.
