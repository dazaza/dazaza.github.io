A few experienced talks from DSP experts

by mylovehisa on 2008-07-17 13:38:46

I. I have been involved in DSP development for a few years now, and seeing many friends' strong interest in DSP development, I would like to share my personal insights based on these past few years of experience. These are just my opinions, and I welcome any feedback or corrections. Since the first day I started my postgraduate studies, I began working with DSP according to my supervisor's arrangement. At that time, DSP development was just starting in domestic universities, and a single DSP development kit cost nearly 10,000 yuan and still used an ISA bus. I started with the TMS320C206, then moved on to the C240 and C2407A, developing products based on these processors. I also studied the TMS320C5402, C2812, and C5471 during product planning and argumentation. Due to the limitations of my research direction, I did not have the opportunity to work with the 6X or 8X series.

In China, whether in companies or universities, many places often divide product development into two relatively independent parts: hardware and software, which are completed by different people. In companies with certain technical and management foundations, where there is a chief designer who can uniformly plan and coordinate tasks, this parallel completion method is feasible and conforms to modern product development patterns. However, in universities, personnel turnover is significant, the effective research time for graduate students is short, and their foundation is weak (many graduate students struggle with even basic concepts such as classification and selection of resistors, transistors, etc., which I also experienced). System planning and design are even more challenging. Moreover, many supervisors themselves do not fully understand DSP development, and senior students are preoccupied with their own tasks, often graduating before they fully grasp the subject. In many universities, DSP development involves finding an algorithm, embedding it into the main program, running it on the board, and considering the project complete once the basic effect is achieved. Reliability is secondary, and industrialization is rarely considered — this already counts as one of the better approaches.

I believe that the completion of a system depends most crucially on systematic planning. Knowledge and understanding of hardware design are decisive in determining what is feasible and what is not. When you also possess software design capabilities, you can reasonably allocate system functions, completing a top-down design process from system behavioral description using VHDL to system functional partitioning and substructure design. This allows you to become a system design expert or project manager, rather than just a hardware or software engineer. Whether you're working with the 8051, 80196, or DSP, the principle remains the same.

Below, I will separately discuss my feelings about hardware and software design:

Hardware design is the key to system design. The gap between domestic and foreign products is often determined by the level of hardware design. Any software design concept without a reliable physical carrier is like building castles in the air, mere theoretical speculation. Many graduate students in universities try to avoid hardware design; for a completely new design, it is less about disdain and more about fear. The pressure of burning a few chips far exceeds the stress of debugging several segments of code, especially when dealing with power devices. Once a chip burns out, it could result in sparks flying, and confidence is lost. Moreover, modifying a circuit board takes a long time, costs high, and there is no guarantee it will work.

In fact, even in foreign companies with average capabilities, hardware redesigns are avoided as much as possible. Once a product is set, upgrades are often made through software. This is a company development strategy. For individuals, hardware designers are rare and valuable, taking longer to cultivate and costing more than software designers.

When designing DSP hardware, start by designing a minimal system board. Divide the system by function and debug each part separately. Note that the stability of individual boards may not be as good as the whole board, so add more interference-resistant measures. Keep the wiring between divided boards, including power and ground lines, as short as possible, preferably within 10 cm. If necessary, add optical couplers for isolation and use isolated power supplies. Remember, interference in power and ground lines can harm the system much more than signal interference, yet this is often overlooked. The prerequisite for a circuit board to work normally is that the power supply is normal! After confirming that the divided circuits work correctly, then design the entire board circuit based on the situation. During debugging, any issues found must be resolved by identifying the root cause. Do not rely on fixing problems in the next iteration unless it is a fundamental design flaw. Prepare multiple backup plans for each functional step. Select the appropriate DSP model based on the system’s functionality. The TMS320C2000 series is a relatively full-featured controller but has lower computational performance, which is sufficient for most control systems, home appliances, and mid-to-low-level industrial bus communication products. The TMS320C281X series is excellent but expensive, and its development technology is not yet mature. The TMS320C54XX series is more like a coprocessor. The high-end TMS320C5471 is very good, with complete functionality, but its BGA packaging makes product development more challenging.
