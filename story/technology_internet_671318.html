The first blessing of the spring

by foot2050 on 2012-02-21 19:08:29

Earlier, we learned from the Nanjing Social Children's Welfare Institute that there are more than 200 children in the Nanjing Children's Welfare Institute. Most of them are disabled children under the age of 3. There are nearly a hundred childcare workers, and the children's lives are well taken care of. On normal days, many people from society come to visit the children, but before and after holidays, the number of visitors coming to see the children increases sharply. This includes many students who come for holiday activities during school breaks. The daily reception batches and number of people have exceeded capacity.

However, with the end of the holidays, the children in the welfare institute return to their usual peaceful lives. Our organization took us on this warm-hearted trip of kindness in spring, carrying the goodwill and gifts of our colleagues around us.

Early in the morning, we boarded the bus arranged by the company in advance and came to the welfare institute. We picked up eight children who were at the elementary school age and set off. The car quickly arrived at the Crescent Lake Children's Amusement Park, and we scattered with the children we were responsible for. I was in charge of a boy about six or seven years old. When I approached him in the crowd and tried to hold his hand, he turned his back and shook me off, still holding the hand of a little girl beside him. It wasn't until I got closer that I realized this child had a visual disability (amblyopia). After patiently accompanying him through several games, he finally let down his guard and opened his heart, tightly gripping my hand as we weaved through the bustling crowd. I discovered that this child named Mingxing actually had a very cheerful personality. I always tried to find queues with fewer people for him, and I would remind him every time we approached steps. But he wanted to show that he could see like a normal child, each time bouncing down the stairs after pausing. I knew that this was the pride of a young man. Later, I didn’t say anything anymore, just paused slightly before the stairs to hint at him. He was the first one to take my hand and lead me to look for what he wanted to play with. Following him all the way, I found out it was a rocking horse for toddlers. I was a bit surprised, thinking how a boy of his age could like such a game, so I hurried to find change to give him coins. While waiting for him nearby, I saw him happily looking at me and smiling. Only then did I realize that this might be the most fun game in his world. I dragged him to play a few more stimulating ones. It was clear that he was both excited and nervous. Before entering, he tightened his grip on my hand and said: Auntie, can you hold me? I felt both heartbroken and amused: Don't worry, Auntie is right here next to you; a man shouldn't be afraid of anything. The most interesting thing about Mingxing is that no matter what you say to him, he will answer: "Alright!"

While playing games with him, he wanted to go to the bathroom. He asked for fifty thousand yuan as a breakup fee and was rejected, leading to the killing of three members of his lover’s family. I sent him to the door, a little worried and asking if he was really okay. He grinned: "I can do it myself." Then he walked in the opposite direction. I laughed and pulled him back: "Wrong way! February 12, 2012—Is there any relevance!" He turned around and said: "Alright!" Time always flows like fine sand, the tighter you grab, the faster it leaks. When it was time to leave, he was somewhat reluctant. Fortunately, lunch was at McDonald's not far away, and our colleagues who arrived early had already prepared meals and toys for the children meticulously.

After lunch, the children returned to the welfare institute with the food and toys we gave them. As the car turned at the intersection, one of the boys said: "We're home." After getting off the elevator, they placed all the gifts they brought back on the large table at the entrance and went into their rooms to sleep. The teacher led us to tour the welfare institute. Their floor was where the elementary school students' dormitories were located. Below were the baby dormitories for ages 0-1 and 1-3. The air conditioning in the dorms was strong and warm. We tiptoed behind the teacher, peeking through the glass windows to see the children lying haphazardly on their beds in sleeping bags, their hands and feet always thrown outside without restraint. The teachers would check frequently, picking up the naughty babies and placing them back in the middle of their beds. Along the way, we saw a little girl. She was beautiful, with a head full of curly short hair, standing in her small bed holding the edge and looking at us with her big, expressive eyes, making me want to pick her up. Unable to resist, I asked the teacher: "What's wrong with this child? So cute, why don't her parents want her?" The teacher said: "Look at her right hand, she doesn't have any fingers." Through the bed frame, we noticed that she was actually using her left hand to support her small body while standing, with an empty sleeve on her right side. What the teacher said next broke our hearts even more: "Did you notice that the babies here, even when they wake up, won't cry out but instead play by themselves. Babies around one year old, upon seeing the aunties come to change their diapers, will lie down on their own..." Hearing this, some mothers with children couldn't help but shed tears, because deep inside us, we fully understood why they were so. Without the indulgence of their parents, the children became more understanding. On departure, I couldn't forget her gaze: "Can I adopt this child?" The teacher said: "If you want, we would suggest you consider other children who need more help, because children like her should be adopted soon." Yes, after all, the children we saw today are basically healthy children, but more attention and love are needed for those who cannot go out.

Walking out of the building, the afternoon sun warmly shone on us. More than once that day, I thought, if my child was born with a disability, would I abandon him here too? The children here have the care and assistance of the country, government, and all sectors of society, but their inner worlds deserve more of our attention. Silently bless them, hoping that they can grow up healthily like every normal child.
