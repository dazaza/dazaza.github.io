How Google's Jeff Dean Became a Legend in the Internet World

by anonymous on 2013-09-11 14:44:40

The Optimizer. How Google’s Jeff Dean became the Chuck Norris of the Internet.

"The speed of light in a vacuum used to be about 35 miles per hour, but then Jeff Dean spent a weekend optimizing fundamental physics." — From "Facts About Jeff Dean"

In reality, the G+ post describing "Facts About Jeff Dean" is not true. However, it's quite extraordinary that someone went through the trouble of creating a website similar to "Facts About Chuck Norris" for him. This is because Jeff Dean is a software engineer, and software engineers typically don’t resemble Chuck Norris, the martial arts legend. On one hand, they aren't lone rangers; software development is inherently a collaborative process. On the other hand, they never use machine guns to shoot cowboys like in this video.

Note: Chuck Norris (查克·诺里斯) is a world champion in karate and an American film actor. He is more commonly known as “罗礼士” from the kung fu movie "The Way of the Dragon," where he played the role of a karate expert named Colt who battled Bruce Lee in the Roman Colosseum, a scene widely regarded as a classic in martial arts cinema. (From Wikipedia)

Jeff Dean

However, on April Fool's Day in 2007, some young Google engineers decided to create a website dedicated to Jeff Dean to celebrate his achievements in programming (Translator: The following are all jokes and not real). For example:

- Compilers don’t give warnings to Jeff Dean; instead, Jeff Dean gives warnings to compilers.

- Jeff Dean writes binary code directly and then writes source code as documentation for other developers.

- When Jeff Dean considers ergonomics, it's to protect his keyboard.

- One day, while optimizing a feature, Jeff Dean was forced to invent asynchronous API calls so that the feature could return results before it was even called.

A true fact about Jeff Dean is that you need to be a computer expert to understand many of the jokes people tell about him. (Readers interested can find explanations of some popular jokes about him on Business Insider.) Without a background in computer science, it would be difficult to grasp the fictional achievements attributed to him in these jokes, let alone appreciate his real accomplishments at work. Systems like MapReduce, BigTable, Spanner, which Dean personally helped build, may be unknown to many Google users, but these programs form the bedrock of Google and modern internet infrastructure. Some of the projects he is currently working on might once again revolutionize information technology.

When thinking about who created today's internet, you might immediately think of founders and CEOs such as Tim Berners-Lee, Marc Andreessen, Larry Page, Sergey Brin, or perhaps Mark Zuckerberg. This makes sense, as each of these individuals invented products or frameworks that have shaped how we use the internet today.

Meanwhile, in the shadows of these giants who have stepped away from daily operational tasks, there is a group of relatively unknown developers who daily type away at their keyboards, producing the products and systems we use. Unlike other industries, in high-tech, these individuals are often irreplaceable. An outstanding accountant might save you 5% on your personal income tax. A great baseball player might have a slightly higher on-base percentage than average players. But the work of an exceptional software developer in a week might take a team of nearly 10 people months to complete. This difference is exponential. This isn't a "fact about Jeff Dean," but rather a common understanding in Silicon Valley's tech industry, which explains why top companies go to great lengths to attract top talent.

When Dean joined Google in mid-1999, he was already recognized as one of America's top young computer scientists. In the early days of home computers, Dean said he was always looking for ways to push the performance limits of a given machine. As a high school student, he wrote software to analyze large amounts of epidemiological data. According to him, his software was 26 times faster than the professional software at the time. This system, called Epi Info, was adopted by the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and translated into 13 languages. While pursuing his Ph.D. in computer science, he studied compilers, which translate program source code into machine-executable language. He said, "I've always liked fast-running code."

But Dean wasn't content to stay in one place or spend his whole life on compilers, so he eventually left academia. Less than three years later, he joined Google, which at the time had only 20 employees. (According to Steven Levy's book "In the Plex," Google, as a search startup, considered Dean a hard-to-come-by talent.) He made significant contributions to the early versions of Google News and AdSense, with the latter product rewriting the rules of the game for internet companies. Afterward, he turned his focus to one of the company's core issues: scalability.

The initial ideas behind Google's foundational algorithms came from Page and Brin, both top-tier developers at the time. In the late '90s, they developed the PageRank algorithm, which returned the most relevant search results based on user queries. Their focus on relevance catapulted Google ahead of competitors like Yahoo! and AltaVista. However, as Google grew more successful, it faced a massive technical challenge. Dean recalled, "We couldn't deploy machines fast enough to keep up with demand."

So Dean, along with colleagues including another outstanding programmer Sanjay Ghemawat, found solutions. This problem, much like his high school work with Epi Info, appeared to be hardware-related. Ghemawat led a team that developed the Google File System (GFS), allowing extremely large files to be stored across numerous inexpensive servers. Then Dean and Ghemawat developed a programming tool called MapReduce, helping developers efficiently utilize these machines to process vast datasets in parallel. Just as compilers allow programmers to write code without worrying about how CPUs handle it, MapReduce enabled Google developers to adjust search algorithms or add new features without worrying about parallelization or hardware failures.

Dean and Ghemawat's method was so powerful that after presenting a research paper at a conference in 2004, it quickly became an industry standard. Even today, MapReduce serves as a cornerstone for many other projects, including the famous open-source framework Hadoop, which has spawned the buzzword "big data" across various fields, from online travel to energy exploration. As Google begins moving beyond MapReduce to newer programming models in some core applications, Dean noted that many summer interns still heavily rely on MapReduce when starting new projects at Google.

MapReduce is a good example of what Page refers to as the "10x effect," meaning something ten times better, not just 10% better. MapReduce didn't just make a specific type of computation faster; it allowed every Google developer to do things they couldn't previously accomplish.

Several of Dean's other projects also exhibit a similar exponential effect. Building on the Google File System, he and Ghemawat created a distributed database system called BigTable, capable of handling 1PB of data (1PB = 10 million GB). They further developed Spanner, described as the world's largest single database system. Case Metz of Wired magazine said that through innovative time synchronization, Spanner operates as if its physical storage were in one location, even though it spans multiple global data centers. In other words, it ensures consistency across different pieces of information in global data centers, even if a particular update request takes varying amounts of time to reach different centers. Metz added that before Spanner was reported on, no one believed such a system could be built.

Nowadays, these true facts about Jeff Dean seem almost too good to be true. Dean himself chuckles at this situation, saying it's a bit awkward but also flattering. However, he emphasizes that all his real accomplishments have been achieved through collaboration with others.

Almost every morning, he goes to the Google headquarters in Mountain View, California, and sits down with the same group of people for coffee. He estimates that over the years, they've collectively consumed around 20,000 cappuccinos. These people don't always work together. In fact, some have moved to different offices on the other side of the Google campus. Nevertheless, when they gather to discuss what they're doing, questions from one person often spark new ideas in others. These coffee chats allow Dean to apply his expertise in optimization, parallel computing, and software architecture to a wide variety of projects, giving him the ambition and confidence to tackle them. As his long-term collaborator Ghemawat put it, "He's always enthusiastic and optimistic about what we can achieve, and nothing stops him."

His recent work provides a good indication of what Google will do next. Last year, he worked with Andrew Ng, a Stanford expert in machine learning and co-founder of Coursera, helping Ng's graduate student Quoc Le conduct an unprecedented experiment in unsupervised machine learning. Part of Google's secret X Lab project, the experiment involved using 16,000 processors to learn from YouTube videos autonomously, identifying cats. Although it seems like a lot of computers to produce a basic result, this experiment lays the groundwork for the next generation of artificial intelligence technologies, which could play roles in potential applications like Google Now's personal assistant technology and image search functionalities beneficial to the Google Glass project.

Jeff Dean might really invent something incredible, like the special keyboard with only 0s and 1s mentioned in "Facts About Jeff Dean" (Translator: Such a keyboard doesn't exist; the webpage humorously suggests that Jeff Dean writes programs directly in binary machine code). Dean admitted he isn't an expert in machine learning but enjoys applying his experience in building scalable, highly available systems to help advance this field.

Contrary to "Facts About Jeff Dean," Dean says that the best way to solve problems isn't always to sit down and start coding immediately. His approach usually involves performing some simple calculations first to find the optimal balance between quality and speed for a specific process. He explained that in many areas, from machine translation to search quality, you're always trying to weigh the amount of computation you can perform per query. You may not find the perfect solution, but you can often get 98% of the benefits with just 1% of the computation through some approximation.

Dean frequently performs these kinds of calculations, leading him to compile a list of "numbers every computer engineer should know." These include figures like how many milliseconds it takes to send a network packet from California to Amsterdam at the speed of light (150 milliseconds). Keeping these numbers in mind allows you to distinguish the best design among three options within 20 minutes on a whiteboard. He added that if you can't compute quickly, approximate all these numbers as powers of two to make multiplication easier.

If Dean truly possessed superhuman powers, then this ability wouldn't be about instantly achieving perfection. It's about having the capacity to evaluate, optimize, and handle problems at different levels. Alternatively, it's about recognizing opportunities and making things as good as possible in a short time rather than striving for perfection right from the start. In Silicon Valley, this is far cooler than wielding machine guns against cowboys.
