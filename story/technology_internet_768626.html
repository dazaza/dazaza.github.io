How Much Longer Will Microsoft Have a Free Internet Ride?

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 20:15:20

For many years, Microsoft Office has dominated the office software market, bringing the company plenty of attention and profit. Now, Google Docs has emerged as a real threat to Microsoft. With years of experience in providing cloud services and the advantage of offering free software, Google Docs may surpass Microsoft Office in the next decade.

Microsoft's Real Threat

In recent years, Microsoft has made substantial profits from the sales of its Office software.

However, over the past year, subtle changes have begun to occur. Microsoft has started allowing users to access Office through the cloud and web downloads, regardless of the type of computer they use. In the past week, Office even added real-time, collaborative document editing features to its free version, Office Web Apps.

So, why is Microsoft going to all the trouble of turning its offline Office cash cow into a cheaper cloud service? The blame lies with Google.

When Google launched its document processing software Docs in 2006, it was initially treated as a "novelty." At that time, cloud services had not yet become a popular lifestyle, and there was little support for Microsoft's Office document formats. However, over the past few years, Google Docs has been steadily improving; now, it has become an extremely useful and increasingly popular high-quality software.

Google Docs is no longer seen as a "novelty;" it has become a real threat to Microsoft.

While Google's office suite may lack some advanced features of Microsoft Office, it is simpler and easier to use—especially in terms of its cloud-related functions.

Of course, Microsoft remains a giant in the office software industry. Microsoft claims that Office is installed on more than one billion computers. According to estimates by market research firm Gartner, Office held a 90% share of the enterprise market in 2012.

Advantage in the Cloud

But when it comes to cloud services, the situation is different.

In the next decade, Google could emerge as the true winner. Gartner estimates that within the next ten years, 1.2 billion people will use office software services, but more than half of them will opt for cloud-based office software. Gartner's research also indicates that Google is rapidly gaining market share in the cloud services sector, with its current market share reaching as high as 50%.

Google recently announced that 120 million users are currently using Google Drive (which includes Docs services), and five million businesses and organizations are using Google Apps (a paid service).

In September this year, Microsoft sold two million copies of Office 365 Home Premium, which runs on five different devices simultaneously. Additionally, Microsoft claims that 60% of Fortune 500 companies have purchased the enterprise version of Office 365. It also stated that 50 million users are currently using Office Web Apps.

However, Google's office suite is quickly becoming the standard for technology startups, small businesses, and emerging large enterprises. Moreover, demographic trends favor Google. People who grew up with the internet instinctively choose free software, saving their documents in the cloud for easy sharing and collaboration.

Office is gradually losing its status as premium software. Gradually, people will only consider using Office when they need specific document formats or deal with people who exclusively use Office.

Even this point is changing. Quickoffice, acquired by Google in 2012, can effectively convert documents between the two office suites. Furthermore, users can use Docs offline.

According to a Google spokesperson, the company's goal is not to align with Microsoft Office software but to work towards eliminating document format barriers, though it still aims to attract most traditional Office users.

The Allure of Free Software

Google can offer free office software, something Microsoft cannot do. Google's office software generates revenue through online advertising. Its goals are broader. It provides services like Docs to draw users into its more profitable offerings, such as Gmail, search, Chrome, and maps.

However, Office remains Microsoft's cash cow. Office sales account for about one-third of the company's total annual revenue. Office 365 charges $100 annually, generating $1.5 billion per quarter. Therefore, it is difficult for Microsoft to make Office free.

Moreover, Microsoft faces competition not only from Google but also from Apple, which recently launched its own cloud-based iWork office software. Additionally, the iOS version of this software is free.

Currently, Office remains the undisputed leader. However, changes are quietly taking place. Microsoft may already realize that it cannot rely solely on its existing installed base to maintain stability. In fact, Microsoft should know this from experience.

In the realm of web browsers, Microsoft's IE browser has seen its market share eroded as Firefox and the later-rising Chrome have taken center stage. In the enterprise market, Windows Mobile has become a stepping stone for Apple's iOS and Google's Android, much like BlackBerry.

This war is far from over and will continue to spread to the cloud. In this battle, Google, with years of experience providing cloud consumer services, already has a home-field advantage.
