Dongguan Texiu Trading Co., Ltd. South China Morning Post: A nationwide phenomenon of calcium supplementation for infants and young children is currently taking place in our country. Experts:

by ekucc152 on 2012-02-21 15:51:50

According to a report by China National Radio's "News纵横", a recent survey shows that within the country, nearly 90% of parents will follow a doctor's advice and start giving their babies calcium supplements before they reach six months old. That is to say, in the eyes of doctors, almost all children in China lack calcium. Moreover, there are many advertisements for calcium supplements in various media, which also exaggerate the dangers of calcium deficiency in a very alarming way. Given China's current standard of living, it is unlikely that 90% of infants suffer from malnutrition. Many people are puzzled: normal full-term babies grow up healthy through breastfeeding and drinking formula milk powder, so why do they always show calcium deficiency when tested? The widespread calcium deficiency among Chinese children must indicate some underlying problem. Is this issue due to a cognitive misunderstanding or interest-driven motives?

Mother: Baby was told at one month old that calcium supplementation was necessary; I am confused.

Xiao Xu is a young mother whose child is just over a month old. Recently, she has been worried about whether to give her child calcium supplements. Originally, she thought her child, being a full-term baby with normal physical indicators, did not need calcium supplements. However, seeing other children around her taking supplements and hearing the same advice from doctors, she began to hesitate.

Xiao Xu: When I was discharged from the hospital, they gave me several boxes of Gaidixi effervescent calcium powder and instructed me to start giving it to my child after one month. At the time, I felt that my child did not lack calcium, so I didn't give it to him. After exchanging information with other mothers from different hospitals, I found that these things were given out universally upon discharge.

Li Yi, who had just returned from abroad, also faced the same issue. She had always lived in the U.S., and her daughter was five months old. In the U.S., during each physical examination, everything was fine, but once back in China, she was told by the doctor that her child needed calcium supplements.

Li Yi: Abroad, we never supplemented calcium, and no one mentioned anything about needing it. They said that pillow baldness was caused by rubbing against the pillow, not due to calcium deficiency. And when I asked if we should supplement anything, my domestic mom friends all suggested doing so. He (the doctor) said there’s no need, as giving a child too much might not be good. But after returning, when I went to our community health center for vaccinations, the doctor directly asked if we were supplementing calcium. I said no, and he replied that we should start considering certain calcium supplements.

Experts: Universal calcium supplementation for infants, I am concerned.

Professor Li Keji from the Department of Nutrition at Peking University School of Public Health and his students conducted a survey in urban Beijing among 218 parents. Among them, 202 were giving their children calcium supplements. 67% of the babies started calcium supplementation before four months, and by six months, 86% of the babies were receiving calcium supplements, mostly based on a doctor's recommendation. So, have Chinese children really experienced such a large-scale calcium deficiency? Do all children need to start calcium supplementation at such a young age? The reporter interviewed multiple authoritative experts, all of whom expressed concern about the current universal calcium supplementation situation.

Professor Su Yixiang, Deputy Director of the Chinese Maternal and Child Nutrition Society: If a child has enough breast milk or sufficient formula milk powder, we do not recommend calcium supplementation. In some remote rural areas, if a child no longer has access to breast milk or formula milk powder, we would consider appropriately supplementing some calcium. If too much calcium is consumed, it needs to be excreted through the kidneys, undoubtedly increasing the burden on the kidneys, especially when a child's kidneys are not yet fully developed. From a safety perspective, we do not recommend excessive calcium supplementation.

Professor Li Keji from the Department of Nutrition at Peking University School of Public Health: If a child is breastfeeding and still receives calcium supplements, that is a great misunderstanding. Based on the panic, misinterpretation, or misleading information regarding calcium nutrition in China over the past decade or so, issues like pillow baldness, night crying, and excessive sweating are merely symptoms. Other causes can also lead to these symptoms; it's not necessarily a cause-and-effect relationship. It's only correlated, and some risks may take decades to become apparent.

Since blind calcium supplementation is not scientific, why has it become a common phenomenon for infants? When "calcium supplementation" evolves into a social phenomenon, the truth behind it becomes more complex, intriguing many to seek answers. During interviews, many parents told reporters that they supplemented calcium because of a doctor's suggestion, while others did so because everyone around them was doing it, fearing their child would fall behind from the start. At the Beijing Obstetrics and Gynecology Hospital, many mothers who had just given birth or brought their 40-day-old babies for follow-up visits reported that doctors prescribed calcium carbonate powder upon discharge, instructing them to start giving it to their children after one month. These children were all normal full-term babies.

Reporter: Did the doctor mention calcium supplementation for such young children?

Parent: Yes, all children need it. The doctor recommended it upon discharge, specifying which weeks to start.

Reporter: Did the doctor mention calcium supplementation during today's follow-up visit?

Parent: He asked if we wanted calcium powder. Since we already had it, we knew the child must have it.

Reporter: Is the child exclusively breastfed?

Parent: Yes.

A strong doctor recommendation for calcium supplementation may hide an interest chain.

The reporter registered as a parent and saw a pediatrician who firmly stated that calcium supplementation was necessary.

Reporter: For breastfeeding or mixed feeding, is calcium supplementation necessary?

Doctor: Necessary.

Reporter: At what age should calcium supplementation begin?

Doctor: Generally, it starts around one month old, theoretically speaking.

The doctor's requirement for calcium supplementation starting at one month could stem from differing understandings and specific diagnoses, but one incident caught the reporter's attention. A mother told the reporter:

Mother: During the 42-day follow-up visit, I mentioned the situation and added that I read online that calcium supplements might cause constipation in children. The doctor then wrote a note prescribing a specific brand of calcium lactate, instructing us to buy it from a small shop near the hospital entrance.

The reporter encountered a similar situation, first noticing a previous parent holding a small note prescribing the calcium brand mentioned by the mother, purchased from a small shop near the hospital entrance. The reporter also received the same note when consulting about calcium supplementation.

Reporter: Did you see the calcium you just prescribed? Is it good?

Doctor: Our type of calcium carbonate, each prescription can only be for one box. Relatively speaking, it is slightly more expensive, but the effect is very good. This needs to be bought at the small shop near the entrance, as it is not stocked in our pharmacy.

Objectively speaking, not all doctors encourage calcium supplementation for babies. For example, it was reported that at Chongqing Medical University Affiliated Children's Hospital, many expert clinics had a sign specifically stating, "Milk products contain an appropriate amount of calcium for children, no additional calcium supplementation is necessary." Some parents' "better safe than sorry" attitude towards their children's nutritional status, combined with some manufacturers' aggressive marketing and exaggerated advertisements, have contributed to the universal calcium supplementation phenomenon among infants in contemporary China. Experts point out that the medical community has not yet reached a conclusion on the harm of moderate calcium supplementation for babies, as this situation only occurs in China and its effects may take many years to become apparent. However, is it necessary for our children to take such risks? This is a question every parent should consider.

Reported by Yang Chao, February 21st, Beijing, China National Radio.
