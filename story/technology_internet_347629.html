Property market policies and RMB exchange rate will influence the later market.

by ww0910844 on 2010-04-18 14:00:57

■ Special to "Red Weekly", Minzu Securities Xu Yiding With the launch of stock index futures, the Shanghai and Shenzhen stock markets are also attempting a style switch. Although there was a brief rise in large-cap blue-chip stocks, before and after the introduction of stock index futures contracts, the much-anticipated rally in large-cap blue-chip stocks did not materialize. On the contrary, on the first day of trading of the CSI 300 futures contract, large-cap blue-chip stocks experienced a significant decline. I believe that it will be difficult for the style switch in the second quarter to shift to large-cap blue-chip stocks. The biggest uncertainty affecting the A-share market currently is the new round of real estate policies and the RMB exchange rate.

Recently, SASAC, CBRC, and the Ministry of Land and Resources have continuously released policy signals. I believe this marks the beginning of a new round of strong real estate regulation. Under this backdrop, I suggest everyone pay attention to: 1) If regulatory policies are too stringent, will they affect the real economy and drag down the performance of related listed companies? 2) Will funds flowing out of the real estate sector impact the A-share market?

In March, RMB credit increased by 510.7 billion yuan. By the end of March, M1 grew year-on-year by 29.94%, and M2 grew by 22.50%. The month-on-month growth of M1 and M2 continued to decline. Currently, the balance of liquidity is still tilting towards tightening. The ratio of the circulating market value of A-shares to the balance of household savings is 56.58%, meaning household savings are 1.77 times the circulating market value. Household savings do not support: 1) A sharp rise in the SSE Composite Index; 2) A style switch to large-cap blue-chip stocks.

Historical experience shows that during a bull market, the decline of strong sectors and leading stocks often precedes an adjustment in the index. On April 15th, apart from Dinglong Shares and Taiji Shares, all other 63 Growth Enterprise Market (GEM) stocks turned red, with 44 of them falling more than 5%. Previously active small and medium-sized stocks, as well as the smart grid, Internet of Things, Hainan, and Tibet sectors, led the declines. Many small and medium-sized stocks have shown high-volume volatility, particularly among the leaders like Shenzhou Ta Yue, which after a cumulative maximum increase of 136%, exhibited significant fluctuations. These signs are worth paying attention to.

On April 16th, for companies that have already announced their 2009 earnings, the overall P/E ratios for all A-shares and the CSI 300 Index were 24.31 times and 19.85 times, respectively. If the net profit of listed companies grows by 20% in 2010, the expected P/E ratios based on 2010 earnings for all A-shares and the CSI 300 Index would be 20.27 times and 16.54 times, respectively. From the perspective of full circulation, the overall valuation level of A-shares is not cheap, and it's currently difficult to find industries or individual stocks whose performance exceeds expectations. Even bank stocks, which have relatively low P/E ratios, face issues such as tightening credit and a potential sharp rise in non-performing loans.

Currently, the A-share market is experiencing internal capital recycling. In the second quarter, A-share prices will continue structural adjustments. The trend of small and medium-sized stocks will diverge, and strategic emerging industry stocks with relatively low absolute prices will still attract attention. The previously underperforming regional planning sector may fill the current hotspot gap. Additionally, undervalued large-cap blue-chip stocks have opportunities for catch-up gains, but the strength of pulse-like rises will depend on the attitude of funds withdrawing from real estate and overseas hot money. ■ There are currently 0 comments.
