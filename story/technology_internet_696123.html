A Brief Discussion on the Construction of Middle School Students' Sense of Social Responsibility under the Background of New Curriculum

by cxxzsty on 2012-03-07 20:49:27

**Keywords**: Social responsibility, New curriculum background, High school students, Human rationality, Moral education, Sound personality, Real human being, Marx

**Abstract**: As the foundation and starting point of all virtues, social responsibility is a concentrated manifestation of human rationality and conscience, and the basis for the continuation of society. The sense of social responsibility among high school students is at the core of the moral education goals under the new curriculum framework. A truly useful person to society should first be someone with a healthy and sound personality, someone who is responsible. Marx and Engels once pointed out: "As definite and real people, you have regulations, missions, and tasks." In the educational report submitted by UNESCO for the 21st century, it was emphasized that "21st-century education must not only enable students to acquire knowledge and skills but also emphasize their ability to become good human beings. Modern societal development requires students to become individuals with a sense of social responsibility and dedication, scientific and cultural knowledge, and pioneering capabilities, as well as those who are ambitious and morally upright." Under the context of the new curriculum, the construction of social responsibility among high school students requires the joint efforts of every educator.

1. **Respecting student subjectivity and enhancing social responsibility awareness through self-education**

Social responsibility is an emotional system that expands and sublimates gradually from oneself to others, from inner feelings to outward actions. The new curriculum emphasizes "student-centered development," focusing on developing students' autonomy and initiative. The key to fostering a sense of social responsibility lies in enabling students to start by taking responsibility for themselves and strengthening self-education. The extent to which students engage in self-awareness, self-experience, self-evaluation, self-control, and self-motivation largely determines the success or failure of responsibility education. It is difficult to imagine that someone who does not take responsibility for themselves would possess a developed sense of social responsibility. Soviet educator Sukhomlinsky believed that "without self-education, there is no true education." He argued that "education cannot always lead by the hand; instead, it must encourage independence, making students responsible for themselves and forming their own attitudes toward life. Students should cultivate their emotions, train their thoughts and willpower, and form and stabilize their character." The formation of responsibility consciousness occurs through repeated internalization and external expression. The reform of the new curriculum values respect for students because "respect is the acknowledgment of individual dignity and the basic condition for its realization, as well as the fundamental condition for the development of the self." It allows students "to judge their purposes in every situation, evaluate various values in life, make autonomous choices, be aware of both external and internal forces, and take responsibility for the consequences of their actions." The cultivation of social responsibility under the new curriculum should never come at the expense of students' individuality and independence. Without respect, genuine social responsibility cannot be nurtured. Trusting students, believing in the existence of goodness in human nature, and recognizing the power of individual personality development fundamentally opposes indoctrination and coercion. Instead, it advocates equal dialogue, inspiration, and guidance. Responsibility is not something imposed externally but rather a response to one's own concerns. Advocating independent individuality, respecting students' rights to develop and pursue happiness, and affirming the value, dignity, and independence of each individual is the foundation of moral education under the new curriculum and a prerequisite for cultivating social responsibility.

2. **Developing the emotional incentive function of 'hidden curricula'**

The new curriculum emphasizes students' emotional experiences, expanding education beyond classroom teaching to discover unexpected pathways and beautiful, unseen landscapes. Through this gradual process, students' dormant potential can be awakened, sparking moments of insight. Stimulating students' awareness of social responsibility through hidden curricula represents the best embodiment of moral education under the new curriculum framework. Renowned Chinese scholar Ban Hua defines "hidden curricula" as "indirect, implicit educational influences inside and outside the classroom that act through unconscious and non-specific psychological reactions of the educated." Hidden curricula subtly influence students, penetrating deeply into their inner worlds, permeating their hearts, and arousing their moral emotions. Such arousal has immense motivational power. Only when we truly recognize ourselves as members of society—"my spiritual and material lives depend on the labor of others (both living and deceased), and I must repay them in equal measure"—will we feel gratitude toward our parents, teachers, families, society, and country, viewing the sharing and contribution of love as a natural process. We will learn to cherish, create, and contribute love, aspiring to become responsible citizens beneficial to society. Schools under the new curriculum must vigorously develop their own hidden curricula, utilizing their functions of cultivation, guidance, motivation, and normative constraints to serve the cultivation of students' social responsibility. In a sense, this supplements and extends the educational functions that explicit curricula cannot reach. For example, the school's mission and philosophy, though not part of the explicit curriculum, serve as the "constitutional heart" of the institution, representing the most important latent hidden curriculum. Students working together, experiencing challenges, gaining exposure to the world, making friends, handling affairs, striving for shared ideals, and fighting for collective honor all stimulate moral emotions and help them understand their responsibilities to the school, group, and peers. The influence of hidden curriculum resources on the cultivation of students' social responsibility is omnipresent. Under the new curriculum, all of society should work together to create the best possible "hidden curriculum" environment for students.
