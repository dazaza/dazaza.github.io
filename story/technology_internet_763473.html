It's 2013, How Bad Is Android Smartphone Fragmentation?

by anonymous on 2013-08-02 10:13:51

The fragmentation of Android may be an old topic, and everyone probably knows that the mobile operating system based on Android released by each manufacturer is more or less different. However, in reality, how much do we intuitively understand about the situation of Android fragmentation? The charts drawn by OpenSignal make it clear at a glance.

The following chart is obtained from the perspective of devices, one is the situation in July 2012, and the other is the situation in July 2013. When comparing these two charts together, it is found that the current situation of Android fragmentation has become more serious. Take Samsung's phones as an example; despite the company releasing the latest flagship Galaxy S4, a considerable number of people are still using the previous flagship models Galaxy S3 and Galaxy S2. In fact, the number of Android models this year is 11,868, while last year the number of Android models was 3,997, which is three times as much.

The chart above shows the fragmentation of Android devices in July 2012.

The chart above shows the fragmentation of Android devices in July 2013.

Dividing by brand, who exactly causes Android fragmentation. From the quantity point of view, Samsung undoubtedly releases the most Android devices. This chart also counts the number of Android models launched by the four domestic phone manufacturers "ZTE, Huawei, Coolpad, Lenovo." Among them, Huawei launched the most Android models, followed by ZTE, then Lenovo, and finally Coolpad. From the market share perspective, Samsung undoubtedly holds the largest share, accounting for 47.5%, nearly half. The market shares held by other Android manufacturers did not exceed 10% — meaning that in the Android market, Samsung dominates alone.

After John Sculley left Apple, he wrote a book called "Odyssey: From Pepsi to Apple," summarizing the rules of commercial competition: generally, the global market cannot accommodate fewer than three main competitors. The industry leader usually occupies sixty to seventy percent of the market, the second player usually takes twenty to thirty percent, and the remaining competitors fight for the remaining ten percent of the market.

Looking at the results of this chart, Samsung's market share has not reached 70%. The second batch of competitors such as Sony, Motorola, HTC, etc., each hold less than 10% of the market share, suggesting that the smartphone market still has some competitive space — looking globally, emerging markets like India and Brazil are gradually becoming the focus of competition among phone manufacturers, and the share in these key markets may further influence the future market share of phone manufacturers.

The chart below looks at the fragmentation of Android from the perspective of Android system versions. It can be seen that from Android 2.3.3 to Android 4.0, the time in between was roughly six months, yet many phones have not been updated or supported new versions of the operating system. After that, Android 4.1 and 4.2 came one after another — even within the Android 4.x family, there is a situation where "three generations coexist." Therefore, the problem of compatibility between different operating systems becomes quite prominent.

However, what troubles developers the most is not the API but screen size. Someone previously counted that just Samsung alone has released Android devices with 26 different sizes. Thus, other manufacturers, considering differentiation, have definitely launched Android devices with various screen sizes and ratios, which are certainly far more than 26 kinds.

If analyzing why Android is so fragmented, we cannot ignore market factors. Just domestically, the low-end Android models launched to stimulate market sales are countless. Due to hardware limitations, these low-end Android devices naturally come equipped with relatively older versions of the Android operating system. They have made significant contributions to expanding Android's market share but have also made the issue of Android fragmentation difficult to resolve.

BTW: All the charts drawn by OpenSignal are interactive, and the original data download is also available.
