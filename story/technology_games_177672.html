Who's influencing the dream economy?

by lk0gamee on 2009-07-16 13:45:49

Dreamland has been through 5 years so far, and every week's updates can be seen as macroeconomic adjustments for Dreamland (such as selling point cards to draw divine beasts, obtaining contributions from the three realms, etc.). Players have been constantly criticizing the planners, and they also deeply hate the game workshops. So, who is influencing the economy of Dreamland? Who is making Dreamland go downhill?

Firstly, let's talk about NetEase's revenue. One hour equals 4 points, which is 0.4 yuan (long-term players from the public beta might know that it was initially 5 points per hour, then it was changed). The consumers of Dreamland can be divided into two phases.

The first phase is when Dreamland started to become popular. Consumers were divided into two groups: 1. Those who spent RMB to buy point cards for top-up, and then sold point cards, etc. (what everyone calls the prototype of RMB players); 2. Those who understood the content and gameplay of Dreamland, had relatively ample time, and used in-game money (MHB) to buy point cards for top-up. These two groups complemented and helped each other. NetEase earned money from point card sales mainly from the first group. At that time, the first group accounted for a large portion (online purchasing of MHB was not yet popular at that time, and the main way to exchange MHB was through point card consignment sales, so the price of point cards was relatively stable, ensuring the existence of the second group of players). The economy of Dreamland was calm and the game was thriving.

The second phase started at the beginning of 2007. At this time, consumers were divided into three groups: 1. Those who spent RMB to buy point cards for top-up, and also purchased a large amount of Dreamland coins from platforms like 5173 and Taobao (RMB players were formed); 2. The emergence of game workshops. When Dreamland started to become popular, a group of people with foresight and who treated it as a business appeared. They began to produce a large amount of MHB (through activities like courier missions and flower planting), and then sold them on 5173 and Taobao. The proportion of MHB they sold was much higher than point card consignment sales, directly leading to the first group no longer consigning point cards; 3. What everyone calls career players, those with plenty of time, and a batch of loyal fans of Dreamland. At this time, MHB depreciated greatly, and the exchange rate of point cards to MHB skyrocketed. From 120W for 150 point cards, to now 170W. This is something career players cannot bear.
