Tour de France sees two fatal accidents within two days: a competitor shot and a fan run over to death

by kaxiya on 2009-07-20 15:30:15

Gendarmes guide the riders of the Tour de France away from the scene of a car accident.

First, a rider was shot, then a fan was run over and killed.

The Tour de France witnesses two fatal tragedies within two days.

After years of consecutive doping scandals, the organizing committee of the Tour de France desperately wishes to present a scandal-free race to the world. However, it is extremely difficult to avoid any blemishes in a 23-day competition. At 14:35 local European time on July 18th, during the 14th stage of the Tour de France, a 61-year-old woman was struck and killed by a police motorcycle that was leading the way.

According to French media reports, the accident occurred 38 kilometers away from the starting point of the 14th stage in Colmar, at Wittersheim. At the time, police motorcycles following behind the lead group were clearing the way for the second group of riders when a 61-year-old woman suddenly appeared on the rural road. The motorcycle could not avoid her in time, resulting in a head-on collision, and the woman died instantly. Subsequently, the out-of-control motorcycle collided with two fans watching by the roadside, injuring a 37-year-old woman's back and fracturing the leg of a 61-year-old man. It is reported that at the time of the accident, the 37-year-old woman was holding a 1-year-old baby in her arms, but fortunately, the baby was unharmed.

French police spokesperson Alain stated, "The specific cause of the accident is still under investigation. The two injured fans are currently receiving treatment in the hospital, and their lives are not in danger. However, according to our preliminary understanding, the motorcycle driver was not negligent." According to eyewitnesses at the scene, the elderly woman who died had repeatedly crossed the track before being hit by the motorcycle. French Interior Minister Alliot-Marie and Sports Minister Roselyne Bachelot therefore urged fans to strictly adhere to traffic regulations while watching the Tour de France. Additionally, the organizers decided that all riders would observe a minute of silence for the deceased before the start of the 15th stage.

This is the second tragedy that has occurred in this edition of the Tour de France in just two days. On the 13th stage of the race on the 17th, New Zealand rider Dean of the Garmin team and Spanish rider Ferrer of the Rabobank team were attacked by an air gun at the 165-kilometer mark. Dean's left finger was hit by an air gun bullet, while Ferrer had a 4.5-millimeter hole in his thigh. Fortunately, both were unhurt and participated in the 14th stage after the bullets were removed.

Recalling the situation at the time, three-time world road race champion Ferrer described it lightly: "After 'bang, bang, bang,' I immediately felt excruciating pain in my thigh." However, Ferrer's team doctor, Dean Van Boomel, expressed concern: "If the bullet had hit his face, he would have certainly gone blind."

The police subsequently arrested two suspects. Shockingly, the alleged culprits turned out to be two underage teenagers, who were charged with hiding on the mountain behind the track and shooting at the riders with an air rifle. After recording his statement at the police station, Ferrer said, "Such behavior is crazy. I can only consider myself unlucky."

The continuous accidents have overshadowed the appeal of the race itself, even though, in yesterday's race, 34-year-old veteran Sergey Ivanov of Russia's Katusha team won his second individual stage victory in the Tour de France. Currently, in terms of overall rider performance, Contador and Armstrong of the Astana team have been overtaken by Colombian rider Nairo Quintana, dropping to third and fourth place respectively, while the yellow jersey remains with Noahentini.

In the history of the Tour de France, whether riders being shot or fans dying in accidents, such occurrences are rare. The last incident resulting in a fan's death happened in 2002, when a 7-year-old child was injured by a police car leading the way and later died in the hospital. Since then, the Tour de France organizing committee has significantly reduced the number of police cars on the track.
