Taobao sheds its grassroots nature; small and medium sellers suffer

by anonymous on 2013-08-20 14:51:22

Since its establishment in 2003, Taobao has once been a paradise for many C2C small and medium sellers. However, these small and medium sellers now find that Alibaba increasingly focuses on Tmall rather than Taobao, and the paradise no longer exists. According to the recently published survey data by E-commerce Observation and Research, currently, on Taobao, 42.67% of store owners say their profits are declining, 38.67% of stores indicate their profits remain unchanged, and only 18.67% of Taobao stores say their profits have increased within half a year.

Entering Taobao without a large budget won't work.

Wu Shang (a pseudonym), an ordinary Class C seller on Taobao, started selling socks on Taobao a year ago. At that time, he thought socks had low costs and low entry barriers. However, after a year, he is planning to switch to maternal and infant products.

Wu Shang said there were no sock factories in his hometown, and the unstable supply chain led to continuously high costs. Another important reason is the change in Taobao's overall environment, which exceeded his expectations. "Ten years ago, investing 100,000 yuan in Taobao was already very good, but now just for goods, it needs at least double." The cost of entering Taobao keeps rising. New stores need traffic, and many financially strong store owners sell at a loss: silk stockings that cost 10 yuan are sold for 6 yuan with free shipping, almost at half price, "they plan to lose 200,000 yuan in the early stage."

From the beginning, financial strength must be compared, "those who enter Taobao now all need big budgets, otherwise, they can't get started." Wu Shang calculated for the reporter: purchase cost, plus hiring graphic designers, operators, and customer service, an annual investment of over 200,000 yuan is not excessive. The sock industry usually has only two peak seasons a year, earning 60,000 yuan in one peak season, 120,000 yuan in two peak seasons, even adding a small amount of income in the off-season, it is still losing money. "It's normal for newcomers to lose money in the first year on Taobao, but the problem is whether they can make money in the second year, which is still unknown," Wu Shang frankly admitted. In the current environment, the survival of grassroots sellers is difficult, "the rapidly developing new sellers now basically rely on heavy investment, either losing money to attract traffic or hiring professional graphic designers and operation teams to help them. In such an environment, small sellers without money and professional operational capabilities find it hard to compete with them." Now Wu Shang is considering a transformation, "let me try for another year, if it doesn't work, I'll have to exit."

Crown sellers have to switch to Tmall.

Xu Bin can be considered a veteran Taobao seller, but he also laments that business is getting harder to do.

In 2005, Xu Bin gradually put his family's idle items on Taobao for sale, and every item he listed was sold, although the volume wasn't large, he felt that Taobao was a place suitable for grassroots development. "At that time, there weren't many shops on Taobao itself, and it hoped you could put your items up for sale." Thus, in 2010, Xu Bin opened a children's clothing store on Taobao, which grew very well at the beginning, and soon he became a crown seller. But 1-2 years later, Xu Bin clearly felt that Taobao's control over shop operations was increasing, which greatly affected the sellers, "Taobao's various rules keep changing, and the search rules change according to these things. Every time there is a change, we must make corresponding adjustments, otherwise, they will quickly show you their power."

In 2012, Taobao gradually shifted its focus to supporting Tmall sellers, constantly tilting policies towards them. At that time, for Xu Bin and a group of Class C store sellers, sales dropped sharply, "almost halved twice, so you can only do one-fourth of the previous business." Seeing no way out, Xu Bin had to switch to Tmall, "Tmall's traffic is relatively fixed, but there is an additional annual operational expenditure and taxation, and profits definitely cannot compare to before." Regarding this, Xu Bin appeared quite helpless, "there's no way, even now, Taobao's rules keep changing, we can only take it step by step."

Taobao business is not as good as physical stores.

On Taobao, many store owners had physical stores before opening online shops, among whom many are brand agents, and they clearly feel that doing business on Taobao is not easy.

Luo Qi exclusively sells Samsung mobile phones offline and has his own physical store. Last year, he saw the online consumption boom and opened a store on Taobao, originally expecting high results, but unexpectedly, after a year, the sales performance was worse than the offline physical store, "mainly because the competition on Taobao is not standardized enough, and the price differences are too obvious." To attract traffic, he also participated in some promotional activities, but the effects were not significant, "your price is always higher." Therefore, he no longer has high expectations for sales on Taobao, "online stores can only supplement the offline ones. If you only do online stores, it's really impossible to survive."

"Now, if you want to enter the Class C market, regardless of what field, it's very difficult." Guo Guo has a food sales physical store offline and has been on Taobao for 5 years. He feels it's getting harder and harder to do business, "as a big seller with two gold crowns, it's like this, only ten orders a day." In his view, the e-commerce environment is now highly competitive, and leading brands have emerged in some categories, "just like cola, only Pepsi and Coca-Cola are left." Other sellers basically have no chance or very little chance, "successful sellers generally have access to the most original sources and directly cooperate with manufacturers. Plus, more and more venture capital is entering, exacerbating the polarization phenomenon."

Previously, Lu Zhijin, the founder of Ali Trustpass, predicted: "Alibaba now has 9.5 million Taobao sellers, among which over 3 million seller websites have stopped operations, and about 80% of the remaining sellers are losing money, with only 10% actually making money." Alibaba, which obviously started from grassroots efforts, is clearly moving further away from grassroots roots. So-called small and beautiful may just look better.
