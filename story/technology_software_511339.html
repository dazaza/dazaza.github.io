CEO's New Awareness of Change Governance_12100

by qlockp41 on 2011-10-19 09:56:42

The core of corporate change is renewal, and the success of innovation comes from change management. There is such a saying: Change may fail, but staying unchanged will definitely lead to failure. Corporate changes arise from global competitive environments and are influenced by leaders who initiate their own models of change.

The slow growth of corporate organizations, the constant emergence of internal issues, and external changes in operating conditions (global competition, environmental protectionism, government regulations, the prominent role of robots in the supply chain, rapid development of high-tech such as space technology) all require companies to adapt to their surroundings for survival.

"Change" means: survival of the fittest, natural selection.

"Change" also means: inevitable, forced by circumstances.

Only when CEOs truly understand the importance and urgency of change management will they initiate a far-reaching "revolution."

Change management includes the following changes:

1. Changes in management models and methods.

2. Changes in organizational structure.

3. Changes in work processes and business processes.

4. Technological changes.

5. Changes in corporate culture.

6. Personnel changes.

Steps in change management:

1. The CEO uses advisory decision-making bodies (strategy teams, staff groups, rationalization suggestion departments, information departments, external consulting management expert organizations) to conduct research on the content of the change. Collect materials and data to form investigation reports. Analyze which part of the enterprise organization needs to be changed, which key points or sections. Conduct an enterprise diagnosis to determine problems and difficulties.

2. Change is top-down. This requires a collaborative leadership coalition consisting of board members, supervisory board members, the CEO, vice presidents, and department managers. The group should not exceed 11 members.

3. Change should be gradual, not rushed. Pilot changes can be implemented. Start with one team or department. Once results are achieved, consolidate them and proceed step-by-step.

4. Change management must involve employees. Fully utilize internal communication tools within the enterprise (broadcasts, television, internal LANs, newspapers, bulletin boards) to create momentum for the change. Promote the change, address doubts and concerns. At the same time, create a sense of urgency and crisis. Let employees understand the necessity of the change, explain why the change is needed, and what immediate and potential benefits it will bring to them.

5. Organize capable management personnel to visit some employee households to gain their trust and establish equal, real-time, two-way communication channels and feedback mechanisms.

6. Set change objectives and develop future plans. Anticipate problems and difficulties that may arise during the change process, prepare countermeasures, and formulate emergency response plans. Make adjustments as needed.

7. Change management must have a reward and punishment system. Rewards and punishments should be timely and fair. The time frame for rewards and punishments should not exceed three working days.

8. The change process should focus on efficiency and effectiveness. Efficiency means "doing things right," while effectiveness means "doing the right thing."

9. Understand where the resistance to change comes from. Pay attention to the adverse effects of gossip and informal organizations on change. If the gossip is negative, the CEO should guide public opinion correctly. Use a combination of incentives and discipline to resolve resistance from key members of informal organizations.

10. The CEO should build trust. The story of Shang Yang's "thousand gold moving wood" during the reforms in the Qin Dynasty can provide inspiration for how CEOs can build trust in their management.

By carrying out change thoroughly, the careers of CEOs will flourish and thrive.
