Who is Jia Pengjun? - Aichen's Blog

by donohue on 2009-07-20 15:34:50

Jia Junpeng, whose pen name is Fanfu, was born in an unknown year, probably around the time when he could tie his hair into a bun. Though he grew up in a rural area, he became familiar with cultural relics at an early age and was as refined as any city dweller. His mother still lives and works on their farm. The Analects of Confucius states: "While your parents are alive, do not travel far away." Jia Junpeng took this to heart. He never ventured more than ten miles from home, often wandering in the fields near his house.

Junpeng was frail, and though his family was poor, his mother always made sure he had enough food. Alas, when they ate together, she would leave the best bites for her son. She would eat what little remained after he had devoured most of the meal like a storm sweeping away the clouds. People speak of Yue Fei's mother stitching words of encouragement onto his back; Mengzi's mother moving three times to find the best environment for her son’s education; and Jia's mother leaving her food for her son - each tale evokes admiration, teaching, and sighs respectively!

Junpeng first encountered a computer during his teenage years and was endlessly amazed by it. In his first year, he learned how to turn it on; the next year, he discovered games; and one day, he found Warcraft, and from then on, he was addicted.

At first, his mother could still track him down and urge him to eat, but over time, Junpeng grew tired of her nagging and began to stay away from home more and more, returning less frequently. His mother was left alone, crying all day. It is said that throughout history, loving mothers have always known loneliness.

On the morning of July 16th in the sixtieth year of the People's Republic of China, Junpeng had been missing for three days. His mother was extremely worried, unable to eat from anxiety. She sent a relative to post on his frequented Baidu Tieba forum, with a simple yet deeply emotional message that moved everyone who read it, prompting thousands to join the search.

Alas, as the saying goes: "The tree wishes for stillness but the wind will not cease; the child wishes to support his parents, but they are no longer there to wait." I extract the following lesson for future generations.
