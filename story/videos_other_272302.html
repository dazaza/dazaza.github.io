Ichida (left) was captured by Japanese police.

Xinhua News Network - Yangtze Evening News reported on November 16 that after 2 years, 7 months, and 3 weeks on the run, Japan's most notorious fugitive, Ichida Ichida, was finally captured last Tuesday. The parents of British teacher Anne Lindsay, who was murdered by Ichida, traveled to Japan several times in an effort to seek justice for their daughter, even seeking assistance from the Yamaguchi-gumi.

According to British media reports, in order to evade police capture, Ichida, who came from a decent family, worked as a laborer at a construction site and used the money earned to undergo plastic surgery multiple times.

The murder case of British teacher Anne Lindsay once shocked both Japan and Britain. Lindsay was an English lecturer at a language school in Japan and suddenly disappeared on March 24, 2007. The police found a note at Lindsay's residence with the name "Ichida Ichida" and address information. At the apartment where Ichida lived, the police discovered a bathtub filled with sand, and upon removing the sand, they found Lindsay buried naked inside. Following the incident, the Japanese police not only distributed a large number of flyers and posters but also increased the reward amount to 10 million yen for the capture of Ichida, making it the highest-rewarded criminal case in the history of Japan's public security.

Hiding at a Construction Site

The whereabouts of Ichida were only known to the police last week. In October, the police discovered that Ichida had undergone plastic surgery multiple times during his escape and confirmed the clinics he had visited. After obtaining the latest photos of Ichida after his plastic surgery, the police widely disseminated them. On Tuesday evening, when Ichida was preparing to board a ferry from Osaka to Okinawa, he was recognized by a ticket seller and subsequently arrested.

After killing Lindsay, Ichida fled hastily without even taking his wallet. How did he manage to survive for two and a half years without money and how did he raise funds for multiple plastic surgeries? This aroused the curiosity of the public. According to Japanese media reports, for the past 13 months, Ichida has been working as a laborer at a construction site in the suburbs of Osaka, remaining undetected and managing to save up 1 million yen until last month.

It is understood that during his escape, Ichida always took care to protect himself. He used the name of someone who had passed away in Osaka and never removed his hat or black-rimmed glasses in public. He rarely socialized with his colleagues and usually stayed in the company dormitory watching TV. During a bowling event in April this year, when he reluctantly agreed to participate, he hid behind everyone else while taking photos. The police also found a passport in his room and believed that Ichida had always intended to flee overseas.

Family Ties Moved the Yamaguchi-gumi

Japan is one of the countries with the lowest crime rates in the world, but since the Japanese police were unable to find the murderer for such a long time, this made Lindsay's parents very dissatisfied with the Japanese police. Her father, Bill Hawk, has come to Japan five times, and her mother, Julia, has come three times. Regardless of the complex feelings each time they visit Japan, they still appeal to countless media outlets and distribute flyers on the streets at 5 o'clock in the morning.

In March this year, Bill connected with a British television station, which agreed to film a documentary about them. The producer even arranged a meeting with the Yamaguchi-gumi, Japan's largest organized crime syndicate. "We met them in a bar; there were about eight of them, all dressed in clothes from the 1970s, smoking cigarette after cigarette, looking completely normal," Bill said. "But the people around them were quite different: their fingers clearly showed signs of being cut off, and tattoos could be faintly seen under their collars." Bill continued, "I brought them two bottles of whiskey, but they refused." Later, I said, "My daughter once told me that if you visit friends in Japan, you should bring some gifts." Under these circumstances, these people eventually accepted the gifts. "They snapped their fingers, and someone took the things away."

The Yakuza Escorts Handing Out Flyers

"They told us they would do their best to find the murderer in their territory, and if they found him, they would deal with him properly." They said they have daughters and families too, which is why they got involved. "I spent about an hour with them, and later I planned to give them some flyers, but they didn't want them. However, they allowed me to distribute flyers in their territory."

Later, two cars took Bill to the crossroads of Shibuya in Tokyo, which is said to be the busiest crossroads in the world. "Members of the Yamaguchi-gumi were waiting nearby while we handed out flyers," Bill recalled later. "When we gave flyers to some young people, they came over and pushed us. But a few minutes later, they came back and apologized, even asking for a few more flyers. I handed out flyers there for nearly an hour until I ran out."

Small Information: The Yamaguchi-gumi

Japan is the only country in the world that recognizes the legitimacy of organized crime groups. The Yamaguchi-gumi is Japan's largest organized crime syndicate, with members accounting for about 70% of Japan's total mafia population. Its headquarters is located in Shinonome-cho, Nada-ku, Kobe City, Hyogo Prefecture. The background of Yamaguchi-gumi members can be divided into three major categories: "Gambling Organizations", "Festival Peripheral Open-air Stall Merchants Organization", and "Delinquent Youth Organization". By the end of 2008, the total membership of the Yamaguchi-gumi was approximately 22,000 people, with about 17,800 quasi-members, and the estimated total number of members is 38,000. In addition to Japan itself, the Yamaguchi-gumi is active overseas, including other Asian countries and the United States. The sixth-generation Yamaguchi-gumi uses the diamond-shaped "Yamaguchi" characters as its gang emblem, and this diamond-shaped emblem later became known as "Yamakou".

The Yamaguchi-gumi places great emphasis on maintaining order. If someone causes trouble on the streets of Japan, the first ones to arrive may not be the police but members of the Yamaguchi-gumi. They will use the most brutal methods to deal with troublemakers in order to maintain order in their territory. If a murder occurs, the Yamaguchi-gumi will investigate the truth as thoroughly as the police and then hand over the murderer to the real police. As a token of gratitude, every time the government launches a crackdown, high-ranking members of the Yamaguchi-gumi will avoid ahead of time. Considering the face of the police, they usually leave a few guns behind for the police to "confiscate." The Yamaguchi-gumi has good relations with the political circles, and every election is backed by funds and votes from the Yamaguchi-gumi.
