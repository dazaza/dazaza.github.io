The Tang Liudian, Volume 16, records the duties of the Wu Ku Ling Cheng: there are four types of knives, the first is called Yi Dao, the second Zhan Dao, the third Heng Dao, and the fourth Mo Dao. Yi Dao refers to ancient Ban Jian-like knives which were known as Yu Dao since the Jin and Song dynasties, later in Wei as Chang Dao, all with dragon and phoenix rings. By the Sui dynasty, they were called Yi Dao, decorated with gold and silver, used for ceremonial purposes. Zhan Dao was used to protect oneself. Heng Dao refers to the knives worn by soldiers, a name that also originated during the Sui dynasty. The Tang Law Commentary, Volume 8, mentions that among weapons, Heng Dao was commonly carried; other weapons like armor, spears, bows, and arrows should not be left far from one's person when on guard duty. When guarding, [Heng Dao was always carried], hence Emperor Gaozong once said to Wang Jishan, who was a general of the Qian Niu Guard: [Others cannot approach me without permission, but you carry a large Heng Dao by my side.] This shows that Heng Dao was the main weapon for guards. Soldiers in the Fu Bing system provided their own Heng Dao, and often wore it while on guard duty. Tang Heng Dao was a common weapon among soldiers.

Mo Dao is a long knife held by infantry, akin to an ancient sword for cutting horse legs. Thus, the so-called Tang Dao includes four types: Yi Dao, Zhan Dao, Heng Dao, all short weapons, divided into ceremonial and combat knives based on usage. Mo Dao differs from Yi Dao, Heng Dao, and Zhan Dao (i.e., ordinary protective knives): first, it does not belong to the weapons of palace guards; as mentioned earlier in the Tang Law Commentary, Volume 8, the weapons carried by guards include Heng Dao, armor, spears, bows, and arrows, but not Mo Dao. Second, Mo Dao is a heavy weapon. The Tang Huiyao, Volume 72, briefly states: In the first year of Kai Cheng (836 AD), in March, the governor of the Imperial City reported: Within the number of ceremonial items managed by various departments within the city, there are Mo Dao sharp weapons... These Mo Dao sharp weapons from various departments, I request to be stored under the Military Equipment Commissioner. If the department needs ceremonial items for ceremonies, please provide Yi Dao. It can be seen that Mo Dao is not a ceremonial item, but a sharp weapon.

The shape of Heng Dao is straight with a short handle. It can be said that the current [Japanese sword] shape overall completely copies the [Heng Dao] style from China's Tang dynasty, although this is embarrassing for Japanese people who like to boast about the attack power of [Japanese swords], but this is indeed the true history. The forging technology of Heng Dao was extremely advanced at the time, producing extremely sharp blades, suitable for both cavalry and infantry. The technology of making Heng Dao was later learned by Japan, resulting in what became known as the Tang-style Da Dao in Japan, considered one of the finest swords. However, authentic ones are rare, especially Tang Dao, difficult to find sellers, requiring experienced blacksmiths to specially forge them. Japanese Wakizashi looks very similar to Tang Dao, the reason need not be explained again, how many things in Japan are truly self-created?

In the Tang dynasty, new types of knives were developed without rings at the hilt, with wooden handles covering iron stems, wrapped with silk ropes, hilts adorned with metal decorations, handles and sheaths inlaid with gold and jade, covered with sharkskin, extremely ornate, known in Japan as "Tang-style Da Dao". To this day, there are still surviving examples of this type in the Shoso-in repository for reference. After careful imitation by the Japanese, this became the basis for the development of later samurai swords.

In addition to short-handled long knives, there was another type of long-handled knife in the Tang dynasty, measuring one zhang (about three meters) in length, called Mo Dao, extremely powerful. Infantry held it in a horizontal dense formation at the front of the battlefield, "advancing like a wall", but there are no records of its use after the Tang dynasty. Both these knives were made in China in the 8th century during the Tang dynasty, transported to Japan, and are now preserved in the Shoso-in owned by the Japanese Imperial Palace.

Compared with Tang Dao, Japanese swords have a curve rather than being straight, the advantage of curved blades is that when chopping, if it hits the opponent, it can deepen the wound, increasing its lethality. We see some nomadic tribes' main battle weapons are curved swords for this reason, relying on the horse's impact force to assist in the slash. Knives are more practical in actual combat than swords, swords first appeared in the era of the Yellow Emperor, when he defeated Chiyou with his sword, later swords became a symbol of power, not allowed to be worn by ordinary people. Early weapons were mostly made of bronze, later through countless wars, the shortcomings of bronze materials were revealed, it breaks easily, most of the weapons unearthed in archaeological excavations from the Warring States period are bronze swords, there is no sign of knives yet, common soldiers' weapons were Ge and crossbows, etc. After the Qin dynasty unified the six kingdoms, it increased the control of weapons, collecting weapons nationwide to cast twelve copper figures. With the development of iron smelting technology, iron weapons became more numerous, during the Han dynasty, except for a few still made of bronze, the vast majority were iron weapons. Because of the practicality of knives, they were increasingly used. During the Song dynasty, due to the strict control of weapons in China, civilian weapons did not develop much, coupled with the lack of warhorses during the Song dynasty, the government developed many peculiar weapons, but knives did not develop much, the most famous at that time was probably Yue Family Spear. The Yuan dynasty was the world of equestrian nations, naturally, the weapons of Han people would be controlled, the Ming dynasty was also obtained by force, so the Ming dynasty never held martial arts exams until once during the Chongzhen period. Although the Qing dynasty relaxed somewhat, many technologies had already been lost in the dust of history.
