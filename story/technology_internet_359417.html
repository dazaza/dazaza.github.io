Earth warming: China heats up faster than the world

by ditan360 on 2010-06-29 12:02:01

Lowcarbon.org Exclusive - China is the world's most populous country, with a population that accounts for one fifth of the world's total population. Therefore, in the globally popular topic of "global warming", China naturally becomes the most eye-catching object.

On October 22 last year, the British government released a climate map revealing China's vulnerability to climate change. The map indicates that if humans are unable to control climate change below 2℃—a global temperature rise of 4℃—China's water shortage will become more severe. By 2050, half of the glaciers in the Himalayas will have disappeared, leading to 23% of China's population lacking glacial meltwater resources during the dry season.

The climate map also shows that although 4℃ is the average temperature increase under the scenario of continuous greenhouse gas emissions growth, this increase is not uniform across the globe. Land compared to oceans will warm faster, and the temperature increase in high latitude regions, especially the Arctic, will be greater. The average surface temperature will rise by 5.5℃ compared to pre-industrial revolution levels. In the densely populated areas of eastern China, the hottest days of the year could see temperature increases of 6℃. The Arctic may experience temperature rises of 6℃~15℃ over several years, leading to the complete melting of ice and snow.

☆ China is heating up faster than the world

A century from now, will the skies of China often look like this? As the world is enthusiastically discussing global warming, an official report published in our country says that China is heating up faster than other places in the world.

This report, recently published by the China Meteorological Administration, states that the consequences of China's climate warming are increasingly frequent and extreme abnormal weather patterns, such as sudden heavy rainstorms, intense typhoons, large-scale droughts, sandstorms, land desertification, etc. The report predicts that in the next ten years, China's climate will warm at a faster rate than it did in the past decade. In the past fifty years, China's temperature has increased on average by 0.22 degrees every ten years, a higher rate than the global average. However, the report does not provide specific reasons.

By 2050, the glaciers in northwestern China will shrink by 27%; by 2020, China's average rainfall will increase by two to three percent, and by 2050, it could increase by five to seven percent. The report also predicts that the chances of extreme heavy rainstorms occurring in eastern China over the next four to five years will be four to five times higher than in the 1980s and 1990s, and the number of typhoon attacks will significantly increase, with even more intense scales.

Compared to the average temperatures from 1961 to 1990, by 2020 China's temperatures may rise by 1.3 to 2.1 degrees, and by mid-century, they may rise by 2.3 to 3.3 degrees. The report predicts that by 2100, temperatures may rise by 3.9 to 6 degrees. Since 1987, China has experienced 19 warm winters in succession, with only the winter of 2004 to 2005 being normally cold.

As the earth warms, the arid regions in northern China will continue to expand, with more and more land becoming desertified, and droughts will make sandstorms more severe. By 2050, the sea level may rise by 15 to 20 centimeters.

☆ Climate Change Seriously Impacts Chinese Agriculture

A new study by American consulting company McKinsey shows that climate change is likely to exacerbate the already quite serious droughts in northern and northeastern China, potentially causing tens of millions of farmers to suffer significant income losses.

The study also indicates that once global temperatures rise above 3℃, the yields of major cereal crops worldwide will decrease, with China's rice production possibly decreasing by as much as 30%. Furthermore, in most regions around the world including China, the rise in sea levels and the impact of storms will severely affect low-lying coastal areas. Coastal towns in China, especially in the Shanghai region, are densely populated and possess important infrastructure and high-quality agricultural land. An increase in coastal flooding will have a more severe impact on the lives and economies of local people. According to this study, if no measures are taken to prepare for the impact of rising temperatures on these two regions, up to 35 million farmers may lose more than 50% of their income.

The North China Plain is less affected by climate change risks because local governments have taken various measures to limit the impact of prolonged droughts. However, even under relatively mild climate change scenarios, the impact of droughts may lead to a 50% increase in losses for farmers in Northeast China.

McKinsey estimates that from now until 2030, China will need to spend 25 billion yuan annually to mitigate the impact of droughts, including investments in irrigation projects, seed engineering, soil and water conservation, and reservoirs. Although these measures are expected to avoid up to half of the expected crop losses, China still needs to expand agricultural insurance, which currently covers only 25% of farmers. The Chinese government's considered goal is to reduce carbon emission intensity by 40% by 2020.

☆ Urban Heat Island Effect and Automobiles

The public has not failed to notice certain changes in urban temperatures in recent years. Previously, domestic "furnace" cities had summer temperatures often lower than non-"furnace" cities, where temperatures reaching 36～39℃ in summer were not uncommon. In residential neighborhoods with dense high-rise buildings, temperatures can reach 39～41℃, with some areas even soaring to 42℃ or higher. This means that differences in urban economic development and lifestyle are also reflected in variations in the "heat island effect."

The urban heat island effect refers to the phenomenon where urban temperatures are significantly higher than those in surrounding suburban areas. On near-surface temperature maps, suburban temperatures vary little, while urban areas appear as high-temperature zones, resembling islands protruding from the sea. Since these islands represent high-temperature urban regions, they are aptly referred to as urban heat islands. The urban heat island effect causes the annual average temperature in cities to be 1℃ higher than in suburban areas, and sometimes even more. In summer, temperatures in some urban areas may be 6℃ higher than in suburban areas.

Successive appearances of "El Niño" and "La Niña" phenomena on Earth are related to the intensification of the greenhouse effect. Cities, due to their dense populations, tall buildings, roads, and vehicles, experience a more severe heat island effect. Greenhouse gases trap heat in the lower atmosphere, preventing it from dissipating. Each kilogram of gasoline burned by a car emits 3.08 kilograms of carbon dioxide. When the concentration of carbon dioxide increases, it enhances the atmosphere's absorption of infrared radiation from sunlight, preventing the Earth's surface heat from radiating outward and causing the average surface temperature of the Earth to rise.

In Europe, an increasing number of countries are transforming their urban transportation policies to minimize car travel, replacing it with public transportation and bicycles. Studies by the EU environmental air quality monitoring agency have found that besides natural climatic factors, human activities in cities are also one of the main culprits behind the formation of urban heat islands, with vehicle exhaust emissions playing a significant role. They are an important cause of excessive ozone content in ground-level air and the intensification of the urban heat island effect.

The relationship between people, the environment, and automobiles is an increasingly clear topic of our times. China is currently in the peak period of automobile popularization, with many medium and large-sized cities experiencing vehicle exhaust emissions as the primary source of atmospheric pollution. Currently, there are over 67 million vehicles in China, and in the next 10～15 years, the number of vehicles in China will grow at an annual rate of 9%. Clearly, improving the structure of urban transportation, rationally popularizing automobiles, reducing car use, and striving to achieve reductions in vehicle exhaust emissions have become quite urgent.

☆ China Has No Choice

Following the industrial revolution and the information revolution, the fifth wave of change in the world economy—"low-carbon economy"—is gradually emerging, with low oil consumption becoming the mainstream mode of future life.

On August 3, 2006, the National Environmental Protection Agency disclosed that China's total sulfur dioxide emissions in 2005 reached as high as 25.49 million tons, ranking first in the world, representing an increase of 27% compared to 2000. Prior to this, the United States ranked first in sulfur dioxide emissions.

For a long time, historical and realistic reasons have made China's ecological environment more fragile, making it more susceptible to adverse factors such as climate change. Preliminary scientific research indicates that climate change will continue to negatively impact China's natural ecosystems and socio-economic systems. China is currently the fastest-developing society in terms of industrialization and urbanization in the world, requiring a larger greenhouse gas emission space. The possible way to coordinate climate goals with development goals is to follow a low-carbon economic development path.
