Next year, the global M&A deal value is expected to fall to $2 trillion.

by jmakeng3 on 2011-06-23 10:54:36

The trend of mergers and acquisitions will continue

Since the beginning of this year, with the spread and deepening of the U.S. subprime crisis evolving into a global financial tsunami, major overseas securities markets have seen continuous declines in stock indices. European and American enterprises are facing financial difficulties, investor confidence has been severely hit, and signs of a global economic recession have emerged. Affected by these factors, global M&A activities have significantly cooled down. The total value of global M&A transactions in 2008 has dropped by 36% compared to the same period last year, with the total amount of M&A transactions for the whole year reaching $2.85 trillion. Looking ahead to 2009 from a global perspective, the severe fluctuations in international financial markets triggered by the U.S. subprime crisis will continue to affect many countries, with the impact intensifying and leading to a deeper global economic crisis. The economic outlook is not optimistic. It is expected that next year's global M&A will continue the credit and leveraged buyout financing difficulties brought about by this year’s global economic crisis, further showing a trend of contraction. Next year's global M&A transaction volume is expected to fall to $2 trillion, a decline of 30%, marking the lowest level in nearly five years.

Rare Opportunities for Chinese Enterprises

Contrary to the global M&A market, the transaction value in China in 2008 still increased by 36%, reaching $167 billion. China accounted for 6.9% of global M&A cases, doubling compared to 2007. In addition, domestic enterprises' overseas M&A value increased by 74% year-on-year, reaching $49 billion. We believe that the current domestic and international economic situation indicates that 2009 will be a good year for corporate M&As. As the chill of the financial crisis further affects the real economy, many physical enterprises in Europe and America are increasingly falling into operational difficulties. For example, the current three major American car giants—General Motors, Ford, and Chrysler—are facing bankruptcy dilemmas. This provides a rare opportunity for Chinese enterprises to "go out." 2009 will be the best opportunity for Chinese enterprises to conduct overseas M&As. Cross-border M&As should seize the opportunity and act accordingly. Recently, the CBRC issued the "Guidelines for Commercial Banks on the Risk Management of M&A Loans," explicitly allowing qualified commercial banks to start M&A loan businesses, providing excellent credit support to the M&A market. The introduction of this measure will strongly support eligible Chinese enterprises going global. Relevant departments are currently studying policies to relax conditions for domestic enterprises conducting cross-border M&As, supporting Chinese enterprises in seizing opportunities and going global.

Participatory M&A Becomes an Effective Method

European and American companies participating in Chinese enterprises is unprecedented, while Chinese enterprises participating in European and American companies are quite common. This situation may reverse in 2009. Chinese enterprises have many motivations for overseas M&As, among which the more common ones include seeking resources, technology, and market strategy considerations. Currently, IT high-tech enterprises, energy, mineral resource-based enterprises, manufacturing, and financial banking sectors are all facing valuation cuts and sharp reductions in profitability, making the acquisition prices very attractive. If the economic situation continues to deteriorate, prices will continue to fall. Chinese enterprises' overseas M&As should mainly focus on these four types of enterprises. Leading enterprises in China, such as those in the IT industry, manufacturing, finance, and core capital enterprises, who have the conditions and capabilities, should take the lead in actively going global and engage in deep cooperation at the equity level with industry leaders and technologically advanced enterprises in Europe and America, as well as capital-based enterprises. These enterprises, when experiencing cash flow disruptions, reduce investment threshold requirements to alleviate funding pressures. By purchasing shares of these enterprises at appropriate prices, Chinese enterprises can acquire relevant technology and capital, seek global resources, and achieve rapid growth in the international market.

Overcoming Risks: Three Major Strategies

The determination of Chinese enterprises to expand overseas is inspiring, but the risks behind it also deserve attention. There is no doubt that the road for Chinese enterprises' overseas M&As will not be smooth. International experience shows that 60% of M&As end in failure. Clearly, due to information asymmetry and cultural differences, the possibility of failure in overseas M&As is much greater than in domestic M&As. Meanwhile, cross-national studies also prove that historically successful overseas M&As can demonstrate strong complementary advantages, helping foreign enterprises, especially those in developing countries, leverage their late-mover advantages to achieve leapfrog development.

Strategy One: Careful Selection, Cash Is King

When selecting M&A target objects, the first thing to consider is the cash situation, followed by whether the main business of the target enterprise is healthy and whether it has significant growth potential. Especially under the backdrop of the financial crisis, Chinese enterprises need to maintain a healthy cash flow. Do not blindly pursue M&As; keep cash reserves and carefully select companies worthy of investment. When conducting M&As, factors such as financing ability and whether cash flow can support must be considered. From this perspective, moderate-scale M&As might be a relatively realistic choice.

Strategy Two: Avoid Overconfident Bottom Fishing, Focus on Core Business

Although this round of the financial crisis places our enterprises in an unprecedented strategic M&A opportunity period, we understand that this opportunity does not only come from tempting prices of foreign enterprises and assets. "Bottom fishing" should not be the sole perspective for understanding the background of enterprise overseas M&As. The focus of M&As should be placed on strengthening core businesses rather than expanding scale. Even in a crisis, one should not purchase companies that appear to have low valuations but cannot generate synergistic effects.

Strategy Three: Strong Participation, Control Is Paramount

A large portion of China's overseas investments or M&As involve a low proportion of participation, resulting in a lack of control or even influence in boardrooms. If Chinese enterprises acquire but do not control, it becomes difficult to obtain what is most beneficial to their own economic interests, let alone gain access to brands, technologies, talents, and market development channels as M&A objectives. Therefore, maintaining control in the hands of the Chinese side is something that Chinese enterprises need to always grasp during overseas participatory M&As.
