In ancient times, there were no hats but headbands. People used silk or hemp-made headbands to wrap their heads or tie up hair buns. Today's "headscarves" used by ethnic minorities in Southwest China are remnants of ancient headbands. During the Northern Zhou Dynasty, Emperor Wu specifically made the headband into a four-cornered shape for easy use by his army. It was called "Fu Tou" at that time, actually the earliest Chinese hat. Later, the four-cornered hat gradually evolved into two long corners extending to the left and right, thus becoming the black gauze hat. The two extended corners crossed behind the head, further developing into helmets for military officers. Not only does China have an ancient history of hat development, but foreign countries in ancient times also regarded hats as symbols of power and status. In early Rome, hats were the marks of free and legal citizens, while slaves could only cover their heads with pieces of cloth.

By the Middle Ages, the concept of class distinctions in hats became clearer: bankrupt people wore yellow hats; kings wore golden crowns; prisoners wore paper hats; citizens wore dark hats, etc.

The ancient crown differs from today's hats. It only has a narrow crown beam covering part of the top of the head, unlike hats that cover the entire head.

Benefits of wearing hats for hair:

① Keeping hair warm in cold weather:

The head is known as the "convergence of all yang." Medical research found that in a stationary state without a hat, a person loses 30% of their body heat through the head at 15°C and 60% at 4°C. If the head gets cold, it causes blood vessels in the brain to contract, leading to headaches or scalp nutrient circulation disorders and follicle metabolic dysfunction, causing hair malnutrition or excessive non-natural shedding. Severe cases can even trigger certain diseases. Therefore, the head needs warmth just like other parts of the body in cold winters.

② Protecting hair from dust and pollution:

In winter, there's more wind and sand, and more dust, especially in today's increasingly polluted environment. When hair is blown into disorder, microorganisms and dust stick to the scalp like sandpaper grit, damaging the scalp and increasing friction when combing or moving. These invisible microorganisms may cause bacteria to grow on the scalp, possibly leading to follicular infections and directly affecting the survival environment and growth quality of hair. Friction causes the outer layer of the hair, the cuticle, to rise, making the hair surface rough. Serious cases can lead to split ends and breakage. At this time, wearing a comfortable and stylish hat is equivalent to giving the hair a protective coat, effectively preventing dust and microorganism invasion.

Hats require proper care and storage. After removing a hat, it should not be placed haphazardly but hung on a hat rack or hook, with no heavy objects pressing on it to prevent deformation. Worn for a long time, oil and dirt accumulate inside and outside the hat, which should be cleaned promptly. The lining can be removed and washed, then reattached to prevent sweat and mold from dampness, prolonging the hat's lifespan. Dust on the hat should be brushed regularly. Mud and grease stuck to the hat can be gently scrubbed with a soft brush dipped in hot soapy water, then rinsed with clear water. When cleaning the hat, find a similarly sized round can or porcelain bowl, place the hat on it, and wash to prevent deformation. When storing hats: brush off dust, wash off dirt, expose to sunlight briefly, then wrap in paper and store in a hat box in a ventilated, dry place, with a desiccant inside the storage box to prevent moisture. Special care is required for some hats that cannot be immersed in water (such as feathered, sequined, or lined hats). Cotton hats can be washed, but hats with paper padding should only be wiped, not washed, as washing would ruin them. Since they are three-dimensional shapes, they should never be machine washed.

General correct methods for washing hats:

1. Remove any decorative items before washing.

2. Soak the hat briefly in clear water with neutral detergent.

3. Gently scrub with a soft brush.

4. Scrub the sweatband part (the part that contacts the head) several times to thoroughly clean sweat and bacteria. Of course, if the material is antibacterial and odor-resistant, this step can be skipped.

5. Fold the hat into quarters, gently shake off excess water, and do not use a washing machine for spin drying.

6. Spread the hat out, stuff it with old towels, and let it air dry in a shaded area. Avoid hanging it to dry.

Correct methods for washing special hats:

1. Leather hats can be cleaned by rubbing with onion slices or wiping along the grain with a cloth dipped in gasoline for good results.

2. Stains on fine felt hats can be wiped with a mixture of ammonia and equal parts alcohol. Use a piece of silk cloth dipped in this solution, then wipe carefully. Do not soak the hat too much, as it may lose its shape.

3. After washing knitted hats, fill them with crumpled paper or cloth balls before drying.

4. Wool hats should not be washed with water, as wool shrinks. If dust or pet hair adheres to the hat, use wide adhesive tape folded over your fingers to remove surface dust. Wool hats do not need frequent washing, as it can shorten their lifespan. If thorough cleaning is necessary, dry cleaning is the best option.
