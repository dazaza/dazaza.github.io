How to Remove Dampness from the Body

How do you know if your body has dampness? How can you remove it? I came across an article online that I found very helpful, so I tried it myself and felt it was effective. It seems to be well-suited for modern people's constitutions, so I wanted to share it with everyone.

There is an old saying: "A thousand chills are easy to remove, but one dampness is hard to get rid of. Dampness is sticky and turbid, like oil mixed into flour." When dampness combines with cold, it’s called cold-damp; with heat, it's called damp-heat; with wind, it's called wind-damp; and with summer heat, it's called summer-damp. If dampness isn't removed, eating as many tonics or medicines as possible will only have a superficial effect, like scratching through boots or hitting a bull from a distance. Many people suffer from fatty liver disease, asthma, hypertension, cardiovascular diseases, and even malignant tumors, but in fact, these illnesses are all related to dampness and phlegm-dampness.

1800 years ago, the great Han dynasty physician Zhang Zhongjing wrote in his work *Treatise on Cold Damage Diseases*: "If the body feels very hot, yet desires more clothing, this indicates that cold has penetrated deep into the bone marrow." This means that although someone with a fever may have a high temperature, they might still feel cold and want to wear more clothes, indicating that the internal cold is very severe, having reached the bone marrow. At this point, using antibiotics or other cooling drugs intravenously to reduce inflammation and fever would directly bring external cold-dampness into the body. (Isn’t this what we usually do?)

Zhang Zhongjing, the author of *Treatise on Cold Damage Diseases*, lived in an era when people often suffered from hunger and cold, and diseases were primarily caused by cold pathogens, which could be treated directly with heat-inducing medicines. Nowadays, with changes in living environments, simple cases of cold damage are rarely seen anymore. Instead, external cold and internal heat intertwine, forming dampness. True cases of cold syndrome are not common; instead, dampness predominates. Dampness is a yin pathogen that restrains yang qi and obstructs the flow of qi. In other words, the era of yang deficiency is plagued by dampness.

There's an interesting phenomenon: nowadays, during winter, there are very few people who truly catch a cold from being chilled, while in summer, such cases are quite common.

Modern people live in environments with heating in winter and air conditioning in summer, making their perception of the four seasons increasingly vague. During summer, when people should sweat, the use of air conditioners prevents sweat from evaporating, causing it to accumulate inside the body. In winter, with central heating, wearing just a single layer of clothing can make one sweat, leading to yang qi dispersing outward and the inability to retain essence. (If one doesn't store essence in winter, illness will surely arise in spring.) Under such environmental conditions, the body becomes most susceptible to disease, with skin functions declining and the ability to resist pathogens weakening. This makes it extremely easy for dampness to accumulate within the body, causing yang qi to weaken. The harm caused by dampness to the human body is greater than that of cold.

During the hottest months of the year, people crave cold air and love drinking cold beverages and eating cold dishes. A glass of ice-cold beer can make one feel cool from head to toe. However, in pursuit of this temporary comfort, dampness is deeply embedded in the body, becoming a major health hazard.

From July to August each year is known as 'long summer,' when dampness prevails. The spleen corresponds to this period in traditional Chinese medicine. Dampness in TCM is referred to as 'yin evil,' and the spleen is the most yin organ, preferring dryness and disliking dampness. Therefore, the spleen's energy is most vigorous during these months. If the dampness during long summer becomes excessive, it can easily damage the spleen. Additionally, the spleen is responsible for transforming water fluids. Its characteristic is that yang qi tends to decline while yin qi tends to increase. Once dampness invades the body, it most easily harms spleen yang, and the weakness of spleen yang further exacerbates the invasion of dampness.

Some people enjoy eating pork, which has a salty-cold nature and promotes phlegm. Eating too much meat can also lead to phlegm-dampness. If you feel you have excessive internal dampness, it's best to stick to a vegetarian diet for a while. Long-term consumption of rice cooked in an electric pot can also lead to internal dampness. People with internal dampness can resolve it by consuming Job's tears (coix seed) and red bean soup or scorched rice. After drinking Job's tears and red bean soup for a while, you can switch to millet porridge to nourish the spleen and stomach. Over time, as the spleen strengthens, dampness naturally dissipates.

Self-diagnosis methods for determining if you have internal dampness:

Upon waking up - check how you feel

If every morning you wake up feeling especially tired, dizzy, lacking energy, or as if you're wearing wet clothes, feeling unrefreshed and unmotivated... then it's certain that you have internal dampness.

Using the toilet - check your stool

After relieving yourself in the morning, observe your stool. Is it sticking to the toilet bowl, requiring more than one flush to clean? Those who don't want to observe the toilet bowl can check the tissue paper. Normally, one or two pieces of paper should suffice, but if three to five pieces are needed and still cannot clean properly, (this is why medical questionnaires ask about stool), it indicates internal dampness. The color and shape of the stool can also help determine if there is internal dampness. Normal stools are golden yellow and banana-shaped, but now very few people have such healthy stools. When there is internal dampness, the stool turns greenish, loose, and unformed, (which is an important sign of spleen deficiency, experienced by many people), always giving a sense of incomplete evacuation. Over time, toxins from accumulated waste in the body can cause various diseases.

Brushing teeth - check the tongue coating

After using the bathroom, we usually brush our teeth. Before brushing, take a few seconds to look at your tongue in the mirror. "The tongue is the sprout of the heart and also the external sign of the spleen," reflecting the state of our body sensitively.

A healthy tongue is pale red and moist, with a thin white and clear coating, neither too dry nor too wet, not slippery or dry. If the tongue does not meet these criteria, it indicates that bodily functions have already been compromised. If the tongue coating is thick, white, and appears slippery and moist, it suggests internal cold; if the tongue coating is rough or very thick, yellow, and greasy, it suggests internal damp-heat; if the tongue is red without any coating, it indicates that internal heat has reached a level where yin is injured.

In addition to the above main points, there are two other ways to judge:

First, check if you feel nauseous when brushing your teeth in the morning. Many people say they vomit or feel nauseous when brushing, with a constant tickling sensation in the throat, producing little phlegm. Second, upon waking up, notice if your calves feel sore or heavy. These are typical signs of dampness. By the way, a good method to remove dampness is to press and rub the Chengshan acupoint.

(Other useful acupoints include Sanyinjiao and Yinlingquan, and moxibustion is highly recommended.)

If there were a kind of medicine in the world that could serve as both food and tea, not only delicious and nourishing, but also able to prevent all kinds of diseases in the whole family, would you be willing to try it?

They are Job's tears and red beans. These two ingredients need not be measured in any particular proportion; simply grab a handful of each, wash them, put them in a pot, add water, and boil them. The result is a wonderful concoction for removing dampness and strengthening the spleen - Job's tears and red bean porridge.

There are many tricks and considerations when cooking Job's tears and red bean porridge. Both Job's tears and red beans are hard, and if left to boil in a pot, they might take over an hour to cook thoroughly, wasting fuel or electricity, and there's a risk of boiling the water dry and scorching the bottom. I suggest two methods: The first method is to add enough water to the pot, bring it to a boil, then turn off the heat and let the Job's tears and red beans steep for half an hour. Then, turn the heat back on, bring it to a boil again, and steep for another half an hour, and the porridge will be ready. The second method is simpler: clean an unused thermos bottle at home, place the Job's tears and red beans inside, pour in boiling water, tightly seal the bottle, and let it steep overnight. In the morning, the contents will be porridge, perfect for breakfast. This method is very convenient, saves energy, and eliminates the need for prolonged cooking.

One advantage of Job's tears and red bean porridge is that no matter how it is cooked, it never becomes sticky or thick. The bottom always contains cooked red beans and Job's tears, while the top is a light red broth. Most of the active components of Job's tears and red beans are in the broth. When cooking the porridge, adding more water allows the broth to last us half a day, making it ideal as a tea substitute. In summer, it's best to drink it in the morning because it may spoil by the afternoon.

As for its efficacy, it is truly remarkable. Job's tears, known as "yi yi ren" in traditional Chinese medicine, was listed as a superior herb in the *Shennong Bencao Jing*. It can treat damp-arthritis, benefit the intestines and stomach, reduce edema, strengthen the spleen and stomach, and long-term consumption can lighten the body and invigorate qi. Red beans, known as "chi xia dou" in traditional Chinese medicine, have a noticeable diuretic, swelling-reducing, and spleen-strengthening effect. Since they are red, red enters the heart, so they can also nourish the heart. Modern people experience high levels of mental stress, have deficient heart qi due to irregular eating habits, and lack exercise, leading to spleen deficiency and dampness. To remove dampness, nourish the heart, and strengthen the spleen and stomach, nothing beats Job's tears and red beans. Cooking them into porridge ensures their active components are fully absorbed by the body without burdening the spleen and stomach.

Regarding the "swelling-reducing" effects of Job's tears and red beans, it's also quite intriguing. We shouldn't assume that swelling is just edema. Looking at today's population, at least 5 to 6 out of 10 people are overweight, which is also a form of swelling, called corpulence. From a TCM perspective, whether it's obesity or edema, it signifies internal dampness. Water cannot circulate with qi and blood, stagnating between cells, causing rapid expansion of the body. Edema and obesity are similar, differing only in degree. Strongly diuretic herbs or foods can eliminate this stagnant water, thus reducing swelling. Therefore, red beans are essential for treating edema, and practical experience shows that Job's tears and red bean porridge has excellent weight-loss effects, helping reduce weight without harming the body, especially beneficial for middle-aged and elderly obese individuals.

People often ask: "Why doesn't Job's tears and red bean porridge become thick and sticky?" Others told me: "When I cook Job's tears and red bean porridge, I add a small amount of rice, and the porridge becomes thick, tasting absolutely delicious!"

I said: "You must not add rice!" Why? Because rice grows in water and contains dampness. Dampness is sticky, so rice cooks quickly and thickens. Red beans and Job's tears both expel dampness and contain no dampness themselves, so no matter how long they are cooked, they never thicken, and the broth remains clear. Traditional Chinese Medicine precisely utilizes this clear property to remove dampness from the body. Once rice is added, it introduces dampness, making the entire porridge thick. Although the taste may improve, it is not beneficial for health. Due to that one handful of rice, all the efforts of red beans and Job's tears are wasted, rendering them ineffective.

People who think excessively, strain their minds, and have little physical activity often appear pale-faced, lack vitality, and may even experience palpitations, drowsiness, a heavy-headed sensation, and a feeling of emptiness in the chest. This reflects internal dampness combined with insufficient heart qi. You can add longan to the Job's tears and red bean porridge. Longan is sweet and warm, capable of opening the stomach, strengthening the spleen, calming the spirit, and replenishing blood. It can invigorate heart yang and warm heart qi, making people energetic and full of vigor. It is particularly suitable for those engaged in intensive mental labor and also for the elderly.

Young people often experience irritability, insomnia, or red rashes and acne, all caused by excessive heart fire and internal damp-heat. You can add lily or lotus seeds to the Job's tears and red bean porridge, or both.

Lily can moisten the lungs, beautify the complexion, clear heart fire, and calm the spirit. Lotus seeds excel at nourishing the heart and clearing heart fire, while also strengthening the spleen and kidneys. However, lotus seeds have a slightly astringent quality, so they are suitable for those who are physically weaker but not for obese individuals with excessive internal stagnation.

When using lotus seeds, remove the bitter core inside. Of course, if you find the porridge lacks flavor, you can add some rock sugar. Among all types of sugar, only rock sugar is cooling and can clear heart fire.

If you catch a cold or have internal cold, stomach cold pain, or poor appetite, you can add a few slices of ginger to the Job's tears and red bean porridge. Ginger is warm in nature, capable of warming the center and dispelling cold, strengthening the spleen, and harmonizing the stomach. Note that ginger should not be added in excess, as too much will make the porridge spicy. If you want to add seasoning to the porridge, you can only add brown sugar, which is also warm in nature.

It is worth mentioning that during postpartum care, women should avoid Job's tears and can add more jujubes instead. Jujubes can warm the center, strengthen the spleen, and nourish the blood, making them very suitable.

For those with kidney deficiency, you can add black beans to the Job's tears and red bean porridge. Black enters the kidneys, and the shape of beans resembles the kidneys, making them excellent for nourishing the kidneys.

Nowadays, people often talk about athlete's foot, which is a typical case of damp-heat descending. You can add soybeans to the Job's tears and red bean porridge, eat the porridge, and use the broth to wash your feet. Treating both internally and externally yields excellent results.

If you have a cough, you can cut raw pears into 1-2 cm cubes, peel and remove the core, and add them to the Job's tears and red bean porridge to cook together, which can moisten the lungs, dissolve phlegm, and quench thirst.

Yams and pumpkins can also be added to the Job's tears and red bean porridge. Yams are known as "the little ginseng that embarrasses ginseng itself," sometimes surpassing ginseng in terms of tonifying effects. They are excellent for strengthening the spleen and stomach, fortifying the kidneys, and benefiting the lungs and qi. Pumpkins can tonify the center and benefit qi, reduce inflammation, relieve pain, kill parasites, and detoxify, making them very beneficial for protecting the pancreas and preventing diabetes.
