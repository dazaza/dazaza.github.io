Google Earth Provides Real Estate Investors with a Valuable Research Tool

Google describes it as "a globe that sits inside your computer." Now, thanks to Google Earth, real estate investors can virtually travel to any property and see it without the inconvenience and hassle of a physical visit. It’s described by Google as a "globe that sits inside your computer." You can now type in any address and, in most instances, get a view so detailed it's almost alarming. Instead of driving to the property, you can examine it safely and conveniently from your computer, avoiding the time and expense of a physical trip.

You can also look up road maps, crime statistics, and access historic maps. The level of detail is remarkable—during a recent scan of my own home, one of our cars parked in the driveway was clearly visible. I could even see my front door quite distinctly. This tool has the potential to revolutionize due diligence for real estate investors. In fact, average users report that while Google Earth does require a fair amount of RAM, it doesn’t have the steep learning curve you might expect. Most people are able to use it effectively within just a few minutes, which is great news for investors who aren’t tech-savvy.

To get an incredible close-up image of a property, all you need to do is click on "Fly To," type in the address, and hit the small magnifying glass icon on the right. Instantly, you're transported from orbit all the way down to the property's roof. With a little experimentation, you can adjust the views not only from above but also from the sides as you zoom in and out. You’ll be able to see the property from both left and right angles while examining as much of the surrounding terrain as you wish.

**Warning:** Google Earth can be addictive and fun. You may find yourself exploring the globe in search of interesting maps, places, and even people captured by Google's cameras. If that wasn’t enough, Google recently acquired Image America, a company already well-known for providing low-cost aerial imagery to county appraisers and assessors. This means even clearer images for real estate investors, as aerial imagery is superior to satellite imaging because it can be captured in near-real-time, making it faster than satellites located 17,000 miles above the earth and more efficient than Google’s photographs taken from airplanes.

One of the biggest obstacles for this otherwise fantastic software—blurry images—has largely been avoided. Nothing is more frustrating than typing in an address and receiving an image so fuzzy it strains your eyes. In the past, this issue was often attributed to clouds or fog. While bad images don't occur frequently, if it happens to be the one property you really want to see, it can be maddening. Generally, however, most larger cities and towns offer very good close-up views.

Google Earth definitively solves one of the biggest problems real estate investors have faced in the past: seeing properties up close. When you're located in another state or country, this important tool makes your due diligence process effortless.
