"Hey Shop" Good movies start with imitation! Domestic TV series

by hoouk260 on 2012-03-03 19:59:12

Author: Zhao Zhu Don't be afraid of imitation. For directors, especially those who shoot domestic TV dramas or movies, they all have to start with imitation. It's like learning to write and having to trace over red characters first, or learning to paint and having to copy. Only in this process can basic skills be solidified, which then gives the opportunity to truly shoot a good movie that can express oneself and also be liked by the audience. "Hey Shop" is such a movie that starts from copying and imitating but at least has its own ideas and the effect is not bad.

Movies such as "Kill Bill", "A Chinese Odyssey", and even "Crazy Stone", these excellent domestic and foreign films' templates are all applied in "Hey Shop". The finished product that comes out of this fusion is crazy but not abrupt, and more effectively avoids some risks that current Chinese films cannot escape. It is full of sincerity and the effect is not simple.

The most surprising thing first must be the "zombie film" within the play. Of course, the special makeup and effects seem a bit rough. In this aspect, it is clearly something that can be improved. But it seems to more accurately reflect the sub-theme of the film's reflection on the shoddy production of Chinese films. Watching those "pseudo-zombies" with simple makeup and simple scenes still performing their duties faithfully, repeating simple actions over and over again, a long-lost sense of movingness surges in my heart. Originally, movie censorship could be avoided in this way?

Of course, the jumping repetitive narrative, choosing to reveal the fragmented clues of the entire story little by little, is also an innovation of "Hey Shop" worth appreciating. Especially the small details set in between are coherent before and after, mutually laying down foreshadowings, layer by layer advancing the progress of the story, moderately testing the audience's understanding ability, yet not too difficult. Several unexpected reversals are quite interesting, making the viewing experience very satisfying. A good movie should probably be like this. The story doesn't necessarily have to be very complicated, but the way of telling the story must be unexpected, yet able to deploy each clue appropriately and highlight each character, so that when the story ends, the characters' personalities can deeply move the audience.

Speaking of characters, "Hey Shop" features altogether four or five groups, nearly ten main characters, each with distinct personality traits. Some are dull and stubborn, some flexible and resourceful but unlucky, some smooth-talking, some suave and debonair, and there's even a thief full of poetry. The multi-threaded narrative style obviously learned from "Crazy Stone" cleverly weaves each character into the story and leaves enough space for them to display their own madness. Renowned Hong Kong actors Zhu Yin and Zhang Daming, as important roles connecting the scenes, fully demonstrate their acting prowess. Zhu Yin's several heartfelt monologues contrast sharply with the classic monologues deliberately learned from "A Chinese Odyssey", revealing the bitterness and sweetness of life outside the play, and laying a good ideological foundation for the protagonist's final behavior.

And the deliberate addition of popular elements such as product placement, Plants vs Zombies, etc., greatly enriches the film's watchability and creates many laugh points. This completes "Hey Shop" as a crazy multi-element comedy.

As a new director, a new genre film, especially in the extremely immature market of China, imitation is a necessary path. As long as the film's creative team can maintain a correct attitude, make every effort to imitate, and treat every detail with the most serious attitude, naturally a good movie can be created!
