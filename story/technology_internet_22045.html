Bill; Gates returned to his Alma mater to receive an honorary degree from Harvard

by huahuaniu on 2007-06-09 03:41:51

"Harvard's most famous dropout" has finally graduated. On June 7, Bill, the world's richest man and the founder of a software empire; Gates returned to his Alma mater, accepted an honorary degree from Harvard University, and realized his wish to wear a "square hat."

Fulfill a promise made 32 years ago

Gates 7 from Harvard provost Steven · Hyman received the certificate of Honorary Doctor of Laws (L.L.D.) degree (above). This kind of degree is mainly awarded to some famous politicians in recognition of their achievements.

Hyman said Gates was Harvard's "most outstanding" member of the class of 1977 and that it was time for his Alma mater to present him with a certificate.

Derrick, acting president of Harvard University In introducing Gates, Bock said, "Our speaker today is the world's most influential entrepreneur, who revolutionized the personal computer industry. From 2004 to 2007, he was named one of the 100 most influential people in the world for four consecutive years." Later, he joked to Gates: "If you had completed the remaining two years of school, what would you have achieved now?"

Gates opened his speech with a witty introduction. "Dad, I always told you I'd come back and get my degree, and now I've done it." "I've waited more than 30 years to say this," Gates said to his father, who attended the ceremony.

Gates entered Harvard law school in 1973 and was supposed to graduate in 1977, but he dropped out in 1975 to start his own business, but Harvard still considers him a Harvard graduate of the class of 1977. This year will mark the 30th anniversary of the class's graduation, so Harvard chose the date to present Gates with a big gift.

With a degree, it is easy to "job-hop"

"I'll be changing jobs next year. It'll be nice to finally have a college degree on my resume," Gates joked during his speech. In recent months, Gates has devoted much of his energy and time to "Bill and Melinda." The Gates Foundation is dedicated to addressing global health and education issues. He will step down from day-to-day management at Microsoft in 2008 to devote himself to the Gates Foundation.

Harvard students sell tickets

Gates encouraged Harvard's younger generation to devote themselves to public welfare, and interestingly, some students sold free tickets to the ceremony online, which is probably no less business sense than Gates.

It was reported that in Bill. Gates received his degree at a ceremony held before former US President Bill Gates. Clinton also arrived at Harvard University on June 6. The fame of the two Bills is so great that tickets to their speeches are in demand. Dozens of students with free tickets had an idea and resold tickets online, some for as much as $100.

The university was upset to learn that some students were selling tickets for profit and sent them an email warning not to do so.

I'm not advocating dropping out of school

In April of this year, Bill. Gates has talked about dropping out of school in interviews.

Reporter: You dropped out of Harvard 32 years ago. Why did you decide to drop out? Did you find something?

Gates: Me and my friend Paul. Alan has been through the whole process of computer development together because we are very keen to use computers, I have been since I was 13. We saw the idea of putting chips in computers, and we realized that it could make a big difference in how computers are used, from systems used by large organizations to tools used by individuals... When we saw the first computers that people could buy for very little money, we decided we should get into the market and wrote BASIC. So we started the company, and then I agreed to drop out and do it with them.

Reporter: You set an example for millions of young people, especially those who are now in college, who will jokingly say, "Look, Bill dropped out of Harvard, we can follow his example."

Gates: I encourage people to finish school unless there's something really urgent or something you can't miss. It would be much better to finish all your studies. In fact, before I left Harvard, I had studied there for three years. I enjoyed that time very much.

Reporter: So you only have one year to finish all your studies.

Gates: In fact, with the credits I had, it would have taken even less time than that. If I had known in advance that I would be there for three years, maybe I would have finished all my studies ahead of time. Of course, this thought did not prevent me from choosing to leave.
