Knockoff phones lie in wait: flooding to third and fourth-tier cities and online shopping platforms

by tuoqing1188 on 2011-08-04 09:45:26

"Shanzhai phones" lying low

Since the beginning of this year, the term "shanzhai phone" seems to have faded from people's sight, so many people believe that shanzhai phones are on the decline.

Survey data also seems to confirm this point. According to market research company iSuppli's statistics, in 2010, the shipment volume of shanzhai phones in China was approximately 24.2 million units, a decrease of nearly 30% compared to 33.2 million units in 2009. This is corroborated by the recent "increasing desolation" of Huaqiangbei Electronics Market in Shenzhen. During last weekend's visit to Huaqiangbei Electronics Market, the reporter discovered that the once bustling Zhenhua Mobile Phone City, Longsheng Mobile Phone City, and Gaokede Communications City, which were major distribution centers for shanzhai phones, have started to show signs of "emptiness." Apart from some stall owners still struggling to hold on at the ground floor shops, the vacancy rate of second and third-floor shops is astonishing. Even the most "famous" Mintong Digital City no longer exhibits its former prosperity; although there are still many merchants, their main products have quietly shifted towards tablet computers.

However, further investigation by the reporter revealed that the withdrawal of shanzhai phones from Huaqiangbei counters is merely a superficial phenomenon. Under the drive of latent market demand, Chinese-style "shanzhai phones" continue to grow, just in a different form.

"Withdrawing from Huaqiangbei counters is partly due to strict crackdowns and partly because the operating costs of physical channels have risen too quickly," Wang Liwu (a pseudonym), who has been handling shanzhai phone business in Huaqiangbei for four years, told the reporter the real reason behind the current "desolate" scene. It was reported that since December last year, the Shenzhen police launched a special operation named 'Bright Sword' targeting crimes related to intellectual property infringement. In just over half a year, the Shenzhen police dismantled a total of 13 dens producing and selling counterfeit and substandard mobile phones in the Huaqiangbei area, seizing 3,923 finished phones and 3,178 phone accessories. According to Wang Liwu, this round of "strict crackdown" differs from previous years as it is not only more intense but also lasts longer, undoubtedly having a significant impact on the operation of shanzhai phones.

"Our products inevitably involve some appearance copying, and with such continuous crackdowns, who would dare to open up for business?" However, for Wang Liwu, the bigger issue is the increasing disparity between operating profits and costs. He introduced that since the second half of 2010, price competition in the shanzhai phone market has become extremely fierce, with the price of 2.4-inch dual-SIM MTK solution straight mobile phones even dropping below 300 yuan. "In the past, selling one phone could earn 200-300 yuan, now one phone only earns 30-40 yuan, the profit is almost the same as selling computers, indeed there isn't much money to be made, I once considered changing industries," Wang Liwu told the reporter. While profit margins have thinned, his operating costs have continued to soar due to rising wages and counter rents. Therefore, after careful consideration, he chose to withdraw from physical counters.

However, this does not mean he has stopped doing shanzhai phone business. "Our main shipping channel has now shifted online," according to Wang Liwu. Currently, both his retail and wholesale businesses have moved to online platforms. "Retail mainly relies on Taobao, Paipai, etc., while wholesale uses shanzhai phone forums for promotion. Actually, many experts are now doing this. Online checks aren't as strict, and sales are pretty good," Wang Liwu's viewpoint seems to be supported by query results from Taobao. According to the query results, the Taobao store operated by Wang Liwu alone sold over 1,000 units of a high imitation iPhone appearance MTK solution dual SIM phone within 30 days, indicating quite a good business. "Selling online means renting a small house in a community is enough, goods can be shipped directly from the factory, saving some warehousing costs," Wang Liwu is quite pleased with his transformation.

Third and fourth-tier markets remain the support

While developing new online selling channels, these shanzhai phone dealers also began adjusting their business development focus in traditional mobile phone retail markets. Recently joined shanzhai phone industry dealer Ran Guang frequently travels between Shenzhen and Chenzhou. This young man from Chenzhou frankly told the reporter that shanzhai phones are indeed becoming harder to sell, but this doesn't mean they can't be sold. "The key is to choose the right market. Business in big cities definitely won't work. Not only have Nokia, Motorola, and other major brands already launched phones under 500 yuan, but telecom operators also offer a large number of prepaid phone fee gifts in urban markets. We simply can't compete by just selling phones. Moreover, city users are picky now, they don't just choose brands but also want smartphones. Crowding into cities is self-inflicted suffering. However, selling phones back in the towns near my hometown still holds great potential."

According to his introduction, compared to first and second-tier city markets, users in third and fourth-tier rural markets are relatively "easy to please." Price remains the primary factor these users consider when purchasing a phone, and features like multiple speakers, long standby times, and large screens inherent to shanzhai phones are very suitable for the needs of users in third and fourth-tier markets. "For example, in one county in our hometown, during good business periods, there can be over a hundred units sold per month, although the quantity isn't large, it can accumulate bit by bit," Ran Guang introduced. Currently, he has distributed goods to several counties around his hometown. Besides traditional husband-and-wife stores, he has even reached supply agreements with some operator business halls. "Business halls in smaller places are mostly franchised, not directly operated, so management in this aspect isn't very strict, but users don't know, they think all phones bought in business halls are genuine!"

After the popularization of 3G, they may make a comeback

Despite the support of online channels and third and fourth-tier cities, the weakening demand for shanzhai phones remains a "cruel" reality that relevant practitioners must face. According to relevant statistical data, the number of domestic manufacturers engaged in shanzhai phones has now decreased to about 2,000, less than one-fifth of the peak period.

Regarding this, Yang Qun, chief analyst of Warring States Strategies, expressed that it is normal. "Those doing shanzhai phones are making quick money, now there isn't much profit to be made, and the volume is also decreasing, naturally some manufacturers will give up." However, he also stated that this does not mean the shanzhai phone industry will completely disappear. "Profit margin is always the key driving force behind capital flow among different industries." Yang Qun said that currently, 3G smartphones are the consumption hotspot, but due to the immaturity of upstream solutions, shanzhai manufacturers cannot massively enter this market, so they have chosen to retreat. However, once 3G phones see an all-in-one solution similar to MTK in the 2G era, it's not impossible for shanzhai manufacturers to make a comeback.

In fact, this possibility is increasing. According to Qualcomm's plan, by the end of 2011, its low-end chip solution will reduce the cost of 3G smartphones to the $150 level, with plans to further lower it to $100 in the future. MediaTek will also mass-produce chips developed for the Android system in the third quarter of this year and launch more advanced 3G smart chips by the end of the year. Additionally, domestic companies like Rockchip and Spreadtrum are actively deploying related product markets. The industry estimates that 2012 will be the year when 3G smartphones priced around 1,000 yuan will appear en masse.

The scaling and commoditization of upstream enterprises in the 3G smartphone chip sector is precisely the "timely rain" that shanzhai manufacturers have been eagerly awaiting. "The transition from 2G to 3G will be a long process. With such a large base of Chinese mobile phone users, relying solely on carrier subsidies is insufficient. A large number of inexpensive terminals from social channels are needed to accelerate the popularization of 3G," Yang Qun stated.

According to MIIT statistics, as of June this year, the number of mobile phone users in our country has nearly reached 900 million, while the total number of 3G users has just exceeded 80 million, with a 3G penetration rate barely exceeding 9%. This indicates that in the coming years, there will be a demand for over 800 million 3G phone replacements. For shanzhai manufacturers that have gone "undercover," this latent replacement demand represents an opportunity for a comeback.
