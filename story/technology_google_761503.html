Tech Master's Experience with Google Glass: Once You Put It On, You Don't Want to Take It Off

by geekzhang on 2013-05-03 14:41:40

The first batch of Google Glasses have gradually reached the hands of the lucky first buyers, and they haven't forgotten to share their experiences with readers. In the past two weeks, tech guru Robert Scoble has been using these smart glasses, and here is his personal review:

This week, I gave five speeches with it; went through four airports (and will do so again in a few hours); I let hundreds of curious people try them on.

Since I got it, except for sleeping time, I rarely took it off. Here are some of my personal feelings after using Google Glass for 2 weeks:

1. Since unboxing it, my life every day has revolved around it because it's truly outstanding.

2. The success of Google Glass completely depends on its price. After each of my speeches, I would ask the audience who wanted to get Google Glass? When I set the price at $200, everyone raised their hand; when the price was raised to $500, some hands went up. This price structure perception is consistent whether for students or more mature, mainstream buyer groups.

3. Almost everyone can't contain their excitement: "Wow", "Amazing", "This is crazy", "Stunning" - I've heard these comments over and over again.

4. At NextWeb, I was surrounded by more than 50 people, each wanting to personally try out these glasses. No one wanted to let me go. It's been a long time since anything else has fascinated people this much. This kind of thing lasted for a week, it was really crazy.

5. Before arriving in Germany, I hardly encountered any privacy issues. There was no negative reaction, which shocked me a bit (only once did an audience member tell me that they wouldn't talk to me while I was wearing Google Glass). Interestingly, some people asked me to wear it in the bathroom, but I refused then.

6. Overall, I found there was a generation gap in acceptance of Google Glass. Older people think they might use it, but still remain skeptical, not as excited as those aged 13 to 21. They seem less enthusiastic about such products becoming future trends.

Reasonable pricing for Google Glass

So, let's talk about the price. I bet Larry Page is considering two possible price points: one is around $500, clearly profitable; the other is $200, which basically just covers the cost. If you're extremely curious or bored, take this glass apart, and you'll immediately find that many of the components aren't expensive. These designs can be mass-produced. In other words, millions can be produced. However, the only way Google can sell so many is to keep the price below $300.

However, if Larry Page really could be so aggressive and set the price of the glasses around $200, I wouldn't be surprised. But why does Google want to do this?

It's very simple: now, I am already very addicted to Google services. My photos and videos automatically upload to Google+, and other services and apps will soon be possible (I just got a Twitter photo app). However, the downside is that enabling automatic uploads to other services significantly reduces the battery life of my phone and glasses (and the battery life of these devices isn't currently ideal). Therefore, I need to maintain some resistance to various apps, not adding Facebook, LinkedIn, Twitter, Evernote, Tumblr to my Google Glass. Moreover, Google's default option, Google+, works well enough to meet my needs.

Google Glass may indicate a business model transformation

In addition, Google bans ads within apps. For Google, this means a huge business model shift. I believe Larry Page is working on transforming Google from an ad-based company into a commerce-based company.

In all my attempts, my first failure happened when I tried to find a sushi restaurant. I believe this will be resolved soon, and Google can collect every small payment I make, like reserving a seat at a restaurant, delivering a book, or having Bloomingdales send me some jeans.

From this perspective, this new commerce-based system will generate billions of dollars in revenue, and we won't have to pause and watch ads as we do on Facebook or other services.

Google Glass: A New Revelation

After wearing these glasses for two weeks, you get a completely different revelation, and it will bring you to a new business world. Why?

1. Google Glass has stronger social functions than traditional smartphones. Why? While I'm talking, I don't have to take my eyes off my friends to use Google, get directions, or do other things.

2. The voice function performs well. It can almost always recognize anyone's speech in any situation. I feel this is basically the first product that everyone can control with their voice. Actually, it's quite amazing. Even though I know that the magic lies in the fact that the glasses only want to hear a small number of commands. OK Glass, Take a Picture, and it works smoothly; however, OK Glass, Take a Photo doesn't work. Google Glass wants you to limit your commands to a specific set, so it can't understand other ways of saying it. This makes the accuracy high, even if you have a strong accent, it doesn't matter.

The camera function amazed me. It completely changed the concept of photography and video. Why? Now I can capture great moments. I deliberately counted, taking out a smartphone from my pocket, unlocking it, finding the photo app, waiting for it to load, and then taking a picture takes about 6 to 12 seconds. Google Glass only needs less than a second. My hands are completely free - when I unload heavy groceries from the car and my child is doing something cute, I don't have to fumble around.

I've been telling people that this feels like going back to 1977, unpacking the Apple II with my dad. The Apple II was very expensive at the time, although it didn't do much, but I knew my life had changed dramatically because of it, and it kept getting better. This week, I got a new New York Times RSS and Twitter app, and more apps will follow.

This is the most interesting new product since the Apple iPhone. Believe me, I never make such judgments lightly.

Yes, we can still nitpick - the camera's performance is average in low light; there aren't enough practical uses yet; it looks a little silly, like a real freak (the appearance issue may improve after entering the market).

But none of that matters because my life has changed because of it, and I can't live without it for a moment.

It's really excellent.

Now, Larry Page, figure out a way to lower the price to $200, and you'll see orders flooding in.
