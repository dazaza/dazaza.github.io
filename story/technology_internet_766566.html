How Google's employees use their 20% time

by anonymous on 2013-09-18 21:58:49

Original English Title: How Googlers Use Their 20% Time

Based on some comments from Googler employees in this article, we can see how Google engineers use their 20% time for pet projects.

Recently, Quartz published a report claiming that Google's famous "20% time" is now effectively defunct. This "20% time" allowed any Google engineer to work on any project they liked. They cited an unnamed internal source:

Google’s "20% time": allowing employees to spend one day per week on other projects has been eliminated.

The following explains how Google effectively shut down the 20% time without actually terminating the program. According to the source, initially, as previously reported, Google began requiring engineers working on independent projects during their 20% time to get managerial approval, which clearly deviated from the company's previous policy: everyone could freely allocate their 20% time.

However, senior Google management recently went further by strongly discouraging managers from approving any 20% projects.

This report sparked a debate on the Hacker News website, but through these comments, we can uncover the truth. Google employees disclosed some details and the current status of the 20% program. Below are direct quotes from individuals who claim to be Google employees, though their identities as Googlers have not been verified.

dekhn denies that 20% time has been abolished but admits that 20% time is actually part of 120%, and he mocks it via a Dilbert cartoon:

"20% time still exists... Over the past three years, I’ve turned my 20% time projects into products twice—once with my previous job and once now. No manager has ever pressured me not to do 20% projects; at the same time, my performance reviews have consistently rated me as a productive Google employee."

"It’s more fair to call it 120% time. Honestly, starting a new project from scratch while maintaining a productive full-time workload is hard. If you want to succeed, you need to put in extra hours beyond your regular work schedule."

"The real meaning of 20% time is that, as a Google engineer, you have the opportunity to access and use Google's infrastructure to experiment with your ideas or build a new system. The infrastructure and related software tools that Google provides act like a lever, making 20% time highly productive. Definitely, many of my colleagues and I have a simple yet strong desire to use our free time to work on these projects using the company's infrastructure because we are genuinely interested in creating new products."

spankalee also said that they can use 20% time to work on plans they like, but added that most people don't actually do so:

"I don’t need approval to get 20% time, and during this time, I and a large group of colleagues work on 20% projects."

"I can confirm that many people don’t use their 20% time. It might be due to cultural changes, new employees, lack of imagination, or the pressure to stand out in their main projects (normal working hours). I don’t know, but it’s frustrating. Nevertheless, it’s necessary in engineering not to require approval."

He added that if the main project is tight, the project manager can ask to delay the 20% time for a quarter:

"If your manager asks you to go all-in on the current main project, he will request you to save your 20% time for a quarter, and then you can use the saved time later."

spankalee admitted that different departments may have different policies:

"I agree, it depends on the culture of each department. For some people who have been working towards deadlines for a long time, I can imagine they might not feel the 20% projects. Continuous work isn’t a good way to work, and when I hear about some departments or projects still doing this, I feel sorry for their teams."

"Personally, I was slightly discouraged by the broad openness of 20% time. I asked around, and many people initially suggested I shouldn’t start 20% projects too early. Instead of starting something new, it would be better to work with higher-level engineers on existing projects. At least, they said, make sure I get feedback from them. This wasn’t company policy, just advice. Everyone has their own theory on the best way to improve themselves. Some people have already started their 20% projects. I think, if you only focus on how to advance in your career, you should take this advice seriously. I hope most Google employees don’t solely focus on promotions."

"I’m glad I ignored this advice because I believe I did very interesting things, and also because I gained recognition. I’m glad that due to our company’s policy, I ignored this advice."

Cookingboy said his manager told them that 20% time could only be used for things directly related to the current project:

"I’m a Google employee, and our manager told us there’s no problem with 20% time, but we can only do things that directly contribute to our core work. 20% time completely depends on your team or department, although the policy allows it, different teams have different cultures. I think, in some low-workload 'old school' departments, the situation with 20% time is better; however, in certain departments, such as Android and social (G+) departments (these teams may be continuously busy under 'production' pressure), the situation with 20% time is more disappointing."

Another Google employee, zhyder, added that although 20% time exists, most employees cannot obtain it because their regular workload is already full:

"I’m a Google employee, and the comments from other Google employees here are correct: 20% time still exists and does not require managerial approval, but only a small portion of engineers can access it. For example, in our team of 14 colleagues, only 2 people have 20% projects."

"I haven’t been at Google for long, but I doubt there was ever a time when most engineers could work on 20% projects. This is hard to say: you not only need to fully commit to your 100% time work, but also focus on the main project (like all software, it may take longer than you like to build). Most engineers are not motivated enough."

There are also some former Google employees who share similar views: billnguyen, So8res, nappy-doo, plywoodtrees.

These statements give us some insights: Google tightly controls its internal workings and strictly manages all relations with the press. These also reflect that the 20% time is not as widespread within Google as you might imagine. Some might feel that many engineers already have enough assigned projects. Perhaps, after the first few years, when hundreds of internal Google projects had faded away, the company developed a more standardized approach to new ideas, allowing only a few engineers to dedicate time to research them.
