Theory and Techniques of Haircutting

1. One Length

When all the hair reaches the outline shape along its natural fall.

All the hair reaches the outline shape along its natural fall, forming a triangular effect with longer hair in front and shorter at the back. We call this the "One Length Triangle."

2. One Length

②All the hair reaches the outline shape along its natural fall, forming an outline parallel to the ground. We refer to this as the "One Length Square."

③All the hair reaches the outline shape along its natural fall, forming an outline that is shorter in front and longer at the back. We call this the "One Length Circle."

3. Graduation

A gradual build-up of weight:

(1) Classic/Pure Graduation

Gradual build-up of weight, out and away from the head, forming a layered weight accumulation that is longer in front and shorter at the back. This is called the "Graduation Triangle" (commonly referred to as the classic weight buildup).

(2) Round Graduation

Gradual build-up of weight following the head shape, forming a layered weight accumulation that is shorter in front and longer at the back. This is called the "Graduation Circle."

(3) Square Graduation

Gradual build-up of weight with internal corners, forming a layered weight accumulation parallel to the ground. This is called the "Graduation Square."

4. Layering

Removal of weight:

(1) Classic/Square Layering

Weight removed from all four surfaces of the head (top, back, right side, and left side), making the cut parallel to the ground. The completed layer is called the "Layer Square."

(2) Round Layering

Each section maintains equal length and proportion without any definite increase or decrease in internal weight. The cut follows the curve of the head, completing the layer as the "Layer Circle."

(3) Concave Layering

A curved line in direct opposition to the natural shape of the head, where center lengths are shorter and outer lengths are longer. The completed layer is called the "Concave Layer."

5. Combination

Combining elements of some or all of the three cutting techniques. Mixing two or more structures in the design of haircuts' layers is referred to as "Combination," and most designs involve combinations.

Haircutting Dynamics

Tension: Pull

The amount of pressure or pull applied to the hair, which is the main factor determining the characteristics of the lines.

Over-direction: Direction

Pulling the hair away from its natural fall direction. Over-direction controls the geometric shape of the hairstyle on the horizontal plane.

Over-direction: Movement

Elevafion: Lift

Elevafion controls the geometric shape of the hairstyle on the vertical plane.

Elevafion: Volume

The volume created by the lift.
