Discussion on the Application and Operation of Indicators

The market is accustomed to using indicators for analysis because they are the simplest tools to identify, and due to their ease of use. However, it is also because of this simplicity that the market tends to learn and apply these simple tools. Yet, often when applying them, it seems that the results are not very satisfactory, not quite effective. But when used retrospectively, they seem particularly accurate. So, where exactly does the problem lie?

The main difference lies in the usage during trading hours versus after the market close. During trading hours, prices are still in motion, so what may look good at one moment can change with just a slight fluctuation, resulting in a time lag in the application of indicator settings.

Is it possible to use them effectively by adjusting this time lag? This is indeed possible because the application of the time lag involves "initial brewing, completion of brewing, initiation period, and finally completion," all of which have certain time-related relationships.

More importantly, indicators are often formed due to price relations, so ultimately it is the changes in price that affect the final relationship values of the indicators, which is the most crucial part. Due to the interference of participants in large or small quantities, this often affects the final outcome. Therefore, if too many people use the same indicator conditions, it will also alter the original pattern.

For example, during the initial brewing phase, there are often people quietly accumulating shares. At this time, the trading volume is generally not large, so a gradual buying trend can be observed. After the time frame is completed and enters the completion of brewing, since the accumulation is finished, any increase in volume will result in an upward movement. At this point, the trading volume becomes a matter of turnover rate. As long as the trading volume is within a controlled range, the original accumulators can steadily control the situation, and the stock price's fluctuation and increase in value will continue.

The question is, how to initiate the move? When to initiate? What is the target? For instance, currently, the market is filled with many short-term trading models: buy today and sell tomorrow, with the goal being to earn only 1% profit. Since the pursuit is daily profits, forming rolling profits, this leads to situations where despite heavy trading today, after a rise tomorrow, immediate adjustments are made without willingness to operate for even one more day. Encountering such an operational model, the market might be left bewildered.

To complete the operational target for each stock, it ultimately depends on the mindset of those involved. If their mindset is stable and they pursue long-term benefits, then after following them, there won't be intentional washouts or fluctuations. Instead, there will be steady and firm progress, which is considered the best operational method. However, such investors are rare.

Thus, eventually, the market resorts to short-term operations, leading to increasingly shorter operational modes for various tools, giving rise to different operational tools accordingly. In fact, any tool for technical analysis has certain limitations. They usually rely on closing prices for calculations, so they only provide statistical probabilities, generally no higher than 50%, or reaching up to 60% probability at most.

Exceeding such ratios is already difficult because there has never been an indicator that can be operated to achieve a probability of over 80%. This is mainly because stock prices are dynamic and change according to market demand, never remaining static. Therefore, when assuming a condition remains unchanged but the market changes, it proves that humans are diverse and variable, making human nature the most complex and hard to discern. Of course, it's impossible to analyze human psychological factors through a single indicator.
