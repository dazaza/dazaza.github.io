The current state of China's economy

by yangliu435sh on 2012-02-18 15:41:28

1. Domestic funds are very abundant. In August, the central bank was still issuing bills to withdraw liquidity, and at the same time, due to insufficient market demand, enterprises' willingness to borrow is also weakening. Moreover, with investment funds flowing back to banks, the loan-to-deposit ratio will decrease.

2. The potential of infrastructure to boost domestic demand is limited. For more than a decade, the country has maintained an extremely high enthusiasm for infrastructure, and fiscal investment has always been expanding. It's unknown how much bank debt various levels of bureaucratic institutions have incurred for this purpose, but the impact on domestic demand is limited because it is a chain of corruption, just like real estate. It only leads to wealth concentration and the expansion of grassroots debt.

3. Fine-tuning of the exchange rate will no longer have a decisive impact on exports. Whether or not the U.S. passes its bailout plan, global financial regulation will become stricter, consumer credit will contract, and the U.S. will prepare for several years of tight days to recuperate. This not only objectively causes global demand insufficiency but also triggers employment crises and the rise of trade protectionism in various countries. The impact of exchange rate fluctuations is relatively small.

4. Insufficient domestic demand and export decline will lead to the withdrawal of foreign capital. This chain reaction is the most fatal to an economy structure that depends on foreign trade. Although the official loudly claims that China's economy will continue to grow, enterprises are the most sensitive and decisive in response to market changes.

5. Rural reforms will not be effective due to systemic influences. Insufficient domestic demand is a distribution structure issue and also a systemic problem. The current top-down management system cannot effectively supervise the vast bureaucratic system. Therefore, insufficient domestic demand is a chronic ailment. Using the same logic, we can deduce that expectations for rural reform are also limited. The imagined interest distribution is one thing, and the actual interest distribution during operation is another. Moreover, China's rural areas are too extensive. Even the highly concentrated urban grassroots bureaucracy cannot be managed well. With the current system, rural areas cannot be effectively supervised.

The characteristics of China's economy are deflationary, while inflation is watered-down (monetary) deflation: The core characteristic of China's economy is deflation, and once monetary policy and fiscal policy loosen, it transforms into inflation.

No economic policy can change the wealth distribution model determined by the system. Therefore, the core characteristic of China's economy with insufficient domestic demand and concentrated wealth is deflation. If economic stimulation is achieved through investment expansion, once the economic cycle from production to distribution is completed, China will re-enter a deflation channel. The newly added capacity will continuously accumulate, further strengthening the foreign trade-dependent economic model. Because government-led investment does not resolve the institutional framework where wealth continuously concentrates; it is not a fiscal transfer payment.

At the same time, under the influence of expansionary policies aimed at reversing deflation, the prices of people's livelihood consumption goods will rise stepwise, and the social money supply will gradually increase. From this inflation, regulation, and price drop, we can see that even if prices fall, they won't return to the starting point. It’s a correction within an upward trend because the money supply is increasing. Deflation-related price drops are relative to the amount of currency. The reduction in enterprise profit margins and economic vitality (a decrease in the number of new enterprises and an increase in the number of closed enterprises) are absolute manifestations of deflation. From these absolute manifestations, deflation and inflation are actually two sides of the same coin of supply-demand imbalance and economic deterioration. Inflation is watered-down (monetary) deflation.

China's insufficient domestic demand mainly stems from heavy investment and light distribution:

It was previously assumed that massive investments, such as in the real estate industry, would naturally boost consumption. However, the sustainable power indicators in the industrial chain - wealth distribution structures - were overlooked.

For example, in the real estate industry, developers, the government, and banks collect 80% of the wealth produced throughout the entire industrial chain operation and deposit it in the form of savings, which then precipitates out of the economic circulation domain. This highly concentrated wealth has a particularly low consumption desire, leading to macroeconomic inefficiency despite massive investments. Although there is a large instantaneous pull effect on GDP, the sustainability is extremely poor, and there are many side effects.

Regardless of what kind of fiscal or monetary policy is used, most of the money circulates within China's political-economic system and ends up concentrated and precipitated again. A visible problem in China is the extreme distortion of the existing wealth distribution structure, while a hidden problem is the so-called characteristic political system.

Almost 99% of economists have been talking nonsense and clichés about remedies for China's economy. Consumption accounts for only 36% of GDP, which can be recorded as a special achievement in world history.

China's economic problems (mainly domestic demand issues) cannot be solved by the system itself:

China's economic model of insufficient domestic demand relying on foreign trade has been shaped by the system according to its own interest distribution characteristics and its powerful unilateral political force. Despite having a large amount of savings, it cannot be converted into consumption, maintaining a model of exploiting people's livelihoods. Can domestic demand simply be boosted? The system's strength lowers the consumption rate. The lower the consumption rate, the stronger the system's strength, making it harder to turn around easily.

The root cause of China's economic problems is the syndrome of systemic wealth concentration, and the solution is to first stabilize the distorted wealth distribution structure. One of the main methods is to adjust the wealth stock through taxation and fiscal transfer payments, restructuring the wealth distribution. After 30 years of reform, assets have inflated, but property taxes have been long absent.

However, the autocratic system does not tax bureaucrats and the rich.

For instance, inheritance tax and property tax require a detailed, transparent, and precise database of the assets of extremely influential taxpayers in society and must have an independent collection system that avoids systemic influence.

These two points cannot be achieved under an autocratic system. First, the transparency and precision of personal assets mean exposing the essence of the system, dismantling all lies and masks, which could trigger social and political unrest. Without the transparency and precision of personal assets, wealth continues to concentrate through systemic power, suppressing terminal consumption capabilities. It's impossible to effectively balance wealth distribution through taxation and transfer payments, ultimately making the economy unsustainable. This road has reached its end.

Second, an independent collection system targeting the wealthy elite must be guaranteed by constitutional democracy. No one likes paying taxes themselves, let alone when many elites' properties aren't just tax issues but legal violations. Self-restraint is something that won't happen and needs to be prevented by force.

From the perspective of national capital security, China, with its highly concentrated wealth, is actually the easiest and most convenient country for capital outflows. Wealth concentrated in a few hands is easier to transfer, whereas if wealth is dispersed among the majority, unless other countries can absorb enough Chinese people, it's almost impossible to absorb most of China's wealth as easily as now through the immigration of a few individuals.

China doesn't even match the system from 100 years ago. Emperors treated the world as private property but still cared about their dynasty's legacy and could produce a few enlightened rulers. Civil and military officials couldn't carry their accumulated wealth and emigrate abroad. Nowadays, in China, it's just replacing one batch, exploiting another, and running away with another.
