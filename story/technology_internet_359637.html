PBOC's Liang Meng: Promoting low-carbon focuses on building carbon market

by ditan360 on 2010-07-01 11:24:47

Mr. Liang Meng, Deputy Researcher of the International Finance Department of the Research Bureau of the People's Bank of China

There is no doubt that "low-carbon" is the most fashionable term these days. All industries are taking various measures to promote low-carbon in a big way. So what exactly is the relationship between the current "low-carbon" and the commonly discussed "environmental protection" in the past? What role will financial institutions play in promoting low-carbon? With these series of questions, the reporter recently interviewed Mr. Liang Meng, Deputy Researcher of the International Finance Department of the Research Bureau of the People's Bank of China.

Low-carbon and environmental protection are not entirely consistent

Liang Meng told reporters that currently many people are talking about low-carbon, but few understand what it means, even mixing low-carbon with environmental protection. In his view, there are mainly four differences between low-carbon and environmental protection:

First, different emissions. The carbon emissions in low-carbon mainly refer to six types of greenhouse gases including carbon dioxide, while environmental protection targets pollutant emissions, mainly including sulfur dioxide, industrial waste (wastewater, exhaust gas, waste residue), etc., which are regional pollutants.

Second, different supervisory departments. In China, the treatment of regional pollutants is supervised by the Ministry of Environmental Protection, while activities related to greenhouse gas reduction are mainly overseen by the National Development and Reform Commission.

Third, different legal foundations. China has an environmental protection law, but this law does not classify carbon dioxide and other greenhouse gases as pollutants. Regional pollution control can be solved within the framework of national laws, while carbon dioxide control falls under the scope of international law. Therefore, promoting low-carbon involves the global scale, requiring legally binding documents recognized by all countries, hence the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change and the Kyoto Protocol. The regrettable aspect of the Copenhagen negotiations was the failure to form an internationally binding agreement.

In short, environmental protection work can be done separately by each country, but low-carbon cannot. That is to say, environmental protection work is regional, while low-carbon is global. From a global environmental perspective, environmental protection and low-carbon can be considered consistent, but for each country, environmental protection and low-carbon have distinct differences.

Fourth, developing the economy does not necessarily have to come at the expense of destroying the environment; the environment can be well protected while developing the economy, as Western industrial development history has shown. However, China is currently in a period of high economic growth, with rapid industrial development, more and more people moving from rural to urban areas, and gradually improving living standards, which will inevitably lead to a rapid increase in energy consumption. Given that China’s energy structure, dominated by fossil fuels, is difficult to change in the short term, greenhouse gas emissions in China will inevitably increase. Therefore, overemphasizing low-carbon in the near future does not conform to China's national conditions. However, China is a responsible developing major power, committing to reducing unit GDP energy consumption by 20% by 2010, and further committing to reducing unit GDP carbon dioxide emissions by 40%-45% by 2020 compared to 2005 levels, with non-fossil energy accounting for about 15% of primary energy consumption, indicating China's commitment to developing a low-carbon economy.

How can finance boost low-carbon?

Regarding the issue of many cities or enterprises wanting to apply for loans related to low-carbon cities or low-carbon enterprises, Liang Meng pointed out that currently, relevant standards have not been issued by the state ministries, so financial institutions do not know whom to support. Throughout the interview, what left the deepest impression on the reporter was Liang Meng's repeated emphasis that to develop a low-carbon economy, financial services must take the lead. To make finance a driving force for low-carbon, the premise is to first establish a carbon trading market; otherwise, financial institutions will have nothing to rely on.

The economy determines finance, and the adjustment of economic and industrial structures caused by the development of a low-carbon economy, as well as changes in economic development models, consumption patterns, and even lifestyles, will inevitably lead to a structural revolution in the international financial industry. Finance will become an important driving force and regulatory lever for achieving transformation in development methods, adjusting economic structures, and innovating optimizations. For finance to play a role in the development of a low-carbon economy, it must follow certain rules, and these rules come from the carbon trading market.

Comprehensively speaking, low-carbon can, to some extent, affect the speed of economic development because for enterprises, while obtaining indirect social benefits, reducing carbon requires paying additional costs. How to compensate for these costs requires the participation of financial institutions. For financial institutions to participate, they need a carbon exchange to form a carbon price, allowing the calculation of the cost that should be compensated to enterprises by multiplying the emission reduction amount by the carbon price.

To form a carbon price, Liang Meng pointed out that strengthening the construction of carbon trading platforms, cultivating a carbon trading market system, and involving more investors is the basis for discovering and forming carbon prices. Now the stock market is very popular, and many people, even some farmers, have started investing in stocks because the stock market has already been completely marketized, making its information relatively credible. Without over 100 million stock investors, it would be impossible to ensure true prices. Similarly, the carbon trading market should also be like this. It cannot only allow emission reduction enterprises to participate; it needs a large number of financial institutions, enterprises, and individuals to truly form a carbon price. Liang Meng emphasized repeatedly that the low-carbon market must involve wide participation; otherwise, it will become a case of me buying and you selling, resulting in false prices. Low-carbon finance has much to do, among which determining the carbon price is the most important. After financial institutions participate, many financial products can be derived, making transactions smoother; additionally, financial resources and social resources can be allocated to low-carbon projects and technologies, including loans, bonds, stocks, and their derivative products.

If the carbon market is formed, financial institutions will find it easy to conduct business. For example, if an enterprise can reduce emissions by 1 million tons per year, and the carbon market price is 100 yuan per ton, then the enterprise will have an income of 100 million yuan, which is predictable cash flow, and financial institutions can lend to such enterprises. Then how can low-carbon become profitable? It can only rely on the market because the market is a long-term, self-compensating mechanism. For enterprises, increasing income through the carbon market by selling carbon indicators is compensation. For example, if an enterprise's emission reduction cost is 50 yuan per ton, and the carbon market price is 60 yuan per ton, not only will the entire emission reduction cost be covered, but an additional income of 10 yuan per ton can be obtained. Conversely, if another enterprise's emission reduction cost is 70 yuan per ton, and the carbon market price is 60 yuan per ton, the enterprise may consider purchasing carbon indicators from the carbon market instead of reducing emissions. For financial institutions, when they discover enterprises with emission reduction costs below 50 yuan per ton, they can support such enterprises. This will promote the rapid development of such enterprises, enabling the entire country's emission reduction actions to form a virtuous cycle.

Additionally, the concept of the carbon market is at least nationwide. For instance, establishing a provincial carbon trading market in Hubei, where many enterprises are assigned emission reduction tasks, these enterprises might feel too much pressure and relocate elsewhere to continue production to avoid emission reduction tasks. Therefore, building a carbon market at the national level is authoritative and can truly play the role of the carbon market.

Liang Meng believes that the path of low-carbon is still a long and arduous one. First, the carbon market must be built, secondly, reasonable distribution is needed, and developed countries mostly adopt a combination of free allocation and auctioning. Thirdly, transactions must be conducted to form futures markets and options markets. In the future, if other countries can purchase carbon indicators from China, then China's carbon trading market will become internationalized.

Liang Meng emphasized that establishing a carbon market is better done earlier rather than later. Establishing a carbon trading market early can avoid the problem of fixed high-carbon investment paths. China's consumption is still insufficient, and GDP still mainly relies on investment-driven growth. Fixed assets formed by investments will create continuous carbon emissions over a long period. Therefore, during the initial stage of investment, it is necessary to consider low-carbon emissions. Otherwise, it will be fixed on a relatively high-carbon path. If carbon emission limits are set for enterprises earlier, and a carbon market and carbon price signals are established, enterprises will consider emission reduction benefits and penalties for exceeding emissions when making investment decisions.

How do developed countries use financial means to promote the development of low-carbon industries?

Globally, the current international carbon trading market is mainly led by financial institutions with participation from large enterprises. For example, the European Carbon Trading Market (EU ETS) and the Chicago Climate Exchange in the United States. Among them, the European market has the largest trading scale, with over 6 billion EUAs traded in 2009, with a total value reaching $118 billion, accounting for nearly 80% of the global carbon trading volume. Currently, CCX has approximately 400 members from various industries such as aviation, automobiles, electricity, environment, transportation, and finance.

For example, the European Emissions Trading (EU ETS) is implemented in three phases. The first phase was from January 1, 2005, to December 31, 2007; the second phase was from January 1, 2008, to December 31, 2012; and the third phase is from 2013 to 2020. It stipulates that starting from 2005, enterprises will be fined 40 euros for every ton of CO2 emissions exceeding the limit; starting from 2008, the fine increases to 100 euros per ton of excess emissions. This plan covers about 12,000 energy and industrial enterprises in the 27 EU countries, applicable to all types of companies and enterprises, including investment banks, funds, and intermediaries. Under the threat of high fines, it greatly promotes the enthusiasm of related enterprises to reduce emissions or engage in carbon trading behavior by purchasing emission credits in developing countries. For example, in Germany, there has been a shift from high-emission coal-fired power generation to the vigorous development of wind power and biomass energy utilization. In the UK, there has been a strong promotion of biomass energy and improvement in the efficiency of coal-fired power plants to reduce CO2 emissions per unit of electricity generated.

Additionally, Liang Meng introduced that developed countries have committed to providing adaptation funds to help the world enhance its adaptive capacity to climate change. For example, although the US does not commit to bearing emission reduction obligations, it has proposed providing $100 billion as an adaptation fund. Furthermore, the German Reconstruction Credit Institute (KfW) provides low-interest long-term loans for old house renovations. Through subsidies from the German government plus financing to offset this part of the interest, commercial banks obtain lower-interest loans, thereby directly lending to individuals at lower interest rates. Moreover, the energy efficiency of renovated old houses is rated, so the relative price of old houses will be higher when resold, increasing residents' participation enthusiasm, thus forming a virtuous cycle.

How can enterprises achieve low-carbon?

During the interview, Liang Meng particularly emphasized that energy conservation and low-carbon are not entirely consistent. For example, many enterprises talk about energy conservation, but energy conservation leads to an increase in national carbon emissions. Because the behavior of enterprises conserving energy results in increased investment, and the production of investment goods consumes energy and increases carbon emissions. If the carbon emissions caused by upstream production cannot be recovered by the enterprise's energy-saving behavior, overall, micro-level energy conservation will lead to macro-level increased emissions. This is a typical example of "fallacy of composition" in economics. A famous example includes the situation where everyone saving money leads to slow economic development; if one person stands up to watch a game, he will see more clearly, but if everyone stands up, it will be the same as sitting down.

Given that China has not yet formed a real carbon market, Liang Meng believes that if enterprises want to implement low-carbon, they can currently focus on doing three things: first, source materials locally to reduce carbon emissions during transportation; second, conduct reasonable design. Ensure that product appearance does not go out of style, extend product life, and reduce material usage; improve processing technology during production to reduce the use of defective products, waste, and scrap materials, lowering the defect rate. Third, reduce unnecessary packaging and use recyclable packaging materials as much as possible, which is both environmentally friendly and low-carbon.
