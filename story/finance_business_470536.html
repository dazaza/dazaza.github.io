M&A in small appliances will continue:警惕loss of domestic brands

by tuoqing1188 on 2011-08-04 10:12:00

Philips Acquires Pentium, SEB Increases Stake in Supor

■ New Express Reporter Chen Xuedong

One is confirmed to be acquired, the other continues to see increased stakes. Pentium Electric and Supor, once giants of domestic small appliance manufacturers, are gradually losing their local characteristics. For domestic small appliance enterprises, while being acquired by strong companies like Philips and SEB (Seb Electrical) can bring substantial industrial capital and foreign technology, it may also lead to the gradual loss of independent brands. This is not good for consumers, as the almost complete acquisition of domestic daily chemical industries by foreign companies serves as a cautionary tale.

1. Mergers and acquisitions follow one after another

On July 11, Philips Electronics announced that it had agreed to acquire Pentium Electric. After completing confirmatory due diligence and other customary closing conditions, the acquisition is expected to be completed in the fourth quarter of 2011. This is the first official statement from Philips regarding the acquisition of Pentium, following the acquisition of Supor by the French small appliance giant SEB (Seb Electrical), another foreign giant moves to acquire a domestic small appliance company.

Kong Xianghui, Chairman and Executive Vice President of Philips Greater China, stated that the global headquarters of the company's home care business department has been relocated to Shanghai. The acquisition of Pentium will help expand product lines and accelerate the layout of the Chinese kitchen appliance market. Hong Shibin, Deputy Director of the Marketing Committee of the China Home Appliance Business Association, told reporters that acquiring Pentium could make up for Philips' shortcomings in third and fourth-tier markets, directly increasing revenue and market share. According to Zhongyi Kang data, in 2007, Pentium's revenue exceeded 1 billion yuan. From January to April 2011, Pentium's rice cooker market share was 7.5%, ranking third in China; its induction cookers and electric pressure cookers ranked fourth.

Three days before the official announcement of Philips' acquisition of Pentium, Su Bao's controlling shareholder, France's SEB (Seb Electrical), announced that it had received approval from China's Ministry of Commerce, which in principle agreed to the transfer of approximately 20% equity from Supor Group and its founder, Su Zengfu. After the transaction is completed, SEB International's holding will reach 71.31%, Supor Group's holding will drop to 11.76%, and founder Su Zengfu will no longer directly hold shares in the company. This means that the Su family is further withdrawing from the company they founded.

Following Philips' acquisition steps, there is also the domestic enterprise Assa Abloy. On July 12, Assa Abloy announced that it agreed for its wholly-owned subsidiary, Zhejiang Assa Abloy Living Appliances Co., Ltd., to acquire related assets of the small appliance business of Dongguan步步High Household Appliances Co., Ltd. at a price of 228.8 million yuan, seeking to cross-expand from cookware into the small appliance field. Previously, SEB had attempted but failed to acquire Assa Abloy's controlling stake, instead opting to acquire Supor.

2. Small appliance mergers and acquisitions continue

After more than ten years of development, the major home appliance industry has now relatively stabilized. Whether for foreign or domestic brands, finding suitable acquisition targets in the television, air conditioning, and refrigeration sectors is not easy. In contrast, the number of Chinese small appliance enterprises is large, developing rapidly, but their scale is not big, mainly concentrated in various cities along the eastern coast. There will inevitably be a process of survival of the fittest and reintegration, making it more suitable for mergers and acquisitions.

According to our reporter's understanding, there are currently over 60 brands of living appliances in home appliance chain stores. If we include second and third-tier markets as well as some miscellaneous brands, the number of small appliance brands exceeds hundreds. The profit margin of small appliances is generally 10% higher than that of major appliances, with some miscellaneous brands having profit margins as high as 50%. For this reason, the small appliance sector is referred to by insiders as "the last piece of profit cheese in the home appliance industry."

A key reason why foreign companies have become major buyers of domestic home appliance enterprises is that rather than "starting anew" to innovate products and cultivate markets, it is more practical to "turn guest into host" and quickly integrate into the Chinese market through the acquisition of local brands. In fact, SEB's success with Supor has accelerated the pace of acquisitions by other foreign giants. Public data shows that in 2010, Supor's revenue scale was 5.622 billion yuan, achieving a net profit of 405 million yuan. In the first quarter of this year, both revenue and net profit grew by over 30%.

The Italian home appliance giant DeLonghi Group recently boldly claimed that it would acquire a "batch" of domestic home appliance brands in China. It is understood that DeLonghi has already drawn up an acquisition list involving several well-known brands in Shanghai, Zhejiang, and Guangdong, including home appliances, home furnishings, and kitchen appliances. Substantial negotiations have already begun, and if the talks go smoothly, the acquisition will be completed in the second half of this year.

3. Loss of independent brands needs vigilance

However, for domestic small appliance enterprises, although being acquired by strong companies can bring substantial industrial capital and foreign technology, enhancing the enterprise's market scale, it may also result in the gradual loss of independent brands. Experts point out that if domestic home appliance enterprises do not establish their own technological innovation systems and rely solely on introducing capital and selling assets to enhance market sales, it is somewhat short-sighted; in the long term, it is无形的kill for their own brand. "From the perspective of Philips' acquisition of Pentium, the value of eliminating competitors is greater. It can be foreseen that after Philips acquires Pentium, it will not use the 'Pentium' brand," said Lu Renbo, deputy secretary-general of the China Electronic Chamber of Commerce.

For domestic small appliance enterprises, simply focusing on cooperating with foreign companies to introduce capital and hoping for a one-time solution might be a narrow and immature approach. The current primary task should be to rely on the advantages of the global small appliance manufacturing center, quickly building a complete back-end innovation system including technology research and development, product design, forming comprehensive competitiveness in technical standards, product innovation, etc., and comprehensively upgrading the entire industrial chain including raw materials, core components, manufacturing, services. With this system and technical standard, Chinese small appliance enterprises can truly possess the capital to compete with foreign enterprises; otherwise, all other discussions remain mere talk.

Of course, the merger and acquisition of enterprises themselves, the weakening or strengthening of brands, depends on the development strategy after the acquisition. One cannot completely negate foreign acquisitions due to the weakening of brands, nor can one fully agree due to the rosy prospects after the acquisition. Instead, one must adapt to the situation. However, regardless, the "massive collapse" of national brands is always something undesirable, worthy of reflection and vigilance within the industry.

Cautionary Tale

Domestic Daily Chemical Brands Swallowed by Foreign Capital

Currently, the mid-to-high-end market of daily chemical products is entirely dominated by foreign brands, with weak presence of domestic brands. Among the top three foreign daily chemical companies, Procter & Gamble's annual sales in China exceed 20 billion yuan, Unilever and L'Oreal are around 10 billion yuan each; whereas most domestic daily chemical companies have sales scales below 100 million yuan. Companies like Shanghai Jiahua, White Cat, and Two-Faced Needle are rare examples of domestic enterprises capable of competing comprehensively with multinational corporations.

Other domestic brands choose joint ventures with foreign companies or are acquired by them, with the acquired brands subsequently shelved. In this joint venture movement, state-owned daily chemical enterprises have almost none left unscathed, giving up brand resources and market resources. Facing the full-scale encirclement of international daily chemical giants like Procter & Gamble, Johnson & Johnson, and Unilever, domestic daily chemical enterprises are struggling.

In the past decade, Procter & Gamble acquired Langqi and Panda; Unilever acquired Zhonghua and Magic Care; Henkel acquired Haiou; L'Oreal acquired Xiaohushi; Beiersdorf acquired Sibao daily chemical products; Dabao was acquired by Johnson & Johnson. The result of these series of acquisitions is that daily chemical giants represented by Unilever and Procter & Gamble often take the lead in price increases, thereby driving price hikes in other related brands.

The lessons learned from the swallowing of domestic daily chemical brands by foreign capital should serve as a warning to domestic small appliance enterprises.
