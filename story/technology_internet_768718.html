Zhou Hongyi, the "Red Robe Master", fires again: Internet giants such as Tencent and Baidu are caught in the crossfire.

by anonymous on 2013-11-20 11:53:46

On the evening of November 19th, Zhou Hongyi, Chairman of Qihoo 360, delivered a keynote speech titled "Why Can't China Produce a Steve Jobs?" at China University of Political Science and Law. As usual, Zhou criticized various tech companies, this time poking fun at Tencent, blasting Baidu, mocking Rising Antivirus, and reviewing the famous 3Q battle. Here’s what he had to say:

1. **China Cannot Cultivate a Steve Jobs**: "In China, it's impossible to cultivate someone like Steve Jobs. If Steve Jobs were born in China, how old would he have survived? Probably beaten up in elementary school, never entering university, and being seen as an oddity in any job."

2. **Lack of True Innovation in China**: Compared to Silicon Valley, China's internet still lacks true innovation. According to Zhou, the reasons are: Chinese culture views innovation as contradictory to mainstream values. China doesn’t lack talent but lacks tolerance and understanding for innovation. Many people talk about innovation but few dare to take on uncertain and risky ventures. The fear of failure is deeply rooted in society due to its 'success or nothing' culture.

3. **Respect for Netscape**: Zhou mentioned visiting Netscape founder Marc Andreessen during his recent trip to Silicon Valley. Although Netscape failed as a company, it laid the groundwork for future browsers like Firefox, Chrome, Safari, and 360. He admired Andreessen because without Netscape's sacrifice, there wouldn't be the current prosperity of the American internet. Successes of large companies often build on the failures of smaller ones.

4. **Internet Giants Want Stability**: "Many Chinese internet giants hope the existing market structure remains unchanged for 50 years, even maintaining balance among themselves - you don't do search, I won't do instant messaging (IM)." Zhou suggested everyone could relate this statement to their own situations.

5. **Free Antivirus Software Challenges**: When 360 initially launched free antivirus software, they faced both internal and external challenges. A competitor once called him late at night accusing him of ruining their business. Zhou also pointed out that the first PR firm in China, named 1024, was founded by a senior executive from Rising Antivirus. This firm specialized in smearing 360.

6. **The Pain of Free Services**: Referring to the saying "To practice supreme kung fu, one must first castrate oneself," Zhou recalled in 2009 when some board members almost cried begging him not to offer services for free. Instead, he took investors to watch “The Founding of a Republic,” drawing inspiration from Mao Zedong's strategy of prioritizing people over land. He believed that sacrificing income to retain users was crucial for long-term success.

7. **Disruptive Innovation Seen as Troublemaking**: In China, disruptive innovation is often viewed as troublemaking. Regardless of competition, user benefits should always be prioritized – the essence of innovation. Any innovation that doesn't prioritize users is meaningless.

8. **Legal System Should Protect Innovation**: China's legal system should protect innovation rather than big corporations or monopolies. Due to local protectionism, Qihoo lost the 3Q battle, but Zhou questioned the ethics of destroying servers in Antarctica while acknowledging the absurdity of being arrested for watching adult content at home.

9. **Tencent's Copying Skills**: He humorously remarked that no one copies better than Tencent, referring to their ability to mimic competitors' products.

**Keynote Speech Highlights**:

Zhou thanked China University of Political Science and Law for the opportunity to share insights. He emphasized the need for cultural change to foster innovation. He argued that China cannot produce a Steve Jobs because its culture suppresses non-conformist personalities. Despite having talented youth, China's environment stifles creativity. Even with abundant venture capital, innovation requires cultural tolerance for failure. Large companies often wait for small startups to experiment before copying successful ideas. This leads to a jungle-like culture where only the wealthy survive.

He recounted meeting Marc Andreessen, founder of Netscape, who despite failing commercially, left a legacy of innovation that spurred further developments in browsers. He highlighted that although Netscape failed, its contributions were significant in shaping modern internet technologies.

Discussing monopolistic practices, he noted how major Chinese internet companies prefer stability over innovation, fearing failure more than embracing risk. Finally, he stressed that real innovation often involves self-sacrifice and courage to challenge established norms, citing 360's free antivirus service as an example of disruptive innovation that initially faced skepticism but ultimately succeeded through perseverance and belief in its mission.
