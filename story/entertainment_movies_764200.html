Synopsis:

Lord Hidetora Ichimonji is a fierce general from the Warring States period. At the age of 70, he decides to divide his estate among his three sons: Taro, Jiro, and Saburo. Using the metaphor that "a single arrow can be broken easily, but three arrows together cannot be broken," Hidetora advises his sons and plans to give the first castle to Taro, the second to Jiro, and the third to Saburo. However, Saburo breaks the three arrows over his knee in front of everyone, condemning his father for not understanding the unreliability of human nature after surviving many turbulent times. Enraged, Lord Hidetora banishes Saburo, who then goes to a neighboring country and marries into another family. Just as Saburo had predicted, chaos erupts between father and brothers. Hidetora goes mad under the pressure, while Saburo, despite wanting to end the tragedy and bring his father back, ultimately dies in the ensuing war.

Behind the Scenes:

The film draws its inspiration from the story of Mori Motonari, a renowned general from Japan's Warring States period, and incorporates elements from Shakespeare's "King Lear" to create the tale of the Ichimonji clan. It attempts to view the human condition from a heavenly perspective ("karma" in Buddhist terms), revealing the truths of life and reflecting the creator's fundamental views on existence and the world. This movie is one of the largest productions in the history of Japanese cinema, costing 2.4 billion yen and taking over 200 working days to complete. Akira Kurosawa considered it his magnum opus. He was determined to create a "total film," and "Ran" embodies this concept. This means that even scenes that could be explained through dialogue are instead shown visually, including the massive battle sequences which were filmed at great expense to ensure the audience could witness every change. As a result, the film's scale is truly grandiose. Kurosawa first adapted a Shakespearean classic with Japanese historical characters in 1957 with "Throne of Blood," earning international acclaim for promoting cultural exchange between East and West. "Ran" is another example of Kurosawa's Eastern Shakespearean films.
