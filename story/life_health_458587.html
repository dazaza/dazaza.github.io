Social Factors: Their Indisputable Role in Tumor Occurrence

For a long time, the relationship between social factors and the occurrence of cancer has been given significant attention by some scholars. Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) also discussed this topic early on. Research now shows that race, lifestyle, customs, religion, culture, economic status, and occupation all have certain associations with the occurrence of cancer. For instance, cervical cancer tends to occur more frequently in populations with lower social and economic status. Japan's high incidence of stomach cancer is partly related to the nation's tendency towards introspection and self-repression. The residents at the southern end of the Himalayas are prone to abdominal skin cancer due to their habit of using a roasting stove on their abdomen. It was determined long ago that chimney sweeps are prone to scrotal cancer; recently, it was found that those who are closely exposed to cooking oil fumes are prone to lung cancer, as well as those who are long-term exposed to dusty environments. Even within the same type of cancer, such as gastric cancer, the proportion of lymphoma in Ningbo people's gastric cancer is significantly higher. Women who engage in sexual activity at an early age or have a history of promiscuity are prone to cervical cancer. These examples indicate that certain social factors can influence the occurrence of cancer. However, research in this area currently remains at the phenomenological level because it involves very complex social factors. Additionally, there are methodological issues that need improvement, such as the sample size in epidemiological studies, the intensity of relevant factors, the sequence and duration of action, the relationship between dosage and effect, the specificity of social factors, and the reproducibility of research results. Despite these challenges, the role of social factors in tumor occurrence is an indisputable fact. The question lies in how people should delve deeper into this issue and reveal its layers.

In fact, the concept that psychological factors can cause disease is not a new discovery. As early as two thousand years ago, Traditional Chinese Medicine had rich records of this. In the book "Huangdi Neijing," it was pointed out that changes in emotions can cause illness. "Emotions" refer to the "seven emotions" and "five wills." Emotions are not isolated but have a material basis. Ancient people believed that humans have five organs transforming into five qi, manifesting as joy, anger, sorrow, worry, and fear, known as the five wills. The seven emotions are joy, anger, worry, thought, sorrow, fear, and shock. Changes in emotions are closely related to internal organs, with specific organs being influenced by specific emotions: the heart governs joy, the liver governs anger, the spleen governs thought, the lungs govern sorrow, and the kidneys govern fear. As the saying goes: "Extreme anger harms the liver and causes fire to rise, lung diseases are most afraid of adding sorrow, excessive contemplation injures the spleen and stomach, fear harms the kidneys and causes involuntary urination, excessive joy disrupts the heart and makes one lose focus, severe pain can be fatal."

Actually, the various aforementioned factors affect individual emotions over time, leading to carcinogenesis. Extensive research has been conducted on the specific mechanisms of carcinogenesis. For example, in the past few decades, a new field of study called Psychoneuroimmunology (PNI) has developed rapidly. It consists of three parts: psychology, neurology, and immunology. This field studies the connection between human emotional states, the central nervous system, and the immune system. Although PNI does not directly mention the endocrine system, its research often involves endocrine hormones. Therefore, some scientists believe that a more precise term for this new field of study should be "Neuropsychiatric Endocrine Immunology." The basic theory of PNI is that the brain can regulate immune function, and the functions of the immune system can change due to neurological and physiological stress. When mental emotions experience persistent abnormal fluctuations, they stimulate the nervous system, prompting it to continuously release multiple substances such as neurotransmitters, neurohormones, and neuropeptides. These mediators can directly interact with receptors on lymphocytes, changing or affecting immune cells, thereby influencing the formation and development of tumors.
