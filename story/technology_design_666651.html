A Different Perspective on Telecom Executive Rotation: The Market Was Not Built by God in Six Days

by yahoohk3168 on 2012-02-16 04:02:13

Not long ago, the rotation of the three major telecom giants caused a sensation. The focus of people's attention is not only because it is a personnel transfer measure, but more importantly, it reflects the trend of China Telecom moving from a monopoly to a market-oriented mechanism reform. After 10 years of telecom reform, there have been difficulties and progress: continuous restructuring, constant competition between the market and the government, paradoxical emergencies, and increasingly deteriorating market competition, etc. Now, with the rotation of the three giants, what is the substantive significance behind it? Can this bring a bright future to the bumpy road of China Telecom?

If one morning you woke up and found that IBM CEO Sam Palmisano and HP CEO Carly Fiorina had swapped positions, how astonishing would that be! But many times, real events are even more surprising than we can imagine! The news of "the top management of China's three major operators swapping positions" first appeared in the Hong Kong Wen Hui Bao on October 29. Subsequently, IT Times Weekly confirmed the news with China Mobile Group. A responsible person from the General Office of China Mobile Group told our reporter that China Mobile also received the appointment decision on the morning of November 1. Zhang Ligui, Chairman and General Manager of China Mobile, retired, and his position was taken over by Wang Jianzhou, Chairman and General Manager of China Unicom. While Wang Xiaochu, Vice General Manager of China Mobile Group, was transferred to China Telecom.

Our reporter learned from informed sources that, in fact, the news of high-level rotation had already spread among the senior management of various telecom groups half a month earlier. At that time, SASAC consulted the three major operators about the adjustment plan. This adjustment should be the result of adjustments made under the agreement of the three major operators.

On the morning of November 1, our reporter learned from China Unicom that although the final appointment notice had not yet arrived, they had already heard related news. Currently, within Unicom, there is a wave of nostalgia for Wang Jianzhou. A responsible person from the Comprehensive Department of Unicom, who wished to remain anonymous, said that the appointment notice will reach the Unicom Group as soon as this week (detoxification still requires abandoning the IPO approval system).

That afternoon, the high-level rotations of China Telecom, China Mobile, and China Unicom were swiftly completed. The three major operators successively held cadre meetings at their respective headquarters. Wang Jianzhou, Chang Xiaobing, and Wang Xiaochu respectively met with the middle-level cadres of the above three operators, thus starting their new roles.

From the disclosure of Credit Suisse First Boston's report to the formal implementation, it took only 3 days, though it was rumored in the industry for two or three months. "This is a planned personnel arrangement." An analyst from Shenwan Hongyuan Securities said. "Such a large-scale personnel change is extremely rare in China's telecommunications industry," another seasoned insider commented.

Does the Rotation Eliminate Malicious Competition?

In our history, personnel issues have always been the most important, subtle, and politically wise. It is inevitable that outsiders speculate on the intentions behind the rotation of telecom executives.

An industry insider frankly told IT Times Weekly: "The reasons are obvious; the increasingly fierce malicious competition in the telecommunications sector at the local level has prompted the decision-making level to take this step." This viewpoint is quite representative.

Currently, the four major problems troubling China's telecommunications industry - interconnection, tariffs, Little Smart phones, and universal service - the first three can all be categorized under "malicious competition." For example, data shows that in the five years since 1998, there were 540 cases of malicious interconnection reported to the Ministry of Information Industry, affecting at least 100 million users and causing approximately 1 billion yuan in direct losses and 2 billion yuan in indirect losses.

However, although SASAC may not have the rich industry management experience of the Ministry of Information Industry, as long as it understands the recent history of the telecommunications industry, there is reason to doubt the significance of such actions. For instance, 2003 was defined as the "Year of Interconnection," during which the Ministry of Information Industry made tremendous efforts. Its work deployment could be summarized as "one measure, two projects": one measure being judicial interpretation, and two projects being cost reassessment, updating the inter-network settlement mechanism, and establishing a monitoring system. If these measures had been highly effective, there wouldn't be such speculation about the ulterior motives of SASAC's move today.

Actually, defining the economically neutral term "competition" with a moral evaluation of "malicious" may itself explain why "malicious competition" remains unresolved.

So-called "malicious competition" requires specific analysis. Part of it is normal market competition, theoretically positioned as a company's "natural right," practically speaking, mobile communications is also a relatively marketized细分 market in China's telecommunications industry. However, "mobile phone (including analog, GSM, CDMA) tariff standards are subject to government pricing and guidance prices. Companies cannot arbitrarily increase or decrease prices, nor can they casually change charging methods."

Most of it is due to monopolies and inappropriate regulation distorting corporate behavior. Taking IP phone interconnection as an example, the U.S. telecommunications reform in 1982 broke the monopoly of long-distance calls and "the last mile" of city calls to promote competition in the long-distance market. However, although China's telecommunications reform opened up IP telephony, it could be said that it never thoroughly resolved the issue. In Jean-Jacques Laffont's "Telecommunications Competition," it points out that promoting interconnection should encourage access without harming the dominant operator. However, China's telecommunications industry has long implemented "asymmetric regulation," which is akin to forcing access under conditions that harm the dominant operator.

The key is, whether the market plays a role or not, it cannot be changed by replacing a few individuals.

If China's telecommunications reform is calculated from the establishment of China Unicom in 1994, then the "split into four" of China Telecom in 1999, and the north-south split of fixed-line operators in 2001, after ten years, today's pattern was established. Reform has always accompanied by a strong public outcry against monopolies. Now, what was once called overseas as the "Ministry of Posts and Telecommunications - China Telecom" grand unified monopoly no longer exists. No single operator can occupy more than 50% of the total telecommunications revenue, but there is still a considerable distance from a true market. Reality is based on "segmented monopolies" formed by business, region, regulation, and other forms.

Each operator is a beneficiary of some form of monopoly while simultaneously being a victim of another form of monopoly, so they both love and hate reality. As rational economic actors, each tries to use their monopoly resources to hit others as much as possible, but at the same time angrily points fingers at peers who similarly damage their own interests. This is the true "malice" present. Therefore, there is no need to switch positions to "put oneself in another's shoes."

Most backbone members of the industry come from a few domestic postal and telecommunications colleges. Five years ago, they were all colleagues, and the lack of communication depends on your understanding. In fact, among the 700,000 employees of China's telecommunications industry, from the top management down to the lowest rank, aren't they consumers when they go home, with their own "people's livelihoods," needing to accept services from other monopolistic industries? If "malicious competition" harms consumer interests, they cannot possibly be unaware. One might say that the chronic ailments of the system distort not only corporate behavior but even human nature.

To perform one's duties in office. Please look at the professional ethics and planning styles of the three giants during their terms: In March 2000, Chang Xiaobing, then Director of the Telecommunications Administration Bureau of the Ministry of Information Industry —— who just assumed the position of Chairman of China Unicom —— denied rumors to the media that the Ministry of Information Industry agreed to China Telecom's large-scale launch of PHS (Little Smart) format. He represented the regulatory agency pointing out that PHS (Little Smart) is not the development direction of mobile communications. However, just two months later, Chang Xiaobing was transferred to China Telecom as Deputy General Manager. During his tenure, China Telecom's Little Smart business made significant progress, and given his position, this achievement is undoubtedly inseparable from his support.

Wang Xiaochu began building a transparent and effective management system externally since entering China Mobile's board in March 1999. By segmenting customer groups and adopting different market strategies, he ensured the profit growth of China Mobile while winning praise from overseas investors, making great contributions to China Mobile.

Also in 1999, in the 15th year of Wang Jianzhou's career in telecommunications, he replaced his title as Director of the Integrated Planning Department of the Ministry of Information Industry and joined the 5-year-old China Unicom. His efforts made the second five years of China Unicom colorful. Compared to Wang Xiaochu's low-profile approach and Wang Jianzhou's mild character, his excellent English debate skills have been legendary in the industry for a decade.

Before the rotation, in their understanding of "market competition," Chang Xiaobing for China Telecom, Wang Xiaochu for China Mobile, and Wang Jianzhou for China Unicom each had different insights but shared the same vision: how to make their respective companies stronger in competition.

Therefore, whether as managers of world-class operators or officials equivalent to deputy ministerial level, the rotated executives must and will fulfill their duties in their new positions. If due to moral reflection like "Do not do unto others what you do not want done to yourself," they become soft-hearted in fierce competition, it would be a crime against investors and the country. Moreover, since there are no secrets between them and their competitors, they can target precisely and hit vital points. Thus, executive rotations actually strengthen "malicious competition."

Two thousand years ago, Han Feizi pointed out incisively, "Thus, cart-makers hope people become wealthy, coffin-makers hope people die young—not because cart-makers are benevolent and coffin-makers wicked. People won't buy carts if they're not wealthy, won't buy coffins if they don't die—young. It's not hatred toward people, but benefit in people's death." The view that job rotation can suppress "malicious competition" is untenable in commerce, politics, or human nature.

Reorganization Dives Deep, Personnel Moves First?

A simpler view holds that this move prepares talent for 3G or is merely a normal generational replacement, nothing remarkable. There was previously an evaluation stating that Wang Jianzhou acts steadily, Chang Xiaobing excels in fixed-line services, and Wang Xiaochu specializes in mobile services. The aforementioned personnel changes aim for the government to utilize the strengths of these three individuals to compensate for the existing shortcomings of the three operators. Wang Jianzhou's entry into China Mobile aims to coordinate quality and quantity development. Chang Xiaobing is expected to revitalize China Unicom's currently stagnant fixed-line business, and Wang Xiaochu's leadership of China Telecom is generally seen as preparation for operating 3G.

However, the claim of revitalizing China Unicom's fixed-line business is somewhat questionable. Unicom possesses the only full-service license but has always focused on mobile services, selectively entering the IP telephone market and ultimately missing the opportunity to merge with Tietong. To date, fixed-line services have become marginal, even for fixed-line operators seeking breakthroughs.

These two perspectives ultimately fail to fill the imagination gap. After several years of splitting, the management systems of the major operators have all developed independently. The impact of 3G and the retirement of two presidents can be fully anticipated. For stability considerations, it is not impossible to handle matters independently and resolve them internally. However, the final plan issued is a comprehensive package of job rotations.

The most impactful conjecture suggests laying the groundwork for another round of large-scale reorganizations in the telecommunications industry. On May 25, 2004, Hong Kong's Shangbao reported that SASAC submitted a "four-to-two" merger plan at the end of 2003, involving a "strong-weak combination" of one mobile operator and one fixed-line operator, meaning China Mobile merging with Netcom and China Telecom merging with Unicom. The report stated that "the main purpose of this merger is also to simplify the complex structure of mainland telecommunications enterprises," thereby solving issues such as third-generation mobile phone licensing, including reducing the number of 3G licenses and benefiting multiple telecom operators. Later, there were reports that this plan was shelved. Now, there is reason to believe it is still in motion, undergoing careful consideration due to its significance, with personnel moves serving as a prelude.

Some views counter that if this were true, the flow of personnel should not be as described now but rather involve rotations between the companies slated for mergers, with only Chang Xiaobing's transfer fitting this need. This may hold commercially. However, historically, in corporate mergers, business alignment is secondary; the most difficult and subtle aspect is personnel, adjusting the existing interest patterns. Moving key personnel away from areas slated for integration will inevitably reduce future resistance.

Moreover, preparing for reorganization does not contradict 3G selection or generational replacement. What is termed as "normal warfare" complements "unusual warfare." Using such reasonable purposes to break the ice for integration reduces shocks and achieves unexpected results when actual integration begins.

Reorganization can also explain the view of Chang Xiaobing's mission to "revitalize China Unicom's fixed-line business," where Unicom will gain through "non-market" means what it cannot obtain in the market.

With reorganization plans already in place, the "malicious competition theory" can be overturned. Since SASAC has reached consensus internally, adopting such radical measures is necessary to solve today's problems in China's telecommunications industry. Although the plan may be shelved due to execution difficulty, there will be no illusion of scratching the surface through personnel adjustments.

Administrative transfers: Who pays for the market?

Regardless of any original intention, the current results clearly violate market principles.

In a well-functioning company, any formal employee joining must sign a non-compete agreement in their labor contract, explicitly committing that once leaving, they will not serve a competitor for a certain period. Moreover, any manager, if led to expect their next position to be at a competitor, would find it hard to maintain a proper work attitude now.

Investors bet on a company often by betting on the team. That's why managerial compensation includes stock options, and the three operators have similar arrangements (this personnel adjustment occurs in the group company, indirectly impacting listed companies). Stock options represent future gains, incentivizing managers for long-term efforts. But if there's a possibility of sudden administrative transfers, what then is the significance of stock options?

No wonder international investors voice dissatisfaction. A portfolio manager at Merrill Lynch expressed that such large-scale personnel adjustments might prompt investors to first sell shares of these three telecom companies, as they are uncertain about the impacts of such adjustments.

At the end of 1999, the Ministry of Information Industry's flip-flopping on implementing one-way mobile phone charges caused severe fluctuations in the stocks of China Mobile and China Unicom, with fluctuations exceeding HKD 140 billion. Post-event analyses suggested, "The true cause of market panic wasn't simply due to reduced investor returns from one-way charges; the chaos in China's decision-making mechanisms and the lack of transparency that instilled fear in investors was the root cause." However, notably, after this personnel reshuffle, the stock prices of the three operators did not experience drastic fluctuations.

During the midway point of China's telecommunications marketization, this major personnel adjustment carries significance that cannot be viewed in isolation. If the expectation was to quell "malicious competition," disappointment is likely. And if it was for 3G selections or generational replacements, it demonstrates tactical positivity but damages already fragile market rules. If this is just a small beginning leading to a new round of bold reorganizations in the telecommunications industry, the prospects involve various aspects, difficult to estimate, but undoubtedly carry significant positives and negatives.
