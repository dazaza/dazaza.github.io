The success of cotton yarn enterprises comes from competition - Fuxin Textiles

by miansha47 on 2009-11-19 23:26:40

In a commercial society, ignorant people sell themselves out. Simply put, they sell themselves. Some sell their physical strength, some sell their intellectual capacity, and others sell their youth. The result of selling oneself is that one will never fetch a good price. When you haggle, others will short-change you. Encountering those who bargain even after a deal is not legend but reality. To survive in a commercial society, one must learn to market oneself rather than just sell oneself. Marketing requires the refinement of selling points, building and promoting brands, choosing markets, and focusing on customers.

"This is an age where cotton yarn marketing succeeds, and also where it fails; this is an era of limited resources, and also one of constant competition; this is a time for managing enterprises, and also navigating careers; this is the season to sell oneself, and also to market oneself; this is the spring of hope for happiness, and also the winter of regret; not doing marketing can be a kind of happiness, and not knowing how to market can be a kind of regret; people are ascending to the heaven of self-marketing, and descending into the hell of self-sales."

In a commercial society, everyone is a unique product with unique value. As products need marketing, all values must be recognized through marketing. Regardless of your profession—cotton yarn or otherwise—marketing is a skill that must be mastered, and a skill that requires training to acquire. Not doing marketing may be a kind of happiness, but not knowing how to market is certainly the biggest regret in life.

The success of all cotton yarn companies begins with successful marketing. Successful marketing means selling one's product or service at a reasonable price and efficiently, in large quantities, while continuously satisfying customers. All enterprises grow and develop through such marketing. Unfortunately, in the field of enterprise management, traditional imperial thinking still dominates. Behind successful marketing, there are often smokeless killings: scenes of 'burning bridges', 'hiding bows when birds are gone', 'cooking dogs when rabbits are dead', 'disarming generals with wine', 'complaining about chefs after being full', 'beating monks after scriptures are read' repeatedly occur, causing lamentation over the success of marketing.

All failures of enterprises ultimately boil down to marketing failure. Enterprise failure has ten thousand sorrowful reasons, but they all concentrate on one point: it’s all marketing's fault! An enterprise's strategy has no right or wrong—it goes wrong when marketing doesn't keep up; an enterprise's tactics have no good or bad—they go bad when our marketers aren't competent. Our quality may not be good, but those with worse quality sell more than us; our price may be a bit high, but those with higher prices sell even more. As long as marketing can still sell goods, the enterprise has not failed. Thus, the banners of 'desperately seeking talent' and 'respectfully recruiting scholars' are raised high, grand plays like 'three visits to the thatched cottage' or even 'angrily smashing A Dou' are staged, but the real truth behind all this is a major overhaul of marketing!

Success comes from marketing, and so does failure. Almost all the rise and fall histories of cotton yarn companies are filled with the blood and tears of marketing. Perhaps not doing marketing really is a kind of happiness. Is marketing really so terrifying? In fact, what's truly frightening are the two things hidden behind marketing: one called 'limited resources', and the other called 'constant competition'. Marketing walks the fine line between success and failure precisely because of limited resources and constant competition.

Our society's material resources are quite abundant. Are the clothes you wear hand-sewn by your mother? 'A loving mother's thread, a wandering child's garment' has long become a beautiful memory. Your clothes come from marketing—you pay labor for currency, and then use currency to exchange for clothes. The shoes you wear are definitely not made by your mother, but also come from marketing. Your glasses are not homemade, the books you read are not self-published, the rice you eat is not homegrown, and the Wuliangye liquor you drink is not self-brewed—all these come from marketing. From the moment you were born, you have not escaped the encirclement of marketing, because you live in a commercial society where resources are limited. It's impossible for everyone to have enough resources to be self-sufficient. As long as resources are limited, marketing is everywhere. In a commercial society, not doing marketing may be a kind of happiness, but not knowing how to market is certainly the greatest regret in life.

Why do you continue to stay when you're not satisfied with the salary of this company? Because the next company pays even lower wages. Why do you persist despite the immense pressure of this position? Because there are 10 people waiting below for this position! Why does the telecom company offer free phones with prepaid airtime? Because mobile services have been doing this for a long time. Why does KFC give out discount coupons after payment? Because McDonald's is right across the street. Our leader once said well: China may be rich, but divided among a billion people, it becomes a small amount; even the smallest thing done by a billion people becomes significant. Survival of the fittest and natural selection are laws of nature. As long as competition persists, marketing will never stop. In a commercial society, not doing marketing may be a kind of happiness, but not knowing how to market is certainly the greatest regret in life.

Do you want to be a leader? This is actually a meaningless question. Those who don’t want to be leaders and prefer being led are surely not Chinese. Practice has proven: a good CEO has, to varying degrees and for varying lengths of time, engaged in marketing-related work in their past careers. If you really want to possess the ability to lead an enterprise or a team, you should know what they learned from marketing. Despite countless enterprises having experienced various marketing failures, those are merely marketing failures. I've never lost at Go, not because I only play against weaker opponents, but because I fundamentally don't know how to play Go. Running an enterprise, not doing marketing may be a kind of happiness, but not knowing how to market is certainly the CEO's greatest regret.

Every employee in a company knows that every department is indispensable, but the marketing department always occupies the central position. When the boss holds meetings on human resources and finance, he always wears a stern face, as if you owe him money. But when holding marketing meetings, he becomes passionate and talkative. You must understand the subtleties here. Dealing with people from the marketing department is the hardest. These guys are always arrogant, but they always manage to hit the nail on the head. Therefore, other departments almost always lose when they sue them. Navigating the workplace, not doing marketing may be a kind of happiness, but not knowing how to market is certainly the greatest regret in the workplace.

The workplace is a market. Before you can manage others, you must first learn to market yourself. A career needs to be managed, starting with yourself and ending with yourself. Between the start and end points lies your own marketing. Everyone living in a commercial society needs to exchange with the environment. No one wants to rely on begging for harvest, and the law prohibits obtaining returns through theft and robbery. The only legal ways are two: sales and marketing.
