The issue of static electricity in the printing process The generation, harm, and elimination measures of static electricity in printing Static electricity has a significant impact on printing. Eliminating the static electricity generated during printing is one of the main issues facing the global printing industry today. In the printing production process, phenomena such as flying paper that cannot be neatly stacked after printing are all caused by static electricity.

Reasons for the generation of static electricity in printing The generation of static electricity in printing is mainly influenced by four factors: technology, equipment, raw materials, and environment.

1. Technological factors

① Squeezing and frictional electrification. Paper is strongly squeezed by the printing plate and cylinder. At the same time, the paper generates intense friction with the cylinder, printing plate, and ink. This friction causes the surface temperature of the above components to rise, easily causing molecular thermal decomposition, thereby leading to the re-allocation of electrons and generating static electricity.

② Contact and stripping electrification. In printing, the rapid contact and separation of paper with line belts, guide rollers, and ink, especially when the paper is bitten by the paper clamp, quickly wraps around the surface of the cylinder. After printing, the paper also quickly peels off from the surface of the cylinder. The surface of the cylinder is wrapped with rubber cloth, cotton cloth, kraft paper, etc., so it will cause static electricity to be generated on the printed paper and the cylinder wrapping.

③ The influence of machine speed on static electricity generation. When the speed of the printing press increases, the friction speed between the paper and the printing plate and ink, as well as the contact and separation speed between the paper and the cylinder, also increase, making the movement of electrons more intense and increasing the energy of charge migration, eventually leading to an increase in static electricity.

2. Equipment and raw material factors Printing plates, cylinders, and other materials or parts of the printing press, being mostly metallic objects, have outer-layer electrons that can easily escape the attraction of the atomic nucleus and become free electrons, thus allowing metal objects to rapidly conduct charges. In printing, the frictional electrification of paper, padding, and line belts is mainly a surface effect, and the surface condition has a significant impact on static electricity generation. If the surface is smooth, less static electricity is generated. Conversely, if the surface is rough or oxidized and corroded or contaminated with dirt, more static electricity will be generated. Additionally, due to the different structural components of the paper, its hygroscopicity also varies, and some papers generate static electricity during production, which greatly affects the generation of static electricity in printing.

3. Environmental factors The air temperature and humidity in the printing area significantly affect the generation of static electricity in printing. During mutual squeezing and friction between printing components and materials, molecular thermal decomposition and thermoelectric effects occur, increasing charged particles or polarizing them, thereby causing the components and materials to become charged. When the air temperature in the printing area rises, these effects become more intense, greatly increasing the accumulation of static electricity.

In summer, due to high relative humidity in the air, less static electricity is generated. However, in spring, autumn, and winter, when the relative humidity of the air is lower, static electricity generated during printing cannot be quickly released, accumulating a large amount of static electricity. Temperature and humidity are interrelated; higher temperatures make the air drier, reducing relative humidity. Humidity has a much greater impact on static electricity than temperature.

Harm of static electricity to printing

1. Harm of static electricity to print quality Due to the influence of static electricity, the position of the paper on the feed belt is skewed, making it difficult to drop at the delivery stack, where the paper sticks together due to mutual attraction, preventing the aligner from functioning properly, affecting the subsequent folding work. Due to the sticking of the paper, double sheets often occur. The paper does not move easily on the feed board, causing register errors, ghosting, and mottling. Additionally, paper with static electricity attracts many impurities and dust, making the print unclear.

During printing, the rapid transfer and leveling of ink by the ink rollers, ink fountain, and printing plate, along with high-speed contact-separation and friction, causes the ink to generate static electricity, resulting in ink mist. Some ink mist directly flies onto the print, dirtying it, while others transfer to the printing plate or blanket and then onto the print, called transferred dirt, forming relatively fixed light-colored spots until the ink mist on the printing plate or blanket is removed. This not only affects print quality but also contaminates the equipment.

2. Harm of printing static electricity to human body Printing workers are prone to carry static electricity during operations. In dry spring and autumn seasons, people generally wear more clothing, often made of synthetic fibers. Friction between the body, clothes, and shoes with the floor generates static electricity. On the other hand, normal people wearing canvas or leather shoes have resistance values over 10gΩ, and plastic or rubber-soled shoes have even higher resistance. Therefore, it is difficult for static electricity on the body and clothes to be discharged (static electricity discharge requires shoe resistance below 10gΩ), leading to static electricity accumulation and making the body a charged object.

Static induction can cause uncharged conductors to become charged, turning them into charged conductors. The human body is equivalent to a conductor and can also become charged through induction in a static electric field. During operation, printing workers frequently come into contact with charged paper and stacks, becoming charged through induction in the electrostatic field of the paper and stacks. Since the human body is well insulated from the ground, it becomes an independent charged object.

After accumulating static electricity, when the fingers touch the shell or metal parts of the printing press during operation, since the machine shell is grounded, the static electricity from the human body discharges through the fingers to the contact point of the shell, grounding the static electricity and causing the person to experience an electric shock.

When the human body does not accumulate static electricity but wears shoes with low insulation resistance standing on the ground, touching paper or receiving stacks with high voltage can still result in an electric shock because the body forms a good ground connection, providing a path for the release of static electricity.

Electric shocks from printing static electricity are instantaneous impact shocks caused by static discharge, with discharge currents in the microampere range. Although the static voltage may reach several kilovolts or tens of kilovolts, it does not pose a fatal threat to humans. However, sudden electric shocks can cause needle-like and numb sensations, making people tense and affecting the operation of printing workers, potentially leading to product or personal accidents in severe cases.

3. Fires and explosions caused by static electricity In high-speed web printing, the generated static voltage is very high, easily causing spark discharges. When the discharge spark energy reaches the minimum ignition energy of gasoline or alcohol, there is a risk of fire and explosion.

Methods to prevent static electricity generation To avoid the harm of static electricity and ensure the normal operation of printing, it is necessary to eliminate the static electricity already present on paper, padding, and line belts, but more importantly, prevention is key, i.e., avoiding or minimizing the generation of static electricity.

1. Humidification treatment of paper Paper generates static electricity during papermaking, so it should be aired when transported to the printing factory to reduce the amount of static electricity. Currently, methods for airing paper include:

① Natural hanging method. Hanging paper in the printing workshop or a room with similar temperature and humidity is called the natural hanging method. The hanging time is usually more than two days, and the longer the hanging time, the better the humidification effect, and the smaller the paper shrinkage during printing.

② Mechanical hanging method. Using a dedicated paper-hanging machine is called the mechanical hanging method. Common types include round and bridge-type paper-hanging machines.

③ For web paper, only the static placement method can be used, requiring months of humidification. Hanging single sheets not only adjusts the temperature and humidity of the paper but also makes the water content uniform, keeping the paper flat and preventing wrinkles during printing due to uneven water content. Additionally, hanging removes surface dust and foreign objects. Due to differences in paper composition and manufacturing processes, the amount of impurities and surface smoothness vary. During printing, choose paper with fewer impurities and a smoother surface to reduce static electricity generated by friction and stripping. Furthermore, the raw material composition affects the hygroscopicity of the paper. From the perspective of reducing static electricity, choose highly hygroscopic paper to quickly lower surface resistance and accelerate the release of static electricity.

2. Influence of printing pressure and machine speed on static electricity The size of the printing pressure significantly affects product quality, plate durability, and even machine maintenance. Too little pressure results in pale and lifeless prints without gloss, while too much pressure causes dot gain, loss of detail, disrupts the normal operation of the cylinders, and accelerates bearing wear. Therefore, scientific control of pressure is necessary. Printing involves transferring ink to paper under cylinder pressure. Adequate pressure ensures solid ink transfer and sharp lines, but it also generates substantial static electricity. Reducing printing pressure can reduce paper charge migration and decrease static electricity, but it may also affect print quality. Thus, under normal printing conditions, printing pressure should be minimized as much as possible.

High machine speeds exacerbate static electricity accumulation. Lowering machine speed reduces contact-separation and friction between paper and components, weakening charge migration and reducing static electricity generation. However, lowering machine speed also increases the printing cycle, so consider adding shifts or increasing the number of printing presses.

3. Control of temperature and humidity in the printing workshop Temperature and humidity have a significant impact on printing. High workshop temperatures increase the fluidity of ink due to increased temperature and mechanical force, weakening molecular attraction. Water usage also increases, accelerating emulsification and causing easy water contamination. Low temperatures increase ink viscosity, making ink application difficult, requiring appropriate additives to lower viscosity and ensure printing. High humidity causes paper to absorb water and deform, making water control difficult and increasing water usage. Therefore, controlling workshop temperature and humidity is crucial for printing. During printing, reasonable control of workshop temperature and humidity, with temperature changes not exceeding 3°C, improves registration accuracy. Temperature and humidity control in the production area can be achieved with air conditioning, but for non-air-conditioned workshops, measures should be taken to mitigate the impact of temperature and humidity changes on the printing process.

Measure one: Minimize the impact of external weather changes on workshop temperature and humidity. This does not mean achieving consistent conditions year-round but rather controlling changes within a certain time frame. Specifically, for the same batch of prints from loading onto the machine to completion, workshop temperature and humidity should not change significantly. Reduce direct exchange of indoor and outdoor air, typically by closing workshop windows. Building structures should scatter summer sunlight to minimize its direct impact on workshop temperature and humidity. Maintain good insulation in winter to prevent cold air from affecting workshop temperature. Workshop doors should not face outward directly but should have turns and passages, with doorways covered by fabric curtains.

Measure two: In case of weather changes, cover semi-finished products awaiting further printing with plastic covers.

Dehumidification strategies for household appliances during damp weather

During this period, damp and gloomy weather makes citizens feel uncomfortable. Such weather not only affects health but also poses risks to home appliances. Recently, a friend complained that their computer monitor would automatically go black after running for three minutes, later found to be due to dampness affecting the cathode ray tube. According to Mr. Yao Wenzhan, president of the Guangzhou Electrical Appliances Repair Industry Association, household appliances are prone to dampness during humid weather, whether it’s color TVs, refrigerators, washing machines, or VCD players. Mr. Yao particularly advises that digital cameras and digital camcorders should not be stored long-term in leather cases but rather in dry boxes with silica gel for moisture protection. So, how do we protect larger appliances like TVs and air conditioners? Here's a detailed introduction:

1. TV moisture protection

First, attention should be paid to maintaining the TV in a well-ventilated position.

Second, during humid weather, the TV can be placed in standby mode, as the transformer and other operating components in standby mode generate heat, helping to drive out moisture inside the set.

Additionally, many small holes on the back of the TV allow dust to enter the internal components. During humid weather, condensation combines with dust, causing "creeping leakage" in the TV. Users can use a hairdryer to blow through these small holes on the back of the TV, moving the blower up, down, left, and right to expel dust and moisture.

Finally, TVs that have been used for a certain number of years should undergo professional maintenance and internal dust cleaning.

2. Washing machine moisture protection

Many people believe washing machines don’t need moisture protection since they deal with water. This is incorrect, especially for washing machines controlled by circuits. Ideally, washing machines should not be placed in bathrooms or areas with high humidity. They can be placed on elevated platforms. Electronic control components should be kept dry, and when faults occur, stop using immediately and seek professional repair.

3. Air conditioner moisture protection

Although air conditioners have dehumidifying functions, they also need maintenance during humid weather. Due to the noticeable temperature difference between indoors and outdoors, condensation forms inside the air conditioner pipes, mixing with dust and creating bacteria harmful to health. Long-term non-use also leads to component aging and hardening of plastic materials. After not being used for three to four months during winter, the air conditioner should be turned on periodically to ensure it remains in good condition.

4. Computer moisture protection

Computers require special attention to moisture protection, as excessive humidity can cause short circuits in internal circuits, leading to machine malfunctions. Moreover, computers without proper grounding during humid weather can easily become electrically charged.

To prevent this, computers should be turned on for at least one hour daily to drive out moisture with the heat generated during operation. In overly humid weather, two packets of desiccant can be placed inside the computer case, and a dust and moisture-proof cloth can be used to cover the case and monitor.

Dehumidifier/extractor usage knowledge

◆ Dehumidifiers should avoid being placed near heat sources. Ensure unobstructed airflow in and out of the vents. Generally, placing the dehumidifier centrally in the space is most suitable, with enough space around it, avoiding stacking items. Maintaining smooth airflow achieves uniform dehumidification.

◆ Refrigeration dehumidifiers may frost or ice in low-temperature environments (below 18°C). Dehumidifiers with defrost functions will defrost automatically, while those without may require manual defrosting (by disconnecting power). Typically, lower temperatures mean lower humidity, and dehumidification may not be obvious with humidity below 40%, and the speed may be slower, which is normal.

◆ Surface dehumidification is measured under standard environmental conditions. For example, a dehumidification rate marked as 50 liters/day refers to the water volume collected in 24 hours at 30°C/RH80%.

◆ During operation, the dehumidifier's outlet emits warm air, which is normal. In winter, when humidity is low, the outlet temperature also decreases significantly, which is normal.

◆ Consumers purchasing dehumidifiers should comprehensively consider noise levels and other indicators. Larger dehumidifiers generally produce more noise and are industrial equipment types. If low noise and effective dehumidification are required, consider purchasing multiple small dehumidifiers.
