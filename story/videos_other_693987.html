Solar-powered embryo incubation

by ucibv623 on 2012-03-06 21:22:39

(By Ed Yong) In 1888, biologist Henry Orr found some spotted salamander eggs in a marshy lake. Some of these eggs were green, and he wrote in his notes that there was a layer of uniformly bright green on the inner membrane of the egg, which was covered with tiny spherical algae. Orr believed this to be the best evidence of symbiosis between salamanders and algae, as they depended on each other for survival in a symbiotic relationship. Orr's conclusion was correct; the salamander eggs had formed a partnership with algae. However, he was wrong on one crucial point. He thought that the algae were merely coincidentally near the salamander eggs, thus forming a symbiotic relationship. This was not the case.

120 years later, Ryan Ker of Dalhousie University discovered that the algae actually invaded the salamander egg cells actively and became part of the salamander embryo, growing together. The algae settled inside the embryo, allowing the salamander embryo to utilize solar energy through the algae, making it one of the animals capable of using solar energy, living a life like plants that directly obtain energy from the sun.

The spotted salamander embryo is not the only animal that forms a symbiotic relationship with algae. The emerald green sea cucumber steals photosynthetic genes from the algae genome after consuming algae. Coral reefs also form symbiotic relationships with algae, obtaining energy through them. Many other animals, from sponges to worms, have similar symbiotic lifestyles. However, the salamander is the only vertebrate with such a symbiotic relationship.

Since Orr’s discovery, several scientists began carefully studying the symbiotic pattern between salamander embryos and algae. Studies have shown that salamander eggs with algae are more likely to hatch into salamanders, taking less time to do so, and the hatched salamanders are larger and develop better. This process depends on sunlight; algae need sunlight for photosynthesis to produce nutrients and provide oxygen to the salamander embryo. If the salamander embryo sinks into darkness, it cannot obtain algae. In return, all the waste products from the salamander's development are given to the algae. If the algae accidentally enter an unfertilized egg, they cannot grow either.

It appears that these relationships have been clarified, but Corneal was the first scientist to show where the algae were located. He observed many green salamander embryos. He used time-lapse video technology to record the entire developmental process of the salamander embryo and began recording when the algae entered the embryo about two weeks after its development. Subsequently, Corneal sequenced the algae within the embryo and removed the algae from it. Finally, Corneal attached a glowing molecule to the DNA of the algae and then reinserted it into the embryo. It turned out that the entire body of the salamander glowed.

After the salamander embryo hatches, most of the algae disappear during the transition from larva to adult. Adult salamanders have opaque bodies and mostly live underground, an environment unsuitable for algae. Corneal believes that in the dark environment, the algae gradually die, leaving behind a bountiful feast for the salamander as a final favor from the algae.

How algae enter salamander eggs has always been a mystery. Perhaps algae track the salamander's excrement to find fertilized eggs. Once found, the algae enter the embryo through the blastopore. Sometimes algae can be passed down through generations of salamanders; Corneal found algae DNA in the oviducts of several female salamanders. This contradicts the earlier idea that algae enter embryos through the blastopore, but perhaps generational transmission is another way, with mother salamanders expelling algae along with the egg cells to help the embryo grow.

In the vertebrate world, the salamander's symbiosis is unique. Normally, the vertebrate immune system attacks foreign organisms, and algae entering the salamander embryo should be cleared by the immune system. Scientists speculate that there might be two possible reasons why salamanders are exceptions. First, maybe the algae enter the embryo before the immune system develops. Second, perhaps salamanders have a unique immune system. This may be related to the salamander's unique regenerative ability, as they can quickly regenerate lost limbs, indicating that their self-recognition patterns differ from other animals. Perhaps this relaxed recognition system provides algae with an opportunity.

There is a final, simpler possibility. No one has found other animals dependent on solar energy simply because others haven't looked closely enough. Now that Corneal has made a good start, perhaps other scientists will discover similar animals in the future.
