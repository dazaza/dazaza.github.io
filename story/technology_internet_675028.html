Physical Bookstores vs. Online Bookstores: Who is Better at "Selling Books"

by lmxbbwudi23 on 2012-02-24 17:18:42

[Books are our lifelong lovers. The fate of physical bookstores should be a bright, warm and resilient part of our lives, not a series of heartless bad news. More bookstore lights mean more sunshine in a city. Protecting, warming, and nourishing them is equivalent to protecting, warming, and nourishing ourselves.] On February 14th, while many people were focusing on Valentine's Day, a Weibo post caught the attention of netizens.

The Shanghai Book City Huaihai store ceased operations due to consecutive years of losses, the impact of online bookstores, and other reasons, prompting deep reflection: who is better at "selling books," online bookstores or physical bookstores?

Physical bookstores under siege

In recent years, physical bookstores have been severely impacted by online bookstores. On one hand, readers' reading habits are changing, with many now reading and purchasing books online. Faced with the competition of online bookstores offering "half-price books," "free books," and "buy 100 get 200" deals, consumers of physical bookstores have increasingly turned to online shopping. In the eyes of some industry insiders, online bookstores now operate without any rules: books bought at a 60% discount are sold at a 40% discount. Online bookstores compete fiercely, with one offering a 40% discount, another a 39%, and yet another a 38%. This leads to mutual complaints and disputes, disrupting the entire book market. Some online bookstores sacrifice the book industry to gather large amounts of customer information for the development of other industries.

Secondly, even though many readers still enjoy visiting physical bookstores, they rarely buy books there. They just browse for the books they need or like, then place their orders online. Recently, an online bookstore launched a "book cover scanning" feature on its mobile app. Mobile users can simply scan the cover of a book with their phone's camera to search for it online.

The emergence of the most beautiful bookstores

When most Chinese people no longer held optimistic views about the future of physical bookstores, a website selected the world's 20 most beautiful bookstores. Regardless of the authority of this selection, the environments of many of these bookstores are truly breathtaking. These successful bookstores demonstrate the irreplaceable importance of physical bookstores—they can be community centers or places worth exploring.

Among these 20 most beautiful bookstores, four from Asia made the list, three of which are from China: Beijing's "Poplar Kids Republic" (蒲蒲兰绘本馆), "the Book Worm" (老书虫书吧), and Taipei's "VVG something" (好样本事). Tokyo's Daikanyama T-Site Bookstore was also included. Poplar Kids Republic's designer, renowned Japanese interior decorator Keiichiro Saka, said that "Poplar Kids Republic" is not only a bookstore but also a dreamlike children's playground, creating a whimsical world full of childlike fun. Another selected Beijing bookstore, "the Book Worm," located in Sanlitun South Street, is the most active foreign language bookstore and library in Beijing. Within three years, it grew from a modest bookstore to a charming small library-style establishment with a collection of 14,000 books, over 8,000 English novels, and various English magazines. At different times each week, "the Book Worm" hosts various activities such as riddles, cocktail parties, classical music appreciation, and invites authors for reader meet-and-greets or readings. Besides Beijing, "the Book Worm" has branches in Suzhou and Chengdu.

Learning how to "sell books" to survive

Among these 20 most beautiful bookstores, there are also "century-old shops." Lello Bookstore in Porto, Portugal's second-largest city, opened in 1881 and has nearly 120 years of history. In the center of the bookstore stands a beautiful Gothic wooden staircase leading all the way to the top floor. Readers can look up through the glass roof for a 360-degree view of the sky. The surrounding bookshelves are filled with various Portuguese and English books. Its grand artistic style and exquisite books make it a place where readers and travelers linger and forget to leave.

As online bookstores increasingly capture the attention of book lovers, what disappears is not just some traditional small humanistic bookstores but also a certain attitude towards life and a way of living. While the advantages of online bookstores are evident, they still fall short of meeting the pursuits of the general public in real life. Beyond just buying books, culturally-oriented readers continue to pursue freedom, independence, and dreams within the tangible space of bookstores—something online bookstores cannot provide.

These 20 most beautiful bookstores undoubtedly tell us that being good at "selling books" does not necessarily mean selling a lot of books; rather, it means whether they can become cultural landmarks of a city. If they can take on this function, they will naturally be able to survive in society. Otherwise, if they rely solely on book sales to compete with online bookstores, physical bookstores will only face a dead end.
