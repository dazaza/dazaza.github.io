Liu Yishou, a white-collar worker, said that 99% of the rural poverty alleviation relies on netizen support.

by anonymous on 2011-05-12 17:47:41

Now dying is not hard, but living without money is even harder," said Mr. Liu Yishou.

At the end of last year, Wei Ran published a book based on his diary called "Grain Farmers".

"I want to write down the problems I see but cannot solve, so that more people know about them and work together to find solutions," he said. The biggest three difficulties for farmers now are: college education, old age, and serious illness," said Liu Yishou.

He visited a farmer's family in Qinghai where three children had gone to university, but this did not change their impoverished fate; instead, it left the family with tens of thousands of yuan in debt. The eldest daughter graduated from university with loans but because she could not repay them, the school withheld her diploma, leaving her to work various jobs as a high school graduate. Next, the two younger siblings were admitted to university in the same year.

The housewife told Wei Ran, "Now dying isn't hard, but living without money is much harder! Adults can't afford to get sick, and children can't afford to go to school, especially university. If either of these happens, it's life-ruining."

Liu Yishou also mentioned elderly farmers, which includes not only bachelors who cannot afford wives, but also older farmers.

When talking about aging in China, people often refer to urban populations, as if rural areas do not or will not face such issues. But according to my observations, the situation in rural areas is actually more severe," Liu Yishou explained. One reason is that the majority of young and middle-aged rural laborers have migrated to cities for work, leaving behind older farmers without social security benefits, thus leading to a situation where they have no one to rely on in old age. Secondly, farmers engaged in heavy agricultural labor age prematurely.

In a small town in Yunnan with little migrant population, Wei Ran encountered an elderly woman in her 80s who scavenged for a living. When the wind blew a small cigarette pack into a ditch, she bent over and crawled into the ditch to retrieve it. She could only earn a few cents per day, using this money to buy rice at a small restaurant. Upon learning that she had a son, Wei Ran was initially angry. However, upon visiting the son's home, his anger dissipated entirely. The son’s condition was worse than his mother's. Honest but uneducated, he dared not leave to work elsewhere and relied on farming meager land. Unable to afford fertilizer, his crops grew poorly, and the little grain he harvested barely sufficed to feed his own two children. To alleviate some of his burden, the old woman scavenged to support herself.

There was a nursing home in the town housing three elderly women. An 80-year-old was bedridden, while two women in their 70s scavenged outside. The nursing home provided only shelter; to sustain themselves, they had to fend for themselves. During the day, the two elderly women scavenged. At night, all three made incense candles. Some of these candles were sold to Buddhist villagers, while others were kept for personal use. Burning incense and praying to Buddha served as their health insurance.

Along the way, farmers frequently complained to Wei Ran: "We have no retirement age, no pension. Even when we can no longer work, we still must. If we don't work today, there will be no food tomorrow! Relying on our children in old age is a farce. Nowadays, how many children can you depend on? They can't even take care of themselves."

"In the village now, the elderly are too old, and the young are too young. The fields rely on us to cultivate. Those who go out to earn money rarely bring any back, leaving behind the elderly and children. It's hard to come up with tuition fees!"

"The government policies towards farmers are getting better. As long as you have money, you can get anything. But why is life getting harder?"

An elderly man named Wang from Jinghechuan in Gansu Province recounted the story of his wife to Wei Ran while sitting on his kang (a traditional heated bed).

"On the day before she passed away, she steamed three pots of steamed buns and made noodles for me and the children. She also washed our clothes. By then, she was already emaciated. In her final days, when the pain came, sweat poured off her like rain, soaking through her clothes. But I couldn't even afford a single painkiller! All I could do was watch helplessly as she suffered from her illness."

Wang was paralyzed, unable to walk a single step. Desperate, he arranged a marriage for his teenage daughter, receiving 2000 yuan, which he used for his wife's chemotherapy. After just two sessions, the money was gone.

"When she was in pain, the only thing I could do was stretch out my left hand to hold hers. I didn't know if it alleviated her pain, but without my left hand to support me, I couldn't sit up, so I lay there reaching for her hand." Speaking of this, Wang broke down in tears.

Wei Ran handed him a tissue, reached out to hold his hand, but did not know what to say to comfort him. After a long silence, Wang continued sobbing: "Since she got sick, enduring such pain, she hardly ever cried out. I saw it in my eyes, feeling sorrow deep within my heart. On her final night, she grabbed my hand and said, 'I haven't cured your illness, and now I've fallen ill myself. I worry about you and the children, but I truly can't hold on anymore. Once I'm gone, you must struggle to rise again, learn to take care of yourself and the children.'"

After saying this, it was the first time since she became ill that she cried. Later, she took out a small mirror they bought during their wedding and combed her hair. Then she said she wanted to sleep and lay down beside him. Since being diagnosed with breast cancer, she had been sleeping alone, but that night she didn't. Lying beside him, her face was yellowish, and he asked if she felt okay or if she was in pain. Closing her eyes, she shook her head slightly. He did not disturb her further, thinking that if she could sleep, she should. After she fell ill, she hardly slept at all, suffering greatly at night. Just as dawn began to break, he sensed something was wrong and called out to her, but she never woke up...

Wang could no longer contain himself and burst into loud sobs.

"You should help build a road for our village! We've been hoping for a road for generations!"

One early morning, Wei Ran received a call from a man with a Sichuan-Guizhou accent shouting loudly: "Is this Wei Ran? We need to pay back the money!" Wei Ran was momentarily confused, thinking subconsciously, "I haven't borrowed money from anyone!"

Not hearing him respond, the man reiterated, "We need to pay back the money!"

"Calm down and tell me where you're from."

"We're from Guizhou, from Anshun."

Wei Ran realized, "Oh, you're from Bamaochong." A thought flashed through his mind: "Uh oh, did it fail?"

A year ago, Wei Ran went to the Anshun region in Guizhou. Pushing his bicycle, he walked along a narrow path in the mountains from 9 am until 3 pm before finally encountering a household. He stayed in the village and discovered that almost every family owned around 10 mu of forest land. Beneath the trees were no shrubs, only thick grass. I suddenly had an idea for poverty alleviation.

Before arriving, he had witnessed traders from Guiyang purchasing local free-range chickens for twelve to thirteen yuan per pound, reselling them in Guiyang for around twenty yuan. Pointing to the forest land, he told the villagers, "You're holding a golden bowl but begging for food!" They responded, "We can't cut down those trees; the government would fine us." He replied, "I'm not asking you to cut down trees, I'm suggesting raising chickens, free-range chickens!"

A young man excitedly responded, "Wow! I've thought about that too, but I lack capital!"

"If you're willing to do it, I'll provide the capital and technical assistance, all free of charge!"

It happened that a netizen from Guizhou had previously told Wei Ran that he could sponsor five to ten students, even from elementary school through university. Wei immediately contacted him about the project, planning to start with four households, investing 5,000 yuan per household to purchase chicks, build chicken coops, and install nets. The netizen agreed right away.

Subsequently, Wei accompanied four farming families to select materials in town, negotiate prices, and have the netizen transfer funds directly to the manufacturers. Not until the chicken coops were built, the slopes enclosed, feed prepared, and chickens raised, did Wei leave.

Suddenly, they called to repay the money. Did it fail? That doesn't make sense; if they lost money, how could they repay it? Sensing urgency and tension in the caller's voice, Wei asked, "Calm down and explain slowly. What money are you repaying?"

"Teacher Wei, all four of our families have made money. Now, another four families want to raise chickens. We've given them the chicks and bought nets for them. Originally, those free-range chickens sold for eighteen yuan per pound in the first batch, nearly covering our costs."

Wei Ran had agreed that the sponsorship money did not need to be repaid but should be passed on to the next group. However, the farmers insisted stubbornly: "We Miao people don't have the habit of owing money. Now that we're making money, we must repay it!"

In Dapanyu Village in Longnan, Gansu, located deep in the mountains and perched on the mountain waist, Wei observed dense shrubs everywhere. Being close to Guangyuan in Sichuan, he believed sheep farming would be a good project. No contamination, the mutton would be absolutely green organic food, and wool could be further processed. When he enthusiastically shared this idea with the villagers, it was unexpectedly rejected.

"Look at our roads. Every year, just transporting fertilizer takes two days. On the first day, carrying fertilizer halfway, we're already exhausted. We must return home to eat and rest overnight, and only on the second day do we have the strength to continue."

"Poor roads mean the pigs we raise sell for lower prices. Pigs sell for six yuan per pound at the foot of the mountain, but we can only sell for four. Why? The pig traders say, looking at your roads, carved straight into steep stone slabs, pigs might fall and die on the way. What's the value of a dead pig?"

"Teacher Wei, you don't need to help with any projects, no need to catch chickens or lambs for us. These things we can manage by borrowing or taking loans. Just help us build a road! We've been hoping for this road for generations."

Upon hearing this, Wei Ran's mind raced. In Shanghai, with good road foundations, merely scraping the surface and relaying asphalt costs tens of thousands of yuan per kilometer. Here, constructing a road would involve blasting rocks, and for seven or eight kilometers, how much would it cost?

For over a decade, the village had multiple times organized villagers to manually construct the road, but due to rocky cliffs, manual excavation was impossible. Plus, the village was too poor to collect sufficient funds, resulting in abandonment each time.

Hearing there was a resident cadre in the village, Wei expressed a desire to meet him. The villagers immediately became upset: "What resident cadre? He's just a resident-at-home cadre. You won't see him all year."

At night, Wei couldn't sleep, searching his mind for usable information and connections. Tossing and turning all night without results, at dawn, he climbed up the mountain to clear his mind.

On the slope, an early-rising elder was building a house. Wei approached and asked how the original house collapsed. The elder said it was during the 5.12 earthquake. Wei then asked, "After the disaster, the state stipulated rebuilding houses with concrete and steel foundations to withstand an eight-magnitude earthquake. Why are you only using wooden structures?" The elder bitterly laughed, "If I carry cement and sand up here, it'd take ten years, and I can't wait that long."

His words immediately reminded Wei: "There's a solution!"

After the 5.12 earthquake, Wei went to Wenchuan and participated in rescue and reconstruction efforts, gaining a relatively thorough understanding of relevant national policies. Post-disaster reconstruction houses must be completed by the end of 2009, meeting national standards, with each household receiving a 20,000-yuan subsidy. Additionally, if inspections fail, local officials would be held accountable.

"I immediately thought of the post-disaster reconstruction office, so I quickly called them. But the signal in the mountains was poor, and I ran all the way to the top, where the phone signal was intermittent. Calling the municipal government's reconstruction office, after listening to his introduction, they asked Wei to write a report and send an email."

"Why bother sending an email? Making a call, I've already run to the top of the mountain, hanging on tree branches. Simply, I'll bring the documents directly."

Excitedly, Wei ran down the mountain into the village secretary's home. Opening his laptop to write the report, he shouted at the secretary, "Get up, quickly, let's go to the city!"

They boarded a long-distance bus, jolting for fifteen to sixteen hours to reach Longnan. Getting off, they were so numb they couldn't feel their legs moving.

At the reconstruction office, Wei introduced himself, emphasizing that he was from Shanghai, then detailed the relevant post-disaster reconstruction policies. The state requires that by the end of this year, reconstruction houses must be built and inhabited. For villages like this, with current construction methods, it's impossible to meet standards. How will you inspect and pass unless you falsify reports?

Even Wei didn't expect that on the third day, on the way to Tongwei, he received a call from the village secretary. The old man's voice was loud, ringing in his ears: "The mayor has come! Along with the transportation bureau director, they've already decided to build a road for the village, hahaha... I represent the eight generations of ancestors in the village to thank you!"

Wei said that every time he helps a household or a place improve their lives, the satisfaction and happiness he feels cannot be described in words. All the hard work and fatigue of recent months dissipate instantly.

China does not lack people willing to do good deeds, but it lacks channels for doing good.

Wei Ran's blog once won the title of Top Ten Social Responsibility Blogs. After being selected, it attracted even more netizens, increasing phone calls, with many inviting Wei to visit them.

Before setting out now, Wei posts travel notices on his blog. Quickly, netizens heading to the same destination contact him. Once, arriving in a certain area in Gansu, Wei called a netizen who had left a comment, only to discover he was actually a deputy district chief.

Issues regarding identity are no longer a problem. Wei says his journey is becoming smoother. Upon arriving somewhere, as long as one person leads him around and attends one market day, almost everyone in the township soon knows him. Word spreads quickly among the people. Then, you just wait, and those earnest farmers will come knocking.

"I help farmers, and many people help me," Wei said. Netizens frequently leave messages for him: "Brother, can you hang on?"

Currently, his assistance involves three main areas: labor, funds, and projects, 99% of which are supported by netizens. Currently, the number of netizens gathered around Wei Ran's blog conservatively estimates to be in the tens of thousands. I collectively refer to them as benevolent individuals. Without them, many things I wouldn't be able to accomplish. China does not lack people willing to do good deeds, but it lacks channels for doing good.

This year, Wei set out on the third day of the Lunar New Year.

A fashion designer found him. This designer not only has his own brand and specialty stores but also owns a factory in Shenzhen producing primarily export garments, all handmade from spinning thread to weaving fabric and sewing. He sought a poor region where women who cannot leave to work and are skilled in needlework could be trained locally to earn money and alleviate poverty.

Wei accompanied him to several villages in Tongwei, Gansu. Before returning to the county, they encountered snowfall lasting three days and nights, with thick ice on the ground, making walking slippery. Initially, they counted how many times they fell during the journey.

Their pant legs were completely soaked, frozen stiff with ice blocks hanging like bells, clinking as they walked. Their shoes filled with water, creating blisters with every step. While struggling on the cliffside, Wei's phone rang.

"Hello! I am the Party Secretary of Guangling County. Our area is a poor region in northern Shanxi. I sincerely invite you to come to our county..."

Each time Wei returns to Shanghai from outside, his business friends warmly welcome him with dust-clearing gatherings. Drinking and chatting, they often lament: "Nowadays, we have money, houses, luxury cars, beautiful women, nothing is lacking, yet why do we still not feel happy?"

"You guy, every time you return, although you're dark and thin, the happiness in your eyes shows you're the happiest among us."
