[1] Stop waiting for me to tell you before you act

Following the statement "It's the people who support you, so you better figure it out yourselves!" Premier Wen again made a remark during the government work report consultation meeting: "Stop waiting for me to tell you before you act," which was once again warmly received by netizens.

Because the urban registered unemployment rate reported in the government work report was 4.6%, while the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences (CASS) published a social blue book revealing an urban surveyed unemployment rate of 9.4%. Why would there be a 5.2% difference between the Ministry of Human Resources and Social Security (MHRSS) and CASS unemployment statistics? The public has significant doubts about this. In response to such "quarreling" behavior, Premier Wen stated:

"You guys at the Ministry of Social Labor and Security should hurry up and go online, or quickly hold a press conference, and explain the differences between the 4.6% and CASS's 9.4%. Don't wait for me to tell you before you act."

Why are these two data points different? Director of the Institute of Sociology at the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences, Li Peilin, explained it like this:

"For the past dozen years, there has been a difference between the urban registered unemployment rate and the urban surveyed unemployment rate, usually the latter being higher than the former. When the job market is relatively stable, these two figures are close; when employment conditions worsen, there will be a larger gap between the two numbers. Because our unemployment insurance does not cover all urban employees, for example, migrant workers who have not participated in unemployment insurance will not be counted after becoming unemployed."

However, the MHRSS had another explanation:

"The 9.4% surveyed unemployment rate comes from a sample survey of seven thousand people, among whom there were 2,288 economically active urban population members, and around 200 of them were unemployed. This number is derived from comparing these two groups."

In fact, regarding these two sets of data, we, the common people, have our own opinions:

"As someone who has only worked in the city for a few years, my hukou (household registration) is still sitting at school. Where am I supposed to register as unemployed? If I become unemployed, I can only quickly look for another job myself. How dare I tarnish the country's unemployment rate."

For this method of calculation that does not include those who are neither employed nor registered, it has long been questioned by scholars both domestically and internationally. Premier Wen should have them explain not just the severely underestimated urban registered unemployment rate, but also provide us with a number that can withstand scrutiny.

[2] Is it because of the "May Day" quarrel?

Some departments indeed take action without waiting for the premier to speak. After the Guangdong provincial government officially announced the "May Day" holiday plan, Hangzhou, Suzhou, and Nanjing proposed to jointly restore the May Day Golden Week, making whether the Golden Week should be restored a focal point of discussion.

In response to the current heated debate, the General Office of the State Council formally responded: no localities may arbitrarily restore the May Day Golden Week.

Should the total number of working days in a year be unified across the country? Although some comments reference Germany's central government only stipulating certain unified statutory holidays, with the rest determined by each state individually, have they considered whether this approach suits China's national conditions under different political systems?

Actually, I just want to ask, why do various regions feel the need to make statements without first obtaining approval from the General Office of the State Council?

[3] Savings that every province could make

The case of a police officer in Yunnan Province shooting and killing someone has recently caused controversy due to a signed agreement. The Mengzi County Public Security Bureau reached a reconciliation agreement with the deceased's family, paying a substantial compensation of 550,000 yuan to the victim's family.

Regarding the case of police officer Ji Zhongchun shooting and killing someone, the Mengzi County Public Security Bureau initially classified it as a personal act, completely unrelated to his identity or position as a police officer. Since it was deemed a personal act, why did the Public Security Bureau pay the compensation? Deputy Director Zhao Xiangrong of the Mengzi County Public Security Bureau explained it like this:

"The reason the Public Security Bureau paid this amount is considering Ji Zhongchun's specific circumstances. Ji Zhongchun is a veteran police officer with over 20 years of service at the Public Security Bureau, with poor economic conditions at home. Out of humanitarianism, the Public Security Bureau gave prior humanitarian compensation to the deceased's family. And this 550,000 yuan is the office expenses saved by the Public Security Bureau."

It is well known that this incident occurred due to a dispute while Ji Zhongchun was reversing his car. Given that he owns a private vehicle, how can he be categorized as having poor economic conditions? This inevitably leads to the question: what exactly constitutes good economic conditions?

According to Article 14 of the "Regulations on the Use of Police Equipment and Weapons by the People's Police," if a police officer illegally uses weapons, causing unnecessary casualties or property damage, the relevant authority to which the police officer belongs shall compensate according to the provisions of the "State Compensation Law." Why doesn't the Mengzi Public Security Bureau admit that this compensation falls under state compensation? If it truly does not, why does the Public Security Bureau use taxpayers' money to benefit individuals?

[6] Overblown internet entrepreneurship

Suddenly, as if overnight, millions of online stores sprouted up everywhere. In the current economic crisis, the internet seems to have become an important way to solve employment problems. Not only do media outlets repeatedly promote online employment for students as a great option, Guangzhou VIPshop Information Technology Co., Ltd., even during the Two Sessions period, a representative suggested that the state should subsidize university students starting businesses on Taobao.

Low cost, low investment, low risk—internet entrepreneurship became an instant favorite overnight. Many domestic colleges and universities have set up online store training classes to help students start businesses. Yes, if everyone now follows the trend and opens online stores, the employment problem can be solved immediately, but what about one year later, or ten years later? How many people can truly succeed? Let alone issues like medical insurance and social security. Internet security and legal standardization have always been the most critical challenges in the information economy wave. While our experts and professors advocate encouraging college students to choose internet entrepreneurship, have they considered this point for them?
