How should an online bookstore be operated well?

by bchenglk63 on 2012-03-09 16:15:55

It is undeniable that management ideas, methods of operation, excessive taxation, unequal competition, and the rise in the cost of human resources and store rent are important reasons for the closure of bookstores. In addition to these factors, the low discount strategy used by online platforms to attract customers has also dealt a fatal blow to physical bookstores. Over more than a decade of buying books in Beijing, whether it was at Wansheng Bookstore, Fengrusong, Guolin Bookstore, Third Pole, or Sanlian Bookstore, Hanfenlou Bookstore, or Beijing Book Building, except for discounted books, the lowest discount was usually no less than 20% off. However, since the price wars among e-commerce platforms like Joyo, Dangdang, and JD.com, I have become a regular customer of these online stores. In terms of academic books which I purchase frequently, very few are priced higher than 20% off. Many readers have adopted the method of noting down book titles at physical bookstores and then purchasing them online. Taking myself as an example, I spent about 20,000 yuan on books last year. Apart from two sets of books with higher code prices purchased directly from publishers in bulk, approximately 15,000 yuan worth of books were all bought from Dangdang, JD.com, and Joyo.

Yan Bofei, the CEO of Shanghai Jifeng Bookstore, once asked me, "If physical bookstores and online bookstores offered the same price, would you still buy online?" I immediately replied, "If that were the case, many books could be bought directly from physical bookstores."

Given the current situation, the task before relevant regulatory departments is undoubtedly to establish fair rules for this disorderly competition, set regulations on book discounts, allow e-commerce platforms and physical bookstores to compete on the same platform, and prevent them from engaging in money-losing promotional games. Of course, implementing a universal tax reduction for the book distribution and sales industry is also necessary. As a reader, while pursuing low prices, I am also aware of one truth: without reasonable profits, how can publishers produce excellent products?

From "Fengrusong" Bookstore to Daya Bookstore, from Guangzhou Sanlian to Guanghezuoyong Bookstore, from Chengdu Time History Bookshop to Shanghai万象Bookstore, from Shanghai Book City Huaihai Store to the main store of Sichuan Hongwen Bookstore going out of business, there have been too many bad news about physical bookstores in recent years. The price war among online bookstores last year allowed many readers to happily buy many cheap books, and Dangdang's 220 million yuan loss may not be unrelated to this. Perhaps the ones most affected are the physical bookstores.

According to Yu Yu, Dangdang does not sell books below cost. Although e-commerce does not have storefront costs, shipping and packaging transportation fees are also significant costs that may not necessarily be lower than the storefront costs of physical bookstores. On the surface, e-commerce book prices do not fall below the cost price, but extremely low profits clearly cannot sustain wages, taxes, and shipping and packaging costs. It is well known that unlike traditional bookstores, e-commerce plays capital games. Under the condition of large capital entry, unfortunate books have become the best commodity for e-commerce to gather popularity and create influence, and low discounts have become an infallible method and mantra. In the absence of game rules, e-commerce uses overall money-losing promotional price wars to disrupt the entire book publishing industry chain, even making authors victims. Some publishers and authors sign agreements specifying that royalties for books sold to e-commerce should be lower by several points.

According to reports, Dangdang.com lost 220 million yuan in 2011. Yu Yu, CEO of Dangdang, stated that publishers, considering their own interests, have stopped e-commerce websites from selling below cost. Dangdang will not sell books below cost but will use its cost advantage. The media recently reported that CPPCC member Zhang Kangkang has submitted a proposal, one of the contents being: "Facing malicious competition in book prices from online bookstores, the government cannot be inactive. It can certainly formulate corresponding policies and regulations according to international price laws to limit the book sales of online bookstores and any other type of bookstore. Regulatory authorities can introduce access measures for online bookstores to impose constraints and punishments." She also cited that the South Korean government stipulates that online bookstore prices must be synchronized with physical bookstores and cannot be less than 20% off. The German government stipulates that a new book must first be sold in physical bookstores for a period before it can be sold on online bookstores, with strict regulations on discount ranges, and sales prices cannot be lower than cost price.
