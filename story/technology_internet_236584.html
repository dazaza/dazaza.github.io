Reason why Indonesian satellite didn't die: Successful self-rescue via "maneuverable orbit change" function

by weixing123 on 2009-09-11 16:21:50

"China's satellite launch success rate is over 90%, with relatively high reliability and safety, which is recognized internationally. Therefore, for the problems that occurred this time, the outside world must rationally view them, and there is no need to deliberately magnify them."

At 17:28 on August 31st, China launched Indonesia's "Palapa-D" commercial communication satellite into space using the "Changzheng-3B" carrier rocket at the Xichang Satellite Launch Center. Due to the thrust of one hydrogen-oxygen engine being lower after the third-stage rocket's second ignition, the satellite was not delivered into its intended orbit. This is the first failure in the launch of Changzheng series rockets in 13 years.

The "Changzheng-3B" rocket used for the Indonesian satellite launch mission can be called a "gold medal rocket" within China's Changzheng rocket family. It is a three-stage liquid rocket with an Earth synchronous transfer orbit carrying capacity of up to 5.1 tons. Because the third stage of the rocket uses advanced low-temperature propulsion technology and has a flexible control system, it possesses the ability to adjust the attitude of the payload significantly and can also perform multiple satellite launches. This launch was the 118th launch of the Changzheng series carrier rockets, and since 1996, the "Changzheng-3B" had created a record of continuous 75 successful launches.

Some media reported that the rocket malfunction should have occurred in the 10th step - "the second ignition of the third-stage engine." The separation of the satellite from the third stage is achieved through the separation force of the separation spring. When the rocket control system issues a star-rocket separation command, the non-polluting explosive bolts on the straps connecting the satellite and the payload bracket explode and unlock. After unlocking, the straps are pulled back by the springs installed on the payload bracket, and the satellite is pushed away from the rocket by the separation spring.

Pang Zhihao, a space expert, told the Beijing Science and Technology News that now there are three common diseases in rocket launches, with the most frequent being malfunctions in the power system. For example, the engine did not start or shut down prematurely. In addition, the attitude control system and structural system are also prone to failures. Any problem in these three systems could lead to abnormal satellite launches or even failures.

"In the process of rocket launching satellites, the detachment of the first, second, and third-stage rockets, the separation of the star and the rocket, as well as the multiple shutdowns and ignitions during the flight of multi-stage carrier rockets, interstellar satellites, and Earth-orbiting satellites are several critical points where problems occur," Professor Jiao Weixin of Peking University's School of Earth and Space Sciences said in an interview with the Beijing Science and Technology News. To reduce the occurrence of faults in rocket launches, scientific and reasonable design must be ensured, and the electronic components must be able to withstand strong vibrations and other factors during the rocket launch.

"Looking at some cases of failed satellite launches currently, many were caused by problems in the rocket design," Pang Zhihao said, referring to South Korea's recent rocket launch failure due to such reasons.

Rocket launches are a vast system engineering project, and often a tiny link going wrong can lead to the entire launch failing. For example, defects and foreign objects cannot appear in the production of rockets. A few years ago, when analyzing the cause of the European Ariane rocket launch failure, it was finally found that a small piece of cloth left over from wiping the nozzle affected the operation of the engine. In addition, human operational errors and weather conditions can also lead to rocket launch failures.

It is precisely because of these various reasons that the current satellite launch rocket technology can only be mastered by a few countries. Due to the high risk and large investment, most countries have not yet ventured into this field, and they adopt the method of "borrowing ladders to climb" when launching satellites.

Pang Zhihao stated that China's current rocket launches rank among the top internationally, and China's Changzheng series rockets are also considered one of the most reliable rockets internationally. From 1996 to before this launch, China's Changzheng series carrier rockets had already achieved 75 consecutive successful launches. "China's satellite launch success rate reaches over 90%, with relatively high reliability and safety, which is internationally recognized. Therefore, for the problems that occurred this time, the outside world must rationally view them, and there is no need to deliberately magnify them," Pang Zhihao said.

Monitoring captures Indonesian satellite

The journalist noticed that after China's satellite launch encountered problems this time, the mainstream foreign media did not hype it up like they did with North Korea's and South Korea's satellite launch failures earlier this year. Only a few media outlets published very brief news.

The "Palapa-D" commercial communication satellite was designed and manufactured by France's Thales Alenia Space and owned by Indonesia's Satelit Nusantara Tiga (SNT) company. With a mass of 4,078 kilograms and a power output of 8,600 watts, it has a design life of 15 years and will be positioned at 113 degrees east longitude above the equator. Once the satellite becomes operational, it will cover Indonesia, ASEAN countries, and extensive regions in Asia, enabling SNT to provide various communication services to its clients. The satellite will replace the "Palapa-C2" satellite, which reached the end of its orbital life in 2010, and will become the sole operational satellite of SNT in the next two to three years. However, the fault in this launch has cast uncertainty over this plan.

After the accident involving the "Palapa-D" commercial communication satellite launch, aerospace experts from both inside and outside China began analyzing the causes of the satellite launch and sought appropriate solutions.

Fortunately, around 22:00 on August 31st, the corresponding satellite monitoring system successfully tracked the satellite's operational trajectory. Monitoring showed that the satellite was still operating normally. Currently, how to rescue this satellite that did not enter the correct orbit has become a major concern for many people.

Precedents for Orbit Repair

Pang Zhihao indicated that according to foreign cases, there is still hope for individual satellites to be salvaged after they fail to successfully enter the predetermined orbit, and currently, the international community has developed multiple methods for satellite orbit repair.

On April 7, 1990, China successfully launched the "AsiaSat-1" commercial satellite for the first time, which was a satellite previously recovered by the U.S. after a failed launch. It was understood that the "AsiaSat-1" satellite was originally the Westar VI artificial satellite designed and manufactured by the U.S. Hughes Aircraft Company. In 1984, after the launch of the Westar VI, it failed to accurately enter the orbit. In November of that year, the U.S. carried out a "satellite rescue action," utilizing a space shuttle to retrieve the satellite and return it to the manufacturer, who then handed it over to China for launch.

Additionally, some satellites equipped with fuel can also repair their orbits themselves, thereby allowing the satellite to enter the predetermined orbit. "A typical case is Europe's Artemis satellite in 2001. After the satellite was launched, it did not enter the predetermined orbit. Since this satellite was equipped with electric rockets much more efficient than chemical fuel, under the control of ground personnel, it continuously changed its orbit and entered the predetermined orbit after more than ten months."

Pang Zhihao mentioned that using lunar gravity is also an important method for repair. In 1998, the "AsiaSat-3" satellite failed to enter the predetermined orbit, but the U.S. Hughes Company managed to let it successfully enter the predetermined orbit through the effect of lunar gravity. Due to unclear information about the "Palapa-D" commercial communication satellite, Pang Zhihao did not evaluate whether the satellite could be successfully repaired.

Satellite "Revival" Capabilities

However, according to Thales Alenia Space, the satellite should be able to gradually enter the predetermined orbit through orbit-changing technology. On September 1st, the satellite performed a perigee orbit change successfully, and the satellite status was normal.

Compared with South Korea's "Naro" launch failure earlier, South Korea's satellite was a small low-orbit satellite. During launch, due to the fairing not separating properly, the additional fairing fragments increased the effective load's weight, preventing the satellite from achieving the minimum speed required to orbit the Earth, causing it to directly fall into the atmosphere and burn up. In contrast, the "Palapa-D" satellite launched by China was a large communications satellite (4.1 tons) operating in geosynchronous orbit (36,000 kilometers above the Earth's surface). Although the third-stage engine of the rocket malfunctioned, the satellite did not enter the predetermined synchronous transfer orbit, it would not fall into the atmosphere, and the satellite itself was functioning normally, so there was still room for rescue.

"The most important thing is that even if South Korea's satellite had not been destroyed, since it does not possess orbit-changing capability, it would not have been able to return to the predetermined orbit. The 'Palapa-D' satellite has the ability for maneuverable orbit changes, so there is still hope for self-rescue when the rocket malfunctions," Jiao Weixin introduced.

However, Jiao Weixin is not optimistic about whether the "Palapa-D" commercial communication satellite can be successfully repaired. "Currently, many details about the satellite are unclear, so it is difficult to judge whether it can be completely repaired." Jiao Weixin told reporters that although satellites can perform orbit corrections once they enter Earth orbit, the specific orbit adjustment capabilities of this satellite are unclear.

This launch, due to the fault of the third-stage engine, resulted in the Chinese-launched satellite not entering the synchronous transfer orbit due to the rocket issue, forcing it to remain in a slightly lower orbit. However, the satellite can be transferred from this lower orbit to a synchronous orbit. The satellite carries an engine with two functions: one is for orbit changes during the launch process, and the other is for orbit maintenance and adjustments throughout its operational period. During the satellite's operational period, it may deviate from the predetermined orbit due to various reasons. When the deviation is significant, the engine needs to be activated to correct the orbit.

The amount of fuel carried by the engine is limited. Even if the "Palapa-D" satellite successfully changes orbit, it will consume fuel originally intended for orbit maintenance. Thus, the fuel available for orbit maintenance may not be sufficient, potentially leading to a premature end of the satellite's life. Whether the satellite's life is shortened and by how much depends on the specific design parameters of the satellite.

"The problems encountered in the Chinese-launched Indonesian satellite and the Korean satellite demonstrate that aerospace engineering is a high-risk, cutting-edge complex engineering project. Any small mistake can bring unforeseen risks," Pang Zhihao said.
