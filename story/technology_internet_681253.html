Mummy Beware: The 3 Snack Culprits That Harm Baby's Intelligence!

by childrenstory126 on 2012-02-28 12:10:30

Snacks are children's favorite food, but now many snacks have added a large amount of artificial ingredients. These ingredients can be harmful to the brain development of children and affect the intellectual development of babies.

Culprit 1: Trans fat

Ms. Zhu has been fond of fried food since she was a child, and this habit seems to have been passed on to her son.

Ms. Zhu's son is in the third grade at a primary school. Recently, during the winter vacation, Ms. Zhu often fries lotus root cakes, lamb skewers, and five-spice strips for her son at home, and they both feast heartily. But yesterday morning, as soon as she got up, Ms. Zhu found that her son had blisters all over his mouth, so she quickly took him to see a doctor at the oral department of the hospital.

When Ms. Zhu received the test report, she was shocked: her son's blood viscosity was twice as high as normal people. After the doctor asked about his living habits, he said it was because he consumed too much trans fatty acids. The doctor also warned her that if he continued eating like this for a long time, he might increase the risk of developing Alzheimer's disease in the future.

Trans fat, also known as trans fatty acid, is generally produced after plant oil is treated with [hydrogenation] technology. Compared with general plant oil, artificial trans fat has the advantages of being heat-resistant, not easy to spoil, and lasting longer when stored. Currently, the use of artificial trans fat in the American fast-food industry is quite common, usually used to make crispy bread, fried French fries, and donuts. Some fats that make pastries flaky, margarine, and edible oils used for frying may contain artificial trans fat.

After pregnant women consume excessive amounts of trans fatty acids, they can pass them through the placenta to the fetus, and lactating mothers will also feed trans fatty acids to their infants through breast milk, thus affecting their growth and development, causing lipid deficiency in the brains of young children, and affecting their intellectual development.

Therefore, parents should pay special attention to the following foods: first, artificial oils, such as margarine (vegetable cream); second, fried foods, such as instant noodles, potato chips, and French fries. Some processed foods containing oils, such as instant soups, fast food, frozen foods (such as glutinous rice balls), baked goods (such as cookies, biscuits, and bread, etc.), various instant powder-like foods (such as powdered oatmeal, coconut powder, sesame paste powder, etc.), various creamy candies, peanut butter, chocolate sauce, etc., may contain trans fatty acids.

Culprit 2: Puffed Food

In recent years, puffed snacks have become popular among children due to their crisp, crunchy, fragrant, and sweet flavors. However, medical experts at home and abroad believe that puffed food is not truly safe or healthy food. If children eat these kinds of food for a long time, it will be harmful rather than beneficial to their brain and physical development.

American nutritional food experts have given warnings many years ago: there are more harmful metallic elements such as lead or aluminum in puffed food, and parents should not let their children eat these foods frequently. The reasons why puffed food contains relatively high levels of lead and aluminum are: one, additives such as leavening agents are often added when processing these foods, some leavening agents (such as alum and sodium bicarbonate) contain a lot of lead or aluminum and other heavy metals; two, during the processing of food, it goes through metal pipes, which usually have lead-tin alloys inside. Under high temperatures, the lead easily vaporizes, and the vaporized lead then contaminates these puffed foods. Regularly eating puffed food can greatly harm children (especially those under 3 years old).

When "lead enters through the mouth" for a long time, it gradually becomes latent in the blood and bones, making it difficult to naturally metabolize. Children are in the stage of growth and development, absorbing several times more lead than adults, while their ability to excrete lead is weaker, making it easier for lead to accumulate in the body. When harmful heavy metals accumulate to a certain level, they can cause significant damage to the nervous, digestive, hematopoietic systems, leading to cognitive impairment or reduced thinking ability, and may even affect the lifelong health of children.

Excessive intake of aluminum mainly damages brain function. For example, it interferes with human thought, consciousness, and memory functions, causing nervous system diseases, manifested as memory decline, visual and motor coordination failure, brain injury, intellectual decline, and severe cases may lead to dementia.

Culprit 3: Artificial Coloring

Nowadays, children's food comes in a wide variety, with exquisite packaging and bright colors, but some colored foods containing coloring agents pose hidden dangers to human health. Excessive or long-term consumption is harmful and offers no benefits to children's health.

During the stage of children's intellectual development, they need a large amount of high-quality protein and lipids for nutrition, and artificial synthetic colorants cannot provide these nutrients. At the same time, artificial synthetic colorants themselves or their metabolites are toxic, and during storage, arsenic, lead, or other toxic intermediates may mix in, all of which can affect children's intellectual development and neurological behavior.

In addition, excessive intake of synthetic colorants can cause allergies, such as asthma, laryngeal edema, rhinitis, urticaria, skin itching, and neurogenic headaches; certain artificial synthetic colorants act on the nervous system, affecting the transmission of nerve impulses, leading to a series of symptoms.

It is recommended that parents strictly control their children's diet, allowing them to eat more natural foods, and choose processed foods without any artificial additives when buying them. To avoid the effects of artificial colorants, children should be cautious in their daily diet. When purchasing brightly colored foods or drinks, be careful not to choose overly bright processed foods, and develop the habit of reading food ingredient lists, paying attention to whether cochineal red, tartrazine, and Sudan red, among other synthetic colorants, are added.
