Hawthorne's Religious Views from The Scarlet Letter

by hocvtry on 2012-02-24 11:06:54

Abstract: Nathaniel Hawthorne was an outstanding Romantic novelist of the 19th century in America. His works are imbued with a strong religious color. Due to the complex nature of Puritanism, Hawthorne's attitude towards it is also complex. "The Scarlet Letter" is Hawthorne's masterpiece and profoundly reflects his identification with and doubts about Puritanism: on one hand, he deeply explores human depravity and the path to redemption; on the other hand, he criticizes and exposes the harsh extremity of Puritanism. In terms of the entire work, the latter occupies the main aspect. This article reveals Hawthorne's contradictory and hesitant religious views through discussing the positive and negative impacts of Puritanism on the main characters in "The Scarlet Letter."

Keywords: Hawthorne, "The Scarlet Letter," Puritanism

Introduction

Christianity, which originated in the first century AD, is the most influential religion in the world with the widest spread. In the history of Europe's development, religion has been omnipresent, especially Christianity, whose rich cultural connotations have permeated European and American literature. Christian spirit, as a collective unconsciousness, has deeply rooted itself in the hearts of those who believe in God, guiding their values and ways of thinking, becoming an indispensable existence in the spiritual world. Nathaniel Hawthorne, the outstanding Romantic novelist of the 19th century in America, focused his attention on the profound and broad field of religion. Born into a Puritan family, Hawthorne lost his father at an early age and lived with his widowed mother in his grandfather's house. Transcendentalism had a deep influence on him, and his early intensive study of the Puritan history of New England became the main source of his novel creation. However, Hawthorne was not a thoroughgoing Puritan. He was confused and skeptical about the complexity of Puritanism itself and the social reality of the time. As Hawthorne's masterpiece, "The Scarlet Letter" most typically embodies the contradictory characteristics of his attitude towards Puritanism. This article reveals Hawthorne's contradictory and hesitant religious views through discussing the positive and negative impacts of Puritanism on the main characters in "The Scarlet Letter."

I. Original Sin and Redemption

In the Bible's "Genesis," Adam and Eve's experience is a typical case of moving from sin and fall to repentance and salvation, a process of self-redemption. Adam and Eve violated God's will and embarked on a painful path of repentance. The Bible points out that after experiencing many hardships, by redeeming original sin and doing good while avoiding evil, people will return to Eden after death. Redemption, as one of the tenets of Puritanism, has important value significance. In this aspect, Hawthorne identifies with such Puritan doctrine. In "The Scarlet Letter," this is reflected in the deep exploration of human sinfulness and the principle of faith where inner repentance and behavior lead to redemption. In Hawthorne's view, the act of adultery itself is one aspect, but more importantly, it is the different attitudes individuals take towards sin after the act and the profound impact it has on their souls.

A resolute and extraordinary woman - Hester Prynne, due to committing adultery with the minister Dimmesdale, is condemned by the Puritan church. She is punished by wearing the scarlet letter [A] on her chest and publicly ridiculed and humiliated. The scarlet letter [A] pierced into Hester Prynne's heart, even if "[covering that mark]," the scarlet letter [A] is proof of sin and punishment, representing the embodiment of a woman's evil. To be reborn, Hester Prynne openly admitted her sins and began a long journey of redemption in a secluded cottage on the outskirts with her daughter. In Hawthorne's view, Hester Prynne is guilty; she committed the adultery that society at the time could not tolerate, but more crucially, her sin of deception. She made a deal with Chillingworth: Chillingworth would not ask who the father of the child was, and she would not expose Chillingworth's true identity to the minister. Such means are questionable, even though it was for love. Hester Prynne's initial intention was to protect Minister Dimmesdale, but it brought him greater pain. And she worked hard to compensate for her mistakes with her good deeds, so much so that many women confided in her secrets and sought comfort and advice. "Puritans believed that humans have a natural tendency towards virtue, however, only through some kind of 'training' can people possibly achieve perfection in virtue." Hester Prynne precisely went through this 'training', striving to make up for her sins with her good deeds, "ultimately purifying her soul," and "forging a soul purer and holier than the one she lost." The scarlet letter no longer symbolized shame and crime but became a sign and symbol inspiring spiritual resurrection.
