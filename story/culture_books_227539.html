"Leaders Manage People, Processes Manage Tasks" - Human-oriented Management and Systematic Management

by jqrxass on 2009-08-23 13:10:40

Chinese-style management is essentially a form of relationship-based (renqing) management. This style fosters a flexible and harmonious atmosphere, maximizing the enthusiasm and loyalty of those being managed. However, since it's based on relationships, those being managed can choose not to accept or reciprocate the manager’s favor, thus putting the initiative in the hands of the managed. It is a management model where the manager loses control.

In recent years, Western management ideas and models have become very popular in Chinese business circles. But after implementing various processes and models with great fanfare, managers found that the results were minimal. Further investigation revealed that Western ideas and models do not fit the current state of Chinese management, often leading to cultural incompatibility. Completely abandoning Chinese-style management is both unscientific and unrealistic.

The author of this book has been active in management consulting, observing the management styles of hundreds of small and medium-sized private enterprises, dedicated to solving the problem of how to integrate Eastern and Western management. Instead of adopting vague propositions like "fusion of East and West" or "Chinese essence, Western application," he clearly proposes "separation of people and affairs," "leaders manage people, processes manage affairs," and "vertical management for people, horizontal management for affairs."

"Leaders manage people, processes manage affairs" focuses on separating the company's operations from the leader's authority, managing them through standardized procedural modes. This allows leaders to focus more on issues that cannot be resolved by processes. This approach can reduce costs, avoid mutual interference, and improve efficiency. This model has been tested in nearly 200 companies and proven to be effective.

Preface

On November 16, 2008, I led a group of students from Sun Yat-sen University's CEO class on a trip to Japan to study lean management. Throughout the journey, we deeply felt the connection between management models and cultural habits.

Lean management as a model is not difficult to understand or master. Why then does its application in domestic enterprises perform so poorly? Some companies applying lean management see some improvements in performance, but suffer significant staff turnover, ultimately resulting in a decline in performance. Other companies apply lean management just like they implemented ISO standards before, creating a lot of buzz initially, but eventually giving up, turning it into a face-saving project.

Our examination of Japan, especially Japanese living habits, helped us uncover the root cause of why lean management struggles to take root in domestic enterprises: human nature.

Chapter One: Separation of People and Affairs

Many leaders or business owners like to bundle personnel management and task management together, delegating them to their deputies and saying: "This department, this factory, this workshop is entrusted to you. Help me manage it well."

They also say: "As long as you manage it well for me, I'll give you whatever you want." A boss from Panyu once told me: "Professor Zeng, I'm not highly educated, but I am sincere with people. If someone really treats me well, I'll give him everything." However, he was disappointed. He treated many subordinates well, but no one could manage his factory properly.

Why does the leader's bundling of people and tasks always end in disappointment?

Chapter Two: Leaders Manage People

In the first chapter, we discussed the problems caused by the lack of separation between personnel and task management in enterprise management, such as "good people doing bad things," "strong presence without strong results," "small matters becoming big issues," etc. We proposed the separation of personnel and task management, advocating that leaders manage people while processes manage tasks. We built a basic framework for the division of labor between "bosses managing people," "departments managing tasks horizontally," and "clear boundaries avoiding pitfalls." In this chapter, we will focus on the four key points of leadership in managing people: recognition, belief, habit, and relationship.

Chapter Three: Processes Manage Tasks

In the second chapter, we mainly analyzed the four key points of leadership in managing people, proposing new concepts such as "recognition before management - management stems from recognition," "helping before management - recognition stems from help," "habits determine success or failure," "a good company is a good school," and "persistence is faith." We designed a "Twelve-Stage Theater for Interpersonal Relationship Transformation" for companies, advocating the establishment of a Chinese-style philosophy of managing people centered on recognition, habits, faith, and relationships. In this chapter, we introduce more Western-style management systems, focusing on six methods of process-based task management: limitation selection method, horizontal control method, three-element method, segmental control method, data flow method, and audit control method.

Chapter Four: Traditional Management – No Separation Between Personnel and Tasks

In the second chapter, we discussed many advantages of traditional Chinese-style management, advocating the cultivation of employee recognition of leadership, helping employees develop good habits, establishing positive beliefs among employees and the entire enterprise, and activating employee enthusiasm through various "relationship networks." These are all efforts to explore the positive factors of Chinese traditional culture in management. I believe that to manage people well and lead teams effectively, one must base it on the traditional Chinese humanistic environment and advocate Chinese-style management. However, every management model has its drawbacks. The problem with Chinese-style management is that it only works for managing people. Expanding its scope to managing tasks leads to endless trouble. Therefore, in the first three chapters, I proposed "separation of personnel and tasks," "leaders manage people, processes manage tasks," and "vertical management for people, horizontal management for tasks." In this chapter, we will thoroughly analyze the shortcomings of traditional Chinese-style management and explain why expanding its scope to task management leads to inefficiency and even endless trouble.
