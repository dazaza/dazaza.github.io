Low-carbon business opportunities in the global desert solar energy industry are emerging.

by ditan360 on 2010-07-05 11:28:48

Low Carbon Network News - The global economic crisis has hit many industries hard, but at the same time, the trend towards low-carbonization is nurturing new market opportunities. Among these, renewable energy undoubtedly stands out as a bright spot. While venture capitalists are wary of the potential adverse effects this economic crisis might bring, they certainly will not miss the chance to seize new money-making opportunities.

In fact, a solar land grab has already swept across the desert regions of the southwestern United States. In the vast Mojave Desert plateau west of Nevada, utility companies such as Goldman Sachs, Pacific Gas and Electric Company, and Florida Power & Light Company, emerging enterprises from Silicon Valley, solar companies from Israel and Germany, Chevron Corporation, speculators... all these institutions have rushed to lock down hundreds of thousands of acres of land. This land, which was long considered worthless, has now become the optimal site for building solar power plants.

Although the competition has just begun and it will take several years for the power plants to be completed, the rush to stake claims in the Mojave Desert area is proceeding with great intensity. A year and a half ago, there were only 5 applications for solar power plant land use submitted to the Bureau of Land Management in California's Mojave region. Now, the number of received applications has reached 104, covering nearly one million acres of land, with a theoretical total power generation capacity of 60 gigawatts, compared to California's annual electricity consumption of 33 gigawatts last year.

Due to the large number of buyers, this competition is not limited to federal lands. There is also fierce competition over private properties. According to a recent survey by Frost & Sullivan, an American market consulting company, over the past five years, Western countries' investment in desert areas such as the Sahara and Mojave for solar power development has increased annually by 19.8%, reaching as high as 22% in 2008. Some buyers have even offered up to $10,000 per acre, whereas a similar acre of sandy land only cost $500 a few years ago.

☆ Promising Appreciation Prospects

The global financial crisis, while severely impacting traditional energy industries, is uncovering a batch of emerging energy sectors. Visionaries see the trend of desert solar power generation becoming irreversible due to its enormous expected wealth-creation potential, coupled with mandates from California and neighboring states to promote renewable energy usage within the next few years. This has made this large desert highly sought after. Experts from the Cambridge New Energy Research Center predict that the desert solar industry will form a $45 billion market by 2020.

An American solar investment company had the foresight to submit its first application for land use back in December 2006, followed by another application within a month for over 125,000 acres of land intended for constructing a solar power plant capable of generating 10 gigawatts of power. Many of these sites are close to electric transmission lines connecting the desert areas with coastal cities. Previously, Goldman Sachs had applied for 40,000 acres of land in the desert region of Nevada.

Arizona farmer Ken John earned $45 million from selling his 1,920-acre land to Spain’s Abengoa Solar Company, which plans to build a 280-megawatt power station there. The land was previously valued at only a few hundred thousand dollars. In California's Mojave Desert, the same company spent $30 million to acquire a 3,000-acre piece of land that was sold for only $1.25 million nine years ago.

Massive development of desert solar power generation has faced resistance from American environmentalists, who argue that large-scale solar panels would impact the habitats of important desert tortoises and other endangered species. To minimize adverse side effects, both U.S. federal and state laws require wildlife conservation officials to consider the overall impact of multiple projects when evaluating whether to approve an individual project.

Currently, most power plants carefully designed for the Mojave Desert will rely on solar thermal reaction technology, which uses groups of mirrors to heat liquid, produce steam, and drive the turbine of a generator. The required solar collectors, high-temperature heat transfer media, heat exchange steam generators, and low-pressure turbines are already mature worldwide, offering significant advantages in manufacturing capabilities and investment scale compared to photovoltaic power generation.

Of interest to the industry is the prospect of exporting solar electricity to Europe, which could become the cheapest method of electricity export. Algeria, a North African country, already sends large amounts of oil and natural gas to Europe via pipelines, but its potential solar resources could make it an even larger energy supplier. Moreover, many OPEC member countries, concerned about alternative energy reducing oil demand, are located in vast desert regions. If solar energy is fully utilized, it could become a new avenue for energy exports and foreign exchange earnings.

☆ Sending Africa's Solar Energy to Europe

Back in the late 1970s, people envisioned transporting solar energy from the Sahara region in Africa to Europe, though implementing this so-called "desert technology" plan wouldn't be easy.

As early as 1985, facilities for utilizing solar energy to generate electricity were invented. These facilities, known as solar power plants, use parabolic mirror-like solar panels to collect sunlight heat and convert it into electricity. The advantage of these power plants is that they can be equipped with energy storage devices, allowing them to supply electricity even on days without sunshine. The less sunlight there is, the more storage batteries are needed. However, North Africa and the Middle East have much richer solar resources than Europe. There is also a lot of unused land there. Theoretically speaking, utilizing approximately 2-3% of the desert area could basically ensure Europe's energy supply.

After a two-year examination of the Sahara Desert region, German senior scientist Professor Robert Pitz-Paal concluded that electricity could be produced at extremely low prices by building solar power stations locally, potentially saving two-thirds of the costs. He said, "Currently, the price of solar power generation through conventional means is still higher than that produced through fossil fuels. Clearly, desert solar power offers a new pathway for humans to obtain renewable energy." Pitz-Paal specializes in solar technology and is currently the head of the solar research project under the Institute of Technical Thermodynamics at the German Aerospace Center.

Professor Pitz-Paal stated that although the start-up capital for building a solar power plant is higher than that for an oil or coal-fired power plant, oil or coal-fired plants always need to purchase fuel. Solar power plants do not require additional purchases. This means that when comparing costs, both the cost of building the power plant and the future fuel costs must be taken into account. Although solar power generation technology is still in its infancy with very high R&D investment costs and requires network infrastructure support, its long-term benefits are certain. Pitz-Paal estimates that through "high-voltage direct current transmission technology," power losses during transmission from North African desert areas to Europe can be controlled below 15%.

The German government attaches great importance to this "desert technology." The German Aerospace Center plans to meet 15% of Europe's total demand by 2050 through electricity transmitted from desert areas.

Scientists suggest that Europe should establish a new high-voltage direct current grid to easily and efficiently collect various alternative energies and deliver them throughout Europe. Besides solar farms in North Africa, wind power from the UK, hydropower from Norway, and biomass and geothermal power generated in Central Europe can all be integrated into this grid. Even if Europe does not use any coal, oil, natural gas, or nuclear power, by 2050, the electricity provided by this grid will be sufficient to meet the entire continent's needs. During this period, carbon dioxide emissions from power generation can be reduced by 70%.

☆ Solving the Global Energy Crisis

Three years ago, scientists Dr. Gerard Knies and Dr. Franz Trieb, employed by the German government, pointed out in two recently submitted reports that Europe, the Middle East, and North Africa should collaborate to use concentrated solar power (CSP) technology to construct extensive solar farms in the deserts of North Africa.

The principle of CSP is to use mirrors to focus sunlight onto a pipe or container filled with some gases or liquids, heating them to 400 degrees Celsius, then driving traditional steam turbines to generate electricity. This technology is as simple as using a magnifying glass in physics class to focus sunlight on paper to burn a hole.

Using desert sunlight could solve the global energy crisis, according to two German scientists in their scientific reports submitted to the German government recently. The scientists point out that using a technology called concentrated solar power, covering just 0.5% of the Earth's desert areas with magnifying glasses could meet the world's electricity needs and simultaneously provide abundant desalinated water to desert regions, making air conditioning comfortable for nearby cities.

CSP is not a new technology. As early as 18 years ago, nine concentrated solar power stations were established in the Mojave Desert in California, USA. Later, similar power stations were built in the deserts of Nevada in the USA, southern Spain, Australia, and even Switzerland, all taking full advantage of abundant solar energy.

CSP comes in various forms, but they all share one common feature: using mirrors to focus sunlight onto a pipe or container filled with some gases or liquids, heating them to 400 degrees Celsius, then driving traditional steam turbines to generate electricity. The process involves collecting solar energy, converting it into thermal energy, and then transforming thermal energy into electrical energy.

Experiments have confirmed that the shaded areas created by large mirrors can be used for gardening, with irrigation water being the cooling desalinated water produced by the power station. This cold water can also be used for air conditioning. This means that building a power station not only provides electricity but also supplies fresh water and air conditioning. This form of solar energy utilization has an unparalleled advantage over other forms: the heated water from solar energy can be stored in large containers, continuing to drive steam turbines for several hours after sunset.

What is the cost of CSP power generation? Repeated experiments have confirmed that each square kilometer covered by CSP can annually produce an equivalent of 1.5 million barrels of oil. With current technology, the cost of building a power station is equivalent to $50 per barrel of oil. Moreover, when the production of giant mirrors reaches industrial levels, the cost may drop to an equivalent of $20 per barrel of oil, fully competitive with oil prices.

If solely considering power generation, concentrated solar power cannot yet compete with natural gas, but adding functions like desalination and air conditioning makes CSP cheaper than natural gas. Furthermore, if the function of reducing carbon dioxide emissions is also included, CSP can absolutely compete with natural gas.

Nuclear power currently accounts for 3.1% of global energy supply, and it is difficult to increase the supply in the short term. However, CSP can meet 30% or even 300% of future energy needs, and it is simpler, safer, and lower in cost.

An undeniable fact is that fossil fuels are becoming increasingly scarce and expensive. This trend is strengthening continuously. More worrying for countries is the increasing environmental pollution caused by burning oil and natural gas. Under such circumstances, developing renewable energy becomes an irreplaceable strategic measure for more and more countries, and developing deserts undoubtedly presents a challenging opportunity. Visionaries point out that wise energy investments should be in sun-rich desert areas rather than uranium mines or oil wells.

A typical concentrated solar power (CSP) system usually consists of two parts: collecting solar energy and converting it into thermal energy, then converting thermal energy into electrical energy. Since CSP utilizes large arrays of parabolic or dish-shaped mirrors to collect solar thermal energy, providing steam through a heat exchanger combined with traditional steam turbine generators, avoiding the expensive silicon photovoltaic conversion process, it can significantly reduce the cost of solar power generation. Moreover, this form of solar energy utilization has an unmatched advantage over other forms of solar energy conversion: the heated water from solar energy can be stored in large containers, continuing to drive steam turbines for several hours after sunset.

The oil crisis of the 1970s triggered the U.S. Department of Energy to organize CSP technology research in the mid-1980s. SUNTN Laboratory, jointly formed by Sandia National Laboratories and the National Renewable Energy Laboratory, was responsible for designing commercial demonstration solar power stations. The first nine parabolic trough mirror concentrated solar power stations were successfully built in the California desert, with a total installed capacity of 354 megawatts (million watts). Subsequently, CSP solar power stations were rapidly adopted in various states in the southwestern United States, as well as in Spain, Israel, Switzerland, and other countries.
