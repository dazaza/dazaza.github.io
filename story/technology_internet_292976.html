A sentence of the old beggar (moving)

by yang5230 on 2009-12-04 17:18:50

A while ago, I casually saw an article online. After reading it, I couldn't help but shed tears. So, I'm reposting it here in a more tactful way.

There was a friend who loved to eat spicy tripe. He often dragged me around the streets of Harbin to find restaurants for wild taste testing. Back then, he found one on Jingwei Street. The restaurant wasn't big, and the hygiene conditions were not satisfactory, but the tripe was indeed well-cooked. For a period of time, we often indulged ourselves there.

One afternoon last spring, the two of us sat in this big restaurant drinking and chatting. It wasn't lunchtime, so there were only the two of us regular customers. The small boss of the restaurant also joined us with a beer in hand. It was a lazy noon.

When we ordered the second plate of spicy tripe, an old beggar walked in.

The restaurant is located in a prosperous area, so there are often down-and-out people or fake ones asking for help. We have seen many such cases. This restaurant's boss is quite compassionate. Every time someone asks for help, he would give them some money, regardless of the amount. This day was no exception. Before the old man even opened his mouth, the boss took out a coin and handed it over. However, the old man refused it, muttering that he didn't want money, just leftovers.

This surprised us - he was a real "beggar" who didn't want money! I couldn't help but take a closer look at the old man. He was probably over 80 years old, but still had a strong body and stood straight. His clothes, though worn, were relatively clean, which was uncommon among beggars.

To ask for food in a restaurant was a good choice, but in reality, it wasn't that simple. This small restaurant mainly served regular customers, and any leftovers were directly thrown away behind the scenes. Their food was made fresh to order, and they didn't have leftovers to give to the old man. Obviously, the boss couldn't serve him a fresh meal either, and this seemingly trivial matter became difficult to resolve.

On our table, there was a steamed bun, something we always ordered whenever we came here. I hadn't touched it, and my buddy only took a bite as a habit. The waitress of this restaurant had a trick up her sleeve. After you finish ordering, she would casually ask: "How many plates of steamed buns do you want?" Her tone left no room for refusal, and you would unconsciously choose a number without being able to refuse their family heirloom skill.

My friend also took an interest in this old man, calling the waitress over to bring the steamed bun that the boss was proud of and let the old man eat it at our table. With no outsiders around, the small boss didn't stop the old man from sitting down and even said there was vinegar and mustard on the table if he wanted to use them.

The old man muttered something and pulled out a enamel teacup from his belongings to ask for water. This cup surprised us. On its battered surface, a line of white characters could still be identified - "Dedicated to the dearest person!"

My buddy is a true descendant of a military family; his grandfather was a general who received his rank in 1955.

Seeing this cup in the hands of such an old beggar puzzled us. My friend hesitantly asked the old man where he got the cup.

The old man mumbled: "It's mine, it's mine, it was given to me."

We all felt speechless. My friend said: "Grandpa, come sit with us, let's chat." The old man declined repeatedly.

I got up and helped the old man to our table, and thus began the following conversation:

"Old man, did you serve in the army?"

"Yes, yes, I served for seven years!"

"Where are you from?"

"Jinzhai, Anhui."

"When did you retire?"

"In 1946, the second year after Japan surrendered."

"What unit were you in?"

"The New Fourth Army Sixth Division, known as the Huajia Sixth Division back then."

"Do you remember your division commander?"

"Wang Bicheng, he was really good at fighting!"

The old man spoke vaguely about these things, and both my friend and I fell silent. A veteran from the countryside wouldn't know these gradually forgotten histories. This was a heroic unit in the history of our army - on Mount Menglianggu, Zhang ZhiZhong was defeated by this unit, leading to their victory.

We continued serving the old man food and drink, continuing our conversation... "Did you participate in the Korean War?"

"Yes, yes, the American planes were fierce. I was injured in Chosin Reservoir and discharged because of that."

"But if you served seven years, shouldn't you be a cadre? Why were you discharged?"

"I didn't have enough education to become a cadre."

Seeing our skeptical expressions, the old man quickly fumbled in his bosom and pulled out a carefully wrapped small cloth package. Inside were two red plastic-covered certificates, one was a discharge certificate, and the other was a second-class disabled veteran certificate. Slowly, he rolled up his right trouser leg, revealing a wooden leg.

In the package, my friend also found a carefully folded piece of paper, which he unfolded and read before passing it to me silently.

It was an introduction letter from the village committee, roughly stating that the bearer of this letter was a discharged disabled veteran from our village, with children but unable to work due to disability. Because of the financial difficulties of the original village, they were unable to support him, so he was allowed to seek alms outside, hoping for assistance from local governments.

The seal of the village committee was prominently visible. We were all shocked by this reality, and the small restaurant owner was dumbfounded. After a long pause, he stammered to the old man: "Old sir, whenever you need to eat, just come here. As long as my restaurant is open, you..."

The old man kept refusing, saying he could still walk and didn't want to bother anyone. He said Northeasterners were good. When he was young, he knew Northeasterners were kind.

I curiously asked the old man why he didn't accept money during his begging journey. Suddenly, the old man stared at me and said: "I served in the army for seven years, and I am still a party member. How can I...?"

At that moment, I was moved to tears...

After reading this, I felt heavy-hearted. I don't know what to say, I just hope that I can post this story on forums across the country! Let more people know that there are still such people in our country today! I have nothing to say, the only thing I can do is to repost it tactfully.
