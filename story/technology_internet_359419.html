International Fund for Animal Welfare: "Sitting Mountain Carver" returns to the mountains

by ditan360 on 2010-06-29 12:03:37

Lowcarbon.org Exclusive Today, the International Fund for Animal Welfare Beijing Raptor Rescue Center (IFAW BRRC) released two vultures in Dazhuangke Township of Yanqing County. One vulture was successfully released into the wild. The other vulture, which had been illegally kept by humans for a long time, still could not adapt to the wild environment and was brought back to the center for further rehabilitation treatment. The vulture, commonly known as the "Zuoshandiao," is a second-level protected animal in China and also the largest bird of prey that can be seen in the Beijing area.

On January 17, 2010, Mr. Deng from Longwan Tun in Shunyi District found a large bird at the village entrance. According to Mr. Deng's recollection, the condition of this large bird was very poor, and it couldn't fly. Therefore, he took the bird home, fed it some meat, and contacted the raptor center for treatment. This vulture was extremely weak, suffering from diarrhea and loss of appetite. After a veterinary examination, it was discovered that due to bacterial infection, most of its toe tips and claws had fallen off. The center devised a detailed rehabilitation plan for it. After five months of careful treatment, the current state of this vulture is good, physically strong, and ready to return to nature.

On March 6 of the same year, the IFAW Beijing Raptor Rescue Center received another vulture from the Fengtai Forest Public Security Bureau. Compared to the vulture received on January 17, its physical condition was much better, with only some external injuries on the wrist joint. However, its behavior was abnormal, which worried the raptor rehabilitators: this vulture was "friendly" and "close" to humans but exhibited more aggressive behavior towards its own kind. Zhang Guanghong of the Forest Public Security Bureau said that this vulture had been illegally kept by humans for a long period.

Shang Yugang, a raptor rehabilitator at IFAW BRRC, said: "Raptors with behavioral problems will encounter many issues and harm in the wild. For example, they might seek food from humans instead of hunting themselves, thus possibly being indirectly harmed by humans; during the breeding season, there’s a possibility it might court what it perceives as its own kind. Regarding the behavioral problems of this vulture, we usually reduce our contact with it and place it with two other healthy vultures in the largest outdoor cage at the center. Some of its bad behaviors have gradually been corrected, and its flying and physical conditions are good. Today, we hope to release them in the same habitat so it can continue learning survival skills in the wild from another healthy vulture. Unfortunately, raptors that should be freely soaring in the blue sky are instead unfamiliar with nature. The best way is to bring it back to the center, allowing it to learn from another vulture currently under rehabilitation at the center and strengthen its flight training. We hope it can return to nature as soon as possible!"

The Beijing area is a habitat for many birds of prey and an important stopover on their migration routes. However, due to the reduction of natural habitats and the acceleration of urbanization, many habitats where birds of prey depend on have disappeared, leading to insufficient food supply for these birds. If they encounter adverse weather, their migration will face greater challenges, possibly resulting in malnutrition, weakness, and dehydration, making them unable to continue flying.

Hua Ning, the director of IFAW China Programs, said: "We are very glad that many citizens can promptly contact the center when encountering injured birds of prey, providing assistance. However, some people illegally keep and 'pamper' birds of prey, severely harming their bodies while also depriving them of the chance to return to nature." According to recent statistics conducted by IFAW BRRC, 29% of the raptors treated at the center were caused by illegal human feeding.

"When the vulture spreads its huge wings and soars toward the blue sky, I feel like I am soaring along with it. I hope everyone can protect animals. Do not raise, do not sell, do not consume!" said volunteer Liu Bin who participated in the release.

Birds of prey are at the top of the food chain and occupy an important position in the ecosystem. They play an irreplaceable role in controlling the number of rodents, maintaining environmental health, and ecological balance. Since its establishment in 2001, IFAW BRRC has received over 2,900 raptors. More than 60% of the raptors have successfully returned to nature.

About the International Fund for Animal Welfare

As one of the world's most influential animal welfare organizations, the International Fund for Animal Welfare (IFAW) has offices in 16 countries and operates animal protection projects in more than 40 countries. Headquartered in the United States, IFAW improves animal welfare by reducing commercial exploitation of animals, protecting wildlife habitats, and rescuing animals in distress. IFAW protects both wild and companion animals through practical rescue work and collaboration with governments. IFAW mobilizes the public to prevent animal abuse through education and publicity, actively promoting animal welfare and protection policies that benefit both humans and animals, achieving harmonious coexistence.

About the International Fund for Animal Welfare Beijing Raptor Rescue Center

The International Fund for Animal Welfare Beijing Raptor Rescue Center (International Fund for Animal Welfare Beijing Raptor Rescue Center - www.brrc.org.cn, abbreviated as IFAW BRRC) is located on the campus of Beijing Normal University, covering more than 1,200 square meters, integrating multiple functions such as raptor rescue, treatment, rehabilitation, management, and education. IFAW BRRC is one of the many animal protection projects of the International Fund for Animal Welfare (IFAW - www.ifaw.org.cn, abbreviated as IFAW) in China. Established in December 2001, IFAW BRRC is a non-profit wildlife rescue organization jointly established by IFAW, Beijing Normal University, and the Beijing Municipal Wildlife Nature Reserve Management Station, designated as the "Specialized Raptor Rescue Center" by the Beijing Forestry Bureau.

IFAW promotes and improves the welfare of wild and companion animals worldwide by protecting wildlife habitats, reducing commercial exploitation and utilization of wildlife, and providing rescue for animals in distress. With the increasing awareness of wildlife protection among the Chinese public, more and more people are beginning to participate in wildlife rescue actions. To help wildlife protection law enforcement agencies solve the proper treatment, rehabilitation, and release of rescued animals into the wild, IFAW BRRC combines international advanced raptor rescue technology and experience with scientific professional rescue methods, providing treatment, care, and rehabilitation training for injured, sick, lost raptors, and those confiscated during law enforcement processes in Beijing and surrounding areas, releasing the recovered raptors in suitable wild habitats in a timely manner.

The mission of IFAW BRRC is to introduce internationally recognized rescue concepts and standards, mature experiences, and advanced technologies to China's nascent wildlife rescue efforts. The rescue model explored by IFAW BRRC combining successful foreign experiences with China's national conditions helps improve the recovery rate of treated raptors, assists China's wild bird rescue efforts to align with international standards, and accumulates valuable experience for the scientific rescue of China's wildlife. By October 2009, nearly three thousand raptors had been received and treated by IFAW BRRC, with more than half successfully returning to nature.
