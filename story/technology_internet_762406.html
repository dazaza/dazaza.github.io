Dianping: A Bone Too Tough for the Giants to Chew, Living Outside the Comfort Zone

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 15:18:23

Zhang Tao, CEO of Dianping.com

Written by Yuan Yin, edited by Yang Jing, photographed by Xiao Nan

Zhang Tao, the CEO of Dianping.com, has postponed the company's IPO once again.

In 2011, Dianping secured a $100 million Series C funding round from Sequoia Capital and others, valuing the company at $1 billion. By convention, it should have gone public within one to two years after that, but it didn't. Instead, in 2012, it raised another $60 million in a Series D round.

With its scale and development momentum, going public would not be difficult for Dianping. However, Zhang Tao feels that an IPO now would be too costly. He breaks down the internet (PC + mobile) into four elements: people, information, goods, and services. Each connection point offers opportunities for giants to emerge. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, and Alibaba connects people with goods. The space between people and services is where the fourth giant could reside. Since 2003, Dianping has been diligently accumulating data, finally waiting for the explosion of mobile internet, which connects location and service through smartphones. According to Zhang Tao's logic, no company seems more fitting than Dianping for the image of the fourth giant.

However, the outside world doesn't see it that way. In recent months, with frequent acquisitions by BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent), Dianping has become a hypothetical acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition target; regardless of which letter of BAT it takes on as a surname, it could help them achieve great success early. On the other hand, Dianping's size pales in comparison to BAT, and it's still too early to declare it a future giant. Moreover, whether it's Tencent WeChat's public platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after investing in AutoNavi and Meituan, they all directly target Dianping's territory - local life services centered around dining, drinking, entertainment, and leisure.

Before proving the following two points, going public for Dianping would be akin to "selling at a discount." First, Zhang Tao needs time to prove that what Dianping is doing now cannot be done by BAT. Second, Dianping is about to enter the final battle of group buying. If it secures the top spot, its valuation could increase several times over.

2013 is Dianping's "year of decisive battles." Zhang Tao ignores speculation and rumors, stating clearly: "The internet era is indeed entering a great fusion age, but Dianping will not be acquired."

Zhang Tao categorizes companies that might be acquired into three types. The first type has platform attributes but is better suited to develop within a larger ecosystem, such as maps and email. These products can easily attract large numbers of users but struggle to find independent business models. Others, like browsers, may generate revenue, but the money earned through navigation services is far less than their strategic value to large companies. "Big companies are willing to invest in email products because they help with brand and user retention, but I've never heard of any standalone email company succeeding." The second type involves industry consolidation, like in the video industry, where acquisitions expand market share. The third type is when a company faces financial difficulties and sells itself to survive.

Now Meituan has been brought under Alibaba's wing. Theoretically, Dianping should quickly seek a "backer" to avoid being defeated due to a rival's capital-fueled rapid expansion. Zhang Tao, however, says, "The big players offer us limited help. We do need users, but execution is more important. Acquisitions would reduce our execution capabilities and we risk being marginalized by a large company." Furthermore, he aims to maintain the most precise "distance" from the giants. Getting too close to any one of them would equate to "taking sides," making him an enemy to the others.

Thus, logically, acquiring Dianping becomes a dead end. Zhang Tao counters, "Why should we take their money? We're not short of funds; what we need are resources. If a major player is willing to take a small stake and provide us with many resources, I might consider it. But would they? Would a major player give you resources if they only hold a small stake?"

Weapon One: Dirty and Hard Work

In the mobile internet space, opportunities for pure internet product companies are becoming fewer. New opportunities lie in those that combine with traditional industries. Zhang Tao believes that lifestyle services mainly revolve around dining, drinking, entertainment, and leisure. Other niche markets, like ride-hailing, can form decent business models, but few have the potential to grow into giants.

For dining, drinking, entertainment, and leisure, user needs are simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is the core of lifestyle services, which is where Dianping started. Whether in content, member accumulation, or branding, newcomers find it hard to surpass Dianping.

The most representative product form of discounts is group buying. Now group buying has entered the championship round, and Dianping plans to face off against Meituan in 2013. This is a winner-takes-all market; as long as there are two strong players, the industry's gross profit margin will be pulled down, preventing stable profitability.

Another foreseeable future is reservations. Reservations can form a true online-to-offline closed loop, but this must be based on Dianping having an absolute advantage in both information and discounts. Otherwise, creating a closed loop is difficult. In 2012, Dianping began experimenting with reservations. Zhang Tao said, "Currently, both users and merchants are giving positive feedback, but if we were to focus solely on reservations, it would be hard to solve the chicken-or-egg problem."

Of course, lifestyle services present some even "heavier" opportunities. For example, food delivery is a rigid demand for users and helps merchants increase sales. In the past couple of years, companies like ToHome Food have flourished. However, the core of food delivery is logistics – delivering within a very short time frame while addressing issues like soups spilling. The logistics requirements are higher than those of fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang Tao said, "If food delivery is executed well, there is opportunity, but it's harder dirty work. What Dianping is doing now is already dirty and labor-intensive enough."

If judged by age, Dianping is already an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has painstakingly accumulated merchant profiles and user reviews, which Zhang Tao calls "dirty and hard work." This primitive growth model once limited its speed, but the pain of the past has become Dianping's core barrier today. It can be said that currently, apart from Meituan, Dianping has no real competitors in the lifestyle services field.

Nevertheless, he still needs to be wary of BAT's "clutches." Baidu's involvement is the most direct. Baidu Maps already has over 100 million users, who can use the map to search for nearby restaurants and other lifestyle services. Tencent, considered to have secured its ticket to the mobile internet era, launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest physical goods trading platform, and expanding into services is a natural progression. Moreover, it has incorporated Meituan and AutoNavi into its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there are no signs of explosive growth in terms of actual usage and data," Zhang Tao said. Brand positioning is crucial; the key is which product users turn to first when they want to dine, drink, entertain, or relax.

Moreover, in the vast field of lifestyle services, competition among the giants is bound to overlap, making it impossible for anyone to completely monopolize the sector. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained through Weibo and WeChat. Zhang Tao feels that this makes Dianping safer. "In niche areas like car rentals and business travel, the massive user base directed by the giants could easily overwhelm existing companies."

As for Alibaba, which has invested in AutoNavi, invading Dianping's territory isn't that simple either. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, which can be categorized no matter how peculiar. A diner's experience depends on various factors such as the day's environment, waitstaff, and chef. Managing merchants cannot rely on systems and tools like Alibaba does; it requires a 'ground force' managed by people.

"All three giants want to go down this path, and there is room for imagination, but it's not a highway; it's a rugged mountain road," Zhang Tao believes. First, providing lifestyle services requires ground teams deployed at various levels, which BAT lacks the genes and ability to manage offline teams. Second, they lack the willingness; lifestyle services require focused effort, not something that can be accomplished by sending out a small team, and profits come slowly. "A company finds it hard to excel at two important things simultaneously, let alone needing to consolidate its advantages in the original field."

Weapon Two: Small Boats Turn Easily

When Dianping secured $100 million in financing in 2011, Zhang Tao told the outside world that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, times change quickly, and before Dianping went public, his analogies had fallen from grace.

He wasn't overly anxious about it. "They failed to become big companies because they lacked offline teams; now they want to be the American version of Dianping."

The current path is one Zhang Tao has explored step by step. When he realized that selling keyword ads and coupons was outdated, the emergence of group buying caught his eye. Initially, like the Americans, he viewed group buying as a marketing platform, offering one deal per day. However, as orders increased, he discovered that group buying had characteristics of a trading platform. Thus, he envisioned Dianping as a lifestyle services trading platform, and the two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were used precisely for Dianping's transition from an information platform to a trading platform.

Without this transformation, Zhang Tao might not have had the confidence to vie for the fourth seat among the giants. However, the transformation sounds simple but is equivalent to Dianping's second entrepreneurship, a process fraught with immense pain.

First, compared to selling advertisements separately, trading platforms have lower gross margins and require stricter cost control and efficiency. Second, trading platforms demand higher cross-departmental communication skills.

To maintain Dianping's impartiality and independence as an information platform, the content team and sales team were previously entirely separate. Zhang Tao even deliberately established a "firewall" between departments – even important clients couldn't alter information on the platform for sales purposes, leading to some mutual animosity between content and sales teams. Now, as a trading platform, editors and sales teams need deep communication to create good marketing plans, requiring editorial cooperation beyond just planning. He said, "Maintaining information quality remains crucial, but there's no need to reinforce enmity anymore; we need to dismantle the firewall."

Though editors can logically accept this, individuals' behaviors and values have inertia, and combined across the entire company, that inertia becomes even greater. Zhang Tao specially convened several meetings to encourage more communication between departments. Months later, an editor manager casually told a sales manager, "I used to think you were my enemy, but now I realize you're my colleague."

The sales department also needed transformation. To promote conversion, Zhang Tao cleverly gave the offline team dual identities. First, for merchants, salespeople should become marketing consultants, not just selling products but helping merchants devise marketing strategies. Second, for the company, marketing consultants are the company's buyers. Dianping is like a supermarket; not only must the product quality be good, but the variety must also be rich. Marketing consultants should aim to help users purchase group-buying products. Of course, dual identities inevitably lead to a series of complex problems. Frontline personnel find this quite challenging, as they must weigh the balance themselves.

Substantially, Dianping's "dual identity" resembles Alibaba's division into 25 business units. Decentralizing decision-making to frontline staff allows for faster response times but increases management difficulty. Similarly, compared to frontline employees, Dianping's management team suffered more during the transformation.

Each year, Zhang Tao breaks down Dianping's corporate goals into various departments, then executives further decompose them layer by layer. However, decomposition is not a simple addition or subtraction. Li Jing, co-founder of Dianping responsible for the sales team, sometimes finds that unresolved merchant demands stem from sales issues, affecting performance. Therefore, Li Jing introduced a new position: communication specialist, tasked with daily gathering information from marketing consultants, submitting it to other departments, and tracking feedback. He often tells his team, "Being adept at embracing change doesn't mean liking change. No one likes change; everyone feels uncomfortable when facing it. All employees must undergo discomfort zones, including me."

The figures reflect the weight of Dianping's execution team. Out of over 3,000 employees, more than 2,000 work offline. The ground force communicates directly with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group buying, and bundled marketing solutions.

For this core force that resists BAT's reach, Dianping has a unique management evaluation system.

They are not called "sales"; their business cards have always been labeled "marketing planning consultant" since day one. Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the era of watching news and chatting on QQ, lagging behind Taobao merchants by at least three to five years in understanding the internet. Marketing planning consultants need to inform merchants of online users' needs and how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing said, "Even for a high-end restaurant, the marketing advisor wouldn't suggest a group buy of abalone or shark fin, possibly switching to other dishes, because online users differ from offline ones."

An excellent marketing advisor must be a versatile player. They need to explain to restaurant owners and barbershop owners the essential differences between discounts and group buying; which kind of discount aligns better with their positioning. If dealing with a hot pot restaurant owner, the marketing advisor must know whether it's the off-season or peak season, which products' costs rise during certain seasons, and which decrease, proposing a group-buying menu that both merchants and users prefer.

This imposes high requirements on marketing advisors, who must not only be experts in internet marketing but also industry experts. Li Jing said, "I must admit, it's impossible for every advisor to be a double expert, so we set star ratings for advisors." Like hotels, marketing advisors range from one-star to five-star, with the best being called Super Stars.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline employees aren't tech-savvy graduates from prestigious universities, making it difficult for them to understand internet products. Dianping established a knowledge base for marketing advisors, containing knowledge points and relevant cases, accessible to both new and experienced employees. If encountering a new issue, employees can ask questions in the knowledge base, and thousands of consultants nationwide will help answer them.

A significant portion of the knowledge points in the knowledge base comes from employees summarizing their practical experiences. Dianping set up a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, rewarding employees who help others the most each quarter. Every quarter, Dianping also conducts skill assessments for employees, with results directly tied to promotions.

Industry knowledge is harder to acquire than internet knowledge. Some experiences are summarized by traditional merchants after decades of industry experience. A Super Star marketing advisor told Li Jing that hair salons should avoid group buys for wash-cut-blow packages, as men have little motivation to buy them, and women mostly blow-dry their hair without washing or cutting. Li Jing was astonished, "We men would never think of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing advisors are divided not only by city but also by industry, such as catering, beauty, and leisure. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is further subdivided into hot pot, Western cuisine, and buffets, with one marketing advisor responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing advisors can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, ensuring merchants don't reject the sale of products," he said.

Each marketing group holds its own case-sharing session: a specific type of merchant encounters difficulties, what marketing needs exist, which products from the company were used to formulate the solution, and what the outcome was. Outstanding cases are reported upwards, and Dianping officially shares one case per week.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition called "Entrepreneurial Winner." Within the first three months of joining, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products but listening to merchants sharing their experiences. If responsible for hot pot restaurants, after three months, they must submit a commercial plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hot Pot Restaurant Owner." Like graduation, employees defend their plans on-site, with industry supervisors evaluating the winners, and the company providing rewards.

Strategically, whether it's Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, the tasks to accomplish are quite similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O are familiar to anyone with a basic understanding of the industry, and their statements sound alike. "The era where storytelling and concept creation alone led to success is over. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model lies in management and execution, not strategy," Zhang Tao stated.
