North Korea's tablets look pretty good.

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 19:49:54

Previously, there were reports about North Korea designing its own tablet that still couldn't connect to the internet. Given the lack of hope for North Korea's technological advancements, this didn't seem surprising. However, recently, The Wall Street Journal reported that the Samjiyon tablet actually functions quite well.

A Mr. Frank, who owns an iPad 4 and a Nexus 7, tried out the North Korean Samjiyon tablet and gave it a positive review, even writing a very detailed review (a 16-page PDF).

Some interesting details include the fact that this Android tablet comes pre-installed with a large number of applications—488 in total—including games like Angry Birds, a full-featured MS Office application suite (Microsoft has not commented on this), a PDF reader, music player, voice recognition, dictionary, encyclopedia, e-books (including works by Kim Il-sung, as well as classics by Dickens, Hugo, and Balzac).

In summary, Frank evaluated that this tablet is "extremely useful" and "quite impressive" for North Koreans. However, it still cannot connect to the internet; reportedly, it can only connect to North Korea's internal network.

The Verge summarized:

North Korea is famous for its overly oppressive politics and laughably inept space program, but new reports suggest that the country is capable of producing tablets that are surprisingly good. A product called Samjiyon has been showcased at Asian trade shows for a year now, drawing attention from The New York Times and The Wall Street Journal. The specifications of this tablet aren't as bad as you might imagine. Notably, it also has apps like Angry Birds, though they experience some lag. Overall, it still runs fairly well.

What makes North Korea's tablet unique is that it includes a simulated TV signal receiver and is preloaded with a revolutionary history, which reviewers consider a very useful search tool.

[island via WSJ & The Verge]

Of course, this tablet still has many drawbacks. Due to North Korea's extremely strict internet censorship, this tablet cannot connect to the internet—it relies on the country’s self-controlled local area network, which provides very limited information and makes it difficult for reviewers to comment. At least for now, Samsung doesn't have to worry about competition from its northern neighbor.
