Weishan's $2 billion claim against Apple could end up being a futile effort

by jinyi268 on 2012-02-20 15:09:05

Claiming $2 billion in damages from Apple, Wei Guan may end up achieving nothing.

The conflict between Apple and Wei Guan mainly focuses on whether the trademark transfer involved the mainland region.

If Apple refuses to settle, and the iPad is renamed, Wei Guan may "end up achieving nothing."

"Apple has made its iPad product perfect, loved by users around the world, but this is not a reason for Apple to enter the Chinese market without obtaining the trademark," said Yang Rongshan, founder of the Wei Guan Group at a press conference. He frankly admitted that Wei Guan "feels wronged" and stated that the circulating claim of 10 billion yuan in damages was not Wei Guan's demand, but merely the opinion of professionals.

The iPad was a product developed by Wei Guan starting in the second half of 1998, with the full name being Internet Personal Access Device, positioned as a network terminal device. Wei Guan invested over 300 million US dollars in this product.

Wei Guan's iPad product is similar to a small desktop computer, officially released in 2000. In the same year, Wistron Taipei under Wei Guan International registered the iPad trademark in multiple countries and regions. In 2001, Shenzhen Wei Guan registered the iPad trademark in mainland China.

In 2009, Apple established a company named IP Application Development in the UK (hereinafter referred to as the "UK IP Company") and approached Wistron Taipei, hoping to purchase the trademark. Due to the financial crisis Wei Guan was facing at the time, they planned to contract their operations and thus agreed to complete the transaction for 35,000 pounds.

In the view of Zhao Zhanling, chief legal advisor of China Network Law, Wei Guan's lawsuit in the US is more of a business strategy, aimed not at reclaiming the global trademark, but at exerting greater pressure on Apple within the US.

Currently, Wei Guan's assets and trademarks have been sealed by eight banks, and these eight banks have decided not to let Wei Guan go bankrupt, but instead to continuously recover debts. For Wei Guan, which has almost no way out, the most valuable asset might be the iPad trademark rights in China.

The main conflict between Apple and Wei Guan centers on whether the trademark transfer back then involved the mainland region. Wei Guan claims that the fact that the iPad trademark in China was not sold to Apple was once acknowledged by Apple itself, and both parties engaged in long-term transfer negotiations; after the eight banks sealed Wei Guan's Shenzhen company in August 2010, Apple even sent lawyers to negotiate with the eight banks for a paid transfer of the trademark, but stopped recognizing it after Apple forcibly used the iPad trademark.

Currently, nationwide, there are investigations into the infringement of the iPad trademark by Apple dealers in more than 40 large and medium-sized cities, and the iPad product has been removed from shelves in many places across the country.

However, Wei Guan said it still hopes to reconcile with Apple. The eight banks believe that if Apple is willing to pay 400 million US dollars in one lump sum, the eight creditor banks will completely abandon all litigation. If Apple thinks the price should be lower, both sides need to negotiate to resolve the issue.

But in the eyes of industry insiders, whether it was the previous 10 billion or now 400 million, Wei Guan's behavior is "exorbitant." Previously, Hanwang had also registered the "i-phone" trademark in China before Apple. After Apple launched the iPhone in 2007 and realized the potential risk of infringement, it signed a transfer agreement with Hanwang in 2009, acquiring all "iPhone" related trademark copyrights for 3.65 million US dollars. Now, if Apple refuses to settle and renames the iPad, Wei Guan may "end up achieving nothing." We will continue to follow the developments.
