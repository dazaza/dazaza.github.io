Microsoft Windows Product Line Restructuring Imminent

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 12:41:49

Beijing Time, July 20th - Microsoft is now in quite a predicament, and they must face this harsh reality. Recently, analysts have suggested that it's time for Microsoft to restructure its Windows product line. Instead of allowing competitors to gradually eat away at their market share, Microsoft should strategically retreat and reorganize the Windows product line to prepare for a comeback. Analysts also mentioned that Apple had faced similar issues in the past, and Microsoft should learn from Apple's successful comeback strategy.

Market research firm IDC analyst Al Gillen stated: "For Microsoft, restructuring the Windows product line will be an extremely difficult decision. It's hard to get Microsoft to accept the fact that times have changed, and the old ways can no longer conquer the market as before."

This transformation not only implies corporate restructuring but also requires Microsoft to embrace a very radical strategy—completely stepping away from the operating system that has been so profitable over the last few decades, and aggressively moving into the lucrative 'services and devices' market.

Corporate Restructuring

As part of the restructuring, Microsoft will consolidate all its Windows clients, including Windows 8, Windows RT, Windows Phone 8, Windows Embedded, and Xbox, into one engineering team. This team will be personally led by Tony Myerson, head of the Windows Phone department. The Windows Phone falls under Microsoft's declining Entertainment and Devices Division.

Gillen described this restructuring as "all under one roof": "The desktop client and mobile client of Windows share many similarities, and their integration can produce significant synergies." Like other analysts, Gillen also believes that the marriage between Microsoft's Windows client and Tony Myerson will lead to a universal codebase platform, meaning apps developed by developers can run not only on Windows 8 with the Modern user interface but also perfectly compatible with Windows Phone devices.

Gillen said: "There is still one question facing Microsoft - is the combination of the operating system engineering team and the client team feasible and trustworthy? I believe Microsoft will make the right decision."

These major decisions may be bitter medicine for Microsoft, and whether the results are good or bad depends not only on the 'Windows' brand name but also on the influence of the 'Microsoft' brand.

To ensure a smooth landing of these sudden changes, Microsoft may have to abandon the high-margin former and focus on the latter.

Gillen said: "This may mean that Microsoft needs to reallocate personnel and scale within each business unit by selling low-cost, low-margin products." "Based on past experience, Microsoft is highly resistant to internal restructuring, so the difficulty of this restructuring can be imagined," Gillen added.

Shifting to Terminal Device Market

Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer hinted at the necessity of restructuring in a memo last week and instilled the concept of a 'family of devices' among employees. In the memo, Ballmer downgraded Windows to a 'shell,' saying that Windows needs to submit to terminal devices.

If so, if Microsoft wants to gain a sufficient share of the smartphone and tablet markets like it did with operating systems, it must either produce itself or collaborate with OEM manufacturers to produce what consumers and businesses want.

Unfortunately, up to now, Microsoft has not achieved this.

IDC believes that if Microsoft wants to dominate the terminal device market, it must sacrifice on pricing and develop relatively inexpensive terminal devices, making profits through strategies such as thin margins but high volumes, along with Windows licenses.

If this move fails, it would mean that competitors Apple and Google will continue to erode Microsoft's PC market by selling more smartphones and tablets—a process known as 'cannibalization.' If successful, Microsoft will initiate a 'virtuous cycle' mode, becoming stronger and bigger.

Apple's success in the smartphone sector exemplifies this. Through the sale of iPhones, Apple created a niche market for developers—mobile apps. These developers have created the largest mobile app ecosystem for Apple, leading more people to buy more iPhones.

Rewriting Apple's Path

Ballmer didn't mention specifically what Microsoft's next step would be, but he was very optimistic about the challenges brought by restructuring: "To be honest, software and hardware are completely different things. Making software basically doesn't cost much; we excel at it. But making hardware does come with costs, and we lack experience. However, even in hardware, we can do as well as we do in software."

He has a ready-made example to learn from: Apple.

The sales volume of Apple products cannot be compared to Microsoft's—Apple sold only 4 million Macs in the first quarter of this year, while Microsoft sold 77 million PCs through OEM manufacturers in three months, nearly 20 times as much. Nevertheless, Apple excels in cannibalization (where similar products eat into each other's market share).

In a summary meeting in January, Apple CEO Tim Cook said: "Cannibalization is a double-edged sword, but I see it as an opportunity for us. Apple never fears cannibalization. If we were worried about it and didn't develop other similar products, then that market would be taken by other competitors. That's why we don't fear cannibalization. We know that the iPad will take some of the iPhone's market share, but we're not worried about it."

Gillen believes: "Compared to tablets or smartphones, operating systems are a completely different concept. Perhaps users don't even know what operating system is installed on their tablets or phones, but they can easily distinguish between an iPhone and a Galaxy."

Gillen also thinks: "It's very difficult for Microsoft to completely abandon the deeply ingrained 'Windows' brand and shift to the relatively new field of 'services and devices.' Microsoft knows that creating a brand as meaningful as 'software to Windows' in other fields is not easy."

However, regardless, this is a step that Microsoft must take. "Microsoft needs to always be ready to conquer new markets instead of clinging to the old ones. One day, in new fields, the 'Microsoft' brand will be more influential than 'Windows'."
