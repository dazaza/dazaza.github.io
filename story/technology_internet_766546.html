Here's what Robert Brunner has to say about iOS 7 and the future of Apple...

by anonymous on 2013-09-18 21:53:15

Designer Robert Brunner on the difficulties of making hardware and the future of Apple

Robert Brunner, former director of industrial design at Apple (now founder of Ammunition Design Group and designer of Beats headphones). He collaborates with many companies that are new to hardware product development — whether it's launching the first headphone line for Beats or developing the Nook tablet with Barnes & Noble. Now, let’s hear his advice for newcomers stepping into the hardware industry.

About Hardware Design

“We’ve worked with many companies that have never ventured into hardware manufacturing before. First, we need to understand their strengths — this could be their audience, technological capabilities, or brand influence, etc.; then, we start to understand what kind of company they are and how their hardware performs. Therefore, we focus on their business operations, their goals, and their expectations for hardware.”

Lessons Learned from Launching Hardware Products

On this point, Brunner speaks confidently. “When people hear 'hardware,' their first reaction is 'hard' (difficult). Many young and inexperienced companies naively think, 'This thing is cool, let’s make it.' In reality, you need to know what you want to do and how to do it. So, we design plans and strategies for such companies, ensuring their foundation for success in the early stages. For a company, the most important thing isn’t capital, but having a great idea that can clearly see itself and knows how to execute. What we do is bring good products to market.”

What Do Companies Overlook Before Making Hardware?

The challenges of making hardware include high time and financial costs; furthermore, the further along you get, the harder it becomes to make changes and adjustments. Therefore, we need to do a lot of groundwork upfront: clarify product content, test, beautify, and finally confirm. Subsequent promotion is also a complex process, which, while consuming a lot of manpower and resources, is an entirely different aspect.

What Are the Future Trends in Consumer Electronics?

Technology is no longer magic; people have become accustomed to using it as a means of communication and convenience in life. Many people talk about app software, and now it’s time for us to step back and seriously consider hardware technology, some real physical interactions: their experiences, and how they interact with our bodies.

About iOS 7 Flat Design and Apple’s Position in the Design World

Brunner served as Apple’s director of industrial design for many years. He occasionally jokes like this: “If I die, I’d inscribe these words on my tombstone: ‘Here lies the guy who once hired Jonathan Ive.’ I founded a company called Lunar, and Ive came for an interview, that’s how we met. My first impression of him was that he was a polite young British man. The models he designed were not only visually impressive, but he truly achieved Form follows function. After interacting with him, I knew: reliable, this is exactly the person my team needed.”

He further interprets the intent behind iOS 7's flat design and analyzes the obstacles Apple faces in continuing to lead in design.

The simpler, clearer interface of iOS 7 is not a new idea; what’s "new" is how we look at it with a more mature perspective. Users seek novel stimulation, but they don't necessarily care about its style. People seem to have been hijacked by an overwhelming amount of information, including images, stories, ideas, etc., so we hope to eliminate these noises, flatten everything, and figure out what's really most important. There has been too much "noise" design in the past five years of design.

In the past few decades, especially in the last ten years, Apple has had a significant impact on the design world. It understands the needs of every user. Therefore, I believe it will always be a leader in the design world, and it's hard to find another company that drives design based on user experience. This largely depends on a company’s culture, which is difficult to establish and even harder to replicate.

However, Apple's biggest challenge comes from its own success. When you're successful enough, it becomes difficult to surpass yourself. People have higher expectations for Apple, and users' appetites become increasingly harder to satisfy. As everyone knows, design isn't just about the appearance of a product, but more about its functions, operation, and how it positively integrates into life. Other companies are learning and imitating, causing boundaries between products to blur and distances to shorten.

In fact, over the next three years, I’m looking forward to seeing how Apple will change. If you carefully review Apple’s products over the years, you'll find that they are exquisitely crafted and highly operational. Despite this, the surprises Apple brings us have diminished. In the coming era, if Apple wants to maintain its leadership position, it may need to change its model.
