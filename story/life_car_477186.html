The Premiums for Red and Black Car Models Are Different: Revealing Four Underlying Rules of the Auto Insurance Industry

Recently, many private car owners have reported encountering various kinds of unfair "underlying rules" while insuring, renewing insurance, or making claims for their beloved cars. As everyone knows, auto insurance is an industry filled with "B doors" and has always been known for its complexity in the minds of car owners. So, as ordinary car owners who lack professional knowledge and information equality, how can we fully understand these underlying rules and protect our legitimate interests to the greatest extent when handling auto insurance business? For this purpose, the author specially consulted Ms. Kong Juan, the manager of Chengdu Business Auto Insurance Sales Center, asking her to reveal the underlying rules of auto insurance from a professional perspective combined with multiple cases, and guide everyone to scientifically and reasonably handle all kinds of auto insurance business.

Underlying Rule One: Insure at New Car Price, Claim at Old Car Price

Mr. Liu bought a Dongfeng Peugeot 307 sedan three years ago for more than 150,000 yuan. According to the latest market valuation, it's worth about 100,000 yuan now. A few days ago, when renewing his insurance, Mr. Liu wanted to purchase a 100,000 yuan vehicle damage insurance, but unexpectedly, the insurance company required him to insure according to the original new car purchase price. "Such a regulation doesn't make any sense. The car has only been used for three years and is now worth 100,000 yuan, yet they still ask me to insure for 150,000 yuan," said Mr. Liu, who could not accept the insurance company's rule but was helpless.

According to Ms. Kong Juan, many private car owners believe that if an accident happens to a car insured with vehicle damage insurance, the loss will be compensated within the insured amount. Taking Mr. Liu as an example, his car is insured for 150,000 yuan of vehicle damage insurance. Does this mean that the highest compensation for the vehicle damage part can reach 150,000 yuan?

However, the fact is not so. Ms. Kong Juan told Chengdu Business Daily that even if a major traffic accident occurs, and the vehicle is totally damaged and reaches the scrapping standard, the compensation from the insurance company is calculated based on the latest valuation of the car, not necessarily resulting in a 150,000 yuan compensation. In other words, even if one insures for 150,000 yuan of vehicle damage insurance, it is impossible to receive 150,000 yuan in compensation. "If that's the case, why should I spend more money to insure for 150,000 yuan?" Mr. Liu expressed his indignation.

"The premium for 100,000 yuan vehicle damage insurance is approximately 1200 yuan. If it's 150,000 yuan vehicle damage insurance, it would be around 1600 yuan," an insurance company salesperson calculated for the reporter, stating that due to this regulation by the insurance company, Mr. Liu had to pay an extra 400 yuan in premiums.

Ms. Kong Juan indicated that the amount of vehicle damage insurance is not mandatory by the insurance company. However, if one does not insure according to the new car price, there will be a difference in compensation during claims. For situations like Mr. Liu's, he can choose to insure for 150,000 yuan, 100,000 yuan, or even 50,000 yuan. But the compensation amount during claims is directly linked to the insured amount. For instance, if an accident occurs and the estimated loss is 10,000 yuan, if Mr. Liu insures for 150,000 yuan, he can fully receive 10,000 yuan in compensation. However, if he only insures for 100,000 yuan, he will only receive two-thirds of the compensation for the same accident.

Underlying Rule Two: "Full Insurance" Does Not Equal "Full Compensation"

One day at the beginning of this year, Mr. Gao parked his car overnight by the roadside. In the morning, he found that the car window glass had been pried open, the hood and left front fender were scratched, and the left rearview mirror was also broken. Since it was a new car, Mr. Gao specifically purchased "full insurance" for his beloved car, including compulsory traffic insurance, commercial third-party liability insurance, no-deductible insurance, body scratch insurance, separate glass breakage insurance, vehicle damage insurance, and theft insurance. During the claim process, Mr. Wang believed that since he had purchased "full insurance", the insurance company should fully compensate according to the theft insurance or vehicle damage insurance. However, the insurance company only agreed to compensate according to the separate glass insurance.

In fact, the term "full insurance" does not exist in legal terms or insurance jargon; it is just a colloquialism. Many people habitually refer to several main types of insurance, including compulsory traffic insurance, commercial third-party liability insurance, no-deductible insurance, vehicle damage insurance, theft insurance, and occupant insurance, as "full insurance". However, this is not truly full coverage, so it is impossible to receive full compensation from the insurance company. Ms. Kong Juan reminds all car owners that before signing an auto insurance contract, one must first clearly understand the responsibility clauses and claim prerequisites under each type of insurance. Even if one purchases so-called "full insurance", it may not necessarily result in full compensation. When choosing motor vehicle insurance, the policyholder should understand their own risks and select the necessary risk coverage based on actual circumstances.

Rule Three: Two Cars Under One Person's Name - No Compensation for Mutual Collision

Two months ago, Mr. Ye bought a Dongfeng Honda CR-V for his newly licensed wife, but registered it under his own name. During the Dragon Boat Festival holiday, the whole Ye family drove to Jiuzhaigou for a trip. Mr. Ye and his wife separately drove the Highlander and CR-V, both registered under Mr. Ye's name, towards their destination. When the car reached Dujiangyan, in order to avoid a motorcycle crossing horizontally, Mr. Ye braked suddenly, and his wife behind him did not react in time, causing the CR-V to collide with the back of the Highlander. Initially, Mr. Ye thought that since both cars were insured, reporting the accident and claiming compensation would suffice. However, during the subsequent accident handling process, the insurance company refused compensation, citing that both cars belonged to Mr. Ye and thus there was no "third party." Mr. Ye felt this was very unfair.

Regarding this, Ms. Kong Juan clearly stated that according to current auto insurance regulations, regardless of how the accident responsibility is determined, if two cars under the same person's name collide, the insurance company will not compensate for claims involving compulsory traffic insurance and third-party liability insurance. The compensation limit for vehicle damage insurance can be calculated based on the accident responsibility ratio. According to the definitions of compulsory traffic insurance and third-party liability insurance, the insured and their families cannot be considered as "third parties," so there is no compensation object. In such situations, the policyholder should report the incident first. After the relevant authorities determine the accident responsibility, compensation for vehicle damage insurance can be applied based on the insurance evidence. Depending on the actual situation, usually one of the two cars involved in the accident may receive compensation from the insurance company.

For the two cars involved in the accident, both purchased auto insurance. According to the insurance company's regulations, only one of them can be compensated, which is hard to accept emotionally and logically. However, Ms. Kong Juan pointed out that the insurance company does this mainly to guard against possible moral hazards, such as staging fake accidents for fraud. But for car owners, fulfilling their insurance obligations without enjoying corresponding rights is obviously unfair. As more and more families own two cars, the chances of collisions between their own vehicles will increase significantly. How can the rights of these car owners be protected?

Underlying Rule Four: "Red and Black Model Lists" Result in Significant Differences in Premiums

"Why does my insurance fee need to increase when my car hasn't been in any accidents last year?" Mr. Tu called last week. Originally, in July last year, Mr. Tu bought an imported coupe. After getting the new car, Mr. Tu drove extremely carefully and didn't even have minor scratches throughout the year. A year passed quickly, and it was time to renew the insurance. In Mr. Tu's view, this year's auto insurance cost should have decreased significantly. Unexpectedly, when he asked the insurance agent, the total price increased from over 15,000 yuan to over 16,000 yuan, increasing by more than 1,000 yuan, which made Mr. Tu very confused.

It turned out that Mr. Tu's car had made it onto the insurance company's "blacklist." The agent informed Mr. Tu that after recent calculations, the company found that the accident rate for this model was very high, so the auto insurance premium was significantly increased. This made Mr. Ma feel it was unfair: "Why should I bear the blame because others have more accidents?"

Ms. Kong Juan told the author that many insurance companies have a so-called "red and black list." Models on the "red list" can get relatively low discounts when buying insurance; however, once a model makes it onto the "black list," buying insurance for them requires a surcharge, or even rejection. For example, the rate coefficient for Mazda 6 might be 1.1 in some insurance companies, while the rate coefficient for Passat can be as low as 0.95. This means that even with the same car price and the same accident record, if you buy commercial insurance, the price for Mazda 6 will be 25% higher than Passat. Moreover, for some ultra-luxury cars, their coefficients might reach 1.2 or even 1.3, leading to rejections. One company even put Porsche, Ferrari, Bentley, and a series of luxury cars on the rejection list.

So, how is this mysterious "red and black list" determined? According to insiders, taking Mazda 6 as an example, the car looks cool and performs well, and most of its owners are males aged between 20 and 35. Accidents happen easily. To avoid losses, insurance companies can only raise its insurance price. Generally speaking, models with mostly young or male drivers often end up on the "black list"; whereas models targeting the female market or used primarily for business or public purposes tend to stay on the "red list."

However, car owners generally find it hard to accept such underlying rules from insurance companies. Although customers of a particular car model may share certain common characteristics, this is not absolute. Moreover, even among the same group of people, individual personalities and driving habits vary greatly. Most car owners believe that the insurance company's approach of "better to mistakenly kill a thousand than let one escape" is indeed questionable.
