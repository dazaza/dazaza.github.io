Internal rules that Google employees will encounter

by anonymous on 2013-10-15 20:49:47

Many internet services, such as email, cloud storage, social networks, and more, can easily find a "Google version," and the vast majority of these can be used for free. Google wants users worldwide to enter its ecosystem and fully trust this all-encompassing cloud service centered around the Google account.

Externally, this is how it works, but what about internally? Do Google employees use the same tools and services? Does Google allow the use of products from other companies within its internal operations?

AllThingsD interviewed Ben Fried, Google's Chief Information Officer, and some Google employees to understand the working methods of Googlers, internal regulations, and some tools used internally by Google that have never been publicly disclosed.

According to AllThingsD, Google employees are somewhat like living in a "cocoon" (cocoon) surrounded by Google products. Most of them use Android smartphones and tablets, with near-perfect Wi-Fi and mobile data services. However, their flexibility in trying productivity tools from other companies is very limited.

This situation represents both a privilege (privileged existence) for Google employees, yet it also prevents them from closely experiencing user perspectives and exciting new products from other companies. Of course, for Google, there are good "reasons" for doing so —

Google is massive, with a great deal of sensitive and confidential information about its own operations and strategies, as well as information on over 5 million enterprise-level customers. Google cannot handle these security issues lightly, nor can it let its employees take risks. Developing applications (services) suitable for enterprises takes time and effort, and they must be properly managed.

In addition to security considerations, Ben Fried mentioned another reason:

The most important thing is that we view it as a form of "empowerment" rather than setting standards, or imposing restrictions, or enforcing rules... We are helping people work better. "Empowerment" means enabling people to find the best way to work.

He then discussed some practical examples. Regarding the prohibition of Google employees using Dropbox, Fried explained that if company users were allowed to use Dropbox, it would mean that company data would be stored in someone else's data center.

Beyond the software level, Google has some regulations regarding the devices employees use.

After suffering a hacking attack in early 2010, Google did not ban the use of Windows internally, but stipulated that using Windows devices required approval. Various platforms of computers are used internally at Google, including Windows, Mac, Linux, and Chromebook. Fried said platform diversity can reduce risks. Generally, Google provides Android smartphones and tablets to employees, but employees can also choose to use other phones (such as iPhones), with reimbursable phone bills. Employees are not allowed to bring their own laptops, but they can bring Chromebooks. This is for security reasons.

During interviews with Google employees, ATD learned that some complained that Google always prefers to create its own alternatives instead of using products from other companies, while others said the best part of Google is its non-commercialized internal products, such as Moma, Trips (which allows for discounted flights), etc.

Notably, Google has a recent technical project internally called Beyond Corp. This technology treats the company's internal network as being just as dangerous as the internet (as risky as the big, bad Internet), thereby enhancing the company's security. Analysts previously referred to it as a "zero-trust network," where all traffic within this network is carefully inspected.

This project can be seen as a perfect example of Ben Fried's management philosophy: as long as the network is controlled, it doesn't matter what devices employees use or where they work. However, according to iFanr, the Beyond Corp project has caused significant frustration among Google employees because they lost the privileges they originally enjoyed on Google's internal network.
