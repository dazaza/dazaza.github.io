Alibaba IPO said to consider conservative valuation to prevent share price from falling sharply

by geekzhang on 2013-05-06 09:08:14

May 6 news: Alibaba does not want to be the next Facebook, at least not in the foreseeable IPO. According to sources, compared to Facebook's valuation last year, Alibaba is considering a more conservative valuation amount.Alibaba officially stated that there is no IPO timetable, but analysts expect it to go public this year or next year. Insiders revealed that the funds raised from the IPO will be used in the future to repurchase shares held by Yahoo.In terms of valuation, Alibaba has already entered the early stages of speculative fever like Facebook. This year, Alibaba's revenue is expected to grow by 59%, and some analysts have estimated that Alibaba's valuation could reach as high as $100 billion, equivalent to Facebook's IPO valuation of $104 billion.Overvaluation may anger shareholders and lead to a decline in stock prices after listing, as Facebook's market value evaporated nearly half after its IPO. The share prices of Chinese companies listed in the U.S. fell 6.8% this year, while the S&P 500 Index rose 13% during the same period and broke records. In such an environment, Alibaba needs to adapt to the overall situation in terms of pricing.Irreplaceable businessAccording to Bloomberg data, since February, investment banks and research institutions have provided eight estimation figures. Taking the median, Alibaba's reasonable valuation is about $62.5 billion, equivalent to 84 times Morgan Stanley's estimated profit for last year. When Facebook went public, its valuation was approximately 107 times the profit of the previous year.Analysts from Analysys Mason believe: If investors want to buy the future, Alibaba's growth potential and dominant position in China's e-commerce sector are irreplaceable. These investors will find Alibaba attractive.A spokesperson for Alibaba said there is no IPO timetable, and last year, Chairman Jack Ma of Alibaba Group had indicated that there would be no IPO within five years.Alibaba itself does not sell goods. Analyst Eric Liu of Guoxin Securities in Hong Kong estimates that the value of goods sold by Alibaba last year reached $180 billion, equivalent to three times that of Amazon.Even taking the lower valuation of $62.5 billion, Alibaba's price-to-profit ratio still exceeds eBay's. eBay's market capitalization is $70.4 billion, equivalent to 27 times the net profit of last year. Amazon's valuation is $117 billion, equivalent to 86 times the target profit for this year. Amazon incurred a net loss last year.More growthIn China, despite Alibaba's dominance, the company may still use the IPO as a bet to penetrate the market deeper and seek growth. According to a McKinsey report in March, from 2003 to 2011, China's online retail market doubled, and it is estimated to triple from 2011 to 2015, reaching $395 billion.In 2007, Alibaba's B2B business was listed in Hong Kong, with a valuation equivalent to 54 times the profit target for the next year (excluding stock incentives). The stock price surged threefold after the listing and then fell 72% compared to the IPO price.During Alibaba's time as a listed company, despite revenue growth exceeding twofold, the stock price still declined, and the Hang Seng Index in Hong Kong fell to its lowest point in four years in 2008. Five years after the IPO, Alibaba spent $2.5 billion to purchase shares from minority shareholders, with a valuation of about 19 times the net profit of the previous year.Josef Schuster, founder of IPOX Schuster LLC in Chicago, believes: Alibaba.com is not a darling of the stock market, and they must be careful; I am sure of that.To make the IPO successful, the key is to continuously attract investors and drive the enterprise value to a reasonable level before the IPO. Analyst Qiu of Guoxin Securities pointed out: If it goes public, it will be the biggest IPO of the year. He said Alibaba is worth $100 billion. According to Morgan Stanley's estimate, a $100 billion valuation is equivalent to 134 times the net profit of last year. Schuster said that $100 billion makes no sense, and he believed: Alibaba needs institutions to complete such a large deal, but institutions have learned from Facebook and will not buy into it.Sales and profitsMorgan Stanley estimated in a report on March 6 that Alibaba's revenue for the 12 months ending December this year will grow by 59% year-on-year, reaching $7.48 billion, and will grow by another 44% next year. This year's net profit is estimated to reach $2.18 billion, doubling last year's $746.3 million.Jack Ma holds 7.4% of the shares in Alibaba Group, and other major shareholders include DST Global, Silver Lake, and SoftBank.Sources say that some shareholders will sell part of their shares, while other shareholders have told the company that they will buy stocks during the IPO. Additionally, when selecting IPO underwriters, Alibaba will adopt a method opposite to Facebook's. According to Alibaba's agreement with Yahoo, Alibaba will be listed in Hong Kong or the U.S.Liu Shengjun of CEIBS pointed out that Alibaba needs funds to develop logistics, finance, and cloud computing businesses, and it has relied on borrowing for development over the past two years.Last year, Alibaba borrowed $4 billion to privatize the company and repurchase $7.1 billion worth of shares from Yahoo. This year, Alibaba borrowed another $8 billion from nine banks.Liu Shengjun said: The management has taken on too much debt to repurchase shares, which is a bottleneck, and the amount borrowed is quite substantial.David Tawil, co-founder of Maglan Capital in New York, said that Alibaba's past performance has been good, which might slightly reassure investors, and future growth expectations may convince investors to accept Alibaba's offering price. He said: They have their own auction market, unique and one-of-a-kind, and their growth speed is also unique. This is a good thing and also their advantage.
