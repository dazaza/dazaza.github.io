Investigation Report on the Safety Production Status of Young Workers

by lk0fwcc on 2009-07-24 04:14:01

Investigation Report on the Safety Production Status of Young Workers in Railways: Strengthening the Safety Production Work of Young Workers for Leapfrog Development - Investigation Report on the Safety Production Status of Young Workers in the Sub-bureau

May 2005

Safety is the eternal theme of railway transportation. To enhance the relevance and effectiveness of the Youth League organization's efforts to ensure the safety work of young people, the Youth League Committee of the sub-bureau formed a working group that conducted an in-depth investigation into the safety production status of young workers within the jurisdiction.

1. Work Process

From March 24 to April 9, the Youth League Committee organized relevant personnel to conduct research at seven units: stations, locomotive segments, South Vehicles, North Vehicles, electric segments, engineering segments, and passenger transport branches. During the investigation, we adhered to the principle of "face-to-face, heart-to-heart, truthful communication, and genuine understanding" with young workers. We adopted methods such as listening to situation introductions, distributing questionnaires, reviewing relevant materials, holding discussions with young employees, soliciting opinions from leaders, and inspecting the on-site operations of young workers. We conducted an in-depth investigation and analysis of various factors affecting the safety production of young workers, including their thoughts, observations, actions, and needs, obtaining a large amount of first-hand material, providing a basis for ensuring the safety production work of young workers. During the investigation, we listened to reports from each unit's Youth League organization on their efforts to ensure the safety of young workers, held seven young worker seminars, had conversations with 32 frontline young workers, solicited opinions and suggestions from relevant station segment leaders regarding the safety production work of young workers, inspected the Qi Ji 215 Youth Locomotive Group and the Qi Dian Sanjianfang Hump Automation Youth Team, and reviewed relevant materials related to the analysis of violations by young workers since the beginning of the year. Meanwhile, 1200 questionnaires were distributed to young employees across all major transport stations and segments within the sub-bureau to comprehensively understand the safety mindset of young employees, providing a real basis for the investigation work.

2. Analysis of the Current Situation of Young Worker Safety Production

During the investigation, we conducted a detailed analysis of the main factors affecting the safety production of young workers, mainly focusing on:

(a) Analysis of the Status of the Young Worker Team

According to statistical analysis, the young worker team under the age of 35 within the sub-bureau has the characteristics of "large proportion, wide distribution, more front-line positions, and heavy responsibility." By "large proportion," there are a total of 25,021 employees and staff members in the 32 primary transportation front-line units (railway, locomotive, engineering, electrical, vehicle, hydroelectric systems) of the Qi Sub-bureau, among whom 10,409 are young workers under the age of 35, accounting for 41.6% of the total workforce, making them the main force in the construction and development of the sub-bureau; widely distributed, the 10,409 young workers are evenly distributed across 32 units in the railway, locomotive, engineering, electrical, vehicle, and hydroelectric systems, meaning that every production team and every job type has young workers and League members; more front-line positions, among the 10,409 young workers, 78.4% work on the front lines of transportation production. Taking Qi Ji as an example, among the 597 young workers, 534 are distributed across major workshops and frontline positions such as operation, maintenance, equipment, and signaling, especially in key positions like operation and maintenance, where young workers account for 480, or 80.4% of the total number of young workers. The proportion of young workers in the railway and passenger transport systems working in frontline positions is even higher; heavy responsibility, from the distribution of young workers, it can be seen that young workers are the backbone force in the revitalization and development of enterprises within our sub-bureau. A large number of young workers are on the front lines of transportation production, directly guarding the safety gate of the sub-bureau and shouldering important responsibilities. The state of young workers' thoughts, work, and quality directly affects the safety production, operation management, and long-term development of the sub-bureau.

(b) Analysis of Young Workers' Safety Ideology

Thoughts guide actions, understanding and mastering what young people think, observe, and need is the key to solving young workers' safety issues, and also the focus of this survey. For this purpose, we raised 10 questions from three aspects for face-to-face candid exchanges with young workers, comprehensively grasping the safety ideology dynamics of young workers.

1. Young People's Perspective on Safety

First Question: We talk about safety every day, do you think safety is really important? The answers to this question were almost unanimous. Most young workers generally believe that as a railway transportation enterprise, the movement of passengers and goods is dynamic. We are always on the edge of accidents, between safety and non-safety. Ensuring safety should be the first responsibility of every employee.

Second Question: What do you think is the most prominent issue in current safety production work? 52% of young workers believe it is not following standardized operations and habitual violations; 26% believe it is the inability of technical skills to meet the needs of safe work; 16% believe it is a lack of strong sense of responsibility; 6% believe that the existing railway technology and equipment directly affect safe production.

Third Question: From your own position, how do you think we can ensure current safety? Regarding the many factors affecting safe production that we listed, the ranking results recognized by young workers are: enhancing a sense of responsibility first, improving business quality second, strengthening supervision by cadres third, and improving technical equipment. Young workers generally believe that stimulating employees' enthusiasm for ensuring safety and enhancing employees' safety responsibility awareness is the internal foundation for ensuring safe production. "I must be safe" control must be premised on the thought of "I want to be safe." Secondly, it is necessary to strengthen business training and provide corresponding quality assurance. On this basis, strengthen the supervision of cadres to ensure flawless on-site operations.

2. Young People's Evaluation of Cadres

Fourth Question: How do you think the image and role of safety management cadres at all levels are? Regarding the image and role of cadres at all levels, young workers have mixed opinions. Overall, in recent years, cadres at all levels have won the trust of employees through solid and effective work and strict and meticulous styles. Especially the dedication and great efforts of cadres at all levels in their work and in safe production have been fully understood and recognized by the majority of employees. Many young workers said: "Nowadays, the style of cadres has significantly improved compared to before. We almost see cadres going deep into the field every day, giving up weekends and holidays, working together with employees to ensure safety." However, for some cadres, young workers hold different opinions. Some young workers believe that some cadres are detached from reality in their work, inconsistent in words and deeds, seriously formalistic, fond of empty talk, and like to make superficial articles, causing employees to feel repelled; some cadres lack ability, are full of official airs, and always put on a high-and-mighty attitude when they go deep into the grassroots, making it difficult for employees to accept; some cadres do not pay attention to improving their own professional qualities, blindly guiding grassroots work based on half-knowledge, bringing unnecessary burdens to the grassroots, and making it hard for employees to trust.

Fifth Question: How do you view the current relationship between cadres and masses? 38.2% think the relationship is harmonious, 31.6% think it is indifferent, and 30.2% think it is tense. From the proportions, it can be seen that young workers' attitudes towards the current cadre-mass relationship are not optimistic. Analyzing the reasons, there are mainly three points. First, current safety management treats the operational level employees as the terminal of management and the object of control. Employees work under the supervision of cadres at all levels every day, which inevitably creates psychological barriers over time. Second, some cadres do not pay attention to the methods in their work, focusing only on control and violation handling without being good at resolving contradictions through ideological work. Some employees say: "Nowadays, cadres only focus on 'two violations,' and the relationship between cadres and masses is like the 'cat and mouse' relationship, where you catch me and I hide, and I catch you and you hide." This creates a sense of distance among employees. Third, some cadres have low capabilities and poor images, making it difficult for employees to trust and support them, affecting the relationship between cadres and masses.

Sixth Question: What do you think cadres should do to make employees accept and trust them? In the hearts of young workers, there are mainly three requirements for cadres. First, they should be proficient in business. Second, they should always represent the interests of employees. Third, they should be fair and reasonable, treating everyone equally.

3. Young People Talking About Management

Seventh Question: What do you think are the most prominent features of the safety management you experience? The common answer from young workers is: "Strict." As they themselves say: "The management now is too strict. Things that used to be considered 'watermelon-sized issues' weren't even issues before, but now 'sesame-sized issues' are treated as serious problems. We have to be cautious every day at work, fearing any mistakes. If something goes wrong, not only will we suffer losses, but sometimes we'll drag down colleagues around us, which is really not worth it."

Eighth Question: How do you view the "five fixed" management by cadres? "Five fixed" management, as an important means of safety management at this stage, plays a positive role in promoting cadres at all levels to fulfill their duties and strengthen on-site operation control. However, employees have different interpretations of "five fixed" management. Prominent issues reflected include: First, "five fixed" is too rigid. In recent years, employees' "two violations" have shown a clear downward trend, but the quantitative standards for "five fixed" by cadres remain unchanged. Many cadres have to "pick bones out of eggs" to meet the quantitative standards, making employees feel that, compared to before, even if they do well, they still get caught, and if they do poorly, they still get penalized, with no difference. Second, using "five fixed" assessment instead of persuasion education makes employees feel the methods are too harsh. Many young workers reflect that for the "two violations" that occur among employees, cadres rarely use persuasion education to solve problems. The only method is to deduct money through assessment, resulting in employees failing to deepen their understanding of the inherent dangers of "two violations," but instead believing that they got caught due to bad luck or poor timing. This does not help solve fundamental safety ideological issues. Third, the assessment standards for "five fixed" are not uniform and subjective. Some employees reflect that for the same violation, if discovered by inspection teams at the section level or above, it would be handled at a higher level; some cadres have serious tendencies toward favoritism in enforcing "five fixed" standards, determining the category of employee violations subjectively, making it difficult for employees to trust.

Ninth Question: How do you view the large-scale safety inspection work? In the survey, nearly 50% of youth league members expressed resistance and weariness towards repeated large-scale safety inspections. The main reasons are two-fold. First, young workers believe that most of the problems reflected during each large-scale inspection are old issues from the past. There isn't much to reflect on regarding employees' on-site operations. Second, the problems raised by young workers, especially those involving employees' immediate interests, are rarely rectified, causing employees to lose confidence and unwillingness to actively identify problems and provide feedback.

Tenth Question: Are you familiar with the safety management methods implemented by the bureau and the rolling performance evaluation of the "three majors"? During the survey, it was found that 80% of young workers could explain the basic content of the eight-character safety management method and the performance evaluation of the three major projects, but nearly 50% of young workers did not understand the connotations of the methods and could not clearly articulate the responsibilities they should bear under the new management approach. Some young workers believed that as employees, they only needed to master the operational standards of their positions and perform their jobs well. Management-related matters should be studied by cadres, and employees neither need nor have the capacity to participate in management.

In summary, the responses of young workers to the ten aspects of questions reflect that under the strict management environment of striving for excellence within the bureau, the safety awareness of young workers has significantly increased, self-discipline has generally improved, and the concept of prioritizing safety has been firmly established. In their work, they are concerned about the development of the enterprise, paying attention to the safety of the bureau, willing to open their hearts, brave enough to reveal problems, demonstrating the thoughts and qualities of young people who "dare to think, can act, seek progress, and strive for excellence." Regarding some erroneous perceptions and one-sided views in the thinking of young workers, as well as different opinions proposed regarding cadres and management, these should also prompt us to reconsider strengthening the education of young workers, reinforcing the role of cadres, and optimizing safety management.

(c) Analysis of the Quality Status of Young Workers

The level of employee quality is an important factor in ensuring safe production. Analyzing the quality characteristics of young workers in our bureau mainly involves the following three aspects:

1. Labor Employment System Reform, Optimizing the Cultural Structure of Young Workers

Since the implementation of labor employment system reform in railway enterprises and the cancellation of the retirement replacement system, although the number of young workers in our bureau has shown a decreasing trend year by year, the overall quality of the young worker team has continuously improved. This is manifested in two aspects: First, young workers with a secondary technical school education or above account for a relatively large proportion. Surveys show that among the 10,409 young workers in the transportation system, those with a technical school education or above account for 55.8%, among which those with a secondary vocational school education or above account for 21.4%, and those with a junior college education or above account for 8.9%. Compared to five years ago, this has increased by 32.6%. The main reason is that in recent years, the bureau has strictly controlled the quality of new hires. Besides accepting graduates from secondary and higher vocational schools, new recruits each year must undergo three years of technical school study before being allowed to take up their posts. This greatly improves the overall quality of young workers, optimizing the cultural structure of the young worker team. Second, party members and League members account for a relatively large proportion. In recent years, party and League organizations at all levels of the bureau, adhering to the principle of actively and plannedly developing party members and League members among young workers on the front-line positions, have strictly examined and intensified educational cultivation, enabling a large number of advanced young workers to honorably join the party and League organizations and play exemplary leading roles. Among young workers aged 35 and below in the bureau, there are 1,216 party members and 2,365 League members, accounting for 11.7% and 22.7% of the total number of young workers, respectively.

2. Increased Training Efforts, Universally Improving the Business Quality of Young Workers

In recent years, party, government, trade union, and Youth League organizations at all levels of the bureau have regarded improving the technical and business quality of employees as a foundational and fundamental project. Through integrating training resources, increasing training intensity, and innovating training methods, they have enhanced the safety protection skills of employees. Particularly through conducting mass-based technical drills, the business quality of young workers has significantly improved. Analyzing the data from three units—Qi Ji, Qi Zhan, and Qi Dian—reveals the following figures: In the 2003 "equal rank, matching position" examination, the pass rate for young workers reached 98.2%, the excellent rate was 24.6%, and 87 young workers received a one-level increase in their position salary; in the business examinations organized by the three units at different stages, the pass rate for young workers consistently reached over 98%; in the technical competitions held continuously over the past five years, young workers accounted for 86.5% of the participants and a large number of young workers successively received titles of technical experts at the bureau level or above. We believe that the overall business quality of young workers can meet the needs of safety work and demonstrates the quality characteristics of young workers in terms of mastering technical theories and practical operations in some job types, surpassing other workers.

3. Technical Equipment Updates, Displaying the Advantages of Young Workers

Young workers have the advantages of good memory, abundant energy, and quick acceptance of new things. Under the circumstances of continuous innovation and development of railway science and technology, young workers have obvious advantages in learning and mastering new technologies and new equipment compared to other workers. In the application and learning of new technologies and equipment such as the introduction of train operation devices—93 in the locomotive system, power locomotive model changes, process flow line transformation and new equipment applications in the vehicle system, high-speed turnouts, eighteen-point information frequency-shift automatic block systems, and automated humps in the electrical system, young workers have fully utilized their intellectual advantages, energetic advantages, and pragmatic innovative spirit. They took the lead in studying diligently, mastering the basic principles and operating methods of new technologies and equipment first, becoming pioneers in the promotion and application of new technologies and playing an important role.

Main Issues:

1. Lack of Interest in Learning

It was found during the survey that a considerable number of young workers have a negative attitude towards daily business learning and have misconceptions. Some young workers believe that most transportation positions are skilled trades, so there is no need to memorize regulations; experience will naturally improve their skills over time. Others believe that the technical content of their positions is low, repetitive, and offers nothing new to learn. Some think that the standards set by regulations are too high to be practically applied, and even if learned, they would not be useful, thus they are unwilling to learn.

2. Lack of Experience in Field Operations

"Young workers have surplus theoretical knowledge but insufficient practical experience" is a significant characteristic of young workers' technical quality. During the survey, young workers generally reflected that they lack experience in field operations, especially in dealing with malfunctions and executing tasks under abnormal conditions, where their lack of experience becomes particularly evident. Analyzing the various incidents involving young workers since the beginning of the year, it is not difficult to see that traffic incidents caused by lack of experience account for 38% of the total.

3. Uneven Business Quality

In some units, the orientation of employee education work deviates, emphasizing theory over practice and focusing on top performers rather than the entire workforce, which is relatively prominent. This leads to uneven business quality among young workers.
