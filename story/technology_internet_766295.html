Facebook to launch search tool to help news organizations understand social conversations

by anonymous on 2013-09-11 13:27:12

　　In the evening of September 9, according to Beijing Time, Facebook will release two new search tools on Monday to help news agencies and marketers better understand real-time social conversations happening on Facebook, especially those surrounding TV shows, major news events, and sporting events.

　　One of the new tools will allow news agencies to use keywords such as "Syria vote" or "Tokyo Olympics" to search for content defined as "public" on Facebook. The other tool will enable news agencies to search private content on Facebook, but the search results will only be some statistical information, such as the geographical distribution, gender distribution, and age distribution of the relevant user groups.

　　Facebook's head of partnerships, Andy Mitchell, said: "This is a way to help news agencies understand what users are discussing. As long as we provide this tool to creative journalists, the possibilities are endless."

　　Recently, Facebook has been taking a series of measures, hoping to catch up with or even surpass Twitter and become the leading online platform for public conversations. This search tool is part of these efforts.

　　Since most Twitter users set their messages as public, Twitter is an extremely valuable tool for understanding societal trends and where public attention lies in a short period. For example, last Sunday, Twitter's trending topics showed that many Americans were discussing football games. Twitter also helps advertisers and TV stations target users by informing them which users have tweeted about popular TV shows and sports events.

　　Facebook has about five times as many global users as Twitter, and its data is equally valuable. For instance, Facebook announced last week that the launch of the NFL's new season attracted more than 20 million "likes," comments, and shares from over 8 million users.

　　However, most content on Facebook is not public; it is only accessible to some of the user’s friends. Therefore, Facebook must carefully weigh which information can be released externally, especially given the backlash against adjustments to privacy policies.

　　Recently, Facebook has begun to take more active measures to position itself as an online square. In June this year, Facebook started offering hashtag search functionality to its 1.2 billion users. Users can search hashtags like "#NFL" on Facebook, similar to Twitter.

　　On the main news feed page, Facebook is testing a "Trending Topics" module that will display the hottest topics currently being discussed by users. Recently, Facebook also launched an embedded content feature that allows all users to embed public Facebook content, including videos, on their personal web pages.

　　The new tools Facebook will release this week represent the latest step in this direction. Initially, these tools will only be available to a limited number of news agencies, such as BuzzFeed, CNN, NBC News, Sky News, and Slate. These tools will be tested and improved. Facebook will also provide these tools to Mass Relevance, a company that offers social media analytics and user engagement services. However, Facebook stated that it plans to open this function to other news agencies and marketing companies in the near future.

　　News agencies participating in the testing have expressed optimism about this tool. Ryan Osborn, Vice President of Digital Innovation and Social Media at NBC News, said: "As a news agency, we have always tried to answer the question: what are people talking about?" He indicated that this week, before the US Congress votes on President Obama's proposal for military action in Syria, NBC plans to use this tool for an online town hall event titled "Taking Sides: Should the U.S. Strike Syria?"

　　KC Estenson, Senior Vice President and General Manager of CNN Digital, said that CNN hopes to obtain information different from Twitter through Facebook's data. He stated: "Compared to Twitter, you may see a more personalized and direct side of users through their use of Facebook."

　　Estenson believes that such tools cannot replace frontline reporters but will complement them. He stated: "This is a tool equivalent to on-the-ground reporting in the field of social media. In the long term, we will be able to obtain more information from it."

　　It is currently unclear how news agencies will use aggregated anonymous data from non-public content, but Estenson said he expects it to provide another way to gauge public opinion, similar to formal polls. He stated: "By observing the topics people are discussing and the demographic distribution, this will become a clue to understanding what is happening in the world." (Viking)
