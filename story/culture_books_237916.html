"The Kite Runner"

by jqrxass on 2009-09-14 19:52:22

"The Kite Runner" has received great praise after its publication due to the vivid portrayal of characters and a touching, shocking storyline. It has won various awards for new authors and topped bestseller lists across the U.S., and is currently being adapted into a movie by DreamWorks.

Part One of "The Kite Runner"

I became who I am today at the age of twelve, on a frigid, overcast winter day in 1975. I clearly remember crouching behind a collapsed mud wall, peeking down that alleyway next to the frozen creek. Many years have passed, and people say you can bury the past. But I’ve learned that’s wrong. The past claws its way out.

Part Two of "The Kite Runner"

My father shaped the world around him as he pleased, with me being the obvious exception. Of course, the problem was, Dad's world only had black and white. And what was black and what was white was entirely up to him. He was the kind of man you loved but also feared, and maybe even hated a little.

Part Three of "The Kite Runner"

Hassan's hands trembled as he struggled to tuck the slingshot back into his waistband. His lips curved, perhaps trying to form an assuring smile. It took him five attempts to secure the slingshot to his pants. We trudged home heavily, knowing full well that Assef and his friends might be waiting around a corner to beat us up, and neither of us spoke a word. We didn't, which should've brought us some relief. But it didn't, not even a bit.

Part Four of "The Kite Runner"

By three o'clock in the afternoon, clouds had gathered, hiding the sun, and shadows began to stretch long. The spectators on the rooftops wrapped themselves in scarves and thick coats. There were only six kites left now, and mine was one of them. My legs ached, my neck stiffened. But as each kite fell, so did my hope grow, accumulating like snow against a wall, one flake at a time.

Part Five of "The Kite Runner"

Another gift from Dad - one he wouldn't even wait for me to open - was a watch. It had a blue face with golden hands shaped like lightning bolts. I didn't even try it on before tossing it into the pile of toys in the corner. The only gift that didn't end up there was Rahim Khan's leather-bound notebook; it alone didn't feel like blood money.

Part Six of "The Kite Runner"

Then, on a sweltering summer Sunday, Dad and I sat at our stall in the flea market, fanning ourselves with newspapers. Despite the sun blazing like a branding iron, the market was crowded that day, and sales were good - by half past twelve, we'd already made $160. I stood up, stretched, and asked Dad if he wanted a Coca-Cola. He said yes.

Ending of "The Kite Runner"

It was just a smile, nothing more. It didn't make everything all right again. It didn't make anything all right again. Just a smile, a small thing, like a leaf in the woods trembling in the wake of a startled bird taking flight.
