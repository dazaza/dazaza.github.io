Blue chips accumulate strength and the market strength will continue - Stock Forum

by xl0958870 on 2009-12-05 02:35:45

From the recent news, investors find it hard to understand why major stocks were weak in the first four trading days of this week. At the Futures Industry Conference, reporters learned from different sources that stock index futures and margin trading have been prepared. These two financial innovations are likely to become important work for the CSRC in 2010. Because domestic institutional investors have always been troubled by the lack of a shorting mechanism, the introduction of stock index futures and margin trading is more conducive to realizing the market value discovery function and repositioning stock prices. Stock index futures focus on the influence on the stock index, while margin trading focuses more on stock liquidity and relative value stability. Therefore, the introduction of these two financial innovations is obviously good news for super-large-cap stocks. So the previous pullback of major blue-chip stocks can only be considered as an intentional pressure behavior because institutions have not yet completed their position adjustments. Once this process of hot spot switching ends, major blue-chip stocks may explode.

Yesterday at the close, all bank stocks and insurance stocks with H-shares were priced lower than H-shares, and the valuation advantage of financial stocks was obvious. Today, it was exactly the comprehensive launch of financial stocks that led the market to close with a medium-sized bullish candle after wide fluctuations. The SSE Composite Index retook the 3300 point mark. The weekly K-line formed a bullish engulfing pattern, showing clear strength. Based on past experience, every time large-cap stocks start, there will be some inertia. The hotspot effect will continue to deepen and orderly rotate within the large-cap stock sector, so the upward trend of the market will continue. Investors are advised to pay close attention to banks, real estate, and resource-related stocks that benefit from inflation, which we have mentioned multiple times recently.

The procedure for buying and selling stocks is as follows: When buying or selling stocks, you must specify whether you are buying or selling, the name (or code) of the stock, the quantity, and the price. This order is only valid on the day it is placed. The content of the order includes the abbreviation (code) of the stock you want to buy or sell, the quantity, and the price of the stock. The abbreviation of a stock usually consists of three to four Chinese characters, and the stock code is six digits. When placing an order, the stock code and abbreviation must be consistent. Also, there are certain regulations regarding the quantity of stocks being traded: the quantity for purchasing stocks must be a whole multiple of 100, but the quantity for selling stocks does not need to be a whole multiple of 100.

There are four ways to place an order: counter submission, telephone automatic order, computer automatic order, and remote terminal order.
