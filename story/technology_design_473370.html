Abstract: In the teaching process of undergraduate procurement management courses, the explanation of knowledge points is merely elaborated from a theoretical perspective. Due to students' lack of relevant work experience, it is inevitably difficult for them to understand. Therefore, the application of case teaching in the explanation of procurement management courses is extremely important. This article takes this as a starting point and studies several key issues in the case teaching of procurement management, including: the principles for selecting cases, sources of cases, and the implementation of cases. Finally, it points out that through case teaching, improvements can be achieved in multiple aspects, which will help students master key knowledge points.

Keywords: Case teaching; Procurement management; Teaching research

For a long time, traditional classroom teaching mainly emphasized the initiative of teachers, meaning that teachers teach what they want, and students learn accordingly. Teachers seem to be just "craftsmen" who impart knowledge, making classrooms a place for showcasing individual teacher skills and simple repetitive labor. As a fundamental course in management disciplines, procurement management has strong practicality. If purely theoretical explanations are given during the teaching process, it is difficult to stimulate students' interest in learning. Moreover, pure theoretical teaching makes it hard for students to deeply grasp the key knowledge points in the course. Given this, one of the methods in reforming procurement management course teaching is to introduce case teaching. Before discussing how to apply case teaching methods in procurement management courses, let's briefly understand the background of the emergence of case teaching and its role.

I. The Emergence and Development of Case Teaching

Case teaching first emerged in 1870 at Harvard Law School in the United States. Its purpose was to achieve an understanding of legal rules by analyzing typical cases, thereby mastering basic legal knowledge and theory. Subsequently, in the early 20th century, case teaching had been adopted by all famous law schools in the United States. In 1919, Delheim, a graduate of Harvard College, became the Dean of Harvard Business School. He highly praised case teaching and advocated its promotion in business schools, emphasizing that business courses should focus on problems in real-life situations, guiding students to participate in analyzing and discussing cases or directly collecting and organizing cases from real business activities. Currently, this teaching method still holds an important position in business education. The application of case teaching in general education began after the 1970s. Although its application is far less widespread and mature in fields like law, medicine, and management, experiments with case teaching in teacher professional education have increased. In the education of professional foundation courses in general higher education institutions, especially in economics and management courses, foreign universities generally adopt it. However, due to the current reasons for teacher selection processes in domestic universities, professors usually have high theoretical levels but lack practical experience, posing a challenge for how domestic universities conduct case teaching. Applying case teaching in the procurement process is undoubtedly very important for students to master key knowledge points, but the selection and arrangement of cases require careful consideration by the teacher.

II. Sources of Teaching Cases

1. Principles for Selecting and Compiling Teaching Cases

The success or failure of case teaching largely depends on the case itself, so teachers should carefully consider when selecting cases. When choosing teaching cases, the following principles are generally followed:

(1) Targeted

One function of procurement management cases is to demonstrate and test the theories taught in textbooks. As carriers of procurement management theories, they must include important management knowledge and concepts, while also reflecting the application of management knowledge and concepts in specific realities. At the same time, as a form of presenting management knowledge, it must meet the actual needs of teaching. Therefore, when selecting teaching cases, materials should be chosen based on teaching content and objectives. Additionally, the overall knowledge level of students should be considered. The selected cases should ensure that students have the ability to analyze them and should align with the students' cognitive levels.

(2) Authenticity

Cases encompass the complexity, uncertainty, and problem-oriented nature of teaching. Cases are records of specific scenarios, describing events or situations that truly occurred. They cannot be replaced by fabricated facts created from "shaking chairs," nor can they be substituted by abstract, generalized facts derived from practice. In case teaching, the case presents truth, not falsehood; otherwise, the entire direction of teaching loses its accuracy. Therefore, teachers must never take shortcuts, use clever tricks, fabricate materials, or concoct cases. The value of a case lies in its authenticity, and the life of case teaching is based on the authenticity of the case. For example, when explaining government procurement, the data provided to students comes from the annual statistics published by the National Bureau of Statistics, whose authority and authenticity are indisputable.

Close to life, most MBA instructors in China prefer to use foreign cases, but due to differences in student backgrounds, foreign cases find it hard to achieve the expected results.

(3) Typicality

Cases must possess teaching value that best reflects the principles and rules of procurement. This is the fundamental characteristic of a case, and not any example can be casually used as a case. Through analysis, the case must best reveal the principles and rules in the procurement management process.

(4) Applicability

Most cases originate from life and reality, but not all true, typical, and vivid cases can enter the classroom. Due to time constraints in class, cases should be selected for their appropriate length, being concise yet comprehensive, fitting the textbook knowledge structure and students' cognitive rules, allowing for case analysis within a short period.
