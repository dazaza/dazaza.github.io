Revisions to the Copyright Law should be based on national conditions and keep pace with the times

by keyizhang008 on 2012-03-06 13:58:26

In the intellectual property legal system, copyright law is the most complex in terms of legal relationships, the richest in content, and the most frequently changing. It is also one of the laws that requires the strictest legislative techniques. Therefore, the third revision of the Copyright Law is particularly important and challenging.

Looking back at the status of revisions to China's Copyright Law, there are two characteristics: one is long-term inactivity. Since its establishment in 1990, it has only been revised twice, with each interval lasting a full decade; the other is relative passivity, as the first revision was mainly due to pressure from WTO accession, and the second revision was based on the final ruling of the World Trade Organization. From the perspective of international reform trends and China's overall development, the third revision must adapt to new situations, making its significance particularly important. I believe that this revision should be based on China's current national conditions and the following guiding principles, focusing on several aspects.

Keeping pace with the times and establishing guiding principles

Based on China's current national conditions, I believe that the revision of China's Copyright Law should conform to the following guiding principles:

Firstly, achieving both localization and internationalization. The third revision of China's Copyright Law is being carried out amidst the trend of intellectual property integration and internationalization. As a contracting party to the Berne Convention and the WIPO Internet Treaties, China must comply with the international obligations stipulated by international conventions. At the same time, the revision of China's Copyright Law should also consider the development level of the country's economy, technology, and culture.

Therefore, we propose achieving both localization and internationalization: emphasizing that the revision conforms to the minimum standards set by international conventions at the international level, while drawing on the legislative experience of developed countries; focusing on China's reality at the local level, solving practical problems, and promoting economic and social development.

Secondly, the revision should keep up with the times. Copyright law is both a technical law and an industrial law. Therefore, the revision of China's Copyright Law should promptly respond to the development trends of network technology, maintaining the advanced nature of the law in keeping with the times; it should also carefully consider the development needs of the copyright industry, promoting the great development and prosperity of socialist culture.

I believe that these principles can enable China's copyright legislation to transition from "forced use" to "for our own use," incentivizing the leapfrog development of China's copyright industry, thereby achieving the strategic goal of building an innovative country.

Balancing modifications and absorption, improving relevant regulations

This revision of the Copyright Law is a summary and enhancement of China's copyright legislation and judicial experience over the past 20 years, as well as a generalization and reflection of China's copyright teaching and research results. I believe that the revision can follow the following approach:

Modifications and absorption should coexist. Based on China's existing copyright system, there are several options for revising the Copyright Law: one is the "[one-to-three]" plan, which involves completely incorporating several regulations related to the Copyright Law into the Copyright Law during this revision, unifying the sources of the Copyright Law; another is the "[one-plus-three]" plan, which involves only modifying the articles within the Copyright Law while fully retaining the integrity of related regulations to avoid excessively high revision costs; the third is the "[one-drag-three]" plan, which involves absorbing relatively mature content from related regulations that have been accepted by judicial practice into the Copyright Law, and then gradually revising and perfecting related copyright regulations after the passage of the Copyright Law revision proposal.

I believe that this revision can adopt the "[one-drag-three]" revision plan, which includes substantial modifications to the content of the Copyright Law itself, as well as absorbing relatively mature parts from related regulations. This plan balances the "revolutionary" and "prudent" aspects of the revision.

Adding definitions of works and restructuring rights types

The purpose of China's Copyright Law is to protect the legitimate rights and interests of copyright holders and promote the widespread dissemination of knowledge information. Based on this, I believe that the revision can focus on the following aspects:

Firstly, scientifically defining the scope of works and adding definitions of works. With the widespread application of new transmission technologies, new types of works continue to emerge. Relying solely on an enumerative legislative model is insufficient to meet the challenges brought about by technological development. Clearly defining the definition of works helps clearly define the objects protected by copyright law, facilitates the widespread dissemination of cultural knowledge, and promotes the realization of a prosperous culture and industrial development.

Therefore, this revision should first add the concept of works, elevating the provisions regarding the definition of works in the Implementation Regulations of the Copyright Law, namely "[the works referred to in this law mean intellectual creations in the fields of literature, art, and science that are original and can be reproduced in some tangible form]," to legal provisions. At the same time, add elements of works not subject to protection, such as not protecting abstract ideas, not protecting practical factors such as operational methods, practical solutions, and practical functions. This clause combines with the aforementioned suggestions for revising the definition of works, accurately defining works under copyright law from both positive and negative perspectives.

Secondly, adapting to the development of transmission technology and reconstructing rights types. On one hand, based on the different properties of personal rights and property rights in copyrights, adopting a separate legislative model, setting two parallel provisions to separately regulate copyright personal rights and copyright property rights. Copyright personal rights and copyright property rights are two different types of rights in copyrights, each having different legal attributes. From the perspective of rights attributes, copyright personal rights emphasize the protection of authors' spiritual interests, generally having no term limit (except for the right of publication), inseparability, and non-deprivation, whereas copyright property rights value the author's economic value, which can be transferred and inherited, thus leading to significant differences in terms of protection period and utilization methods, making separate regulations more appropriate.

On the other hand, I believe that the various types of rights in copyrights should be revised. Including expanding the scope of reproduction rights, incorporating the reproduction of works from two-dimensional forms to three-dimensional forms into the scope of reproduction under copyright law; introducing the principle of exhaustion of distribution rights to coordinate conflicts between copyrights and ownership of work carriers; expanding the scope of rental rights, adding the right to rent audio recordings; revising the definition of performance rights, including live performances (public performances of works) and mechanical performances (rights to publicly perform works using various means), aligning with international convention regulations while eliminating confusion in the rights system; introducing public communication rights, directly covering wired, wireless, interactive, and non-interactive communications, providing necessary legal support for information dissemination in the network era.

Improving limitations on rights and increasing protection efforts

Besides the above key contents of the revision, I believe the following aspects should also be revised:

Firstly, reasonably borrowing international experiences to improve the limitation system of rights. On one hand, the determination of the applicable scope of China's reasonable use system of copyrights should incorporate abstract judgment criteria based on an enumerative legislative model, allowing for a unified standard in judicial interpretations of listed behaviors in copyright law. Therefore, in this revision, the "three-step test" originating from the Berne Convention can be added as a criterion for judging reasonable use, and it is necessary to adjust and merge the limitation systems of traditional copyright property rights and information network dissemination rights so that the reasonable use clauses apply to all copyright property rights.

On the other hand, expand the scope of statutory licenses, adding a statutory license system for "producing courseware to implement nine-year compulsory education and national education plans." Combining the legislative purposes and principles of copyright law, as well as the development of statutory licensing systems for copyrights in the network environment, based on the public welfare of distance education and the characteristics of the network, incorporating the production of courseware into statutory licenses allows low-cost implementation of distance education and other network-based teaching methods, achieving a balance between the interests of authors and the public interest.

Secondly, responding to the needs of industrial development, constructing a related rights system. On one hand, directly naming Chapter Four of the Copyright Law as "Related Rights," elevating Article 26 of the Implementation Regulations to the content of the Copyright Law can correspond with Chapter Two "Copyright" of the Copyright Law, while unifying the contents of each section of Chapter Four, making the Copyright Law system more logically coherent and easily recognizable.

On the other hand, improve the types of related rights. Add performers' secondary performance rights. Many countries' copyright laws stipulate that radio and television stations should pay performers when broadcasting recorded performances. In China's Copyright Law, only copyright holders enjoy broadcasting rights and mechanical performance rights, while performers do not have these rights, resulting in an imbalance in rights protection; improve broadcast organization rights. Main modifications should include clarifying the object of broadcast organization rights as program signals, adding public communication rights for pre-recorded radio and television programs, and adding public communication rights in profit-making venues.

Lastly, intensify efforts against infringement and enhance the level of rights protection. Given the characteristics of low cost and wide range of copyright infringement, it is necessary to increase statutory compensation standards and introduce punitive damages. This revision can raise the maximum statutory compensation for copyright infringement to 1 million yuan. Theoretically, China's 2008 Patent Law amendment increased the maximum statutory compensation for patent infringement to 1 million yuan, providing a realistic reference for adjusting the maximum statutory compensation for copyright infringement; from the perspective of administrative law enforcement practices, copyright infringement remains very prominent at this stage, necessitating stricter liability mechanisms to curb the high incidence of infringement.

This revision can introduce a punitive damages system for copyright infringement. Internationally, South Africa, Brazil, Russia, and the United States have adopted punitive damages beyond losses in their copyright legislation; domestically, strengthening civil liabilities for copyright infringement aligns with judicial policies aimed at building an innovative country, increasing infringement costs, and intensifying efforts against infringement, as well as with industrial policies promoting healthy development of the copyright industry and enhancing the contribution rate of the copyright industry to economic development.
