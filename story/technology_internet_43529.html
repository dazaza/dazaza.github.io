Changes in Japan's student visa policy ushers in a "thaw" for further education in Japan

by chinatop on 2008-01-17 18:27:35

Affected by Japan's tightened visa policy for studying in Japan, the originally warm and flowery month of April has become a "severe winter" for studying in Japan. The visa approval rate has dropped sharply from 78.6% last year to 45.8%, with the rejection rate for Chinese freshmen reaching as high as 70%. However, many Japanese language schools and universities still rely on foreign students for survival. After experiencing a "financial difficulties - teacher layoffs - closures" spring, Japan's study abroad policies have once again undergone subtle changes.

Recently, reporters learned from domestic Japanese institutions that the "high threshold" has "knocked down" some Japanese schools. Ms. Kuroda Hisae, Dean of the Oriental Language Institute, told reporters that since the beginning of 2004, due to the strictness of the Immigration Bureau's document review process, there has been a sharp decrease in student numbers, causing operational difficulties for some schools, leading to teacher layoffs. If this situation continues, not only will school operations be affected, but students will also feel uncertain about their future. Mr. Yu Dongzhen, Vice President of Kyushu Ei Su Gaku-en International Language Institute, also expressed that at the current rate, some Japanese language schools will go bankrupt, and some private universities will face operational difficulties.

However, reporters also learned that while all Japanese language schools and universities have been more or less affected, the extent varies significantly among different schools. According to Ms. Tsuchida Chiyo, head of recruitment at Hokuriku University, one school in Kanto applied for over 100 students but only accepted 10, severely impacting its operations. Schools with better acceptance rates were less affected.

The "severe winter" for studying in Japan is almost over, perhaps following the rule of "extremes inevitably lead to reversals." Recently, after interviewing several language schools and universities in Japan, reporters obtained reliable information: the current visa policy for studying in Japan has quietly relaxed, and Chinese students will soon benefit. Professor Katou Yukio, head of recruitment at Waseda University's Information Production System Research Department, told reporters that to ensure the continued attraction of students from China, which accounts for more than half of all Japanese international students, the Japanese government has recently made corresponding adjustments to its study abroad policies to make them more practical. Key contents include re-establishing the preliminary approval rights of qualified Japanese language schools. That is, for applicants from language schools with an illegal stay rate of less than 3%, the main approval process remains within the language schools; financial guarantees can be provided through Renminbi deposit certificates; students can work part-time in Japan to help cover expenses; additionally, application materials have been simplified. Mr. Nakamura Tokunori, Dean of Kyoto International College, stated, "Currently, the requirements for Chinese student qualifications and documentation are at their highest point, mainly due to some students using study abroad as a cover for 'illegal entry and work,' with relatively high crime rates. If the situation improves, the number of Chinese international students could increase by 8% annually. Furthermore, proposals to expand scholarship systems and abolish distinctions between preparatory students and international students have been placed on the agenda. In response, institutions such as Hokuriku University, Oriental Language Institute, and Kyushu Ei Su Gaku-en International Language Institute have either already begun or plan to offer substantial scholarships to outstanding international students and implement tuition reductions. Specifically, Ms. Tsuchida Chiyo, head of recruitment at Hokuriku University's special program (preparatory course), clearly stated that students admitted to the special program will have opportunities to receive Ishikawa Prefecture International Student Study Awards, Ministry of Education Scholarships, Hokuriku University Scholarships, and other civilian scholarships. Students who advance from the special program to Hokuriku University’s undergraduate program will also have their 200,000 yen entrance fee waived.

At the same time, reporters learned that the new Japanese study abroad visa policy is expected to relax requirements for Chinese students with high school diplomas or higher, including bachelor's and associate degrees. For example, no rigid language level requirements will be imposed, and students will only need to provide proof of 150 hours of Japanese language study; when filling out the "funding payment form," students can list part-time work income as one of their funding sources.
