Mozilla CEO Denies Google Decided Thunderbird's Fate

by jagger on 2007-07-28 21:47:30

The news that Mozilla is going to abandon Thunderbird has greatly surprised people, and now Google stands at the center of this controversy.

A netizen named Vaughn Reid left a message on the blog of Mozilla CEO Mitchell Baker pointing out that Mozilla's abandonment of Thunderbird development is related to Google's promotion of web-based Gmail applications. He said "Because Google is the primary sponsor of the Mozilla Foundation, and because Google actively develops and provides enterprise-level E-mail systems through Gmail and Google Apps, they may want to kill or stop the development of Thunderbird so as to turn Outlook users towards Google rather than Thunderbird."

In response to this, Mozilla CEO Baker said that it was not true. "I want to make it as clear as possible that Google has nothing to do with this matter. Google and its products have no relation to this decision. In the past, we indeed did not ask Google about the product plans and revenue of Thunderbird, nor the product plans and revenue of Gmail, and we indeed did not seek Google's opinion."

She said "Google's plans for Gmail - whatever they are, they are unknown to us - are irrelevant to our decision."

As for Reid's point that Google is the main source of income for Mozilla Corporation, and the partnership makes Google the default homepage and default search engine for Firefox, the last time it was made public was in 2005 when Google paid Mozilla approximately $52 million. Mozilla has not disclosed the figures for 2006.

Thunderbird users were alarmed by Mozilla's decision, worrying whether the email client they use would receive enough attention. Scott MacGregor and David Bienvenu, the main developers of Thunderbird, support setting up a small, independent company to continue developing Thunderbird with the help of supporters. In an email, MacGregor said he and Bienvenu want Thunderbird to continue existing. "My team and I will not walk away," he promised that the development work for the next version, Thunderbird 3.0, would continue. He also pointed out that there is no set timeline for Thunderbird being separated from Mozilla.
