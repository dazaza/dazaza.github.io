Causes and Treatment of Cervical Erosion

Cervical erosion: Cervical erosion is one form of other cervical inflammations. When the epithelium at the external os of the cervix falls off and is replaced by another type of epithelial tissue from the cervical os, due to the thinness of the newly formed epithelium, blood vessels and red tissues beneath can sometimes be seen, making it look like real erosion; hence the term cervical erosion.

Causes of Cervical Erosion:

Cervical erosion has a certain relationship with sexual activity. It's necessary to remind you that although cervical erosion is more common in married women, it does not mean that unmarried women cannot have cervical erosion. In addition to childbirth, abortion, infection, surgery, etc., all of which can cause cervical erosion, sexual intercourse can also cause some degree of damage to the cervix, thereby leading to symptoms.

Some women are very particular about hygiene but lack necessary knowledge. They often use highly concentrated cleaning solutions to rinse the vagina, which often produces counterproductive results. This not only affects the normal growth of vaginal flora and reduces its ability to inhibit pathogenic bacteria, but can also cause varying degrees of injury to the cervical epithelium, ultimately resulting in erosion.

Types of Cervical Erosion:

Cervical erosion is one of the local characteristics of chronic cervicitis. For a long time, clinical doctors have regarded chronic cervicitis and cervical erosion as synonymous terms. However, according to the different stages of their pathological processes, cervical erosion is divided into the following types:

1. Pseudo-erosion: The pathological concept of erosion refers to "[the shedding of surface epithelium]", while the commonly seen cervical erosion in clinical work is mostly the red area around the external os of the cervix. During colposcopy, when 3% acetic acid is applied to the surface of the red area, it can be observed that the local area appears to change into "[grape-like]" formations due to columnar epithelial edema. If a biopsy is performed on this area, under the microscope, the surface is covered by columnar epithelium. Therefore, if strictly adhering to the pathological definition of erosion, this area does not belong to erosion, hence some suggest calling it pseudo-erosion. Most cervical erosions seen in clinical work belong to this type.

2. True erosion: Due to the frequent presence of a large amount of mucus or purulent secretions covering the cervical surface, these secretions, over a long period, stimulate and soak the squamous epithelium surrounding the external os of the cervix. Combined with inflammatory infiltration in deeper cervical tissues, the squamous epithelium covering the cervix loses vitality and falls off, forming ulcers, which is true erosion. However, this eroded area is soon covered by the surrounding columnar epithelium, forming a red area with a granular appearance and a certain luster, i.e., pseudo-erosion. Most cases of cervical erosion found in our daily work belong to pseudo-erosion, while true erosion is just a brief stage in this pathological process, eventually becoming pseudo-erosion as the surface is covered by columnar epithelium.

3. Congenital erosion: During fetal development, except for vaginal epithelium, all other reproductive tract epithelia originate from body cavity epithelium. When the embryo develops to the third or fourth month, the cylindrical epithelium within the cervical canal and the squamous epithelium on the surface of the cervical-vaginal part can already be clearly distinguished. At this point, the junction of the two types of epithelia is not at the cervical os but inside the cervical canal. When the embryo develops to the sixth to seventh month, the cervical columnar epithelium has already developed secretory functions.

4. Acquired erosion: Acquired erosion contrasts with congenital erosion and mostly occurs in women of childbearing age with vigorous ovarian function. The cervical canal's columnar epithelium is influenced by estrogen produced by the ovaries and undergoes excessive hyperplasia, exceeding the external os of the cervix, thereby making the external os of the cervix appear eroded. The appearance of such erosion is no different from inflammation-induced erosion, but the causes differ. This type of erosion is commonly seen during pregnancy, and most cases regress after childbirth. Due to differing causes, there may be some differences in clinical manifestations. The leucorrhea of patients with this condition will increase, but the nature of the leucorrhea is clear mucoid, tightly adhering to the cervical surface and difficult to wipe off. Pathological examination shows no inflammatory cell infiltration under the columnar epithelial cells, but there is glandular and interstitial hyperplasia. This suggests that this type of erosion may be somewhat related to endocrine changes and seems to have no direct relationship with inflammation. Of course, inflammation infections can easily occur on the basis of erosion, but such inflammatory manifestations are merely the result and not the cause of the erosion.

How to Examine Cervical Erosion:

To examine cervical erosion, a routine gynecological examination must first be done. A routine gynecological examination generally includes a regular check-up, a vaginal discharge test, a transvaginal ultrasound, a Pap smear test, and a breast examination. Specifically, the routine gynecological examination includes checks on the vulva, vagina, cervix, size, shape, position of the uterus, as well as the fallopian tubes and ovaries.
