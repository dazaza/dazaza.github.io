Whether you use an iPhone or not, "complaining about Cook" has become a kind of fashion.

by anonymous on 2013-09-18 21:43:36

— "The Peak Moment of the New Apple Core"

A colleague told me that now "trolling Cook" has become a fashion. Regardless of whether one knows Apple or uses an iPhone, they might join the ranks of criticizing Apple's CEO Tim Cook.

If we were to rank the executives with "strong hearts" in the global IT industry, Cook might only be second to Nokia's CEO Stephen Elop, who is known as the "Century Inside Job." Perhaps Cook doesn't get trolled as harshly as Elop, but he endures greater "chronic damage"—starting from the day Steve Jobs, Apple's soul figure, passed away. Moreover, this injury seems endless.

Facing Cook, many people feel "hate iron for not becoming steel," why isn't he someone like Jobs with extraordinary foresight and creativity? But if there was another Jobs, it would absolutely not be Cook; they are fundamentally two different types of people.

Recalling the era when Jobs was CEO, Cook held the position of COO, remaining relatively unknown. At the time, his main work was the most tedious part of the company—supply chain management and operations. Yes, even a "dreamlike" company like Apple needs someone responsible for this task, and it's very important. In many articles describing Cook, it's mentioned that his and Jobs' personalities differ greatly, yet complement each other perfectly. Jobs was like a storm, while Cook was a calming force.

Few people know how Jobs entrusted Apple to Cook before his death or how the board approved it. If we were to guess in the sentimental style of "Steve Jobs: The Biography," it might have been a standard "Liu Bei entrusting his orphan." Zhuge Liang didn't share Liu Bei's traits, but he was the only person who could be entrusted with such a heavy responsibility.

This actually explains why Apple still doesn't have a COO, because Cook is still doing what he does best. However, under the title of CEO, he also needs to make important decisions, plan the company's direction, and speak at the beginning and end of every product launch.

Therefore, the "Apple core" originally formed by Jobs and Cook is now left with only Cook, who, despite having great skills, cannot handle everything alone. But in my view, that was just the situation during the first year after the boss's death. Now things are a bit different—Jonathan Ive (Jony Ive) is rising.

In June this year, after interviewing at Apple's WWDC, I wrote a commentary titled "The New Legend of Apple: Co-starring Cook and Ive." With the release of the iPhone 5C and iPhone 5S, I am more convinced of this point. Cook and Ive are deliberately forming a new "Apple core," and Ive is the one closest to Jobs in terms of creativity, the one who can complement Cook.

Ive is a world-renowned industrial designer; the ultra-thin MacBook Air, the glass and metal materials used in the iPhone 4, all come from Ive's design. Over the past 20 years at Apple, Jobs usually set the design direction, then Ive applied his inspiration to solidify the details, creating one classic Apple product after another.

After Jobs' death, we could clearly feel Ive's elevated status; he is now the one setting the design direction.

Last October, Apple announced that Scott Forstall, the former head of the operating system, would leave. In many subsequent reports, Forstall's departure was described as "taking the fall," being blamed for the poor experience of the first generation Siri and Apple Maps. Regardless of the truth, Forstall's exit effectively removed the last obstacle in front of Ive, concentrating the power of Apple's software and hardware design into his hands. From the "flattened" iOS7 that made its debut at this year's WWDC to the newly released plastic-bodied iPhone 5C, all come from Ive's creativity. Who else but Ive truly inherits Jobs' mantle?

Last week, doubts about Apple's new products filled the air, but most people haven't seen the actual iPhone 5C and iPhone 5S; they will officially meet consumers this Friday. This isn't me speaking well of Apple; on the contrary, I believe that for the "new Apple core" of Cook + Ive, the true challenge begins after the new products hit the market.

First, will the two products sell well? Which one sells better? The "scalpers" will provide the first round of answers. More importantly, although iOS7 was released three months ago, compared to the massive number of iPhone users, the beta version of iOS7 circulated only among enthusiasts, and most users haven't touched it. For them, the iPhone 5S with iOS7 feels like a completely new product compared to their old iPhones, requiring some readjustment in certain aspects.

What kind of process will this be? Will users accept it? Apart from Apple itself, every link in the supply chain, from suppliers to app developers, is highly attentive. Only after this battle is won can the status of the "new Apple core" be considered firmly established, and the stable post-Jobs era can truly begin.

The iPhone event will be held on September 10th.
