Four Great Classical Love Stories of Buddhism

by vinuu on 2011-05-22 13:15:29

(1) Who Was Your Previous Life Lover?

Once upon a time, there was a scholar who had agreed with his fiancée to get married on a certain day. But when that day came, his fiancée married someone else instead. The scholar was so heartbroken that he fell seriously ill and could not be helped by any means. His family watched helplessly as he lay dying. At this moment, a wandering monk passed by, learned about the situation, and decided to enlighten him.

The monk approached the scholar's bedside and took out a mirror for him to look into. In the mirror, the scholar saw a vast ocean, and on the beach lay a naked woman who had been murdered. A passerby glanced at her, shook his head, and walked away... Another passerby took off his clothes, covered the corpse, and then left... Yet another passerby came, dug a hole, and carefully buried the body...

Perplexed, the scholar saw the scene shift. He saw his own fiancée in her wedding chamber, the moment her husband lifted her veil... The scholar was confused.

The monk explained: "Do you see the female corpse on the beach? That was your fiancée's previous life. You were the second passerby who once gave her a piece of clothing. This lifetime, she fell in love with you to repay your kindness. However, the person she ultimately has to spend her entire life repaying is the last one who buried her, and that person is her current husband." Upon hearing this, the scholar suddenly understood, sat up from his bed with a swish, and was cured.

(2) Zhuer and Zicao

Long ago, there was a temple named Yuan Yin Temple. Every day, many people came to offer incense and worship Buddha, making it a very popular place. On the beam in front of the Yuan Yin Temple, a spider had woven a web. Due to being constantly exposed to the incense and sincere offerings, the spider gradually gained Buddhist nature. After over a thousand years of cultivation, its Buddhist nature increased significantly.

One day, the Buddha visited the Yuan Yin Temple. Seeing the thriving incense offerings, he was very pleased. As he was leaving the temple, he inadvertently looked up and saw the spider on the beam. The Buddha stopped and asked the spider: "We have met, which is quite an encounter. Let me ask you a question to see what insights you've gained after cultivating for more than a thousand years."

The spider was delighted to meet the Buddha and quickly agreed. The Buddha asked: "What is most precious in the world?" The spider thought for a moment and answered: "The most precious things in the world are 'what cannot be obtained' and 'what has been lost.'" The Buddha nodded and left.

Another thousand years passed, and the spider continued to cultivate on the beam of the Yuan Yin Temple, increasing its Buddhist nature even further. One day, the Buddha returned to the temple and asked the spider: "Are you doing well? Have you gained deeper insights into the question I asked you a thousand years ago?" The spider replied: "I still believe that the most precious things in the world are 'what cannot be obtained' and 'what has been lost.'" The Buddha said: "Think about it carefully, and I will come back to visit you again."

Another thousand years went by, and one day, a strong wind blew a drop of dew onto the spider's web. The spider gazed at the dewdrop, admiring its crystalline brilliance, and grew fond of it. Each day, the spider found joy in looking at the dewdrop, feeling it was the happiest few days in three thousand years. Suddenly, another strong wind blew, carrying the dewdrop away. The spider felt as if something was missing, experiencing loneliness and sorrow. At this moment, the Buddha appeared again and asked the spider: "Spider, have you thought deeply about the question: What is most precious in the world?" Remembering the dewdrop, the spider told the Buddha: "The most precious things in the world are 'what cannot be obtained' and 'what has been lost.'" The Buddha said: "Very well, since you have such an understanding, I will let you experience human life for a while."

Thus, the spider reincarnated into a wealthy official's family, becoming a rich young lady named Zhuer. Time flew, and soon Zhuer turned sixteen, blossoming into a graceful and beautiful young woman.

One day, Gan Lu, the newly crowned top scholar, was invited by the emperor to hold a celebratory banquet in the back garden. Many young ladies attended, including Zhuer and the emperor's youngest princess, Chang Feng. During the banquet, Gan Lu showcased his talent through poetry and literature, captivating all the young ladies present. However, Zhuer remained calm and unjealous, knowing that this was the karmic relationship granted by the Buddha.

A few days later, coincidentally, Zhuer accompanied her mother to offer incense and worship Buddha, and Gan Lu happened to be there with his mother as well. After offering incense and worshipping Buddha, the two elders began talking. Zhuer and Gan Lu went to the corridor to chat. Zhuer was happy, finally getting the chance to be with the one she loved, but Gan Lu did not show any affection towards her.

Zhuer asked Gan Lu: "Don't you remember the incident with the spider's web at Yuan Yin Temple sixteen years ago?" Gan Lu was surprised and said: "Miss Zhuer, you are beautiful and likable, but your imagination is too vivid." With that, he left with his mother.

After returning home, Zhuer wondered why the Buddha arranged this karmic relationship but didn't let Gan Lu remember the incident. Why did Gan Lu feel nothing for her? A few days later, the emperor issued an edict, ordering Gan Lu to marry Princess Chang Feng and Zhuer to marry Crown Prince Zicao. This news was like a thunderbolt to Zhuer; she couldn't understand why the Buddha treated her this way.

For several days, she neither ate nor drank, deeply pondering, her soul nearly departing her body. Crown Prince Zicao learned of this and rushed over, falling beside her bed and saying to the barely alive Zhuer: "That day, among all the young ladies in the back garden, I fell in love with you at first sight. I earnestly pleaded with my father, and he finally agreed. If you die, then I won't live either." Saying this, he picked up a sword, preparing to commit suicide.

At this moment, the Buddha appeared and spoke to Zhuer's soul: "Spider, have you ever thought about who brought the dewdrop (Gan Lu) to you? It was the wind (Princess Chang Feng) who brought it, and eventually, it was the wind that carried it away. Gan Lu belongs to Princess Chang Feng; he is just a passing episode in your life. Crown Prince Zicao was a small grass plant in front of Yuan Yin Temple thousands of years ago. He observed you for three thousand years and admired you for three thousand years, but you never lowered your head to notice him. Spider, let me ask you again: What is most precious in the world?"

Upon hearing these truths, the spider seemed to gain sudden enlightenment. She told the Buddha: "The most precious things in the world are not 'what cannot be obtained' and 'what has been lost,' but rather the happiness that can be grasped now!"

As soon as she finished speaking, the Buddha disappeared, and Zhuer's soul returned to her body. She opened her eyes, saw Crown Prince Zicao about to commit suicide, immediately knocked the sword away, and embraced him passionately...

(3) A Thousand-Year Wait

There was a young and beautiful girl from a wealthy family, talented in many ways. Her family's doorstep was almost worn down by matchmakers, yet she refused to marry because she was always waiting for her destined lover.

One day, while enjoying herself at a temple fair, she caught a glimpse of a young man amidst the bustling crowd. She knew instantly that he was the person she had been waiting for, but the chaotic crowd prevented her from approaching him. Eventually, she helplessly watched as her beloved disappeared into the crowd. Afterwards, the girl searched everywhere for him, but it was as if he had vanished from the earth, never to appear again. Disheartened, she prayed devoutly to the Buddha morning and evening, hoping to see the man again. Her sincerity moved the Buddha, who then appeared to fulfill her wish.

The Buddha asked her: "Do you want to see that man again?"

"Yes, even a glance would suffice!"

"If you must give up everything you currently have, including your loving family and happy life?"

"I am willing," the girl declared resolutely.

"You must cultivate for five hundred years to see him once. Will you regret it?"

"I will not regret it," she stated firmly.

Thus, the girl transformed into a large rock, lying in the wilderness for four hundred and ninety-nine years under the sun and rain. Though she did not find it arduous, the fact that she hadn't seen anyone for over four hundred years, without any hope, nearly drove her to despair. In the final year, a stone mining team arrived, selected her, and chiseled her into a stone slab, transporting her into the city. A bridge was being constructed there, and thus, the girl became a railing on the bridge. On the first day the bridge was completed, the girl saw the man she had waited five hundred years for! He hurriedly crossed the bridge, naturally unaware that a stone railing was watching him intently. Once again, the man disappeared.

The Buddha's voice echoed again: "Are you satisfied?"

"No! Why am I only the bridge railing? If I were in the middle of the bridge, I could have touched him or brushed against him!"

"Want to touch him? Then you must cultivate for another five hundred years!"

"I am willing!"

"Will it be hard? Will you regret it?"

"I will not regret it!"

This time, the girl turned into a large tree standing by a busy road. Every day, many people passed by, and the girl watched anxiously, but each time her hopes were dashed. Had it not been for the cultivation of the previous five hundred years, the girl would have collapsed long ago! As the days passed, the girl's heart gradually calmed. She knew that he wouldn't appear until the last day. On the final day of another five hundred years, the girl knew he would come, but surprisingly, her heart wasn't excited anymore. He finally came! Still wearing the white robe she liked best, his face as handsome as ever, the girl gazed at him with longing. This time, he didn't rush past. Because it was extremely hot, he noticed the big tree by the side of the road and decided to rest beneath it. He leaned against the roots of the tree, closed his eyes, and fell asleep. The girl touched him, and he was right next to her! But she couldn't express her thousand-year-long yearning to him. All she could do was gather the shade as much as possible to protect him from the scorching sun. The man only napped briefly because he had other things to do. Before leaving, he patted the dust off his robes, glanced back at the tree, lightly touched the trunk, and then walked away without looking back!

At the moment the man disappeared, the Buddha reappeared.

"Do you still want to become his wife? Then you must continue to cultivate."

The girl calmly interrupted the Buddha: "I do want to, but it's unnecessary."

"Oh?"

"This is already good enough. Loving him doesn't necessarily mean becoming his wife."

"Oh!"

"Has his current wife suffered like me?" The girl mused.

The Buddha nodded slightly.

The girl smiled faintly: "I can endure it too, but it's unnecessary."

At this moment, the girl seemed to notice the Buddha sighing slightly.

The girl was somewhat surprised: "Does the Buddha also have worries?"

"So this is good. A boy can avoid waiting for you for a thousand years. To catch a glimpse of you, he has already cultivated for two thousand years." The Buddha's face lit up with a smile.

(4) A Merchant's Four Wives

In ancient times, there was a man who had four wives. The fourth wife was deeply loved by her husband, who stayed with her whether sitting or standing. The third wife was obtained after much effort, and the husband often sweet-talked her, though not as much as he doted on the fourth wife. The second wife frequently met with her husband, consoling each other like friends. They were content being together, and missed each other when apart. The first wife was like a servant, handling all the heavy work at home, enduring various hardships without complaint, and held little place in her husband's heart.

One day, the man planned to travel abroad for a long journey and asked his four wives, "Will you go with me?"

The fourth wife replied, "I don't want to go with you."

The husband was upset by her lack of affection and turned to the third wife, asking, "Can you accompany me?"

The third wife responded, "If your favorite fourth wife doesn't want to go with you, why should I?"

The husband then called the second wife and said, "Can you accompany me on this trip abroad?" The second wife replied, "I am grateful for your kindness and can send you to the outskirts of the city. But if you want me to accompany you abroad, I'm sorry, I can't do that."

The husband also resented the second wife's lack of sentiment and turned to the first wife, saying, "I am going on a trip abroad. Will you go with me?"

The first wife replied, "I left my parents and entrusted myself to you. Whether in joy or sorrow, life or death, I will not leave your side. No matter where you go or how far, I will definitely go with you." While the three wives he usually cherished refused to go with him, the first wife, whom he had never truly valued, was the only one willing to accompany him. Thus, he had no choice but to take her along and leave the city.

In reality, the foreign land he was heading to was the world of death. The man with four wives symbolized human consciousness.

The fourth wife represented the physical body. Humans cherish their bodies just as the husband cherished his fourth wife. But when the end comes, life ends, and the soul carries alone the blessings and sins of the present life, leaving the body behind, unable to follow.

The third wife symbolized worldly wealth. No matter how hard-earned treasures may be, they cannot be taken with us when we die.

The second wife stood for parents, spouse, children, siblings, relatives, friends, and servants. People in life love each other, miss each other, and find it hard to part. When death approaches, they cry and mourn, sending the deceased to graves outside the city. Not long after, they forget and return to the hustle and bustle of life.

The first wife symbolizes the heart, which stays with us throughout life and death. It is closely related to us, yet we easily overlook it, focusing instead on our fleeting physical form.

These four stories carry profound philosophies, evoking sighs and reflections on the complexities of human love, which can easily entangle and confuse us. In truth, if we can see through the essence, it boils down to matters of gain and loss. As the Buddha in the story of Zhuer and Zicao suggested, cherishing the happiness before us and valuing love might be the best outcome. Love pursued by humans is not achieved through one-sided desire or silent waiting alone. There is destiny in marriage, and once encountered, one must learn to seize and cherish it.
