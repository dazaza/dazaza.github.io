Dianping: A Bone the Giants Can't Chew, Living Outside the Comfort Zone

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 18:15:22

Dianping.com CEO Zhang Tao

Written by Yuan Yin, edited by Yang Jing, photographed by Xiao Nan

Dianping.com CEO Zhang Tao has once again postponed the company's IPO.

In 2011, Dianping received a $100 million Series C funding round from Sequoia Capital and others, valuing the company at $1 billion. According to convention, it should have chosen an IPO within the following one to two years, but it didn't. Instead, in 2012, it raised another $60 million in a Series D round.

Given Dianping's scale and development momentum, going public wouldn't be difficult. However, Zhang Tao feels that an IPO now would be too costly. He breaks down the large internet (PC + mobile) into four elements—people, information, goods, and services—with each connection point offering the opportunity for giants to emerge. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, and Alibaba connects people with goods. The space between people and services is the seat for the fourth giant. Since 2003, Dianping has been accumulating data for this exact purpose, finally waiting for the explosion of mobile internet, where phones merge "location" and "service." Following Zhang Tao's logic, no company seems more suited to be imagined as the fourth giant than Dianping.

However, the outside world doesn't see it this way. In recent months, with BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent) making frequent acquisition moves, Dianping has become a hypothetical acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition target; regardless of which family name it takes on from BAT, it could help them achieve great success early. On the other hand, Dianping's size pales in comparison to BAT, and it's still too early to predict its potential to become a giant. Moreover, whether it's Tencent WeChat's public platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after investing in AutoNavi and Meituan, all are targeting Dianping's territory—local life services focused on food, drink, entertainment, and leisure.

Before proving the following two points, Dianping going public would equal a "discounted sale." First, Zhang Tao needs time to prove that what Dianping is doing now, BAT cannot do. Second, Dianping is about to enter the ultimate battle of group buying. If it gains the top position, its valuation could increase several times over.

2013 is Dianping's "decisive year." For speculation and rumors, Zhang Tao pays no heed. He is clear: "The internet has indeed entered an era of great integration, but Dianping will not be acquired."

Zhang Tao categorizes companies that might be acquired into three types. The first type has platform attributes but is better suited to develop within the system of a giant, such as maps and emails. These products can easily attract a large number of users but struggle to find an independent business model. Others, like browsers, can make money, but the revenue earned through navigation services is far less than their strategic value to large companies. "Giants are willing to spend money to maintain an email product because it helps with branding and user retention, but there hasn't been any successful standalone email company." The second type involves industry mergers, like in the video industry, where acquisitions expand market share. The third type is when a company encounters financial difficulties and sells itself to survive.

Now Meituan has already been brought under Alibaba's wing. In theory, Dianping should quickly seek a "backer" to avoid defeat due to the rapid expansion of competitors fueled by capital. Zhang Tao, however, says, "The giants' help to us is limited. We do need users, but execution is more important. Mergers and acquisitions would reduce our execution ability and could even lead to marginalization within a large company." Furthermore, he aims to keep Dianping at the most precise "distance" from the giants. Getting too close to any one of them would be akin to "taking sides," making Dianping an enemy to the others.

Thus, logically, the acquisition of Dianping has reached a dead end. Zhang Tao counters, "Why would we take their money? We don't lack funds; what we need are resources. If a giant is willing to invest a small amount and give us many resources, I could consider it. But would they? Would a giant provide resources if they only own a small stake?"

Weapon One: Dirty and Hard Work

In mobile internet, opportunities for companies that only create pure internet products are becoming fewer. New opportunities come from those that intersect with traditional industries. Zhang Tao believes that life services mainly revolve around food, drink, entertainment, and leisure. Other细分 markets, such as ride-hailing, can form good business models, but it’s hard for them to grow into giants.

For food, drink, entertainment, and leisure, user demands are simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is the core of life services, which is exactly where Dianping started. Whether in content, member accumulation, or brand, newcomers will find it hard to surpass Dianping.

The most representative product form for discounts is group buying. Now group buying has entered the championship contention phase, and Dianping plans to battle Meituan in 2013. This is a winner-takes-all market. As long as there are two strong players coexisting, the industry's gross profit margin will be reduced, making stable profitability impossible.

A foreseeable future includes reservations. Reservations can form a true online-to-offline closed loop, but this must be built on the foundation of Dianping having absolute advantages in both information and discounts. Otherwise, creating a closed loop would be difficult. In 2012, Dianping began experimenting with reservations. Zhang Tao said, "Currently, both user and merchant feedback is good, but if we were to focus solely on reservations, it would be hard to solve the chicken-or-egg problem."

Of course, life services also present some heavier opportunities. For example, food delivery is a rigid demand for users and can help merchants increase sales. In the past couple of years, food delivery companies like "To Home Gourmet Society" have flourished. However, the core of food delivery is delivery, which means delivering within a very short time frame while solving issues like soup spilling. The logistics requirements are higher than those of fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang Tao said, "If food delivery is executed well, there is still opportunity, but this is harder dirty and hard work, and Dianping is already doing enough dirty and hard work."

If judged by age, Dianping is already an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has painstakingly accumulated merchant profiles and user reviews, which Zhang Tao calls "dirty and hard work." This primitive growth model once limited its speed, but the past pain has become Dianping's core barrier today. It can be said that currently, aside from Meituan, Dianping has no real competitors in the life services field.

Nevertheless, he still needs to be wary of BAT's "clutches." Baidu's involvement is the most direct. Baidu Maps already has over 100 million users, and users can use the map to search for nearby dining and other life services. Tencent, considered to have secured a ticket to mobile internet, has launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest goods trading platform, and expanding goods from physical items to services is logical. Moreover, it has included Meituan and AutoNavi in its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there are no signs of explosive growth in reality or data," Zhang Tao said. Brand positioning is crucial; the key is which product users think of first when they want to eat, drink, play, or relax.

Besides, in the vast field of life services, competition among giants is bound to cross over, and no one can completely monopolize the sector. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained on Weibo and WeChat. Zhang Tao feels that this makes Dianping safer instead, "In niche areas like car rentals and business travel, if giants divert their massive users, they could easily overwhelm existing companies."

As for Alibaba, which invested in AutoNavi, invading Dianping's territory won't be that simple either. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, which can be categorized no matter how peculiar. A user's experience of a meal depends on various factors such as the day's environment, waitstaff, and chefs. Managing merchants cannot rely on systems and tools like Alibaba does; it requires building a 'ground force' managed by people.

"All three giants want to go this route, and there is room for imagination, but this is not a highway; it's a rugged mountain road," Zhang Tao believes. First, providing life services necessitates deploying ground teams at various levels, something BAT lacks the genes and capability for managing offline teams. Second, they lack the intention; life services require focused effort and aren't something a small team can accomplish, and profits come slowly. "It's hard for a company to excel at two important things simultaneously, let alone needing to consolidate its advantage in the original field."

Weapon Two: Small Boats Are Easier to Turn

In 2011, when Dianping secured $100 million in financing, Zhang Tao told the outside world that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, times change fast, and before Dianping went public, his comparable objects fell from grace one by one.

He wasn’t particularly anxious about it. "They failed to become big companies because they lacked offline teams, and now they all want to become the American version of Dianping."

The current path is one Zhang Tao has explored step by step. When he felt that selling keyword ads and discount coupons was too outdated, the emergence of group buying caught his eye. Initially, like the Americans, he understood group buying as a marketing platform, one deal per day. But as orders increased, he realized group buying had transaction platform characteristics. Thus, he envisioned Dianping's future as a life service transaction platform, and the two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were precisely used for Dianping's transformation from an information platform to a transaction platform.

Without this "genetic modification," Zhang Tao might not have had the confidence to compete for the fourth major position today. But genetic modification sounds easy; in reality, it's equivalent to Dianping's second entrepreneurship, a process that was extremely painful.

First, compared to selling ads alone, the gross margin of a transaction platform decreases, requiring higher cost control and efficiency. Second, a transaction platform demands stronger inter-departmental communication capabilities.

In the past, to maintain Dianping's impartiality and independence as an information platform, the content team and the sales team were completely separated. Zhang Tao even deliberately established a "firewall" between departments—no matter how important the client, the information on the platform couldn't be modified for sales purposes, leading to some mutual hostility between the content and sales teams. Now, as a transaction platform, the editorial and sales teams need deep communication to create a good marketing plan, which requires not just planning but also editorial cooperation. He said, "Maintaining information quality is still important, but there's no need to reinforce animosity; we need to dismantle the firewall."

Though editors can logically accept this, individual behavior and values have inertia, and when combined across the entire company, that inertia becomes even greater. To address this, Zhang Tao specifically held several meetings to encourage more communication between departments. After a few months, an editor manager casually mentioned to a sales manager, "I used to think you were my enemy, but now I realize you're my colleague."

The sales department also needed genetic modification. To promote conversion, Zhang Tao cleverly gave the offline team a dual identity. First, for merchants, salespeople should become marketing consultants, not just selling products but helping merchants devise marketing strategies. Second, for the company, marketing consultants are the company's buyers. Dianping is like a supermarket; not only must the product quality be good, but the variety of goods must be extensive. Marketing consultants should aim to help users purchase group-buying products. Of course, a dual identity will inevitably generate a series of complex issues. For frontline staff, this is quite headache-inducing, as they must weigh the balance themselves.

Substantially, Dianping's "dual identity" is very similar to Alibaba's division into 25 business units. By decentralizing decision-making to the front lines, the company can respond faster, but management difficulty increases. Similarly, compared to frontline employees, Dianping's management team suffered more after the genetic modification.

Each year, Zhang Tao breaks down Dianping's corporate goals into various departments, which are then further broken down by senior executives layer by layer, but breaking down isn't a simple addition or subtraction. Li Jing, co-founder of Dianping, responsible for the sales team, sometimes finds that unresolved merchant needs are due to sales problems, affecting sales. Thus, Li Jing created a new position, setting up communication specialists who gather information daily from marketing consultants, submit it to other departments, and track feedback. He often tells his team, embracing changes doesn't mean liking changes; no one likes changes, and everyone feels uncomfortable when encountering them. "All employees must go through discomfort zones, including myself."

Numbers reflect the "heaviness" of Dianping's execution team. Currently, out of more than 3000 employees, over 2000 work offline. The ground force directly communicates with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group buys, and bundled marketing solutions.

For this core team that resists BAT's clutches, Dianping has a unique management evaluation system.

They are not called "sales;" from the first day, their business cards read "Marketing Planning Consultant." Many traditional merchants are still at the stage of watching news and chatting on QQ, lagging behind Taobao merchants by at least three to five years in their understanding of the internet. Marketing planning consultants must inform merchants of online users' needs and how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing said, "Even a high-end restaurant, the marketing consultant wouldn't suggest a grouponsale for abalone or shark fin, perhaps suggesting other dishes instead, because online users and offline users have different needs."

An outstanding marketing consultant must be a versatile player. They need to explain to restaurant owners and hair salon owners the essential differences between discounts and group buying; which discount suits their positioning better. If facing a hot pot restaurant owner, the marketing consultant must know whether it's the off-season or peak season, which products' costs rise during this season, which costs fall, and propose a group-buying menu that both merchants and users like.

This places high demands on marketing consultants, requiring them to be not only internet marketing experts but also industry experts. Li Jing said, "I must admit, it's impossible for every consultant to be a double expert, so we set star ratings for consultants." Like hotels, marketing consultants range from one-star to five-stars, with the best being superstars called Super Stars.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline employees are not tech-savvy graduates from prestigious universities, making it challenging for them to understand internet products. Dianping has established a knowledge base for marketing consultants, containing knowledge points and relevant cases, accessible to both new and old employees. If encountering new problems, employees can ask questions in the knowledge base, and thousands of consultants nationwide will help answer them.

Many knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees based on practical experiences. Dianping has set up a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, rewarding employees who help others the most each quarter. Each quarter, Dianping conducts skill assessments for employees, with results directly tied to promotions.

Compared to internet knowledge, industry knowledge is harder to acquire. Some experiences are summarized by traditional merchants after decades of practice in the industry. A Super Star marketing consultant told Li Jing that hair salons should avoid offering wash-cut-blow packages because men have no motivation to buy, and women prefer blow-drying without washing and cutting together. Li Jing was astonished, "We men would never think of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing consultants are divided not only by city but also by industries such as catering, beauty, and leisure. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is even subdivided into hot pot, Western cuisine, buffet, with a marketing consultant responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing consultants can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, making merchants less resistant when selling products," he said.

Each marketing group must hold their own case-sharing sessions: what difficulties a certain type of merchant encountered, what marketing needs they had, which company products were paired to solve the issue, and the effect. Outstanding cases are reported upwards, and Dianping officially shares one case each week.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition called "Entrepreneur Winner." In the first three months of employment, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products, but listening to merchants sharing experiences. If responsible for a hot pot restaurant, after three months, they must submit a business plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hot Pot Restaurant Owner." Similar to graduation, employees defend their plans on-site, and industry supervisors evaluate the winners, rewarding them accordingly.

Strategically speaking, whether it's Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, their objectives are quite similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O are familiar to anyone with a basic understanding of the industry, and their spoken words are almost identical. "The era where storytelling and concept creation could lead to success is over. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model lies in management and execution, not strategy," Zhang Tao stated.
