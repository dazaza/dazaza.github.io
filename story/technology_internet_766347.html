In the crowded mid-range market, is there still room for the iPhone 5C?

by anonymous on 2013-09-11 14:06:33

According to foreign media predictions, the price of the Iphone 5C might be around $400-500 (approximately 2500-3000 RMB), which happens to be a price range that domestic manufacturers are very eager to occupy. The phone market around 2500 RMB is the largest potential consumer segment and also the most competitive area among various mobile phone manufacturers. With an appropriate psychological price, extremely high cost-performance ratio, and a vast potential user base, one should not underestimate the astonishing sales volume achieved by phones in this price range.

In other words, while domestic manufacturers clustered in this price range are trying to move into the mid-to-high-end market to align with the wealthy and stylish crowd, does Apple's decision to compete in the mid-to-low-end market, catering to the so-called "diaosi" (underdog) group, make sense? Compared to domestic manufacturers who are already experienced and familiar with the mid-to-low-end market, what advantages does Apple have?

Apple's market share in China has been declining under the impact of various domestic smartphone manufacturers. According to data from market research firm Canalys, Apple only accounted for 4.8% of China's second-quarter smartphone shipments.

The launch of the low-cost version of the iphone5C is seen as Apple's emphasis on markets like China and other Asian regions, targeting the large base of mid-to-low-end consumers. To some extent, entering the mid-to-low-end market is a reluctant choice for Apple at present, aimed at attracting more potential users to abandon Android and join the Apple ecosystem. Once they enter the kingdom of the Apple ecosystem, Apple is confident in cultivating a new generation of "Guo Fen" (Apple fans).

Direct confrontation with domestic manufacturers in hardware + software + ecosystem competition

Let's take a look at the local competitors in this price range: Xiaomi, Huawei, ZTE, Meizu, HTC, Lenovo, Coolpad, vivo. Domestic manufacturers have the advantage of being native to the region, performing better than foreign giants in channels, pricing, developing partners, consumer relations, media relations, etc. Lastly, domestic manufacturers can design products more tailored to the needs of local users.

After several years of rapid growth in smartphones, the updates and technological developments in hardware have reached their peak. For example, features originally not designed for small-screen devices such as large screens, ultra-thin designs, quad-core processors, etc., have all been incorporated into mobile phones.

Outstanding specifications, fashionable product appearances, and high-performance cost-effectiveness have brought smartphone development to a bottleneck phase. Now, the competition between phones has shifted to the entire ecosystem. Domestic manufacturers struggling in the low-price segment are also striving to build their own mobile phone ecosystems; companies like Xiaomi and vivo are taking this path.

One focus of the ecosystem competition is the user experience of the operating system and the differentiation of software applications, such as the extended development of voice and touch screen control, the improvement of maps and location services, the construction of mobile payment systems, etc. Features like the rumored fingerprint recognition technology of the iPhone 5, the micro-location function iBeacons of iOS7, and the colorful shells of the iPhone 5C are considered highlights of this new product launch.

Only a complete ecosystem is something others cannot imitate. How to keep users within the ecosystem is something domestic manufacturers need to learn from Apple. An ecosystem is composed of products + applications + content. Leveraging this mature ecosystem, Apple can effortlessly venture into areas such as smart home appliances, smart homes, and smart cars, where objects and people are interconnected.
