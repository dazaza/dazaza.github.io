Samsung Electronics Seeks Obama Administration Support to Overturn Apple Sales Ban

by anonymous on 2013-10-08 18:37:49

Samsung Electronics will seek to gain the support of President Obama in rejecting the sales ban that Apple has applied for regarding its products. Previously, Apple products were judged to be banned from import into the US due to infringing on Samsung Electronics' patents, but this sales ban was subsequently rejected by Obama.

Unless the White House overturns the previously issued ban, starting at midnight on October 8th, some specific older models of Samsung Electronics' products will be prohibited from being sold in the US market. Previously, some of Samsung's products were judged to infringe on two of Apple's patents and were banned from being sold in the US market.

The Obama administration had faced such a situation before. On October 3rd, the Obama administration overturned a ban that Samsung Electronics had won, which involved older Apple iPhone 4 and iPad 2 3G models. Bloomberg's statistical data shows that Samsung Electronics and Apple are currently the two largest companies in the global smartphone market with a production value reaching $279.9 billion. Samsung Electronics had previously stated that blocking one competitor's product while allowing another's to remain on the US market is a form of favoritism towards domestic manufacturers.

Jim Altman, a lawyer at Foster, Murphy, Altman & Nickel representing Asian companies at the US International Trade Commission, said, "Samsung Electronics will undoubtedly feel frustrated; the company originally thought it had achieved a major victory over Apple. But the US President overturned the ruling. Regarding Apple's new victory, he also said 'unaffected by others' opinions'."

Samsung Electronics announced on October 4th that driven by its smartphone business, the company's third-quarter profit hit a record high again. The company also stated that any sales ban would have an extremely limited impact on its mobile phone sales. Bloomberg's statistical data also shows that Samsung Electronics held a 32% share of the global smartphone market in the second quarter of this year.

Favoritism and Protectionism

Samsung Electronics had stated on August 28th, "In this smartphone battle, the world is watching what kind of treatment Samsung Electronics will receive from the US government. The US government has an inescapable responsibility to avoid favoritism and protect American enterprises."

Michael Froman, the US Trade Representative, said in an interview with Bloomberg TV last Saturday that he had not yet decided whether to issue a stay order to Samsung Electronics regarding this matter. He said that he had "clearly expressed" to Samsung Electronics and the South Korean government that this decision "has no connection to the nationality of the parties involved," and is entirely based on "the policy judgment of the patent and the applicability of the sales ban in this case."

Altman said that foreign companies usually worry that the US legal system favors American companies. He said, "I have never seen cases of 'xenophobia' or 'protection of American products'."

The President of the United States has the authority to overturn the sales ban issued by the International Trade Commission based on national policies of the US. The final decision of the US government cannot be appealed.

Functions and Product Features

Jung Dong Joon, a patent attorney at SU Intellectual Property based in Seoul, said that Samsung Electronics is unlikely to win in the US.

He said, "During the shutdown of the US government, the Obama administration is unlikely to overturn the previous sales ban issued by the International Trade Commission. Samsung Electronics can take some comfort in the fact that the affected products are mainly older models."

The situations of Apple and Samsung Electronics in their litigation cases are not the same. When overturning the sales ban on iPhone products, the Obama administration mainly did so to limit industry standard patents to hinder competitors. Samsung Electronics' patents involve standards for transmitting data over networks.

In the case of Samsung Electronics, Apple's patents are considered product features that distinguish the iPhone from other smartphones. Samsung Electronics may lobby the US government, arguing that other public policies are equally important.

Complex Devices

Samsung Electronics' other arguments focus on two main issues - sales bans should not be imposed when involving complex devices with one or two functions; the broad wording of the International Trade Commission could lead to non-infringing products being blocked at the border. Samsung Electronics told the US Trade Representative, "Courts, scholars, and industry insiders all believe that issuing a sales ban on the entire product because of one or a few features gives disproportionate benefits to patent holders and causes consumer losses."

The US Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit is considering the issue of banning multifunctional product sales after the jury found some functions to infringe on patents. The International Trade Commission, which protects the US market from unfair trade practices, typically does not take the same actions as courts. After ruling in 2012 that Samsung Electronics infringed on Apple's patents, the federal court in California still allowed Samsung Electronics' products to be sold in the market.

Product Design

Apple stated that the company must prevent competitors from copying these ideas to retain users and attract new ones through the appearance of the iPhone. Apple told the appeal court that Samsung Electronics' stance is contradictory. The company has been promoting that the copied functions are not important, but on the other hand, they continue to use these functions.

The International Trade Commission found that since the patents involved in Apple's sales ban covered functions rather than standards, Samsung Electronics could successfully innovate around these designs. These Samsung Electronics products should be freely allowed entry into the US market.

Samsung Electronics stated, "In fact, the affected products have very low sales." In the third quarter ending September 30th, driven by strong demand for low-end Galaxy smartphones in China, India, and the Middle East, Samsung Electronics' operating profit increased to 10.1 trillion won (approximately $9.4 billion). Samsung Electronics, which also produces washing machines, computer chips, and televisions, did not divide performance by department.

Extremely Limited Impact

Besides being Apple's biggest competitor, Samsung Electronics is also one of Apple's largest accessory suppliers. Eventually, the two companies may reach an agreement on patent issues.

Jeong Woo Sung, a patent attorney at Lim@Jeong Patent Law Firm in Korea, said, "The market changes quite quickly, and patent litigation takes a long time, which is why the impact of the trial results on market sales will be extremely limited. Samsung Electronics might feel embarrassed, but the company must find a way out and resolve the long-term patent disputes with Apple."
