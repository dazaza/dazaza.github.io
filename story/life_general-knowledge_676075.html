What to do when babies have hiccups

It is relatively common for babies to have hiccups, especially in the spring when the heating is turned off. This is mainly due to the diaphragm going into spasm after the baby has been exposed to cold stimulation, which causes continuous hiccups. There are several reasons why babies get hiccups: First, newborns generally have thin abdominal walls and poor adaptability to temperature changes. Second, inexperienced parents, seeing their baby's tender limbs, may be hesitant to move or touch them, leading to slow and overly cautious actions during feeding, bathing, and diaper changing. This can cause the baby to be exposed to the external environment for too long and get chilled. Additionally, during bottle feeding, if the feeding time is too long, the milk at the end of the feeding may become cold. In breastfeeding, if the baby feeds too quickly, they may swallow both milk and air, causing excessive ingestion of cool air that stimulates diaphragmatic spasm, resulting in continuous hiccups. It is important to identify the cause of the hiccups to remove the trigger and solve the problem at its root. Therefore, in infant care, preparation should be done in advance, with quick yet gentle movements, not prolonging feeding times, and gently patting the baby's back after feeding to allow swallowed air to escape. In summary, it is crucial to avoid stimulating diaphragmatic spasm to reduce hiccups.

What are hiccups?

Medically, hiccups are referred to as "singultus." Temporary hiccups can occur after exposure to cold, overeating, eating too quickly, or consuming dry hard food, but this is not considered a disease, so there is no need to see a doctor.

Why do we hiccup?

Between our chest and abdomen, there is a thick muscular membrane shaped like a cap called the diaphragm, which separates the chest and abdomen. Like other body organs, the diaphragm has nerve distribution and blood supply. When stimuli that trigger hiccups are transmitted to the brain, the brain sends instructions causing the diaphragm to undergo intermittent and spasmodic contractions, thus resulting in hiccups.

Continuous or stubborn hiccups are often caused by urgent conditions such as brain diseases, uremia, diabetic ketoacidosis, etc. Many serious illnesses can also lead to persistent hiccups. Particularly noteworthy is that if a critically ill person experiences persistent hiccups, it often indicates a poor prognosis.
