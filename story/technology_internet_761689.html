Microsoft says that in three years' time Windows Phone will be the biggest smartphone platform in South Africa

by geekzhang on 2013-05-06 21:47:44

According to the evening news on May 6th, Beijing time, Gustavo Fuchs, Microsoft's Windows Phone business director for the Middle East and Africa region, said in an interview that Microsoft plans to surpass BlackBerry and Android to become the largest smartphone provider in South Africa before 2016.Microsoft plans to launch a variety of Windows Phone smartphones at different price levels in South Africa to attract users from all strata of this largest African economy. Currently, the average annual income per household in South Africa is about $13,500.The research company A.T. Kearney estimates that within the next five years, Africa will become the fastest-growing mobile phone market globally in terms of new mobile phone users. Therefore, Microsoft hopes to expand its mobile business here. Currently, Apple's iOS and Google's Android collectively hold nearly 90% of the global smartphone market share.Fuchs said in a telephone interview in Johannesburg, South Africa: Due to the low penetration rate of smartphones, there is hope for Windows Phones to become the first smartphone for consumers here. Therefore, the snowball effect of smartphones here has just begun.A.T. Kearney estimates that by 2015, the number of mobile phone users in Africa will reach 900 million, accounting for 85% of the total population, compared to 73% last year. In contrast, the development speed of mobile phones in many mature markets in Europe, North America, and Asia has slowed down because the mobile phone penetration rate has even exceeded 100%.Strong demandIDC data shows that in the fourth quarter of last year, the mobile phone shipment volume in the Africa and Middle East regions was 59 million units, an increase of 16% quarter-over-quarter.Data from Deloitte shows that in the 18 months prior to June 2012, the number of active smartphone users of MTN Group, the largest mobile operator in Africa, doubled to 4.4 million. From 2010 to the end of 2012, the number of smartphone users of competitor Vodacom Group increased from 3.1 million to 5.8 million.Deloitte stated that large screens, touch interfaces, and higher costs for accessing the internet in other ways have driven the development of the smartphone market in South Africa.Data from research company Canalys shows that in 2012, BlackBerry's share of the South African smartphone market was 51%, slightly lower than 55% in 2011. The market share of Android doubled to 34%. Meanwhile, Microsoft's Windows Phone market share increased from zero in 2011 to 2%.Benefiting from BlackBerryRecently, however, Microsoft has developed rapidly, with its market share in South Africa reaching 10%. Fuchs said this is mainly due to the launch of the new platform, Windows Phone 8, in November last year.Fuchs said that Microsoft has benefited from the challenges faced by BlackBerry. Fuchs noted that applications like WhatsApp imitate BlackBerry's free messaging service, thus weakening BlackBerry's dominance in South Africa.However, BlackBerry spokesperson El Loubser said that BlackBerry will continue to introduce new smartphones in South Africa at prices that consumers can afford. Loubser stated in an email declaration: BBM (BlackBerry Messenger) has achieved great success in South Africa. Among all mobile phone users in South Africa, the proportion of users using BBM functions has increased from 3% 18 months ago to 17% today.Other marketsFuchs said that while expanding in the South African market, Microsoft will also expand its business in other African markets, such as Kenya and Nigeria. Fuchs said that the smartphone penetration rate in Nigeria doubles every year, and the current penetration rate is 10%. In Kenya, Nokia launched the Lumia phone in February this year, which quickly sold out.Fuchs said: Kenya is not just a single market but a hub. There may be some challenges in terms of GDP and price, but this market will become a cultural driving force for the East African market. What happens in Kenya will eventually extend to Tanzania or Uganda.Bloomberg data shows that as of the end of 2012, Nigeria had 113 million mobile phone users, and Kenya had 31.2 million. Fuchs said that with more and more African consumers switching from feature phones to smartphones, there is enough room here for multiple manufacturers to develop simultaneously.Fuchs said: Here, what matters is not competing with other manufacturers, but convincing consumers that compared to feature phones, smartphones are a better choice.
