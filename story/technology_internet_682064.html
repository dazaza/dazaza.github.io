Is overtime pay subject to individual income tax?

by qingian007 on 2012-02-28 17:12:39

According to the provisions of the Rest Law, overtime pay must be provided during statutory holidays. However, in reality, the implementation of overtime pay varies greatly; in some places, industries, and even units, it is not implemented at all. In public-owned enterprises and most service industries, the distribution of overtime pay has become an empty promise. A hairdresser working in Wuyang New City, Guangzhou, frankly said that there is no such thing as overtime pay in their industry, only a base salary plus commission, and holidays are precisely their busiest times: [Too busy to even think about asking for triple pay.]

Paying taxes according to law is a citizen's duty. According to individual income tax regulations, overtime pay must also be taxed. If it should be paid, then pay it; not paying it would be illegal. However, who will ensure the payment of overtime wages? If one works overtime but does not receive overtime pay, how can they pay taxes? Without the skin, where will the hair attach?

If you don't receive your overtime pay, how should you claim it? Lawyer Zhou Jianqiang from Guangdong Defa Law Firm suggests first making a request to the responsible person in your unit. If negotiations fail, then file a complaint with the Labor Bureau and request arbitration from the Labor Arbitration Committee.

The lawyer's suggestion is good, but filing complaints comes with many difficulties. Although applying for labor dispute arbitration no longer incurs arbitration fees, handling related arbitration matters yourself requires a significant amount of time and mental effort. If you entrust a lawyer to handle it, you need to pay legal fees. Who has so much time and energy to protect their rights? For just a few hundred yuan, you might end up losing your job. This is the real mentality of many people, and this is what many people actually do!

When laws exist but are not followed, enforcement is lax, and violations are not investigated, this is the root problem of the Chinese system. In the issue of overtime pay, this problem is particularly prominent. If the issue of overtime pay is not resolved, how can we talk about taxing overtime pay? If the tax department merely sits in the office collecting taxes, will money fall from the sky? The issue of taxing overtime pay is not a "chicken or egg" problem; rather, there must first be overtime pay before there can be taxation on overtime pay.

Because of this, the tax department should cooperate with relevant labor departments, putting more effort and sweat into resolving the issue of overtime pay. It is not only solving wage problems for overtime workers but also creating revenue for themselves. If overtime pay were fully implemented 100%, taxing overtime pay would certainly not be a problem. Conversely, if overtime pay becomes an empty promise, taxing overtime pay can only remain an empty promise as well!
