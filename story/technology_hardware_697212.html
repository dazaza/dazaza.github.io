If we say that the huge profits of high-tech enterprises trigger incentives for the depth of economic structural adjustment, then what is the key to the development of high-tech enterprises?

Recently, James, a well-known American businessman and Vice President of Career Strategy and Business Development, was in Shanghai to participate in China’s LED Lighting Forum organized by Science and Technology Daily. A reporter interviewed him from the perspective of U.S. corporate executives to understand their views on the development of high-tech enterprises.

James commented that areas rich in talent have been regarded as innovation hubs and the development centers of the U.S. semiconductor and electronics industry. He mentioned places like North Carolina's Research Triangle Park when talking about U.S. science and technology parks. According to James, these parks played an important role in stimulating economic growth in the United States during the 1990s. He noted that it is difficult to find such a concentration of talent, university research, national military orders, and active venture capital anywhere else, which he sees as crucial factors behind the rapid rise of U.S. science and technology parks.

In these functional areas, initially based around world-renowned universities like Stanford, Berkeley, and the California Institute of Technology, micro-high-tech companies were founded and, after several years of development, grew into some of the world's top 500 enterprises. For instance, since the 1990s, these roots in world-famous universities have made science and technology parks an important engine for the active U.S. economy.

As for a high-tech enterprise like Career, its Chairman and CEO Chuck Shiwoboda told reporters that LED lighting represents the future direction of the energy-efficient lighting industry and contains inexhaustible power to promote the development of new industries. James pointed out that Cree, first used by six young Americans in the 1980s, was initiated in North Carolina and now its LED lighting products are accepted in markets across different countries and regions worldwide. According to statistics, in the past year, Career's global revenue exceeded 1.1 billion U.S. dollars. In 2010, Career established its first chip production base outside North America in Guangdong.

What, then, prompted such performance from U.S. high-tech enterprises? James believes that government support is very important for high-tech enterprises during their start-up period. In the first eight years of Career's entrepreneurship, the main source of income came from 'government contracts,' with annual income reaching $2-4 million, which James described as crucial for Career's income payments.

James sees government departments supporting high-tech enterprises as respecting the market's forward-looking needs for continuous R&D. Career spends a lot of money on this. James said, "Regarding LED lights, you must close doors and windows tightly; in complete darkness, you can see a little faint blue light."

Although the international community has no uniform understanding of the new economy, relevant academic information can be initially summarized as follows: the new economy is driven by economic globalization and IT revolution, involving new materials, new energy technology, life sciences, space technology, marine technology, environmental technology, and management techniques. High-tech industries lead the new economy, with innovation as its core concept, innovative operation modes, and technological innovation.

High-tech industries, although requiring huge investments, see rising demand and rapidly declining production costs and prices of high-tech products, thus promoting economic growth without being driven by price increases.

According to Lin Tie, Marketing Director of Shanghai Career Development Co., Ltd., LED semiconductor lighting has features of energy saving, emission reduction, consumption, long service life, environmental protection, and information management. The upstream of the LED industry involves light chips and epitaxial silicon processing, midstream and downstream packaging and testing, and application. Among them, the upstream and midstream have higher technical content, capital investment density, intense international competition, and larger business risks.

Career holds independent core technology in the upstream LED industry with 100 U.S. patents and 800 international patents. Career Company, headquartered in North Carolina, was founded in 1987 and listed as a public company (NASDAQ: CREE in 1993), is one of the leading manufacturers and industry leaders in the world for compound semiconductor materials, epitaxy, chip packaging, and LED lighting solutions. Cree LED lighting products have advantages in gallium nitride (GaN) and silicon carbide (SiC) and other unique material technologies and advanced white light technology.
