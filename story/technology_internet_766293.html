How will 3D printing be integrated into traditional mainstream manufacturing?

by anonymous on 2013-09-11 13:26:52

As an emerging digital production technology, the advent of 3D printing has captured people's attention. A recent article in The Economist states that the integration of 3D printing with mainstream manufacturing is increasing. Below is the full text of the article.

By looking through the window at nearly 100 quietly working 3D printers at RedEye, one can glimpse how future factories will operate. They are more than just machines; the things they produce demonstrate how this revolutionary production process is becoming a mainstream manufacturing method.

3D printers create physical objects layer by layer using special materials without cutting, drilling, or other mechanical processing, hence the process is also called rapid prototyping. Each printed item requires slightly different software and does not need expensive reprocessing machines, making 3D printing popular for producing one-off items, especially molds, small devices, and physical models.

Skeptics believe that the benefits of 3D printing end there. Terry Gou of Foxconn calls 3D printing just a trick with no commercial value in the production of real goods. He even vowed to write his name backward if he was wrong.

Mr. Gou got one thing right: the purpose of rapid prototyping is not to replace mass production. Even as the technology advances, some printed items' durability still falls short of manufacturers' requirements. Moreover, 3D printers cannot produce thousands of identical components like assembly lines do. Still, 3D printers have their own advantages; otherwise, world-renowned large producers such as General Electric, Ford, Siemens, Boeing, and Airbus would not have started using them.

The 3D printing market is small but growing rapidly. Last year, its market value reached $2.2 billion, an increase of 29% over 2011. As manufacturers become more familiar with the technology, they can quickly move from models to final products. Last year, statistics showed that 25% of the 3D printing market involved producing "near-products" for use in production.

This is taking shape at RedEye. In many cases, they are low-volume goods, such as components for specialized pharmaceutical equipment or papermaking equipment. Other parts, such as production tools printed in 3D, actually enhance the mass production capabilities of manufacturing: workers on BMW's production line designed and printed custom tools to make supporting and positioning car parts easier; 3D-printed molds can help companies set up experimental production lines; when parts that take weeks to replace are needed, 3D-printed models can temporarily come into play; hard-to-find spare parts can also be 3D-printed, which once helped several old planes of a U.S. airline successfully take off.

RedEye manager Tim Thellin says that as 3D printing technology and materials improve, the quality of finished products also increases, almost indistinguishable from samples produced in traditional factories. Despite all the hype around desktop 3D printers targeting consumers and enthusiasts, large industrial-grade printers meet the needs of printing large-volume items, something traditional plastic injection molding machines find difficult to achieve.

Some of RedEye's 3D printers are not open to guests because they produce items related to national defense or with high commercial sensitivity. One printer produces components for 3D printers made by RedEye's parent company Stratasys. Stratasys and another company called 3D Systems are leaders in the 3D printing market.

Headquartered in Southern California, 3D Systems has many examples of using 3D printers to manufacture finished products. The healthcare industry was one of the earliest adopters of this technology. In this field, a large amount of customization is necessary, as each patient's condition varies. Cathy Lewis, market director of 3D Systems, said that after millions of patients had their inner ears scanned, the shells of hearing aids were 3D printed. Initially, the shells were cast using 3D models. Later, when non-irritating materials appeared, they could be directly printed.

In another example, 3D Systems partnered with Align Technology. Align produces sets of transparent plastic dental aligners instead of traditional metal braces. After doctors scan a patient's mouth, they design a treatment plan. The scanning process generates a digital file used to print 20 models. Each model is slightly different, and through them, a complete set of plastic aligners can be manufactured. After wearing them for several months, the patient's teeth will gradually straighten. Last year, Align printed 17 million plastic aligners.

Shining in the aviation industry

The aviation manufacturing industry, with lower production volumes, also adopts 3D technology. Typically, it prints non-critical components, but this is soon to change. 3D Systems states that a regular F-18 fighter jet may contain 90 3D-printed components, even if the F-18 has been in service for 20 years. This is because replacement parts, such as those for the cockpit and air ducts, are now 3D-printed; while the newly commissioned F-35 has about 900 components suitable for 3D printing.

3D printing is of great importance to General Electric across multiple businesses from energy to healthcare, and it plans to internalize several 3D printing technologies to maintain competitiveness. In November 2011, General Electric acquired Morris Technologies, a rapid prototyping manufacturer. Additionally, Morris manufactures lightweight components for unmanned aerial vehicles. One reason this technology attracts General Electric is its potential to produce complex lightweight components, which traditional methods struggle to achieve without special materials. By 2020, General Electric aims to print tens of thousands of components for its jet engines.

These technological advantages are applied in China. As labor costs rise, some factories in Beijing are using rapid prototyping as a way to upgrade their production bases. Although China's technology is not as advanced as America's, it has greater ambitions.

Most of China's 3D printing business fits well with traditional factories. For example, Beijing Longyuan Automatic Assembly System uses a 3D printing technology called laser sintering to create models using specially treated casting sand, which are then sent to traditional foundries to be cast with metal parts in the traditional way. In this way, all parts of a model car engine can be completed in just a few weeks instead of months.

Some of the largest 3D printers in the world can be found in China. Chinese astronauts' 3D-printed seats are specifically designed according to their body shapes.

Printing circuit boards becomes possible

When 3D printing can directly print circuit boards, its value as a production tool will further increase. Disney, Xerox, and UK-based GKN Aerospace are experimenting with this processing method. In a collaborative project with Warwick University, GKN developed a material called carbomorph. When under pressure, it exhibits changes in electrical impedance. It can be used to print switches, buttons, and sensors.

New Mexico-based Optomec proposes an electronic printing method called Aerosol Jet. It sprays liquid electronic materials into a dense aerosol, then deposits them layer by layer using special methods. Circuits and components such as wires, resistors, capacitors, and semiconductors can be manufactured this way, with dimensions accurate to 10 microns. Optomec has already tried printing LED lighting systems into wallpaper and also printing control circuits for aircraft wings.

Optomec is also collaborating with several mobile phone manufacturers to try directly printing phone circuits. The latest smartphones contain various antennas for mobile phones, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, and GPS functions. Their chemical processing pollutes the environment. Optomec can directly print antennas using conductive silver ink, and a similar experimental printing system was recently installed on the production line of a Chinese company.

Michael Renn, head of Optomec's research lab, predicts that most electronic components can be directly printed into products. However, although the system can print resistors and logic circuits, it cannot print the billions of miniature transistors required for microprocessors and other chips. These still need to be obtained through traditional methods and then embedded in 3D-printed finished products.

3D printing is not perfect

Rapid prototyping has other limitations. First, it is slow - for example, printing a car dashboard. However, speed is relative; the speed requirements for mass production and one-off models are different. Second, the cost of materials is very high. Acrylonitrile-butadiene-styrene (ABS) is the most commonly used 3D printing material. Manufacturers using plastic injection molding techniques buy ABS in bulk at $2 per kilogram; but as powder for 3D printers, its price reaches $80 per kilogram.

Part of the reason for the price difference is the higher purity and composition requirements for 3D printing. Of course, a bigger reason is that many 3D printer manufacturers require users to purchase raw materials from them and thereby raise prices. Industry insiders say that when third-party suppliers enter the industry, the situation will improve. Moreover, the customizable 3D printing systems being designed by large manufacturers like General Electric require equipment and materials from multiple suppliers.

A report released by McKinsey states that after early patents involving 3D printing technology expired in 2009, factors that could stimulate the 3D industry include price reductions and increased competition. Some printer prices have already dropped below $1000.

The entire industry is also consolidating. Last year, Stratasys acquired Objet from Israel; in June, the company acquired MakerBot; in July, 3D Systems purchased 81% of French supplier Phenix Systems specializing in laser sintering technology.

With the expiration of patents related to laser sintering technology, 2014 will enter a new stage of intensified competition in 3D printing technology. Laser sintering technology can print more detailed finished products using plastics, metals, and ceramics, not just models.

Complementary to traditional manufacturing

Meanwhile, 3D printing is becoming increasingly practical for the general public. Shapeways in New York, Sculpteo in France, and Materialise in Belgium can print medical implants for surgeons, architectural models for architects, and lightweight parts for industrial robots.

If Mr. Gou's production line encounters small problems, these companies can help. The boss of Sculpteo said that a large Chinese manufacturer was setting up a new production line but discovered that some plastic parts had been forgotten to order. Facing the risk of the project being delayed for weeks, they chose 3D printing technology. Sculpteo shipped 5000 plastic parts within days. This example does not show that 3D technology competes with traditional manufacturing technology, but rather that it can complement traditional technology. When 3D printing can solve problems encountered in mass production, its position in the future factory is secured.
