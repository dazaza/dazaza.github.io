Aunt Zhang, aged 50, was recently diagnosed with advanced cervical cancer. She cried and said, "My child just graduated, I just retired, good days have just begun, and I didn't expect to get cancer..." Aunt Zhang asked the doctor in confusion, "I have a physical examination every year, and I do an ultrasound. Why wasn't cervical cancer discovered? Why was it only found at an advanced stage?"

What symptoms indicate the transformation of cervical inflammation into cancer? Sexual activity affects the incidence of cervical cancer. Unprotected sex is the main cause of cervical cancer in young girls. Seven lifetime HPV tests can help prevent cervical cancer. The more sexual partners a woman has, the higher her risk of cervical cancer.

Further inquiry revealed that although Aunt Zhang had breast and abdominal ultrasounds every year, she hadn't had a gynecological exam for several years. "This is the reason. In detecting cervical cancer early, gynecological exams are more effective than ultrasounds." Gynecological examinations can better help detect cervical cancer early on. Although cervical cancer is a malignant tumor, it is relatively easy to detect. If there is bleeding during sexual intercourse or if the cervix bleeds easily upon contact during a gynecological exam, it often indicates cervical lesions.

However, many patients with cervical lesions are very afraid of gynecological exams and prefer ultrasounds instead. There are two main reasons for this: some people think ultrasounds are more advanced and can see tumors more clearly; others are psychologically afraid of gynecological exams because they involve using a speculum, which makes some people nervous and causes full-body spasms, making the procedure more painful. In reality, a gynecological exam isn't painful if you relax. A gynecological exam mainly includes external genitalia, internal genitalia, and cervical checks. Doctors can discover early signs of disease by visually examining and touching the patient's cervix after using a speculum to open the vagina. Diseases like ovarian tumors, endometriosis, and uterine fibroids can all be detected through gynecological exams.

Specifically for cervical cancer, if cervical lesions are found, doctors usually recommend further testing such as a Pap smear and HPV virus test to confirm the diagnosis. Early cervical cancer is primarily detected through gynecological exams. Since early cervical cancer tumors are very small, ultrasounds cannot detect them (ultrasounds can only see tumors larger than 1 cm). By the time they are visible, the tumors are often large and in the middle to late stages. Cervical lesions can be detected before they become cancerous, as long as annual gynecological exams are conducted.

Misconception Two: Bleeding after sexual intercourse means irregular menstruation or hormonal imbalance.

Lin Fang, 30 years old from Jieyang, has been married for two years. Last weekend, after having sex with her husband, she noticed slight blood stains when wiping herself. Since her period had just ended, she thought it was residual menstrual blood and didn't pay much attention. This situation continued for a week, then she noticed increased vaginal discharge, sometimes accompanied by lower back pain and pelvic discomfort. She visited a nearby hospital's gynecology department, where the doctor prescribed washes and suppositories. After a week, Lin Fang felt some improvement, but soon the problem recurred, fluctuating between better and worse. Two months later, Lin Fang eventually came to Sun Yat-sen University's First Affiliated Hospital for treatment. After a gynecological exam, the doctor recommended a biopsy. The results showed that Lin Fang already had advanced cervical cancer.

Gynecological consultation expert's words:

Bleeding after sexual intercourse is a typical symptom of cervical cancer.

Many people, even some grassroots doctors, when hearing about bleeding after sexual intercourse, first think of irregular menstruation or menopausal symptoms. However, bleeding after sexual intercourse is one of the typical symptoms of cervical cancer. Many mistakenly believe that irregular menstruation is simply due to hormonal imbalance. Actually, irregular menstruation can be caused by various factors, including endometrial hyperplasia, endometrial polyps, endometrial cancer, and cervical lesions. It is not merely a matter of hormonal imbalance.

Generally, continuous vaginal bleeding for a month should raise high suspicion of endometrial cancer, followed by cervical cancer. Bleeding after sexual intercourse is a dangerous signal and must be taken seriously. You must go to the hospital to check for cervical cancer.

Misconception Three: Removing the uterus means no longer being a woman.

Ms. Wang was diagnosed with cervical cancer three days ago. The doctor said her uterus must be removed. "I heard that after removing the uterus, female characteristics gradually disappear and turn male. I'm only 35 years old! How will I live in the future? And removing the uterus will affect sexual life, I don't want this to affect my marriage." Ms. Wang considered other treatments for cervical cancer and asked, "I heard that Chinese medicine can also treat it. Doctor, can I avoid surgery and use Chinese medicine for treatment?"

Clinically, many cervical cancer patients resist surgery, believing that removing the uterus eliminates female characteristics and hope to use Chinese medicine for treatment. Experts say this is a completely wrong view.

Gynecological consultation expert's words:

Removing the uterus does not affect female characteristics.

"Firstly, I want to emphasize that without a uterus, the most significant impact is the inability to bear children, but it does not affect female characteristics or sexual life," explained Dr. Ke Peiqi. Hysterectomy is one of the most commonly used surgeries in obstetrics and gynecology, and many conditions require this operation. Many patients fear hysterectomies, worrying that their removal will affect their femininity and make them masculine. However, this is not true. Female sexual characteristics depend on hormones produced by the ovaries. The ovaries, located on either side of the uterus, cyclically develop follicles, mature, and ovulate while producing estrogen and progesterone, maintaining female characteristics. After a hysterectomy, as long as one ovary is retained, hormone levels remain largely normal, so female characteristics and sexual life are not affected.

As for treating cervical cancer with Chinese medicine, it is not advisable. Once cervical cancer is confirmed as a malignant tumor, the most appropriate comprehensive treatment plan should be established. Generally, early-stage cases are treated surgically, while mid-to-late stages use radiotherapy or combined surgery and radiotherapy, supplemented by chemotherapy and Chinese medicine. Chinese medicine can only regulate the body, improve immunity, and serve as an auxiliary role, but it cannot address the root cause. The treatment outcomes for early-stage cervical cancer are very good. Clinical statistics show that the five-year survival rate for early surgical treatment can reach over 90%.

Gynecological consultation experts remind:

Regular examinations to stay away from cervical cancer.

From the onset of cervical lesions to the development of cancer, it generally takes several years. If early detection and timely treatment occur during this time, most patients can be effectively cured. Therefore, regular cervical examinations are crucial. Women who are sexually active, regardless of age, should undergo at least one cervical examination per year. In terms of prevention, note the following points:

1. Do not marry too early; marrying under the age of 16 increases the risk of cervical cancer;

2. Minimize abortions and excessive childbirth;

3. Avoid smoking and drinking, maintain regular sleep schedules;

4. Practice safe sexual behavior. Surveys show that having more than six sexual partners and starting sexual activity before the age of 15 increases the risk of cervical cancer by over ten times.
