Love Needs to Be Slowly Experienced

The story happened many years ago, and I first heard about it in the winter of 1984. At that time, both my senior brother Zhi and I were studying at a local newspaper office and sharing the same room. Brother Zhi was then a mid-level manager at an enterprise in his county, which is located at the southernmost part of Hebei Province, bordering Henan Province. Brother Zhi was a typical northern Chinese man: tall, with a square face and big eyes, sincere in dealing with people, simple-hearted, and humorous. We became good friends who could talk about anything not long after we met.

By then, Brother Zhi was already the father of two children, while I had not yet married and, like other young people of my age, I was full of mystery about romantic love. One snowy night, we each went to bed early but neither of us could fall asleep. After chatting casually for a while, the topic turned to his family and love. Under my repeated questioning, Brother Zhi finally broke his long silence and began to tell his story:

Our place was a remote and backward rural area. I am an only child, so my parents took my marriage very seriously. When I was sixteen and in the second year of junior high school, my parents arranged a marriage for me. The girl was three years older than me. My parents hoped that I would get married as soon as possible, carry on the family line, and have children. The reason they chose a girl three years older was because they believed she would be more mature and capable of taking care of the family sooner.

Coincidentally, the place where I attended junior high school was in the village where my fiancée lived. Even more coincidentally, her home was just next to the school. Although I passed by her house every day on my way to and from school, I didn't have much chance to see her. Moreover, since this was an arranged marriage by my parents, and I was still young and didn't understand much about it, I didn't take this engagement seriously. She was more mature than me; every time we met at the door, she would look at me shyly and wait for me to speak to her. However, I felt nervous and would quickly lower my head and run away. All my classmates knew that I was engaged, and after class, they would tease me and even lift me up and carry me to her doorstep before scattering away.

At that time, the Cultural Revolution had just begun, and it was fashionable for young people to wear military uniforms. I also wanted a set of military uniform, but I couldn't get one. She understood my thoughts well and bought green cloth from the store to make me a set. It was clear that she liked me, but I didn't have any special feelings for her. I always felt uneasy inside and thought about breaking off the engagement, but I was afraid that my parents wouldn't agree. So, I tried to let the girl side break it off first. During the Mid-Autumn Festival that year when I visited her home, she suggested that I should stop going to school and implied that we should get married early. At that time, I was young and proud, so I said: "After I graduate from junior high school, I will take the high school entrance exam, and after high school, I will take the university entrance exam. Marriage is still far away. If you can't wait, then we should break up." Seeing my firm attitude, the girl's side feared that waiting longer might not be safe, so they decided to break off the engagement themselves. Thus, my first love ended in a rather lackluster way.

My second love interest was the most unforgettable person in my life. Her name was Ju Hua and she was my classmate. She was from Henan Province. Her father worked in the county town. After her mother passed away, her father remarried and had a son. Her stepmother treated her poorly, so she came to her aunt's home in our county to attend junior high school. Ju Hua was beautiful, talented in singing and dancing, and was an active member of our school’s cultural activities. My academic performance was relatively good, and I was the class monitor. Some classmates often bullied her because she was new, so I stepped in to help her out. To be honest, at that time, I didn't think too much about it. I just felt that protecting younger students was my responsibility as a class monitor. She, on the other hand, often sought my help with difficult homework problems. Initially, I didn't pay much attention to it, thinking that helping each other with studies was normal interaction among classmates. Gradually, I noticed that her gaze towards me changed. When we were alone in the classroom, her eyes would intensely focus on me, completely ignoring the lessons I was trying to explain. In order not to affect my studies, I tried to avoid her as much as possible. However, starting from the second semester, she almost wrote me a note every day, inviting me to go out at night. At that time, I had mixed feelings. On one hand, I did indeed like her and didn't want to hurt her, but on the other hand, I still had dreams of attending university and was afraid it would affect my future. So, I neither agreed nor went out with her. But she didn't lose heart and continued to write me notes every day. Our classmates all noticed what was happening. Some drew caricatures of us on the blackboard with chalk and teased us. She didn't seem to care. As for me, although I appeared resentful on the surface and scolded them, secretly I felt happy. Love is something that connects hearts without words. After that, whenever we saw each other, even if we didn't speak, just a glance between us made us feel warm inside.

Before we officially graduated, the school started suspending classes for revolutionary activities. Those who didn't participate in these activities graduated early and returned home. On the graduation day, Ju Hua gave me a notebook. On the first page, there were two sunflowers she had drawn herself, along with a note inviting me to meet her. Considering that we were about to part ways and the school wasn't having classes anymore, this time I readily accepted her invitation. Her aunt's village was two li (about 1 kilometer) away from ours. That night, we talked for a long time. When it was time to say goodbye, she insisted on seeing me off. Unconsciously, we walked all the way to the entrance of my village. It was dark, and I was worried about her safety returning alone, so I sent her back. But we had so much to say, and she insisted on seeing me off again. This back-and-forth happened four times between the two villages. As dawn approached, I firmly refused to let her see me off again. She held my hand and cried, telling me that she would return to her hometown in Henan the next day and asked me to wait for her. I solemnly nodded. Then we hugged tightly and exchanged vows of eternal love.

Not long after returning to the village, I joined a group of laborers digging the Hai River in Tianjin. Although I missed her dearly, there was no way to communicate. After the cold winter set in, I returned to the village. As soon as I entered the house, my mother smiled and told me that she had arranged another marriage for me with the daughter of the village secretary. Upon hearing this, I became furious and shouted loudly: "I won't agree! You don't need to meddle in my affairs!" My mother was shocked by my outburst and burst into tears. My father added: "Anyway, the secretary's daughter has chosen you. If you refuse, our family won't be able to stay in the village. Besides, marrying the secretary's daughter is something many people aspire to but cannot achieve. You really don't know what's good for you."

The village secretary wielded absolute power in the village, and at that time, class struggle was rampant. No one dared to say "no" to the secretary, or they would immediately be labeled as counter-revolutionaries. Our family was a small household in the village, and my parents were powerless. How could we resist when the secretary's daughter favored me? There was nothing I could do but tacitly accept. Seeing my acceptance, my parents were overjoyed and immediately went to the secretary's house to discuss the engagement. They decided to exchange betrothal gifts the next day, which was a formal procedure in the engagement process.

I could only follow my parents' arrangements and prepare for the betrothal ceremony. The family sent me to exchange flour, so I loaded dozens of pounds of wheat onto my bicycle and headed to the county where Ju Hua's family lived. Most people from our village went there, and another reason was that I wanted to try my luck and see if I could meet Ju Hua.

There were many people exchanging flour, and I was waiting in line. Just as it was almost my turn, suddenly a woman walked up from behind and threw my sack aside. At that moment, I was talking with someone in front of me and didn't see who it was, so I was quite angry, thinking this person was too domineering. Others in line also stood up for me. But when I looked up, I was stunned—it was Ju Hua. When she actually appeared in front of me, I felt scared inside. Truly, I didn't know how to explain myself to her.

She glanced at me a few times and then returned to the ticket booth. Coincidentally, she was in charge of issuing tickets at the flour mill. I thought this was bad news, considering her earlier attitude, she must be upset with me. Therefore, I decided to test her reaction, pretending to leave with my bike loaded with wheat. Unexpectedly, she rushed out from the ticket booth, unloaded my wheat, locked my bike, and took the key, angrily saying: "You can't leave." This confirmed my suspicion, and I became even more flustered. Knowing she wanted me to stay and talk, others who didn't know the situation whispered and commented, saying this person's behavior was truly unacceptable—she shouldn't have prevented me from exchanging flour, nor should she have stopped me from leaving.

When it was lunchtime and work paused, she pulled me into the ticket booth. Before she could say anything, her eyes were already red. She asked me: "Why haven't you replied to my letters?" This question caught me off guard. I replied: "I haven't received any letters." She accused: "You're lying. I've written you more than ten letters!" I countered: "I went to Tianjin to dig the Hai River shortly after graduation and just returned a few days ago. Could my family have hidden your letters?"

Seeing that I was telling the truth, she asked if I had become engaged. I replied: "I'm here to exchange flour because I'm preparing for tomorrow's betrothal ceremony. My family is waiting for me to steam buns." She asked if I could call it off. I explained that I couldn't make the decision myself since it was arranged by my family. Moreover, the girl was from the same village, and her father was the village secretary. If I backed out, my whole family wouldn't be able to live in the village anymore.

Realizing there was no room for negotiation, she burst into tears, crying on the table. After wiping her tears, she specially went to the canteen to buy me steamed buns and meat dishes. My mind was in chaos, and I had no appetite. Seeing that I wasn't eating, she picked up the food and fed me, insisting that I eat. I turned my head to the left, and she moved the food to the left; I turned my head to the right, and she moved the food to the right. She said: "If you don't eat, I'll keep holding the food." Only then did I reluctantly take a few bites.

It wasn't until just before the afternoon shift ended that she issued my ticket. As I walked out of the flour mill, light rain began to fall. She accompanied me for two li, and I pushed my bike to send her back to the county. We spoke a lot, both feeling heavy-hearted and shedding tears. When we parted ways, she said: "Since you're already engaged, I won't force you. But please remember, I love you, I truly loved you."

I stumbled home in the dark. My parents scolded me for coming home late. I recounted everything that happened with Ju Hua and asked my father if anyone had mailed me letters. My father remained silent for a long time before quietly saying: "I burned them all." I was furious and questioned my father: "Why did you burn my letters? Do you realize you ruined my life?" My father squatted down and explained: "Our family has little influence in the village. Without finding a supportive match, we would be looked down upon. Besides, the girl from Henan has a non-agricultural household registration and a job. You, as a farmer, even if you got married, there would be countless issues ahead. It's better to be realistic."

I cried bitterly and sat in the courtyard all night. My mother, fearing I would catch a cold, quietly draped a cotton coat over me. Neither of them slept that night. In the end, I forgave my parents' well-meaning intentions and accepted their arrangements.

Later, I got married, had children, and eventually found employment.

By this point, Brother Zhi seemed ready to conclude his story. I asked him: "How is your relationship with your current sister-in-law?" Brother Zhi gave a bitter smile: "What kind of feelings are we talking about? We just muddle through." He had an air of someone who had seen too much of life's complexities. After a long pause, Brother Zhi said to me: "Brother, you haven't started a family yet, so you haven't experienced it. Love is something indescribable. Not getting what you want is regrettable, but even getting it doesn't necessarily mean happiness." Brother Zhi's words left me somewhat confused and unable to fully grasp their meaning.

The night grew deep, and the wind outside the window ceased its howling. When we turned off the light, the white snowlight illuminated the room. Brother Zhi had fallen asleep, but I was wide awake. Accompanied by his snoring, that night I thought deeply about many things.
