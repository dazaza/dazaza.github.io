Moreover, the diploma for foreign affairs and overseas studies

by fengmi5x on 2012-02-24 22:37:18

In 1979, American sociologist Randall Collins published "The Credential Society." This marked the advent of a credential society, or more specifically, a degree-oriented society. In modern society, diplomas and certificates have become ubiquitous, creating a suffocating atmosphere. Although the imperial examination system and state governance were closely related in ancient China, the infiltration of degrees and certificates into every aspect of social, political, economic, and cultural life is a relatively recent phenomenon. Since the 1970s, the managerial class has become increasingly specialized, with a diploma almost becoming a prerequisite for companies to select managers. Now, leaders are also expected to be educated, as diplomas symbolize what Gary Becker refers to as "human capital." To produce more diplomas and certificates, universities and various training institutions have emerged and expanded. However, the expansion of universities will not eliminate the degree-oriented society. As Bell stated, "At that point, what will matter most is the internal differentiation within the knowledge society itself. It will continue indefinitely and exist permanently."

II. The Expansion of Higher Education in Response to the Diploma Society

The demand for diplomas in the market has intensified. Taking postgraduate education as an example, the number of people registering for postgraduate studies exceeded one million in 2005, setting a new record. Looking back at the past few years, the competition for the national postgraduate entrance examination has indeed become increasingly fierce. The following set of statistics on the number of people registering for postgraduate studies across the country clearly shows this:

Year: 1997, 1998, 1999, 2000, 2001, 2002, 2003, 2004

Number of Registrants (in ten thousand): 24.3, 27.4, 31, 39.2, 46, 62, 79, 94.5

To meet the demands of the social education market, higher education institutions have expanded enrollment. In 2005, the total enrollment for undergraduate and junior college students nationwide reached 4.75 million, an increase of 8% compared to the previous year. In 2005, the total enrollment for postgraduates was 370,000. Among them, 316,000 were master's degree candidates, an increase of about 15% compared to the previous year, while 54,000 were doctoral candidates, an increase of 2%. In 1998, the number of people registering for postgraduate studies was less than 280,000, but by 2005, it had increased to over one million, approximately four times the number in 1998. The number of admissions increased from about 80,000 to 370,000, more than four times the number seven years ago.

What lies behind this pursuit of higher education? Employment pressure, investment in human capital after economic prosperity, enhancing personal qualities, finding high-paying jobs, further promotion, etc., all serve as reasons. However, fundamentally, the correlation between educational credentials and social stratification is too strong; in other words, educational credentials are directly linked to benefits—higher credentials, higher benefits. Throughout history and across cultures, anything that brings fame and benefit inevitably attracts many followers. As the so-called knowledge economy and the notion that science and technology are the primary productive forces take root in people's minds, the myth of diplomas and credentials becomes increasingly difficult to break. In his 1979 book "The Credential Society: Educational Stratification in Comparative Perspective," Collins exposed the myth of the technocratic elite. On the surface, people pursue higher education to acquire knowledge and skills, but in reality, this is not entirely true. Many high-level degrees are tied to high positions, making these diplomas a stepping stone to obtaining prestigious roles. Therefore, people aim for these higher degrees not for knowledge or skills but for the stepping stones themselves. Many positions do not require master's or doctoral degrees, and ordinary individuals could suffice. However, to enhance the status and value of employers or organizations, hiring is often restricted to postgraduates only.

Education is closely related to social stratification. In a sense, this is a good thing; gaining high status and position through credentials and certificates is better than achieving success through denunciations and revolutions. Philip Kuhn, author of "Soulstealers," conversed with an old Red Guard while researching materials in the Forbidden City. The old Red Guard expressed dissatisfaction with the current society because now everything requires credentials and certificates, unlike the past when bringing down others could lead to promotions and wealth. The disillusionment of the old Red Guard reflects the positive aspects of a degree-oriented society.

However, the expansion of education driven by the degree-oriented society leads to inflationary effects, devaluing diplomas. With more people holding bachelor's degrees, employers raise their standards, refusing anyone who isn't a postgraduate. After obtaining a doctoral degree, we still seek postdoctoral qualifications. A popular saying goes: "A bachelor's degree is worth less than a dog's, master's degrees are everywhere, foreign diplomas can be processed, and those with doctorates still have some sway." Thus, everyone strives for postgraduate and doctoral studies. Despite the fact that even a bachelor's degree is now considered less valuable, lacking one would make job hunting and career advancement even worse. Consequently, young people strive to obtain at least a bachelor's degree as higher education becomes more widespread. This is a socially locked-in fact, similar to what Sun Liping analyzes in "Broken Arrow"—the disadvantaged groups falling behind in society. To avoid being left out by the larger society, one must pursue higher educational credentials and various qualification certificates. The inevitable result is intense competition, which continues to escalate.

The number of people registering for postgraduate studies increases annually, fortunately accompanied by expanding enrollment. Similar to the situation where countless imperial examination candidates prepared year after year, studying the Four Books and Five Classics, this represents a waste of human capital. The same situation persists today, such as students retaking their senior year and "postgraduate professional households." The consequence of expanded enrollment leading to diploma devaluation results in significant societal waste. Collins is one of the representatives of the view that educational expansion and diploma devaluation lead to societal waste. He believes that the cost of currency devaluation is low (though he is not an economist and does not consider issues like social consequences, inefficiency, and waste), whereas cultivating someone to obtain a diploma is costly and wasteful. In an interview, he asked, "Does an ordinary employee at McDonald's need a Ph.D.?" However, in this stratified society, people strive for higher positions despite their helplessness. Under the allure of "gold houses" and "beautiful women" promised by diplomas, how can people escape the "education obsession" of "only studying leads to greatness"?

III. Dropout Issues Among Rural Children in a Diploma-Oriented Society

In a diploma-oriented society, diplomas hold immense importance, serving as entry tickets to advanced and respectable workplaces. If we shift our focus to rural areas, we see a complex picture: on one hand, farmers endure hardships and economize to send their children to school, hoping for a better future; on the other hand, many middle and elementary school students drop out due to economic difficulties, lack of hope for advancement, or the belief that even if they pass exams, they cannot afford schooling or find jobs after graduation. Thus, the relationship between farmers' economic status and prospects becomes more pronounced in a diploma-oriented society. Bourdieu revealed in "The State Nobility" how economic capital and cultural capital jointly reproduce the ruling class and lower classes. Through the educational system and diploma system, rigid social hierarchies are masked, transforming the historically arbitrary social order rooted in economic and political material power into the elegant external expressions of cultural elites. Social hierarchies, as measures of excellence among members of society, grant dignity to some while relegating others to lower strata. Institutions like the École Normale Supérieure in Paris, alma mater of Bourdieu, cultivate intellectuals and elites. Admission requires excellent cultural refinement and academic temperament, aligning well with students from bourgeois aristocratic backgrounds, producing cultural capitalists. Business schools and institutions like the École Polytechnique aim to cultivate leaders in the state and industry, attracting students primarily from economically affluent upper-middle-class French families. The National Institute of Public Administration occupies a middle ground between these two elite institutions, focusing on cabinet members and high-ranking civil servants, emphasizing both cultural and economic capabilities, with students mainly coming from rare families with inherited wealth and diplomas.

Bourdieu's fundamental idea that one's origin determines their future prospects was already present in Fei Xiaotong's essay "Heredity and Legacy," dated July 3, 1943. However, Bourdieu's work is systematic and supported by detailed evidence and empirical data, while Fei's is a miscellaneous article. Fei states, "If you analyze the history of successes and failures, except for a few exceptions, most can attribute their success to the legacy provided by their fathers. Especially in a meritocratic society, if your father did not leave you enough wealth to live comfortably for twenty years, you should not dream of entering school. Without school, no matter how hard you study, you will never obtain a graduation certificate. Many positions do not allow those without such qualifications to occupy them. Superficially, selecting talent based on knowledge seems most consistent with eugenic principles, but since the prerequisite for entering school is economic, the ultimate determinant remains inheritance." Fei uses the example of Britain, where according to Tony's calculations, most British cabinet ministers, except for a few exceptions, come from four famous British secondary schools. These schools charge exorbitant fees, making them inaccessible to commoners, thus cutting off their paths to officialdom. What about China? Based on his investigation of rural China, Fei concludes, "I have investigated numerous cases in rural areas, and my conclusion is that it is impossible to admit that a hardworking farmer can establish a home capable of supporting retirement. If you want a comfortable life, remember my words—you must have a good father. Only after he gives you a fortune do you have the right to talk about effort and perseverance." What about contemporary China? Given the large number of rural middle and elementary school students dropping out, we believe the principles outlined by Bourdieu and Mr. Fei remain relevant and vivid.

Can this situation be changed? How can children without wealthy fathers still gain access to education and have the opportunity to work hard? From my own experiences, there is hope. Hope lies in institutional arrangements. Different institutional arrangements can influence this unfairness and inequality. My generation of genuine peasant children greatly benefited from China's educational institutional arrangements. When I was young, my family was one of the impoverished households in a poor village. In 1983, the tuition fee for the first grade was only two yuan. Because my family was a poor household, the school refunded one yuan during the Spring Festival for our New Year expenses. In the second year of junior high school, I almost went to work in Xuzhou and embarked on a long journey as a migrant worker. Fortunately, with the low tuition and special care from the school, I persisted and excelled in the entrance exam for a normal school. With the treatment of normal schools at the time, I smoothly studied for three years and was recommended to attend a normal university without paying tuition. Until I pursued a public-funded graduate program in Beijing, I continuously benefited from the national educational system. Reflecting now, I feel very fortunate to have caught a good opportunity. Even after adjusting for inflation, the tuition fees in my hometown's primary and secondary schools are much higher than they were then. Moreover, the imposition of various review materials and learning supplies is severe. This is very unfair to rural children. They should not lose the opportunity to develop their talents simply because their fathers are poor. Although the situation described by Bourdieu and Fei Xiaotong is difficult to completely avoid, we can certainly improve it with effort. Careful consideration reveals how crucial it is for the state to properly implement nine-year compulsory education. Simultaneously, we hope the state establishes comprehensive scholarship systems for financially disadvantaged college students. Specific methods of discussion are beyond the scope of this short article.

IV. Diplomas and Trust

Etymologically, the word "credentials" comes from the root "credere," meaning trust. Modern social life depends on two major "system trusts": the monetary system and the expert system based on academic qualifications (Zheng Yafu, "Trust Theory"). However, both "genuine fake diplomas" and "fake genuine diplomas" have emerged. "Genuine fake diplomas" refer to diplomas sold by vendors near Renmin University, Tianqiao, Zhongguancun Street, and the west gate of Peking University, which are indeed counterfeit diplomas. "Fake genuine diplomas" refer to real diplomas obtained by businesspeople and officials whose secretaries attend classes and write papers on their behalf, graduating from legitimate universities. The issuing and processing institutions are legal, but in terms of actual value, these diplomas are counterfeit. Addressing counterfeit diplomas, "genuine fake diplomas" are easier to handle, as there can be a system to check them on computers, and employers, unless foolish, will surely recognize them. I've seen counterfeit diplomas with poor-quality stamps. More importantly, addressing "fake genuine diplomas" is a systemic issue, quite challenging.

Another point is that the attitudes of teachers and students towards diplomas significantly impact the public's trust in them. In Western societies, like the UK and the US, granting degrees is a solemn and serious occasion. Anyone who has attended a degree-granting ceremony at a major university in the UK or the US will be struck by their classical reverence. The similar designs of doctoral and master's robes and caps, worn by faculty and students alike, resemble the grand ceremonies held by archbishops in churches. Professors solemnly grant graduates their diplomas. In contrast, during my undergraduate and master's studies in China, I never participated in a graduation ceremony or degree-granting event. It wasn't due to laziness on my part, but rather because such events did not exist. Class student cadres simply brought stacks of certificates for us to collect. Or perhaps the cadres represented the class, but as an ordinary student, I was unaware. There was no sense of solemnity or reverence. In the US, although I haven't yet graduated, I have attended others' graduation ceremonies. Not only do the graduates participate, but also their friends and family, making it a significant life event, solemn and grand, requiring no further explanation. Through these practices, trust in diplomas is enhanced. Otherwise, treating them as trivial undermines credibility.

V. Diplomas and Examinations

The fatigue caused by the imperial examination system and its inherent limitations. Recently, I came across a book titled "Research on the Zeng Family of Xiangxiang." Flipping through it, I found that the path of the imperial examinations was indeed a disadvantage for many talented individuals. Apart from Zeng Guofan himself, who succeeded through the imperial examinations, most of Zeng's younger brothers and nephews avoided this path and achieved success in other ways, while those who pursued the imperial examination route struggled to advance. For instance, Zeng Jize, though highly gifted, consistently performed poorly in exams. After failing twice, he abandoned his pursuit of the imperial examinations and instead focused on practical knowledge and self-taught foreign languages. Later, he became a commendable diplomat and served as an ambassador abroad. His younger brother, Zeng JiHong, was exceptionally intelligent from a young age. He passed the provincial examination as a prodigy and excelled in mathematics and astronomy. Though capable of great achievements, he chose the imperial examination path, repeatedly failed, and died in despair at the age of thirty-four.

The limitations of examinations stem from the logic of the examination itself, concerning the content and validity of the tests. Debates over the content and validity of examinations have a long history (see Zheng Yafu, "Trust Theory"). The first debate on the content and validity of examinations in the imperial examination system occurred during the Tang Dynasty, revolving around whether the Mingjing (primarily testing Confucian classics) or Jinshi (primarily testing poetry and prose) category was superior. Testing Confucian classics mainly evaluated memorization skills, while poetry and prose differentiated creative talents. Hence, the Jinshi category eventually gained prominence, contributing to the flourishing poetry of the Tang Dynasty. However, grading memorized Confucian classics was more objective, while evaluating poetry and prose was more challenging, leading to ongoing debates.

During the Song Dynasty, the second major debate arose, primarily between Fan Zhongyan and Wang Anshi on one side, and Sima Guang and Su Dongpo on the other. The former argued that poetry and prose were irrelevant to state governance, as Confucian classics were used to govern the country at the time. The latter contended that testing memorization of Confucian classics could not adequately measure intelligence and talent. Both sides had valid points. Neither foresaw nor achieved the later fusion of classical studies, moral philosophy, and poetry/prose into the eight-legged essay, which dominated the Ming and Qing Dynasties.

Even today, this ancient debate continues because the logic of examinations dictates that useful things may not be suitable for testing, and what is suitable for testing may not be useful. Furthermore, akin to natural selection in evolution, passing one exam only means adapting to that specific test, not necessarily future situations. Some individuals might excel if selected, potentially achieving great success. However, mass production of students, including undergraduates, necessitates standardized tests involving large numbers of participants. Such tests often assess useless content or fail to effectively identify talent. This process may eliminate individuals described by Hayek as "confused thinkers." Therefore, those who top exams, especially the gaokao (college entrance exam) champions, should not overly pride themselves on their titles. After all, how many historical figures who made great contributions to humanity were actually top scorers? Success or failure is a long road ahead. The story of Fang Zhongyong serves as a cautionary tale, while those who fail exams should seize opportunities to learn, not necessarily in formal schools. However, we live in a diploma-oriented society!
