Scary discounts - profit increase

by yangliu435sh on 2012-02-20 11:22:12

The "rock-bottom prices! Big clearance!" slogans shouted during merchants' discount activities are merely smokescreens to confuse consumers. In reality, merchants often double or even further increase the original price of goods before marking them as "50% off," creating an illusion of a discount while covertly raising prices to gain substantial profits.

During holidays, shopping malls become crowded with people. Because they are too busy working on regular days, many people can only shop during holidays to purchase items they need. Savvy merchants certainly won't miss these lucrative opportunities, so various promotional discount events are densely launched during holidays.

Walking down the street, one can see merchants' promotional signs everywhere: "Spend 400, get 400" and "Buy one, get one free." Merchants spare no effort in trying to attract every passerby into their stores.

But are merchants willing to act foolishly and do business at a loss? Merchants inherently aim for profit, so how would they allow benefits to go to the general consumers?

In fact, the principle is simple. Merchants first use smokescreens like "discounts" and "clearance sales" to confuse consumers, then double or more than double the marked prices of goods, and finally advertise them as "50% off." As a result, consumers mistakenly believe they have stumbled upon a windfall, not realizing they've fallen into a trap. Merchants take advantage of consumers' desire for bargains, thus reaping significant profits.

From an economic perspective, merchants' costs consist of two parts: fixed costs and variable costs. Fixed costs, relative to variable costs, refer to costs that remain constant within a certain period and business volume range, unaffected by changes in business volume, such as factory buildings, machinery, and store rents, which all fall under fixed costs. Variable costs, on the other hand, change linearly with production or business volume, such as workers' wages. These two components form the total cost required to produce goods, and the average total cost is the total cost allocated per item.

As long as the selling price of goods exceeds the average total cost, merchants can make a profit. And merchants precisely calculate to ensure that even after discounts, the price remains higher than the average total cost. Increased sales can even allow them to earn higher profits than usual.

Therefore, even with discount promotions, merchants' profits still rise.

By understanding the essence of discount phenomena, consumers become more aware when facing promotional waves. They recognize that merchants aren't foolish and always manage to turn a profit, so they no longer easily believe in so-called "loss-making clearance sales."

Discount fever continues to rise and persist year after year. Although we cannot influence merchants' behaviors, we can enhance our economic knowledge to become more rational and proactive in this promotional game.

What kind of gift can please your lover without being heavily overcharged by merchants? This question troubles many people. Merchants tend to overcharge you because you don't understand the market conditions. Smart merchants typically set prices based on the customer's willingness to pay, known in economics as "price discrimination."

After years of marriage, the romance and passion of love may fade. To create some romantic atmosphere between couples and keep the love fresh, exchanging unique gifts becomes one of the best methods. However, for husbands, giving gifts isn't always easy. Married wives often make husbands feel harder to please. Perhaps women are naturally picky, or perhaps the price perceptions of husbands and wives differ. For instance, wives often complain about husbands wasting money and buying cosmetics or clothes that they don't like, which can leave enthusiastic husbands feeling defeated.

Take silk scarves as an example. A husband might think a price of 350 yuan or less is reasonable, but a wife might consider 150 yuan as reasonable. If the actual marked price of the scarf is 300 yuan, the husband might feel it's 50 yuan cheaper than expected, whereas from the wife's perspective, the scarf is already far beyond a reasonable price, so she wouldn't buy it easily. This explains why things husbands buy often draw complaints from their wives. Therefore, if you want to buy a gift your wife will like, try purchasing something unfamiliar to her.

For merchants, if they are familiar with the different price expectations between husbands and wives, their strategy becomes straightforward: sell the scarf for 350 yuan to the husband and 150 yuan to the wife, adopting a typical "customer-based pricing" or price differentiation strategy, also known as "price discrimination" (price discrimination).

Why do merchants do this?

Merchants conduct business with the goal of making a profit, aiming for maximum profit. Thus, they hope to sell products at the highest possible price. However, high-priced products may not suit all customers, leading to stockpiling and unsold goods. Consequently, merchants devise ways to sell products while achieving the highest profit, specifically pricing according to each consumer's acceptable level. But they also know that consumer demand is elastic. Elasticity here refers to the sensitivity of consumers to the price of a product; the higher the elasticity, the more sensitive consumers are to price. Therefore, merchants charge lower prices to consumers with high elasticity of demand since setting high prices would easily lose these price-sensitive consumers. Conversely, they charge higher prices to consumers with low elasticity of demand because these consumers are less sensitive to price changes and their demand is relatively stable.

Certain products are essential purchases for some people but optional for others. Profit-maximizing merchants base their strategies on the psychological characteristic of "demand elasticity" among consumers, applying differential pricing to their goods and reasonably integrating "price discrimination" into their business strategies.

Of course, this does not mean that "price discrimination" can be used arbitrarily. Since consumer demand elasticity varies significantly, differentially priced goods must be difficult to transfer, as consumers buying at a low price and reselling could expose the merchant's pricing strategy. However, merchants often struggle to measure consumer expected prices, ensure no resale between consumers, and segment markets effectively, which imposes many limitations on the use of "price discrimination."

In summary, everyone should stay cautious while shopping in malls to avoid becoming targets of merchants' "price discrimination." After understanding the concept of "price discrimination," husbands should weigh and think rationally when buying gifts for their wives. Remember, spending more money is minor compared to enduring complaints from your wife.
