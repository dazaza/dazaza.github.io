Single hand or double hands? Left hand or right hand? How do you hold your mobile phone?

by anonymous on 2013-10-22 19:21:50

As the shape and size of mobile phones change, the design of phone interfaces has become increasingly important. But how do general users actually hold their phones? 49% of people use one hand to hold their phones, 36% use one hand to hold and the other to operate, 15% use both hands simultaneously for operation, and the proportion of people using their left hand to hold the phone exceeds the percentage of left-handed individuals.

Phones are getting larger, evolving from sizes that could be used as a weapon to ones that can fit in the palm of your hand, and now even reaching the size of flip-flops. Among all these differently sized phones, how do people use today's smartphones? The UX MATTERS user experience discussion website conducted a survey, collecting data from public places such as bus stops and airports across the U.S., gathering a total of 1,333 data points, with the survey concluding in January this year. There are many intriguing numbers within this data that are worth considering by app developers.

Data shows that people spend nearly 40% of their time not actively operating their phones.

The first set of data reveals that when using their phones, users spend nearly 40% of their time not "operating" them—22% of the time is spent on calls, and 18.9% of the time users are not actively "manipulating" the device (they might be watching videos or listening to music). In the remaining operational time, there are three ways users interact with their phones: single-handed use at 49%, one hand holding and the other operating at 36%, and both hands operating simultaneously at 15%.

When using the phone with one hand, users may grip it either at the bottom or in the middle, which affects the range of control accessible by the fingers.

Another interesting statistic is that during single-handed phone use, 67% of the time users control the phone with their right thumb, while 33% use their left thumb. The proportion of left-thumb usage exceeds the population percentage of left-handed individuals (approximately 10%, varying slightly by region). There are several reasons for this result: while using the phone with one hand, users often carry something else (such as a shopping bag) in their dominant hand, which forces them to use their non-dominant hand to operate the phone.

In the case where one hand holds the phone and the other operates it, some users use their thumb while others use their index finger.

Between single-handed operation and simultaneous two-handed operation, people also use one hand to hold the phone and the other to operate it. Of these, 72% use their thumb for operation, while 28% use other fingers. During the survey, very few people were observed using a stylus, but the number was too small (only six individuals) to include in the calculation. Additionally, 79% of people use their left hand to hold the phone, while 21% use their right hand.

Dual-hand operation accounts for the smallest proportion.

Dual-hand operation tends to be more extreme, with 90% of users operating the phone in portrait mode and only 10% in landscape mode. Another interesting observation is that even when using both hands, users often operate the phone with just one finger—either the right or left thumb or another finger.
