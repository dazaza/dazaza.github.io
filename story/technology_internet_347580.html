Stock Index Futures Launch - Hexun Exclusive Interview with Yuan Lei, Analyst at Hengji Investment, Full Transcript

by huaxun924 on 2010-04-18 12:24:40

Interview Time: April 16, 2010, 14:55-15:20

Host: We are now broadcasting the Hexun website series of CSI 300 Index Futures interviews. Our analyst today is Mr. Yuan Lei from Hengji Investment. Mr. Yuan, hello! (14:57)

Yuan Lei: Hello, netizens and investors. Hello, host! (14:57)

Host: Let's get straight to the point. Has our market experienced a round of index futures trend? (14:57)

Yuan Lei: First, I think the trend isn't very clear. From today's trading floor, we can see that the correlation between the stock index futures and the A-share market is not obvious. For now, the market is transitioning smoothly. After all, it has just been launched, and the number of accounts opened for stock index futures is not particularly high, currently at 9137. Although there may be some increase in enthusiasm compared to before, overall, we have not yet reached a truly complete and good level. This will probably require a process. The most important thing is stability. (14:58)

Host: The reason financial legal entities haven’t entered the market yet. (14:58)

Yuan Lei: Yes. (14:58)

Host: What do you think about the management not allowing financial legal entities into the market? (14:58)

Yuan Lei: It might be to protect the interests of small and medium-sized investors, affecting their investment and participation methods. Current policies and strategies are not yet perfect, so they might enter after improvements. This would be a relatively stable and excellent approach for investors. (14:58)

Host: Is the risk greater before financial legal entities enter the market? (15:01)

Yuan Lei: Risks definitely exist. There is a viewpoint in the market that the launch of stock index futures marks the arrival of an institutional era. At this time, more investors may worry whether the listing of stock index futures means that we, as small and medium-sized investors, have no place to play anymore. I don't think so. Participating in this market involves significant risks, and one needs to enhance their professionalism. Nowadays, not only is the risk high, but participating in stock investments also requires a certain level of professionalism; blind investments are impossible. (15:01)

Host: The biggest problem brought by stock index futures is that the 5 million yuan threshold excludes 90% of individual investors. Do you think having stock index futures increases or decreases the risk for those who cannot participate in stock index futures? (15:02)

Yuan Lei: I think it reduces their risks. 5 million yuan is just a threshold, and more investors are excluded by this threshold for reasons beyond these 5 million yuan. As investors, many small and medium-sized investors do not have major issues with investing 5 million yuan, but why are they still excluded? Because stock index futures have their special characteristics, including the leverage principle. After investing 5 million yuan, the amount required for future investments will far exceed 5 million yuan. (15:02)

Host: It's not the threshold itself, but the issue of additional investments behind the threshold that keeps them out? (15:02)

Yuan Lei: Yes. (15:02)

Host: Let's continue discussing the stock index futures market. You mentioned that the divergence between today's spot and futures markets indicates that there is no stock index futures market. However, since 2890 points, the main driver has been the rise of 'two' stocks, while 'eight' stocks fell sharply the other day. Can this be considered a kind of stock index futures market? (15:02)

Yuan Lei: Personally, I believe this should still be based on the inherent operating rules of the A-share market, and the impact of stock index futures is very limited. In the early stages of the A-share market, there were regional investments, including Expo concepts and geographical speculation concepts. Small-cap stocks and concept stocks performed well, and the 'eight' among 'two-eight' performed well. Later, there was a slight shift, which I believed underwent at least three attempts at the beginning of the year, but none were successful. This time, the shift happened quietly. In the early part of this week, heavyweight stocks rose for a day and then fell back again, especially real estate and finance, which led the market down. The 'two-eight' effect was not particularly evident. Overall, the best method for the market is that stock index futures have more long-term than short-term effects. (15:02)

Host: If stock index futures cannot change the existing market structure, do you see more bullish, bearish, or neutral trends for the next seven or eight months of the year? (15:02)

Yuan Lei: I think neutral. The A-share market has its own independent operating rules. The weight of 'two' stocks in the market is very prominent, such as bank stocks and real estate stocks. To state a straightforward investment strategy, the more they fall, the more we should buy because their valuation and prices are very low, and their advantages are very clear. The performance of listed companies themselves is excellent, so we focus on investing in them. On the policy side, recent real estate policies have been successively introduced and are becoming increasingly tight, which will undoubtedly limit the upward trend of real estate stocks. On one hand, there is a long position, and on the other, a short position. The final result of the long-short battle may likely be a smooth transition because the forces of long and short are evenly matched, making it impossible for a large-scale trend to occur. (15:02)

Host: It seems there are factors unfavorable to the bulls, as market funds do not support the 'two' stock trend. (15:09)

Yuan Lei: Correct, the volume is insufficient. (15:10)

Host: What level of volume do you think would support a continuous trend for 'two'? (15:10)

Yuan Lei: I think it needs to exceed 20 billion yuan. (15:10)

Host: Two markets or one market? (15:10)

Yuan Lei: Two markets. Without sufficient volume, 'two' remains under observation. (15:11)

Host: Many bullish individuals shout loudly, believing there should be a heavyweight stock trend, but they may not notice the impact of trading volume on the market. Do you think that after the introduction of stock index futures, in terms of market trends, stock index futures will not significantly affect the spot market trends, but will have a significant impact on profit models and arbitrage models? (15:11)

Yuan Lei: I believe there will be arbitrage models involved, and some companies will make certain profits. Investors need to improve their professionalism. Old investors who previously traded both spot and futures markets, although the underlying assets have changed, the model remains the same, and techniques can be found. For new investors, the professionalism in this area might be less. Before this, I think improving professionalism is very important. Afterward, arbitrage hedging operations are feasible. (15:12)

Host: You view this market neutrally. Using the Shanghai Composite Index as a benchmark, what is the fluctuation range of the market? Below 3000 points or above 3000 points? (15:13)

Yuan Lei: Optimistically speaking, it should be between 2900 and 3300 points, which is a larger range. Pessimistically, it could adjust to 2800 or 2700 points. I think the possibility of a sharp rise or fall is not great. (15:13)

Host: The fluctuation range isn't large either? (15:13)

Yuan Lei: Yes, approximately a 200 to 300-point trend. (15:13)

Host: In the history of A-shares, such rises represent only about ten percent. It has already remained flat for eight months. Do you think it can remain flat for another eight months within the year? (15:13)

Yuan Lei: Not eight months, but five or six months is possible. (15:15)

Host: Neither the bulls nor the bears can achieve a directional breakthrough? (15:15)

Yuan Lei: Yes. (15:15)

Host: If external forces change this balance, what do you think could change it? (15:15)

Yuan Lei: Policies. China is a policy-driven market, and we cannot ignore policy attention. First, we need policy support, followed by volume support. (15:15)

Host: What policy support? The end of real estate regulation? (15:15)

Yuan Lei: Currently, house prices are too high, so this is not very realistic. If the RMB appreciates... (15:15)

Host: Do you think RMB appreciation would be beneficial? (15:15)

Yuan Lei: Yes. (15:15)

Host: The short-term possibility of RMB appreciation is not great. (15:15)

Yuan Lei: Yes. (15:16)

Host: You mentioned upward breakthroughs, but what about downward ones? (15:16)

Yuan Lei: That depends on policy factors. We do not know what unfavorable policies the government might introduce. (15:16)

Host: If the government does not introduce any unfavorable policies, there will be no downward breakthrough? (15:16)

Yuan Lei: It might be a balanced situation. (15:16)

Host: Would adjustments in overseas markets pull it down? (15:16)

Yuan Lei: The A-share market is a relatively closed market. Many people in the market believe that if U.S. stocks fall, A-shares will also fall, and if U.S. stocks soar, A-shares will follow suit. I completely disagree with this theory. The A-share market has its own uniqueness and is relatively closed. Recently, U.S. stocks have risen significantly, but A-shares have not moved much. This has some influence, but the trend of the external market is not the decisive factor in determining the direction of A-shares. Last year, when U.S. stocks kept adjusting downward, A-shares showed an independent rebound. I think the divergence between them is not normal联动性. Actually, A-shares adjusted earlier than U.S. stocks, and when A-shares adjusted, U.S. stocks just started adjusting, so they should have rebounded. When we rebounded before the adjustment, there was no clear linkage. (15:17)

Host: Do you think changes in external markets will not alter the trend of the market? (15:17)

Yuan Lei: Yes. (15:17)

Host: At 6124 points, U.S., A-share, and Hong Kong markets bottomed out at the same time in October 2008. Of course, Hong Kong had a secondary bottoming issue. Historically, they have never linked up so well. Of course, this round of rebounds saw A-shares rebound before U.S. stocks, but A-shares also adjusted before U.S. stocks. Adjustments took longer, much longer than the start time, several months longer. Can you explain the linkage between them, from joint adjustments to consecutive rebounds? (15:18)

Yuan Lei: The market shows phenomena like peaking together, bottoming together, and rebounding consecutively. Also, U.S. stocks rise while A-shares fall, or A-shares rise while U.S. stocks fall. Therefore, the direct impact of external markets on A-shares is not a valid theory or reasonable. Since it lacks validity, this matter must be viewed dialectically and divided by time windows. Divergences between A-shares and U.S. stocks occur at different time windows, leading to different trends. (15:18)

Host: Mainstream opinions believe that after the introduction of stock index futures, the volatility of A-shares will decrease, and risks will be partially hedged. How do you explain that despite having stock index futures, the Dow Jones and Hong Kong stocks experienced severe declines after the financial crisis, especially Hong Kong stocks, where the decline was similar to ours, with state-owned enterprise shares falling by seventy percent? We did not see stock index futures reducing their volatility. Historically, a seventy percent drop in Hong Kong stocks is extremely rare. How do you view this issue? (15:18)

Yuan Lei: Stock index futures were introduced in the U.S. quite early, in 1987. Initially, their impact on the U.S. stock market was also minimal, and they became mature markets only after improvements. Currently, A-shares have always been a one-sided bullish market, and only today has the process of one-sided bulling been completed, perfecting the entire mechanism of the A-share market. This includes hedging and risk avoidance, and the purpose of stock index futures is to avoid risks, which is the fundamental reason. However, when the overall market and the macroeconomic environment face issues like the U.S. subprime mortgage crisis, the financial crisis, or the Asian financial crisis in Hong Kong, it depends on the dynamics and mindset of the funds. They may believe that hedging risks cannot be achieved regardless of which side they are on, and one result is temporarily leaving the market to avoid risks. Therefore, after major economic problems arise, both markets will adjust because funds and institutions withdraw, believing they are unsafe anywhere. This leads to simultaneous declines without achieving the goal of avoiding risks. (15:18)

Host: Hedging risks through stock index futures is conditionally restricted. (15:18)

Yuan Lei: Yes. (15:18)

Host: Another interesting perspective is that after the introduction of the Dow Jones Index in 1987, it rose 15 times over 30 years, not much less than the rise in A-shares. Do you think stock index futures cannot change the mechanism of a one-sided market? (15:19)

Yuan Lei: Stock index futures have just been introduced, and at this stage, there are more people doing short selling than going long. After all, we have had a one-sided market for so long that the trend has not completely changed yet, requiring a process. Initially, there will be more longs than shorts, which may drive the market upward. Gradually, after two or three months of maturation, our thinking matures, and shorts begin to appear. When the two forces combine, it may stabilize. Afterwards, we still need to judge, based on the macroeconomic environment, policy environment, and internal market rules, which force dominates at different times. Only then can we determine the market trend. (15:19)

Host: We have considered both the normal and extreme states of the market. In an extreme situation, when the market forms a consistent view, unanimously bullish or bearish, do you think the futures market will lose liquidity? Stock index futures require counterparties. If everyone is bullish and no one goes short, long contracts cannot be established, and vice versa for short contracts. In such a situation, how do stock index futures hedge risks? (15:19)

Yuan Lei: This is a very extreme phenomenon, and I can confirm that it is impossible. Regardless of the environment, each investor's direction will definitely have differences, and it is impossible to reach complete consistency. (15:19)

Host: Let's say a relatively extreme situation where most people look one way, and a few look another. What happens if they cannot find enough counterparties? What if they want to go long or short but cannot? (15:19)

Yuan Lei: I think the market will be in a temporarily quiet stagnation phase. Like in the active A-share market, there is high volume, and when it is inactive, there is no volume. At that time, the market clearly lacks money and volume, but the market will not disappear; it can only stop. (15:19)

Host: Just now, I discussed the stock index futures turnover with colleagues, and after calculating, the actual turnover today might only be tens of billions, which is very little. If the turnover is so low, indeed, no one would want to go long or short for just tens of billions, as it is not worth it. Does the sluggish turnover mean what some people say—that the market will stay flat for a period, first satisfying sufficient turnover before choosing a direction? Otherwise, people might think it’s not worth fighting for this amount unless they use it not as a hedging or arbitrage tool but abandon it entirely. To want it, patience is needed to build up sufficient turnover, establish necessary positions, and then proceed. (15:20)

Yuan Lei: Today's turnover is not large, but this is a normal phenomenon. There are only over 9000 accounts opened, and it is impossible for all 9000 accounts to act. I think at least half of the people will be in a wait-and-see state, observing the trend for today or next week, and acting after seeing the trend. Now, it has just begun, and people won't sell easily. Some people will move, and we need to wait for the true volume to come in, gradually becoming more active to achieve arbitrage or risk avoidance, thereby truly realizing these goals. (15:20)

Host: I am concerned that in the future, there may be more shorts than longs. Management ultimately requires public mutual funds to hedge, but with such a large market value, who can be the counterparty for public mutual funds? Public mutual funds need to open short contracts for hedging, but how many longs can absorb them? If there aren't enough contracts to support the large contracts of public mutual funds, how big must the contracts be to sustain them? (15:20)

Yuan Lei: There must be a sufficiently strong counterparty; otherwise, it cannot be realized. Public mutual funds hedging can only lead to the market not rising, possibly trending downward or remaining stagnant, with a higher likelihood of moving downward. However, public mutual funds cannot all engage in hedging. (15:20)

Host: Currently, only 20% hedging is allowed. (15:20)

Yuan Lei: Yes, this is limited, and the state has policy regulations. Even if such an extreme phenomenon occurs, the state stands behind it. The "national team" will ultimately resolve this issue. If there isn't a sufficiently large counterparty to handle this extreme situation, the "national team" will step forward. (15:21)

Host: We currently do not have a stabilization fund, and social security can only make profits, not losses. No matter what, let it activate the market first? (15:21)

Yuan Lei: This is impossible. (15:21)

Host: Then who will step forward? (15:21)

Yuan Lei: That depends on the state's planning and strategy. Simultaneously, restrictions must be placed on large funds because risks are controlled within them. They cannot act arbitrarily or go to extremes. This is something that policies and plans must determine. (15:21)

Host: The series of stock index futures interviews ends here. Thank you, Mr. Yuan Lei, analyst from Hengji Investment, for exchanging ideas with netizens. Thank you, everyone! (15:21)
