What is Hypersexuality?

So everyone would ask, does this count as hypersexuality? Male hypersexuality, also known as excessive male libido, refers to a condition where sexual desires and impulses are excessively strong and vigorous. The clinical characteristics include frequent sexual excitement, abnormally urgent sexual behavior demands, increased frequency of sexual intercourse, prolonged duration of intercourse, far exceeding the normal level acceptable to most people. In some severe cases of hypersexuality, individuals may reach a point where they don't avoid strange or familiar partners, lack shame, and don't choose their partner, time, or location. They can also experience intense sexual desire and orgasm from handshakes, kisses, hugs, and touches. This is what hypersexuality is. In Traditional Chinese Medicine, it's referred to as "flower madness," "flower obsession," "flower heart wind," or "lust." In 1983, Patrick called it "sexual addiction" in his book "Addiction Forming," pointing out that sexual addiction is similar to drug addiction. This state of sexual addiction dominates their consciousness, thoughts, and lives, resulting in daily addictive activities. Their degree of hypersexuality presents a compulsive need, disregarding any conditions or environments, seeking sexual contact. High levels of sexual desire prevent them from living normally, affecting personal physical and mental health, as well as interpersonal relationships.

Male Libido and Age

After puberty, hormones are secreted in large quantities, causing heightened libido. During this period, human libido reaches its peak, while self-control is at its lowest. After the peak, libido gradually decreases, and instead, erections require a certain amount of time. The secretion of testosterone, which drives libido, decreases. Researchers have found that after the age of thirty, testosterone secretion begins to decline. Monthly libido shows regular fluctuations, and when entering the fifties and sixties, testosterone significantly reduces. Experts introduce that in the twenties, the average weekly frequency of sexual intercourse is 4-5 times; in the thirties, it's 2-4 times; in the fifties, it's 1-2 times. Approximately two-thirds of people over sixty still maintain a sex life, and half of those over seventy do so as well. Maintaining a healthy sex life can keep both mind and body relaxed and happy, making one younger and more energetic.

In young men, the hyperactive libido reaction can last for a certain period. For example, after reaching a climax, an erection can be maintained for a few minutes or even longer. However, for men in their thirties and forties, almost immediately after climax, they become flaccid. For men in their fifties, certain auxiliary methods are needed to achieve sexual arousal and climax. The strength and intensity of intercourse greatly weaken, the force of ejaculation decreases, and the volume of semen is less than before. The intensity level of hypersexuality also drops significantly.

For people over thirty, slightly extending the time of sexual response is not a bad thing. It allows for more coordination between men and women, reducing the time difference in reaching climaxes.

How to determine if someone has hypersexuality?

Clinically, there are very few complaints of hypersexuality. Unless discovered through sexual consultation or described by a spouse, it is quite rare for men to seek medical help themselves. Often, newlyweds consult more frequently. Generally speaking, during the early stages of marriage, especially within the first six months, males may have stronger and more frequent sexual desires, which is a normal phenomenon and requires understanding from the wife without undue worry.

Generally, if both partners are satisfied with the level of sexual excitement and behavior, even if there is an increase, but without adverse consequences, it cannot be considered pathological. Due to varying environments and difficulties distinguishing between normal and excessive sexual desires, there is still no exact standard for judgment.

How to treat hypersexuality?

Firstly, it's important to identify the cause of hypersexuality. If no organic disease is found, couples may separate for a period to reduce sexual stimulation. At the same time, psychological treatment and sexual education should be conducted, encouraging participation in cultural and sports activities, applying energy to work and study, allowing the sexual nerves appropriate rest opportunities.

Additionally, appropriate medication can be used for treatment, such as tranquilizers to alleviate the patient's sexual impulses, using Diazepam 25 mg three times a day, or Miltown 0.2 g three times a day, or Chlorpromazine 25 mg three times a day. Sex hormone therapy can also be used to counteract its effects, such as using estrogen for male hypersexuality caused by organic diseases. For hypersexuality caused by organic lesions, treatments targeting the specific lesion can be applied.
