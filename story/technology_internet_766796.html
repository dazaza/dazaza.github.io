Lian Shanyou: What should Apple learn from Xiaomi?

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 21:35:10

Apple plans to trial produce and sell 2.5 million to 3 million affordable iPhones. Following this, Lei Jun, known as "LeiBus," announced that Xiaomi would launch the Redmi priced at 799 yuan.

Although Xiaomi is already a low-end market brand compared to Apple, both brands have announced they will lower their positioning in their respective markets, which is their common point. The debates surrounding these announcements are also focused on the potential harm to their brands.

Apple's high-end image is self-evident. In 2012, iPhone had an 8.8% market share in the global mobile phone market but accounted for 73% of the global mobile phone profits, making it absolutely one of the top-tier high-end brands. However, a large part of this is due to its brand premium, so many people worry that once it penetrates the low-end market, it might not only lose the premium portion but also be unable to return to the high-end market. As for Xiaomi, it has always claimed to make phones for enthusiasts, and the 799-yuan price tag will inevitably attract many college students and migrant workers. Such a mixed situation will necessarily affect the parent brand Xiaomi.

However, the predicament Apple faces forces it to make such a choice. In the second quarter of 2013, iPhone shipped 31.2 million units, growing by 20%, with a 14% share of the global smartphone market; its old rival Samsung grew by 56%, shipping a record-breaking 76 million units, capturing a 33% market share. In the same second quarter, Apple sold 4.3 million units in China, even losing to Xiaomi for the first time, being 100,000 units less than Xiaomi, while Samsung sold 15.5 million units.

Worse still, the global smartphone high-end market has become saturated and is even shrinking. However, the growth potential of the low-end market remains significant.

Apple has been forced into a narrow alley with no way out. On the surface, Apple's price reduction indirectly verifies the sustainability of the Samsung model. Although Samsung's products do not have the revolutionary nature of Apple's, consumers can always find good options here. This is still considering from the perspective of pure hardware genes, but there are signs of failure in Apple's hardware market.

Amazon's development path may provide some reference value for Apple. Its Kindle devices themselves are quite affordable, and the real source of income is paid downloads for reading. Is Apple going to continue focusing on being a hardware company, where the future is uncertain, or will it "soften" and earn money through software via hardware carriers?

After accumulating iPhone sales to a certain extent, application software has also reached a considerable level. Currently, Apple charges a 30% entry fee for apps, which is not cheap. Theoretically, this market and revenue are substantial. However, I found that app revenue currently accounts for only 10% of the total income from Apple phones, which is too low.

If Apple switches its business model to earning income through the App platform or places more emphasis on this area, then launching low-end products makes sense and has value. As long as users download various applications, fees can be charged, and reading also requires payment, making the market's potential space enormous.

Using this logic, it becomes easier to understand Xiaomi. If you consider Xiaomi as a hardware company, the price reduction of Redmi will undoubtedly harm the brand. But Lei Jun said that Xiaomi consists of three components: "hardware + software + internet." It claims to be a new category, not a smartphone but an internet phone. Moreover, Xiaomi phone users have relatively high activity levels, ranking third domestically. Making money from game joint operations and software profits still holds some advantages. Currently, Xiaomi is the fifth-largest game joint operation platform in China, but the monthly revenue from this sector is only 20 million yuan now, and they claim it will reach 150 million yuan per month by the end of next year.

In a recent interview with Reuters, Lei Jun said: "Don't compare me to Apple; I'm more like Amazon. Xiaomi phones are just an entry point." From this perspective, Apple should instead learn from Xiaomi's approach to making phones.
