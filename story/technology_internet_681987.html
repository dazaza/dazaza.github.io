[Parent-Child Channel] The consequences of irregular prenatal check-ups for expectant mothers can be very serious.

by qssqwe215 on 2012-02-28 16:39:56

Recently, the weather in Qingdao has been changing drastically, with unpredictable cold and hot spells. This has led to an increase in citizens seeking medical treatment for respiratory diseases or colds. Many expectant mothers in Qingdao are worried about the high bacterial count in hospitals, fearing it might affect their fetus's health. Even when feeling unwell or sick, they avoid going to the hospital and instead take random medications at home. Experts warn that taking random medication is harmful and offers no benefits, while failing to undergo regular prenatal examinations can have even more severe consequences.

Fear of [viruses] keeps them away from the hospital

Last week, [expectant mother] Aixiu (a pseudonym) underwent a painful induction surgery at the hospital. A few weeks prior, during a routine prenatal checkup, the doctor discovered that Aixiu’s fetus was a [encephalocele], meaning the baby had no brain development. Looking at the CT scan showing the undeveloped fetal brain, Aixiu cried bitterly, deeply regretting her decision.

It turned out that due to the unpredictable weather, Aixiu caught a cold and developed a sore throat. Her husband suggested she go to the hospital for a checkup, but Aixiu insisted that hospitals were prone to infections, fearing it might harm her fetus. Consequently, she took some random antibiotics and cold medicine at home and reduced the frequency of her regular prenatal checkups. As a result, she failed to detect the baby's abnormality early on. [Because of the fear of viruses in the hospital, I avoided going there, only to lose something much more important.] Aixiu lamented.

The first three months of pregnancy are crucial

[With the recent unpredictable weather, cases of various respiratory diseases have increased, making expectant mothers nervous and reluctant to seek medical examinations even when sick.] Experts stated: [For most adults, certain illnesses like colds may not be serious, but for pregnant women, especially those in the early stages of pregnancy, untreated illness or random medication use could lead to fetal abnormalities.]

Dr. Du Wenmin, Deputy Director of the Municipal Drug Adverse Reaction Monitoring Center, told reporters: [During pregnancy, it is essential to be cautious with medication, especially in the first three months, which is a critical period for the development and formation of the fetus's organs. During this time, the fetus is particularly sensitive to drugs.] In spring, with large temperature fluctuations between morning and evening, catching a cold becomes more common. Pregnant women experience a decrease in immunity, increasing the chances of falling ill. Taking random medication at home, such as sedatives, antihistamines, anti-epileptic drugs, or hormones, can all lead to fetal abnormalities.

Experts remind pregnant women to stay warm, avoid getting chilled, minimize visits to crowded and noisy places, and frequently ventilate by opening windows. Engaging in outdoor activities to breathe fresh air is also recommended. [If illness occurs, try to avoid using medication and immediately consult the gynecology department of a reputable hospital, following medical advice and taking medication under the guidance of a doctor.]

[Prenatal checkups + nutrition] Neither should be missed

Regular prenatal checkups and cooperation with doctors are proactive measures to prevent fetal abnormalities. Early in pregnancy, around the third month, pregnant women should visit the hospital for their first screening. This primarily involves estimating the fetus's size and other developmental aspects through ultrasound imaging, checking for neural tube defects such as encephaloceles, hydrocephalus, or spina bifida.

Additionally, [expectant mothers should avoid all types of pollution, such as newly renovated houses, as these could lead to infant deformities. During pregnancy, exposure to harmful substances should be minimized as soon as possible. Avoid smoking, drinking alcohol, and ensure family members do not smoke, providing a smoke-free environment for the pregnant woman and fetus.]

According to large-scale medical research, supplementing 0.4 milligrams of folic acid daily from three months before conception to the third month of pregnancy and eating foods rich in folic acid, such as red amaranth, spinach, lettuce, asparagus, beans, yeast, animal liver, apples, and citrus fruits, can reduce the chance of neural tube defects in the fetus by 40% to 80%. The supply of proteins and vitamins should not be overlooked either. Especially after the third month of pregnancy, when the fetus grows rapidly, more nutrients are required. Poor nutrition during pregnancy can easily lead to fetal deformities or cause intellectual disabilities due to insufficient prenatal development.
