By 2016, Nokia will be able to launch phones again.

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 16:00:39

**Beijing Time, September 4th — Nokia is selling its devices and services business to Microsoft for $7.2 billion, a move that saddened many as it marked the end of an era!**

In this deal, there is also a licensing agreement worth €1.65 billion ($2.2 billion). Nokia has licensed its brand to Microsoft, and today it revealed more details.

From the description, Nokia may still launch hardware in the future. After 30 months (starting January 2016), Nokia will be allowed to produce phones again.

**The Intriguing Brand Licensing Terms**

Here’s what the documents say:

"Microsoft has agreed to a 10-year licensing deal with Nokia to use the Nokia brand on existing 'Mobile Phones' products."

Note that "Mobile Phones" here refers to non-smartphone devices, which Nokia calls "Smart Devices."

"Nokia continues to own and retain the Nokia brand. According to the terms of the transaction, Microsoft has agreed to a 10-year license to use the Nokia brand on existing and upcoming Series 30 and Series 40 operating systems. For 30 months after the transaction closes, Nokia is prohibited from using the Nokia brand in mobile device sales. Additionally, Nokia cannot use the Nokia brand on its own mobile devices until December 31, 2015."

**Explanation:**

- Microsoft now fully owns the Lumia and Asha brands.

- In existing Nokia 'Mobile Phones' (non-smartphones), Microsoft has secured a 10-year license for the Nokia brand.

- The Nokia brand remains owned by Nokia; Microsoft only holds the license.

- For Series 30 and Series 40 low-end devices, Microsoft can use the Nokia brand for 10 years.

- Since Asha is based on the Series 40 operating system, it can be sold under the Nokia brand.

- If Microsoft launches new smartphones or high-end feature phones, Nokia prohibits Microsoft from using the Nokia brand. Microsoft can use the Lumia brand for new smartphones and the Asha brand for high-end feature phones.

Overall, Microsoft permanently acquired the Lumia and Asha brands and can use the Nokia brand for Series 30 and Series 40 low-end devices for 10 years. However, if Microsoft introduces new smartphones or high-end feature phones, it is not allowed to use the Nokia brand.

**Nokia Can Launch Mobile Devices Starting in 2016**

There are some loopholes in the agreement. Nokia still has ongoing businesses and can license patents. It can also authorize its brand through patent licensing channels: if not for mobile devices, it can authorize the brand immediately; if for mobile devices, it must wait 30 months.

After the transaction takes effect, if Nokia develops non-mobile-device products, it can still use the Nokia brand. After December 31, 2015, Nokia can once again use the Nokia brand on mobile devices.

**Nokia May Reintroduce New Mobile Devices**

Nokia has left itself room to potentially re-enter the mobile device market or other areas. Despite significant layoffs and closing down Symbian, Nokia embraced Windows Phone and continued to explore new technologies, considering how to apply them.

David Wood, one of the co-founders of Symbian (who no longer works at Nokia), believes: "Over time, various types of devices may emerge that don’t resemble today's phones. From a long-term perspective, who knows what attractive devices might appear? With innovations in wearable computing, such as those around Google Glass, Nokia could still enter these device categories."

A Nokia spokesperson responded: "Our existing CTO organization has been exploring certain areas that have not been fully disclosed, which could present opportunities for Nokia or partner products without conflicting with the agreement. Currently, we are conducting detailed strategic planning for our Advanced Technologies business, which builds on many activities of our existing CTO and IPR organizations. We will disclose more details after the transaction is completed."

Nokia's CTO office includes some very smart individuals who have learned an important lesson: how a once-great company can fail.

What technology is Nokia focusing on? It's quite broad, including touchscreens, augmented reality, and others. Because Nokia was too successful in the past, these technologies were not fully utilized. Nokia’s Advanced Technologies division is also exploring carbon graphene, nanotechnology, flexible displays, and more. Some of these have been publicly disclosed, while others remain undisclosed.

Yes, Nokia has rediscovered its lost roots of innovation and disruption. Considering its less than 3% share in the smartphone market, the brand has little left to lose.

One question remains: If Microsoft doesn't want to cause market confusion, why not sign a longer brand licensing agreement? This is puzzling, as after 30 months, Microsoft allows Nokia to use its own brand. Perhaps Microsoft never intended for the brand license to last 30 months and instead plans to cut the business or sell its assets to someone else.

The transaction still needs regulatory approval. If the final approval does not go through, Microsoft will pay Nokia a breakup fee of $750 million.
