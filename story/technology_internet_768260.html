The Story of Facebook's First Female Engineer

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 19:50:14

　　English Title: Digital Indians: How Ruchi Sanghvi engineered her rise

　　The story goes back to 2005, when Ruchi Sanghvi first arrived at Facebook's office in downtown Palo Alto for an interview. She noticed a sign outside that read "Engineers wanted."

　　At the time, Facebook was located above a Chinese restaurant in downtown Palo Alto. It was a place full of modernity and young engineers. The office was filled with black sofas and lava lamps, and wall hangings and movie posters covered most of the walls.

　　Earlier that year, having graduated from Carnegie Mellon University’s computer science department, she landed a job on Wall Street just three weeks after graduation. "I panicked; I wanted a place where I could utilize my core knowledge," she said.

　　When her friend told her about Facebook, she was already in California and had interviewed at Oracle.

　　"I didn’t know much about Facebook. I didn't even know they had moved to California. I thought they were still in Harvard dorms in Boston," she joked.

　　Skateboard Culture

　　We sat in Dropbox's stylish office in downtown San Francisco, where Ruchi Sanghvi, now 31, serves as Executive Vice President.

　　In the corridors of this online storage company, employees zoomed past us on skateboards or bicycles. Some were playing pool or video games. A karaoke room was ready for employees to showcase their singing talents.

　　Meanwhile, at the other end of the company, we were talking to Ruchi Sanghvi about how she got her job at Facebook and became its first female engineer.

　　"When I started at Facebook, it only had around 20 employees. I witnessed its growth into a company with thousands of employees; from a few million users to billions; from a site serving only college students to one serving the entire world," she said.

　　"It was quite chaotic, but also quite wonderful. I learned everything there."

　　At Facebook, she was responsible for developing the News Feed.

　　"What was it like working at Facebook?" I asked.

　　Ruchi Sanghvi replied that she was used to being part of a minority. In her university classes, out of 150 people, only five were women.

　　At Facebook, she found her place.

　　"You need to have strong opinions, make sure your voice is heard, ask questions. Sometimes, others will tell you that your ideas are too naive and you need to redo them. But overall, it’s an elite education process. It offers the best learning environment," she said.

　　Ruchi Sanghvi comes from an entrepreneurial family

　　Facebook was also where she met her husband, who was the first Indian engineer hired by Facebook.

　　I asked her for a story about Mark Zuckerberg (Facebook's founder and current CEO). She furrowed her brow in thought, saying she wasn't very keen to talk about Mark. Then she shared the following story.

　　She told us about how aggressive user backlash almost led to the discontinuation of the News Feed feature.

　　"When we launched the News Feed, our total user base was less than ten million. When Mark announced the launch at a press conference, nearly a million users opposed the News Feed," she said.

　　Ruchi Sanghvi gave a more detailed version of this story at a conference last year.

　　"Anti-Facebook groups or 'Ruchi is the devil' groups were formed. People gathered outside Facebook's offices to protest. But we realized that those opposing the News Feed were using it to spread their dissatisfaction with Facebook's News Feed to more people," she explained.

　　Mark stood firm under the pressure, Ruchi Sanghvi told us.

　　"Generally, in other companies, if more than 10% of users oppose your product, you would definitely revert to the original state or do something about the new change. But Mark was very confident in his belief, he was convinced of the potential of the News Feed," she said.

　　When Ruchi Sanghvi left Facebook in 2010 to start her own company, Facebook already had over 1,500 employees and more than half a billion users.

　　Growing up in Pune, an industrial city in India, she once dreamed of taking over her family business. Her father, a second-generation businessman, ran a large engineering company. Her grandfather was in the steel business. "We are an entrepreneurial family," she said. But now, she is in the U.S., studying computers, and working at Facebook. The world was calling her.

　　In 2010, she and her husband founded their own company—Cove. There, with the help of a team of engineers, they developed "collaboration software" for communities and networks.

　　"For someone like me, a new immigrant, transitioning from a regular employee to a startup founder is a complex process," Ruchi Sanghvi is assisting the U.S. Immigration Bureau in simplifying the immigration process. "When I started Cove, I consulted with three immigration lawyers about the process of hiring foreign workers. They gave me a long list of requirements to legally hire a foreigner to work for me," she said.

　　Multiple Roles

　　Two years later, in February 2012, Cove was acquired by Dropbox, a cloud storage service company.

　　At Dropbox, a company barely six years old, it boasts over 175 million users. Ruchi Sanghvi plays multiple roles. She leads recruitment, believing that "only top talent can create excellent products." She also manages the marketing and communications departments.

　　I asked her if she wanted to do anything in India.

　　"I would love to do something for India. There are many barriers and restrictions in India, making it difficult to achieve exciting things. Even small things like high-speed internet in the office are restricted. India isn't like Silicon Valley, where if you have a dream and work hard, you'll be rewarded. However, I admire those who work in India. They need more courage and determination," she added, then smiled and hurried off to her next meeting.
