Principles and Implementation of Wiring Network Design during Office Decoration

by bjqunban on 2010-05-25 13:33:28

The wiring system is the network within a building or complex, forming the basis for achieving intelligent functionality. It not only connects voice and data communication devices, switching equipment, and other information management systems within a building, but also links these devices to external communication networks. It enables networking of office computers and related office equipment with flexible usability, stable operation, long-term use, and an aesthetically pleasing appearance.

When decorating an office, the placement of information points (information sockets) on walls, their convenience of use, the number of information points, and whether future issues can be easily maintained are all factors we must consider in designing an office network cabling system. Therefore, the orientation of strong and weak electrical lines (power supply and image, data, voice) in offices are all within our planning and design scope. Thus, details determine success or failure in office cabling networks. Our design philosophy is to implement using the standard for office cabling systems, making management and maintenance convenient, ensuring system design has a certain forward-looking nature, and considering future high-demand applications.

1. Principles of Office Network Cabling Design

1. Comprehensiveness

The office cabling system must meet various transmission requirements for different analog or digital signals, combining all language, data, image, and surveillance device cabling into one standardized system. Devices and information outlets need only one standard connection line through a standard interface to connect them.

2. Reliability

Products used in the office cabling system must pass international organization certification. The design, installation, and testing of the cabling system follow ANSI EIA/TIA 568A and GB/T50311-200 standards, adhering to domestic cabling norms and testing specifications.

3. Flexibility

Each office location's information point usage should not only meet current user needs but also align with future expectations for information systems. Data and voice twisted-pair cabling should have interchangeable properties, forming a complete cabling system.

4. Rationality

The orientation of strong and weak electrical cabling in offices should be reasonably matched, avoiding interference while maintaining aesthetic appeal. Users operating computers, phones, and network cables should find it easy to manage and maintain in the future.

5. Complementarity of Wired and Wireless Networks

Depending on specific architectural environments and office requirements, such as long-term or temporary network usage, decide between wired or wireless cabling. Generally, combining wired and wireless networks leverages each method's strengths to achieve internet connectivity for office purposes.

2. Implementation of Office Network Cabling

1. Office Cabling Requirements

As part of the horizontal subsystem of the cabling system, office information sockets must meet the following requirements regardless of how corporate office applications change.

- For telephone: Utilize a PBX to effectively connect the company with the outside world and facilitate internal calls.

- For computer networks: Use a star topology structure that supports current and future network applications—10Mb Ethernet, 100Mb Fast Ethernet, 1Gb Gigabit Ethernet.

- For image transmission: Analog images, digital images, video conferencing, etc.

A modern office environment requires reliable communication at all times. Consider the cabling system carefully in the following situations: before constructing a new building, during construction, during renovation of old buildings, when relocating, when increasing phone and computer usage without adopting integrated cabling, and when existing cabling cannot meet demands.

2. Installation Position of Information Sockets

In office environments, office spaces include large open-plan areas and small enclosed rooms. Different installation methods apply to work area subsystem panels in these two scenarios. Small rooms do not require partition boards; information sockets can simply be installed on walls. For large open-plan areas, the following two installation methods are recommended:

- Installing information sockets on the floor: Metal boxes installed on the floor should be sealed, waterproof, dustproof, and equipped with adjustable height functions. This method has higher design and installation costs. Since staff positions cannot be predicted beforehand, flexibility is limited. It is suggested to pre-bury based on room function and avoid extensive use to maintain aesthetics.

- Installing information sockets on walls: If partition board locations are not determined, RJ45 embedded sockets can be evenly installed along the walls of large open-plan areas at regular intervals. RJ45 embedded information sockets should maintain a 20cm distance from adjacent power sockets, with the bottom edges of both information and power sockets 30cm above the floor level. When connecting information modules with twisted-pair cables, pay attention to color code matching for correct connections. There are two connection methods: 568A and 568B. Either can be used, but ensure consistency across the entire system.

3. Office Cable Routing Methods

Office cables can directly run through ceiling racks, placed in troughs, or buried in walls. Industrial standards allow combining points near connection locations.

- Using suspended lightweight trough cable trays: Suitable for large buildings, these provide mechanical protection and support for horizontal cables. These closed metal trays are installed inside ceilings, leading from weak current shafts to rooms with information points via pre-buried iron pipes of varying sizes, directing lines to concealed iron boxes on walls.

- Ground-level cable routing: Suitable for large open-plan offices with dense ground-based information outlets, suggest pre-burying metal troughs or trough floors in the ground layer. Main trunk troughs extend from weak current shafts along corridors to rooms with information points, then branch out to information outlet ports using bracket troughs. Strong current lines can be parallelly configured with weak current lines but must be separated into different troughs. This provides users with an integrated panel including data, voice, uninterruptible power supply, and lighting power outlets, achieving true office automation in a clean environment.

4. Other Details of Office Cabling

Information sockets (information outlets) in each office should be arranged horizontally with power sockets, about 30cm apart. This facilitates simultaneous connection of power and twisted-pair network cables and simplifies future maintenance. Currently, each information socket should ideally use a four-port wiring panel, with two network interfaces (one external network, one internal network), and two voice points (one internal line, one external line). Network data and voice cabling should be interchangeable. Based on actual corporate needs, design the number of information and voice points in each office, and place power sockets near each information point. Sometimes video points, fire extinguishing system probes, and monitoring points may also need to be considered in appropriate locations.

The office cabling system is closely linked to office decoration and furniture configuration. It is a standardized wiring system integrating all voice, data, image, and surveillance equipment, allowing multiple device terminal plugs to be inserted into standard information sockets. The office cabling system is compatible with different manufacturers' voice and data devices, using the same cables and patch panels, the same plugs, and modular plug-in sockets.

Various office network cabling designs should consider approximately 60% redundancy based on the number of computer installations during enterprise network construction to prevent insufficient port numbers requiring hub or switch extensions, which could lead to network communication failures.

The office cabling system uses modular design; except for fixed horizontal cables within the office, all other connectors are modular standard components, making expansion and reconfiguration easy. The office cabling system can easily incorporate current and future voice, data, network, interconnection, and surveillance equipment, representing truly advanced technology oriented towards the future.

5. Office Wireless Networking ("Flexible Office")

To adapt to market changes, adjustments have been made within the company, resulting in increased personnel in original offices where existing cabling ports no longer meet adjusted office needs. Additionally, the company’s work mode has changed, with frequent temporary personnel handling business in offices. If the company’s office environment is an old building or if development necessitates non-fixed office locations where wired cabling is impossible, consider establishing a wireless network to provide mobile office personnel with network access. Using wireless "cabling" allows users to have a movable office area. For companies needing to frequently add office nodes yet still require network connectivity, this is particularly useful. The "flexible office" enables newly added office access points to use existing shared devices like printers and storage equipment within the office, with simple operations involving just inserting a wireless network card. This compensates for the shortcomings of wired cabling by combining wired and wireless solutions to meet practical corporate networking needs.
