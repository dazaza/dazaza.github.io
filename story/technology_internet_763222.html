Xiaomi Co-founder: Why Our Success Can't Be Duplicated

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 17:14:43

Xiaomi was founded in April 2010, and this year marks its three-and-a-half-year anniversary. In our first year, we sold seven million units with a revenue of 12 billion yuan (including tax), and last year we paid 1.9 billion yuan in taxes. This year, it will double. We just announced our performance for the first half of the year, surpassing last year's total earnings with an income of 13.27 billion yuan and total sales reaching 7.03 million units.

How did Xiaomi phones come into being?

The reason we ventured into making phones is quite simple: by the end of 2009, the first, second, and third Android phones had emerged, but as phone enthusiasts, we felt that these phones were far from what we wanted. We wanted to create a phone that would excite us, the enthusiasts.

In fact, deep down, we thought it was quite natural to make phones. However, when we initially faced the media and said we would make phones, everyone thought we were crazy because none of the seven founding members understood hardware; we had never touched it before. Lei Jun succeeded with Kingsoft software and Joyo.com, Lin Bin with Kugou, and I myself came from the software industry.

At that time, no one believed we could succeed. They questioned whether there were enough Chinese phone enthusiasts, especially given that ours was an internet-based phone brand without physical stores or backing from telecom operators. So how could Xiaomi succeed? Although the market for enthusiasts seemed narrow, recent third-party data shows that among the top ten most active Android models, one is Xiaomi. I believe there are some experiences worth sharing: in the mobile internet era, you need to define your product precisely. When everyone talks about changing the world, you should start with a small issue. Always think about fixing problems around you that irritate you.

Many successful companies took their first steps this way. For example, YouTube founder Chen Shijun decided to create a video platform due to a surprisingly simple idea: he couldn't share videos from his family gathering with friends in different locations. Thus, he wanted to create a site where friends could easily download videos. The original motivation behind his entrepreneurship was very pure and simple.

We at Xiaomi did the same thing. Regardless of the various niche phones on the market like music phones or senior-friendly phones, we focused on creating a phone that enthusiasts would love. What do enthusiasts like? First, speed — which can be achieved through both software and hardware optimization. Therefore, I believe that precise positioning is crucial in the mobile internet space.

When we take the first step to change the world starting with small issues, who exactly are we designing the product for? Once, I gave my father a Xiaomi phone. He lived in our hometown while I was in Beijing. On the third day, he called me saying he couldn’t access Weibo. It turned out he had accidentally turned off the network connection switch. Xiaomi had made a convenient feature by moving the switch from the system settings to the menu. For enthusiast users, this was highly appreciated, but if you're designing a phone for seniors, they might easily mis-touch it. Hence, when taking the first step, it’s important to clarify who your target audience is and understand how your users interact with the product.

What makes Xiaomi different from traditional phones?

Changes in sales channels

We conduct our sales entirely online. For marketing, we rely mainly on new media platforms such as WeChat and Weibo. In terms of sales distribution, 70% comes from Xiaomi's website and 30% from telecom operator channels. This is contrary to Huawei, Lenovo, and others who primarily depend on operator channels while we have no physical stores.

Changes in phone R&D methods

Traditionally, phone development was relatively closed, both in software operations and hardware research. Xiaomi's software and hardware R&D processes leverage user feedback obtained from forums and social media platforms like Weibo, thus utilizing customer needs more effectively.

Changes in business model

Traditional phones make money from hardware sales, usually in one-off transactions. At Xiaomi, we view our phones as internet products, akin to a "large QQ." We believe that in the future, the entire phone ecosystem could potentially make money not from hardware itself, but through value-added services such as accessories, cloud-based software services, and app distribution channels. Because of this value-added service model, we focus heavily on scaling up and building ecosystems.

Why can't Xiaomi's success be replicated?

Can Xiaomi's model be replicated? My answer is always the same: the principles and models can certainly be learned, but achieving success is difficult. Those wanting to study and emulate Xiaomi fall into two categories: one consists of already-successful phone manufacturers, like Samsung, Lenovo, Huawei, etc., who are giants in the industry; the other consists of startups trying to enter the market.

For established manufacturers, replicating Xiaomi poses challenges and pressures. Firstly, there’s the challenge of product structure. Xiaomi's commercial product strategy focuses on premium products with only one or two models, whereas giants like Samsung, Huawei, and Lenovo often have 30-50 models. Selling a phone at 2,000 yuan when others sell at 3,000-5,000 yuan is a significant disadvantage.

Secondly, organizational structure is a challenge. Xiaomi currently has 3,000 employees, but due to our internet-based business model, our organization remains flat. Traditional phone manufacturers have established structures aligned with their existing business models, and transforming them would be a painful process involving fundamental changes.

A shift in mindset is essential. Without the right mindset, everything else becomes futile. If you’re the decision-maker in your company, it's easier to implement changes; however, if it's a public company, things get complicated. Many people talk about relying on professional managers to drive change, but this rarely works. I don’t believe anyone is a genius; many decisions involve significant risks, require long-term planning, and may even need constant adjustments. For well-established companies like Nokia, this leaves little room for opportunity.

Moreover, many startups see Xiaomi's model as glamorous but overlook that our model involves heavy brick-laying work. Our operations closely resemble Apple's: industrial design, hardware design, component procurement, production control, software development, hardware manufacturing... There are seven or eight major chains involved, each incredibly complex.

Many people assume we are a marketing company, but I believe our core lies in product experience. To achieve excellence, ensuring each chain performs optimally presents enormous challenges. Consequently, many startups or internet companies entering the phone market either opt for branding or embed software into hardware, but none have been truly successful.

What new opportunities exist in mobile internet?

Xiaomi's current product philosophy is "Born for Enthusiasts," yet our consumers aren't merely China's 100,000-200,000 enthusiasts but millions of users. This is because we've been fortunate to ride the wave of smartphone upgrades. Previously, many used feature phones, now they want smartphones.

In the next 3-5 years, a critical trend will emerge: redefining consumer electronics. Whether it's open-source hardware or smart home appliances, all are related. For instance, imagine turning on your air conditioner via WiFi and your phone half an hour before arriving at the office. Or during winter, instead of struggling to turn off switches, simply press a button on your smart device.

Therefore, in mobile internet innovation, consider how products and ideas can integrate with phones and mobile internet. Think about aspects of daily life and health, such as wristbands, watches, and other specialized products.
