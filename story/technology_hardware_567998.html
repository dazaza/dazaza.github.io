Group buying now is the last push that developers need.

by beetgk2958 on 2011-11-18 13:16:13

Who organizes them, what is their nature, and what long-term impact will they have on the real estate market? These questions are not given much weight by most homebuyers. The only question they focus on is: how much of a discount can buying groups secure? When homebuyers fixate solely on the discount amount, underlying concerns arise. You might buy a house at a 10% discount today, but what happens if the developer offers an 8.5% discount a month later? This scenario is highly likely because no one knows where the bottom of the market lies, and even group-buying cannot control the direction of the market. It’s similar to department store promotions; although the last day of the event usually offers the lowest discounts, it's also when inventory is most scarce because the best-priced items are often snapped up on the first day. In most cases, among the same batch of housing units in a project, the houses you bought earlier at a 10% discount are generally better than those bought later at an 8.5% discount. For homebuyers with genuine needs, as long as you choose a sound and reliable group, it’s best to act early.

It seems that these masterminds believe that group-buying can break the current deadlock in the real estate market and has, to some extent, exerted a stabilizing effect. As a result, we see various kinds of home-buying groups emerging like mushrooms after rain, including "ten-thousand-person home-buying groups" popping up across the country, and even in Xi'an, such groups exist. For ordinary people, does "group-buying" bring nothing but discounts? In Beijing, does group-buying come with its own set of concerns?

The reason group-buying has spread like wildfire across the country is clear. In the game between homebuyers and developers, housing prices are always controlled by the developers, leaving buyers often passively following. Moreover, when disputes occur, individual buyers are often overwhelmed. However, buying groups can solve these problems. Large-scale group purchases can enjoy exclusive discounts and offer organizational guarantees in terms of rights coordination and protection. With a cold market and few customers visiting sales offices, developers' strategies mostly involve thin-profit high-volume sales, making them naturally receptive to group-buying activities. As buyers, the interests of homebuyers are amplified during the process of group-buying, playing a more prominent role. Significant discounts become the greatest privilege for "group-buyers," and this kind of discount is often exclusive, available only to group members.

Regardless of the model, the role played by group-buying during a sluggish market period is undeniable. Faced with the wave of returns in first-tier cities and the weak "copper September, iron October" market, forming groups first and then offering discounts can both strengthen customer stability and maintain the confidence of existing and potential customers while promoting sales, preserving the project image, and achieving quick results in terms of volume and funds. Currently, group-buying is the decisive move for developers rather than a last-minute scramble. From this perspective, group-buying has certain market-stabilizing significance. Even during prosperous market periods, this model can still save human resources and marketing costs by selling large quantities of products and services all at once.

Group-buying itself represents a disruption of traditional marketing models, allowing people to constantly feel freedom and sharing during the process. In the traditional one-on-one sales model, homebuyers lack initiative, but through group-buying, they can gain greater collective bargaining power by banding together. This single point alone is the reason why buying groups have flourished so enthusiastically, and currently, group-buying houses can lead to win-win outcomes for both buyers and sellers. However, some in the industry have raised doubts, labeling it as "a real estate gimmick full of entertainment effects." This view holds that the real estate industry operates according to market rules, and neither price levels nor the specific determination of discount magnitudes can be uniformly applied without considering product value and supply-demand structures. Since it's a group purchasing behavior, it's difficult to achieve uniformity in many aspects such as interest expression, organization, and communication. Although every group-buying member initially joins with firm group intentions, different buyers hold different purchasing philosophies and needs. Despite the advantage of "more people," group-buying cannot avoid the problem of catering to diverse preferences.
