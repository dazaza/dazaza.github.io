When purchasing a built-in wardrobe, it is generally more expensive than buying an ordinary wardrobe. Calculated by projection, it includes doors and all components, making pricing simple and quick. In fact, the pricing method for panel-structured built-in wardrobes mainly consists of four parts: panels, shelves, bottom boards, and other accessories. The first three are calculated per square meter, with prices varying depending on different manufacturers and materials. Accessories include full-length mirrors, grid racks, L-shaped racks, trouser racks, shirt racks, and TV racks, which are priced according to specifications. Adding these four parts together constitutes the entire price of the wardrobe. The pricing method for metal-frame structures is similar to that of built-in wardrobes, except that the metal frame part is calculated per meter.

When selecting a built-in wardrobe, be sure to pay special attention to whether there are any omissions in the design drawings, the price, and the quality... In short, don't miss any detail!

Environmental Protection:

The selection of materials is crucial as it relates to the health of you and your family. Therefore, the cabinet board must have a national authoritative inspection report, conforming to the national mandatory standard GB18584-2001, with formaldehyde emissions ≤ 1.5mg/L, and moisture content between 5-11%. Check if it is produced by a reputable brand manufacturer, if it has undergone environmental protection certification and health certification, and preferably choose products with anti-counterfeiting marks, promising multiple years of warranty and lifelong maintenance.

Durability:

The solidity of the boards determines the lifespan of the cabinet. The standard thickness for its shelves and panels should be 18mm, with the bottom board slightly thinner. Its lifespan can reach over 15 years. Of course, if you want to increase the thickness, the price will definitely be higher accordingly.

Another key to durability is the craftsmanship. First is the edging of the boards. Machine-edged boards are usually uniform and consistent, without black lines or serrations, while hand-edged boards may feel rough and could damage clothing, especially valuable silk items. Second, check if the backboard is slotted for installation to ensure stability.

Aesthetics:

Look at how the built-in wardrobe hides and decorates the beams and columns in your home. This relates to the overall aesthetic effect of the wardrobe. High-end built-in wardrobes can be designed according to specific spatial structures and provide non-standard custom services.

Practicality:

Before customizing a wardrobe, consider the categories of your clothes and see which type predominates, then add corresponding spaces accordingly. For example, people who prefer long dresses and coats can leave more space for hanging long garments; those with more casual wear can configure more shelves for stacking clothes.

Service:

Whether the installation service personnel have undergone unified and standardized technical training, and what kind of after-sales service they offer. In summary, when buying a built-in wardrobe, compare and analyze thoroughly before making a decision.

Price:

First, ask clearly about the manufacturer’s calculation method, such as whether it includes drawers and shelves, and the price of hardware accessories (usually, the expanded area algorithm does not include the prices of drawers, shelves, and hardware accessories). The most popular and fair calculation method internationally is based on the actual standard materials used. Paying for what you buy is the most reasonable. Also, make sure you understand the design drawings to avoid being deceived.
