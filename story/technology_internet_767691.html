Microsoft WP goes big screen: Will it eliminate WinRT by supporting 10-inch devices next year?

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 12:30:44

Recently, Microsoft launched the third update package for the Windows-Phone8 operating system, which began to support larger screen resolutions and started to enter the small tablet market of five to seven inches. Some analysts pointed out that Microsoft is planning to let WP8 support more sizes of tablets, even ten-inch tablets, in order to expand the market position of WP. Microsoft will also face the problem of what to do with the Windows-RT system in the future.

Last week, Microsoft launched the third update package for WP8, which will be fully updated for users in the coming months. An engineer from Microsoft's WP8 technology team wrote on the official blog that this update has paved the way for the launch of five-inch and six-inch WP8 devices.

The latest third update package can support 1080P resolution (1920X1080), doubling the currently supported resolution.

On October 22nd, Nokia will hold a product launch event, and it is expected to launch the six-inch phone Lumia1520, which will be equipped with a 1080P screen. This will be the highest-resolution WP8 device in history. The timing of Microsoft's update package was arranged one week before Nokia's launch event.

Currently, in the global smartphone market, so-called "phablets" (i.e., large-screen phones) account for 20% of the market, and large-screen phones are especially popular in East Asian countries such as China and South Korea. Some analysts pointed out that by updating the system, Microsoft has the potential to gain more share in the large-screen phone market, but if Microsoft takes further steps, there is still great market potential.

American tech industry analyst Jack Gold said that there is a weakness in Microsoft's mobile system strategy: the Windows 8 system is not very suitable for smaller-sized, cheaper tablets. However, the WP8 system requires fewer resources and is more suitable for smaller-sized tablets, thus enhancing competitiveness against Android systems.

Analyst Ben Bajarin from the research company "Innovative Strategies" said that if Microsoft truly wants to seize opportunities in the small tablet market (less than eight inches), they must adopt the WP8 system.

This analyst believes that compared to developers of Windows 8 and Windows-RT systems, WP8 app developers have more experience in developing software for small touch-screen devices. If Microsoft extends WP8 into the small tablet market, this will also stimulate developer interest because the same software, after modifications, will gain more installed user bases.

Ryan Reith, an analyst at IDC, said last week that as Microsoft makes changes to the WP8 system, this will open up a market window, and in the future, more large-screen WP phones will be seen on the market. However, Reith's view differs from the aforementioned analysts; he believes that Microsoft will not use the WP8 system to capture the tablet market. Microsoft's option for tablet operating systems remains Windows 8 and Windows-RT.

Microsoft's Windows-RT operating system for ARM tablets is generally considered a complete failure by the tech industry. Almost all computer manufacturers have stopped producing Windows-RT tablets, and Microsoft's Surface-RT product has become a lone item on the shelves. Many analysts believe that Microsoft should completely abandon the failed Windows-RT system and unify the use of an upgraded WP8 system (perhaps under a different name) on ARM-based mobile devices.

Recently, several tech experts who follow Microsoft cited sources saying that next year's upgraded version of WP8 will support tablets up to ten inches, meaning that WP-supported devices will comprehensively cover both smartphones and tablets.

In fact, in mobile operating systems, Microsoft itself has fallen into a fragmentation trap by launching multiple versions, but each version's market performance has been abysmal. According to IDC statistics, in the second quarter, among the smartphones delivered globally, only 3.7% used WP, which represents a mere 0.6 percentage point increase compared to the same period last year.

After BlackBerry's "collapse," Microsoft's WP has become the world's third-largest mobile ecosystem, but in terms of market share, WP is far less than a fraction of Android and iOS. Some industry insiders pointed out that Microsoft should end its own fragmentation and learn from Apple's iOS approach by using a unified operating system for smartphones and tablets. This would not only help increase market share but also motivate software developers within the WP ecosystem.

IDC's Ryan Reith said that as WP supports higher resolutions, the market share of this system will continue to grow, but it is still far from catching up with Android and iOS.
