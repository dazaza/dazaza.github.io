The 2007 English中考 preparation advocates a "steady and unhurried" approach.

by kayaedu on 2009-08-28 17:02:23

(Kaya Education www.kakayaya.com)

In 2007, the English subject of Beijing's high school entrance examination was still divided into two types of tests: "Outline Test" and "Curriculum Standard Test". The latter was only used in six districts and counties: Dongcheng, Chaoyang, Haidian, Fengtai, Huairou, and Yanqing. This year's "Outline Test" was the last year it would be used, and there were not many changes in terms of requirements. Relatively speaking, except for Haidian District, the other districts and counties were using the "Curriculum Standard Test" for the first time. There were differences in vocabulary range and question types compared to the "Outline Test". However, whether using the "Curriculum Standard Test" or the "Outline Test", candidates can rest assured that the overall difficulty of the two tests should be consistent.

Students in different districts and counties use different English textbooks. Although these textbooks are written according to the curriculum standards, they each have their own characteristics and vocabulary ranges. Candidates should review based on the exam instructions.

Characteristics of the "Curriculum Standard Test"

There are differences between the "Curriculum Standard Test" and the "Outline Test" in terms of listening, reading, and written expression.

The "Curriculum Standard Test" focuses not only on listening skills but also emphasizes the combination of listening and writing abilities in the listening section. Candidates are required not only to understand the information but also to extract important information and summarize the main ideas. The answers to such questions are not unique, so they are open-ended. In the reading section, in addition to the usual multiple-choice questions, there are also comprehensive skill tests, namely integrated reading and writing questions. In the written expression section, the "Curriculum Standard Test" provides two questions with the same topic and difficulty level but different titles for candidates to choose from. This method of question setting is more humane. Candidates can choose a question they feel more confident about based on their mastery of vocabulary, allowing them to fully demonstrate their written expression ability and level in English.

Key to "Preparation" is to find your own positioning

In the final 80-day review stage, candidates are advised to pay attention to the following issues.

Firstly, it is necessary to consolidate the foundation. By reviewing the high school entrance examination questions from recent years, it is not difficult to find that a considerable part of the middle school entrance examination English questions examine basic knowledge. Therefore, candidates should first do enough work on basic knowledge before considering doing "high-difficulty actions".

In addition, students at different levels should make planned, targeted reviews and training suitable for themselves. For students who have difficulty learning English, it is especially important to focus on basic knowledge. For students who perform well in English, they need to identify their weak points, know in which areas they still have room for improvement, and make targeted reviews.

After positioning yourself, you should set feasible goals for yourself. At the beginning of the review stage, identifying gaps and making up for deficiencies is essential. Organize the knowledge you have learned systematically, do some high-quality practice questions, and through practicing, find the gaps in your knowledge. Spend more time on areas where you lack, and do corresponding exercises.

Written expression ability is based on the accumulation of daily vocabulary, grammar, and sentence patterns. If there is no process-based training in the early stages of learning, it will be very difficult to output in the later stages. Currently, some students with learning difficulties have prominent problems with vocabulary, and the common practice for these students is to memorize words. Of course, memorizing words is necessary, but mechanical memorization is an inefficient approach. We advocate understanding and memorizing words in context. Polysemy is a common phenomenon in English. Some students spend a lot of time memorizing words, but when they encounter words in reading, they may feel familiar but cannot connect the whole sentence; others remember many words but still cannot use them in written expression. Therefore, only by memorizing words in different texts can one understand the exact meaning of the words. To solve the problem of written expression, it is better to memorize phrases or some common fixed expressions and collocations than individual words, which can fundamentally solve the problem of written expression.

Generally speaking, the topics of written expression questions should revolve around the topics learned in the textbook, which are closely related to students' lives, so students can have something to say. Therefore, when preparing for written expression, students should focus on the topics learned in the textbook. Nowadays, the high school entrance examination pays more attention to examining students' ability to use English to solve practical problems in relatively real contexts.

Whether it is the "Outline Test" or the "Curriculum Standard Test", the exam instructions for different tests will list Vocabulary List One and Vocabulary List Two. The former includes the most commonly used words, which candidates are required to apply in written expression. The second category consists of reading vocabulary, which candidates just need to recognize without overly high demands, especially for students who find learning difficult. It is important to address vocabulary issues in a targeted manner.

A little bit every day, without being too eager for quick success

English is different from other subjects. Whether it is practical application ability or the score reflected on the exam paper, it requires long-term accumulation. Therefore, persistence is the key to English learning and high school entrance examination English review. Reading and writing cannot be relaxed in the final period, especially reading. Candidates should require themselves to spend a certain amount of time reading one or two articles every day. In terms of listening, candidates can persist in listening for 10 to 15 minutes every day.

English cannot be improved by being too eager for quick success. Daily practice may not show much progress in a short time, but if you stop practicing for a long time, you will become "rusty." Therefore, English preparation advocates "not rushing." The time spent on the English subject does not lie in its length, but in daily persistence. English learning and preparation are actually also an examination of the candidate's willpower quality. Of course, because there are many subjects, the English subject often gets impacted by science subjects during the review process. It is suggested that candidates alternate between reviewing liberal arts and science subjects and allocate time reasonably to achieve better results.

In the final preparation process, it is recommended that candidates carefully select questions to practice. There is a trend in the review process where some students think the harder and more questions they practice, the better. Actually, this is not necessarily a good thing. Preparation should be as targeted as possible. It is recommended that candidates practice past high school entrance examination true questions or high-quality simulation questions.
