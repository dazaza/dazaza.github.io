Netizens expose that their husbands are eunuchs. Wow, this is originally a dirty word.

by jyq2011668 on 2011-06-28 16:44:20

Nowadays, it is a rather bizarre thing for a wife to affectionately call her husband "lao gong" (hubby), because this word has a bit of a foreign flavor. Many people thus assume that "lao gong" is a borrowed term. In fact, the word "lao gong" is an entirely local Chinese creation. Yesterday, a netizen enthusiastic about studying classical Chinese posted on the Chongqing Net Forum introducing in detail the origin of the word "lao gong" and specifically pointed out that in ancient times, "lao gong" actually referred to eunuchs. She also revealed to the reporter that words like "gan mao" (cold), "dian xin" (dim sum), "cai niao" (newbie), "tiao cao" (job hopping), and "wa sai" (wow) which seem like foreign terms, are actually from ancient China.

"Lao Gong" Is Not A Borrowed Term

In the post titled "Lao Gong - Not A Foreign Word, And In Ancient Times It Referred To Eunuchs," the poster "Cherry Girl" expressed that after recently reviewing materials, she discovered that the word "lao gong" was not brought into China from abroad but had existed in ancient China and at that time, it referred to eunuchs.

"Cherry Girl" cited evidence that in the book "Zao Lin Za Zu" written by Tan Qian during the late Ming and early Qing dynasties, there was mention of Li Zicheng entering Beijing to "beat lao gong," where "lao gong" refers to palace eunuchs. It is understood that during the Ming dynasty, eunuchs in the palace often bullied the common people. During the Wanli era, the appearance of "mine supervisors and tax collectors" who bullied the people caused public resistance, so incidents of "beating lao gong" frequently occurred.

Why were eunuchs called "lao gong"? "Cherry Girl" believed that since from ancient times to the present, the Chinese have liked to add the character "lao" before some words - teachers are called "lao shi," older people are called "lao Zhang" or "lao Wang," and nowadays people even refer to company bosses as "lao zong"... Thus, calling eunuchs (gongs) "lao gong" in ancient times was understandable.

These Words Are Also Often Misunderstood

The reporter contacted "Cherry Girl" yesterday. She claimed to be a student of Chinese Language and Literature at Chongqing Education College and has always been fond of ancient Chinese language. By chance, she learned online that the word "lao gong" originated from ancient Chinese texts, so she went to review relevant materials.

"Cherry Girl" also introduced that words like "gan mao" (cold), "dian xin" (dim sum), "cai niao" (newbie), "tiao cao" (job hopping), and "wa sai" (wow) which seem like foreign terms actually all come from ancient China.

"Cherry Girl" gave examples: The term "gan mao" did not exist in ancient Chinese medical texts. Originally, this common phrase was a specialized term in the officialdom, used as an excuse for officials to take leave; The Song dynasty writer Zhou Hui described in "Bei Yuan Lu": "After washing up and putting on hats, dian xin arrived," pointing out that dian xin refers to steamed buns, dumplings, etc.; "Cai niao" and "wa sai" both evolved from Minnan dialects. Cai niao originally referred to young birds learning to fly, later extended to mean someone inexperienced in certain matters. In the original Minnan meaning of "wa sai," "wa" is the first-person pronoun "I," and "sai" is a verb indicating sexual behavior, making it essentially a swearword in Minnan dialect.

Expert: Eunuchs Were Indeed One Of The Meanings Of "Lao Gong" In Ancient Times

Did the word "lao gong" really mean eunuch in ancient China? Associate Professor Feng Fu, deputy director of the library at Chongqing University of Arts and Sciences and a member of the Chongqing Linguistics Society, confirmed this after consulting relevant literature.

Professor Feng pointed out that in Chapter 83 of "Dream of the Red Chamber," it is written: "The gatekeeper came back and said, 'There are two eunuchs outside who want to see the two masters.'" Jia She said, "Please let them in." The gatekeeper led the lao gong in - in the text, lao gong refers to palace eunuchs. He also introduced that in Chapter 9 of "Officialdom Unmasked," it is written: "He immediately went to the capital and took the path of the lao gong." Professor Feng explained that during the Ming and Qing dynasties, eunuchs were commonly called "gong gong" (lord) or "lao gong," and the common people simply referred to them as lao gong.

Professor Feng also indicated that from ancient times to the present, the word "lao gong" has developed multiple meanings and has also been used as a general term for the elderly, a colloquial term for husband, and an alternative term for father in ancient times.
