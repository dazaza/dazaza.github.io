Early Apple Employee on 'Jobs' Movie: Plausible Fabrications

by anonymous on 2013-08-20 14:49:29

Screenshot from the movie "Jobs"

Chrisann Brennan and Steve Wozniak were among the pioneers in the PC industry when it was just beginning, and they were also part of Apple's early days. They were some of the first employees at Apple, with Brennan working on hardware development and Wozniak focusing on user interfaces.

Regarding the recently released movie "Jobs," they have their own opinions to share. This film dramatizes the personality traits and career activities of Steve Jobs and his partner Steve Wozniak. Brennan provided advice for an early version of the script, attended the premiere at Sundance Film Festival in February, and plans to watch it again after its general release on August 16th. Wozniak, however, has not seen the movie and does not intend to. He believes it is fictional content that might be disappointing. In the following exclusive interview, they both attempt to distinguish between historical facts and the creative liberties taken in the movie.

Brennan: Bill, have you seen this movie?

Fernandez: No, I haven't.

Brennan: You'd laugh a lot; you'd find it very entertaining.

Fernandez: I don't want to see it; I think it would be strange. I also don't want to see someone else playing Jobs.

Brennan: Well, Ashton Kutcher did a good job. I have no complaints about him or his portrayal of Jobs. The dissatisfaction people have with the movie may stem from how Wozniak is depicted as lacking the same vision as Jobs, which is unfair. This morning, Wozniak responded saying, "If I go to see this movie, I'll only go alone." There's a scene uploaded to YouTube where Wozniak and Jobs walk through HP's garage, and Jobs says, "Everyone wants a home computer." Wozniak replies, "No, no one will buy such a thing." That's completely wrong.

Question: Aside from that issue, how about the rest?

Brennan: The movie clearly and powerfully conveys Wozniak's emotions as a co-founder. After Jobs lied to him about the payment for the Atari game "Breakout," he felt betrayed. This is true. In reality, Wozniak is also very generous. He said, "Yes, it hurt me, but I forgive Jobs."

Question: Daniel, this movie is often compared to the 2010 Facebook movie "The Social Network." It seems like Eduardo Saverin's role has been split between you and Wozniak.

Brennan: Yes, I'd also like to meet Saverin. I share many of his feelings, which is beneficial for me. That was one of the toughest times in my life, and I think no one knows or has heard about it. Bill, do you know about it? It was the summer of 1980 when I didn't get my shares. No one came to talk to me about it. All I wanted was to talk to Jobs, but he refused. He made me wait outside his office for hours, and this happened multiple times. It was cold then. You know what he'd do; he'd keep making calls until I left because he didn't want to communicate with me.

Question: The departure of Wozniak from Apple is portrayed quite dramatically in the movie.

Brennan: Yes, but it didn't actually happen that way. It's completely fictional.

Question: Really? His lines are the sharpest part of the entire movie.

Brennan: Yes, they are sharp. I think it's reasonable for the movie, even if it wasn't exactly like that. In reality, Wozniak was welcomed and invited to be part of the Macintosh project. However, he had a plane crash and couldn't participate.

Question: The movie doesn't mention this, does it?

Brennan: No, it doesn't. Bill Atkinson put it well: Wozniak's sense of humor is entirely missing from the movie, yet it's a crucial part of his life.

Fernandez: If that's true, then it's terrible. Because a significant part of Wozniak's personality is his humor.

Question: He used to tell joke after joke.

Brennan: There was an interesting article in the San Francisco Chronicle discussing the overall problems with biographical movies, pointing out that it's a risky genre. If you're extremely famous and a movie is made about you, it usually spans several decades to depict a complex life. Therefore, they try to cram too much into the movie, losing the emotional parts and coherent storyline. Interestingly, the article also mentioned Aaron Sorkin's script for another Sony Pictures movie about Jobs, which has only three acts!

Question: What do you think about another such movie being made? It's quite unusual.

Brennan: I don't know. But someone should make a movie about Wozniak. I think it will eventually happen.

Fernandez: It could be an independent film.

Question: In Walter Isaacson's 2011 biography "Steve Jobs," my favorite part is the pranks Jobs pulled, such as the fake brochures from the "West Coast Computer Faire."

Brennan: That's an important scene in the movie. They spent several days shooting it, brilliantly recreating the "West Coast Computer Faire." There were 50 different booths selling all kinds of computer-related items. It was a massive studio. They did an excellent job recreating it based on the descriptions in the biography.

This really shocked me. Kutcher's line is: "Ladies and gentlemen, I am Steve Jobs. I will introduce you to the Apple II computer." This line didn't exist in reality. It was just one booth at the computer show.

Fernandez: The movie also doesn't address another important aspect: the broader context of personal computing. At the time, Commodore PET had just come out, and we were worried we'd lose to them. Radio Shack TRS-80 was also available. Based on what I know, the movie doesn't include such information. Many people were involved in the personal computing industry, and Apple wasn't always the dominant competitor.

Question: What was the working environment in the garage like?

Brennan: I can tell you what it wasn't like. Bill, what did you see in the movie, like in "Pirates of Silicon Valley"?

Fernandez: No, I haven't seen it.

Brennan: Is that so? You should like it; it's a great movie. Noah Wyle looks a lot like Jobs. It's incredible; I thought Jobs himself was on the screen. "Pirates of Silicon Valley" shows the garage scene, with six or seven people working, moving things around, using oscilloscopes.

In reality, I was the only one working in the garage. Wozniak would appear once a week with his latest ideas that needed verification. Jobs was usually in the kitchen making phone calls. But in this movie, Bill, you, me, Atkinson, Rod Holt, we're all there in the garage.

Fernandez: We never had that many people in the garage at the same time.

Brennan: The moment that completely broke us was when Rod first appeared, wearing a leather jacket, riding a motorcycle, with long flowing hair.

Fernandez: What!

Brennan: He looked like a racing playboy, which made us completely fall apart. Rod thought it was hilarious.

Fernandez: Did he ever ride a motorcycle?

Brennan: Actually, he only rode a dirty old bicycle. I've never seen him on a motorcycle, but he always talked about it. So the scriptwriter let him ride one. I liked the actor. When I saw him in the movie, I didn't know who he was because he didn't look anything like Rod, with long hair and a leather jacket.

Fernandez: So you'd say, "Who in the world of Apple could this be?"

Question: Who in the movie didn't appear at all, and who do you think would be the best actors to play them?

Brennan: Wendell Sanders or Mike Scott.

Fernandez: For various reasons, Sanders was another Wozniak, and Scott was the guy who got the company running.

Question: Who do you think would be suitable to play them?

Fernandez: How about the guy who played Doc Brown in "Back to the Future"? Actually, he would be better suited to play Rod? Isn't he the best Rod Holt?

Brennan: You mean Christopher Lloyd?

Fernandez: For me, there are many fan fiction stories about Apple and Jobs. I think this is the most important and dazzling fan fiction so far.

Question: "Fan fiction" is indeed a very appropriate definition.

Brennan: I don't think it's fan fiction because I've been involved with the movie from the very beginning. They tried hard to make it factually accurate.

Fernandez: That's one of the reasons I didn't want to see the movie, because it's all fictional. I don't want to be disappointed by something created by the scriptwriter that isn't factual.

Brennan: Were you ever on set during filming?

Fernandez: No. The actor who played me, Victor Rasuk, interviewed me several times over the phone, and that was my only involvement.

Brennan: The original version had serious problems, big problems. I sent the initial script draft to Mike Markkula because they wanted his advice. Mike's reaction to the script was terrible; he didn't want to have anything to do with the project. By the fourth draft, things improved. It didn't discourage me. They still arranged a scene where Jobs and I argue, but it never actually happened. Around 1980, my relationship with Jobs was that he simply didn't talk to me. But that wouldn't fit in a movie; arguments work better for the movie.

Fernandez: I spoke with the actor Rasuk a few times, and he would ask, "Did this happen?" My response was, "No." Eventually, I understood that they weren't trying to make a historical documentary but a narrative film. From these clues, I concluded that watching this movie would disappoint me. You know it makes sense. They have their project, which is to create entertainment around a hot topic. If they just presented real scenes—assuming anyone told them what those real scenes were—it wouldn't be entertaining at all. As Daniel said, if someone completely ignores you, what kind of dramatic conflict is there? What could you possibly show on screen?

Brennan: Such things never happened; I never put Jobs in a difficult situation.

Question: Jobs' speaking ability is often referred to as a "reality distortion field." Does this term apply to the movie as well?

Fernandez: It happens around any film. Especially such biographical films.

Brennan: I think the producers would want there to be a "reality distortion field," which would bring huge success.
