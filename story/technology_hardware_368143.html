Ten Windows Phone 7 phones super comparison

by shootero on 2010-10-15 22:00:30

Do you feel that the specs of each phone released this time are all similar? Your intuition is not wrong. In order to ensure that all Windows Phone 7 phones provide a similar user experience, although there are quite a number of first-batch released Windows Phone 7 phones, all the phones seem to be using the same level (1GHz Qualcomm Snapdragon) chips, so in actual use they might feel very similar. If having the same CPU makes sense, then adopting the same camera specifications is somewhat baffling. With the exception of the only outlier (HTC Mozart), all devices are 5MP and have the ability to record 720p video. It's hard to believe that this could be such a coincidence unless it was pre-arranged. Another point worth noting is that very few of the released phones have front-facing cameras (or it’s even unclear if they do, as it doesn't seem to be mentioned in the specifications), indicating that competing with FaceTime was not part of Microsoft's plan (or perhaps this wasn't something that had been hyped up yet). Therefore, in the end, the phones can only differ in terms of screen and hardware configuration. Out of ten phones, three and a half are sliders (three slide out to reveal a keyboard, one slides out to reveal a speaker), and the rest are fairly standard full-screen smartphone designs, even having the same number of buttons (a mandatory requirement from Microsoft). Screen sizes vary, with the largest being the 4.3-inch HD7 and the smallest being the 3.5-inch LG Quantum, but the resolution is exactly the same at 840x600 (WVGA). In terms of screen types, we only know that Samsung's two phones are AMOLED, while the rest of the phones don't specify what kind of technology they use. Although everyone's specs are similar, this actually represents a relatively high specification in today's smartphone market. If you put the HD7 alongside the newly released Desire HD, the differences in their specs are actually not significant. Therefore, as long as the Windows Phone 7 user experience indeed meets users' expectations and Microsoft's App Store runs smoothly, there is no need to worry about its ability to compete with other systems. I think what Microsoft and various manufacturers should really be concerned about is whether having so many models with similar functions stuck in the same price range will result in too small a customer base, leading to self-cannibalization instead?
