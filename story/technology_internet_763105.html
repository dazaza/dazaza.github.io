Humans originated from Mars? New tool can detect Earth-like life on Mars

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 15:30:38

According to the American Space website, did life on Earth originate from Mars? Or did life on Earth spread to the surface of Mars via meteorites 3.5 billion years ago? To investigate the origins of life on Earth and Mars, a research team from MIT, Harvard University, and Massachusetts General Hospital hopes that NASA's next-generation Mars rover will use a DNA sequencing microchip to analyze soil and ice crystal samples, revealing the genetic material origins on the Martian surface.

Earth and Mars may have experienced cross-contamination, with possible Earth-like life existing on the Martian surface.

Does life exist on the Martian surface? Scientists are developing new instruments to uncover the mysteries of Martian life.

Discovering ancient signs of life has become one of the primary tasks for the successor to the "Curiosity" rover. The latest study claims that NASA's next-generation Mars rover will launch in 2020, aiming to search for existing or recently deceased life forms, where "recently" refers to up to one million years ago.

The lead author of this study report, Christopher Carr from MIT, stated that if Martian life is based on RNA and DNA molecular structures, then humans might not be the only intelligent life in the solar system. More DNA sequences need further research and analysis; they could potentially be "distant relatives" of any Earth life. By using this method, we can detect whether it is contamination originating from Earth or authentic Martian life, simultaneously revealing the correlation between Martian and Earth life.

A New Method for Searching for Extraterrestrial Life

The latest study hypothesizes that life on Earth and Mars shares a common genetic ancestor. During the "Late Heavy Bombardment" period 3.5 to 4 billion years ago, there were numerous asteroid collisions within the solar system. Approximately one billion tons of asteroids traveled through space between Earth and Mars, possibly causing "cross-contamination" between the two planets.

Carr said: "Some space rocks transfer life in ways consistent with our understanding. These asteroids do not get too hot, and they can quickly transport life forms without being damaged by space radiation. If life exists on one planet, it could hitch a ride on an asteroid traveling through space and eventually reach other planets. The question arises: if life exists on Mars and is related to Earth life, how should we detect it?"

To seek out this type of life form, the research team hopes to equip NASA's next-generation Mars rover, launching in 2020, with a device capable of sequencing RNA and DNA — a life indicator.

Carr said: "Such life indicators can only detect current or recently deceased life forms because nucleic acids do not remain attached for long periods. The oldest nucleic acid detectable would not exceed one million years. Mars is colder, so we can trace back further into its ancient history, but one million years is likely the optimal limit."

Radiation Protection

For sensitive DNA chips on the Martian surface, they must first survive the months-long journey through space. In space missions, they will be exposed to high levels of cosmic radiation. Mars' natural conditions are harsher than Earth's, with its atmosphere primarily composed of carbon dioxide, which is 100 times thinner than Earth's atmosphere, and surface temperatures reaching as low as -126 degrees Celsius.

However, the environment beneath the Martian surface may resemble Earth's, containing all the major elements necessary for life. The research team tested a sequencing microchip with 1.3 million microwells, ensuring each well contained a single porous bead. Amplifying its DNA segments produced genetic sequences. Researchers placed the microchip and sequencing reagents under Martian radiation levels, then used the device to analyze E. coli DNA.

Carr said: "The main component of space radiation is protons, and people will also be exposed to significant proton radiation from the sun. We tested this chip at NASA's Brookhaven National Laboratory, with radiation doses ranging from 1-5 Gy. The results showed that the chip could survive more than two years of a Mars exploration mission, meaning such microchips could endure the journey to Mars, sufficient to collect surface sample information for one and a half years."

After loading the chip with E. coli DNA fragments, researchers found that it could still analyze and identify the bacterial genetic sequence. Chris McKay, a planetary scientist in the Space Science Division at NASA Ames Research Center, stated that a radiation-resistant DNA sequencing chip is crucial for future Mars exploration and other celestial bodies. It can detect and sequence DNA, including any life forms encountered during the mission. It also has high sensitivity, essential for biomolecules. The next step is to improve this technology so that it can sequence nucleotide molecules of varying backbone lengths—deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA), ribonucleic acid (RNA), threose nucleic acid (TNA), and peptide nucleic acid (PNA).
