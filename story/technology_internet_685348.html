Enterprises' Acceptance and Abandonment of Patents

by keyizhang1 on 2012-03-01 14:49:33

Under the enterprise intellectual property protection strategy, due to filing a large number of patents, enterprises will inevitably have to make decisions about which patents to keep and which to abandon in order to save operating costs. So, under what circumstances should patents be maintained, and when can they be abandoned?

**The value of a patent is the basis for determining whether to retain or abandon it.** The value of a patent includes its technical content, market value, etc. The value of a patent should be more reflected in the market economy, and the market value of a patent particularly gives an enterprise vitality.

### **Patents That Need Priority Maintenance**

1. **Core Patents:** Generally speaking, core patents refer to patents corresponding to technologies that must be used to manufacture a certain product in a specific technical field (scientific and technological achievements [http://www.k8008.com/]), and these are difficult to bypass through some avoidance design techniques. Core patents are key for enterprises to enhance their competitiveness.

Whether a patent is a core patent can generally be judged from the following aspects:

- **Number of Citations:** Usually, the more important a patent is, the more times it will be cited.

- **Number of Family Patent Members:** The number of family patents is an important indicator of the economic value of a patent. It reflects the potential technical market and economic influence of a particular invention.

- **Patent Litigation Information:** If there is no significant interest conflict behind a patent, enterprises will not rashly engage in patent litigation.

2. **Peripheral Patents:** Research and improvements on peripheral patents are based on core patents for scientific and technological achievements [http://www.k8008.com]. By filing a large number of improvement patents around the core technology patent, a surrounding situation can be formed. In this way, although the owner of the peripheral patents still cannot directly use someone else's core patent and may still cause infringement issues, in the market, if the owner of the core patent wants to implement it specifically, they will encounter obstacles from these peripheral patents. This can form "cross-licensing," bringing benefits to the enterprise.

3. **Annual Stage of the Patent:** First, the lower the annual stage of the patent, the more it relatively indicates the value of the patent. Higher annual patents are more likely to lose their technical value [scientific and technological achievements http://www.k8008.com]. Moreover, some enterprises enjoy national or local government policies for reducing patent fees, where the first three years of annual fees after authorization can be reduced or fully reimbursed. Therefore, considering enterprise costs, enterprises usually maintain patents for the first three years after authorization and only consider abandoning patents in the fourth year and beyond.

4. **Huge Market Value:** Some patents may not have high technical content, but their product positioning is very well aligned with market demand, and they can bring high economic benefits to the enterprise. Such patents are also worth maintaining.

### **Patents That Can Be Abandoned Early**

1. **Junk Patents:** Junk patents generally refer to patents with no innovative content. Enterprises sometimes apply for "junk patents" out of intellectual property strategy considerations, such as increasing the number of patents, intimidating competitors, or setting up confusion tactics to confuse the technical monitoring of competitors. When enterprises conduct patent value assessments, they should clarify the true purpose of the patent at the time of application and prioritize abandoning junk patents that have lost their function.

2. **Problematic Patents:** Problematic patents refer to patents whose protection scope is too broad or do not fully comply with relevant provisions of the patent law after being granted patent rights. The legal stability of problematic patents is poor, and the possibility of the patent being invalidated by others is high. Therefore, they cannot truly protect the patented product ([http://product.k8008.com/]).

Problematic patents mainly include the following situations:

- Lack of creativity and practicality;

- The description has not been clear and complete enough to reach the level that can be achieved by technicians in the relevant technical field;

- The claims have not been clear and concise enough and cannot be supported by the description;

- Modification exceeds the scope;

- Lacks necessary technical features, etc.

3. **Annual Stage of the Patent:** Since patent maintenance fees increase progressively, for patents of the same value, enterprises should prioritize abandoning patents in higher annual stages to reduce maintenance costs.

4. **Loss of Market Value:** Some patents were once implemented in specific products, but due to product upgrades or poor market conditions, production has ceased. These patents no longer need to be implemented or reserved, so enterprises should prioritize abandoning them during patent maintenance.
