"Bento Clan" shows off their lunch boxes online

by hdghu19056 on 2012-02-22 13:44:54

"Special circumstances" make some "lunch carriers" waver

Xiaoding from a certain recruitment website basically "brings lunch every day." Fortunately, his company has a microwave oven to heat up food, so he brings the dinner prepared by his family the night before to eat at work. As for what dishes he brings, he said, "I eat whatever my family makes."

When Xiaoding analyzed the reasons for the formation of the "lunch carrying group," he said, "This depends on different groups of people, such as time, location, salary, etc. For example, I finish work early and my company is close to home, so I can go directly to the market after work to buy groceries and cook at home; but if I finish work late and someone in my family finishes work early, then they can prepare the meal for me, so I don't have to worry about tomorrow's meal." However, Xiaoding also frankly admitted that "bringing lunch" is often constrained by many "special circumstances." For example: Some companies have too short of a lunch break, and not having enough time to go out for meals may lead to an increase in the number of "lunch carriers" during a certain period; once everyone starts "bringing lunch," there might not be enough microwaves, even causing spectacular scenes of people queuing up to heat their meals; these factors will inevitably make some people waver on whether to bring lunch or not. Another example: Bringing lunch also depends on time and season.

Regarding the issue of leftover food, Xiaoding and Ms. Jin from Shenzhen both know the harm of eating leftover food from the previous night. He is one of the few "lunch carriers" who knows the health risks of eating leftover food. "Green vegetables, if reheated twice, can easily produce harmful substances, which are indeed not good for health. As experts say: leafy vegetables should not be brought."

"The trouble of cooking and bringing lunch" and the story of sausages and stir-fried pork

Beijing netizen "August" no longer brings lunch now because she finds it too troublesome. "Each time I bring lunch, I need to spend a certain amount of time cooking. Cooking is tiring, and bringing lunch is even more exhausting. Besides, I live far away, which is another reason why I stopped bringing lunch later: Every morning at dawn, I had to carry my lunch box and squeeze onto the early bus, taking the bus from Xisanqi to Xierqi subway station, then transferring several times to reach the Maglev Line 5 Ciqikou station, and then take the bus again... I had to ride various modes of transportation multiple times, spending at least an hour and a half on the road."

"It's like traveling across half of Beijing from the outer ring of North Fifth Ring to the southeastern corner of the Second Ring, and doing it twice every day. Month after month, it's not easy," she told the reporter.

Sichuan netizen "Tina" (pseudonym) told China Workers' Net that this was the first time she heard the term "lunch carrier group", "but this phenomenon existed long before." Since she lives very close to her workplace, "Tina" usually goes home to eat. She occasionally brings lunch when she works overtime over the weekend and doesn't want to eat out because it would waste a lot of time going back and forth. The food she brings is always ready-made food prepared by her family. For example, she loves Sichuan-style stir-fried pork with sausage and often brings it.

Although "Tina" doesn't bring lunch very often, she noticed that choosing what to bring is important. For example, in terms of seasonal choices, bringing lunch in winter generally poses no problems, but in summer, even with air conditioning in the office, the food can easily spoil. If the company does not have a refrigerator, it's best not to bring lunch. She said, "It's best to choose dishes that can be kept well in both winter and summer. Dishes that spoil easily not only can go bad quickly but also emit odors that some colleagues in the office may find unbearable."

"Going out to eat is a waste of time" and the boredom of takeout

"Sometimes I feel that going to restaurants outside is just a waste of time!" In interviews, China Workers' Net reporters found that Sichuan netizen "Yingzi" (pseudonym) was not the first person to express such feelings, as many "lunch carriers" share similar sentiments.

"So many colleagues began ordering takeout. But gradually, we got tired of even the takeout. No matter what dish, it all tastes the same when you eat it. So every day, we started worrying about what to eat for lunch?" Yingzi told China Workers' Net reporters.

Yingzi also told the reporter: "Although our company has a cafeteria, it's shared by all the units in the entire building. There are many people during lunchtime, and if you go late, you won't get any food. Plus, with busy work schedules, sometimes we're too lazy to go downstairs to eat. Gradually, the 'lunch carrier group' grew larger. The variety of dishes brought made our eyes dazzle: some bring braised pork, others bring fried fish, and some even bring pickled vegetables..."

"Our company's fridge contains many canned side dishes. For example, 'Laoganma' chili bean paste, preserved vegetables, etc. People from Sichuan tend to prefer flavorful foods. Whoever buys them puts them in the fridge, and whoever wants to eat can taste them. Private purchases are also shared among everyone, making it lively," Yingzi said.

"Lunch carriers" save dining time = improve work efficiency

Ms. Jin, who is studying broadcasting and film, said in a QQ chat with China Workers' Net reporters that she hadn't particularly noticed the term "lunch carrier group." She understands "lunch carriers" as those in the working class who bring lunch to save expenses and ensure healthy eating habits. She expressed understanding towards "lunch carriers." In a fast-paced city like Shenzhen, each meal outside takes up too much time, which seems unwise. Therefore, "lunch carriers" save dining time, which in turn improves work efficiency.

She mentioned that bringing lunch in Shenzhen is quite common—since most companies do not provide accommodation or meals, especially in certain locations within the urban area where it’s hard to find nearby and tasty fast-food restaurants, many people choose to cook and bring their own lunches. In the Pearl River Delta cities, the main reason for choosing to bring lunch is economic considerations: saving money, deliciousness, health, and hygiene.

Ms. Jin told reporters that she also prepares her lunch in advance the night before. Knowing the harm of eating leftover food from the previous night, she tries not to use leafy vegetables for her packed lunches and strives to preserve the nutrients in the food as much as possible: "Generally, I don’t cook them too thoroughly, and after cooking in the evening, I don’t pack them immediately but leave them on the plate, packing them in the morning. Or if packed, I don’t cover them."

Regarding the question of whether it is worth spending time cooking every time one brings lunch, Ms. Jin said that simple home-cooked dishes don't take up much time. A single dish is quite straightforward, and less than half an hour can get everything done. For her, "after weighing all factors, I still think bringing lunch is suitable and not too troublesome; and one dish per day keeps things varied and nutritious."

"Behind the Scenes of Not Dining Out and Bringing Lunch"

Through QQ chats and interviews with numerous "lunch carriers," China Workers' Net reporters learned many "secrets" of this special group.

(I) Rocky's "Lunch Carrying History"

"House husband" refers to men who know how to cook and frequently cook at home, a popular and much-liked term among women, even more "hot" than "otaku." China Workers' Net joked about naming Rocky a "house husband." He didn't mind at all, seeming even slightly pleased—because he had once referred to himself that way. He admitted that he enjoys cooking—"cooking itself is fun." His culinary skills are also pretty good, admired even by his girlfriend. Whenever guests come to his house, he personally cooks.

Saving on mortgage payments by bringing lunch is like adding water to a fire

Mentioning Rocky's identity as a "house husband," naturally, his "lunch carrying history" must be mentioned. His "lunch carrying history" is closely tied to his "home buying history." Rocky told China Workers' Net reporters that five years ago, right after graduation, he was recruited by a certain news agency. As a "Beijinger adrift," he said he might pay less attention to inner feelings and has little sense of "floating." Initially, he rented a place and didn't bring lunch, finding it convenient to eat out. He lived frugally with his girlfriend, saved some money, borrowed part of it, and bought his current house when the housing prices were cheapest.

Since buying the house, Rocky started his "lunch carrying journey," persisting for over half a year. He brings lunch approximately three to four times a week, being one of the dozen or so "lunch carriers" in a unit of over a hundred people. However, due to buying the house, he owes a considerable amount of money to relatives and friends. Currently, the "debt" is being repaid bit by bit, and life continues day by day. "Naturally, where savings are needed, we must save a little." Despite this, "if one thinks that the small amount of money saved by bringing lunch can repay the mortgage, it is obviously like adding water to a fire and unrealistic. Ultimately, one must work hard and earn more money," he told the reporter.

"Two yuan potatoes can feed two meals"

In response to questions about the average cost and expense of bringing lunch, Rocky calculated for China Workers' Net reporters: "If I eat one meal in the company cafeteria, it costs around 8 to 10 yuan. But if I bring my own lunch, it saves a lot. Taking my favorite sour cabbage as an example, a bag costs 5 yuan, which can last for three meals plus one more meal at home; two yuan worth of potatoes can also feed two meals." He also told reporters: "The meat and oil used for cooking each time are provided by my girlfriend's workplace. The cost is just the vegetable money. I only need to buy some greens to cook, spend a little effort, and can make fragrant meals."

Bringing lunch even in the coldest weather with cold waves and strong winds

Rocky's most unforgettable moment was last Friday morning—December 25th, when Beijing experienced a cold wave and temperature drop. The highest temperature that day was only -4℃, and the temperature at 8 am in the urban area plummeted to -10.1℃, making it one of the coldest days since winter began in Beijing. But even so, Rocky did not abandon the idea of bringing lunch.

That day, braving the 5-6 level northern wind, he rode his electric bicycle for a one-way distance of 15 kilometers, spending over 50 minutes on the road, which was quite challenging. However, he did not think there was any necessary connection between the weather and bringing lunch, nor did he think it was difficult. "When I was a child, it was colder than this, and I still had to ride my bike to school, didn't I? Moreover, life is like this! Are you afraid of the cold? Then don't go to work! If you can work, you can bring lunch!"

Rocky told reporters that while riding his bike, he was extremely cautious, always watching out for people suddenly appearing from the side, and avoiding cars that randomly changed lanes. He had also encountered dangerous moments but managed to avoid accidents. Riding in such weather was undoubtedly difficult. However, he did not think it was hard, nor did he find it exhausting or troublesome. "Everything is natural, and I am just an ordinary small employee, and there is no difficulty that cannot be overcome."

The reporter also worried that it might be inconvenient to bring lunch while riding in such cold weather, but he replied: "Don't worry. I carry a backpack, and the lunch box is inside the backpack, so there's no problem—as long as I don't bring soupy food."

"Leftover food can cause trouble"

During interviews with the aforementioned numerous "lunch carriers," China Workers' Net discovered that most friends are not fully aware of the harm caused by eating leftover food, especially leafy vegetables. Regarding this common issue, the reporter consulted nutritionist and chief physician Gao Huiying of the Chinese Nutrition Society, obtaining authoritative answers and some good suggestions.

Gao Huiying said in a telephone consultation with the reporter, "We should try to avoid eating leftovers, especially overnight leftovers; leftovers contain large amounts of nitrites, which can combine with protein decomposition products in the body to form nitrosamines. And as we all know, nitrosamines are very harmful to human health."

Gao Huiying stated that the nutrition field generally agrees that eating leftovers, especially overnight leftovers, is detrimental to health. Overnight leftovers, after being salted overnight, contain even higher levels of nitrites. "The nutrients in leftovers themselves will also be lost, so we should eat fresh vegetables and preferably cook and eat immediately."

Gao Huiying suggested that if one must bring lunch, it's best to prepare it in the morning, leaving only about a 5-hour interval until lunchtime; if young people prepare it in the morning...
