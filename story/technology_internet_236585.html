Reason for Indonesian satellite's survival: Successful self-rescue via "maneuverable orbit change" function

by weixing123 on 2009-09-11 16:21:51

"China's satellite launch success rate is over 90%, with relatively high reliability and safety, which is internationally recognized. Therefore, the outside world must rationally view the issues that have arisen this time, and there is no need for deliberate exaggeration."

At 17:28 on August 31, China launched the Indonesian commercial communication satellite "PALAPA-D" from the Xichang Satellite Launch Center using the "Changzheng-3B" carrier rocket. Due to one of the hydrogen-oxygen engines having low thrust after the second ignition of the third-stage rocket, the satellite was not sent into its intended orbit. This is the first failure in the launch of China's Changzheng series rockets in 13 years.

The "Changzheng-3B" responsible for the Indonesian satellite launch mission can be called a "gold medal rocket" within China's Changzheng rocket family. It is a three-stage liquid rocket with a geosynchronous transfer orbit carrying capacity of up to 5.1 tons. Because the third stage of the rocket uses advanced cryogenic propulsion technology and has a flexible control system, it is capable of making large attitude adjustments for the payload and can also perform multiple satellite launches. This launch is the 118th launch of the Changzheng series carrier rockets. Since 1996, the "Changzheng-3B" has created a record of 75 consecutive successful launches.

Some media reports suggest that the rocket malfunction should have occurred during the tenth step - the "second ignition of the third-stage engine." The separation of the satellite and the third stage is achieved through the separation force of the separation spring. When the rocket control system issues the command to separate the satellite from the rocket, the non-polluting explosive bolts on the straps connecting the satellite and the payload bracket explode and unlock, and the unlocked straps are pulled back by the springs installed on the payload bracket, pushing the satellite away from the rocket via the separation spring.

Pang Zhihao, a space expert, told the Beijing Science and Technology News that there are currently three common problems in rocket launches, the most frequent being malfunctions in the power system. For example, the engine did not start or shut down prematurely. In addition, the attitude control system and structural system are also prone to failures. Problems in any of these three systems could lead to anomalies in satellite launches, or even failures.

"In the process of launching satellites, the detachment of the first, second, and third stages of the rocket, the separation of the satellite from the rocket, as well as the multiple shutdowns and ignitions of multi-stage carrier rockets, interstellar satellites, and Earth-orbit satellites during flight are key points where problems often occur," said Jiao Weixin, a professor at Peking University's School of Earth and Space Sciences in an interview with the Beijing Science and Technology News. To reduce rocket launch failures, scientific and reasonable design is essential, and the electronic components must be able to withstand strong vibrations and various other factors during the rocket launch.

"From some current cases of failed satellite launches, many are caused by problems in the rocket design," Pang Zhihao said. South Korea's recent rocket launch failure was due to such reasons.

Rocket launches are massive system engineering projects where even a minor issue can lead to the entire launch failing. For instance, production defects or foreign objects in the rocket must be avoided. A few years ago, when analyzing the cause of the failure of Europe's Ariane rocket launch, it was found that a small piece of cloth used to clean the nozzle affected the engine's operation. Human operational errors and weather conditions can also lead to rocket launch failures.

Due to the above reasons, only a few countries currently master the technology of launching satellites via rockets. Because of the high risk and significant investment, most countries have yet to venture into this field and instead opt for the "borrowed ladder" approach when launching satellites.

Pang Zhihao stated that China's current rocket launches rank among the top internationally, and China's Changzheng series rockets are considered one of the most reliable rockets internationally. From 1996 until before this launch, China's Changzheng series carrier rockets had successfully launched consecutively 75 times. "China's satellite launch success rate is over 90%, with relatively high reliability and safety, which is internationally recognized. Therefore, the outside world must rationally view the issues that have arisen this time, and there is no need for deliberate exaggeration," Pang Zhihao said.

Monitoring Captures Indonesian Satellite

The reporter noticed that after China's satellite launch encountered problems this time, the mainstream foreign media did not hype it up as much as they did for North Korea and South Korea's satellite launch failures earlier this year. Only a few media outlets published very brief news.

The "Palapa-D" commercial communications satellite was designed and manufactured by France's Thales Alenia Space and owned by Indonesia’s Satelindo. With a mass of 4,078 kilograms and 8,600 watts of power, it has a designed lifespan of 15 years and will be positioned at 113 degrees east longitude above the equator. Once operational, it will cover Indonesia, ASEAN countries, and a wide area of Asia, enabling Satelindo to provide various communication services to its customers. This satellite will replace the "Palapa-C2" satellite whose orbital life expires in 2010 and will become the only in-orbit satellite of Satelindo for the next two to three years. However, the fault in this launch has added uncertainty to this plan.

After the accident in the launch of the "Palapa-D" commercial communications satellite, relevant aerospace experts from home and abroad began analyzing the causes and seeking appropriate solutions.

Fortunately, around 22:00 on August 31, the corresponding satellite monitoring system successfully tracked the satellite's trajectory. Monitoring showed that the satellite was still operating normally. Currently, how to rescue this satellite that is not running on the correct orbit has become the concern of many people.

Precedents for Orbit Repair

Pang Zhihao indicated that internationally, there are precedents where satellites that failed to enter the intended orbit after launch were salvaged, and several methods for repairing satellite orbits have already been developed.

On April 7, 1990, China successfully launched its first commercial satellite, "AsiaSat-1". This was a satellite that was previously recovered by the U.S. after a failed launch. According to information, the predecessor of "AsiaSat-1" was the Westar VI artificial satellite designed and manufactured by the American Hughes Aircraft Company. In 1984, after its launch, the Westar VI did not accurately enter orbit. In November of that year, the U.S. carried out a "satellite rescue operation", retrieving the satellite using a space shuttle and handing it over to the manufacturer, who then entrusted it to China for launch.

Additionally, some satellites equipped with fuel can also repair their own orbits, allowing the satellite to enter the predetermined orbit. "The most typical case is the European Artemis satellite in 2001. After its launch, it did not enter the predetermined orbit. Since this satellite had electric rockets with propellants far more efficient than chemical fuels, under the control of ground personnel, it continuously changed its orbit and entered the predetermined orbit more than ten months later."

Pang Zhihao mentioned that utilizing lunar gravity is also an important method of repair. In 1998, the "AsiaSat-3" satellite failed to enter the predetermined orbit, but the American Hughes company managed to let it successfully enter the predetermined orbit through the effect of lunar gravity. Due to unclear specifics about the "Palapa-D" commercial communications satellite, Pang Zhihao did not evaluate whether this satellite could be successfully repaired.

Satellite “Revival” Possesses Functionality

However, according to statements from the French Thales Alenia Space company, the satellite should be able to gradually enter the predetermined orbit through orbit-changing technology. On September 1, the satellite performed a perigee orbit change and succeeded, with the satellite status normal.

Compared to South Korea's "Naro" launch failure, South Korea's satellite was a low-orbiting small satellite. During launch, due to the fairing not separating normally, the residual fairing increased the weight of the effective payload, preventing the satellite from gaining the minimum speed needed to orbit the Earth and causing it to directly fall into the atmosphere and burn up. In contrast, the Indonesian "Palapa-D" satellite launched by China is a large communication satellite (4.1 tons) operating in a geosynchronous orbit (36,000 kilometers above the Earth's surface). Although the third-stage engine of the rocket malfunctioned and the satellite did not enter the predetermined synchronous transfer orbit, it would not fall into the atmosphere, and the satellite itself is normal, so there is still room for rescue.

"The most important thing is that even if South Korea's satellite had not fallen, since it does not have the ability to change orbit, it cannot return to the predetermined orbit. However, the 'Palapa-D' satellite has the ability for maneuverable orbit changes, so there is still hope for self-rescue when the rocket malfunctions," Jiao Weixin introduced.

However, Jiao Weixin is not optimistic about whether the "Palapa-D" commercial communications satellite can be successfully repaired. "Currently, many situations of the satellite are unclear, so it is difficult to determine whether it can be completely repaired," Jiao Weixin told reporters. Although the satellite can make orbit corrections once it enters Earth's orbit, the specific orbit adjustment capability of the satellite is not clear.

This launch failed due to a fault in the third-stage engine, and the Chinese-launched satellite, due to the rocket reason, failed to send the Indonesian satellite into the synchronous transfer orbit, forcing it to stay in a slightly lower orbit. But the satellite can transfer from this lower orbit to the synchronous orbit. The satellite's engine has two functions: one is for orbit changing during the launch process, and the other is for maintaining and adjusting the orbit throughout the work period. During the satellite's working period, due to various reasons, it may deviate from the predetermined orbit. When the deviation is large, the engine needs to be started to correct the orbit.

The fuel carried by the engine is limited. Even if the "Palapa-D" satellite successfully changes its orbit, it will consume the fuel originally intended for orbit maintenance. Thus, the fuel available for orbit keeping may not be enough, and the satellite may end its life prematurely. Whether the satellite's life will be shortened and by how much depends on the specific design parameters of the satellite.

"The problems encountered by the Chinese-launched Indonesian satellite and the South Korean satellite prove that aerospace engineering is a high-risk, cutting-edge, complex project. Any tiny mistake can bring unpredictable risks," Pang Zhihao said.
