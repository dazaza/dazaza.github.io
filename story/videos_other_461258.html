Central heating systems can implement individual household heat metering

Residential heating issues involve thousands of households. Central heating, after long-term application and testing, is a relatively mature technology. With the continuous development of science and technology, like other applied technologies, central heating technology should also be continuously improved and developed, making its system design more reasonable, operation control more flexible, equipment manufacturing more advanced, especially making its operation more energy-saving, thus quickly adapting to the requirements of building energy conservation. In recent years, due to changes in the energy structure, because of developers' interests and property management needs, also due to the improvement in people's living standards leading to higher demands for thermal comfort, especially due to the increasingly serious problem of delayed heating fee payments in various places, the issue of individual household heat metering and individual household heat charging has become very urgent. Meanwhile, various heating technologies and corresponding product developments such as low-temperature radiative electric heating membrane heating, setting gas water heaters or electric water heaters for each household have suddenly emerged. The diversification of heating methods is a manifestation of the development of heating technology. People, according to various different usage situations, now have multiple choices for heating methods. Precisely because of this, it undoubtedly forms a great "impact" on traditional centralized heating methods.

But there is a phenomenon worth noting currently, in some newly designed engineering projects, some developers do not consider the specific circumstances of the project, do not conduct specific analysis of various heating methods, and do not make technical and economic comparisons, excessively emphasizing the convenience of non-centralized heating methods for implementing individual household heat metering and facilitating individual household heat charging, blindly adopting them, and thus abandoning centralized heating methods. Even in high-rise residential buildings, one-household-style gas water heaters are used for heating, posing safety hazards. Some administrative departments are also strongly promoting these non-centralized heating methods. Many design personnel, despite striving for more reasonable heating designs based on engineering principles, often fail. Even our "Residential Design Code", regarding the regulations on centralized heating, the new version released on March 24, 1999, has retreated significantly from the old version in 1987, changing the original requirement that high-rise and mid-rise buildings "should" have centralized heating systems, multi-story and low-rise buildings "preferably" have centralized heating systems, to all being "preferably" set.

This forces people to think: does the overall efficiency of energy utilization, heating quality, hygiene conditions, environmental protection, pollution reduction, fire prevention, security guarantees, as well as initial investment and long-term operating costs of centralized heating (which should also include centralized heat supply) no longer exist just because individual household heat metering and heat charging need to be introduced? In fact, in the Ministry of Construction's issued "Civil Building Energy Conservation Management Regulations," this question has already been answered. The "Management Regulations" list "centralized heating" as an encouraged building energy conservation technology by the state, clearly stating that "new residential buildings" should have "centralized heating" systems. It should be pointed out that implementing individual household heat metering is a technical measure to achieve building energy conservation management. Researching and developing individual household heat metering and heat charging, along with upholding the advantages of centralized heating (including centralized heat supply), are all aimed at better realizing building energy conservation. These two aspects are completely consistent and should not be opposed. Introducing the concept of individual household heat metering can further promote the development of centralized heating technology. The practice of urgently seeking a way that can both provide heating and facilitate heat charging while blindly abandoning centralized heating systems is undesirable, and if allowed to continue unchecked, the consequences would be unimaginable.

2 Premises for Individual Household Heat Metering in Centralized Heating Systems

To achieve individual household heat metering in centralized heating systems, whether in system design or in the required corresponding equipment, it is currently feasible. The primary purpose of individual household heat metering is to save energy during heating operations, providing users with means to regulate and control their heating consumption based on their thermal comfort needs. For this, the heating must be of high quality. Only with high-quality heating, where users have adjustable heat, is energy-efficient operation possible; otherwise, individual household heat metering will become merely a decorative feature. Therefore, high-quality heating is a prerequisite for individual household heat metering in centralized heating systems.

To ensure high-quality heating, good indoor heating system design is first needed. After implementing individual household heat metering, the design requirements for indoor heating systems are even higher, reflected in accurate heat load calculations, correct selection of heating system types, and careful hydraulic balance design calculations. Heat load calculation is the foundation of indoor heating system design. The method of estimating heat load using heat indicators is unacceptable. Blindly increasing the heat load value due to fear of insufficient heating results in oversized radiator installation areas, which will hinder the regulation and control of temperature control valves on radiator branch pipes. This practice should be avoided. Currently, it is generally believed that a dual-pipe system with shared supply and return risers plus independent branch circuits for each household is a better indoor heating system type suitable for implementing individual household heat metering. However, many designers neglect system hydraulic balance design calculations, believing that installing balancing valves, temperature control valves, and other regulation and control devices automatically achieves hydraulic balance during operation. This view is incorrect. A system with severely unbalanced hydraulic conditions cannot rely solely on these adjustment measures to achieve hydraulic balance during operation because any regulating device has its own adjustment characteristics and limited adjustment range. For example, a thermostatic valve produced by a foreign company allows pre-set values between 1 and 7, but generally, the pre-set value should not be below 3, meaning the valve should not be closed too much. Otherwise, it may lead to other problems, such as blockage at the valve and unfavorable operation energy savings.

To ensure high-quality heating, the heating system requires comprehensive regulation and control measures and high-level operational management. After implementing individual household heat metering, the system's regulation performance needs to be enhanced to adapt to variable flow operation throughout the system. Users may automatically or manually adjust their heat consumption, but at the heat source, the heat supply should be automatically adjusted to track changes in the required heat load. Therefore, differential pressure control devices should be designed to ensure stable hydraulic conditions during system regulation and operation. System operation should combine both quality regulation and quantity regulation. Traditional designs with a 25°C difference between supply and return water temperatures are not conducive to hydraulic stability during system operation. A "small temperature difference and large flow" approach in heating systems can improve hydraulic stability. Therefore, when the heating load changes, such as variations in outdoor temperature, quality regulation should be performed first, followed by quantity regulation based on changes in user end-load, allowing for good operation of individually metered heating systems. If only quantity regulation is performed without quality regulation, it will lead to frequent opening and closing of thermostatic valves at user locations, which is detrimental to system stability. Therefore, implementing individual household heat metering represents a shift from current extensive heating operations to energy-efficient ones, indicating further advancements in operational management levels and heating technology.

Of course, a well-designed indoor heating system for individual household heat metering requires high-quality construction and installation to ensure its effectiveness. We know that the supply and return branch pipes connected to each household’s independent branch circuit in a double-pipe system are mostly pre-buried in the floor slab underlay. They are embedded during civil construction. If the installation process lags behind, the pipe joints are exposed on the construction site, and even slight negligence could cause blockages in the branch pipes, making it difficult to clean them thoroughly later. Installing radiators afterward could result in poor water flow and inadequate heating, making individual household heat metering impossible.

3 Relationship Between Implementing Individual Household Heat Metering and Charging

The initial purpose of installing thermostatic valves and heat meters at user locations was for energy saving, providing technical measures for operation energy saving while better satisfying people's thermal comfort needs. Users reducing or even completely shutting off their heating due to their own economic considerations, abandoning thermal comfort requirements, should not be considered as operational energy saving. Implementing individual household heat metering treats heat as a commodity, providing a technical means for potentially achieving more accurate individual household heat charging. Since heat becomes a commodity, users have the freedom to choose whether to buy and how much to buy, and they have the means to realize this choice. However, heat is not a regular commodity; it has many special attributes, one of which is its transmissibility—it transfers between users. After implementing individual household heat metering, this heat transfer cannot be ignored.
