"The Inevitable 'Death of Yougou': Belle's E-commerce Has Many Hard Injuries"

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 16:18:04

Recently, Yougou.com, which has been suffering from a severe loss of senior executives, received another blow. On July 17th, it was reported that according to insiders at Yougou.com, the company's COO, Zhang Xiaojun, will be leaving and is currently processing his resignation. As one of the founding members of Yougou.com, Zhang Xiaojun's departure carries significant implications, indicating that Yougou.com is indeed facing serious crises.

Let us first review the e-commerce journey of Belle International, to which Yougou.com belongs:

Belle quietly tested the waters in e-commerce several years ago, developing steadily through Taobao distribution, Tmall, and B2C distribution (B2C distribution completed via Taixiu.com). Led by veteran group member Xie Yunli, a not-so-luxurious e-commerce team started by selling products under Belle’s own brands, then gradually launched exclusive online models, followed by new network brands. This development path became a successful case study for traditional brand enterprises entering e-commerce before 2011, considered more solid than Li-Ning's approach.

With the rise of Letao and Haolemai receiving over $100 million in financing in 2010 and rapidly developing thereafter, Belle Group, which supplied these two companies, began to feel uneasy. On one hand, they were concerned about becoming overly reliant on distribution channels again; on the other hand, they assessed their potential for great growth within the e-commerce sector. These advantages included:

Firstly, numerous brands: In addition to Belle's own major women's shoe brands, the agency-managed international sports brands like Adidas and Nike experienced astonishing growth in online sales;

Secondly, easier category expansion: For Belle Group, known for its women's shoes and agency-managed well-known sports brands, how to gain absolute market advantage and status through multi-brand and multi-category strategies was the most thought-provoking question over the years. Expanding offline stores to add brands and categories was evidently too slow, while ongoing e-commerce operations could easily expand brands but not quickly enough with categories;

Thirdly, financial advantage: Through collaboration with e-commerce enterprises, Belle realized that e-commerce is a capital-intensive business. The financial strength of Belle Group allowed it to develop without relying on external capital, inevitably defeating many vertical e-commerce companies dependent on capital and achieving the "survivor takes all" status;

Fourthly, talent advantage: After years of e-commerce practice, Belle had developed an execution-strong, highly professional e-commerce team, whose talents could handle large-scale e-commerce business or even more;

Fifthly, supply chain advantage: This excited Belle internally and was the core reason attracting high-level executives from brand e-commerce or platform e-commerce. After all, for e-commerce, the supply chain is always fascinating as it seems to be the life-or-death determinant aside from capital. The supply chain includes product research and development, production, warehouse management, delivery, after-sales service, and a series of "business" contents. In traditional enterprises emphasizing "business" over "e-commerce," this is considered the most advantageous competitive edge over e-commerce companies.

Thus, with these advantages, Belle e-commerce decided to make a big move, purchasing the double-pinyin domain name "yougou.com,"

abandoning the somewhat copycat "topshoes.cn" Taixiu website and redirecting it, stopping supplies to all distribution websites. While maintaining direct third-party shops like Taobao and Tmall Mall, the investment and attention decreased. High salaries were offered to attract JD.com VP and Vancl VP to serve as CMO and COO respectively, with group veterans appointed as CEO, forming a golden triangle. They aimed to build a full-category vertical B2C mall, implementing a multi-brand strategy on one hand, expanding into clothing and other categories on the other, realizing the dream of a department store empire impossible to achieve offline!

All sounded perfect, but every dream needs grounding in reality. The actual situation was:

Firstly, higher technical requirements for independent B2C: Being headquartered in Shenzhen, Belle couldn't recruit more excellent technical talents, and technical outsourcing service companies were scarce there. Technology determined user experience, backend management efficiency, and reliable data. Independent B2C involves dynamic technology, different from relatively static technologies like ERP. Continuous changes remain a hurdle for many traditional enterprises;

Secondly, increased management difficulty with category expansion: Someone once said that doing well in women's shoes doesn't necessarily mean doing well in men's shoes, something Belle probably deeply felt when expanding into men's shoes. Expanding into women's wear and accessories in just one year would obviously pose immense challenges at the backend. For traditional brand enterprises accustomed to meticulous planning, such rapid expansion might have worked during Vancl's special period, understandable within the systems of Vancl and JD.com. Although Belle acquired Vancl's COO and JD.com's CMO, whether they could be understood and executed with "exaggerated costs" remains questionable!

Thirdly, system issues: Many people don’t understand the conflicts between e-commerce and traditional enterprises unless they’ve crossed both fields. Simply put, departmental settings (organizational structure), approvals, finance and budget, reporting methods, corporate culture, communication styles, execution methods, risk control, processes and regulations form what’s called a company’s “mechanism.” This conflict results in e-commerce trying to persuade the acceptance of its "system," while the traditional team attempts to understand but can never fully grasp e-commerce’s "system," leading to low efficiency and instability in the execution team. Ultimately, the chairman or board may say: "E-commerce and traditional enterprises each have their strengths and weaknesses, and the future must involve integration. Therefore, you must integrate in these areas..." If this so-called "integration without harmony" appears, it leads to so-called management innovation, and thus the "CEO rotation system" actually emerged in a company like Belle!

Originally, it was believed that Yougou.com of Belle e-commerce had already taken the lead, and the senior executives were waiting for the spin-off listing to enjoy success, wealth, and countless honors of traditional enterprises succeeding in the e-commerce era. Instead, they found themselves tangled and distressed by these hard injuries.

After all, whether the veterans who grew up with Belle or the e-commerce professional VPs poached from elsewhere, it's clear that they aren't leaving due to lack of ability. When JD.com's VP returns to JD.com, they continue to perform admirably; when Vancl's VP ventures into entrepreneurship with courage. So are these three points the hard injuries? Or are these not only Belle's hard injuries but also those of many traditional enterprises attempting to enter e-commerce?
