The Truth of Labor Shortage in Dongguan: Low-education Workers Unwelcome

by 3yll869c5t on 2009-11-23 13:11:07

No matter how serious the labor shortage is, Guangdong will not reopen its doors to migrant workers with low education. The large army of migrant workers causing the paralysis of transportation during the Spring Festival travel rush is becoming less and less possible to reappear. A year ago, due to the bankruptcy of his factory, 27-year-old Shaanxi migrant worker Fang Bing was forced to return home. After waiting for some time on the barren loess land, he returned to Dongguan. On August 15th at the Dongguan Zhítong Talent Market job fair, Fang Bing carried a stack of resumes and promoted himself in front of corporate recruitment booths. To his slight surprise, every company that talked to him gave him a second interview notice, and some companies even wanted him to go to the factory for a second interview immediately. Fang Bing has worked in Dongguan for nearly 10 years and has never encountered a company so eager to hire workers. Fang Bing noticed that there were few job seekers inquiring at many recruitment booths, while in previous years each booth was bustling with people, and one had to sweat profusely just to submit a resume. What Fang Bing encountered was precisely the new change that appeared in the second quarter of 2009 in Dongguan's labor employment market: the number of job vacancies increased, but the number of job seekers relatively decreased. According to statistics from Zhítong Talent Market in August, for every 100 job seekers, there were 151 positions available to choose from. Compared to the past when there was a shortage of general workers, this labor shortage showed a higher demand for the quality and ability of workers. Post-crisis "labor shortage" From 2008 to the first half of this year, according to official figures released by Dongguan, a total of 1,207 companies went bankrupt, resulting in the loss of 630,000 jobs. It is estimated that over a million migrant workers left Dongguan. At that time, in Dongguan, rumors of factory closures spread like wildfire, and scenes of migrant workers demanding wages and packing up to return home were everywhere. However, just six months later, starting from the second quarter of this year, there was a warming trend in corporate employment, and the momentum became increasingly evident. In April, the owner of a Taiwanese-funded jewelry factory told reporters that orders had increased slightly, allowing the company to struggle through the slump. By May, she told reporters again that the workers could no longer handle the influx of orders, and more hands were needed. The recovery of foreign trade orders brought about an increase in labor demand. The labor demand in the second quarter of this year was 187,000 people, approaching the level of the same period last year. The third quarter continued the trend of the second quarter. According to statistics from Zhítong Talent Market, the labor demand in July increased by 15% compared to June, and in August it remained stable with a slight increase. The number of recruitment positions reached 90% of the same period last year. On average, there was only one job seeker per position, almost reaching a state of "zero competition." For job seekers, less intense employment competition allowed them to be picky, making it undoubtedly the best time to look for work. But for companies, having orders but not finding people to do the work meant watching profits slip away, like seeing crops rot in the fields. Wanshidar Plastic Packaging Co., Ltd.'s booth at Zhítong Talent Market listed design, slitting, copperplate printing, and other recruitment positions, but two staff members sat idly all day without hiring a single employee. They packed up their items and left early before the job fair ended. Companies like Wanshidar were not alone in encountering cold recruitment. At Zhítong Talent Market, 80,000 positions are generated each month, but there are only 60,000 job seekers, leaving a gap of about 20,000 people, meaning some companies are bound to fail in recruitment. Labor shortages are not uncommon in Dongguan. Before the outbreak of the crisis, Dongguan would experience seasonal "migrant worker shortages" every July and August because these months were peak production seasons, leading to insufficient labor supply. Each year during the peak production season, there would be a labor shortfall of around 100,000 people. The labor shortage after the financial crisis differs from the past in specific aspects, but one thing remains the same: the labor-intensive enterprises are the ones experiencing the shortage, determined by the characteristics of Dongguan's industrial structure. Every fourth quarter, companies typically recruit some people for next year's reserve, known as "storage cadres," but in the fourth quarter of 2008, very few companies did so, leading to a bleak talent market. The usual peak employment season started in the first quarter, but in the first quarter of 2009, there was no sign of a peak. "The anomaly in the employment cycle is related to the impact of the financial crisis on Dongguan's outward-oriented economy," said Cai Xiaomei, public relations manager of Zhítong Talent Market, to this magazine. This labor shortage occurring one year after the financial crisis draws extra attention. Structural labor shortage Under the backdrop of the financial crisis, Dongguan's labor shortage has distinct features, among which the most prominent is the pursuit and shortage of design, R&D, and marketing talents caused by economic transformation. The global industrial system was hit by the 2008 financial crisis, with Dongguan's external sales orders decreasing by thirty percent. Processing trade enterprises relying on the "three comes, one supplement" model for thirty years found themselves stuck in a situation where they couldn't export or import. Numerous companies, for survival, began to transform and seek domestic sales channels. To open domestic sales channels, one cannot simply copy foreign blueprints but must design and develop products suitable for the domestic consumer market. Taking the furniture manufacturing industry in Houjie Town as an example, previously, they received orders along with accompanying blueprints, so factories didn't need their own designers or structural engineers. However, for domestic sales, furniture styled with European and American influences may not cater to domestic consumers' tastes, thus necessitating the recruitment of such personnel to design the entire set of procedures including styles, structures, and processes. Based on this, professional design and R&D talents have become highly sought after. After product designs are completed, the next step is establishing marketing channels, which requires significant manpower and financial resources. According to Huang Jing, marketing director of Ideal Talent Market, as early as 2007, before the financial storm affected the Chinese market, many furniture companies in Houjie had already begun constructing marketing channels within the mainland. External and internal sales channels differ, and the required talents are entirely different concepts. The Pearl River Delta's external sales system was initially established by Hong Kong-funded enterprises in a "front store, back factory" pattern, followed by countless foreign trade companies forming a complete process of order receiving, order dispatching, shipping, and settlement. The cluster of customs brokers near Dongguan Customs indicates that this system has become quite mature and professionalized. Internal sales, however, emphasize familiarity with the mainland market, requiring business warriors with market acumen and expansion capabilities. The scarcity of marketing and design personnel clearly differs from the market condition of lacking general workers. In the field of human resource management, this is referred to as "structural labor shortage." Data provided by Zhítong Talent Market shows that the employment tension since April of this year exhibits obvious characteristics of structural shortages. Enterprises favor technical, production, and marketing talents with 3 to 5 years or more of experience, accounting for approximately 45% of the total recruitment positions, making this segment the most scarce. Unveiling the Order Mystery Manufacturing in Dongguan accounts for nearly 90% of industrial revenue. In the absence of significant success in domestic sales, changes in corporate labor demands can be seen as a barometer of foreign trade orders. Entering May, there was a sharp increase in labor demand in industries such as electronics, clothing, furniture, food, and printing, indicating a substantial increase in received foreign trade orders. The rebound in orders directly boosted labor demand. Statistical data from recent months shows that Guangdong's foreign trade indeed has signs of warming. Expressed as a narrowing decline in foreign trade exports, the export declines in May, June, and July were 21.2%, 18%, and 15.1%, respectively. Previously sharply declining high-tech products and mechanical and electrical products have significantly recovered. However, the warming of Guangdong's export situation cannot be simply regarded as an overall improvement in economic conditions, nor can the recovery of foreign trade orders be viewed as a sign of global economic recovery. To accurately evaluate the recovery of foreign trade, one must stand at a higher perspective and examine the operating environment of Guangdong's foreign trade enterprises. The prevailing view currently is that a year after the outbreak of the crisis, Western inventories have been mostly cleared, although residents' purchasing power has declined, basic consumption needs still need to be met. Therefore, they continue to look globally for suppliers and manufacturers. Orders being placed back in Dongguan does not mean that Western developed countries have boldly stepped out of the crisis; it merely indicates that the West has yet to find a world factory cheaper and more convenient than Dongguan. After 30 years of cultivation, Dongguan has become a monopolistic producer in certain fields. Although at the bottom of the global value chain with negligible added value, its production volume is unmatched globally. For instance, out of every ten pairs of shoes in the world, one is made in Dongguan. The astonishing market share won't bring Dongguan more bargaining power because Dongguan is merely a workshop, a production line, with the product pricing power held by brand owners and multinational groups. The greatest benefit of having a high market share is that, due to its pivotal role in the global supply chain, if you don't take goods from here, someone will go barefoot elsewhere. On the other hand, Dongguan possesses the most complete industrial support in some processing and manufacturing fields globally. Although the prices of production factors such as land, water, and electricity have risen, the comprehensive cost remains competitive. This is another important factor attracting orders. Moreover, some supportive policies issued by the government for export enterprises under the crisis also help attract orders. Dongguan shoe manufacturing enterprises enjoy a 15% export tax rebate. Even if the foreign buyers lower prices citing the financial crisis, shoe enterprises can still profit through the export tax rebate policy when their profits are non-existent. According to Huang Jing's introduction, Houjie shoe manufacturing enterprises have a large demand for general workers and frequently interact with Ideal Talent Market. After the outbreak of the financial crisis, some shoe enterprises in Dongguan were indeed subjected to price pressures from buyers, with their meager profits shrinking by another 30%. However, under the protection of the export tax rebate policy, they accept all orders. Then what they need to do is recruit workers and expand production. In addition, seasonal increases in orders should not be overlooked. Several months before Christmas, Halloween, and Thanksgiving, Dongguan toy and gift factories receive some orders. The increase in seasonal orders also boosts corporate labor demand. Such seasonal orders are usually unstable and can be completed within a month. Huang Jing stated that he heard some companies say, "After rushing to finish the goods in September, we'll have to give the workers a vacation again." This sentence reminded him of the great depression last October. Migrant Worker Retreat in the Pearl River Delta Previously, after Dongguan's processing trade factories received orders, the common practice was to make workers work overtime continuously to "rush goods." However, the implementation of the new Labor Law somewhat curbed this behavior, stipulating that overtime pay is 1.5 times the regular salary. Compelling workers to work overtime without restraint not only places enterprises in a position of violating laws and regulations and facing severe penalties but also, considering cost calculations, leads to a sharp rise in overtime pay. Remember, in the meager income of migrant workers, overtime pay constitutes a significant portion. For a long time, the issue of low income for migrant workers in Dongguan has not improved, with workers' salaries remaining unchanged for decades, driving people away invisibly due to low income. After the outbreak of the financial crisis, Dongguan workers earned between 1200-1600 yuan, a decrease of 100-200 yuan compared to before the crisis. Currently, the minimum wage standard in Dongguan is 770 yuan/month, lower than Guangzhou and Shenzhen, and also lower than many cities in the Yangtze River Delta region. Low income and high consumption make many migrant workers unable to bear it, prompting many to leave Dongguan and move elsewhere. In the case of tight labor supply, Dongguan enterprises have not effectively utilized income leverage to attract talent. According to statistics from Zhítong Talent Market, compared to before the crisis, there has been little change in employment benefits in Dongguan this year, with wages not significantly rising and overall wage levels remaining the same as last year. In the eyes of Liang Bairu, former director of the Hong Kong Trade and Investment Office in Guangdong, the Pearl River Delta is losing its advantage in attracting talent. A few years ago, when he traveled to Huangjiang Town in Dongguan on business, he saw young people crowding into every large snack stall, but not a single factory offered vocational training for workers. During working hours, these young people were exploited, and in their free time, they were left to waste away. Factories were satisfied with the proficiency of workers on the assembly line but did not provide them with career advancement opportunities, making everything related to workers seem temporary. "No matter how proficient one is in operating on the assembly line, it is impossible to become a qualified industrial worker," Liang Bairu said. Under the dual transfer background, low-education, low-quality workers are no longer welcomed in the Pearl River Delta, becoming the target of relocation. Regardless of whether the government forces factories and people to leave, the phenomenon of a sharp reduction in migrant workers is something that the implementers of the dual transfer policy would like to see. To ensure local officials firmly implement the dual transfer, the main indicator for evaluating officials' performance is that the growth rate of per capita GDP exceeds the growth rate of GDP. Whether new residents or migrant workers, the Dongguan government hopes that fewer people will drag down the per capita GDP. Imagine, no matter how serious the labor shortage is, Guangdong will not reopen its doors to migrant workers with low education. The large army of migrant workers causing the paralysis of transportation during the Spring Festival travel rush is becoming less and less possible to reappear.
