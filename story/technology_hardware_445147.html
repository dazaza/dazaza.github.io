● Why has Lenovo maintained vigorous growth vitality for 26 years since its establishment?

The Confidence of Liu Chuanzhi

"Have you ever said that Lenovo is your life? But is it Lenovo Holdings or Lenovo Group that is your life?" In an early September gathering of entrepreneurs, Wang Jianlin turned his body towards Liu Chuanzhi and asked sharp questions: "Since the investment sector of Lenovo Holdings is so profitable, would you consider selling off the less profitable IT sector?"

...

In May 2009, Lenovo Group suffered its biggest loss ever: a net loss of $226 million in the 2008-2009 fiscal year. "There are countless challenges in Lenovo's internationalization process, but that was the most painful period, even forcing us to abandon," recalled Chen Shaopeng, Senior Vice President of Lenovo Group, when talking about the tense situation last year when they were on the brink.

After Liu Chuanzhi reassumed the position of Chairman of Lenovo Group, a detail he encountered during a board meeting in Europe sufficiently explained the severity of the situation at that time. After acquiring IBM, Lenovo's European branch always set promising profit targets at the beginning of the year, but performance decreased month by month, and by the end of the year, profits turned into losses, which made Liu very angry. Once, he asked an old IBM employee from the German branch: How do you view the inability to meet performance commitments?

The employee replied: "When the CEO makes a decision, I show my agreement out of respect for the CEO."

"What if the target cannot be achieved?" Liu was confused.

"That is the CEO's tolerance towards me."

"If I don't stand up to reverse this downward trend, Lenovo might just keep sliding downhill." In September, during the "West Lake Sword Forum" held in Hangzhou, Liu Chuanzhi openly admitted for the first time that the decline in Lenovo Group's performance was not only due to the financial crisis but also directly related to short-term actions taken by the management team.

According to Chen Shaopeng, in recent years, Lenovo Group has been focusing on large institutions and enterprise clients, while expanding slowly into the rapidly emerging small and medium-sized enterprises and individual consumer sectors, which caused Lenovo to suffer more intensely during the financial crisis. On this point, Liu Chuanzhi believed that former CEO Amelio wasn't unaware of it; however, developing PC personal consumer products and their supply chain requires an investment of over $700 million and will take at least three years before generating returns. Sacrificing high profits for long-term growth within a five-year term clearly deterred Amelio from taking this step.

Another classic example of Amelio pursuing "short-term benefits" was cutting off Lenovo Mobile - a product that Liu Chuanzhi and Yang Yuanqing thought should be retained, aligning with the future trend of 3G convergence. Liu Chuanzhi once again adopted the method of "taking a big turn" to indirectly save the company: Hongyi Investment stepped in and took over Lenovo Mobile for $100 million. When Lenovo Group needed the mobile business back, they repurchased it for $200 million.

Poor financial reports led to Amelio stepping down. After Liu Chuanzhi returned, Lenovo Group smoothly restructured its management team, forming a new leadership team of nine people, replacing the previous cumbersome team of over twenty people. In the new team, Chinese executives finally gained a voice, and foreign executives began to truly see themselves as owners. Lenovo finally had the opportunity to implement a change that should have been done long ago: to address the imbalance between large and small clients, Lenovo launched the "double-fist strategy," dividing the global market into emerging markets centered around China for further expansion and mature markets centered around the US and Japan to strengthen and grow. The product lines were also clarified into Think business targeting institutional clients represented by ThinkPad and Idea business targeting individual consumers. Chen Shaopeng vividly compared it to: "the left hand throws a defensive punch, defending the Chinese market and global large client business; the right hand throws an offensive punch, heavily attacking the emerging markets and individual computer business."

The unfulfilled promises that annoyed Liu Chuanzhi were completely eliminated. Led by Liu Chuanzhi and Yang Yuanqing, Lenovo executives started visiting every key market globally to meet customers, distributors, media, hold discussions with local employees, and spread the culture. They identified corporate behavior standards such as "company interests above all", "say what you mean and do what you say without reservation", "think clearly before making a promise", and "once a promise is made, it must be resolutely fulfilled", quickly unifying Lenovo's culture. Liu Chuanzhi expressed his satisfaction: "It feels so good," he said when discussing his feelings upon returning to Europe for the board meeting this year: "Because the results we deliver will definitely exceed your expectations."

Liu Chuanzhi never fights an unprepared battle. "When did I dare to boast that I could quickly turn things around? It was after Yang Yuanqing stabilized the team that I felt secure." According to analysis firm IDC's data published in July, Lenovo Group's global PC market share broke through 10% for the first time, reaching 10.2%, with a 48% annual increase in personal computer sales, marking the third consecutive quarter where Lenovo led in growth among the top five global computer manufacturers.

"The hardest times for Lenovo are already past, and we are back on track for high-speed growth." On August 19, holding the first-quarter financial report showing $82 million in profits for the 2010-2011 fiscal year, Liu Chuanzhi smiled. At an entrepreneur gathering, Liu emphasized that the computer industry would not shrink and would continue to accompany human society indefinitely. "Now Yang Yuanqing's team is ambitious, aiming to challenge the global PC leader within five years." Liu's passion extended to Lenovo's mobile internet strategy, passionately delivering a speech at the LePhone launch event in April, challenging Apple head-on.

Lenovo's Diversification "Secrets"

On September 12, Lenovo Holdings invested 18 billion yuan to build a coal chemical base in Zaozhuang, Shandong Province. Three days later, Lenovo Holdings invested another 1.2 billion yuan to gain over 51% controlling equity in神州Renting. Additionally, Lenovo Holdings' grand agricultural strategy is under tight preparation. Zhu Linan, Executive Vice President of Lenovo Holdings, stated that the goal is to build core operating assets through investment and hold them long-term, aiming for overall listing of the holding group within 5-7 years.

As early as around 2000, during a lecture at the China Europe International Business School, Liu Chuanzhi first revealed Lenovo's intention to enter real estate and pursue unrelated diversification. After the class, a senior executive from the real estate industry bluntly told Liu Chuanzhi: "Mr. Liu, you can still make computers, but I think you'd better not try real estate."

Ten years later, not only has Rongke Zhidi established its name in the real estate world, but Lenovo Capital and Flagship Fund under Lenovo Holdings have become the leading forces in domestic investment circles. From Lenovo Group to Lenovo Holdings, reviewing Lenovo's history, Liu Chuanzhi is transforming this company from a large ship into a fleet. Zhu Linan described the prototype of this ocean-going fleet, consisting of three parts:

The first part consists of core operating assets. Besides Lenovo Group and Rongke Zhidi, in the next five to seven years, Lenovo Holdings will create new core assets, investing in directions including large agriculture, chemical industries, consumer goods, and modern services. Zhu Linan hopes that a series of outstanding companies with scale and continuous growth potential will emerge in these fields, driving the overall listing of the holding group;

The second part is asset management. This includes two fund management companies, Lenovo Capital and Flagship Fund. While providing project capital guarantees for Lenovo Holdings, they are also important contributors to the company's medium and short-term finances, supporting Lenovo Holdings' long-term strategic investments;

The third part is the incubator. In 2008, Lenovo partnered with the Chinese Academy of Sciences to launch a project called "Lenovo Star" that does not seek financial returns. Lenovo Holdings established a 400 million yuan angel investment fund, using a "entrepreneurship training + angel investment" approach to discover projects and lead talents, helping them become tomorrow's stars in technology entrepreneurship.

Zhu Linan expressed that the three businesses operate independently but form a "full army synergy": the asset management sector continuously generates cash flow to support investments in core operating assets, whose performance then supports the overall listing of the holding group; on the other hand, the asset management and incubator sectors also serve as reservoirs for project reserves for core operating assets. "All Lenovo Holdings' troops and strategies are based on Lenovo Holdings' vision," Zhu Linan reiterated Lenovo Holdings' vision: dedicated to industrial patriotism, committed to becoming a trustworthy and respected company with leading enterprises in multiple industries, having influence internationally. Liu Chuanzhi has also revealed his heart in many occasions: "Industrial patriotism is my first vision."

Why does Lenovo succeed in everything it does, achieving significant success in non-related diversification? Zhao Linghuan, Senior Vice President of Lenovo Holdings and President of Flagship Fund, used the successes of Lenovo Capital and Flagship Fund as examples: when Lenovo Holdings allocated $34 million to establish Lenovo Capital in 2001, they were prepared to "pay tuition fees." However, by today's market value estimates, the return on investment for the first phase has reached over six times. Zhao Linghuan summarized four rules:

The first rule, "defining strategy" has always been the first priority in company management. Having graduated from a military academy, Liu Chuanzhi is familiar with Lin Biao's military combat principle of "three fasts and one slow": understanding enemy conditions before battle must be quick, breakthroughs during attacks must be quick, consolidating victories must be quick, but strategic decisions and launching attacks must be slow. "Plan carefully before action" and "step out of the picture to observe it" are some of Liu Chuanzhi's famous quotes regarding defining strategy. "Before making computers, I was a carpenter; the tabletop and table were screwed together. You must tighten one screw first, or the remaining three screw holes won't align." Liu Chuanzhi consistently emphasizes the importance of systematic thinking. If one rushes ahead blindly, the entire battle situation may come at a great cost.

Zhao Linghuan says that each company under Lenovo must have a clear strategy before making decisions, and there are no one-time-for-all strategies in the world. Therefore, during execution, one must constantly review and analyze shortcomings painfully, summarizing and refining through trial and error. As a result, Lenovo's strategy remains precise and highly targeted.

The second rule, running a company is about managing people. "No matter how good the plan is, without capable people and an excellent team, the best strategy can only hang on the wall," Zhao Linghuan said. In the investment industry, "people" are the most critical factor. Strong leadership and professional teams significantly increase the chances of success. Zhao Linghuan is proud that despite the frequent movement of top talents in the investment field, the core teams of Lenovo and Flagship Fund have remained quite stable in recent years.

The third rule, the power of culture is genuine power. Zhao Linghuan introduced that under the root culture of Lenovo Holdings, which prioritizes "corporate interests first, being pragmatic and progressive, and people-oriented," each subsidiary absorbs its essence and combines it with its own characteristics to create its application system. Lenovo Capital summarizes it as "team triumphs, talent capital, wealth with principles, entrepreneurial spirit," while Flagship Fund is "respecting trust and truth, thick benevolence and constant perseverance." Lenovo's profound cultural genes deeply penetrate into the bones of every subsidiary.

The fourth rule, Lenovo Capital and Flagship Fund provide companies with "capital with capabilities." What is capital? It is a set of enterprise growth methods explored by Lenovo over more than twenty years of trials and tribulations. Zhao Linghuan introduced that before Lenovo Capital entered iFlytek in 2001, the latter was preparing to go public as a systems integrator. However, after repeated discussions with the iFlytek team about business, Lenovo Capital found that the company's technological advantage in intelligent voice was more prominent, so it switched to going public as a high-tech enterprise, and its stock price has performed well since then. Flagship Fund uses Lenovo-style PE style to reform the ownership structure of large enterprises, creating value and "releasing tigers back into the wild."

In 2000, Lenovo experienced a painful failure in diversification when it ambitiously entered FM365 and launched Yingshitong, both of which ended in failure. Summarizing the reasons, Liu Chuanzhi attributed it to neglecting the factor of leaders' attention: "At that time, Yang Yuanqing felt extremely good about the PC business, but after engaging in multi-front battles simultaneously, many difficult points rather than key ones came before him, causing trouble immediately." After Lenovo split into Lenovo Group and Digital China in 2001, a smarter move was the establishment of Lenovo Holdings, making a good organizational cut for Lenovo's future non-related diversification.

Zhang Weiying believes that from Liu Chuanzhi's perspective, Lenovo is diversified; but from Yang Yuanqing's perspective, it is entirely specialized. "If Liu Chuanzhi interfered in Yang Yuanqing's work and Yang Yuanqing meddled in Liu Chuanzhi's work, Lenovo's path to diversification wouldn't have come this far." A firm supporter of diversification strategy, Guo Guangchang summarized, "Doing diversification is a very 'professional' matter."

The Power of Ownership

Recently, in different venues of exchanges with entrepreneurs, Liu Chuanzhi often discusses the rise and fall of Lenovo's old rival in the PC business—Hewlett-Packard (HP). In 1999, HP hired a star CEO, Carly Fiorina, who lacked any HP cultural foundation. Eager to quickly establish her reputation, Carly's "feverish brain" announced the shocking plan to acquire Compaq upon taking office. Members of the HP family fiercely opposed it, but due to holding too few shares, they helplessly watched the acquisition worth $25 billion pass. After acquiring Compaq, HP's performance plummeted, and Carly was forced to resign. Her successor, Mark Hurd, indeed revived HP's glory, putting pressure on Lenovo. Unexpectedly, a false reimbursement claim of $20,000 led to the board firing him, making HP once again a target for other global PC giants.

"A typical consequence of a company losing its owner!" Liu Chuanzhi raised his voice, eyes shining, evaluating word by word: "A company doesn't necessarily have its CEO as the true shareholder, but the company must have a real owner. If a company lacks a true major shareholder, and instead has only small shareholders or independent directors, it is detrimental to the company's long-term growth."

The standard of an "owner" is the willingness and ability to take responsibility for the long-term fate of the enterprise. As Liu Chuanzhi once said: A true entrepreneur is someone who dares to administer heavy medicine, possibly lethal at first, to a critically ill mother, yet ensures her revival and recovery.

For 26 years, Lenovo has struggled forward amidst intense conflicts between old and new systems, gradually becoming a company "with an owner." Zhu Linan introduced:

From 1984 to 2000, it focused solely on the IT industry, with a simple ownership structure where the Chinese Academy of Sciences held 100% control. In 1993, it secured 35% of the revenue-sharing rights from the Chinese Academy of Sciences, laying the foundation for future group ownership reforms.

Starting from 2000, it began entering new business areas. From 2000 to 2001, Lenovo split into Lenovo Group and Digital China, and subsequently established Rongke Zhidi, Lenovo Capital, and Flagship Fund. Also in 2001, with the support of the Chinese Academy of Sciences, it purchased the remaining 35% revenue-sharing rights as equity, which was held by the Lenovo Employee Stock Ownership Association.

Until China Oceanwide entered in 2009, holding 29% of Lenovo Holdings' equity, marking the basic completion of its ownership reform "big turn," transforming from a state-controlled company into a limited liability company with balanced equity...

At this point, Liu Chuanzhi politely interrupted Zhu Linan's speech, clearly eager to express himself: "Adjusting the ownership mechanism is crucial for Lenovo, essentially ensuring whether a company truly has an owner." The elder's eyes shone sincerely: "Having an owner versus not having one is fundamentally different. After I retire, if Lenovo is to be passed down, it must have a true owner. They won't be overflowing with riches, but equity incentives are indispensable, and then cultural connections will bind them together."

For 26 years, Lenovo has struggled forward amidst intense conflicts between old and new systems. Writer Ling Zhijun believes that Lenovo's remarkable uniqueness lies in mastering the way to coexist with the old system while astonishingly enduring, patiently, and skillfully negotiating with the nauseating flaws of the old system, gradually freeing itself from constraints and moving towards a new world. Lenovo's history tells us what ideals are, what reality is, and where the bridge to creating greatness lies between dreams and reality.

To ensure Lenovo forever remains a company with an owner, Liu Chuanzhi is designing a "mountain-stabilizing treasure." Liu Chuanzhi stated that after Lenovo Holdings goes public in the future, the shares held by the Chinese Academy of Sciences and China Oceanwide can be sold, but the employee stock ownership will transform into a company, at least retaining a portion of Lenovo Holdings' shares as the "mountain-stabilizing treasure" that won't be diluted after listing. The person chosen to manage the "mountain-stabilizing treasure" will be Liu Chuanzhi himself. The management of the "mountain-stabilizing treasure" will separate distribution rights from management rights, ensuring that the actions of the "owner" can be fully implemented through equity.

Additionally, the cultural gene has always been the richest and most effective treasure trove of this company. To summarize the essence of Lenovo's culture in one sentence, Liu Chuanzhi would say: "Teaching by words and example is not just an important way to inspire others—it's the only way." Spiritually inheriting Lenovo's bloodline is equally important because it aims to become a "family-less family enterprise."
