"Hatching Twitter: The Inside Story of a Social Media Phenomenon"

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 20:19:03

We like to read stories about tech companies, especially the entrepreneurial process, because they are often filled with friendship and idealism. However, the truth is that tech companies also have a dark side. The entrepreneurial journey is not just about friendship and ideals; it can also involve greed and betrayal. Thus, the Twitter story told by New York Times columnist Nick Bilton holds particular significance.

Among Twitter's co-founders, Jack Dorsey and Evan Williams stand out the most. Even after stepping away from Twitter’s daily operations, they continue to do remarkable things. Jack Dorsey founded Square, aiming to revolutionize the mobile payment industry, while Evan Williams created Medium, continuing to explore new forms of internet content publishing.

There’s no doubt about their talents or their important contributions to Twitter. However, this is not the whole story. When discussing Twitter's founding, they tend to avoid mentioning another crucial figure: Noah Glass. While the seed for Twitter may have come from Jack Dorsey, Noah Glass was the one who first developed the mature idea and was responsible for implementing the project. The name "Twitter" was also chosen by him.

Nevertheless, internal power struggles were inevitable. Jack Dorsey used his resignation as leverage to force Evan Williams to fire Noah Glass. From then on, the story of Twitter’s origins no longer included Noah Glass. It became solely Jack Dorsey's brilliant idea.

In Nick Bilton's new book, *Hatching Twitter*, he recounts the conflicts and contradictions in Twitter's growth. Wired magazine described it as “an adventure story, but also like a crime novel,” saying, “It tells the story of a group of lonely people coming together to design a product that connects others and the world around them, but in trying to control this thing, they tore each other apart.”

In Nick Bilton's book, Biz Stone, one of Twitter's founders, is portrayed as the moral voice of the company. He emphasized the importance of political neutrality and supporting free speech. He always provided support and help to his friends. However, his other contributions to the company are less clear.

The book focuses on the personality clash between Jack Dorsey and Evan Williams. In Bilton's portrayal, Jack Dorsey is not a clear-minded, visionary leader. He is an ill-tempered amateur who sometimes even wanted to abandon everything to become a tailor. Under his leadership, Twitter was a disaster. On a personal level, he was petty, arrogant, and greedy. While everyone else was working, he left the office to meditate or attend art classes. He was not good at communication. Moreover, during his time running Twitter, there were frequent technical issues and server crashes. Yet, he seemed to have good luck, as Twitter still became wildly popular.

Evan Williams is depicted as a more sympathetic character. He financially supported the company's operations and dedicated all his time to it. Perhaps due to his sincere nature, he failed to see that he would eventually be pushed out of the company.

Their personality conflicts affected Twitter's development. Jack Dorsey saw Twitter as a tool for discussing personal life, while Evan Williams had already begun viewing it as a window to observe the world.

Ultimately, Jack gained greater power and recognition, but Evan’s vision for Twitter gradually prevailed. Twitter is more about the world than just individuals; it is more of a collective consciousness rather than merely a place to see what friends are doing. However, this book presents another perspective. It more accurately describes what Twitter is and best captures its essence. This idea did not come from Jack or Evan but from Twitter's first leader, Noah Glass. Twitter is not just a personal diary or a news wire; in most cases, it is a strange and wonderful place constructed by users. “It can be anything you need it to be,” Noah once predicted.
