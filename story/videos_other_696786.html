More than ten days ago, a three-year-old girl named Sisi, who was attending the small class at Jiapai Kindergarten in Jiapai Village, Yinglin Town, Qingdao, fell in the kindergarten kitchen and sat in a bucket full of boiling water. Thirty-eight percent of her skin was scalded all over her body. Heaven spared her beautiful face, and she looked at her scars with clear big eyes and asked her mother: "Why did this happen?" Fell in the Kitchen and Sat in the Bucket of Boiling Water "Grandma, I'm dying from the burn" Last September, Sisi (a pseudonym) enrolled at Jiapai Kindergarten in Yinglin Town, Qingdao for the small class. "Her grandma is the nursery worker at the kindergarten. The kindergarten is also close to Grandma's house. We both have jobs, and Grandma has to take care of another grandchild. We thought it would be better if Grandma could look after her, so we let her enroll in that kindergarten," said Ms. Yao, Sisi's mother. Ms. Yao said that every morning at 7:30, Sisi would go to the kindergarten with her grandma, which was 15 minutes earlier than other children. This was exactly when the aunts were preparing hot water and hot soy milk. On the morning of December 22 last year, after arriving at the kindergarten, Grandma took Sisi's schoolbag into the classroom and then went about her business. Not long after, Sisi entered the kitchen alone. Due to the slippery floor in the kitchen, Sisi accidentally slipped and sat down on a bucket full of boiling water. "Grandma, I'm dying from the burn," Sisi cried out. Seeing the danger, Grandma quickly grabbed Sisi by her hair and pulled her out of the hot water. "It's lucky that the water in the bucket wasn't freshly boiled; otherwise, Sisi might not have survived," said Ms. Yao. After the incident, Jiapai Kindergarten arranged for a nearby car to send Sisi to a hospital in Jinjing, Jinjiang for treatment accompanied by the kindergarten teacher. However, due to the severity of her injuries, the doctor suggested transferring her to the No. 180 Hospital for treatment promptly. Unable to Take Painkillers Enduring Severe Pain Every Day "I don't want to take a bath" As of yesterday, Sisi had been hospitalized for 16 days at the No. 180 Hospital. The burn unit ward was warm. There was an incubator on the sickbed, and three bright white incandescent lamps continuously dried Sisi’s body to help her wounds dry faster. Sisi lay on the bed, showing her pretty little face. "She only has this face left," Sisi's grandmother said sorrowfully. Diagnosed with burns covering 38% of her body, lifting the blanket covering the incubator revealed the heart-wrenching sight of Sisi's injured body. Below her shoulders were large areas of burnt skin; her back, buttocks, and thighs were almost entirely devoid of intact skin, and her calves and arms were also scorched to varying degrees. Because she had fallen into the boiling water, the most severely injured areas were her buttocks and perineum, which had become infected. Her left arm and armpit were also burned, and to prevent the skin from adhering when her arm was bent, her mother had to tie her left hand to the bed with gauze. The wound on her right elbow was relatively lighter and had healed, growing new skin. When Sisi was admitted to the hospital, she was almost in shock. By the third day, Sisi began to feel pain, but because of her young age, she couldn’t take painkillers and had to endure it silently, clenching her hands and shivering all over. Due to continuous intravenous infusions, her tiny hands had been punctured countless times until her veins could no longer bear it, forcing a pause. However, the most painful part was taking baths and changing dressings. A few days ago, the nurse washed Sisi with warm water to remove the scabs that had formed on her body. "It was like peeling off a layer of skin; the child cried until her face turned black," Ms. Yao said, unable to bear witnessing such scenes. These painful experiences made Sisi particularly sensitive. Whenever a doctor or nurse entered the room, she would anxiously ask her mother, "What are they here for?" Upon hearing that Ms. Yao planned to bathe her again the next day, Sisi fearfully pleaded, "Don't give me a bath." Most of the time, however, Sisi lay quietly. Occasionally, when the pain became unbearable, she would silently shed a few tears. "The doctor said that even going through this doesn't necessarily mean the critical period is over; there have been cases where other children developed acute diseases more than 20 days after being scalded," Ms. Yao worriedly said. Parents: The Kindergarten Cannot Escape Responsibility Initially upon admission, Sisi was lying on a "burn suspension bed," which can double-bake the patient's injured skin. However, Sisi's parents couldn't afford the nearly 3,000 yuan daily cost, so they switched to a regular bed, placing Sisi on a wooden mesh bed set up separately. This reduced the daily medical expenses to around 1,500 yuan but increased Sisi's suffering. After roasting the front skin for two hours, they needed to turn her over and continue roasting the back skin for three hours, repeating this process. Each time they turned her over, Sisi endured excruciating pain, and some wounds that had already formed scabs might crack open and bleed again. Sisi's parents have already spent about 30,000 yuan on medical expenses. In the future, Sisi will need to undergo skin grafting and plastic surgery, as well as rehabilitation to prevent muscle atrophy, all of which will incur substantial costs. "We both work, and we're really at our wit's end," Ms. Yao said. "The accident happened in the school's kitchen, indicating a flaw in the kindergarten's safety management," said Mr. Hong, Sisi's father. He stated that the kindergarten bore undeniable responsibility. Mr. Hong added that after the incident, instead of calling 120, the kindergarten used a small vehicle to directly send Sisi to the hospital. After the first hospital recommended transferring her, she was then taken to a hospital in Quanzhou city, a process that took three hours. If there had been professional doctors or nurses present during this time, it might have created better conditions for Sisi's recovery. Ms. Yao mentioned that so far, the kindergarten principal had sent 20,000 yuan. "They said if we need medical fees, we should photocopy the receipts and send them to the kindergarten, which would then forward them to the insurance company," Ms. Yao felt that the kindergarten's attitude was more like that of an intermediary. Ms. Yao believed that although they had purchased accidental injury insurance when their child entered the kindergarten, the kindergarten could not escape responsibility because of it. Kindergarten: Hopes Both Parties Can Negotiate a Solution Around 5:30 PM yesterday, reporters visited Jiapai Kindergarten. Principal Feng of the kindergarten said, "The kindergarten has already advanced 20,000 yuan through the insurance company as Sisi's medical fee. We will further cooperate with the parents and relevant departments to handle the situation and hope to help Sisi regain her health." Principal Feng said that such an occurrence is something everyone regrets very much. The incident happened around 7:30 AM on the day, and he was not present at the scene. Teachers usually sign in for work at 7:50. "We will further coordinate with the insurance company, cooperate with various tasks, and help Sisi recover her health as soon as possible," Principal Feng indicated. Regarding the incident happening at the kindergarten, the school staff and teachers were all very concerned about Sisi and understood the parents' feelings. They hoped both parties could negotiate a solution together. Education Department: Whoever Is Responsible Must Bear It "Whoever is responsible must bear the responsibility." Yesterday, regarding this matter, a relevant official from the Early Childhood Education Section of the Jinjiang Education Bureau said that the education department had not received any reports from the school or the parents. Regarding this matter, they would conduct an investigation. Such an occurrence is regrettable, and they hope the student recovers soon. Secondly, whoever is responsible must bear the responsibility. They hope the parents and the school can negotiate well, jointly solve the problem, and help the child recover health. If it is caused by improper school safety management, etc., the school or relevant personnel's responsibilities must be pursued.
