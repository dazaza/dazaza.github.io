The first to recover from the astonishment was the doctor. He rang the bell to let the servants know to bring out the hot dishes while the visitors didn't like them to stand beside the dining table. At this time, the editor mumbled and picked up the knife and fork, and the person who had been as mute as a cicada in winter also picked them up.

Dinner continued. The conversation at the table turned into shouting for a while, with exclamations popping up now and then. At this point, the editor could no longer contain his curiosity: Is our friend resorting to petty tricks to supplement his meager income? Or is he imitating Nebuchadnezzar II?" he asked. "I'm sure it has something to do with the time machine," I replied, picking up the topic of our last meeting that the psychologist had narrated.

The new guests at the tea party apparently didn't trust what they heard; the editor expressed opposition: "What on earth is this time travel? A person can't wallow in absurdities all their life, can they?"

As he spoke, he seemed to remember something, and began to mock sarcastically - isn't it true that people from the future don't even have clothes brushes?" The reporter also refused to believe it, standing by the editor's side and mocking the entire event. Both were lively but somewhat impolite young men, typical modern news workers.

Our special correspondent for "The Day After Tomorrow" newspaper reported that just as the reporter was speaking—rather shouting—the Time Traveller returned. He was wearing an ordinary evening suit, and except for still appearing slightly flustered, there was no trace left of the shocking figure we had seen earlier.

"I say," the editor said gleefully, "these fellows claim you just went off to next week! Tell us about little Rosberrys, will you? How do you think his luck's holding up?"

The Time Traveller silently walked to his reserved seat and smiled peacefully as usual. "Where's my lamb chop?" he said. "It's a real pleasure to get some meat on these forks again!"
