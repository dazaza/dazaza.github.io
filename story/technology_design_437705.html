The inn of the desolate village: a peasant woman spent 3 years and more than 800,000 stitches to embroider a huge cross-stitch.

by letaoeoe on 2011-05-26 23:47:34

Reporters found that the biggest difference between this cross-stitch work and ordinary cross-stitch works lay not only in its large area, but also in its beautiful craftsmanship - it looks the same on the reverse side as on the front, with lifelike human images, and not even a single knot can be found. The foundation is as beautiful as the front.

This is an 18-lady "Instrument, Chess, Calligraphy and Painting" embroidery painting, measuring 3.6 meters long and 1.5 meters wide. On the canvas, there are eighteen elegant beauties with delicate skin. Some of them look up in deep thought while others smile gently, their long sleeves fluttering gracefully, each presenting different postures, seemingly ready to bring viewers back to ancient times.

ECNS, Zhengzhou, April 15th (reporter Zhi Ping Li) Recently, in the living area of Nanyang Normal University, a giant cross-stitch embroidered by Zhao Yun with over 800,000 stitches has become a rare item in the eyes of residents. Everyone is vying to watch and admire it.

"Embroidering this painting used more than 100 kinds of colored threads and over 800,000 stitches. If there's just one stitch wrong, a large part has to be torn off and re-embroidered," Zhao Yun told reporters. She said she is a farmer, embroidering things to supplement her family income. She had previously woven carpets and had some knitting skills, so embroidering this piece was relatively easy for her, though it did take a long time - nearly three years in total.

It is understood that Zhao Yun lives with her daughter who is studying at the normal university. In daily life, apart from taking care of her daughter's daily needs, she is busy with embroidery. To start work as soon as possible, she almost never stops working, often unconsciously embroidering until late into the night. Although it is tiring, she feels happy when the work is completed. Zhao Yun said she hopes this giant embroidery will sell for a good price, allowing her to cover her daughter's school expenses. Next, she plans to embroider a "Qingming Scroll" measuring 6.2 meters long and 1.5 meters wide. (Editor: SN034)
