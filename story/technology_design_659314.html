Mitsubishi Heavy Industries (MHI)

Mitsubishi Heavy Industries, the largest military production enterprise in Japan. In 2003, it received defense orders worth 280 billion yen from the Defense Agency, ranking first among all defense companies. Equipment produced by MHI, such as F-2 and F-15J fighter jets, and Type 90 tanks, play a core role in the Air Self-Defense Force and Ground Self-Defense Force. In the Maritime Self-Defense Force, MHI has built nearly half of the submarines and one-third of the destroyers. Its position in Japan's defense industry is evident.

Historical Background and Overview

The predecessor of Mitsubishi Heavy Industries can be traced back to the Meiji Restoration period. In 1884, Iwasaki Yanosuke, the founder of Mitsubishi, leased the Nagasaki Shipbuilding Bureau from the Ministry of Engineering and renamed it Nagasaki Shipyard. It later developed into Mitsubishi Shipbuilding Co., Ltd. By 1934, due to the company's business expansion into heavy machinery, aircraft, railway vehicles, etc., it was renamed Mitsubishi Heavy Industries, Ltd.

After the end of World War II, due to the policy of the U.S. occupation authorities to dismantle zaibatsu, Mitsubishi Heavy Industries was split in 1950 into three companies: West Japan Heavy Industries, Ltd., Central Japan Heavy Industries, Ltd., and East Japan Heavy Industries, Ltd. However, with changes in U.S. policy and Japanese domestic politics, the three companies merged in 1964 to re-establish Mitsubishi Heavy Industries, Ltd. During this period, Mitsubishi Heavy Industries' automobile department became independent in 1970 as "Mitsubishi Motors Corporation."

Currently, Mitsubishi Heavy Industries covers mechanical, shipbuilding, aerospace, nuclear energy, electric power, transportation, and other fields. As of April 1, 2004, its registered capital was 265.6 billion yen, with 34,306 employees. It owns nine overseas offices or representative offices, nine branches, six research institutes, and nine business units. The annual order amount is 2,159.2 billion yen (April 1, 2003 – March 31, 2004), and the annual sales are 1,940.1 billion yen (same statistical period). Among these, the proportion of sales by department to total sales is: Ship & Ocean Department 8%, Nuclear Energy Department 23%, Machinery & Steel Structure Department 20%, Aerospace Department 17%, Vehicle, Machine Tool, etc., Industrial Department 29%, Other Industries 3%.

Mitsubishi Heavy Industries is a member of the Mitsubishi conglomerate. Today's Japanese conglomerates appear in the form known as keiretsu. Mitsubishi series companies are all members of the Mitsubishi Group organization "Kin'yōkai" (Friday Club), including:

Asahi Glass (glass, chemical company), Kirin Beer, Nippon Oil, Tokyo Marine & Fire Insurance Company, Bank of Tokyo-Mitsubishi, Nikon Camera, NYK Line, P.S. Mitsubishi (construction industry), Mitsubishi Aluminum, Mitsubishi Chemical, Mitsubishi Chemical Machinery, Mitsubishi Gas Chemical, Mitsubishi Estate (real estate), Mitsubishi Motors, Mitsubishi Plastics, Mitsubishi Corporation, Mitsubishi Trust Bank, Mitsubishi Shindo Copper, Mitsubishi Steel Manufacturing, Mitsubishi Paper Mills, Mitsubishi Warehouse, Mitsubishi Research Institute, Mitsubishi Electric, Mitsubishi Cable Industries, Mitsubishi Fuso Truck and Bus Corporation, Mitsubishi Materials, Mitsubishi Rayon, Meiji Yasuda Life Insurance.

Companies within the same series can collaborate closely. Each company provides good products or lower prices to their sister companies in their respective responsible fields. For example, NYK Line is a major customer for Mitsubishi Heavy Industries' shipbuilding department, while Mitsubishi Electric provides electromechanical products to Mitsubishi Heavy Industries. Mitsubishi Steel, Mitsubishi Shindo Copper, Mitsubishi Aluminum, and Mitsubishi Materials provide reliable raw materials for Mitsubishi's shipbuilding and aerospace departments. Mitsubishi Bank and Tokyo Marine provide loan and special insurance services (such as space launch insurance). Nikon's optical products and Mitsubishi Plastics' new materials are indispensable components for high-tech weapons.

The Mitsubishi Group also has some affiliated companies and social organizations as peripherals, including:

"IP Talk" Group (phone card business), Atami Yowa Cave (Iwasaki Komataro Memorial Museum), MT Insurance Service Co., Ltd., Kanto Pavilion (a hotel renovated from the Iwasaki family home), Iwakura Livestock and Agriculture, Shonan Suburban Club, Seikado Bunko Art Museum, Sontoku Group (mainly property management company facing Bank of Tokyo-Mitsubishi), Dainippon Toryo, Dia Geriatric Society Research Foundation, Diamond Family Club (a matchmaking agency jointly funded by the 29 companies of Kin'yokai, mainly serving the group internally), Chitose Kogyo, Toyo Bunko, Marunouchi Yorozu (a company engaged in real estate brokerage and advertising agency), Mitsubishi Kin'yokai (a friendship organization for the presidents and general managers of 29 Mitsubishi-related companies), Mitsubishi Club, Mitsubishi Economic Research Institute, Mitsubishi Public Relations Committee, Mitsubishi Foundation, Mitsubishi CC Research Association (IT industry research institution), Mitsubishi Corporate Name Trademark Committee, Mitsubishi Market Research Association, Mitsubishi Yangwa Association (a club mainly focusing on sports and health), LEOC Japan (a company engaged in medical and company cafeteria management).

It can be seen that through collaboration and division of labor within the large group, Mitsubishi Heavy Industries can obtain various supports such as funds, technology, raw materials, upstream products, financial services, and sales from sister companies and groups. This is very beneficial for the development of the enterprise.

The following is an introduction to the relevant military departments of Mitsubishi Heavy Industries:

Ship & Ocean Headquarters

The shipbuilding industry is the earliest department where Mitsubishi Heavy Industries started. Currently, among the four subordinate divisions, three shipyards in Nagasaki, Kobe, and Shimonoseki build military products.

Nagasaki Shipyard: The Nagasaki Shipyard once built the battleship "Musashi" during World War II and is currently the main producer of destroyers for the Self-Defense Forces. It has successively built multiple types of ships, including the Tachikaze-class, Harukaze-class, Haze-class, Asagiri-class, Kongo-class, and Takaba-class destroyers. The latest fourth ship of the Takaba-class destroyer, the "Sazanami" (さざなみ, sazanami), was commissioned at the Nagasaki Shipyard in February 2005.

It oversees the headquarters, Koshiki Works, Sachimachi Works, and Kawara Works. The total land area is 2,420,127 square meters (all data below are from 2004), the building area is 929,976 square meters, and the number of employees is 6,299. This shipyard is the main construction site for surface vessels of the Maritime Self-Defense Force. As of April 2004, it has built 105 ships with a total displacement of 522,000 tons and repaired 266 ships with a total displacement of 858,000 tons.

The headquarters currently owns a dock with a capacity of 225,000 tons, a slipway with a capacity of 80,000 tons, and repair docks with capacities of 300,000 tons and 95,000 tons respectively. It also has quayside walls spanning five sections totaling 1,120 meters in length with water depths ranging from 6 to 10 meters.

Koshiki Works is a shipbuilding factory completed in 1972, featuring a 1 million-ton class dry dock measuring 990 meters long, 100 meters wide, and 9.55 to 14.5 meters deep, as well as a 500,000-ton class repair dock. It also has quayside walls spanning three sections totaling 1,053 meters in length with a water depth of 9.5 meters.

Sachimachi Works is a production factory for ship engines, generators, rudders, compressors, and other equipment.

Kawara Works is the final assembly plant for military products and also manufactures attitude control systems, orbit control systems, and solar panels used in rockets and satellites.

According to the data provided by Mitsubishi Heavy Industries, the annual production capacity of the Nagasaki Shipyard is: 1.9 million gross tons of new ships, 5.5 million gross tons of repairs. On land, the steam turbines and propulsion systems for ships amount to 4 million kilowatts and 100 sets respectively. In 2004, the production value of this factory was 379.6 billion yen, among which ship and steel structure products accounted for 39%, marine equipment for 9%, land-based boilers and steam turbines for 42%, aerospace and other products for 10%.

Kobe Shipyard: The Kobe Shipyard produced submarines before World War II and became a professional submarine manufacturer for the Self-Defense Forces after the war. All submarines of the Maritime Self-Defense Force are built at this shipyard and Kawasaki Heavy Industries' Kobe Shipyard. The Kobe Shipyard has successively built the Harushio-class, Yushio-class, and Oyashio-class submarines, with the latest Oyashio-class submarine "Takayuki" being commissioned at this shipyard in March 2005.

In fact, the main products of this factory are concentrated in the nuclear energy and power generation fields, including pressurized water reactors and nuclear power generating units. To date, it has completed the construction of 23 nuclear power units with a total power output of 20 million kilowatts, forming a complete design and production capability for nuclear power and nuclear power units. It is noteworthy that one factory is responsible for both submarine and reactor construction.

The construction department of this shipyard includes the headquarters and Futami Works. The headquarters is responsible for ship repair and construction work, while Futami Works is responsible for casting, large-scale machinery assembly, and the production of nuclear power-related products. The two factories currently have a total land area of 1.17 million square meters, a building area of 452,300 square meters, and 4,607 employees.

Shimonoseki Shipyard: Located at the western end of Honshu in Shimonoseki, it is one of the production sites for small vessels of the Self-Defense Forces. The first and second ships of the Maritime Self-Defense Force's latest Hayabusa-class missile boats were produced here. Currently, the factory has a slipway with a capacity of 33,000 tons, a dedicated slipway for light alloy hulls with a capacity of 600 tons. There are docks with capacities of 40,000 tons, 17,000 tons, 4,000 tons, and 1,000 tons each.

Yokohama Plant: This factory mainly produces diesel engines, steam turbines, and auxiliary ship machinery and also undertakes ship repair business. Its Hommoku Plant has two repair docks.

General Machinery Special Vehicles Headquarters

"General Machinery" refers to various civilian machines, and "Special Vehicles" is the abbreviation of "special vehicles" in Japanese, referring to military or police vehicles. This division produces the main armored combat vehicles of the Ground Self-Defense Force, including the Type 90 tank, Type 89 infantry fighting vehicle, Type 87 self-propelled anti-aircraft gun, Type 90 tank recovery vehicle, Type 91 bridge-laying tank, etc. It also produces non-magnetic engines for minesweepers of the Maritime Self-Defense Force, model 6NMU.

Sagamihara Plant: This plant belongs to the General Machinery Special Vehicles Headquarters and is located in Sagamihara City, Kanagawa Prefecture, near Tokyo. It is also the production site for military vehicles under the "Special Vehicles Division." Besides military vehicles, this plant also produces construction engineering machinery, various generators, and engines for civilian products.

In recent years, the General Machinery Special Vehicles Division has established a new production base in Chitose, Hokkaido.

Aerospace Headquarters

The Aerospace Headquarters oversees the Aerospace Division, Military Aircraft Division (Japanese name "Defense Aviation Division"), Guidance Weapons Division (Japanese name "Induction Machines Division"), Civil Aircraft Division (Japanese name "Civil Aviation Division"), and Space Division (Japanese name "Space Machines Division").

Mitsubishi Heavy Industries' aviation department has a long history, having manufactured Japan's earliest domestically produced naval fighter, the Type 10 Carrier Fighter, in the 1920s. Notable products from before and during World War II include the Type 89 carrier attack bomber, Type 96 land attack bomber, Zero fighter, and Type 1 land attack bomber. After the war, in the 1950s, the department reverse-engineered the F-86F fighter, in the 1960s the F-104J fighter, and entered the large-scale military production stage in the 1970s. Currently, the main products of this division are as follows:

Aircraft products (including licensed production and assembly): Including F-2, F-15J, F-1, F-4EJ fighter jets, T-2 trainer aircraft, SH-60J, UH-60J, HSS-2B, MH-53E helicopters, YS-11, MU-300 civil aircraft, various aviation engines, and subcontracted production of parts for Boeing 777, Boeing 747, Airbus A-340 aircraft.

Space products: H-IIA rocket, Japanese part of the International Space Station. It is also developing larger thrust cryogenic rocket engines and other spacecraft such as space shuttles.

Other weapon systems: "Patriot" missiles, ASM-2 air-to-ship missiles, SSM-1 ship-to-ship missiles, AAM-3 air-to-air missiles, Type 97 torpedoes.

Nagoya Aerospace Systems Plant: This plant is Mitsubishi Heavy Industries' fighter and helicopter production facility. Its products cover all aircraft and rockets mentioned earlier produced by Mitsubishi Heavy Industries, making it the primary producer of fighters and helicopters for the Air Self-Defense Force and Maritime Self-Defense Force. This plant consists of three factories: the Oe Factory is responsible for R&D, management, and partial component manufacturing; the Hibishima Factory handles the final assembly of space products and partial subassembly of aviation products; the Komaki South Factory conducts the final assembly and test flights of aviation products.

This plant is also responsible for the testing and production of Japan's maglev train technology. The maglev trains currently in use on Japan's Yamanashi maglev test line were co-developed with Central Japan Railway Company.

Nagoya Guidance and Propulsion Systems Plant: This plant produces various types of aviation engines, rocket engines, guided weapons, and guidance heads. It comprises the headquarters factory and Tarai Test Site. In the fiscal year 2003–2004, the plant's production value was 139.6 billion yen, of which 50% came from missile products, 34% from aviation and rocket engines, and 16% from other guidance equipment. Its products cover all types of missiles and guided weapons mentioned earlier produced by Mitsubishi Heavy Industries, as well as the engines used in the H-2A rocket.

Currently, this company is the primary producer of "Patriot"-type missiles for the Self-Defense Forces and will soon begin producing the "Patriot" III missile. If Japan participates in the ballistic missile defense system, this plant will be the main contractor for sea-based and land-based interceptors.

Other Production Facilities

Hiroshima Plant: This plant's product range covers various mechanical products. Notably, this plant is responsible for constructing Japan's uranium enrichment plant in Rokkasho, Aomori Prefecture. The plant uses centrifugal methods to produce enriched uranium with a designed capacity of 1.5 million SWU (Separative Work Units), making it the world's largest nuclear waste processing base, nominally for safe handling of nuclear waste.

Takasago Plant: This plant primarily produces various power installations, with an annual production capacity of: thermal and nuclear power generation units of 4 million kilowatts, gas turbines of 7.2 million kilowatts, and hydroelectric units of 2 million kilowatts. The plant covers an area of 980,500 square meters, with a building area of 223,206 square meters and 3,380 employees.

Research and Development System and Institutions

Mitsubishi Heavy Industries' R&D institution is the Technical Headquarters.

The Technical Headquarters oversees various research institutes, technical training departments, technical planning departments, and intellectual property departments.

The main R&D institutions of the Technical Headquarters are the various research institutes, which include:

Advanced Technology Research Center: Located in Yokohama, it mainly focuses on cutting-edge technologies in power, environment, transportation, defense, and industry, such as solar cells, thin-film technology, new elements, laser technology, and various sensor technologies.

Nagasaki Research Institute: Mainly focused on fluid dynamics, materials, processes, acoustics, guidance, and other technologies related to the shipbuilding industry. This institute owns the world's largest experimental pool complex belonging to a private enterprise, including propulsion performance pools, seakeeping pools, shallow-water pools, water tunnels, and ice seawater pools. This institute can be considered one of the most important scientific research bases for Japan's shipbuilding industry.

Takasago Research Institute: Located in Takasago City, Hyōgo Prefecture, it mainly engages in research on energy, transportation, and power, including gas turbines, nuclear power, highway information systems, air conditioning or heating equipment, robots, power unit management, etc.

Hiroshima Research Institute: Focuses on advanced technologies, including new materials, solid polymer batteries, fuel cells, X-ray cameras, high-purity hydrogen production equipment, etc. This institute also conducts research on steel, chemicals, construction, transportation, printing, and wind power generation technologies.

Yokohama Research Institute: Mainly focuses on environmental protection-related technologies, including waste treatment, sewage treatment, etc., and also conducts research in engine fields such as diesel engines and steam turbines.

Nagoya Research Institute: This institute specializes in providing industrial design solutions for various fields, including machinery, ships, and construction. It also conducts research in fluid dynamics, acoustics, electronic guidance, polymer chemistry, new materials, and new processes.

In summary, after more than a hundred years of development, Mitsubishi Heavy Industries has a complete R&D, manufacturing, and sales system within the enterprise, forming a good division of labor and cooperation relationship with other brother enterprises in the Mitsubishi Group. Its current scale of military production only reflects the state of "maintenance," and compared to its true potential for military production behind it, it is just the tip of the iceberg.
