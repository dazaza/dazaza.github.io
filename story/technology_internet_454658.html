Sculpture is one of the oldest art forms. At least from a physiological age perspective, humans have begun to create with their own hands for survival. Labor is the most primitive form of social action; it not only fosters human culture but more importantly, creates a colorful world. In tandem with societal, political, economic, cultural, and lifestyle developments, the decorative language of sculpture has also evolved. Decorative sculpture has a relatively broad range and variety of forms. The choice of its language has a certain degree of randomness, which in turn shapes its own rules and aesthetic forms through both language and technique. It does not merely imitate the surface of nature but creates based on internal rules, combining beauty with psychological and emotional factors to produce richer subjective images. Refinement, summarization, exaggeration, and deformation are the preferred language forms and creative methods of decorative sculpture. Here, we make a brief discussion about the sculptural language and decorative patterns.

In summary, decorative sculpture selects the most attractive elements from an aesthetic standpoint, simplifying natural compositions into clear and simple images that convey some essential characteristics of the language, shaping form, style, aesthetics, and the most fundamental aspects of decorative sculpture. Many techniques and forms of visual art emphasize this as one of the most important vocabularies of decorative art. Focused on decorative sculpture, it seeks grandeur, simplicity, rhythm, and artistic atmosphere through changes in form. Thus, general practice includes one of the functions of decorative sculpture's language. Artists, making their work more beautiful and expressive, naturally distill complex and detailed images into simpler, more characteristic forms—reducing complexity while emphasizing the most essential features clearly. This makes the language more vivid and expresses the artist’s emotions and desires more explicitly, allowing the work to show strong rhythm through simple forms and effectively convey expression to the audience. For example, sculptor Brancusi's works or those by our artists like Liu Baodong and Infinite Power exemplify the importance of common practices in projects.

In modern society, the rapid development of science and technology, materialism, and the distortion of humanity make people feel fatigued by the hurried pace of life. People yearn for clarity and simplicity. Therefore, exaggeration is a powerful tool for artists to amplify the expressiveness of their work, making it more infectious. Exaggeration is not arbitrary but stems from the subjective feelings and emotional needs concentrated in the creation of an artistic image. It is a bold and romantic imagination that demonstrates creative talent and wisdom, all appearing natural and dignified, vividly reflecting both spirit and excellence. As such, exaggeration is not only an effort to enhance the central decorative beauty of sculpture but also a historically significant vocabulary in decorative sculpture. During the Paleolithic era, statues of fertility goddesses (such as the Venus of Willendorf) exaggerated breasts, hips, and abdomens related to fertility while diminishing other features, allowing us to imagine the goddess's strong reproductive ability. Contemporary sculptures also widely use exaggeration to highlight feminine characteristics before the audience, creating a sense of mystery. From primitive times to the present, exaggeration has always been one of the most important languages in sculpture, especially prominent in Eastern art and folk art.
