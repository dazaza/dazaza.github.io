IE6, 7, 8

by fearless on 2009-03-23 03:50:56

Yesterday, I showed my new work to Naitou Wei. First, it was to boast a bit and prove that I still have the skills, able to accomplish tasks with ease. Second, I hoped he could help test if there were any errors. As expected, he said the page display was misaligned.

Clearly, Naitou Wei uses IE6. It's well known that IE6 cannot properly support W3C standards. A friend named Zhang Wei once wrote on his blog: "Save developers, stop using IE6." Many overseas personal websites also judge the user's browser at the start of loading the site. If the user is using IE6, access will be restricted.

Many friends who are good at accepting new things always feel confused: IE8 is already out, so why are there still so few people using IE7 in China? In fact, this so-called strange phenomenon is easy to explain.

Example one: System recommendation

I once applied for domain hosting services for Cacake. I opened accounts for everyone using the initials of their names, with a maximum of three characters. But when I told everyone the accounts, I got a headache because they were unwilling to accept "short and easy-to-remember" accounts. Some even requested formats like "lala0214". When asked why, she replied that it was what the system recommended before... This is indeed a big joke, but what can you do? Either force usage or cater to the user's demands. I am usually restless and stubborn, so I say "use it or not, it's up to you."

Example two: New doesn't mean better

The current version of Photoshop is CS4, yet many friends still use version 7.0. When asked why they don't upgrade to the new version, they answer that it's not user-friendly and seriously say, "Not necessarily newer is better." It's hard to imagine how Daobidage's R&D team would feel knowing that after spending so much manpower and resources developing the new version, they receive such evaluations. And those who refuse to try new things, I regretfully think you are fools because you lack the ability to adapt to changes and instead only blame others.

Example three: IE7 keeps having issues

Back to the topic of IE browsers, actually many users have upgraded from IE6 to IE7, but soon they uninstalled IE7 because it couldn't support websites that didn't meet W3C standards, including major domestic portals and other types of sites. When you ask others why they don't use IE7, the answer you get is predictable: "IE7 is bad, it often has errors when accessing websites," and many sites display abnormally. Poor IE7 can only lament - does being violated by non-standard sites mean I'm promiscuous?

Through these matters, everyone can understand the power of user habits. Should we compromise with bad user habits that cannot adapt to development? The answer is absolutely not, and I have always held this view. Of course, user guidance work must be done, but I firmly oppose blindly believing in the principle of "user is king." Always treating users like babies will only result in them becoming mentally disabled when they grow up. When setting up user accounts for Cacake, I carefully considered for them. When PS upgrades its versions, it is always making correct arrangements for users, and Microsoft IE is the same. But what results do we get? A sea of opposition and refusal. For such stupid phenomena, I think only brutal methods can solve the problem effectively. After all, human nature cannot completely exclude the gene of self-debasement, and the same applies in business dealings with customers. That's why the book "Brainwashing" became popular. It's not about belittling customers, but providing correct, better, and reliable services.

IE8 has been officially put into use, and when accessing domestic sites, there will be more errors, or even no display at all. Are designers and engineers still troubled by how to guide users? I think there's no need for that. Directly blocking IE6 treats both the symptoms and the root cause.
