Historical Past Regarding Football Use Following Trend Craze

In recent times, folks are becoming a lot more adventurous in how they outfit themselves, thus incorporating sports garments such as football attire items. It is intriguing to trace the origins of football clothing and how they have evolved over the last decades.

Men's Clothing

The initial football clothing for men was composed of knitwear or jumper vests, tops, and long cotton trousers. In the 1930s, male football players changed their football clothing by eliminating cotton trousers and opting only for shorts. English football player Bunny Austin holds the distinction of being the first leading football player to wear white shorts in a football match. Since then, the standard trend for men's clothing included white shorts and trousers. However, these were replaced by brightly colored lycra shorts. Several football players preferred glowing blue denim shorts or cut-off shorts instead of the longer lycra shorts. Fast forward to 2005, the time when men's football clothing began to keep pace with the boldness of modern football attire for women. In many football tournaments, you can notice male participants sporting Capri pants and tight-fitting lycra clothing that expose their muscular legs and abdominals. Modern football clothing for men also saw changes regarding color. A great deal of men's clothing is now available in traditionally feminine colors such as hot pink.

Women's Clothing

The evolution of football clothing for women is quite similar to that of men. Women began to join football in the 1860s. At that time, female football players wore heavy garments made from materials such as serge or cotton. Early football clothing for women included corsets with high-collared blouses and ties, long bustle dresses (sometimes with fur), and skimmer hats. However, the fashion trend changed when Maud Watson won the Wimbledon Ladies' Lawn Tennis Championship in 1884. Watson, the first female Wimbledon champion, wore a white, long, bustled two-piece outfit during that competition, popularizing the white football clothing. Women noticed that white was the best color for football clothing as it did not show as much sweat staining as other colors. This started the so-called "football whites" which dominated the football landscape for quite some time. Bustles, corsets, and hats ceased to be common fashion items in football between 1903 and 1914. After World War I, it was French tennis star Suzanne Lenglen who set the football fashion trend. Lenglen wore an adventurous calf-length, short-sleeved cotton dress with white nylons beneath her top. The French tennis star complemented her sportswear with multi-colored cotton chiffon, a headpiece, and sparkly white nylons. Her 'revolutionary' football clothing caused a stir on the football court since fans were used to the heavy, constricted, and well-toned down clothing of female football players.

In the 1920s, renowned American tennis player Sue Wills Moody became a trendsetter in women's football clothing due to her school-type sleeveless white shirt and pleated skirt with stylish sunglasses. She also added a lambswool cardigan to her outfit on cool nights. The post-World War II period saw female football players wearing clothing that made playing football easier and safer. The clothing included short-sleeved tops, shorts or skirts, and caps. In 1949, Gertrude Moran stunned football enthusiasts when she wore lace-trimmed underwear beneath her white football outfit. This started the striking fashion trend for football clothing that continues even today.
