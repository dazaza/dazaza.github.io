Twitter's patent shortfall boosts investment risks

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 11:49:12

Introduction: Bloomberg published an article on Friday titled "Twitter's Lack of Patents Seen as a Risk to Investors," which commented that due to granting most patent ownerships to employees, Twitter has a rather limited patent portfolio, potentially increasing its investment risks.

The following is the full text of the article:

Twitter lacks not only profits but also patents.

This soon-to-be-listed microblogging service disclosed in this week's prospectus that it currently owns only nine formally effective U.S. patents. In comparison, Facebook had 774 patents just before its IPO in May 2012, and IBM alone received 6,478 patents last year. The reason for Twitter’s small patent portfolio stems from their unique development philosophy: allowing engineers and designers to own their inventions.

Although Twitter's policy aims to limit patent litigation, some investors and analysts are concerned that it could backfire. There is evidence showing that intellectual property helps companies raise more funds during the listing process because patents can help investors quantify the value of technological breakthroughs. These safeguards are crucial for potential Twitter investors, given the company's expanding losses and lack of other convincing financial indicators.

"The lack of a large number of patents is worrying," said Maulin Shah, Managing Director of Envision, an American investment and research company. "If Twitter really encounters an infringement lawsuit, they won't have many patents available for counter-suits. This would put Twitter at a disadvantage."

Unique Agreement

Twitter stated in the prospectus that many competitors have substantial patent portfolios, making the company a possible target for lawsuits.

At the same time, Twitter also indicated that excessive patents might hinder innovation. In May this year, the company implemented the "Innovator's Patent Agreement" (IPA), which keeps the ownership of inventions in the hands of the real inventors. As part of this policy, Twitter cannot initiate offensive litigation without the inventor's permission. According to information on Twitter's website, the IPA ensures that these patents can only be used as shields, not as weapons.

Partner Robert Clarkson of the law firm Jones Day said that the IPA will help the company attract and retain more engineering talent. According to PwC data, the total number of patent lawsuits last year exceeded 5,000, setting a historical record.

"Twitter has taken a different approach through this agreement," Clarkson said. "This helps them attract top-tier talents."

Investment Risks

Jeff Sica, President and Chief Investment Officer of Sica Wealth Management, an American investment company, said the biggest risk of this policy lies in the fact that since patents belong to employees, if they leave, it could pose a threat to the company.

"This is a radical way of handling patents, completely different from what investors have encountered before," Sica said. "People may appreciate this open and welcoming attitude towards competition."

Sica is considering purchasing Twitter shares and stated that the company has developed a sufficiently strong brand to resist competition.

A Twitter spokesperson declined to comment. According to informed sources, the company plans to raise over $1 billion through its IPO and is likely to start roadshows in the last week of October. In August, Twitter stated that the fair value of its common stock was $20.62 per share.

Active Users

Analyst Robert Peck of SunTrust Robinson Humphrey believes that with over 230 million monthly active users attracting advertisers, Twitter's stock price could reach $50 by 2014. User scale is the key means by which Twitter competes against rivals.

"Investors are worried that Twitter isn’t actively protecting its business," Peck said. "I think there is indeed a commercial risk, but at this stage, network effects protect Twitter."

A survey of 234 U.S. software companies showed that increasing the number of patents before going public helps increase financing amounts. A paper published last year by Diego Useche, a researcher at the University of Bordeaux, showed that obtaining one additional patent before going public could increase financing by about 0.9%.

"The more intellectual property protection there is, the fewer opportunities for infringement, thus increasing valuation," said Peter Adriaens, professor at the University of Michigan's Stephen M. Ross School of Business.

Patent Litigation

According to patent statistics company Envision IP, Facebook significantly expanded its patent portfolio before going public to avoid infringement lawsuits. The company purchased 750 patents from IBM and 650 patents from Microsoft before its IPO.

Although professional social networking site LinkedIn and social gaming developer Zynga had few patents when they went public, both were already profitable at that time. However, Twitter's net loss for the first nine months of this year expanded to $133.9 million.

According to Bloomberg statistics, Twitter has been involved in more than a dozen infringement lawsuits. But so far, the company has successfully defended against all related lawsuits.

In August, Twitter won an infringement lawsuit initiated by Eaton Company in the U.S. Court of Appeals, where the latter accused it of infringing on a large-scale information system patent. In April 2012, Twitter also won a lawsuit regarding virtual community patents initiated by VS Technologies.

TechRadium, an emergency response service provider, sued Twitter in 2009 after multiple local governments adopted Twitter systems, but both parties reached a settlement in December 2010.

Pending Patents

According to a report by Envision IP, Twitter may gain eight more patents through recent acquisitions of TweetDeck, Bluefin Labs, and Dasient, though these patents have not yet been granted to the company. Twitter also stated that it has 95 patent applications under review in the U.S. or overseas.

Promising not to initiate offensive litigation also helps cut Twitter's costs. According to data from the American Intellectual Property Law Association, each patent lawsuit can cost companies up to $5.5 million depending on the size of the related intellectual property.
