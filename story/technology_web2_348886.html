Dear judges and teachers: Hello! My name is Qi Feng. I come from the beautiful ice city of Harbin, and I am a physical education teacher at No. 124 Middle School in Nangang District. Today, my lesson plan focuses on teaching Tai Chi's "Lan Que Wei" (Grasp Sparrow's Tail) to second-year junior high school students.

I. Teaching Philosophy:

This class adheres to the basic philosophy of the Physical Education Curriculum Standards, following the principle that "students are the main subject, and teachers guide them to stimulate autonomous learning, actively exploring and discovering knowledge and skills." Through scenario-based teaching models and using games throughout the entire teaching process, students will master knowledge in a pleasant atmosphere. Learning through play and practice helps achieve teaching objectives, fully demonstrating the teacher's leading role while emphasizing the student's primary position. All teaching content is designed around these goals.

In daily teaching, we have found that most students lose interest in monotonous technical exercises. If we incorporate technical training into games that students enjoy, it greatly enhances their learning outcomes. Interest is the key to promoting autonomous learning and exercise. Only by effectively stimulating and maintaining student interest can they actively engage in sports activities. Therefore, in physical education teaching, valuing student interest is an effective guarantee for achieving the goals and values of sports and health curricula. Thus, I aim to implement a "learning through fun" teaching philosophy, allowing students to learn and practice while playing, improving under guidance within games, fully mobilizing their initiative, promoting healthy growth, and cultivating lifelong sports awareness.

II. Teaching Objectives:

1. Make every student enjoy Tai Chi practice and actively participate in sports activities.

2. Enable students to basically master the "Lan Que Wei" technical movements and its offensive and defensive implications.

3. Develop students' lower body strength, reaction speed, and body coordination.

4. Cultivate students' autonomous learning abilities and adaptability, encouraging them to learn and enjoy in the process.

5. Foster a rigorous attitude, steady and positive qualities, independent thinking, and team collaboration awareness among students.

III. Key Points and Difficulties in Teaching and Breakthroughs:

Tai Chi techniques include eight methods and five steps, such as peng (ward off), lu (roll back), ji (press), and an (push). In this class, mastering the four methods of peng, lu, ji, and an in "Lan Que Wei" is the teaching focus. The application of these four methods in offensive and defensive drills and the coordination with the body is the teaching difficulty. To aid memorization, the four methods are compiled into a mnemonic:

- Peng hand requires circular support of both arms, attacking dynamically or statically as needed.

- Lu hand opens and presses the palm, making it difficult for the opponent to counterattack.

- Ji hand uses pressure similar to pushing someone over, ensuring firm grip without letting go.

- An hand applies force like tipping someone over, firmly grasping without releasing.

IV. Student Situation Analysis:

Through years of teaching and training, I’ve noticed that second-year junior high students are in a rapid growth and development phase. They are lively, active, easily distracted, and lack self-control due to physical developmental factors. Their strength isn’t yet strong, but they are highly malleable, competitive, and willing to overcome challenges. They prefer combative sports and activity-based games. Since our school is widely implementing martial arts education, many students already have some basic knowledge of Tai Chi techniques. Therefore, I encourage students to develop autonomous learning skills, cultivate group leaders’ organizational abilities, and expand their thinking by selecting appropriate teaching methods and tools that match their physiological and psychological development. This cultivates independence and self-awareness, encouraging them to think critically during practice, developing their physical and intellectual capabilities. Practicing in a joyful atmosphere, incorporating various forms of practice methods and small games, along with timely encouragement from the teacher, boosts confidence, fosters mutual assistance, and deepens their love for Tai Chi, benefiting them for life.

V. Teaching Preparation (Teaching Aids, Venue Setup):

For this class, I prepared detailed pictorial explanations and made simple animated manuals to boost student enthusiasm and compensate for any shortcomings in teacher demonstrations. By creating scenarios and using recorded music, videos, multimedia presentations, and slideshows, I attract student attention. A simple venue setup creates an excellent learning environment.

VI. Teaching Methods:

Based on the course's teaching objectives and student realities, following the principle of fostering student development and innovation, I mainly employ the following teaching methods:

1. Explanatory Teaching: Concise explanations with ample practice, focusing on key points and difficulties.

2. Demonstrative Teaching: Standardized, aesthetically pleasing actions that are easy to mimic. Pictorial demonstrations provide specific, imitable examples.

3. Circulating Demonstration Teaching: During student practice, offering guidance and promptly correcting technical errors to prevent forming incorrect habits.

4. Free Practice Teaching: Providing space for student creativity and imagination, enabling autonomous learning and practice, developing innovative capabilities.

5. Scenario-Based Teaching: Creating situations to guide students in practical analysis and skill application, enhancing problem-solving abilities.

6. Tiered Teaching: Grouping students based on their understanding of the movements, ensuring all students receive appropriate exercise according to their actual situations, increasing learning enthusiasm.

7. Competitive Teaching: Through inter-group competitions, promoting mutual improvement, strengthening teamwork, and fostering a collective consciousness of mutual assistance and shared progress.

VII. Teaching Design:

The design and arrangement of the teaching flow includes:

1. Basic Tai Chi Skill Practice

2. Grasp Sparrow’s Tail Movement Practice

3. Group Practice of the Four Techniques: Peng, Lu, Ji, and An

4. Setting Scenarios for Offensive and Defensive Practice

5. Cooperative Great Challenge

This new lesson primarily focuses on teaching the technical movements of Grasp Sparrow’s Tail in Tai Chi. Following the principle of gradual progression, I start with basic Tai Chi skill practice to familiarize students with Tai Chi movement principles, then transition to the main content of this class: teaching Grasp Sparrow’s Tail. Using pictorial demonstrations, teacher demonstrations, etc., students gain a basic understanding of the technical concepts. Through paired free practice, students autonomously learn and master the movements. Using simplified animated illustrations and multimedia tools, I enhance the learning atmosphere, boosting enthusiasm and effectiveness. After students grasp the basic movements, I set up scenarios for one-on-one offensive and defensive practice, increasing difficulty, expanding classroom teaching, and enriching content. Under group leader organization, students engage in tiered reactive practice, raising interest again. Through tiered teaching and group offensive-defensive competitions, after multiple rounds, select top-tier Tai Chi experts for defense while others attempt breakthroughs, reaching the peak of student enthusiasm. This not only reinforces the main content but also enlivens the classroom atmosphere, bringing the teaching to a climax. Finally, conclude the class in an intense competition and joyful atmosphere.

1. Motivating Emotions and Stimulating Interest Stage

Using the excitement of learning as the entry point, interest acts as the best teacher. Whether students form exercise habits through learning depends significantly on interest.

Teacher introduces via video: Playing Olympic Tai Chi competition clips.

Teacher: Students, our school has been conducting Tai Chi learning for some time now. What thoughts do you have after watching this video?

Student: Teacher, the Tai Chi performance was amazing! But what do the athletes' movements mean? Why haven't we learned them?

Teacher: Actually, these movements mainly demonstrate Tai Chi techniques like peng, lu, ji, and an, which you've studied before, though not very clearly. Today, we will continue learning a representative Tai Chi movement - Grasp Sparrow’s Tail, where the four techniques are clearly demonstrated and hold significant practical value in offensive and defensive contexts.

Student: Will we be able to defend ourselves after learning this? Is it really effective?

Teacher: Of course! We'll also organize group competitions to see which group masters this movement best and reacts most quickly, selecting our class Tai Chi experts for a performance.

Students cheer happily, and the teacher confirms learning groups. Through video and dialogue, students are guided into the class, establishing a sense of collective honor closely tied to the current lesson, seamlessly transitioning into roles naturally and generating keen interest in Tai Chi teaching.

2. Physical and Mental Preparation Stage

This session's warm-up format consists of Tai Chi fundamental skill practice. These basics mainly involve hand shapes, body methods, and Tai Chi steps. Through practice, students fully activate all parts of their bodies, achieving mental and physical relaxation and joint mobility, accompanied by soft, soothing music that highlights Tai Chi's essence while effectively focusing student attention. In fundamental practice, the progressive teaching principle is applied, starting with stationary basic skills, followed by moving basic skills under teacher guidance, and finally directional changes, fully engaging student interest.

3. Inquiry, Demonstration, Collaboration, and Creative Stages

a. People-oriented, creating a good teaching atmosphere.

Firstly, distribute a simplified animated manual to each group, guiding students to watch together. Through pictorial videos combined with mnemonics, students remember key phrases for Grasp Sparrow’s Tail movements and identify corresponding pictorial content. This ensures detailed theoretical knowledge before learning the main content and solidifies mastery. Then, the teacher demonstrates the Grasp Sparrow’s Tail movements according to the pictorial content and instructions, with students mimicking the teacher's demonstration. Next, the teacher explains key requirements while simulating combat drills, with students following suit. Subsequently, select a proficient student as the attacker and the teacher as the defender for one-on-one combat simulation, emphasizing practice difficulties. Finally, students pair up for autonomous learning and free practice, with the teacher circulating for guidance. Utilizing pictorial and demonstrative teaching methods, characterized by standardization, aesthetics, strong imitation, and flexibility, enables students to simulate practice under direct conditions, reinforcing theoretical knowledge and swiftly mastering technical movements. During free practice, students desire concern and encouragement; teachers must focus on student emotional states, providing timely guidance and motivation based on technical mastery and external behavior, creating a relaxed and enjoyable context. Encouraging thought expansion fosters good innovative awareness. Teachers further guide students to view multimedia presentations, invigorating the classroom atmosphere while enhancing practice enthusiasm and effectiveness. Next, establish scenarios—what to do when encountering an attacker—and guide students through one-on-one offensive and defensive drills, increasing difficulty, expanding classroom teaching, and enriching content.

b. Group-based, cultivating mutual assistance spirit.

Upon basic mastery of Grasp Sparrow’s Tail techniques, adopt group teaching under group leader organization, enhancing practice interest. Diverse group formats use the four techniques to address different attacks, enriching classroom teaching and raising practice enthusiasm. Through tiered teaching, divide students into groups based on comprehension ability. Students choose exercises suited to their capabilities, progressing from easy to challenging, improving technical movements and fostering positive qualities like independent thinking and continuous improvement.

c. Ultimate goal: experiencing success and cultivating resilience.

In group-based competitive matches, first practice and understand rules before gaming. Competition achieves familiarity and skill mastery, reinforcing the main content and enlivening classroom atmosphere, culminating in a climax. This class primarily employs game and competition formats, with the first half laying groundwork for the latter. Fundamental practice, Grasp Sparrow’s Tail movement drills, and application of the four techniques prepare for the competition. On another level, group-based matches tie students closely to their teams, enhancing collective honor. Through group leader organization, various games improve organizational skills, broaden thinking, encourage autonomous learning and exercise, significantly enhancing classroom quality. Finally, I designed the "Cooperative Great Challenge" game. Tai Chi emphasizes rigor and steadiness. To foster a rigorous academic attitude and steady character, I divided students who excelled in group competitions into three levels. Other students attempted to break through these levels within two minutes. Note: feet cannot move during the challenge; opponents must be moved using learned techniques. Time expiration results in failure. Through game competition, students develop perseverance and independent thinking. Timely praise and rewards for successful participants encourage active exercise habits, while encouragement for unsuccessful ones fosters resilience, courage, and proactive attitudes. Games are the source of student interest in technical practice and character cultivation.

4. Emotional Stability and Recovery Stage

Under the backdrop of graceful, soothing music, students follow teacher demonstrations to learn yoga relaxation techniques, easing minds and gradually relaxing. Since students have formed teams, occasional team-based competitions necessitate conclusions. I conclude with an award ceremony, organizing group exchanges and student representative speeches, selecting the best defenders and challengers, and recognizing outstanding "Tai Chi Stars." Distributing awards and motivating students create a positive atmosphere of progress, innovation, and teamwork.

IX. Teaching Effectiveness

Through teaching, it is expected that over 85% of students will achieve the class objectives. Students will learn joyfully, positively, and successfully. Practice density: approximately 45%; Exercise load: approximately 30%
