Qualcomm's Criticism of Apple's 64-bit Chip: A Myopic View?

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 17:57:22

This year, Apple equipped its flagship iPhone 5s with a 64-bit A7 chip, making it the world's first smartphone to feature a 64-bit processor. The A7 chip received widespread praise from reviewers and also prompted a response from competitor Samsung, which announced that the next generation of phones (Galaxy S5) would come with a 64-bit chip, potentially for use in an octa-core product.

Qualcomm has now directly responded to Apple’s 64-bit chip. Since Qualcomm is a chip manufacturer and a leading mobile chip supplier, their statement is particularly noteworthy. The spokesperson was Qualcomm CMO Anand Chandrasekher:

"I know there has been a lot of talk because Apple used a 64-bit architecture on the A7 chip. I think this is a marketing gimmick. It offers zero benefits to consumers."

Anand criticized the fact that the iPhone 5s only has 1GB of RAM, far from the over 4GB supported by 64-bit chips. "You don't really need that level of performance; most 64-bit chips are used in large applications and server-level tasks."

Regarding whether Qualcomm will follow up with a 64-bit chip, Anand said that from an "engineering efficiency" perspective, developing a 64-bit chip "makes sense." However, he did not disclose when Qualcomm would release a 64-bit mobile chip. Meanwhile, just over a week ago, it was reported that Samsung’s 64-bit chip had entered the final stages of development.

The attention given to 64-bit mobile chips represents a significant technological advancement, reflected in two aspects: doubled processing power and enhanced memory addressing capabilities.

In terms of processing power, we know that 32-bit processors can only handle 32 bits or 4 bytes of data at once, while 64-bit processors can handle data widths of 64 bits or 8 bytes. Thus, theoretically, a 64-bit processor operating at the same clock frequency as a 32-bit processor would have double the computational power. However, whether the advantages of a 64-bit processor can be fully realized depends not only on hardware configuration but also on whether the software (apps) supports it. Before Apple’s September 10th announcement, developers were unaware of Apple’s plans, and currently, only Apple's own apps are optimized for 64-bit. Although many major developers have announced plans to optimize for 64-bit chips, the overall number remains quite small. One of Anand’s criticisms focuses precisely on the lack of apps supporting 64-bit processors, stating that "this performance isn’t truly necessary."

Besides doubling computational power (theoretically), another advantage of 64-bit processors is their memory addressing capability. The memory addressing space of a 32-bit processor is 3.2GB, whereas the addressing space of a 64-bit processor greatly exceeds 4GB, theoretically reaching up to 18 million TB. The ARMv8 64-bit architecture used in Apple's A7 chip allows A7 to process data more efficiently through additional registers, making tasks such as video processing and large games requiring substantial encoding/decoding work easier. It can support larger iOS applications (such as business and enterprise software), though it also requires greater memory support. Anand's main argument is that the iPhone 5s only has 1GB of RAM, akin to "using a sledgehammer to crack a nut."

The attention given to 64-bit mobile chips also holds greater strategic significance. Steve Jobs once mentioned the "post-PC era" during a 2007 AllThingsD D5 conference interview in front of Bill Gates. Jobs believed that iOS devices wouldn’t be as comprehensive as PCs, instead focusing on certain specific functions. In reality, however, as discussed by The Verge regarding the significance of 64-bit chips, iPhones and iPads are not "post-PC devices"; they are increasingly becoming PC-like devices. The deployment of 64-bit chips further blurs the line between mobile devices and PCs, enhancing the computational power of mobile devices to approach that of PCs while continuing to mobilize PC software. Apple released a 128GB version of the iPad in late January and made the iWork office suite free at the iPhone launch event in September, gradually unfolding its strategy.

As for the asymmetry in hardware configuration and the lagging software ecosystem mentioned by Anand, the former is relatively easier to resolve. Through upgrades in one or two generations of iPads and iPhones, the RAM configuration will improve. iPads, with broader application in workplaces, may see faster improvements.

Solving the software ecosystem problem will take much longer. In the PC domain, application conversion has been a barrier preventing the full transition of PC software to 64-bit systems, and it has been eight years since Microsoft began supporting 64-bit home computers. However, referencing Apple's actions with OS X, where they provided rapid application conversion features in OS X Snow Leopard in 2009, Mac computers successfully completed a full upgrade to 64-bit. Similarly, if 64-bit applications become the default option in Xcode, leveraging Apple's strong control over its software ecosystem, it might enable the swift upgrading of 950,000 applications to 64-bit.

Therefore, for Apple, 64-bit chips may not be a mere "marketing gimmick," but rather an important strategic step towards consolidating the mobile device ecosystem against PCs and preparing for the next wave of innovation. Preparations must precede action, and it cannot be overlooked that the A7 64-bit chip and the M7 coprocessor, which supports accelerometer, gyroscope, and compass calculations, represent two crucial strides Apple has taken toward the future.
