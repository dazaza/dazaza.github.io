Apple does such a good job of keeping secrets. How do they do it?

by anonymous on 2013-08-06 18:48:01

Quora Question Link: Apple Inc.: How does Apple keep secrets so well?

How does Apple keep secrets so well? (Why is Apple's secrecy so effective?) This is a quite interesting question asked on Quora. Kim Scheinberg, the spouse of an Apple employee, shared a story...

It was one day in 2000 when my husband (JK) had already been working at Apple for 13 years. At that time, our son was only one year old, and we were planning to move back to the East Coast to live with our parents. This meant my husband would need to work from home, which would limit his ability to participate in team projects, so he needed something he could handle independently. The moving process seemed like a marathon. For this reason, JK started taking action early, trying to choose as many tasks as possible that could be done from home (by 2002, he was already working full-time from home). On June 20, 2000, he sent an email to his boss, who happened to be his first employer at Apple.

Date: Tue, 20 Jun 2000 10:31:04 -0700

(PDT) From: John Kullmann jk@apple.com>

To: Joe Sokol Subject: Intel

In this email, I wanted to discuss with you the possibility of letting me take charge of the Intel version of the Mac OS X project.

I have no objections whether it is under the title of engineer or project manager.

In the past week, my experience working on the Intel platform has shown me how much I enjoy this work. If this project is also important to the company, I plan to devote all my energy to taking responsibility for this project.

JK

Eighteen months passed quickly. One day in December 2001, Joe said to JK, "Tell me what you're working on. I need to understand why I'm paying your salary."

At that point, JK had three computers at Apple and three more at home, all running the Mac operating system. These six computers were custom-made by a friend (since no one in the entire company knew what he was working on, he couldn't order them from the regular Apple store).

In the office, JK turned on an Intel computer, and the familiar words "Welcome to Macintosh" immediately appeared on the screen, stunning Joe completely. After a moment of silence, Joe said, "Wait a minute." A few minutes later, Joe brought Bertrand Serlet.

Since I happened to be picking up JK from work at that time, my one-year-old son Max and I witnessed this scene. Bertrand walked in, watched the computer boot up, and asked JK, "If we make it run on a Sony Vaio, how long will it take?" JK replied, "It shouldn't take too long." Bertrand then asked, "Two or three weeks?" It seemed like JK said two hours, at most three hours.

Bertrand told JK to go buy the highest-end model in the Vaio series from Fry's (a famous West Coast computer chain brand). So, Max and I went with JK to Fry's. In less than an hour, we were back at Apple. By 7:30 PM, the Vaio was already running Mac OS.

The next morning, Jobs flew to Japan to meet with Sony's president.

In January 2002, Apple assigned two engineers to this project. By August, the team had expanded to 12 people. At this point, there was a little bit of news about the project. But in the previous 18 months, only six people knew about its existence.

Finally, we get to the crux of the matter. After Jobs flew to Japan, Bertrand had a conversation with JK about how to ensure the absolute confidentiality of the project, emphasizing that no one unrelated to the project should know about it. Suddenly, renovations were required for the home office to meet Apple's security standards. JK frankly told Bertrand that I, as his wife, knew about it. In fact, I not only knew, but I was also the one who named the project. Bertrand told JK that I must act as if I know nothing about it. And JK himself could not tell me a single word before the announcement. I think Bertrand must have wanted to erase all knowledge of the project from my mind.

I can no longer recall why JK shifted to the Intel project, but one thing is clear to me: for 18 months, no one reported on it. Project Marklar existed solely because an engineer willing to lower his own rank just wanted his son to see his grandparents more often.
