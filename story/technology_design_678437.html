The function of soy isoflavones - a reasonable diet is essential

by sunbiand on 2012-02-27 14:22:09

A reasonable diet must be appropriate. Balanced nutrition is an important principle of a reasonable diet. Its reasonableness lies in its scientific nature. It is the conclusion drawn from countless examples through observation and research. This conclusion applies to everyone, has wide universality, and is also commonality. However, as individuals, people are similar in many ways but also have small differences, each with their own characteristics of demand, which is individuality. Ignoring this individuality and not paying attention to individual differences makes it difficult to achieve the purpose of balance. Therefore, for each specific person, it is necessary to pursue appropriateness. This appropriateness means that it should match the individual's nutritional needs. Pursuing appropriateness means formulating a balanced diet based on personal nutritional needs. This requires attention to individual differences such as age, physical condition, health status, and labor intensity, all of which affect nutritional needs. Only by noticing these differences can balanced nutrition be achieved.

In terms of physical constitution, according to traditional Chinese medicine, human constitutions can be classified into yin deficiency constitution, yang deficiency constitution, phlegm-dampness constitution, yin deficiency fire exuberance constitution, etc. Different constitutions have different nutritional needs. For example, those with yang deficiency and fire exuberance constitution should eat less or avoid hot-natured foods such as spicy foods, grilled foods, tobacco, and alcohol, and instead consume more cooling and yin-nourishing foods like duck meat, abalone, and silver ear fungus. Although fruits can be eaten, "hot-natured" fruits like lychees and longans should not be consumed excessively. Soy isoflavones play a role here too. People with phlegm-dampness constitution, especially obese individuals, who often have excessive phlegm and dampness retention, should avoid or reduce consumption of foods that easily produce phlegm and assist dampness, such as glutinous rice products, sweets, fried foods, fatty meat, and cream.

Regarding health status, it is a comprehensive evaluation of a person's mental state, physical strength, thinking ability, and stress response capability. It includes different states such as normal times (when there is no disease), illness, and recovery periods, each with corresponding nutritional needs. When formulating a diet, this appropriateness should be considered. For example, diabetic patients during periods of abnormal blood sugar should strictly control carbohydrate intake. To supplement sufficient calories, protein intake from fish, meat, beans, and vegetables can be appropriately increased, but the amount should be controlled. Excessive protein intake can lead to abnormal protein metabolism and increased uric acid levels. This illustrates that balance and appropriateness must be combined to achieve reasonable and scientific nutritional needs. Specific operational content can be found in relevant materials, which will not be detailed here.

In terms of age, during the process of individual growth and development, different periods have different nutritional needs. Adolescence is a critical period for physical growth and development, requiring appropriate increases in energy and nutrition. Protein, carbohydrate, and fat intake should be increased to meet increasing energy demands. At the same time, sufficient vitamin A, vitamin D, vitamin C, calcium, zinc, and other minerals should be supplemented to meet developmental needs. In old age, physical strength gradually declines, activity decreases relatively, and energy needs decrease accordingly. Therefore, nutritional supply should also decrease relatively. Food selection should focus on being easy to digest yet rich in nutrition. The nutritional characteristics and needs of the elderly can be found in Chapter Four.

In terms of labor intensity, different types of labor have varying nutritional needs. Generally speaking, physical labor requires increased energy needs, and the proportion of proteins, carbohydrates, and fats should be correspondingly increased. Physical labor can be divided into heavy and light categories, with different nutritional requirements. Mental workers, due to less physical activity, consume less energy but expend more mental energy. In terms of nutrition, protein intake, especially fish and meat protein, should be appropriately increased. Due to less activity, fat intake should not be increased to avoid fat accumulation and obesity. In certain working conditions, such as high-temperature work in metallurgy and glass industries or flight operations like pilots and astronauts, they have corresponding nutritional needs, which will not be detailed here.

From this, it can be seen that a reasonable and scientific diet not only needs to be balanced but also appropriate. Only by paying attention to individual appropriateness can the goal of universal balance be achieved.
