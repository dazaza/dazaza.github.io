Bittersweet: Appliance Giants Face Awkward Challenges in Entering the LED Lighting Industry

Since the beginning of this year, there have been frequent reports about traditional domestic TV giants forming alliances with the emerging industry of LED lighting. In May, Shenzhen Raytech Optoelectronics Co., Ltd. successfully completed its initial public offering and was smoothly listed on the stock market, with Konka Group, a major domestic TV manufacturer, being its second-largest shareholder. A month after Raytech's listing, news about TCL Corporation's joint venture with a Taiwanese company to establish an LED component packaging company drew significant attention. Even earlier, Skyworth's layout in the LED lighting industry was already widely known. The LED lighting industry has become like a newly discovered continent that has caught the eye of domestic TV giants. However, it is important to note that due to the special requirements for technical expertise and brand professionalism, outdoor LED lighting cannot be categorized as part of the profit-making scope of appliance companies' LED lighting projects in the short term. Therefore, appliance companies are currently focusing mainly on the indoor segment of the LED lighting market.

Domestic TV giants have high hopes for the LED lighting industry. TCL Lighting was established as early as 2000, sparking the first wave of home appliance companies entering the lighting industry. A year later, Konka Visual Information Systems Engineering Co., Ltd., a wholly-owned subsidiary of Konka, was born and officially ventured into the research and development and manufacturing of LED displays in 2003. Subsequently, Skyworth, which had been low-key until then, also announced its entry into the LED lighting industry with great fanfare—its wholly-owned subsidiary, Shenzhen Skyworth Lighting Electrical Appliances Co., Ltd., formally appeared on the scene that year. There is no doubt that the development prospects of the LED lighting industry are vast; relevant data shows that from January to April 2010, LED industry investments continued to grow at a high speed, with total investment exceeding 30 billion yuan, and growth exceeding 90%. For the current overall sluggish home appliance market environment, the growth rate and scale of LED lighting are very tempting, which is why top domestic TV companies are using their industrial advantages to join the ranks of the LED lighting industry.

For domestic TV companies, joining the LED lighting industry has inherent advantages: after years of development in LED TVs, these companies have mastered sufficient LED-related technologies, allowing them to enter the LED lighting field without any technological barriers, thus saving both time and funds. But can technology reserves alone ensure smooth sailing for TV companies in the LED lighting industry? The facts tell us otherwise.

TCL entered the lighting industry with much fanfare in 2000 and quickly became a dominant player, showing great potential in market expansion and product promotion, gradually moving from indoors to outdoors. By 2004, TCL Lighting’s annual sales exceeded 500 million yuan. However, after 2004, TCL's lighting business began to decline, with sales dropping to less than 200 million yuan in 2009. Despite leadership changes, TCL Lighting was unable to regain its former glory.

TCL Lighting's failure is not an isolated case. To some extent, it is a direct result of all TV companies' conceptual shifts and improper operations when entering the LED lighting industry.

The lighting industry differs from the traditional home appliance industry, especially in terms of sales performance. It is understood that general group-type home appliance enterprises usually have annual sales exceeding 3 billion yuan, leading these companies to naturally believe that annual sales should be calculated in units of billions. At this point, even if the lighting project painstakingly achieves annual sales of 1 billion or even 2 billion yuan, it remains insignificant in the eyes of the entire group. To gain recognition from the group, the lighting project will inevitably spare no effort, exhausting all available resources to boost sales. While this may lead to a noticeable increase in sales, it is akin to pulling up seedlings to help them grow faster, detrimental to the long-term development of the lighting industry. After overextending channels or even the entire market, the lighting industry might suddenly collapse like pulled-up seedlings.

This outcome is not the fault of TV companies or lighting projects but rather a matter of concept. Lighting products are not home appliances. Just considering product prices, large home appliances often cost thousands of yuan, while household lighting products are priced in hundreds or even tens of yuan.

Although other indoor areas such as entertainment venues, commercial lighting, and public lighting (such as underground parking lots and hotel corridors) use LED lighting products that are also expensive, the usage volume of these places does not compare to the usage of household appliances by ordinary families. Comparing this way makes it clear that the 3 billion yuan in home appliances and the 1 billion yuan in lighting may represent similar numbers, but for companies accustomed to seeing 3 billion yuan, they may only see the surface 1 billion yuan. This natural concept makes the lighting project the sprout and the company the farmer pulling up the sprouts, resulting in one dying and the other losing out.

Of course, the problems within the LED lighting industry itself cannot be ignored when analyzing the setbacks faced by TV companies in LED lighting projects.

First, consider household LED lighting. Although the market potential looks promising, the current development is still immature, especially compared to European and American countries where Chinese consumers' understanding of LED lighting products is particularly limited.

Although incandescent bulbs are gradually being replaced, the vast consumer base in China still habitually chooses incandescent bulbs or simple energy-saving lamps primarily because of their low cost. It is understood that a regular 3.2-watt LED lighting bulb costs at least 80 yuan, while the same amount of money could buy several or even more than ten ordinary incandescent bulbs or energy-saving lamps. The excessively high price of LED lighting bulbs makes it difficult for consumers to accept them immediately, and the inherent pursuit of cheapness by ordinary consumers hinders the smooth progress of LED lighting bulbs in the mass consumer market. The lack of consumer awareness is also a major reason for the slow advancement of LED lighting bulbs. Both incandescent bulbs and ordinary energy-saving lamps have deep roots in the domestic market, and overturning this foundation is not something that can be achieved overnight. Clearly, LED lighting companies have not done enough work in educating the market about LED lighting knowledge. The most direct manifestation of the lack of market environment cultivation by LED lighting companies is that most consumers do not even know what LED lighting bulbs are. The lack of awareness among end-consumers leads to the harsh reality of LED lighting bulbs being shelved by the market. Currently, LED lighting has not truly entered the ordinary household market in China, undoubtedly losing a significant portion of the market.

Next, look at other indoor LED lighting markets. Although entertainment venues and public places are gradually starting to use LED lighting, it is still in its infancy, and LED lighting has yet to be widely accepted by most merchants.

This primarily stems from its technical shortcomings. It is understood that current LED lighting technology still cannot solve the brightness problem, and in various entertainment and public places, brightness is obviously the primary consideration, which has led many merchants to abandon the use of LED lights.

Additionally, similar to ordinary household LED lighting, the large indoor LED lighting used in entertainment venues and public places is also very expensive, sometimes even hundreds of times more expensive than ordinary large incandescent bulbs. For merchants whose main purpose is profit, it is difficult for them to adopt LED lighting, and their decision to abandon it is reasonable.

Finally, the LED lighting industry is still in its early stages of development, which means that it lacks established industry standards and norms. No one can definitively state what constitutes a standard LED lighting bulb, nor can anyone specify what does not meet the standard. This further hinders merchants from choosing LED lighting bulbs because they do not know the quality of the LED bulbs they purchase or under what circumstances they would be protected. Without guarantees, the market for goods will inevitably fall short of expectations.

Of course, the fact that the LED lighting industry can attract home appliance companies to enter en masse indicates that it has its merits: the gross margin of LED lighting is relatively high, which is a growth point for profits for companies; additionally, there are not many brands deeply involved in the LED lighting industry at present, so competition is relatively low after companies enter this industry, and they can leverage their existing reputation to bring a certain brand value to this emerging industry; finally, the LED-related technologies already owned by TV companies make their entry into the LED lighting industry smoother.

If home appliance companies can correctly view the LED lighting industry and market, and treat the LED lighting industry as a long-term strategic plan, it is believed that in the near future, LED lighting will allow the first batch of home appliance companies entering the market to see the light at the end of the tunnel.
