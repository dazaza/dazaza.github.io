Google is making another push into mobile payments with the launch of a physical Google Wallet card.

by anonymous on 2013-11-22 11:49:55

The long-rumored Google Wallet Card has finally become a reality. Google launched its first physical payment card today, with early support from MasterCard.

Unfortunately, the Google Wallet Card is currently only available in the U.S. Verified Google Wallet users can apply for this physical card on desktop and mobile apps. After applying, it takes 10-12 days to receive the card, and after receiving it, you can activate it online and start using it.

The Google Wallet Card can be used at all outlets covered by MasterCard, but it can only function as a debit card, cannot be used for direct deposits, and cannot be overdrawn. Google has set a daily spending limit of $5,000, but withdrawals from ATMs and banks are not counted within the $5,000 spending limit.

Google initially launched the NFC-based Google Wallet in 2011, hoping to make smartphones a replacement for traditional wallets. However, due to issues with the普及of NFC devices at the time and obstacles related to established usage habits, Google Wallet did not achieve the expected results for a long time after its launch.

The physical Google Wallet payment card represents another effort by Google. This time, however, besides the competitors that have existed for two years, Google also faces new entrants in the industry. Isis, which was adopted collectively by AT&T, Verizon Wireless, and T-Mobile last week and targets carriers, is one such competitor. Moreover, the Google Wallet Card must compete with the newly launched Coin card.

The Coin card resolves the annoying issue of multiple cards faced by "card slaves" in the past. It can store up to 8 debit cards at once, while theoretically, the Coin app can store an unlimited number of cards. After launching a crowdfunding project for the Coin card on its own website, this San Francisco-based startup reached its $50,000 fundraising goal in less than 40 minutes.

The strong sales of the Coin card seem to suggest that public enthusiasm for the mobile payment ecosystem has changed significantly. For Google Wallet, this is an unexpected surprise hidden beneath the competitive pressure. Considering the awkward situation of being coldly received after its launch two years ago, will Google Wallet see a spring this time?
