First-line brand enterprises value skills.

The author analyzes that the standardized growth of the market is an inevitable trend, but it will take a long process to transition from disorder to order. The solar energy market in Chinese rural areas is just like this. The helpless moves of leading enterprises have exposed many problems in the rural market. Among these, product quality and after-sales service are especially worth reflecting on by many enterprises, especially those concentrating their efforts on entering rural areas. From an industry-wide perspective, this situation reflects the principle of "you reap what you sow," and ultimately, the issues within the industry must be resolved by the industry itself.

The author analyzes that the situation of big brands in rural areas is similar to Best Buy's situation in China, where they aren't very dominant. The rural solar water heater market has enormous potential, but at the same time, it is also a gathering place for inferior and counterfeit solar products, leading to chaotic competition. Moreover, due to the brand awareness of rural consumers, cheaper but lower-quality products are more popular. Big brand enterprises need to invest heavily in advertising to educate and guide consumers, which leads to increased costs, loss of price advantage, and slow sales growth. As such, withdrawing from the rural market naturally becomes a "sadly unavoidable" move for some enterprises.

Internationally renowned electronics retailer Best Buy's withdrawal from China has become a hot topic in the business world. Although Best Buy claims that closing nine stores is only a temporary adjustment by the headquarters, the public generally views this as Best Buy's failure in the Chinese market. In other words, it’s a defeat of foreign trade models when pitted against traditional Chinese models.

Is Best Buy not strong enough? Or does its business model have flaws? In fact, it might be neither. This nearly invincible business model in the U.S. failed in China, much like how "oranges grown in the north taste bitter." The "soil" factor likely became the primary reason. In China, Best Buy encountered unprecedented rivals—domestic electronics retail groups using traditional models saw Best Buy as a formidable enemy due to its local advantages, which made it overlook business ethics—for example, gaining cost advantages by delaying payments to suppliers, creating unequal competition by accepting bribes from suppliers, seeking loopholes in government policies to gain privileges, and blindly pursuing cheap strategies that mislead commercial order and consumer perceptions... These practices do not exist in Best Buy's model. Unable to change the environment—i.e., the local business environment in China—it chose to change itself, meaning withdrawing from China.

Similarly, recently, Huang Ming, chairman of Himin Solar Energy Group, one of the industry's leading enterprises, stated at the second Leadership Annual Meeting held by Winner Enterprise Management Group in Hong Kong that in 2011, Himin would allocate seventy percent of its resources to developing urban markets. There are even rumors that all Himin Solar products will exit rural markets to focus on urban ones. Once this statement was made, it caused quite a stir. After all, the campaign to promote solar water heaters in rural areas is in full swing, and many solar companies are enthusiastically launching comprehensive attacks on rural markets. Why then is Himin going against the flow and shifting its focus? Indeed, this aligns with Himin Solar's consistently upheld "above competition" blue strategy, but the specific reasons remain tightly sealed.
