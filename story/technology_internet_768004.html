Marissa Mayer's Secret to Solving Yahoo's 1,000 Problems

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 16:48:02

More than a year ago, when Mayer had just taken over her position, an old employee of Yahoo told her that she had 1,000 problems to solve. Today, apart from acquiring 24 start-ups and restructuring the top-level team, Mayer has done many things, solved many problems, and gradually transformed the corporate culture of Yahoo. These factors were crucial for the doubling of Yahoo's stock price during the same period. How did she achieve this?

Fifteen months ago, when Marissa Mayer parachuted into Yahoo as its new CEO, some employees told her, "You have 1,000 problems to solve."

"It was really stressful to hear people say that. How do you solve 1,000 problems?" She said last Thursday on the stage of Fortune’s Most Powerful Women International Summit.

Mayer successfully solved these 1,000 problems thanks to advice she received from her former boss, Eric Schmidt of Google. "Eric said that even excellent managers often confuse one point, firmly believing their job is to do things. Your work is defense rather than offense. Clear the road. Remove obstacles" —— then let employees "run forward as fast as possible."

The self-proclaimed tech enthusiast Mayer turned Schmidt's advice into her own scientific leadership theory. At the Most Powerful Women Summit, she told the audience, including Warren Buffett and Ursula Burns, CEO of Xerox, that she views culture as DNA. "I don't know much about genetics, but I know a little," Mayer said. "What we need are those positive expression genes or highly expressed genes."

In order to motivate the 12,000 employees worldwide at Yahoo, Mayer said that she tried to "eliminate some negative expression genes that become obstacles. It's not about injecting new mutant DNA, not about changing the culture; the culture is still the original culture, but refined to become the best."

For this purpose, Mayer launched the PB&J plan, hoping to remove complex processes and bureaucratic red tape, and eliminate local congestion. As part of PB&J, Mayer and her new management team created an online tool to collect employee complaints and vote on whether these issues are worth trying to solve. Any complaint that receives at least 50 votes, such as low-powered laptops or numerous regulations in the company gym, will attract the attention of the management, and ordinary employees will be responsible for solving the problem. Yahoo will evaluate the employees' approaches.

More than a year after taking office, Mayer said at this summit, "We have solved 1,000 problems, big and small."

Clearly, the reshaping of the corporate culture is making Yahoo a more attractive workplace. Mayer said that in the last quarter, Yahoo received 17,000 resumes from job seekers, significantly higher than the 2,000 resumes received in the same period last year.
