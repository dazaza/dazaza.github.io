Characteristic products are the original energy source to attract customers. For large department stores, there are basically two ways to win in competition: one is the product itself, and the other is the service attached to the product. The fundamental goal of customers entering a store is to buy products, and the goal of merchants opening a store is also to sell their products. The condition for merchants to sell their products is reliant on the product's own value, namely its utility value. If a store’s products have unique characteristics, it will gain a comparative advantage (an economic term, referring to possessing an essential advantage) in market competition. Characteristic products are the original energy source to attract customers. If products lack distinct features, they won’t have a comparative advantage and will be at a significant disadvantage in competition. Therefore, having distinctive products is the first step in successfully running a store.

Characteristics are the only way to attract customers. To some extent, adding unique characteristics is the exclusive method for merchants to enhance their competitive advantage.

In today's market where most goods are in surplus, the products offered by various merchants are largely similar. Maintaining the relative uniqueness of products is indeed difficult. In such a situation, customers will compare and choose among available options. How can merchants appeal more strongly to consumers during this comparison and selection process? This is a critical issue that merchants need to address. We know that if a store wants to attract people, the only way is through distinctiveness. If merchants cannot make their products distinctive, another path to achieving distinctiveness is through added services. With the same product, different additional services can create different characteristics, thus varying the level of attraction to customers. Under market conditions where goods are in surplus, providing added-value services naturally becomes the direction that merchants strive towards. If a product has unique characteristics and these are further enhanced by additional services, then the comparative advantage of characteristic products can be transformed into a competitive advantage, which is a matter of improving quality continuously. In summary, adding services is a crucial method for department stores to maintain their competitive edge, and to some extent, it is the sole method.

The principle of adding characteristics is to target customer needs. The principle of adding services to products is to align with customer needs. Adding content that does not meet customer needs is called wasteful addition. For example, a few years ago, many large department stores blindly added tourism, sightseeing, and leisure service projects, which turned out to be counterproductive and inefficient, known as ineffective addition. Some malls even added outdated cultural elements related to feudal superstitions, which were desperate measures that exhausted all tricks.

Customers have five types of needs; therefore, additions should be made based on these five needs.

What methods are there for adding services to products? Or rather, what are the needs of customers? Customer needs can be divided into general needs and specific needs. General needs include the need for benefits, convenience, compensating for ability deficiencies, and shopping environment needs. Specific needs refer to personalized customer needs. Combining these two categories results in five major types of needs.

1. Customers' need for benefit means making them feel that they are getting more than their money's worth. The need for benefit is an important requirement for customers. People often have a misconception that the need for benefit is solely understood as buying cheap items or low-priced goods. However, "benefit" does not merely refer to price discounts but refers to value for money or exceeding expectations. Currently, many merchants try to satisfy customers' need for benefit by relentless discounting and promotions. After all the discounts, even though the goods may be sold, profits are minimal, and trust might be lost. Eventually, when they can no longer sustain themselves, they have no choice but to close down.

What does "value for money" or "exceeding expectations" mean? It means creating a psychological comparative advantage for customers, where they feel that the price paid is worth it when purchasing goods. For example, the same piece of clothing might cost 100 yuan in a free market and seem expensive, while it might cost 300 yuan in a high-end department store and still seem like a bargain. This is because department stores add many service contents that free markets cannot offer, such as shopping environment, reliability, credibility, service quality, and psychological satisfaction, all of which have intrinsic value. Especially in today's era of high material satisfaction, satisfaction beyond material aspects is even more important. Therefore, "benefit" is not simply about low prices but about customers subjectively feeling that they are getting value for money or exceeding expectations. It is the price that customers are willing to pay after careful consideration and comparison. Of course, department stores satisfying customers' need for benefit and making them feel that they are getting value for money or exceeding expectations fundamentally relies on the scope and skills of services. As the scope of services increases, the average service cost per individual product decreases; improvements in service skills and proficiency lead to increased efficiency and reduced service costs. Objectively, this gives customers the feeling that they are getting more than their money's worth.

2. Customers' need for convenience satisfies their inherent "laziness." Convenience means that customers should feel comfortable and easy when shopping. They should feel that the merchant has considered everything thoroughly for them. For instance, when customers want to know about the product structure, they don't need to ask verbally, and any questions they have should be answered quickly.

Of course, there is much work to be done to meet customers' need for convenience. For example, the product layout should consider the convenience of shopping for customers. However, currently, most department stores focus on their own perspective rather than the customers'. Therefore, many large department stores place cosmetics and gold jewelry, which are meant to decorate the storefront, on the first floor, while placing products that should be closer to the mall exit on higher floors. Merchants place cosmetics and gold jewelry on the first floor mainly to enhance the visual image of the store, making it neat and beautiful. However, cosmetics and gold are not random purchases but highly selective ones that require a quiet and elegant selection environment. Placing them on the first floor, where there is heavy foot traffic and noise, makes the store look good but worsens the shopping environment for customers, inadvertently causing a loss of some foot traffic.
