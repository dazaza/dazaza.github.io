Health and Longevity

March 13, 2010

Health and longevity are eternal themes for humanity.

The 21st century is a great era of health. Humanity will pursue safety in terms of mental, physical, and environmental health. The increasingly affluent Chinese people, facing the rapid improvement in living standards, are becoming more aware that health is the most precious wealth in life. It surpasses the highest bank deposits and everything else in the world. The emphasis on physical and mental health signifies social progress. Whether it's the development of humanity itself and the realization of self-worth, or participation in social development and enjoyment of its fruits, all must be premised on health. Without healthy mind and body, nothing can be achieved, and pursuing health is equivalent to pursuing civilization and progress. Pursuing health is a guarantee for social development and national strength. Developing extensive and profound health education is a great project that concerns future generations and is an urgent need for the Chinese people.

One, Definition of Health

1. Definition

In the 1970s, the United Nations World Health Organization (WHO) defined health in the World Health Charter as follows: "Health is not merely the absence of disease but also includes perfect physiological, psychological state, and social adaptability."

(1) No illness in the body: This is the most basic condition for health.

(2) Mental health: Attitude determines everything in life, and good psychology is the guarantee for everything.

(3) Physiological health: Maintaining coordination and perfection of functions among the tissues' cells in the body.

(4) Ability to adapt to society: The three major characteristics of today's society are "speed, change, and crisis." The strategies are "learning, changing, and entrepreneurship."

2. Ten Standards of Health

(1) Abundant energy, able to handle daily life pressures calmly without feeling overly tense, and you can engage in any work you desire.

(2) Optimistic outlook, positive attitude, willing to take responsibility, strict with oneself and lenient with others.

(3) Strong adaptability, able to adjust well to various changes in the environment.

(4) Resistance to common colds and infectious diseases.

(5) Standard weight, well-proportioned body, harmonious standing posture.

(6) Bright eyes, quick reflexes, no inflammation.

(7) Shiny hair, no dandruff or minimal.

(8) Clean teeth, no jagged edges, no pain, normal gum color, no bleeding.

(9) Elastic muscles and skin, walking feels light.

(10) Good at resting, sleeps well.

Two, Disease and Lifespan

1. Human Ideal Lifespan

(1) According to biological principles

The lifespan of mammals is 5 to 7 times their growth period. For humans, the growth period ends when the last tooth emerges (ages 20-25). Therefore, the human lifespan should be between 125 to 175 years, averaging 150 years. At minimum, it should be 100 years.

From 0 to 60 years old is the first spring of life, sowing and cultivating through hard work.

From 61 to 120 years old is the second spring of life, golden harvest, warm and relaxed.

(2) Changes in human lifespan:

The average life expectancy of the Chinese increased from 35 years before liberation to 71 years by the end of 2000. In the past, living to 70 was rare. Now, living to 80 isn't considered old, and 70 is still youthful.

2. Diseases and Lifespan

People's ideal is beautiful; everyone should live over 100 years, which is a normal biological rule. However, in real life, more than 90% of people cannot live out their full potential lifespan. People who could have lived to 120 only make it to 70, those who should have lived to 70, 80, or 90 now fall ill in their 40s, get coronary heart disease, diabetes, cancer, etc., in their 50s, and pass away in their 60s or 70s. They get sick 50 years earlier than they should. Getting sick early, becoming disabled early, and dying early have become common phenomena in today's society.

Human disease-related deaths far outnumber accidental deaths, and chronic diseases account for over 80% of all human deaths. Therefore, we conclude that the entire human life process is a struggle against disease, for which many people pay a heavy price.

Our dream is to control our own lives and health. Looking back, we have sent away yesterday's diseases and welcomed today's.

Before the 19th century, we faced infectious diseases such as smallpox, cholera, plague, and diphtheria caused by pathogens.

In the mid-20th century, we faced chronic diseases such as malnutrition, anemia, stomach ulcers, and slow growth. By the end of the 20th century and into the 21st century, we face modern diseases such as hypertension, heart disease, diabetes, gallstones, cancer, AIDS, etc., which many experts call lifestyle diseases.

(1) Cardiovascular disease

Worldwide, 17 million people die each year from cardiovascular diseases, meaning one out of every three deaths is due to cardiovascular disease. If this trend continues, by 2020, the mortality rate from cardiovascular diseases will increase by more than 50%.

Each year in China, there are 2 million new cases of cardiovascular disease, about 1.5 million die from strokes, and the death rate from coronary heart disease has increased by 53.4% in urban areas over the past eight years. The economic losses caused by these two diseases amount to nearly 100 billion RMB, making cardiovascular disease the number one killer of humans. Causes include high blood lipids, high cholesterol, high blood pressure, obesity, nutritional imbalance, long-term calcium deficiency, and smoking.

A survey in Beijing found that there are 97 million people with high blood pressure, elementary school students have obesity and high blood pressure, and middle school students have fatty liver.

(2) Cancer - The Second Killer

It affects all levels of society, terrorizing the world, with rising incidence rates. The incidence and mortality rates are alarming, cruelly depriving countless lives and causing irreparable damage to society, families, and individuals.

(3) Diabetes - The Third Killer

A. In the 1970s, the prevalence of diabetes was less than 1%, rising to 3.2% in 1996. Currently, there are nearly 4 million Type I diabetics and 40 million Type II diabetics, increasing by 1.2 million annually or at least 3,000 daily.

B. Younger onset of diabetes

Grandparents: Ages 60-70. Parents: Ages 40-50. Children: Ages 20-30.

Diabetes triggers include stress, fatigue, mental stimulation, infection, injury, surgery, childbirth, other serious illnesses, and medications that raise blood sugar.

(4) Respiratory Diseases

Respiratory system diseases are among the top three causes of human death. Particularly, tuberculosis has made a comeback, with 330 million people infected worldwide, 120 million in China, and 6 million active cases.

(5) Infectious Diseases

Emerging infectious diseases include hemorrhagic fever, mad cow disease, Legionnaires' disease, viral hepatitis, etc. Globally, 69 million people are infected with viral hepatitis, and in 1997, 150,000 of the 300,000 deaths from liver cancer in China were caused by hepatitis.

In 2000, the global number of HIV infections reached 40 million, with 25 million in Asia.

6. Classification of Modern Medicine

Medicine - is a skill for repair and maintenance of health.

As times evolve, medicine has developed from single clinical science into the following five major categories:

First Medicine: Clinical Medicine

No disease → Wait for disease → Get sick → Treat → Get sick again

Second Medicine: Preventive Medicine

No disease → Health maintenance → Boost immunity → Stay healthy

Third Medicine: Rehabilitation Medicine

During the recovery phase, treatment involves nutrition and functional exercises, with medication as auxiliary. For post-disease patients, nutrition and treatment are equally important, especially during the recovery phase where nutrition is more critical than treatment.

Fourth Medicine: Health Maintenance Medicine

No disease → Health maintenance → (Nourish cells) → Boost self-healing ability → Stay healthy → Extend lifespan → Enhance vitality

Fifth Medicine: Genetic Medicine

3. People Shouldn't Get Sick

We shouldn't get sick because we were all healthy when we came into this world. The development of diseases originates from cell starvation, leading to decreased immunity and function, gradually evolving into disease. This is a long and complex process. During this process, we must first achieve balanced nutrition to nourish cells, activate cellular capabilities, and achieve health. To put it simply, life is like a sapling; it needs continuous watering, fertilizing, soil cultivation, and pruning to grow vigorously. Life needs cherishing, and health needs active pursuit. We must elevate eating to the level of health. Step out of the traditional lifestyle of waiting for disease when healthy and seeking medical care when sick. Before liberation, our diet was whatever was available. In the early stages of reform and opening up, we reached the point of eating whatever we wanted. Today, we should establish a new concept, that is, eat what we should eat. We need scientific and reasonable approaches, eating for quality of life and health. In the past, people often said, "Prevent minor illnesses, treat major ones." Today, we advocate preventing major illnesses while staying healthy, establishing the new concept of prevention.

Health is present in everyday life. Through changing lifestyles, the most important goal is to adjust the immune function of the human body and maximize work efficiency. Immune function relies on nutrition to maintain. The immune system is God-given personal doctor, and the best doctor. It acts like police officers in life, diligently monitoring the various tissues and organs within us. Our task is to provide comprehensive and balanced nutrition for all the "police officers."

With the advancement of science and societal development, human understanding of nutrition has greatly improved. Although our economy has developed and material levels have improved, some people die prematurely. Some believe that the increase in cardiovascular diseases, tumors, and diabetes is due to economic development and affluent living. This is wrong. These diseases are caused by a lack of health knowledge and incorrect lifestyles.
