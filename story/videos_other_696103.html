Long Yongtu: Purchasing US Treasury Bonds Still the Best Way for Foreign Exchange Reserves

by x7r2r5x6q5 on 2012-03-07 20:35:39

Shanghai (reported by Zhou Yuwu) - The 2009 Fudan International Management Forum was held on the 17th and 18th in Shanghai. The theme of the forum was "to devise strategies for the sustainable development of China's economy amid a global economic crisis." Long Yongtu, Secretary General of the Boao Forum, told reporters that the impact of the international financial crisis on China’s real economy has been only partial and temporary, and is reversible. He predicted that by the end of this year or the beginning of next year, the world economy will gradually recover, with the U.S. leading the way out of the crisis.

Regarding the greatest lesson learned from this financial crisis, Long Yongtu believed it was the realization across the country of the importance of employment, by both ordinary people and the government. To expand employment, further development and support of small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) must be pursued. "The treatment of SMEs and private enterprises has never been properly addressed. The root cause is that banks are unwilling to lend to private enterprises and prefer to give loans to state-owned enterprises because, in their view, bad debts from loans to state-owned enterprises are a minor issue, whereas bad debts from loans to SMEs would be a major problem."

Long Yongtu called for further efforts to create conditions for the development of private and SME enterprises through additional reforms and appropriately relaxing restrictions to allow them entry into major infrastructure construction, healthcare, finance, insurance, and other industries. In his opinion, without further promoting the development of SMEs, the employment issue would never be resolved. "If there were only a few large state-owned enterprises, China's economy would be a hollow giant, lacking substance."

When discussing the negotiations for joining the World Trade Organization, Long Yongtu expressed relief that one key bottom line had been maintained: not committing to floating the RMB exchange rate, even if only providing a timeline. "In the final stages of negotiation, the U.S. asked us to commit to floating the RMB within five years. We almost agreed but finally the central authorities decided we could not commit to this. Holding this position at the time was very correct; otherwise, the ability of the central bank to control overall macroeconomic policy and monetary policy would have been greatly impacted."

Regarding the increase in China's foreign exchange reserves, Long Yongtu stated that this is a natural result of China's economic development and not the outcome of currency manipulation, and there is no need to overreact. However, it also reflects structural issues in China's economy. Long Yongtu believed that although the dollar carries the risk of depreciation, the entire international financial system is fraught with risks. Weighing the pros and cons of various options, under current circumstances, purchasing U.S. Treasury bonds remains the best option for China's foreign exchange reserves. Compared to the euro and other currencies, the dollar still maintains a relatively strong position.
