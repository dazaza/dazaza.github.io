[Eastern Treasure] Food, Accommodation and Transportation in Northeast China

In the grand system of delicacies, there is no sign of Northeastern cuisine. It was not until the end of the 20th century that Northeast China put forward the brands of Liaoning cuisine and Jilin cuisine. However, there are two things that are known to all around the world: the Manchu Han Imperial Feast and hotpot lamb slices. The former originated from the luxurious banquets of the Qing Dynasty court; the latter was born from the nomadic outdoor cooking of the Mongolian people.

In fact, Northeasterners eat with sophistication, openness, invention, and creativity.

Firstly, eating with sophistication: one is the emphasis on freshness and rawness, such as eating raw fish, meat, and eggs, as well as raw green onions, garlic, and peppers. The raw fish killing tradition of the Hezhe people has become a delicious accompaniment for drinking along the Heilongjiang (Amur), Songhua, and Wusuli rivers. Coastal raw seafood consumption has also spread to inland towns. Northeasterners' habit of eating "three spicy" foods (green onions, garlic, and peppers) is particularly famous. Eating these raw ingredients can enhance appetite, disinfect the mouth, and even prevent cancer. Second is the emphasis on green food. Northeasterners have the habit of eating dipping sauce vegetables. The best vegetables for dipping sauce are wild herbs such as dandelions, small garlics, bitter silkweed, and willow mugwort sprouts... Since they are called wild vegetables, they haven't been fertilized with either chemical or organic fertilizers. They are safe to eat, satisfying, and cooling. There's a saying, "Bringing willow mugwort sprouts to boil, makes old ladies crawl all over the kang bed," which describes people's love for wild vegetables. Third is the emphasis on variety. The farmer’s potatoes, pumpkins, sweet potatoes, corn on the cob, green onions, raw garlic, tofu, cornmeal pancakes, large pancakes, coarse porridge, pumpkin porridge, sweet potato porridge, garlic sauce, fish sauce, egg sauce, eggplant pepper sauce... Here you have both dry and liquid dishes; cooked and raw; stewed and dipped in sauce. From these, you can create many combinations of farmhouse set meals. Additionally, there are preserved vegetable series: chicken stewed with mushrooms, pork stewed with vermicelli, catfish stewed with eggplants, knife fish stewed with tofu, potatoes stewed with pumpkins, beef stewed with turnips, spare ribs stewed with bok choy, cabbage stewed with tofu, sour cabbage frozen tofu braised pork belly... Northeasterners prefer big stews in summer more than stir-fried dishes. There are also two common sayings about food: "Pork stewed with vermicelli, go all out." "Catfish stewed with eggplants, enough to kill an old man."

Northeasterners eat openly: they absorb not only from ethnic minorities but also from all over the country; they accept what foreigners bring and also go abroad to introduce cuisines from all over the world. Mongolian roasted whole lamb, Hezhe people's raw fish, Ewenki people's willow mugwort soup, Korean-Chinese dog meat kimchi, Daur hand-pulled mutton with leek flowers... In the past, there were Shandong large pancakes and Hebei white flour steamed buns, now there are Sichuan hot pot... In the past, there were Russian bread (Xieleba), sausage, and drinks (Gowas), recently Korean barbecue, Japanese cuisine, Italian pizza, and Russian large meat skewers... All of these are taken for our enjoyment, used for our benefit, and fully digested.

Not only do we take, but we also invent and create. In the 1980s, a sign appeared on the streets of Harbin: "Pig-killing dish." One word, "kill," shocks everyone. Even if you don’t want to eat it, you still feel compelled to enter the restaurant for a bit of excitement. Once seated, the server brings up the plates and bowls, oh, it turns out to be a whole pig banquet: meat, bones, tendons, blood, head, hooves, offal, stir-fried, deep-fried, stewed, baked, mixed—everything is there. Accompanied by a large bowl of liquor, diners can't help but reveal their wild nature of "drinking from large bowls and eating large chunks of meat," just like eating in the hometown of Jin Wushu, the marshal of the Great Jin Kingdom. Imagine, you feel quite heroic yourself. Consulting about "pig-killing dish," it has become widespread. However, authentic "pig-killing dish" requires fresh killing and immediate eating. Can city restaurants achieve this? To eat real "pig-killing dish," it's best to go to small towns and small restaurants.

Eating is particular about the environment. When enjoying the Manchu Han Imperial Feast, it emphasizes the traditional eight-person tables in old shops; when entering a Korean restaurant, shoes must be removed and one should sit on the kang bed around a small table; for Mongolian dishes, one enters a yurt, sits cross-legged on the ground, and serves oneself. For any farmhouse meal, regardless of ethnicity, it emphasizes going into the farmhouses, sitting on heated brick beds, crossing legs and sitting comfortably, warm and cozy, eating and drinking heartily.

However, there is one point that needs to be emphasized: Hui people do not eat large pieces of meat, Manchu and Mongolian people do not eat dog meat. The reason is that dogs are close friends. For the Manchu, dogs are the righteous dogs that saved the life of Nurhachi, the founding emperor of the Qing dynasty. How could they eat the righteous dogs that saved their master?

"Northeastern cuisine" is a distinctive brand that can be seen in Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, Hainan... However, can one experience a bit of Northeastern culture and feel the character of Northeastern people while enjoying Northeastern cuisine in daily life? That depends on whether you have been to Northeast China.

Special Recommendations:

Tasting: Pig-killing dish, whole fish banquet, whole sheep feast, Lian Gui large pancake, Laobian dumplings, Russian feast.

Regular visits: "Er Si Liu Yuan Shop" or "San Liu Jiu Yuan Shop" (the numbers are both the shop names and the price of the dishes).

Travel snacks: Bread, sausage; pancake, salted fish.

Breakfast: Fried dough sticks, sesame seed cakes, buns, steamed buns, soy milk, coarse porridge, tofu pudding.

Eat richly and colorfully.
