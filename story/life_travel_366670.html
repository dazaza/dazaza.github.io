More than 20 categories of personal items not duty-free upon entry

by dwzhanghao on 2010-09-30 10:04:22

More than 20 categories of personal items cannot be tax-free upon entry

On September 1, the adjustment made by the General Administration of Customs to the management measures for inbound and outbound personal mail items was officially implemented. Readers have many doubts about this: Is it because of the implementation of the new regulations that the inspection of personal items carried upon entry has become stricter? Can expensive items such as luxury watches and iPads pass through customs? What things cannot be brought? What is the tax-free limit for carrying items? How is the excess part taxed? What are the specific regulations for inbound and outbound personal mail items?

Relevant person in charge of the General Administration of Customs stated that although there is a certain tax-free allowance for items newly purchased by individuals abroad, more than 20 categories of items cannot enjoy tax-free treatment beyond the allowance. These regulations are not the same as the new regulations on personal mail items that started on September 1.

[Case]

New wristwatch taxed nearly 10,000 yuan upon entry

Mr. Wang, who works at a foreign company, travels to Hong Kong via Shenzhen almost every two months. Not long ago, he bought an Italian luxury watch Panerai (Panerai) priced over RMB 30,000 in Hong Kong. When entering the country, he wore it on his wrist and put the originally worn Casio watch into the packaging box of the Panerai inside the luggage box. Unexpectedly, when entering Shenzhen Futian Customs, the customs personnel taxed him 30% import tax, approximately RMB 9,000, due to the value of the self-carried items exceeding the limit. And he was not the only one who was "unlucky" on-site.

Miss Wang encountered a customs return handling. Not long ago, her relatives in the United States mailed her an iPad. However, in August, she received a phone notification from Pudong Customs, stating that since the iPad did not have 3C certification, it could not enter the country, and the customs would handle the return.

[Airport Investigation]

Passengers: No particularly strict inspections were noticed

Are customs inspections on personally carried or mailed items becoming stricter?

Recently, I randomly interviewed more than 20 passengers at Terminal 1 of Pudong International Airport. Most passengers told me they had not been inspected for valuable personal items, and a few passengers who were randomly checked also belonged to routine safety inspections. During the interview, one passenger who was randomly checked said, "The luggage just went through the customs' security inspection equipment, and no requirement to open the bag was made." Only one lady told me that during the random check, a camera she brought from abroad last time was seized by customs, but she eventually retrieved it smoothly because it was not purchased during this trip, so no tax payment was required. Although Pudong Airport has customs checks for international arrivals and departures, the current stage mainly focuses on safety checks, and there is no particularly stringent situation regarding the declaration and inspection of valuable personal items.

[Customs Interpretation]

The new regulation adjusts the "mail items" for inbound and outbound individuals

Relevant persons from Shanghai Customs stated that the "September 1st New Regulation" adjusts the management measures for "mail items" for inbound and outbound individuals. Mr. Wang's case of being taxed for the luxury watch involves the management measures for "personal carry-on" items for inbound and outbound individuals, which are not the same thing.

For "personal carry-on" items for inbound and outbound individuals, the customs department has always had relevant regulations, which can be found on the website of the General Administration of Customs. Staff from the Passenger Inspection Division of Shanghai Customs Airport Customs introduced that according to relevant customs regulations, if the value of items brought back by passengers from overseas exceeds RMB 5,000, they must pay certain taxes as per regulations.

Regarding Miss Wang's case of the iPad being returned, staff from the Shanghai Customs Office stationed at the post office explained that according to relevant regulations, personal items sent from other countries and regions are limited to RMB 1,000 per item. Once the specified limit is exceeded, the return procedures should be handled or the goods clearance procedures should be followed according to regulations. However, if the mail contains only one indivisible item, even if it exceeds the specified limit, it can still be cleared as personal items after customs verifies that it indeed belongs to personal use.

Not all personal items can be duty-free upon entry

Relevant persons in charge of the General Administration of Customs stated in an interview with Morning Post that they hope inbound passengers carefully study the relevant policies of the General Administration, and the work of customs personnel strictly follows the regulations. "‘Personal items can be duty-free’ is a misunderstanding. For items newly purchased abroad, there is a certain tax-free allowance, but relevant policies clearly stipulate that for items personally carried by travelers, more than 20 categories cannot enjoy tax-free treatment beyond the allowance." This person suggests that passengers with questions refer to the "Classification Table of Import Duties for Inbound Passengers' Luggage Items and Personal Mail Items" on the website of the General Administration of Customs to inquire about non-duty-free items.

Practical Links

Customs regulations state that 20 taxable commodities include: televisions, video cameras, VCRs, VCD players, audio equipment, air conditioners, refrigerators (cabinets), washing machines, cameras, copiers, programmable telephone switches, microcomputers, telephones, wireless paging systems, fax machines, electronic calculators, typewriters and word processors, furniture, lighting fixtures, and catering materials, etc.

It is worth reminding that among the above 20 commodities, some only list the more representative names of that category, such as televisions and VCD players. In fact, products like projectors also belong to the same category and require taxation. Additionally, customs officers have "discretionary power" at the on-site inspection.
