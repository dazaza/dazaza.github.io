57. Slap Seven

Divide the entire class into several groups by rows. Each group sends one to two representatives to form a circle at the front. The teacher arbitrarily selects a student to start with "one," then the second person says "two," the third person says "three," and so on. When it comes to "seven," "seventeen," or multiples of seven (such as fourteen, twenty-one, etc.), instead of saying the number, the students clap their hands. If someone claps when they should say a number or says a number when they should clap, they are eliminated. The game restarts until only two people remain. These two groups receive 10 points. This game can also be combined with memorizing words; for example, when clapping is required, the student must say a difficult word they just learned, such as "language." If they make a mistake, they are eliminated, and a new word is chosen to restart the game. This not only helps students pronounce English words but also reviews vocabulary. Besides group competition, this game can also be played with the whole class.

58. Find the Odd Word Out

The teacher says a group of words (for example, car, bus, jeep, sheep), and students compete to see who can find the word that doesn't belong the fastest. In the above group, "sheep" doesn't belong because car, bus, and jeep are all means of transportation, while sheep is an animal.

59. Add or Subtract Letters to Form New Words

The teacher provides a word and asks students to add or subtract a letter to change it into another word, for example: it-its, read-red. This game can also be played as a quick response game.

60. Rearrange Letters to Form New Words

The teacher provides a word, and students rearrange the letters to form a new word, such as east-seat. This game can also be played as a quick response game. Here are some reference words: are-ear; read-dear; meat-team; stop-spot; now-won, etc.

61. Break the Word Chain

The teacher presents a concatenated word chain, such as theredoorunder, and asks students to break it down into as many separate words as possible within a certain time limit. For example: the, he, her, here, there, red, door, or, run, under.

62. Word Within a Word

The teacher gives a word, and based on its letters, students create new words. The goal is to see who can create the most words. For example, late(5): a, at, ate, let, tea. Below are some reference words, with the number in parentheses indicating the minimum number of words that can be formed: team(5), table(10), woman(10), answer(10), strong(10), mountain(20), honest(15), nothing(15), father(20), cart(5).

63. Tug-of-War with Antonyms (Synonyms, Opposites)

First, prepare a tug-of-war rope by drawing a horizontal line on a projector screen or magnetic whiteboard. Mark a vertical line in the center as the "river." Draw five small boxes on each side, with the outermost boxes being the "riverbanks." Place a chess piece in the center as the center of the rope. If you don't have a magnetic whiteboard, you can hang a rope across the blackboard and attach a red paper ring in the middle to represent the center of the rope. Divide the students into several teams, and let two teams play tug-of-war. The game begins with the first person from Team A saying a word, and the first person from Team B must immediately say its antonym (synonym or opposite). If they get it right, the chess piece (or red paper ring) moves one box toward Team A. If they can't answer or get it wrong, the chess piece moves one box toward Team B. Then, the first person from Team B says a word, and the second person from Team A responds with the antonym (synonym or opposite). When the chess piece moves five boxes and reaches Team B's riverbank, Team A wins.

64. Noun Plural Tug-of-War

This game is similar to the previous one, except after a member of Team A says a word, the first person from Team B must say and spell out its plural form. Determining the winner follows the same rules as the previous game.

English Classroom Sentence Pattern Dialogue Teaching Games (65-70)

Sentence Dialogue Games

65. Follow Instructions and Act

This game trains students' ability to listen to imperative sentences and react quickly. The teacher can quickly say some imperative sentences, such as: Touch your head (eye, ear, nose...), Put up your hands, Close your eyes, etc. After hearing them, the students perform the action, with the fastest and most accurate being the winner. The instructions can include things like: Touch your book (your pen, the desk...), etc. This game can also be conducted in a competitive format by dividing the entire class into several groups. Each group selects a student to come to the front and perform the actions. Those who make mistakes are eliminated, and the last remaining one or two students win, earning 10 points for their group. Then each group selects another student to continue the game.

66. Sam Says

In the junior high school new textbook, this game is called "Polly says." The gameplay is similar to the previous game, but before issuing instructions, the teacher can say "Sam says," which means the students should perform the action. If the teacher does not say "Sam says" before the instruction, the students should not perform the action. For example, if the teacher says "touch your nose" and the student touches their nose, they are wrong and lose points. This game can also be played competitively, with each group selecting a student to come to the front and perform the actions. Those who make mistakes are eliminated, and the last remaining one or two students win.

67. Guess Who by Listening

The teacher invites a student to come to the front and face away from the class. Then, a student stands up and speaks a sentence in English to the guessing student. Based on the voice, the guessing student tries to determine who it is and asks, "Is he/she XXX?" The entire class answers "Yes" or "No." You can set a rule that the questions cannot exceed five. If the student cannot guess after asking five questions, they lose.

68. Five Questions to Guess Who

The teacher invites the first group's guesser to come to the front and face away from the class. Then, a student stands up and sits back down. The guesser turns around and accepts questions from any student in the second group, such as: "Is the one a boy?" "What row is he/she in?" "What line is he/she in?" "What color clothes does he/she wear?" If the guesser cannot guess after asking five questions, they lose.

69. Ten Questions

This game can be played with one person posing questions and one person guessing, or one person posing questions and multiple people guessing. The students can also be divided into two groups of ten people each to compete against each other. The question poser can think of any object, such as an animal, plant, mode of transportation, or person, write it on a piece of paper, and place the paper face down on the podium. The guessers can ask ten questions, but only general yes/no questions, such as "Is it an animal?" "Does it have four legs?" The question poser only answers "Yes" or "No." If the guesser cannot guess after asking ten questions, they lose. If two groups are competing, one group poses the questions and the other group guesses. Each member of the guessing group takes turns asking one question, and each member of the question-posing group takes turns answering.

70. Whisper Down the Lane

Each vertical row forms a group, dividing the entire class into several groups. The teacher gives a piece of paper with a sentence written on it to the last student in each group. After the teacher says "start," the last student whispers the sentence on the paper to the student in front of them, who then whispers what they heard to the next student in front of them... This continues sequentially. The last student writes the transmitted sentence on the board or says it aloud. The group that transmits the sentence the fastest and most accurately wins.

Children's English Classroom Games

1. "Step on a Landmine": In English word teaching, students are reluctant to follow along with reading. This method works well. Place a landmine under one word, which is not to be read. If a student accidentally reads it, they've stepped on the mine. Everyone counts together: one, two, three, bomb, and throws it at them! It's fun, creates a good atmosphere, and students learn effectively through play.

2. Complete the Sentence: While learning sentences, read slowly, and break down complex sentences if necessary. After following along with reading a few times, let the students complete the unfinished parts of the teacher’s sentences. For example: My name is Rebecca. Students could fill in Rebecca, is Rebecca, name is Rebecca. This is a reaction exercise that helps students master sentences fluently.

3. Call and Response:

(1) All students sit in their original positions.

(2) The teacher describes some things. If the description is correct, for example: The ducks are swimming, the students quack "quack." If the description is incorrect, the students remain silent.

(3) Students who make mistakes temporarily exit the game. The game continues.

(4) Variation: The bike is coming. Or A cat can run, etc.

(5) Purpose: Can be used for teaching the present continuous tense and can sentence patterns, training students' listening skills.

4. Bouncing the Ball:

(1) Students are divided into two groups sitting in their original positions with their eyes closed, and the teacher scores the groups.

(2) The teacher bounces a ball, and the students silently count how many times the teacher bounces it. Suppose the teacher bounces it 19 times.

(3) The teacher suddenly stops and asks a student, "What's the number?" The student should answer, "Nineteen." If they answer correctly, their group scores a point, and that student replaces the teacher to bounce the ball, continuing the game. If they answer incorrectly, another student corrects them.

(4) The group with the most points wins.

(5) Variation: The teacher can pause during the bouncing. For example, bounce 4 times, pause, then bounce 3 more times, and ask, "What's the number?" The student should answer "Seven" or "Four plus three is seven."

(6) Purpose: Practice number words and addition operation sentence patterns.

5. Continuous Addition and Subtraction:

(1) Students are divided into two groups, challenging each other while the teacher arbitrates and scores.

(2) A student from Group A starts by posing a problem, such as: Five plus five... A student from Group B must immediately answer: Five plus five is ten. Then that student continues with, for example: Minus four, and a student from Group A answers: Ten minus four is six. If they make a mistake, their group loses a point.

(3) The sum or difference can be limited to a specific range, such as 20, 30, etc.

(4) Variation: The teacher can have a student write out the calculation: 5+5-4+8...

(5) Purpose: Practice number words and addition/subtraction operation sentence patterns.

6. Go Along with the Mistake:

(1) Students sit in their original positions, watching the teacher. The teacher walks around the classroom randomly and suddenly stops in front of a student, pointing to their nose and saying: This is my eyes (intentionally making a mistake). That student should go along with the mistake, pointing to their eyes and saying: This is my nose.

(2) If the student makes a mistake, they replace the teacher and repeat the process.

(3) Variation: The teacher can hold some stationery, pointing to a pen and saying: This is my pencil. The student then points to their own pencil and says: This is my pen.

(4) Purpose: Reinforce vocabulary and practice sentence patterns.

7. Take Your Position:

(1) The teacher reads a dialogue or a text, or a short passage compiled by themselves.

(2) Let a few students take on the roles in the dialogue or text (people or characters).

(3) The teacher first reads the entire passage once or explains it once.

(4) When the teacher reads the passage again, every time a person or thing is mentioned, the student playing that role must stand up immediately. For example, the teacher says: Saturday afternoon. The pupils are cleaning their classroom. The teacher is with them. ("Teacher" stands up) Look, Wang Hai is cleaning the window (Wang Hai stands up).

(5) Variation: The content can be arranged, such as At the Zoo or In the Park.

(6) Purpose: Cultivate students' ability to understand the content of a short passage and identify key words.

8. Rolling the Ball (Suitable for beginners)

Purpose: Check students' mastery of the order of letters.

Rules:

1. The host stands in the middle with a ball.

2. The host says a letter while rolling the ball around to the students. The student receiving the ball must say the next letter in the sequence. If the host says two letters, the receiver must say the next two letters, and so on.

9. Find Friends

Purpose: Check students' familiarity with the four types of letter forms (uppercase, lowercase).

1. Prepare several sets of uppercase and lowercase letter cards in print and handwriting styles.

2. Select a few sets of cards, distribute one card to each student, allowing them to look at each other's cards but not read them aloud.

3. The teacher gives the command, and the three students who find the same letter the fastest win.

10. Guessing Game

Purpose: Check students' mastery of letters and cultivate their imagination.

Rules:

1. Divide the students into several groups.

2. Imitate the teacher or decide on a pose independently, forming various letter shapes.

3. The teacher gives the command, and whoever can guess within the specified time quickly writes it on the blackboard. The teacher scores, and the highest scorer wins.

11. Roll Call

Purpose: Check students' ability to listen to letters.

Rules:

1. Prepare letter cards.

2. Require each student to take one card, with the letter on the card serving as their name.

3. The teacher calls out a letter (e.g., Aa), and the student holding the Aa card says, "Here I am."

4. Speed up the calling, and those who make mistakes leave the game. The ones who persist to the end win.

12. Silent Letter Reading

Purpose: Check students' mastery of vowel letters.

Rules:

1. Organize the students to repeatedly recite the 26 letters.

2. When encountering a vowel letter, use gestures instead. For example, for A, raise the right hand; for B, raise the left hand; for I, point to one eye; for U, point both hands towards the teacher.

3. Those who make mistakes exit the game.

13. Buzz (Maz)

Purpose: Check students' phonetic discrimination ability.

Rules:

1. Students sit in their seats or form a circle.

2. Select a letter, such as Aa[].

3. Then proceed sequentially from Aa, saying letters, but do not say letters containing the [] sound, replacing them with Buzz. You can increase the number of letters, such as Aa[], Ee[:], etc.

4. Those who make mistakes exit the game. See who can last the longest.

14. "Warm and Cold"

When teaching the word "pen," you can hide a pen in a certain student's drawer beforehand and let one student search for it. Other students help by guiding the searcher with the volume of their collective reading of "pen." When the searcher gets closer to the target, the reading becomes louder; when moving further away, the reading becomes softer, until the searcher finds the target.

15. Posters on the Blackboard

Several pictures are posted on the blackboard, and a student holds an inflatable hammer. The teacher says a word, and the student repeats it and then smashes the corresponding picture with the hammer.

16. Clapping Hands

First, divide the children into groups. Then, the children clap hands with the teacher while saying words. When the teacher suddenly stops, if the children in a group stop simultaneously, they earn points. (If they're not paying attention, hahaha...)

17. Word Relay

The teacher starts by clapping once and saying a word, then quickly pointing to a student. That student then says another word while clapping and pointing to another student, and so on (quickly, and without repeating words). This method is best used for reviewing a large number of words.

18. Moving Forward

Ideally, there are grids on the ground. The first student from each group lines up. The teacher says a Chinese word, and the children spell it out. If they are fast, they can move forward one grid (you can set the number of grids yourself). Then the second student continues, and the group that completes the required number of grids first wins.
