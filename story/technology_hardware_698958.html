An octogenarian guards his fallen comrade's tomb for 30 years; the real-life Assembly touches netizens

by xue94fwsh on 2012-03-09 12:28:44

Xinhua News Agency, Hefei, April 2nd - China Web Story: An 80-Year-Old Man Keeps Watch Over His Comrades' Graves for Thirty Years; Real-Life "Assembly" Moves Netizens. Reporters Xiong Runpin and Zhang Qin of Xinhua News Agency's "China Web Story".

A monument, a more than 20-mu cemetery, and over 2400 souls of anti-Japanese heroes. Because of a promise made to his comrades more than 60 years ago, Ou Xing Tian, at the age of 86, gave up family happiness, despite being plagued by illness, to travel far and wide to rebuild the Anti-Japanese Martyrs Cemetery of the Huabei Western Gate, and persisted in guarding the graves of his fallen comrades. This watch lasted for thirty years.

Ou Xing Tian's story of keeping watch has been widely spread through media like the Internet, moving countless netizens. He was voted as one of the "Good People of China" in the category of honesty and trustworthiness by netizens. On the eve of Qingming Festival, reporters from "China Web Story" brought blessings from netizens to the Anti-Japanese Martyrs Cemetery of the Huabei Western Gate located in Liang Village, Guyun County, Anhui Province, to listen to this extraordinary old man's extraordinary story.

[Core Web Story] Salute, Old Soldier!

An old soldier, for a promise made more than 60 years ago, guarded the graves of his comrades in a remote village for thirty years. After Ou Xing Tian's story was accidentally posted online, it moved many netizens and he was voted as one of the "Good People of China" in the category of honesty and trustworthiness by netizens in 2010.

The story of the old soldier did not end there; his deeds continued to circulate on the Internet. Many blogs, microblogs, and local forums reprinted his story. "Salute to the old soldier!" Numerous netizens expressed their inner feelings in unison online. Some netizens even spontaneously visited the martyrs cemetery from places like Beijing and Jiangsu to pay respects to revolutionary forebears, express admiration for the old man, and donate money and goods to the cemetery.

A commenter named "Lao Wang Renjia" on a local forum wrote: "Guarding the tranquility in his heart with his life, alive, he guards the spirits of the martyrs; dead, he sleeps with the martyrs. The beauty of this old man's heart is touching and natural..."

Sina Weibo user "Kai Jin Bulage" commented after reposting the old man's story: "Rather than saying the old man is building and guarding a monument, it would be better to say he is continuing and writing an epic belief in the blood of this nation. Heroes never fade, and military songs remain forever loud!"

In Liang Village, Renqiao Town, Guyun County, Ou Xing Tian, with silver hair, stood smiling in the martyrs cemetery he had guarded. As Qingming Festival approached, the old man prepared good wine again, carrying a bottle and a glass, sprinkling wine at each martyr's grave, drinking a cup and talking with the comrades resting eternally in the cemetery.

(1) A Fertile Land, Two Thousand Souls

Liang Village, this obscure small mountain village, was a key traffic route for the Fourth Division of the New Fourth Army crossing hundreds of miles of enemy-occupied areas during the Anti-Japanese War, and thus became the western gate of the Huabei Su-Wan Border Area Anti-Japanese Base. Generals Peng Xuefeng, Zhang Aiping, Zhang Zhen, and many other famous anti-Japanese generals once left their fighting footprints here. This place was also the only place in the Su-Wan Border Area that did not fall during the Anti-Japanese War.

Born in Oujiazhuang of this place in 1925, Ou Xing Tian joined the revolution at the age of 14, serving as a clerk for General Zhang Aiping, and held positions such as staff officer, lieutenant colonel staff officer, head of the administrative department of the Central Military Commission's First Artillery School, before retiring and returning to his hometown.

As a reconnaissance officer of the Fourth Division of the New Fourth Army, Ou Xing Tian personally witnessed more than 2400 soldiers of the New Fourth Army bleeding and sacrificing their lives to protect this key route. The most tragic battle involved 60 soldiers resisting hundreds of Japanese puppet troops. The battle lasted for a day and a night, until ammunition and food were exhausted, and the blood of the fallen soldiers dyed the nearby river red.

The elderly man, leaning on a cane with silver hair, recalled the past without missing any comrade-in-arms who fought side by side, nor calling any martyr's name wrong. "Every time we went out, my comrades and I never thought about coming back alive," said the old man.

(2) A Promise in Words, Thirty Years of Guarding

After the victory of the Anti-Japanese War, Ou Xing Tian's unit built the Anti-Japanese Martyrs Cemetery of the Huabei Western Gate in Liang Village, which was destroyed in the Huaihai Campaign. By the 1980s, some old comrades who had fought in Huabei revisited the old place and proposed rebuilding the cemetery.

"My body was still very healthy, so I volunteered to take it on," said Ou Xing Tian. When he took on the task of rebuilding the cemetery, he also had a "selfish motive." "There were nine people in the sharpshooter squad I was in, and each of us used needles and ink to carve our names on our arms, promising that whoever survived would guard the graves of the fallen comrades. Now, only me and another comrade far away in Beijing are left. I promised my comrades, so I must do it," said the old man simply.

For this reason, shortly after returning home from the army, the old man left behind his grandchildren and a comfortable retirement life, moving with his wife from the bustling city center of Suzhou to the remote countryside of Liang Town, just to guard the fallen comrades.

"I lived here with my grandfather for a long time. I remember the small tile house had no electricity, and we had to use kerosene lamps. The difference from urban life was too great, so I went back home later. But my grandfather never left, nor did he want to leave," recalled Ou Yang, Ou Xing Tian's 23-year-old grandson.

To rebuild the cemetery, Ou Xing Tian, despite his frail body, carried his own food, climbed mountains and crossed rivers, making four trips to Beijing within two years and visiting six provinces, finding 36 old leaders who had fought on this land to discuss the construction of the cemetery, receiving widespread support.

For a promise in words, the old man rode a bicycle or walked, traveling long distances to surrounding counties and cities to visit the descendants of heroes, collecting and verifying the names of heroes, and finding all the remains of heroes.

One member of the sharpshooter squad, Qiao Jingkun, was buried in his hometown of Luling Town, Suzhou, after his sacrifice. To relocate his grave to the cemetery, Ou Xing Tian visited Qiao Jingkun's son, Qiao Junqing, four times. "I was busy at that time, and my living conditions were also difficult, so I couldn't pay attention to this. Old Ou visited four times for my father's relocation. He is truly an extraordinary person, and we admire him for his efforts for his comrades over the years!" Qiao Junqing sighed.

(3) "After a hundred years, scatter my ashes here to accompany my comrades"

After thirty years of guarding, the Anti-Japanese Martyrs Cemetery of the Huabei Western Gate has now taken initial shape. A 9-meter-tall elegant archway overlooks the wilderness, and a 19-meter-tall grand memorial tower stands tall on the earth; the cemetery is filled with evergreen pines and cypresses; more than twenty martyrs' graves are neatly arranged, all engraved with stone tablets, allowing future generations to pay their respects.

Since the passing of his wife three years ago, Ou Xing Tian began guarding the cemetery alone. Managing construction, watering trees, sweeping the ground, washing clothes, cooking meals, telling visitors the stories of the martyrs; in his spare time, practicing calligraphy, compiling historical materials. In the old man's bedroom, the reporter saw only a few simple and worn pieces of furniture: a bed, a wardrobe, and a sofa. The television was black-and-white, and the empty refrigerator contained only a few raw eggs and half a bowl of leftover scrambled eggs with green onions. The few pairs of shoes on the simple shoe rack were worn beyond repair, covered in dirt.

"This kind of life is enough, I'm not used to living in my daughter's big house," the old man waved his hand and said.

However, Ou Xing Tian admitted to being "very stingy." Enjoying the benefits of a retired senior officer, Ou Xing Tian earned six to seven thousand yuan per month, but for many years, apart from an old house in Suzhou that is older than his grandson Ou Yang, he has no other "property" to leave to his descendants. He almost invested all his money and energy into the construction and maintenance of the martyrs cemetery. Meanwhile, regarding employment, enlistment, and other issues of his children and grandchildren, he refused to use his "connections" to "grease the wheels." For this, his children and grandchildren have sometimes been confused, even leading to "strained relations."

The one who understands the old man best is Ou Yang. In Ou Yang's view, someone like his grandfather "may be hard to find another nowadays."

Ou Yang said, "I was raised by my grandparents since childhood and lived with them for 14 years. My grandmother was paralyzed for eight years before she passed away. At that time, I was in junior high school, and my grandfather got up at five every morning, first frying me an egg, then cooking a bowl of noodles, then taking care of my grandmother, and doing many things related to the cemetery. My grandfather is a good person, and from him, I learned the principles of being a person and the simplicity of life."

The pine and cypress saplings planted by Ou Xing Tian years ago have now grown into ten-meter-high large trees, while the old man's body has gradually weakened. "My grandfather endured much hardship and委屈for the cemetery, but he didn't want to tell anyone or argue with anyone. He is already 86 years old, and his health has deteriorated in recent years. His legs are not good, and I am really worried about him living alone here, but he doesn't want to return to the city with me," Ou Yang said. "My grandfather said that after he passes away, there should be no funeral, just cremation, and then scatter his ashes here to accompany his comrades. I understand this is his wish, but hearing it still makes me sad. Moreover, what will happen to the cemetery then? Whether I can continue alone is also something I am very confused about."

What comforts Ou Yang is that after his grandfather's story became known, more and more people voluntarily came to help at the cemetery. This year during the Qingming Festival, Ou Yang's classmate Zhang Xiang set aside work and took nearly a week off to come to the cemetery from the city of Suzhou to help the old man with work and receive visitors.

"For the old man, I genuinely admire him and am willing to often come and help with whatever I can. I think, compared to the labor I put in, I gain more here," Zhang Xiang said. Just as the reporter was interviewing, a group of Young Pioneers wearing red scarves were paying tribute at the martyrs' graves. Ou Xing Tian happily saluted the children with a military salute.
