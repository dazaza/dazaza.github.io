Car Beauty Knowledge -- The Overall Structure of a Car

I have worked in the local car repair industry for many years and now I would like to share my experience with everyone.

A car is typically composed of four parts: the body, engine, electrical equipment, and chassis.

1. Body

The body is the place where the driver works, as well as the place to carry passengers and goods. The body should provide convenient operating conditions for the driver and offer a comfortable and safe environment for the passengers or ensure that the goods remain intact. A typical truck body includes components such as front sheet metal parts, the cab, and the cargo compartment; a typical three-box sedan consists of the engine compartment, trunk, and passenger cabin.

2. Engine

The function of the engine is to burn fuel and output power. Most cars use reciprocating internal combustion engines. They are generally made up of several parts including the engine block, crankshaft connecting rod mechanism, valve train, supply system, cooling system, lubrication system, ignition system (used in gasoline engines), and starting system.

3. Electrical Equipment

Electrical equipment consists of the power source, the engine's starting system and ignition system, car lighting, and signaling devices. In addition, modern cars are increasingly equipped with various electronic devices such as microprocessors, microcomputers, and various human-machine intelligent interfaces, significantly enhancing the performance of the car!

4. Chassis

The chassis receives power from the engine, causing the car to move and ensuring it travels normally according to the driver’s control. The chassis mainly consists of the following parts:

1) Transmission System: Transmits the engine's power to the drive wheels. The transmission system includes components such as the clutch, gearbox, driveshaft, and drive axle.

2) Running Gear: Connects all assemblies and components into one unit and supports the entire vehicle to ensure normal travel. The running gear includes components such as the frame, front axle (non-drive axle), drive axle housing, wheels (steering wheels and drive wheels), suspension (front suspension and rear suspension), etc.

3) Steering System: Ensures the car can travel in the direction chosen by the driver, consisting of a steering gear with a steering wheel and steering linkage.

4) Braking System: Slows down or stops the car and ensures it remains reliably parked when the driver leaves. Each car's braking system includes several independent braking systems, each consisting of an energy supply device, control device, transmission device, and brake.

Top Speed of a Car

This refers to the maximum speed a car can achieve on a level, good asphalt or concrete road surface under windless conditions. The top speed listed in a car's manual is measured over the final 500 meters of a 1.6 km test section at the highest gear and full throttle. The top speeds of highway sedans range from 120 km/h to 280 km/h. There are also specially designed racing cars and sports cars whose top speeds can approach or even exceed 400 km/h (some pure speed racing cars are equipped with jet engines, reaching top speeds close to 1000 km/h, requiring drag parachutes for braking!).

Acceleration Time (Acceleration Capability)

This refers to the ability of a car to increase its speed while driving, usually expressed in terms of acceleration time and acceleration distance. Acceleration capability includes two aspects: standing start acceleration and overtaking acceleration. Standing start acceleration refers to the time needed to accelerate continuously through the gears to the highest gear and reach a certain distance or speed from a stationary state. There are two ways to express this: the number of seconds required to accelerate from 0 km/h to 1 km (or 400 m, or 1/4 mile); or the number of seconds required to accelerate from 0 km/h to 50 km/h (80 km/h, 100 km/h, 50 mph). Of course, the shorter the time, the better. For small sedans, the 0-100 km/h acceleration generally exceeds 10 seconds; for mid-to-high-end sedans, it is generally around 10 seconds or less. Some racing cars and sports cars have 0-100 km/h acceleration times of less than four seconds. Overtaking acceleration refers to the time required to fully accelerate from the lowest stable speed of the highest or second-highest gear to a certain high speed. When overtaking on the road, traveling alongside the overtaken vehicle for a period increases the risk of accidents. Therefore, overtaking acceleration has significant importance, which is closely related to the torque characteristics of the engine. Ideally, the engine's maximum torque segment should be smooth with a slight decrease.

Overall Performance of the Car

In addition to the highest speed, acceleration time, minimum turning diameter, fuel consumption, and wind resistance coefficient, the main performance parameters of a car also include indicators representing its off-road capabilities, such as the maximum gradeability, minimum ground clearance, approach angle, departure angle, longitudinal passing angle, etc.

Ground Clearance of a Car

This refers to the distance between the ground and the rigid objects on the bottom of the vehicle. The minimum ground clearance in the car specifications refers to the distance between the ground (flat) and the lowest point of the rigid objects on the bottom of the vehicle. Determining the ground clearance involves factors such as fuel tank height and the lowest profile line of the engine.

Maximum Gradeability of a Car

This refers to the maximum slope a car can climb while traveling in first gear on a good road surface. For off-road vehicles, climbing ability is a quite important indicator because they often need to drive on bad roads or even no roads at all. Generally, they are required to climb slopes of no less than 60% or 30 degrees. For cargo trucks, a climbing ability of around 30% is required. Sedans have higher top speeds and frequently travel on better roads, so their climbing ability is not emphasized; generally, it is around 20%.

Minimum Turning Diameter of a Car

This refers to the diameter of the trajectory circle traced by the outermost end of the outer wheel when the steering wheel is turned to its limit position on a wide and flat area. Because left turns and right turns may slightly differ, the average value is taken after three left turns. The smaller this value, the better the vehicle's maneuverability and flexibility, making it one of the performance indicators where small vehicles can outperform large luxury cars.

Fuel Consumption (Fuel Economy)

"Fuel consumption per 100 kilometers" (in European and American countries, the fuel economy index is "fuel mileage"), i.e., the liters of fuel consumed while traveling 100 kilometers. This indicator is divided into the following two types. One is the equal-speed fuel consumption per 100 kilometers: the national standard stipulates the fuel consumption when a car travels at a constant speed for 100 kilometers in the highest gear under rated load on a level good road surface; the other is the cycle condition fuel consumption per 100 kilometers: the fuel consumption when traveling 100 kilometers under a combination of specified typical driving conditions. Manufacturers mostly refer to the equal-speed fuel consumption rate per 100 kilometers at the economic speed when promoting their car products (this speed can be calculated from the rotational speed corresponding to the lowest fuel consumption point on the external characteristic curve of the engine). The cycle condition fuel consumption per 100 kilometers is closer to the actual operation of the car, and the difference between the two is quite obvious. Therefore, when the manufacturer claims that the car consumes only 6.5L per 100 kilometers, but your actual result is 10L, you should understand why.
