Electric Sedan History of Electric Sedan Development

by ddjiaoche95 on 2012-03-02 21:02:16

Initially, Huayang new energy vehicles were a hot topic, and the electric cars in Huayang new energy vehicles were the first to be listed. However, looking back at the history of automobile development, it is somewhat far-fetched to classify electric vehicles as new energy vehicles. In 1886, Karl Benz created an automobile powered by an internal combustion engine, but electric vehicles have a longer history than internal combustion engine-powered cars. The history of electric vehicles can be traced back to 1834, when Thomas Davenport made an electric tricycle that year. It was driven by a set of non-rechargeable dry batteries and could only travel a short distance. The first electric vehicle powered by rechargeable batteries appeared in Paris, France in 1881. It was a three-wheeled vehicle powered by lead-acid batteries assembled by the French engineer Gustave Trouve.

Compared with the internal combustion engine-powered cars at the end of the 19th century, electric vehicles had many advantages except for slightly lower speed, such as easy start-up, no noise, vibration of the engine, and unpleasant gasoline smell during operation. Moreover, the characteristic of high torque output at low speed of DC motors makes it unnecessary to use complex transmission systems and simple operation when used as automotive power, so electric vehicles became an important direction for the development of mobile transportation.

From the late 19th century to the early 20th century, this was the golden age of electric vehicles. Electric vehicle manufacturing companies appeared in France and the UK. On April 29, 1899, Belgian Camille Jenatzy drove a bullet-shaped electric car named La Jamais Contente to break the world's highest car speed record maintained by gasoline-powered engines, reaching a speed of 105.88 kilometers per hour. This was the first time that car speed broke the 100-kilometer-per-hour mark, and La Jamais Contente electric car maintained this car speed record into the 20th century.

On the other side of the ocean, the United States lagged behind Europe in the popularization of automobiles, but they had their own advantages. The United States led Europe in the development and popularization of electric power technology. The famous American scientist Thomas Edison, who invented the light bulb and phonograph, was a staunch supporter of electric vehicles. In 1911, the New York Times once commented on electric vehicles: "It is economical, does not emit exhaust gas, and is an ideal means of transportation." Public opinion and celebrity effects undoubtedly played a role in promoting and popularizing electric vehicles in the United States. Electric vehicle manufacturers such as Anthony Electric, Baker, Detroit Electric, Columbia, and Riker emerged accordingly. At that time, the United States not only had a large number of electric cars and electric trucks, but Bailey Electric Company even developed the earliest electric sports car in 1907. The first electric taxi appeared in New York in 1897. Meanwhile, related supporting service facilities for electric vehicles also emerged. Hartford Electric Light Company provided replaceable batteries for electric vehicles. Detroit Electric Company not only manufactured electric vehicles but also established battery charging stations for user convenience. The supporting facilities needed by modern electric vehicles were already established more than 90 years ago.

However, the golden age of electric vehicles did not last long. After the 1920s, internal combustion engine technology reached a new level. Cars equipped with internal combustion engines were faster, and the continuous cruising range after one refueling was about three times that of electric vehicles, with lower usage costs. In contrast, the development of electric vehicles entered a bottleneck period, with no significant progress in reducing manufacturing costs and improving usability...

In this context, electric vehicles quickly lost their meaning and basically disappeared from the European and American automobile markets around 1940. The oil crisis in the Middle East in 1973 plunged the world into a state of oil shortage, and people began to pay attention to other types of cars again, and electric vehicles once again came into people's sight. From the 1980s to the 1990s, Japanese and American car manufacturers produced a series of electric vehicles, such as Chrysler TEVan and Toyota RAV 4 EV. The most famous one was the EV1 electric car produced by General Motors in 1996, but they all eventually faded away.

After decades of development, although opportunities appeared multiple times, it wasn't until the early 21st century that electric vehicles failed to reproduce the glory from the late 19th century to the early 1920s. The root cause lies in its relatively high production cost, troublesome charging, high maintenance cost, and short driving range caused by low battery energy density and poor charging convenience. These shortcomings seriously hindered the popularization of electric vehicles.
