Intel IDF Day 1: It's Not Just About Mobility, But Also Wearables and Internet of Things

by anonymous on 2013-09-11 14:45:11

Just one hour before Apple's event, Intel's annual Information Technology Summit, IDF, was also held in San Francisco. Coincidentally, this year’s IDF venue was next to the Yerba Buena Center for the Arts, though Apple had moved its event to its headquarters in Cupertino.

Apple is actively seeking change, and Intel is also undergoing a transformation. The first day's keynote was led by new CEO Brian Krzanich and new President Renée James, both of whom took office in May this year. Their primary task is to mobilize Intel in the mobile space. Today, their speech theme was "Mobilizing Intel."

This "mobilization" goes far beyond smartphones and tablets. Brian Krzanich repeatedly claimed that this IDF is more innovative than any previous edition. As the slogan suggests, Intel's goal is all areas related to computing. With the rise of wearable devices and the Internet of Things (IoT), Intel is rapidly deploying in these niche markets, introducing more flexible, lower-power processors.

From data centers to smartphones and wearable devices, Intel is brewing an exciting storm of mobile computing.

Accelerating the transition to mobility

In recent years, Intel processors have played a significant role in the evolution of PCs. Due to the impact of tablets, PCs have had to break through in terms of size and battery life. After the birth of Ultrabooks and the launch of the Haswell processor, PCs finally achieved lightweight design and long battery life while maintaining strong performance.

At the same time, a large number of thin notebooks with Windows 8 systems and convertible designs are entering the market, which are important tools for PCs to resist the erosion of tablets. A major barrier to the widespread adoption of these 2-in-1 devices has been price, but low-cost devices are an inevitable trend. Krzanich claimed that the cheapest 2-in-1 device will be under $400.

Meanwhile, the next-generation processor "Broadwell," which uses 14nm technology, will begin mass production by the end of this year and hit the market next year. The next microarchitecture "Airmont" will also be launched next year.

The "Bay Trail" product, which supports both Windows 8 and Android systems, will be released this week. This will be the first tablet using a 22nm processor. Krzanich also demonstrated the first smartphone with a 22nm processor, likely just an engineering prototype. Krzanich stated that "Bay Trail" tablet devices could be as low as $100.

The Quark Processor Series

The most exciting part of the event was the description of the chip platform for wearable devices and the Internet of Things (IoT), and Intel even showcased multiple terminal devices. This requires processors not only to have high performance but also lower power consumption, smaller size, and more flexible design. For this purpose, Intel introduced the Quark processor series.

The Quark processor can be used in wristbands, watches, other wearables, as well as in industrial, energy, and transportation fields, aiming to enter the IoT market. Intel even plans to release reference design circuit boards based on this processor to accelerate partners' development of terminal products.

Krzanich showcased two sample designs for wristbands, stating that these reference designs are under development, with the first product expected to be launched in the fourth quarter.

Renee James focused on smart cities and personalized healthcare, demonstrating a fitness-monitoring watch worn on a wristband and a chip patch that can be attached to the skin. She believes many biological issues from the past can now be solved through computation, signaling Intel's entry into healthcare, which falls under the IoT category.

This conference strongly signals that Intel processors are no longer limited to data centers, PCs, smartphones, or tablets but are moving toward all devices in daily life that may be connected to the internet. Due to Intel's lead in manufacturing processes, high performance, low power consumption, and small size make it more suitable for small devices, giving Intel a more advantageous position in technology. The rest depends on support from terminal manufacturers and developers.
