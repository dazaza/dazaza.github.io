Nielsen Starts Tracking TV-Related Tweets

by anonymous on 2013-10-08 18:21:03

According to a report released by Nielsen on the morning of October 7 Beijing time, last month, 98,600 people posted messages on Twitter discussing the two-hour premiere of the new season of "Grey's Anatomy". This is only a small part of the 9.3 million viewers who watched this drama that night.

Nielsen's data also showed that the total number of related messages was 225,000, but these messages were viewed by millions of Twitter users. Some users may have subsequently watched this episode using a digital video recorder or laptop.

Nielsen is tracking the "unique audience size" of Twitter messages related to TV series to help better understand the development of "social TV". On Monday this week, Nielsen released the "Twitter TV Ratings" report. Nielsen said that this report will focus on the activity level and coverage of Twitter conversations related to TV programs. Nielsen announced last year that it would launch this product, but the product has not yet been fully accepted by the TV industry and has not gained many customers.

The CEO of Nielsen's analysis company SocialGuide, Andrew Somosi, said: "We feel that this will be the moment for the industry to build trust." Nielsen acquired SocialGuide in November last year, and the company participated in the creation of this new product.

Somosi said in an interview that analyzing Twitter messages about TV shows is just the tip of the iceberg. "The whole iceberg is how many people these Twitter messages cover." For example, according to Nielsen's algorithm, the 225,000 messages about "Grey's Anatomy" on September 26 were viewed by 2.8 million different Twitter accounts.

It is difficult to determine how many users watched the drama because of these Twitter messages, but previous studies have found that Twitter activities can sometimes increase ratings. Twitter has regarded cooperation with the TV industry as one of its top priorities. In the IPO prospectus published by Twitter last Thursday, television was mentioned 42 times.

Nielsen executives expect that, similar to traditional TV ratings indicators, TV networks will begin to use the "Twitter TV Ratings" indicator. However, it is currently unclear how many TV networks and advertisers are willing to pay for overnight data about Twitter messages related to TV shows. Nielsen refused to disclose specific customer information, mentioning only two media companies when launching this new product: Discovery Communications under the Discovery Channel and advertising giant McCann Worldgroup.

Sean Casey, founder of SocialGuide and senior vice president of Nielsen responsible for this product, said: "This is just the beginning, there is no data so far." For example, in the week of September 23, the final episode of AMC's "Breaking Bad" ranked first in the "Twitter TV Ratings", with 1.2 million related messages covering 9.3 million Twitter accounts. Two episodes of NBC's "The Voice" also made it into the top 10, covering 3.8 million and 2.7 million accounts respectively.

Casey said that he and his colleagues have mastered various technologies that can accurately identify which Twitter messages are related to TV programs and which are just similar keywords. In the future, they also plan to distinguish between messages about TV series posted by TV stars and those posted by audiences, helping TV networks understand how influential the messages posted by stars are.

Some TV networks may question Nielsen's statistical methods. As long as the message is loaded on the screen of a web page or mobile device, Nielsen considers the message to have covered this account.

In addition, there are doubts about the representativeness of Twitter messages. Ed Keller, CEO of marketing research company Keller Fay Group specializing in word-of-mouth marketing, said: "People often don't see that the majority of conversations about TV shows still happen offline." The company's data shows that 80% of conversations about TV shows are word-of-mouth, 10% occur over the phone, and most of the remaining 10% occur online. Keller said: "Conversations happening in the real world are often very different from those on social media." (Waijin)
