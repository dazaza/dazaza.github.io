The Acceptance and Abandonment of Patents by Enterprises

by keyizhang008 on 2012-03-02 15:39:29

Under the strategy of corporate intellectual property protection, enterprises, due to filing a large number of patents and for reasons such as saving operating costs, will inevitably face decisions on which patents to keep and which to abandon. In what situations should patents be maintained, and in what situations can they be abandoned?

The value of a patent is the fundamental basis for deciding whether to keep or abandon it. The value of a patent includes its technical content and market value. Patent value should especially manifest itself in the market economy, where the market value of a patent can particularly endow an enterprise with vitality.

### Patents That Need Priority Maintenance

1. **Core Patents**: Generally speaking, core patents refer to patents corresponding to technologies that must be used to manufacture a certain product in a specific technical field and are difficult to circumvent through some design-around techniques. Core patents are key to enhancing a company's competitiveness.

Whether a patent is a core patent can generally be judged from the following aspects:

- **Number of citations**: Typically, the more important a patent is, the more times it is cited.

- **Number of family members within the same patent family**: The quantity of family patents is an important indicator of the economic value of a patent, reflecting the potential technical market and economic influence of a particular invention.

- **Patent litigation information**: If there is no significant interest conflict behind a patent, companies would not rashly initiate patent litigation.

2. **Peripheral Patents**: Research and improvement of peripheral patents are based on core patents. Filing a large number of improvement patents around core technology patents creates a surrounding effect. Even though the owner of peripheral patents still cannot directly use others' core patents and may still face infringement issues, in the market, when the owner of core patents attempts implementation, they will encounter obstacles posed by these peripheral patents. This can lead to "cross-licensing," bringing benefits to the enterprise.

3. **Annual Stage of the Patent**: First, the lower the annual stage of the patent, the higher the relative value of the patent. Patents at higher annual stages have a greater possibility of losing their technical value. Moreover, some enterprises enjoy national or local government policies that reduce or fully reimburse patent fees for the first three years after authorization. Therefore, from an enterprise cost perspective, enterprises typically maintain patents for the first three years after authorization and only consider abandoning patents in the fourth year and beyond.

4. **Huge Market Value**: Some patents, although low in technical content, align well with market demand and can bring high economic benefits to the enterprise. Such patents are also worth maintaining.

### Patents That Can Be Abandoned Early

1. **Junk Patents**: Junk patents generally refer to patents with no innovative content. Sometimes, enterprises apply for "junk patents" out of intellectual property strategy considerations, such as increasing the number of patents, intimidating competitors, or setting up smokescreens to confuse the technical monitoring of competitors. When enterprises conduct patent value assessments, they should clarify the true purpose of the patent at the time of application and prioritize abandoning junk patents that have lost their function.

2. **Problematic Patents**: Problematic patents refer to patents that, after being granted patent rights, have too broad a scope of protection or do not fully comply with relevant provisions of the patent law. These patents have poor legal stability, and the possibility of the patent right being invalidated by others is high, so they cannot achieve the true purpose of protecting patented products.

Problematic patents mainly include the following scenarios:

- Lack of creativity and practicality;

- The description does not meet the requirements of clarity and completeness, making it impossible for technicians in the relevant technical field to implement it;

- The claims do not meet the requirements of clarity and conciseness and cannot be supported by the description;

- Exceeding the scope during modifications;

- Missing necessary technical features, etc.

3. **Annual Stage of the Patent**: Since patent maintenance fees increase progressively over time, for patents of equal value, enterprises should prioritize abandoning patents at higher annual stages to reduce maintenance costs.

4. **Loss of Market Value**: Some patents were once implemented in specific products, but due to product upgrades or poor market conditions, production has been discontinued. There is no longer a need to implement or reserve these patents, so enterprises should prioritize abandoning them during patent maintenance.
