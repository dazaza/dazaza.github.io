Love Poems as Elusive as Love Itself in the "Book of Songs" - Elegant Essays -

by 66kkk on 2009-10-06 12:07:58

The ancients said, "Poetry cannot be interpreted definitively." This means that due to the subtlety of its language and its inability to fully express meaning, it is very difficult for others or later generations to provide an exact interpretation. How true this statement is! Among all types of poetry, I believe love poems are the hardest to interpret. Most of Li Shangyin's love poems from the Tang Dynasty cannot be clearly explained. Among these, *Jin Se* (*The Zither*) is considered the most challenging to interpret. It has been noted that from Liu Ban of the Northern Song Dynasty to Zhang Caitian at the end of the Qing Dynasty and the beginning of the Republic era, there have been over 70 scholars who provided more than 100 interpretations, which can roughly be categorized into ten different readings. Truly, opinions vary widely, with no consensus reached. For this reason, the famous Qing Dynasty poet Wang Shizhen once lamented, "It is hard for people to understand *Jin Se*."

Yesterday, while teaching a few love poems from the *Book of Songs* to foreign students, in order to fully engage their enthusiasm, after explaining some words in the poems and giving a general explanation of their meanings, I intentionally left some time for the students to discuss and share their own understanding of the poems. As it turned out, this confirmed an old saying: teaching and learning mutually enhance each other. The students spoke up enthusiastically, presenting views I had never heard or read before, nor had I thought of myself. By the time we finished discussing several poems, it became clear that each could be understood in several different ways.

In the *Book of Songs*, *Qin Feng - Jian Jia* ("Reeds in Autumn Dew") is a famous example where literary scholars have debated extensively. Some say it expresses the unattainability of love; others suggest it might generally refer to the difficulty of realizing life’s ideals, while still others argue it simply portrays a beautiful but melancholic dream. The students in my class, however, offered their own perspectives on two other poems.
