Only with employees can we talk about governance, only with a team can we talk about organization, only with human capital can we talk about organizational development, and only with organizational development can we discuss organizational capability and organizational value. What is organizational value? I think it is reflected in two aspects: one is the value that can be shown on financial statements because it can be presented in various reports in the form of financial data, which we can call "on-balance-sheet value"; the second is the value that cannot be reflected in financial statements but truly exists within the organization, such as the organization's team, culture, management model, relationship resources, growth potential, etc., which cannot be calculated using traditional valuation methods, so we can call it "off-balance-sheet value." The complete value of an enterprise is the sum of "on-balance-sheet value" and "off-balance-sheet value." Human capital and its management model in a company are undoubtedly a significant part of the "off-balance-sheet value," which usually appears in the form of costs on financial statements but shines in the entire corporate value as assets.

At different stages of development, companies face different turning points in human capital management. In different stages of development, the task of human capital management in the organization resembles the three characters "Ren" (人), "Cong" (从), and "Zhong" (众). Mr. Chen Huixiang mentioned in the chapter "Leadership·Solo Hero" of his book "Breaking the Turning Point" how to achieve the transformation from individual heroism to collective heroism in the process of moving from one stage to another. Because this is an era that needs business heroes. We are a country that needs business heroes. We hope that Chinese corporate leaders have good personal charm so they can attract talent and cohere teams. We hope they have excellent business experience and wisdom so that the company can maintain good business growth and avoid mistakes. Chinese corporate leaders are the backbone of China. But we are a country with an immature business tradition, business culture, and business system. Modern commodity economy in China has only a history of over twenty years. At present, more than ninety percent of Chinese corporate leaders are grassroots heroes who succeeded in entrepreneurship after China's reform and opening-up. According to the old saying "three generations make a nobleman," most of these grassroots heroes find it very difficult to transform from farmers to nobles. Although we often say people can change, actually changing people is the hardest thing. And for those already successful entrepreneurs, their transformation is even harder. Why should they change? Their previous business experiences were very successful, and they made a lot of money. It seems like they don't necessarily have reasons to change.

The value of human capital management lies in creating wealth for enterprises through human capital management, which is reflected in the perspective of transforming from "talent" to "capable person" and finally to "financial resource." Traditional personnel management merely views people as "materials," realizing only material value, "I am a brick in the enterprise, wherever needed, I will go." Organizations that view people as "capable persons" emphasize the development of employees' abilities and talents, providing them with career stages through human capital management methods. The ideal human capital management treats people as "financial resources," managing wealth and assets to achieve the best input-output benefits of human capital investment. In such organizations, employees follow a growth path from "specialization," to "profession," and ultimately to "career," finding reasonable professional positioning through the application of specialized knowledge and skills, combining organizational strategies with personal life strategies to realize the mission value given by their careers.

Organizations at different stages possess and rely on different capabilities. Start-up companies depend on individual capabilities, growing companies depend on partner capabilities, and mature companies depend on organizational capabilities; this matching relationship objectively exists.

Starting from "Ren" — Start-up Organization

The main characteristic of start-up companies is reliance on individual capabilities, primarily leadership capabilities. At this point, the organizational features are basically like stars surrounding the moon. Everyone revolves around the leader. The team will at this time essentially reflects the leader's individual will, and the organization and systems are virtually non-existent or not set up at all. Whatever the leader says, we do. This is the typical feature of a start-up organization, equivalent to the "Ren" phase in "Ren·Cong·Zhong."

In small-model start-up organizations that highlight individual capabilities, the theme of human capital management is to highlight capable individuals, building organizational capabilities through the highlighted individual capabilities (primarily the leader and key cooperative partners). At this time, focusing on external fairness is more important than internal fairness. Individual heroism, an "eagle" culture, becomes the theme of human capital management in enterprises. During this phase, the organization does not need detailed and rigid management systems. Only flexible and suitable basic rules need to be established, with human capital management mainly focused on meeting business needs, emphasizing the construction of unified value concepts and benefit mechanisms.

From "Ren" to "Cong" — Growing Organization

Growing organizations—partner companies—are equivalent to the "Cong" phase in "Ren·Cong·Zhong." Growing organizations begin to rely on team capabilities. At this stage, organizations already have relatively fixed basic rules and implicit rules, with personnel division of labor, positions, and departments playing essential roles. Allocation and employment systems become increasingly perfect and clearer. Thus, incentive mechanisms emerge. The team capabilities generated by partner companies support the growth of growing organizations. They no longer rely entirely on the capabilities of one individual to sustain the enterprise, as in start-up organizations.

A major feature of growing organizations is having relatively stable businesses, which require relatively stable teams to accomplish these tasks. The human capital management theme for growing organizations first satisfies business needs and then constructs a set of job management and incentive mechanism models for the organization's short-term goals—business-related, financial-related, customer-related, brand-related, etc. These human capital management models must precede the organization's short-term goals to avoid organizational fragmentation when the organization grows to a certain stage, including issues like benefit distribution among founding veterans, loss of key employees, and attraction of external talent.

From "Cong" to "Zhong" — Mature Organization

Finally, there is the mature organization—systematic company, equivalent to the "Zhong" phase in "Ren·Cong·Zhong." Mature organizations already possess organizational capabilities, with their systems, processes, culture, and teams becoming more mature. Such organizational capabilities allow the enterprise to gain strong support in both economies of scale and continuous growth.

After years of refinement and testing, the cultural characteristics accumulated by mature organizations are clear and unique, whether these cultural aspects are positive or negative. All human capital management methods at this stage are immersed in this cultural atmosphere, either being influenced or overshadowed by it. Whether out of compliance considerations or other needs, organizations at this stage will raise the banner of "people-orientedness," at least making some surface efforts in areas like humanistic care.

If being people-oriented, there is a question of whether people are seen as "costs" or "capital." Some enterprises based on overall cost pre-strategies, due to market and competition requirements, must compress all kinds of costs as much as possible, so "being people-oriented" remains only at the spiritual level; some knowledge-intensive industries where intellectual capital controls or even binds capital, "being people-oriented" must be a means to enrich intellectual capitalists materially.

Organizations that can truly achieve the transformation from "Ren" to "Zhong" can realize the harmony and balance of being people-oriented (including costs and capital) at any stage. In some highly substitutable general positions, in order to reasonably control human capital, salary levels adopt strategies of following or lagging behind market levels, supported by standardized training methods and strict elimination mechanisms. This ensures sufficient backup candidates for promotion and training in those highly substitutable job positions, thereby satisfying strategic cost requirements. In some less substitutable key positions, to attract and incentivize human capital, salary levels adopt strategies of being relatively leading in the market, supported by star-like talent management models, thus meeting strategic and business growth needs.

In fact, any successful "Zhong"-type organization adopts a mixed approach in its human capital management model to create a reasonable talent structure within the organization, which matches the employment requirements of different organizations. In mature organizations, there exist three different talent selection methods: First, the enterprise-centric system, where demands in strategy, R&D, technology, customers, etc., make it best for key talents to be cultivated and promoted internally within the enterprise. For example, in some industries with core technologies or unique management models, a typical case is Procter & Gamble, where over 70% of mid-to-high-level management talents are promoted internally. Second, the market-centric system, where relatively mature and stable businesses in terms of operations and markets do not require long-term experience in the enterprise for most job positions but instead require deep understanding of industries and sectors. Talents recruited from the market can quickly identify the core responsibilities and critical points of their duties, such as marketing and finance talents in some fast-moving consumer goods industries. By flexibly mastering professional skills and borrowing advanced management models from the industry, they can achieve twice the result with half the effort. Third, the talent-centric system, which simply put means designing positions for specific talents. This might seem hard to understand, but within a specific period of time for an enterprise, a necessary talent can push or even turn the organization around. To retain such key talents, discovering their intrinsic motivation and unleashing their individual talents by designing a suitable job position for them is sometimes necessary. Before Li Kaifu joined Google, Google designed the position of Senior Advisor for him, which not only properly facilitated the recruitment of high-level talents from competitors but also reduced some legal disputes. This is the essence of the talent-centric system. Successful "Zhong"-type organizations can skillfully combine these three human capital management models, achieving the goal of being people-oriented while meeting the organizational needs during a specific period.

Many organizations consider institutional construction as the primary task of human capital management. These organizations continuously issue and update various management rules, striving for more refined systems, hoping for comprehensive coverage of every detail. In fact, the institutional construction of organizations and business growth are like a yin-yang balanced bagua. There is always a mutual adaptation and game between the two, striving for harmony. If business growth is rapid but institutional construction lags behind, it will affect the balance between efficiency and order, plunging the enterprise into chaos, providing opportunities for "taking advantage of loopholes." On the other hand, if institutional construction is overly detailed, exceeding the needs of business growth, the organization will have to pay a high management cost. Just like when the organization's business does not break through and stagnates, instead of concentrating efforts on studying the market and technology, it spends its days shouting about quantified evaluations, which lacks persuasiveness.

Therefore, human capital management and institutional construction are meant to meet the strategic growth and business needs of enterprises, not to "control" people. Otherwise, "people" enclosed in a frame would become "prisoners." And "prisoners" cannot talk about being people-oriented, and no matter what, they will not transform from "talent" to "wealth."

Management is used to allocate resources, and organizations are used to allocate power. Successful "Zhong"-type human capital management is not only good at optimally configuring resources carried by people but also achieves synchronization with the organization through rule-making and institutional construction, enhancing the organization's sensitivity and creating the greatest "human wealth" value through appropriate use of power.

When the organization realizes the transformation from "Ren" to "Zhong" and from "talent" to "wealth," the complete organizational value can also achieve a joint enhancement of "on-balance-sheet value" and "off-balance-sheet value."
