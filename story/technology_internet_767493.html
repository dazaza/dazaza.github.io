Pandora CFO: Apple, Microsoft, Google, Twitter Are All Threats

by anonymous on 2013-10-19 11:54:40

Pandora, the largest online radio service, has long been the envy of the music industry. Some artists complain that it pays them too little. Record companies have strongly resisted Pandora's lobbying of Congress to pass the "Internet Radio Fairness Act" because the act could reduce their royalty income. Music publishers have refused to provide Pandora with their music catalogs.

Amidst all this controversy, iTunes Radio was born. Unlike Pandora, iTunes Radio directly signs contracts with record companies and seeks global development. In the first five days following its launch with iOS 7, iTunes Radio garnered over 11 million unique listeners. Meanwhile, Pandora had 72.7 million active users last month.

A foreign media outlet interviewed Pandora's Chief Financial Officer, Mike Herring, who joined Pandora from Adobe in February this year. Below is the interview transcript.

Q: Why do you think competitors like Apple don't pose a threat to Pandora?

Herring: We didn't say they wouldn't pose a threat to Pandora. Don't misunderstand us; we take them very seriously and consider them real threats. Remember, over the years we've faced many significant threats, from startups to Microsoft to Google, and now Apple and Twitter this year.

We absolutely see iTunes as our competitor, but we believe our service is top-notch and we do it better than anyone else. The new entrants recently have all been well-funded big companies, and they have other purposes besides providing a good music experience, such as selling phones or download software. We sell download software too, but our top priority isn't to sell as much software as possible. That's how we differ from them.

Q: Pandora pays lower royalties to record companies than Apple does, because you don't sign direct contracts with them but follow government regulations. You're also striving to further reduce royalties. What recent progress has there been?

Herring: We're not trying to reduce royalties, but rather create a fair royalty payment mechanism across all distribution channels. We've clearly stated that the royalties we will pay in the future won't be lower than what we pay now, and they will increase year by year. However, no one accepts our view due to a lack of trust. The current situation is that we need to engage in constructive conversations. And achieving that will take some time.

People oppose Pandora's stance, thinking we always want to pay artists less. In fact, we hope artists receive more compensation. We also understand that this distrust stems from over a decade of difficulties in the music industry. First, there's the issue of piracy in the music industry, which still runs rampant both domestically and internationally. Second, some download platforms have emerged, especially iTunes, which has harmed the entire CD business. Those who've experienced these negative events find it hard to listen to reasonable opinions.

Q: Yes, many artists have criticized Pandora.

Herring: The amount of royalties we pay to artists each year is substantial. Last year, we paid $250 million in royalties, which accounts for a quarter of the total royalties paid globally by radio stations to artists. However, we only hold about 7% of the U.S. radio market share. What we're saying is that we pay artists quite a lot. Yet, we are often criticized for paying too low royalties or not proportionate to our market share.

Q: How can you pay copyright holders more reasonable royalties?

Herring: We are the only ones who pay royalties per song, while satellite radio only needs to pay 10% of its revenue as royalties, and cable TV doesn't have to pay anything at all. I'm not asking for zero payments like cable TV. I just want everyone to pay royalties equally. Pandora pays royalties per song, and the problem with this approach is that our costs are high. We hold over 70% of the internet radio market share partly because we have an excellent product, and partly because the competition in this high-cost market is fierce.

Frankly speaking, only companies like Apple, with ample cash reserves and good financial conditions, can eventually build truly competitive services.

Q: What is the biggest challenge you face on your learning curve at Pandora?

Herring: All the issues boil down to music licensing. Very few people truly understand this - not the artists, nor the public. The current situation is chaotic, but where there are significant differences of opinion, there are also great opportunities. I've had in-depth conversations with musicians, record companies, and agents, and unfortunately, with lawyers. I think one of Pandora's opportunities is to consolidate all these views over the next few years and then find solutions that benefit everyone. Let the artists get appropriate returns, let services like Pandora have good development opportunities, and let listeners have proper ways to enjoy music.

Q: How far are you from this ideal state?

Herring: We've just started. It's too early to reach that state, but we're starting from the ruins. Initially, the issue of piracy set a bad tone for internet music. The problem isn't that Pandora promotes piracy, but that the government hasn't resolved historical copyright issues well, resulting in lawyers setting policies and engaging in debates instead of service providers and artists having direct dialogues. In the future, one of our opportunities is to move away from legal disputes and engage in commercial negotiations about fair compensation.

Q: When Brian McAndrews took over as Pandora's new CEO, what direction did he chart for Pandora?

Pandora's Chief Financial Officer, Mike Herring

Herring: Joe Kennedy (Pandora's previous CEO) achieved remarkable success during his nine years leading Pandora. Employees in Pandora's hallways are always cheerful. They admire Joe deeply. Brian brings exciting things to Pandora.

Brian is a highly respected executive in the technology industry who always integrates media, technology, and advertising perfectly to maximize effectiveness. Moreover, Brian once founded a digital marketing company called aQuantive, which was acquired by Microsoft for $6 billion in 2007.

Q: Speaking of entering new markets, Pandora's international expansion has been difficult due to the lack of direct contracts with record companies. Is there a possibility of moving beyond the U.S., perhaps to New Zealand and Australia?

Herring: Simply put, international expansion is not a short-term endeavor. What we aim to do in Australia and New Jersey isn't just to open a promising market, but to prove to the world that Pandora's entry, with a reasonable royalty structure, will have a very positive impact on the local music industry. If we can prove our methods are correct in the experimental fields of Australia and New Zealand, we will be able to smoothly enter other markets.

Those internet music services that have directly signed contracts with record companies in international markets haven't performed well. Just look at Spotify's financial report from last year, and you'll see the situation isn't optimistic.
