Microsoft's Choice: Bigger or Stronger?

by anonymous on 2013-09-11 13:48:02

This article is from the New York Times, analyzing the challenges Microsoft faces after acquiring Nokia's phone division. One perspective suggests that Microsoft's extensive product range can generate significant synergy; another viewpoint argues that having to consider other products while developing each one prevents Microsoft from making the best decisions. The article expresses concern that in an industry moving towards specialization, the next CEO of Microsoft may struggle to manage its increasingly vast product line. Should Microsoft aim to become bigger or stronger? This is the challenge left for the next CEO.

Many in the business world believe Microsoft should reduce its number of products, yet it is expanding significantly instead.

This expansion comes from the $7.2 billion acquisition of Nokia's phone division. Once this deal is finalized early next year, not only will Microsoft’s workforce increase by 30%, but it will also add a new and large hardware department to its already diverse product line—a move that stands out in today’s industry which values focus over breadth.

From academics to former Microsoft employees, there is widespread concern. Over the past decade, a series of missed opportunities and disappointing investments—such as in smartphones, tablets, and internet search—have led many to believe that Microsoft should take a more focused and agile path to better counteract the disruptive technologies that are gradually eroding its foundation.

Harvard Business School professor David Yoffie points out how difficult it is to become a solid large technical conglomerate.

Thirteen years ago, due to Microsoft's overwhelming market power, its competitors and a federal judge both demanded its breakup. Now, Microsoft's problem isn't excessive power, but rather wanting to do too much.

Microsoft already owns a video game console, the second-largest search engine, a mainstream web portal, a substantial enterprise software business, PC operating systems, cloud computing services, and various application softwares. The company operates in areas that are the specialties of its competitors like Google, Yahoo, Oracle, Apple, and Nintendo, a situation that has overwhelmed CEO Steve Ballmer, who has decided to step down within the next year.

Microsoft's complexity makes the task of finding Ballmer's successor even more challenging, as it requires extraordinary skills, including familiarity with both enterprise and consumer markets, and knowledge of software, hardware, and internet services. Ballmer recently announced a thorough reorganization of Microsoft to enhance the company's agility, but its massive product portfolio remains unchanged.

"This will make management more difficult, regardless of who the successor is," says Yoffie. Before Ballmer announced his retirement, he and Bill Gates had quietly acknowledged the difficulty of finding a new leader for Microsoft. According to someone who discussed the topic with Gates a few years ago, the Microsoft chairman said that if he could find someone who could do better, he would replace Ballmer. Ballmer made a similar statement several years ago as well.

In 2000, despite Microsoft's simpler product line at the time, Federal Judge Thomas Penfield Jackson ruled that Microsoft should be split into two companies—one for Windows and one for applications. However, the ruling was overturned when the court found that Jackson's comments to the media tarnished the legal proceedings.

For years afterward, many experts, business professors, and Microsoft insiders reflected on whether a split would have made Microsoft more agile and competitive.

One argument supporting the split is the so-called "strategy tax" within Microsoft, where product divisions prioritize decisions that benefit other Microsoft products over what might be the best decision overall.

A frequently cited example is the Office product group's reluctance to release full versions of Word and Excel for iPad and Android devices. In theory, this decision should favor Microsoft tablets running Windows, but in practice, the effect hasn't materialized.

Vivek Wadhwa of Stanford Law School suggests that Microsoft should split because he doesn't believe that consumer and enterprise businesses can coexist harmoniously. "Their strategies are completely different," he says: "Because Microsoft tries to protect its enterprise market, it cannot fully unleash its potential in the consumer market."

While there are successful corporate conglomerates like GE and Berkshire Hathaway, fewer exist in the technology sector. IBM, which sold its personal computer business to Lenovo ten years ago, was once considered a model for tech conglomerates. Although IBM offers many products and services, Wadhwa says the company's focus on enterprise customers makes it easier to manage.

Another larger example is Samsung. This South Korean giant spans electronics, life insurance, and petrochemicals. However, these businesses operate highly independently. Despite being a primary competitor to Samsung in the smartphone market, Apple still relies on Samsung for screens and semiconductors.

Investors view Microsoft's unprofitable or less profitable businesses as a drag on stock prices and call for their removal. Nomura Securities analyst Richard Sherlund, who has long studied Microsoft, believes Xbox video games and Bing search engines would be good candidates.

Sherlund speculates that Microsoft might transfer Bing to Facebook, with which it has a partnership. Microsoft's cumulative losses in search and online services have exceeded $17 billion.

However, although Microsoft has sold some smaller businesses in the past, such as the travel website Expedia in the 1990s, there is no indication that it is interested in abandoning its major products.

Microsoft's rationale is that its extensive product line generates significant synergy. Xbox uses a variant of the Windows operating system, and Bing's search service is tightly integrated with Windows, Xbox, and the Windows Phone mobile operating system.

There is also the argument that Microsoft's competitors may realize they need to develop areas once considered outside their expertise, just like Microsoft. Google acquired Motorola Mobility to help enter the hardware field. Apple has been aggressively acquiring startups to improve its online map services. And Amazon, once solely focused on consumers, has developed into a key provider of enterprise cloud services.
