Improper use of drugs for anorectal diseases can lead to cancer_Zhengzhou Anorectal Disease Hospital

by anonymous on 2011-04-12 10:58:08

Summer is the high season for intestinal diseases. Diseases of the anorectum are often referred to as the "source of all diseases" and are among the most common human illnesses. Constipation, hemorrhoids, anal fistulas, perianal abscesses, and other anorectal diseases pose significant risks to many people's intestines and can even develop into colorectal cancer. Recently, a hospital in Zhengzhou collaborated with the promotion team of An’s Therapy under the Ministry of Health's "Hundred Projects in Ten Years Plan" to launch its first anorectal disease screening campaign. The event drew significant attention and active participation from the general public. However, through surveys and statistics of patients who came for examination during the campaign, the expert group discovered some worrying phenomena regarding medication safety for common anorectal diseases such as hemorrhoids, anal fistulas, and diarrhea. For example, Ms. Yang, 25 years old, came for examination. She claimed that she had been relying on colon-cleansing tea and detoxifying beauty products for weight loss since three years ago. After consuming them, she experienced diarrhea, and after several months, her weight decreased. However, six months later, Ms. Yang found that if she stopped drinking the colon-cleansing tea, she would experience difficulty defecating, so she had no choice but to continue drinking it long-term to aid bowel movements. Recently, however, her constipation worsened, and drinking the tea was no longer effective. She tried various detoxification products advertised as effective for constipation, which instead caused bloating and abdominal pain. Later, she learned online that Zhengzhou Gangtai Anorectal Hospital was conducting an anorectal disease screening campaign, so she came for a free examination. A colonoscopy revealed that a section of her small intestine had completely necrotized, losing its intestinal motility function, leading to intestinal obstruction. Regrettably, the doctors could only remove this section of her small intestine and connect the two cut ends of the intestine to reconstruct a smooth digestive tract. Ms. Yang's attending physician, Director Zhang, explained that now many young women who value beauty misuse detoxification products as weight-loss drugs, taking them long-term to achieve weight loss through diarrhea, believing that this method can "flush out" fat from their bodies. This practice, however, is extremely dangerous because these products often contain stimulating laxative components, and some unscrupulous manufacturers add banned ingredients to pursue "efficacy." They also avoid discussing indications and side effects in their promotions, misleading consumers in reality. Long-term use of these products can lead to dependency, causing intestinal function decline, triggering melanosis coli, and may even induce colon cancer. Director Zhang warned young women that constipation should be regulated through diet. If eating vegetables and fruits does not help, they should seek medical attention at a hospital promptly. They should carefully choose various "detoxification," "colon cleansing" weight-loss foods, and health supplements. Patients with anorectal diseases, unlike those with acute trauma or major detected diseases, often lack urgency in treatment. Additionally, due to insufficient scientific knowledge dissemination about this type of disease in our country, most patients do not take it seriously enough and usually handle symptoms based on others' experiences and advice. Although these experiences occasionally match the symptoms, more people delay the best treatment opportunities, complicating their conditions and requiring longer treatments. Director Zhang appealed that anorectal diseases should be taken seriously, as even "minor issues" like hemorrhoids and diarrhea can come with significant risks. The current state of普及 (popularization) of anorectal disease prevention and control knowledge in our country is not optimistic. It is estimated that the incidence of rectal cancer alone in our country is approximately fourteen cases per hundred thousand people, while urban residents have a much higher incidence than the national average, and this rate increases annually. Popularizing anorectal disease knowledge, guiding citizens to use medications correctly, and treating anorectal diseases has become an urgent issue.
