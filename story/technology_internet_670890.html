The news that Google might withdraw from China triggered a shock in public opinion regarding the famous video website, and a hotly debated topic was whether Google made an accurate "trade" decision. Without a doubt, Google is a commercial company, and despite the sacred cultural mantle that it or public opinion has draped over it, commercial value will always be its unavoidable mission as a listed company. On the surface, withdrawing from China seems to be a decision completely contrary to commercial law (even though its sales in China are still just a fraction of its global revenue), so the former CEO of Microsoft China, Zhang Yaqin, stepped out to say that with so many netizens in China, this is a very foolish decision. Jack Ma, on the other hand, believed that Google's abandonment of the Chinese market is the biggest failure. In the eyes of these two CEOs who are familiar with the Chinese market, such a large market means that difficulties are only temporary, and giving up easily is the stupidest move. However, if one carefully examines Google's core competitiveness, there is ample commercial rationality for its withdrawal from China.

Google was not the first company to commercialize search engines, but it later grew into a global internet giant by overturning the previous practice of search engine companies determining web page rankings solely based on advertising investments. Google grew into a towering tree because of the absolute objectivity and accuracy of its search results. All the efforts Google later made in technology and products were aimed at allowing users to quickly find the information they needed. Google did not need to consider what content users required; Google only knew that as long as all the information that could be accessed online was on the internet, it would succeed.

In other words, due to Google's significant technical advantage in the presentation of content in search engines, the freer and more open the flow of information, the greater the commercial value Google would obtain. Therefore, even at the risk of legal challenges, Google proceeded with book digitization, and despite public protests, it pursued street view searches. Even in emerging platforms for information flow like videos, blogs, and mobile operating systems, Google directly participated in content platform construction to promote the liquidity and openness of digital information as quickly as possible. Why else would Google offer Android without charging for OS licensing fees?

By promoting the free flow of information, this became the most critical foundation of Google's success and its core winning formula.

However, in the Chinese market, this winning formula failed. google.cn’s selective content searching goes completely against its corporate mission of promoting the free flow of information. Some say this is a moral issue, which it may be, but it is also a significant commercial problem. Because in a competitive environment where information is artificially filtered, Google's winning formula becomes useless. Catering to users by providing customized content has never been Google's strength (Google News' comment feature ended without any issues, and Google Finance is not the world's best).

Therefore, despite the size of the Chinese market, if Google cannot leverage its core competitiveness, it will forever be a secondary player to Baidu in China. More critically, this act of interfering with search results would cause significant damage to its global brand image. The commercial returns that google.cn could bring compared to Google's brand value are negligible.

There are additional benefits to Google's withdrawal from the Chinese market. Although Google claims "don't be evil," there are voices in many places around the world accusing Google of doing evil. The EU data privacy protection organization has conducted legitimate investigations into Google, Japanese legal experts have jointly protested against Google Street View, and the American IT industry has started worrying about Google becoming a new monopoly. The accusations of Google doing evil are not light. In this situation, Google's withdrawal from China inadvertently shifts attention and reduces the pressure applied by various organizations. Google gets to re-establish itself as a cultural benchmark.

Because of market rules and currency exchange rates, a 10% increase in brand reputation in the European and American markets will certainly have more commercial value than a 100% increase in its share in China. It may seem to defy commercial logic, but when weighing two harms, choosing the lesser one, Google's actions actually conform most closely to commercial logic.
