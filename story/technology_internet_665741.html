In February 1944, Charles L. McNary, who had been Wendell Willkie's running mate four years earlier, died, and Willkie himself died in October of that year, meaning it is the only time in U.S. history when both nominees of a party died at either end of the next election cycle. The shortest-lived Vice Presidents: James S. Sherman, William H. Taft’s Vice President, died on October 30, 1912, just a week before election day while running for re-election. His electoral votes were taken by Nicholas Butler, although Taft did not nominate a replacement before election day. Until the 25th Amendment was passed in 1967, when a Vice President died, there was no mechanism in the Constitution to fill the vacancy. Only two Vice Presidents have resigned from the office: John C. Calhoun to take a seat in Congress in December 1832 and Spiro T. Agnew in October 1973 after being placed on indictment on charges of taking bribes while Governor of Maryland. There are currently five living former Vice Presidents: Walter Mondale, George H.W. Bush, Dan Quayle, Al Gore, and Dick Cheney. Daniel Tompkins, who served as James Monroe's Vice President, died just ninety days after leaving office in 1825, making him the shortest-lived Former Vice President. The second being Nelson A. Rockefeller who died 736 days after leaving office on January 26, 1979, just two years and six days after leaving office as Gerald Ford's Vice President. Although if Ford had won, he would not have been in office when he died as Rockefeller had been replaced as Ford's running mate by Robert Dole. The current Vice President, Joe Biden, was the sixth oldest to be sworn into office at 66 years and 61 days, the oldest being Alben Barkley who was 71 years and 57 days. The youngest is John C. Breckenridge who was just 36 years and 47 days old.

Currently Living Vice Presidents: Unlike the President, no Vice President has been assassinated, and all have died of natural causes. Vice Presidents Who Have Died in Office: No Vice President since Sherman has died in office. Although if they had been elected to the office, Charles W. Fairbanks would have died in office in June 1918, had Charles Evans Hughes won the 1916 election. Vice President Charles Curtis, who had served under Herbert Hoover, would have died in office in February 1936 had Hoover won a second term in 1932. Garret A. Hobart, William McKinley’s first Vice President, passed away on November 21, 1899. Thomas A. Hendricks, Grover Cleveland’s first Vice President, died on November 25, 1885, within seven months of entering office. Eight Vice Presidents have served two full terms: Daniel Tompkins, Thomas Marshall, Richard Nixon, George H. W. Bush, Al Gore, Dick Cheney, John Nance Garner, and John Adams. Clinton's successor, Elbridge Gerry, James Madison’s second Vice President, passed away on November 23, 1814. William Rufus King, Franklin Pierce’s Vice President, passed away after a month in office on April 18, 1853, and is the shortest-serving Vice President. George Clinton, who served both Thomas Jefferson and James Madison and passed away on April 20, 1812, became the first man either President or Vice President to die in office, and is the only man (so far) to serve under two different Presidents.
