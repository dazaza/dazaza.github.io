Post-80s Graduate Returns to Village to Open Kindergarten, Says Starting a Business at Home is Tough

by o6nihao8h on 2012-03-06 19:06:38

For the initial intention of setting up this rural kindergarten, as the name "common people's kindergarten" reveals, I want to fairly popularize "common people's quality education" in today's Chinese countryside starting from the early childhood stage. This mainly includes the morality, living habits, language habits, creative thinking, and physical health of rural children. In this process, courses related to rural culture, geography, science, art, and more will be offered, incorporating the advantages of urban early childhood education, so that developing children can begin to rebuild a new local culture from themselves, changing their own and the countryside's current situation at the spiritual level.

In October, I was ready to return home and set up a kindergarten. My parents' doubts were very clear and realistic: first, setting up a kindergarten lacks funds, and I even find it difficult to solve my own personal life problems. Second, postgraduate studies should be a symbol of status in our family. With me making such a move, this status becomes a burden. Third, I am unable to solve my own personal life and marriage issues.

It is a time when spring rain brings cooling weather, yet this rural courtyard located in western China was unusually bustling. More than 20 rural children cheered and jumped out of the classroom, filling the not-so-large rural courtyard with laughter and joy immediately.

I did not expect that upon my arrival in the village to start a kindergarten, I could enroll six children, later increasing to nine. In fact, I was already very happy, indicating that some villagers were beginning to trust me, which greatly increased my confidence. All things are difficult at the start. Real pressures almost made me unable to hold on. But I firmly believe that "as long as you persevere, there will surely be success."

Due to differences in educational philosophy and starting points, my friend and I were forced to temporarily part ways at the end of April 2009. Of course, he later became one of the most powerful witnesses to the establishment of my kindergarten. I then drifted around in Lanzhou helping others with exhibitions, publishing books, and creating sculptures to address my own survival issues.

In July 2008, I went to Beijing alone, with an empty suitcase but full of hope. Passing by 798, I was excited; this contemporary art "temple" that had been legendary in books and online was now within reach. That year was a time of grand celebrations for the country, filled with festive revelry, but for those of us who were part of the "Beijing drifters," it carried unspeakable pain.

"The Common People's Kindergarten" continues my desire to speak for more grassroots people when I left Beijing, and it is further improving. The urban-rural disparity in China has worsened the situation in rural areas. Under the existing conditions in rural areas, forming a professional and stable teaching team for rural kindergartens is quite challenging, and these realities affect the daily lives of rural kindergartens, let alone their ideals.

This reporter Zhang Peng took the photograph. In my mind, all the children who come to the kindergarten are equal, regardless of whether their parents are village officials or workers. "The Common People's Kindergarten" should not be just my kindergarten; rural education should have its own characteristics, and this place should be an experimental base for rural education.

The days spent with these children bring me great joy, and they have sustained me until today. Now the kindergarten has initially taken shape, with more than 20 children.

Persistence

After painstakingly jumping out of the "farm gate" and obtaining a graduate degree, choosing to return to the countryside to be a "children's king" (teacher), Hu Jianqiang's actions are admirable. However, we cannot deny that among tens of millions of college students nationwide, Hu Jianqiang is in the minority, a rare case. We all know that university graduates going to the grassroots, going to the countryside, is a key link in solving the employment problem of university graduates, cracking the "urban disease" dilemma, and promoting the construction of a new countryside. The government, education departments, and public opinion have always strongly advocated and introduced various incentive policies, but with little effect. Let alone university students from the city, even rural university students born and raised here would rather wander in crowded cities, labeled as the "ant tribe," than return to their hometowns and families.

To be honest, I cannot answer everyone's doubts and suspicions. Just like some questions cannot be solved by mere talk, I am too lazy to deal with those who look down on me and can only solve all the problems I encounter. The first step was to conduct a survey of rural early childhood education. To ensure that the future kindergarten could have a scale, I spent tens of thousands of yuan to buy a vacant courtyard from my neighbor that had been unused for 30 years. Of course, the fundraising process was full of difficulties and setbacks, during which I received substantial support from my father. Subsequently, with the sole support of my second brother, we organized the yard and classrooms currently used by the kindergarten, purchasing tables, benches, blackboards, and other kindergarten teaching aids. I don't know how to convince my family, nor do I need to convince others in the village. By taking real action, I truly started the kindergarten, appearing in a place where people thought I shouldn't be and in a profession where they thought I couldn't handle it. "Graduate Principal" Hu Jianqiang and his students. At present, it is the most difficult time for this rural kindergarten.

Of course, this is just my ideal. I face many challenges, much like almost all rural kindergartens in China. Relying solely on myself or the kindergarten itself to sustain is impossible. Attempting profit-driven early childhood education among farmers—the lowest class—sounds unreliable. I'm not foolish enough to return from Beijing to start such an entrepreneurial venture in the countryside.

Although personal life issues naturally resolved over time, during the following long period, I was pondering what I could actually do, what I wanted to achieve, what stance an intellectual should take when speaking, and more. Some trivial encounters in Beijing also constantly pricked me. Therefore, leaving Beijing wasn't an escape for me.

This young man born in 1981, a rural "post-80s," relied on his talent and efforts to graduate smoothly two years ago from the Northwest Normal University's Fine Arts Department. Afterwards, he went to Beijing and worked as an exhibition assistant for a well-known gallery in the famous 798 Art District, earning a monthly salary of 3000 yuan. Like many "Beijing drifters," he carried dreams, hoping to carve out a world in Beijing.

In February 2009, just before the Spring Festival, I left Beijing. The winter wind in Beijing felt like a knife cutting through.

On the low kitchen mud wall to the east, one can easily identify the black portraits of celebrities like Mao Zedong, Lu Xun, and Tao Xingzhi, all vividly painted by the founder of this rural kindergarten.

He is Hu Jianqiang, the teacher of more than 20 rural children and the young principal of a struggling rural kindergarten. Recently, this reporter visited this western rural kindergarten located in Yebao Village, Qin'an County, Gansu Province, listening to the heartfelt voice of this rural college student. He said, "As someone from the grassroots, if I still have ideals, I should speak for more grassroots people"—

The reason for painting these portraits is because he believes they are all "people who spoke for the lower classes."

As someone from the grassroots, if I still have ideals, I should speak for more grassroots people. I feel uneasy about being called a so-called graduate yet having nothing remarkable to offer, while simultaneously feeling proud that there are worse and more boring graduates, overseas returnees, and postgraduates from prestigious universities than me. I don't have much hope for my own rise from the grassroots, but I hope more people can remain acutely aware of themselves. Education is something I can do, and going to places where education is needed is self-evident. Thus, I thought of my hometown—a place that remains poor but beautiful. Starting with the children is the first step.

At a media debate, someone criticized me: Is your decision to return to the countryside to establish a kindergarten too idealistic? This question is so silly that I cannot answer it. Everyone has ideals. Some people's ideals are skyscrapers, luxury cars, and beauties. Others' ideals are power and money. Some people's ideal is simply to have enough food to eat. And some people's ideals are not about talking but doing.

Beijing Drifter

This 29-year-old young man began his ideal experiment. He tried to rebuild rural culture to help rural children regain confidence and reconnect with their cultural roots. He collected animal and plant specimens and treated embroidered shoe pads made by rural women as artworks hung in the children's dormitories. In the spring and autumn months, he took the children up the mountain to sketch, understand their hometown rivers, and make rural children love the countryside and their hometowns. He even conceived the idea of compiling a local textbook...

The reason for painting these portraits is because he believed they were all "people who spoke for the lower classes."

As someone from the grassroots, if I still have ideals, I should speak for more grassroots people. I feel uneasy about being called a so-called graduate yet having nothing remarkable to offer, while simultaneously feeling proud that there are worse and more boring graduates, overseas returnees, and postgraduates from prestigious universities than me. I don't have much hope for my own rise from the grassroots, but I hope more people can remain acutely aware of themselves. Education is something I can do, and going to places where education is needed is self-evident. Thus, I thought of my hometown—a place that remains poor but beautiful. Starting with the children is the first step.

My first job in Beijing was helping a family of four Americans set up an exhibition. After working for three days, I didn't receive a single cent in compensation. The first time I made money in Beijing, which now seems both disappointing and beautiful, was when a friend and I drew simple sketches for someone. We spent half a night completing ten drawings, and initially, they used four of them, earning us 40 yuan. Two months later, by chance, I entered the gallery of a famous Chinese curator as an assistant. But this also accelerated my decision to abandon the capital and choose my own path.

After arriving in Beijing, I spent two months wandering, learning, adapting, and getting up early and staying up late. First, I came into close contact with so-called contemporary art. During these two months, I visited many galleries in 798 and Caoyuan Art District, viewing exhibitions, wandering around, or looking for jobs. During this period, I also continuously attended interviews and tests, thereby realizing Beijing's prosperity and noise, diversity and complexity, sophistication and poverty, freedom and rigidity, etc. For someone who spends half their time in the city and the other half wandering in the countryside, floating between urban and rural areas, Beijing, with its unique Chinese characteristics, gave me hope. Life in Beijing belongs to a few people, more people are just trying their luck, and more unfortunately, they lose themselves, leaving their fate to random encounters.

Therefore, I think: instead of mixing days in the system, it's better to walk my own path. After finding work proved unfruitful, I prepared with a friend to co-found a kindergarten in the county town of Qin'an, and conflicts with my family gradually escalated. During this time, I conducted surveys on rural education, including early childhood education. Later, living at home became impossible, so I moved to the county town and lived with my friend. Emotions ran high, but this is the place where I was born and grew up, making it difficult and distant to do my own thing.

However, he soon realized that Beijing was too vast, and he was too insignificant, "using art as a means of livelihood is too hard." At a crossroads in life, the soil that nourished him became the source of his continued dream pursuit, "rural matters attracted me." Therefore, he resolutely returned to his hometown and founded this rural kindergarten, naming it "Common People's Kindergarten."

Back home, during the New Year, I announced my plan to open a kindergarten to my family. Initially, my family did not agree; my mother and relatives all hoped that I would find a stable job. Naturally, like everyone else, I wanted to find an ideal job, but I discovered that my repeated failures in job hunting were not entirely due to my own shortcomings. For rural college students like me, who lack social capital and family background, finding a fair cooperation platform is not easy.

Resistance

Villagers talked more: I have no skills, wandered for a few years outside and couldn't make it, and now I've returned to the village to run a kindergarten, deceiving children to earn a living.

I have never thought that being a graduate student makes me anything special. If I hadn't put down the halo of being a graduate student, I couldn't do this. Perhaps, putting aside my identity first would make things better.
