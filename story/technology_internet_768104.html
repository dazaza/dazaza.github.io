Communications, why should WeChat be made?

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 17:49:44

WeChat and Laiwang, born with different commercial genes from their parent companies, have become increasingly entangled as the conflicts between the two major empires, Tencent (A) and Alibaba (T), escalate. On one side, WeChat Pay advances step by step; on the other, Jack Ma fiercely declares "abandoning WeChat" and mobilizes the entire company to attract users for Laiwang. Can the rivalry between WeChat and Laiwang, reminiscent of the fated conflict between Zhou Yu and Lu Xiang, be avoided? More importantly, can they each achieve their own goals like plum blossoms and chrysanthemums blooming in their respective seasons? This is the main point this article seeks to explore.

The conflict between WeChat and Laiwang is directly felt by many users who find it confusing to have both apps installed on their phones. Looking back, why doesn't installing QQ and AliWangWang on a computer feel confusing? Why doesn't having QQ, WeChat, and a contact list on your phone feel disordered? That's because each has its own use case for users, and from the provider's perspective, they are positioned differently. The confusion caused by the coexistence of WeChat and Laiwang stems from their overly similar positioning.

WeChat, with over two years of early-mover advantage and a user relationship foundation provided by Tencent, is almost irreplaceable for close acquaintances' social networking. To develop a differentiated positioning, we must consider how Laiwang can carve out its own path. Alibaba envisions a future direction from e-commerce to social commerce, but does social commerce necessarily mean replicating WeChat?

Social Networking is not Only About Close Acquaintances

Firstly, we need to understand that "socialization" is not a new concept. The frequent mention of "socialization" in recent years refers to the socialization brought about by the rapid development of the internet and mobile internet into our daily lives. Thus, what exactly "socialization" entails can be explored through life experiences.

In daily life, close acquaintances' social networking is just one form of socialization. People also interact due to shared interests or topics and may even form groups. For example, Greek philosophers gathering in squares to debate is one instance, Beijing park-goers discussing seasonal dog shedding is another, and today's children attending interest classes is yet another.

Social networking sites on the internet naturally have their own unique characteristics. On QQ Space and WeChat Moments, you keep up with updates from close acquaintances. On Mofang, a community gathers avid travelers sharing travel plans, comments, and guides. Douban is undoubtedly a hub for hipsters where book reviews and movie reviews serve as important references for selecting books and movies. When you want to read a literary book, the influence of an anonymous individual on Douban could outweigh that of a childhood friend who knows your deepest secrets (unless this friend shares the same interest). In this case, it's not about who's closer but who's more knowledgeable.

Laiwang: Social Networking Based on Product Interest

Jack Ma once pointed out incisively that "the type of product purchased reflects the consumer's aspirations." Therefore, shopping is also driven by interests and preferences. For example, I am quite fond of traditional elements, friend A prides themselves on being "virtuous and thrifty," taking great pleasure in finding "cheap goods," while friend B, nearing house renovation, is highly interested in styles and furniture choices. These interests and preferences are at least partially expressed through shopping.

However, existing friends may not share the same product interests. The various motivational posts flooding WeChat Moments are already nauseating, let alone incessantly posting product reviews. On the other hand, people with similar product interests do not necessarily need to be friends. Having information exchange and product evaluations suffices, as content is the main focus here. Products can be categorized by type, and consumers by their consumption interests and preferences.

Product interests can vary with time and can be diverse. Groups like the "Gift Gang" who gather only during holidays and the "Thrifty Spenders" who shop smart all year round are examples. Based on Taobao's vast consumption data, recommendations for interested groups and topics can be made to consumers. As for information, product, and store evaluations, and consumption insights, brands and stores are naturally willing to have another touchpoint to reach customers, especially one so close to the moment of purchase. User-generated content (UGC) is also a trend. Taobao first needs to leverage its strength in creating platforms, then incentivize users to open up and share (here, JD's review system is worth referencing), and finally manage the content well to ensure credibility and prevent overload.

Let Go of Obsession, and New Opportunities Will Emerge

Perhaps Laiwang is reluctant to follow Wangwang's footsteps as merely a small tool used only when buyers and sellers communicate. But upon reflection, why cling to the obsession of "becoming WeChat?" Wangwang's disadvantage is QQ's advantage, and Wangwang's advantage is precisely QQ's disadvantage. The chain of close acquaintances and the chain of product relationships are inherently contradictory. Think about how much time is spent talking about products when chatting or meeting with friends. If you insist on discussing products at a friend gathering, how likely are you to be invited back next time?

A group of jewelry enthusiasts discussing this year's design trends, evaluating a lesser-known store's bracelet uniqueness, or finding out where Tiffani classics are on sale is far more natural and interesting. It's not unlikely that someone might immediately click to the purchase page after liking something during the conversation. Alipay backs the payment process.

Wangwang became merely a tool for buyer-seller dialogue partly because its basic functions were similar to QQ—communication (chatting)—and it failed to find a key path to engage users that aligned with its commercial genes.

Taobao seems to have considered building user groups where buyers could add each other as friends, but the execution was lackluster. Products represent weak relationships, and what people need more is a square rather than a one-on-one booth. Forums and Douban primarily function as platforms where a group gathers to voice opinions, with one-on-one communication as a supplement. Similarly, Alibaba's Sina Weibo is forum-like, whereas Tencent's QQ and WeChat focus more on private point-to-point communication.

Conclusion:

HandQ losing to WeChat is not because HandQ is bad, but because when the battlefield shifts from PC to mobile, people don't want a simple extension. Some features need simplification—mobile screens are too small to handle excessive information. Others need enhancement—people-centered, anytime-anywhere communication and interaction (which is also an expression of sociability). Products are not just about their utility; they reflect preferences and identity, making them conversational topics. Taobao's success on PCs cannot be simply extended. Laiwang might be one of the new paths to explore. Different commercial genes, different social scenarios and habits—Laiwang can forge its own path.
