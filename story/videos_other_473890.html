Thousands of residents ate at the feast, with tables set up in three alleys.

by anonymous on 2011-08-10 14:06:37

Yesterday, thousands of residents in Xuexi Alley Community gathered for a "Hundred Families Feast."

Yesterday morning, the residents of Xuexi Alley Community in North Yumen Street, Lianhu District, experienced the taste of eating "communal food." The "Hundred Families Feast" held in the alley attracted over a thousand residents to come and feast. While eating, the connections between neighbors became closer.

Scene: Various dishes filled the tables, with more than a thousand people eating in four or five shifts.

At 11 a.m. yesterday, Xuexi Alley Community was bustling with noise from clinking bowls and chopsticks and laughter. A closer look revealed that long tables were set up in three alleys, filled with a variety of delicious dishes. The food was very rich, including various stir-fried dishes, stews, steamed wheat meals, vegetable rolls, fried oil cakes, dumplings, buns, braised noodles, bean cakes, watermelons, and more. There were stir-fried, pan-fried, boiled, deep-fried, and steamed dishes—everything you could imagine. A rough count showed that there might be about 15 types of food on each table.

Looking at the people eating, there were elderly people holding children, young girls and boys, as well as middle-aged individuals around thirty or forty years old. Everyone squeezed together at the tables, some tasting with their chopsticks, others drinking beverages, and still others eating fruits. They chatted happily while eating.

Ms. Wang, who was serving the food, introduced that this was not an ordinary dinner party but the community's "Hundred Families Feast." "Each alley is about 500 meters long, and we used hundreds of tables." Afterwards, a resident responsible for washing dishes smiled and said, "Everyone ate so well today, one group after another, totaling four or five groups. Many neighbors from other communities also came when they smelled the food. I estimate that there were about a thousand people."

Story: To eat "communal food," some working professionals even took leave.

"I saw many aunts and uncles from my childhood, it was so exciting, truly a beautiful memory," said Ms. Li, who also grew up in Xuexi Alley Community. She now lives in Beijing but recently returned to Xi'an with her son to visit the World Horticultural Exposition. When she heard about the "Hundred Families Feast" yesterday noon, she brought her son over.

Ms. Chen, who works in the Hi-Tech Zone, said she was especially excited about this event and took half a day off to return and participate. "I just wanted to feel the harmonious atmosphere among neighbors."

"Are there any more braised noodles? Are there any more?" At around 12 p.m., a group of students in school uniforms ran over, shouting as they came. They sat down and within minutes, several bowls of hot braised noodles were served.

These students said they had known about the "Hundred Families Feast" in advance, so they didn't bring lunch to school and came directly after school to experience the feeling of eating "communal food." "We feel everyone is so friendly, it feels like a big family."

Preparation: 300 residents worked together, 40+ households contributed signature dishes.

It is quite rare to have so many people eating together. What happened? It turns out that this was the second "Neighbor Festival" organized by Xuexi Alley Community.

Community Party Branch Secretary and Director Bai Xiulan introduced that since last year, every summer, the residents' committee organizes the community residents to celebrate the "Neighbor Festival" by having a "Hundred Families Feast." "Everyone is usually busy and finds it hard to gather together. We just want to find a time for everyone to gather, promote neighborly feelings, and help each other when something happens. This year also happens to be the 90th birthday of the Communist Party, so we celebrated together."

A community resident, Grandma Li, said that about 20 days ago, Director Bai announced on the broadcast: "The second 'Neighbor Festival' is coming! Please bring homemade delicacies to share with everyone. If you don't like cooking, you can voluntarily contribute money to buy ingredients. Let's all have a meal together, gather, and chat."

Within a few days of the announcement, nearly 300 residents voluntarily signed up as volunteers, and the local Qingzhen Temple offered to provide chairs and tables free of charge. The volunteers expressed their willingness to cook personally on the day of the "Neighbor Festival." The day before the festival, many volunteers arrived early in one of the community's alleys, picking vegetables, frying tofu...

At 6 a.m. yesterday, the "temporary kitchen" in one of the alleys of Xuexi Alley Community was already bustling with sounds of clanging pots and pans. Volunteers started washing vegetables, cooking, washing fruits, cutting them into pieces and placing them in fruit plates, arranging chairs and tables in the alley. A 50-year-old man even rode his tricycle to deliver a box of clean water...

Bai Xiulan introduced that about 40 families brought their "signature dishes," and more than 100 families each contributed 50 yuan or 100 yuan to buy various vegetables, drinks, fruits, etc.

Tradition: Eating the "Hundred Families Feast" to celebrate the "Neighbor Festival" will continue year by year.

This "Hundred Families Feast" lasted from 11 a.m. to 1 p.m. After finishing the meal, people still gathered in small groups chatting and exchanging feelings.

Several young women who married out of the community sat on the stone steps by the wall holding hands. "We used to often play here together. It's been many years since we've seen each other. Today, it's nice to meet again, and there's so much to talk about." "Seeing the lively scene today and the warmth between neighbors, I am really moved," Bai Xiulan said. Next summer, the community will celebrate the third "Neighbor Festival" in the same way. This festival will become a tradition of Xuexi Alley Community, passed down year by year.

Intern Ren Ting, reporter Qing Rongbo
