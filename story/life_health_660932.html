Don't Easily Pull Teeth When Having a Toothache

Author: Station Chief Source: Internet Time: January 13, 2012, 21:04:17 Hits: 2021 times

What should you do when you have a toothache? Pull it out? That's foolish. If it can be cured, then that's good. Once it's pulled, the pain may stop, but how will you eat?

Toothache or trigeminal neuralgia. As the saying goes: "A toothache is not a disease, but it can be excruciating." Some toothaches can be relieved after treatment by a dentist, but some "dental problems" persist even after the tooth is pulled, and are eventually found to be caused by trigeminal neuralgia.

In most cases, a toothache may originate from the nerve and blood vessels inside the tooth (also known as the pulp) or inflammation and infection of the periodontal tissue. The pain caused by periodontitis often feels like a dull throbbing pain, while the pain caused by pulpitis presents in various ways. It worsens with cold or hot stimulation, the pain location is deep without electric-like pain, and headaches often accompany this condition. At this time, it’s best to see a dentist, who can discover dental issues through an oral examination, such as cavities, broken roots, or damaged crowns, and provide corresponding treatment.

Sometimes, the cause of the toothache isn't the tooth itself, but rather trigeminal neuralgia. Trigeminal neuralgia often occurs in the second branch (maxillary nerve) and the third branch (mandibular nerve), and the pain in these two branches is particularly easy to confuse with tooth pain.

Therefore, it's suggested that one shouldn’t easily pull teeth when having a toothache. Further differential diagnosis should be conducted before treatment. Remember, no matter how good artificial teeth are, they're never as good as natural ones!
