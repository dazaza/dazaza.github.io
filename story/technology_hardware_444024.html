The solar wind particles have already been intercepted by a stronger particle flow in the interstellar space.

by prkwe1mwc6 on 2011-06-10 14:18:11

After flying for more than 11 billion miles (approximately 17.7 billion kilometers), the spacecraft Voyager 1 is now nearing the edge of the solar system. On December 13th, NASA announced that the spacecraft will cross the boundary of the solar system within the next four years and enter interstellar space for the first time.

Currently, measurements taken by Voyager 1 at its current position show that the speed of solar wind particles there is nearly stationary. The solar wind consists of intense high-speed streams of charged particles emitted by the sun. Scientists from NASA believe that this sudden drop in the speed of solar wind particles indicates a boundary region where these particles are encountering stronger particle flows from interstellar space.

"It's clear that the solar wind has been pushed into a corner," said Edward Stone (Ed Stone), the project scientist for the Voyager mission, who hails from the California Institute of Technology in Pasadena. "Voyager 1 is approaching interstellar space."

Launched on September 5, 1977, Voyager 1 was initially designed to explore Jupiter and Saturn. However, it has continued to function well, having traveled nearly 17.7 billion kilometers through space.

The Voyager 1 spacecraft quietly glides through space. In 2004, it crossed the termination shock zone of the solar system, an area where the solar wind begins to decelerate due to contact with interstellar medium. Here, the speed of solar wind particles drops below subsonic levels for the first time, and they heat up due to collisions and friction. Beyond the termination shock lies the heliosheath, the current location of the Voyager 1 spacecraft, and the official boundary of the solar system is called the heliopause.

The heliosheath marks the edge of the Sun’s influence, characterized by strong instabilities and turbulence, which scientists refer to as the "boundary of influence" of the Sun. Once Voyager 1 crosses the heliosheath and ultimately breaks through the heliopause, it will, in a strict sense, enter interstellar space. Currently, the spacecraft is traveling at a speed of over 61,155 km/h.

NASA believes that Voyager 1 will cross this boundary layer before 2014. Officials state that once the spacecraft crosses the boundary, the number of heated particles in its current environment will rapidly decrease, replaced by cooler particles from interstellar space.

Voyager 1 carries a device called the Low-Energy Charged Particle (LECP) detector, which records the speed of particles in the surrounding environment. In August 2007, this instrument recorded the solar wind passing the spacecraft like a steady strong wind at nearly 21,000 km/h. However, since then, their speed has gradually decreased, dropping by approximately 72,420 km/h per year.

In June of this year, the solar wind detector on the Voyager spacecraft reported that the speed of the solar wind had dropped to zero. Scientists then examined the instrument to ensure it wasn't malfunctioning, as this seemed somewhat unbelievable.

"When I confirmed that we indeed recorded a zero velocity, I was amazed," said Rob Decker, the scientist responsible for the low-energy charged particle detector aboard the spacecraft, who works at the Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory (APL) in Maryland. "This is Voyager 1, a spacecraft that has been fully operational for 33 years, and now it's about to give us new surprises."

Details of this event were presented at the 2010 Fall Meeting of the American Geophysical Union held in San Francisco.

Voyager 1 is one of a pair of twin probes launched in 1977. A few weeks before Voyager 1's launch, on August 20, 1977, NASA launched Voyager 2 with the ambitious goal of exploring Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune. Both spacecraft are powered by nuclear energy.

Although Voyager 2 was launched first, due to different orbital designs, it travels slower and follows a different flight path. After completing their planetary exploration missions, Voyager 1 continued flying northward, while Voyager 2 flew southward. They will exit the solar system from above and below the plane of the solar system, respectively.

Voyager 2 is currently about 8.8 billion miles (approximately 14.1 billion kilometers) away from the Sun, traveling at a speed of approximately 56,330 km/h. According to NASA officials, in the near future, Voyager 2 will also reach the boundary of the solar system.
