Must-know: the earlier you supplement nutrition before pregnancy, the better

by childrenstory126 on 2012-02-24 17:38:07

During pregnancy, the baby lives in the mother's uterus for approximately 40 weeks. Starting from a fertilized egg that differentiates and develops, it eventually grows into an individual with well-proportioned facial features and complete internal organs, with its weight increasing to around 3200 grams.

Pregnancy is the fastest period of growth and development in a baby’s life, and it requires a great deal of nutrients, all of which come from the mother. The mother must ensure her child's healthy growth by meeting the needs of the uterus, placenta, amniotic fluid, and mammary glands. Therefore, pregnant women should start supplementing their nutrition from the time they begin planning to conceive. If the pregnant woman herself lacks sufficient nutrition, the baby will not be able to obtain enough nutrients from the mother's daily diet.

If you plan to become pregnant, then based on your personal physical condition, you should make dietary adjustments early before conception. Waiting until after becoming pregnant to pay attention would be akin to "closing the barn door after the sheep are gone."

Nutrition not only builds the foundation of your health but also has a very close relationship with the health of newborns in relation to your nutritional status before pregnancy. Good pre-pregnancy nutrition leads to higher birth weights in newborns, who tend to be healthy and lively, rarely getting sick during the perinatal period, and can even positively influence the child's intelligence. Thus, the nutritional reserve status of pregnant women before pregnancy plays a significant role in ensuring optimal birth outcomes.

Due to individual differences among women of varying constitutions, issues such as pre-pregnancy nutritional supplementation, dietary adjustment, start time, types of nutrition, and the amount of increase can vary from person to person.

Women with average constitutional and nutritional statuses should start paying attention to dietary adjustments three months to half a year before conception. They need to consume adequate amounts of high-quality protein, vitamins, minerals, trace elements, and moderate fat daily. These nutrients form the material basis for fetal growth and development.

High-quality protein refers to easily digestible proteins, such as chicken, duck, fish, lean meat, shrimp, eggs, tofu, and soy products. Vitamins A, D, C, and B are particularly important. Fresh vegetables and fruits contain abundant vitamins, minerals, and trace elements, among which calcium, iron, phosphorus, zinc, and iodine are most crucial. Calcium and phosphorus play direct roles in the formation and development of fetal bones and teeth, iron in blood-making functions, and zinc and iodine in fetal intellectual development and preventing malformations. Milk, eggs, bone soup, animal livers, shrimp shells, seafood, and nut-based foods all contain these substances. Moderate intake of fat helps in the absorption and utilization of fat-soluble vitamins.

For those who are physically weak and have poor nutritional status, pre-pregnancy dietary adjustments are even more important. It is best to start paying attention about a year before conception. Besides ensuring sufficient intake of the aforementioned nutrients, attention should also be paid to having a balanced diet without being picky or selective, reasonable food combinations, good cooking techniques, and frequently changing flavors. This should be done gradually, without rushing for results, aiming for the best possible nutritional state before pregnancy.

For those who are overweight and already in good nutritional condition, generally speaking, there is no need to add more nutrients, but the intake of high-quality protein, vitamins, minerals, and trace elements should still not be neglected. However, one should limit the consumption of foods high in fat and sugar.
