This is my draft analysis of the KA system situation for the company. Much of the content is universal, so I haven't made any revisions. Please feel free to offer suggestions!

Currently, the most important sales channel for our company's products is the KA channel. The performance of KA channel sales directly determines the overall sales of our products. For individual stores or systems, their sales performance directly decides the life or death of our products in that store or system. KA systems are generally based on category management, usually with a bottom-up elimination system. Once our product sales (or profits) fall below average, minor consequences include increased costs or difficulties in recovering payments, while severe ones may lead to clearance from the shelves, resulting in wasted prior investments, causing significant economic losses to the company and damaging the reputation of our products, group, and even our Riyuexing brand. Based on the actual situation of our company's products in the KA system, the following aspects are analyzed:

1. Preparation work before product entry into stores. Systematic investigation and selection should be conducted from the following aspects.

1) First, select suitable distributors and increase support for existing distributors.

In selecting distributors, we prioritize large distributors who typically represent well-known brands, operate a wide range of products, and have relatively lower KA cooperation fees due to scale. They are familiar with local conditions, possess rich resources, and maintain good customer relations. Additionally, they have a complete marketing team with strong KA system operation capabilities, which can effectively avoid our products being eliminated at the bottom. Secondly, choose distributors with vision, strong market operation capabilities, and potential, even if their current strength is limited. Through deep support from the company, they can gradually grow stronger. This requires the company to be responsible for market planning and reasonable tactical design, allowing distributors to operate while controlling the operational process. Such complementary advantages can achieve a win-win situation. A significant portion of the company's current distributors need deep support. It is suggested that the company formulate corresponding plans and systems in personnel training, market planning, sales policies, and business management to train and guide them. Thirdly, eliminate distributors who contribute little or even harm the company's sales.

2) Choose appropriate KA systems.

Based on the positioning and target audience of our company’s products, currently, they are suitable only for large KA systems or A/B class stores. When entering KA systems, selective operations must be considered because the sales volume generated by different systems and stores in different regions varies greatly.

To choose KA systems, we often consider factors such as the number of stores, distribution areas (whether consistent with the company's key sales areas, beneficial for brand enhancement), store formats, operating conditions, competition brand entries, trade terms, and distributor-customer relationships to comprehensively evaluate the system's attractiveness.

Therefore, in choosing KA systems, attention should be paid to not treating all equally. We should enter systems with influence, large market share, good credit, and high foot traffic. Enter A/B class stores in large communities, high-end communities, commercial centers, business districts, and areas with highly educated populations such as universities, avoiding stores or systems with low influence, poor credit, low foot traffic, and insufficient purchasing power.

This also requires changes in the evaluation of provincial managers. Excessive emphasis on the number of newly opened KA stores each month can easily lead provincial managers to develop KA systems indiscriminately to meet assessment targets, resulting in massive returns and wasted expenses.

3) Select suitable operating stores.

The company's resources are limited, and so are the resources of distributors. Therefore, we must spend money wisely. Before our products enter the stores, provincial managers must thoroughly study the local KA stores, analyzing local consumption habits, geographical locations of the stores, foot traffic, and competitive situations of similar products within the stores to find the most suitable stores for our product sales.

The general guiding principle for selecting KA system stores is to focus on stores with higher regional sales. Practically, we can choose stores with the highest retail market share locally, stores with leading sales of similar products, stores with the best sales of competing products, and stores with the highest popularity or the best location locally. Additionally, choose stores where local distributors have good customer relationships. This can reduce entrance costs and minimize the risk of being eliminated at the bottom in case of poor sales.

4) Select suitable product combinations for store operations.

Currently, our company's products include four categories: protein powder, soybean powder, cookies, and protein beverages. Each category's packaging and specifications should target specific consumer segments, and their roles for the company differ. Some are used to establish the brand, like our protein powder; some attract customers, like our soybean powder products; some generate profit (protein powder); some are strategic products targeting competitors, etc. After a period of market operation, the acceptance levels of our products vary in different markets. Provincial managers should formulate reasonable product combinations based on market conditions, eliminating products with unclear target customers and poor market feedback. Simultaneously, the company should accelerate the research and development and launch of new products. Provincial managers should combine local consumption habits, KA store competition status, seasonal product sales trends, etc., to help local distributors select salable product items, thereby determining the product combination for specific stores and formulating suitable product strategies, specifying the required product types and quantities.

KA system management is based on category management, such as barcode fees and shelf space size, which are allocated according to products. The company's products must be able to match these requirements. For different product item combinations, our company's marketing work should be more detailed, such as providing guidance on product item combinations for specific regions or key stores, and breaking down sales and cost targets for each product item. Besides making future sales management more professional and detailed, the most feasible approach currently is to strengthen the training and concept infusion of provincial managers and distributors, along with necessary guidance.

Two, how to effectively control and continuously improve sales in already entered KA systems.

1) Do a good job in KA store displays.

For suppliers of each KA system, the most valuable resource in stores is shelf space. The company should try to obtain as much shelf, endcap, stack, and header space as possible in KA terminal stores with limited investment. Our products must have a strong visual impact to promote more impulse buying.

(1) Establish display evaluation standards to ensure good shelf display performance.

We need to enhance training for provincial managers, city supervisors, and promoters on basic display knowledge, combined with the display requirements of different KA systems, to set our company's unique display evaluation standards, such as centralized displays, vertical displays, and highlighting key items, ensuring good shelf display performance.

(2) Strive for special displays, multi-point presentations.

There are many forms of displays in KA stores, besides normal shelf displays. We should strive for special displays in KA stores, i.e., multi-point displays, such as endcaps, stacks, dedicated display racks, cabinets, column wraps, wall wraps, etc., to further increase product display area and attract consumer attention. However, this usually incurs certain costs. Besides providing certain cost support in these areas, the company should quickly design standard modes for special displays. Meanwhile, provincial managers should consciously guide or instruct distributors to reasonably use the 25% point cost support provided by the company.

(3) Do a good job in paid display design.

When distributors conduct paid displays, they must pay attention to cost efficiency. Below are reference items for paid display design:

• Display objectives: Brand promotion, product showcase, intercepting competitors, maintaining customer relationships;

• Display items: Products with the highest sales, new products, products currently advertised, promoted products with promotional resources, products with the highest fame;

• Display location: Near entrances or checkout counters, center of main aisles, ends, corners, front of the category section, adjacent to main competitors;

• Display area: Operating area of the KA store for this category, number of items and display area of competitor items;

• Display format: Endcap, header, stack, cabinet, column wrap;

• Display combination: Position, order, shelf space;

• Display time: Duration, period (holidays, promotions);

• Display cost planning: Ratio of sales growth during the display period to display costs.

(4) "Fixed visits" to maintain store display performance.

In cities with city supervisors, provincial managers should strengthen the training and management of city supervisors, reinforcing their visits to KA stores, and making "fixed visits" a primary clause in evaluating city supervisors. In cities without city supervisors, provincial managers should assist distributors in establishing a "fixed visit" system to ensure the maintenance of KA system stores. This includes restocking, where promoters must understand that shelf space is not bought with money but "seized" bit by bit through negotiation, not relying on the store giving three shelf spaces but rather negotiating for them, sometimes leveraging promotional activities to gain advantageous display conditions or positions, or using other brands' delisting or clearance as an opportunity to request expanded display areas, and also "seizing" space from competitors during regular restocking, achieving this through small actions like "pulling one more shelf space."

2) Promotions and sales to boost sales.

(1) Assisted selling creates shopping atmosphere, guiding impulse purchases.

While displaying products, we should reasonably use some assisted selling materials, such as shelf stickers, small standing signs, jump cards, roll-up banners, product brochures, flyers, etc. These assisted selling materials generally have low costs, and if used appropriately, they can create a shopping atmosphere, guide impulse purchases, and thus increase sales.

(2) Conduct theme promotions to intercept competitors at the terminal.

Promotions in KA stores are essential, with statistics showing that 70% of purchase decisions are made in-store, demonstrating the necessity of in-store promotions. When investing in promotions, provincial managers and distributors need to consider the existing resource allocation, choose good promotion opportunities and methods, and create a lively on-site atmosphere to achieve good promotion results and intercept competitors at the terminal. Below are some reference items for designing theme promotion plans:

• Promotion objectives: Why conduct this promotion? What goals are to be achieved?

• Promotion theme: What theme is presented to consumers and terminals?

• Promotion products: Which products or categories are promoted?

• Promotion schedule: When to start? How long does it last?

• Promotion location: Which KA stores, inside or outside the store?

• Promotion method: What form to use? (Discounts, gifts, bundling, coupons, raffles, points, exchanges, joint promotions, etc.)

• Promotion props: What props are needed? (Posters, gifts, brochures, etc.)

• Execution steps and methods: What steps and key points during execution?

• Cost estimation: How much will it cost? (Personnel costs, terminal costs, prop costs, etc.)

Regarding terminal promotions, both provincial managers and the company are relatively weak. Provincial managers lack the concept of terminal promotions or relevant professional knowledge and do not have enough time to study the terminal, resulting in unsatisfactory promotion effects, which fail to effectively boost sales or promote the brand. This aspect requires continuous improvement and learning.

(3) Promoter Management

Currently, our company has nearly a thousand full-time and part-time promoters in KA terminals nationwide. The company invests a significant amount of money in this area (promoter salaries, bonuses, KA store personnel management fees, etc.), but the performance of promoters varies widely. We need to strengthen the management of KA store promoters, particularly focusing on the following three aspects:

• Choose employees with good comprehensive qualities. Such as locals or those who speak the local dialect, have some promotional experience, are proactive communicators, friendly, hardworking, responsible, and capable of understanding and filling out relevant sales reports;

• Provide comprehensive training for promoters. Provincial managers must train promoters on product knowledge, sales skills, store management, and customer relationship maintenance before they start working. Later, the company should also provide periodic training for promoters. It is suggested to establish a promoter supervisor position in some key areas responsible for in-store promotional activities, material usage, and promoter training;

• Develop a complete promoter incentive mechanism. Currently, our company only covers the basic salary of promoters, while sales bonuses are borne by distributors. This requires our company and provincial managers to jointly formulate a sales reward policy suitable for the store volume, ensuring that the final salary level of promoters can withstand horizontal comparison with other promoters since this is their primary need. Additionally, attention should be paid to spiritual incentives, such as establishing a sales champion and excellent promoter award system. Besides economic rewards, tours to the Beijing headquarters or sightseeing in Beijing could also be given. Ensuring and maintaining their positive spirit is crucial since they represent our company directly facing consumers and are the real frontline personnel.

Promoters have always been a significant issue for the company, and also the area with the most opinions. We must change the current approval system to improve efficiency and refine the incentive system to allow them to truly play their role. Additionally, a part-time promoter system should be established to effectively supplement the terminal.

3) Maintain good customer relationships with KA stores.

Building good customer relationships with KA staff at all levels is a necessary foundation for conducting business activities, including procurement personnel, various store managers, store stockers, warehouse managers, cashiers, financial personnel, etc. After establishing good customer relationships, your various fees (such as purchasing stack space) will receive discounts, and your various tasks (such as orders, reconciliation, etc.) will receive support. Although currently in various regions across the country, distributors are responsible for KA store operations and management, and most customer relationships are handled by distributors themselves, saving the company a lot of costs in the short term, in the long run, it is very detrimental to the company's operations and management in KA stores. If a distributor encounters an issue, product withdrawal would cause serious economic losses. It is recommended that the company and provincial managers give sufficient attention to maintaining good customer relationships with KA stores and provide them with some fee support.

4) Distributors should manage store inventory and delivery well.

Large KA systems generally have strict return requirements, and on the other hand, they also strictly require responsibility for stockouts, especially during promotional periods. This will be specified in the contract, and they will also hold your payment as a guarantee. This requires provincial managers and distributors to closely monitor the inventory of KA system stores (store inventory and shelf inventory) to prevent near-expiry products or stockouts.

In actual operations, work should revolve around the orders of KA system stores. If KA stores place automatic orders, distributors should promptly track store orders and manage shelf and turnover warehouse inventories to improve sales forecasting capabilities; if KA stores place manual orders, city supervisors and promoters should ensure the satisfaction rate of store orders.

5) Establish a professional KA management team.

Currently, a considerable portion of the KA system involves regional joint procurement, which cannot be operated by single stores or provinces/cities. This requires the company to conduct centralized negotiations. Our provincial managers currently lack professional KA negotiation experience, and there are significant shortcomings in KA store management. Moreover, with the upcoming launches of our soybean powder series, beverages, biscuits, etc., and KA systems being a major sales channel, failing to give sufficient importance to KA would pose challenges in future sales work, hindering the company's long-term development. Establishing a professional KA team within the company is necessary and should be taken seriously.

Three, Establish a Complete Incentive and Elimination System

1) Establish a terminal store elimination system.

Eliminate KA stores, A/B class stores with low sales, no potential, and no contribution to the brand enhancement of our products, using limited funds on markets or terminals that truly matter.

2) Establish a dealer elimination system.

Eliminate dealers with low sales, low coverage rates, minimal product sales
