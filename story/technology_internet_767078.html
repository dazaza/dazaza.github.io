Uncovering the Strange Phenomenon of Smartphones: Crashes Like Playing the Lottery, New Models Always Out of Stock

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 17:36:01

When it comes to smartphones, you often hear complaints like: the battery doesn't last long; playing games isn't smooth at all; what a terrible camera, making me look so ugly...

Of course, at other times, the complaints you hear might be these: why does my phone always crash? Why is the new product out of stock as soon as it hits the market? Why did it drop in price shortly after I bought it...

The first type of complaint is basically related to hardware configuration. While we may feel helpless, there are still clues to follow. The second type of problem, however, is relatively more mysterious. At least, its phenomena and essence aren't too easy to figure out.

Today, we will focus on analyzing the second type of user complaints —— those strange phenomena about smartphones.

Crashing: It's a bit like buying a lottery ticket, the difference lies in the winning rate

Many smartphone users have experienced "crashes," where the screen freezes or goes black, and normal operations cannot restore its functionality. We call this: "the phone has crashed."

Crashing has the most fundamental relationship with quality. An Apple iPhone may not crash for months, while a knockoff phone from the street can drive you crazy. Why do smartphones crash?

Now assume that you and I both put on invisibility cloaks, and we quietly infiltrate the testing department of a phone company to see if the testing process of a smartphone follows the rules, and why products that might crash pass inspection.

Before entering the mobile phone R&D building, let’s briefly introduce the birth process of a phone, so we don’t get lost once inside.

The birth of a phone can simply be divided into several stages: “pre-research - design - development - testing - manufacturing - sales - maintenance.” During this period, many processes overlap, and sometimes they go back and forth endlessly. For example, testing will intervene early in the development stage to ensure issues are discovered ahead of time, and even if it enters the late stage of development and testing, the design phase may still propose new changes — which then require some or even most of the results to be redone.

Upon entering the testing department, you'll find one phone after another connected to computers, and the computer screens quickly running lines of magical code. This is the core part of phone testing — running test cases.

We’ll simplify this: test cases are designed based on experience and requirements, covering various scenarios a phone might encounter. Something as simple as "making a call" can be tested. When the system runs this case, the computer sends instructions to the phone, and the phone actually dials a number, then returns a result: success or failure.

In general, a mobile phone company's test case library contains tens of thousands, even hundreds of thousands of test cases. Then, according to the different models of phones, a portion of these is selected for targeted testing. If a test passes, it gets a checkmark; if it fails, the tester submits an "issue report" to the test manager, who then passes it through the R&D manager to the engineers responsible for developing the corresponding code or hardware.

If the issue report is resolved by the R&D personnel, the modified hardware or software is resubmitted to the testing department for secondary verification (a term called regression testing).

The R&D process of a phone is essentially a game of chess between the development and testing departments, full of conflict. The KPI of the testing department is how many and how serious the issue reports they submit are, while the performance of the development department is ensuring progress and minimizing problems. So, these two departments are natural enemies.

Phone R&D is a systematic job, and it’s impossible to wait until the end to see if it qualifies. Smart managers set multiple checkpoints (called TR points) throughout the entire process, each point achieving part of the goal. This way, risks can be controlled.

For temporarily unresolved issues, after friendly or intense discussions between testing and R&D, the decision is made to "fix them later," ignoring them for now, pretending nothing happened.

OK, this is the root cause of crashes. Some issue reports, due to capability limitations or overly complex testing processes, are shielded by phone manufacturers based on risk assessments, with a comforting reason — to be fixed in subsequent versions. Therefore, if these failed test cases are occasionally reproduced during your operation, the result is basically the same — a crash.

Another common possibility for phone crashes, unrelated to context, is called "memory overflow."

In reality, every time a phone runs a program, it occupies part of the memory, which should be released upon exit. But sometimes, due to certain design bugs, the program doesn't release the memory or releases it incompletely after exiting, causing the phone's available memory to decrease over time. At this point, if a large program is suddenly launched and the remaining memory is insufficient to handle it, the only options left are crashing or restarting.

For most functions and application scenarios, phone manufacturers will ensure that tests are passed, and it's impossible for a phone to crash just by making a call.

As for why some phones are prone to crashing while others are not, the principle is quite simple. R&D capabilities determine the number of unresolved issue reports, testing capabilities and responsibility determine the number of test cases, and various uncontrollable factors, such as rough manufacturing processes and extreme operating environments, also increase the probability of crashes.

Shortage: What's lacking is not goods but the ability to estimate sales volume

We've pretty much figured out the issue of crashes. Let's now take a look at the procurement and manufacturing departments to understand why shortages happen.

There's a strange phenomenon that's become very popular recently. A flagship model is released, everyone queues up to buy it, but before the queue finishes, the phones are sold out.

Is the phone manufacturer unwilling to make money? Of course not. For the procurement department, they need to pre-order or stockpile various components for phone production, from CPUs and memory to cameras, casings, and even packaging boxes.

But there's a small problem here: the procurement price is generally determined by the order quantity, something that anyone who's ever bought vegetables understands. However, it's difficult to accurately estimate the exact sales volume of a phone before its launch. Ordering too many components lowers the price per unit, but excess inventory can lead to greater losses.

So, why not stock fewer components? This is one of the reasons for phone shortages. To avoid possible risks, phone manufacturers are cautious when procuring parts, but if the phone's sales exceed expectations and the stocked components run out, then there's no negotiation — there's a shortage.

There's another possibility: unexpected component supply shortages. Thus, phone manufacturers try to mitigate this risk by sourcing critical components from multiple suppliers.

However, this isn't the full story behind shortages. Let's dig deeper into the manufacturing department. Here, the manufacturing department includes not only the manufacturer's own production facilities but also contract manufacturers since most phones are outsourced for production. For convenience, we'll collectively refer to them as the "manufacturing department."

In the manufacturing department, it's a different scene. All machines are fully operational, working overtime for mass production. But why are only two production lines producing this best-selling phone while the other eight are processing other products?

Most phone manufacturing is outsourced to contract manufacturers, and a single contract manufacturer receives orders from different companies, which brings scheduling issues. Manufacturers estimate their product sales and place orders with contract factories, arranging work schedules and specific production lines. If this sales estimate is inaccurate and the other production lines of the contract factory are already full, the result is the same — a shortage.

Therefore, estimating sales volume is extremely important.

Of course, the above-mentioned reasons are not the entirety of why phones are out of stock. There are many human factors or deliberate actions by manufacturers involved.

Price Drops: Between lowering prices or not, it's a matter of face

In the history of smartphone price drops, there are two extreme representatives: Apple and Nokia. Apple products almost never discount before the next generation is released; Nokia's flagships typically drop in price every few months. The Lumia 920, contemporary with the iPhone 5, now costs as much as a Xiaomi phone.

Regarding price drops, we need to investigate the cost control department.

The pricing of a phone is the result of collective wisdom. First, the marketing department estimates the possible sales volume of the phone. Then, the R&D department calculates the R&D cost per phone based on the invested manpower and resources. The procurement department, using Excel sheets, figures out the hardware cost per unit. Finally, other support departments and the marketing team add the promotional and channel costs to each phone, and this total sum represents the basic value of the phone.

But economists have long taught us that value does not equal price. How much to mark up depends on the manufacturer's conscience and confidence in the brand.

Price drops can be caused by internal and external factors, manifested as either voluntary or forced price reductions.

Let's start with internal factors. In most cases, the price of a phone is higher than its cost. Components like hardware, packaging, and channels are tied to each phone and remain largely unchanged (procurement prices may fluctuate slightly, but not significantly). Meanwhile, software R&D costs stabilize after the phone is launched (partially ignoring maintenance costs). The more phones sold, the lower the R&D cost and initial marketing expenses per unit. When sales exceed projections, the cost per phone begins to drop, and at this point, a price reduction becomes a rational option.

Now for external factors. Intense market competition and the rapid release of new phones force each model to fight fiercely in the market. When a new generation of phones is launched or competitors lower prices, phone manufacturers must consider adjusting their product pricing. If sales have exceeded expectations, then reducing prices aligns with market trends. If sales are poor but a price cut is still necessary, the only option is to cut into profit margins.

Would any phone be priced lower than its cost? Remember, phone costs are a fluctuating process.

When a phone earns a lot of money at a high price in its early stages, it may sell at cost or even below cost in its later stages. Although selling each unit incurs a loss at this point, combined with the profits earned earlier, the total cost remains far below the total revenue.

Why not just stop production altogether? Wouldn't that yield higher profits?

While this makes sense on paper, manufacturers pursue more than just profit; they also aim for shipping volumes and market position among other factors. If they can sacrifice part of their profit under the premise of already making substantial gains, in exchange for higher sales and a larger market share, it promotes the brand, user base, and even stock prices positively.

In short, one truth always exists: in the market, companies are always smarter than consumers.
