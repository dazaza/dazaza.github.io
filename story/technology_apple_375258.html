David Pogue: A Decade of Tech, Mixed

by diglog on 2010-12-19 13:51:26

David Pogue's tech column in The New York Times has been running for ten years. On Thanksgiving Day, while waiting for the turkey to be served, David took a moment to reflect and wrote down some personal conclusions about technology. (Read the original article)

Products don’t replace each other; they coexist

There are always experts who say that this will be the "iPhone killer," or that Kindle is doomed once a new product hits the market. Brother, the main theme of technological development in consumer products is complementarity and improvement, not a zero-sum game. For years now, television hasn't killed radio. This weekend, some people go to the cinema, others stay home to watch DVDs. Paperbacks and e-books serve different markets: iPhone and Android won't kill each other. That’s it.

The Internet changes lives

In the past decade, the way information is delivered has gradually shifted from tapes and paper to digital downloads. We have started demanding music, TV shows, movies, and pictures from the Internet, followed by books and newspapers. What we want is convenience.

In another thirty years, it will be hard to explain to our grandchildren what it means to "rent a disc to watch at home."

Gear matters personally

Writing a column is like performing in a play: you get immediate and profound feedback from your audience. Over these years, I've been troubled by various levels of emotional comments. Later, I suddenly realized: choosing a product is a very personal decision. Your phone expresses your taste and personality. Therefore, some people project their criticism of a product onto its users.

Blue eyes everywhere

In today's tech world, when you mention "Apple," "Microsoft," or "Google," it often triggers personal emotions in readers.

After testing the iPad, I wrote two equally long reviews, one positive and one negative. My plan was to help readers understand the device from different angles based on their own technical backgrounds. As a result, anti-Apple folks didn't buy my "love letter" to the iPad, and Apple fans were dissatisfied because I criticized the iPad. I can't understand why both camps didn't check out the other review I wrote.

You know if it works

An extra joy of writing a tech column is getting access to some lesser-known products and bringing them into the public eye. Occasionally, startups complain to me because after I introduce their product, their servers crash due to the sudden influx of traffic.

But I want to say, judging whether a product is good or not isn't that difficult. You can do it too.

In 2003, Microsoft launched a watch called "Spot Watch." It could display your schedule and receive text messages, but it cost $10 per month for service, had to be charged every night, and if you needed to leave the city, you had to switch networks in advance to ensure it continued to function properly.

The Akimbo set-top box, which launched in 2005, allowed you to watch TV programs on your computer. What channels could it receive? AdvenTV, the first Turkish-language channel in the U.S.; Veg TV, a vegetarian food channel; Skyworks, a British channel featuring helicopter shots.

Would you want one?

Some concepts just don't catch on

Every year, tech magazines select several of the most disruptive creative products.

Video calling technology is a big breakthrough, but that doesn't mean video phones will necessarily gain popularity. Maybe I want to take out the trash while on a call, or do something else. Since I'm already making a phone call, seeing you in person isn't as important. If I miss my girlfriend who is far away, we can use Skype or FaceTime. At least for regular calls, there's no need to make such a big deal.

Also, forget about "Internet kitchen appliances." Who do you know who actually buys them?

What "forever" really means is probably about a year.

In the ten years I've been testing thousands of products, very few have made it to today.

You might say: iPods, BlackBerrys, IE browsers—these old friends are still innovating nonstop, right? True, but you don't see how many hours of labor and piles of dollars' worth of products just bubble up and then disappear.

That's how trendy products work: by the time you buy one, it's already outdated. Every summer, a new iPhone model comes out. So if your phone accidentally falls into the toilet, it's not really much of a loss.

Drinking water from a fire hydrant

People often express this view: in today's consumer-tech landscape, countless events happen every day. It's hard to keep up with the trends and avoid being labeled as "outdated"...
