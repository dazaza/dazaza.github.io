Study-abroad tip: France's public universities focus on recruiting graduate students

by arkabroadt on 2012-02-13 09:47:04

, Overseas Study Institutions Beijing Examination Bulletin (reporter Ma Haili) - Studying abroad in lesser-taught languages is gaining more attention, with France's free public education attracting many students. Through interviews, it was learned that many French public universities prefer to enroll undergraduate graduates, while being more selective towards high school graduates.

Huantailai International Education's study abroad advisor, Wang Fang, analyzed that the main reason is that some high school graduates' ability and self-discipline in learning languages are lower than those of undergraduate graduates as reflected by French schools. This language disadvantage directly affects the admission rate for specialized programs, causing some students to be stuck in preparatory classes or language courses, unable to keep up with normal study abroad curriculums.

At the same time, French universities have stricter requirements during the undergraduate stage compared to the graduate stage, with a high elimination rate. Medical and architecture programs even use a bottom-ranking elimination system to remove students. Public comprehensive universities treat international students equally with domestic students and will not lower their standards for international students. Therefore, international students face the risk of elimination due to language disadvantages.

It is understood that when applying for studying in France as an undergraduate graduate, there is often a need to change majors, which can be quite challenging in French public comprehensive universities. French schools emphasize foundational studies in one’s major, requiring students to apply for majors similar or related to what they studied previously. If students really want to switch majors, they may consider some higher business schools, where certain majors accept students switching fields; some business schools do not require any specific prior major from all applicants, but these students must first complete a preparatory year.

French institutions generally require students to have around 500 hours of French language study and corresponding test scores. For those without any French foundation, the preparation period for learning French is about 3 to 5 months. This means that candidates without a French foundation who wish to study in France should prepare at least half a year in advance.

In terms of financial guarantees, France requires candidates to deposit the tuition fees and living expenses for the first year, and their parents must provide bank card transaction records for the past six months. Tuition fees at French public schools are very low, with registration fees ranging from 130 euros to 700 euros per year depending on the type of diploma pursued. In other schools, especially management and applied arts schools, tuition fees are much higher, typically ranging from 3800 euros to 14000 euros.
