Elop: The Biggest Winner of Microsoft's Acquisition of Nokia

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 15:52:10

Purchasing Nokia, can Microsoft break through the mobile stronghold of Apple and Google? By selling its phone business and obtaining a large amount of cash to develop enterprise services, can Nokia successfully transform?

These all need time to prove.

But there is no doubt that Stephen Elop, who left Microsoft three years ago, came to Finland, and now returns to Microsoft by selling Nokia, has become the most prominent winner of this transaction.

Even if Elop hasn't been able to prove himself at Nokia, today's Microsoft indeed needs someone with a resume similar to Elop's. Especially after Ballmer announced his intention to leave the CEO position within a year.

Before praising Elop too much, it must be clear that Nokia under Elop’s leadership was not doing well. But the question is, if someone else had taken over, would Nokia be any different from what it is now?

After Elop took office, Nokia's market value fell from $39.5 billion in 2010 to $14 billion now. Since February 2011, when Nokia announced its cooperation with Microsoft, Nokia's stock price has fallen by more than 55%.

Let's look at Nokia's financial statements. In 2012, Nokia's mobile phone business revenue reached $20.7 billion, accounting for 52% of total revenue; map business income reached $1.45 billion; Nokia Siemens Networks (NSN) revenue reached $18.17 billion. The above three businesses lost: $1.45 billion, $400 million, $1.05 billion respectively.

In Nokia's mobile phone business, the smartphone business had a full-year revenue of about $7.2 billion in 2012, a year-on-year decrease of 50%, with a gross profit margin of 8.8%; non-smartphone business revenue reached $12.5 billion, a year-on-year decrease of 21%, with a gross profit margin of 23.4%. These two businesses lost: $2.06 billion, $700 million respectively.

That is to say, the deep cooperation between Nokia and Microsoft since 2011 was not successful and did not help Nokia reverse its declining trend. If no action was taken, Nokia's assets might further shrink.

To prevent further shrinkage, selling is the best choice. But facing an annual loss of $2 billion, continuously declining market share, and little advantage in the smartphone field, who could buy it? Google had already acquired Motorola Mobility in 2011. Moreover, Nokia had already tied its smartphone business to Microsoft's WP in 2011, and WP's global market share was less than 5%. Samsung and China's emerging companies also showed little interest. Only Microsoft.

And if Nokia decides to sell, it will have an opportunity to obtain a large amount of cash for business transformation.

In July 2013, Nokia spent $2.2 billion to repurchase NSN, a joint subsidiary established with Siemens, which had a revenue of $18.17 billion in 2012. Therefore, I believe that from the day Nokia decided to repurchase NSN, Nokia had already decided to sell its mobile phone business.

Once the mobile phone business is sold, Nokia's remaining assets will be the map business and NSN. Although the map business had only $1.45 billion in revenue last year, its gross profit margin was 79.3%, far higher than the 21.3% of the mobile phone business. Adding the 30.3% gross profit margin of NSN, Nokia has the opportunity to transform into enterprise service-level business.

Therefore, from the perspective of selling Nokia to Microsoft, Elop did nothing wrong. And at this point, on one hand, Microsoft performed extremely poorly on Surface and urgently needed to enhance its hardware capabilities; on the other hand, Ballmer faced unprecedented pressure within Microsoft due to the poor market performance of Win8 and Surface. This was also the space Nokia could seek at the negotiation table.

Some people think that Elop's mistakes were more reflected in the deep cooperation with Microsoft in 2011 while abandoning Android.

Okay, perhaps if Nokia cooperated with Android, its financial statements might look slightly better. But look at Motorola, Sony and other old brands, they didn't gain much advantage in the Android red ocean. Samsung's rise was due to its advantages in the global supply chain, and Nokia did not possess such advantages. Nokia's communication service and channel advantages are becoming insignificant in the era of smartphones.

Of course, choosing to cooperate with Microsoft, besides differentiation, may also reflect Elop's personal career politics. Perhaps before announcing cooperation with Microsoft in February 2011, Elop had already determined the fate of himself and Nokia on Microsoft, preparing to sell Nokia to Microsoft one day and return to work at Microsoft. After all, this was not the first time in Elop's career. In 2004, Elop became the CEO of Macromedia. In 2005, Elop sold Macromedia to Adobe and served as Adobe's president.

Even so, both Elop and Ballmer have given themselves two years of buffer time. These two years have proven that Microsoft lacks the ability to independently venture into hardware, and the inevitable trend of the industry towards the integration of software and hardware. For Nokia, it has also proven that if it does not sell, the mobile phone business might become a burden for its transformation.

In fact, from the acquisition price, Microsoft did not lose out. In this acquisition framework by Microsoft, $5 billion was used to acquire Nokia's mobile phone business, and the remaining $2.18 billion was the patent price. In the mobile phone business part, Microsoft will receive functional phones, smart device business, design teams, all production, sales, marketing activities, and related support departments associated with Nokia's devices and services.

Even just Nokia's smartphone business, its revenue last year was $7.2 billion. Considering the high loss of $2.06 billion, Microsoft's $5 billion acquisition price did not bring much premium to Nokia. After all, Microsoft currently has a cash flow of $77 billion.

However, opposing opinions argue that unlike Google's acquisition of Motorola mainly for patents, Microsoft's acquisition of Nokia is more about developing its mobile hardware business. Due to the failure of Surface, the market has insufficient confidence in Microsoft's development in the hardware field. Besides, the reason why Windows Phone is not popular in the market is not due to hardware but because of the small application ecosystem and the software user experience lagging behind mainstream operating systems.

The above viewpoint believes that the most important factor for Microsoft's long-term development is still the selection of the new CEO.

Elop is obviously the hottest candidate.

On one hand, Elop enjoys great trust from Ballmer, and Ballmer has driven out executives he doesn't trust, clearing obstacles for Elop. Among them was Steve Sinofsky, the father of Windows 8.

On the other hand, by acquiring Nokia, Microsoft has clearly defined its strategy of integrating software and hardware. Before serving as Nokia's CEO, Elop was responsible for Microsoft's most profitable software business, Office; during his tenure at Nokia, Elop also gained some understanding of the hardware field where Microsoft is not adept.

Besides, it is worth noting that in the Microsoft personnel adjustments after acquiring Nokia, Larsen-Green, who has served at Microsoft for 20 years, replaced Steve Sinofsky to take charge of Xbox and Surface businesses, and was considered a potential successor to Ballmer, will now report to Elop.

By acquiring Nokia, at least it shows that Microsoft has officially clarified its core strategy of integrating software and hardware. In the past 12 months, the operational profit margin of Microsoft's entertainment department, where Xbox is located, was only 8.7%, significantly lower compared to Microsoft's overall 34% profit margin, and Xbox's annual revenue was only $7 billion. After acquiring Nokia, Microsoft integrates all hardware devices into one business unit, which will be more beneficial for Microsoft's development under the mobile wave.

Underneath this, Elop's influence in Microsoft will certainly be particularly important.
