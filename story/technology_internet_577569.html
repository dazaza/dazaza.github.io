Olympus: Too big to fail?

by diglog on 2011-11-22 12:50:36

On November 16, a sudden northern wind chilled the entire Tokyo overnight. The staff in charge of accounting and financial affairs at Olympus Corporation (hereinafter referred to as "Olympus", 7733.TO, OCPNY.PK), which had been embroiled in scandal for exactly a month, felt the biting cold even more acutely. On October 14, Olympus abruptly announced the dismissal of its British CEO, Michael Woodford, after which Chairman Tsuyoshi Kikukawa assumed the role of CEO. Barely 12 days later, Kikukawa resigned from all his positions, including that of chairman and CEO. After a turbulent month, on November 14, Olympus was required to submit its latest financial statements to the Tokyo Stock Exchange; otherwise, it might have been forced to delist due to falsified accounts. However, on that day, Olympus failed to publish its interim financial report for the first half of the year and was consequently placed on the Tokyo Stock Exchange's monitoring list. On November 16, Olympus still had to report its latest financial situation to the corporate banking syndicate and major investors. Of course, for a company that holds around 70% of the global market share in endoscopes, the Japanese government would not allow it to collapse. The principle of "too big to fail" still applies in Japan. The voices of media and scholars condemning Olympus for falsifying accounts were not particularly loud, and there were even some defending the company.
