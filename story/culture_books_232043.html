"The Rise and Fall of the Ying Qin Family over Five Hundred Years in the Great Qin Empire"

by jqrxass on 2009-09-02 10:59:22

"The Empire of Great Qin" narrates the rise of the Ying Qin family from their fate of exile and migration, their dominance over the Western Rong during the Spring and Autumn period, their rivalry with the world during the Warring States period, to King Ying Zheng's sweeping destruction of the six kingdoms and the establishment of an unprecedented unified empire. However, this empire quickly collapsed amidst the rallying cries of Chen Sheng and Wu Guang, who were followed by a nationwide response.

The author strictly adheres to historical facts, fully absorbing the latest historical research results, allowing deep insights to transcend time and space. With grandeur and passionate writing, he vividly portrays a large number of distinct historical figures, painting a magnificent and vast historical tapestry. This displays the 500-year struggle of the Ying Qin family and the rise and fall of the Qin Kingdom and Qin Empire. With compassion, it shows the desires, passions, ambitions, and strength exhibited by all, from royalty and generals to commoners, amidst the immense historical waves.

The author, with a rich life experience, profound thoughts, and mature yet passionate writing, elevates this book above today's popular historical works that lack substance yet intentionally mystify, providing unique reading and appreciation value.

The Empire of Great Qin Relevant Content

Every nation or country has standout moments that capture the world's attention, eras worth remembering.

In China, even a three-year-old knows of the remarkable figure, Emperor Qin Shi Huang; many peasant women can sing a folk song since their youth: "Meng Jiangnv Cries Over the Great Wall."

Qin Shi Huang and Meng Jiangnv, one a giant who created a new world with grandeur and ferocity, the other representing the suffering of common people who contributed to this new world, together form such a brief yet brilliant era: The Empire of Great Qin.

The First Part of The Empire of Great Qin

Nowadays, we are accustomed to referring to the clan that established the Qin Kingdom and Qin Empire as "Ying Qin." In fact, the surname "Ying" and the clan name "Qin," derived from their fiefdom, were only obtained after a considerable historical phase. But for narrative convenience, let us call them the Qin people right from the start!

The history of the Qin people seems closely tied to birds and beasts.

Swifts joyfully chirp, flying over the fields, dropping a swift egg. A young girl named Nü Xiu, who was herding sheep, swallowed the swift egg and gave birth to a boy named Da Ye, who became the progenitor of the Qin people - "Records of the Grand Historian: Chronicles of Qin" uses this miraculous legend to open the history of the origin of the Qin tribe.

The Second Part of The Empire of Great Qin

Zhang Yi traveled extensively but could not secure employment. He arrived at Yingcheng, where he attended a banquet as a minister (the highest administrative official in Chu, equivalent to prime ministers in other states). Unexpectedly, he was suspected by the minister's subordinates of stealing a jade disc called "He Shi Bi," reputedly worth a fortune, and ended up beaten into this state.

A few days later, someone with a Zhao dialect came knocking. This person reminded Zhang Yi that Qin was pursuing hegemony and suggested he visit. As for travel funds, didn't he have an old friend Su Qin? Now prime minister of the six states, he would surely provide substantial assistance out of friendship.

Thus, Zhang Yi left Chu for Zhao to seek his old classmate Su Qin.

The Third Part of The Empire of Great Qin

When King Ying Zheng's heart was filled with restless agitation, a man destined to play a significant role in Qin history, especially in the Qin Empire, approached King Ying Zheng and quickly gained his respect and favor.

This man was Li Si.

Li Si was from Shangcai in the State of Chu. He had studied the art of kingship under Xunzi. Feeling he couldn't fulfill his ambitions in Chu and seeing the decline of other feudal lords, he chose to travel westward to Qin, requesting to become a guest of Lü Buwei. Lü Buwei soon recognized his talent and promoted him to a palace guard officer, giving him access to King Ying Zheng. Whether this arrangement was intentional on Lü Buwei's part is unrecorded in history. With Li Si's intelligence, he had already discerned the details of the tripartite division within the upper echelons of Qin.

The Fourth Part of The Empire of Great Qin

Life and death are two different forms of existence that ancient people believed represented human value. Emperor Qin Shi Huang sought immortality, attempting to surpass the brevity of life. In daily life, Qin Shi Huang abhorred talk of death, and none of his ministers dared mention it. Yet, he eventually realized the inevitability of the life cycle. Indeed, his pursuit of longevity and aversion to death stemmed from his awareness of mortality. Since he had an Afang Palace in the mortal world, naturally, there had to be an underground "Afang Palace" in the afterlife. As stated in "The Book of Rites: Doctrine of the Mean," "To serve the dead as if they were alive, to serve those who have passed away as if they were present," this was the psychological reason behind constructing the unprecedented grand mausoleum of Emperor Qin Shi Huang.

A tragedy was about to unfold in the general's tent.

Prince Fu Su now received the imperial edict from his father with excitement. Hearing that his father was touring the land, praying at famous mountains for longevity, made him worry and feel uneasy as the eldest son for not being able to fulfill filial duties; unexpectedly, the edict continued with "bestow a sword for self-execution," followed by the messenger tossing a cold, gleaming sword. Like a bolt of lightning on a clear day, he was stunned by the sudden calamity, returning to the inner camp, unable to stop crying, and without much thought, prepared to commit suicide. Meng Tian stepped forward to dissuade him, saying: His Majesty entrusted me with thirty thousand troops to guard the borders, with Prince as supervisor, a responsibility crucial to the safety of the empire, which cannot be easily handed to anyone but His Majesty's closest confidants. Now, with the emperor touring outside and the crown prince not yet established, how can we know that this single messenger does not harbor deceitful intentions? Let us send someone to rush to His Majesty's whereabouts and request instructions. If it proves true, then dying will not be too late!
