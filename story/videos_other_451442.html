They will focus all their attention on the talks.

by vmakezi5 on 2011-06-24 16:53:07

Pressure, like air, permeates every corner of the negotiation table. All parties involved are either overtly or covertly exerting various pressures on their opponents. Each participant understands clearly that the side which cannot withstand the pressure will lose a large portion of benefits. Time is strength; most people, when dealing with their negotiating counterparts, unconsciously place time pressure on themselves, and this pressure will certainly affect their normal performance.

In business negotiations, time can become an invisible pressure. Under the pressure of time, opponents often make compromises they would otherwise be unwilling to make, and mistakes that should not happen also occur frequently. Both sides will consider how to use time more effectively, and the side that uses it better will gain a negotiation advantage.

Please recall your negotiation experiences. Wasn't it true that as you approached the end of the negotiations, there was a greater tendency to reach consensus? Although there are individual cases, I believe this point still has universality. In a long negotiation, for the majority of the time, both sides fiercely argue and firmly hold their principle bottom lines. But at the final closing stage, due perhaps to the limitation of negotiation time, physical and mental fatigue, or other reasons, everyone unconsciously accelerates the negotiation rhythm. On issues that were previously quite sharp, both sides make a certain degree of compromise. The thoughts of both sides seem suddenly to become more flexible, and actions more默契 (tacitly coordinated). This would have been unimaginable at the beginning of the negotiation. This phenomenon is not intentional on the part of negotiators but rather a normal psychological response.

This kind of loose-first, tight-later negotiation phenomenon is sometimes also used by negotiators, who call it the "time pressure strategy." The specific steps are to first discuss issues unrelated to the overall situation. When you are preparing to conclude the negotiation, they suddenly raise issues that you are unwilling to accept. They know that under the pressure of time, your negotiation principles may loosen up a bit, cleverly pressuring you. Many times, this strategy is successful because at this point, it's difficult for you to adjust back to the mental state you had at the beginning of the negotiation. You might think this strategy is immoral, but there’s no way around it—negotiation is negotiation. You can refuse to use such a strategy, but you cannot fail to guard against your opponent using it on you.

Of course, there are ways to prevent this from happening. For example, when you plan the negotiation agenda, list all possible topics involved, categorize them in order of importance, and start discussing sensitive issues at the beginning of the negotiation. As you approach the conclusion, the topics discussed should become less significant. Additionally, if the other party sets aside certain issues early in the negotiation or suggests postponing discussion, be cautious. You need to comprehensively analyze whether these issues are truly unimportant and whether the other party might bring them up again at the end of the negotiation. In short, don’t fall into traps set by your opponent.

During the negotiation period, you must avoid revealing your negotiation deadline to the other party. Due to work needs, I often travel to negotiate with regional distributors. After getting off the plane, the other party usually asks me first: "How long do you plan to stay here?" I generally answer without hesitation: "No specific deadline, it depends on the progress of the negotiations." It cannot be denied that the other party may ask about my itinerary out of necessity for planning the negotiation schedule or arranging time to show me around local customs, but still, remember that negotiation is negotiation. Any weakness revealed to skilled negotiators will be exploited at the negotiation table. If the other party knows your ultimate negotiation deadline, then they will definitely drag important issues until the last moment, exerting enormous pressure on you under a harmonious atmosphere, forcing you to compromise at the last moment.

Regarding negotiation deadlines, I suggest you not make specific plans at the time. Because, regardless of which stage of the negotiation you are in, as long as you have the idea of concluding the negotiation in mind, you will inevitably make concessions in some aspect. Don't add unnecessary pressure to yourself. Imagine nearing the deadline with no substantial progress in the negotiation—how much pressure would you feel? Would you strictly adhere to the negotiation time you set through compromise? This sounds absurd, but reality often turns out this way. Indeed, all parties involved in the negotiation will have corresponding expectations regarding time, and negotiations cannot go on indefinitely. However, skilled negotiators will downplay this concept. They focus all their energy on the negotiation and pass the time pressure onto the other party.

In my negotiation experiences, regardless of how much my company desires to quickly reach a deal with the other party, I will not show even a trace of impatience at the negotiation table.
