Common printing imposition methods introduction  

1. Single-sided: This method refers to printed materials that only need to be printed on one side, such as posters, etc., which only need to print the front side while the back does not require printing.

2. Double-sided: Commonly known as "[bottom and top plate]", it refers to printed materials that need to be printed on both sides, such as some small promotional flyers, small posters, cards, etc.

3. Horizontal rotation: Commonly known as "[self-flip plate]" or "[flip face on the same plate]", this is suitable for magazine and book type printed materials. For example, a 16-page magazine cover has four pages including the front cover, inside front cover, inside back cover, and back cover that need to be printed. During imposition, the front cover and back cover, inside front cover and inside back cover are horizontally combined together. Then, the front cover and back cover, inside front cover and inside back cover are head-to-head combined on a quarto page for printing. After one side of the paper is printed, the paper is rotated horizontally by 180 degrees, and the other side is printed. Once completed, the printed material can be cut in half to get two identical copies.

4. Flip: Using the same printing [http://www.sxywgs.com] plate, after printing one side of the paper, flip the paper over to print on the back, but using the other long edge of the paper as the "bite edge". This method is commonly known as "[flipping the deck]", but now very few people use this method anymore.

Imposition is an important process in the platemaking and photography technology. Although its technical requirements are not high, it requires accuracy and great responsibility. The following must be achieved:

① Correct layout paper, completely consistent with the publisher's format.

② Accurate alignment of images, text positions, borders, and background colors for each color plate; otherwise, it can easily cause errors.

As electronic color separation and direct photographic screening processes become more widely used, the imposition process continues to evolve, resulting in various imposition methods. However, currently both domestically and internationally, manual imposition still predominates. In recent years, various types of computer-controlled imposition machines and electronic color separation machines that combine color separation and imposition have been introduced abroad. However, due to poor efficiency of these machines, they haven't been widely adopted. Recently, Germany introduced the latest color imposition system, which has already been introduced into China. Due to complex operational procedures and high costs, it is still in the trial stage. Therefore, within a relatively long period, the imposition process will still primarily rely on manual methods.

1. Purpose of imposition.

① To assemble multiple or dozens of colored manuscripts, borders, backgrounds, and texts according to the customer's design format requirements onto one plate, referred to as imposition. Since the reduction and enlargement ratios of each original manuscript vary, electronic color separation or photographic color separation must be performed first to adjust the proportions correctly before proceeding with the soft film imposition work. Customer-designed graphics come in many varieties, some requiring circles, others squares, some requiring pictures within pictures, and some requiring words within pictures. All of these requirements must be addressed during the imposition process to meet the design format requirements.

② To improve the efficiency and quality of plate-making and printing, large plates must be assembled based on printing [http://www.sxywgs.com] conditions. For instance, assembling plates for open-size printing, three-open size printing, or four-open size printing, avoiding issues arising from small originals that require multiple exposures, which often lead to misalignment affecting the quality and efficiency of printing. It also reduces plate exposure time, improving the quality and efficiency of plate making.

In summary, through the imposition process, all required images, texts, textures, and borders are assembled onto a single open-size, three-open size, or four-open size plate according to the design format requirements and printing conditions. This is the purpose of imposition.

With the diversification of customer designs and the widespread adoption of electronic color separation technology, the imposition process has become more complex and the workload heavier. Therefore, it is essential to place great emphasis on the imposition work and promote the use of advanced imposition methods.

2. Nested picture imposition. Nested picture imposition is a new method adopted in recent years, achieving both accuracy and speed, and has been widely used.

There are several ways to perform nested picture imposition, which can be selected based on the conditions of each unit. They are described as follows:

① Nested picture imposition using positioning strips. Using positioning strips and hole-punching for nested picture imposition is the most accurate and fastest method. The procedure is as follows: First, use a quarto (or open-size) polyester transparent sheet base, make a reverse-direction positive image layout paper according to the customer-specified format, then punch holes using a positioning machine, attach positioning strips, and fix the positive image layout paper. Take an SO soft film, punch holes, align it with the positive image layout paper, copy out a forward-direction negative image layout paper, and you can proceed with imposition.

Before imposing, consider the following: If the original is a landscape picture, first impose and copy the cyan positive image, making it easier for the next step of imposing yellow, magenta, and black versions. If the original is a portrait, start by imposing and copying the magenta positive image. In short, decide which positive image version to impose and copy first based on the main tone of the original.

Taking the landscape picture as an example, first impose and copy a cyan positive image, handle it quickly for drying, then attach positioning strips and secure it. Place the cyan positive image soft film emulsion side down on the viewing table, then remove the cyan negative screen from the SO soft film layout paper, align it with the rules lines of the positive screen. Next, use the method of aligning the negative image with the positive image to sequentially paste and copy the second color negative screen. Repeat this process for the third and fourth colors.

② Nested picture imposition without positioning strips. Some units do not have positioning strips or hole-punching machines, but nested picture imposition can still be performed. The principle of imposing without positioning strips is the same as with positioning strips.

For nested picture imposition without positioning strips, when creating the positive image reverse-direction layout paper, draw four large format rule lines first, then copy a negative image forward-direction layout paper, and align each figure's cutting specifications and sizes for imposition.

Still taking the landscape picture as an example: first impose and copy out the cyan positive image, dry it quickly, then overlay it on the negative image layout paper, aligning the four rule lines for imposition, and sequentially impose and copy out the yellow, magenta, and black versions.

③ Nested picture imposition using positioning strips and rule lines. Adopting positioning strips and rule lines, using the method of aligning the negative image with the negative image, is currently the most widely used, popular, and best imposition method. Because current electronic color separation films all come with rule lines, and they are very precise and accurate, making them very suitable for this imposition method. The advantages of this imposition method include: Not only can you align according to the rule lines, but you can also double-check the accuracy of nesting pictures within pictures, making the imposition accuracy even more reliable. At the same time, after the four plates are imposed, they can be sent together to the copy process, providing convenience for the next process.

The sequence and method of nested picture imposition using positioning strips and rule lines are as follows:

a. First, draw a correct format specification layout paper.

b. Reverse the layout paper, stack it with a quarto (open-size) transparent polyester sheet, and punch holes for positioning. Then fix it on the positioning pin.

c. Adhere the pre-made positive cross lines and corner lines to the required positions on the layout paper. Then cut opaque red paper to the specifications of each picture, and adhere it according to the layout picture position to make a positive reverse layout paper.

d. Use Lise sheets or SO soft films to copy a negative forward layout paper as the "cover plate", unified for all four color plates.

e. Cut four quarto (or open-size) transparent polyester sheets, punch holes, take one and overlay it on the "cover plate", fixing it with positioning pins.

f. First use a complete cyan negative screen sheet, according to the format composition and cropping ratio, sequentially paste it on (emulsion side up should be forward). When pasting, first compare with the small photos on the layout to avoid mistakenly reversing the image.

g. After the cyan plate is completed, remove the "cover plate" and use the cyan plate as the standard mother plate. Then take the second white sheet base, overlay it with the cyan plate, and fix it on the positioning pin. Align the second color negative image on the cyan image and paste according to the rule lines.

h. Follow the same process for the third and fourth colors. After the entire set of four-color plates is completed, send them along with the "cover plate" to the next process for copying the positive image.

3. Small Plate and Large Plate Imposition Method. Due to the current variety of imposition processes, the workflow also varies, resulting in diverse imposition methods. Recently, a new imposition process has been introduced, namely the small plate and large plate method, which is a fast and efficient imposition method.

Its production process flow is: color separation → impose small plate → proofing → impose large plate (output blue sample) → plate making → printing.

① Impose small plate. Imposing the small plate is the premise for imposing the large plate. Of course, after imposing the small plate, direct printing is also possible, such as small four-open size or zero-print jobs. The method of imposing the small plate is to combine pictures and texts into one plate.

At first glance, imposing the small plate seems easy, just utilizing positive and negative image transfers to obtain the composed small plate. In reality, it’s not so simple. First, you need to choose the optimal solution among feasible methods, ensuring quick time, saving materials, and maintaining good quality. Second, you need to understand some basic color knowledge and master revision techniques such as sketching and outlining. Third, you need to impose accurately, paying attention to avoid omissions or excesses, ensuring proper alignment of combined images without exposing fine gaps or overlapping.

To impose the small plate, you need necessary tools and materials, such as polyester specification sheets, white polyester sheet bases, steel rulers, scrapers, adhesive tapes, stands, positioning strips, regular adhesive tapes, red adhesive tapes, brushes, various mesh sheets, and color spectrum charts, etc. Some plate-making factories use positioning strips in three sizes: 12 inches, 8 inches, and 5 inches.

The composition of bottom color or lines, texts, patterns, etc., in some factories besides using special color plates, relies on photography to solve the color composition, which is hard to control and economically impractical due to too many printing colors. An advanced method is to use mesh sheets to achieve the desired result. Mesh sheets come in various types, with dot percentages ranging from 10% to 90%, generally in circular dots. Even those who have been engaged in color plate-making for a short time can select mesh sheets according to the color sample ratio on the color spectrum chart, achieving high accuracy.

② Impose large plate. Imposing the large plate involves three steps: preparation work, imposing, and checking samples and modifications.

a. Preparation work. According to customer requirements, paper size, finished product specifications, etc., create one or more format grid papers. Commonly used format grid papers need to be made of transparent polyester sheet bases with both sides fixed, with positioning holes punched at the bite end, labeled with the name of the plate being imposed, indicating the direction, and with cross rule lines and color marks attached at corresponding positions. Simultaneously cut the polyester sheet bases, dimensions, and number of grids according to the needs of imposition, punch holes after cutting. Then fix a polyester sheet (as the base plate for imposition) with the format grid using open-size positioning strips, and attach rule lines and color marks at the same position on the grid, labeling the name and color of the color plate being imposed at the bite end. The remaining three use the red plate as the base plate for attachment. Sort the color separation sheets according to the sample and color categories, clean them with a cleaner, and wait for imposition.

b. Imposition work. Consider which color plate to use as the base mother plate for the imposition work. Generally, landscape original manuscripts use the cyan plate, and portrait original manuscripts use the magenta plate. Positive or negative image imposition has different front and back sides, but one point is completely consistent: the emulsion side of the soft film faces up (outside), and during plate making, the emulsion side is tightly attached to the PS plate to ensure quality. Pay attention to placing the sample in reverse, starting with the bite end. Because the polyester plate grid is composed of fine grids, it is relatively convenient to paste the first magenta plate as the mother plate, as long as the image is not reversed or incorrectly aligned, leaving appropriate gaps between soft films.

There are three methods for attaching soft films to polyester sheets: one is using ordinary transparent adhesive tape; the second is using thin double-sided adhesive to attach between the soft film and the polyester sheet; the third is using spray adhesive, which is most advantageous for mesh parts (such as full-mesh plates with bleed on three sides). Spray adhesive has strong adhesion and dries quickly.

After the first plate is completed as the mother plate, remove the plate grid, overlay the second polyester sheet on the first plate, check if the rule lines of the two plates coincide, then use double-sided adhesive at both ends to attach the two plates, preventing misalignment. The second plate usually starts with the black plate, stacking the two plates together makes it easy to verify the alignment of images and text. The cyan and yellow plates are imposed similarly.

After the four colors are completed, carefully check once again by placing all plates on the positioning strips. You can also check each plate individually using the magenta plate as a reference. To protect the soft films from scratches and prevent stretching, after completing each plate, place it flat on a glass table or hang it in a soft film cabinet.

c. Output samples for inspection and modification. After all the plates are completed, output the blue sample. The blue sample obtained is not the so-called color sample, but rather a black-and-white sample in blue or purple-black, used to check the specifications, dimensions, image positions, texts, and sequences, etc.

The blue sample output work is completed by the imposition personnel themselves. The material is similar to blueprints. The output is done on a plate-making frame. Generally, the black plate is placed below, and the magenta and cyan plates are placed above, aligned on the positioning strips. After organizing, the samples can be sent for inspection and correction.

Outputting blue samples saves time compared to color proofs and is economical and simple. Why not produce color proofs? Besides the aforementioned reasons, it is also because: color separation sheets have already been color-proofed before imposing the large plate. If the initial sample is unsatisfactory, minor adjustments can be made and reproofed, or significant differences may lead to re-separation since major repairs involve considerable labor without significant quality improvement; under the same conditions, uniform ink color and paper are used, combining them on one large plate ensures basically balanced tones; the original manuscript quality is good, and the printing paper is superior to the proof paper, customers often focus on actual printing results rather than demanding exact original effects; soft film inspections occur before typesetting. If the sample lacks contrast or vivid colors, the magenta, cyan, or yellow sheets can be taken out and appropriately adjusted or recopied, controlling exposure time to increase contrast beyond the original, facilitating printing; the workplace maintains uniform temperature and humidity. The imposition method is accurate and reliable. Attention should be paid to technique and soft film during imposition, and after completion, they should be properly hung to avoid stretching deformation. If misalignment occurs, it can be detected and corrected before plate making.
