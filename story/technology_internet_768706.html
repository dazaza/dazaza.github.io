It's helpless for iPod to be replaced by iPhone, just like flowers falling.

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 21:04:49

The iPhone, which was launched in 2007, sparked a personal computing revolution that shook the world. The smartphone, which could be carried around, became an all-powerful device. Meanwhile, the portable music playback function of the iPhone and smartphones gradually marginalized the iPod, Apple's first transformative product since its launch in 2001.

On October 19, the American tech website CNET wrote in the lead of an article: "Apple's iTunes business continues to grow, but the product (iPod) that gave birth to iTunes has already entered its twilight years."

To what extent has the iPod been marginalized? In the fourth fiscal quarter analysts meeting, Apple CEO Cook said: "We are excited to enter the upcoming holiday sales season with products such as the iPhone 5c, iPhone 5s, iOS 7, Retina display iPad mini, new lightweight iPad Air, new MacBook Pros, greatly improved new Mac Pro, OS X Mavericks, and new iWork and iLife applications." In Cook's list, the iPod wasn't even mentioned.

A sudden collapse

For the declining iPod, it is no longer news that sales or revenue are declining. In Apple's 2013 fiscal year summary, Tencent Technology saw that the iPod is experiencing an "accelerated collapse."

In the 2013 fiscal year ending at the end of September, Apple sold 26.38 million iPods, a significant drop of 25% compared to 35.17 million units in the 2012 fiscal year. In comparison, the decline between 2012 and 2011 fiscal years was only 17%.

From a revenue perspective, the iPod generated $4.4 billion in revenue for Apple in the 2013 fiscal year, a substantial drop of 21% year-over-year, slightly less than the 25% decline in the 2012 fiscal year.

When the decline of a product or business reaches over 20%, the term "twilight" may seem too mild. A more precise description might be "plummet" or "collapse".

In Apple's analyst meetings, the iPod has been completely forgotten. The fundamental reason is that the iPad and iPhone have become Apple's two star businesses, and the revenue contributed by the iPod is almost negligible for Apple.

In the 2013 fiscal year, Apple's total revenue was $170.9 billion, with the iPod contributing only $4.4 billion, accounting for just 2.6% of total revenue. In contrast, the iPhone contributed 54% of Apple's revenue, while the iPad contributed 18.8%.

In various parts of the financial report, after describing the high growth brought to company revenue by the iPhone and iPad, Apple would always add a sentence: "This growth was partially offset by the decline in iPod sales."

Facing increasingly meager revenue contributions, Apple has lost interest in "tinkering" with the iPod. Earlier this year, Apple launched a lower-end version of the iPod Touch without a rear camera. However, it is well known that although the iPod Touch carries the "iPod" name, its use is closer to a portable smart device or a gaming console rather than a portable player.

The evolution of white fashion

Apple has become a globally recognized high-tech company because of its beautifully designed innovative products that not only captured the hearts of fans but also repeatedly shook the global high-tech industry. Among these, the iPhone introduced in 2007 and the iPad introduced in 2010 completely changed the trajectory of global personal computing development.

After the advent of these two great inventions, the global IT industry underwent tremendous changes. Home users favored smartphones and tablets, leading to a gradual loss of appeal for personal computers, which reached their peak and then began a historic decline.

In the smartphone sector, a vast number of applications expanded phone functionality. A range of electronic products, including portable music players, digital book readers, portable digital cameras, video recorders, and dedicated GPS navigation devices, faced being replaced or pushed out by smartphones.

While Apple dealt heavy blows to its competitors in the global tech industry, it also dealt a blow to itself.

The fate of the iPod today was perhaps determined on January 9, 2007, the day the iPhone was announced. At the time, Jobs declared: "Today, we are going to release three revolutionary products. First, a wide-screen iPod with touch control, second, a revolutionary phone, third, a completely new internet communication tool." In Jobs' view, the iPhone was not only a brand-new iPod but also had innovative functions like making calls and accessing the internet.

As iPhone sales continued to soar, reaching ubiquity, the iPod, which led Apple through its first transformation and helped it recover from the low point of its personal computer battles, began to decline rapidly.

In the 2000s, Apple's iPod and iconic white earphones created a fashion wave on the streets of America. Young people wore their white earphones exposed as a symbol of being fashionable trendsetters. White earphones became symbolic in many of Apple's iPod advertisements.

Almost a decade later, in Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, and other first-tier cities in China, even in inland and western second- and third-tier cities, a kind of "white earphone" Apple fashion culture emerged. On buses, subways, and streets, people saw white-collar workers, students, and even migrant workers immersed in the musical enjoyment brought by white earphones.

Only in this new fashion trend, the white earphones were no longer connected to the former iPod but to an iPhone.

Sympathetic MAC

It should be noted that Apple products experiencing "self-inflicted damage" are not limited to the iPod alone. As part of the global personal computer market, the MAC also faces the collective decline of its peers, though not with the rapid collapse seen by the iPod.

Tencent Technology found in Apple's reports that MAC computer sales in the 2013 fiscal year were only 16.34 million units, a 10% drop compared to 18.16 million units in the 2012 fiscal year, which had seen a 9% year-over-year increase. Similarly, in terms of revenue, Apple's MAC shrank by 7% in the 2013 fiscal year.

Notably, the 10% decline in the MAC business aligns closely with the approximately 10% global PC market decline reported by IDC and Gartner.

After losing in the PC vs MAC battle in the 1990s, MAC became a niche market in the personal computer segment. However, in recent years, Apple has innovated in ultrathin laptops, cushioning the sharp decline in desktops and overall computer business contraction.

A treasure in the hall of science and technology

The replacement of portable music players, including the iPod, by smartphones has clearly become an irreversible process. The reasons are simple: smartphones are becoming increasingly affordable and multifunctional, fully capable of replacing portable music players.

A tech analyst at the market research company HIS, Ian Fogg, stated that the penetration rate of smartphones has caught up with the peak penetration rate of iPods during its heyday. "Smartphones are more convenient than MP3 players. Because smartphones are something users carry around all the time. Smartphones also offer customers more options; they can be carried around like MP3 players to play music, but additionally, smartphone users can listen to FM radio or use streaming services like Spotify, making them more powerful than MP3 players."

Through the iPod, Apple bid farewell to the shadow of failure in the PC market and regained confidence. Today's Apple leads the evolution of global smartphones and tablets. Rapid product iteration has become a feature of today's high-tech market. We have every reason to believe that whether it's smartwatches or smart TVs, Apple will continue to introduce more innovative products and create new markets in the future. And the aging iPod will become a remarkable treasure in the history of human culture and technological development.
