Preventing a Year-End Loan Surge at Commercial Banks

Insiders have revealed that the credit lending intensity of the four major state-owned banks has suddenly increased recently. On October 30th and 31st alone, they issued a massive amount of 900 billion yuan in loans, bringing the total new loan volume for the entire month to approximately 240 billion yuan.

I noticed that after several months of national loan disbursements being lower than the same period in previous years, in October, RMB loans surged by 586.8 billion yuan, increasing by 17.5 billion yuan year-on-year, and adding 116.8 billion yuan more compared to September. In September, RMB loans grew by 470 billion yuan, decreasing by 131.1 billion yuan year-on-year. The overall data indicates a sharp increase in loans in October.

The four large banks—ICBC, ABC, BOC, and CCB—issued 900 billion yuan in just two days on October 30th and 31st, accounting for 37.5% of the four major banks' new loans in October and 15.3% of the total new loans from all financial institutions in October. This can be defined as a sudden surge in lending. If this situation is not addressed and curbed promptly, there will likely be a flood of year-end loan surges.

From the perspective of macroeconomic policy, in response to potential deep economic downturns, especially to address the funding difficulties faced by small and medium-sized enterprises and other real economy entities, it is necessary to fine-tune macro policies. At the same time, in October, the CPI rose by 5.5% year-on-year, declining continuously for three quarters. Some domestic experts have strongly advocated relaxing monetary and credit policies. Even IMF Managing Director Christine Lagarde recommended that China adjust its tightened monetary policy. This provides the macro background for commercial banks' year-end loan surges.

Analyzing from the perspective of commercial banks themselves, affected by regulatory pressures such as loan-to-deposit ratios and capital adequacy ratios, along with internal tightness in credit funds, credit issuance has been consistently tight this year. Many committed project loans or loans that have completed preliminary evaluations and entered the approval stage are eagerly awaiting disbursement. Moreover, as we approach the end of the year, operating profits need to be consolidated. To perfectly complete the annual performance indicators, commercial banks, which have been holding back loan issuance for ten months, have a strong incentive to engage in year-end loan surges.

The biggest argument and excuse for year-end loan surges is the fine-tuning of macroeconomic policies. However, it must be clearly understood that the goal of "maintaining reasonable growth in money and credit" lies in ensuring that monetary and credit policies support certain sectors while restricting others. The focus of reasonable growth in money and credit should be on ensuring funding needs for key ongoing national projects, supporting the real economy, especially SMEs that align with industrial policies, and supporting livelihood projects, particularly affordable housing projects. Loans to these projects, enterprises, and engineering works require a gradual process to ensure that the real economy can absorb them and ensure that the funds flow into the real economy.

If commercial banks engage in year-end loan surges, for example issuing 900 billion yuan in loans in just two days, the real economy may struggle to digest and absorb these funds. Ultimately, these loans could flow into speculative trading areas, fueling asset bubbles, exacerbating inflation, or even entering the usury industry, disrupting financial order and creating financial risks.

For commercial banks, year-end loan surges will inevitably lead to overcapacity operations, exceeding the loan-to-deposit ratio regulatory red line. In October, a peculiar phenomenon occurred: RMB loans increased by 586.8 billion yuan, but RMB deposits decreased by 201 billion yuan, decreasing by 361.8 billion yuan year-on-year. This "one increase, one decrease" will place dual pressures on commercial banks: one being the loan-to-deposit ratio regulatory red line pressure, and the other being funding pressure. This poses a significant threat to the prudent operation of commercial banks.

Therefore, it is necessary to strictly prevent year-end loan surges at commercial banks and rigorously supervise their credit operations. Fortunately, Shang Fulin, who recently took the helm at the CBRC, quickly observed the year-end loan surge phenomenon. At the fourth economic and financial situation briefing and analysis meeting held on November 11th, Shang Fulin emphasized maintaining high pressure on year-end and early next year's funds and strictly enforcing daily average deposit and loan indicator assessments. This statement shattered the banks' hopes for year-end loan surges.

After taking office as chairman of the CBRC, Shang Fulin proposed strengthening tracking inspections of loan fund flows to ensure that credit funds effectively support the real economy. He required banking financial institutions to adhere to risk management bottom lines, strengthen the prevention of key area risks, avoid systemic and regional risks, and strictly prohibit raising interest rates through short-term wealth management products to attract deposits. He also set strict regulatory requirements to prevent year-end loan surges, fully demonstrating his expertise as a senior leader in banking and securities regulatory departments and as the head of a large commercial bank.
