Cervical Disease Knowledge: Issues Related to the Cervix

Regarding the cervix, we actually know very little. Zhang Dihe said that the cervix is located at the bottom of the uterus, resembling a cone, measuring 2.5-3cm in length. Its lower end connects with the uterine body, and its upper end extends into the vagina. The cervix is an important line of defense against pathogens entering the uterus, fallopian tubes, and ovaries. This line of defense is also particularly susceptible to various pathogenic bacteria.

Question 1: If I pay attention to hygiene, will I not get cervicitis?

Case: Fresh Sister is 27 years old and has been married for half a year. But in the past two weeks, every time she had intercourse, she felt some pain in her vagina, with increased discharge that turned yellow. After examination, the doctor said she had cervicitis. However, Fresh Sister believed that both she and her partner were very hygienic, so how did she get cervicitis?

Expert Answer: When sexual activity is frequent, alkaline semen often enters the acidic vagina, causing the pH value of the vagina to change, which favors harmful bacteria growth and easily leads to acute cervicitis. Additionally, poor hygiene habits, using highly concentrated acidic or alkaline solutions to wash the vagina, childbirth, curettage, abortion, or other improper cervical operations may cause cervical infections leading to cervicitis.

Question 2: Can cervical erosion be left untreated?

Case: Ms. Lin was found to have mild cervical erosion during her last routine physical examination. She heard that cervical erosion is a more serious form of cervicitis, which worried her.

Expert Answer: Cervical erosion does not entirely equate to chronic cervicitis; only when there is cervical epithelial penetration and ulceration is it truly considered cervical erosion. In some physiological conditions, such as puberty, pregnancy, or taking oral contraceptives, due to increased levels of estrogen, the cervical tube columnar epithelium proliferates, which can result in physiological "cervical erosion." When estrogen levels decrease, the columnar epithelium retreats back into the cervical canal, and the "cervical erosion" resolves on its own without medication.

Question 3: Is cervical erosion a precancerous lesion of cervical cancer?

Case: Ms. Liu discovered she had cervical erosion during a physical examination. She heard that cervical erosion is a precancerous lesion of cervical cancer. At 45 years old, she is worried that she might develop cervical cancer like Anita Mui.

Expert Answer: The severity of cervical erosion, whether mild, moderate, or severe, merely represents the area of visible erosion seen by the naked eye and is not directly associated with cancerous changes. The real culprit behind cervical cancer is the Human Papillomavirus (HPV). HPV infection peaks in two age groups: 20-24 years and 40-44 years. Most women can naturally clear HPV infections. However, less than 10% of women cannot completely eliminate HPV from their bodies. In these cases, HPV integrates with DNA, altering the nature of squamous cells on the cervical surface, gradually eroding the normal cervix until cancer develops.

Cervical Disease Alert: Cervical Diseases Are Closer Than You Think

Gynecological experts presented a multiple-choice question about cervical diseases, asking which of the following statements are incorrect:

A. Cervicitis can lead to infertility.

B. Cervical cancer is an infectious disease.

C. Being young and avoiding promiscuity can prevent cervical cancer.

D. Mild cervical erosion can be left untreated.

The answer is C. "Keeping oneself safe, having multiple sexual partners, does not protect one from the dangers of cervical cancer," the expert emphasized. "Every woman, if not careful, can become a victim of this deadly disease."

However, studies show that the average number of lifetime sexual partners for Chinese women is much lower than for Western women. According to the logic that "sexual abstinence prevents cervical cancer," Chinese women should be less likely to suffer from cervical cancer. Wrong! In reality, Zhang Dier said that every year in China, 130,000 women are diagnosed with cervical cancer, and approximately 30,000 women die from it.

Preventing Cervical Diseases: Say No to Cervical Diseases

The five-year survival rate for cervical carcinoma in situ is 100%. The key lies in early detection and early diagnosis, and early detection of cervical cancer and precancerous lesions is entirely achievable.

First Prevention: Avoid promiscuity. HPV infection mainly spreads through sexual contact (other forms of transmission cannot be ruled out), so women should avoid having multiple sexual partners, starting sexual activity too early, and repeated abortions. Remember, promiscuity is a very risky behavior.

Second Prevention: Get checked regularly. Cervicitis is not equivalent to HPV infection or precancerous lesions of cervical cancer. However, women with cervicitis have an incidence of cervical cancer that is approximately 11 times higher than those without cervicitis. Therefore, when doctors tell you that you have cervical erosion, it's best to undergo cervical cancer screening, either through a cervical cytology test or TCT.

Third Prevention: Detect early. Women should be alert if they notice any of the following situations: 1. Contact bleeding (bleeding after intercourse); 2. Pus-like vaginal discharge; 3. Irregular vaginal bleeding; 4. Vaginal bleeding during pregnancy; 5. Young women experiencing blood-streaked vaginal discharge. All these cases should prompt a visit to a regular hospital for examination.
