Introduction:

The story takes place in the early 17th century when the American continent was still inhabited by a large number of indigenous peoples of different races. Each race had its own distinct culture within the same environment, and they coexisted relatively peacefully. This balance was eventually broken by the arrival of outsiders. In the spring of March 1607, three ships carrying 103 British settlers slowly entered the tribal territory. Their goal was to establish a foothold for their national culture, religion, and economy in the "New World." Onboard was a 27-year-old young man named John Smith who was shackled and scheduled to be hanged upon arrival. Smith, a veteran survivor of European wars, possessed enough wisdom and experience to save his own life. After anchoring, he successfully convinced the captain to release him, arguing that every able-bodied person was needed to build the colony.

During their exploration journey, the colonial group unknowingly entered the territory of Chief Powhatan. For the chief and his tribe, it was a secluded world where they had lived for generations. Thus, these unexpected visitors from Britain were unwelcome, and there were many communication barriers between the two groups. Smith hoped to find an assistant among the locals to help him overcome the language difficulties. At this moment, he encountered a young woman who seemed like an ethereal forest sprite. Her family and friends called her "Pocahontas," the beloved daughter of Chief Powhatan. A unique bond developed between Smith and Pocahontas, transcending friendship and even romance.

Behind-the-scenes Production:

[Live-action version of "Pocahontas"]

In 1995, Disney produced the classic 2D animated film "Pocahontas," bringing the legendary love story of British pioneer John Smith and Native American princess Pocahontas to the big screen. However, as early as the 1970s, director Terrence Malick was moved by the beautiful emotions between the two and intended to make it into a movie. Producer Sarah Green said: "Terrence completed the first draft of 'The New World' 25 years ago. Like his other directed films, 'The New World' is filled with deep analysis and understanding of human nature. The film tells our history, weaving together our flaws, strengths, virtues, and evolving ideologies through love. The characters in the story betray each other repeatedly but ultimately learn many truths, discovering that they can only rely on themselves. There are no true heroes or villains here; everyone is sympathetic and flawed."

Uniquely, Malick conducted in-depth research on various events that occurred in Jamestown 400 years ago and incorporated his insights into the film. "We don't know much about the events of 1607," producer Green said, "but we must follow the literary records of the time. Among them, the most famous figure is John Smith. We strive to use the legend of John Smith and Pocahontas to serve Terrence's intentions and base the film's story on real events. Some details and fates of real-life figures have been altered to advance the plot and drama. Certain events have also been compressed because this is not a documentary; it should be presented through dramatic storytelling."

When looking for an actor to play John Smith, Malick clearly saw Colin Farrell as the top choice. The 28-year-old Farrell was the same age as Smith at the time of his landing in North America and was also an energetic and charming explorer. Malick contacted Farrell from the very beginning.

Before joining this film, Farrell had already gained consistent praise from audiences and critics for his performances in popular works such as "Phone Booth," "Daredevil," and "Tigerland." Making the decision to join this film wasn't difficult for him. He said, "You don't even need to read the script to decide, because every film by Terrence is the best proof."

Regarding Christian Bale, who played John Rolfe, producer Green said: "For years, Terrence and I have highly regarded Christian Bale. He is not only intelligent and talented in acting but also has a keen insight. He is willing to disappear into the role of John Rolfe and deliver understated performances when necessary."

In the casting process, finding the best actress to portray Pocahontas was the most important and challenging task. There has always been controversy regarding her identity, age, appearance, and relationship with John Smith. The only certainty is that she was very young, only 12 or 13 years old, which was considered adult by native standards. The actress not only had to display her innocence and vitality but also convey her experienced qualities. Therefore, the production team was once in a predicament.

Producer Green recalled: "It's simple to find mature or innocent actresses, but those who embody both traits are rare. We spent months searching throughout the U.S. and Canada and later expanded our search globally. In the last month, we narrowed down our options to actresses with talent, charm, and beauty. At the time, casting director Rene Haynes was signing Q'orianka Kilcher for another movie, and one of Rene's assistants noticed that she would be suitable for 'The New World,' so she recommended her. We eventually met her, and she was not only captivating in appearance but also exuded calmness and depth beyond her years."

Kilcher's father is a Peruvian native, and the 15-year-old was skilled in music, dance, and singing. She had only starred in one movie before, which was the 2000 film "How the Grinch Stole Christmas." Kilcher not only radiated youthful energy but also possessed noble grace. Although casting a newcomer as the lead carried significant risks, her Native American heritage helped her portray Pocahontas. She didn't need training because that was her way of life. Appearing without makeup during the audition, Kilcher instantly captivated the main creative team, and everyone believed she was Pocahontas.

[About Filming]

The cinematographer for this film was Emmanuel Lubezki, known for works such as "Chocolat," "Where the Wild Things Are," "Sleepy Hollow," and "Lemony Snicket's A Series of Unfortunate Events." He helped director Malick realize the idea of shooting the entire film under natural light. Actor Noah Taylor said: "Terrence didn't use any artificial lighting, so there were no cables or various lights on set. This not only created a genuine atmosphere but also allowed the cast and crew to focus on filming without being distracted by lighting issues."

To ensure the film was as historically accurate as possible, the production team organized a consulting panel, including William Kelso, the archaeological director of the Jamestown Restoration Project, independent scholar Frederic Gleach, and Blair Rudes, an associate professor of linguistics at the University of North Carolina.

The production team first needed to solve the problem of selecting shooting locations. Producer Green believed that in today's America, it was impossible to find places as remote and pristine as the James River and Chickahominy River in 1607. Green said: "We thought it should be a mysterious, uninhabited place, so we hoped to find forests and rivers out of sight in Canada. However, production designer Jack Fisk believed our efforts would be in vain, and we would end up back where we started. So, Terrence, Jack, and I explored the original site of James Fort and the surrounding areas. Surprisingly, while traveling upstream along the Chickahominy River, we unexpectedly found many suitable places for filming. With the help of the local government, we decided to shoot there because Virginia's landscape is irreplaceable."

For production designer Jack Fisk, rewinding time to recreate Virginia in the early 17th century was an unprecedented and daunting challenge. He said: "Although I live in Virginia, I knew little about the Native American and British cultures of the time when Jamestown was founded. Everything in my memory only came from history textbooks." To make the film as authentic as possible, Fisk read all relevant books and was delighted to discover that the Jamestown Restoration Project greatly aided the filming. Archaeologists dedicated to the project provided valuable insights based on actual discoveries. During their first meeting, Fisk gained invaluable knowledge and immediately revised his initial designs.

However, completing the design was just the first step. The subsequent construction work also had to meet high standards because Malick was an unconventional director who required all sets to be as realistic as possible and three-dimensional. Fisk said: "Directors like Terrence who don't look at blueprints are rare. He simply said that whatever you're building, we will document it as if we're making a documentary. So, the more complete the construction, the more he could utilize. He doesn't like shooting from one direction because he doesn't use any lighting equipment, so he changes angles according to the position of the sun."

Compared to the original colonists, the production crew was fortunate because they could use trucks, chainsaws, and bulldozers. Nevertheless, the construction process still took considerable time. It took 30 days just to build the outer walls, and the inner walls took a whole month and a half. Although the final James Fort shown in the film was one-quarter smaller than the actual size, its internal structure, materials, and many details were absolutely authentic.

In rebuilding the Native American village featured in the film, since there were no relevant written records available, Fisk and his team had to rely on the sketches and descriptions left by John White and John Smith from that era. Fisk said: "Recreating the Native American civilization of the time posed some challenges for me because the Native Americans were consumed by the colonizers' greed for land, and many historical documents have disappeared. Tragically, they lacked a written language to record their history. We can only understand their culture through surviving materials and archaeology, so there is inevitably some imagination involved."

In March 1607, 103 British settlers arrived in North America aboard three large ships, and the film aims to recreate this historic scene. If they had to rebuild these three ships, it would have taken a long time and cost $2 million. Fortunately, the production team could use the three ships preserved and displayed at the Jamestown Colony History Museum, located less than five miles away from the main exterior location, making it almost too ideal. Executive producer Trish Hofmann said: "We had to closely cooperate with the Jamestown-Yorktown Foundation because these ships were exhibited in the Jamestown Colony and were important tourist attractions. We treated them with great care, like stars."

Production designer Jack Fisk said: "We repainted these ships, aged them, and created the effect of having endured several months of wind and rain. I think they were like the trucks we see on highways, rough and merely transportation tools. They couldn't be comfortable, and they had no special features. When they arrived at Jamestown, they were definitely overloaded."

There’s an important scene in the film where the three ships sail towards the landing point on the James River. This was challenging for the crew because, according to the agreement, one ship had to remain in place for exhibition purposes. Moreover, the ship portraying Susan Constant had too deep a draft to appear on the Chickahominy River. The production team had to improvise and used a replica of Henry Hudson's "Half Moon" to substitute convincingly.

Fun Facts:

To enhance the lighting during interior shots, each candle used by the crew had four wicks.

Before filming began, director Terrence Malick and cinematographer Emmanuel Lubezki established a series of photography guidelines: no use of artificial light sources; no use of cranes or dolly tracks, only handheld cameras or stabilizers; shooting from a subjective perspective; using only deep-focus lenses; encouraging the crew to capture unexpected moments; selective shooting, discarding unimportant shots.

Actors playing indigenous people had to learn Algonquian, a language spoken today by only 3,000 people worldwide. Some of them were hired as dialect coaches.

More than one million feet of film were used during the shooting.

Over 2,000 actors were auditioned before the right actress was found to play Pocahontas.

To prepare for his role, Colin Farrell read all seven books published by John Smith after he returned to England.

All actors had to lose 20 pounds within a month and learn how to use weapons and live like colonists.

Despite budget constraints, the film is still the first feature-length non-effects movie shot on 65mm film in nine years. The last film entirely shot on 65mm was "Hamlet" in 1996.

The original version of the film was 150 minutes long and premiered in December 2005. A few days after its limited release, director Terrence Malick decided to retrieve the copies and re-edit the film, reducing its runtime to 135 minutes.

Both Christian Bale and Irene Bedard, who starred in this film, previously provided voices for the 1995 Disney animated film "Pocahontas."

In an interview, Christian Bale mentioned Terrence Malick's peculiar directing style. He once wondered how the director would react if he stepped out of frame and approached the crew members. Unexpectedly, Malick followed him with the camera, causing the crew to scatter and vehicles to hide behind trees to avoid the lens.

Most of the dialogues in the film were recorded post-production because the background sounds during the actual shooting often included Terrence Malick's voice.

Bloopers:

The film mentions that Smith left Jamestown due to the king's orders, but in reality, it was because he suffered severe injuries in a gunpowder accident and had to return to England for treatment and recovery.
