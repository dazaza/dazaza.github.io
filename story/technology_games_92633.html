Underestimated Chinese game manufacturers fight back - repurchase shares - Age1983

by hexiaoyao on 2008-09-17 17:04:00

Post-Olympic China has yet to fully come out from the Olympic fervor. Domestic game companies may seem calm on the surface, but underneath the apparent tranquility lies a brewing storm. However, this round of turbulence does not manifest in the gaming world, but rather in the capital market. In just a few days, several major domestic online game companies or companies involved in online games have successively announced high-profile stock repurchase plans.

China's online game enterprises are undervalued

The first to raise the banner of repurchase was Shi Yuzhu and his Giant company. In mid-August, Giant Network announced a stock repurchase plan worth up to $1.5 billion. Subsequently, on September 8, NetDragon announced that it had made four moves to repurchase shares between September 3 and September 8, accumulating a total of 2.328 million shares repurchased; On September 9, Shanda Interactive Entertainment approved a $2 billion stock repurchase plan, gradually repurchasing American Depositary Receipts (ADRs) worth up to $2 billion over the next 12 months; On September 11, Tencent stated that it repurchased 250,000 shares of the company on that day, with a total value of HK$143.9505 million; On September 12, The9 Limited approved a $50 million ADR repurchase plan; On September 13, NetEase approved a $1 billion ADR repurchase plan...

In other words, within about a week, almost all domestic online game companies listed overseas announced a similarly aimed plan: stock repurchase, with each company's plan involving amounts reaching hundreds of millions. Is this merely a coincidence or a bandwagon effect? It is highly unlikely. The formulation of such a large-scale plan cannot be simply due to following trends and requires a considerable amount of time for operation. The real key lies in the fact that almost all online game companies discovered the same issue at roughly the same time: they were being undervalued.

Shi Yuzhu's explanation seems to represent the common public statement of other game companies repurchasing stocks: "The current stock price does not reflect the potential value of Giant Network's stock. The repurchase plan reflects our consistent commitment to enhancing shareholder value." As a common operational model in the capital market, one of the main purposes of stock repurchases abroad is to stabilize the company's stock price. An excessively low stock price will undoubtedly have a significant impact on the company's operations. A low stock price reduces people's confidence in the company, causes consumers to doubt the company's products, and weakens the company's ability to sell products and expand markets. In such a situation, the company repurchases its own shares to support its stock price, which helps improve the company's image. As the stock price rises, investors refocus on the company's operations, consumer trust in the company's products increases, and the company also gains further opportunities for equity financing. The current situation indeed aligns with this. The current stock prices of the several online game companies repurchasing stocks are close to or even lower than their issue prices, forcing them to take measures to protect their image and the interests of investors.
