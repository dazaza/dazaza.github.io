It's been a few years since I fell in love with hazelnuts. Funny enough, it all started because of their unsaturated fatty acids, phosphorus, iron, potassium, as well as carotene, vitamins, and niacin. As for the taste, I wasn't really too fond of it; it was more like something that could be there or not.

Back then, I would flip through a hefty classic parenting book every day. The experts said hazelnuts were good, so naturally, they became a "mandatory item" on the menu.

At that time, online shopping wasn't popular yet, and it was rare to find hazelnuts in local malls. We had to rely on family and friends traveling to different parts of the country to "hunt" them down. Thus, each hazelnut carried several times more weight in sentiment. Every time I cracked open the shell with a click, along with its unique fragrance, came the affectionate warmth of family ties.

Gradually, I fell in love with it. Perhaps due to this story, Miss Kangaroo has loved hazelnuts since she was little. Sometimes, she would even choose hazelnut-flavored chocolate sauce.

There was one exception though. One morning, she took a bag of hazelnuts to school as a snack, and surprisingly, half of it was returned by evening (keep in mind, no snacks ever made it back from school intact). When asked, "Does everyone dislike it?" The answer came, "No, they like it. It's just too dry. You have to drink a lot of water after eating it..."

This actually gave me an idea. The next day, I specifically bought a bag of hazelnuts as raw material, crushed them in a food processor, and made cookies.

It was the packaged roasted hazelnuts from the supermarket, indeed dry and crispy. After a few dozen seconds in the food processor, they turned out like this. If you prefer the texture of nuts, you can crush them for a few seconds less, leaving larger chunks.

The recipe referenced butter shortbread and chocolate shortbread cookies, with slight adjustments: 85g butter, 50g sugar, 50g hazelnuts, 1 egg, 180g all-purpose flour. Baking: middle rack, preheated to 160 degrees for 18 minutes.

I used molds of two different sizes. Originally, I thought the small stars and hearts would be more popular, but unexpectedly, the kids were more interested in the larger ones, mainly curious about how these double-layered ones were made. Let her guess, and she got it right. Haha, was that a deliberate question?

Two batches were baked. The first batch was thicker, the second batch thinner.

At the same temperature and time (160 degrees for 18 minutes), the second batch was noticeably more golden brown (still edible, fortunately). Apparently, the baking time needs to be adjusted according to thickness. Even a difference of a minute or dozens of seconds shouldn't be overlooked.

Because of the reduced butter and sugar, the cookies weren't at all greasy, further highlighting the fragrance of the hazelnuts.
