Baidu Xiaosheng: The Android app I'm using

by xdash on 2011-01-18 21:06:24

(The author of this article, Xiaosheng, is currently a product manager at Baidu's Mobile Department and co-founder of Mobile Design China.)

As professionals in the mobile internet industry, we need to study various popular Android/iOS applications, especially those similar to our own projects. We must understand them from the perspectives of visual design, interaction design, information architecture, and operations. However, as a Moto Milestone user, I also use various applications in daily life. Coming from an industrial design background, I have a preference for simplicity, favoring brands like MIJI, IKEA, 1984, and Douban. When choosing mobile apps, I naturally have high visual interface requirements, much like how programmers are drawn to cutting-edge technology. Professional backgrounds can even influence the way we choose products in our daily lives.

Invited by XDash to discuss commonly used applications, there’s no need to mention familiar ones like Gmail, Gtalk, and browsers. Instead, I’ll focus on four lesser-known "daily essentials."

Jorte

Jorte Notebook is akin to a simplified version of Google Calendar, with its core functions being straightforward and easy to use. Less frequently used features are tucked away in the settings. Upon opening the app, the first page displays the calendar, allowing for a quick overview of schedules. You can select different visual styles and arrangement methods, with dynamic effects during view transitions and swipe gestures to flip pages. It supports synchronization with Jorte or Google calendars, data export and import, making it appealing to those who prioritize syncing capabilities.

The app efficiently meets most users' calendar needs quickly. It offers up to 16 different styles of desktop widgets—more than any other application—which can satisfy any screen display space requirement.

When clicking a date to create a new event, a dialog box pops up with options for "previous day" and "next day," which are somewhat redundant. The Moto Milestone has a resolution of 480*854 pixels; even when in landscape mode with a resolution of 320 pixels, each date's clickable area reaches 45 pixels. After converting precision, this already satisfies the average adult user's fingertip click area, with minimal chances of accidental clicks.

Clicking on a single task allows you to view task details, set status, and edit. The underline style for “show details” follows web design norms, but personally, I find such designs in client applications off-putting. This may also be related to Android's own control design. Hiding some less commonly used "advanced" options within the page aligns well with user operation habits.

Compared to Jorte, the sixth-ranked 365 Calendar in the Android market has a slightly more complex design, with more information and features on its pages. 365 Calendar is a typical example of iOS design being directly transplanted onto Android, responding poorly to physical back button operations, making personal interactions feel awkward.

365 Calendar includes lunar terms, fitting the national context. Schedules and calendars merely differ in viewing modes, with significant repetition of information between adjacent views. Initially, the default display shows Beijing weather, which I thought could be modified in “more” or “settings,” but after two failed attempts, I realized that in the “tools” weather section, automatic location isn’t supported—a common issue when too many features are added. In terms of desktop widgets, combining with time makes 365's widget more convenient to use than Jorte's, occupying prime screen real estate.

Baidu Around

Just released in December, Baidu Around already has a WAP version and an Android version. The polar bear on the launch screen is quite cute and looks very friendly. From a personal aesthetic perspective, the previous Dianping app's interface design needed significant changes, yet after six months of use, the interface design remained largely unchanged. Newer LBS products like Baidu Around, Shengda Check-in, and QQ Food are all more visually appealing. However, currently, Dianping still holds a clear data advantage.

For LBS products, personally, I don't care about check-in functions but rather what convenient information services they provide. After asking several peers in the LBS field about improvements to user check-ins, the answer was simply that users enjoy checking in, sharing their current location and activities. Personally, I think this mindset is overly simplistic, as it makes the product extremely pure. There's still much design potential around check-ins or user geographic locations.

Regarding Baidu Around, I'm interested in how it helps me quickly discover nearby merchants, obtain information to help me set action goals, and what I can do once I've reached my destination. Baidu Around requires further refinement in its interface details; seeing so many ">" on the homepage drives me crazy. What's displayed on a mobile product's main interface reflects the product's design priorities. For instance, Dianping's main page shows commercial districts and channels, while Jiapang's main page focuses on friends' check-in information.

Mtime Movie Network

If you frequently go to the movies, Mtime is indispensable. Last year, I worked on a mobile product for movie inquiries and seat selection, so I paid close attention to Mtime, especially since I genuinely had a need to check movie information.
