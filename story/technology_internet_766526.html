The Finnish People's Nostalgia for Nokia

by anonymous on 2013-09-18 21:43:57

Amid a chorus of recriminations, some Finns have claimed that Elop's deliberate course made Microsoft the only potential bidder. However, despite Nokia's recent dramatic fall in market value, the reality is more complex. Former executives also bear responsibility, including those who, under former chairman and chief executive Jorma Ollila, rescued Nokia from near obscurity during its first transformation.

As Nokia's position strengthened in the early years of the 21st century, hubris and bureaucracy began to creep in. The group headquarters in Espoo, a suburb of Helsinki, was known as the "PowerPoint Palace," filled with middle managers obsessed with internal politics and fond of using Microsoft PowerPoint for presentations. In light of Microsoft's acquisition, this nickname seems both fittingly ironic and poignant today. Riitta Nieminen-Sundell, a sociologist who worked at Nokia until 2005, described Nokia's story as a "Greek tragedy."

If so, Finns hope the next act of this tragedy will be more compelling. Valtteri Halla, who led the development of Nokia's own MeeGo operating system and is now the CTO of start-up Leia Media, said: "The Nokia palace has collapsed. It was once a magnificent fortress, and after its collapse, large blocks of stone are scattered everywhere, and people can still make something out of them."

Nieminen-Sundell stated: "Nokia educated one or two generations of Finns on how to conduct international business, instilling the belief that if you want something badly enough, you can achieve it, making people realize that we are not just a small country next to Sweden, but as strong as Russia."

Vapaavuori, whose ministerial office overlooks the port and the historic Aleksanterinkatu district in central Helsinki, said that the psychological blow to Finnish self-esteem caused by Nokia's decline far exceeds its economic impact.

Finnish start-ups and small tech companies are trying to validate his point, including game companies Supercell (maker of Clash of Clans) and Rovio (creator of Angry Birds). Another is Jolla, whose staff are mostly former Nokia employees, developing open-source software and innovative phones, sharing a building with Supercell. (Jolla means "little sailboat" in Finnish, subtly referring to it as a lifeboat leaving a sinking ship.)

Jolla co-founder Antti Saarnio said that the acquisition message sent a signal to Finland that "it's time to wake up," suggesting that more support should be given to small and medium-sized tech enterprises. Supercell CEO Ilkka Paananen echoed this view on Twitter, saying "Finland needs this" after the announcement of the deal.

Although the transition to the "post-Nokia era" began a couple of years ago, an obvious question remains: even as these smaller companies grow, they cannot be expected to replace the 14,000 jobs Nokia cut in Finland over the past three years, along with other positions that previously relied on Nokia.

Former Nokia employee Ebba Dahli said: "One of the biggest challenges is that restructuring is not just happening at Nokia, but also in the shipbuilding and paper industries." Dahli is now a partner at Kaato, which finds business opportunities and employees for foreign investors in Finland.

Ara-Pitkälä's report on the future prospects of the technology industry partly aims to make the most of what Nokia has, applying its technological skills in many different fields to industries beyond telecommunications.

Despite the enthusiasm and ambition of start-ups, their importance in job creation does not match the continued investment by Microsoft and other large multinational corporations operating in Finland.

While Finns may appear calm on the surface about the deal involving Microsoft's acquisition of Nokia's phone business, there was a noticeable sigh of relief shortly after Microsoft's announcement when U.S.-based Broadcom stepped in to rescue Renesas' Finnish company, deciding to retain hundreds of jobs in Oulu, where Nokia remains a major employer.

Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer went to great lengths to assure Finnish politicians and Nokia employees that Finland would remain an important part of Microsoft's strategy. At a Nokia smartphone factory in Salo, northwest of Helsinki, when one of the 1,200 employees asked Ballmer, "Does this mean we'll all get Xboxes?" Ballmer replied affirmatively.

Kirjander, currently a director at computer security firm F-Secure, said that regardless of the pain it causes those who contributed to Nokia's success, selling the mobile phone business to Microsoft makes sense. He stated: "Nokia has rid itself of things that could have drowned the entire company, while Microsoft has obtained something that allows it to continue advancing its mobile strategy."

In the long term, to fill the hole left by Nokia's contraction in Finland, Microsoft needs to do more than just give certain employees gaming consoles or invest £250 million in building a new data center in Finland.

Strangely, Finns believe that this hole may feel larger to some who had no direct stake in Nokia but have become accustomed to people using Nokia as a calling card to recognize their country from Barcelona to Beijing. Now that Nokia's fate has been settled, Finns inside and outside of Nokia may find it easier to stop tallying past mistakes and start working towards building the future.

Samuli Hanninen, an engineer and Nokia vice president responsible for smartphone imaging technology, said that when he learned of the deal on a Saturday, he "opened a beer and did some gardening." When he announced the news to his team and asked if anyone had questions, one engineer raised his hand and said, "Can we go back to work now?"
