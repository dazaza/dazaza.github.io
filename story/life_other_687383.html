Love comes first, resentment comes later...

by huiming on 2012-03-02 15:28:11

Love comes first, resentment comes later...

Gradually, she felt that the complaints and arguments between them were increasing for no reason, so much so that it seemed like they couldn't go on living like this anymore.

He probably had the same feeling.

After marriage, all the real-life habits, hobbies, personalities... gradually surfaced. At first, they still accommodated each other, but slowly they started to feel tired and began to complain, then argue.

They also argued during their courtship, but arguments are not the same as arguments. Back then, even when they fought, there was a sweet taste to it, a soft sense of委屈and an urgent expectation for reconciliation.

Back then, they both felt deeply in love, even fights were out of love. That's why they got married.

But why did the flavor change after marriage?

For example, one evening, they had agreed to have dinner at his place. So they went. His parents loved her, although there was a deliberate component of loving her because of him. She understood this relationship and got along well with them.

However, during that dinner, his mother suddenly brought up the topic of children and asked, "Why don't you two want a child? You've been married for two years."

She stopped eating, holding a mouthful of food, and looked up at him. He was eating with his head down, and without thinking replied, "She doesn't want one."

She almost choked on her food.

That's right, she didn't want one, but he didn't explain clearly that she just wasn't ready yet. She was only 26, and in her plan, having a child was something for after 30 or even 33. They had discussed this before marriage, and he had strongly supported it. Now, how did it become that she didn't want one?

His mother's face darkened slightly and muttered, "Anyway, I need to hold my grandson, you can't be like those who don't want children."

She reluctantly explained, "We want to have one later."

Whether early or late, you must have one, earlier is better. The mother-in-law stated her point of view.

At such times, she expected him to help ease the tension for her, after all, he was closer to his mother. As long as he took some responsibility, she wouldn't be troubled.

But he said, "You're right."

She was so angry she almost threw the remaining half bowl of rice at him.

It was exactly this small matter that became the last straw in her accumulated emotions, making her finally feel sad. She thought that they really didn't love each other as much as before, resentment had slowly covered and replaced love, whether it was his feelings towards her or hers towards him.

Thinking about this, she cried.

Because of her silence and her many tears, he finally started to panic a little. Clearly wanting to comfort her, but not knowing how. All the tricks he used during their courtship were now old hat.

She angrily said, "I never want to see you again!" After saying this, she felt a pang in her heart, this sentence seemed to have been said too many times after marriage, more and more often.

He sighed, finally bending down to squat in front of her, "It's my fault, I didn't mean it just now."

However, she felt the weakness in his apology.

They both fell silent.

Later, she finally stopped crying, raised her head. She saw him sitting opposite her, looking at her with the same somewhat sad eyes.

Their gazes seemed to be asking the same question: Do we really not love each other anymore?

Then, there was silence again. After a long while, he spoke first, and the topic he chose made her feel a bit baffled. He asked her, "Let's think about this: after getting married, what are the most common things we say to each other, okay?"

She was stunned for a moment, vaguely understanding his meaning, and nodded.

He also nodded, stood up and went to the study, taking out paper and pen and handing them to her, then returned to the study.

She sat there, starting to think about their life over these two years, the words they often said to each other. After a while, she bent down and started writing seriously on the paper.

When she finished, he also came out with a piece of paper, then sat next to her and handed her the paper with a few lines written on it.

She took it, and saw these words:

"In these two years, the things I've said to you the most are 'be good', 'baby', 'dear', 'little fool'... Then 'look at the traffic lights when crossing the road, don't send messages when going downstairs'... Then 'don't make me angry, I'm really mad'... And then 'have you said enough? Why are you becoming more unreasonable? Can you give me some face?'..."

As she read, the tears she had just stopped fell again, and she clearly saw his vision blur, because on her paper, it was written this way:

"In these two years, the things I've said to you the most are 'pig', 'pig head', 'big fool', 'dear bear'... Then 'drink less alcohol', 'no smoking', 'after playing ball, don't take a cold shower'... Then 'you wash the dishes, the house isn't mine'... And then 'how have you become like this? I never want to see you again! I knew your love for me was fake'..."

His handwriting was square and neat, hers was small and round, writing seemingly different content, but expressing the same meaning: between them, resentment existed, but always ranked last. What always ranked first was love, a love that far exceeded resentment by many times. It was just that their young and stubborn selves had magnified the resentment, allowing the amplified resentment to bury such deep love.

Now, they know. In that moment, they filled their love with new courage, because as long as love comes first and resentment comes last, they will always be able to walk forward hand in hand.
