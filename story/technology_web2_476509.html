(1) Jar Breeding

It can be used as a breeding ground for centipede reproduction and for raising small and medium-sized centipedes. The soil should not contain brick debris, weeds, grass leaves, melon shells, fruit peels, or grass roots. Its humidity should be such that when squeezed by hand, it forms a lump but scatters upon landing (with a moisture content of 15% to 17%). In midsummer and early autumn, it can be slightly moister. Similarly, breeding baby and medium-sized centipedes can also be made moister. The soil particles must not be too large. Each board can hold 15 jars, which can prevent natural enemies and stack the jars layer by layer, fully utilizing space. Clean the bottles thoroughly with concentrated alkali water, rinse them with clean water until they are transparent and odorless, then dry them and fill them with 2-3 cm thick moist soil. Using canned jars as centipede nests is ideal. Each jar can house one breeding centipede, or 20-80 baby centipedes, or 2-6 medium-sized centipedes. This ensures both reasonable density and solves the problem of mutual cannibalism.

(2) Greenhouse Breeding

Build a double wall around the breeding room 10 cm away from the walls, filling the gap between the walls tightly with sawdust or dry fine soil. The ceiling should be made into a double layer filled tightly to form an insulating layer. The door of the greenhouse should not be too large; just enough for one person to pass through. Attach velvet cloth around the door frame and nail burlap or cotton padding outside the door for insulation. Coal briquette stoves, electric heaters, wood shavings stoves, and firewood stoves can all be used for heating. Generally, for a breeding room measuring 3m x 3m x 2.25m, one coal briquette stove suffices.

Factors Affecting Centipede Activity: Centipedes generally come out at night, with peak activity between 20:00 and 23:00. Their characteristics include more nighttime activity and less daytime activity; more activity when temperatures exceed 25°C and less when temperatures are between 10°C and 15°C; more activity on rainy nights and less on rainy days; more activity on windless or slightly windy nights and less on windy nights (wind force above six). Thus, the frequency of centipede activity is closely related to meteorological factors such as temperature, air pressure, relative humidity, rainfall, and light intensity.

Centipede Feed Sources:

To succeed in farming, solving the feed issue is paramount. Centipedes primarily consume meat-based feeds, which must be fresh and clean. Suitable options include pork lungs, hearts, chicken intestines, small fish, frogs, snakes, earthworms, various insects, and meats. Depending on local conditions, choose the most cost-effective and stable supply as the main food source, while using relatively higher-cost items as supplementary feeds. This approach reduces costs while enriching the nutritional value of the feed.

Handling Centipede Bites:

When ordinary people are bitten by centipedes, swelling, pain, redness, and itching occur. The pain is sharp and needle-like, recurring several times per second, though it remains within tolerable limits.

Upon being bitten, immediately squeeze out the venomous blood and wash with alkaline water. Without treatment, the pain will completely disappear within six to eight hours. During this time, you can apply mosquito repellent or menthol oil to alleviate symptoms. If the pain becomes unbearable, seek medical attention for a local anesthetic injection.

One important point to note: Centipede venom is similar to bee venom. Those with a history of bee venom allergies should avoid centipede farming. Although only one in ten thousand cases result in severe reactions, centipede bites can be dangerous for those with allergic constitutions. A centipede's venom is equivalent to being stung by multiple bees simultaneously. If symptoms such as dizziness, fainting, full-body rashes, chills, etc., occur after a bite, seek immediate medical attention for desensitization treatment.
