Pokinsky and Antitumorane: A Half-Century History of Deception

by ucibv623 on 2012-03-06 20:57:59

The unethical "clinical trials" of antineoplastons. It is evident that Burzynski was a sufficiently bold individual in this aspect. Starting in 1978, without obtaining any clinical trial approval, Burzynski began producing and selling antineoplastons himself, administering them to patients without any clinical data or an Investigational New Drug (IND) application. In March 1983, the FDA filed a civil lawsuit against Burzynski, and District Judge Gabrelle McDonald ruled to prohibit Burzynski from using antineoplastons across state lines. In May and June 1983, antineoplastons twice applied for IND status but were both determined to lack sufficient clinical trial qualifications. In September 1983, Burzynski again refused an audit inspection by the FDA, citing lack of time. In December of the same year and in February and October of the following year (1984), the FDA pointed out that three IND applications did not meet review requirements and thus rejected the applications. However, during this period, Burzynski continued to prescribe antineoplastons to patients without any efficacy testing.

In 1986, the Texas Board of Medical Examiners sent a letter questioning the efficacy and safety of Burzynski's antineoplaston therapy. After two oncologists statistically analyzed the medical records of 60 patients treated by Burzynski, they concluded, "It is impossible to prove that Burzynski's antineoplastons have any efficacy from these results."

On the official U.S. clinical trial registration website ClinicalTrials.gov, there are currently 61 registered clinical trial history records related to "Burzynski" and "Antineoplaston." Among them, only one Phase II clinical trial is marked as "completed," while two are marked as "terminated," seven as "withdrawn," one as "recruiting," and the remaining 50 clinical trials have a status of "unknown." Most of these unknown-status experiments were registered within one month in March 1996, taking advantage of a congressional hearing opportunity with the FDA. As for whether patients were recruited after registration, whether there were clinical results, whether they met clinical trial ethical standards, and what the drug side effects were, there is no further information.

The only clinical trial marked as "completed" (NCT00003509) was a Phase II randomized open-label trial attempting to treat Stage IV melanoma, without a control group. Patients received intravenous infusions of antineoplastons A10 and AS2-1, six times daily for three consecutive months.

In 1993, Burzynski was charged with administering unapproved drugs to patients by the FDA. In 1998, the Texas court ruled to prohibit Burzynski from disseminating false drug advertisements and ordered him to stop promoting these products. Burzynski subsequently appealed on the grounds of interference with freedom of speech, but the Texas Court of Appeals upheld the original verdict, ruling that "Burzynski's advertising language was illegal." The appellate court wrote:

"Dr. Burzynski's patients are gravely ill and at death's door. Even if treatment offers only a glimmer of hope, they strive tirelessly, thirsting for survival, hoping that even a slim chance is better than succumbing to fate. However, this desire for life can lead to irrational actions, providing opportunities for those who lack ethics, leading to manipulation and financial harm. Texas and the FDA will make every effort to protect such vulnerable patients from being trampled upon. This court does not imply that Dr. Burzynski is such an unethical person, but he is certainly not a good sort and has not observed his duty to protect patients according to the law."

Burzynski's medical license was under scrutiny for ten years between 1994 and 2004.
