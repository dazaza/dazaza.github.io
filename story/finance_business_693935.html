New Scientist: Exactly how do species form?

by ucibv623 on 2012-03-06 20:51:10

Although Darwin named his book "The Origin of Species," he could not explain exactly how species are formed, referring to it as a "mystery among mysteries." Even after a century and a half, the mechanism by which two groups of animals become genetically incompatible remains one of the greatest challenges in biology. We understand that Darwin's Galapagos finches, separated on different islands, gradually adapted to different environments, developed reproductive isolation, and evolved into distinct species. However, what is difficult to explain is how new species can rapidly emerge in populations that have not been physically isolated. Surprisingly, a gene with an unexciting name, Prdm9, may hold the key to speciation.

In 1974, Czech geneticist Jiri Forejt discovered during the hybridization of two subspecies of mice that there might be an unknown gene associated with reproductive isolation. By the 21st century, Forejt and his colleagues had finally identified this gene causing reproductive isolation on the 17th chromosome of mice—Prdm9. Meanwhile, biologist Chris Ponting from Oxford University was searching for genes that give humans their uniqueness, believing it should be the fastest-evolving gene in human genomes. This gene turned out to also be Prdm9, which evolves at an extremely high frequency throughout the animal kingdom.

Ponting's research seemed to hit a dead end, but Forejt's mouse gene became increasingly intriguing. No gene's evolutionary purpose would be to make organisms infertile; the role of Prdm9 in hybrid sterility must be a byproduct of its normal function. Soon, research in another direction revealed the true function of the Prdm9 gene.

When organisms produce sperm or eggs, chromosomes pair up and exchange parts of DNA segments, causing the recombination of inherited genes from parents. Research has found that the positions of recombined DNA segments are not random but mostly occur at specific "hotspots." Statistical geneticist Gil McVean from Oxford University discovered that about 40% of these hotspots share a common thirteen-base-pair DNA sequence. He also found that a zinc-finger protein likely activates these recombination hotspots, triggering recombination. Subsequent studies confirmed that the gene encoding this zinc-finger protein is indeed Prdm9.

During genetic recombination, original hotspots may sometimes be erased, while variations in Prdm9 create new hotspots for gene recombination. Over hundreds or thousands of generations, hotspots in the genome keep changing. Individuals with different Prdm9 genes also have different hotspots during genetic recombination.

Most researchers believe that this function of specifying DNA exchange locations makes certain combinations of eggs and sperm incompatible due to Prdm9. However, the difficulty lies in explaining why individuals with different recombination hotspots are reproductively incompatible. Hybrid sterility in mice is indeed associated with Prdm9, but whether Prdm9 is linked to sterility in other organisms still lacks evidence. Researchers are currently studying the interactions between Prdm9 and other genes in mice to ultimately clarify the relationship between Prdm9 and reproductive isolation.

If it is eventually proven that Prdm9 is indeed the gene causing reproductive isolation, then it will be the key to solving Darwin's "mystery among mysteries" of how new species form—the undeniably worthy evolution X factor.

Publication: New Scientist, February 12th
