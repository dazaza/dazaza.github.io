Women's Oral Health and Overall Health

November 16, 2009

Therefore, Colgate will provide you with information regarding women's oral health and its connection to overall health.

Is there a connection between oral health and overall health?

The Surgeon General’s Report on Oral Health states that good oral health is extremely important for good overall health. This is particularly true for women. A study on physical development indicates that periodontal disease is closely linked to women's health. Since periodontal disease is a bacterial infection, and bacteria can enter the bloodstream, it may be one of the causes of other systemic complications:

Heart Disease: People with periodontal disease are more likely to suffer from heart disease, and their chances of having a fatal heart attack are almost twice as high as those of the general population. Heart disease is also the leading cause of death among women in the United States.

Stroke: A study found an uncertain relationship between oral infections and the risk factors for stroke.

Diabetes: People with diabetes are more likely to develop periodontal disease, which may make it harder for diabetics to control their blood sugar levels. Diabetes could also be a risk factor for periodontal disease, even for those who are otherwise healthy.

Respiratory Problems: Bacteria growing in cavities can reach the lungs, causing respiratory diseases such as pneumonia—especially in people with periodontal disease.

Pregnancy: Pregnant women with periodontal disease are more likely to give birth to premature or low-weight babies. Periodontal disease may also trigger elevated fluid levels, which can induce labor.

Since periodontal disease often has no pain symptoms, many women may not realize they have it until it progresses to an advanced stage. Your best defense is daily brushing, flossing, and regular visits to the dentist.

How will my oral health needs change throughout my life?

Throughout a woman's life, she has specific oral health needs at different stages. Changes in estrogen levels during puberty, menstruation, pregnancy, and menopause exacerbate the gums' response to plaque. Therefore, to prevent periodontal disease, women need to brush and floss their teeth thoroughly every day during these periods.

Here are some other important things you need to know:

Menstruation: Some women find that their gums swell and bleed before each menstrual cycle, while others may experience cold sores or mouth ulcers. These symptoms usually disappear when your period starts.

Oral Contraceptives: Gum inflammation is one of the most common side effects.

Pregnancy: Studies show that many pregnant women experience pregnancy gingivitis, where plaque deposits on the teeth and irritates the gums. Symptoms include redness, inflammation, and bleeding of the gums. Prenatal care is especially important.

Menopause: Women during this period may experience oral symptoms such as red or inflamed gums, oral pain and discomfort, burning sensation, changes in taste, and dry mouth.

Osteoporosis: Many studies indicate a link between osteoporosis and jawbone loss. Researchers believe that due to reduced bone density supporting the teeth, this might lead to tooth loss. When combined with periodontal disease, osteoporosis can accelerate the rate of alveolar bone loss.
