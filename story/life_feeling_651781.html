The Love Between My Parents

Both of my parents are now in their seventies. To young people, it seems that elderly couples like them don't have any romantic feelings anymore. But in my eyes, they still love each other deeply and affectionately.

They don't call each other with intimate nicknames, nor do they do anything extraordinary, but every little detail of their daily lives is filled with love.

My father has boundless energy. Every time he goes out to work, my mother stays at home to tidy up. When it's mealtime, she prepares the tea, cooks the food, keeps it warm in the pot, and waits for my father to come back. If it's summer, my mother will prepare a basin of water in advance so that my father can wash when he returns. After my father finishes washing, my mother has already cooled down the tea. Once the food is ready on the table, my father starts eating and drinking while my mother finally sits down at the dining table. If my father goes out to work and doesn't return by nightfall, my mother anxiously goes to the gate multiple times to look for him. Only when she sees my father's figure does her heart finally settle.

My mother has been in poor health for many years and rarely goes to the market or shops. If there’s something missing at home, my father always buys it. My father knows that my mother fears the heat in summer, so he bought her a bamboo green mat and hung a small fan inside the mosquito net. In winter, when she feels cold, he got her soft, long-haired bed sheets. Despite this, my mother still criticizes my father sometimes, nagging occasionally, but my father has long gotten used to it, just smiling happily. Listening to his spouse's nagging is also a kind of love.

One year when I returned home, my mother told me something that moved me deeply. My mother's legs were not good, and she couldn't walk far. After much treatment, there was no improvement. My sister and I had taken her to the county hospital several times to see experts, who said it was an age-related disease - menopause combined with osteoporosis and bone hyperplasia. There is no special medicine that can cure it completely; only increasing nutrition and taking some medication regularly can help manage it. The aging process is one of fighting diseases. Every time my sister and I go back home, we bring her medicines for menopause and leg pain. She has been taking these medications continuously, and my father has also tried various herbal remedies for her. We heard that there was an old Chinese doctor in a village south of the mountain who could treat leg pain. My father pushed my mother on a small wooden cart, climbing over mountains and hills, traveling more than twenty miles to reach that village. According to my mother, my father didn’t rest once along the way. Even when going uphill, he didn’t let my mother get off, instead pushing the cart forward alone with great effort, sweating profusely. Hearing this, I seemed to see the touching scene: on the rugged mountain road, my white-haired father, with a rope loop around his neck and holding the cart handle tightly, was pushing my white-haired mother. He had already taken off his sweater and was wearing only a fall shirt, sweat streaming down his face as he strained to push forward. My mother, sitting on the small wooden cart, had tears welling up in her eyes out of pity. My dear parents, it's not because your children are unfilial, but because you don't want to bother us. My older and second brothers work far away in Beijing and Shanghai, my sister and brother-in-law also work elsewhere, and my younger sister and I are busy with our jobs, each having our own lives. You say that as long as you can solve your problems yourselves, you won't burden your children. But hearing this makes my heart ache, and I feel a sense of guilt. However, I admire my mother; the unwavering devotion of my father, who always keeps her in mind, is truly moving.

My parents have lived together through thick and thin all their lives, and they have also quarreled and fought all their lives. After arguing and fighting, they ultimately cannot live without each other. My father's well-being is always on my mother's mind. If my father feels unwell, my mother quickly goes to fetch the doctor. Although they may not understand the concept of love, I think that these little details of care, supporting each other, and relying on each other, surpass thousands of sweet words. Their love lasts forever, and as long as they are alive, this feeling will never end.
