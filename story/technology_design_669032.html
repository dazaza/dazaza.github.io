Introduction to SNK Cartridge Circuit and Troubleshooting

by gzqldz3b5y on 2012-02-18 20:20:04

Introduction to SNK Cartridge Slot Circuit and Fault Repair: There are two common versions of the SNK cartridge slot - small and large slots. The power amplifiers come in two types: TA8201 and HA13001. Although the circuits of various versions differ, their main circuit parts are basically the same.

1. Main CPU - 68000

Main Control ROM - 271024 (the large slot is a standard dual train 40-pin package, while the small slot uses surface mount packaging, but the data is the same); Working RAM - 2 pieces of 62256; BACK UP RAM - 2 pieces of 62256

2. Audio Section: CPU - Z80; WORK RAM - 6116; Main Control ROM - Large slot MASK4 (27010); Small slot integrated into DCR-T square chip; Voice Synthesis - YM2610 and YM3016; Pre-amplifier - LM324; Amplifier - TA820 or HA13001.

3. Clock Circuit:

(1) Small Slot: A 24MHz crystal and peripheral resistors/capacitors form an external oscillator circuit. Its output signal is input from P190 to drive the internal clock generator of NRO-GRC2 square chip, providing clock signals separately to CPU1 (6800), CPU2 (Z80), and YM2610 via P204, P205, P206. From P2, it provides a CK signal to the three primary color output IC (74273). Signals from P4, P5, P8, P199, and P207 provide working clocks to the ICs inside the cartridge.

(2) Large Slot: The P18 and P19 pins of the NEO-DO square chip serve as the input/output terminals for the internal inverter, forming an oscillator with an external 24MHz crystal and resistors/capacitors, driving the internal clock generator and producing various clock signals.

4. Calendar Circuit: Composed of a 32MHz crystal, UPD4990, DCR-T square chip (F0 chip for large slots), T518, one 74HC32, and one HC259 form the reset and interrupt circuit.

5. Common Fault Inspection:

Original SNK cartridge slots have extremely low component failure rates, with most faults caused by poor usage environments such as moisture or dust leading to short circuits or open circuits. For these cases, thorough cleaning of the circuit board usually reveals the fault and allows for its resolution. However, improper use like excessive voltage or poor heat dissipation can also lead to component damage causing faults.

(1) Full screen with flashing patterns indicates abnormal operation of the main CPU. However, if the clock and reset circuits are normal, start troubleshooting from the 68000 and peripherals like ROM (1024) and WORK RAM (62256).

(2) No display on startup should be investigated starting from the clock and reset circuits.

(3) BACKUP RAM ERROR on startup indicates damaged BACKUP RAM (62256) or faulty HC32, HC04, HC259 chips.

(4) Startup with non-flashing patterns often means issues with the 32MHz crystal, 4990, or HC32.

(5) Coin insertion sounds but no coin display, mostly due to poor performance of HC32 or WORK RAM (62256).

(6) Only able to play low-capacity cartridges like "Samurai Spirits", but high-capacity ones like "97 Fighting" freeze the machine, typically indicating performance issues with HC32 or WORK RAM (62256).

(7) CALLENDER ERROR (calendar error) points to faults in the 32MHz crystal, UPD4990, or F0 square chip.

(8) Audio section commonly shows Z80 ERROR upon startup (for large slots only, small slots won't show Z80 ERROR). This fault is mostly due to a bad surface mount Z80 or line faults. Other components like MASK40010 and 6116 rarely fail, but severe damage to 2610 can also cause Z80 ERROR.

(9) Small slots showing no NEO-GEO characters or figures indicate faults in MASK2 (27C010) or U3 (HC04).

(10) Small slots with no image and non-functional CPU, measured constant high level at CPU clock pin, later found U3 (HC04) was faulty.

(11) Large slots showing pattern flashes on startup, sometimes showing BACKUP RAM ERROR, measured HC32P1 as constant low level, traced to find a faulty HC259. After replacing the HC259, startup showed Z80 ERROR, measuring RAM6116 found its A2 pin was constant low level. Based on experience, this is mostly due to a faulty Z80. Replacing the Z80 resolved the issue.

(12) Large slots with fine vertical stripes on characters, mostly due to broken lines between ZMC2 and the socket or GRC large chip.

(13) Small slots with intermittent sound, amplifier section OK, using oscilloscope testing YM2610P41 when sound appears, later found bias resistor open circuit at this pin.

(14) Large slots showing squares when inserting cartridges. Cartridges OK, replaced with maintenance cartridges, tested CS of main program (27C080) on the cartridge's motherboard and found constant low level, traced back to V1 (HC32) P8 provided by the seat, while UI input was normal. Replacing the HC32 resolved the issue.

(15) "97 Fighting" cartridge shows Z80 ERROR after insertion but the slot is OK. Mostly due to faulty ZMC chip on the lower board of the cartridge; if sound changes pitch or there's no sound without showing Z80 ERROR, mostly due to ROMM1 (27512) being faulty.
