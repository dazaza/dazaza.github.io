Hair Dye and the Risk of Lymphoma

Many urban residents enjoy dyeing their hair, some for beauty, others for fun, going to great lengths to color their hair in various hues. However, they are unaware that this act hides a dangerous risk - it can lead to cancer. A study by the American Cancer Society has drawn a shocking conclusion: dyeing your hair a darker color might jeopardize your health; the darker and more permanent the color, the higher the risk. This report immediately caused concern among millions of people worldwide who dye their hair.

Ms. Sham, the expert leading the research, stated that anyone who dyes their hair increases their chances of developing lymphoma, a fatal type of cancer. According to the American Cancer Society's report, the use of permanent hair dyes raises the risk of cancer by 40%. What Asians should note is that using permanent brown, red, and black dyes is more dangerous than lighter ones, increasing the likelihood of cancer as follows: brown by 100%; red by 200%; black by 310%. In contrast, silver-white or golden-yellow dyes almost completely avoid increasing the risk of cancer.

Permanent hair dyes refer to those that do not fade after application, except for new growth at the roots. Once dyed, the original hair color cannot be restored. Additionally, there are semi-permanent or non-permanent dyes which may fade after several washes. These types still increase the risk of cancer: golden by 30%, brown by 40%, black by 80%, and red by 70%. Moreover, researchers found that the frequency of dyeing does not affect the risk of cancer but rather the duration of continuous dyeing. Dr. Blair explained that certain chemicals in hair dyes might cause cancer, one being P-phenylenediamine, a synthetic chemical that often triggers allergic reactions. P-phenylenediamine mixes with hydrogen peroxide, causing a chemical reaction that changes the hair color when dried.

Different colored dyes are made from different pigments; the darker the dye, the more P-phenylenediamine it contains. P-phenylenediamine can interact with the human immune system, damaging health. The most effective way to reduce the risk is, of course, to stop dyeing your hair. If dyeing is unavoidable, the following measures can help lower risks: 1. Use lighter-colored dyes; 2. Use non-permanent dyes; 3. Wear gloves while dyeing; 4. Avoid rubbing dye forcefully into the scalp; 5. Wash off any dye that comes into contact with the skin immediately; 6. Use dyes that do not contain P-phenylenediamine. Excerpt from China Molecular Cancer Prevention Network (http://www.bjyaolinfa.com)

Cancer Prevention Knowledge: Beware of Early Signs of Lymphoma

The world's 7th Lymphoma Day has just passed, and authoritative statistics show that in China, the incidence rate of this disease is about 6.91 per 100,000 people, rising annually by 5%. Molecular cancer prevention experts say that lymphoma primarily affects children and young adults, is difficult to detect, and susceptible populations should undergo regular checks; if confirmed to have the disease, early treatment is more beneficial.

Susceptible populations should undergo timely checks: Molecular cancer prevention experts explain: "Lymphoma has become one of the hardest malignant tumors to detect, with many lifestyle factors potentially contributing to its onset." For example, vegetables or fruits we often consume may have pesticide or insecticide residues if not thoroughly washed. Or, modern life competition pressures leading to weakened immunity in some people, frequent hair dyeing, and long-term exposure to electronic radiation environments are all risk factors.

According to experts, if symptoms such as stomach bleeding, coughing, or abdominal pain occur, most people would choose to visit corresponding departments rather than go to hematology for examination to check for lymphoma. And lymphoma cannot be detected through routine ultrasound tests, so some grassroots medical institutions often fail to accurately diagnose patients' conditions and instead treat based on presented symptoms. After ineffective treatment, patients may choose to visit larger hospitals for examination, only then discovering they have lymphoma, possibly missing the optimal treatment window. Scientific Cancer Prevention: Be Cautious of Long-Term Fever and Malignant Lymphoma

Molecular cancer prevention experts point out that the incidence of malignant lymphoma has shown an upward trend in recent years in our country. Due to its stealthy nature, it generally fails to draw patient attention in the early stages. However, approximately 10% of patients experience systemic symptoms such as fever, rash, night sweats, and weight loss during the early stages of the disease. Therefore, they remind the public to stay vigilant and seek hospital examinations early. With current treatment technology and standards, many early-detected cases of malignant lymphoma can be cured. Malignant lymphoma presents with relatively hidden signs, but some early signals deserve special attention:

1. Swollen lymph nodes (commonly known as lumps in the neck, armpits, or groin) Progressive swelling is often the first symptom, especially in the neck, followed by the armpits. Enlarged lymph nodes can move and feel like cartilage. As the disease progresses, new lymph node swellings of varying sizes appear around the area and can merge into lumps. If lymph nodes enlarge rapidly, even invading nerves, they can cause pain.

2. Fever Malignant lymphoma can also cause systemic symptoms, including fever, night sweats, and weight loss, with fever being the second most common symptom. The fever pattern is often irregular, intermittent low-grade fever, with a few cases showing periodic fever. The characteristic of the fever is a gradual rise, with temperatures between 38°C and 40°C, lasting for several days. It then gradually decreases, with an interval period of 10 days to 6 weeks or longer before rising again. This cycle repeats itself, shortening the interval period over time. For unexplained long-term fevers, one should consult a hematology department for further examination to rule out lymphoma.

3. Enlarged spleen Manifested as an enlarged spleen, feeling firm with possible nodules; liver involvement is less common than spleen involvement, presenting as diffuse liver enlargement, moderate hardness, with a few palpable nodules. Liver biopsy or exploratory surgery can confirm the diagnosis.

4. Gastrointestinal Symptoms include decreased appetite, abdominal pain, diarrhea, abdominal masses, intestinal obstruction, and bleeding.

5. Skin Manifestations vary, including lumps, subcutaneous nodules, infiltrative plaques, ulcers, papules, macules, etc. Pruritus is a relatively common symptom in Hodgkin's disease.
