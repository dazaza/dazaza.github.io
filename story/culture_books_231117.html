"Passion and Confusion" A Comparison of Sexual Cultures between China and Rome

by jqrxass on 2009-08-31 12:51:53

In the human world two thousand years ago, there were two of the most powerful countries: Rome in the West and China in the East. Ancient Rome inherited the culture of ancient Greece and expanded its influence, forming the source of Western culture. Two thousand years ago, monarchic rule was prevalent worldwide, including in China, but Rome practiced a republic system for a long time. The Roman army could be said to be invincible; they campaigned far and wide, establishing a vast and incredibly prosperous country. They were very rough, brutal, bloodthirsty, with wild and unrestrained sexual lives, yet also interwoven with tenderness and civilization. Some even say that the fall of the Roman Empire lay in their sexual indulgence — all this forms an extremely strange and unique historical picture that has influenced the entire history of humanity, and to some extent, even the civilization of contemporary Western society.

"The Pleasure and Confusion" Part One

To study the sexual culture of ancient Rome, one must first review the history of ancient Rome. Love and sex are certainly not the main driving forces behind the development of human history, but at some critical turning points in history, love and sex often become influencing factors. In the history of ancient Rome, from the legend of the wolf children to the abduction of Sabine women; from the rape of a noblewoman leading to the establishment of the republican system, to the great Caesar gradually losing popularity due to supporting Cleopatra, the Queen of Egypt; from the relics of Pompeii showing the prosperity of ancient Rome, to the fall of the Roman Empire due to excessive indulgence and corruption. All these make people reflect on the past with deep emotion.

"The Pleasure and Confusion" Part Two

Ancient Rome was a slave society, and when humans entered the slave society, they also entered the patriarchal society, where fathers and husbands were the masters of the family. After marriage, women escaped the control of paternal authority only to fall under the control of their husbands. This situation is quite similar to the "three obediences" (obeying the father while at home, obeying the husband after marriage, and obeying the son after the husband's death) for ancient Chinese women. According to some early Roman laws, men's power within the family was almost unlimited and supreme, such as having the authority over family rituals and trials, controlling all family property, and even selling children. Paternal authority could arise from the birth of legitimate children, the recognition of illegitimate children, and adoption. The patriarchy dominated Roman social family life for a long historical period. It is generally believed that this kind of paternal concept reached this degree nowhere else in the world except in Rome. However, ancient China seems no less inferior in this aspect, as exemplified by the saying: "If the husband wants the wife to die, the wife cannot refuse; if the father wants the child to die, the child cannot refuse."
