How to Avoid Making Mistakes in Photography?

by king5c5t on 2012-03-06 14:15:22

Don't be surprised; even the most skilled photographers sometimes make common mistakes. However, errors are not good excuses, and rational photographers strive to avoid them. In this article, renowned photojournalist Joe McNally summarizes several common photography mistakes and methods to prevent them. These mistakes are highly relevant for photography beginners, enthusiasts, and professional photographers alike. Jinhua Photography Training School [http://www.jhainisi.org].

Joe McNally is an internationally renowned photographer with a 30-year career in photography. He has worked in over 50 countries and regions, shooting for magazines such as *Time*, *Newsweek*, and *Fortune*. He was a contracted photographer for *Sports Illustrated* and *Life* magazine and has been called "the most versatile photojournalist" by *American Photo* magazine.

### Don't Take Just One Shot

Jinhua Photography Training School [http://www.jhainisi.org]

If the scene or subject in front of your lens excites you enough to raise your camera, then absolutely do not take just one shot. Take 20 or even 200 shots because the "decisive moment" never comes back. If you're using a digital camera, remember: pixels are free.

### Interact with People When Shooting Portraits

Don’t hide behind the lens. I know that warm, dark little frame makes you feel safe and comfortable, but your subjects don’t feel the same way. Step out from behind the lens and interact with your subjects. Engage in their conversations and activities. Let your subjects feel respected and let them know that you're both working together to create successful photos. Make sure to convey the message that "they matter to you." Remember, if you're unconfident or uncomfortable during the shoot, it will affect the emotions of your subjects.

### Don't Assume Beauty Only Exists in Bali or Antarctica

Beauty is right under your nose. Shoot what you love and consider the convenience of shooting. If you think climbing to the top of a mountain or jumping out of a plane is necessary to capture great images, no wonder photography feels like hard work to you.

![A fishing shack in Maine, USA]

### Remember: The Camera Is Just a Machine

Your camera has no feelings. It’s not a sensitive object that needs pampering. Don’t treat it as fragile. Treat your camera properly as a machine and tool.

### Avoid Shooting Under Harsh Sunlight

The sun is like a giant wild dog, and nobody wants to fight a wild dog.

### Don’t Always Shoot at Eye Level

Get higher, get lower, climb up somewhere, or lie down—it doesn’t matter. Just remember: aim for different and unique perspectives.

![Students at the University of Pittsburgh watching a distant cathedral from the rooftop]

### If You Want Your Photos to Be Better...

Shoot more interesting subjects.

![Hansel Mieth's famous monkey underwater photo]

### Get the Right Image in the Camera

Don’t say, "We’ll fix it in post." Photoshop isn’t a hospital for wounded photos. Strive to learn and master the knowledge of cameras, lenses, and shooting techniques. Unless you enjoy sitting in a dark basement for hours like a mushroom, staring at a glowing computer screen to adjust the exposure of photo #101 or the composition of photo #209—something you could have easily fixed while shooting outdoors.

![Photographer Dennis Stock holding his camera]

### The Best Focusing Tool: Your Feet

Don’t be a houseplant! Go out and explore. Besides, zoom lenses are nice, but they can never replace your feet. Remember: get closer, much closer!

### Carry Your Camera Everywhere

As the famous photographer Jay Maisel said, "It's hard to take pictures without one."

![In September 1959, Life Magazine photographer Carl Mydans waits for Russian leader Nikita Khrushchev in Hyde Park, New York. This photo was taken by another legendary Life Magazine photographer, Alfred Eisenstaedt.]

### Don’t Use Yesterday’s Settings

Remember, before you start shooting each day, reset your camera settings. The previous day, you might have been shooting inside a mine at ISO 32000 or capturing low light with a 90-second shutter speed at midnight. Every day, you need to reconfigure your camera settings.

![Andreas Feininger's photograph of the Brooklyn Bridge]

### Remember to Remove the Lens Cap

You might laugh at this tip, but countless photographers—both professionals and amateurs—make this basic mistake.

### Finally: Have Fun

Photography is not brain surgery. Enjoy your visual life to the fullest.
