Differentiation between hemorrhoids and colorectal cancer

by zhihai1188 on 2012-02-09 02:09:44

The differentiation between hemorrhoids and rectal cancer: Hemorrhoids are rare clinical diseases of the rectum and anus, mainly seen in elderly people, with increasing incidence as age increases. The clinical manifestation of hemorrhoids is bleeding during defecation, or a mass protruding from the anus. Depending on their location, hemorrhoids can be classified as external or internal. Most asymptomatic hemorrhoids do not require special treatment and have little impact on one's health. However, if they affect daily life, it is necessary to consider surgical treatment at a regular hospital or other treatments to alleviate patient suffering.

In my practice, I often encounter many patients who mistakenly self-diagnose themselves with hemorrhoids, treating them according to advertisements or non-standard methods, without improvement. They only seek further examination here when the condition worsens, sometimes to find out that it is actually cancer, which is regrettable. This diagnosis could have been achieved through a simple examination - the digital rectal exam, a basic anal examination, yet many patients remain unaware of this. Therefore, many patients take unnecessary detours in treatment, causing regret and pain. For this reason, I will introduce a simple distinction between hemorrhoids and rectal cancer, hoping that those with such issues avoid taking detours, stay far away from illness, and live healthily.

Rectal cancer is a malignant tumor with a relatively high incidence rate. If not treated early, it can endanger life. Early-stage rectal cancer is easily misdiagnosed as hemorrhoids, either due to the patient's own misjudgment or assumptions made by medical personnel. There is a high misdiagnosis rate because rectal cancer and hemorrhoids share similar symptoms such as bloody stools and increased bowel movements. However, with vigilance, early detection of rectal cancer is entirely possible. Early detection leads to very good treatment outcomes. Detecting rectal cancer early isn't difficult. In clinical settings, distinguishing between hemorrhoids and rectal cancer can be achieved through some simple tests, which are also cost-effective. For example, a single digital rectal exam costs 13 yuan (Nanyang City, Henan Province standard), and a fiberoptic sigmoidoscopy costs about 200 yuan (Hebei Province Nanyang City standard). To detect rectal cancer early, I suggest paying attention to the following points:

1. Changes in bowel habits. Alterations in bowel habits are the earliest and most common symptoms of rectal cancer, primarily due to rectal irritation. Due to the stimulation of the cancerous tumor, patients may experience an increase in bowel movement frequency without obvious cause within a short period, alternating constipation and diarrhea, incomplete evacuation sensation, sometimes up to dozens of bowel movements daily but with small amounts each time. As the disease progresses and the lesion enlarges, the cancerous tumor may obstruct the rectal opening, causing constipation, thinning or deformation of stools, and abdominal distension.

2. Changes in stool characteristics. Stools may lose their normal shape, become blood-streaked, and contain mucus, mainly due to ulceration and infection caused by the cancerous tumor. About 80% to 90% of rectal cancer cases present with bloody stools, where the blood is dark red or bright red, often mixed with mucus or pus. Sometimes tumor tissue can be seen in the feces, though this is usually not in the early stages.

3. Early treatment. Upon noticing these symptoms, immediate examination at a regular hospital is recommended. Be cautious not to easily trust diagnoses made without thorough inspection, such as hemorrhoids or bacillary dysentery. If the doctor does not recommend further tests, patients should request them. Generally, a digital rectal exam can detect more than 75% of rectal cancers, and a simple sigmoidoscopy can almost detect all rectal cancers.

4. Metastasis signs. If such symptoms appear, it indicates the late stage or advanced stage of cancer. Rectal cancer generally causes no pain unless the tumor infiltrates the anal canal and sphincter muscle, then the pain becomes significant. If sphincter function is lost, pus and blood may frequently flow from the anus; in female patients, the tumor may infiltrate the urinary tract, prostate, or bladder anterior wall, causing frequent urination, painful urination, and difficulty in urination; in male patients, the tumor may infiltrate the posterior wall of the vagina; early infiltration of the sacral plexus can cause intense continuous pain in the perineal and sacral areas, radiating to the upper abdomen, waist, and lower legs; liver metastasis can lead to liver area pain, liver enlargement, jaundice, ascites. Late-stage rectal cancer patients may show signs of anemia, weight loss, edema, etc., due to chronic consumption.

5. Take seriously. Many patients may feel embarrassed or fear examinations, ignoring physician recommendations for checks, thereby missing early detection opportunities. Such cases are quite common in clinical practice.
