"The Inevitable 'Death of Yougou': Belle's E-commerce Has Many Serious Issues"

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 16:47:33

Recently, Yougou.com, which has been suffering from a serious loss of senior executives, received another blow. On July 17th, according to insiders from Yougou.com, the COO of Yougou.com, Zhang Xiaojun, is leaving and currently in the process of handling his resignation procedures. As one of the founding members of Yougou.com, Zhang Xiaojun's departure carries significant symbolic meaning, indicating that Yougou.com is indeed facing severe crises.

Let us first review the e-commerce journey of Belle International, the parent company of Yougou.com:

A few years ago, Belle quietly ventured into e-commerce, steadily developing through three channels: Taobao distribution, Tmall, and B2C distribution (B2C distribution completed via Taoxiu.com). Led by group veteran Xie Yunli with a not-so-luxurious e-commerce team, they initially focused on selling products under Belle's own brands. Subsequently, they launched exclusive online models and then new online brands. This development path was considered a successful case of traditional brand enterprises entering e-commerce before 2011, surpassing Li-Ning's approach.

With the rise of Letao.com and Haolemai receiving over $100 million in financing and rapidly expanding in 2010, Belle Group, which supplied these two platforms, became restless. On one hand, they were concerned about being overly dependent on distribution channels; on the other hand, after evaluation, they realized their significant development advantages in the e-commerce sector, including:

1. Numerous brands: Besides Belle's several women's shoe brands, international sports brands such as Adidas and Nike experienced rapid growth in online sales;

2. Easier category expansion: For Belle Group, known for its women's shoes and agency of well-known sports brands, achieving absolute market advantage and status through multi-brands and multi-categories became a top priority. Expanding offline stores for brands and categories was obviously too slow, while their ongoing e-commerce business could easily expand brands but struggled to quickly add categories;

3. Financial advantage: During collaboration with e-commerce companies, Belle discovered that e-commerce is a capital-intensive business. Belle Group's financial strength allowed them to develop without relying on external capital, enabling them to outlast many vertical e-commerce companies that depended heavily on funding, ultimately becoming the "survivor" in the industry;

4. Talent advantage: After several years of e-commerce practice, they had developed a highly professional and efficient e-commerce team capable of scaling up or even further improving;

5. Supply chain advantage: This was what excited Belle internally the most and also the core reason for attracting high-level executives from brand e-commerce or platform e-commerce companies. After all, supply chains always fascinated e-commerce operators as it seemed to be the life-or-death factor aside from capital. The supply chain includes product research and development, production, warehouse management, delivery, after-sales service, and more. In traditional enterprises focusing more on "commerce" than "e-commerce," this was considered the greatest competitive advantage over pure e-commerce companies.

With these advantages, Belle's e-commerce decided to go all-in. They first purchased the double-character domain yougou.com,

abandoned the somewhat copycat "topshoes.cn" Taixiu website for redirection, stopped supplying all distribution websites, though retained direct third-party online stores like Taobao and Tmall Mall with reduced investment and focus. They recruited high-profile executives from JD.com and Vancl as CMO and COO respectively, and appointed a group veteran as CEO, forming a golden triangle. Their goal was to create a full-category vertical B2C mall, implementing a multi-brand strategy and expanding into clothing and other categories, realizing the dream of an impossible-to-achieve department store empire offline!

Everything sounded perfect, but every dream must face the harsh reality of implementation. The actual situation was:

1. Higher technical requirements for independent B2C: Located in Shenzhen headquarters, Belle couldn't recruit more excellent technical talents, and outsourcing tech services were scarce there. However, technology determines user experience, backend management efficiency, and reliable data. Independent B2C involves dynamic technology, different from the relatively static ERP technology, making continuous changes a hurdle for many traditional enterprises;

2. Increased management difficulty due to category expansion: Someone once said that excelling in women's shoes doesn't necessarily mean success in men's shoes, something Belle likely felt deeply when expanding into men's shoes. Expanding into women's wear and accessories within a year clearly indicated the challenges faced at the backend. For traditional brand enterprises accustomed to meticulous cost control, this fast-paced approach worked for Vancl during its special period, understandable within Vancl and JD.com’s corporate cultures. However, whether Belle could embrace and execute with “exaggerated costs” despite having Vancl's COO and JD.com's CMO remained questionable;

3. Organizational structure: Many people find it hard to understand the differences between e-commerce and traditional enterprises. Simply put, departments, organizational structures, approvals, finance and budgeting, reporting methods, corporate culture, communication styles, execution methods, risk control, processes, and systems constitute an enterprise's so-called "mechanism." This conflict results in e-commerce trying to convince traditional teams to accept e-commerce's "structure," whereas traditional teams attempt but fail to fully understand e-commerce's "structure," leading to inefficiency and instability in execution teams. Eventually, the chairman or board might say, "E-commerce and traditional enterprises each have strengths and weaknesses, and the future lies in integration, so you must integrate in these areas..." If this "integration without harmony" occurs, it leads to supposed "management innovation," resulting in bizarre practices like "CEO rotation system" even appearing in a company like Belle!

Initially, it seemed Yougou.com would dominate, with its senior executives aiming for a spin-off IPO, enjoying the prestige and wealth of countless traditional enterprises' successful e-commerce cases. Instead, they are now grappling with these hard issues.

After all, whether it's the veterans who grew with Belle or the recruited e-commerce professionals, it's evident that their departures aren't due to lack of capability. When the VP from JD.com returns to JD.com, they continue to perform admirably, and the VP from Vancl bravely ventures into entrepreneurship. Are these hard issues limited to the three mentioned above? Or are these not just Belle's problems but common obstacles for many traditional enterprises attempting to enter e-commerce?
