In Zhengzhou, it is not uncommon for social vehicles, pedestrians, and non-motorized vehicles to fail to actively give way to special-purpose vehicles carrying out emergency tasks. According to a statistical result from the Zhengzhou Traffic Police, in traffic accidents involving ambulances, fire trucks, and other special-purpose vehicles, 70% are caused by social vehicles that have the obligation to yield but failed to do so in time.

Surveys show that nearly 90% of citizens believe that failing to yield to special-purpose vehicles is wrong, against public morality, but not illegal, nor would it result in punishment. The police stated that failing to yield to special-purpose vehicles not only falls within the realm of morality, but according to traffic law regulations, the police can impose a fine of 200 yuan.

[Current Situation]

The situation where vehicles do not yield to special-purpose vehicles happens from time to time.

Since the beginning of this year, precipitation in our province has been less than usual. Due to dry weather and lack of moisture, there have been multiple fire incidents recently in Zhengzhou. Relevant officials from the Zhengzhou Public Security Bureau introduced that during fire truck operations to extinguish fires, situations where social vehicles do not timely yield to fire trucks occur frequently.

Similar to Zhengzhou, on the evening of February 16, a noodle shop at Guizhou Street near Dongzhimen in Beijing caught fire. During the fire truck's rush to the scene, not only did social vehicles fail to make way, but some even overtook the fire truck. On February 18, a netizen uploaded a video of "fire trucks being cut off at Guizhou Street" online, which sparked heated discussions. Netizens were shocked after comparing this video with another showing "German vehicles yielding to fire trucks."

Not just fire trucks, ambulances often encounter such situations as well, sometimes even causing delays in emergency treatment.

"Bang!" An ambulance sounding its siren collided with a fully loaded bus. This accident, which occurred at the intersection of Tongbai Road and Longhai Road, left a deep impression on Chang Zhonghe, head of the Accident Department of the Zhengzhou Traffic Police Brigade. At the time of the accident, the ambulance was carrying an emergency patient and had run a red light under emergency conditions; the bus was driving normally under green light conditions, but it did not yield to the ambulance in time. Fortunately, no one was injured in the accident.

When encountering fire trucks, ambulances, and other special-purpose vehicles performing official duties, how should one yield? What consequences will arise if one does not yield? Yesterday, I conducted an investigation in Zhengzhou.

[Experience]

Eighty percent of motor vehicles will voluntarily yield to ambulances

At 11:00 AM on February 28, I followed an emergency 120 ambulance from the Henan Provincial People's Hospital for observation. As soon as the ambulance left the emergency center, driver Master Shen turned on the siren, and the urgent sound echoed outside the vehicle.

The ambulance headed north upon leaving. When reaching the intersection of Jing San Road and Yellow River Road, it was discovered that the road was heavily congested. Seeing that the sidewalk could be used, Master Shen drove the ambulance onto the sidewalk. On the sidewalk, a car was also ahead. Upon hearing the siren behind, the car driver quickly stopped to let the ambulance pass. After slowly making way, the ambulance finally smoothly passed through the section.

At 11:15, the 120 ambulance entered the intersection of Weilai Road and Jinshui Road. With significant traffic flow, the ambulance prepared to change lanes and turn right, coinciding with vehicles traveling from north to south. Master Shen signaled the vehicles on the right to yield using the turn signal, and most vehicles slowed down to yield. However, one small car did not yield and continued forward without hesitation.

Master Shen, who has driven ambulances for over 20 years, said that with the continuous improvement of citizens' civilized traffic awareness, when the ambulance sounds its siren on the road, 80% of vehicles hear the siren and voluntarily yield. But there are still individual drivers who do not yield.

[Observation]

Pedestrians and non-motorized vehicles have poor voluntary yielding awareness

"Regardless of how loud the siren is, some pedestrians act as if they didn't hear it and do not actively make way for the ambulance." Master Shen told reporters that it is common for some pedestrians and non-motorized vehicles not to actively yield to emergency vehicles.

At 11:20, when the ambulance reached the intersection of Jinshui Road and Jing Er Road, a man riding an electric bike wearing a hat blocked the front of the ambulance. Despite the ambulance continuously sounding its siren, the man remained unmoved and continued moving forward. The reporter sitting in the passenger seat couldn’t help but roll down the window and loudly prompt him. Only then did the man unwillingly move aside.

At 11:25, the ambulance arrived at the intersection of Jing San Road and Wei Si Road, where a man riding a bicycle stopped waiting for the red light, with a child seated on the backseat. Seeing the flashing lights of the approaching ambulance, the man showed no reaction, forcing Master Shen to abruptly apply the brakes.

[Interpretation]

Failing to yield to special-purpose vehicles executing tasks is illegal

On February 27 and 28, at the intersection of Huayuan Road and Guoji Road in Zhengzhou and the intersection of Wenhu Road and Sanquan Road, the publicity department of the Zhengzhou Traffic Police Brigade conducted a simple questionnaire survey among 100 pedestrians and 200 vehicle drivers.

The survey results showed that 88% of respondents believed that special-purpose vehicles have the right to priority passage, and pedestrians and social vehicles should yield, considering failure to yield as contrary to public morality. When asked whether failing to yield to special-purpose vehicles should incur a fine, 30% of respondents were unclear, 10% thought there should be a fine, and 60% believed "it deserves criticism, but no fine."

"Special-purpose vehicles executing emergency tasks will be penalized if social vehicles do not yield, and this is clearly stipulated by law," said Zhang Xiaowei, head of the Legal Affairs Department of the Zhengzhou Traffic Police Brigade on February 28.

Zhang Xiaowei explained that according to Article 53 of the Road Traffic Safety Law, special-purpose vehicles executing emergency tasks enjoy the right of way on roads, and other vehicles and pedestrians should yield. Article 90 of the Road Traffic Safety Law stipulates: If a motor vehicle driver violates the provisions of the Road Traffic Safety Law and Regulations concerning road traffic, they will be warned or fined between 20 and 200 yuan. If otherwise stipulated by this law, penalties shall be imposed accordingly.

"Article 53 is about road traffic regulations, and Article 90 pertains to penalties for violating these regulations. The police can enforce based on these two articles," Zhang Xiaowei said. In actual enforcement in Zhengzhou, the police have always required compliance with these regulations, but due to the difficulty in obtaining evidence regarding whether yielding occurred and the fleeting nature of the scene, on-duty officers rarely issue tickets for such offenses.

Zhang Xiaowei mentioned that if vehicles or pedestrians obviously confront special-purpose vehicles, resulting in serious consequences, apart from a fine of 200 yuan, they may also bear other legal responsibilities. For example, intentional delay of an ambulance causing a patient to miss treatment or die could lead to civil litigation initiated by the family demanding compensation. Those refusing to yield to police cars or fire trucks causing severe consequences will be dealt with by the police as obstructing official duties.

[Consequences]

Failing to yield to special-purpose vehicles can easily cause accidents

On February 28, Deputy Director Zhang Wenge of the Accident Department of the Traffic Police Brigade introduced that according to police statistics over the past three years, in traffic accidents involving special-purpose vehicles, 70% were caused by social vehicles failing to timely yield to special-purpose vehicles. This shows that many people have not yet developed the habit of voluntarily yielding to special-purpose vehicles.

"When special-purpose vehicles are involved in traffic accidents with social vehicles, handling them differs slightly compared to general accidents between social vehicles. Overall, the police tend to favor special-purpose vehicles," Zhang Wenge said yesterday morning.

According to police statistics, accidents involving special-purpose vehicles generally occur at intersections, mainly due to special-purpose vehicles running red lights or forcibly turning. If general vehicles run red lights or forcibly turn causing traffic accidents, they will bear full responsibility for the accident, but special-purpose vehicles usually only bear partial responsibility.

Special-purpose vehicles executing emergency tasks are considered "special" because "under the premise of ensuring safety, they are not restricted by driving routes, directions, speeds, and traffic signals." In other words, these vehicles have the right to run red lights while on duty. Meanwhile, social vehicles have the obligation to yield and should also bear certain responsibilities.

"In actual police operations, scenarios where social vehicles bear full responsibility would typically be assigned primary or equal responsibility when it comes to special-purpose vehicles executing emergency tasks," Zhang Wenge said. Specific assignment depends on the accident scene conditions.

Definition: Special Characteristics of Special-Purpose Vehicles

What are special-purpose vehicles?

Vehicles executing emergency tasks, such as police cars, fire trucks, ambulances, and engineering rescue vehicles, are conventionally referred to as special-purpose vehicles.

What privileges do special-purpose vehicles have?

Article 53 of the Road Traffic Safety Law stipulates: Police cars, fire trucks, ambulances, and engineering rescue vehicles executing emergency tasks may use sirens and marking lamps; under the premise of ensuring safety, they are not restricted by driving routes, directions, speeds, and traffic signals, and other vehicles and pedestrians must yield.

Police cars, fire trucks, ambulances, and engineering rescue vehicles must not use sirens and marking lamps when not executing emergency tasks and do not enjoy the aforementioned right-of-way privileges.

The Interpretation of Articles of the Road Traffic Safety Law points out: In principle of road passage, special-purpose vehicles have the right of priority passage. Under the premise of ensuring safety, they are not constrained by lane-specific driving rules, whether reversing or proceeding straight, all pedestrian and vehicle lanes they traverse temporarily belong to their designated lanes, and pedestrians and other vehicles must unconditionally yield to them. They can also bypass speed limits and traffic signals. Pedestrians and other vehicles must ensure the priority passage rights of special-purpose vehicles and immediately yield if there is any possibility of hindering their passage.
