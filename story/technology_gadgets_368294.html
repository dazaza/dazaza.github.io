An extraordinary experience to deeply understand the development process of Windows Phone 7.

by shootero on 2010-10-16 21:22:06

Although iPhoneOS is very popular, and although Android is hyped to the heavens, and although Windows Mobile has been belittled time and again, and although Windows Phone 7 is not favored by people, how can a little prediction and commentary sway the progress of the mobile market? The final say still lies with the users and their experiences. Today, I have compiled an article from Znet's exclusive interview with Microsoft’s Windows Phone 7 project team, which provides detailed reports on the status, experience, and cooperation direction of Microsoft Windows Phone 7. I believe it will help users re-understand the Windows Phone 7 system.

Terry Myerson is watching the prototype circuit board of the Windows Phone, which is built using Qualcomm's new chip.

The first Windows Phone 7 device will not be available until the end of this year, but as early as the last Thursday of May, various meeting rooms at Microsoft's headquarters were already bustling with activity.

There are only a few weeks left for Microsoft to hand over the nearly completed code to the telecom operators so that they can start several months of pre-launch testing.

In one of the three new buildings occupied by the phone team, Studio F, the group responsible for the operating system is holding its daily routine progress meeting. Microsoft developers call these meeting rooms shiprooms. Just across the street in Studio H, another team responsible for network services such as positioning and application markets is also having meetings. Back in Studio F, in the third shiproom, another engineering team along with personnel from chipmaker Qualcomm are discussing the chips for the new phones.

The launch of the new phone is an important battle for Microsoft to catch up with Apple and Google. Despite entering the mobile phone market years ahead of its two competitors, complacency, lack of focus, and strategic errors have relegated Microsoft to the back of the market. Now the company's market share in the smartphone operating system has dropped to single digits, lagging behind Symbian, RIM BlackBerry, Apple iPhone, and Google Android. Assuming it's not too late, Windows Phone 7 will be the only hope to reverse years of decline.

The person in charge of this major task is Microsoft Vice President Terry Myerson. At the age of 37, he once led the Exchange Server development team and is one of the few Microsoft executives who understand how to rise from a disadvantaged position. In 1997, when Myserson joined Microsoft, Exchange was seriously lagging behind IBM's Lotus Notes.

Myserson has one more quality: facing reality and speaking frankly. He said in an interview with our site: "Subjectively, there are some parts that seem impossible, but if you break them down and look at them separately, you can see a clear path." (ZDNET was given this rare opportunity to observe all key shiproom meetings during the final stages of Microsoft's Windows Phone 7 development, listening to the most candid and genuine discussions between Microsoft executives and developers about the mobile market and their current work.)

Myerson took on the arduous task of developing Microsoft's next-generation mobile operating system in October 2008. Despite the long journey, Myerson remains firmly convinced that Windows Phone 7 has a chance to break through. As long as personal and work contact lists, Xbox Live games, Zune music and videos, mobile versions of Office, and images from various social networks are smoothly integrated, Microsoft can make a comeback.

Microsoft has prepared the core software, but must ensure that its software works well on the hardware of different phone manufacturers. The main functions have been completed. The current goal of the Microsoft development team is to make these feature experiences smooth, ensuring that small bugs in the program do not cause crashes or drain the battery power too quickly.

Windows 7 still retains Windows CE as its core, but has a completely new look and interface. Myerson said: "The entire user interface of Windows Phone 6 is built on a certain graphical framework. We decided to change this part. This was a unilateral decision made at the top level, so each team had to force themselves to accept it."

But starting over with so many things means that in many aspects, Windows Phone 7 is more like a first version product rather than a seventh version. Although breakthroughs in some areas have won external praise, Windows Phone 7 has taken steps backward in other areas. For example, it does not support copy and paste, nor the multi-tasking function of the old Windows Mobile.

Myerson said: "We will have some very high-quality and unique things. We will not be better in every aspect, and we cannot improve in all aspects functionally... Actually, I regard this as the first achievement release of the entire team."

It is impossible to catch up with competitors in all functions at once, so Myerson shifted the focus to completing this product and personally participated in every progress meeting, playing the triple roles of arbitrator, promoter, and guide.
