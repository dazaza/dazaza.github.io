Night of Beijing Cultural City: Music and Dance Performance from the Western Zhou Dynasty

by yerui0021 on 2009-12-08 16:40:39

The Western Zhou Dynasty was the period when the slave system reached its peak. Based on the various regulations and systems of the Yin-Shang dynasties, it formulated a complete set of rites and music systems to maintain and consolidate feudal rule. The rulers of the Western Zhou paid special attention to the social roles of rites and music, believing that rites could distinguish between the noble and the base, and music could make people harmonious and respectful to each other. Combined together, they could maintain the hierarchical order of the nobility and effectively rule the people.

The court music of the Western Zhou could be roughly divided into six types: the music of six generations, elegant music, hymn music, chamber music, and the music of the four barbarians. The music of six generations referred to six large-scale musical dances passed down through the generations. These musical dances were mainly used for sacrifices to heaven and earth, mountains and rivers, and ancestors. Their characteristics were grand in scale but monotonous and slow in tone, giving people a sense of solemnity and silence. Hymns were also grand ceremony songs, used in important ceremonies such as emperors sacrificing to their ancestors, archery, academic viewing, and meetings between two rulers. Most of the contents were epic, often with some mythological elements; the tones were also characterized by slowness, but compared to the music of six generations, they might be clearer and sharper, sung with the accompaniment of a se.

Elegant music could be divided into Greater Elegant Music and Lesser Elegant Music. The content of Greater Elegant Music was similar to hymns, and the occasions used were also largely the same; Lesser Elegant Music was closer to folk songs or adapted from folk songs. It was often used in drinking ceremonies among scholars, sung with the accompaniment of a se or qin, called "string song". Chamber music was the songs sung by the concubines during banquets in the palace, mainly based on folk songs like those from Zhou South and Zhao South, without bells or chimes, only accompanied by qin or se. The music of the four barbarians referred to the music from various ethnic groups in the border areas such as Qin, Chu, Wu, Yue, mostly belonging to song-dance nature, with wind instruments as the main accompaniment.

From ancient times to the early period of the slave society, it was the period when Chinese music gradually took shape and began to develop. Due to the development of smelting and other technologies in the Shang Dynasty, it promoted the progress of instrument manufacturing. Bronze percussion instruments such as bells and cymbals appeared, and the development of pipe instruments also progressed.
