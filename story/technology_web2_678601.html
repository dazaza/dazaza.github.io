The flowers have withered, walking on the country path.

by wangquan1986 on 2012-02-27 15:38:54

The flowers have withered, and I am walking on a country path, always wanting to go to the mountains, to smell the taste of green grass, to listen to the ethereal bird songs, and to watch the gently flowing stream. This is enough to forget the烦恼of the mundane world and release the fatigue of the body. In the depths of winter, the flowers have already faded, and I am walking on a mountain path.

The mountain paths are winding and lead to secluded places; the mountain paths twist and turn endlessly.

By the side of the road leading to the mountains, there is a spring with a sweet and clear taste. This spring has also attracted many people from the city, who fill plastic buckets with the spring water and carry them away in large and small quantities. To fetch this clear spring water at such an early hour is considered a form of exercise. There was once a relative of my family who did this, but he passed away just after turning seventy. People said he overdid it. It seems that exercising still needs to be done in moderation.

Climbing the steps feels like ascending into the clouds. Looking back at the cliffs and ravines, the Chinaberry tree bears a few unripe fruits; under the tree, several big white geese are squawking, their red markings on their foreheads clearly visible. The sourgrass lies silently on the ground, its lush greenery unforgettable at a glance. I remember when I was a child, I used to play in the fields and chew on a few leaves of sourgrass, filling my mouth with a sour sensation. Now no one picks and eats this grass, but the joy of childhood remains etched in memory.

"Two Trees" is engraved on a large stone, perhaps as a creative idea. Here, there is no village ahead and no shop behind. Looking around, there are only a few brick-tiled houses down the hill. The farm stay we are going to offers a simple pleasure: the fragrant tofu served at noon, the steaming hot chicken with taro on the table, and the side dish of pigweed mixed with chili giving a "coarse" taste. This kind of tranquil happiness can relax our nerves for the whole day.

Walking along, a fragrant aroma suddenly wafts by. Following the scent, I see the plum blossoms blooming on branches higher than a person's head, coldly unfurling their petals. Although some flowers have withered, the wildness cannot confine the fragrance from spreading. Smelling the fragrance, I suddenly recall Wang Anshi's poem: "From afar, I know it isn't snow, for there is a faint fragrance coming." The plum blossoms attract Mr. Zhang, leaving behind a flurry of photos where both the flowers and people are in motion, seeking the release of emotions in the dynamism. Smelling the flower's fragrance, one hears no sound of the flowers blooming, and at this moment, the subtle feeling is akin to seeing a beautiful woman silently without words, making the mood instantly delicate and transparent.

Pomegranates are lovers of happiness. On previous visits to farmers' homes, bright red pomegranates hung on trees, catching the eye. After the Winter Solstice, the pomegranates still hang on the trees, dark brown and wilted, indicating that the farmers are reluctant to pick them, treating them as ornamental fruits. Prose master Feng Zikai often wrote about rural life, which sometimes feels particularly ingenious. Bean pods on tree branches may seem ordinary, but returning to the essence of real life naturally gives insight. Empress Wu Zetian, whom I initially thought was evil while reading history books, moved me when I read her poem "Ruyi Niang," which reads: "Seeing red turns green with thoughts swirling, emaciated and broken due to missing you. If you don't believe I've been shedding tears lately, open the box and check my pomegranate skirt." A deep emotion leaps off the page!

Apart from plum blossoms, it's hard to find other flowers in the mountains. That rustling sound lightly stirs the eardrums and then emerges again, softly brushing against the wilderness. Bamboo is evergreen, its leaves resembling flutes in winter, playing distant flute music; the widespread bamboo grass sways with purplish-white tassels in the wind. At this moment, the mood flows like a gentle brook, naturally setting free. Even when Lingzi says her fur coat is unique, we all echo: solitary item, fake item. Loving the mountains means loving the mountains, humans are extremely small compared to nature, so what do people desire? More than just a fur coat.

Leisure is a way of life, and we didn't come here to admire flowers. We just want to color our mood, whether green, blue, or purple. Our footprints will be left on a thin calendar. Mr. Zhong looks like a backpacker, carrying a travel bag, his face flushed red, sweat dripping from his forehead; Fangfang lags behind, her peach blossom-patterned sweater swaying among the bamboo grass; Cousin sings loudly all the way, his song brushing away the chill on the tips of the grass. The scattered group resembles a collage of defeat.

The mountain farmers also have karaoke. Occasionally hearing such a song boosts one's spirit: "Walking on a country path, the old ox returning home is my companion, the blue sky adorned with a sunset on my chest, colorful clouds are the evening glow's clothing." Life is not possible to be guarded everywhere. When loneliness is cast aside, a joyful emotion quietly sprouts in one's heart.

Not thinking about past lives or present ones, letting footsteps trample through the grass stems; not thinking about the warmth and coldness of the world, gathering all the tenderness in one's heart. Even if revisiting the past, I do not feel sorrowful, the fleeting moment of wonder I locked onto does not require understanding by others.

For writers, they often think about the momentum, rhyme, sound, and rhythm. In the mountains, extending delicate fingers to the babbling brook produces melodious notes; the mountainous terrain, though not steep, is sufficient to accommodate fluttering black hair. What else is there? These mountains and waters, even in winter, appear profound, storing the breath of spring, restrained yet full of sentiment, like a devoted young woman.

On the green grass slope, half a mu of lotus pond shines clean and calm. The surrounding mountain scenery is like an ink painting. Remember every March, the hillsides here are covered with golden rape flowers; the rape flowers support the white plum blossoms, walking among them feels distant yet close, steps breaking light clouds. Looking down at the farmer's courtyard, fiery mountain tea flowers peek out from the walls, their crimson hue intoxicating those on a spring outing. At this moment, the only hope lies in the peak in the mountains. Passing through a grove of camphor trees, the tassels of bamboo grass greet us at shoulder height. These tassels are often collected, bundled together, and taken home to be placed in vases as decorations. The peak is not high, abruptly rising on the plain, as if split by a knife, exposing yellow-brown rocks, surprisingly without any moss.

Finally, I see colorful plants, a dry shrub standing alone in the middle of the dam, its leaves pale red. We circle around this withered shrub, taking photos or playing train games, pouring uncontrollable laughter onto the round leaves.

The flowers have withered, and I walk on the mountain path. Not thinking, with whom shall I return?
