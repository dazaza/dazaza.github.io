How Apple's iPhone Conquered Japan

by geekzhang on 2013-05-08 13:59:14

May 8, according to Beijing time, Japanese customers used mobile phones to watch TV, use GPS navigation services, download music, make movies, pay bills and send emails years earlier than American consumers. Moreover, eight years before the advent of Apple's iPhone, Japan already had a touch screen phone, Pioneer J-PE01. But last year, it was not surprising that the iPhone became the best-selling phone in the Japanese market.In the past decade, any overseas mobile phone manufacturer has been defeated in the Japanese market; but today, those once legendary Japanese mobile phone manufacturers are facing the situation of being turned away by consumers, while mobile phones from overseas are very popular.Take NEC as an example, this company was once one of the largest IT and telecommunications companies in the world, its fate can be regarded as a typical representative of the other seven Japanese mobile phone manufacturers. After two years of losses and a decline in stock value of more than 90% over ten years, the company is selling its mobile phone sales department and cutting 10,000 mobile-related jobs. Analysts pointed out that NEC no longer has the strength to compete with Apple and Samsung.So, what exactly happened? Japanese mobile phone industry expert Nobuyuki Hayashi believes that there are three main reasons why Japanese consumers no longer like their own mobile phone manufacturers. First, it is necessary to understand how great and unexpected the impact of the iPhone on Japanese female consumers is. From the earliest days, the iPhone has always had a very strong demand performance among female consumers. The initial iPhone 3G series quickly became a fashion prop for Japanese women, and various phone cases and accessories were also very loved by them. Subsequently, Japanese women developed brand loyalty (to the iPhone), said Hayashi.Japanese mobile phone manufacturers also have phones that look as pretty as the iPhone. The once-popular Infobar candy-shaped phone even won an international design award. Japanese telecommunications executives originally thought that the Apple iPhone lacked additional selling points (such as electronic wallets and TVs), which they believed were essential; but despite this, the iPhone still swept through Japanese consumers irresistibly.According to data released by IDC Japan, in 2012, whether calculated by mobile phones or smartphones, the iPhone was the best-selling phone in the Japanese market. Japanese industry observers and industry leaders have repeatedly pointed out that the iPhone would lose miserably; compared to their views, the iPhone's achievements in the Japanese market are undoubtedly sufficiently glorious. IDC data shows that now the iPhone has captured 15% of the Japanese market share, leading Japanese local manufacturers such as Sharp and Fujitsu, both of which hold a 14% share. Japan's largest mobile operator, NTT Docomo, does not sell the iPhone, but the company has also taken countermeasures, aggressively promoting overseas-produced smartphones like the Samsung Galaxy.But Hayashi believes that these measures will not help attempts to defeat Apple. He believes that the way the Japanese mobile phone industry operates leads to the production of substandard products. Mobile operators modify almost every type of mobile phone sold in the Japanese market, including phones produced by Nokia or Samsung, to meet the special requirements of operators, adding functions that operators consider important, such as electronic wallets, One-Seg TV receivers, and a series of special services provided by operators, he said.In contrast, the iPhone is the only unmodified phone in the Japanese market (that is, what the manufacturer produces is what is sold in Japan), Hayashi said. He also added that modifications made by operators to Android phones make these phones too feature-packed, overly complex, and unable to provide stable battery life.Also, the software added by Japanese operators in overseas-manufactured phones only suits domestic phones, and does not match Apple or pure Samsung phones. As Steve Jobs (the late co-founder of Apple) said, the biggest mistake of Japanese manufacturers is that they did not realize how important software technology has become. In Japanese consumer electronics manufacturers, most executives are hardware engineers who do not understand the importance of software or how the software business works, Hayashi said.However, electronic companies that produce components for Japanese and global smartphones still have some hope. While Japanese companies may have failed to produce a phone that fascinates the whole world, Japanese electronic manufacturers still hold about 50% of the global smartphone component market, including producing components for the iPhone. It is true that the current market share held by Japanese mobile phone manufacturers is less than five years ago; however, compared to the number of components they provide to all mobile phone manufacturers during the same period, their sales value has been offset (many times). Lars Cosh-Ishi, a Japanese mobile market consultant pointed out. Moreover, it’s not just a hardware issue. It seems that no one mentions the contributions of Japanese companies in IP to the wireless industry, crucial 3G patent technologies developed by Japanese manufacturers enable billions of phones worldwide to connect to networks.
