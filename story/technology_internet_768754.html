Mayer: The keyword for Yahoo is entertainment

by anonymous on 2013-11-21 14:04:23

In the past few years, every time critics talked about Yahoo and its predicaments, they always said that Yahoo could not figure out what kind of company it is. So, what exactly does Yahoo do?

On Tuesday, Yahoo CEO Marissa Mayer finally gave an answer to this question: entertainment!

"Every company has its own keyword. Some companies' keyword is connection, while others' keyword is organization. So what is Yahoo's keyword? Our keyword is entertainment." During the Dreamforce conference on Tuesday, Yahoo CEO Mayer said this in an interview with Salesforce CEO Marc Benioff.

This is not a very precise answer, but it at least shows what Mayer has been doing at Yahoo so far. Moreover, it aligns with the latest news from Yahoo, which is doubling down on content investment. Yahoo recruited David Pogue, the technology columnist from The New York Times, to cover consumer technology. In addition, the company reportedly intends to hire well-known American hosts Katie Couric and Ryan Seacrest.

Of course, entertainment is not just media. When Benioff asked about the importance of design to Yahoo, Mayer responded that design should serve the mission of entertainment. "When your website gets visited by billions of users each month, you really have to consider the design aspect within the user experience," she said.

Steve Jobs, co-founder of Apple, once stated that in his company, the chief designer plays a pivotal role. However, unlike Apple, Mayer believes design should not be mythologized. "I think we shouldn't let design lead everything, but rather make it part of the product," she said. Then, she told a story about a failed tech company before the dot-com bubble burst. In that company, a friend designed a product that was "simple and easy to use, but not practical." Mayer indicated that the position of Senior Vice President of Design has remained vacant in her executive team.

Mayer also discussed other aspects of Yahoo's business and culture. In implementing the "mobile-first" strategy, she increased the number of staff in the mobile products department from 40 to 60, and later to 400. She also implemented a policy called PB&J (Process, Bureaucracy, and Jam), used to resolve various conflicts and issues.

Since taking the helm at Yahoo in July 2012, Mayer has become the focus of attention. Her efforts to turn Yahoo around have received mixed reviews. One of her most controversial actions was canceling the company's work-from-home policy and implementing a bell curve employee evaluation system.

Yahoo announced on Tuesday that it would increase its stock repurchase program to $5 billion. The company also stated that it plans to offer $1 billion in convertible notes, with some of the proceeds intended for acquisitions or strategic investments. In fact, under her leadership, Yahoo has acquired approximately 20 startups, including the blog platform Tumblr, which it purchased for $1.1 billion.

According to Edward Snowden, a former contractor for the U.S. National Security Agency (NSA), Yahoo, like other tech giants, has also been subject to government surveillance and scrutiny. Last month, The Washington Post reported that the U.S. NSA and the UK's GCHQ (Government Communications Headquarters) had infiltrated the connections between Yahoo and Google data centers. In response, Mayer wrote a blog post on Monday stating that Yahoo is taking measures to encrypt the information flow between its data centers.
