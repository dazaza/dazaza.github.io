【Qing Dynasty Culture】The Origin of Emperor Qianlong

The origin of Emperor Qianlong has always been a legend, and there are many stories about his background. People who have read Jin Yong's novel "Book and Sword, Gratitude and Revenge" must be curious about one claim in the book, as it states that Qianlong is the brother of Chen Jialuo. In fact, the claim in the novel is not baseless; it has some historical basis.

In late Qing dynasty, from bureaucrats to commoners, almost everyone knew such a legend: an emperor at the beginning of the Qing dynasty was the son of the Chen family from Haining, Zhejiang. Who was this emperor? Some people say it was Emperor Qianlong (Hongli). This legend also appears in some unofficial histories. In the article "Emperor Gaozong and the Chen Family of Haining" in Volume One of "A Grand View of Unofficial Qing Histories", there is such a narrative: When Prince Yin was still a prince, he had a good relationship with the Chen family of Haining, and the two families frequently interacted. That year, coincidentally, both families gave birth to children on the same day, month, and hour. However, Prince Yin’s family had a girl, while the Chen family had a boy. Prince Yin asked for the children to be brought over for a look, but secretly swapped them. The Chen family was shocked upon discovering the switch but, due to the other party's power, dared not pursue or publicize the matter. Soon after, Kangxi passed away, and the throne was passed to Prince Yin. After ascending the throne, several members of the Chen family were promoted to high-ranking officials. Later, when Emperor Qianlong ascended the throne, he treated the Chen family with even greater respect. During his six southern tours of Jiangsu and Zhejiang, four of them included visits to the Chen family in Haining. On his last visit, before leaving, he walked to the central gate and said to the Chen family: “In the future, unless the emperor personally arrives, do not open this gate easily.” From then on, this gate was never opened again.

Supporters of the above view also present other evidence. In the residence hall of the Chen family of Haining, there are two plaques personally inscribed by the emperor. One is titled "Airitang" (Love Day Hall), and the other is titled "Chunhuitang" (Spring Glow Hall). The term "Airi" comes from Yang Xiong's essay "Xiaozhi" during the Han dynasty, where "Airi" refers to a filial son's love for time spent with his parents. Later generations refer to the days a son serves his parents as "Airi." The term "Chunhui" comes from Meng Jiao's Tang dynasty poem "Youzi Yin," with the lines "Who says a blade of grass can repay the glow of three springs?" Later generations often use "Chunhui" to metaphorically represent maternal love. The inscriptions on these two plaques carry the meaning of a son respecting and being filial to his parents. Later, the girl who was exchanged with the Chen family's son grew up in the Chen household. Upon reaching marriageable age, she married into the Jiang family of Changshu, Jiangsu. The Jiang family specially built her a small tower, later known as the "Princess Tower." These historical records further strengthen the belief that Qianlong was the son of a Han Chinese family.

However, others have raised objections.

Emperor Yongzheng had ten sons and six daughters. Emperor Qianlong was his fourth son. Logically, there was no need to bring a child of another surname to pass off as his own to inherit the throne. This is the most convincing argument.

Secondly, looking at the relationship between the Qing emperors and the Chen family of Haining, it was purely a friendship between ruler and minister. The Chen family was a prominent noble family at the beginning of the Qing dynasty. Throughout the reigns of Kangxi, Yongzheng, and Qianlong, the Chen family enjoyed smooth official careers, holding high offices and enjoying great prestige. In the early years of Yongzheng's reign, to meet the economic development and living needs of the lower reaches of the Qiantang River, large-scale construction of the Haining seawall in Zhejiang was undertaken. However, due to his busy state affairs and the fact that the damage caused by sea tides to the embankment had not yet reached a very serious level, Yongzheng did not personally visit the site. After Qianlong ascended the throne, he placed great importance on this project, making several southern tours, four of which included visits to Haining for inspections. Since he arrived in Haining, there needed to be a suitable residence, and since the Chen family were ministers of Kangxi, Yongzheng, and Qianlong, their estate, known for its beautiful pavilions and lush flowers, naturally became the place where the emperor stayed. This garden was originally called "Yuyuan" but was renamed "Anlan Yuan" by Emperor Qianlong. "Anlan" means calm water, indicating that Qianlong's visit to Haining was for inspecting the seawall project rather than visiting his parents.

As for those two plaques, according to historian Meng Sen's research, in the "Biography of Chen Yuanlong" compiled by the Qing National History Office, it is stated that in April of the 39th year of Kangxi (1700 AD), Kangxi summoned the ministers in a side hall and said: "Each of your homes has a hall name. You may write it down for me now, and I will write it out and bestow it upon you." Chen Yuanlong reported that his father was over eighty years old, so he proposed the name "Airitang." The "Haining Prefecture Gazetteer" also mentions that in June of the 54th year of Kangxi (1715 AD), because Chen Yuanlong's younger brother Chen Weikun's wife Huang had been widowed for forty-one years, Kangxi personally wrote the words "Jie Xiao" (Filial Piety) and bestowed the plaque "Chunhuitang" upon her. This means that the inscriptions on the two plaques were written by Kangxi based on the requests of his subjects and have nothing to do with filial piety towards parents. Therefore, the claim that Qianlong was the son of a Han Chinese family is unfounded.

In "Palace Poems of the Qing Dynasty," there is a poem that says: "It is difficult to restore the Han system of imperial robes, but they once stayed in Anlan Garden." The poem subtly refers to the relationship between Qianlong and the Chen family of Haining. However, the exact nature of this relationship and the true origin of Qianlong remain unsolved mysteries.
