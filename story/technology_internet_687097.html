The real-name system for express delivery is just a rumor, and there is a lack of legal basis for the real name of the sender.

Source:站长之家365的空间 & nbsp; 2012-03-02

Whether the real-name system for express delivery will be implemented has been widely concerned by the society, with supporters and opponents holding different views. Not long ago, a subsidiary company of YTO Express began to check the ID card of the sender, which was interpreted by the society as a trial run of the real-name system for express delivery. Later, senior management of YTO clarified that what YTO was promoting was [opening the box for inspection], not the real-name system for express delivery.

No precedent for real-name express delivery

The Shanghai Postal Administration confirmed to reporters that the previous rumors of the real-name system were merely the actions of some franchisees of YTO and ZTO enterprises. The postal authorities have never studied or promoted the real-name system for express delivery, neither now nor in the foreseeable future. Xia Yi, director of the Market Supervision Department of the Shanghai Postal Administration, said that the conditions for implementing the real-name system are not yet in place. Currently, the pilot project for the real-name express delivery in Shaoxing, Zhejiang, due to its short duration, has limited effects. Although it has achieved some success in addressing security loopholes, many thorny issues such as legality and efficiency have emerged, requiring research and summary.

Industry insiders generally do not see the real-name system for express delivery positively. Apart from the lack of legal basis, it is extremely difficult to implement and is likely to [become a mere formality]. "There is no precedent in the global express industry requiring all senders to provide identity information," said Xu Yong, chief consultant of China Express Consulting Network. Taking the United States as an example, dealing with risky mail such as parcel bombs mainly relies on technical means, requiring all transfer centers of courier companies to be equipped with advanced X-ray machines to inspect parcels, with key suspicious areas being checked by the security department.

Responsibility shifting seems far-fetched

Not long ago, the parcel explosion incidents in Guangdong and Zhejiang became an important reason for the public's call for a real-name system for express delivery.

The real-name system for express delivery requires customers to show their ID cards when sending parcels. The courier company’s staff needs to open the package for inspection, verify the items being sent, and register this information into the computer. Recipients also need to show their ID cards to receive the delivery. Wang Xiaorong, Secretary-General of the Zhejiang Provincial Express Association, believes that "[implementing the real-name system is more difficult than imagined, and the issue goes beyond personal privacy]." A manager of YTO Express at Xinzhuang branch stated that the greater resistance lies in the fact that national laws do not grant couriers the right to check ID documents. Moreover, given the current low status of couriers, many office buildings, units, and large compounds are inaccessible, let alone requiring senders to provide identity information.

Regarding the earlier operations of YTO Zhejiang Company ["requiring ID checks only for individual customers"], Wei Jiang, Director of the Service Industry Research Institute of Zhejiang University's School of Economics, believes that the safety of courier items is the responsibility of courier companies and the government. If the responsibility is shifted onto consumers under the pretext of ensuring safety, it indeed seems somewhat far-fetched.

Challenges in enforcement inspections

The national standard for "Express Service" issued by the General Administration of Quality Supervision, Inspection and Quarantine and the National Standardization Management Committee will be officially implemented on May 1st this year. The standard clearly stipulates the strict implementation of opening boxes for inspection. Xia Yi believed that ensuring safety still depends on enterprises strictly enforcing the opening box inspection system and blocking unsafe factors at the source.

However, "parcels being shipped without being inspected according to requirements is not an isolated phenomenon," said Deputy Secretary-General Shao Zhonglin of the China Express Association. The explosions and prohibited items in express packages mainly result from the failure to properly conduct inspections. In response, the reporter consulted multiple front-line couriers who stated that it is [almost impossible] to fully inspect every parcel before collection. A courier surnamed Wang from STO Express said that sometimes hundreds of parcels are collected at once, and even if they could be opened, there would be no time to inspect them, let alone the fact that some customers are unwilling to allow the inspection of their parcels. However, regarding opening boxes for inspection, there are also positive responses within the courier industry. Courier Xiao Xie from Yunda Express introduced that starting next month, he will carry an inspection stamp with him every day. For suspicious items or those refused for inspection, the company strictly prohibits collection.
