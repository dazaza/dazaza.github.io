Microsoft needs someone who doesn't have baggage from its past.

by anonymous on 2013-08-26 14:07:48

With Ballmer stepping down, who will take over? Foreign media reports suggest that Microsoft needs a successor with no ties to its past.

The report mentions that in Ballmer's resignation letter, he stated “Microsoft’s best days lie ahead.”

However, the harsh reality is that Microsoft won't experience the same miracle as when Jobs returned to Apple. Microsoft's best days are already behind it.

In the Ballmer era, Microsoft struggled to transition into the mobile and social 21st century. As mobile cloud computing and consumer technology soared at the expense of PCs and enterprise applications, Microsoft's response was slow.

The PC business, which drives Windows sales, has shown a steady decline. In the second quarter of 2013, global PC shipments fell by 11% year-over-year.

Ballmer witnessed the success of Xbox and Windows 7, as well as the less popular Windows Vista and Windows 8. In July, the company had to write down $900 million related to these products.

In 2007, when the iPhone was introduced, Ballmer claimed "The iPhone has no chance of getting significant market share. No chance." In 2008, Microsoft acquired Danger for $500 million, but this did not resolve Microsoft's mobile strategy issues, leaving it lagging behind in the smartphone market.

Ballmer did avoid the acquisition of Yahoo!, but Microsoft still competes with Google for lucrative search revenue. Microsoft's Bing search engine increased its U.S. market share from 8.9% in July 2009 to 17.9% in July 2013. However, the company's overall online business division remains unprofitable.

Although Microsoft continues to generate billions of dollars in revenue for shareholders, its stock performance has been lackluster. Meanwhile, competitors like Apple and Google have gained momentum.

In his farewell letter, Ballmer stated that Microsoft needs a CEO who can focus on a new direction for the long term. This new direction requires a turnaround that could last another 30 years.

With 100,000 employees, $74 billion in revenue, and a range of new products, Microsoft proves that completely disrupting user experiences like its younger competitors is quite challenging. The new Microsoft reorganization is unlikely to quickly eliminate internal divisions or immediately accelerate innovation and expand the audience for Windows 8 or Surface tablets.

Ballmer is correct; Microsoft can no longer afford any failures. The good times may lie ahead, but that will require leadership from an external source.

Several internal candidates and former Microsoft employees have been mentioned as potential replacements. However, paving the way for the next decade requires someone with no connection to the past, someone without deep ties or entanglement in Microsoft's internal conflicts. Perhaps Marc Benioff of Salesforce.com, Sheryl Sandberg of Facebook, John Donahoe of eBay, or Jeff Bezos of Amazon might be interested in a new career challenge.
