Comprehensive Multiplex Cinema Architectural Design Standards and Renovation Plans

Multiplex - Comprehensive Multi-hall Cinemas

The English term for comprehensive multi-hall cinemas is "Multiplex," referring to multiple theaters under one roof showing films simultaneously, often integrated with other entertainment industries, catering services, and retail businesses. Multi-hall cinemas provide audiences with more choices in terms of the number of film programs and screening times, while also offering cinemas the opportunity to fully utilize various resources for scaled operations. The network of comprehensive cinemas has already formed a new pattern and development trend in the current film exhibition industry, marking an important upgrade in this sector.

Development of Multi-hall Cinemas

The world's first iconic six-screen multiplex was built by AMC International Theatres in Omaha, Nebraska, USA, in 1969, originally named Six West Cinema, later renamed Westroads Theatre. Asia's first comprehensive cinema was the GV Yishun Cinema, constructed by Golden Village (GV) in Singapore in 1992. It was then Asia's largest integrated entertainment cinema, housing ten screens along with shopping centers, food courts, and other entertainment facilities within a single building. Golden Village in Singapore is therefore known as the "pioneer of Asian comprehensive cinemas."

China’s transition to multi-hall cinemas can be traced back to the early 1990s. After several renovations, Chengdu's two movie complexes each had over a dozen screens by 1994. This was followed by Changzhou’s Asia Cinema, Kunming New Cinema; today, Hangzhou Qingchun Movie World and Guangzhou Tianhe Movie City are among the most famous and successful examples. Starting in 1997, foreign capital theater chains entered China, establishing truly modern multi-hall cinemas in Shanghai, Wuhan, Chongqing, by companies like UME, Xingyue, and Golden Harvest.

In recent years, the construction of multi-hall cinemas has become the mainstream of China's film industry development. A large number of genuinely modern multi-hall cinemas have emerged in major cities like Beijing Xin Dong'an Movie Complex, Huaxing Movie Complex, Wuhan Wuhan Movie Complex, Hubei Theater, Hangzhou Cuizhuang Movie World, Guangzhou Zhonghua Plaza, Shenzhen Urban Plaza, etc.

Gaps in China's Multi-hall Cinemas

Tying together 1,000 small boats will not create an aircraft carrier; similarly, hanging a screen in an office and setting up a projector does not constitute a modern comprehensive multi-hall cinema. From the beginning, China's multi-hall cinemas have achieved excellent results and become box office pillars in their respective cities. However, compared to foreign-funded cinemas, Chinese cinemas are often renovations based on original one or two halls, lacking overall modern design and coordination, thus having certain gaps with the modern concept of comprehensive multi-hall cinemas.

Besides multi-hall setups, we often overlook the technical performance of cinemas when constructing modern ones, ignoring the role of movies as the best audio-visual carriers and cinemas as the best viewing environments. Issues such as screen size, surround sound, audience line-of-sight obstruction, and seat comfort all reflect deficiencies in our cinemas' viewing experience. On the other hand, the layout, decoration, and acoustics/optics handling of the auditoriums still follow traditional patterns, neither reducing construction and operating costs, nor providing better consumer environments, nor creating freshness and modernity. Additionally, services from computerized ticket sales to snack bars both lower the audience's consumption quality and lose potential additional profits.

Site Selection and Planning for Multi-hall Cinemas

Site Selection and Investment Returns for Multi-hall Cinemas

Cinema site selection involves population trend forecasting and market screen capacity analysis. For example, when a city's area becomes a target, you need to examine economic and demographic issues such as population density, households, income, age, education, and occupation, focusing on understanding young people with college educations and families, who form the core audience (and future customers). When considering a location, you should also assess nearby competing cinemas ("market clearing") and the feasibility of nearby retail business. Since distributors divide cities into screening areas or retail commercial zones, another consideration is whether the cinema can easily obtain film prints.

Cinema investment and site selection must involve detailed investment return rate analyses, based on factors such as investment motivation, investor composition, and market expectations. Generally, projects that recover investments within five years are considered operationally meaningful.

Based on existing resources and experiences in the industry, combined with the distribution patterns of films and the layouts of local small commodities, catering, and entertainment industries, detailed market feasibility analyses should be conducted.

Planning for Multi-hall Cinemas

Below, we borrow from foreign planning methods. After site selection, there are at least two ways to proceed. One is to buy the land, the other is to sign a long-term land lease agreement and build a multi-hall cinema without using cash. A typical method is to sign a standard "build-to-suit" lease agreement, where the developer provides funds and construction, either built by you or by the developer according to your specifications. In any case, costs include legal matters, architectural design, and pre-opening expenses (commonly referred to as "soft costs"), as well as equipment (seats, screens, snack counters, wall decorations, projection room equipment, carpets, etc.), usually borne by the cinema operator.

Another factor to consider is that the cinema's floor level should not be too high, as it may fail to meet local fire and safety requirements and inconvenience attracting audiences. If the cinema is located on the second or third floor, it is recommended to install escalators.

This naturally involves many complex legal issues between the tenant and landlord, such as public elevators, restrooms, air conditioning systems, etc., regarding investment or rent. The cinema needs careful, even arduous, negotiations to minimize construction costs.

Based on feasibility analysis, cinema design determines the number of seats, screens, and overall configuration of the comprehensive multi-hall cinema. This is generally assisted by external architects and typically ensures the ability to conduct nearby retail business. Develop a complete budget and select contract partners based on competitive bids. Construction time depends on the complexity of the cinema, usually requiring 5 to 6 months, plus an additional month or two for a "stabilization period." To create maximum impact, cinemas typically open during peak seasons like summer vacation and Christmas.

The establishment of a cinema involves complex system engineering related to law, economics, architecture, and film expertise. Therefore, we need a professional department or hire professional consultants to coordinate with property owners or lessors, architectural design institutes, equipment suppliers, and contractors, overseeing leasing, interior decoration, equipment procurement, installation, and delivery.

Design and Layout of Multi-hall Cinemas

The operational advantages of multi-hall cinemas mainly lie in two aspects: one is providing audiences with multiple program and time options through multiple halls, and the other is fully utilizing the cinema's various resources to save operating costs. Therefore, these points should be fully reflected in the design and structural layout of multi-hall cinemas.

Number of Total Seats and Screening Halls in Multi-hall Cinemas

It is difficult to define a standard scale for multi-hall cinemas. In fact, the market research and feasibility analysis conducted earlier should have already considered this issue. We should take into account specific situations such as the local film market conditions, foot traffic, supply of film programs, and architectural structure restrictions. Generally, we believe:

There should be no fewer than three halls, and the total number of seats should be no less than 500, otherwise it would be difficult to demonstrate the scale operation and benefits of a cinema;

The number of seats in each hall should not be less than 60, and preferably not exceed 500; apart from luxury small halls like "Golden Class", overly small halls not only fail to demonstrate scale benefits but also affect the viewing experience;

The number of halls should not exceed 12; too many halls would make it difficult to ensure sufficient program supply, and scale benefits would also be constrained by upper limits.

Structure and Layout of Multi-hall Cinemas

Due to different film schedules, film quality, and audience demographics, corresponding variations in audience numbers occur. Therefore, multi-hall cinemas can be designed with halls of varying sizes and seating capacities. This can be combined with architects' considerations for building structures, fire safety, evacuation, and other requirements to meet various needs. Clearly, it contradicts actual conditions and requirements if all halls in a multi-hall cinema have identical scales and seating capacities.

However, it is necessary to consider that, regardless of the size of the halls, there should be no significant differences in architectural style, decoration, or sound and light quality across all halls in a multi-hall cinema. Opposing temporary patchwork of small halls, otherwise, audiences who only visit small halls might think movies are just so-so, while those who visit large halls would not tolerate poorer viewing quality in small halls, lowering the overall quality of the entire cinema complex. Similarly, we do not recommend using video rooms to assemble multi-hall cinemas for the same reason.

Multi-hall cinemas should aim to share a common lobby, a single ticket counter, a check-in entrance, and a projection room, avoiding spanning multiple buildings, floors, or having multiple entrances. These measures can reduce investment and operating costs, improve projection techniques and quality, and facilitate management.

Since the snack bar is an important source of cinema revenue, designing it in a prominent position between the cinema box office and entrance is also crucial.

Projection Techniques for Multi-hall Cinemas

The construction of multi-hall cinemas abroad has gone through nearly half a century of exploration, resulting in a set of high-standard film projection technique and equipment requirements. Multi-hall setups merely provide audiences with more convenient choice space and flexibility. Enhancing and ensuring the technical performance of film projections, guaranteeing the optimal audio-visual carrier effect of movies, is the fundamental way for cinemas to stand out in competition with numerous entertainment forms and media. Therefore, domestic and international experts regard "high technical performance" alongside multi-hall setups as marks of the new round of cinema renovations.

Regarding cinema projection techniques, various industry and commercial standards have been introduced abroad, such as SMPTE, THX, Dolby Laboratory standards, etc. China also launched GB/T3557-94 'Technical Requirements for Audio-Visual Environment of Cinemas' and 'Technical Specifications for Digital Stereo Sound Cinemas' in 1994 and 1998 respectively, which are mandatory principles and regulations during cinema construction. All norms and standards summarize past experiences aimed at highlighting the high technical performance of cinema projections and showcasing the effects and charm of movies. The upcoming 'Star-rated Cinema Evaluation Standard' establishes norms for the construction of multi-hall cinemas in China.

We briefly describe the technical requirements of modern multi-hall cinemas as four "S":

Screen - Large Screen

Compared to projection and television (even high-definition digital TV), movie projection offers the best fidelity and clarity, making ensuring a large screen an important means to display the superiority and effect of movies. Under current conditions, we should strive to maximize the screen size of cinemas, ideally achieving wall-to-wall screens. Due to aspect ratio issues, it is essential to consider sufficient ceiling height when designing movie halls. Some cinemas converted from offices or guesthouses suffer from insufficient ceiling height, affecting screen width and thereby reducing movie projection quality.

Currently, most cinemas in China use three equal-height screens. We suggest, if conditions allow, considering achieving equal-width screens for masking and wide screens, effectively increasing the screen area for masking films.

Stereo Sound - Digital Surround Sound

The effect of movie projection is composed of sound and light. Besides large screens, Dolby Laboratories and DTS Company have developed 6.1-channel surround sound systems and technologies, which have been adopted by major cinemas worldwide to perfect the language of movies. We will introduce this in detail below. Here, we emphasize that we hope there will not be significant differences in audio levels across halls; it is undesirable for one hall to adopt digital surround sound while others continue to use mono sound.

Stadium Seating - High Slope

Stadium seating is regarded as a key component of American cinema innovation in the 1990s, meaning the seating area is sloped like a stadium, ensuring absolute unobstructed views for viewers and appropriately lowering screen height, enhancing comfort when watching large screens. High slope imposes strict requirements on hall height and building structure.

Seats - Comfortable Seating

Allowing viewers to comfortably sit for two hours on fixed seats and frequently revisit for two hours places higher demands on seating, beyond just "soft seats." Both domestic and international seating manufacturers have introduced new high-back chairs that not only conform better to ergonomics but also offer diverse functions like cup holders.

Other Considerations in Hall Construction Include:

1. Background Noise: The most important aspect is controlling and reducing all sources of noise affecting cinema background noise. Possible noise sources include air conditioning systems, projectors, exhaust fans, lounges, transformers, and noise from the projection booth. Audience hall noise cannot exceed NC-30 at any frequency.

2. Isolation: The audience hall must be completely isolated from other noise sources, including preventing sound leakage from adjacent halls. This is a newly emphasized factor for multi-hall cinemas compared to before. THX requires sound insulation to reach STC60.

3. Reverberation: Reverberation time must be calculated to remain within the required reverberation limit. Direct reverberation like "direct echoes" must not be heard during movie screenings. However, it is also necessary to control excessively short reverberation times, making sounds "dry."

4. Viewing Angle: The recommended viewing angle for wide screens (2.35:1) from the screen to the farthest seat is 36 degrees, with 26 degrees being the minimum acceptable value. (According to Dolby's suggestion, the viewing angle measured from the screen to the farthest seat is approximately 31 degrees.)

5. Projection: Projectors must be placed at the center of the image (projection angle-induced image error should be less than 3%, with a maximum not exceeding ±5%). All SMPTE projection standards must be met. Additionally, the screen brightness must confirm compliance with SMPTE's 16-foot lamberts (±4-foot lamberts) standard.

Others – Internal Decoration, Lobby, and Snack Bar of Multi-hall Cinemas

Multi-hall cinemas are comprehensive systematic projects involving knowledge from multiple fields, including film expertise, architecture, decoration, advertising, etc., drawing on construction and management experiences from malls, amusement parks, hotels, etc. We focus on introducing some aspects where misconceptions exist in China. The internal decoration style of the cinema should fully reflect the characteristics of science fiction movies, utilizing the advertising effects of movie posters, promotional images, and celebrity photos, regularly updating posters and promotional images to create hotspots for new releases and maintain freshness. As an entertainment venue and cultural consumption place, cinemas should avoid excessive luxury and fashion, creating a strong movie atmosphere and certain cultural taste. Building a grand "five-star hotel"-style lobby will definitely attract audiences. The cinema lobby (grand hall) should adopt bright, comfortable, clean, and lively tones and decorations, gradually transitioning these tones towards the auditoriums, whose interiors should avoid luxury, adopting dark tones, and avoiding shallow colors and reflective materials, retaining the screen as the only highlight. Auditorium walls should minimize complex decorative effects, as audiences come to watch movies.

With continuous innovations in building materials and acoustic treatment methods, as well as advancements in cinema decoration concepts, traditional perforated gypsum boards and 1.2-meter wooden wall skirts that satisfy acoustic requirements while offering decorative effects have long been abandoned. An experienced architect or acoustics consultant will employ more modern and economical methods for acoustic treatment within the hall.

The cinema lobby (grand hall) and entrances to individual auditoriums should be clearly marked, properly directed, and reasonably laid out, with the box office, snack bar, movie trailer board, and restrooms all clearly and reasonably positioned.

Snack bar sales account for an important part of cinema revenue abroad, yet China's cinemas have not paid enough attention to this aspect. Bright, clean snack bars and distinctive foods and beverages are crucial for attracting audiences. Foreign cinemas place great emphasis on popcorn sales, filling the cinema with its aroma as a marketing strategy. Some joke that cinemas sell not movie tickets but the smell of popcorn.
