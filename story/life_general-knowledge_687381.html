Debt - who owes whom?

Debt

"Why don't you give it a good bath? Look how dirty it is!" A young girl opened the car door, glanced back at the old lady, and drove off, leaving behind a trail of exhaust fumes. She was very fashionable, with her hair piled high on her head, wearing a half-transparent chiffon mini-skirt in Korean style, and her slippers glinted with diamond-like reflections...

As the car disappeared into the distance, the old woman sat down by the roadside. Her hair was completely white, neatly combed but still somewhat disheveled. Her eyes were sunken deep into their sockets, slightly cloudy. Without any teeth, her cheeks were hollow, and thick and thin wrinkles covered her weather-beaten face. The chronic pain from her back had tormented her for most of her life, making sitting down and standing up excruciatingly painful. Her gaze was blank, and tears glistened in the corners of her eyes. The little dog lay beside her, not knowing whether it was because its owner called it dirty or if it was saddened by the old woman's plight, but its eyes also held tears. Its mouth was open, tongue out, and it panted heavily.

"Grandma Zhang, how could you walk the dog when your foot is sprained?"

"Ah, this is a task given to me by my granddaughter-in-law. She says if the dog doesn't get walked, it will get fat. After walking, I need to bathe it and comb its fur. Just now, she scolded me, saying I didn't wash it clean."

...

The old woman had six children, the eldest already over fifty. Back in the day, she relied on her husband alone to make a living, supporting the entire family. Eating sparingly and mending clothes repeatedly, she raised each child to adulthood. When they should have been enjoying a good life, her husband passed away. With no source of income, Grandma could only rotate between her children's homes. The eldest son had a son, and naturally, the responsibility of looking after him fell on Grandma. After all, it was her own grandson—how could she let others take care of him? If he got hurt, it would be a lifetime regret. When the grandson started primary school, her daughter gave birth to a baby girl, and Grandma's battlefield shifted once again. "Girls are different from boys; they must be kept clean and handled carefully. Not just anyone can hold or kiss them." Grandma remembered these instructions well, and her granddaughter grew up without any calamities until she turned six. Then it was the third son's turn... It was the sixth child who didn't want children. Grandma thought she could finally enjoy some comfort, but unexpectedly, her eldest grandson found a girlfriend. The girl liked dogs but had no time to manage them, so Grandma naturally became the dual nanny for both people and pets.

Grandma couldn't understand why she was destined for such a life of toil. When her husband was alive, although they occasionally argued, when they calmed down and reminisced about their journey together, the fruits of their labor seemed sweet. On the ground, Grandma wrote her husband's name, erasing it and writing it again, placing a cross over it. "Why did you leave so early? Why didn't you wait for me..."

On the seventh day of the seventh lunar month, China's Valentine's Day, the day when the Cowherd and Weaver Girl meet in legend, Grandma missed her husband again. She recalled the moment her veil was lifted by her bridegroom, and she smiled, her eyes narrowing into slits. Remembering the birth of her first child, despite the excruciating pain, her heart was filled with joy. Recalling how she picked vegetable leaves and boiled gruel, raising her children one by one, she felt she hadn't betrayed her late husband's expectations. Thinking about the vows her children made as they knelt before their dying father, she wasn't sure whether today's life was bitter or sweet...

"Old man, I'm tired, I miss you. I've never missed you as much as I do now. Are you doing well there? You're enjoying peace and happiness there while leaving me here alone. If you still think of me, send me a dream..."

Tears flowed from Grandma's dry eyes, falling onto her collar, leaving patches of marks. After burning the last piece of paper money, she lingered for a long time, unwilling to leave. Her husband's face was right in front of her, as if she could touch it with her hand. Watching the ashes being blown by the wind, she felt that it was clearly her husband waving at her, calling her...

Cars roared past nearby, weaving through the streets. The scent of tempting food wafted out from hotels. Adults and children chased each other forward, and couples embraced passionately under neon lights...
