How "7080" parents educate their children: Reviewing various "parenting tips"

by edurqusr on 2012-02-28 15:12:51

From "Tiger Mom", "Eagle Dad" to spending 30,000 yuan for a Spring Festival for their daughter, children's family education has always been a common concern of parents from different generations. Whether it be "leading by example", "tempering the will"; whether it be "spare the rod and spoil the child", or insisting on "education of love" & hellip; & hellip; no model has become an eternal exemplar.

nbsp;

Parents of the Post-70s: Over-indulgence, Strong Compensation

The childhood of the Post-70s was relatively materially impoverished. This bitter memory of childhood causes the Post-70s parents to have a strong sense of "compensation" towards their own children, transferring all the conditions they themselves could not enjoy onto their children. However, the development of the times has brought about increasingly abundant material conditions, as well as excessive indulgence from many parents.

Li Jiaxin spent 5 million yuan to create a fairy-tale hundred-day banquet for her son, while David Beckham and his wife spent tens of thousands of pounds to build a mini-kindergarten for their daughter. Wu Zongxian splurged 150 million Taiwanese dollars to buy a "degree house" for his son. Although the indulgence of ordinary families cannot match the luxury of celebrities, now many parents also excessively indulge their children, sparing nothing for them.

To celebrate Children's Day, parents lavishly spend money to book five-star hotels for their children to enjoy a series of entertainment programs; money is trivial, but there are plenty of parents who are willing to spend extravagantly for their children's education.

Parents of the Post-80s: Superior Conditions, Fulfilling Every Wish

If the indulgence of Post-70s parents can still be understood, the indulgence of Post-80s parents is truly astonishing. As the first generation to grow up under China's one-child policy, the Post-80s themselves were a generation that was highly cherished. Time flies, and the Post-80s have grown from children into parents. Due to their own favorable growing conditions, when it comes to their children's generation, all conditions are even more superior, and Post-80s parents fulfill every wish of their children.

Nowadays, children haven't even been born yet, but they start enjoying various high-end consumption. During pregnancy, there are numerous expensive prenatal education, prenatal check-ups, and antenatal examinations; at birth, there are VIP-priced hospital maternity rooms, where having a baby can cost 180,000 yuan in just seven days; in order to "not lose at the starting line," there are sky-high early education classes costing fortunes; following the principle of "raising boys in poverty and girls in wealth," Post-80s fathers accompany their four-year-old daughters on extravagant trips, fulfilling every wish of their daughters; to broaden their children's horizons, during vacations, they send their children abroad for study tours, costing tens of thousands of yuan.

Role Models:

Understanding, Respect, Leading by Example

In the face of these phenomena, how should we correctly view "family education"? Many film and television works have made parents reflect deeply.

In the movie "Dad Camp" (a comedy), two dads take a group of kids on an outdoor camping trip. During this process, the children learn teamwork, self-protection, and tolerance. The movie reflects the American educational philosophy of teaching through fun. They respect the children's natural inclination to play and are happy to become their playmates, accompanying them with an equal attitude, truly converting understanding into action.

It is often said that parents are the most influential teachers in a child's life. In the South Korean drama "It's Okay, It's My Daughter" (Japanese/Korean TV series), the father's love is the nourishment of the entire family. The father in the drama, despite being in difficult circumstances, consistently influences the children with his kind and steadfast qualities. Under his influence, the children, who originally only knew how to complain, gradually matured, learning strength and empathy.

"The Family" (Emotional TV series) runs through the story of three children's "happy growth." Although Xia Dad and Liu Mom have parenting pitfalls such as nagging, deceiving, and double standards, these cannot replace their tolerance and democracy. In the process of outsmarting their parents, the children develop a delightful authenticity.

Negative Examples:

Rudeness, Favoritism, Excessive Interference

There are also negative examples in films and TV dramas. In the Japanese/Korean TV series "The Legend of the Seven Princesses," Father Luo Yangba is a typical example of failed family education. Under Luo Yangba's brutal education, his daughter developed a timid personality. If he hadn't been so authoritarian and instead given his daughter the opportunity to express herself, perhaps she wouldn't have fallen into early romance and pregnancy.

The movie "Aftershock" (Arts Film) tells us that favoritism can become a thorn in the hearts of children. In families with multiple children, it is easy to show preference for one child over another. This seemingly harmless favoritism is extremely "destructive." Research shows that parental favoritism can lead to emotional suppression in children. Therefore, smart parents will try to minimize the deviation in their love, striving to keep everything balanced.

At the same time, parents should not forget that children are independent individuals and should not overstep boundaries when educating them. The clashes between the young and old in "Adolescence Meets Menopause" (Emotional TV series) are not just generational gaps caused by adolescence meeting menopause, but also the children's "non-violent non-cooperation" against their parents' excessive interference.
