The Strategic Turning Point of the Smart Home Security Industry in 2012

Operating an industry in China is simple, yet also very difficult. It's easy to get a hang of things, but hard due to the ever-changing landscape that can instantly alter an industry's survival environment. Without forward-looking vision and courage, it's easy to lose everything!

To operate an industry in China, three points must be considered as important references: first is government policy; second is the direction of major brands; third is consumer demand. The current predicament faced by the once booming Chinese home appliance industry is due to significant changes in these three areas. Whether it’s national macro policies, brand concentration, rising costs, or the overall shift in consumer demographics, all have had negative impacts on the home appliance industry, making it a very risky investment area.

The crisis of one industry marks the rise of another. In 2012, industry insiders predicted that the smart home security industry could likely replace the home appliance industry to become the new sunrise industry.

From a policy perspective, the government has listed the smart home security industry alongside new energy and cultural creative industries as strategic emerging industries in the 12th Five-Year Plan, committing substantial funds for support and development. From the consumer level, due to China currently being in a major transformation period with many social instability factors, and the widening wealth gap, many Chinese people have increased their emphasis on personal safety and property protection.

According to statistics, last year, China's household consumption expenditure was 11.2 trillion yuan. Market surveys show that in developed regions of China, household expenditures on safety account for about 8% to 10%, meaning this market size is roughly between 900 billion to 1.1 trillion yuan. Currently, China's relatively independent security industry is around 100 billion yuan in scale, which means there is nearly 1 trillion yuan worth of development space, ten times its current size. Therefore, it is destined that 2012 will be a major strategic turning point for the entire security industry.

However, to truly seize such a turning point, having only policy and consumers is clearly not enough; enterprises as the backbone force are also needed. Currently, the biggest issue in promoting the security industry is technology and communication. Technologically, enterprises need to invest more funds, learn from and adopt the latest technologies and achievements from developed countries' security industries and intelligent communication industries, converting them into products that suit Chinese consumer habits.

In terms of communication, many consumers are not very familiar with the characteristics and trends of security products. They often think security products are just cameras or anti-theft doors, lacking an awareness of comprehensive protection. Therefore, enterprises not only need to enhance technology but also focus on related promotional work, fully launching such products in the market so that security products are no longer the first choice for high-end communities or affluent populations, but also essential for ordinary families.

Currently, the overall security industry is still mixed with good and bad players. There are famous international brands from Japan and South Korea, as well as some relatively well-known domestic brands. However, due to differences in technical focus and target markets, the entire industry lacks a leading brand or benchmark brand. If an industry lacks a leading brand, it is difficult for consumers to genuinely accept such a category and product, ultimately making it hard to drive the entire industry to grow stronger and bigger. Therefore, such a deadlock must be fundamentally changed.
