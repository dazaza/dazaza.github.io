The market style may "turn without changing"

by 51hicsb on 2009-12-09 10:34:10

The issue of market style transformation has been receiving increasing attention, with both bullish and bearish views having sufficient reasons. In fact, behind the proposition of switching strength between large-cap and small-cap stocks, there is also an implicit conversion from "weak-cycle" industries to "strong-cycle" industries. We believe that neither type of conversion can be achieved overnight, and for a period in the future, the market will still face a relatively balanced situation of "switching but not converting". The gradually clarified expectation of the launch of stock index futures might be one of the major factors that could truly trigger a sustained rally in large-cap stocks.

When the Shanghai Composite Index fell from 3478 points to 2639 points and then oscillated upwards, the market style was clearly biased towards small and medium-sized cap stocks during this period. The CSI 100, 200, and 500 indices rose by 23.89%, 33.40%, and 41.00% respectively. As the premium rate of small-cap stocks relative to large-cap stocks continued to rise, voices suggesting a style switch began to emerge and became increasingly strong. With the abnormal fluctuations of large-cap stocks on Thursday and Friday last week, discussions on this topic became more heated, and many institutions proposed paying attention to the style switch in their weekly strategy reports.

Guosen Securities believes that the conditions have been met, and the switch itself is underway. CICC believes that due to the premium rate of small-cap stocks relative to large-cap stocks reaching a historical high, the market style gradually shifting to large-cap stocks is a high-probability event. China Merchants Securities believes that the timing for switching industry configurations is gradually approaching, suggesting gradually reducing holdings in consumption and over-allocating finance, midstream, and upstream sectors in the first half of next year. Guotai Junan pointed out that the "consumption rally" is nearing its end, and cyclical sectors are beginning to start under the impetus of rising product prices expectations.

We believe that whether it's the conversion from small-cap to large-cap stocks or the tilt from consumption-oriented to cyclical sectors, the process cannot be achieved overnight. Short-term "one-day excursion-style" anomalies cannot be used as evidence of successful conversion.

Firstly, the relationship between fund positions and the activation of large-cap stocks is unexpected. Generally, it is believed that the upward movement of fund positions can drive large-cap stocks stronger, but when we compare the changes in equity fund positions with the valuation premium ratio of large and small-cap stocks, we find that large-cap stocks only have relatively strong performances when equity fund positions decline.

Secondly, the GDP ring ratio annualized rate is more convincing than the year-on-year rate for the current quarter. Many investors believe that the year-on-year GDP will continue to rise, thus providing more opportunities for cyclical industries. However, we find that the ring ratio annualized rate aligns more closely with the performance of strong and weak cycle industries: the GDP ring ratio annualized rates for the first three quarters of 2009 were 8.5%, 17.5%, and 8.7% respectively. Accordingly, the best performance of strong cycle sectors occurred in the second quarter, while the starting point for weak cycle stocks to gain momentum was after October.

Finally, the upward trend of the "PPI-CPI" scissors difference indicates profit concentration in mid and upstream industries, directing market attention towards cyclical industries. We largely agree with the view of China Merchants Securities. The CSI Research conducted statistics on trends since 1999, and empirically speaking, during the upward process of the "PPI-CPI" scissors difference, the strengthening of cyclical industries is a high-probability event.

Rotation and balance will still be the main characteristics

Although there are a series of possible conditions that may trigger the conversion, we still do not believe that large-cap and cyclical stocks will have a sustained rally. Before the second quarter of 2010, the characteristic of the A-share market will still be sector rotation and balance.

Firstly, the external factors for the continuous rise of large-cap stocks are not yet mature. We believe that besides needing super-expected earnings support, there also needs to be event-driven factors to stimulate a continuous upward movement of large-cap stocks. In the past, this was manifested as adjustments in stamp duty, and in the future, we expect it to be the gradually clarified expectation of the launch of stock index futures.

Secondly, strong cycle industries face the pressure of digesting overcapacity. The steel, chemical, and non-ferrous metal industries have always had issues of overcapacity. If they cannot transition from cost-driven to demand-pulled, then price increases will not be sustainable. We expect that when supply and demand transition from loose balance to tight balance in the second half of next year, strong cycle sectors may have a rally.

We agree with GF Securities' view that during the phase where economic growth returns to potential levels, the yield gap between industries narrows, and rotation and balance will be the main characteristics of the next stage of the market. In all industries, "performance white horses" worthy of investment can be found, and the significance of selecting individual stocks begins to outweigh selecting industries.
