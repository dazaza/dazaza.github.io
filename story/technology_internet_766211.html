Microsoft's Incomplete Copy of Apple: Industry Suggests Acquiring Lenovo or HP

by anonymous on 2013-09-08 19:52:46

Everyone on Earth knows that Microsoft's Ballmer's "devices + services" strategy is essentially a copy of Apple's software-hardware integration model. However, in the PC market, Microsoft continues to sell Windows to computer manufacturers and does not sell computers directly, which does not align with the software-hardware integration approach. Forbes recently published an article pointing out that Microsoft needs to adjust its Windows licensing model, one option being the acquisition of companies like Lenovo or HP to directly sell computer hardware.

After acquiring Nokia's mobile phone business, Microsoft's development strategy will inevitably face adjustments. Forbes has proposed six suggestions for Microsoft. One of these is to "adjust the OEM (i.e., computer manufacturers pre-installing Windows) partner model."

Forbes points out that Microsoft faces a choice: either change the current method of licensing Windows to OEMs or acquire a large computer manufacturer such as HP, Lenovo, or Dell. This would mean that Windows computers, like Apple products, would become exclusive hardware products sold by Microsoft.

Financial report data shows that out of the $19.2 billion total revenue from Microsoft's Windows business, 65% comes from selling Windows licenses to computer manufacturers. However, this business is declining. In the 2012/2013 fiscal year, Microsoft's revenue from this area fell by 10%, a drop of $1.3 billion.

It is well known that PCs have become a sunset industry. IDC recently predicted that the global PC market will continue to shrink by about 10% this year, marking the second consecutive year of decline. Traditional PC manufacturers like Dell and HP are currently struggling. However, fortunately, as a pillar of the computer industry, Microsoft's Windows business is slightly less affected by the sluggish PC market. Compared to the booming mobile devices and mobile operating systems, Windows and PCs have limited prospects for development.

It should be noted that with the launch of the Surface tablet, entering the hardware market, Microsoft's relationship with traditional computer manufacturers has deteriorated rapidly. PC manufacturers like Acer oppose Microsoft's entry into hardware, viewing it as competition with partners. Acer's CEO Wang Zhentang stated that Microsoft is killing the PC ecosystem.

Clearly, after acquiring Nokia, Microsoft will enter the mobile phone hardware sector. Microsoft will then directly compete with Samsung Electronics, HTC, and other Windows Phone system licensees, making their relationships more delicate.

In contrast, in its MAC business, Apple adopts a software-hardware integration model without licensing its proprietary operating system to other manufacturers.

Besides adjusting the OEM cooperation model, Forbes' other reform suggestions to Microsoft include: A comprehensive overhaul of the company's organizational structure, far surpassing the "One Microsoft" concept previously proposed by Ballmer; addressing internal frictions that hinder innovation; investing in consumer behavior analysis, marketing, and interactive resources; actively spending and investing in technology reserves; resolving the fragmentation chaos in the Windows operating system and integrating systems like Windows 8, WinRT, and WP8.
