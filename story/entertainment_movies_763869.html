Synopsis:

When love enters a wrong and obsessive dead-end, it often triggers boundless anger in a person, which eventually leads to retaliation against others... Mike (Matt Long) is an undisputed star of the small town where he was born and raised. He was not only a popular figure in high school but also the first one in his family to attend college - Mike's football talent was recognized, and with a scholarship, he entered the prestigious Northwestern University for further studies.

Soon, the annual Christmas family reunion was approaching, and Mike, as expected, returned to the small town to celebrate the holiday with his family and friends... However, what surprised everyone slightly was that Mike was not alone this time; standing beside him was his new girlfriend Elizabeth (Jessica Stroup), a beautiful and wealthy girl from Chicago.

However, no one was more shocked than Shelby (Mischa Barton), Mike's ex-girlfriend from high school, who was also the "queen" of the school, the true campus queen. Even though they had broken up, Shelby could not forgive Mike for moving on so quickly. As love turned into hatred, she began to methodically implement her plan to help Mike get rid of Elizabeth's influence. She had to do it secretly and deal with this pressing issue properly. Meanwhile, Shelby decided to use every means necessary to win Mike back, making Elizabeth the biggest obstacle in her way, which Shelby wanted to eliminate at all costs.

Behind-the-Scenes:

["Love Turns to Hate"]

When you discover your love has entered a completely wrong corner, what would you choose to do? Perhaps, through the scenario set by filmmaker Morgan J. Freeman in his new directorial work "Homecoming," you can find a terrifying, thrilling, and extreme answer and solution... His previous directed work, "American Psycho 2: All American Girl," also told a bloody story filled with sensuality and a thick atmosphere of terror.

The letter "J" in Morgan J. Freeman's name holds great significance for him because without this letter, he would share the same name as the most famous veteran black star in contemporary times. Therefore, this "J" became the only marker distinguishing the two. Actually, many years ago, Freeman had already started putting in relentless effort and research in the horror genre. Although "American Psycho 2" ended up being released directly to home video instead of theaters, it still had a unique impact on Freeman. With "Homecoming," Freeman explores the potential terrifying consequences when misplaced love combines with an extreme sense of possession. Freeman stated: "For me, the most impressive part of the film occurs in a chilling farmhouse. This is also why I chose to set the story's environment in a quiet small town. I have always liked horror movies that take place in old houses with decaying charm, hoping for a chance to try it myself. 'Homecoming' is the crystallization of my long-held dream. Before officially shooting, the farmhouse was naturally the last location we needed to finalize, and the reason we put it at the end is due to its crucial role in the entire film. We need a place that feels perpetually dark, as if in the past, someone here was truly murdered or tortured - the farmhouse plays an important catalytic role in the development of the plot."

In fact, what surprises horror movie fans the most about "Homecoming" is that Morgan J. Freeman gathered two "scream queens" known for their wholesome images through the TV series "The O.C." and "90210," Mischa Barton and Jessica Stroup. Freeman said, "Collaborating with Barton and Stroup was perfect, they are very talented actresses who bring surprises, and the complex emotional entanglements required in the film were perfectly conveyed by them... I think they must also want to temporarily step away from the overly youthful TV series like 'The O.C.' and '90210,' using an antagonistic performance to complete the brutal game of hostage versus captor in 'Homecoming.' Besides Barton and Stroup, there's also Matt Long, who plays Mike, the one who makes them 'resentful.' The reason I made every effort to gather such a cast of actors popular among teenagers is naturally to attract more viewers to the cinema."

Since the first time he saw the original script created by Katie L. Fetting, Morgan J. Freeman found himself unable to resist the interest in the story and thoroughly fascinated by the emotional thoughts triggered by Shelby, the "antagonist" character - she resembles Kathy Bates' character Annie in "Misery." Freeman said: "At the beginning of the film, you will definitely feel sympathy for the character played by Mischa Barton, and you may be just as surprised as I am... Shelby is a very tragic girl, the princess adored by all in that small town, with conditions that can be described as perfect. So when I saw her fall into madness due to her ex-boyfriend's infidelity, I was completely stunned. Undoubtedly, 'Homecoming' pays tribute to the classic horror film 'Misery' from 1990, and that is one of the reasons it attracted my attention. Plus, I was convinced by the potential contained within the story - after all, I have always been the most loyal fan of 'Misery,' which is the most unforgettable and intelligent classic in my heart. And I see the same essence in 'Homecoming,' hoping it will conquer the new generation of horror movie enthusiasts."

In fact, few people may know that Morgan J. Freeman is a crazy fan of cult films, having participated in the production of "Welcome to the Dollhouse" as an assistant director in 1995. Freeman recalled: "I still remember how passionate I was at the time, and it was precisely because of 'Welcome to the Dollhouse' that I learned all the knowledge of independent films that I possess now. While making the film, I had the opportunity to meet Brendan Sexton III, and then specifically wrote a movie called 'Hurricane' for him, which was also my directorial debut... For me, 'Welcome to the Dollhouse' was a very critical turning point in my career. You could say everything started with that job."

Previously, in "American Psycho 2," Morgan J. Freeman also explored through the film the terrifying consequences of "mentally unstable girls going crazy," and his return to this cinematic genre naturally meant there were still aspects that attracted him. However, this type of film had long fallen into a dead end, and unless some new and attractive topics could be derived, it would be difficult to make a comeback. Freeman said: "Actually, I have always been very interested in similar horror stories, where a woman with excellent conditions transforms into a 'goddess of vengeance' due to anger and unwillingness after being abandoned by her ex-boyfriend... I believe most girls find their first loves in high school, leading them to think not of letting go when facing loss, but rather resorting to almost criminal acts of revenge - Shelby is merely putting into action the hatred that others only dare to imagine. Therefore, I believe everyone can find similar emotional resonance in her and feel compassion for this young girl. In my view, for this long-lasting cinematic genre, this itself is a new way of presentation."

Visually, "Homecoming" has a more classical color style compared to "American Psycho 2," with more powerful visual impact, paying unique tribute to old-school horror films. Morgan J. Freeman admitted: "I maintain a very close cooperative relationship with cinematographer Stephen Kazmierski and art director Mark White. They have created a background environment as realistic as possible for this film because we all hope that the farmhouse playing a key role in the story will give people a particularly dim feeling, as if it occasionally emits a stench and decay - only then will the audience believe in the madness happening inside, maintaining the basic authenticity of the content."

Since "Homecoming" is also a small-audience horror production, insufficient funds and an overly tight shooting schedule became the primary challenges that Morgan J. Freeman needed to overcome. He continued: "This means we only had 20 days to complete all the shooting work, and unfortunately, we encountered freezing weather, having to put warming items in our boots to avoid frostbite... Fortunately, we overcame all adverse conditions together, and here I must praise Mischa Barton and Jessica Stroup. These two seemingly delicate little girls endure hardships without complaint."

Even though it is labeled as a youth horror film, "Homecoming" remains quite implicit and cautious in terms of violence and sex. In fact, this is a storytelling approach consciously chosen by Morgan J. Freeman, who indicated: "We have always adhered to the filming philosophy of 'less is more'... We hope to create real horror on the big screen, whether visually or psychologically, yet we still aim to let the suspense outweigh the shock of being scared because I want every viewer who watches the film to have the feeling that 'Homecoming' is much more than just a simple horror movie."

After "Homecoming," Morgan J. Freeman decided to retreat temporarily as he is currently conceptualizing a new script with semi-autobiographical elements, focusing on the darkest and hardest year he has ever experienced. Freeman stated: "You can imagine 'Training Day,' learning how to survive in a chaotic environment. Looking back now, it still evokes a nightmare-like panic."

Due to sharing the same name as the heavyweight Hollywood black star, Morgan J. Freeman has naturally caused many harmless misunderstandings. Laughing, he admitted: "Sometimes I sit outside someone's office waiting for a meeting, and I hear an incredulous voice coming from inside, 'No way! Is it really Morgan Freeman?' Then when they see me, their expression drops instantly, and that feeling is really crazy. And whenever I check in at hotels or rent cars, the same drama unfolds, with someone shouting, 'Look, there's a white Morgan Freeman.' However, apart from that, the name issue hasn't caused me too much trouble. I think it's mainly because we rarely intersect - even so, I deeply admire his acting. We met once at the Deauville Film Festival, and when he learned our names were the same, he came over to greet me without any airs of a superstar... Luckily, the 'J' in my name has a completely different and distinctive pronunciation in French, so we were hardly mistaken there."
