It has been two months since Benson passed away, but the "murderer" is still at large. It could be any one of the following suspects: raw horse chestnuts and other junk food, Benson himself, or over-greedy anglers. Or, this was a case of natural death.

To this day, the case that took place in the hometown of Holmes remains unresolved.

The passing of a fish leaves a nation in mourning

On the morning of July 28th, anglers eagerly carried their fishing rods and chilled beer to the Golden Fisher Lake in Peterborough, UK. Everything seemed normal, with green reeds rippling on the shimmering lake surface.

Benson lived in this private fishery. On such hot days, this golden carp occasionally surfaced, covered by a lotus leaf like holding an umbrella, astonishing the worshipers.

Many people drove hundreds of miles to Golden Fisher Lake just to catch a glimpse of him. A German fishing club even flew in from Germany. One fan's last wish was to have his ashes scattered in Golden Fisher Lake so as to stay with Benson forever.

"I knew she would become a superstar," said Tony Bridgefoot, the owner of the fishery. In 1994, he placed the juvenile Benson into the lake. Despite being only about 5 years old at the time, her size was already twice that of her peers, weighing nearly 20 kilograms.

Over the course of 15 years, this big fish grew at an astonishing speed. In 2006, she reached her peak weight of 58 kilograms, almost round like an inflated flounder. However, she had been famous long before that. Bridgefoot always claimed she was the largest carp in the UK. In 2005, this "puppy-heavy" carp was voted as the "Most Popular Carp in Great Britain" by readers of the British magazine 'Anglers' Letter'.

Besides her extraordinary weight, Benson's remarkable appearance also became a topic of discussion among fans. Some saw Marilyn Monroe in her, others thought of Raquel Welch. "When this lazy golden beauty swam through the aquatic plants, no beauty could compare to her elegance," praised a fishing enthusiast. "The shiny scales looked as if they were painted on, and the small black holes on the dorsal fin resembled the beauty spots on the faces of 18th-century noblewomen."

Catching this Queen of Carps became the dream of many anglers. Every year, at least ten thousand anglers came to try their luck, but so far only 63 people succeeded. Mark Peaches was one of them. He caught Benson on his third visit to Golden Fisher Lake. "I knew it must be Benson when I lifted the rod," he still remembers the feeling today, "It was incredible, I couldn't sleep for two or three nights in a row."

Another admirer, Ray Armstrong, spent seven years chasing Benson and finally succeeded at 2 o'clock one night last year, "It was like winning the lottery, it was a magical night."

Bridgefoot attributed this popularity to a successful business strategy, "Here, you don't need to join expensive fishing annual meetings. Two people just need to buy a £27.5 daily ticket and can chase the largest carp in Britain for a full 24 hours."

Benson was hailed as the "People's Carp", and naturally, this lake became the "People's Fishery". She earned at least £20,000 per year for Bridgefoot, which is equivalent to more than 220,000 RMB. Bridgefoot estimated that about 40% of the anglers at Golden Fisher Lake were there to catch this big fish.

However, when the fishing enthusiasts sat by the shore holding their breath on July 28th, Bridgefoot, who was on vacation at his Norfolk villa, received a call from the fisherman Jimmy. On the other end of the line, Jimmy hesitated, "There's a fish floating in the lake, you'd better come take a look."

Bridgefoot immediately drove home. "Sure enough, it was Benson, she looked like she was sleeping," he recalled how his heart sank instantly.

When the news broke, many "fans" were too sad to believe it. An enthusiast said he received three calls from friends, "before daring to believe it was true." For the past eight years, he almost used all his vacations to pursue Benson, but failed every time. Even a few weeks ago, when Benson bit the hook, she managed to escape in the end.

In a torrential downpour, a Nottingham angler stood by the Golden Fisher Lake silently mourning Benson. He said in the fishing world, Benson's status was akin to the Italian "ageless goddess" Sophia Loren, both equally hard to surpass.

The passing of Benson even made the entire nation mournful. A media person described this "carp storm" thusly: "From the 7:30 AM international broadcast in the UK to the 7:30 PM Canadian television station, this carp and this fishery became the focus of media attention."

The suspect might be all the admirers who came here for her name

Amidst the sorrow, people began speculating about the cause of Benson's death. Normally, a carp can live up to 60 years, but Benson only lived for 20.

The first to investigate was Bridgefoot. He soon found some clues: a few scattered raw horse chestnuts and an empty paper bag on the shore. Horse chestnuts are known as the "king of oil crops", and in Golden Fisher Lake, they are strictly prohibited. Some anglers who like shortcuts use these "aromatic nuts" to lure fish to the surface, but if they are in a humid environment and not cooked, carps will be poisoned to death.

"Could Benson have been poisoned by her admirers?" Bridgefoot couldn't help but doubt.

Peter Burgess, who was 100 kilometers away, did not agree with this theory. This expert in fish health from Plymouth University in the UK believed that without a mass death event of carps, it indicates that Benson's death was not due to horse chestnuts.

After analyzing Benson's photos, Burgess identified two suspects. The top suspect wasn't an individual, but all the admirers who came for her fame. "She was too fat, fed too much junk food."

This point, even Bridgefoot had to admit: "Benson was a greedy girl, she would eat everything around her."

Everything? Yes, let's take a look at Benson's diet. According to records from the British magazine 'Economist', it included: crayfish, waterweed, lily roots, but more importantly, cheese chunks, minced lunch meat, bread crumbs, Peperami brand sausages, dog biscuits, assorted fruit-flavored candy balls...

Another possibility is harder for anglers to accept. Benson's death might be because she couldn't endure the repeated "catch and release" game. According to a research result published by the University of Edinburgh in 2003, fish are similar to mammals, their brains react to destructive stimuli. Researchers even suspect that the pain sensation of fish might be stronger than humans, because fish skin is much thinner than human skin. Over 15 years, Benson was caught 63 times and released 63 times. The common process was always: catching, weighing, photographers taking pictures of the angler and Benson together, then releasing Benson back into the lake, leaving the lucky one laughing foolishly by the shore.

"But all of this absolutely wouldn't exceed 5 minutes," Bridgefoot quickly added, "and she seemed to enjoy the photo-taking process."

This owner claimed to have a harmonious relationship with Benson. "When she was caught, I would talk to her, telling her to make more anglers happy, while also taking care of herself," he said. However, this mind communicator also admitted that harm was unavoidable.

Ultimately, the task of solving the case fell on a taxidermist. The day after Benson's death, Bridgefoot called his friend Barry Williams. "I have bad news, Benson died," he paused for a moment before continuing, "could you help me find out the real cause of death?"

This taxidermist was busy making a red stag head specimen at the time, but he was still shocked by the news, because Williams was very clear about the status of this carp, "although I'm not a fan of Benson, my son and I often go fishing at that lake."

Bridgefoot first met Williams a few years ago, when Bridgefoot bought a crocodile specimen from this taxidermist whose customer refused to pay, and thus they became friends.

"How can you hand the body over to a butcher to examine the cause of death?"

On August 14th, Williams drove to Golden Fisher Lake and took the body, which still weighed 47 pounds, back to his studio. "I spent the whole day examining Benson, including suspicious places inside and outside the body."

When Williams took off his rubber gloves, he determined that he had found the fatal cause - because Benson was unable to lay eggs, she accumulated a large amount of eggs inside her body. These eggs blocked the uterus and decomposed inside her, and she eventually died from the toxins produced by these eggs. Over the years, on this cold stainless steel autopsy table, he studied more than ten carps, and most of the cases were due to egg blockage in the uterus, which caused infections.

But Dr. Burgess couldn't believe this conclusion, because the body had already been damaged. In his view, Benson's owner should have "kept the body refrigerated instead of freezing it, freezing would destroy a large amount of information and clues."

"That man isn't a professional fish anatomist," he repeatedly emphasized to the Chinese reporter, "how can you hand the body over to a butcher to examine the cause of death?"

Burgess is an expert who has dissected "countless" fish bodies, and he has ruled out some common causes of fish death, such as low oxygen levels in the lake or infections, because no other bodies were found at the same time as Benson's.

Regarding this debate, anglers tend to lean towards the former. After all, if the real cause of Benson's death was the side effects brought by anglers' excessive admiration - such as tempting but nutritionally worthless junk food, or the pain of oral tearing and suffocation outside the lake water each time she was caught and released for photos - it would be hard to accept.

Columnist Gary Newman of 'Anglers' Letter' bluntly expressed his opinion that, just like people question every celebrity's age, Benson, without an accurate origin or birthday, "might have died of old age." "After all, there aren't many carps that can weigh 20 kilograms at the age of five."

Everything about Benson didn't end with her departure. Instead of a funeral, she was greeted with a specimen. For William, skilled in Victorian taxidermy, this would be the most famous fish specimen among all he had ever made. "This is a complex project, maybe it will be completed by December this year," Williams said, "although precise calculations cannot be made now, the fee for Benson's specimen will exceed £600."

Bridgefoot is willing to pay this price. He has already planned to place Benson's specimen in his fishing supplies store or café. Because "she is like my family, the most precious wealth of this fishery."

Even so, this savvy businessman doesn't think Benson is irreplaceable, "Now I have a fish that weighs 45 pounds, I think it might replace Benson's position." To create the next "superstar," he has already planned to capture all the big fish in Golden Fisher Lake before winter comes, to feed them collectively and ensure "they gain weight this winter."

Nevertheless, he will never forget what Benson brought to this fishery in the past 15 years. Sometimes, when he sits by the shore, he seems to see a magnificent carp leaping on the shimmering golden water surface, but in fact, it's just a ripple, from the last sunset.
