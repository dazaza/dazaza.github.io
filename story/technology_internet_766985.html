The Backstory of the First iPhone's Development

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 10:58:38

When the iPhone was introduced in 2007, its sleek design, revolutionary interface, and multi-touch functionality stunned the industry and ushered in a new era of smartphones. Due to Apple's strict confidentiality policies, the development process of the iPhone has remained largely unknown. Recently, Fastcompany website, while creating an "oral history of Apple design," interviewed former Apple executives and uncovered several stories from behind the scenes of the iPhone's development.

Apple's experimentation with multi-touch technology began in 2003, but at that time, the company had no plans to create a smartphone.

"We always wanted to put a PC into a tablet," said Doug Satzger, Apple's former head of industrial design creativity. "Duncan Karr (an industrial designer) had people sit down for hours talking non-stop about multi-touch. Wouldn't it be great if you could flip pages like in real life? Wouldn't it be great if you could zoom using some kind of gesture? A few weeks later, we were all captivated by the prototype Duncan and his team created. There was a screen on a table connected to a PC with a multi-touch layer on top."

In a sense, this was an iPad connected to a PC. However, in the eyes of the development team, it did not meet Apple's standards and was not the device people yearned for. "It was the iPad, but at the same time, it wasn't the iPad," said Jon Rubinstein, who once served as vice president of hardware. "It was cool, but not good enough. So these technologies were applied to the iPhone instead."

In 2005, Apple formed a small team to begin developing the iPhone project. Initially, however, Apple did not consider a touch screen. The team aimed to turn the iPod into a phone. "We started with the iPod Mini, trying to turn it into a phone," said Tony Fadell, a former executive of Apple's iPod division. "We really made a phone with a click wheel that operated similarly to a rotary dial phone."

The iPod-like phone was codenamed Purple, later shortened to P1. According to Andy Grignon, a former iPhone executive, Scott Forstall became involved in the project, wanting to port Mac OS onto a phone. At this time, Apple had just acquired Fingerworks, a company developing multi-touch software. Thus, the idea of a full-touchscreen phone was born. Forstall’s plan was called P2 and eventually gained Steve Jobs' approval.

Jony Ive’s team designed the hardware for the phone, while Forstall's software team tested the system on a simulator. They used the worst Macs available. "We knew there wouldn’t be actual hardware for a long time," said Nitin Ganatra, former iOS applications engineering lead. "We developed a simulator on the Power Mac G5 and tried our best to make it independent of the software running on Mac OS. When it ran, we said, 'Let's find the worst Mac,' because we knew running the simulator on a supercomputer processor would mask many performance issues."

The one they found was a Power Mac G3, the slowest device capable of running the latest version of OS X. Later, the Forstall team obtained the iPhone processor, which was a Freescale MX-31 development board connected to a modem, antenna, and display. It occupied half of Forstall's lab table. "When everything compiled and ran on the MX-31, that was our first moment of enlightenment," Ganatra said.

To ensure confidentiality, most people involved in P2, including the majority of Ive’s industrial design team, were unable to see the complete iPhone system. "We ended up creating two user interfaces," Grignon recalled. "One interface required Steve Jobs’ approval to view because it would blow your mind. The other interface was terrible; it allowed you to make calls and send messages, but it had ugly red buttons and input fields, known as Skankphone."

Even those granted special permission had to go through multiple procedures. "You had to sign separate legal documents, Jobs had to approve, then you went to Forstall, the true owner of the secret list," Grignon said. "He would tell you, 'Don't tell anyone. Don't tell your wife.'"

Insiders recount that this secrecy reached absurd levels. Ganatra said he had to act as a secret translator, running between different teams in different rooms. "I would go to the room with the complete interface and then come back to sketch it for other engineers," he said. "This went on for days. Eventually, Scott approached Jobs saying, 'It would be very helpful if the engineers implementing the interface could actually see it.'"

Afterwards, more engineers were allowed to see the complete interface, but not everyone received permission. According to Grignon's recollection, sometimes extreme measures had to be taken. One time, he had two engineers sit together to test code. One engineer was permitted to see the complete interface, while the other could only see the Skankphone interface. Therefore, a curtain was hung between them.
