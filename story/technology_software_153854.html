Landing on the Beach: Nine Principles for Choosing a CMS (2)

Misconception 2: Bigger is Better

Pay attention to any CMS, whether open-source or not, the size of a website is related to the system it uses. Don't be deceived by promises of large, powerful user modules, attractive extension functions, or system plugins. Because this is a trap.

It's precisely because too many people use CMS systems without optimizing them properly. For example, there are many users who stick with the Linux system, and I still don’t understand why people impose suffering on themselves. Simply put, choosing a CMS based on these misconceptions is a poor idea.

Another factor for users choosing a CMS is seeing a large number of developers and funding behind a particular CMS. Regarding these issues, I would say:

"I wish developing a great product was as simple as writing a check. If that were true, Microsoft would have a lot more products."

If you haven’t guessed already, let me tell you, this quote is from Steve Jobs. He used this statement to explain why being a giant isn’t always the best approach. What truly matters are the people behind the product, and these people must genuinely focus on developing the product rather than just existing within an enterprise.

When it comes to open-source CMS platforms, we often find that no one is actively developing the software, and it’s hard to find suitable individuals to develop products according to user needs. The main features come from the demands of ordinary members of society. As a result, while there may be many features, the real standout qualities get buried. So I’ll remind you again not to choose a CMS just because it has a lot of features.

Misconception 3: CMS Features Are Too Simple

Similarly, this is close to Misconception 2—using a very simple CMS system to build a large-scale website. Ensure that the CMS you choose includes all the necessary features you need. In many cases, you might find a CMS that looks good at first glance, but don't rush to buy it until you confirm it contains all the features you need. Unfortunately, some of the best CMS platforms are still in development.

Misconception 4: Not Doing Adequate Research

Before committing to a CMS, make sure you conduct thorough research. Look at web templates created using the CMS. Reach out to site administrators to learn about the CMS's strengths and weaknesses. Ideally, communicate with the CMS developers and keep an open mind.

You must invest a significant amount of time testing the system and trying to build a website with it. These steps must be completed before using the CMS. Sometimes you'll find extension modules, but upon testing, you discover they have serious flaws. By following the above methods to select a CMS, you'll find one that truly suits your needs.

Misconception 5: Letting an IT Person Choose the CMS

Never let an IT person choose the content management system you want to use at any time. They rarely understand both code and user-friendly interfaces simultaneously. Whether you're a large company or a small team, the priority is to stop considering any suggestions from IT personnel. This can be a headache, but ensuring that the CMS has a good interface is essential—it will save a lot of pain in the long run.
