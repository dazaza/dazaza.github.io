Beijing Fengyi Anus and Intestine Hospital: The key to preventing and treating hemorrhoids is to correct bad habits

by s1r35wh2 on 2012-03-05 11:23:45

Elderly people are prone to constipation, which increases the chances of developing hemorrhoids. Moreover, many elderly individuals already have existing hemorrhoids that were previously unnoticed but gradually worsened with age. This is mainly due to unhealthy lifestyle habits among seniors. Experts from Beijing Fengyi Anus and Intestine Hospital point out that prolonged reading while on the toilet, extended squatting during bowel movements, and long periods of sitting or standing without changing positions can exacerbate blood pooling and varicosity in the veins around the anus.

Hemorrhoids are a chronic condition characterized by the formation of venous masses in the lower rectal mucosa or anal skin edges due to blood pooling and varicosity. The upright posture of humans, combined with the lack of venous valves in the rectal and anal vessels, makes it easier for blood to pool. Irregular bowel habits, constipation, prolonged squatting, straining during defecation, consumption of spicy and stimulating foods like chili peppers, raw onions, raw garlic, excessive alcohol intake, irregular lifestyles, and prolonged sitting or standing without changing positions can all lead to venous varicosity, tortuosity, proliferation, and obstruction of venous return in the anal and rectal areas, slowing down blood circulation and forming hemorrhoids. The main symptoms include anal pain and prolapse of hemorrhoidal masses during defecation, bleeding during bowel movements, etc., directly affecting the patient's daily life, work, and rest. Long-term bleeding can also lead to secondary anemia.

Clinically, hemorrhoids can be divided into three types based on their location: internal hemorrhoids, external hemorrhoids, and mixed hemorrhoids. This classification is based on the dentate line (the junction between the rectal mucosa and anal skin, forming an irregular, saw-toothed line). Internal hemorrhoids occur above the dentate line, covered by mucosa, and are usually not visible. They may prolapse during defecation and sometimes retract spontaneously, but they often accompany bleeding. The bleeding manifests as blood-streaked stools, blood on toilet paper, dripping blood, or spurting blood. There may also be mucus secretion, pain, and itching around the anus. External hemorrhoids form below the dentate line, covered by skin, and are visible externally but cannot be pushed back into the anus. Patients with external hemorrhoids often also have internal hemorrhoids. Mixed hemorrhoids occur near the dentate line and possess characteristics of both internal and external hemorrhoids. Hemorrhoids can also be classified into physiological and pathological types. Physiological hemorrhoids, or normal anal cushions, do not require treatment unless they descend, become swollen with blood, prolapse, or bleed, at which point they become pathological hemorrhoids and require treatment.

Once hemorrhoids flare up, local medication y can be used, and if necessary, local injection of sclerosing agents, cryotherapy, laser therapy, or surgery can be considered. For cases of prolapsed hemorrhoids accompanied by edema and infection, non-surgical treatments are generally given first, such as maintaining smooth bowel movements, soaking in hot water after defecation, applying hemorrhoid ointment or suppositories locally, and taking antibiotics if necessary. Pain-relieving agents or analgesics can be given for severe pain. After the inflammation subsides, surgical treatment can be considered. If internal hemorrhoids bleed heavily, frequently prolapse, or become severely inflamed and painful, surgical removal should be considered. Regardless of the treatment method, reducing the recurrence of hemorrhoids after treatment depends on correcting bad habits. Maintaining good dietary and hygiene habits, avoiding spicy and stimulating foods, keeping bowel movements regular, correcting bad defecation habits (such as reading while on the toilet), frequently changing positions when sitting or standing for long periods, combining work with rest, promptly soaking in warm water and pushing prolapsed hemorrhoids back into the anus to prevent strangulation, and timely treating chronic intestinal diseases like diarrhea, dysentery, and enteritis are all important measures. Additionally, appropriate exercise to strengthen physical fitness is recommended.

Experts from Beijing Fengyi Anus and Intestine Hospital warn:

After hemorrhoid surgery, soaking in warm water is essential. Due to the special location of anal and intestinal surgeries, postoperative observations should include:

1. On the day of surgery, carefully observe for any bleeding. If bleeding occurs, immediate hemostatic measures should be taken.

2. Provide liquid diet for 3 days postoperatively, then gradually transition to semi-liquid, soft, and regular diets.

3. Start changing dressings on the second day after surgery. Soak in potassium permanganate solution for 15 minutes before sleeping and after defecation to reduce swelling and relieve pain. The solution concentration should be 1:1000, with a light pink color being ideal. Change dressings promptly after soaking.

4. For patients who have not had a bowel movement 3-4 days postoperatively, use mild laxatives. Observe the shape of the stool during defecation and check for signs of anal stenosis, performing anal dilation if necessary.
