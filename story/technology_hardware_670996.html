[Repost] Rules for Handling Office Interpersonal Relationships

by koucai7346 on 2012-02-21 14:21:20

When interacting with people, the most important thing is not how others treat you, but how you make others treat you. Do you understand the meaning of this sentence? What the public speaking training website wants to express is that if you want others to respect you, it's not about them, but about what you do. I heard elders say that it doesn't matter how others look at you, but how you look at yourself. In other words, the key lies in what you do. If you do well, others will naturally respect you, rather than forcing others to respect you, because such respect won't last long.

In a team, team communication is very important. For a leader, besides leadership skills and eloquence, personal charm is also an essential part of their qualities. I remember reading online a sentence about Jack Ma, chairman of Alibaba's board: "Ma doesn't understand technology, but he understands people." Do you know what this sentence means? And do you know how heavy this sentence weighs? To put it simply, this sentence means that Ma is a good talent scout who can use people wisely. On a deeper level, it means that Ma has strong personal charm and great appeal, which can tightly unite the people around him, so his team building is so powerful. Then, the public speaking training website would like to ask, how did he achieve this? You can all think about it. Today, I will not start from the aspect of leadership, but talk about the grassroots, namely the common office, on how we should act to build good interpersonal relationships and team communication.

1. Avoid complaining to everyone. Do you always unintentionally "share" your pain with others? If so, then I have to say here that if you continue to do this, you are in danger. Taking painful experiences as topics that are talked about again and again, without change, will inevitably make people avoid you. Forget the sad things of the past, focus on the hopeful future, and be a strong person in life. At this point, people will look at you with admiration rather than pity. Talking less about your painful things and more about your happy things is actually a very good way to improve your interpersonal relationships. People attract each other, meaning that good things are attracted by you, and bad things are also attracted by you. This is the famous "Law of Attraction."

2. Avoid forming small cliques and spreading rumors. The office can be said to be a kind of "jungle." Although what I just said might not be entirely correct, since I've said it, I'll finish it. In the jungle, there are always groups and factions. Actually, the cultural environment of many offices is very bad. They create small jungles with their own factions, which is extremely harmful to the unity of the team. In the office, it is strictly forbidden to form private cliques and small circles, which easily provoke opposition from those outside the circle. Even worse, spreading rumors inside and outside the circle and pretending to be well-informed will never get sincere treatment from others, but only make them try to avoid you.

3. Avoid being sycophantic or seeking favor through influential connections. One should be upright, honest, and straightforward, not having two faces before and behind people. Fully express yourself in front of the leader, be positive and proactive in handling affairs, and go all out for flattery; in front of colleagues or subordinates, be reluctant, indifferent, and wear a face of giving favors. In the long run, this situation is not good.
