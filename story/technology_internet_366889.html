Movie recommendation: "12 Monkeys"

by jimwang on 2010-10-04 05:06:04

The story of *12 Monkeys*, built on an incredibly simple framework of time travel, posits that the past and future are unchangeable. There’s no talk of parallel universes, wormholes, or butterfly effects—“thanks, Einstein.” Despite incorporating elements of apocalypse, viruses, suspense, and social critique, at its core, the film is more of a love story about memory, cycles, and time.

In truth, *12 Monkeys* has nothing to do with actual monkeys. Accompanied by eerie accordion music, the opening scene features a clockwise-spinning monkey motif that decreases in size as it moves outward, forming a clock-like dial. Due to differing relative speeds, the outer ring seems to rotate counterclockwise, symbolizing the future when moving forward and the past when reversing. Moreover, since a clock consists of 12 numbers, one cycle represents a complete loop, much like fate turning on this dial amidst uncertainties, ultimately leading back to the predestined "twelve into one," echoing the poster's tagline: "the future is history." This is the true meaning behind the number 12 in the film.

Anti-Consumerism and Animal Rights

Throughout the movie, the most impressive performance doesn't come from the lead actors Bruce Willis and Madeleine Stowe but rather from supporting actor Brad Pitt, who plays a deranged mental patient named Jebediah Goines. Through his character, Pitt delivers some of the most realistic and striking warnings, earning him an Oscar nomination for Best Supporting Actor.

On one hand, through Goines' words, the film conveys an anti-consumerist ideology: “The system is in the TV, TV commercials! We stop working, everything becomes automated, so what are we? Consumers! Buying stuff makes you a good citizen, not buying anything makes you... what? What? A lunatic!" Consumerism promotes "seize the day, enjoy life," and its advanced form might be materialism. Anti-consumerism isn't against consumption itself but opposes excessive, uncontrolled spending and short-sightedness focused solely on the present. Interestingly, for Cole, who has been physically and mentally drained by time travel and can no longer distinguish between the past, present, and future, even enjoying a fleeting moment of reality has become a luxurious dream. That's why he requests to return to the past again—he just wants to be with the one he loves and savor what he truly considers the present.

On the other hand, the so-called Army of the Twelve Monkeys in the film is merely a group of radical animal rights activists. The dim-witted Goines looks at TV footage of animal testing and mutters, “We're all monkeys,” revealing his stance. Advocating for animal rights is essentially an extension of natural human rights, sometimes referred to as "broadened human rights," implying that animals and perhaps other living beings should also have "rights." In Wang Jinkang's science fiction novel *Cross*, the protagonist fights for the survival "right" of the smallpox virus, arguing that biodiversity (including viruses) is sacred and humans must protect it at all costs. Another extension of human rights involves restrictions—if humans abuse their rights, then those rights should be limited. This idea appears in Robert Heinlein's classic sci-fi *Starship Troopers*, where citizenship rights are only granted after military service, though this theme was lost in the film adaptation *Starship Soldiers*.

Fulfilling the Past and Completing the Future

Through the simple concept of time travel, *12 Monkeys* raises a proposition: your actions upon returning to the past will not alter the future; instead, they make the already occurred future seem more justified. In fact, it is precisely because of traveling back in time that the future comes to pass. A popular explanation would be: when you attempt to return to the past to save someone who has already died, you discover that it was your actions in the past that caused their death.

In the movie, Cole mistakenly travels back to 1990 and spreads the rumor of a virus that will destroy humanity, meeting psychiatrist Dr. Railly and sparking her interest in apocalyptic prophecies. Then, due to another malfunction with the time machine, Cole and other volunteers are sent to the battlefield of World War I, where they spread the same rumor about a 1996 virus apocalypse. This becomes part of Dr. Railly's lecture materials and forms the basis of the real poisoner's doctrine. By 1996, because of a boy's prank involving a World War I antique bullet, Dr. Railly begins to believe Cole's account, prompting her phone call to a virologist and alerting the poisoner. Her later graffiti on the wall and voicemail to the carpet cleaner become critical clues for the underground humans studying the virus. Therefore, the verdict on the twelve-monkey virus is debunked—it was merely a group of insane animal rights activists.

Everything happens because of a series of actions taken in the past, making the progression of the past more coherent and the occurrence of the future more certain. The protagonist cannot change the past or future but instead fills out the past's narrative and solidifies the inevitability of the future. All uncertainties eventually converge into a predetermined destiny, and any attempt to change the past or future is seen as "monkey business," mocked by the monkeys on the dial. However, science does not believe in destiny, hence some critics argue that *12 Monkeys* is not science fiction but anti-science fiction.

You Dreamt of Me, I'll Remember You

However, the film's true message is not about various ideologies or theories nor humanity's insignificance in the river of time; it is simply a love story about destiny. A man considered a madman falls in love with a beautiful and kind female psychologist. Though seemingly irrational and extreme, it feels more like something fated and unavoidable. Such a sense of destiny is often called love at first sight. When Dr. Railly first encounters the convulsing Cole, she remarks, “I've seen you somewhere before.” Gradually, Cole realizes that the recurring dreams featuring a woman seem to depict none other than Dr. Railly. Many people experience similar déjà vu moments, believing in karmic connections from past lives. As the climax approaches, with the sound of gunfire, Cole's recurring nightmare becomes reality, and this reality turns into young Cole's recurring nightmare. The one who dies is none other than himself. He forgets that history cannot be changed and underestimates his own insignificance, collapsing right there. Amidst despair, Dr. Railly's meaningful smile directed at young Cole in the crowd reveals a glimmer of hope, for she knows they will meet again. She knows: "You once (will) dreamt of me, you'll meet me… I'll lose you, I'll remember you…" Birth and death, memory and reincarnation, all merge into the endless flow of love woven by time, mysterious yet destined.

There's also a scene where the two watch a movie in a cinema, which is quite symbolic. Hitchcock's classic suspense film *Vertigo* elevates the theme of *12 Monkeys*. The dialogue and character lines resonate deeply. Watching movies, Cole says, “Movies never change, but how you feel watching them does, because people change…” Three years ago, when I first watched *12 Monkeys*, I was stunned by its bizarre theory of time travel. Three years later, watching it again, I am moved by its extreme love story. Good films always bring new feelings; you never think of them as disposable entertainment.

The camera focuses on young Cole's eyes, echoing the beginning of the film—a cycle completes itself. Eyes in science fiction always carry profound meanings. Through them, do we see through the past or foresee the future? As the credits roll, the music Cole likes plays, Armstrong's hoarse and desperate voice singing “What a Wonderful World.” Are you moved again? This is the charm of movies, the charm of music, making our lives more colorful.
