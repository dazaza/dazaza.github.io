Four major factors push up CPI

by splinta24 on 2010-05-14 10:30:05

Currently, China's agricultural production has been affected by disasters, and the international commodity prices are rising under the accelerated depreciation of the US dollar. The inflation pressure will remain high. Considering the further decline in export growth, it is unlikely that the government will take a one-time appreciation measure. Therefore, coping with inflationary pressures cannot rely on a single policy but will continue to adopt a combination of tight monetary policies and loose fiscal policies. Administrative price controls will continue, subsidies for agricultural production and urban low-income groups will be increased, and the RMB will also appreciate faster. Given the current high inflationary pressures and the large amount of central bank bills and treasury repurchase agreements maturing in March, the central bank may raise interest rates and increase the statutory deposit reserve ratio this month. Inflationary pressures will persist for some time, and no single tool can be effective. The government will continue to use a combination of policies: Can the government quickly curb the rise in prices? This depends on which factors driving the rise in prices are controllable by the government. The current inflationary pressures mainly come from four aspects:

1. Short-term snow disaster factors: The snow disaster not only caused transportation disruptions and material shortages but also had a significant impact on agricultural production, which may increase this year's food inflation pressure. Historically, China's agricultural product price increases have always been caused by supply shocks, with demand growth remaining very stable, but supply fluctuations being relatively large. Once there is a reduction in production, it will cause price increases.

2. Factors of the US dollar depreciation pushing up international commodity prices: Since 2007, this round of inflation has been global, fundamentally due to the US dollar depreciation exporting inflation globally. This round of commodity and agricultural product inflation began completely synchronously with the US dollar depreciation. Over the past five years, the nominal effective exchange rate of the US dollar has depreciated by 35%, while during the same period, the CRB price index reflecting international commodity prices rose by 100% (including energy) and 118% (excluding energy), and the S&P Goldman Sachs Agricultural Index reflecting global agricultural product/food prices rose by 152%. The inflation pressure brought by the US dollar depreciation is most evident in emerging markets. All major developing countries, including China, have seen their recent inflation pressures continuously rise. This is because the Engel coefficient of emerging markets and developing countries is relatively high (a large portion of consumption expenditure is on food), and the weight of food in their CPI is relatively high. Moreover, emerging markets generally have considerable construction investment demands. Therefore, the inflationary pressures brought by agricultural product inflation and the rise in international raw material prices significantly outweigh the impact on developed countries. Before the US dollar rebounds, this imported inflationary pressure will not disappear.

3. Cost-push inflationary pressures brought about by the rise in factor prices such as labor, land, and resources in China itself.

4. The lack of flexibility in the RMB exchange rate has become the greatest constraint on monetary policy, causing a sustained expansion of money supply and liquidity surplus for many years.

Among the above four factors, the government can only control the exchange rate and factor prices but cannot control the impact of snow disasters on agricultural production or the input-type inflationary pressures brought by the US dollar depreciation. Therefore, inflationary pressures will remain high for some time. Currently, China's agricultural production has been affected by disasters, and the international commodity prices are soaring under the accelerated depreciation of the US dollar. The inflationary pressures will remain high. Given the further decline in export growth, it is unlikely that the government will take a one-time appreciation measure. Administrative price controls will continue, subsidies for agricultural production and urban low-income groups will be increased, and the RMB will also appreciate faster. In view of the current high inflationary pressures and the large amount of central bank bills and treasury repurchase agreements maturing in March, the central bank may raise interest rates and increase the statutory deposit reserve ratio this month.
