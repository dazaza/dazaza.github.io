Microsoft Hints Touchscreen Office Apps Are Coming To Non-Microsoft Platforms

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 20:23:38

Microsoft admitted earlier this year that it was developing a touch-enabled version of Office. At the Financial Analyst Meeting (FAM) held on Thursday, Microsoft officially confirmed that the touch-based Office would also be available on non-Microsoft platforms.

The core applications of the touch-enabled Office include Word, Excel, PowerPoint, and OneNote, and the product has been nicknamed Gemini. Microsoft officials stated that Gemini will launch on the Windows platform at some point in 2014.

At the FAM conference, Qi Lu, Executive Vice President of Microsoft's newly formed Applications and Services Engineering department, hinted that Microsoft would release more touch-based Office software for non-Microsoft platforms. "We are developing touch versions of the core Office applications," Lu said at the conference, "We will bring these applications to Windows devices, and at the appropriate time, we will bring them to other devices in a way that aligns with Microsoft's economic interests while meeting customer needs and values."

What Lu referred to as "other devices" might be non-Windows devices. Lu mentioned that Microsoft would consider multiple factors when deciding which applications to roll out to which devices and when. He stated that Microsoft's evaluation criteria include customer benefits, the feasibility of providing a high-quality user experience, and economic interests.

Currently, OneNote and Lync are already available on iPad and Android devices, and Skype and SkyDrive.com can run on Android and iOS platforms. Additionally, the Office Mobile version derived from Office 365 can run on iPhones. The web versions of the core Office applications, namely Office Web Apps, can also run on multiple browsers and platforms, including iPads and Android tablets. There were previous reports indicating that Microsoft planned to launch an iPad version of Office in the fall of 2014, but at the FAM conference, Lu did not disclose a specific timeline.

Regarding Office software, Microsoft's Chief Operating Officer Kevin Turner stated at the FAM conference on Thursday that the cloud-based Office 365 software is expected to generate annual sales of $1.5 billion. In April of this year, Microsoft had estimated that the software’s annual sales would be approximately $1 billion.

Microsoft claims that the transition from software licensing to cloud services is going smoothly, with Office 365 serving as a prime example. Like other software companies, Microsoft also hopes to transition from software licensing to a continuous revenue-generating and subscription-based cloud model while maintaining sales growth during the process. Adobe managed this transition quite smoothly, and Oracle and SAP have also adopted a shift towards cloud-based models.
