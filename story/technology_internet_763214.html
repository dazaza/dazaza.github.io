Mobile QQ will launch a game center - racing against WeChat with quick and small steps

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 17:13:23

By He Feng

CJ was decked out in festive lights, a grand feast of showgirls flaunting their charms and exchanging flirtatious glances, leaving the mobile gaming industry in a frenzy. But the biggest wildcard — Mobile QQ and WeChat — didn’t join the party, instead postponing their big moves.

I learned from inside Tencent that Mobile QQ is set to add a game center in its next version (4.2.1), originally planned for launch on Monday, July 22nd, but has now been postponed again. The WeChat Game Center continues to keep everyone guessing. The delay is not only due to further optimization of the product but also because Pony Ma requested that Mobile QQ, WeChat, and the Interactive Entertainment Division align the timing of their public announcements regarding game information.

Back to the focus of this article, I’d like to share with you the screenshots of the new version of Mobile QQ that I saw last week. Here are some key points:

1. The Mobile QQ Game Center was initially named “Game Circle,” but it was renamed to Game Center last Friday. I understand this decision, as “Game Circle” could be easily mistaken for something similar to WeChat’s Moments, which would hinder product recognition. The QQ Game Center is similar to Line and KakaoTalk’s game centers, where games are directly installed via plugins in the “Updates” tab. This makes it different from channel-based game platforms like 91 Game Center or DangLe Game Center, which require separate installations. Although the Mobile QQ Game Center doesn’t require an additional standalone client, the games themselves still need to be downloaded.

2. QQ’s social relationship chain is the biggest selling point of the Game Center. Every game on the platform deeply integrates QQ’s friend relationship chain. For example, leaderboards display rankings of friends playing the game, ensuring no strangers appear on the list. This feature is absent in channel-based game platforms and marks a significant difference. Sharing with QQ friends or posting updates to your space are standard features and don’t need further elaboration.

3. The first game to be operated by the QQ Game Center will be "Everyday Love Elimination" rather than "Everyday Chain Hero." This change might be related to the fact that "Everyday Chain Hero," after being launched exclusively on Yyb, did not become a hit. However, promoting self-developed casual games remains a fixed plan for both the QQ Game Center and WeChat Game Center.

4. High recommendation weight. In the iOS version, the Game Center and friend updates have equal weight. In the Android version, the Game Center, friend updates, and app center all have equal weight, forming a "community + distribution + monetization" model. The recommendation weight of the Game Center is higher than features like "Nearby People" and "Scan." The reason why the App Center isn't pre-installed in the iOS version may be to avoid competition with the App Store and thus avoid the risk of being taken down.

A brief analysis:

1. Unlike WeChat's long-delayed release of version 5.0, Mobile QQ is catching up to WeChat through small-version updates, taking "small steps fast."

The Android version of Mobile QQ 4.2 was released on July 3rd, followed by the iPhone version 10 days later. If calculated according to the original release date of July 22nd for the next version, it would only be about half a month apart. Meanwhile, WeChat 5.0 has had us waiting for nearly four months. The newly added colorful bubbles and emoticons in version 4.2 are also features expected to be included in WeChat 5.0. According to insiders, Mobile QQ will maintain this rapid update rhythm going forward.

Here’s a bit of gossip. While outsiders say that WeChat is Tencent's ticket, colleagues at Mobile QQ often joke privately, "Ticket? You’ve got to hold onto it!" You get it, right?

2. If membership services were left by our forefathers, if colorful bubbles and emoticons were just a trial run, then the Game Center undoubtedly marks the start of comprehensive monetization.

Previously, my colleague Lin Fenglei found out that the KPI for WeChat games' revenue this year is 1 billion yuan. From what I've learned, Mobile QQ hasn't set a revenue target for its Game Center. However, in my opinion, with a larger user base, more novice users, and better understanding of payment models, Mobile QQ's game community will have greater money-making potential than WeChat's Game Center.

3. While WeChat and Mobile QQ compete fiercely, they are also forced to collaborate. The QQ Game Center and WeChat Game Center will become the "two poles" of Tencent's mobile gaming platform, inevitably competing. This is something Pony Ma wants to see and can be considered part of Tencent's DNA, though it’s not left unchecked. I’ve learned that Pony Ma has already required alignment of information between Mobile QQ, WeChat, and the Interactive Entertainment Division. There are already signs of this. For example, the postponement of the next version of Mobile QQ.

4. Redefining the game joint-operation platform landscape. Baidu’s acquisition of 91 Wireless isn’t the end of changes in the game joint-operation platform structure; WeChat Game Center + QQ Game Center is the ultimate boss. This symbolizes that game joint-operation platforms will enter a dual structure of distribution channels + super apps. Distribution channels are entering a reshuffling phase, with traffic flowing towards oligarchs, while super apps are entering a period of traffic monetization, showing more vitality.
