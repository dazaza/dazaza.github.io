Hong Kong Company FAQs

Q: I want to set up a limited company in Hong Kong. What are the requirements?

A: One or more shareholders aged 18 years or older. Citizens from mainland China or overseas with a passport or ID card are eligible. A registered address and a statutory secretary for the limited company (usually provided by us) are required.

Q: How do I go about setting up a Hong Kong company?

A: You just need to fill out a power of attorney form. The form includes three aspects:

First, you need to confirm the Chinese and English names of the company.

Second, you need to determine the amount of registered capital. The minimum registered capital is HKD 10,000 (usually divided into 10,000 shares). It does not have to be fully paid, but it can also be increased according to actual needs.

Third, you need to submit the shareholder's information and a copy of their ID card. If there are more than two shareholders, not every shareholder has to become a director, but there must be at least one director. After we receive the above three aspects of information from you, we will prepare the complete legal documents for your signature on the next day. After signing the documents, the process will be completed within approximately 10 working days.

Q: How long does it take to establish a new company? How long does it take if I buy an existing shell company? Can I change the name after purchasing an existing company? How long does it take to change the name?

A: Establishing a new Hong Kong company takes 10 working days after all legal documents are signed.

Purchasing an existing company takes 1-2 working days, and expedited service can be completed on the same day.

Setting up a branch office takes 3 working days.

After purchasing a company, you can change its name, which requires 15 working days.

Q: What is the difference between a shareholder and a director?

A: A shareholder is an investor in a joint-stock company, while a director is elected by the shareholders and is responsible for managing the company. The chairman of the board is the head of the board of directors. More often than not, shareholders are also directors.

Q: What are the differences between naming a Hong Kong company and a mainland company?

A: Naming a Hong Kong company is relatively free. Some companies that are difficult to register on the mainland can be registered in Hong Kong as long as they are not duplicated. Regardless of the size of the registered capital, the Hong Kong government allows company names to include words such as international, group, holding, industry, investment, college, association, center, foundation, institute, etc. The company name can also add a favorite place name in front, such as France, USA, Italy, Japan, Hong Kong, Shanghai, etc., or use your own name and hometown name before the company name, fully reflecting personalization.

Q: Are there any restrictions on the business scope of a Hong Kong limited company?

A: Hong Kong is one of the freest trading ports in the world. In principle, there are no major restrictions on the business scope. Companies can engage in any type of business, such as finance and investment, healthcare, shipping, import and export trade, real estate, construction, decoration, information networks, clothing textiles, tourism, cultural publishing, etc. Many small and medium-sized enterprise owners do not specify the business scope so that they are not limited to a particular area.

Q: Where is the registered capital of a Hong Kong company reflected? Does the registered capital need to be paid in full?

A: The minimum registered capital for a Hong Kong limited liability company is HKD 10,000, and it does not need to be fully paid (no capital verification). There is no registered capital column on the company certificate; the registered capital is only reflected in the company's articles of association. If the client wants to prove their registered capital, showing the articles of association is sufficient. We can also provide a letter of creditworthiness issued by our accountant (CERTIFIED TRUE COPIES).

Q: What services are included in setting up a Hong Kong limited company?

A: Setting up a Hong Kong limited company includes the following services:

1. Government certificate fees (company registration certificate, business registration certificate)

2. Printed materials (18 copies of the articles of association, 1 share certificate book, 1 shareholder register, 1 set of application documents)

3. Stamp fees (1 signature stamp, 1 steel seal, 1 amendment seal)

4. Annual registered address fees

5. Annual statutory secretary service fees for the limited company

6. Miscellaneous fees (document fees, courier fees, travel expenses)

7. Professional service fees for accountants

Q: How should we pay after confirming the establishment of an offshore company?

A: Payment methods: You can pay in cash, by transfer, telegraphic transfer, check, or promissory note to our designated Hong Kong or mainland account. Cash payment locations include Hong Kong, LA, KUALA LUMPUR, Shenzhen, Guangzhou, Chengdu, Yiwu, etc. Overseas individuals can remit funds to the Hong Kong account; mainland China can remit funds to the Shenzhen account. Account details are available upon request.

Payment method: After the client fills out the power of attorney, 70% of the fee must be prepaid, and the remaining 30% is due when the company is established. The full registration capital stamp duty at 1/1000 is prepaid. The payment method for agents and clients is the same.

Q: After establishing a Hong Kong company, what annual fees are required?

A: Two types of annual fees are required: annual review and annual report, and accounting and auditing.

1. Tax Bureau annual review and company registration office annual report fees are mandatory annual fees. Prices may vary with government adjustments and generally fluctuate between HKD 3,000 and HKD 5,000. Even if the company has no business activities, these fees must still be paid.

2. Regarding accounting and auditing, if the company begins operations and generates business, it must conduct accounting and auditing. If the company has no business activity, it does not need to conduct accounting and auditing. However, tax reporting is mandatory.

Q: After establishing a Hong Kong company, who is responsible for the annual accounting and auditing? If the accounting is handled by you, what documents are required? Specific charge standards? How long does it take?

A: Accounting can be done by the company itself or entrusted to a Hong Kong accounting firm. However, auditing must be entrusted to an accountant or auditor. If handled by us, you must submit all records of business transactions: ledgers, invoices, receipts, payment vouchers, bank statements, accounts receivable, accounts payable. Generally speaking, bookkeeping takes about a month, and auditing takes 1-2 months.

Q: What are the differences between the statutory secretary services and business secretary services you provide?

A: According to government regulations, a limited company must have a statutory secretary, who can be a local individual or a corporation in Hong Kong. Generally, we can arrange a statutory secretary for you. A statutory secretary is a formal secretary required by the Hong Kong government, facilitating government contact and handling official correspondence.

A business secretary provides tailored business practical services based on customer needs, including answering calls, transmitting messages, receiving and sending mail, letters of credit, logistics services, etc. The advantage of a business secretary is that it helps build your corporate image, equivalent to having your own employees and office in Hong Kong with a full suite of company resources. This makes conducting business smooth and efficient without missing any deals.

Q: Our company has no office or employees in Hong Kong. How do we manage the company after establishing it?

A: Our business secretary service is specifically designed for this purpose. For more details, please refer to the business secretary service details.

Q: How does Hong Kong tax businesses?

A: Hong Kong is a free port with few taxes and low rates. The only tax a business must pay annually is profits tax, which is calculated at 17.5% of the company's profit. For example, if a company has an annual turnover of HKD 10 million, gross profit of HKD 2 million, and net profit of HKD 1 million, the profits tax payable to the government would be HKD 1 million x 17.5% = HKD 175,000. If the company does not make a profit, it does not need to pay taxes.

Q: Are products entering or leaving Hong Kong taxed?

A: Except for tobacco and alcohol, goods generally entering or leaving Hong Kong are not taxed. However, all imported and exported goods must be declared, and customs duties are charged at 0.5% of the actual value of the goods (up to HKD 47,000) and 0.3% (over HKD 47,000).

Q: Are there any taxes to pay when setting up a company?

A: Yes. When setting up a Hong Kong company, the registration capital stamp duty must be paid. The stamp duty is calculated at 1/1000 of the registered capital. For example, if the registered capital is HKD 1 million, the tax payable is HKD 1,000, which is paid once to the government during registration and does not need to be paid again.

Q: Which department issues the invoices and receipts for Hong Kong companies?

A: Invoices and receipts for Hong Kong companies are not printed uniformly by the tax authority but are printed and issued by the company's board of directors based on the nature of the business. Invoices, receipts, and other documents become effective after being stamped with the company seal and signed by the responsible person.

Q: Can all shareholders of my Hong Kong company be residents of mainland China if the purpose is to apply for a joint venture or wholly foreign-owned enterprise in the mainland?

A: Generally, a Hong Kong company can consist entirely of shareholders from mainland China. However, if the purpose is to set up a joint venture or wholly foreign-owned enterprise, the Ministry of Foreign Trade and Economic Cooperation usually invites at least one overseas shareholder to sign the documents. Therefore, arranging for an overseas shareholder to join the company is necessary.

Q: Can our newly established Hong Kong company directly apply for a joint venture or wholly foreign-owned enterprise in mainland China?

A: Generally, foreign investors coming to China to invest must have the foreign enterprise established for more than one year. Specific conditions depend on local policies.

Q: If I register a company in Hong Kong, can I set up the company account in a foreign-funded bank in mainland China, such as a branch of a Hong Kong bank in Shenzhen?

A: Yes. After the company is established, the directors can use their mainland ID cards to open an offshore account at a foreign-funded bank in mainland China. However, the directors must personally visit the bank to open the account.

Q: How are various daily tasks, such as tax reporting, handled for Hong Kong companies? Can you handle them on our behalf? What are the annual fees?

A: In Hong Kong, taxes are reported once a year. The specific charges depend on your company's actual situation. If you choose our business secretary service, tax reporting is free of additional charges. However, accounting and auditing must be paid for, and the costs depend on the turnover and the size of the invoices. If the company has no business activities, there are no related costs.

Q: How is the tax rate for Hong Kong companies calculated? What is the specific deadline for tax reporting?

A: Hong Kong's tax rate is calculated at 17.5% of actual profits. Hong Kong companies typically use March 31st each year as the fiscal year-end date, and you can file taxes up to one year later.

Q: What do the terms CORP and BODY CORPORATE mean in the business nature section of the Business Registration Certificate?

A: In the business nature section, CORP means there is no limitation on a specific business. Any business allowed under the Companies Ordinance can be operated. Of course, you can also specify electronic, chemical, or trade as the business scope in this section. BODY CORPORATE refers to a corporate entity and represents the legal status.

Q: If I need to rent an office, how should I proceed?

A: Based on different customer requirements, we offer company incubation, desk rental, office rental, and building rental. Just tell us your needs, and we will immediately find suitable office space for you.

Q: Can I query the background information of a Hong Kong limited company online?

A: Currently, it is not possible. Anyone with questions about a company can visit the Companies Registry in Hong Kong's Admiralty district for inquiries.

Q: After setting up a publishing house, how can I legally publish books?

A: After setting up a publishing house, you already have the qualifications to handle publishing matters. However, there are two tasks you need to complete: first, apply for an International Standard Book Number (ISBN) or International Standard Serial Number (ISSN). Second, apply for publication registration before publishing. After completing these two tasks, you can legally publish books. If you want to distribute in mainland China, you can apply to the relevant authorities with the qualifications of a Hong Kong publishing house.

Q: What do ISBN and ISSN mean? What are the differences between ISBN and ISSN?

A: ISBN stands for International Standard Book Number, and ISSN stands for International Standard Serial Number. Having these two numbers indicates that our publications have reached international standards and have been registered with the ISBN and ISSN international organizations. You can always query the background information of the publication through this number. The fundamental difference between ISBN and ISSN lies in the objects they are issued to. The former is issued to book publishers, while the latter is issued to magazine publishers.

Q: If business is not good, how much must I pay annually to suspend the company?

A: A Hong Kong company can apply for temporary suspension at any time. Apart from paying the annual report fee to the Companies Registry and the annual review fee to the Tax Department, no other fees are required. If the market improves, the company can resume operations at any time, saving many costs.

Q: Can my Hong Kong company be dissolved?

A: Yes. If your company is no longer in use and you do not cancel it, the Hong Kong government will assume that your company continues to exist and engages in illegal operations. The government will impose cumulative fines on illegally operating companies according to Hong Kong law, and serious cases may result in imprisonment of the board of directors. Since dissolving a company also incurs fees, we recommend selling the company as the best option.
