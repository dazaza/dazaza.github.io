The Legendary Story of the ZIPPO Lighter!

In 1932, an American named George Blaisdell saw a friend awkwardly using a cheap Austrian-made lighter to light a cigarette. To hide the embarrassing lighter, his friend shrugged and said: "It's practical!" Afterward, Blaisdell invented a simpler-designed lighter that was unaffected by air pressure or low temperatures. He named it Zippo, taking inspiration from the sound of another great invention at the time, the zipper (ZIPPER), naming it with the principle of "it works."

In 1936, the windproof wall design of the Zippo lighter successfully obtained a patent from the U.S. government, proving that Zippo is a high-tech lighter designed with precision. It redesigned a sleek rectangular casing connected by a hinge between the cover and the body, overcoming design difficulties by adding a perforated windproof wall around the flame core specifically designed for wind resistance. In the early 1940s, Zippo became a military supply for the U.S. army, and with the outbreak of World War II, American soldiers quickly fell in love with it, praising its instant ignition and excellent windproof performance. In fact, over the past 65 years, the appearance of Zippo has not changed much, and this patent has been developed almost perfectly. To better distinguish itself from ordinary lighters, the ZIPPO company conducted a scientific wind resistance test for Zippo in 1979. Using the Model 200 Zippo as the test subject, the results showed its wind resistance at 32 miles per hour, with a wind speed of 14.2 meters!

It was concluded that Zippo's wind resistance depends on four factors: the size and structure of the windproof wall, the number and size of the ventilation holes on the windproof wall of the Zippo lighter, the construction of the wick, and the boiling point of the specialized lighter fluid.
