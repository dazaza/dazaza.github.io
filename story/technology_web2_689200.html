A Movie Not Suitable for Elementary School Students - Reflections on "Confucius" by Chen Miao

The school organized a free outdoor screening of the movie "Confucius," with the stipulation that parents must accompany their children to watch it. Since I had some work unfinished that night, I could only let my wife go with our child. After watching the movie, the teacher assigned homework: write a reflection on the movie. Thus, among us parents, the division of labor was tacitly understood: those who accompanied did not write, and those who didn't accompany wrote the article.

In order to complete the glorious task assigned by the teacher, over the course of several days, I watched this movie online in bits and pieces. Finally, my reflections on the movie can be frankly stated: this is not a film suitable for children to watch. The reasons are as follows:

Firstly, the dialogues in the movie are largely taken from classical texts such as "The Analects" and other Confucian classics, filled with classical Chinese phrases mixed with modern language, and full of literary allusions... This is not only like listening to heavenly scriptures for children, but also very headache-inducing for parents who generally dislike reading classical Chinese. If they cannot even understand the words, how are students supposed to watch this movie? From this perspective, it is clear that the filmmakers never intended to target children; this is entirely a movie aimed at adults, specifically those who are passionate about traditional Chinese learning. Therefore, making elementary school students watch it is akin to "advancing despite difficulties", commendable in spirit but questionable in effect.

Secondly, the mindset of the director while shooting this blockbuster is clearly very contradictory, and the direction of effort is chaotic, which directly leads to the consequence that the movie lacks a good perspective and narrative method, making it difficult to move the audience, including children. A blockbuster means big production, which can either be an art film crafted with painstaking effort or a commercial film with many highlights. However, "Confucius" seems to be stuck in a dilemma and ultimately leans towards being a commercial blockbuster. As such, inviting high-popularity, high-cost actors like Hong Kong's famous actor Chow Yun-fat and renowned actress Zhou Xun to boost box office sales becomes logical. Based on the commercial film mindset, the movie vividly portrays the noisy scenes of two armies confronting each other and warfare, hoping to create a visual and auditory shock typical of blockbusters. However, these heavily emphasized segments have little connection to portraying Confucius' character, instead having the effect of adding unnecessary embellishments. Confucius represents "civilization", not "military". Using "military" to portray him, even if there is a reasonable historical logic, is far off-topic in terms of Confucius, and can be classified as irrelevant. "Military" is at most a historical background; portraying the inner logical truth should be the focus of the film. If the director's mindset were more upright and decisive, then characters like Nanz子弹 played by Zhou Xun would not have been necessary. War and beauties are basic elements of many vulgar commercial films. Attempting to use them to "attach" to "Confucius" to attract attention reveals the director's inner weakness, ultimately leading to the intertwining of multiple demands in the film, deviating from the original intention – which was to create a Confucius who is endearing, respectable, and genuinely moving.

Thirdly, the portrayal of Confucius lacks credible plot designs. Given the demand for a commercial film, this is understandable. The focus is on surface-level excitement rather than reasonableness, which has already been discussed above. Many parts of the movie's storyline feel awkward, such as the death of Confucius' student Yan Hui. Familiar records indicate he starved to death, but the movie shows him falling into an ice hole while rescuing wooden slips and books. This is a completely contrived story design, whose motivation directly reminds one of the image of revolutionary heroism during the era of "high, large, and complete". In summary, many of the plot designs surrounding Confucius give viewers the impression of artificiality and unreliability. This reflects the director's average overall quality and insufficient control. Based on my personal viewing experience, in Chinese cinema, well-made and profound films are often adapted from excellent novels. Movies whose scripts are directly "randomly fabricated" by screenwriters are mostly unbearable to watch. So, if adults find it unbelievable, why bother showing it to children?

I see Han Han used extreme words to evaluate "Confucius." Although I don't fully agree, I still resonate with his comments. He said: "This movie 'Confucius', regardless of its filming significance, commercial profit, artistic pursuit, cinematic exploration, educational enlightenment, warning emotional impact, audio-visual shock, entertainment leisure, or historical recording, does not need to exist from any angle. It is a movie that can be completely erased." Although the words may be harsh, they hit the key points of this oddball.

However, from an educational perspective, allowing children to come into contact with and be influenced by traditional culture from a young age is a direction worth praising. Unfortunately, many things we do have few mistakes in the general direction, but when it comes to execution, they become incomprehensible. This is indeed something worth reflecting on after watching the movie. Whether our education system has been swayed by the commercial tactics of movie companies is a secondary matter.

Note: The child's school organized a free screening of the movie "Confucius," and the teacher required both the child and the parents to submit a reflection. This is the reflection submitted by the parent, while the child couldn't write one at all.
