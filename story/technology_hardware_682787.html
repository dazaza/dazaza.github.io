Relevant parties who wish to improve the social image of central state-owned enterprises (SOEs) should, on one hand, fully open up and promote public understanding of these SOEs; on the other hand, they should vigorously combat monopolies to prevent some monopolistic central SOEs from affecting the overall social image of all central SOEs. According to reports by The Beijing News, in order to promote public understanding of central SOEs, starting from February 27th, the State-owned Assets Supervision and Administration Commission (SASAC) organized more than 30 media outlets to visit central SOEs including China National Building Materials Group and Huadian Group. A responsible person from the SASAC news department stated that central SOEs have developed rapidly in recent years with high social attention, but many people's views on central SOEs are partial, or even distorted. Through such activities, it is hoped to promote public understanding of central SOEs and see whether they make money through monopolies. Frankly speaking, in recent years, the image of certain central SOEs in the minds of the public has indeed been poor. The direction of the SASAC organizing media visits to central SOEs for understanding is worth affirming and is necessary. To repair the social image of central SOEs and bridge the gap between central SOEs and the public, openness rather than conservatism is clearly needed, as well as active and positive communication instead of passively avoiding public scrutiny. However, it is still difficult to hastily conclude the effectiveness of the current communication method. No one denies that there are indeed many central SOEs like China National Building Materials that have succeeded in market competition, and there are also many central SOEs such as Huadian and other power companies that continue to generate electricity despite years of huge losses, truly being "living Lei Fengs" in the market economy era. Yet, a few or even a dozen central SOEs cannot represent all central SOEs. With such a large business scope for one central SOE, having media interview two central SOEs in a day probably makes it hard to delve deeply. Clearly, the media and the public need comprehensive, real, and objective understanding of central SOEs, not just a selected understanding. In this age of massive information, relying solely on a few exemplary central SOEs may not provide enough convincing information to the public. Can relevant parties take it a step further on this basis and allow all central SOEs to be open to the media and the public? Of course, besides letting central SOE executives meet with the public, the media should also be given greater space for interviews, to understand and listen to the true thoughts of frontline employees of central SOEs. Additionally, relevant parties should realize that the "prejudice" the public holds against central SOEs is not directed at all central SOEs. In fact, in the eyes of the public, there are two types of central SOEs: competitive central SOEs and monopolistic central SOEs. Competitive central SOEs are no different from private enterprises, such as China National Building Materials, CR Snow, and OCT, which operate in market competition without monopoly capital. Frankly speaking, the public does not resent this type of central SOE; public opinions mainly target some monopolistic central SOEs. These monopolistic central SOEs control energy, telecommunications, and other economic lifelines related to national livelihood. While enjoying monopoly benefits, they harm market fairness, distort price formation mechanisms, and easily come into conflict with public interests. If relevant parties wish to further promote reforms in central SOEs and improve their social image, it is not about blindly pursuing the expansion of central SOEs, but rather understanding public concerns, pinpointing focal points, and correctly distinguishing between the two types of central SOEs. While allowing the public to fully understand central SOEs, there should also be vigorous anti-monopoly efforts to promote market fairness. Only then can we hope to escape the paradox of central SOEs growing larger while their social image worsens.
