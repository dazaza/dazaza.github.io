Beggar's hands: what should you do?

by sz5151749 on 2009-12-07 15:28:43

This is a scene I experienced yesterday, and it lingers in my mind, unable to fade away.

Yesterday was a bright weekend. I hadn't been out for a walk on the streets for a long time, so after lunch, I tidied up and went out with a cheerful heart.

The midday sun was warm. I walked along the road, watching passersby, feeling very peaceful inside. However, when I reached a larger bus transfer station, my mood changed - I couldn't describe it.

As I passed by that bus stop, a middle-aged woman dressed plainly and looking haggard approached me. She said as soon as she got to me: "Young man, I haven't eaten anything for days. Can you give me some money to buy lunch?" I was surprised. I've encountered situations like this too many times. When I was younger, I would still give them whatever little I could afford. But now, although I know one should be kind-hearted and willing to help others, whether she really has no money for food or not, my silent resistance to her behavior has already been firmly established. I didn't say anything, just walked past her. At that very moment, I felt an abrupt sense of conflict within me. I was thinking: Is my behavior too inconsistent with being a cultured person? Shouldn't people have more compassion, especially those who have received higher education? The subsequent relaxation was barely satisfactory, but the scene at the bus stop kept lingering in my mind, unable to forget. Although I've experienced such things before, none of them left as deep an impression as this one.

The reason for my inner resistance towards beggars, I believe everyone understands. There are simply too many rumors about these kinds of people. Negative news makes it hard for me to trust their words because I can't determine if what they say is true. In fact, I'm afraid that my supposed kindness will be cruelly deceived by these people. As for real beggars, I don't mind giving them a little, even though my income isn't high. Even if I don't have five yuan, I can always spare one or two. But often, the pitiful person begging on the street turns around and shops confidently in the supermarket the next moment. Such examples do exist.

My hometown is in a rural area, a very poor one. The local belief is: to get rich, you must go out. So many people went to Shanghai, Jiangsu, Zhejiang. By the end of the year, when they returned, many had thousands or even ten thousand yuan, which was already a large sum in my hometown. Some worked as workshop operators in factories, some collected scraps from the streets, and some begged. An experienced fellow townsman told me: as long as you dress shabbily and look pitiful, you can earn dozens or even hundreds of yuan a day, sometimes even more. This is much more than doing a day's work back home for twenty or thirty yuan. I approve of those working in the factory, I admire those collecting scraps even more, but I look down on that fellow townsman.

Of course, all these happened five years ago, but it cannot be denied that this indeed exists, and I cannot determine whether it still exists now. Regarding those who beg, I don't know if they take it as their profession. For someone like me who only earns a thousand yuan a month, sometimes I really make less than they do.

In fact, I feel more anger towards those who beg using deceitful means: since you're physically strong, why not find a job within your capability? Why do you have to walk on the street, openly and shamelessly deceiving my sympathy? Your actions not only hurt my kindness but also harm those who truly lack food and clothing.

Nowadays, I am becoming increasingly numb to the beggars on the roadside. Many times, I ignore them and pass by without asking. I think many people are the same as me, right? Ignoring the beggars on the road, enclosing themselves in coldness. Especially when discussing these people with others, many will say: Don't believe them, they're definitely frauds.

Faced with the advice of the masses, faced with one example after another, when you encounter the outstretched hands of a beggar again, what should you do?
