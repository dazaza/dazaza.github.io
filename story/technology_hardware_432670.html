I. What causes fungal vaginitis?

Fungal vaginitis (monilial or mycotic vaginitis) is caused by fungal infection. Its incidence has surpassed that of trichomonal vaginitis. Medically, fungal infections are referred to as candida infections; therefore, fungal vaginitis is also called candida vaginitis. It is commonly seen in young girls, pregnant women, diabetic patients, and postmenopausal women who have been treated with high doses of estrogen.

The main causes often include: sexual contact transmission, contaminated clothing and utensils, unqualified sanitary napkins, sanitary paper, and panty liners.

There are many types of fungi (fungus), and the most common one in the human body belongs to the Candida albicans genus. 80-90% of vaginal infections are caused by Candida albicans, while the remaining cases are due to other species of candida and Torulopsis. Therefore, fungal vaginitis is essentially candida vaginitis or vaginal candidiasis (vaginal candidiasis). Candida albicans is ovoid in shape, with budding spores and pseudohyphae formed by cell germination elongation. Pseudohyphae connect with spores to form branches or chains. Candida is usually a saprophytic parasite that can live on normal human skin, mucous membranes, digestive tract, or other organs. It often exists in the vagina without symptoms. Among non-pregnant women with increased vaginal discharge, about 10%, and among pregnant women, about 30% have this fungus present in their vaginas. When the glycogen content in the vagina increases and acidity rises, or when the body's resistance decreases, it can become a cause of disease. Long-term use of broad-spectrum antibiotics and adrenal corticosteroids can greatly increase fungal infections. These two drugs can lead to dysbiosis within the body, altering the mutual restraint relationship between microorganisms in the vagina, thereby reducing the body's ability to fight infections. Additionally, vitamin deficiency (complex vitamin B), severe infectious diseases, and other consumptive diseases can all become favorable conditions for the proliferation of Candida albicans. During pregnancy, the glycogen content in vaginal epithelial cells increases, enhancing the acidity of the vagina. Moreover, pregnant women have lower renal glucose thresholds, often resulting in nutritional glycosuria, which elevates sugar content in the urine and promotes the growth and reproduction of Candida albicans.

II. How to treat fungal vaginitis?

Basic treatment

1. Altering the pH of the vagina, such as using alkaline drugs to rinse the vagina. Sodium bicarbonate solution (2-4%) can be used to rinse the vagina, thereby changing the living environment of fungi.

2. Drug treatment

(1) Apply compound nystatin cold cream locally, twice daily.

(2) Oral ketoconazole (ketoconazole) 400mg, twice daily, for 5 days.

(3) Trichomycin (trichomycin), see the treatment for trichomonal vaginitis. In addition, oral clotrimazole (clotrimazole) or chlorobenzyl imidazole 0.5~1g, three times daily, or local application of 1~5% ointment cream or liniment, three to four times daily, also yields good results.

(4) Wantong Xiaomiao suppository, vaginal administration, once daily, gynecological consultation, one tablet per time, three boxes per course, no side effects, does not recur.

(5) Local application of 1%~2% gentian violet solution is an age-old therapy, extremely effective, widely used, gynecological consultation, but it stains underwear (currently, the state has banned the use of gentian violet drugs because they have strong carcinogenic properties, so be cautious).

Pregnant women suffering from fungal vaginitis may recover after childbirth, but there is a risk of neonatal infection, so timely treatment is still necessary. Local drug application is preferable, methods are the same as above.
