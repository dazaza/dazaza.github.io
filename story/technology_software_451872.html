Many sales personnel consider the orders lost in sales negotiations to be a chronic problem. How to speculate on the psychology of customers and maximize the benefits? Let's take a look at an example below.

There was a mother who gave a neighbor's two children an orange. The two children then discussed how to divide this orange. They argued back and forth, and eventually reached an agreement: one child would be responsible for cutting the orange, while the other would choose their half. As a result, according to the agreed method, each child received half of the orange and happily took it home. The first child took the half orange home, peeled off the skin and threw it into the garbage can, putting the flesh into the juicer to make juice. The other child went home, dug out the flesh and threw it into the garbage can, keeping the orange peel, which they ground up and mixed with flour to bake a cake.

The result shows that the child who wanted the entire orange suggested that other issues could be negotiated together. He said, "If you give me the whole orange, you don't have to pay back the lollipop you owe me." In fact, his teeth were already damaged by sugar, so his parents did not let him eat candy last week. The other child thought about it for a moment and quickly agreed. He had just asked his parents for five yuan, planning to buy candy to repay the debt. This time, he could use the five yuan to play games and didn't care about the sour orange at all.

From the above situation, we can see that although the two children each got what seemed to be a fair half, the things they obtained were not fully utilized. This indicates that they did not communicate well at the time, meaning neither of the two children explained their respective interests. Not explaining the value and leading to blindly pursuing formal and postural fairness resulted in both parties' interests not being maximized in the negotiation. If we imagine that the two children fully exchanged their needs, there might be multiple plans and scenarios. One possible scenario is that the two children found a way to separate the peel from the flesh, one taking the flesh to drink juice and the other taking the peel to make cakes. However, after communication, another scenario might occur where one child wants both the peel to make a cake and the juice to drink. At this point, it becomes very important to create value.

The negotiation process between the two children is actually a continuous process of communication and creating value. Both sides are seeking solutions that maximize their own benefits while also satisfying the need for the other side to maximize their benefits.

The sales negotiation process is actually the same. On the basis of maximizing the benefits of both parties, if there are still obstacles to reaching an agreement, then it might be helpful to stand in the other party's position, think for them, and clear all obstacles to reaching an agreement. In this way, the final agreement should not be difficult to reach.

Good sales negotiators do not merely insist on tough stances or refuse to compromise. Instead, they exchange ideas fully with the other party, starting from the greatest benefit for both sides, creating various solutions, and using relatively small compromises to achieve the greatest benefit. The other party also seeks to adopt conditions through mutual exchanges.
