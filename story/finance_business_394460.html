In Shaji Town, Northern Jiangsu, with its population of 60,000, over 1,200 online shops have emerged, forming a massive group of web merchants. The farmers here have carved out a unique path to prosperity.

On August 20th, the *2009 First Half Online Shopping Market Development Report* showed that in just the first half of the year, Taobao alone had an online transaction volume of 80.9 billion yuan, nearly reaching the full-year total of 2008 at 99.96 billion yuan. With 140 million registered members, this number accounts for more than 40% of the country's internet users, a staggering figure.

Once again, the people of Guangdong, always ahead of the curve, contributed the most. In just six months, Guangdong consumers spent close to 10 billion yuan on online shopping, firmly securing their "leading position." Meanwhile, Guangdong also had nearly 20 million registered users on Taobao.

However, stepping into the rural areas of Northern Jiangsu, the penetration and vitality of e-commerce among the common folk is equally astonishing. In Xuzhou’s Shaji Town, with its population of 60,000, there are over 1,200 online shops. Traditionally far removed from the internet—let alone e-commerce—Chinese farmers are now typing away in mud houses, amidst barking dogs, crafting yet another miracle of the internet age.

On August 29th, Yang Yang, 23 years old, was fully focused on his computer, paying no attention to anything happening around him. He barely touched his lunch. Shipping forms were within easy reach for immediate filling out.

Yang Yang's work enthusiasm is remarkable. From 9:00 am to 12:00 midnight, he stays online waiting for business. No wonder, after only four months of running his online shop, his monthly income has already approached ten thousand yuan.

Dialog boxes kept popping up on the computer screen. Yang Yang typed letters with his two index fingers and used his left thumb for the space bar, typing astonishingly fast. Locals call this typing style the "three-finger Zen," which most have gradually mastered from the "one-finger Zen."

A computer, scattered shipping forms, simple furniture, red brick houses, black native dogs guarding the home—these stark visual contrasts form the most ordinary household in Dongfeng Village, Shaji Town, Xuzhou City, Jiangsu Province. Of course, the most important element is the villager bent over the computer—people are even hesitant to interrupt him for fear of affecting his business.

Replication

In a very short time, 10, 100, over a thousand online shops sprang up dramatically, Sun Han's success was replicated over 1,200 times.

Yang Yang is not satisfied with his current income; his role model is Sun Han, 27 years old, from the same village—the earliest person to open an online shop in the village, with an annual income counted in hundreds of thousands. Dongfeng Village was once known as the "waste village," primarily engaged in scrap collection. Now, more than 500 households sell simple furniture through over 400 online shops, and this transformation took only two years.

All this started with Sun Han and a few friends' entrepreneurial journey. In 2006, Sun Han quit his stable job at the local mobile company, giving up a monthly income of two or three thousand yuan, determined to start a business, spending all day tinkering with computers.

Sun Han's mother, Xia Cuixia, was traditionally involved in the curtain business and could not understand her son's obsession with the intangible internet, believing he was deceiving her. "Children playing with computers don't do proper work," Xia Cuixia thought. When Sun Han wanted to replace his computer but was short by several thousand yuan, Xia Cuixia refused outright. Later, "he patted my head and said, Mom, I'm using the computer to do business, doing proper work, I'm not deceiving you," Xia Cuixia recounted the scene at the time, her expression now filled with pride.

Unable to resist her son's pleading, the family added 3,000 yuan to buy a new computer. Sun Han then set to work in earnest. He registered an online shop on Taobao, selling small accessories, sourcing goods from elsewhere, scattering them all over the house. It was only then that Xia Cuixia finally confirmed that her son had indeed started a business. A few days later, Sun Han made his first sale, worth a dozen or so yuan.

Because of his fondness for IKEA's style, around May 2007, Sun Han decided to switch to selling assemblable simple furniture. There was a lack of carpenters, wood, and channels locally, everything had to be started from scratch.

It took a whole month to convert pictures into the first product. Fortunately, Sun Han succeeded. The product was warmly welcomed online, and the people around him were also inspired. Some came to ask about the secrets of opening an online shop, Sun Han taught them, knowing the principle of "collecting shops to form a market," let alone "there's not enough time to handle one's own business."

In a very short time, 10, 100, over a thousand online shops sprang up dramatically, Sun Han's success was replicated over 1,200 times—across the entire town with its population of over 60,000, more than 1,200 online shops were opened.

The attitude shifted from initial hesitation to a dramatic replication, and special products emerged in just two years for the farmers of Shaji Town.

Whether selling phones, clothes, shoes, hair salons, or agricultural machinery stores, 80% have computers and run online shops.

Similarly impressive scenes can be found dozens of kilometers away in Yandaxia Village, Yanji Town. The elders sitting in circles chatting under the shade are discussing topics related to computers. The traditional industry in Yanji Town is flower and tree production, and in Yandaxia Village, with its population of over 700 households, more than 200 online flower shops have been opened.

Jiang Puhui, 31 years old, still remembers the panic when she received her first online order. Last March, just 10 days after opening her online store, like usual, she was taking care of her small convenience store while looking after her child. Suddenly, the "big belly" computer placed in the corner rang, it turned out to be a customer from Zhejiang sending a message wanting to buy something.

"I panicked, I didn't even know how to send a link, so I asked him to teach me. Luckily, the customer was patient and kindly guided me step by step on what to do." Her first order amounted to over 300 yuan.

Business improved, and this year, her husband, who worked as a plumber in Shanghai, stopped working outside. Young people returned home one after another. Reflecting on the hardships of raising children and managing the store alone in recent years, Jiang Puhui now feels genuine happiness.

Every afternoon, white minivans would weave through the streets and alleys of Shaji Town to collect goods. Yang Yang would hand over all the accumulated orders and goods of the day to the courier service, destined for various places across the country and even overseas.

At the beginning, there were only one or two courier companies in the town. If delivering to the Jiang-Zhe-Shanghai region, the price for 15 kilograms or less was 10 yuan, now it has dropped to 6 yuan. More than a dozen courier companies compete fiercely, "painful" yet happy. Just EMS alone estimates daily revenue reaching 7,000 yuan. Previously ranked last in Jiangsu Province, EMS now ranks in the top three. Every year, the courier revenue in Shaji Town reaches several million yuan.

Wood that used to go unsold is now in high demand, and if unavailable, it must be purchased from Anhui, Hunan, Guangxi, and other places. Carpenters no longer need to go to Shanghai or Guangdong for decoration work. Now, even at 80 yuan per day, it's hard to find willing workers.

The simple furniture in Shaji Town requires customers to assemble themselves. Manufacturers only need to prepare wooden strips, boards, and screws. In the processing plants, people from nearby towns come to work, grinding wooden strips, packing, earning wages of 30 yuan per day.

Walking along the main road of the town, on both sides of the street, whether selling phones, clothes, shoes, hair salons, or agricultural machinery stores, 80% have computers and run Taobao shops, all selling wooden home furnishings.

Sun Han says that every product that comes out is immediately copied, even the promotional images on the webpage are instantly duplicated and circulated throughout the village. Taking pictures is interesting too. Some families run photography studios, and their pictures look better and are naturally more popular. Whichever family's furniture looks good becomes the perfect choice for neighbors to photograph. Even the children in the house are "borrowed" as models.

Shaji Town has over 1,200 online shops and 33 furniture processing plants. This means that most online shops do not have their own processing plants and merely act as "agents" for sales, sourcing goods from others. "For example, if this product is taken, sold for a profit of 10 yuan, the processing plant takes 7 yuan, and the seller gets 3 yuan."

In Yandaxia Village, the villagers have devised even more interesting methods. Wires hang among lush green trees, each pole carrying seven or eight large loudspeakers facing all directions. Originally, if any household runs out of stock, they just turn on their loudspeakers and broadcast throughout the village. During peak seasons, the loudspeaker sounds echo continuously.

Zhou Xiao is a barefoot doctor, running a clinic at home. Upon entering, people are always surprised to see three computers on the table. "Three computers, three Taobao shops, one Alibaba shop."

Zhou Xiao is proud. Every morning, he works as a doctor in the clinic, and starting from noon, he manages the online shops, receiving orders, packing, and shipping, truly becoming a practical web merchant... Dual roles, tradition and modernity, thus separated by the network.

Villagers all have their tricks for running online shops. Zhou Xiao is proficient in computers, "taking shortcuts" by buying software, allowing messages to be sent automatically every day, ensuring his online shop remains visible to customers from morning till night. When others ask, Zhou Xiao never holds back. For those not skilled at typing, they simply have common phrases pre-typed and saved, ready to copy directly when needed.

Prospects

As e-commerce rapidly gains popularity in the rural areas of Northern Jiangsu, it will also bring problems with local characteristics.

"First, sell at low prices to gain credit ratings, then promote the website." Yang Yang clearly understands the practical effects of credit and "attention economy," but this knowledge does not necessarily come from books, rather from oral and experiential transmission.

Friedman once predicted the performance of developing countries in the information age: "(Developing countries) will first use cheap labor, high technology, and highly creative ideas for reimagining the future to solve their own problems." At this moment, Chinese farmers are proving with actual actions the immense potential they possess.

Without the internet, without opening an online shop, Zhou Xiaolong should still be repairing cars in Changzhou, earning 700 yuan per month, paying rent, and spending all the remaining money. Now, at 21 years old, lying on a leather chair, his face slightly youthful, he strives to display the demeanor of a boss.

He is the "agent" for his parents, each doing their own business. He purchases flowers grown by his parents and sells them online, making a profit from the difference. By the end of August, his sales for the year have already reached sixty to seventy thousand yuan.

Unlike young people who strive to escape rural life for the city, Zhou Xiaolong does not mind being called a farmer. "Earned through one's own labor." His words reveal a calmness and composure beyond his years.

As e-commerce rapidly gains popularity in the rural areas of Northern Jiangsu, it will also bring problems with local characteristics. Excessive replication leads to homogenized competition, backward production methods reduce efficiency, and villagers sometimes engage in price wars, further reducing profits.

Fortunately, Sun Han has hired a designer, striving to create more original works, introducing more advanced equipment, and growing stronger.
