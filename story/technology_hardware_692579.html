Is it easy to pass the self-study examination for a bachelor's or associate degree? Civil Education Service Network

by sudaa4t5 on 2012-03-06 10:29:05

All you need to do is choose a school you like, at least with good faculty, good benefits, and no discrimination against self-candidates. Then choose a major you like, study hard, don't skip classes, don't leave early, read more books, and you can basically pass it once. If you skip class, read less books, not enough effort, as long as you review the key points before the exam, you can also pass it. If you fail, you just need to retake the exam and spend more energy. Whether it's a first-class, second-class or third-class university, self-study examination is actually the same thing. It's just that when you graduate, the seal on your diploma will be from which school. Of course, if it's a first-class or second-class university, it will look better when you take it out. All are unified national exams, there is no difference.

The difficulty of self-study examination depends on the major you apply for. I don't know whether you are inclined to liberal arts or science. If you're not good at math, it's best not to choose science and engineering. For liberal arts or English, it's best to apply for journalism, because this major is easier to pass, and there are fewer exam courses. There are about 14 courses for the journalism associate degree, no math, but there is English (it's really not difficult, not like the college entrance exam, but you have to finish studying the textbook. You can take the exam four times a year, in January, April, July, and October. You can take up to 4 courses each month. If everything goes smoothly, you can pass all the associate degree courses within a year and get the associate degree). Then you can apply for the journalism bachelor's degree. There are even fewer courses for the bachelor's degree, about 12 or 13, no math, with English, which is also not difficult. Similarly, with another year of effort, you can get your bachelor's degree.

That is to say, you only spent two years getting a bachelor's degree, at this point you can choose to look for a job, take postgraduate entrance examination, or civil service examination. I suggest candidates for self-study examination try to take the postgraduate entrance examination if possible. Although self-study examination is not difficult, it requires perseverance and confidence. It's really not difficult (I've said three times it's not difficult). As long as you stick to it, spend two years, and get your self-study bachelor's degree. (This situation applies to students who study at home or work while taking exams.)
