Golden-collar profession -- Simultaneous interpretation business market

by translatec on 2010-04-07 11:49:15

1. The highest earning "Simultaneous Interpreter" makes four to five thousand daily

In the "Full-time Employment Personnel Salary Guidance Position", among the 54 industries listed, simultaneous interpreters top the list with a price of up to 2000 RMB per hour.

In the simultaneous interpreter rate table, English category is priced between 12,000 to 21,000 RMB per day, and for other languages it's about 18,000 RMB per day. Generally, 2-3 interpreters form a simultaneous interpretation group to work together. The prices mentioned are for one group's daily translation cost. It's reported that even the lowest income for simultaneous interpreters is around 4000 RMB per day. The renowned international translation company in Nanjing charges almost double this amount. This shows the appeal of the industry. Many language learners consider simultaneous interpreting as the pinnacle of translation careers. A simultaneous interpreter's daily salary is equivalent to an ordinary person's monthly income, and they can easily earn four to five thousand in a day.

The daily remuneration for participating in a conference is around 5000 RMB, but some conferences offer even higher pay. During busy conference periods, like March to July and September to December, schedules often get fully booked, and some conferences require booking a month or more in advance. Excellent interpreters can make up to 40,000 RMB or more during peak conference months. Therefore, it's not uncommon for excellent interpreters to earn over 500,000 RMB annually.

Currently in Beijing, the remuneration for simultaneous interpreters is generally around 4000 RMB per day. This is the average income for a team of three people. If the interpreter works independently without assistance, the highest daily income can reach over 10,000 RMB. The time calculation follows an 8-hour workday, counting half a day for four hours and a full day for eight hours. Additionally, clients will cover the interpreters' accommodation, airfare, ground transportation, and other related expenses. Overall, the income is quite substantial.

There is no shortage of money, just a shortage of talent. Simultaneous interpreters are globally scarce talents and also among the hardest talents to cultivate. There are only about 2000 professional simultaneous interpreters worldwide, and China has an even greater shortage. According to incomplete statistics, there are only about 30 professional simultaneous interpreters in China. As for specialized simultaneous interpreters proficient in various fields such as economics, trade, science, technology, law, and politics, they remain non-existent.

With increasing exchanges between China and the world, and with China's rising international status, domestic and international conferences cannot proceed without Chinese interpreters. International conferences held in China are becoming increasingly frequent, making the market for simultaneous interpreters both domestically and internationally vast. It's said that the EU and Australia even invite Chinese trainers to teach simultaneous interpretation, indicating a promising future for the simultaneous interpretation market.

Although the demand for simultaneous interpreters is increasing exponentially, the number of qualified simultaneous interpreters is growing very slowly. Currently, most professional simultaneous interpreters are concentrated in cities like Nanjing, Shanghai, and Guangzhou. Other regions hosting international conferences often have to hire interpreters from Beijing and Shanghai.

Becoming a "simultaneous interpreter" is extremely difficult. Simultaneous interpreters earn high salaries because of their scarcity. The reason for their scarcity is the difficulty of the job. Becoming a simultaneous interpreter is incredibly challenging.

Entering the field of simultaneous interpretation requires a high threshold. Simply obtaining a master's degree, relevant training, or certification does not guarantee entry into the profession. Clients highly value the conference experience of interpreters. Fresh graduates without experience hardly have the qualifications to enter the simultaneous interpretation circle.

Besides having conference experience, entering the simultaneous interpretation industry requires good interpersonal skills.

Success is mandatory; mistakes are unacceptable. Currently, there is no fixed institution in China responsible for managing simultaneous interpretation-related affairs, nor is there a unified standard for evaluating the work of simultaneous interpreters. The quality of a simultaneous interpreter's performance is directly assessed by the parties involved in the conference. After the conference, feedback from the organizing unit, attendees, experts, Chinese delegation, and foreign delegations determines whether the interpreter is praised or criticized. Outstanding interpreters may be invited for further collaboration, while poor performance could lead to immediate negative reactions from the audience.

People perceive simultaneous interpreters as the elite at the top of the pyramid, attending international conferences and earning in a day what others earn in a month. However, entering this profession is extraordinary, and the pressure within the industry is immense. Here, failure is not an option.

Even small details matter during simultaneous interpretation. The environment inside the translation booth must be quiet, as the microphones are highly sensitive. Even the sound of flipping pages can cause significant noise to the audience, so interpreters must avoid unnecessary movements or collisions. Female interpreters are advised to avoid wearing large earrings to prevent unwanted sounds.

2. Simultaneous Interpretation: High Pay, Hard Work

Recently, viewers following the Iraq war coverage on TV would notice a non-anchor announcer. When overseas live scenes are broadcasted, these individuals wear headphones, focus intently on the screen, and quickly translate the content for eager audiences. Without them, many viewers who do not understand foreign languages might only see images without understanding the situation. These professionals are simultaneous interpreters, playing a crucial role in promoting communication between China and the world.

Simultaneous interpretation, also known as "simultaneous translation," abbreviated as "ST," is typically used in various languages during small-scale domestic meetings. Simultaneous interpreters usually work behind the scenes, ensuring that when someone speaks, the attendees hear the translation in their familiar language almost instantly.

In Guangzhou, the number of simultaneous interpreters is less than 30.

In recent years, with increasing exchanges between China and the international community, simultaneous interpretation has become an admired emerging profession internationally. China's accession to the WTO has further increased the demand for simultaneous interpreters. Currently, the number of professionals in this field is far from meeting market demands due to the rapid rise of China's economy, making Chinese an increasingly international language. International conferences held in China are becoming more frequent, highlighting the vast market potential for simultaneous interpreters both domestically and internationally. Many simultaneous interpreters now travel frequently between different cities to participate in various international conferences.

The lack of simultaneous interpreters in Guangzhou is surprising. Professor Huang Jianhua, chairman of the Guangdong Provincial Translators Association, stated that there is a severe shortage of simultaneous interpreters in Guangzhou, with fewer than 30 professionals in the entire city. Specialized interpreters in fields like economics, trade, science, technology, law, and politics are virtually non-existent, which does not match Guangzhou's status as a modern metropolis. Whether a conference has simultaneous interpretation has become a key indicator of its importance. In some major international conferences in Guangzhou, the municipal government had to seek support from the Ministry of Foreign Affairs for simultaneous interpreters.

Not only Guangzhou but also cities like Beijing and Shanghai face a shortage of simultaneous interpreters. However, Guangzhou has the fewest interpreters compared to these cities. Industry insiders analyze that the requirements for simultaneous interpreters are too high, and the entry threshold is also very high. Extensive practical experience is needed to perform well, so the number of qualified interpreters remains limited. Currently, the main talents active in Guangzhou's simultaneous interpretation market include former government employees who dealt with foreigners, such as those from foreign affairs units, and university graduates with foreign language backgrounds. Many have participated in the EU's simultaneous interpretation training program or have extensive overseas study backgrounds.

High Difficulty "Language Gold"

Simultaneous interpreters are highly valued because this is a job with high difficulty, high skill requirements, and high intensity, which few professionals can achieve. "Simultaneous interpretation timing is tight, almost no room for error, and feedback is immediate." Ma Xina, a graduate student in translation at Guangdong University of Foreign Studies with rich experience in English simultaneous interpretation, described the characteristics of the job. Ma said that the most challenging part of simultaneous interpretation is translating spontaneous questions from the audience, which means real-time interaction between the speaker and the audience. If the translation is inaccurate, it can lead to awkward situations where the answers do not match the questions. This requires interpreters to be highly familiar with the expression habits of the target language to ensure smooth communication. Not only must the interpreter have accurate language control, but the time requirement is also strict, even requiring a "lead time" of about two seconds ahead of the speaker's thoughts. That is, when hearing a sentence, the interpreter must immediately predict what the speaker will say next and translate it within two seconds. Ma emphasized that simultaneous interpretation requires quick thinking, fast reaction, and synchronization with the speaker's rhythm. The interpreter must "do two things at once," listening with the ears while speaking with the mouth, which is difficult for many ordinary translators.

In addition to responding on-site, the preparation work for simultaneous interpreters is also demanding. Ma introduced that even experienced interpreters need thorough preparation before each conference because the content varies. This requires interpreters to inquire about the theme and titles of the participants in advance and familiarize themselves with relevant Chinese expressions. She said that sometimes unexpected issues arise on-site because the speaker may deviate from the prepared script, requiring the interpreter to be detail-oriented, bold, and ready to speak without hesitation. Stopping mid-translation can lead to awkward situations, so the work requires high concentration and attention.

Rare Talent, Lucrative Income

Due to the scarcity of simultaneous interpreters, their income is quite substantial. Ma revealed that the income level for simultaneous interpreters abroad aligns with domestic standards, and they belong to the highest-paid category in the translation industry. She calculated that a regular day's simultaneous interpretation fee is generally 700 RMB, while simultaneous interpretation is 3900 RMB. For oral translation of a general conference, the fee is 1500 RMB, but for simultaneous interpretation, it is 4200 RMB. According to data, the current remuneration for simultaneous interpreters is generally 4000 RMB per day, and if working independently without assistance, it can reach over 10,000 RMB per day. Time is calculated based on an 8-hour workday, with four hours counted as half a day and four to eight hours counted as a full day. A certain translation center's quote for English simultaneous interpretation is 2500 RMB/hour without a manuscript, and 3500-4000 RMB/hour if handled by an experienced translator. If there is no manuscript provided, it is 4000 RMB/hour, and 8000 RMB/hour if handled by an experienced translator. Additionally, clients will cover the interpreters' accommodation, airfare, ground transportation, and all other related expenses. Although interpreters cannot have meetings every day throughout the year, having 100 working days annually is reasonable, making the income quite substantial. Such income levels can be described as "earning a fortune daily."

Hidden Qualification Certification for Simultaneous Interpreters

Nowadays, many people attend simultaneous interpretation training programs and obtain relevant qualification certificates hoping to enter this lucrative industry. It is understood that there are only dozens of senior simultaneous interpreters capable of handling domestic conferences nationwide, while Guangzhou alone needs hundreds for various international conferences. The foreign language simultaneous interpretation qualification certificate examination held in Shanghai was exceptionally popular.

On March 10, 2002, the simultaneous interpretation position qualification certificate examination jointly organized by the Shanghai Chinese Language Simultaneous Interpretation Examination Office attracted over ten thousand candidates. The exam included English, Japanese, and French sections, emphasizing linguistic transformation abilities. This certificate is currently the highest-level interpreting certificate internationally, thus highly valued by employers. Over the past seven years, more than 60,000 people have participated in the "Shanghai Chinese Language Simultaneous Interpretation Position Qualification Certificate" examination, but fewer than 6,000 have obtained the certificate.

Does obtaining a senior simultaneous interpretation qualification certificate mean one can become a simultaneous interpreter? Zhang Chenying from the Cultural and Educational Section of the British Consulate-General in Guangzhou told reporters that having a senior simultaneous interpretation qualification certificate does not necessarily mean one can handle simultaneous interpretation. Simultaneous interpretation requires specialized skill training, and some qualified simultaneous interpreters may not even have a certificate. Moreover, even qualified simultaneous interpreters may not be able to handle all types of simultaneous interpretation tasks due to the varying complexity and themes of conferences.

How can one become an excellent simultaneous interpreter? Professor Zhong Weiqi from Guangdong University of Foreign Studies believes that cultivating a qualified simultaneous interpreter requires extensive training and practical translation skills, along with a solid foundation in both Chinese and foreign languages. One must have fluent verbal expression abilities in both languages to navigate seamlessly between them. All these require long-term accumulation. A strong educational background is also essential, and aspiring simultaneous interpreters should consciously expand their knowledge and continuously enrich their perspectives through ongoing training.

Simultaneous interpretation is a highly practical job, so mentorship is crucial. Networking within the industry is also important. Beginners without experienced mentors on-site find it challenging to excel in simultaneous interpretation.

Professor Zhong emphasizes that overcoming these basic qualifications, combined with a high educational background, language training, and networking, is necessary to become an excellent simultaneous interpreter.
