How to make money sitting at home

by abctskoe on 2012-03-03 17:59:14

Among people doing networking, which group of people can make money - technical experts or technical novices?

The current situation that I observe is that people with technical skills more or less have some income. The vast majority of them earn a salary, so the proportion of people in this group who make money is quite high. On the other hand, a large portion of those who don't understand technology haven't made any money yet, but the people who make big money are precisely these individuals who don't understand technology.

I think understanding technology is a good thing. Having a special skill makes it easier for newcomers to find direction and make money online quickly, regardless of the amount. The significance of this money is substantial.

People who don't understand technology at all are at a disadvantage when they first come into contact with the network. Some friends who bought my tutorials ask me questions about how to bind domains or how to add hyperlinks to images. Although I also tell them how to do it, I feel that they still have a long way to go before they can make money.

Some friends might say, does Brother Bo advocate that everyone should learn more technology? Actually, not at all. I've already mentioned above that the people who make big money are exactly those who don't understand technology. People who study too much technology often become rigid in their thinking, pursuing perfection and doing everything themselves. When they consider problems, especially how to make money, they are often not as adaptable as those who don't understand technology. In our industry, there are very few people who are both proficient in technology and good at making money. This might be where fate is fair - you can't have everything good fall into one person's lap. [When fate opens a window for you, it also closes a door.] Am I saying the opposite?

Doing networking involves various kinds of technology. It's impossible to master all of them. Being able to understand the basics of most technologies is enough. There's no need to delve deeper into studying them.

Most of the projects I personally develop focus on the word "ingenious," meaning that the technical aspects involved are minimal. They mostly emphasize strategy and technique. Often, a breakthrough is found first, then according to this breakthrough, consideration is given to how to combine other promotion or marketing methods to utilize it.

When friends ask me, [I tell them I actually don't understand any technology.] Many people don't believe this, thinking I'm being modest. But I'm not being modest at all. I am a typical example of "knowing a little about everything, but not mastering anything." Most of my skills related to networking are self-taught, so I only know the basics and never had the time or intention to delve deeper. If you take out any software, I probably have it, but anyone who has studied that software will definitely use it better than me.

Do people who make money online need to learn technology?

I believe that people who like to research technology are not suitable for making money online; those who reject technology and refuse to learn anything are also not suitable for making money online.

The most suitable people for making money online should have strong learning abilities, understand most of the technologies involved in online earnings, and possess one core technical skill. (This core ability doesn't necessarily have to be technical; it could be your ability to make friends, innovation ability, replication ability, project extension ability, etc...)

There is also a very critical point - investment. Being willing to invest in hiring others to help you and daring to invest in promotion to increase income. These two points seem simple, but they are actually the hardest to break through. I will write a dedicated article later to discuss the investment mindset.

Being knowledgeable in many things, excelling in one - this is the state I advocate for the best online earners. As for what to choose as your "one expertise," it should be based on your interests and hobbies.

Although doing a project always requires the cooperation of many aspects of technology, as long as you can achieve: doing simple technology yourself, knowing what words to input in Baidu to find answers for unfamiliar technology, and being able to express the effect you want when asking others to handle complex technology, that's enough.

Don't assume that finding answers on Baidu and communicating with technical personnel is something everyone can do. All these are based on having basic knowledge. Otherwise, even if there are answers to solve problems on the internet, you won't be able to find them.
