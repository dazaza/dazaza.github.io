People's Daily Online Medical Beauty Channel Today is Lixia, one of the 24 solar terms. Lixia signifies the farewell to spring and the beginning of summer. This term represents a period of hot weather and vigorous growth of all living things. In ancient times, some emperors would hold ceremonies at the onset of summer, traveling outside the capital city to welcome summer, and the day of their journey was Lixia.

Characteristics of Lixia

Lixia is the seventh of the 24 solar terms. It represents a noticeable rise in temperature, the imminent arrival of the hot summer, more rainfall than during Guyu (the Grain Rain), and for crops, an important season of robust growth. For example, after Lixia, early rice enters a crucial period of large-scale planting (earlier in the south than in the north). Of course, the amount of rain after Lixia has a significant impact on harvests, hence the saying: "If it doesn't rain at Lixia, the plow will be hung high; if there's no rain at Lixia, the pestle will have no rice." The Book of Rites - Monthly Orders explains the characteristics of Lixia as follows: "The crickets chirp, earthworms appear, the five grains grow, and bitter herbs flourish." From this, we can see that during Lixia, crickets begin to herald the arrival of summer with their noise, and earthworms help farmers loosen the soil. If Guyu is the time when "seedlings are first planted, and crops newly sown," then Lixia is the season when "seedlings grow taller and quickly mature."

Focus on Nourishing the Heart During Lixia

The Yellow Emperor's Inner Canon states: "In the month of summer, this is called flourishing beauty, with abundant energy in heaven and earth, and lush fruits on all plants." Lixia marks the beginning of summer, as the weather gradually becomes hotter and plants enter their most vigorous growth phase. According to traditional Chinese medicine theory, this is beneficial for the physiological activities of the heart. The concept of the heart in traditional Chinese medicine differs from that in modern medicine. Western medicine views the human heart as a key organ of the circulatory system, propelling blood flow and delivering oxygen and nutrients throughout the body for physiological use. Traditional Chinese medicine, however, regards the "heart as the master of the body, with all internal organs following its commands, thus making it the sovereign." The heart governs spirit and serves as the function of consciousness, meaning the heart plays a dominant role among the various organs, and mental and cognitive activities are related to the heart. In traditional Chinese medical theory, humans and nature form a unified whole, with the seasonal changes of nature being interconnected with the functional activities of the five internal organs. The heart corresponds to "summer," which means that during summer, the yang energy of the heart is most vigorous, reminding people to adapt to the changing weather between spring and summer and focus on nurturing the heart.

Cultivating the Spirit Requires Calmness

Traditional Chinese medicine believes that "the heart governs the spirit," meaning that human spiritual activity is managed by the heart. When summer temperatures rise, people easily become irritable and prone to anger. Some individuals who are usually calm and composed may become impatient and quick-tempered in the summer. This is because high temperatures exacerbate psychological tension and lead to excessive heart fire.

Modern medical research has found that a person's psychology, emotions, and physical body are interconnected through the neuro-endocrine-immune system. Therefore, this period is not only marked by emotional fluctuations but also by a relatively weakened immune function. Improper daily routines or diet can easily lead to various diseases, affecting health. Especially in older adults, instances of myocardial ischemia, arrhythmia, and elevated blood pressure caused by anger are not uncommon, sometimes even leading to sudden death. Therefore, during the Lixia season, it is essential to focus on cultivating the spirit, maintaining calmness, smiling often, self-regulating, and controlling anger to maintain harmony.

For mental cultivation during Lixia, it is advisable to engage in quiet activities such as painting, calligraphy, listening to music, playing chess, gardening, or fishing. These activities can regulate the spirit and maintain a cheerful mood.
