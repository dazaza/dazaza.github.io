Apple Must Innovate; Cook May Not Be Up to the Task

by geekzhang on 2013-05-03 17:00:39

On April 29, according to Beijing time, a foreign technology website DigitalTonto recently published an analytical article stating that after the passing of Jobs, Apple would become a company different from before. It must find its own way to establish its innovative image, but current CEO Tim Cook (TimCook) may not be up to the task.The main content of the article is as follows:From the perspective of innovation, Apple's CEO Tim Cook has a heavy responsibility. He not only needs to manage the world's most valuable tech company, but also must follow and learn from history's greatest CEO, Steve Jobs.Can Cook handle this job? I began to doubt this point.Of course, Apple has made some missteps, such as releasing financial reports that disappointed investors, a map service that caused catastrophic effects, failing to incorporate near-field communication technology into its products in a timely manner, and the long-delayed launch of Apple TV, but these are not the reasons for my doubts.It is easy to believe that if Jobs were still alive, those missteps might not have happened. Of course, perhaps the map service would be an exception. Apple's legendary founder may not have been omnipotent, but he had a keen sense of the role of technology. After Jobs' passing, Apple must learn to innovate in a different way, and it seems that Cook is not up to the task.When I read what Cook said in an interview with BusinessWeek, I first started doubting his ability to innovate. Cook said: Many companies have set up innovation departments. When you set up a position like Vice President of Innovation, it indicates that something is wrong. You know, it’s like putting a 'for sale' sign on the door. Every employee at Apple, whether they work in operations, product development, or customer service, everyone has the responsibility to innovate.In a few words, he explained why Apple has been so successful and why the future of the company is so dangerous and uncertain. The legend left by Jobs will make the company blind to changes happening outside.Many successful companies have set up innovation departments, such as Google's GoogleX department. Why does Cook want to dismantle the innovation team?Walter Isaacson (WalterIsaacson) described in his biography of Jobs how Jobs decided to develop the iPad. At the time, he had dinner with an employee of Microsoft, who showed him a sample of Microsoft's latest tablet computer. Jobs immediately became furious when he saw that the prototype was equipped with a stylus because he had always disliked the concept of a stylus.But Jobs did not dismiss it outright. He thought this design was stupid, just as he thought MP3 players and countless other products were stupid, but he did not completely abandon those products; instead, he developed better products to replace them. It was precisely because Jobs made such decisions that Apple became the world's most valuable company.Although Apple has gained a reputation as a breakthrough innovator, most of its products, except for iTunes, do not actually fit the definition of breakthrough innovation. What makes Apple stand out is that its products have consistently been excellent, even in existing product categories, Apple's releases always give people a feeling of newness.I think there are three factors that played an important role in the company's success in innovation. First, is the discipline that Tim Cook mentioned above. Second, is Apple's world-class talent, such as Jony Ive (JonyIvy). The third factor, of course, is Jobs himself. Jobs could use his reality distortion field to see infinite possibilities in technology and lead the company to develop these fascinating products.The last key factor is now gone, and Apple must adjust accordingly. Jobs was a rare genius, and he is irreplaceable. To continue succeeding in the future, Apple must find a new model of success.That does not mean that Apple's core values, such as product quality and design, must change, nor does it mean that the company is in crisis. With $150 billion in cash reserves and maintaining a strong position in the rapidly developing smartphone and tablet markets, Apple still holds enviable advantages.However, it means that Cook needs to work harder than ever to find the direction for the future, and Apple needs to expand its vision and learn to innovate.Of course, Apple is not the only innovative company in the world. Google, IBM, Procter & Gamble, and many other companies are also capable in developing innovative products and new services, and they consistently maintain an advantage over competitors. However, if you delve deeper, you'll find that each company's method of innovation is different.To better explain innovation, I created an innovation management matrix, which raises two important questions: How is the problem defined? Who is the best person to solve the problem?Apple, like Toyota, thrives in well-defined fields. They make significant improvements to existing products, so Cook's strategy of involving everyone in innovation can work.However, when deciding the development direction of the company, it was Jobs alone who made the call. Now that Jobs has passed away, finding other methods of innovation has become urgent.Basic Research: In 1993, IBM's research department achieved the world's first case of quantum teleportation, which will form the next digital frontier of quantum computing research. Quantum computing won't start gaining momentum until around 2020. It is a long-term consideration, so IBM can develop products like Watson.Apple doesn't invest much in the development of new technologies and even less or nothing at all in basic research. Investing in entirely new fields rather than simply improving existing products might be something Cook and Apple need to consider.Breakthrough Innovation: Another thing Cook emphasized in the interview is how united Apple's executive team is. This is certainly good from the perspective of company operations, but if executives spend too much time together, they won't be thinking about innovation.This makes things more challenging because breakthrough innovations mainly require collaboration across multiple fields, and this kind of work is hard to accomplish in a highly focused environment.Many companies have achieved great success through open innovation (OpenInnovation) platforms, such as Innocentive or Procter & Gamble's internal Connect+Develop project. For Apple, to truly collaborate with the outside world, they would need to make real adjustments to their corporate culture.Disruptive Innovation: Google and 3M have maintained a leading edge in disruptive innovation, giving rise to many new products and services, such as Google Maps.These companies have established their own versions of the 15%/20% rule, encouraging a wide range of exploration rather than laser-like focus on one point.Therefore, although Apple's method of innovation worked well in the past, it cannot guarantee future success. Before Cook dismisses other companies' successful innovation methods, he should perhaps consider them more carefully.Cook mentioned in the interview with BusinessWeek what Jobs said to him when handing over power. Cook said: Jobs said at the time, I want to make it clear that I've seen what happened when Walt Disney died. When Walt really passed away, the company collapsed instantly. People panicked, constantly asking what Walt would have done if he were still alive. He continued, I don't want you to ask such questions someday. You just need to do the right thing. He was always very clear about this situation.Perhaps Jobs knew that this situation would eventually happen. After Jobs' passing, Apple must become a company very different from before. They must find their own way to innovate, build their own reputation, and operate in a different way. Perhaps, they will also set up an innovation department.
