Is what you cannot have bad?

On New Year's Day, our family of three went to IKEA to hang out. It is a home supermarket that integrates shopping, food, and children's play. Starting from New Year's Day, for every plush toy purchased by customers, the company would donate one euro to support early education in countries around the world. We explained to our child the way and significance of helping others with our meager strength, allowing him to initially understand the meaning of "helping others helps oneself" (even if he doesn't understand it, it doesn't matter).

In the end, in the doll area, Tongtong discovered a set of wooden cars. Three small cars for 100 yuan. As long as there are wheels, Tongtong will be very interested. I saw him holding the car, extremely excited, demanding that we buy it for him. Since he already had too many cars (at least hundreds of large and small ones), we thought it was unnecessary to buy more. Besides, because he just got two new cars for Christmas, we agreed not to buy any cars in the near future.

So I squatted down and explained the reasons to him, asking him to abandon his original idea. After a few words, the sensible Tongtong readily put down the car and came back to comfort me: "This set of cars is not beautiful or fun. The ones at my house are much better!"

Actually, I should immediately praise Tongtong for being reasonable, listening to advice, and learning self-consolation and emotional transfer. However, I suddenly felt something was wrong. In his thinking, "What I cannot own must be bad." Isn't this like saying the grapes are sour because you can't eat them? - An extremely unhealthy mindset.

So I immediately said: "Son, this set of cars is very beautiful and fun, but we don't need to buy it today. First, Tongtong has too many cars; second, we've agreed not to buy cars in the near future; third, the price is also quite expensive. We shouldn't waste money casually."

"Yes, our family already has too many cars, we can't waste money anymore."

"Then do you think it becomes ugly or unfun just because we don't buy it?"

"No, these three cars are very beautiful, they must be fun too." My son's expression was serious, and his tone was very calm.

I was greatly comforted.

Son, mom just wants you to know: Not everything you think is good must be owned, and things you cannot own are not necessarily valueless.

Respect for Children

After dinner, Tongtong took transparent tape and tried hard to find an exit. After a while without success, he sought my help. Seeing his serious demeanor, I knew he must have an important use for it, so I helped him. After saying "thank you", he stretched the tape very long. At this moment, grandma and Dad spoke up simultaneously: "Tongtong, you're causing trouble again, pulling it out so long is wasteful."

The boy didn't say anything, maybe he was too focused and didn't hear the scolding; or maybe he heard it but ignored the misunderstanding of the adults. These two adults persisted: "This is useful, not for playing."

I couldn't help myself, so I gently walked over and asked: "Tongtong, you must have an important thing to do with the tape, right?"

"Yes, look, our work blanket has a hole, I want to use the tape to fix it!"

How much like a little master!

Maybe because I always treat him like a big kid when 'managing' everything at home, he gradually developed a sense of ownership. He could independently come up with ways to solve problems, which is such a commendable behavior!

I praised him highly: "It's great to have a son! Tongtong is a real man, as important as dad!" After making him understand that this is a behavior worth continuing, I gave a suggestion: "Tongtong, when you have ideas and others don't understand, you must speak up, so others won't misunderstand you. Expressing your thoughts can reduce a lot of trouble!"

Tongtong nodded understandingly!

Looking back, I sincerely criticized the two adults in the house: "When facing children, we cannot look down on them and assume all their behaviors are mischief. Respecting and understanding children will prevent us from stifling their young, curious minds."

In fact, in our lives, aren't there many children who are misunderstood? Gradually, the children feel wronged, scared, annoyed, numb. Is this merely a day or two's issue?

Childish Words

Grandma wore a new pink scarf.

"Grandma, you look so pretty today!"

"Where am I pretty?" Grandma was secretly pleased after receiving the compliment.

"The scarf is pretty, your eyes sparkle beautifully, everything about you is pretty."

"Do you think grandma is prettier or mom is prettier?" It seems grandma got a bit carried away. (Actually, this is a very difficult and extremely foolish question for the child.)

"Mom is the prettiest." The little smartie didn't fall into the trap.

"Who is prettier, grandpa or dad?" Grandma kept asking.

"Men aren't described as pretty, they're called handsome. They are all handsome, because dad is grandpa's son, Tongtong is also handsome, I am dad's son."

Grandma had no choice but to admit defeat!
