First: The issue of promotion and planning. Everything requires a goal, an awareness, before action can be taken. This is especially true in the commercial market. How big does a company want to grow? What percentage of the market share should it occupy? What sales targets need to be achieved (100 million yuan annually or over 3 billion yuan within two years)? While considering how to promote, these questions must be considered because having clear goals makes promotional actions more effective. Promotion isn't just about holding one or two events, doing sales promotions, or running advertisements. Promotion must be viewed from a broad perspective and must have planning, strategy, and steps, aiming to achieve the most effective results with optimal resources. Second: The issue of promotion and positioning. In the "thunderous" early stages, new brands and products cannot do without promotion. Many companies often experience "small raindrops" during their initial promotion phase. Why? It's due to inaccurate positioning. A brand needs clear positioning, and products need targeted customers (especially as today's market demand becomes increasingly diversified and customer needs become more varied). Therefore, we must clarify where our target customers are. Only when we know our customers can we understand their needs, consumption characteristics, what our competitors are doing, and how to invent our unique advantages (this is the work that market research needs to accomplish). Only after clarifying these aspects can we formulate an effective promotion plan and achieve victory step-by-step.

What is the positioning of the **** brand? Slogan - this is too vague. If the positioning of the **** brand is mid-to-high-end, it's still unclear! This isn't positioning! Because positioning should address the future development direction of the enterprise and brand, and positioning should be clear.

Third: The issue of promotion and budget. Doing promotion involves costs. Different budget allocations lead to different promotion plans. The biggest fear is "a clever housewife can't cook without rice," which means if there aren't enough resources, it's hard to execute properly. As the old saying goes, "provisions must come first." To reach the company's planned goals, how much promotion budget can the **** brand allocate?

Fourth: The issue of promotion and product channels. There are many forms of promotion, and they differ depending on the products and customers involved. The key lies in the choices the company makes. Promotion must have planned priorities for channel promotion and terminal promotion, with different methods for each. The **** brand will focus on franchise recruitment, agent channel expansion, and some local specialty store terminal formats in the future. Additionally, for new brands, the early promotion should focus more on the company and brand itself. These are all things I need to consider carefully because I cannot waste resources on ineffective promotions.

Fifth: The issue of promotion and industry long-term development. Since market demand requires collective efforts to push forward, a company's development cannot be separated from the larger industry environment. Promotion also needs to align with the industry's overall situation. The most feared outcome is being at the forefront but not sharing in the benefits of success. Collaborating with large enterprises, industry-related associations, and institutions to create effective promotions and build one's reputation ensures that only those who start high can see further. Aim high, and you'll reach the middle. Therefore, I need to view certain matters from a higher starting point in the industry.
