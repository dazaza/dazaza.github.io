Laser Treatment for Age Spots

1. What are age spots: Age spots, medically known as "senile pigmented spots," refer to lipofuscin pigment patches that appear on the skin of elderly people. They are commonly seen in the elderly and are often referred to as "longevity spots." Modern medical research has shown that this elegant term does not match reality, as they are not a sign of longevity. Experts have found that with the increase in average life expectancy, age spots are not common among the elderly, accounting for only 27%.

2. What age spots represent: Age spots symbolize the aging of internal organs. They leave traces and cause harm in places that cannot be seen by the naked eye. For example, when they appear on brain cells, they can lead to reduced intelligence and memory; when they gather on the walls of blood vessels, vascular fibrous lesions occur, leading to high blood pressure, arteriosclerosis, and heart disease. This lipofuscin pigment is a product of cell oxidation, and once it accumulates excessively, it affects organ function, causing gradual aging. Therefore, age spots convey information about internal organ aging and are also morphological markers of human aging. The appearance of age spots is not isolated but often accompanied by other visible signs of aging, forming an image of old age.

3. Causes of age spots: After entering the age of 60-70, with the increase in age, the number of age spots can gradually increase, and the area also expands progressively. So, how do age spots form? Currently, there are three theories: The first suggests that after entering old age, cellular metabolic functions decline, making it easier for body fat to oxidize and produce senile pigments. These pigments cannot be expelled from the body and thus deposit on the cell bodies, forming age spots. The second theory suggests that as people age, metabolism declines, cellular functions deteriorate annually, and blood circulation slows down. Combined with changes in dietary structure and imbalance in the intake ratio of animal and plant fats, this promotes the accumulation of a very small brown particle called lipofuscin in the basal layer cells of the skin. This brown particle is a product of the lipid peroxidation reaction process. Aging tissues lose their ability to decompose and expel these particles, resulting in excessive accumulation within local cell basements, forming age spots on the body's surface. The third theory suggests that in old age, the activity of superoxide dismutase, which has anti-peroxidation effects, decreases, leading to an increase in free radicals and their induced peroxidation reactions, which long-term poison biological organisms.

4. Can age spots be prevented and treated: In recent years, age spots have increasingly caught the attention of medical professionals who hope to control their formation to extend human lifespan. Scholars have conducted experiments by adding various antioxidants, yielding unexpectedly good results. Current studies have proven that vitamin E is a relatively ideal antioxidant, as it can prevent the formation of lipofuscin pigments from unsaturated fatty acids. Blood plasma checks on healthy seniors over 60 have shown that vitamin E levels decrease with age, indicating a relationship between vitamin E and chemically active free radicals. Animal experiments have confirmed that vitamin E can prevent the formation of lipofuscin and has the effect of clearing free radicals and extending lifespan. Therefore, in addition to taking a certain amount of vitamin E, one can also consume foods rich in vitamin E, such as plant oils, grains, legumes, dark green plants, and animal-based foods like liver, eggs, and dairy products. It is important to adjust the intake ratio of animal and plant fats (normal ratio is 1:2). Although plant fats contain more unsaturated fatty acids, eating only vegetarian food cannot stop the increase in brown pigments, so it is necessary to change the habit of avoiding meat and preferring vegetables. Additionally, maintaining a cheerful mood and smooth bowel movements is beneficial. Eating more fresh vegetables, especially onions, radishes, celery, spinach, almonds, and jujubes, is recommended. Taking 100 international units of vitamin E and 300-500 mg of vitamin C daily for two years can significantly slow the development of arterial hardening plaques and age spots. Moreover, exposed parts of the body should avoid prolonged ultraviolet radiation. Do not scratch age spots or misuse irritating topical medications. If necessary, under a doctor's guidance, one can use 5% fluorouracil ointment, liquid nitrogen cryotherapy, or laser treatment to remove age spots. Additionally, American experts have found that actors and singers develop facial age spots 8-10 years later than ordinary people, which is attributed to more facial muscle movement. Therefore, chewing gum for 10-15 minutes daily or eating slowly during meals can improve facial blood circulation and skin metabolism, delaying the onset of age spots. Besides medication, physical exercise can also serve as an important measure to prevent the deposition of this senile pigment in blood vessels and stop vascular degeneration.
