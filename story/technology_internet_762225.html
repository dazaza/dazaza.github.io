Why Tencent and Chery are fighting for the QQ trademark: neither can afford to lose

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 14:25:42

Does Tencent produce cars? The answer is no, but the company had already applied to register the "QQ" trademark for goods such as cars in class 12 back in 2005, and received approval in 2008. However, this did not sit well with Chery Automobile Co., Ltd., which produces cars. In 2009, Chery filed a request to cancel Tencent's automobile-related trademarks on grounds of identical or similar trademarks for similar goods, and received support. With its trademark revoked, Tencent took the Trademark Review and Adjudication Board (TRAB) of the State Administration for Industry and Commerce to court. Yesterday, the case was heard at the First Intermediate People's Court, where plaintiff Tencent, defendant TRAB, and third party Chery faced off in court.

As is widely known, the two enterprises most closely associated with "QQ" in China are Tencent Technology and Chery Automobile, each operating in different fields. Tencent holds a place in the internet sector, while Chery's QQ car has sold over a million units cumulatively. In terms of the chronological order of using "QQ", Tencent launched its instant messaging software QQ in 1999, while Chery introduced its microcar, the Chery QQ, in 2003. The question of who should own "QQ" has been a point of contention between these two companies for nearly ten years.

On May 19, 2005, Tencent applied to register the "QQ" trademark for goods such as cars in class 12, receiving approval on March 7, 2008. On November 26, 2009, Chery filed a request to revoke the disputed trademark on grounds that it violated the Trademark Law by replicating and imitating famous trademarks, being identical or similar trademarks for similar goods, and infringing upon others' prior rights. After deliberation, the TRAB ruled that the registration of the disputed trademark constituted the preemptive registration of another party’s trademark that had already been used and had gained some influence, as stipulated in Article 31 of the Trademark Law, thus revoking Tencent's registered trademark.

Tencent argued that the controversial ruling made by the TRAB contained obvious errors and omissions, claiming that the disputed trademark did not violate any laws and should be maintained. Their representative explained that on August 31, 2001, Tencent had applied to register the "QQ" trademark for services in class 38, including information transmission and computer terminal communications. Through extensive promotion, the number of registered users for QQ communication services grew from 5 million in 2000 to over 200 million in 2003. Therefore, before Chery Automobile used the "QQ" trademark, Tencent's "QQ" trademark had already become a well-known trademark. In 2009, Tencent had received a certificate of recognition for its well-known trademark from relevant national departments, meaning any other company using the "QQ" trademark would constitute an infringement.

Chery Automobile, however, believed that Tencent's registration of the "QQ" trademark in the automotive field was malicious. As the third party in this case, Chery's representative stated that the trademark registered by Tencent in 2001 was not the "QQ" letters but a graphic. In contrast, Chery Automobile registered the "QQ" letter trademark in the automotive field in 2003. Therefore, the two enterprises were using their respective trademarks in different fields, with Chery's "QQ" trademark in the automotive field predating Tencent's. Moreover, Chery QQ has sold over a million units, with advertising costs amounting to over 200 million yuan, and should therefore be protected.

Furthermore, Tencent believed that its registration of the "QQ" trademark across multiple fields was a protective measure for "QQ". However, Chery Automobile contended that from the initial planning of the car name to later promotional activities, everything was based on the already well-known "QQ" trademark and penguin mascot to achieve publicity effects, constituting an infringement that could not generate legitimate prior rights.

The verdict will be announced on another day, but for both Tencent and Chery, having operated under the "QQ" trademark for nearly ten years, neither can afford to lose this lawsuit.

Is there a third way in the QQ dispute?

Tencent QQ is the first domestic instant messaging software launched by Tencent in 1999, while Chery QQ is a microcar launched by Chery in July 2003. Both "QQ"s enjoy high renown in their respective fields.

Research revealed that as early as 2003, when the "QQ" car was introduced, Tencent had raised issues regarding trademark infringement against Chery. Tencent claimed that they were busy preparing for their IPO at the time and thus did not file a public lawsuit. Later, Tencent sent a legal notice to Chery, but Chery never provided a substantive response to Tencent.

At the end of 2005, media reports emerged about both parties taking legal action over the QQ trademark, but this lawsuit did not progress further, with some media even labeling it as a publicity stunt.

This time, the trademark dispute has once again come to court with Tencent as the plaintiff, TRAB as the defendant, and Chery as the third party. Trademarks are the banners of enterprises in the market, and for Tencent and Chery, who have cultivated their brands for around ten years, neither can afford to lose. Some believe that whoever is determined to have infringed will face substantial compensation. Is it possible, in this trademark dispute, that there is only victory or defeat, with no third way? Tang Guangliang, an intellectual property expert at the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences, told reporters that Tencent and Chery could fully collaborate to achieve a win-win situation.

Tang Guangliang said that the role of a trademark is to attract consumer attention, serving as a link between merchants and consumers, and also as the image of merchants in the minds of consumers. A trademark itself is a means rather than an end, and how to use this means depends on each merchant's considerations. In recent years, China's trademark protection has become increasingly mature. Whether there is infringement does not solely depend on the similarity of two marks; the key lies in whether consumers would be confused. If there is no confusion, then the use by two or more merchants can expand the influence of the trademark, which benefits all users of the trademark.

"For example, with the 'QQ' trademark, if there appears a situation where 'consumers buy Chery's QQ car because they think it is produced by Tencent,' or 'consumers use the QQ chat tool because they like Chery QQ cars,' then we can say there is confusion," Tang Guangliang said. If the above situation does not occur, then both parties do not need to fight over it. Because regarding trademarks, it is not necessarily true that whichever merchant claims ownership gets it, and even if one owns it, it doesn't mean they must eliminate every other user. As long as the trademark is not maliciously defamed or the enterprise's market is not seized, it might even have a promoting effect, and both parties can coexist peacefully and share benefits.

Moreover, Tang Guangliang said that from a rational market perspective, Tencent and Chery do not conflict. Chatting tools primarily target young people, and the QQ car is also popular among young people. Both parties can fully cooperate, operate together, share the market, and achieve mutual benefit without needing to contest the ownership of the trademark in the absence of harm.
