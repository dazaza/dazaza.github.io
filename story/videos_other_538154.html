1. Facing Competition Directly

Market economy is an economic model that is mainly guided by market demand and freely adjusts the market with price as the lever. Its core is competition. In a market economy environment, competition is everywhere, at any time. Eternal competition drives the prosperity of the market economy.

The market is like this, and the evolution of life is also like this. Which kind of life or species in the world has not gone through fierce competition? The so-called survival of the fittest means exactly this. Species evolve in competition, and life extends in competition.

What exactly is competition? There is a story here that can illustrate the point well. It says that two children went to play in the forest, and they were playing happily when they suddenly saw a bear not far from the woods running towards them. One child said to the other, "Hey! We need to run away." The other one was calmly putting on his running shoes. The first child asked in confusion, "Can you run faster than the bear?" The child who was putting on his running shoes replied without looking up, "No! I can't outrun the bear, but as long as I can outrun you, I'm fine."

This is competition. This simple story tells us three principles about management: First, to survive, we must win in competition. Second, we must clearly identify who our competitors are; if you mistakenly regard the bear as your competitor, it will affect your courage and may lead you to give up the competition and become a loser. Third, winning in competition only ensures survival, but survival is not the goal; survival only provides the time conditions for development. The term "facing competition directly" has dual meanings of "always" and "directly" facing competition, warning leaders that occasional success in competition does not equate to overall success.

Competition is the truth, competition is a natural law, we need to follow it, master it, apply it, and even endow it with new connotations. Competition has evolved from the past where big fish eat small fish to today where fast fish eat slow fish, and smart fish eat dumb fish.

2. Transforming Concepts

How to adapt to the current medical market? I think first we need to recognize the severe situation of current medical market competition, then transform our concepts, liberate our thoughts, and dare to compete. I hope that all hospital leaders have the sharp thinking and clever minds of politicians, seize opportunities, and meet challenges from all sides. The most important thing is to transform our concepts. Past achievements and former glories have all become history. Owning yesterday does not necessarily mean embracing tomorrow. Tomorrow, we face more severe and greater challenges. How to compete now and open up the future? This is a new topic that leaders need to study under the new situation. Going against the current means moving forward; standing still means going backward. In this era of sifting sand, the thought of small-scale operation for survival and being content with small wealth for peace can only lose opportunities and miss success, leaving eternal regrets.

As we all know, daring to think, speak, and act is a quality that innovative and enterprising leaders should possess. Market economy requires enterprise leaders to have the belief that "there is nothing that cannot be done, only things that cannot be imagined." If one doesn't even dare to imagine, the result is self-evident - one will definitely achieve nothing. This belief should be instilled into the minds of every employee.

3. Surpassing Oneself

Market competition has evolved from single economic strength competition to competition of concepts and foresight. Let's look back at the past. Some medical institutions took the lead in entering the medical market by investing in joint ventures during the early stages of reform and opening-up. They introduced competitive mechanisms, employment systems, distribution systems, advertising operations, quantitative management, and other forward-thinking concepts and practices, thereby occupying the market and completing the original accumulation of capital, leading to the scale of today's hospitals.

Currently, multiple disciplines in several private hospitals in Beijing have already formed regional advantages and occupied the market. They have introduced high-tech diagnostic and treatment technologies and recruited compound talents proficient in professional technology, market marketing, and enterprise management. They have adopted advanced management models. If you are still lingering at the level of specialized outpatient clinics, implementing family-style management, and adopting workshop-style operation modes, what do you have to catch up with others and compare with them? How to surpass oneself? I believe the following management warnings are worth our deep reflection. Extraordinary enterprises must undergo "shedding skin and changing shells" after completing their original accumulation in order to grow! However, "shedding skin and changing shells" is a tough challenge! Establishing a scientific management model is not difficult, but the difficulty lies with the boss. Because they are too extraordinary, because they have too many successful experiences of making money by simply brainstorming. The bosses who most desire to establish a scientific management model and have the most driving force are also the ones who can most destroy the management model! Especially those enterprise bosses who make all decisions alone, they lack enough courage to overcome themselves! Managers lament the difficulty of finding good ones, and once they find some, they regret their incompetence and anger their inefficiency. They seek renowned doctors for treatment and medication, yet they don't realize that the problem lies within themselves and that divine pills are useless. If you want to break through the haze and recreate brilliance, you might first invite a mentor to correct yourself, reconstruct a mode that accommodates others, and success will be within reach! History proves that there are no doomed enterprises, only unsalvageable bosses! This is not an exaggerated statement; the bear is really coming towards you. If you don't want to be eaten, you need to surpass competition, quickly catch up, run fast, and change quickly. Essentially, it requires leaders to constantly surpass themselves, conquer themselves, renew themselves, and let our hospitals successfully go through "shedding skin and changing shells" in order to grow and strengthen!

4. Seeking Benefits Through Management

Management is a science, and management models can be considered the essence of corporate culture. Management art is the most exquisite art in the world, transcending social systems and national boundaries. Whoever masters this science can produce results and create benefits. With China's entry into WTO, management becomes increasingly charming and important. In the initial stage of establishing private hospitals, since the cause was just starting, it required silent and diligent talent akin to the old yellow ox. Family-style management was suitable for small-scale workshop operations and had played a historical role. During the extremely difficult period of establishing the foundation, family members fighting together had played a decisive role. Now things are different. Small-scale medical institutions have transformed into large hospitals, thus requiring a shift from diligent old yellow ox-type talents to knowledge and technical application talents and compound management talents who understand their professions, possess marketing experience, and have managerial expertise. With the development of circumstances, family-style management begins to hinder the development of hospitals, encountering practical problems such as whether or when to establish the cultural and management models of their own enterprises, whether to absorb highly qualified management talents to join the hospital, whether to introduce advanced human resources systems, and whether to rely on external brains and learn to play supporting roles.

The scientific nature, standardization, institutionalization, and modeling of management are all very important, but the most crucial element is top-notch management talent. Only through the management talent at the front line of the hospital can the hospital's management be creatively promoted, and the potential, intelligence, and wisdom of each member of the hospital be brought into play, thereby producing results and creating benefits. Of course, to manage people well and use people well, one must first be a good person oneself. One needs to continuously strengthen one's learning and cultivation, improve one's quality and level, and possess personal charisma to attract and retain talent, thereby having cohesion and uniting talent around oneself to work, strive, and compete.

Of course, management also requires strategies and methods. Balance tension and relaxation appropriately, keep tight without being rigid, remain loose without chaos. Stick to principles while being flexible in methods, and create a united, intense, serious, and lively team. In short, treat others sincerely, empathize, exchange hearts, communicate frequently, and place integrity first.

At present, most private hospitals' main managers lean towards experiential management, which primarily relies on personal practical accumulated management knowledge, especially for leaders and entrepreneurs who started from scratch, whose personal experience forms the primary source of the hospital's management philosophy and framework. Although these experiences are precious, they have significant limitations and randomness. Once the environment and resources change, past successful experiences may become reasons for failure.

Some private hospitals have invested heavily in management skills training for middle and lower-level managers, but neglected the training of the main managers themselves. Due to the lack of modern hospital management concepts and knowledge among the main managers, tasks such as building the hospital's basic management system, establishing management systems, and reforms that require judgment and decision-making by the main managers are difficult to carry out.

5. Implanting Successful Management Genes in Private Hospitals

In biology, the definition of a gene is: A gene is a segment of nucleotide sequence in a DNA molecule that contains specific genetic information, which is the smallest functional unit of hereditary material. In other words, genes are the most fundamental factors determining all life phenomena of a living species.

An increasing number of facts prove that the decisive factor guiding the success of enterprises is implanting successful management genes. Similarly, private hospitals as business-operated entities should never rely solely on personal or borrowed successful experiences. The management genes we implant are the most critical factors determining a hospital's management characteristics. By "management characteristics," we mean the features that distinguish a hospital's management from others and are unique to itself. In my opinion, successful management genes are neither the management qualities and abilities that managers need to possess nor should include the systematic knowledge structure of modern enterprise management that managers should have. Instead, they are a series of mature management ideas and philosophies that have been developed over nearly a century of enterprise management theory. We call these advanced management concepts. As time passes, these concepts become increasingly perfect and show their unique truthfulness and powerful practical functions. These concepts are worth remembering by every enterprise manager.
