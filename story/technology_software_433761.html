"Living off one’s parents" refers to the social phenomenon where young people of working age have no fixed source of income and rely on their parents for livelihood. "Living off one’s parents" can easily lead to tension in parent-child relationships, and when conflicts accumulate to a certain extent, trivial disputes can easily escalate into family tragedies. Recently, the Criminal Division One of the Intermediate People's Court accepted three consecutive matricide cases, all closely related to "living off one’s parents". Editorial note (edited by SN026)

26 years ago, Xue Bing was born into an ordinary intellectual youth family in Shanghai. His father was disabled and in poor health, but in order to cultivate his son into talent, he spared no expense. Xue Bing did not disappoint his parents; he showed extraordinary intelligence and talent from a young age. After starting school, he was academically excellent, often bringing home award certificates from various subject competitions, praised by neighbors.

The four years of university passed quickly, and soon, Xue Bing also had to enter society. However, this good child in his parents' eyes kept hitting walls in job hunting. He applied to more than ten companies but failed each time, his self-esteem was hurt, Xue Bing was disheartened and stayed at home without going out.

To help their son integrate into society as soon as possible, the father pulled strings to get him a job. However, he thought this job was not in line with his major and required working shifts, so he quit after doing it for a short while. Soon, through a classmate's recommendation, he worked at a mold company, but he thought the salary was too low and the workload too heavy, so he resigned after working for more than a month.

Judge's commentary

Matricide cases triggered by "living off one’s parents" urgently need societal attention

Society usually regards "living off one’s parents" as a family issue and pays less attention to it. However, from the above cases, "living off one’s parents" has already severely affected family harmony and social stability. In response, judges from the Criminal Division One of the Shanghai No. 1 Intermediate People's Court suggest that both parents and children in families where offspring live off their elders should adjust their mindsets in time. Relevant social departments should pay attention to groups living off their elders and families with such situations, strengthen psychological counseling, and fully assist these groups in gaining employment as soon as possible.
