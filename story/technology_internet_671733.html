http://www.niugu8.com My Wandering College Life

Truly, time flies like an arrow and the years pass swiftly! What seemed to be a long three years is about to be over in the blink of an eye. With only a week left until our classes conclude, my college studies can also come to an official end. I believe it's necessary to make a simple summary of my college life, which could be considered as aimless doodling when I have nothing better to do (in fact, I haven't written anything casually for a long time. It took me several days of deliberation before deciding to put these words down. Sigh, I wouldn't dare call this a casual essay... it's better to be humble, hehe).

I came to this school with a mix of excitement and guilt. Excitement: Finally, I'm going to college! My dreams might not be so far away anymore, especially since I've heard from previous teachers that "once you're in college, things will be easier and freer." Guilt: Because I didn't perform well on the college entrance exam and didn't want to endure another year of high school, I reluctantly let my family bear the high tuition fees to send me here (though I've always firmly believed that gold will shine wherever it is... a bit exaggerated, huh? Hehe). After arriving at school, I realized that the school wasn't as bad as I had imagined. The campus was quite large, with mountains, water, grass, trees, and even uniquely yellow-painted walls (the term "dog-shit yellow" actually originated from my roommate No. 2. Out of respect for copyright and patent rights, I didn't dare use it frivolously... hehe).

Youth Digest Electronic Magazine

Let's talk about these past two years! When I first arrived at this brand-new place, I spent half the time studying closely and the other half adapting to the environment: going out with three or five roommates to explore the city, go on outings, climb mountains, and see big Buddhas. After a month, we'd almost visited all the good spots and didn't feel like going out anymore. So, we all just went to internet cafes instead. My roommates would only be seen on campus for class and sleep; otherwise, no matter the weather, they were always at the internet cafe. During this period, I started learning how to play "CrossFire" (which experts simply call CF). This game is something you don't know about until you play it, and once you start, it feels like heaven and earth are upside down. I calculated every night, making sure to return to the dormitory 10 minutes before the lights went out. This went on for about a month. Money was being used up quickly, so after considering various factors, I decided it would be more cost-effective to buy a computer and play in the dormitory rather than walking to the internet cafe every day. So, I bought a computer and placed it in the dormitory, rushing back there immediately after class every day. Later, I learned online that people call this lifestyle "hikikomori," and naturally, I became a "hikikomori male." After getting tired of CF, I moved on to QQ Speed, but due to lacking gaming talent, I couldn't reach a high level in either game and eventually gave them both up. Thus, I spent my entire freshman year in a daze.

They say sophomore year is the turning point, and indeed, by sophomore year, the courses got tighter and the workload increased. There was no longer much time to play games. I gradually lost interest in gaming, especially since Tencent kept updating their games and the campus network speed kept deteriorating. Just waiting for updates became annoying. Even the most attractive games couldn't attract me anymore, but computers are essential tools for modernization, and I still liked using them and couldn't stay away. Sometimes, I would turn on the computer even when I had nothing to do, mostly out of boredom! Recently, Weibo has become popular, so for a while, I played it to relieve boredom. But later, I stopped for some reason. Sigh... the computer I bought had too many problems. Once, the monitor started smoking while I was using it. Apparently, the repair fee was very high, so I just replaced it. I've changed monitors three times, from CRT to LCD! The main unit was sent for repair twice, and I was scammed out of nearly 100 yuan! From this, I realized that sending it for repair whenever there's a problem isn't wise, and carrying it back and forth is too troublesome. In a fit of anger, I borrowed a book from the library called roughly "Beginner's Guide to Computer Repair." From hardware to software, from installing systems to maintaining and backing up systems, I studied everything myself and even bought some professional repair and testing tools. Now that I'm familiar with computers, I can handle most common computer issues, and classmates often come to me for help when their computers break down (hehe... a bit proud!). A computer is a tool, not a toy! I'm a "computer enthusiast" but not really one. Personally, I have a wide range of interests. Lately, I suddenly felt like playing the six-string guitar, so I bought one at a low price and downloaded a bunch of tutorials using my limited monthly data of 10G. Whenever I have free time, I take it out and make some noise. Unexpectedly, I learned two simple songs! Hehe.

Everyone says interpersonal relationships are important. Over these two years, my personal relationships have been pretty good. I have a few close buddies whom I hang out with regularly. Whenever I have troubles, we can always solve them over drinks. Hehe...

Talking about sensitive topics: during my freshman year, everything was new, and since I hadn't yet experienced society, I never thought about having a girlfriend. By sophomore year, I had gotten to know this small social circle well and began to feel lonely, so I wanted to find a girlfriend. Maybe because I'm just a country bumpkin, it didn't work out. Thus, I stuck to the principle that it's better to be single than to settle for less, looking forward (after all, among my roommates No. 3 to No. 6, four of us are still bachelors, and none of us are in a rush).

Speaking of academics, apart from my national language getting worse and my English becoming less familiar, I've become more proficient in professional 3D drawing software and have developed a habit of making mechanical parts for fun. Personally, I think my academic performance over these two years has been okay. At least, I've managed to narrowly miss the 5000-yuan scholarship each time without ever actually getting it. (What a tragedy!) Nowadays, it's the peak job-hunting season, and many companies have come to campus to recruit. The positions they offer are only loosely related to our major: paper packaging, cement companies, display manufacturers, etc. I feel none of these align with my original aspirations. Perhaps, as someone who studies mechanics, I can only find a better development prospect in the mechanical industry! But as a vocational student concurrently pursuing a correspondence bachelor's degree, how easy is it to find the job I aspire to? This has thoroughly confused me!

My college life may be... just like this! But personally, I still feel these past two years have been somewhat wasted and idle, even a little...
