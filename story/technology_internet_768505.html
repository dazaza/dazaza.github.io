What can we see from the top 40 free charts of Android vs iOS in the US region?

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 18:57:41

Android and iOS Demographics in the US

Fred Wilson recently compared the top 40 free apps on both Android and iOS platforms in the U.S. (the table is here) and drew some interesting conclusions:

(Fred Wilson's classification method: any app that becomes useless without friends' support is categorized as "social"; apps used for shopping are classified as "shopping, not lifestyle"; "general" integrates several common categories.)

1) Only 14 apps appear on both platforms’ top 40 lists.

Facebook, Pandora, Instagram, Candy Crush, Bitstrips, Snapchat, Skype, Kik, Twitter, Pet Rescue, WhatsApp, Deer Hunter, Despicable Me, Vine.

It's not hard to notice that most of these are social apps, driven by friends rather than App Store distribution. These social apps simultaneously appear in the top 40 free apps on both platforms.

In other words, 26 of the apps in the top 40 are different. If it's not a social app, it's difficult to rank in the top 40 on both platforms simultaneously.

2) In iOS, there are 15 games in the top 40 with an average ranking of 19; in Android, there are 12 games in the top 40 with an average ranking of 22. Among the top 10, iOS has 5 games while Android has only 1. Although Apple may have its own algorithm for "trending," the results suggest that iOS users prefer games more.

3) For general category apps, there are 9 in the top 40 on Android and only 3 on iOS.

4) In the top 40 on Android, there are only two shopping apps: eBay and Amazon. On iOS, there are three: Groupon, Black Friday, and Target Cartwheel. There is no overlap.

He said that if the algorithms behind iOS and Android are similar, then the user demographics of these two platforms in the U.S. clearly differ. Even if the algorithms differ, the conclusion might be the same.

Thinking about it this way, Wilson concludes: Android users may form a dumbbell-shaped demographic. On one side are teenagers or those nearing adulthood, who use relatively cheap phones and won't spend money to repair or buy a new one if they lose it; on the other side are people like me, who prefer large screens and focus more on very "general" things rather than games. Meanwhile, iOS users fall right in the middle — college students, recent graduates, and newlyweds starting families. The possible age range for Android users could be under 18 + over 40, while iOS users lie between these ranges.

This speculation can undoubtedly help developers. Identifying your product’s audience and understanding these demographic statistics can assist you in better choosing which platform to launch on next, and how to design your future development schedule. Similarly, it can also influence your distribution strategy. For instance, if you have a social app, you can acquire a large number of users on the iOS platform first, then use that to gain more users on the Android platform, just like Instagram did last year.
