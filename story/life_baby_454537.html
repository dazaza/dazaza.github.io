Caution: Lead poisoning affects children's IQ | Baby Games | Sina Blog

by toywu on 2011-06-30 22:28:05

Lead is currently recognized as one of the environmental toxins that affect the development of the central nervous system. In recent years, lead has become one of the factors harming human health and children's intelligence. According to authoritative investigation reports, the average lead content in modern humans has exceeded that of ancient people from a thousand years ago by 500 times.

Lead in food can be slowly absorbed by the human body. The absorption rate of lead by children's gastrointestinal tract is about five times higher than that of adults. Since the central nervous system of children is not fully developed, they are more sensitive to the toxicity of lead.

Lead poisoning is called the "invisible killer" for children. It differs from other types of poisoning; children do not necessarily exhibit clinical symptoms visible to parents. When the concentration of lead in an adult's blood reaches 400 micrograms per liter, it can be diagnosed as occupational lead poisoning; when the concentration of lead in a child's blood exceeds 100 micrograms per liter, it will harm their bodies. Scientific research has found that if the blood lead level exceeds 100 micrograms per liter, it could lead to neurodevelopmental damage. For every 100 micrograms per liter increase in a child’s blood lead level, their IQ may drop by around five points. Therefore, lead-containing foods pose the greatest danger to children and adolescents.

Lead poisoning affects children's thinking, judgment ability, reaction speed, reading ability, and attention, leading to poor academic performance and increased dropout rates. Teachers often complain about underperforming students, who might actually be affected by lead poisoning in their brains. Toys and pictures made with lead-based paint or paint are one of the main pathways for lead exposure, causing lead poisoning in infants and toddlers.

Mild cases of lead poisoning can cause weight loss, fatigue, limb pain, pallor, dizziness, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal distension, abdominal pain, constipation, loss of appetite, indigestion, insomnia, metallic taste in the mouth, visible lead lines on gums, memory loss, anemia, neurological diseases, cardiovascular organic diseases, etc. Severe cases may lead to significant anemia, neurological organic diseases, obvious liver and kidney diseases, cardiovascular organic diseases, and respiratory system diseases, even resulting in reduced intelligence. Especially for children in the growth and development stage, they are more sensitive to lead than adults. Lead entering the body has a strong affinity for the nervous system, so the absorption rate of lead in children is several times higher than in adults, making them suffer more severely. Lead poisoning in children can significantly affect their IQ, potentially reducing their IQ by about 20% when they grow up. If a pregnant woman accidentally gets lead poisoning during pregnancy, it can result in miscarriage, stillbirth, or deformed babies. Lead poisoning is difficult to cure, and severe lead poisoning causes irreversible damage to the human body.

Lead enters the human body through three pathways: the respiratory tract, digestive tract, and skin. Organic lead can be directly absorbed into the blood through the skin (such as lead in gasoline). Approximately 90% to 95% of the lead in the human body is stored in bones, with only a small amount present in organs like the liver and spleen, leading to lead poisoning. Lead is mainly metabolized through the kidneys and excreted in urine, but this process is slow.

According to surveys, Chinese residents consume about 82.5 micrograms of lead per day from food, which is below the World Health Organization standard of 86 micrograms per day, but much higher than the levels in Western developed countries, where intake is just a few micrograms per day. We have neglected the harm caused by lead in food for many years. Although China has long established hygiene standards for lead limits in food, compared to international standards, some indicators are much higher. For example, the current lead content standard in grains is 0.4 mg/kg, while the international standard is 0.2 mg/kg; for vegetables, the Chinese standard is 0.2 mg/kg, and the international standard is 0.1 mg/kg. This is an important reason why China is preparing to adjust the lead limit standards in food.

Notably, 80% of children with lead poisoning come from industrial areas and busy traffic zones. As early as the 1970s, the United States and others legislated to require all levels of health institutions to conduct annual blood lead screenings for children aged 0-6, controlling childhood lead poisoning at its source. Currently, China has not included blood lead testing in routine checks for children.
