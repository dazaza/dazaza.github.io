thinks the team is unwilling to embrace him

by 45871139 on 2012-03-07 14:54:04

This article was published in the March 2012 issue of New Sports magazine. If you wish to reprint, please credit the source. On a summer day in 1999, standing in the home dressing room at Bernabeu, Nicolas Anelka couldn’t find his place. No one introduced him to his teammates, no one told him where to sit, and there wasn't even a locker with his name on it. The 19-year-old Anelka could only stand awkwardly as his teammates walked by one by one: McManaman, Hierro, Redondo, Guti... At his first entry into Real Madrid's dressing room, he couldn't understand why he was being ignored until Samuel Eto'o and Geremi whispered the secret, "Be careful! We heard someone questioning the president: 'We already have Morientes, why buy you?'"

"From that moment on, I realized my teammates didn’t welcome me. It hurt me deeply," Anelka recalled in an interview with British magazine 442 in 2008, revealing this little-known episode from his past. "I realized I could only stay in Madrid for a year; otherwise, something worse would happen." From being a prodigy who joined Real Madrid with great fanfare to becoming a discarded talent, Anelka indeed took just a year.

In the 1999-2000 season, he scored only five goals for Real Madrid. What left a deeper impression on fans were the controversies he created off the pitch, such as publicly criticizing coach Bosque's tactics as unsuitable for himself, calling Raul "not a good striker, nor a good person," and once being fined and suspended for 45 days for missing training. In the two legs of the Champions League semifinal, he scored against Bayern Munich both times, but during the championship celebration ceremony in Paris after the final, he seemed like an isolated outsider, estranged from his clubmates.

Anelka's poor experience at Real Madrid is not hard to explain. Wandering players often feel lonely, and his early fame inflated his ego, making him more sensitive and rebellious to external criticism. As a non-Spanish young new recruit, it wasn't surprising for him to face resistance or suppression from local players when joining Real Madrid. Similar incidents have occurred in many clubs, but Anelka mistakenly intensified these emotions and adopted a confrontational stance from the very beginning. He didn't know how to maintain an appropriate distance with his teammates; anyone was either his friend or his enemy. Therefore, Anelka wouldn't actively seek his own place within the team. He mistook his teammates' indifference for hostility, thinking the team didn't want to accept him, so he refused to integrate, acting colder than his teammates.

In world football, there are others besides Anelka who have clashed with team authority due to youthful arrogance, leading to tense situations—Samuel Eto'o, who also had a wandering career and was once a guest at Real Madrid, has also caused trouble with several clubs. But more than a decade later, while Eto'o, once known for his blunt "big mouth" character, has become more tactful, learned to shed tears blessing Inter Milan, and sworn loyalty to Russia's Anzhi Makhachkala by pressing his right hand to his chest, Anelka, despite trying to maintain professional sharpness, remains clumsy in interpersonal communication. Silent and expressionless, rarely smiling, he has never formed close friendships with any teammates at any club he has played for. When he scores a goal, he doesn't celebrate wildly; when he misses a chance, he seldom regrets it. Football seems to be just a routine task for him, and he doesn't care much about the game's outcome, the cheers, jeers, or shouts of fans.

While we marvel at his extraordinary talent, we often feel stifled by his aloofness—these impressions are what Anelka brings to people on the field. However, paradoxically, beneath his cold exterior lies a fiery passion that erupts whenever the team does not meet his expectations. In 1999, the year he was named Premier League Young Player of the Year, Anelka complained that Overmars didn't pass to him; when he decided to leave Arsenal for Real Madrid, he broke ties with his mentor Wenger, refusing phone calls and threatening to skip training; upon moving to Real Madrid, he targeted Bosque and Raul; returning to Paris Saint-Germain, he turned against Louis Fernandez, who had once recognized his talent; on loan to Liverpool, he criticized manager Houllier for "blocking his permanent transfer." Within the French national team, he once demanded Santini "get down on his knees and apologize" and insulted Domenech as "whore-born"—despite Domenech having fought against public opinion to recall him to the Gallic squad. Even upon leaving Chelsea, Anelka remained bitter about the cold treatment he received in his last days there, harshly criticizing Portuguese coach Andre Villas-Boas for revoking his parking spot and demoting him to train with the reserve team. Although Villas-Boas' treatment of Anelka lacked grace, it accurately struck at his hypersensitive, suspicious, and extremely self-respecting personality flaws, forcing the player, who had been the team's top scorer for two consecutive years, to submit a transfer request.

By the end of 2011, Anelka finally parted ways with the longest-serving club of his career (nearly four years), and his frequent conflicts with former clubs unfortunately became a blemish on his career that critics point out. But in reality, Anelka may not be as incorrigibly arrogant as most fans imagine. British football scholar Simon Kuper pointed out that the main reason for Anelka's maladjustment during his time at Real Madrid was his lack of interpersonal skills and communication with teammates. In his book "Soccernomics," Kuper wrote: "After talking with Anelka for half an hour, you'll find that he always lives in his own world, afraid of others, and overall isn't an easy player to deal with. His language ability isn't very good either, since he has been in England for ten years but still has mediocre oral expression. I think he really needs a PR advisor."

Facing the media, Anelka lacks a sense of proportion. On the field, Anelka rarely engages in physical battles with opponents or picks up red or yellow cards, and he doesn't talk much. However, unlike emotional players like Drogba and Rooney, whose moods come and go quickly, once Anelka loses his temper, there’s no turning back. Just like his words to Bernabeu fans while playing for Real Madrid, "Either adapt to me, or stay away,"—black or white, life or death, he refuses to give the other party any negotiating space, nor does he leave himself room for compromise or retreat, or the option to choose again. Anelka, like Tevez, is a troublemaker in the dressing room, but unlike the Argentine beast who ties transfers to homesickness, he openly states that he pursues only two things—money and adventure. He also wrote an autobiography like Ibrahimovic, but showed no regard for human relations, writing that Vieira whipped his face with his genitals—not only ungentlemanly, but also announcing a complete break with his former club Arsenal… His statements and autobiographies may be pure and true, but they are too real to be accepted. Thus, although Anelka dislikes the media fabricating stories, most of the time, it is his own words that provide the media with fuel to amplify the drama. Over time, the roaring media machine gradually formatted, labeled, and even demonized his image. A typical example: the media often refers to Anelka as Le Sulk (Mr. Sulky), implying that his character is moody. Is this really the case? Probably not.

Anelka spent a brief but quiet year far away from the European mainstream league with Fenerbahce, and during Jose Mourinho's and Carlo Ancelotti's tenures at Chelsea, he learned to play with a smile, producing his best season at age thirty. Similarly, those who mock him as a mercenary conveniently forget the precious goals he contributed to each of his employers, and the substantial transfer fees he left behind when departing… To date, Anelka's career record has been quite successful. He won double crowns twice with Arsenal and Chelsea, claimed the UEFA Champions League trophy with Real Madrid, and made significant contributions to Fenerbahce winning the Turkish Super League title. Except for his disappointing performance with Real Madrid, he has had decent scoring records with other teams. However, for perfectionists, Anelka remains a regrettable failure case.

In the 1997 U20 World Cup quarterfinal match between France and Uruguay, Anelka missed a penalty kick. Then-team coach Houllier believed he lacked mental toughness, but admitted this truthfully. On May 21, 2008, in Moscow, Chelsea faced Manchester United in the Champions League final. Anelka's penalty was saved by Van der Sar, resulting in Chelsea's heartbreaking defeat. After the game, Chelsea coach Grant recalled, "He is the most talented player I've ever seen, but I think he lacks the passion to pursue success." Nine years apart, he took two penalties but failed both times. This resembles the epitome of Anelka's career: technically gifted and highly anticipated by fans, yet he seems unable to reach the heights of his potential career.

In the French national team, Anelka participated in a total of 455 minutes in the finals of the World Cup and European Championship without scoring a single goal. He was long excluded from the national team for provoking Santini, but helped France qualify for the South Africa finals with a golden goal against Ireland in late 2009. The following year, at the age of 31, he made his debut in the senior national team but was sent back home early for insulting coach Domeneck and received an 18-game ban, ending his international career. Meanwhile, on the club level, fans who love Anelka have speculated countless fatal beauties about him: if Anelka had never left Arsenal; if he could have integrated with Real Madrid; if he hadn't wasted time at Fenerbahce, Bolton, etc.; if... then what would have happened? Anelka is Anelka, and his life refuses hypotheticals. When Henry returned to Arsenal on loan to continue his legend, Trezeguet joined his childhood idol club River Plate in Argentina, Anelka came to China. "Don't ask why, the truth is—" ESPN commentator Will Tide wrote. "Anelka has a self-belief to break old rules and say goodbye to the past, as well as the confidence and ambition to wander through any famous stadium in the world and conquer local fans. He has achieved success in four leagues and is about to step onto his fifth country (China). The challenges brought by such a career trajectory may not be smaller than proving oneself in the same club." From a prodigy to a late bloomer, Nicolas Anelka takes an unconventional path, drawing a unique U-shaped trajectory in world football. Many fans believe that personality defects hindered Anelka's professional development and led him to make many unreasonable wrong choices, but for Anelka himself, perhaps it is precisely these defects and "mistakes" incomprehensible to the world that have shaped his complete life. In the eyes of mainstream British football circles, joining Shenhua is another mistake Anelka has committed. Commentator Jason Daisy wrote an article titled "Can Anelka Climb China's Great Wall?" using Paul Gascoigne's difficulties in 2003 when he played for Lanzhou Tianma as a warning, questioning Anelka's prospects at Shanghai Shenhua. This doubt is not new; as early as 2000, former Real Madrid coach Bosque had publicly criticized Anelka's adaptability—but the latter managed to score prolifically at Fenerbahce, Manchester City, Bolton, and Chelsea. In other words, as long as Shenhua wants Anelka's goals rather than his smiles and cuteness, this former Premier League Golden Boot winner will not disappoint. The only concern remains Anelka's personality. To this day, Anelka remains cold, arrogant, stubborn, and quick-tempered deep down inside... He was calm under the guidance of Carlo Ancelotti, who excels in motivating player emotions, but in the Chinese Super League, where there is a language barrier, the league's professionalization is relatively backward, and the dressing room interpersonal relationships are not transparent, he might repeat past mistakes, viewing Tiaga, owner Zhu Jun, or even all Shanghai fans as his imaginary enemies when his temper flares. Therefore, this chessboard rumored to be worth over 100 million RMB is actually a broken game with variables. Nevertheless, Shenhua's signing of Anelka deserves Shanghai fans' cheers, because being able to witness this former Premier League champion player up close is always a rare privilege.
