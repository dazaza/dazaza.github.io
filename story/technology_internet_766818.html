Forget Surface 2, the Surface Pro 2 is what's important

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 21:54:50

Last night, Microsoft updated its Surface product series by launching the Surface 2 running on Windows RT and the Surface Pro 2 equipped with Windows Pro. The event not only showcased enhanced configurations but also introduced new peripherals, including the Power Cover, Type Cover with backlighting, a dock, and a dedicated Surface Music Kit for musicians.

Better configurations and more accessories indicate that the Surface is evolving into an increasingly mature product line. If the first generation was considered a trial run, then the second generation clearly carries higher expectations.

However, while the second generation brings many configuration improvements, it has also made the position of the Surface 2 even more awkward. The original purpose of the first-generation Surface RT was to capture the tablet market for Windows, merging ARM architecture for mobility. However, after its release, it faced widespread criticism — although its appearance was eye-catching, its system struggled to compete with iOS and Android. Unfortunately, the newly launched Surface 2 does not address this issue.

Forget about the Surface 2; the Surface Pro 2 is the real focus.

Throughout the entire launch event, the most emphasis on the Surface 2 was placed on its configuration and peripherals, while the crucial ecosystem was barely mentioned. Panos Panay, head of Surface (whom we interviewed not long ago), only briefly mentioned that the Windows Store offers 100,000 apps and games, with new additions like Flipboard, Facebook, and Outlook.

But this is clearly insufficient. Even regarding those 100,000 apps (which is a high number), Panos did not provide much in-depth interpretation. There were no "Killer Apps" showcased, unlike what Windows Phone typically does. From our foreign media hands-on articles, the focus remains largely on appearance and peripherals, with little discussion about the system itself.

From this perspective, the Surface 2 does not offer much improvement over the Surface RT. The Surface Pro 2 is the true highlight of this event.

The Surface Pro 2 features a Haswell processor, which delivers improved performance along with a battery life of up to nine hours. Officially, the Surface Pro 2 is referred to as "the most productive tablet ever created."

The Surface Pro 2 comes with excellent peripherals, a complete ecosystem, and all-day battery life. Initially, Microsoft's goal with the Surface was to counter the impact of iPads and Android tablets on the Windows system. The Surface Pro 2 boasts a complete ecosystem, long battery life, and powerful performance, aligning perfectly with Microsoft's definition of the Surface. Therefore, forget about the Surface 2; the Surface Pro 2 is Microsoft's true trump card.

A niche product, but one of great significance

The label "the most productive tablet" straightforwardly suggests that the Surface Pro 2 is more suited for business professionals rather than general consumers. Hence, the price of the Surface Pro 2 starts at $899, almost double that of an iPad, let alone low-cost Android tablets.

However, the Surface Pro 2 holds significant importance for Microsoft. It proves that a full-function Windows system tablet can achieve both strong performance and long battery life. In the future, derivatives of the Surface Pro 2 could become thinner, lighter, and more affordable, undoubtedly appealing to the consumer market. From an experiential standpoint, a full-function Windows system adapts well to touch controls, similar to Windows RT.

The Surface Pro 2 has already successfully mobilized the full-function Windows experience, so why bother with Windows RT? These two-in-one devices, strongly promoted by Microsoft, are becoming a new product category, potentially becoming the norm for future PCs. Such PCs can transform into tablets at any time, reducing the motivation for users to buy another tablet.

Microsoft has already revealed its ambitious plans in hardware, aiming to drive the development of its ecosystem through hardware, as evidenced by the acquisition of Nokia. After the triumvirate of Microsoft, Google, and Apple stabilizes, and as phones, tablets, and PCs continue to converge, Surface will become Microsoft's main brand going forward.
