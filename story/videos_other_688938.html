What will we do if Kodak goes under?

by songsdp82pm on 2012-03-03 16:26:29

Kodak has fallen, what should we do? In the face of technology, no one can remain supreme forever; the times will discard all laggards. For old-established enterprises, they either die in stubbornness and arrogance or rejuvenate through continuous innovation. The lessons from Kodak's collapse offer more inspiration to Chinese enterprises actively exploring the path of transformation and innovation. (Workers' Daily, January 11, 2012, Page 05)

Kodak, once the industry's leader and absolute top dog, has maintained a leading position in the global imaging industry since its establishment in 1880, creating countless firsts in the industry. Kodak's 130-year history is a microcosm of the development of the world's imaging industry. At the beginning of the New Year, Kodak declared bankruptcy, shocking and saddening many people. People couldn't help but ask: What happened to Kodak? What lessons does Kodak's collapse offer to Chinese enterprises?

Kodak's Misfortune: Arrogance, Bad Luck, Judgment Errors

In fact, Kodak had many advantages: heavy investment in scientific research, continuous improvement in product quality, maintaining good relations with local communities, and owning multiple patents in digital photography. In 1975, it created the world's earliest digital camera. However, compared to competitors like Fuji and Olympus, Kodak was too slow, still focusing primarily on the traditional analog camera film business. In the competition for sponsors at the 1984 Los Angeles Olympics, Fuji exploited Kodak's overconfidence and defeated it, successfully penetrating Kodak's home base - the American market.

Former Kodak consultant and Harvard Business School professor Kantor stated: Kodak's presidents pursued product perfection but paid little attention to product innovation and the application of high technology. They rarely listened to outside advice. Due to the conservatism of the company's management, they were satisfied with their market share and monopoly position in the traditional film market, lacked forward-looking analysis and research on the market, reacted slowly when the photographic film era entered the digital era, made indecisive policies, had ambiguous market positioning, and lacked determination in product transformation. They failed to adjust the company's operating focus and department structure in time, missing development opportunities and causing the company to stagnate. By 2000, Kodak's digital products only sold for $3 billion, accounting for only 22% of its total revenue. In 2002, Kodak's product digitization rate was only about 25%, while its competitor Fuji had reached 60%.

Kodak also failed to correctly recognize emerging markets. The company hoped that China's rising middle class would buy a lot of film. But these middle classes quickly found that digital cameras were cooler, more economical, and more practical. Additionally, Kodak's luck wasn't good. Kodak hoped that the tens of thousands of chemicals developed during the process of film production could be repurposed for drug production, but unfortunately, Kodak's pharmaceutical division operated poorly and was sold off in the 1990s.

Uniquely, since last year, Nokia, HP, and other industry giants have retreated due to insufficient innovation. In 2011, Google acquired Motorola for approximately $12.5 billion; Apple replaced Nokia as the world's largest mobile phone manufacturer. Many large companies are struggling simultaneously, partly due to the poor overall global economic environment, but more so because these companies lack innovation capabilities and respond slowly to new technologies, ultimately leading to falling behind step by step and gradually losing their foothold, ending up being acquired or going bankrupt.

Lessons from Kodak's Collapse for Chinese Enterprises:

Currently, our country places great emphasis on the importance of scientific and technological innovation and set long-term goals for technological development years ago, aiming to build an innovative country by 2020, making technological development a strong support for economic and social development.

However, there are still many factors in reality that restrict enterprise innovation. First, Chinese enterprises are generally short-sighted, lacking an innovative spirit, greedy for immediate benefits, without long-term planning, and investing very little in scientific research. This mindset is extremely detrimental to innovation and must increase investment in scientific research, encourage and support scientific and technological innovation, reward inventions, and incorporate innovation into medium- and long-term plans.

Secondly, China still has shortcomings in intellectual property protection: piracy cannot be completely eradicated, intellectual property supervision systems are not perfect, and enforcement is insufficient. The state should establish a complete intellectual property supervision system, effectively protect intellectual property rights, and severely punish all forms of piracy and actions detrimental to intellectual property protection.

Thirdly, enterprises lack sufficient motivation for innovation, and the phenomenon of replacing technology with labor remains significant. Therefore, the state and enterprises need to formulate scientific and technological innovation plans and objectives, establish innovation concepts and heights, and phase out industries and businesses that are labor-intensive and have low technological added value at the right time. The state should provide adequate financial support to high-tech and advanced enterprises with R&D capabilities, enhancing enterprises' scientific and technological innovation capabilities and motivation from institutional and financial perspectives.

Fourthly, enterprises need to develop new strategies to meet the challenges of the economic downturn period. One, strive for more profits from traditional businesses; two, prepare for the transition to new businesses; three, vigorously develop new businesses.

"Stones from other mountains can polish jade." We sincerely hope that the event of Kodak's collapse will sound the alarm for Chinese enterprises, enabling them to recognize the direction, avoid detours, develop healthily, and thrive forever.
