Ren Dan

The main ingredients are peppermint ice, talc, catechu, clove, wood incense, fennel, cardamom, and tangerine peel. It has the effect of clearing heat and relieving summer-heat, avoiding foulness, and stopping vomiting, making it a common medicine for preventing heatstroke in summer. It is mainly used for headaches, dizziness, nausea, abdominal pain, and discomfort due to climate change caused by high temperatures. This medicine can promote intestinal peristalsis and relieve intestinal spasm. It is suitable for those suffering from heatstroke, acute gastroenteritis, and excessive phlegm cough.

Salted Boiling Water

Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) refers to "boiling water as the king of all medicines." From a nutritional perspective, no sugary drink is more valuable than plain boiled water because pure boiled water can be quickly absorbed by the stomach, enter the bloodstream to perform metabolic functions, while also regulating body temperature and cleaning the internal environment of the human body. Drinking boiled water should choose fresh cool boiled water (20°C to 25°C) after natural cooling. This kind of boiled water has unique biological activity, easily penetrates cell membranes into cells, and is quickly absorbed and utilized. It's best to add some salt when drinking boiled water. In the summer with high temperatures, excessive sweating reduces the amount of salt in the body, causing the body's osmotic pressure to lose balance, leading to heatstroke. Drinking more salted boiling water or salt tea can replenish the lost salt in the body, thereby achieving the effect of preventing heatstroke.

Tea Water

Experienced health doctors often guide people to replenish potassium by drinking tea. Potassium is an important trace element in the human body. Potassium can maintain the normal function of nerves and muscles, especially the normal movement of the heart muscle. If there is a lack of potassium, a person will feel tired and weak, and their heat tolerance will decrease. Severe potassium deficiency can lead to arrhythmia and general muscle weakness. Tea contains a relatively large amount of potassium, accounting for about 1.5% of its weight. Potassium is easily excreted through sweat, so appropriately warm tea water should be the preferred beverage in summer.

Mung Bean Soup

Mung bean soup has a unique effect of eliminating summer-heat and reducing fever. According to TCM, mung beans have the effects of eliminating summer-heat, invigorating qi, detoxifying heat, quenching thirst, and promoting diuresis and reducing swelling, which can prevent heatstroke. Relevant experiments have shown that mung beans have significant effects on treating arteriosclerosis, reducing cholesterol in the blood, and protecting the liver. The only drawback is that mung beans are too cold in nature, and people with physical weakness should not consume them.

Bitter Melon

Some people consider watermelon as the treasure for relieving summer-heat, but theoretically, the most appetizing, refreshing, and cooling melon food is bitter melon. There has been a long-standing saying in Chinese folk culture that "bitter taste can clear heat" and "bitter taste can strengthen the stomach." According to TCM, bitter melon tastes bitter, is cold in nature, and can clear heat and reduce fire. The slightly bitter taste of bitter melon stimulates saliva and gastric juice secretion after eating, greatly increasing appetite and helping to clear heat and prevent heatstroke. Therefore, eating bitter melon in summer is appropriate. Fresh bitter melon juice or decoction is beneficial for auxiliary dietary therapy for red eyes due to liver fire, dry mouth and thirst due to stomach heat, stomach pain, and damp-heat dysentery. Bitter melon brewed as cool tea refreshes and soothes the mind after drinking.

Chen Vinegar

In summer, people drink more water, and the amount of gastric acid decreases accordingly, causing loss of appetite. An appropriate amount of vinegar can increase the concentration of gastric acid, stimulate saliva production, open the stomach, and help digestion. If vinegar is added during cooking, it can increase the amount and concentration of gastric acid, thereby increasing appetite. Summer is the season when intestinal infectious diseases are prevalent, and eating vinegar can enhance the bactericidal effect of the gastrointestinal tract. Additionally, if a few drops of vinegar are added during cooking, it will reduce the loss of vitamin C in vegetables and facilitate the absorption of iron from food.
