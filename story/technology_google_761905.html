The convergence between Android and Chrome OS is worth watching.

by geekzhang on 2013-05-15 08:15:33

As the saying goes, "When the mountains are about to burst with rain, the wind fills the building." The Google I/O of last year still lingers in our memories, while this year's event is just around the corner. This time, what kind of topics will Larry Page and Sergey Brin create?The Google I/O of last year had two memorable highlights: the new type of smart device, Google Glass, and Android 4.1, which brought new changes in design. Even now, Google Glass remains a highly discussed device, with mixed reviews from the public. However, for this year's Google I/O, it is already a relic of last year and cannot once again take center stage. Recently, in the first half of the year, Google has continuously released information about Google Glass, describing its basic product form. Unless it immediately releases a second-generation product or achieves a significant technological breakthrough, Google Glass is not very suitable as the main event for this conference.As for the Android operating system, it is naturally one of the main attractions at the conference. However, based on recent news, unless its version number advances from 4.x to 5.0, it won't easily attract attention. In fact, after several years of development, smartphones have become common items, and their topic value has weakened. From a product perspective, whether it be Android phone hardware or software, both are quite mature and do not suit radical advancements that would startle people.So, what could startle people? Perhaps Chrome OS, though this product has always been underestimated.Reviewing the news from the past six months, there hasn't been a lack of updates about Chrome, and these updates have considerable imaginative potential. For instance:We can see that Google's approach is similar to Apple's—integrating the characteristics of desktop and mobile operating systems to provide a unified user experience. What makes Google different from Apple is that Chrome OS and Android are entirely separate operating systems. Their design philosophies, user experiences, and even system kernels differ significantly, making unification difficult. After all, Android was something Google acquired, whereas Chrome OS is its own creation.However, with Andy Rubin no longer overseeing Android's development, its future independence is hard to guarantee. Its future direction will gradually lose Rubin's personal imprint. Some believe that Android and Chrome OS will eventually integrate. Since Android already exists, Google doesn't need to reinvent the wheel; they just need to gradually refine the product, transforming it into something more aligned with Google's DNA over time.Last year, Google added Chrome Packaged Apps, allowing web applications developed based on Chrome to possess local characteristics. They also offer excellent features such as cross-platform development, simplicity, and ease of deployment. In other words, what Firefox OS and Ubuntu are promoting—web-based operating systems and a unified desktop and mobile experience—is something Chrome OS is capable of achieving.In reality, Sundar Pichai, Rubin's successor and also the head of Chrome, aims to unify the experience between Android and Chrome. But after five or six years of development, it's impossible to completely change Android all at once, unless they scrap Android and develop a new operating system from scratch. Google's advantage lies in the fact that it doesn't need to start anew; it can simply take Chrome OS and put it on phones. Google has the full capability to do this; it’s only a matter of whether they choose to.Perhaps, in the future, Android will become more like Chrome OS, using cloud and web applications to transform the Internet into a large operating system. At that point, it's unclear whether Android should be called a Chrome OS phone. In any case, the trends regarding Chrome OS at this year's Google I/O conference deserve close attention.
