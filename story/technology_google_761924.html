How Samsung is slowly eroding Google's Android OS

by geekzhang on 2013-05-16 17:01:39

On May 9, Samsung announced a collaboration with ITV, the largest independent broadcaster in the UK. The Android version of ITV's mobile player app, ITVplayer, will be exclusively available for Samsung mobile devices. This means that it can be freely downloaded and used on Samsung devices like the Galaxy S4, Note 8.0, and Galaxy Mega, but not on other Android devices such as Google's Nexus 4 or HTC One.

Although publicly everyone talks about cooperation, there are many controversies between Google and Samsung regarding Android issues. For instance, Andy Rubin, the former head of Android, once privately expressed that Samsung is a threat to Google. Therefore, in response to this collaboration, Trustedreviews wrote an article suggesting that Samsung's move would inevitably accelerate the fragmentation of Android, further damaging Android, which might be a result Samsung would gladly see. The article presented several points:

- The weaker Android becomes, the stronger Samsung gets.

There is a hidden fact here: Samsung is willing to harm Android.

Every generation of Samsung's Android phones comes with custom TouchWiz and a permanent package of Samsung apps, and each new generation has more than the last. For example, the Galaxy S4 has custom apps and a highly modified version of Android that take up 7GB of memory, which is 1GB more than the Galaxy S3 and over twice as much as a standard Android installation partition. It's as if Samsung has built another operating system on top of Android.

This is a classic Trojan horse strategy. With Android's rapid development, Samsung wants to use its power to win the market, then control the fate of Android and become the sole beneficiary, just like Apple. Samsung adds software to Android phones without restraint, claiming it enhances Android's performance, but once Google upgrades Android with similar features, Samsung responds aggressively.

Given the deep fragmentation of Android, most users find that only Samsung's Android phones are worth buying because only Samsung can upgrade the installed operating system. Of course, once users have alternatives, Samsung's strategy becomes worthless.

- Samsung wants to be the next Apple.

The alternative is Tizen, a mobile operating system developed by the Linux Foundation, but primarily funded by Intel and Samsung. Although Tizen is purely open-source, it has scared off many competitors due to its two main investors.

Tizen also uses the TouchWiz desktop, so at first glance, it looks very similar to Samsung's Android phones. Tizen can easily run Android apps with simple code adjustments, and Samsung could secure exclusive rights to some well-known native code apps through certain collaborations.

So Samsung's strategy is to use Android to crush competitors, then use Tizen to inherit the advantages of Android apps and promise consumers that Android cannot become a closed system. To open up another operating system market, Samsung's strategy is to first introduce Android, damage it from the inside out, then retreat and focus on developing the new system market.

Samsung already has a track record. Bada ended in failure (later merged into Tizen), and Samsung will undoubtedly put more effort into this attempt. A new Tizen phone is set to be launched in September.

Of course, Tizen is only part of it. Apple's experience has taught Samsung that having its own platform and autonomous ecosystem is equally important. Samsung already leads in the TV market, and displays and laptops are gradually aligning with Samsung phones. Samsung phones also have dedicated audio docks. The multi-terminal联动form is taking shape.

Don't feel sorry for Google. While Samsung tries to control Android, Google is also planning to invade the territories of Windows, Mac, and iOS. If you visit the Google homepage with a non-Chrome browser, you'll be constantly reminded to install Chrome. When using Google apps on iOS devices, they form their own Trojan horse ecosystem. Since the beginning of this month, Google has intensified this offensive by tying Gmail, Google Search, Google Voice Search, and Google Maps together, seemingly determined to kick out Apple's main apps.

Try using all the Google apps on your iPhone, and you'll definitely wonder why you don't just switch to an Android phone. Using one's own tricks against oneself isn't a good thing.

- Android's troubles

Nevertheless, Google still reaps considerable rewards. Android's openness encourages more people to collaborate on development, making it a formidable competitor to iOS in smart mobile terminal devices.

For Samsung, this is just the beginning. Compared to Amazon, which seems reckless, Samsung takes every step carefully. If Amazon follows up its tablet products with a phone, the strong alliance between Samsung and Amazon will further hit Android.

In a sense, Google is under attack from all sides. The EU anti-monopoly investigation has already filed a lawsuit against Google, accusing it of monopolistic tendencies in search, while Android has already captured 70% of the smartphone market share, reaching the critical point of monopoly.

Google is working hard to find solutions. Letting cooperative developers use the official version of Android makes it easier to upgrade the phone system, further reducing fragmentation trends, and providing consumers with a relatively consistent Android experience. This may cause some developers to leave, but it appropriately lowers Android's market share, bringing the anti-monopoly investigation to a close.

Of course, Samsung might be one of those who leave, but that's not necessarily a bad thing.
