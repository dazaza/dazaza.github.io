Mobile Internet Interview: Qin Dai, the "Big Head King" of Mobile Phones, Speaks on SoLoMoCo

by jianfei1895 on 2011-08-26 12:56:58

Introduction: With the advent of the 3G era, mobile internet has gained prominence and become a trendsetter of this technological age, riding on the development of mobile communications and the flourishing of the internet. Against this backdrop, Sina Tech launched a series of interviews on mobile internet, focusing on the development trends, challenges, and issues faced by practitioners in the mobile internet sector.

Video: Qin Dai, CEO of Phone Bighead, discusses SoLoMoCo. Media Source: Sina Tech

Only to realize the essence upon seeing you.

Currently, entrepreneurs in the mobile internet industry can roughly be divided into three categories: one group transitioned from the internet industry to mobile internet as a natural extension; another group directly chose mobile internet, foreseeing its potential even when the industry outlook was unclear, quietly striving until the dawn of mobile internet arrived or perished just before it; the last group transitioned from the telecommunications industry to mobile internet, shifting their focus after the "prosperity" of mobile communication and value-added services faded. Wang Qindai, CEO of Phone Bighead, is an example of the third category of entrepreneurs, humorously referring to himself as a "veteran."

Naturally, his comprehensive experience and ongoing involvement in the development of telecommunications, internet, and mobile internet are the reasons I invited him for this inaugural interview in our mobile internet series. I believe that only through experience can one truly understand, gain insight, and have faith. Only through experience can one know the joys and pains of this industry.

This interview marks the third "face-to-face" session with Mr. Wang Qindai. In this edition, regarding the judgment of entering and exiting industries, he emphasizes that being "interested" is most important. He hopes that the industries he engages in can change people's lives, which is indeed the perfect realm we all strive for in our work.

Regarding the theme of this issue, SoLoMo was first proposed in February this year by John Doerr, a renowned venture capital partner in the U.S. It was further discussed by Charles Chao, CEO of Sina, and practitioners in the mobile internet industry at the Mobile Internet Conference in April. Over the months, it has become the most popular term in the mobile internet sector. From Wang Qindai's interpretation, I discovered that SoLoMo is not merely a fashionable concept but also an inevitable outcome of the development of mobile communication and the internet. Social, local, and mobile will continue to influence the development trends of mobile internet.

Wang Qindai stated that mobile internet has gone through three phases: the first phase being the regrettable SP (Service Provider) phase, the second phase being the "crouching and wanting to make money but not yet making it" phase, and the third phase being the stage of killer applications unique to mobile devices. In this phase, SoLoMo provides the most appropriate description of mobile internet.

However, Wang Qindai added Co (Commercial) to SoLoMo, emphasizing the importance of a business model. Without a business model, mobile internet is incomplete.

"Following it to uncover unprecedented user needs related to SoLoMo, if you can meet such unprecedented user needs, you have the opportunity to become a disruptive or killer application," Wang Qindai remarked.

At the same time, he believed that merely identifying the SoLoMo concept without finding a corresponding product application or service is meaningless.

"Has the boat of mobile internet reached the red sea without passing through the blue sea? Everyone suddenly shifts their focus from the internet to mobile internet, flocking to the SoLoMo camp, creating turbulent waves. But only when the tide recedes will you discover who lies stranded on the beach and who reaches the other side of the ocean," Wang Qindai said.

Below is the full interview transcript:

Sina Tech: Hello netizens! I am Zong Xiuqian from the Sina Tech channel. It's great to meet you again. With the arrival of the 3G era, driven by the development of mobile communications and the thriving internet, mobile internet has gained prominence, becoming a trailblazer of this technological age. Against this backdrop, Sina Tech naturally cannot miss the opportunity to witness the industry's development. We hope to regularly invite guests to our interview room to discuss various aspects of mobile internet. We hope this interview can be long-term, continuous, and part of a series, and today is just the beginning.

As the opening of this series of interviews, we have invited a heavyweight guest to discuss a popular concept, SoLoMo. Please welcome today's guest, Mr. Wang Qindai, CEO of Phone Bighead. Would you like to greet our netizens?

Wang Qindai: Good afternoon, everyone! It's a pleasure to have this opportunity to exchange views on the development trends of mobile internet and possible killer or disruptive applications.

Sina Tech: Mr. Wang, we chose you as the leading guest for this series with careful consideration. You have worked in world-renowned telecommunications companies like AT&T, Lucent, and Nortel, founded AsiaInfo and Huayou Century, and then moved into mobile internet. Throughout your career, you have made several impressive transitions. How do you possess such strong judgment to predict the overall direction of mobile internet development? Each time, you enter during significant growth periods and exit just before they decline, moving into another rising field.

Wang Qindai: You've summarized my career quite well - telecommunications, internet, and mobile internet are the three main segments. What I originally studied had little relevance to these fields. Many colleagues, friends, partners, and co-workers have ventured into areas unrelated to their studies. Mine might be slightly more connected, as I studied laser physics. At the time, I focused on various types of laser equipment, their physical principles, and potential applications.

One developmental area of laser physics is communication, such as fiber optic communication, where the original signal source was a laser. When I graduated, I first chose communication because, at that time, the deployment of communication networks and the popularization of communication terminals significantly altered people's lifestyles, working methods, and even entertainment styles.

What interests me, or what we all find interesting, is how the popularization of a particular application technology and system deployment can truly change our daily lives, work, and entertainment. If you can be involved in such changes, you'll find it fascinating. Initially, when I graduated, communication fulfilled this purpose and produced such results.

However, once you immerse yourself in the communication industry, whether in sales, marketing, or technical roles, you'll realize that the rise of the internet is the future. The internet will significantly impact and potentially replace traditional communication equipment, means, and methods.

My first stage was working at AT&T, the American Telephone & Telegraph Company, equivalent to China's earliest postal and telecommunications department, telecommunications headquarters, plus current operators. AT&T was the sole long-distance and local telephone company in the U.S., providing both equipment and telecommunication services. Next was Nortel, one of North America's largest providers of communication equipment, terminals, and technologies, with certain expertise in wireless communications. This marked the first stage.

The second stage came when we realized that the internet would be the dominant force influencing people's lifestyles, learning methods, and entertainment over the next ten to twenty years. That realization inspired us to eagerly learn about and understand the internet. Around that time, there was an opportunity to join a team returning from overseas to start AsiaInfo. AsiaInfo performed exceptionally well, positioning itself as China's internet architect. During that period, large-scale internet projects like 163, 165, and 169 were largely completed by teams led by AsiaInfo, including equipment, system integration, design, provision, and final implementation.

After leaving AsiaInfo, I founded Huayou Century, where we entered wireless internet. The idea was simple: the internet is the future, wireless is the future, and combining them forms wireless internet, which could significantly impact our surroundings and loved ones over the next ten to twenty years. We believed it had more promising prospects. Thus, at Huayou Century, we began offering mobile internet-related services.

From technology to communication products and internet construction, we progressed to mobile internet services. Throughout, I believe in adapting to trends, maintaining interest in new things, and living life in sync with the times.

Sina Tech: Recently, I've noticed many people discussing the concept of SoLoMo. This concept was first introduced in February this year by John Doerr, a prominent venture capitalist in the U.S. Later, in April, Charles Chao, CEO of Sina, also mentioned it at the Mobile Internet Conference, stating that future networks would develop in the direction of SoLoMo - social, local, and mobile - becoming the trend of internet development. Do you think this concept is merely conceptual or can genuinely guide development?

Wang Qindai: Later, we joked with a few friends about adding CO to SoLoMo, forming SoLoMoCo. This concept was initially presented around February this year when I saw our investors, KPMG, referencing Mary Meeker's report. Mary Meeker, known as the first analyst of the American internet, introduced SoLoMo and conducted some analysis. This was my earliest exposure to it in January or February. What you mentioned is intriguing; at the Mobile Internet Conference, Charles Chao was the keynote speaker in the morning, but I may not have heard him discuss the network's development towards SoLoMoCo. In the afternoon, I attended the first Mobile Internet Forum, assuming I was the only one heavily emphasizing SoLoMoCo.

Sina Tech: Because great minds think alike.

Wang Qindai: Exactly. I have my own observations and opinions. I believe mobile internet will go through three stages. The first stage jokingly referred to as the regrettable SP phase involved companies like AirMobile, Huayou Century, TOM, and Lingtong, which were pioneers during this period. Through their efforts, Huayou Century became the fastest to list on NASDAQ within three and a half years. However, summarizing the first stage, all SP companies, including these four flagships, succeeded due to their early mover advantage and fortunate timing. They provided ringtones, images, simple games, news, weather forecasts, etc., directly charging users via mobile phones. This was the early stage.

The first stage quickly passed due to two reasons. First, the mobile value-added services provided by SP lacked core value. Why? Offering pictures, ringtones, small games, news, weather forecasts, etc., on mobile phones could all be freely obtained on the internet. They lacked irreplaceable value, and over time, users felt paying one yuan for a picture or two yuan for a song wasn't worth it unless impulsive. It couldn't serve as a fundamental, long-term need.

Second, the value chain was incomplete. Service providers, whether content or service-based, couldn't sustain themselves independently. Why? The entire network was established, owned, and operated by telecom operators. To reach users for marketing or collect payments, all marketing, promotion, and payment channels were concentrated in one to three operators' hands. Operators promoting your services brought you users; operators collecting payments enabled your business model. Without operator support, you had nothing.

Since only a few could provide promotion and payment services—just three powerful entities—and tens of thousands could offer pictures, music, small games, and information, the value was insufficient. Thus, we considered the SP business model unsustainable, lacking core competitiveness. The first generation of SP would fade, as I noted on Weibo: that generation earned money effortlessly lying down.

The second era, jokingly called "crouching and wanting to make money but not yet making it," includes today's Phone Bighead and many other mobile internet companies. They transplant good internet applications or services onto mobile phones. Many companies excel in this field, like UCWeb in navigation and browsers, 3G portals, clients, and platforms. Phone Bighead originally offered news aggregation, music aggregation, video aggregation, and reading aggregation. All these services existed on the internet, extending and transplanting them to mobile phones for users to enjoy PC-based content, services, and applications. This marks the second generation.

Why do I say "crouching and wanting to make money but struggling"? Personally, I believe it's a value chain issue. A good business requires a solid value chain. Mobile internet's value chain involves three layers: first, the terminal—this phone must be capable of accessing the internet. Early functional phones couldn't access the internet or MPK, let alone enjoy internet content, services, and applications. Then smartphones emerged, allowing better internet access though still uncomfortable compared to PCs. Second, the network—if 2G was unreliable and slow, 2.5G was marginally usable, and 2.75G was barely functional, 3G took years to become widespread in China. Third, a viable application must monetize effectively. Even if it uniquely satisfies a user need, how do you collect payment conveniently? This is the third layer of infrastructure—third-party convenient payment systems.

Thus, the second generation of mobile internet transplanted internet services, applications, and businesses onto mobile phones. Some have been doing this for over six years, others for three to four years. Due to the incompleteness of the three infrastructure layers, it has been challenging and unprofitable.

The third stage, recently often mentioned, represents what I consider the third stage or these three stages, which may vary depending on individual perspectives. For instance, Kai-Fu Lee and Lei Jun might have different views. I believe the third stage involves discovering killer or disruptive applications unique to mobile phones. Traditional computers extend from the internet, but mobile phones bring unprecedented possibilities. In other words, the SOLOMO era may arrive. Why? Among the three infrastructure layers, one has begun to break through and evolve rapidly—the smartphone or client. Smartphones boil down to two points: iPhones transformed uncomfortable mobile internet use into enjoyable experiences, and Android or gPhones democratized this enjoyment to mid-to-low-end users. Originally, using the internet on phones was inconvenient or fragmented, but touch-screen iPhones made it effortless and enjoyable. Consequently, during fragmented times away from PCs, users could easily access the internet. While iPhones targeted high-end users, Android systems brought iPhone-like experiences to the masses, with prices ranging from hundreds to thousands of dollars. The existence of touch-screen smartphones signifies the beginning of a crucial breakthrough in the value chain or infrastructure. What can smartphones do? For instance, smartphones allow constant mobility and activity unlike desktops. Secondly, wherever you go, smartphones can determine your location, unlike computers. Additionally, smartphones contain contact lists, enable instant photography and videography, and offer mature applications previously unavailable on larger devices.

On such application technologies, there's potential to meet unprecedented user needs by leveraging these technologies effectively. What are these needs? As explained earlier, regardless of willingness, we live in a social era. Before virtual networks and computers, we socialized but were limited by distance and time. Virtual networks drastically reduced social costs, enabling interactions with more people irrespective of distance. Don't narrowly define social networking as merely dating; I disapprove of such interpretations. Social interaction is a fundamental human need, and the internet greatly expands its possibilities while reducing costs. Therefore, we refer to it as a social era. Nowadays, we might read news, watch movies, listen to music, shop, and engage in numerous activities starting from social networks. We prefer to see what our friends, role models, celebrities, or experts are doing, appreciating, listening to, recommending, watching, or purchasing, along with their opinions on those purchases. Previously, we desired such insights but lacked a constantly accessible and convenient virtual social network to obtain them. Now that we can access such information, we often bypass web1.0 sites for news or content and instead consult our social networks to see what our favorite and trusted friends, opinion leaders, and industry peers are engaging with. They might have already filtered out what we like, and obtaining insights through them constitutes what we call Social.

I believe it's a long-standing demand that the internet offers an opportunity to release.

The second is Local, combined with mobile phones, characterized by immediate accessibility. Immediate accessibility differs entirely from mere imagination. What does immediate accessibility mean? It means having something here or within ten minutes of walking. This isn't possible on the virtual internet. For example, needing to buy something immediately defines Local, with its distinctive feature. Mobile means anytime, anywhere with a phone, complemented by a business model (CEO). Together, these elements represent Social, Local, Mobile, and even SOLOMO, best experienced on today's smartphones. Following this path uncovers unprecedented user demands related to SOLOMO, meeting which could lead to disruptive or killer applications.

Sina Tech: After this concept was introduced, many people used it for hype and promotion. Do you think there's a risk of blind following or a bubble forming?

Sina Tech: Critically analyzing, first, SOLOMO applications will certainly exist, but will they satisfy unprecedented demands at this moment and among this group of people? This, I believe, requires practical testing, trying to create such products, launching them to the market, and gaining market feedback to confirm if they truly meet unprecedented user needs. Second, are these applications currently favored by users with sufficient frequency, intensity, and stickiness? Personally, I think this opens a door and gives direction, allowing for good attempts and exploration. Reflecting, are SOLOMO applications necessarily killer applications? Are all applications labeled SOLOMO destined to succeed?

I jokingly tweeted yesterday, "Is the boat of mobile internet reaching the red sea without passing through the blue sea?" Suddenly, everyone, whether individuals or groups, whether imprisoned in the internet or pioneers in mobile internet, flocked to the SOLOMO camp. Personally, I feel the waves are tumultuous, and only when the tide recedes will we discover who lies stranded on the beach and who reaches the other shore. From this perspective, finding merely a concept without truly discovering a suitable product or service is meaningless. Second, finding a potential service or product but lacking the ability to develop, promote, operate, maintain, and continuously iterate it leads to nothing. Returning to entrepreneurship, I still believe keeping up with the times is essential, but balancing your foundation, capabilities, specialties, and suitability is crucial. Moreover, relying solely on concepts for sufficient funding should no longer be feasible.

Sina Tech: Including Chinese concept stocks, similar issues arise. Some companies benefited from these concepts in the past, but perhaps in the future, NASDAQ will be more cautious. Of course, every industry follows the TOP3 rule, and the mobile internet industry will be no exception. Ultimately, a few major players will emerge and form a general landscape.

Wang Qindai: I completely agree. Every industry has its TOP3, but when the industry base is very large, such as e-commerce, search engines, or mobile internet, involving vast user groups and achieving significant scale, it diversifies. Diversification occurs in two ways: vertical segmentation and regional segmentation. For instance, search engines: Baidu offers general parallel searches for anything, providing the broadest, most timely, and ordinary answers. What do we do when searching on Fang.com or Dazhong Dianping?

Sina Tech: These revolve around our needs.

Wang Qindai: These are all searches. Our daily needs require extensive searches. When searching for good apartments in Beijing, I could theoretically use Baidu, but I wouldn't. Instead, I'd use Fang.com or 58同城. These are vertical searches. Have you heard of Jingtan.com? Baidu collaborated with the Beijing News to establish Jingtan.com, specializing in Beijing's dining, drinking, playing, and entertainment, claiming to provide the most detailed, comprehensive, and timely information about Beijing's lifestyle. This is localized search. When a field or industry becomes massive, beyond general services, vertical and regional services emerge. Taobao is huge, but JD.com, Vancl, and countless others exist—
