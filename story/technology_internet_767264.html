Comment: Ma and Alibaba emerge from the jungle

by anonymous on 2013-10-11 19:11:30

“Out of the Jungle” - Jack Ma and Alibaba

This time, Jack Ma and Alibaba are challenging the stock listing rules of the Hong Kong Stock Exchange, with the battle being prolonged. I sincerely hope that Ma and Alibaba win and list successfully in Hong Kong.

Listing in Hong Kong using Alibaba's customized "partnership" system is beneficial to Alibaba, beneficial to the two major shareholders, Softbank and Yahoo, and most beneficial to Ma himself — indirectly controlling the partners and influencing the board seats, allowing for continued control of the company with a smaller percentage of shares. But don't forget, this also benefits future entrepreneurs, especially tech entrepreneurs, who wish to list in Hong Kong: a new system has been created — founders won't lose control over the direction of their companies due to heavy dilution of shares upon listing, thus being swayed by capital.

If you think that Google and Facebook were correct in setting up "dual-class structures" in the U.S. securities market, where A-shares and B-shares have entirely different voting rights, and maintaining control through "voting agreements" allowing Page, Brin, and Zuckerberg to continue ensuring control, then there's no reason from the start to consider Ma's attempt to maintain control over the company's direction under unrestricted stock reduction as a selfish, rule-breaking, and shareholder interest-ignoring behavior — besides, both of Alibaba's current major shareholders, Softbank and Yahoo, have expressed support for this move.

The only issue is: the stock price performance after the last listing and the approach taken during the 2011 Alipay VIE incident have made American capital markets highly cautious about Alibaba and Ma himself, making it unprecedentedly difficult to exercise control following the precedent set by Google and Facebook and smoothly list in the U.S. In the American capital market, Ma and Alibaba’s credibility has indeed dropped significantly. But still, if we don't consider Ma's personal strategy, should his invented "partnership system" be truly accepted in the Hong Kong capital market, the beneficiaries will not just be Alibaba itself. At least, we might hear fewer stories about company founders being expelled by the will of capital — more examples prove that at least in the internet and tech industries, companies without their founders at the helm die faster.

Laws should not be the killers of institutional innovation but should leave room for corporate institutional innovation, allowing the market to test the vitality of innovative systems — such common sense doesn't need much elaboration. However, the difficulty for Alibaba and Ma in challenging Hong Kong's securities regulatory system, along with the complexity behind it, the twists and turns, and the mixed reactions from outside, make this event even more dramatic. Up until now, I still optimistically believe that Alibaba will definitely list in Hong Kong. Both the U.S. and mainland China (which is completely unreliable) are merely feints. The more twists and turns, the more thrilling the matter seems.

Thus, I can't help but marvel that such an unfounded crazy challenge, in China's internet sector, only Ma would dare to play with.

Look at who Ma challenges each time: the listing incident challenges Hong Kong's securities regulatory system; "Ali Finance" challenges state-owned banks and traditional credit lending and financial products; "Cainiao Logistics" challenges traditional basic logistics construction methods... The threads behind these "big events" are all very clear: first, the "challenged" parties are either extremely fortified traditional industries or power bases with administrative monopoly advantages. Ma knows their bottom lines, not just brushing against the edge but carefully "crossing the line", then forcing the other side to pull back the red line; second, to challenge these traditional forces, Ma often leverages another force within the traditional system, such as introducing heavyweight investors like CITIC Capital, Boyu Capital, and the China Development Bank into Ali Finance (this time excluded from the listing scope); third, these challenges maximize Ma's ambition and Alibaba's interests, but simultaneously maximize the interests of Alibaba's users, peers, and even rivals, such as challenging Hong Kong's securities regulatory system helping other tech companies list in Hong Kong, Ali Finance providing users with faster and more internet-based services, and "Cainiao Network" not only maximizing the logistics efficiency of Alibaba's e-commerce but also benefiting peers and even competitors' logistics.

Of course, there are exceptions: challenging the VIE structure harmed the entire Chinese concept stock market and the vast majority of domestic tech companies; challenging science (yes, I'm talking about Li Yi) damaged his own reputation; he seemed to have challenged another consensus of China's intellectual community not long ago, stopping at the point.

But you cannot deny that such a pattern exists only in Alibaba among China's internet companies; among China's internet tycoons, only Ma dares to think and act this way — even if it's somewhat carried away.

If I were to summarize the difference between Ma and Pony Ma in one sentence, it would be: when their own interest expansion touches traditional vested interests and traditional systems, Ma chooses to break boundaries and continue disrupting; Pony Ma chooses to stop and compromise.

Ma once publicly revealed his intention to change the banking industry; when Tencent applied for a bank license, Pony Ma quickly clarified that Tencent's payment service was "just to support government policies and cooperate with banks." A more typical example is that applications like Whatsapp, LINE, and WeChat would inevitably change people's past ways of sending messages and purchasing value-added services through telecom operators' networks, but Tencent ultimately chose to collaborate with operators on "Wo Card" to alleviate the operators' concerns.

You often hear Ma talk about "idealism" in public, which can sound a bit pretentious after hearing it too many times, but if you look at each of these events, he is indeed more idealistic than many other "tycoons" in China's internet sector. As an additional note, Ma and his wife recently became donors to the "Breakthrough Prize in Life Sciences" foundation, which mainly targets breakthroughs in life and medical research, particularly in cancer. Before Ma, the foundation's donors included only Sergey Brin, co-founder of Google, Mark Zuckerberg, founder of Facebook, and Russian famous investor Yuri Milner.

In terms of changing the lives of Chinese people through the internet, Alibaba is indeed the most radical one. The opponents that Alibaba and Ma challenge — those regulatory bodies, state-owned banks, and backbone logistics systems — are entities that most of China's internet giants and near-giants have never attempted to challenge. On the other hand, Alibaba seems uninterested in the "jungle games" of other giants and near-giants. Despite Alibaba being more adept at manipulating public opinion than any domestic internet peer, and despite launching products like "Laiwang" that aim to compete with WeChat, and despite Alibaba's rapid and decisive investment and acquisition moves putting pressure on other peers, you'll find that these are all because Alibaba cares deeply about what it wants to become. Alibaba rarely invests in a competitor's rival or forms alliances simply to counter another competitor, nor does it participate in the "jungle wars" where several giants and near-giants gang up on another giant or near-giant.

Only by stepping out of the jungle does the game become more exciting.
