Bad habits can also induce osteoporosis.

by po13929357028 on 2012-03-03 17:35:59

The relationship between osteoporosis and gender is not significant, although women are a high-risk group. However, osteoporosis is closely related to dietary habits, exercise, and other lifestyle factors. Therefore, preventing osteoporosis should start from daily life by ensuring good living habits to stay away from osteoporosis.

One of the most important aspects of a healthy lifestyle is exercise. The importance of exercise for bones is most prominently demonstrated in astronauts. During space missions, astronauts experience a rapid decrease in bone mass, with bone loss occurring at a rate more than ten times faster than on Earth. Normally, bone metabolism is in dynamic equilibrium. Once the rate of bone loss exceeds the rate of bone formation, bone mass decreases. To increase bone mass, it is necessary to consume sufficient calcium along with appropriate weight-bearing exercises. Astronauts are in a state of weightlessness during their missions, so increasing bone load is crucial. During their time on the space station, astronauts are required to exercise physically for 2.5 hours a day, six days a week, to prevent the reduction of bone mass and muscle strength.

Recent studies have found that applying pressure to bones can stimulate bone growth. To stimulate bone growth, muscle strength training is needed. Experiments have shown that postmenopausal women who engage in rowing exercises experience a 4.5% increase in vertebral bone mass and a 20% increase in back muscle strength. The experiments also revealed that performing light weightlifting repeatedly does not sufficiently stimulate bone growth; only increasing the weight can achieve this effect.

Calcium in the human body is essential for bones, teeth, and blood. If there is insufficient calcium, it will be extracted from the bones, leading to thinner bones. Recent research has confirmed that a lack of vitamin D weakens and damages muscle tissue, resulting in poor physical performance, slow movement, and an increased likelihood of falls in the elderly. A survey found that individuals with insufficient levels of vitamin D in their blood had weaker grip strength, slower walking speeds, shorter single-leg standing times, and were more prone to falling. Researchers pointed out that if we assume the optimal ratio of bone mass to muscle mass is 1+1=2, then when bone mass decreases to 0.9, muscle mass needs to increase to 1.1. For the elderly, increasing muscle mass is relatively easier than increasing bone mass. Experts advocate that the elderly should walk, run, or engage in other physical activities under the sun to increase their exercise volume while supplementing vitamin D. Suzuki informed that, despite individual differences, one square centimeter of skin exposed to the sun for one hour can produce enough vitamin D to meet a person's daily needs. Therefore, exposing an area of skin the size of a palm to the sun for just a few minutes can meet the daily requirement for vitamin D.
