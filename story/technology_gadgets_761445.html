Imagining the landscape of the mobile phone world: a tripartite confrontation, with Motorola out of the race.

by geekzhang on 2013-05-03 14:02:19

In the smartphone era, a multitude of mobile device manufacturers and operating systems have left users dazzled. So who's good and who's bad? Who will survive and who will perish? The author attempts to provide some answers from his own perspective for discussion. This is an age of smartphone euphoria, and there are many participants vying for a piece of this big pie: there are pure OS platform providers, such as Microsoft with WP8, Firefox with FirefoxOS, and Ubuntu which has entered the smartphone market; there are pure hardware manufacturers, such as LG and Sony focusing on Android, HTC producing both WP8 and Android devices, while Nokia has chosen to focus on the WP8 platform.

There are also giants who dream of integrating software and hardware: Apple leads the smartphone market with iOS and iPhone; Google tries to combine software and hardware with Android and Moto; BlackBerry struggles with the Z10/Q10 and BlackBerry 10 system; Samsung, in addition to involving itself with Android and WP8, also attempts to create its own Tizen system.

It can be seen that the fierce competition among smartphones is intensifying. So, how many will ultimately survive? I believe that in the competition of mobile ecosystems, only iOS, Android, and WP8 can form a tripartite balance of power, and the companies relying on these ecosystems—Apple, Samsung, and Nokia—will become the biggest winners.

First, we must mention Apple and the iPhone. As the pioneer of touch-screen smartphones, many topics need not be reiterated by us: In 2007, Apple launched the first generation iPhone; in 2008, Apple's App Store officially went online; in 2010, the sales of the iPhone 4 in the first three days exceeded 1.7 million units.

However, in the technology industry, there is no eternal overlord. After Apple introduced the iPhone 5, the plot began to take a sharp turn downwards: the stock price fell below $500, media criticism of Apple's lack of innovation and sales forecasts not meeting expectations were incessant. Will Apple really become the "rotten apple" of the tech industry? Of course, the facts will not be like that!

Although there is an issue of insufficient innovation, the iPhone is still not inferior to other star phones in the market; maintaining the current update frequency (even two updates per year), the iPhone's hardware will not fall behind in the smartphone market; relying on the App Store and iOS, Apple still leads its competitors in the ecosystem; additionally, considering user habits, I believe that the future market will certainly not lack Apple's presence.

If Apple's iPhone led the touch-screen era of smartphones and dealt a heavy blow to Symbian and Windows Mobile operating systems, then Google's Android was the last straw that broke the camel's back. Relying on Android's openness and excellent Google online services, Android successfully seized the top position in the smartphone market.

Unlike Apple, the Google Android camp consists of many phone manufacturers, whether it’s Motorola, which has been acquired by Google, HTC, which once occupied the top spot in the Android camp, or Sony Ericsson and LG, etc., all have now been overshadowed by Samsung: the performance of Samsung's Galaxy series of phones in the market even surpasses that of Apple's iPhone.

However, both Google Android and Samsung have their own problems: due to being open-source, Android has difficulty solving the fragmentation problem; and because of the personalized customization by manufacturers, Google lacks control over Android; furthermore, security and privacy issues are troubling both manufacturers and users. For Samsung and other manufacturers, if Google decides to no longer open Android, it could deal them a fatal blow.

Looking at the market, we can see: Samsung is already attempting to build the Tizen operating system; after being acquired by Google, rumors persist that Motorola is developing the XPhone. Clearly, both Google and Samsung are concerned about the current market situation and also harbor greater ambitions. However, considering various factors, I believe that Google Android and Samsung will remain active partners in the smartphone market for a long time.

Faced with the powerful Apple iOS and Google Android, Nokia and its former competitor Microsoft chose to ally: Nokia CEO's memo "The Burning Platform" is still fresh in our minds; the mocking remark from a Google vice president that "two turkeys cannot make an eagle," and the current low market share of WP8, all clearly show the difficulties faced by Nokia and Microsoft's WP8.

Official data shows that Nokia sold a total of 5.6 million Lumia series phones last quarter, which still lags significantly behind Samsung's Galaxy and Apple's iPhone; due to WP8's lack of support for 1080p, Nokia and other manufacturers can only watch as Android produces cross-over phone products; and the lack of applications similar to Instagram and the relatively low quality of WP8 applications also restricts users' willingness to join the WP8 camp.

Despite numerous difficulties, Nokia and Microsoft's WP8 are not without hope: Microsoft's WP8 provides users with a third choice outside of iOS and Android, especially for those who find it hard to tolerate Android's insecurity and fragmentation; although the application ecosystem is still weak, Microsoft's WP8 has indeed formed a complete ecosystem chain, what is needed now is a process of perfection and development; relying on Nokia's camera technology and design expertise, it will still attract numerous users' attention, what is needed now is time for accumulation.

In summary, in the market environment where Apple iOS and Google Android dominate, with Microsoft WP8's unique user experience, Microsoft's Win8+WP8+Cloud concept, and Nokia's efforts in applications and experiences, Microsoft's WP8 and Nokia will have their place in the future smartphone market.

Other Participants

Ubuntu has entered the smartphone market, but merely being open-source is not enough to open the market. According to industry insiders, Ubuntu's mobile operating system currently cannot guarantee smooth operation, let alone the lack of an ecosystem. Although FirefoxOS has gained support from many carriers and phone manufacturers, due to entering the market too late, being based on HTML5 development characteristics, and targeting the low-end market, I do not think they will make a significant impact in the mobile market.

In the Android camp: Samsung dominates alone. In terms of product configuration and industrial chain competition, there is currently no manufacturer that can match up; HTC and Sony have good designs, but there is a huge gap in marketing; after being acquired by Google, Motorola has yet to produce a phone that convinces users to buy; as for ZTE and Huawei, there are also various international market problems that are difficult to solve.

Looking at the WP camp: Nokia occupies the dominant position in WP with its photography and unique personalized applications; Samsung, which has chosen to play second fiddle, has almost been forgotten by everyone; although HTC has launched multiple WP8 phones, its status in the WP camp is as awkward as in the Android camp, and Huawei, etc., give people the feeling that there are more participants than competitors.

Finally, looking at BlackBerry and Samsung Tizen: the former's once proud email service and full keyboard have lost their luster in today's competition, and the newly launched BlackBerry 10 operating system has failed to attract users' attention; the latter's Tizen is more like Samsung's bet and reserved backup plan, and it is difficult to expect Tizen to carry the banner of challenger.

Finally, a special mention should be made of Motorola. As a former king, Motorola's development trajectory is lamentable, whether it is layoffs or the slow release of new products, it is hard to believe that Motorola can return to the mainstream market with the XPhone. Perhaps the only contribution Motorola can make to Google is its vast patent portfolio.

Summary: Market competition is like rowing against the current, if you don't advance, you retreat. Whether it's the currently leading Apple, Google, and Samsung, or the challengers Nokia, Microsoft, and HTC, all need to innovate continuously to gain their market share; users always expect more choices, which is also the motivation and hope for manufacturers in the challenger position.

From the current market perspective, I still hold the view mentioned earlier in the article: in the competition of mobile ecosystems, iOS, Android, and WP8 can form a tripartite balance of power, and the companies relying on these ecosystems—Apple, Samsung, and Nokia—will become the biggest winners. However, as a user, I hope the market forms a hundred-flower blooming situation.
