Synopsis:

Kawashima Haruka (played by Takayama Yūko), a member of the Komatsu Rescue Team of the Air Self-Defense Force, is flying a UH-60J helicopter over mountainous terrain. Kawashima's mother was once saved by the rescue team, inspiring her to pursue this noble profession. She is currently undergoing practical training as a rescue helicopter pilot.

After survivors are rescued onto the helicopter, Kawashima hastily ascends but accidentally loses control, causing the aircraft to shake violently. Captain Kikuta (played by Miura Tomoya) shouts in anger and takes over the controls.

Kawashima quietly lands back at the Komatsu base, where she is met by Flight Sergeant Takasu Mina (played by Kimura Kanako). Rescue team member Seyonan (played by Watanabe Daisuke) shows distrust towards Kawashima, while F-15J fighter pilot Yokosuka (played by Kaneko Ken), who likes to joke around, enthusiastically encourages her. Regardless, as the first female rescue helicopter pilot, she draws much attention.

A fishing boat runs aground during a storm. Kawashima receives her first mission, with Captain Wada (played by Haruta Junichi) piloting the helicopter while Kawashima handles navigation and communications from the co-pilot seat. Rescue crew member Kakiuzi (played by Nakamura Shuntai) saves the stranded fishermen one by one. However, when searching for the last survivor, the helicopter reaches its fuel limit and must return. As the helicopter gradually moves away from the scene, Kawashima sees an oil-covered man climbing onto the deck and pleads with the captain to approach the ship, but is sternly refused. Kawashima helplessly watches as the figure is swallowed by the waves...

The next day, Kawashima, feeling dejected, sits alone hugging her knees in front of the warehouse. Seyonan comes to chat with her, telling her about his experience of losing loved ones and the difficulties of rescue work. Soon after, Seyonan is seriously injured in an accident during a mission. Pilot Oda (played by Iizaka Toshuya) feels it is his responsibility, and mechanic Ao (played by Suzuki Sena) also blames herself. Encouraged by their teammates, they both rise again.

A month passes, Seyonan recovers and returns to the team, and news spreads that Yokosuka and Ao are engaged, filling the team with joyous atmosphere.

Suddenly, the alarm bell rings, and the Komatsu Rescue Team sets out. The plane piloted by Yokosuka disappears from the radar, and the UH helicopter joins the search and rescue operation. Kawashima is entrusted with the task of replacing the injured captain to pilot the plane. With fuel almost depleted and still no sign of Yokosuka, what choice will Kawashima make?!

Behind-the-scenes Production:

Passion and Emotion - The Air Rescue Team Hits the Big Screen

This winter in the Japanese film industry has seen a "rescue craze," with a cluster of films on this theme being released. Following Ito Eiichi's disaster movie "252 Survivors," the depiction of the air rescue team in "Rescue Wings" hits the big screen, and after the New Year, there will be another medical drama "Island of Infection" starring Tsutsui Munetaka.

The air rescue team is a branch organization of the Japanese Air Self-Defense Force, responsible for aerial search and rescue in emergencies, making films on this subject quite rare. This movie is adapted from the anime "Awakening Sky" and the manga "Rescue Wings." Director Tezuka Masaki may not be well-known, but he has extensive experience collaborating with the Self-Defense Forces. He directed the star-studded "Sengoku Jieitai 1549" and also had many notable scenes involving the Self-Defense Forces in the Godzilla series he directed.

However, this time Tezuka Masaki is not filming the Self-Defense Forces in combat, but rather focusing on the Self-Defense Forces' rescue missions, filled with passionate and moving rescue scenes, which is the biggest highlight of this film. Tezuka himself is very interested in this topic and has always hoped to personally direct such a movie. Now, with his wish fulfilled, Tezuka is giving it his all, pursuing perfection in shooting this film. Tezuka boldly raises the banner of "reality." "In fact, there are no female rescue helicopter pilots, so the protagonist's setting is fictional. Therefore, I decided to avoid fiction in other areas and pursue absolute reality."

With the assistance of the Self-Defense Forces, this film almost entirely uses real locations and actual equipment for shooting, showcasing a realism that will bring unprecedented excitement to the audience.

Realistic Shooting with Actual Equipment - Full Support from the Air Self-Defense Force

This film focuses on the members of the Komatsu Rescue Team under the Air Self-Defense Force. Notably, all relevant scenes such as the team quarters, aircraft hangars, and helipads were shot on location at the Komatsu Rescue Team's base in Ishikawa Prefecture, not in studios or with artificial sets. Even the flight equipment worn by the actors is mostly provided by the rescue team.

Not only the Komatsu base assisted in the filming, but also the Hamamatsu Rescue Team base in Shizuoka Prefecture, the Hyakuri Rescue Base in Ibaraki Prefecture, the Komaki Rescue Education Team base in Aichi Prefecture, the Sixth and First Air Groups of the Self-Defense Forces, and even the Maritime Self-Defense Force dispatched the destroyer "Harusame" and SH-60K patrol helicopter to assist. The UH-60J rescue helicopter, V-107A large helicopter, U-125A search aircraft, and F-15J main fighter jets appearing in the film are all currently operational aircraft used by the Self-Defense Forces. Such a luxurious "cast" is a rare spectacle in Japanese cinema, not only adding grandeur to the film but also attracting the attention of many military enthusiasts.

As the highlight of the film, many of the rescue scenes were completed by stunt doubles, who are not ordinary people but professional rescue team members! Their participation allowed many high-difficulty actions to be presented in the film. For example, the rescue scene in the beginning of the film, which is part of the protagonist's flashback, was shot at an abandoned elementary school in Shizuoka Prefecture, where a large rescue helicopter dramatically lands in the small playground surrounded by hills, school buildings, and a large ginkgo tree. Shooting this scene proved more difficult than expected. The elementary school only had a small open space surrounded by a hill, school buildings, and a large ginkgo tree. When the helicopter landed, the tips of the rotors were only three meters away from the ginkgo tree and the school buildings. Dangerous rescue scenes are common throughout the film. On Mount Hakusan and Mount Kongo, where the air density is very low, two UH-60Js were used to shoot the hovering scenes; on the sea rocks, the nighttime fishing boat rescue scenes were filmed, with the helicopter landing on the cliffside of the fishing port, only 50 centimeters away from the edge of the cliff.

Additionally, the film features impressive aerial combat scenes of the Self-Defense Force's maritime training exercises. These scenes involved three F-15Js, and the footage was captured by an aviation cinematographer seated in the rear seat of a tandem-seat F-15DJ.

Fated Heroine: 15-Year-Old Girl Plays 23-Year-Old Female Pilot

Takayama Yūko, who plays the female rescue pilot, is truly the "fated heroine." Her father, Takayama Kazushi, was a member of the Niigata Rescue Team of the Air Self-Defense Force and participated in the rescue operations during the Niigata Chuetsu earthquake. In 2005, Kazushi tragically died in a training crash, and the Sankei News awarded him the "National Defense Officer" award posthumously. To attend the funeral, Yūko came to Tokyo for the first time and was unexpectedly scouted in Harajuku, thus embarking on her acting career.

Yūko inherited her father's excellent athletic nerve, and as the eldest daughter, she quickly recovered from the loss of her father. In February 2007, Yūko made her debut in the TV drama "Thirty Billion Diamond Robbery!" She then appeared in the series "Dad and Daughter's Seven Days" and two short films, becoming an exclusive model for the middle school fashion magazine "Pichi Lemon." Becoming the lead actress of this film marked a turning point in Yūko's acting career. She said, "It was my father who guided me to encounter this movie." Although it was her first time acting in a feature-length film, she was not unfamiliar with the subject matter of Self-Defense Force pilots because she often visited the Self-Defense Force bases with her father since childhood, and her mother taught her how to salute and many Self-Defense Force terms.

The challenge Yūko faced was the "age issue." At the time of filming, she was only fifteen years old, while the character's age is set at twenty-three! It's not easy for a teenage girl to play someone eight years older. However, director Tezuka Masaki wasn't worried at all: "When I first met her, I was convinced from her expression that she could play a twenty-three-year-old role."

Tezuka required her to lower her voice since it still had a childish tone. To understand the mindset of mature women, Yūko would cook and do laundry for her family at home, trying to get closer to adult life. Wearing the rescue uniform once worn by her father, Yūko displayed a strikingly handsome demeanor, showing no difference in age from the character. Her straightforward acting has its own touching quality. This rising star might shine brightly due to this film.

Fun Facts:

• There's a scene in the movie where Yokosuka and Ao ride motorcycles along the beach, with a helicopter flying extremely low beside them. This romantic scene was shot with the help of professionals, and the helicopter was piloted by the captain of the Komatsu Rescue Team.

• Originally, Takayama Yūko's career aspiration was to become a veterinarian.

• The fifteen-year-old Yūko showed remarkable professionalism during filming. In the sweltering summer heat, the temperature in the cockpit reached forty degrees Celsius, causing her to faint multiple times from heatstroke, yet she never complained. However, she occasionally exhibited her youthful side. When filming on the destroyer "Harusame," her cell phone had no signal. Once off the ship, seeing the signal restored, Yūko was particularly delighted.

• Kadokawa Pictures highly regards Takayama Yūko, considering her a "traditional actress with mystique and star quality."
