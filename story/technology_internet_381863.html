Seven Network Business Logics Based on Human Nature

by jimwang on 2011-02-22 18:03:43

There is a website called "Geek Park" which recently summarized five Internet business methods based on human nature: greed, lust, vanity, voyeurism, and laziness. I found it quite interesting and thought that by adding two more, we could form the "Seven Weapons." Later, after some consideration, I made small changes to the five methods proposed by this website and added two more: vanity, free, laziness, curiosity, fear, competitiveness, and erotica.

Vanity:

Tencent plays vanity the best. A large number of virtual item transactions are all due to vanity. Even a small QQ number (such as a five-digit one) can be traded in so-called underground black markets. In terms of usage, if you don't pay for any services on your QQ, you will still have all the functions. However, these seemingly useless "fringe functions" have built the commercial empire of QQ. Ultimately, human vanity is the strongest motivation.

Recently, some LBS businesses have followed Foursquare's example by introducing "badges," which are a typical representation of vanity. Although badges cannot directly generate income (there are no paid badges yet), the number of badges can stimulate users to repeatedly "check-in," making users more active. This is also an appeal to vanity.

Free:

There is a concept called the "free economy." An important component of the Internet: mass media, was able to establish its dominant position because it is free compared to traditional media. The various network services that followed are also branded as free to attract users and form scale.

Internet economy is ultimately an attention economy, and thus, scale almost determines survival. It is difficult for small and beautiful companies to survive in the network domain: either they are eliminated or acquired. The reason I changed "greed" to "free" is for this reason. After all, the word "greed" is too broad (greed is the original driving force behind all commercial activities), while free is the fundamental meaning of scale economy.

Laziness:

Free can achieve scale, but charging isn't necessarily unable to achieve scale if the latter can create a very convenient user experience. People on the Internet prefer to read articles for free, in my view, not just to save money, but also because paying is inconvenient. If you can make people unconsciously take out their money, the paid model can also work, such as the many applications on iPad — remember, there are many things in app store that require payment. Many mobile subscriptions with paid options follow the same principle.

Therefore, for micro payments, it's not that people are unwilling to pay, but rather that people are lazy to pay. Once you solve this "laziness" problem, you can still collect money.

Another type of laziness mode is search. Without a search engine, finding information is extremely laborious. But once you have Google or Baidu, pretending to be well-versed in Tang poetry on MSN or QQ becomes a matter of minutes.

Curiosity:

In one of my previous articles, I mentioned that people invent due to laziness and discover due to curiosity. Geek Park used the term "voyeurism," but I think voyeurism is also a kind of curiosity, and to satisfy curiosity, paying a little fee is reasonable.

Curiosity drives people to search, wander around portals, try new applications, and accidentally become heavy users.

The network world itself is an "invention" by a group of people, and the reason why this invention has more users is due to the curiosity of others. Curiosity is the initial lever to drive all business models, and this statement is roughly true.

Fear:

All security software appeals to fear, and the most famous 3Q battle to date is a battle of fear: 360 says Tencent is spying on your privacy, and Tencent counters that 360 is hijacking your QQ.

It must be admitted that 360 is the company that best exploits fear. In an article, I mentioned that Pang Shengdong of 51.com was imitating Tencent's use of vanity, but Zhou Hongyi innovatively found "fear" - although he was still playing with human nature. It is precisely because of this innovation by 360 that it has quietly become a big rival to Tencent, while 51.com, after a brief period of excitement, is now basically out of the public eye.

After fear, people will inevitably seek safety, and under this psychological drive, sometimes they will spare no expense. Earlier, people were calculating how to make their computers safer, but now they are worried about account security. Do you want a sense of security? Just follow me and do what I say!

Competitiveness:

Another person who learned from Tencent and played with human nature is Shi Yuzhu. He appeals to another aspect of human nature: everyone has a competitive spirit. Moreover, he creatively performed a "mashup": first, you notice because of curiosity, then you get involved because of free, and finally your competitive spirit is aroused: got cut down? Pay a little money to quickly obtain top-tier equipment, then cut back.

Essentially, games all appeal to "competitiveness," and the reason why online games are more fun than single-player games is because: single-player games get boring because the computer is only at this level, once you figure out its patterns, you win every time, where is the competitiveness? But online games are different. Everyone is human, figuring out patterns is too difficult.

Erotica:

Hehe, this one, I think there is no need to elaborate further, you know.

These seven business methods based on human nature are not exactly the "seven sins of the network." From a business perspective, moral criticism is left to others. I only state the facts.
