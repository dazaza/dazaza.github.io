Synopsis:

This film is a direct adaptation of the famous work directed by Sidney Lumet with the same name, telling the story of a jury member who turns the tide in a murder case before sentencing.

The Venice Film Festival - Russian version of "Twelve Angry Men" ("12"): Russia reveals its great conscience through the plight of a Chechen orphan.

The Venice Film Festival entry "Twelve Angry Men" is a remake of the successful 1957 American version, which won the Golden Bear at the 1957 Berlin Film Festival. The essence of the idea—"how the minority challenges the majority"—has become a celebrated model for future generations. Directed by Sydney Lumet and starring Henry Fonda, the American version tells the story of one member of the jury who convinces the other eleven to change their verdict, thus achieving justice and altering the fate of an individual. The American version was highly acclaimed and received three Oscar nominations for Best Picture, Best Director, and Best Screenplay. Although it ultimately lost to "The Bridge on the River Kwai," its brilliance has not faded over 50 years, making it an unforgettable classic in cinema history.

Directed by Nikita Mikhalkov, this year's entry "Twelve Angry Men" uses the same framework structure as the original, presenting a vivid depiction of the realities of post-Soviet Union Russia facing ethnic conflicts. The film ends amidst rounds of applause because it deceives the audience twice before the official end, leading them to believe it’s over only for the film to continue—its resonance lingering long after the credits roll.

Besides the brilliant dialogues, all twelve male leads deliver outstanding performances, forming a highly expressive ensemble. Through this film, post-Soviet Russia reveals its reflections on life and justice under new rules, showcasing its pride and deep concerns about the rich ethnic diversity of Russia, as well as its confusion about reality. Historically, suffering Russia possesses a colorful personality; it can be humane like a saint, domineering like a thug, romantic like a poet, beautiful like a maiden, drunken like a man, or philosophical like a thinker gazing at the heavens. However, unanimously agreed upon is that Russian art has a great tradition. From classical music, literature, theater, poetry, dance to film, it has reached heights that must be looked up to. Now, developing and wavering Russia uses its adapted movie "Twelve Angry Men," through the trial of the fate of a Chechen orphan, to once again reveal its ability to reflect on itself and think independently. This time, Russia exposes its conscience towards the Chechen orphan, shedding a tear for its suffering nation.

Twelve jurors from different professions and social statuses gather to deliberate on the first-degree murder charge against a Chechen teenager for allegedly killing his stepfather. The victim was a former Soviet special forces officer who had participated in controlling Chechnya. One juror strongly opposes the others and requests further discussion of the case. This request angers the other jurors. In their view, when the investigator and prosecutor have already accused the defendant of being guilty, his guilt is already determined beyond doubt, leaving no room for argument. After all, professionals are wiser and more capable than anyone else. So what is there left to discuss? In fact, everyone harbors a stingy and self-protective armor against injustices that have occurred to themselves, their loved ones, and friends. Moreover, we find that law cannot be presented in a completely impersonal form in Russia. The law touches the souls of those chosen as decision-makers. Therefore, there must be something to discuss: pain, suffering, and compassion—but only if the boy were Russian! However, he is a Chechen boy, a nationality that has been in armed conflict with Russia for hundreds of years. This makes the entire event not just a legal matter but more about the psychological perceptions of the jurors regarding those providing unfavorable testimonies against the boy. These gradually alter their verdicts…

However, the question that still confuses people is, what does freedom mean for this boy, these jurors, and all those living in Russia?
