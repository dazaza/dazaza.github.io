The era that a five-month-old baby is in is called the "semi-weaning period" by us.

by sadr586444 on 2011-06-13 16:29:08

Weaning transition does not mean immediate weaning and switching to other foods, but refers to feeding infants semi-liquid pureed complementary foods to gradually transition them to harder varieties. The process of transitioning an infant from breastfeeding or formula milk to adult food takes about half a year. Gradually transitioning the infant from breastfeeding or formula milk to the habit of eating rice involves a period of feeding easily digestible foods, which is also known as the semi-weaned phase.

The food consumed during the semi-weaned phase is called transitional food. It should be specially made for infants during this unique period and is what we usually refer to as "complementary food."

Complementary foods can be divided into two major categories: one category consists of baby complementary foods processed from ordinary adult diets.

For example, baby complementary foods made by blending with a juicer or mashing with a spoon at home are good choices, such as eggs, tofu, sweet potatoes, fish, and pork.

However, there are inevitably some shortcomings in these homemade complementary foods. First, the nutrition in each type of complementary food is relatively single, lacking balanced combinations; secondly, bacteria may be unavoidable during the preparation of homemade complementary foods, which can easily cause discomfort to babies; thirdly, the preparation process of each complementary food is slow,

which is truly "meticulous preparation." When the baby cries or feels hungry, making complementary food on the spot is undoubtedly very troublesome. Therefore, besides these types of complementary foods, smart moms have another option. That is the second category of complementary foods we are going to talk about - baby rice cereal.

Baby rice cereals are generally produced by professional manufacturers, with multiple nutrient elements combined, convenient to use, safe and hygienic, making up for the deficiencies of homemade complementary foods.

Five-month-old infant diet and feeding. Currently, there are many varieties of baby rice cereals available in the market, offering a wide range of choices. However, considering the unique physiological structure of Chinese babies, mothers should pay attention to the following issues when selecting rice cereals:

1. Pay attention to the protein content in the product formula.

It is well known that proteins are essential substances for forming body tissues, and only sufficient protein supply can ensure the complete development of all organs.

Nowadays, nutritional baby cereals are normally divided into two types: full-value infant formula powder and supplementary infant grain powder. The protein content in full-value infant formula powder (such as Beingmate Nutritional Rice Cereal) is as high as 10%, which can fully meet the needs of normal growth and development of infants without adding other foods; while supplementary grain powder is a supplementary food for infants over four months old, with only 5% protein content and even lower essential fatty acid content.

There is a difference in sensitivity to proteins between Chinese babies and Western babies. If supplementary grain powder is used as full-value formula powder for Chinese babies over a long period, it will hinder the intelligence and development of babies and have significant effects on their entire lives.

2. Pay attention to whether the rice cereal contains sufficient "iodine" element.

Iodine has always been known as the "intelligence element," and it is indeed so. Iodine is an important nutritional element in the human body, and iodine deficiency in children can lead to cretinism, characterized by low intelligence, hearing, speech, and movement disorders, high incidence of deafness, and possible deformities. China is a country with iodine deficiency, accounting for 40% of the global iodine-deficient population concentrated in China, and Chinese babies are more prone to iodine deficiency. Iodine deficiency should be prevented from an early age. For the health of babies, parents must not overlook the amount of "iodine" when selecting rice cereals!

3. Pay attention to whether the product formula is specifically designed for Chinese babies.

With distinct population characteristics and different dietary cultures, babies consuming food from other races may result in unbalanced nutrient absorption, affecting growth. Truly suitable rice cereals for Chinese babies should be specifically designed for Chinese infants based on their physiological characteristics (such as Beingmate Nutritional Rice Cereal).

4. Pay attention to the comprehensiveness of nutritional elements.

The physiological composition of the human body is complex, requiring diverse nutrients. Good nutritional rice cereals contain comprehensive nutrients, including eighteen amino acids and many essential special nutrients for the human body, such as iodine elements and high-protein content, fully meeting the normal growth and development needs of infants.

5. Pay attention to the fineness of the rice cereal particles.

The gastrointestinal function of infants has not yet fully developed, and coarser particles can hinder the absorption of nutrients by babies. High-quality rice cereals must conform to the principles of being finely textured and easily digestible.

6. Pay attention to whether the contents are individually packaged. Individual packaging is not only easier to measure but also more hygienic and less likely to become damp.

Five-month-old infant diet and feeding. After choosing the rice cereal, we can officially start feeding the baby:

A. Principles of complementary food addition:

Small amounts for trial feeding, gradual attempts. Start with one kind of food in small quantities, gradually increasing the amount from less to more, from liquid to paste, from bland to flavorful, from fine to coarse, from one type to multiple types, proceeding step by step.

B. Methods of complementary food addition:

① Attention should be paid to sitting while eating.

When feeding complementary food, let the baby's body stand upright, support him/her with something, and let the baby sit while eating. Because the baby can truly enjoy eating complementary food after learning to sit independently;

② Create a happy atmosphere for eating.

Use a warm attitude and joyful emotions to infect the baby, making him/her willing to accept complementary food;

③ Start with one spoonful.

Every new food should start with one spoonful. Use a small spoon to pick up a little food, gently place it in the middle of the baby's tongue, wait for the baby to swallow, and then take out the small spoon.

Here, mothers need to pay special attention to eczema.

Eczema is one of the most common skin diseases in infants. It is a type of epidermal inflammation related to abnormal reactions and is sensitive to various stimuli. Therefore, if the baby is currently suffering from eczema, we should avoid feeding allergenic foods, even if it's just a small spoonful.

Common allergenic foods include fish, shrimp, beef, lamb, milk, and eggs;

④ Observe the stool.

In the first few days, the food the baby eats may be expelled unchanged through the stool. At this time, do not increase the amount of complementary food or switch the food. Wait until the stool returns to normal before increasing the quantity. After stabilizing for a period, you can try feeding the baby other flavors of complementary food.

Additionally, if the baby develops egg-like or watery stools and the frequency of bowel movements increases compared to usual, it is a typical symptom of diarrhea. When the baby's diarrhea is severe, consider reducing the intake of new complementary foods and supplementing some physiological saline to prevent dehydration;

⑤ Time arrangement. If the baby refuses complementary food, feed the complementary food when the baby is hungry before each breastfeeding session. Also, after the last breastfeeding session before the child goes to sleep, supplement the baby with rice cereal, which helps maintain stable sleep at night. As time passes, gradually reduce the duration of this breastfeeding session and increase the amount of complementary food, helping the baby completely replace breastfeeding with complementary food.

Dietary arrangements and calcium supplementation recipes for infants aged four months to one year

Author: Parenting Forum Source: Parenting Forum

Infant dietary arrangements and calcium supplementation recipes are suitable for babies aged four months to one year.

Adding complementary foods for four-month-old babies, adding complementary foods steadily.

As the baby grows, after four months, breast milk alone cannot fully meet their nutritional needs. At this point, mothers can consider adding complementary foods to the baby's diet. However, adding complementary foods can be challenging for all mothers. Babies often experience discomfort when trying new foods, and their digestive and absorption functions are not yet mature, making them prone to gastrointestinal dysfunction. Therefore, certain principles must be followed when adding complementary foods:

1. The amount added should gradually increase from less to more;

2. Foods should progress from thin to thick, from fine to coarse, from liquid to semi-liquid, and finally to solid foods;

3. The variety of foods added should allow the baby to adapt to one before adding another. If an attempt fails, it does not necessarily mean the child dislikes or cannot adapt to it; try again after some time. Feeding should be patient and not forced. After adding new food, closely monitor the baby's digestion. If symptoms like indigestion, vomiting, abnormal stools, or other conditions occur, stop feeding this complementary food.

When the gastrointestinal function returns to normal, start with the initial amount or smaller amounts. If the baby is sick, temporarily avoid introducing new complementary foods;

4. Flexibly control the types and quantities of complementary foods added: During specific feeding, adjustments should be made according to the individual characteristics of the child. The objective indicator of appropriate feeding is that the baby does not cry after eating, sleeps well, digests properly, and gains weight satisfactorily.

Four taboos for adding complementary foods: Malnutrition is a common disease among infants, especially in babies under one year old. Besides congenital factors (such as premature birth, twins, large birth weight, congenital malformations), most malnutrition cases are due to problems with postnatal feeding, especially unreasonable addition of complementary foods. Common situations include:

1. Too early

After birth, the digestive organs of the baby are very delicate, with underdeveloped digestive glands and poor secretion functions. Many digestive enzymes have not yet formed, so the baby does not yet possess the ability to digest complementary foods. Adding complementary foods too early would burden the baby's digestive system. Undigested complementary foods either stagnate in the abdomen causing fermentation, leading to bloating, constipation, and loss of appetite, or increase intestinal peristalsis, resulting in increased stool volume and frequency, eventually causing diarrhea. Therefore, babies younger than four months should avoid adding complementary foods too early.

2. Too late

Some parents are overly cautious about adding complementary foods. Even though the child has passed the four-month mark and relies solely on breast milk or formula, they fail to realize that the child has grown and requires more nutrition and energy. Breast milk or formula alone cannot meet their developmental needs, and it is time to reasonably add complementary foods. Meanwhile, the child's digestive and taste organs have gradually matured, providing the necessary conditions for adding complementary foods.

Additionally, the immunity the child received from the mother has almost depleted, and their own resistance needs to be strengthened through increased nutrition. At this stage, failing to timely add complementary foods will not only affect the child's growth and development but also make them susceptible to diseases due to weakened resistance. Therefore, complementary foods should be appropriately introduced to children older than four months.

3. Overabundance

Although children can start eating complementary foods, their digestive systems remain delicate and should not be rushed. Foods should be gradually added based on the child's digestive capabilities. Arbitrary additions could lead to indigestion or obesity. Allowing the child free rein, giving them whatever they want, and feeding them however much they desire, could result in unbalanced nutrition and foster bad eating habits such as pickiness and favoritism. Thus, adding complementary foods excessively is also inappropriate.

4. Excessive refinement

Some parents are overly meticulous, ensuring the homemade complementary foods or store-bought infant nutritional foods are highly refined. This prevents the child's chewing abilities from receiving adequate exercise, affecting the eruption and alignment of their teeth. Food that isn't chewed also doesn't stimulate taste sensations, reducing the child's appetite and hindering taste development, impacting cheek development as well.

As a result, the child can only eat porridge and noodles and struggles with eating solid foods. Slight carelessness in preparation leads to nausea and vomiting, causing the child to simply refuse or vomit after eating. Long-term, this affects the child's growth and brain development.

What to pay attention to when adding complementary foods?

Milk products are the main food for infants, but as the infant's body grows, complementary foods should be added in a timely manner. An infant's digestive tract is tender and weak.

If the appropriate amount of complementary foods is not added according to the child's age and actual needs, or if normal feeding continues when the infant is sick, it can cause indigestion and diarrhea. Therefore, caution is required when adding complementary foods, and arbitrary actions should be avoided.

Based on the infant's digestive capacity and nutritional needs, complementary foods should be gradually increased in variety. Try one type first, and after 3-4 days or a week, once the baby adapts, add another type.

Increase the quantity gradually from less to more, and from mild to strong flavors. If the weather is too hot, the baby may easily suffer from indigestion, so the introduction of new foods can be postponed until the weather cools down slightly.

After adding a new food, closely observe the baby's digestion. If signs of indigestion, vomiting, abnormal stools, or other issues appear, pause feeding this complementary food. Once the gastrointestinal function returns to normal, restart with a smaller amount.

If the baby becomes ill, stop all complementary foods (except fruit juice) until recovery, then resume.

These are general considerations when adding complementary foods. However, since infants vary greatly, the types and quantities of complementary foods need to be flexibly adjusted and cannot follow a rigid pattern.

Methods for preparing complementary foods

Orange Juice: One orange, wash the outer peel, cut it in half; place each half on a juice extractor and rotate several times, allowing the juice to flow into the groove. Filter it and it's ready. Each orange yields approximately 40 milliliters of juice. Dilute with water before drinking.

Tomato Juice: 50 grams of ripe tomatoes, a little sugar, and an appropriate amount of warm water. Wash the tomatoes, scald them with boiling water to remove the skin, then chop them finely. Wrap them in clean double-layer gauze and squeeze the tomato juice into a small bowl. Add sugar to the juice and dilute with warm water before drinking.

Note to use fresh, ripe tomatoes.

Green Vegetable Water: 50 grams of green vegetables (spinach, oilseed rape, or cabbage), 50 grams of water. Wash the vegetables thoroughly, chop them finely. Place a stainless steel pot on the fire, boil the water, add the chopped vegetables, cover the pot, and boil for 5-6 minutes. Remove from heat, let it sit for another 10 minutes, strain out the vegetable residue, and keep the broth.

Watermelon Juice: 100 grams of watermelon pulp, 10 grams of sugar. Place the watermelon pulp in a bowl, mash it with a spoon, then filter it through gauze. Add sugar to the juice, mix evenly.

Carrot Soup: 50 grams of carrots, a little sugar, 50 grams of water. Wash the carrots, chop them finely, place them in a stainless steel pot, add water, and boil for about 2 minutes on high heat. Filter through gauze to remove the residue, add sugar, and mix evenly.

Hawthorn Water: 50 grams of dried hawthorn slices, a little sugar, 150 grams of boiling water. Quickly rinse the hawthorn slices in cold water to remove surface dust, then place them in a bowl. Pour boiling water over the hawthorn slices, cover and steep for 10 minutes. When the water temperature drops to lukewarm, pour the hawthorn water into a cup, add sugar, stir until dissolved.

Apple Sauce: 1/8 of an apple, a little sugar.

Wash the apple, peel and remove the seeds, then slice it thinly. Place it in a pot and cook with a little sugar, cook for a moment, add a little water, and continue cooking over medium heat until it turns into a paste. Turn off the heat and mash it with the back of a spoon.

Potato Puree:

Take 1/7 of a medium-sized potato, 1 tablespoon of milk, and 1/4 teaspoon of butter. Wash and peel the potato, then cook it in a pot either by boiling or steaming. Once cooked, mash the potato with a spoon (or buy ready-made potato puree from the market), then add milk and butter, stirring until it reaches a thick consistency.

Fresh Sweet Potato Puree: 50 grams of sweet potato, a little sugar. Wash the sweet potato, peel it, chop it and mash it slightly, add a little warm water, put it in a pot and cook for about 15 minutes until soft, add a little sugar and cook for a little longer.

Egg Yolk Potato Puree: 1 teaspoon of filtered potato puree, 1 large teaspoon of mashed apple. Take half of a boiled egg yolk and filter it; boil the potato until soft, filter it, then mix it with egg yolk and milk, and heat it slightly on the fire.

Red Date Paste: 100 grams of red dates, 20 grams of sugar. Wash the red dates, put them in a pot, add water and cook for 15-20 minutes until soft. Remove the red date skins and seeds, add sugar, and mix evenly.

Complementary Foods for Four-Month-Old Babies

Start by adding rice powder and egg yolk, gradually moving to creamy carrot puree, fruit puree, other vegetable purees, meat puree, fish puree, etc., starting with small amounts. Observe the baby's stool condition after adding new foods or increasing the quantity. If the stool is normal, complementary foods can be gradually added. If the stool is abnormal, the speed of adding can be slowed down or fed with Mama Ai for a few days to aid digestion.

1. Begin by feeding one type of food at a time, not multiple foods simultaneously, so that if an allergic reaction occurs, it is easy to identify which food caused it. When starting to feed a new food, there should be a gap of 1-2 weeks.

2. When feeding one type of food at a time, start with a small amount, such as one teaspoon, gradually increasing to 2-3 large tablespoons or half a bowl, depending on the type of food. During the 4-6 month period, one meal of other foods should be given daily, along with breastfeeding or formula milk.

If the baby initially refuses to eat, such as closing their mouth, turning away, or spitting out the food, don't force them to eat because it might negatively affect future feeding. Give the baby time to try, or pause for 3-4 days and try again. Usually, the baby will eventually accept the food.

However, when the baby refuses to eat, parents should persistently try feeding until they are willing to eat, even if it's a small amount. Otherwise, bad eating habits may develop, leading to potential nutritional deficiencies later on.

Complementary foods can be added after breastfeeding or formula feeding, or during breaks between feedings.

Use a small spoon to help the child get accustomed to eating with a spoon and practice swallowing skills.

Methods for making several complementary foods: Fruit juice: Choose fresh fruit, wash and cut in half, squeeze the juice through a sieve, and dilute with an equal amount of warm water before feeding.

Vegetable juice: Choose fresh green vegetables, remove large stems, boil in boiling water, discard the leaves, cool, and serve.

Fruit puree: Choose ripe, fiberless, fleshy fruits, wash and peel, scrape the flesh with a spoon, and mash into a puree.

Vegetable puree: Wash fresh green vegetables, peel or remove stems, cut into segments, boil in boiling water, and mash into a puree with a spoon after cooling.

Egg yolk puree: Boil a fresh egg whole in boiling water, remove the shell, separate the yolk, and mash with a spoon.

Carrot puree: Peel and core a half-carrot, cut into small pieces, steam in a bowl, mash with a stainless steel spoon after about ten minutes, add sesame oil or walnut oil, a little salt (the salt for baby complementary foods should be mild), and simmer a bit longer.

Other options include cucumber puree, pumpkin puree, winter melon puree, bok choy puree, etc. Almost any seasonal fresh vegetable can be tried.

Practical discoveries show that some vegetables are best steamed, such as carrots, cucumbers, and pumpkins; others are better boiled, like bok choy, spinach, and Swiss chard, while some can be either steamed or boiled.
