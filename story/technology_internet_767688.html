Nokia Gives Microsoft Hope: 6 New Devices Coming

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 12:30:14

Former Nokia CEO Stephen Elop

Introduction: An article titled "Hope Springs at Nokia" was published on the website of The Wall Street Journal last Sunday. The commentary stated that due to hardware business becoming a major weakness for Microsoft, Nokia's phone business would provide beneficial supplementation for the company. Meanwhile, a series of new products about to be released by Nokia would reveal a glimmer of hope for Microsoft.

Below is the full text of the article:

Launching New Products

Stephen Elop's time to shine has arrived. He will unveil six new devices in Abu Dhabi this Tuesday, including Nokia's first tablet computer and phablet.

This plan coincides with Apple's release of its new iPad. Although it is challenging to create a buzz during such a period, Elop can use these devices as an opportunity to showcase a ray of hope for Microsoft, which acquired Nokia's mobile phone division just a month ago for $7 billion.

Microsoft has consistently struggled with device manufacturing, meaning that the new products unveiled by Elop hold far greater significance for Microsoft than for Nokia. Once the dominant force in the global mobile phone market, Nokia is gradually shifting its focus elsewhere.

Elop has kept a low profile recently. The majority of his $25 million severance package upon stepping down as Nokia's CEO was paid by Microsoft, but this payment drew criticism from numerous media outlets and Finnish officials, including the Prime Minister of Finland. Despite significantly cutting costs and undergoing a historic restructuring during his tenure, the main focus remained on financial losses and declining stock prices.

Sucession Plans

According to informed sources, 49-year-old Elop has become one of the few candidates likely to succeed Steve Ballmer in leading Microsoft over the next few months. This is primarily because the board believes he can contribute significantly to Microsoft using Nokia's mobile phone business at a critical historical moment. As a veteran software company, Microsoft's recent blunders in the hardware sector have led the market to doubt its ability to remain vibrant in the future.

Elop previously served as an executive at Microsoft before moving to Nokia as CEO in 2010. His move surprised many executives and board members at Microsoft, as they believed Nokia was on its last legs. However, after Microsoft announced its acquisition of Nokia, Elop stepped down in September and will rejoin Microsoft as the head of its terminal device business once the deal is finalized, expected to be completed in the first quarter of next year.

According to informed sources, Elop has quickly met with employees across multiple continents and visited board members. Whether he can lead Microsoft to success depends on his ability to end the technical obstacles and marketing missteps faced by Windows Phone.

There are many notable figures among the candidates for Microsoft's CEO position, including Ford CEO Alan Mulally. However, Elop possesses decades of experience in the technology industry. A Microsoft spokesperson declined to comment on the CEO selection process.

Improving Performance

Both Nokia and Microsoft need to change their fortunes. Their collaboration has not borne fruit; although Nokia is currently the largest manufacturer of Windows Phones globally, its share in the entire smart device market remains negligible compared to Apple and Samsung Electronics. These latter companies outspend Nokia on marketing, and their operating systems are more popular than Windows Phone.

The key messages released by Elop have become a driving force for Microsoft's development.

Informed sources state that Nokia Lumia phones running on the Windows Phone system have seen consecutive quarterly growth in sales both year-over-year and quarter-over-quarter for the fourth time. Between June and September, the company sold at least 8 million Lumia phones, up from 7.4 million in the second quarter and more than double compared to the same period last year.

Nokia will announce its third-quarter results on October 29. A company spokesperson declined to comment on the results.

Microsoft's board is disappointed that the company has failed to generate profits through the sale of mobile devices equipped with the Windows system, whereas companies like Samsung and Apple, due to their hardware businesses, have greater motivation to promote their hardware products.

Microsoft executives and directors were hesitant to heavily promote Windows Phone, as hardware manufacturers like Nokia would benefit most from increased phone sales. Becoming a company akin to Apple, possessing both software and hardware businesses, is crucial for gaining board support.

To Nokia's advantage, according to market research firms IDC and Gartner, PC sales dropped by 18% year-over-year in the third quarter of this year, and PC shipments are expected to see their first annual decline in 11 years. Microsoft and its allies hope to win consumer favor with the new version of Windows and corresponding new hardware generations.

Enhancing Technology

Nokia's long history in device manufacturing and its former leadership in the mobile device market may meet Microsoft's needs. Elop and the current Nokia executive team have already begun planning a new strategy, which could be further clarified when new devices are announced on Tuesday.

Unlike the poorly selling Surface tablets, Nokia's first tablet will feature LTE technology, providing better internet connectivity and potentially enabling it to compete better with the iPad. LTE could enhance the appeal of this product, codenamed "Sirius," to corporate users.

Microsoft has stated that the Surface will be equipped with LTE technology early next year. Nokia is prepared to launch its tablet and phablet but must wait for Microsoft to update the system, including functions to better use Outlook email on tablets.

After a series of missteps, Microsoft has prioritized hardware. Within the fiscal year ending June 30, the company's Surface tablets generated $853 million in revenue, but due to price cuts on Surface RT, they incurred a $900 million loss. Previously, the company had also launched mobile hardware devices such as phones and MP3 players, all of which ultimately ended in failure.
