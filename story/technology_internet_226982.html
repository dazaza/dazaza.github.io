What is "Symbolism"?

by lk0zswf on 2009-08-22 09:35:08

Precursor and origins

French symbolism was in large part a reaction against naturalism and realism, attempting to capture reality by infusing it with a mystical side of life. The movement began with a group of poets including Charles Baudelaire, Stéphane Mallarmé, and Paul Verlaine. These poets were reacting against the previous romantic generation, as well as the realistic novel. The symbolists believed that art should aim at capturing an absolute truth indirectly through the use of metaphors and suggestive imagery. In 1886, Jean Moréas published the Symbolist Manifesto, which defined the aims of the movement. Moréas declared that symbolism was opposed to plain meanings, declamations, false sentimentality, and matter-of-fact description, and that its goal was to clothe the ideal in a perceptible form whose task was not to define it but merely to express it through a series of suggestive images.

Symbolism as a movement

The symbolists believed that art should aspire to the condition of music, that is, to convey meaning indirectly through a series of evocative forms. Thus, they wrote in highly metaphorical and suggestive manners, endowing particular images or objects with symbolic meaning. In 1886, Jean Moréas published the Symbolist Manifesto, which outlined the principles of the movement. Moréas announced that symbolism was hostile to plain meanings, declamatory modes, false sentimentality, and matter-of-fact description, and that its goal was to clothe the ideal in a perceptible form whose task was not to define it but merely to express it through a series of evocative images.

Perhaps no attempt at defining the essence of symbolism is more notable than a series of articles by Paul Verlaine published in 1884, which became a significant publication on Tristan Corbière, Arthur Rimbaud, and Stéphane Mallarmé, each of whom Verlaine numbered among the poètes maudits, or accursed poets.

Verlaine argued that in their individual and very different ways, each of these hitherto neglected poets had discovered the curse of genius; it isolated them from their contemporaries, and thus these poets were concerned with avoiding hermeticism and having a temperamental writing style. In this conception of the role of genius and the poet, Verlaine obliquely referred to Arthur Schopenhauer, the pessimistic philosopher of aesthetics, who held that the purpose of art would provide a temporary refuge from the world of blind willing.

Schopenhauer's aesthetics reflected concerns common to the symbolist project; they tended to see art as a contemplative haven from the world of conflict and will. From this desire for an artistic refuge from the world, the symbolists took up themes of mysticism and otherworldliness, acute awareness of mortality, and the malign power of sexuality. Mallarmé's poem "Les fenêtres" clearly expresses all these themes. A dying man in a hospital bed surrounded by his physical pain seeks escape and solace, turns to his window; wheels away in disgust from:

... l'homme l'âme dure

Vautré dans le bonheur, où ses seuls appétits

Mangent, et qui s'entête à chercher cette ordure

Pour l'offrir à la femme allaitant ses petits,

(The hard-souled man, wallowing in happiness, where only his appetites feed, and who insists on seeking out this filth to offer to the woman nursing her young)

And instead, he marvels:

Je mire et je vois l'ange! Et je meurs, et j'aime -

Que la vitre soit l'art, soit la mysticité -

Renaître, portant mon rêve en diadème,

Au ciel antérieur où fleurit Beauté!

(I marvel and I see the angel! And I die, and I love - whether the glass be art, or mysticism - to be reborn, bearing my dream as a diadem, under the former sky where Beauty once flourished!)

The distinction between the decadent movement and symbolism frequently blurs. Several young writers calling themselves 'decadents' in the mid-1880s were primarily mockery responses to Jean Moréas' manifesto mainly in the press. Some of these writers accepted the label while most avoided it. Although symbolism and decadence can appear to overlap in some areas, the two remain distinct.

In other media

Symbolism in literature is distinct from symbolism in art although the two overlapped on a number of points. There were several, rather dissimilar, groups of symbolist painters and visual artists, including Gustave Moreau, Odilon Redon, Pierre Puvis de Chavannes, Henri Fantin-Latour, Edvard Munch, Félicien Rops, and Jan Toorop. Symbolism in painting had a larger geographical reach than symbolism in poetry, reaching several Russian artists, and American artists such as Elihu Vedder. Auguste Rodin is sometimes considered a symbolist in sculpture.

Symbolism has had some influence in music as well. Many symbolist writers and critics were early enthusiasts of the music of Richard Wagner, a student of Schopenhauer. The symbolist aesthetic greatly affected the work of Claude Debussy. His choice of operatic, text, and subject matter was almost entirely derived from the symbolist canon: in particular, compositions like his settings of Cinq poèmes de Baudelaire, various art songs on poems by Verlaine, the opera Pelléas et Mélisande with a libretto by Maurice Maeterlinck, and the unfinished sketches of two Poe stories, The Devil in the Belfry and The Fall of the House of Usher, all indicate that Debussy was deeply influenced by symbolist themes and sensibilities. His best-known work, Prélude à l'après-midi d'un faune, was inspired by a poem by Mallarmé, L'après-midi d'un faune. Arnold Schoenberg's Pierrot Lunaire takes its texts from German translations of symbolist poems by Albert Giraud, showing a link between German expressionism and symbolism.

The symbolist worship of the static and hieratic adapted less well to narrative fiction than it did to poetry. Joris-Karl Huysmans' 1884 novel À rebours (Against Nature) became a statement of symbolist aesthetics in fiction, dealing with many of the themes common within the symbolist movement. Little happens in this novel; it is mostly a catalog of the tastes and inner life of Des Esseintes, an eccentric reclusive aristocrat who lives in a house near Paris.
