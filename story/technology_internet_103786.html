Baidu's Technical Battle Against Spam - Age1983

by hexiaoyao on 2008-11-15 20:48:02

Baidu said: spam is the No. 1 enemy...

Recently, Baidu, the world's largest Chinese search engine, was embroiled in an "anti-monopoly scandal": a website company from Hebei province petitioned the State Administration for Industry and Commerce to conduct an anti-monopoly investigation on Baidu, suggesting that Baidu be ordered to stop its illegal practice of blocking other websites, and be fined up to 10% of its sales revenue from the previous year, which amounts to RMB 174.44 million.

It is understood that this is the first case to occur in China's internet sector since the implementation of the national Anti-Monopoly Law on August 1 this year.

In today's world where "Baidu it" has become a habitual online application for hundreds of millions of citizens, this lawsuit immediately caused a great stir in the market. In response to increasing doubts from netizens, after keeping silent for 10 days, Baidu issued a five-point statement, directly responding to the much-hyped "first case of China's internet anti-monopoly investigation", stating that its "refusal to index certain web pages" was due to spam flooding the search engines, and was necessary for "winning this technical war against spam."

### The Origin

Was the site blocked because it didn't renew its fees?

The origin of the matter lies in Baidu's pay-per-click ranking service targeted at small and medium-sized enterprises across China.

In 2007, Wang Guanjue from Tangshan, Hebei, founded the Universal Medicine Network. The website introduction claims that it is "China's first industrial chain-style pharmaceutical industry portal website," including four major sections: Health Channel, Recruitment Channel, Wholesale Supermarket, and Retail Supermarket, gathering all links from production to consumption in the pharmaceutical industry.

In March this year, in order to increase click-through rates, Wang Guanjue signed a "Baidu Pay-Per-Click Ranking Agreement" with Baidu's Hebei agent, purchasing Baidu's pay-per-click ranking service: when users search for certain keywords, the names of companies that purchased the service will rank higher in the search results. Each time a user clicks into the company's homepage, the company must pay a fee to the search engine service provider, which becomes the search engine's advertising income.

Wang Guanjue stated that during the initial months of participating in the pay-per-click system, traffic indeed surged, with the Universal Medicine Network ranking first in Baidu searches.

"At its peak, daily IP addresses (short for Internet Protocol, commonly understood as internet addresses used to uniquely identify computers on the internet) even soared to over 8,000 per day, and normal daily average traffic remained at 3,000 IPs/day," according to data provided by Baidu.

However, on July 10, four months later, the Universal Medicine Network's traffic plummeted from 2,961 IPs/day the previous day to only 701 IPs/day, and the more than 80,000 pieces of information indexed by Baidu were reduced to just one page with four records. Afterwards, the website's traffic never recovered.

According to the Universal Medicine Network, during this period, due to website redesign, they had reduced their pay-per-click payment price to the lowest level, lowering their bidding price on Baidu. Wang Guanjue further suspected that the abnormal ranking of the Universal Medicine Network was related to this.

Based on this, the Universal Medicine Network drew a simple conclusion: Baidu attempted to use its dominant market position to coerce the Universal Medicine Network into continuing participation in Baidu's pay-per-click ranking-related business.

Wang Guanjue entrusted lawyer Li Changqing from Beijing Bangdao Law Firm to submit an "Anti-Monopoly Investigation Application" to the State Administration for Industry and Commerce, applying for an anti-monopoly investigation on Baidu for abusing its dominant market position.

On October 31, in a thick 91-page A4-sized "Anti-Monopoly Investigation Application" sent to the Anti-Monopoly Division of the State Administration for Industry and Commerce, Li Changqing believed that Baidu's blocking of other websites constituted abuse of its dominant market position. He suggested ordering Baidu to stop its illegal practice of blocking other websites, and imposing the maximum fine under anti-monopoly regulations, which is 10% of its sales revenue from the previous year, or RMB 174.44 million.

"There hasn't been any progress yet," Li Changqing said, noting that the State Administration for Industry and Commerce has not responded at all, "I estimate it's because the Anti-Monopoly Law has just come into effect, and specific implementation articles have not yet been released."

### The Confrontation

Debate Over Spam Information

Facing public uproar, after remaining silent for 10 days, Baidu issued a statement to the media on November 10, providing a direct response to the incident.

In the first point of its statement, Baidu noted that just as spam text messages disrupt people's lives, spam information, spam emails, and rogue software on the internet also affect people's normal use of the network. The ranking on search engines can generate enormous commercial value, so large amounts of spam information proliferate on the internet, aiming to gain commercial benefits through search engines. However, these spam messages severely impact people's normal use of search engines.

Baidu emphasized its "golden rule" of "user experience first," stating that "the ability to handle spam information is the core competitiveness of search engines. Every year, the technical investment in anti-spam exceeds the total market size of global Chinese search engines."

"Whether a webpage is indexed by a search engine has nothing to do with the fairness of the search engine or its profit model," Baidu stated, explaining that they would refuse to index some webpages for one reason only: indexing such information would not provide good search experiences for Baidu users. Additionally, due to technical reasons, search engines cannot guarantee the indexing of all webpages.

Baidu introduced that currently, the number of Chinese webpages indexed is counted in the billions. If these webpages were printed on A4 paper and connected, they could circle the earth more than 100 times. The biggest technical challenge faced by search engines is how to quickly find the page users need from these vast amounts of information. However, driven by immense commercial interests, the growth rate of spam information on the internet far exceeds the growth rate of valuable webpages.
