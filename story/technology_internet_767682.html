The market explains everything, Android and Chrome OS, stop causing trouble.

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 12:29:13

For a tech enthusiast, the relationship between Android and Chrome OS is akin to a palace drama on the silver screen. People always try to discern some meaning from manufacturers' new product releases and personnel changes within Google, imagining that there's also a game of intrigue going on inside Google.

In fact, when Hugo Barra left for Xiaomi (or even before that), some internal relationships within Google were revealed by the media. For instance, the Android department under Andy Rubin was always at odds with other departments in Google, and the current situation isn't much better either. On the other hand, Android is now at its peak, dominating the majority of the global smartphone system market share, and it's also developing rapidly in the tablet sector. As for desktops, Google has been pushing Chrome OS, and a series of Chromebooks have performed well in the low-cost laptop market.

Looking at some recent products and news, Android has basically taken center stage. Whether it's the Nexus 5 or KitKat, Google keeps "accidentally leaking" information, capturing our attention. Two other new products also reveal some issues: Android continues to progress, while the integration of Chrome OS still needs time.

Notably, Lenovo released its first Android notebook, the IdeaPad A10. This notebook features a 10.1-inch HD (720p) display, a quad-core 1.6 GHz Cortex A9 processor, 2 GB of RAM, 32 GB of storage space, and a battery life of 9 hours. Lenovo has also specially customized an Android interface suitable for a desktop environment. According to the description, the A10 cannot detach the keyboard but can flip like the Yoga model.

Besides some off-brand manufacturers, this is the first truly meaningful Android notebook produced by a major manufacturer that comes to mind. When most PC manufacturers were experimenting with Chromebooks recently, why did Lenovo suddenly release such a product?

According to Lenovo's sales plan, the A10 will mainly be sold in Asia-Pacific, the Middle East, Africa, and Europe, while North America remains the main battleground for low-end laptops dominated by Chromebooks. For Chromebooks that become useless without an internet connection, the main sales regions for the A10 are not very suitable for using Chromebooks, especially in Lenovo's home base of China. From a market perspective, it's more prudent for Lenovo to release a notebook with the familiar Android system than a Chromebook with limited applicability.

Additionally, Intel, which is actively seeking transformation, is collaborating with medium and small-sized manufacturers like Lamobo, opening up a new battlefield in the low-end tablet market. Generally speaking, domestic low-end tablets use processors from companies like Rockchip, whose technical capabilities definitely cannot compete with Intel. Traditional manufacturers like Dell have also equipped their recently launched Venue series of tablets (including both Android and Windows 8.1 models) with Intel's Atom processors. Although they are latecomers, it is clear that Intel has already begun a strong offensive into the Android territory.

Android represents the app-ification of the Internet, while Chrome represents cloud-ification; the former is the present, and the latter is the future. Although we are always looking forward to the future, manufacturers still need to make money in the present. Therefore, judging from the actions of Lenovo, Lamobo, and Intel, Android remains the focus. As for the question of who will absorb whom, it's too early to tell right now. Google understands that keeping these two systems independent and excelling in both is reasonable.

Although we can interpret some things from Andy Rubin's reassignment, Sundar Pichai taking charge of both the Android and Chrome departments, and Google's recent acceleration of Chrome OS, the market is the most realistic factor. Chrome OS has too many limitations: North America, low-end, desktop, whereas Android is versatile.

The acquired Android and Google's native Chrome OS are essentially distinct and each has its own role. The flourishing Android is like a valiant general, while the yet-to-mature Chrome OS is like a young crown prince. Google naturally understands this state. Should it be a peaceful succession or a hereditary monarchy? Google only holds half of the decision-making power.
