Missing business civilization

by lieencq09 on 2011-09-20 21:14:36

A business is first and foremost an organizational system that operates through a combination of internal structures, products, market operations, and teams. As the business progresses, with the expansion of its scope and deepening market presence, entrepreneurs need to establish long-term plans for the company to ensure continuous development capabilities. We often see businesses with comprehensive regulations, technology research and development, product lines, and market systems, but they neglect management structures and lack corporate culture. Such enterprises are not what we truly understand as businesses. This relates to the entrepreneur's ability, but more importantly, it is closely tied to the entrepreneur's vision and breadth of mind.

When most people focus on technology, markets, teams, or even business models to evaluate a company's value, perhaps we overlook the most fundamental perspective: Are you operating your business with the mindset of an entrepreneur?

The total number of Chinese enterprises exceeds 40 million, yet only a few can attract the attention of venture capital firms. Even among those that receive venture capital investments, a significant portion either fail or struggle. However, years of experience working alongside businesses have made us admire every entrepreneur who is dedicated to their dreams. It is precisely due to their efforts that we see hope in China's industrial rise, especially in the creativity of emerging industries, which will be a key engine driving China's economic development.

Therefore, without self-reflection on whether one possesses the qualities of an entrepreneur or whether one's enterprise deserves the title of a business, one will diminish their enterprise's value in front of venture capitalists. Regardless of the size or stage of the enterprise, from the very beginning, one should establish grand aspirations, build corporate culture, deeply understand the industry's essence, and embed this DNA into every aspect and corner of the business. Only then can such enterprises become the targets pursued by venture capital.

Meng Fanhua, General Manager of Northeast Regional Company, Shenzhen Venture Capital

When most people focus on technology, markets, teams, or even business models to assess and evaluate a company's value, perhaps we overlook the most fundamental point: Are you operating your business with the mindset of an entrepreneur?

KFC’s development speed and profitability in China have consistently outpaced McDonald's, and consumer feedback provides all the answers. Its signature spicy hamburgers and Original Recipe Chicken cater better to Chinese tastes than the Big Mac ever could. Yoshinoya's beef bowl always leaves a lasting impression, whereas True Colors has yet to produce a product that becomes a habitual consumption mainstream. From this analysis, we question the prospects of Yum! Brands' acquisition of Little Sheep because, if the food isn't good, consumers will vote with their feet. They won’t care if it shares the same roots as KFC, as demonstrated by the failure of Oriental Crispy.

Venture capital is not solely about achieving an IPO or creating capital market myths. Genuine venture capitalists must also possess great ideals and heroism, positioning themselves to serve small and medium-sized enterprises, finding joy in their growth, and honing their stress resistance and deep understanding of industries. When we demand that entrepreneurs fully unleash their talents, we also push ourselves to improve, strive, and create!

Often, I pose the first question to companies under review: Is what you're currently operating really a business?

We find that companies that understand the essence of their industry are more likely to excel within it, gaining strong competitive advantages. In the internet sector, Google's commitment to "do no evil," Qihoo 360's user-first approach, and Amazon's platform integration support set examples. Recently, when City Base went public, founder Li Hong stated that deliciousness is the hard truth of the catering industry, revealing its core essence. Though I haven't tried City Base, I refrain from making rash judgments, but comparing it with other dining establishments offers the best explanation.

Business success is often a low-probability event, where essential factors like products and services, organizational structure, business model, and teams are all indispensable. Here, entrepreneurs play a crucial role. Entrepreneurial abilities encompass a wide range, but primarily include: 1) Integrity; 2) Understanding the trends of their industry; 3) The ability to manage and balance complex organizational systems; 4) A grand vision.

Venture capital is an industry that tolerates failure, but without learning from these failures and gaining deeper insights into the industry, it is impossible to cultivate top-tier teams and service capabilities for enterprises. Innovation in an industry means an increased likelihood of failure, and venture capital takes on this risk, reducing pressure on entrepreneurs and greatly stimulating their innovative potential and energy. In high-tech fields, it's unimaginable to exclude the influence of venture capital. Otherwise, we wouldn't see the birth of Silicon Valley or talk about industrial structure upgrades.
