The use of DSA technology in the treatment of bone tumors in 15 cases

1 General Information:

In a group of 15 cases (10 male, 5 female), aged between 25 and 53 years old with an average age of 34 years. Clinical manifestations included local pain and masses. All cases underwent X-ray, CT or MRI and were confirmed by pathology. The types of tumors included chondrosarcoma (2 cases), osteosarcoma (10 cases), nerve fiber sarcoma, and giant cell tumor (3 cases). Two cases involved tumors located in the ilium and sacrum, 8 cases in the femur, and 4 cases in the humerus.

1.2 Equipment:

A PHILIPS INTEGRIS V5000 digital subtraction angiography system was used, along with 36 compound diatrizoate for embolization agents, lipiodol (40 ml) or a mixture of lipiodol and gelatin sponge.

1.3 Method:

Using the Seldinger method via femoral artery puncture, a 5-6F catheter was inserted under fluoroscopic guidance into the arteries supplying the tumor. Then DSA was performed, and based on the subtraction processing of the tumor blood supply, superselective DSA vascular path was achieved where the catheter tip reached the target vessel. Chemotherapy drugs and embolic agents were slowly injected through the catheter.

2 Results:

Angiography was successful in all 15 cases. The DSA images were clear, clearly showing the tumor feeding vessels and tumor vasculature as well as the extent of the tumor. All patients underwent local chemotherapy and embolization. Surgical resection in all cases showed less blood loss, and the difficulty of operation and resection compared favorably with similar surgeries.

3 Discussion:

3.1 DSA Technology:

Providing good, clear images of blood vessels is the premise of interventional treatment of bone tumors. Superselective catheterization using DSA vascular paths is the key to successful interventional treatment. DSA can eliminate overlapping in the blood vessels of the bone, clearly showing the tumor feeding vessels, tumor vessels, and tumor range, identifying vessel bifurcations and openings. By utilizing the DSA vascular road received diameter intubation ultra-election, the catheter tip reaches the target organ, making local chemotherapy and embolization viable. In this group of 15 cases, we first performed major arterial angiography: 8 patients underwent angiography, 4 cases of subclavian artery angiography, and 3 cases of common iliac artery angiography. This allowed us to fully understand the tumor feeding arteries and scope. The main supply artery was ultra-making intubation before chemotherapy and embolization, and angiography was confirmed again afterward. This not only greatly shortened the operative time but also avoided embolization of non-tumor areas to prevent complications.

3.2 Ensuring Good DSA Images:

To ensure good and clear images of blood vessels and show the relationship between the tumor and surrounding blood vessels, the following points should be noted:

① Preoperative DSA examination process and normal reactions such as a sense of pain after injection should be explained in detail to the patient to eliminate their fears and seek active cooperation. Meanwhile, during the inspection process, the check site should remain still to prevent movement artifacts in the subtraction images. In this group, 12 cases had lower limb lesions, and detailed preoperative instructions ensured no adverse image effects occurred.

② DSA technology has high density resolution, high sensitivity of iodine signal detection, and strong capabilities [3]. Therefore, the concentration of contrast agent used in the inspection process, dose, and injection rate should be appropriate. Too high a concentration may irritate the vessel wall, causing involuntary movements in the patient's limb, affecting vascular imaging. A low dose makes it difficult to obtain high-quality images. Our experience shows that using 36 diatrizoate with a dose of 15~30ml and an injection rate of ~6ml/s can obtain satisfactory image quality.

③ During DSA examination of limbs, especially the upper and lower extremities, both sides of the space are more open. Using pads or stereotypes or aperture blocks preoperatively ensures the object density of the irradiated region is as consistent as possible and enhances the filtration of DSA compensation, avoiding saturation artifacts caused by lost image information [2], which could affect tumor vessels and tumor extent.

In summary, DSA is a detailed, complex process, and the omission of any one link will affect the vascular image quality. Therefore, being familiar with DSA operations and taking full advantage of various DSA techniques to guide clinicians in successfully completing interventional treatments is crucial.
