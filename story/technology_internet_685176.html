Tasting wine requires understanding wine, and those who understand wine can better appreciate the charm of white wine. White wine is also called spirit or sorghum wine. The "spirit" we often hear in martial arts dramas is actually white wine. Maotai, Wuliangye, Yanghe, and other Chinese famous wines, although all classified as white wine, each has its own characteristics.

Firstly, from the perspective of aroma types, white wine has five major aroma types: soy sauce aroma, strong aroma, clear aroma, rice aroma, and other aromas. Strong aroma white wine occupies more than 80% of the white wine market, with Wuliangye and Yanghe being outstanding representatives of strong aroma white wine. In recent years, the soft and mellow type of white wine that consumers favor greatly innovates the strong aroma type, and Yanghe's Blue Classic series is the pioneer of soft and mellow type white wine. Guizhou Maotai, which has seen rapid price increases this year, represents the soy sauce aroma type, Shanxi Fenjiu represents the clear aroma type, Guilin Sanhua Wine and Guangdong Changle Burn represent the rice aroma type, while Xifeng and Sitte belong to other aroma types.

In the industry, the commonly mentioned [Chuan Qian Su, Mao Wu Yang] refers to the classification based on white wine production areas. [Chuan Qian Su] refers to the three major production areas of white wine: Sichuan Province, Guizhou Province, Jiangsu Province. Sichuan's rich and thick aroma is represented by Wuliangye, Luzhou Laojiao, Jian Nan Chun, etc. Corresponding to Sichuan's "six flowers" are Jiangsu's two Chinese famous wines Yanghe and Shuanggou (now merged into Sujiu Group), with a gentle and elegant style characteristic of Sujiu. Guizhou Province is the main production area of soy sauce aroma type white wine, with Maotai leading the way. [Mao Wu Yang] refers to first-line famous wines from these three production areas: Maotai, Wuliangye, Yanghe. Drinking in one gulp shows superior alcohol tolerance; sipping slowly brings another kind of taste. When tasting wine, one should first observe the color, then smell the aroma, and finally taste the flavor. Place the wine glass two inches below your nose and gently sniff the scent. Initially, don't swirl the glass; smell the volatile aroma of the wine; then swirl the glass to smell the stronger aroma. When tasting the wine, hold it in your mouth, spread it throughout your oral cavity, and then swallow. Use your tongue to press against the front palate and exhale the wine vapor through your nostrils. After drinking white wine, there is a lingering fragrance, and good wine has a longer-lasting aftertaste.
