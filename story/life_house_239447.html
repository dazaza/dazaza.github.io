Non-demolishable walls in home decoration

by longersing on 2009-09-17 16:21:31

Seismic Prevention Knowledge: Which walls cannot be removed during home renovation? We may focus on aesthetics and functionality during the renovation process, but overlook safety factors for moving in. Resistance assessments for some disasters are also easily ignored. Today, let's take a detailed look at this important knowledge and tell you which walls are absolutely non-removable seismic walls. In front of life safety, luxury and beauty can take a back seat. What places should not be arbitrarily altered for earthquake resistance during home renovation?

1. Load-bearing Walls

What kind of wall is a load-bearing wall? In general brick-concrete structures, all precast panel walls are load-bearing walls and cannot be removed or have doors and windows installed. Brick walls over 24 centimeters thick also belong to load-bearing walls. During renovation, load-bearing walls must not be tampered with, even making large holes in them is not allowed. Arbitrary demolition will destroy the load-bearing capacity of the wall. If we compare the house structure to a human body, the rebar in the walls would be like human tendons and bones. If the rebar is damaged while laying pipelines, it will affect the load-bearing capacity of the walls and floors. If an earthquake occurs, such walls and floors are prone to collapse or breakage. Additionally, beams and columns in rooms support the upper floor slabs; if they are removed, the upper floors will fall, so they must not be touched.

How to identify non-load-bearing walls and load-bearing walls?

Judging by sound: Tap on the wall, if there is a clear, loud echo, it is a lightweight wall, whereas a load-bearing wall should not produce much sound.

Judging by thickness: The thickness of non-load-bearing walls in the floor plan is clearly thinner than that of load-bearing walls. Load-bearing walls are generally thicker, almost as thick as exterior walls, usually around 24 centimeters.

Generally speaking, when load-bearing walls are brick walls, the structural thickness is 24 cm, in cold regions the exterior wall structure thickness is 37 cm, concrete wall structure thickness is 20 cm or 16 cm, and non-load-bearing walls vary between 12 cm, 10 cm, and 8 cm.

Judging by location: Exterior walls are usually load-bearing walls; walls shared with neighbors are also load-bearing walls.

2. Bathrooms and Kitchens

People with basic earthquake knowledge know that small spaces like kitchens and bathrooms, which have smaller spans, are relatively safer during earthquakes. However, these spaces' floors cannot be arbitrarily renovated; otherwise, they will transform from "safe zones" into "dangerous areas." For half-brick walls in bathrooms, if the building design includes supporting beams for the walls, then the walls can be removed; otherwise, they cannot. Moreover, the floors of kitchens and bathrooms have waterproof layers. If these are damaged, water will seep down, turning the downstairs area into a "waterfall cave." Therefore, when replacing floor materials, be sure not to damage the waterproof layer. If it gets damaged, remember to repair it promptly and conduct a "24-hour water seepage test," which involves filling the kitchen or bathroom with water. If no leakage occurs after 24 hours, it is considered qualified.

Renovating Brick-Mix Old Houses Requires Caution

Unreasonable layouts, too small areas, unreasonable functional distribution, unreasonable lighting, etc., are common problems with old houses. Therefore, many people equate renovating old houses with demolishing walls and knocking down floors as part of a series of reconstructive projects. In fact, most old houses are brick-mix structures where walls primarily serve load-bearing and seismic functions before acting as partitioning elements. If load-bearing walls are demolished just to achieve ideal spatial layouts, the walls' load-bearing and seismic capabilities will weaken, creating safety hazards.

Design experts remind owners renovating old houses to consider the structure and safety issues of their homes when redesigning, allowing for bold creativity within this framework. Otherwise, once renovations begin without proper planning, irreversible damage could occur.

3. Short Walls Next to Balconies

Most houses have a door and window on the wall between the room and the balcony. Many families choose to remove the wall between the room and the balcony to create an open-air terrace, which looks aesthetically pleasing but poses significant risks. The doors and windows at the connection between the balcony and the room can be modified, but the wall below the window cannot be touched. This wall is called the "counterweight wall," its function is to support the balcony. Arbitrary alterations will reduce the load-bearing capacity of the balcony, leading to the risk of collapse.

4. Entrance Doors

Many families make extensive modifications to their main entrance during renovation to "highlight the design." This will damage the building structure and lower the safety coefficient. The frames of these "three-proof" or "five-proof" doors are embedded in concrete. If arbitrary modifications destroy the door's architectural structure, reinstalling a new door will become even more difficult.

Indoor Seismic Prevention Measures:

a. Secure tall furniture to prevent tipping over and injuring people. Arrange items with heavier objects at the bottom and lighter ones on top. Avoid placing heavy items on top of tall furniture.

b. Remove or secure hanging items like lamps and clocks to prevent them from falling and injuring people.

c. Clear clutter to ensure smooth passage through the house, doorway, and corridors, facilitating quick evacuation during an earthquake.

d. Place the bed near interior walls (load-bearing walls) and away from hanging lights.
