With Hugo Barra leaving, what faces are left for Android?

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 13:59:38

For mysterious reasons, Hugo Barra, the head of Android product management, suddenly announced he would join Xiaomi. This was undoubtedly good news for Xiaomi, but a significant loss for Google and its Android department on the other side of the ocean.

Prior to this announcement, Android co-founder Andy Rubin had stepped down to work on other projects within Google. To outsiders, Rubin and Barra were the faces of Android, especially Barra, who frequently appeared at Android events to present and promote the platform. Rubin, Barra, and Vice President of Engineering Hiroshi Lockheimer were once seen as the three key decision-makers in Android product strategy.

Currently, Android is led by Sundar Pichai, the talented executive who also oversees the Chrome and Google Apps departments. Google states that Barra was an important member of the Android leadership team, with Android being positioned as a ubiquitous channel to carry Google services. So, with both Barra and Rubin leaving, where should the Android department go next?

To address this, The Verge interviewed some Google employees to understand the current leadership structure and development priorities of the Android team. Although it's unclear how Google will fill the void left by Barra, these interviews provide insight into what the Android team loses and how future leaders might guide Android forward.

Who is Barra?

In the past, the Android division felt disconnected from the rest of Google. Some employees attributed this insular culture to Rubin, who, despite his visionary leadership, showed little interest in the outside world and only collaborated with other departments on a technical level, through code file exchanges.

Barra emerged as a smart, witty manager with exceptional design talent within the Android team. Additionally, he excelled at live product demonstrations and public speaking. Originally from Brazil, Barra worked at Nuance Communications for five years on mobile voice search and communication before joining Google in 2008 and taking over Android product management two years later.

Unlike Rubin, Barra presented a more approachable face of the Android department. He could easily engage with people outside the Android team and possessed strong presentation skills. An employee from another department said:

"If you need someone to give a great demonstration, especially about Android, Barra is the first person you think of because Rubin wouldn't do those things."

Regarding products, Barra had deep insights. A colleague mentioned that within the Android team, phrases like "Barra wouldn't like that" or "This won't work because Barra will have strong objections" were often heard.

Of course, in a talent-rich environment like Google, Barra wasn't the only standout. With a deep bench of skilled employees, others are ready to step up.

Google's Deep Bench

Sundar Pichai regularly participates in various Google conferences. After Barra's departure, Google hopes other team leaders can continue driving Android forward, with Pichai playing a crucial role. He is integrating Google's other services with Android. Similar to Barra, Pichai has a good reputation and is leading Android toward greater openness compared to the Rubin era.

Pichai oversees Google's Android, Chrome, and Apps departments, all critical to the company. People wonder if he can focus solely on Android? Since taking over Android in March, he has been deeply involved in every major decision.

Hiroshi Lockheimer, Vice President of Engineering for Android, doesn't have the same public exposure as Rubin or Barra, but holds a key position. Both Rubin and Barra mentioned him in their resignation letters. After Pichai took over Android, Lockheimer became even more important, often representing Android in the company's weekly internal Q&A sessions.

Matias Duarte is the design director for Android and previously worked on Palm's webOS. Starting with Android 4.0, he focused on enhancing system design. Duarte collaborated with other teams to develop a new design language that spread throughout the company. As such, he is considered a pioneer in Android's design revival. A Google employee said:

"Android didn't have any design rules or styles to guide us, but after Duarte came, we made rapid progress in design."

Dave Burke is the engineering lead for Android and has a deep understanding of the challenges facing Android. Recognized as an extremely important part of the team, he works on platform engineering, which is at the core of Android. At last year's Google I/O conference, he introduced the 'Butter Project,' aimed at achieving 60 FPS animations in the Android system. In terms of engineering, Burke is the natural choice for promotion.

Additionally, Johanna Wright, responsible for Google Now; Jamie Rosenberg, Vice President of Digital Content and head of Google Play; and Vic Gundotra, head of Google+, all have the capability and opportunity to be transferred to the Android department.

It can be said that Google is like a team with deep bench strength. Even when key players are unavailable, the substitutes can still perform well.

Challenges Remain

The leaders of the Android team listed above still face significant challenges. Despite Android's 79.5% market share in smartphones, issues such as ecosystem fragmentation persist. It's also difficult for Android developers to attract paying users.

Moreover, wearable devices are gaining momentum, and pushing Android onto smartwatches and similar devices poses a challenge. Smart TVs face similar issues. These products require a product manager like Barra, who can guide development efforts and effectively promote the products to the public. Now, such a trusted and charismatic figure has joined Xiaomi.

The new generation of Android leaders faces many challenges, including longstanding issues like ecosystem fragmentation and the frustration developers feel about attracting paying Android users on Google Play. Newer challenges include expanding Android to new device categories like wearables and TV accessories. Those products will need a dedicated manager who can guide their development and explain the final product to the public. Google had just such a candidate in Barra until this week. Now, Google faces the pressure of finding a new ambassador for the Android brand who is trusted by the team and maintains a professional demeanor.

For the smartphone industry over the past few years, the emergence of the Android system has been groundbreaking. In recent months, Rubin has moved to secret projects, Barra has gone to China, and Pichai has taken on significant responsibilities. His biggest challenge lies in whether he can lead the team to fulfill Android's promise. The decisions he makes will significantly impact the future of Android and Google.

Google has some of the smartest minds working on the next generation of the Android system, and suddenly, for the first time in three years, Barra will not be involved. Rubin remains at Google working on secret projects, while his successor Pichai focuses on integrating the Android kingdom with the rest of the company's services. Pichai's greatest challenge is ensuring the rest of the leadership team can realize Android's vision. The decisions he makes now will shape the future of Android and Google for years to come.
