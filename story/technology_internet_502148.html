The evaluation of talent can be traced back to the ancient times of Yao and Shun, when the idea of entrusting responsibilities to capable individuals began to sprout. In the Western Zhou Dynasty, the Son of Heaven used "archery trials" to promote talented people, with evaluations including whether their actions conformed to propriety, whether their movements followed musical rhythm, and how many targets they hit. This was the earliest prototype of talent evaluation in our country. The imperial examination system practiced from the Sui Dynasty to the mid-Qing Dynasty was the largest talent evaluation practice in the world. In long-term personnel practices, our ancient sages made rich records of talent evaluations at that time and distilled many valuable ideas worth studying by later generations. Regrettably, these ancient ideas on talent evaluation are scattered throughout their writings or articles, with discussions found throughout classical texts. However, the "Character Appraisal" written by Liu Shao of the Wei Kingdom during the Three Kingdoms period provided a relatively systematic and complete discussion on talent evaluation, representing the culmination of ancient talent evaluation thought. Its ideas are profound, its content deep, and it is considered a classic of Chinese psychology, even referred to as a magical book. In 1937, American scholars translated it into English, naming it "The Study of Human Ability."

China accumulated rich thoughts on talent evaluation in ancient times. However, due to social, historical conditions, economic development levels, and cultural mentality, these thoughts were fragmented, experiential, and primarily qualitative evaluations. First, the economic development level at the time was low, mainly based on small-scale peasant economies or natural economies, with relatively primitive and backward production methods. The closed nature of the natural economy could not foster advanced talent evaluation techniques, and practical production relied mainly on experience, lacking a social foundation for the development of such techniques. Second, traditional Chinese culture emphasized goodness over truth, valuing moral rationality while despising scientific technology and instrumental rationality, dismissing all technical inventions as trivial skills or ingenious tricks. Due to this cultural mentality, talent evaluation techniques did not receive sufficient attention and, if they existed, could only be applied in rural folk contexts. The maturity of talent evaluation techniques is marked by an emphasis on quantitative evaluation. Although Mencius proposed the idea of quantitative evaluation over two thousand years ago—"only after weighing do we know lightness and heaviness; only after measuring do we know length and shortness; all things are so, especially the mind"—it was never concretely developed into technique. Third, relevant scientific technologies were either insufficiently developed or nonexistent, making talent evaluation techniques impossible. Modern talent evaluation techniques are established on the basis of modern psychology (especially psychometrics) and statistics. Without the development of psychology and the application of statistics, talent evaluation would be like water without a source or a tree without roots. As for behavioral science and computers, which are fundamental to modern talent evaluation techniques, these are more recent developments. The true rise of modern talent evaluation techniques occurred after the Western Industrial Revolution. The Industrial Revolution prompted a profound change in Western social production methods, highlighting the role of laborers in the productive system. Various enterprises and organizations must possess top-tier talents to remain competitive, with the key to talent selection being scientific evaluation. Thus, societal needs have provided broad space and solid ground for the development of the talent evaluation industry. In 1879, German psychologist Wilhelm Wundt established the world's first psychological laboratory, initiating research on individual behavioral differences, thereby triggering a long-lasting psychological testing movement. Psychological testing evolved from early psychological defect diagnosis to psychological assessment, expanding from the educational field to other areas of social management, providing guidance and services for student advancement, employment, talent selection, promotion, training, etc., becoming an important industry in the West. Since the 1950s and 60s, Western talent evaluation ideas and methods have rapidly advanced, developing numerous and diverse evaluation techniques, including intelligence tests, ability tests, aptitude tests, achievement tests, situational simulations, etc. These techniques are objective and scientific, widely utilized in Western society. Nowadays, whether government agencies promote civil servants, companies hire new employees, or individuals plan their careers, strict evaluations are required. According to reports from the American Human Capital Association, 50% of companies in developed countries use talent evaluation to select candidates. As early as the 1950s, AT&T in the United States tested hundreds of management candidates, sealing the results.
