Looking back at the past ten years: how Microsoft gradually became "MicroHard"

by anonymous on 2013-09-08 20:02:55

On September 3, when I learned that Microsoft had acquired Nokia, I, along with my peers, was utterly astonished. Although everyone had previously agreed that it was expected and reasonable, the suddenness of the news and the rapid development of events were breathtaking. For several days, even some industry veterans who have been focused on the field for ten or twenty years struggled to calm their emotions because this news conveyed two meanings: first, a century-old store with a solid foundation in industrial design and deep-rooted public affection was acquired at an extremely low price and in an unimaginable way (even if it was just the mobile phone business); second, the industry's development is increasingly trending towards a tripartite confrontation between Microsoft, Google, and Apple.

Ten years ago, Microsoft was the only upstream company in the industry, and almost all companies downstream were revolving around it, its hegemonic status evident. Nowadays, there are already two other upstream companies that can match Microsoft, and in the face of threats from Google and Apple, Microsoft has suffered repeated defeats and lost its luster.

Is such a change accidental or inevitable? Let's trace back through the timeline of Microsoft's strategic steps over the past decade.

In 2013, Microsoft acquired Nokia, which may be the most explosive news in the industry in 2013. There is nothing wrong with Microsoft's acquisition of Nokia's mobile phone business itself. According to various leaks, the reasons are as follows: 1) Expand the mobile phone market share and increase the profitability of the mobile phone business; 2) Create the ultimate experience for Microsoft phones; 3) Guard against patent wars between Google and Apple; 4) Leverage this acquisition to build Microsoft's smart mobile ecosystem and strive to win the mobile internet battle; 5) If there is anything else, it would be the return of good buddy Elop (please ignore this point).

On the surface, Microsoft's acquisition of Nokia seems like a good strategy, fast, accurate, and ruthless, consistent with Microsoft's usual hegemonic style.

In reality, from the above reasons, we see a few key words: "mobile phone", "Microsoft mobile phone", "Microsoft smart mobile ecosystem". Sensitive people will immediately realize that this is Microsoft's predetermined strategy, and it is a long-term product closed development strategy that Microsoft has adhered to. If we continue to trace back through the timeline, we will find that Microsoft's intentions are obvious.

From 2012 to 2013, Microsoft released Windows 8 and Windows Phone 8, intending to integrate and unify the three major platforms of PC, mobile phone, and tablet through the new generation operating system to compete with Google and Apple. At that time, Google's Android system and Apple phones were already very popular, each leading the trend, while Microsoft attempted to quickly suppress its competitors through the integration of mobile and PC, which was clearly against market rules. The market's reaction needs time, especially since Microsoft was at a disadvantage in mobile competition.

Although Microsoft released the Windows Phone system in 2010 and had a certain foundation, its response had already lagged behind Apple and Google before then (iPhone was launched in 2007, and Android phones in 2008). Moreover, the enormous and cumbersome strategy of integrating the PC platform and the mobile platform not only disrupted the mid-to-lower end of the PC industry chain but also hindered the development of the wp mobile ecosystem.

From 2007 to 2011, 2007 was the year the iPhone made its debut. What was Microsoft doing that year? Vista! Yes, Microsoft officially released Windows Vista. Perhaps from the moment Microsoft released Windows Vista, it began to decline; perhaps while Apple was developing the iPhone, Microsoft was also synchronously developing its own mobile product; perhaps if time could be rewound, Microsoft could have developed the iPhone earlier than Apple, hence the later claim by a former Microsoft executive: "When I first saw the iPhone, I exclaimed 'This is actually what we've always wanted (but didn't think of)'."

But there are no such ifs! In 2009, Microsoft released Windows 7, but it wasn't until October 2010 that it released the first version of Windows Phone - WP 7.0. By this time, the Android system had already reached its first milestone, with the number of officially certified Android applications in the electronic market reaching 100,000, showing how slow Microsoft's response was.

From 2001 to 2007, although Microsoft had just gone through the dot-com bubble, it continued to expand its influence with the release of Windows XP. These seven years were the peak of Microsoft's hegemony. On one hand, it calmly faced antitrust charges, and on the other hand, it continuously suppressed competitors in software, hardware, and the internet fields and tried to enter other areas such as video games.

One point worth noting is that Microsoft has always regarded free software, especially Linux, as its main competitor, akin to different ideologies. It is rumored that there is a famous book in the open-source community called "The Cathedral and the Bazaar," and many people easily compare the relationship between Microsoft products and Linux to that of the cathedral and the bazaar, which is incorrect. The "cathedral" in the book refers to "open-source cathedral," strictly speaking, Microsoft product development should be compared to "closed cathedral."

Microsoft's business model was the mainstream of the market in that era. Simply put, it was more "open" than Apple but more "closed" than free software. Microsoft once used this business model that fit the market at the time to suppress Apple and prevent the growth of free software. However, in 2001, Microsoft's arch-rival Google quietly started.

Ironically, later Google leveraged the convenience and benefits of open source to grow stronger and break through, achieving today's strength to rival Microsoft; and Jobs' return also succeeded through an upgraded "another kind of closed" mode, leading Apple to create industry miracles. Google and Apple adapted to the later market trends with different models, while Microsoft's model was abandoned by the later market.

Looking back at Microsoft's past decade, its strategic mistakes are multifaceted, but the main reason is deviating from timing. The development of the industry is not as fast as imagined, but it does not mean that the market has not changed. Just like why the internet bubble happened in the industry back then, yet today's big data hides huge gold mines. When Microsoft realized it, it was already too late.

Last month, there was news that Ballmer would soon retire, and he instantly became a target of criticism. But there is no need to overly criticize Ballmer; Gates also bears some responsibility. Many people believe that Gates' return could save the situation, but this is not objective rationality but rather a belief. If Gates could have saved the situation, he wouldn't have watched helplessly as Jobs returned and restored Apple. Regardless, both Jobs and Gates are great figures of this era, and their glories have become part of history. Now, Microsoft needs fresh blood, and recommending new talent is the only way out for Microsoft.
