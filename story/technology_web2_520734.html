In order to save money for Alia's treatment, Mr. Ababekeli and his wife have to skip a meal every day. Yaxin.com reporter Zhang Wand took the photo. Yaxin.com news (reporter Qin Jilli): 10-year-old Alia is a Han girl who was abandoned at birth and adopted by a Uyghur couple on low-income security with disabilities. In the past ten years, in order to treat her frequent illnesses, her adoptive parents not only spent all their savings but also incurred debts.

At noon on September 2nd, in the ward of the People's Hospital of the Autonomous Region, Alia, sitting on the hospital bed, was happily eating pilaf while her father, Ababekeli, smiled beside her and her mother, Amira, was busy serving tea and water. Alia wanted her mother to eat together, but seeing the only portion of pilaf, Amira quickly said, "I'm not hungry."

Alia has been hospitalized for over ten days due to urinary incontinence. As they couldn't afford to rent a room or stay at a hotel, her mother sleeps with her on the hospital bed at night while her father sleeps on the floor in the hospital. The couple provides three nutritious meals a day for Alia, while they themselves survive on just two naan breads.

Ababekeli, aged 52, and his wife both have leg disabilities and live in Wensu County, Aksu Prefecture. Before adopting their youngest daughter Alia, they already had four children. A document showing their low-income security indicates that the family receives a monthly allowance of 488 yuan.

Bringing the abandoned baby home in the cold winter

"Dad, you haven't eaten yet today, come and have some pilaf," Alia pouted as she looked into her dad's loving eyes. The couple always sent their daughter to a Han school, and the little girl speaks both Mandarin and Uyghur fluently.

Alia is very delicate-looking, with slightly dark skin and a height that surpasses her father's chest. She is cheerful and outgoing, making it hard to imagine that she is a sick child.

After finishing the meal, Amira took a stack of cotton cloth and led her daughter to the bathroom. Ababekeli explained that his wife was going to change Alia's diaper.

"She can't control her bladder, it has been like this since she was small. She needs to change diapers several times a day, but we are used to it," Ababekeli said. No matter how much it costs, he will do everything he can to cure his child.

Ababekeli said that he brought Alia home on December 11, 2000. At that time, the child was barely alive and her umbilical cord was still bleeding. Initially, his wife did not agree to adopt another child because their own four children were already in school and the burden was heavy. However, when they saw the child's umbilical cord still bleeding and her face turning purple, his wife's heart melted.

They bought medicine to disinfect the child's wound and saved a month's worth of grocery money to buy her formula. After careful care, three days later, the child finally opened her tightly closed eyes, and her face regained its rosy color. Only in Amira's arms did the child never cry or fuss, and she could no longer bear to give her away.

For the sake of treating their daughter, the family became destitute

Alia's arrival undoubtedly made life even harder for the Ababekeli family, who relied on low-income security. Their two elder daughters dropped out of school one after another, and Ababekeli, suffering from severe prostatitis, had to stop taking medication, saving the money to buy formula for Alia.

Not long after bringing Alia home, the couple noticed that the child often coughed and ran fevers. Upon examination at the hospital, she was diagnosed with congenital pneumonia. Since then, they frequently carried their child to and from the hospital, spending over 10,000 yuan on medical expenses. To gather this sum, Amira sold all her dowry jewelry, the most valuable television and carpet in the house, and borrowed some from relatives.

A year later, the child's pneumonia improved. Afterward, repaying the debt became the main task for Ababekeli and his wife.

"Our home has nothing valuable except for old beds and tables," Ababekeli laughed when talking about the state of his home, "As long as the girl is fine, this doesn't matter!"

Since Alia was physically weak, Ababekeli and his wife devoted most of their attention to her.

"Whenever there's something delicious, our parents give it to her first. They buy her new clothes. Every time we go out, despite Dad's bad legs, he carries her on his back, worrying that walking too much might tire her out," said Ababekeli's youngest son.

When Alia was young, she would often wet her pants and bed. Even at the age of eight, this problem persisted. After the couple took her to the hospital for examination, they found out she suffered from urinary incontinence. Thus, they started frequently taking their daughter to the hospital, but the treatment results were not significant. Neighbors advised them to simply send the girl to the civil affairs department, but they firmly refused.

"She is our lifeline, I cannot hand her over to someone else," Ababekeli said.

The greatest wish: to rent an affordable apartment

Half a month ago, the couple decided to take their daughter to a large hospital in Urumqi for treatment. They once again sorted through their belongings, selling whatever they could. A kind-hearted person in the local area gave them over 2000 yuan, and they borrowed some more from relatives.

Dr. Wang Yangang, Alia's attending physician at the People's Hospital of the Autonomous Region, said that preliminary diagnosis indicated that Alia suffers from primary urinary incontinence. Upon examination, it was discovered that there was a congenital fissure in the lumbar sacral region, but the urinary system showed no abnormalities. Recently, a sacral marrow MRI test is needed to check if there are any abnormalities in the spinal cord to make a final diagnosis. If the spinal cord shows no abnormalities, perhaps the child will recover as she grows older.

Before being hospitalized, Alia was in the third grade at a primary school in Wensu County. A few days ago, as the new semester was about to start, she insisted on going to school. The couple approached the leadership of the People's Hospital of the Autonomous Region. After diagnosis, the doctors believed that Alia could attend classes in between treatments. Therefore, the hospital helped her enroll in the fourth grade at a nearby primary school.

"Our biggest wish now is to be able to rent an affordable apartment close to the hospital. This way, cooking our own meals would save us a lot of money, and the girl still needs a lot of money for treatment!" Ababekeli said.

Aziguli, a staff member of the Fushouxiang Community in Urumqi, said that the community is currently voluntarily raising donations for the Ababekeli family and helping them find a nearby cheaper place to rent.
