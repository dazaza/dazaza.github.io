The impact of the US-Japan Security Treaty on China?

by lk0cswk on 2009-08-13 16:39:09

The US and Japan are in cahoots, but their internal interests are not aligned. The "Japan-US Security Treaty" seriously threatens China's security and interests, yet it can't do much to China.

The current issue is whether China's naval and air force can defeat Japan's naval and air force. With the current strength, it indeed doesn't look optimistic. The US military is even more powerful. After the US and Japan join hands, wouldn't that put China in a more disadvantageous position? Not entirely. The US is a superpower and to maintain its status as a superpower, it must have an absolute advantage over all countries in the world, not just an advantage over one country. Therefore, the US has too many things to deal with: preventing the rise of China, the revival of Russia, the growth of its ally Japan and the EU, guarding against India, being wary of Brazil, and dealing with the "Green Civilization" and counter-terrorism issues in the Middle East.

If there were a local conflict between China and Japan over maritime or island areas, the US would be in a very difficult position. The US will absolutely not blindly protect Japan's interests because that would not be in line with American interests. Both China and Japan are the most powerful countries in East Asia. China has been listed as a strategic competitor of the US. At the same time, Americans are very clear that Japan is also a potential rival of the US. Its huge economic power is enough to make any country uneasy. The US does not want either of the two major powers to fall, which would cause chaos in East Asia and instability in the entire world, and lose the two large markets on which economic development depends. If China and Japan engage in a local war and a war of attrition without causing turmoil, it would be most beneficial to the US, while simultaneously weakening the strength of two potential rivals. The disputes between China and Japan over the East China Sea and the Diaoyu Islands should be typical examples of this category. If China has an absolute advantage, the US will spare no effort to help Japan. If the two countries are evenly matched and Japan asks the US to implement the "Security Treaty", the US may be evasive, saying that the treaty is not for Japan's benefit but only to ensure that Japan's mainland is not attacked. If Japan has an advantage, the US will limit Japan's military development under the guise of maintaining security. Without the support of the US military, Japan's Self-Defense Forces cannot develop. The whole world opposes Japan's vigorous development of military strength. Only the US pretends not to hear. If the US opposes, Japan's situation can be imagined. Japan is like a variable resistor for the US. When China is strong, the US lets Japan counterbalance China to contain China. When China is weak, the US will find ways to stop Japan to avoid becoming its competitor in the future.

Rationally viewing the "Japan-US Security Treaty" will be greatly beneficial for China in formulating strategies.

Under the suppression of the US and Japan, China is developing its military and economy, chasing after others in all aspects. Without anything new, it is impossible to break through their blockade. In the course of development, China's pursuit of unification and energy crisis could possibly lead to wars between China and the current world's top powers, the US and Japan. According to China's current economic and military development, it will be impossible to defeat these powers within one or two hundred years. This is a hard fact. We should not irrationally hype up the idea of dealing with the US and Japan just because we are Chinese. That would just be like comparing an arm to a thigh.
