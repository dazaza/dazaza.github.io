Learning a Foreign Language

by mxhjjyl9r on 2010-04-19 20:04:19

Question for today: Has anybody ever learned a foreign language without living for a stretch of time in the country that speaks that language? With Italian, just through taking classes, I got to the point that I could have a halting conversation with any Italian willing to talk to me. I could make jokes in Italian. I could say almost anything I needed to say. One time I did an eight-minute monologue to a pharmacist about all my cold symptoms over the course of a week, in order to find out whether I should be taking antibiotics, etc. However, I still can’t watch an Italian movie without subtitles (I’m not even close to being able to do that), and if two Italians are talking, I have no idea what they’re saying and might not even know what they’re talking about.

With French, I can more or less READ conversational French and say MOST things I need to say, and I can have a VERY rudimentary conversation, but even then, I’m not even where I was with Italian. For the most part, I’m at the stage where I can ask a question, hear the response, and then go off for about two or three minutes to figure out what the person said.

The question is, is it even possible to get reasonably fluent in a language without spending at least six months straight in the country whose language you want to learn? Or without studying it in high school or college because obviously, pour moi (and per me), that ship has sailed. I’m still a good student. My memory is great. I have more than an average knack for language. But is it even possible to learn?

So: If you have or know someone who has learned a foreign language simply by study, how did you or he or she do it? A salesman was trying to sell me Rosetta Stone French the other day, but basically, you have to buy either levels 1-3 or 1-5, and from the look of things, I’m at least at level 3 right now. On the other hand, if you want to buy, say, levels 3 and 4, it costs more than buying all five levels. So I’m reluctant to spend six hundred dollars unless the program is really good.

I read on Wikipedia that if you study French (or Italian) for 480 hours, you can more or less learn it. That’s encouraging. I’m going back to Paris in September for more interviews, and it would be great to be better this year than I was last year.
