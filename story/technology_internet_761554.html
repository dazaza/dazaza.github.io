Facebook Earnings Preview: Wall Street Skeptical

by geekzhang on 2013-05-03 19:00:32

Facebook CEO Mark ZuckerbergMay 1, Beijing Time - A recent article from foreign media outlets suggested that just like an increasing number of young users, Wall Street is also starting to doubt Facebook. The article pointed out that although significant improvements are expected in Facebook's mobile business, investor confidence in the stock remains low due to unease about Facebook's future prospects. At the same time, user fatigue (especially among younger users) toward Facebook is growing.Below is the full text of the article:Just like more and more young people, Wall Street is also beginning to doubt Facebook. Although this social network giant is expected to make major improvements in its mobile business and its advertising revenue could see a 43% annual increase, investor confidence in the stock remains at a very low level. Since Facebook released its last quarterly earnings report, the company's stock price has remained stagnant, indicating that trust in the long-term health of the company is as uncertain as the trust parents have in their teenage children.In terms of data, Wall Street expects Facebook's first-quarter earnings report to show revenues of $1.44 billion and adjusted earnings per share of $0.13. Facebook is scheduled to release its first-quarter earnings report after the closing of the U.S. market on Wednesday (early Thursday morning Beijing Time).Goldman Sachs set a 12-month target price of $40 for Facebook. The firm is very optimistic about Facebook's ability to develop its mobile business, expecting its first-quarter mobile revenue to be $382 million, a 25% increase from the previous quarter, accounting for approximately 31% of the estimated total advertising revenue of $1.25 billion for the quarter. Market research firm eMarketer also cast its vote of confidence in Facebook's ability to generate mobile advertising revenue, predicting in a report earlier this month that Facebook will capture nearly 30% of the mobile display ad market share this year.These figures are not insignificant facts, especially since mobile business is seen by many as Facebook's Achilles' heel. Even RBC Capital Markets, the research firm whose outlook on Facebook's future is far less optimistic than Goldman Sachs with a target price set at only $32, agrees on one thing: mobile business is no longer the fatal flaw it once was for Facebook. According to RBC Capital Markets' forecast, mobile business will account for more than one-third of Facebook's revenue this year.So far, Facebook has successfully addressed one of the most important issues at the time of its IPO: the lack of mobile monetization capabilities, wrote RBC Capital Markets analyst Mark Mahaney in a research report on Facebook issued on Monday. Facebook's mobile advertising revenue is now showing significant growth and is becoming an important part of overall advertising revenue.But despite this, Facebook's stock performance remains sluggish. In the past 90 days, Facebook's stock price experienced several rebounds but overall, this period has not been friendly to the market value of the social networking giant. When Facebook announced its Q4 2012 earnings report on January 30 this year, investors were optimistic about its future, but they have now abandoned these favorable expectations for Facebook's stock price.In the stock market transactions of last November, December, and early January, Facebook's stock price once rebounded to $31.24, recovering 60% of the market value lost since its IPO; but then market concerns re-emerged. The core of these concerns is the increase in Facebook's expenses and the gradual decline in user attention to its desktop service. On Monday's regular trading session on NASDAQ, Facebook's stock closed at $26.98, meaning that even though Facebook released some of the most important products in its nine-year history over the past three months, this period has undoubtedly been a bleak one for its stock price.As investors feel uneasy about Facebook's future, user fatigue (especially among younger users) towards Facebook is also growing. Facebook has acknowledged that young users are spending more time on other apps such as photo-sharing platform Instagram. Although Facebook acquired Instagram, it does not use Instagram to sell ads. Meanwhile, messaging apps like WhatsApp, Kik, Line, and even Path are gaining popularity, providing hundreds of millions of users with more private social spaces.Facebook is likely to confirm again that users of its social network service are increasingly shifting from desktop to mobile; however, the majority of the company's revenue still comes from the desktop. In the previous quarter, the number of daily active users on Facebook's mobile platform surpassed the desktop for the first time, and this trend is expected to continue. In the short term, this could be a big issue for Facebook's revenue.Facebook has introduced retargeting ads in news feeds and oversized ads in an attempt to control the bleeding of desktop income; but for the first quarter, these hemostatic measures came too late. Also, whether larger ads will scare away users remains a question. Meanwhile, Facebook Gifts or social graph search function Graph Search cannot become saviors in the near term.Goldman Sachs estimates that Facebook's first-quarter revenue from the desktop could reach $864 million, down 16% from Q4 2012 and down 1% from the same period last year. Market research company Sterne Agee estimates that Facebook's first-quarter revenue from desktop ads could reach $907 million.For investors, what they need is evidence that the time users spend on this social site is as much as before (if not more). They also want to know whether Facebook's daily active user numbers and monthly active user numbers in markets like the United States are still growing; in these markets, Facebook appears to be nearing saturation.As for the products Facebook releases or acquires, unless each product brings some significant news, investors will not be satisfied. Is Facebook Gifts still a dud? How is the performance of Graph Search? Can Facebook use the new version of the news feed to improve user engagement? What is the revenue from news feed ads? What is the status of Facebook's acquisition of Microsoft's online ad platform Atlas? Can Parse, the mobile startup acquired by Facebook, bring significant new revenue streams? Will Facebook control its expenses? All these questions require satisfactory answers from Facebook. However, historically, Facebook only discloses basic information and does not provide answers that satisfy investors.Additionally, although Facebook's newly launched Facebook Home is clearly aimed at capturing the mobile market, it will not have much impact on Facebook's first-quarter results. Moreover, investors will hope for a detailed explanation from Facebook CEO Mark Zuckerberg regarding Facebook Home. They want to know how Facebook will address the unfavorable views about Facebook Home and determine the specific timing for placing ads in Cover Feed.However, no matter how enthusiastic Zuckerberg's answers may be, and no matter how promising Facebook's prospects might seem, for Wall Street, the disappointment brought by shattered illusions may already be so high that skepticism remains the prevailing sentiment.
