Google CEO Explains Vocal Cord Disorder: Rare Condition, Cause Unclear

by geekzhang on 2013-05-15 10:15:33

According to foreign media reports, on May 15th Beijing time, one year ago, Google CEO Larry Page was infected with a mysterious vocal cord disease. Due to this disease, he did not attend last year's Google shareholders' meeting and also missed the company's large-scale developers conference.When Page first appeared in public after contracting the disease, his voice was still very soft and seemed not quite well. Even now, Page's voice is still gentle. Given that Apple CEO Steve Jobs quickly deteriorated from a healthy state to illness within a short period and soon passed away without any public explanation during that time, the industry began to worry about Page. After all, no one yet knows exactly what disease Page has.This Tuesday, Page explained his condition via Google+. From the current explanation, Page's condition seems far less serious than Jobs'. Page explained that his vocal cords still had partial paralysis, which curbed his speaking ability. He also said that 14 years ago, his voice became hoarse due to a relatively common ailment at the time, but since then, his voice has never fully recovered. According to the doctor's diagnosis back then, Page's left vocal cord was paralyzed.Last summer, Page's vocal cord disease recurred. At that time, his voice was very hoarse and never fully recovered. Now, his second vocal cord can only move partially, affecting his voice to be even softer. Regarding this, Page said: "I am completely able to do all the work I need to do at home and in the office, although my voice is much lower than before. However, making long speeches might not be suitable." Page joked: "Sergey Brin said I might be a better CEO because I am more careful in choosing when to speak."When talking about his health issues, Page mentioned that his breathing would be somewhat restricted due to strenuous aerobic activities, but he still engages in kite surfing, so his overall health remains quite good. Page has not yet fully understood the cause of his voice disorder. He himself suspects it may be related to his lymphomatous thyroiditis, a type of thyroid inflammation where the vocal cord nerves pass through the thyroid gland. More than 10 years ago, Page was diagnosed with lymphomatous thyroiditis.Page's condition is extremely rare. Therefore, Page plans to donate funds to the Voice Health Institute to research causes of diseases like his.The following is the full text of the explanation issued by Page:About 14 years ago, I caught a severe cold, and my voice became hoarse as a result. At the time, I didn't pay much attention to this condition, but afterward, my voice never fully recovered. Later, I saw a doctor and was diagnosed with left vocal cord paralysis, a neurological condition that prevents the left vocal cord from moving normally. Despite extensive tests, the doctors were unable to determine the exact cause; there was speculation that it might have been caused by a virus resulting from the cold. Even now, cases where the final cause cannot be determined are quite common.This condition has never seriously affected me—my voice was just deeper and weaker than normal, and some people even found it interesting—but it started raising concerns about my second vocal cord. To my knowledge, the phenomenon of vocal cords being paralyzed one after another is extremely rare.Then, last summer, something similar happened again after more than 10 years. I caught a cold, and my voice became hoarse once more. The condition did not improve, so I went to the hospital for an examination. The doctor told me that the activity of my second vocal cord was also restricted. After further thorough examinations, the doctors were still unable to determine the exact cause.Thankfully, after some initial recovery, I am completely able to handle all the tasks I need to do at home and in the office, despite my voice being deeper and weaker than before. Delivering long speeches might be very exhausting, but overall, in the past year, my voice seems to have improved somewhat, with some people telling me they think it sounds better. Vocal cord nerve problems can also affect breathing, so my capacity for aerobic exercise has weakened. That said, my friends still think I have better endurance than them while kite surfing. Sergey Brin said I might be a better CEO because I choose my words more carefully. So, surprisingly, overall, I feel very fortunate.Interestingly, despite the fact that my vocal cord nerves take different routes through my body, they both pass through the thyroid gland. Therefore, when looking for reasons concerning both nerves, the thyroid will be a very obvious place to check. In 2003, I was diagnosed with lymphomatous thyroiditis, which is a very common benign thyroid inflammation and hasn't caused me any problems. It is unclear whether this is the cause of my current vocal cord condition or if my condition is virus-induced.During this process, I learned more about voice problems. Although my condition seems very rare, many patients suffer from some form of vocal nerve disorder. Among many experts, I met one expert, Dr. Steven Zeitels from Harvard Medical School and Massachusetts General Hospital Voice Center. This doctor is optimistic about the prospects of improving vocal cord nerve function, so I have decided to fund a meaningful research project through the Voice Health Research Institute, which is led by Dr. Zeitels. Many thanks to my wife Lucy, who has accompanied me throughout and helped take charge of this project and get it off the ground. Also, thank you to everyone who has provided advice and information, whom I haven't had the chance to thank before.Finally, we will conduct surveys among patients to collect information related to other similar cases. Since this condition is very rare, the currently available data is very limited, and the research team hopes to obtain more information so they can make breakthroughs quickly. If you have similar symptoms, you can log onto the website and submit relevant information at voicehealth.org/ip.
