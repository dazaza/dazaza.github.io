Man buys a bomb from the antique market of Song Dynasty, a priceless treasure

by ereia0354 on 2010-05-30 20:12:34

Nanjing Morning Post - Wang Shiqing is an antique enthusiast who enjoys collecting ceramics and paintings in his spare time. He often frequents the antique markets at Chaotian Palace and Nanjing Arts University's Back Street. A few days ago, he returned to the antique market at Nanjing Arts University's Back Street, where among a large pile of antiques, Wang Shiqing singled out an unremarkable "lump of iron." Surprisingly, this lump of iron might be a "bomb from the Song Dynasty."

According to Mr. Wang Shiqing, he usually likes collecting and frequently visits the antique markets at Chaotian Palace and Nanjing Arts University's Back Street for treasure hunting. This "lump of iron" was something he found during one of these trips. "The seller said it was a cannonball. He judged it to be from the Taiping Heavenly Kingdom period, so he priced it at 1000 yuan. After some haggling, we settled on 500 yuan," Wang recounted.

The reporter noticed that this lump of iron was about the size of a small lead ball, weighing three or four catties when held in hand. It was covered in rust and had a tiny hole on its surface.

However, Wang Shiqing disagreed with the seller's claim, stating, "Based on my years of collecting experience, I believe this item is a 'Zhen Tian Lei' from the Song Dynasty, not a cannonball from the Taiping Heavenly Kingdom period as the seller claimed." The Zhen Tian Lei is known to be the world's earliest bomb, used extensively during battles between the Central Plains and the Jin Dynasty during the Song era. Its use was discontinued later due to the inability to control the timing and distance of the explosion. The one Wang Shiqing purchased turned out to be a dud, which is why it survived. If it truly is a "Zhen Tian Lei" from the Song Dynasty, then this "lump of iron" would hold significant value. "To my knowledge, there are very few surviving Zhen Tian Lei bombs. Neither museums nor military museums have official collections of them. In Nanjing, I know of only one or two pieces. Therefore, this artifact has great research and historical value. At 500 yuan, it was quite a bargain; I stumbled upon a rare find," Wang remarked.

Mr. Zhou Daoyang, a senior national cultural relics appraiser, once collected a Zhen Tian Lei. The reporter reached out to Mr. Zhou, who explained that a genuine Zhen Tian Lei should be round and have a two-centimeter-long protruding "tumor" on top to facilitate the filling of gunpowder. However, the one Mr. Wang bought lacks this feature, making it uncertain whether it is an authentic piece. But if it were genuine, it could confirm that the Zhen Tian Lei was indeed widely used in warfare historically. "Currently, the National Museum only has records of the Zhen Tian Lei but no physical specimens in its collection, so it can be considered extremely precious," Zhou added.
