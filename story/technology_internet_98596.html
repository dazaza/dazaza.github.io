Interpreting the Details of "Chibi" that are Misunderstood by the Public with Historical Knowledge

by ufo on 2008-10-24 17:56:49

The movie "Red Cliff" can be considered as Wu Yusen's version of the Romance of the Three Kingdoms, and it is actually more historically accurate than the Luo Guanzhong edition that is familiar to the masses. Unfortunately, for a long time, many people have mistaken the literary work "Romance of the Three Kingdoms" for history, and all interpretations of the history of the Three Kingdoms that differ from the novel have been labeled as "distorting history". This applies to both Yi Zhongtian's "Commentary on the Three Kingdoms" in the past, and more recently, this movie "Red Cliff".

Since its release, I've seen many reviews that criticize Wu Yusen's movie for being closer to historical facts than the "Romance of the Three Kingdoms", under the guise of respecting history. For example, some people are very dissatisfied with the lack of scenes such as "Zhao Yun's seven entries and exits at Changban; Zhang Fei's roar breaking the bridge at Dangyang; Zhuge Liang's debate with a group of Confucian scholars". In fact, these are all fictitious creations by the Luo version of the "Romance of the Three Kingdoms" to glorify Shuhan characters. Director Wu eventually restored the historical figures to a more realistic state by eliminating similar plots, which we, as viewers, should appreciate rather than criticize based on the content of the novel.

Apart from similar storylines, "Red Cliff" also has many detailed parts that, whether intentionally or coincidentally by the screenwriters, are very consistent with the historical situation at the time. However, due to the public's lack of historical knowledge, elements that are closer to history are instead criticized as "fatal flaws" or even laughed at. Some of these comments even come from so-called "Three Kingdoms experts". Such ignorant misplacement is heartbreaking in my view, so this article attempts to defend some details in "Red Cliff" that are currently misunderstood by many people, from the perspective of popularizing historical knowledge.

1. The generals dismounting to fight on foot in the movie "Red Cliff" is reasonable.

This issue may be because shooting horse battles is dangerous and technically challenging, so there are more scenes of infantry combat in the movie. But from a historical perspective, it is reasonable for there to be more infantry combat during the Three Kingdoms period. The large amount of descriptions in the Luo version of the novel about generals holding their weapons in both hands, letting go of the reins while riding horses and engaging in individual duels, is actually wrong. Why? Because stirrups did not exist during the Three Kingdoms period.

Stirrups, mostly made of iron, serve to free up both hands. With stirrups, cavalry can control balance on horseback while charging, thrusting, slashing, and striking, greatly enhancing the combat effectiveness of cavalry. However, stirrups did not exist during the Three Kingdoms period. They only appeared in single form during the Western Jin Dynasty and in dual form during the Eastern Jin Dynasty and the Sixteen Kingdoms period. In the era without stirrups, people had to ride bareback, relying solely on holding onto the reins or horse mane and tightly pressing their legs against the horse's belly to prevent falling off during high-speed gallops. However, this method was unreliable, dangerous, and extremely tiring.

Therefore, Cao Cao's cavalry or Guan Yu, Zhang Fei, Zhao Yun, etc., could charge and pursue enemies on horseback, like Guan Yu once killing Yan Liang amidst thousands of soldiers while riding a horse. However, once they got close to engage in combat, they should dismount to fight on foot as shown in the movie. The criticism of Guan Yu and others fighting on foot in the movie, regardless of whether it was intentional or not, is actually commendable for accurately reflecting the reality of the time.

2. Zhuge Liang's small steps when meeting Sun Quan were a sign of respect in ancient times.

When I saw this scene, I deeply admired the meticulousness and respect of the screenwriters towards history. However, tragically, Zhuge Liang's actions were criticized as "taking overly effeminate small steps, completely losing his composure and calmness." Alas, the simple historical knowledge of "quickly walking to show respect to elders" is unknown to the public, who are in a state of complete ignorance. We can only lament that our ancient Chinese civilization is fading away.

"Tending" refers to taking quick small steps, which was a necessary etiquette for inferiors meeting superiors in ancient times. It can only be said that Japanese women still maintain this Chinese ancient etiquette learned from the Tang Dynasty, but it cannot be reversed to say that Zhuge Liang's walk in the movie resembles that of Japanese women.

3. Cao Cao's infatuation with Xiao Qiao is cleverly inferred, but Cao Cao has the suspicion of being a Lolicon.

Historically, Cao Cao indeed had the habit of being lustful and fond of married women, combined with Du Mu's poem "In spring, the copper sparrow locks two Qiaos," if the Battle of Red Cliff resulted in a defeat for Dongwu, it would be a very reasonable imagination for the two Qiaos, both beauties, to become war trophies for Cao Cao. Moreover, since the film is also intended for foreign audiences, Xiao Qiao assumes the role similar to Helen in "Troy." However, the same inference in "Red Cliff" is much smarter and more reasonable than in the "Romance of the Three Kingdoms."

In the "Romance of the Three Kingdoms," Zhuge Liang altered Cao Zhi's "Copper Sparrow Terrace Fu" to provoke Zhou Yu. The question is, isn't it strange for a father's lustful thoughts to be written into a son's fu? Moreover, altering a famous work like "Copper Sparrow Terrace Fu" obviously makes it fake.

In "Red Cliff," the movie cleverly uses historical records of Cao Cao and Qiao Xuan to infer that Qiao Xuan was the father of the two Qiaos, "Qiao Gong." Through the old acquaintance relationship between Qiao Xuan and Cao Cao, it is reasonable that Cao Cao met Xiao Qiao early on and became infatuated with her.

Qiao Xuan served as Grand Tutor during Emperor Ling of Han's reign. When Cao Cao was young, he alone received Qiao Xuan's admiration, who praised him saying, "Today the world will be in chaos, the one who will pacify the common people is you." Qiao Xuan also entrusted his wife to Cao Cao, making them forgetful old friends. Although the age and origin of "Qiao Gong," the father of the two Qiaos, do not match those of Grand Tutor Qiao Xuan, it's not a big problem for cinematic works to equate Qiao Xuan and Qiao Gong. However, in doing so, Cao Cao becomes someone who likes young girls.

Because Xiao Qiao's age during the Battle of Red Cliff can be inferred, she was approximately 25-26 years old (Zhou Yu married Xiao Qiao at 16-17 years old). During the Battle of Red Cliff, Zhou Yu was 34, and Xiao Qiao was at most 26, while Cao Cao was 54. Assuming Cao Cao was "young" at 40 when he met Xiao Qiao, she would have been only 12 years old at the time. Cao Cao's infatuation with such a young girl adds another flaw to his character besides being lustful and fond of married women - now he also has the tendency of being a "Lolicon."

4. Zhou Yu personally joining the battlefield has historical basis and cannot be considered a flaw.

I read a film review that listed five "major" flaws of "Red Cliff," one of which was the inability to understand why "Governor Zhou personally went to battle." Many people are still stuck on the idea of the strategical Zhou Yu from the "Romance of the Three Kingdoms" who never personally engaged in combat and only gave orders from behind a banner. In fact, historically, Zhou Yu was extremely brave in battle and liked to personally join the fray. This portrayal in the movie is actually more reliable than in the "Romance of the Three Kingdoms."

Historical records state that "Gongjin was heroic (the same evaluation given to Ma Chao) with great courage and strategy," plus his preference for "personally facing arrows and stones, regarding death as returning home," and he indeed "personally rode a horse into battle and was hit by an arrow." The movie just moved this event forward in time. However, in the context of the already determined battle situation, Governor Zhou personally entering the field to show off his martial arts and shielding Zhao Yun with an arrow does reflect the bravery and fearlessness of the historical Zhou Yu, though it might be slightly exaggerated.

5. Zhou Yu's child was a girl named "Ping'an."

It is unclear whether the screenwriter carefully researched and deliberately constructed this detail or if it was a coincidence like the random creation of the little horse "Meng Meng" which can be explained by the "Book of Rites," but there is a scene in the movie where Xiao Qiao is pregnant during the Battle of Red Cliff, and Zhou Yu listens to the fetal heartbeat, which perfectly aligns with historical timing.

Some people questioned why Zhou Yu's son Zhou Xun or Zhou Yin was renamed "Zhou Ping'an." In fact, in the movie, the one who was pregnant in 208 and gave birth in 209 was Zhou Yu's daughter, who later married Crown Prince Sun Deng. Historical records do not mention her name, so the screenwriter arbitrarily giving her a name is not a problem.

Why can we confirm that Zhou Yu's daughter was born in this year? Because Crown Prince Sun Deng was born in the year of the Battle of Red Cliff in 208, and his Crown Princess Zhou must have been close in age to him. Furthermore, the "Records of the Three Kingdoms: Biography of Cheng Bing" clearly records that Sun Deng married Zhou Yu's daughter in the fourth year of Huangwu, which is 225. In ancient times, women reached marriageable age at 16, so it can be deduced that Zhou Yu's younger sister was born in 209. If the screenwriters of "Red Cliff" meticulously researched and planned out Xiao Qiao's pregnancy in 208 after going through rigorous investigation like me, it would be truly amazing how perfectly it aligns with historical facts.
