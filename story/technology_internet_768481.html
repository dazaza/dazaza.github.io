Twitter's "Black Hole": Coexistence of User Growth Ailments and Revenue Potential

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 18:44:50

At 8 a.m. Beijing time on November 8, Twitter, which was founded in 2006, landed on the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE), opening at $45.1 per share, an increase of 73.46% compared to its issue price of $26. On its first day of trading, it closed at $44.90, up 72.96%, with a market capitalization reaching $24.5 billion.

After a year and a half, aside from differences in market capitalizations of billions and tens of billions of dollars, there is little that prevents one from associating Twitter's IPO journey with Facebook's.

First, both have had well-publicized "internal power struggles." Two years ago, the movie "The Social Network" dramatically portrayed Zuckerberg's past conflicts with the Winklevoss brothers and his former friend Eduardo Saverin. Now, a book titled "Hatching Twitter: A True Story of Money, Power, Friendship, and Betrayal" is selling hotly on Amazon. One of the main characters is Noah Glass, one of Twitter's co-founders who was kicked out in 2006. These two stories have caused global reflection within the internet industry.

Second, just as Facebook did when it went public in May 2012, Twitter was highly anticipated to lead the glory and dreams of internet stocks. "In the internet sector, Facebook's IPO in May 2012 brought hope to internet stocks, and this year, that mission will be handed over to Twitter," said a tech insider from Silicon Valley who wished to remain anonymous.

However, similar to Facebook, there are also continuous doubts about Twitter's future. Less than two hours after Twitter began trading, research firm Pivotal Research Group downgraded it to "sell." Denmark's Saxo Bank also believed that Twitter's IPO performance reflected the "absurd" nature of the capital markets.

"Twitter's stock price is currently about 77% higher than its IPO pricing, showing a separation between the stock price and the company's fundamentals," pointed out Peter Garnry, Director of Equity Strategy at Saxo Bank. "Twitter's current price reflects significant downside risk. If it cannot meet expectations every quarter, for investors, it will be a nerve-wracking journey tightly watching whether Twitter can satisfy ultra-high expectations."

In his newly completed article "Twitter's Question: To Buy or Not to Buy," New York University finance professor Aswath Damodaran also questioned whether Twitter has sufficient revenue capabilities to support such a high stock price.

"My valuation for Twitter is $18 per share, assuming that Twitter's revenue will climb to approximately $11.5 billion by 2023. But if the valuation of $45 per share is reasonable, then you would need this company to grow even higher. Calculated at $45 per share, Twitter would need to generate around $32 billion in revenue by 2023 and capture approximately 15% of the online advertising market by then," said Damodaran.

According to Twitter's IPO prospectus, Twitter's revenue reached $254 million in the first half of this year, with a net loss of $69.25 million. Over the past three years (2010, 2011, and 2012), Twitter's net revenues were $28.28 million, $106 million, and $317 million respectively, while its net losses were $67.32 million, $128 million, and $79.4 million respectively.

Avoiding Facebook's Pitfalls

Notably, the market's enthusiasm for Twitter seems completely unaffected by Pivotal Research Group's downgrade to "sell." Even during the first few hours of Twitter's opening, whenever its stock price dipped below $45, there were always investors willing to buy more shares. This contrasts sharply with Facebook's performance when it went public.

On May 18, 2012, Facebook went public, and its stock price fell more than 4% in pre-market trading the following Monday (May 21), breaking the IPO price of $38 on May 18. By the opening of May 21, it dropped significantly, falling 8.35% to $35.00.

An interesting question arises: why didn't Twitter's IPO suffer the same fate as Facebook? Forbes' latest article reveals that this is because the volume of stock purchases before Twitter's IPO was much lower than Facebook's, meaning stock supply choices made a difference.

The Forbes article points out that Twitter provided its stock for buying and selling transactions on secondary markets before its IPO. However, on websites like Sharespost.com or Secondmarket.com, the number of Twitter shares available for trading was typically less than the amount offered during the IPO transaction.

Facebook's stock price was pushed up to $45 before its IPO, far exceeding its initial valuation. The problem lay in the severe imbalance between buyers and sellers of Facebook stock before its IPO, which drove the stock price too high before the IPO.

"By contrast, the level of purchasing for Twitter stock before its IPO was much lower than Facebook's, thus increasing demand after the IPO. Therefore, the declining trend experienced by Facebook's stock is unlikely to reoccur with Twitter's stock," Forbes predicted. Investors will likely continue to favor Twitter stock in the coming months.

But there are other reasons for Twitter's nearly 60 times sales-to-market ratio, such as timing. The capital market has been extremely eager. Former Yahoo China General Manager Xie Wen, who is also a member of the expert panel of Sina Weibo Community Committee, wrote in an article: "After Facebook, the capital market has not seen an IPO for a long time, and there is too much accumulated capital. This causes new companies' offering prices to keep rising, and even their stock prices to rise continuously upon opening. Many people hoped to wait for Alibaba's IPO but waited too long without success. When new companies go public, funds flow toward these companies."

User Growth Slowdown

However, Twitter is also facing widespread doubts and issues that Facebook once encountered. According to my review of Twitter's IPO prospectus, Twitter currently has 200 million users, but its user growth rate is slowing down.

In its latest disclosed S-1 supplementary filing, Twitter announced that its monthly active users at the end of the third quarter of 2013 were 231.7 million, an increase of 6.13% from 218.3 million at the end of the second quarter. Twitter's growth rates in the second and first quarters were 6.86% and 10.27% respectively, and in the fourth quarter of 2012, it was 10.77%. This indicates that Twitter is encountering problems in attracting new users or retaining old ones.

"The decline in Twitter's user growth rate in Q3 was not as large as in Q2, indicating that its efforts to integrate into mainstream markets through TV and website embedding functions have helped," Josh Constine, a columnist for TechCrunch, pointed out in the article "Twitter User Growth Decelerating: +6% In Q3 To 231.7 Million Now Vs +10% In Q1": "However, looking at year-over-year data, Twitter's growth rate is also slowing down. Monthly active users grew by 38.74% year-over-year in Q3, 44.37% in Q2, and 47.82% in Q1."

"I said earlier this month that Twitter's lack of filtering information streams is one reason for its slowing user growth. As Twitter users follow more accounts, unless they use the already abandoned list function, they will get lost in the massive amounts of information and find it hard to discover points of interest," Constine also pointed out. "This could reduce user engagement and cause them to stop following more people. It also makes it increasingly difficult for new users to attract audiences, reducing platform stickiness."

Notably, "unfiltered information streams" may also present some difficulties for Twitter's monetization conversion.

Previously, a Silicon Valley insider told our reporter: "Twitter's information stream has the characteristics of being short, emotional, raw, and unfiltered. Although it is a good place to study consumer emotions and feelings towards a product or service, it is relatively difficult to convert it into something else. Advertisers need to monetize consumer behavior by simultaneously 'buying' user emotions (emotional) and logic (rational). A purely emotional appeal might make consumers feel uneasy when they see ads, as they perceive that they are being monitored for what they say."

Currently, Twitter's business model mainly revolves around advertising, including three types: "Promoted Accounts," "Promoted Tweets," and "Promoted Trends."

Promoted Accounts were launched in October 2010, allowing corporate users to purchase ad space in the Twitter sidebar to promote their Twitter accounts to relevant users. Its working principle involves deciding whether to recommend the advertiser's Twitter account based on the type of accounts users follow, using algorithms.

Promoted Tweets started in April 2010, where corporate users could purchase specific keywords so that related ads would appear when users search for those keywords. This marked the first major attempt by the four-year-old company to turn Twitter services into a profitable business. Ads appear at the top of search results when users search on Twitter, a model similar to Google's highly successful search ad system.

Promoted Trends refers to advertisers purchasing positions in trending topics discussions, allowing them to place ads at the very top of discussions on popular topics on Twitter. Its highlight is that it can trigger user discussions about brands, enhancing brand influence. For example, Coca-Cola, as the second-largest buyer on Twitter, saw its ad appear 86 million times, with a participation rate as high as 6%.

Revenue Potential to Be Explored

However, in terms of monetizing advertisements, Twitter has been criticized by U.S. media for being selective in disclosing public information and lacking sufficient advertising data. The Wall Street Journal website published an article yesterday titled "The Big Black Hole in Twitter's IPO Documents," arguing that although Twitter's IPO documents disclosed numerous growth figures, they did not reveal much about its core advertising business, making it difficult to truly understand its advertising operations.

"After spending a week carefully reading through the 120,000-word Twitter IPO document, I suddenly felt quite disheartened. The document is filled with various data and charts showcasing the rapid growth of the service. But if you look closely, you'll find it's impossible to find detailed information about its most important business areas," the article pointed out.

Its first point is that one day, Twitter's ad-buying process may become automated, similar to Google's, but currently, the most profitable ads are still handled manually. These flesh-and-blood salespeople call flesh-and-blood ad buyers. The progress of these sales processes will fundamentally determine the success or failure of the entire Twitter business. Any astute venture capitalist would want to know: how many advertisers are there? Who are they? What is the average scale and duration of ad campaigns? How many advertisers repeatedly use Twitter? They would also want to understand the numbers and costs of salespeople through detailed data tables to analyze the advertising business more deeply.

"These advertisers are Twitter's real customers, but how much content does the S-1 file provide? There is no data on advertisers, quality, or retention rates; there is no data on salespeople, only indicating that the sales budget has 'significantly increased,' surpassing $77 million in the first six months of 2013; and there is no real effectiveness data on Twitter ads, only stating that better-performing ads generate more revenue," the article pointed out.

Nevertheless, in terms of exploring revenue potential, Twitter is still considered to have considerable room for imagination. American investment bank SunTrust Robinson Humphrey released a research report stating that Twitter has four major revenue-generating businesses to explore.

The first is video promotion. This is seen as one of Twitter's biggest opportunities—utilizing its video content promotion tool Amplify to gain a share in the $200 billion global television market. Amplify allows broadcasters to release video clips and ads via Twitter, synchronized with programs aired on television.

The second is keyword search. The keyword targeting feature that Twitter launched in May this year could also boost its revenue. Twitter could leverage its search function to launch products similar to Google and Baidu's keyword bidding ranking systems. The third is e-commerce, the path taken by Sina Weibo, embedding e-commerce into Twitter. The fourth is mobile app distribution channels. In its IPO documents, Twitter disclosed that three-quarters of its monthly active users are mobile users.

Buying into the future of mobile seems to already be a consensus among capital markets. According to Twitter's IPO prospectus, 75% of its users access the platform through mobile devices, and approximately 71% of its advertising revenue comes from mobile, with this proportion continuing to rise. These factors are undoubtedly reasons for the elevated price of Twitter's IPO on its first day.

However, the Wall Street Journal remains sharp-tongued. "I estimate that Twitter will say that the IPO is an extraordinary opportunity, and these minor details (advertiser data) are not important. There are many trends and opportunities in the mobile advertising industry, so why focus on these?"

"This is indeed one theory for buying Twitter stock," the Wall Street Journal continued. "But investors must understand that they are buying the stock based solely on a theory, with little factual basis. In Wall Street parlance, Twitter remains a mysterious 'black box.' Its exterior may be beautifully decorated, but since it always remains covered, it is ultimately still a black box."
