Concept For A Social App Store On Firefox OS

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 14:08:58

There is no doubt that Firefox OS is still very niche, but this does not mean it is a system without innovation.

Not long ago, Mozilla announced a new attempt on its official blog: a socialized app store.

For many people, the app store is usually just a place where you download something and leave. A fixed homepage, cold app recommendations. After entering the app store, users either check out what's good in the Top 10, or directly input the name of a game recommended by a friend into the search bar.

The app store is too cold, making it hard for users to want to stay.

The biggest problem is that this is a place without interaction, only official information output, and users can only accept it as it is. Even if the "editor's choice" on the homepage is not attractive, even if your eyes are repeatedly assaulted by the top three apps like "Everyday Chain Hero," "Everyday Puzzle & Dragons," and "Everyday Shoot 'Em Up" when you enter the Top 10 rankings, this is life, and you can't say no.

We desperately search for applications that suit us. Is there a little corner that can leave the decision-making power to the user?

Well, the app store is still lacking a bit of human touch, at least that's what Mozilla thinks.

Mozilla's idea is that the homepage of the app store should always be tailored to serve the individual user, introducing a concept similar to the Feed in RSS subscriptions.

When users first enter the app store, the homepage will recommend apps based on their preferences. What sets it apart from other app stores is that each app introduction will have a like button similar to Facebook's (here represented by a heart icon). By analyzing the user's likes and unlikes over a long period, the content pushed by the app store will become increasingly precise.

A thousand people will have a thousand different homepages for the app store, which is the goal of the Mozilla team.

In addition, Firefox OS aims to make the app store more human-centered. In traditional app stores, "editor's choice" may just be a title aimed at pleasing users, because it is just a link, but users cannot see who the so-called "editor" is.

However, Mozilla's idea is that app recommendations should be more like a social network, with apps being recommended under the names of community moderators and experts, much like posting tweets. Each "tweet" includes the recommender's photo and name, and clearly records the number of views and likes for that recommendation. If at this point you click to enter or give a like, the numbers immediately reflect the feedback, thus creating a sense of participation.

Of course, in this socialized app store, there are no longer cold "content summaries" or "more" buttons, but rather more lifelike information exchanges. For example, the system might push a new app based on your preferences, and Firefox's iconic fox would pop up and say, "Wow, this could help you discover how cool this app is!"

The official statement indicates that this socialized app store is currently in the conceptual stage. However, given its high level of completion, there is a good chance it will go live.

It looks like a pretty neat idea. (Appso: Isn't this what I've been doing all along!? Quick, give it a thumbs up!)

But don't forget, for an ecosystem to shine, it needs a sufficient number of apps!
