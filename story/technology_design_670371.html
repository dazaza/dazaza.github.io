Patients requiring indwelling double J tubes can reduce vesicoureteral reflux symptoms by avoiding bilateral ureteral indwelling double J tubes.

4.2 Ureter laparoscopic surgery itself generally results in damage to the ureteral mucosa after the procedure, leading to varying degrees of hematuria. As a stent material, the double J catheter itself can stimulate edema of the ureter or renal pelvis mucosa. Over an extended period within the body, it may cause mucosal damage and bleeding. Since blood and urine are good culture media for bacteria, to avoid urinary tract infections, clinical routine involves administering antibiotics to reduce or mitigate urinary irritation symptoms in patients with the catheter.

4.3 The double J catheter is usually placed in the correct position, so shifting rarely occurs, with an incidence of 1.85% in this group. The main reasons for shifting are inappropriate use of double J tube length or incorrect placement. A relatively short double J catheter may shift upward in patients with a relatively long ureter. In two cases, inappropriate lengths led to ureteroscopic occurrences where relatively short ureters in patients had relatively long double J catheters. Afterward, the distal end of the double J catheter pushed into the urethral bladder opening, causing incontinence-like symptoms. Pushing the distal end of the double J catheter back into the bladder or removing it can relieve symptoms. Therefore, choosing the appropriate length and method of double J catheter placement, especially under C-arm monitoring or follow-up KUB, can significantly reduce the occurrence of double J catheter displacement.
