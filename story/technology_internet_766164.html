US privacy groups try to block Facebook's attempt to change policy

by anonymous on 2013-09-08 19:25:08

On the afternoon of September 5, according to Beijing time, the network edition of The New York Times reported that a coalition of six well-known consumer privacy protection groups sent a letter to the US Federal Trade Commission (hereinafter referred to as "FTC") on Wednesday, demanding that it prevent Facebook from adjusting its privacy policy.

Violation of Important FTC Regulations

The new Facebook privacy policy will take effect later this week. These consumer privacy protection groups believe that under the new policy, Facebook will be able to more easily use personal data in advertisements posted on its website, including data from minors under the age of 18.

In the letter, the coalition stated that Facebook's adjustment of its privacy policy violates the settlement agreement reached with the FTC in 2011 regarding user privacy. The letter reads: "Facebook users think that without their permission, their images and content will not be used for commercial purposes, which is actually just wishful thinking. In reality, users will find that photos displayed on their friends' pages include products from Facebook advertisers. Notably, Facebook also uses their photos and content in products they dislike or do not even use."

Vindu Goel, a contributor to The New York Times, wrote last week that based on the wording in Facebook's new privacy policy, Facebook appears to render default user privacy settings ineffective when it comes to advertising issues. Previously, Facebook's language clearly indicated that users have the right to control how their names, photos, and other information are used for advertisements and other commercial purposes.

Under the new Facebook privacy policy, users automatically grant Facebook the right to use their information unless they explicitly state otherwise. However, because Facebook has set up numerous obstacles for users to exercise this right, it becomes more difficult to prevent Facebook from using their information.

Infringement on Minors' Privacy Rights

Marc Rotenberg, Executive Director of the Electronic Privacy Information Center, said in an interview: "Facebook now claims that under default settings, the company can use users' names and photos for advertisements and other commercial purposes without their consent, including those of minors. A red light has already lit up in the privacy domain."

These privacy protection groups helped persuade the FTC to issue the 2011 order, stating that Facebook's privacy adjustments concerning minors are particularly problematic. Facebook's new policy states that if the user is under 18 years old, "this means at least one parent or legal guardian also agrees to these terms, i.e., Facebook has the right to use names, photos, content, and other information."

Rotenberg said: "Such statements are extremely rare. This is actually a taboo area; Facebook should not do these things without explicit user consent." He also noted that courts and regulators have concluded that teenagers' personal information should receive special protection.

With nearly 1.2 billion users worldwide, Facebook explained some of the reasons for adjusting its privacy policy last week, saying that due to recent class action lawsuits in the United States over privacy practices, it must provide further clarification on how it uses user information.

New Policy Criticized by Users

Debbie Frost, a spokesperson for Facebook, stated in a statement on Wednesday: "According to this policy update, we revised the description of how user names, photos, and content are used in advertisements or commercial content-related activities, clearly pointing out that once users use our services, it means they allow Facebook to use this information. We have not changed our advertising practices or policies; we are simply further explaining how users use our services."

On the official page where Erin Egan, Facebook's head of privacy policy, announced the privacy policy adjustment, almost all comments from users were negative. In addition to the Electronic Privacy Information Center, groups requesting that the FTC stop Facebook from adjusting its privacy policy include the Center for Digital Democracy, Consumer Watchdog, Patient Privacy Rights, U.S. PIRG, and Privacy Rights Clearinghouse.

Later on Wednesday, an FTC spokesperson had not yet confirmed whether the letter was received or made any other comments. Typically, the FTC reviews such letters. For example, after reaching a settlement with Facebook in 2011, the FTC regularly examines Facebook's practices to determine whether the company complies with FTC regulations.
