Why Nokia launches a notebook? Maybe to help Microsoft maintain the advantage of Windows.

by anonymous on 2013-10-15 21:30:38

Recently, Nokia released a poster for its upcoming "Nokia World" event on October 22nd. The poster suggests that Nokia may not only unveil new phones and the widely rumored tablet at this event, but also introduce a notebook computer.

In the meantime, market research firm IDC also released the latest data: In the third quarter of 2013, global PC shipments amounted to 81.6 million units, a near 8% year-over-year decrease. This marks the sixth consecutive quarter of year-over-year declines in global PC shipments.

In light of the cooling demand for global PCs, why would traditional mobile phone manufacturer Nokia still want to enter this field?

Nokia's Notebook Launch May Have Been Tacitly Approved by Microsoft

After selling its mobile phone business to Microsoft, Nokia is left with three divisions: network equipment, Nokia Maps, and advanced technologies—none of which are involved in the development, design, or production of notebook computers. Additionally, Nokia only launched one notebook computer in 2009, the Booklet, which ultimately failed, leaving little accumulated experience. Therefore, it’s unlikely that the post-sale Nokia would establish a new department specifically for notebook computers.

From another perspective, Nokia announced the sale of its mobile phone business to Microsoft on September 5th. From September 5th to October 22nd, in less than fifty days, it would be difficult to complete the entire process from project initiation, design, R&D, production, to testing for a notebook. Therefore, it can be inferred that Nokia had already started this project before Microsoft's acquisition. However, after the acquisition, Microsoft did not halt this product, suggesting that Nokia's strategy might have been tacitly approved by Microsoft.

Why Does Microsoft Support Nokia's Notebook Launch?

Before launching the Surface tablet, Microsoft was positioned externally as a software and services-focused tech company. However, with the growing popularity of tablets and smartphones, Microsoft also saw the vast business opportunities in the hardware market. Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer repeatedly emphasized that Microsoft is undergoing a comprehensive transformation, continuing to focus on Windows while evolving into a device and service company. Thus, Microsoft launched its own brand of Surface tablets and acquired Nokia's mobile phone business.

However, Microsoft's ambitions seem to extend beyond this. Ballmer's statements subtly suggest that Microsoft aims to layout across the entire consumer electronics industry. Although traditional PCs are currently experiencing a downturn, they remain an important part of this industry. HP and Dell's financial reports show that notebook computers continue to bring in several billion dollars in revenue each quarter for these traditional PC manufacturers—a significant attraction for Microsoft.

Moreover, although Windows remains the operating system with the highest market share, it has shown signs of decline. In September, Windows' market share was 90.83%, down 0.36 percentage points from August and nearly one percentage point from the end of last year. Meanwhile, Lenovo, Dell, HP, and other former allies of Microsoft are gradually aligning with Google, developing notebooks that run on the Android system.

Perhaps Microsoft has already sensed the impending crisis, so it intends to develop its own branded notebook computers. It aims to leverage the years of reputation built by Windows and Nokia's advantages in traditional hardware to stabilize Windows' market share. After all, Microsoft has yet to establish its dominance in the mobile device sector, so it naturally cannot afford to lose ground in the traditional PC sector.
