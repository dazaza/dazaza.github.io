Nursing Care for Patients with Senile Dementia

Encourage patients to study, read newspapers, listen to the radio, and watch TV, to accept a variety of stimuli from the outside world. This plays an important role in preventing further intellectual decline.

4.1.2 Orientation Training: In patients with Alzheimer’s disease, disorientation is often manifested as confusion about time and place, with poor sense of direction. It is necessary to help them recognize roads and identify their homes, assisting them in continuously strengthening this ability to reduce difficulties caused by disorientation.

4.1.3 Promotion of Self-care Ability Maintenance: Patients with Alzheimer’s may have poor health, diet, toileting, and living self-care abilities. Severe cases may lose self-care ability altogether. For these patients, help them lead a good life. However, for patients with mild to moderate dementia, nursing staff should train and enhance their living skills. The functionality of the human brain, body, and limbs can deteriorate if not used. If patients often forget about eating, washing, bathing, dressing, toileting due to memory loss, or even cannot complete these daily activities because of severe illness, nurses should provide timely reminders, supervision, and assistance. Assistance should only be given when absolutely necessary, encouraging patients to dress, wash, eat, and use the toilet themselves. Help them take the right posture and movements. These must not be overly arranged, as doing so would accelerate the development of dementia. Patients with Alzheimer’s often move slowly; don't rush them. Allow patients adequate time to complete tasks. Arrange specific times for patients to read newspapers, watch TV, and interact with their surroundings, to spread morbid thinking and foster interest in life. Nurses and family members should talk more with patients, encourage them to make friends and participate in social activities, and strengthen the training of thinking, memory, and calculation abilities.

4.2 Coordination and Guidance for Families: During hospitalization, most elderly dementia patients are accompanied by family members. It is essential to maintain close contact with families and help them understand the patient's condition, teaching them how to take care of the patient’s daily life. Encourage them to talk more with the patient about past people and events to stimulate the patient’s long-term memory, which facilitates recent memory. Additionally, regarding the feelings of the families towards the patients, loved ones they once knew and loved may seem non-existent, leading to feelings of apathy. Caring for elderly patients with dementia can be a long and arduous task, during which family members may feel helpless, frustrated, and lose confidence. Nurses, while caring for patients, should also enlighten family members, encouraging them to patiently care for the patient and give them the warmth of family. This allows patients to appreciate the care and support of their family, giving them the confidence to overcome the disease.
