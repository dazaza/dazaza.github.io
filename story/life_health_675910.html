What are the early symptoms of phlebitis?

What are the early symptoms of phlebitis? So what are the early symptoms of phlebitis?

1. Thrombophlebitis of Superficial Veins

Thrombophlebitis of superficial veins generally includes thrombophlebitis of superficial veins and migratory thrombophlebitis. Thrombophlebitis of superficial veins often occurs in the superficial veins of the limbs, such as the great saphenous vein, small saphenous vein, cephalic vein, or main veins. The early symptoms of phlebitis mainly include local pain and swelling. Along the path of the affected vein, a tender cord-like object can be felt, with increased skin temperature and slight redness around it. Generally, there are no systemic symptoms. After 1-3 weeks, the venous inflammation gradually subsides, leaving behind a hard cord-like object and brown pigmentation on the skin that does not fade easily, recurring over time.

2. Deep Vein Thrombosis (Thrombophlebitis of Deep Veins)

The severity of symptoms of deep vein thrombophlebitis varies, depending on the location of the affected vein, the degree of blockage, and the extent of the obstruction. Some patients may have no symptoms at all, while others may present with large pulmonary embolism as the first symptom. The inflammation and thrombus formation mostly occur in the calf veins or popliteal veins of the lower limbs, causing local pain that worsens when walking. Mild cases may only experience a feeling of heaviness locally, which becomes more noticeable when standing.

Swelling of the affected limb, tenderness in the calf muscles, popliteal fossa, inner groin, and other areas may occur. At the same time, mild systemic symptoms such as fever, fatigue, tachycardia, elevated white blood cells, and increased sedimentation rate may accompany these local symptoms. When the iliac and femoral vein thrombi extend into the inferior vena cava, the symptoms and signs of bilateral iliac and femoral vein thrombosis may appear.

Both lower limbs and the external genitalia may show significant edema, with the pain extending upward. Later, varicose veins may develop in both abdominal walls, chest walls, and buttocks. However, sometimes this varicose veins can be masked by obvious edema. Occasionally, due to a sharp reduction in the return blood flow from the lower limbs, hypovolemic shock may occur.
