Water price hikes in many Chinese cities, experts blame government failure

Some local governments simply "sold off" state-owned enterprises, leading to a series of problems as the government lost control over water companies. Economic Information Daily reported on December 2 that the impulse for the rise in water prices came from two aspects: one was the necessity for resource prices to gradually rise from their original low levels so as to ensure the transformation of China's economic growth model from extensive to intensive; the other was the implementation of an active fiscal policy at present, which put great financial pressure on local governments and made them increasingly reluctant to subsidize the water sector.

Since the comprehensive price increase of Guangzhou tap water on January 1 this year, many cities across the country have successively started price hikes. The debate over the reasons for the rise in water prices has not reached a conclusion. At the recently held Fourth International Symposium on the Development of Urban Waterworks in China, reporters once again felt the pressure of rising water prices and the fierce debate about water reform.

Focus 1: "Price Increase" is a topic that cannot be avoided in water reform

On the one hand, urban residents have many complaints about water quality and water prices; on the other hand, water supply companies also have a lot of grievances. And "price increase" is a topic that the entire water industry cannot avoid. At the symposium, representatives of water companies and associations generally expressed that water prices were too low, affecting the development of the industry and water supply security, and that a price increase was imminent.

Regarding why water prices are rising, Gao Huiqing, a member of the Expert Committee of the National Information Center, told the Economic Information Daily that there were two reasons for the price increase: one was that China was currently facing an economic transformation period, and it was necessary for resource prices to gradually rise from their original low levels, so as to ensure the transformation of China's economic growth model from extensive to intensive; the second was the current implementation of an active fiscal policy, which put great financial pressure on local governments and made them increasingly reluctant to subsidize the water sector.

Hong Juemin, former secretary-general of the Zhejiang Urban Water Industry Association, attributed the "price increase" to insufficient government investment. He pointed out at the symposium: "The main investor in urban water supply should be the local government, but now all the funds for water plant expansion, pipeline renovation and extension, and integrated water supply are self-raised by enterprises. Where does the money come from? The result is a price increase."

Ma Zhong, professor at the School of Environment of Renmin University of China, believed that water price reform was a "seemingly correct but actually incorrect" issue, and all the reasons for price increases could actually be explained in the opposite way. For example, the "enterprise loss theory", but the public did not know how the losses occurred; for example, the "international alignment theory", but national income had not aligned with international standards; for example, the "resource conservation theory", but water was a basic need for residents.

Gao Huiqing pointed out the path that water price increases must follow, namely, from the perspective of sustainable development, operations should allow water companies to break even, preferably with a small profit, so as to enable independent operation when local government finances are difficult. When financial resources improve, upgrades and expansions can be carried out. At the same time, water companies are non-profit institutions, so prices cannot rise indefinitely. From the perspective of public acceptance, the magnitude of water price increases should not be too large, and cities should not concentrate on price increases. Instead, they should be staggered.

Focus 2: "Government" temporarily becomes the "target"

China's water marketization has gone through seven years, "should we continue down the marketization path or seek new breakthroughs in reform." Hong Juemin, former secretary-general of the Zhejiang Urban Water Industry Association, gave the answer that it was now time for "public water supply".

"Public water supply" emphasizes the responsibility of the government in public utilities and clarifies that the government is the main investor in public utilities.

Xu Zongwei, deputy director of the Department of Regulations of the Ministry of Housing and Urban-Rural Development, also pointed out that urban water supply and drainage is a public service, and no matter how reforms develop, the public functions of the government should never change.

"The relationship between government and enterprises" is one of the three major relationships that Gao Huiqing believed needed to be adjusted in water reform. In fact, the close relationship between the Chinese government and water companies has not been long. It wasn't until 2002, when the Ministry of Construction issued the "Opinion on Accelerating the Marketization Process of Municipal Public Utilities," that enterprises became deeply involved in the water market. In 2004, the Ministry of Construction also issued the "Administrative Measures for Franchise Operations in Municipal Public Utilities," clearly defining the responsibilities of the competent authorities and enterprises granted franchise operating rights.

However, water marketization seemed to be equated with the government's withdrawal from public utilities. Some local governments simply "sold off" state-owned enterprises, inevitably leading to tragedies like the "Chifeng water pollution incident."

Gao Huiqing pointed out to the Economic Information Daily that the previous water market was government-led, with enterprise operating expenses directly allocated from the treasury. Now, it should be more market-oriented, introducing private capital into enterprises. However, water companies are different from ordinary enterprises and have a strong public nature, so the government's control over enterprises must be ensured. In other words, even if people have no money to pay water fees, water supply must be guaranteed, which relies solely on the government's control.

The second relationship in water reform is "market and regulation." Guo Peng described the "pain" caused by inadequate government regulation as "enterprises being greatly hurt," hoping that the government would firmly crack down on "bad kids" and firmly support "good kids."

Zhang Guoxiang, chairman of Shenyang Water Group Co., Ltd., vividly said it was like "digging channels without doing it well," meaning that rushing into market reform before establishing and improving regulatory mechanisms would inevitably lead to various adverse consequences.

An important aspect of the regulatory mechanism is to establish an exit mechanism for "bad kids." As Gao Huiqing told the Economic Information Daily, "Enterprises operating water services should make profits, but if there is profit transfer or profiteering through under-the-table operations, they must be firmly expelled."

Gao Huiqing believed that water reform must handle the relationship between "enterprise profit and public responsibility." Even when the economy is bad, water supply must be guaranteed even if the company is losing money. Price increases are the same; the big environment at the time must be considered. If the CPI is rising too fast, it is not appropriate to adjust prices, but to wait for the right opportunity.

From the enterprise side, Zhang Guoxiang felt deeply about "public responsibility," pointing out that "public enterprises must take social responsibility. Without social responsibility, there is no foothold, and will be eliminated by the people and the government."

Regardless of balancing which aspects, Xu Zongwei pointed out that the essence of water reform is to use market mechanisms to utilize social resources and forces, accelerate the development of social public undertakings, and solve public product demand issues. "Water benefits all things," the ultimate goal of water reform still lies in the word "public," and only then can it truly gain public applause.

Focus 3: The government needs to "manage ownership well and liberalize management rights"

In the face of chaos in domestic water reform, many people look to foreign countries, hoping to find successful experiences in the water markets of Europe and America.

"In developed market economies abroad, there is no such openness in the water sector as in China," Zhang Guoxiang believed that the reform experience of public utilities in these countries should be learned, mainly that the government should "manage ownership well and liberalize management rights."

Take France as an example, where the city water industry model retains the public nature of property rights, and introduces private companies to participate in the construction and operation of water facilities through entrusted management contracts. At the same time, France implements a water resources supervision system based on river basin management and public participation supervision, namely "national-river basin-regional-local."

France's current entrusted management is established on a complete set of contractual forms, which can be divided into four types according to the type of contract: leased operation, franchised operation, corporate operation, and agency operation. Leased contract operations account for 60% of the private water market, franchised operations account for 30%, and others account for 10%. Regardless of the type of entrusted operation method used, private companies only have usage rights.

The French Water Law explicitly stipulates that "water is part of the common assets of the state," and the government bears the responsibility for water resource management and supervises private water companies. This ensures both the efficiency and water quality of urban water supply networks, and effectively avoids companies monopolizing water as a scarce resource and arbitrarily raising prices.

France's water market also has flexibility, allowing rural and remote areas to implement location-specific water policies, provided that operating companies are subject to government and citizen supervision.

In contrast, the United States adopts a completely state-owned approach, absolutely prohibiting foreign capital from entering. Water prices in the U.S. vary depending on regional water resource conditions and project characteristics. For example, the eastern region has relatively abundant water resources and implements a regressive water pricing system, where large water users have lower water prices and small water users have higher water prices. The western region, with scarce water resources, commonly uses service cost pricing models and full market pricing models.

Additionally, water prices are usually determined individually per project, with each project setting its own water price. Agricultural water prices are interest-free repayment prices, while industrial and urban water prices are repayment plus interest prices.

Since most of the U.S. water supply companies and sewage treatment companies are primarily invested in and constructed by municipal governments, most are publicly owned and belong to municipal, county, and regional governments, resulting in lower water prices. Statistics show that compared to other developed countries, the proportion of water and sewage treatment fees to household disposable income is the lowest in the U.S., with an average annual water bill expenditure of $474 per household. The Congressional Budget Office estimates that water bill expenditures average 0.5% of household disposable income.

Moreover, the water policies of countries like the UK, Germany, and Japan also emphasize the special nature of water as a livelihood guarantee resource. In specific operations, they avoid imposing excessively high water fees on residents and provide certain subsidies for water prices based on different regions and situations.

Focus 4: Linkage: Beijing plans to raise residential water prices by 0.9 yuan per cubic meter

On December 1, the Beijing Municipal Development and Reform Commission announced key points of the hearing plan for the adjustment of residential water prices to the public, proposing a price increase of 0.9 yuan per cubic meter for residential water.

Relevant officials from the Beijing Development and Reform Commission stated that according to the unified deployment of national water price reform, localities need to strengthen the collection and management of sewage treatment fees and water resource fees, and gradually raise the collection standards based on the degree of water resource scarcity. Therefore, Beijing's residential water price adjustment plan does not increase the price of the water production phase but only adjusts the collection standards of water resource fees and sewage treatment fees. The overall plan is to increase residential water prices by 0.9 yuan per cubic meter, including a 0.48 yuan increase in water resource fees and a 0.42 yuan increase in sewage treatment fees.

It was introduced that the residential water price adjustment plan will be publicly heard at the hearing on December 16 to solicit opinions from all sectors of society. Specific price adjustments, timing, and implementation arrangements will fully consider opinions from all sectors of society and be implemented after approval following procedures to ensure the normal order of citizens' production and life.
