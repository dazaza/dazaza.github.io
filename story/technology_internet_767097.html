Twitter founder reveals the secrets of getting rich on the Internet

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 17:56:01

Evan Williams, the founder of Twitter

Introduction: An article titled "Twitter Founder Reveals Secret Formula for Getting Rich Online" was published on the website of the American magazine Wired on Monday. It comments that to launch top-notch products on the Internet and quickly make profits, one must satisfy people's basic needs through faster and simpler methods rather than merely pursuing innovation.

The following is the full text of the article:

Maintaining Traditional Thinking

Evan Williams (Evan Williams) has finally understood the essence of the Internet.

This was his statement to a group of technology industry leaders at the XOXO Conference held in Portland, USA. Although he said this with a hint of humor, during his 30-minute speech, he combined current industry theories, his personal thoughts over 20 years, and the experience gained from founding top internet companies like Blogger and Twitter to give a comprehensive explanation.

In his speech, Williams elaborated on the nature, operation mode, and methods of getting rich through the Internet. It should be acknowledged that Williams is not an outstanding speaker, but the message he conveyed was very clear: in today’s era where many internet entrepreneurs gather in Silicon Valley to create groundbreaking products, the real secret lies in pursuing existing ideas and striving to improve them. This speech can serve as a signpost, guiding the younger generation in Silicon Valley who are eager for direction.

Williams said that fundamentally, the Internet "is a huge machine that satisfies people's needs." It is not a utopia, not magic, but an engine providing various conveniences. As long as one can fully adapt to this engine and solve basic human problems more quickly and simply, immediate profit can be created. On the other hand, ignoring fundamental human needs and blindly pursuing extraordinary innovations will inevitably encounter resistance.

"We often think that the Internet allows us to do new things," Williams said, "but people actually just want to pursue what they have always been seeking."

Building More Connections

In 1994, Williams dropped out of a university in Nebraska and started selling videos that taught people how to get online. In these videos, he described this global computer network as "a puzzle made up of three things: computers, information, and people." But he has changed his views now.

After leaving Twitter in 2011 and founding the Medium blog network, Williams began reflecting on his original ideas. Computers have become widespread, greatly diversified in size and function, and even commonplace. Information has also greatly enriched and become an indispensable part of people's lives. After 20 years of development, the people people communicate with online are almost identical to their real-life relationships. Now, what truly matters is the connection between humans and machines.

"There are connections between hardware, and all these interactions involve data and software," Williams said. "A closer look at the giants of the Internet industry reveals that they all involve numerous connections. 'Follow' is a type of connection, and 'Like' is another type of connection."

"What the Internet does currently is connect everyone and everything, all times and all thoughts through various methods - layer upon layer of connections. Gradually, everything that happens in the world and everything we do, every place you've been or lived, every thought you've had or shared, every person you've liked or missed, will be connected. These connections will continue to grow exponentially."

Convenience is Key

These connections are not only increasing rapidly, but also growing in specific directions. The same principle applies whether explaining the current prosperity of the Internet or predicting future prosperity: convenience.

"The Internet makes it easier for people to achieve what they desire. In other words, it provides convenience," he said. "The principle of convenience on the Internet has two key elements: extremely fast speed and easy cognition." In other words, people don't want to wait, nor do they want to think - the Internet should meet this demand. "If you carefully study the important products on the Internet, you'll find that they all increase speed and simplify the thinking process."

Williams believes that Google, Facebook, Amazon, and Apple excel at providing such convenience. Their achievements are mostly due to simplifying originally complex processes, which was also the secret behind Williams' creation of Blogger. With this service, users no longer need to create new documents separately, save them, and upload them online; instead, they can directly view and edit within the web browser by simply entering content and clicking the 'Publish' button.

Williams said that the key to getting rich online is to eliminate unnecessary steps from routine activities, just like Blogger did.

"If you want to build a billion-dollar internet company, you should fulfill people's desires, preferably a desire that has existed for a long time... Once you identify the desire, then use modern technology to realize it," he said.

He gave a recent example: Uber. "How long has the desire to go from one place to another existed? How difficult was it to achieve? Uber took some measures to establish a connection between you and the driver."

Returning to Human Nature

Williams' concept may lack imagination, but that is exactly the point. The Internet has been around in the mass market for 20 years, so it's time to understand this: the Internet is not limitless magic; it is merely an engine to improve the quality of life.

"The Internet is very different from what we thought it would be 20 years ago," Williams said, "It is not a utopia; it is similar to other major technological revolutions in history." He even compared the Internet to agriculture, "Agriculture improved life; it not only fed people but also gave them more time to do other things - create art, invent things."

However, we often take convenience too far. "Look at the technologies that pushed agriculture to extremes - we industrialized agriculture, damaging the environment, affecting animal survival, and causing nutritional imbalances," he said, "Because calories were too easily accessible, it led to addiction, obesity, and diseases." In his view, our excessive obsession with retweets, likes, followers, and friends in internet data is akin to the nightmare in the agricultural field.

But Williams’ remarks were not meant to criticize Twitter; they were just observations about human nature. People are ultimately people, and the role of the Internet should be to meet their needs. Those who know how to utilize this point will eventually gain unparalleled power.
