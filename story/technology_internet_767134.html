Google, Apple, and Facebook: The Tripartite Battle for Voice Search

by anonymous on 2013-10-08 18:20:43

Facebook CEO Zuckerberg

　　As everyone knows, in the search domain, Google is unbeatable. In all internet search activities, Google's share usually hovers around 70%. Although Microsoft's Bing ranks second, its share is far behind Google's.

　　However, contributor to the tech news site BusinessInsider, Jim Edwards, believes that Google's dominance in the search field may be under threat. It is facing a series of challenges from an emerging new search pattern known as "implicit," "abstract," or "conversational" search.

　　So far, if you want to conduct a search on the web, you need to input keywords. Google has accustomed us to inputting keywords based on importance rather than their meaning.

　　However, this might change in the future. Google, Facebook, and Apple are all working hard to develop tools that allow users to search using everyday speech patterns, even for the vague, abstract, or implicit concepts we use in real life, such as "Which restaurant serves good food?" Traditional search methods often yield useless results for such questions.

　　Google's "primitive English"

　　Larry Kim, founder of online advertising analytics firm WordStream and a search expert, described this new type of search as follows: "People have become accustomed to inputting various fragmented, strange keywords when conducting desktop searches on Google. For example, to get the best search results, people usually need to input a long string of complex keywords. If someone wants to find directions to a certain beach in Hawaii, they would have to input 'directions, black, beach, best, Hawaii.' I call this 'Primitive English.' Reading these query terms reveals how nonsensical they sound, like something a caveman would say. I estimate that about 20% of Google searches are conducted using this kind of primitive English."

Google CEO Larry Page

　　Recently, Facebook launched Graph Search to U.S. users, and this service will soon be available globally to all Facebook users. If you've ever tried searching on Facebook, you'd know the subtleties of this search mode. In this method, ordinary keyword searches generally yield unsatisfactory results. However, Graph Search is different. Using it to find your friends' favorite movies yields impressive results.

　　The difference between Facebook's Graph Search and Google's keyword search lies in the fact that on Facebook, you don't know the answer to what you're searching for, which is "implicit" search. On Google, you're searching for known content, such as 'history of the White House.'

　　The Hummingbird Algorithm Era

　　Of course, Google won't sit idly by. Not long ago, Google introduced the new Hummingbird algorithm, which can handle voice search requests, such as 'How to tie a bowtie?' Google views Hummingbird as the largest adjustment to its core product since 2001.

　　For laypeople, talking about search algorithms is like chewing wax. Who cares if search engines change their algorithms to make searches easier to use?

　　Users Choose Facebook Over Google

　　According to a recent report by market research firm comScore, people spend more time on average on Facebook than on Google. Google's search empire is worth $60 billion, while Facebook's empire is worth $6 billion, but not in search.

　　If Facebook were to seriously develop a search engine to replace Google, teaching users to search using implicit questions instead of keywords, there could be a fundamental shift in how search market revenues are distributed.

　　Regarding this, Larry Kim stated, "We are now in the mobile search era (mobile search accounts for nearly half of all search activities), and an increasing number of mobile searches are done through voice. As modern people, we are unwilling to loudly articulate those primitive English words one by one. Therefore, improving the handling of natural language queries has become key to enhancing the relevance of search results."

　　Apple's True Intent with Siri

　　Next comes Apple. If you own an iPhone, you might wonder why Apple is so obsessed with Siri, a not-so-reliable voice assistant. One reason is that Apple understands the future of computing is mobile, and it's more convenient to tell your phone what you want to search for via voice than typing in keywords.

Apple CEO Cook

　　If Siri gets significantly improved, Siri's voice-controlled mobile search will become very useful, and iPhone users will shift en masse to Siri instead of Google Search.

　　Of course, this is a qualitative leap, and Apple isn't afraid to make "great leaps forward."

　　Siri's Default Search Engine is Bing

　　Currently, Siri's default search engine is Bing, and Microsoft has also reached an agreement with Facebook to set Bing as the default search engine for this social networking site. Which search engine Siri uses is up to Apple. In the latest iOS 7 mobile operating system released by Apple, Siri's default search engine changed from Google to Bing. Although Apple and Microsoft are competitors, neither of them particularly "likes" Google.

　　Google Glass vs. Siri

　　In the Android operating system, Google has its own voice assistant, which is better than Siri. Google hopes to use this to firmly anchor Android users within the Google search ecosystem, especially now that the Hummingbird algorithm has the capability to handle conversational searches. Additionally, Google is developing Google Glass, placing high hopes on this voice-controlled smart glasses device, hoping it becomes a daily wearable item. The key to all of this lies in voice control.

　　It seems that Apple, Google, and Facebook foresee that voice search will eventually replace keyword input, which is why the age of conversational search powerhouses is here.
