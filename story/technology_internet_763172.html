Will Ubuntu for phones become the fourth largest phone operating system?

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 16:30:38

In the early hours of today, Canonical, the developer of Ubuntu, released a smartphone codenamed Edge. It would be more accurate to say that this is the launch of the project rather than a product release, as the official start of the project requires the raising of at least $32 million within 30 days.

The first Ubuntu phone, the Edge, has a stylish square design, with dimensions of 64x9x124mm. It features a 4.5-inch sapphire crystal screen with a resolution of 1280x720 pixels, powered by a multi-core processor. The memory configuration looks impressive: 4GB RAM + 128GB storage space.

Additionally, the phone includes an 8-megapixel camera (with a 2-megapixel front-facing camera), running dual operating systems: Ubuntu Mobile OS and Android. It also provides functionalities such as an accelerometer, gyroscope, compass, barometer, and supports LTE 4G networks.

The price is approximately $600 (equivalent to 3680 RMB), with delivery expected in May 2014.

Currently, Ubuntu has received support from major global carriers. In its list of Ubuntu Carrier Advisory Board members, there are eight carriers all from non-American regions, including Deutsche Telekom, Everything Everywhere, Korea Telecom, Telecom Italia, LG UPlus, Portugal Telecom, SK Telecom, etc. With Verizon's potential inclusion, the probability of Ubuntu phones entering the US market has increased. Confirmed members joining the advisory board also include China Unicom and Smartfren.

This indicates that the company will collaborate with these members to advance the development of the Ubuntu operating system.

Advantages of the Ubuntu system: Gesture operation + PC desktop integration

As a highly anticipated new mobile system, the most significant feature of the Ubuntu system is gesture operation and the combination of mobile and PC functions. Its gesture operations can almost eliminate the need for any virtual buttons. All edges of the frame—left, right, top, and bottom—are equipped with gesture controls. Swiping on the left edge brings up the sidebar, which contains some commonly used applications. In the final version, this section should be customizable. This setting is essential for single-handed operation and suggests that future Ubuntu systems may develop towards larger screen phones around 5 inches. Swiping on the right edge brings back the last closed application, whether you were making a call, playing games, or browsing the internet, allowing quick access to the previously closed app. This greatly reduces memory pressure and only requires one responsive action to complete.

At the same time, high-end versions of the Ubuntu phone have the ability to convert between mobile and desktop systems. When the phone is connected to an external display via a dock, it automatically starts the desktop mode, allowing users to directly operate the desktop version of the Ubuntu system, just like using a regular Ubuntu computer.

Regarding the cultivation of the application ecosystem, BlackBerry 10 offers four methods for application development and conversion, and Ubuntu adopts a similar approach, trying to maximize the sources of applications. A large part of Ubuntu's ecosystem relies on web applications.

Canonical stated that Ubuntu will adopt a dual approach of web applications and native applications. This means that you can use all applications developed based on HTML5 technology, such as Facebook, Google Maps, Spotify, and Twitter. The Ubuntu mobile operating system will also provide developers with a complete software development kit (SDK).

Canonical promises to provide deep API interfaces for web applications for the Ubuntu mobile operating system, allowing them to run independently in the system like native applications and use various system services. They also indicated that web applications built through methods like PhoneGap should be able to run directly in Ubuntu. Therefore, the Ubuntu mobile operating system may not lack application support.

For Android, which shares the same origin (both are based on Linux), Ubuntu is indeed a formidable rival.

However, Ubuntu has always been a relatively loose and underfunded team; otherwise, they wouldn't need to raise at least $32 million within 30 days as the starting capital for the project.

A netizen commented on Ubuntu: "This company is no less innovative and technically proficient than Google and Apple; it just lacks some business acumen."

Even if it does not split the smartphone system landscape into four equal parts, it will still be a much-admired item among niche users.

Before this year's MWC exhibition, Canonical, the developer of the Ubuntu mobile operating system, announced the touch developer preview version and released ROM packages for multiple phones, including Google Nexus. This once again showed us another competitor in mobile operating systems. Can this lesser-known new system carve out a share of the market? Will it become the fourth largest mobile operating system?

Angel investor Zhu Yesheng once wrote an article stating that in the current market landscape, having an acceptable operating system alone is clearly insufficient to stand out and capture a significantly influential market share. It also needs the support of an ecosystem, the most important of which is the support of handset manufacturers.

Although Android currently enjoys the most manufacturer support and the largest market share, these manufacturers inevitably have doubts and hope for more choices.

In fact, what these big phone manufacturers want most is the path taken by Apple—a superior, closed, and unique operating system like iOS. However, iOS has reached its current state due to years of accumulation, sedimentation, and massive R&D investment, which Samsung and Huawei find difficult to achieve in the short term.

As a second choice, these big phone manufacturers want an open-source system as excellent as Android but do not wish for it to be controlled by a dominant player like Google. They need an alternative to counterbalance Google.

Therefore, considering the intentions of the phone manufacturers, Ubuntu has a certain chance to become a backup operating system outside of Android. However, apart from facing competition from other systems like Tizen and Sailfish, it must overcome three hurdles:

1. Consumer usage habits

Consumers have formed their own usage habits over the years of smartphone development. Whether Ubuntu's full gesture operation will win favor with consumers remains uncertain.

2. Path dependency of phone manufacturers and app developers

Phone manufacturers and app developers have already adapted to the Android ecosystem. To build a new ecosystem, Ubuntu must break free from existing path dependencies, which could take a considerable amount of time. Android will respond to the competition accordingly.

3. Substantial investment

Developing an operating system is not easy. Previously, Intel and Nokia jointly developed the MeeGo platform, but it ultimately failed. It inherently requires substantial financial, human resource investments, and time accumulation. Ubuntu's current fund size and personnel scale are relatively small, posing a significant question about whether it can sustain its development.

It appears challenging for Ubuntu to shine brilliantly and compete on par with Android. Perhaps it will repeat its fate in the PC field, becoming a mobile operating system with distinct features but only favored by niche users.
