"Decision Engine": Bing's Differentiated Path to Challenge Google

by diglog on 2011-11-20 12:30:11

Microsoft (Asia) Internet Engineering Institute was established yesterday in Beijing. Its rank is the same as Microsoft Asia Engineering Institute, both belonging to Microsoft Asia-Pacific R&D Group. The first director is Dr. Yongdong Wang. The business scope mainly includes Bing search, online advertising technology, MSN platform technology and mobile Internet services. Currently, the Internet Engineering Institute has more than 400 researchers, 80% of whom are master's degree candidates, with a female proportion of 20%, and an average age under 35 years old. It also manages a team of about 20 people in Tokyo. On November 18th, the day the Internet Engineering Institute was founded, the Microsoft team communicated with the media regarding Bing search technology.

Bing holds over 30% of the market share in the United States, a figure that includes Yahoo search engines driven by Bing technology. The expansion of the U.S. market share was hard-earned; according to CNNMoney reports, since its launch in May 2009, Microsoft has incurred a loss of $5.5 billion in Bing search operations. In comparison to the U.S. market, Bing (Bing) in the Chinese market remains relatively quiet. Regarding this situation, Director Yongdong Wang frankly admitted that "Bing is in a catch-up situation in the Chinese market," focusing primarily on the U.S. market earlier, hoping to create a breakthrough through it. As for the Chinese market, "there will be surprises for outsiders in the next one or two years."

Bing’s “surprise” can be glimpsed from some experimental projects currently underway in the U.S., striving to avoid direct competition with Google. President of Microsoft's Internet Business Division, Qi Lu, said at the September financial analysts meeting that Bing aims to restructure the network, "fundamentally changing the rules of the game."

"Decision Engine" Bing's differentiated competitive strategy is to position itself as a "decision engine." A "decision engine" directly presents users with the information they need in search results rather than simply providing a link. Similar technology has been partially realized in Baidu searches, such as searching "National Day holiday," which directly marks the National Day vacation time in calendar form. This is a very noticeable diversion of web traffic —— after users obtain useful information, they no longer open linked pages (even if the example mentioned involves their own "Baidu Zhidao" page).

Bing goes further, completing "decisions" for users right in the search box. See the figure below: when searching for Seattle Weather, the local weather conditions are directly displayed below the search box.

Similarly, searching for a movie integrates deep movie information, including cast and crew, rating information; searching for a location integrates local weather and scenic spot information; searching for an airline integrates check-in and ticket purchasing functions; searching for software leads directly to the download page... For users, this is a great boon — efficiency is greatly improved; but for "linked pages," it is nothing short of a disaster. Users can meet their needs just in the search box or "quick channel," making the previous method of finding results by clicking through multiple layers of pages seem bloated and inefficient. From now on, these junk level pages' ads won't reach users, so who will pay? Of course, in the long term, this will inevitably drive web pages to provide quality content in order to appear in the "quick channel" and reach users first. But in the short term, due to severe diversion, could it lead to web pages blocking Bing's search engine crawler?

In response to this question, Jing Kun, senior development manager of Bing, explained that Bing focuses on providing "informational answers," only offering the most direct answer in the search box or preview page (quick channel) when it clearly understands user intent, helping users make decisions. "Bing treats user needs as a single question, if it can clearly provide an answer, it quickly presents this answer — like asking about the weather somewhere, regardless of where the answer comes from. If it's just a keyword fuzzy search, users still need to click links in the search results to find information."

Currently, many functions of the "decision engine" are still in the experimental stage, with only part of them launched in the U.S., such as the previously mentioned weather and place name query functions, while some more avant-garde functions showing more information below the search box are still in the experimental stage. The "decision engine" has not yet covered the entire web.
