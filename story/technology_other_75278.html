**Synopsis:**

Does a "superman" with godlike powers also have high emotional intelligence? Will Smith and Charlize Theron star in this comedic spin-off of the superhero genre...

A rule-breaking superhero (played by Will Smith) causes so much trouble that he becomes a thorn in the side of the citizens. To regain public favor, the superhero decides to hire a new PR consultant to manage crises and improve his public image. However, the PR consultant's wife, Mary (Charlize Theron), turns out to be a female superhero hiding her identity! As sparks fly between them, the superhero realizes his superpowers are gradually weakening. How will he team up with Mary to fight a group of villains who are about to be released from prison and seek revenge against him?

**Film Review:**

At first, I thought the title was lackluster. "Hancock," or "Universal Superman"—does it mean everyone is a superhero? Instantly, my mind filled with muscled men and action-packed women, leaving no room for imagination. Later, when I saw Will Smith, I remembered *Enemy of the State* and *Hitch*, realizing that "Universal Superman" actually means "the superhero of all people." My initial impression was wrong. But why did the translator create this confusion? Was it just to continue Will Smith's "universal" brand? It wasn't necessary; after all, *I Am Legend* wasn't translated as "Universal Zombie!" After watching the movie, I somewhat understood the meaning behind "Universal Superman." From the film's expressed ideas, this movie downplays the conflict between justice and evil while amplifying the contradictions between ordinary people and superheroes. Compared to previous films of similar genres, there are two standout points:

First, the film measures the public's tolerance for superheroes. Some friends might doubt: Don't we all like superheroes? Why would we need to tolerate them? First, note that we like superheroes only during critical moments when we need their help. Most of the time, we face issues that can be resolved by ourselves or the people around us, and we don't need them. This is normal selfishness. Second, people are wary of a superhero's abilities. Everyone shouts for freedom, and the foundation of freedom is equality. The biggest problem with the appearance of a superhero is that the vast difference in abilities between them and us disrupts the equality among humans. If the superhero turns evil, who will protect our rights and freedoms? Lastly, we have a sour grapes mentality that needs soothing. Imagine if heaven gave us a chance to choose a partner—would we pick a superhero? Of course not! Their abilities far surpass ours, to the point where all our hard work is overshadowed by their brilliance and ignored by others. We don't want to be overlooked, so we hate superheroes. Jealousy works this way: people can tolerate a beggar living at home but cannot tolerate a billionaire living nearby. Sand in the eye is uncomfortable, but how does it feel to have a diamond forced in? Our tolerance for superheroes is very limited. Therefore, the best choice for a superhero is to remain mysterious, appearing only when needed, doing good deeds, and then leaving quickly. Hancock clearly didn't understand this. His arrogant and self-important behavior led to deep hatred and waves of criticism from the public, eventually plunging him into a situation of betrayal and isolation.
