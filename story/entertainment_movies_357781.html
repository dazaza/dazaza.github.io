Movie Review: The Last Shrek - A Lifetime in Cinema

by 303729213 on 2010-06-08 23:59:56

In "Shrek Forever After," when our lovable protagonist sees a wanted poster with his own likeness nailed to a tree, he quips, "Another one wants to come and catch me. Feels great." It's true that someone wants to capture Shrek, but the real question is: does anyone need him? And even if they do, it probably isn't because they like this movie. This film is the fourth in the series, likely also the last. The movie uses 3D effects, which is unsurprising given the trend. After going through the motions of the third installment, this one has its ups and downs, but overall feels quite dragging. It tells a story about testing true love (not just for Shrek, but for all of us).

The movie begins with Shrek having a happy family, but unfortunately, this life doesn't suit him at all. He has his beloved wife Fiona and three adorable little ones (of course, he still has Mike Myers' charming voice), but he can't stand changing diapers all day, thus falling into the predicament of Buffalo Bill - a hero without a battlefield. To reclaim the monster within himself, Shrek signs a contract with the imp, to be a monster for one day again. If this contract illustrates anything, it’s that you should always read the fine print carefully. Before you can marvel at how “wonderful” life is, Shrek finds himself in an entirely different world where he and Fiona are strangers, and old friends like Eddie Murphy's donkey and Antonio Banderas' Puss in Boots don’t recognize him or Fiona. Fiona is still voiced by Cameron Diaz, her voice as sweet as ever. This time, she leaves her castle to lead a guerrilla group against the tyranny of the imp.

Directed by Mike Mitchell, there are some delightful scenes: the set looks like a toy kingdom with very cute colors; Banderas’ voice has an incredible charm, every line he delivers gets laughter from the audience; the tune played by the Pied Piper brings to mind the song "Man Piaba" from "Beetlejuice"; additionally, the witches' ball and the scene where the witches chase Shrek in the palace are spectacular. (Only after watching such overwhelming scenes will you realize how surprising computer animation can be.)

However, when choosing a script, you should also look closely. This episode was written by Josh Klausner and Darren Lemke, feeling like a recycling machine that revisits the highlights of the first three films without showing any intention to innovate. The movie does have some suspense: whether Shrek can escape the imp's contract through a loophole, which states that a true love's kiss has the power to break the contract. However, the audience still feels that the filmmakers have pieced together content indiscriminately just to fill up time. 93 minutes isn't long, yet some parts feel very, very slow.
