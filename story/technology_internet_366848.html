Xue Manzi: Why I Invested in Cai Wensheng and Li Xiang

by swsw007 on 2010-10-03 14:12:39

Xue Manzi: "The core of entrepreneurship is to meet the needs of customers. Entrepreneurship is not difficult, what is difficult is whether you can think clearly about what you want to do before starting a business. Success certainly requires intelligence, a team, good insight, and perseverance." Editor's note: Xue Manzi, one of the founders of UTStarcom, recently gave an interview to a reporter from the magazine "Chuangye Bang" and answered questions on the core of entrepreneurship, why he invested in Cai Wensheng and Li Xiang, business models, and other issues. When talking about the business model of small and medium-sized enterprises, Xue Manzi said, "The vast majority of Chinese entrepreneurs like to copy foreign business models." He believed that for SMEs to succeed, they must have three conditions: first, there must be a certain technical threshold; second, there must be a huge user base; third, there must be excellent execution.

The following is the full text of Xue Manzi's views:

The core of entrepreneurship is to solve customer needs

Nowadays, people's understanding of entrepreneurship is too broad. For example, graduating from high school and coming to the city, pulling a flatbed cart or opening a fruit stall, I don't think this is entrepreneurship. My understanding of entrepreneurship is that you used to work for others (such as being a professional manager), then you have your own commercial idea, so you start doing it yourself as a boss, which is called entrepreneurship.

As long as you have a little bit of skill, you can be slightly different from the other 1.3 billion people in China. If you have a trick that is better than 1.3 billion people, you will immediately become the richest man in China; as long as you think a little more than others, you can also become the most successful person. So I would like to spend a lot of time, at least carefully consider for three months, which industry you should do, and what kind of bottleneck it solves, etc. In fact, all the things in the world are one thing: meeting customer needs. Mao Zedong solved the problem of land distribution for Chinese farmers; Deng Xiaoping solved the question of how to make China's 1 billion population rich and enter modern society.

Think clearly about what you want to do

I was Cai Wensheng's angel investor back then. When he was doing 265.com, many "sea turtles" who returned from the United States did not understand, saying what’s the use of this, why do you need this to surf the Internet? In fact, at that time, a large number of Internet users in China had the "three low" characteristics: low education, low age, and low income. These people could not type "Yahoo" on the keyboard, nor could they type "Google". So when I went to Xiamen to see Cai Wensheng, I thought this young man was extraordinary. His office was not even as big as a podium, and there were more than 30 people sitting in it. I asked him if he wanted to be a big fish in a small pond in Xiamen or go back to Beijing with me and maybe become a big fish in a medium-sized pond. A month later, 265.com raised $2 million, six months later Google invested $5 million, and finally he sold the company to Google.

Another example is Li Xiang, the founder of PaoPao.com. When he founded Autohome, I asked him, Sina, Sohu, and other large portal sites also have similar vertical channels, how can you compete with them? The two reasons he gave ultimately prompted me to invest $1 million in him: First, he said his biggest advantage was that he himself was an outsider to the PC industry and had a very deep understanding of the needs of hundreds of millions of grassroots Internet users in China. He didn’t go to university, but he was passionate about cars, so he started a car website as an outsider, while 99% of those who look at car websites are outsiders; Second, Sina, NetEase, Sohu all rest on weekends, but Autohome can continue to work because every person looking at cars only has time on weekends, and they will definitely check the latest prices and information every day. Today, Autohome is the largest independent car website in the world.

The significance of these two examples is that both entrepreneurs are very clear about what they are doing, thinking very clearly about what they want to do, and thinking differently from others. This is the opportunity.

Capital relates to whether a company can grow bigger

I believe that the success of Alibaba, Baidu, Tencent, and others has a significant reason: they all went through the winter of the Internet. After the U.S. bubble burst in 2001, there was no money in this industry, and their competitors froze to death in the harsh winter, so they had such a big opportunity. Now some companies may not grow bigger due to having too much money. There are dozens of companies in the video website sector, not to mention the recently popular group-buying websites. Every company has a VC, and everyone can get $5-10 million to compete, but there are not many good talents and teams, so now the leader invests, the second place also invests, by the fifth place someone still invests, and in the end, no one grows big.

Success requires innovation

The issue of knock-offs is very interesting. In the West, there is no outright copying or imitation, no one does that, while the vast majority of Chinese entrepreneurs like to copy when they start, I think this might be due to cultural reasons. The Japanese also like to copy, also admiring Western culture. In China, people who take shortcuts and save effort are much more than Western entrepreneurs.

There must be opportunities. I believe that first, there should be a certain technical threshold; second, there should be a huge user base; third, the company's execution is good, able to quickly expand using viral marketing methods, while having a certain amount of financial support. But I think the Chinese are too eager, always wanting to create a giant in three to five years, which is impossible. I think there will be obvious giants in mobile Internet, I don't know when, but this industry will definitely have them, especially suitable for China. We have a simplest example: China Mobile accidentally became the world's largest newspaper company, with 80 million newspaper users, and the total number of users of the world's largest newspapers is less than its.

If our 8848 had gone public back then, there would be no difference between us and Sina, NetEase, and Sohu now. Because if we had a few hundred million dollars in hand, we could have survived the winter of the Internet, and then we would have encountered the very popular SMS service back then. Both Tan Zhi and I came out of UTStarcom, and we were in the telecommunications industry. If we had done SMS, our ability would have been much stronger than others, but how could we have thought of such a thing. My point is that success is accidental, and failure is inevitable. This principle is very simple, meaning that every successful person has his own reasons, because success certainly requires intelligence, a team, good insight, and persistence. But there are many people with these comprehensive qualities who haven't succeeded, otherwise success wouldn't be rare.

The standard of the world is definitely that the more money you make, the greater your success. But for me, a 57-year-old angel investor, it's enough to hang out with more young guys and watch them meet some people's needs. It's like the process of having children, quickly finding something to do and making it big quickly, watching it grow from A to Z, which gives a great sense of psychological satisfaction. Of course, wanting to make more money is definitely true.

Masayoshi Son is our investor

We met in May 1980. At that time, he was an undergraduate in the Economics Department of the University of California, Berkeley, and he founded a company in his spare time. He first went to the head of the computer science department and told him that he had a good idea, which was that you say a sentence, such as where the public restroom is, and this machine can translate it into dozens of different languages. He asked the department head if this problem could be solved technically, and the other party said it could be solved. Masayoshi Son said okay, if you can solve it, I'll give you $300,000 to buy this patent. Then he took this idea back to Japan and sold it to Sharp for $1 million, and finally gave the professor $300,000. I worked for him during that summer and earned $7,000, the most money I had ever seen in my life. Later, when we founded UTStarcom, he became our investor. I think he is a very smart person who understands his position and customer needs very well.
