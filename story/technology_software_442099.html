Recently, I saw a report.

by was25135 on 2011-06-06 14:19:48

Guangzhou banned electric bicycles from entering the city some time ago. Recently, I saw a report that merchants have introduced so-called "invisible electric bicycles" which look exactly like regular bicycles. Upon reading this news, I couldn't help but rejoice: The Guangzhou government has once again done a great thing to promote national technological progress. Imagine, if it weren't for this ban, how could such a high-tech product be created? The relevant departments of the Guangzhou government truly have a profound understanding of the spirit that "science and technology is the primary productive force."

I don't know why Guangzhou banned electric bicycles. To be honest, I really don't think there's anything wrong with them. Without mentioning the far-sighted statement about conserving energy, simply making travel more convenient for ordinary people or even facilitating tourism isn't a bad thing at all. We all know that housing prices in Guangzhou are getting higher and higher, but those without money still need a place to live. Unable to afford houses in the city, they can only buy houses further away. Without an appropriate means of transportation, it's not practical to expect everyone to run to work.

Another reason for banning these might be that many of them are substandard. If we were to eliminate entire markets just because there are substandard products, then in an era where even toxic rice keeps appearing, not only should everyone run to work, but everyone should also learn from Sichuan’s old TCM practitioners and practice fasting.

Unfortunately, among the citizens of Guangzhou who need electric bicycles, there aren't many long-distance runners or TCM practitioners. Therefore, merchants quickly developed this product, almost as fast as the invention of radar during World War II to counter air raids. This is just another proof of a stable economic principle: Where there is demand, there will always be supply. This is practically common knowledge in modern commodity society.

But there are always people who hope to break this common sense. Their methods range from inventing perpetual motion machines or trying to lift themselves off the ground by pulling their own hair, to attempting administrative orders for experiments. However, to my knowledge, none of these efforts have ever been successful. Not even beneficial things for the common people like electric bicycles can be banned, let alone alcohol, which most people acknowledge has no significant benefits. Still, these administrative orders are issued, leaving everyone asking why.

In my view, this is the result of the government being lazy. Generally speaking, a government that doesn't slack off would conduct thorough investigations and research on items that may not benefit the whole but are indeed necessary for some groups, striving to balance the interests of both sides. But a lazy government will simply cut off one side's interests that seem easier to handle.

In such a situation, anyone who has studied economics knows that there will definitely be goods that come to fill this market gap. In times when technology wasn't so advanced, creating such products was relatively troublesome, but now, all it takes is sticking a piece of leather together with universal glue, and you're ready to hit the streets.

Moreover, the lazy government originally thought that cutting off the entire market would solve the problem. However, governments that use this approach always find out that their administrative costs are certainly higher than if they had seriously managed the market. But all governments planning to slack off are too lazy to consider this. So, they will always use this method to drive rapid technological advancement.
