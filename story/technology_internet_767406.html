The启示 of Nokia's Decline

by anonymous on 2013-10-15 21:00:35

Last month, Nokia, the former global leader in the mobile phone industry and once the most valuable listed company in the world, sold its core mobile phone business for a mere $7.2 billion—a price far less than a fraction of its peak market capitalization worth hundreds of billions.

From selling its headquarters building at the end of last year to today's fire sale of its core business at a "cabbage price," it seems that this former giant struggling in adversity is truly nearing its end. A company that shone brilliantly for over a decade has fallen into decline within just two or three years. The downfall of Nokia gives us three insights.

Firstly, brand isn't everything; relying solely on the power of a brand cannot ensure consumer loyalty forever.

Nokia’s brand was undeniably prominent. Even during its decline, its brand value still ranked among the top globally. In 2011, although it fell out of the top ten, it remained in fourteenth place, with a brand value even higher than BMW and Louis Vuitton. However, this golden brand name did not stop Nokia’s rapid loss of market share.

I was once a loyal supporter of the Nokia brand, using Nokia phones for over a decade. After Apple and Samsung rose to prominence, I tried to resist accepting these upstarts due to my preference for Nokia. But eventually, I had to surrender because there were no competitive products left in Nokia’s lineup. After using Apple and Samsung products, I deeply realized how outdated Nokia had become.

This shows that while a brand is undoubtedly important for a company, without competitive products as support, the power of a brand becomes powerless and cannot ensure unwavering consumer loyalty.

Speaking of products, many people have a misconception that quality decides everything. In reality, in an era of increasingly diverse demands, product quality is by no means the sole standard consumers use when choosing products. This is Nokia’s second lesson for us.

To be fair, even today, Nokia phones are still renowned for their stability and durability, with repair rates lower than those of competitors. However, stable quality and long-lasting durability did not help Nokia retain its market share. This indicates that for fashion-oriented electronics like mobile phones, which lie between fast-moving consumer goods and durable goods, relying solely on high-quality products is insufficient to maintain dominance.

The reason is simple: stable and reliable quality is only one factor consumers consider when choosing a product, not the entire picture. For mobile phones, whose replacement cycle is only 18 months, factors such as appearance, functionality, and price often take precedence over durability and repair rates, especially among young users who change phones most frequently. Thus, relying on the selling point of stable quality, Nokia failed to attract the most dynamic young users in the market.

For companies, choosing the right development strategy is crucial. Whether to boldly innovate or simply repeat past successes essentially sets the stage for future outcomes. Nokia’s third lesson for us is: innovation may carry risks, but clinging to the old and repeating oneself leads only to a dead end.

Speaking of repetition, Nokia’s sale of its core business is actually a repetition of its past successful strategy. With the courage of “cutting off one's wrist,” it completely shed its traditional business, focusing instead on developing new businesses like wireless network equipment and maps, hoping for a resurgence.

This bold and dramatic move indeed succeeded twice in Nokia's century-long history. For instance, a hundred years ago, Nokia, which started with wood and rubber, decisively sold its traditional businesses and moved into electronics to produce televisions. In the 1980s and 90s, Nokia again sold its already second-largest television business in Europe, venturing into the then-new mobile communications field and quickly becoming the largest player globally. However, what worked twice might not work a third time. I believe that this repeated cross-sector strategy carries much greater risk than the previous two attempts, and whether it can lead to Nokia's resurgence or accelerate its demise remains uncertain.

Firstly, the previous transformations were proactive, whereas this time it is a reactive choice. Proactivity versus reactiveness is a very critical factor in whether a corporate transformation succeeds or fails.

Secondly, in the past two cross-sector transformations, Nokia proactively entered sunrise industries that were revolutionary and had massive markets. Today, however, Nokia does not seem to have found such a successor industry.

More importantly, Nokia's size during the previous two transitions cannot compare to its current scale. In other words, back then, Nokia was still a regional company, enjoying the advantage of agility. Now, having become the industry leader and a giant corporation, Nokia is abandoning its core business entirely and starting anew, a risky endeavor akin to a bear discarding corn cobs.

Apple CEO Tim Cook, speaking about Nokia, said that failure to innovate inevitably leads to extinction. Nokia is a case of withering due to lack of innovation, despite its significant position in global market share. This could very well be the warning bell Nokia sounds for all enterprises.
