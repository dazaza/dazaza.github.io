Rhinoplasty has been one of the most important plastic surgeries on the face for many years. However, because it is a relatively significant plastic surgery, even a small flaw can attract attention. The failure rate of rhinoplasty is low, but even a single case is a major issue. What are the results of repairing failed rhinoplasty? Is there any danger in repairing failed rhinoplasty? Many questions are of concern to patients.

Reasons for rhinoplasty failure:

1. The choice of hospital, the level of the doctor, rhinoplasty materials, personal aesthetic concepts, or rejection reactions have caused unnatural appearance of the nose, such as being fake, too high, and easily movable, leaving a heavy burden after a failed rhinoplasty.

2. Most cases of rhinoplasty failure or postoperative dissatisfaction result from poor communication between the patient and the doctor. As an external facial organ, whether the nose looks beautiful cannot be hidden. If the patient dislikes the result or the surgery fails, they will inevitably face the question of whether to undergo a second surgery.

3. Preoperative or intraoperative evaluation errors - failing to assess the original defects of the nose before surgery is a major cause of surgical failure. For example: deviated nasal septum, asymmetry of alar cartilage, etc., may lead to postoperative tilting or asymmetry. During surgery, if appropriate measures are not taken for defects that were not assessed preoperatively, such as previously injured nasal bones or cartilage deficiencies, postoperative problems may arise.

4. The "unnatural" feeling of L-shaped artificial nasal bone rhinoplasty is largely due to capsule contracture. Capsule contracture is not exclusive to implants; artificial nasal bones can also produce capsules. Severe capsule contracture can lead to upturned noses.

Appropriate candidates for repair after rhinoplasty failure:

1. Those with deformed nostrils, excessively recessed alar grooves, and blunt nasal tips.

2. Those with overly long silicone rubber nasal implants and insufficient stripping of the nasal root area.

3. Those who experience hardening of the skin on the back of the nose or increased reflection after surgery; changes in skin color, whitening or grayish darkness, with signs of impending skin necrosis; or those with small concave pits on the back of the nose.

4. Those whose post-surgery nose shape appears unnatural, lacking normal nasofrontal angle, nasofacial angle, and nasolabial angle, without ideal aesthetic curves and natural harmony with the face.

5. Those who feel the implanted silicon rubber nasal implant floating or moving under the skin when touching the nasal root; tilted implants, with step-like deformities at the nasal root.
