If your Weibo is infringed, how should you protect your rights on Weibo?

by edivista on 2011-04-08 11:10:47

(Weibo News Network) ► News Background

(News Review: The live Weibo broadcast of Big S and Wang Xiaofei's wedding by Zhang Chaoyang of Sohu; Zhang refuses to apologize, denies breaking the law or violating ethics)

On March 22nd, Big S and Wang Xiaofei held their grand wedding in Sanya. The ceremony was kept low-key and mysterious, with media prohibited from entering the site. Unexpectedly, guest Zhang Chaoyang live-broadcasted the wedding via Weibo. Recently, Wang Xiaofei and Big S sent a legal letter demanding that Zhang Chaoyang apologize for infringing on portrait rights, privacy rights, and reputation rights. However, Zhang Chaoyang published a statement on his Weibo, claiming he had no fault: neither admitting mistakes nor apologizing.

Weibo creates transparent people

With the popularization of intelligent communication tools such as 3G mobile phones and the explosive expansion of Weibo users, casually posting Weibos anytime, anywhere has become a typical feature of the era. In the continuous comments and reposts by users, the speed of Weibo information transmission basically reaches real-time, especially for celebrities' Weibos, which, due to the bloggers' huge fan base, make Weibo information spread like lightning. The arrival of the micro-era has shortened the connection between the so-called elite class and the grassroots class. Celebrities who were previously only accessible through media reports can now be followed anytime, anywhere by simply joining their large fan groups, making it seem as if celebrities are right beside us.

However, in the micro-era, an open photo, a mood-sharing post, or an unintentional action may reveal important personal information. Especially teenagers who have grown up in the network world like to share photos and audio-visual content online. The fun and convenience of Weibo often make people forget the dangers. Some people talk about private matters or publicly criticize classmates and friends on Weibo without any reservations, not realizing that the rapid transmission and preservation characteristics of the network allow these discussions to be easily found, possibly affecting job hunting, promotion, or insurance claims several years later.

A Canadian woman named Natalie Blanchard took long-term leave from her company and received insurance compensation due to severe depression. Later, the insurance company found photos of Blanchard happily skiing with friends on Facebook, determining that Blanchard did not have depression and stopped paying compensation.

Whether there is infringement depends on subjective fault

Returning to the dispute between Big S, Wang Xiaofei, and Zhang Chaoyang, did Zhang Chaoyang infringe? When the law measures whether an act constitutes an infringement responsibility, the most important consideration standard is whether the actor has subjective fault. Specifically regarding Weibo infringement, it is whether the person posting the Weibo has fulfilled reasonable care obligations. If the information posted on Weibo is basically true or does not have malicious intent, it can be presumed that the actor has fulfilled their duty of care, and thus has no fault and does not need to bear responsibility. However, if the posted information seriously deviates from the facts and lacks good intentions, and objectively infringes on others' legitimate rights and interests, then corresponding infringement responsibilities must be borne.

A single Weibo post can be at most 140 characters, and some believe that freedom of speech on Weibo is greater than other media, but this understanding is incorrect. Essentially, citizens' freedom of speech on Weibo is not more free than other forms of expression; it is just that the law requires less caution.

Courts, in determining whether an act constitutes an infringement, focus on whether the actor subjectively had fault as the core standard for determining the nature of the behavior. Due to the immediacy and randomness of Weibo posts, bloggers cannot be expected to bear a high level of caution for the information they post. Therefore, from this perspective, the law imposes lower requirements for caution on Weibo publishers, generally holding them responsible only when intentional infringement occurs, as negligence-based infringement can be legally exempted.

Famous blogger should be more cautious

Recently, those involved in Weibo infringement lawsuits have all been celebrities. Celebrities, in legal terms, are public figures who attract widespread attention. Academically, public figures can be roughly divided into political, entertainment, and voluntary types. Generally, when the privacy rights and reputation rights of celebrities are subject to public commentary and attention, parties cannot use civil legal rules to stop it and can mostly only endure silently.

Legally, Big S cannot claim infringement responsibility against Zhang Chaoyang for the violation of privacy rights because Big S is a public figure, and therefore her protection of privacy rights must be subject to certain restrictions.

Another characteristic of celebrities is their enormous social influence. Judging from the number of Weibo fans, the scale of celebrity fans far exceeds that of ordinary people, making the social influence of celebrities' Weibos much higher than that of ordinary people. For this reason, laws must impose stricter caution obligations on celebrities' Weibos compared to ordinary people. Generally, the establishment of Weibo infringement by celebrities centers on fault, which includes both intention and negligence.

The court's reasoning for the first Weibo case also affirmed this point. As a public figure, Zhou Hongyi has more fans and more discourse power. When he publicly criticizes competitors, he should think twice and restrain himself, as the restriction on his Weibo freedom of speech should be higher than that of ordinary netizens. Celebrities can use their great social influence of speech to gain substantial economic rewards, such as advertising, while also bearing greater social responsibilities due to their wide social influence. Only by doing so can the law balance the delicate relationship between the two.

Website has notification deletion responsibility

Once Weibo infringement is determined, what kind of responsibility should the website bear?

Article 36, Paragraph 2 of the Tort Liability Law makes abstract provisions on the circumstances under which websites bear responsibility: If network users use network services to commit tortious acts, the infringed party has the right to notify the network service provider to take necessary measures such as deletion, shielding, and disconnection of links. If the network service provider fails to take necessary measures after receiving the notice, it will bear joint liability for the expanded part of the damage together with the network user.

In short, this provision is the notification-deletion responsibility. Normally, websites cannot pre-review every Weibo message. If netizens find that Weibo content involves infringement, they can choose to notify the website; after receiving the notification from the infringed party, the website is responsible for taking measures. If it fails to take timely measures, it will bear corresponding responsibility. Of course, netizens can also choose not to notify the website and directly negotiate with the Weibo poster, but this method is time-consuming and labor-intensive, especially when unable to contact the blogger in a timely manner, making it impossible to effectively prevent the expansion of the infringement results. Meanwhile, if the infringed party did not notify the website of the infringement beforehand, then in future legal disputes, it will not be able to claim tort liability from the website.

In the first Weibo case, the plaintiff did not notify the website of the Weibo infringement beforehand, so the website does not bear tort liability according to the law and thus does not bear responsibility.

Future Weibo infringement cases will continue to occur. Weibo is not a lawless territory. If netizens find themselves being infringed upon, they should immediately contact the website and request it to take necessary measures to prevent the expansion of the infringement results, rather than retaliating on their own Weibo. Using violence to fight violence violates the spirit of the law, not only failing to receive legal recognition but also facing legal punishment.

► Weibo Disputes

First Weibo Case

(News Review: Zhou Hongyi dissatisfied with the first-instance judgment of the first Weibo case and expressed intention to appeal)

In May 2010, Zhou Hongyi, chairman of Qihoo 360, published a series of articles exposing the true face of Kingsoft Company via Weibo on multiple websites. That month, Kingsoft Company filed a lawsuit, demanding that Zhou Hongyi stop the infringement, issue a public apology, and compensate for 12 million yuan. On March 26th this year, the Haidian Court made a first-instance ruling that Zhou Hongyi's use of Weibo to infringe on others' reputation constituted infringement, and he should bear tort liability including a public apology and compensation of over 80,000 yuan. This case was called the "first Weibo case" online.

Fake Weibo

Recently, the official Weibo secretary of Sina Weibo issued a statement saying that they had received numerous complaints from Weibo users reflecting that their Weibos were maliciously copied by other websites. Sina Weibo declared firm opposition to such malicious copying and infringement behaviors and would protect user data from malicious infringement to the greatest extent possible.

Writer Luo Zhiyuan issued a statement on Sina Weibo: I discovered that Sohu Weibo copied my Sina Weibo, including my name, avatar, and Weibo content, and I do not have the password for this Weibo account. Luo Zhiyuan stated that he would entrust his lawyer to fully handle the rights protection matter, first issuing a legal letter, requiring Sohu to delete the fake Weibo and stop the infringement.
