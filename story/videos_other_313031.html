Barca's Alleged "Championship Handover" Ignites Shockwaves: Rivals Claim - "We'll Give You 70 Points, We'll Compete for Second Place"

by qinglai888 on 2009-12-17 22:49:25

Barcelona Accused of Being Gifted the Title, Rivals React: "Give Them 70 Points, We'll Compete for Second"

Sina Sports - Last weekend's Barcelona derby left Espanyol feeling aggrieved by their loss, while Barcelona felt their victory lacked dignity. All this was due to referee Iturralde. He mysteriously awarded Barcelona a penalty, helping them secure a 1-0 win over their fierce local rivals Espanyol. Subsequently, in the player tunnel after the match, he red-carded Spanish player Baena, who had consistently questioned his penalty decision. Honestly speaking, this penalty seemed intentionally detrimental to Espanyol, and intriguingly, from this single penalty, two different versions of the truth emerged from the Barcelona and Espanyol camps.

If Brualla is considered a “Real Madrid faction” referee, then Iturralde, one of Spain’s most famous referees officiating this game, is part of the “Barcelona faction.” In previous matches at Camp Nou that he refereed, Barcelona has never lost. This season, he officiated only one Barcelona match, where they triumphed 2-0 away against Getafe. Another telling fact is that in Iturralde's past six games refereeing Barcelona, five penalties were awarded, all benefiting Barcelona. According to Marca, four of these penalties are questionable and suspected of being "gifted" penalties.

The contentious moment occurred in the 39th minute of the Espanyol match when Ibrahimovic crossed from the left edge of the box, and Xavi suddenly fell while contesting the ball. Iturralde penalized Espanyol, claiming Baena had pulled Xavi during his retreat, awarding a penalty. This decision immediately sparked strong dissatisfaction from the Espanyol side. Coach Mauricio Pochettino cryptically remarked post-match, “I didn’t see clearly what happened inside the box, maybe the referee did. Even after watching the TV replay, I can't be sure it was a penalty. My players told me at halftime that nothing happened, so in my view, there was no penalty.”

Espanyol player Luis Garcia expressed frustration, complaining that Iturralde's unfair decisions harmed Espanyol, “If we use this standard, we could also get a penalty in Barcelona's box. Yes, Barcelona is very powerful, but they shouldn't score through such penalties.”

In contrast, the reaction within Barcelona's camp was entirely different. The involved party, Xavi, testified, “This was an obvious penalty, didn’t you see? Inside the box, my jersey was grabbed; Baena pulled my shirt, preventing me from jumping. There's no doubt; it was a penalty. He pulled me, and because I couldn't exert force with my legs, I fell.” After Xavi fell, Guardiola innocently spread his hands on the sidelines, indicating support for his player's penalty claim. However, discussing this call post-game, he admitted he wasn't sure if it was a penalty, saying merely, “Xavi told me Baena pulled him, and I believe Xavi.”

Clearly, Espanyol manager Planes didn't trust Xavi, “How can Xavi say that? According to Iturralde's standards, this Barcelona derby could have resulted in 12 penalties! I insist there was no physical contact between Baena and Xavi, and even if there was, it wasn't enough to cause him to fall.” CEO Collet added, “Why does our team always suffer in every derby? Last time, we conceded a penalty in the 93rd minute, and there was Messi's handball. Now this... If the opponent were Real Madrid, someone would have already called for Franco's overthrow. The federation should consider this issue. If it always happens this way, why not just give Barcelona 70 points and let the rest of us fight for second place?”

No amount of talking would sway anyone. Regarding the penalty controversy, the involved party Baena remained silent, refusing to comment until two days later, when the Espanyol player, trained in Barcelona's youth academy, appeared on Catalan Radio to recount the incident. “Yes, I did pull Xavi.” This statement confirmed the existence of the penalty, though either Xavi's fall was exaggerated, or Iturralde made an incorrect call. He continued, “But it definitely wasn't a penalty. The pull wasn't strong enough to make Xavi lose balance and fall. Xavi was in a very forward position, and I reached back to feel his location. I obstructed Xavi slightly, but the force wasn't enough to make him fall. You can check the TV replay. Honestly, I couldn't believe it was called a penalty because before turning around, I thought Xavi might shoot.”

Reports also mentioned that Baena confronted Iturralde in the player tunnel post-game to seek justice and was shown a red card. What exactly did Baena say that angered Iturralde? “I simply told him, ‘Go watch the TV replay!’ But I certainly didn't use an aggressive tone. That's it.” What upset Baena further was Guardiola's remarks post-game in the press conference, “Xavi told me that Espanyol's players admitted it was a penalty.” “I really don’t know how they could say that; I never told Xavi it was a penalty. If we ever had any conversation about it, I would have definitely told him it wasn't a penalty.”

This controversy provided fodder for Madrid-based media. Both Marca and AS targeted Iturralde, accusing him of protecting Barcelona and securing three points for them; otherwise, Real Madrid, having played one fewer game, would have been only two points behind Barcelona. Conversely, the Catalan media, despite Espanyol being a Barcelona team, wouldn't support a club bearing the Spanish royal crest. To counter the capital's media, Sport analyzed the game footage, attaching screenshots showing Baena indeed pulling Xavi. Deputy editor Carazo stated, “Clearly, it was a penalty.” More provocatively, the paper included a screenshot of Real Madrid's controversial first goal against Valencia, where Benzema was possibly offside, titling it sarcastically, “Real Madrid's first goal was offside, so what’s there to argue about?”

Fortunately, the Barcelona-related penalty controversy involving Xavi and Guardiola has moved beyond Spain's spotlight as they've traveled to Abu Dhabi for the Club World Cup. In their eyes, winning this title holds much more significance than debating whether it was a penalty or not. (Liu Hui)
