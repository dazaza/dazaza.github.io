An Accurate Introduction to SF's Rebounding Secrets

by lk0gamek on 2009-07-20 00:43:14

Hello everyone, I'm here again to write an article about SF (Small Forward). Haha, there's no way around it - I just love this position so much. It's not that I haven't tried others, but after playing them all, I still find SF the most enjoyable. However, when playing SF in matches, the probability of losing is higher than with other positions. Why? Because apart from mid-range shooting, SF doesn't excel in any particular area. If you're not careful, you'll get dominated by others, especially when it comes to rebounding. Therefore, today I will share some of my experiences in grabbing rebounds against various positions and methods for grabbing high and low boards from different angles.

Rebounding as an SF relies on four words: charging, jumping, and reacting. Charging involves running up to a certain spot and jumping to grab the board, usually referring to high boards. Reaction doesn't need much explanation; it's about your adaptability when grabbing the board, which mostly shows up when going for low boards. First, let me clarify a common misconception: some players claim that you should jump as soon as the ball bounces off the rim. After countless attempts, I've found that whether it's a low or high board, as soon as the ball leaves the rim, it's unlikely to be grabbed immediately. How can an SF snatch a high board the moment the ball appears? Even a full-level center (C) couldn't do that with a high board. So, this is a misunderstanding, possibly because those players have become accustomed to snatching low boards instantly. I hope such misleading statements aren't written down, because if they are, new players might try them out, and we'd see the whole game filled with monkeys excitedly jumping up and down regardless of the type of board. Personally, I think this might be a feature designed to enhance the 3D effect of the game. Another point to note is that since the rebound value of SF is far lower than that of C and PF, you must press the direction key when rebounding. This not only increases the range of your hand extension but also makes the rebounding pose cooler. Interestingly, I feel that pressing the direction key makes grabbing boards faster.

Now let's talk about some frequently occurring low boards (I categorize mid-boards relative to high boards as low boards, further dividing low boards into straight-line low boards, parabolic low boards, and multi-hop low boards):

1. The ball bounces off the iron plate behind the hoop, rising straight to the top of the backboard before falling: This type of board falls under the "charge and snatch" category. You should jump to grab the board when the ball reaches its highest point at the top of the backboard. Ideally, stand one to two body lengths away from the hoop for the fastest snatch. A full-level SF can grab the board just before landing (straight-line low board).

2. The ball bounces off any side of the hoop in a parabolic arc and falls toward the sides: This type of board is relatively easy to grab. Simply jump when the ball reaches the apex of the parabola. The closer you are to the hoop, the better (parabolic low board).

3. The ball bounces off the hoop, then the backboard, then the hoop again, and finally bounces off the backboard and shoots upward diagonally after one or two rounds: Despite its long name, this board isn't hard to grab. Just wait until the ball reaches the apex of the parabola and snatch it just before it starts descending. You can stand two body lengths away from the hoop but not more. Also, don't try to grab it while it's bouncing; wait until it has settled. Otherwise, you'll be laughed at by opponents. This board tests your patience when grabbing (multi-hop low board).

4. The ball bounces back and forth inside the hoop before jumping out, but for a short time: For players unfamiliar with PF and C positions, it's difficult to know the best moment to snatch this board. Many people wait until the ball exits the hoop before jumping. Generally, jump when the ball is still inside the hoop during its last bounce before exiting. The closer you are to the hoop, the better (multi-hop low board).

5. The ball spins several times inside the hoop before slowly dropping: By the time it's about to fall, there are already N people waiting under the hoop to grab it. Generally, jump when the basketball is almost falling and forms a vertical line with the hoop. In other words, imagine the basketball as a vertical line and the hoop as a horizontal line. The angle between them is 90 degrees. Jump at this moment. This board doesn't require pressing the direction key. The best position is directly below the hoop. It requires a certain amount of reaction, experience, and observational skills. Among all low boards, this is the most challenging and difficult one. I personally recommend using the tip-in shot because it allows you to slightly advance the timing and avoid a second offensive attempt (multi-hop low board).

6. The ball bounces several times on the hoop before finally falling: This is my second-best board to snatch, and it looks very stylish when done correctly. Generally, grab the ball during its final upward bounce. You can stand farther away, but not more than three body lengths (multi-hop low board).
