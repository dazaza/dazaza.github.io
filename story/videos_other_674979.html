September Chrysanthemum

by songsdp82pm on 2012-02-24 17:03:34

Xuan Yue's Wild Chrysanthemum (Part 1) Behind, the wild chrysanthemums are in full bloom. In the early morning, the whistle blew on the playground for everyone to gather. Many people were still half-asleep when they started running to the playground with their camouflage jackets. I was waiting on the playground with a steamed bun I had just bought from the canteen, listening for the instructor’s whistle. Watching my classmates arrive one by one with bright faces, smiling at each other despite our mutual unfamiliarity and joking around, I instinctively put the steamed bun quickly into my jacket pocket because a roommate who likes to joke was coming towards me. We were still strangers but exchanged faint smiles.

This is my second military training, and it's also the first lesson at this unfamiliar new school. Early morning runs followed by breakfast time filled my week. My mind was occupied with commands like "stand at ease," "attention," "march in step," and "parade march" then back again. Occasionally, during midday, we would see some physically weak individuals collapse, and the cute little girls making funny poses while standing at attention. It's no surprise; many city kids have been spoiled by their parents. But I am just a child from the depths of Daba Mountain.

Due to the recent hot weather, the school exceptionally granted an afternoon off. The boys rushed out of the gate without even changing out of their uniforms, while some fashion-conscious girls went to the dormitory to apply light makeup, change clothes, and slowly leave with small bags. I quietly sat by the door of the canteen watching the bustling scene gradually fade away. Seeing those couples walking ambiguously, as their figures disappeared into the distance, a sense of strangeness and oppression arose within me. Not until their figures blurred amidst the noisy playground and only the lonely tree-lined corridors remained did I calmly leave the already closed canteen. Back at the dormitory, I found it deserted except for the life teacher sweeping the remaining cola bottles with a green broom, the swishing sound clearly audible even on the third floor.

This school is located on the outskirts of a bustling city, in a sparsely populated small town, quite remote.

I pushed open the dormitory door, changed my clothes, and quietly lay down on the top bunk with a blank mind. I could still hear the cicadas chirping outside, echoing through the afternoon's sunset. I walked to the windowsill and looked at the city that doesn't see snow in winter under the neon lights' glow, places I cannot see due to their dazzling allure. Looking at the nearby hill covered in lush grass with spots of yellow highlighting the coffee-colored sunset and orange clouds in the sky brought a certain excitement, drawing me to run down the dormitory building to the small hill behind.

Soon, I arrived at this unfamiliar place with a mysterious feeling of intimacy. It was right behind the school, not far, just separated by a wall. The quiet expanse of green grass and moss made the path invisible. Now stepping on the grass, I felt its earthy fragrance. Slowly moving forward, I bit a piece of grass leaf and tasted the familiar flavor. Recalling past memories, before reaching the mountaintop, recollections already clouded my mind. Suddenly, I saw the vibrant wild chrysanthemums blooming in the cracks of the riverbank stones, swaying carefree in the breeze.

Lying on the stone slab, I watched the twilight and orange clouds above, inhaling the mixed scents of soil, grass, and wild chrysanthemums, gradually getting intoxicated. Slowly, I immersed myself in the melancholy brought by reminiscence, remembering much as the moment lingered.

(Part 2) Unbloomed Wild Chrysanthemums

The night before school started, I packed my luggage ready for school, dragging a large load of things like a refugee to the western train station.

I've long been accustomed to my parents' morning reminders to be careful on the road or wear more clothes if it's cold. From junior high school to now, I've been used to handling my own affairs alone—enrolling in school, attending parent-teacher meetings, including every distant journey where I'd hear my parents say they're busy over the phone. This time was no exception. In high school, because my parents worked hard outside, I often heard them talking about overtime. Now hearing them say they're discussing business with others, there's an added sentence: Am I grown up and still need someone's care?

Sitting in the taxi after hanging up the phone, I kept complaining about how they could treat me this way. That night, the sudden downpour blurred my vision, surrounding this familiar place I was about to leave.

With my early morning train ticket, I queued in a long line, suddenly hearing a familiar shout behind me — Lin. I hurriedly turned around and saw a familiar figure with wet hair dripping water onto his clothes.

Yes, it was my father.

Looking at my father's face and forehead soaked with rain, clear wrinkles revealed the fine lines etched deep into his skin. His wet hair shimmered with grayish-white strands, piercingly visible on his brow, tugging at my heartstrings. His white shirt was already soaked, clinging to his increasingly aged skin, showing the broadness of his muscular arms and shoulders. Listening to the water sounds in his shoes clanking toward me, my throat tightened, unable to softly call out — Dad.

"You always forget things." Saying this, he handed me a plastic bag revealing the Kind chocolate my mother had brought for me on her business trip. I didn’t dare look up at my father's weary face, afraid of seeing more signs of aging I didn’t want to acknowledge, or perhaps in my heart, my forever-young dad will never grow old, ever.

My fingers trembled slightly as I took the plastic bag, stiffly squeezing out a sentence: "Why bring chocolates in such heavy rain? Don't I know how to buy them myself?" Out of the corner of my eye, I saw the calloused hands of my father, leaving me powerless, even starting to scold myself for speaking too harshly.

Father didn’t scold me for spending money recklessly like he used to, instead unexpectedly nagging: "This is specifically brought by your mom, knowing you love eating chocolates. I happened to see it when I returned home, knowing you tend to lose things. And since it was raining outside, I guessed you wouldn't come back to get it. I was also worried about being late and not delivering it to you, so I forgot to bring an umbrella." Then he chuckled like a child, his eyes never leaving mine. However, I still kept my head low, lacking the courage to look up.

"It's already morning, go home. I know how to board the train, no need to see me off, especially since you have work tomorrow. Go rest early." My originally trembling voice was disguised perfectly, seemingly giving orders instead. Father simply nodded, preparing to turn back, saying, "If anything happens, give us a call. Take care of yourself and don't catch a cold."

At that moment, I don't know where the courage came from to grab my father's hand and say, "It's raining heavily outside, take my umbrella." However, father gently refused, saying he would take a taxi after exiting the station and let me keep it for the road.

After saying this, father turned and dragged his clanging leather shoes, most likely because of the heavy rain and rushing. He usually cares about appearances, but today he appeared unusually disheveled in front of me. Every tiny action tugged at my pain, even the rainwater dripping on the ground forming puddles like unspoken bitterness.

Watching father's retreating back and the footprints left behind, each imprint deeper than the last. Until they were permanently imprinted in my heart, flowing silently yet blossoming unseen wounds.

Just like the wind-blown petals of freshly bloomed wild chrysanthemums falling pitifully to the ground in golden patches. Each petal a scene of heartbreak scattered everywhere, not even worth a serious glance.

Time rewinds ten years.

Father had just returned from working locally to our hometown. At that time, I lived in the countryside, and my parents weren't as successful as they are now.

It was also the year I finished fourth grade. In my remote mountain village, a system of adjusting exams was implemented. The best students from each grade would compete in the district, and I luckily became one of the top students selected for adjustment exams, bringing fame to this rural elementary school.

I had always lived with my grandmother, and the neighbors were already used to it. For years, I had been the one chosen for these adjustment exams. So, it wasn't surprising or something worth celebrating anymore, but rather a difficult task. Because, at that time, with our family's poor economic situation, we often struggled, let alone the dozens of yuan needed for transportation to the district.

Grandma said all the money for buying food at the market had gone to agricultural taxes, and what was left was for this month's electricity bill. She frowned deeply. Such situations were not uncommon, foreshadowing that I would walk to the district for the exam as usual. Grandma kept complaining that father hadn't earned a single penny in all these years, but father kept saying his investments had failed, leading to a prolonged silent war between mother and son.

That day, it wasn't yet dawn.

I was woken up in my sleep, seeing father busy by the earthen boiler. I didn't bother asking what he was doing, just gathering the rulers and pens I would need for the exam, sitting on the threshold of the old house reading the key points given by the teacher. When the birds began chirping, I prepared to set off alone along the narrow path to the district. Because grandma said the rooster crowing isn't the earliest sign of dawn; it's when the birds chirp searching for food.

I pushed the old wooden door, its red paint worn away by time, leaving a mottled trace. The door creaked as usual, and I tried not to wake grandma who suffered from insomnia. Opening a small gap, I squeezed out quietly, but was stopped by father.

Father held a greasy pig leg from the pot, telling me to eat it on the way and handing me five yuan in case I got thirsty and wanted to buy a soda. As a young boy, I grabbed the pig leg and money and hurried off, fearing I might be late for the exam. But I never paid attention to father, who was still sitting on the threshold, lost in thought. When I crossed the fields and reached the top of the hill, looking back at our courtyard, I still saw a man in a white shirt sitting on our threshold, a strange pressure I couldn't understand at that age.

And, that hillside was covered with wild chrysanthemums, green and unknown at the time, blooming with a resilient brilliance.

But, as a child, I never saw the chrysanthemums blooming on that hillside. They were said to be cut by an old man for medicinal purposes, and I never saw them bloom in a sea of orange-yellow. Perhaps in youth, I didn't understand the sorrow of such brilliance, nor the guilt father felt watching me walk such a long mountain path.

(Part 3) Still Blooming Wild Chrysanthemums in September

I watched the coffee-colored clouds scattered by the wind in the sky and the orange sunset gradually disappear. Watching them vanish, a wave of indelible longing gathered in my heart.

Thinking deeply, my eyes began to moisten. Those wordless loves, sincere actions, were deeply engraved in the depths of my memory.

I never expected that less than a month after school started, I would miss the warmth of home so much. Even though meals were often leftovers eaten alone, it was a kind of happiness without worry or longing. I watched those scattered wild chrysanthemums blooming in small clusters, swaying in the wind.

Now, I slowly understand, slowly learn to change, slowly stop burdening them for my sake.

Understanding that, before the flowers bloom, the heart sleeps.

Wild chrysanthemums don't possess the romance of Provence's flower season, the colors of Las Vegas, or the sadness of lavender.

But, once the flowers bloom, the heart remembers.

In the end of September, I gradually comprehend those thoughts of missing, those yet-to-be-missed things. Crisp shadows, captivating objects, deeply imprinted in the moments of life's blossoms, then blooming fiercely apart.

I turned back and prepared to leave, slowly turning around to gaze at the small, melancholic wild chrysanthemums blooming in the stone crevices. Perhaps, the next turn will continue the missing, or, another year. Soon, they will bloom for those fathers standing firmly in resilience. Tomorrow, the next September will still be filled with wild chrysanthemums blooming in remembrance.

And I will let the air far away yet near refract before you, whispering softly.

"Dad, I love you."
