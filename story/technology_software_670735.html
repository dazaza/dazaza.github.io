October in Xi'an - Other Photos

by baojian6764 on 2012-02-21 10:50:01

Lunchtime, we went straight to the Muslim Street. It's a relatively wide street with some big shady trees on both sides. However, the air was still not fresh and the haze lingered among the leaves, fortunately not mixed with cooking fumes from restaurants. The Muslim Street is quite bustling, mostly filled with dining places, this family or that family. During meal times, the hawkers were very loud. Large woks steamed with mutton soup, which I plan to try tomorrow. After strolling for a bit and eating some green bean cakes and persimmon cakes, we finally decided where to eat. We ended up at Jia San灌soup buns, along with yellow osmanthus porridge, Zhen cake, cold noodles, and three delicacies dumplings, etc. The cold noodles were particularly delicious, stir-fried with chili powder and chopped pickled mustard, extremely spicy and fragrant. Actually, I think the osmanthus persimmon cake was pretty good too, but it was fried too much and became greasy.

In one courtyard of the Muslim Street, it seemed as if it once had a noble background, and the seal on its plaque appeared to be granted by Emperor Qianlong.

Xiyang Market is a smaller and livelier alley. Small stalls and restaurant electronic signs stereotypically occupy most of the space. Women in headscarves add more ethnic charm here. The facades of the restaurants are smaller than those on the Muslim Street and more "natural". Some porridge-like food looked like hot and spicy soup, and the kitchen counters and vegetable racks dripped with black grease. On the other side, the waiters and boss were tearing bread into small pieces on the roadside while chatting casually. Fortunately, there were grilled skewers and bright pomegranates around, and the smell of mutton soup wafted through the air. In the afternoon, under the lazy sunlight, plants grew in the cracks of old rooftiles, experiencing all these lively scenes every day. The lane was long, and deeper inside it became quieter, full of life. Old-fashioned barber shops, fresh meat stalls, slogans, elders walking through the alleys, smiling children, and those rough handmade crafts passed by, just a bit sore in the legs. When we arrived at a place called Daxuexiang, perhaps in ancient times there was a school here, so this street was named after it. Around one o'clock, the drowsiest moment of the day arrived. We ate at DQ, took a short nap, and then felt fully restored.

As a child, I loved southern goods stores with their dazzling array of snacks.

Shaanxi produces a lot of pomegranates, said to be very sweet, but they're too hard to eat, and even unattractive, lacking any purchase appeal.

Mirror cakes painted with cartoon pictures.

Naan, mutton soup, various dried fruits, skewers, time passing through the alleys, various rampant tourist souvenirs.

Haircuts given on such chairs from age two to fourteen.

They were playing a role-playing game; the little boy in red stood seriously with his hands behind his back.

We took a taxi to Defu Lane, where it was said there were book markets and entertainment venues. Unexpectedly, the lane was under construction, earthmovers rumbling, shaking the ground. Staying in any bar or teahouse would probably not bring peace. We found a bookstore but only skimmed through it. Most books were about poetry, calligraphy, and art criticism. As semi-illiterates without much research interest, we didn't feel like lingering long. Exiting Defu Lane, we didn't find the Beilin Temple mentioned in our guidebook. Instead, we stumbled upon Yongning Gate's city tower, realizing this was Xi'an!

I really liked the broad road on top of the city wall. This should be the most authentic "horse" road, with blue-gray bricks and moss in the seams, as if the sound of soldiers patrolling on horseback still lingered. The slightly inclined road surface might have been specially built for drainage. Yongning Gate section was relatively noisy, with tourists watching performances and cycling continuously. Further east and west, tourists gradually thinned out. By evening, mist diffused both inside and outside the city walls. Inside the walls were old buildings near Shuyuanmen, with round roofs and gray bricks. Outside the walls were modern steel and concrete structures. Walking on the high walls, one could see the past and present simultaneously. There was a sense of proud solitude and barely contained joy. Perhaps in the sunny spring, one should fly a kite here, or walk in the snow in the winter. Or, just now, as the sun sets, hear the sound of footsteps tapping on the stones, feel the cool autumn breeze, and watch the battlements and gate towers fade in and out of the mist.

Originally planned to visit the city wall early the next morning, but overslept and missed the time. Last night's dinner at Xi'an Restaurant was almost worthless. Mass点评can be watched like CCTV. After packing up again, we returned to the Muslim Street, choosing a store reportedly favored by locals for mutton soup. Expensive, 25 yuan per bowl with just a few slices of mutton. The taste of mutton and broth was good, but the bread was hard to swallow. For those who don't particularly like noodles, water pot mutton is recommended, easier to accept.

Xiaoyanta is not far from the Muslim Street, just a few turns by taxi. With ID, you can get free tickets. Compared to yesterday, the sun was much warmer, though the fog was still heavy, the sunshine still generously penetrated through, giving the buildings and leaves a soft golden hue. Xiaoyanta Park is a quiet garden. Even on this weekend morning, there were fewer tourists. Perhaps because there weren't many spots for "here I am" photos, it made our trip peaceful and serene. The tower itself has a single color palette, brown-yellow bricks stacked, weathered over time. Happily, the government seems to have no intention of artificial restoration, allowing it to remain authentically aged. One can still clearly see the brick layers and delicate edges, graceful angles and curves, simple yet grand and dignified. Here might have once been a temple, neatly arranged from south to north with front halls, main halls, and bell and drum towers. The south entrance is closed, the north has become the park entrance. Without incense, Buddha statues, and praying crowds, there remains ancient trees shading the sky, bamboo grove shadows, and those Sui-Tang style stone carvings. The horse posts and stone tablets are also beautiful. Simple stone columns carved with lively figures, besides dragon sons carrying the tablets, each tablet was covered with intricate carvings, like carefully framed paintings, standing out even more.

Wish: I stay healthy and safe all my life, truly someone who knows how to cherish oneself.

Horse posts, are these all incarnations of grooms?

Dayanta is the most recommended to skip in the guidebook, perhaps because the ticket price is average and not cost-effective. Most people come here at night to see the music fountain in the square. We could only catch a glimpse during the day. The square is grand, crowded with people, fake maple trees. Far away, Dayanta peeked through the mist. The fountain played without music, but as long as there was water, it added a touch of charm.

After a quick lunch, Gan Zhou Restaurant tasted pretty good, not completely disappointed with Xi'an's formal meals. With little time left in the afternoon, we considered visiting Shuyuanmen to see if we could find some interesting trinkets. As someone who once dreamed of calligraphy and painting arts, browsing in Shuyuanmen made me tense. Art meeting wholesale is like a weak woman meeting bandits, unable to produce anything refined. Beautifully framed three-foot calligraphy sold for only twenty or thirty yuan, not even covering the framing costs, let alone the expenses of ink, brushes, and paper. In an era valuing famous artists and industrialization, beginners can only paint with spirit.

Other antiques and jade, like tourist attractions across the country, are indistinguishable between truth and falsehood. But the Hu County farmers' paintings at a small stall caught my attention. Made on thick cardboard with watercolors, the style is full of woodcut and Northwest folk characteristics, simple yet interesting. I chose two framed ones and rushed to catch the train back to Beijing, not leaving empty-handed.

The dilapidated tower may really hide treasures, attracting countless people searching here.

Industrialized shadow plays and paper cuttings.

Various indistinguishable trinkets.

Made on-site, still requiring some skill.

The sunset illuminated the murals on the tower railing gold, often enlightening the inner world of cultured people, because for them, sunlight is something that can be written, painted, and imagined.
