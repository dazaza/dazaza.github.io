Sina Tech presents a series of interviews titled "The New Force of E-Commerce," which will focus on the diversified development environment of e-commerce, new entrepreneurial opportunities, new business models, and the psychological journey of these entrepreneurs. E-commerce New Force Interview: Xu Xiaohui, Founder of Chuke's Literary Brand

Media Source: Sina Tech

Host of this column @Cui Xi welcomes private messages from entrepreneurs who wish to connect with us. We also welcome any valuable opinions and suggestions.

Second Guest: Xu Xiaohui, Founder of B2C "Literary" Brand Chuke

Due to his entrepreneurial endeavors, Xu Xiaohui, who has just become a father, doesn't have much time to spend with his eight-month-old son; he only gets to play with him in the morning. If he hadn't left Vancl, holding the title of vice president and stock options, he could have waited until next year for the IPO and cash out.

"But there's no way, this is my choice," said Xu Xiaohui.

Born in 1977, Xu Xiaohui believes that this entrepreneurship feels like being pushed by the tide, not entirely against one’s will, but with many predestined elements. When you don’t necessarily intend to do something, you'll find countless people pushing you towards it. In the rapidly changing environment of the internet, good opportunities vanish in the blink of an eye.

When Xu Xiaohui left Vancl, his boss Chen Nian once asked him what he intended to do. Upon hearing the answer "B2C apparel," Chen Nian helplessly replied, "I'm sorry I can't invest in you because I'm also in the apparel industry."

And Lei Jun, Xu Xiaohui's boss whom he got to know well at Kingsoft, also couldn't invest in Chuke due to having invested in Vancl. "But they gave me a lot of advice and resources, which are more important than investment," said Xu Xiaohui. Just a few days ago, Lei Jun called to inquire about Chuke's current operations.

In the vertical e-commerce industry, it is generally believed that clothing items have the highest gross margin, and among them, self-branded items have the highest margin. Vancl successfully followed this path. Naturally, Xu Xiaohui, who came from Vancl, wouldn't give up on this familiar market.

Where does the funding come from? Although neither Lei Jun nor Chen Nian invested, there are plenty of angels who see potential in e-commerce. Xu Xiaohui's startup capital is approximately tens of millions of RMB, and he is currently negotiating the first round of financing.

Graduating from Peking University's Chinese Department left a mark of literary flair on Xu Xiaohui. The name "Chuke" comes from the Ming Dynasty's "Chuke Pai An Qi Xiao." On Chuke's website, you'll often see quotes like "If only life were as beautiful as our first meeting," attributed to Nalan Rongru and beloved by the bourgeois literati youth.

There are two interesting evaluations in the industry: "This might be China's most culturally-rich e-commerce site" and "A Douban version of Vancl." Regarding the question of whether it's too niche, Xu Xiaohui has heard it many times but responds with two reasons: first, any new B2C brand starts off niche. Second, Chuke aims to reflect the mainstream urban population's desire to escape the anxiety of modern life, or the slower-paced fashion.

Only two months since its launch on April 6th, the debate is still premature and can only be measured by data.

These figures show an average order value of 240 yuan, with daily orders ranging between 400 to 500. The registered user base is close to 100,000. If we make a rough calculation, this month's sales would be around 3.5 million yuan. "This is a very good number for us," Xu Xiaohui revealed that Chuke's gross margin is around 50%.

Leading this entrepreneurial endeavor for half a year, Xu Xiaohui has experienced various difficulties but also unprecedented gains. He now understands some decisions made by Chen Nian during his time at Vancl better.

At 34 years old, Xu Xiaohui believes this is the best time for him to start a business.

"If I had waited for Vancl's IPO and then spent another 4-5 years or even longer, I'd be almost 40 by then, and choosing the path of entrepreneurship would face greater pressure. Since entrepreneurship is a high-failure-rate venture, I've placed the trial-and-error period as early as possible. If this attempt fails, I still have the chance to rise again," said Xu Xiaohui (Cui Xi).

Below is the interview transcript:

Host Cui Xi: Good morning, everyone. Welcome to Sina Tech's series of interviews on the new force of e-commerce. Today, we are delighted to have our second guest, a long-time friend in the internet industry, Mr. Xu Xiaohui, who recently started a B2C vertical website with a literary style. Let's start with a greeting from him to our netizens.

Xu Xiaohui: Hello, everyone. I am Xu Xiaohui.

Host: Your website has a strong literary flavor, and it is an original clothing brand called Chuke. Could you please introduce the specific operational situation after two months online?

Xu Xiaohui: First, let me explain the name. Many people have different associations with this name. From our perspective, I majored in Chinese literature, and the name originates from the Ming Dynasty's "Chuke Pai An Qi Xiao." Chuke also means the first edition or original. During the project preparation phase, we continuously thought about how to differentiate ourselves in the highly competitive clothing B2C market. Our current brand positioning is slow life. After the website went live, there were two evaluations that I found quite amusing and accurate from consumers' perspectives. One said this might be China's most culturally-rich e-commerce site. Another compared it to a Douban version of Vancl. These two evaluations align with our positioning as we indeed hope this brand embodies cultural sophistication and spiritual depth.

Our current photo shoots feature albums where listeners understand our positioning emotionally. The current special is called Private City, where we photograph many representative figures in Beijing, expressing their individual connections to the city. This includes many personal experiences and emotions. When the site launched, people saw that it wasn't merely a commercial clothing-selling platform. More importantly, we express the lifestyle of young people in the city and their thoughts about the city. In this process, we can convey many ideas about discovering beauty and touching moments in everyday life. This is what our brand primarily wants to communicate. Only then do we consider how to sell our clothes, products, and goods. These two aspects complement each other and are crucial for a clothing brand.

Host: You mentioned external evaluations stating this might be the most culturally rich e-commerce site, which could be dangerous as the target audience is small. You might think this niche isn't a big issue. Douban defines itself as part of the literary circle. Despite the internet's long development, Douban hasn't done poorly, but it hasn't exploded either. Do you think Chuke might end up in a lukewarm development state?

Xu Xiaohui: Last week, I met an investor friend who provided a piece of data that may or may not be accurate. Douban is reportedly entering an explosive growth phase, with traffic surpassing Kaixin001. I haven't verified the data yet, but last week I heard this as good news. Communities like Douban possess the potential for explosive growth after accumulating strength over time. Kaixin001 gained rapid recognition through certain applications, but platforms like Sina Weibo diverted users to new media platforms. However, media or communities like Douban won't face such issues because their user retention is very strong. At a certain stage, they experience explosive growth. This data needs further verification.

Regarding the mass versus niche debate, many friends are concerned about this issue. Two points explain this: Any new brand, in my view, starts off niche when it launches. For example, someone might say your former employer, Vancl, did so well with clear positioning and a large market. Why didn't you create another Vancl? I would respond that Vancl has a four-year history with much accumulated experience and growth that cannot be replicated. Furthermore, if you immediately try to replicate Vancl without a clear positioning, you would likely fail quickly due to resource, brand recognition, financial strength, and talent reserve disadvantages compared to established brands.

Additionally, although our positioning seems niche, I believe it reflects the mainstream. Our target audience consists of mainstream young people in cities whose lifestyles mirror ours, including yours and mine. Everyone spends much time working or commuting, leading to stress and busy schedules. People often work late into the night. This is a universal mindset, and many may wish to escape the city and lead a slower-paced life.

Chuke positions itself as slow living. While not everyone can lead a slow-paced life, this positioning packages idealism or fairy tales. Within these stories, people relieve much of their stress because, in reality, they cannot leave the city or abandon their livelihoods. In this context, you provide a medium to alleviate anxiety.

After creating this brand, I learned a new term: healing system, originating from Japan. I hope it carries a sense of healing. When people see non-commercial topics presented on our website, it helps relieve internal stress. In the seemingly dreamlike fairy tales we weave, touching emotions suffice. Google optimization company. Mainstream young people need a medium to relieve the stress of their anxious lives. This is the common sentiment among netizens that we aim to address.

Host: Perhaps you're talking about brand positioning. Industry insiders and outsiders are very interested in the operational data after two months online. Would you mind sharing it? Also, I know in vertical e-commerce, the highest gross margins belong to clothing categories. What is your gross margin?

Xu Xiaohui: Our gross margin is 50%, with a markup of double the factory price. Traditional markups are typically 4-5 times higher, making B2C pricing more cost-effective. Our pricing strategy emphasizes better value for money. We maintain roughly 50% gross profit, but sometimes lower, even below 10%, especially for promotional items or popular goods.

Host: That is already a relatively high gross margin in the entire B2C industry?

Xu Xiaohui: Yes, in the B2C industry, doing a private label brand, including Vancl, yields gross margins of 40-50%. This is determined by the business model of private labels. Channel-based models may yield slightly lower margins. Yesterday, I reviewed Mecox Lane's financial report, which showed a gross margin of 36% last quarter, which is quite high for a channel-based model.

Host: What are your average order value and user numbers?

Xu Xiaohui: The average order value is about 240 yuan, with daily order volumes ranging from 400 to 500. Our registered user base is nearly 100,000, which is a very good figure for us.

Host: How does spending around 200 yuan compare within the industry?

Xu Xiaohui: It's hard to compare directly, but within the clothing sector, it falls above average. Given our position as a budget-friendly clothing brand, promotions at Vancl often bring prices below 200 yuan. Mazamao may have higher prices, ranging from 500 to 600 yuan. Therefore, 200-240 yuan places us in the upper-middle range.

Host: Where does the biggest cost lie? Producing large-scale photoshoots and theme planning must be labor-intensive.

Xu Xiaohui: Our team size isn't large, around 50 people, with over 40 focused on visual content creation. Last week, we counted and discovered that the largest department, with 11 members, is responsible for the website, including visual planning, photography, topic production, and web design—all tasks related to visuals and branding. Thus, this department has the most personnel. In my opinion, B2C, especially clothing B2C, is essentially selling photos. Therefore, we invest significant effort into visuals. This week, we will roll out a completely revamped website, showcasing an even more artistic Chuke.

Host: How is the capital aspect going?

Xu Xiaohui: Our startup funds included angel investments amounting to tens of millions of RMB. Currently, we are raising funds for the next round, with details and progress ongoing and not yet suitable for public disclosure.

Host: Since you worked at Vancl for a long time, some might wonder why you left to start your own business when Vancl was doing well, including having multiple platforms and plans for an IPO.

Xu Xiaohui: As you know, I'm not young anymore—I was born in 1977 and am 34 years old. While at Vancl, many people asked me why I wanted to leave given Vancl's excellent performance and imminent IPO. However, for a man under significant pressure in society, one must consider future plans or whether the benefits match the time invested. If you expect great returns from staying at Vancl, the trade-off might involve dedicating 4-5 or even more years. By the time you're nearing 40, starting a business would face greater pressures—family, personal, and health-related. Entrepreneurship inherently carries a high failure rate, so I moved my trial-and-error period forward. Even if this venture fails, I still have the opportunity to rise again. Starting at 40 would present fewer opportunities. During my time at Vancl, I grew significantly, thanks to Chen Nian's mentorship and the company's impact on my career. Throughout my work at Vancl, I faced various external temptations—project lures, offers from traditional enterprises, or desires to accomplish many things. Everyone harbors an entrepreneurial dream, often suppressed. But in the fast-paced development of Vancl's industry, these temptations amplify even the smallest dreams until they become irresistible. When external conditions allow—recognition or investment—you feel compelled to break free and pursue your dream. Whether through personal acknowledgment or financial backing, this amplifies the desire to independently achieve greatness. Eventually, you take the leap, despite later realizing the hardships and challenges that differ from idealistic expectations. Yet, this process brings immense growth. A former colleague from Kingsoft, the founder of Storm Codec, was asked about the biggest change transitioning from a large company to a startup. His response, which left a deep impression, was that in one year of entrepreneurship, he experienced more than eight years at Kingsoft.

Host: Is that your answer as well?

Xu Xiaohui: Yes. Though we haven't reached eight years, in just six months, I've encountered more than ever before in my career. Many things are unexpected, revealing gaps in your understanding and allowing you to empathize with previous bosses—Lei Jun or Chen Nian—whose decisions you once questioned or misunderstood. Reflecting on their past choices today, you realize why they made those decisions and would likely make similar ones yourself, despite the harm caused to others—internally or externally. In this process, you discover that while painful, such harm is necessary to ensure the safety of a project or team.

Host: Some rumors suggest you left Vancl due to ideological differences with senior management?

Xu Xiaohui: Regardless of whether at Vancl or Yahoo, I personally enjoyed my work and learned a lot. Every job has its problems, and no company is perfect. When I first joined Kingsoft right out of school, it seemed like a haloed enterprise—a 20+ year IT company also venturing into the internet. Entering it, however, revealed many unseen aspects externally. Similarly, both Vancl and Chuke have imperfections upon closer inspection. Different people handle situations differently. My approach is to discover the lovable aspects of a company. For the shortcomings or imperfections, I strive to improve them as much as possible. When unable to do so, I compromise because work inherently involves compromises. Of course, many issues arise during this process. For entrepreneurship, these aren't the sole concerns. Irresponsibility toward oneself or the project isn't justified by any circumstances. Personally walking away angrily due to dissatisfaction wouldn't be responsible.

I don't know the specifics of the rumors, but engaging in such ventures inevitably invites speculation. The key is whether the task succeeds. We still collaborate with Vancl. In the coming weeks, V+ distribution will go live, and I spoke with Chen Nian last week about meeting up. He was in a noisy place and suggested rescheduling. We remain excellent colleagues, and communication with current Vancl colleagues remains frequent. I am grateful to all my former employers.

Host: You have a good relationship with Lei Jun and Chen Nian. Lei Jun invested in Vancl as an angel investor, so did you receive his support? Did Lei Jun and Chen Nian offer any advice on your current venture?

Xu Xiaohui: When I left, Chen Nian mentioned that since you're in the clothing industry, I regretfully cannot invest in you as Vancl is also in clothing.

Host: So, if you weren't in clothing, he would have invested in another brand?

Xu Xiaohui: Yes, he could have invested with available resources. Unfortunately, clothing overlaps.

Regarding Lei Jun, when I visited Xiaomi Technology, I discussed angel investing with him. He emphasized avoiding conflicts between projects I invest in. Clothing inevitably competes with Vancl. Although we perceive our brand as niche, Lei Jun acknowledged this. He refrained from asking detailed questions about the industry, respecting confidentiality. Throughout the process, they offered invaluable advice and resources, which outweigh mere financial investment.

Host: Observing Vancl's development, it initially positioned itself as high-end with higher prices, gradually adjusting to become fast-fashion—affordable and mass-market. Have you considered that your current literary positioning might prove too narrow, eventually shifting to affordable or mass-market consumption?

Xu Xiaohui: During Vancl's evolution, I closely observed its trajectory. Initially aiming for a middle-class demographic, Vancl's final character largely reflected its founder's personality. Chen Nian sought a different positioning beyond middle-class ideals, embodying a literary person with idealistic tendencies. Similarly, I share this inclination, having grown up in a rural area. We both seek a populist appeal, akin to Chen Nian's concept of 'People's Fashion.' Once he aligned the brand with figures like Wang Luodan, Han Han, and Huang Xiaoming saying 'I am Vancl,' the essence crystallized. Like Uniqlo in Japan, the goal was to create a widely accessible, mass-market brand. This involved a gradual process of brand repositioning.

Similarly, Chuke began with a campus streetwear concept targeting university or high school students. Japanese streetwear entering China commands high prices. Personally nostalgic for campus life, interacting with students appealed to me. Realizing this group was overly specific, we adjusted our focus to white-collar professionals. Hence, my company is named Gode Technology, reflecting the evolving clarity of Chuke's identity, including its 'slow life' positioning. Established in November, by December, we clarified our intent to embrace 'slow life.' Brands undergo exploration phases. To date, this positioning grows increasingly clear. First, embracing 'slow life' or Muji's aesthetic will never waver—it resonates with me personally.

Packaging or presenting cultural elements, you may notice in the upcoming weeks that Chuke now sells books. Among the thirteen titles personally selected by me, works like 'Six Records of Floating Life' align with our brand positioning without conflict. Personal touches infuse our products, shared by colleagues with literary inclinations. Together, we aspire to incorporate a meaningful element into our work despite its demanding nature. Chuke represents the kind of literary clothing or lifestyle brand we enjoy creating. Tasks like designing Seven Wolves wouldn't suit us—we dislike it. We prefer this aesthetic, as do many consumers. This passion drives us to use our talents meaningfully. Enjoyment trumps all.

Host: Have you considered that while personal imprints benefit smaller companies, they may hinder larger organizations? As the company scales, maintaining such a personal touch becomes challenging. For instance, finding time daily to curate book selections will soon prove impractical.

Xu Xiaohui: Selection processes are becoming increasingly systematic and collective rather than reliant on my personal preferences. This streamlining evolves step-by-step. Interestingly, investors' perspectives contrast with your concerns. Some worry about rapid expansion or agency products diluting our current aesthetic and commitment to a wholly self-branded approach. Investors question whether our style might deviate. From our standpoint, we will uphold this positioning. Should business constraints necessitate diversification, perhaps we’ll explore additional brands akin to Vancl's V+. Outside Chuke, we might develop Erke or Sanke, offering distinct aesthetics but rooted in the same ethos. The space for 'slow life' positioning remains vast. Our definition focuses on style and demographics without categorical boundaries. Next week, we'll launch a miscellaneous goods channel selling home products, sandalwood, sachets, etc., expanding infinitely like Muji. Over the next three to five years, we aim to solidify our foundation.

Host: Do you maintain inventory?

Xu Xiaohui: Yes, we do have inventory.

Host: At a smaller scale, inventory isn't a major concern. Liu Qiangdong of JD.com once stated that one shouldn't enter e-commerce without ten billion yuan. What plans do you have to address this?

Xu Xiaohui: There's a point I didn't fully address earlier—substantial capital is tied up in inventory. With a 50% gross margin, achieving 10 million yuan in sales requires tying up 5 million in inventory. Under these circumstances, inventory naturally poses a significant challenge. Having no categorical boundaries doesn't mean unlimited expansion in styles due to resource limitations. Despite numerous brands, each category follows a premium strategy—approximately 100 styles each for men's and women's wear, ideal for this stage or the next year. We aim to produce refined items, unlike JD's ability to handle thousands of SKUs across various categories. While we have no strict limitations on categories, we will adhere to a premium strategy regarding styles.

Host: Given time constraints, here's our final question about entrepreneurship. Some argue that as a literary person studying Chinese literature and enjoying literary books, doubts arise whether such individuals can succeed entrepreneurially. Chen Nian sold his company before founding Vancl step-by-step. Do you feel pressured or how do you handle criticism and skepticism?

Xu Xiaohui: Pressure exists, but combining my interests with commerce requires exploration. After one and a half months of the website's launch, consumers validated this aesthetic through their orders. Such pressure accompanies any endeavor undeniably.

Opinions for or against me hold little significance. I disregard such voices entirely. The only consideration is, as a journalist asked, what is my greatest hope for the future? My greatest hope is that if interviewed again in a year and the company still exists, I would feel immensely gratified. That is my ultimate aspiration.

Host: Speaking of male entrepreneurs' ages, you've just become a father. At this stage, balancing family, son, and immense pressure is daunting, given the inherent anxiety of entrepreneurship. One investor suggests that after 40, with stable family life, initial wealth accumulation, and children in junior high reducing worries compared to newborns, entrepreneurship might be more feasible. How do you view this?

Xu Xiaohui: Choosing the timing for entrepreneurship feels like being propelled by a wave, not entirely involuntary but guided by destiny. During my entrepreneurial journey, even when not intending to, countless people push you toward it—a fascinating, albeit mystical, experience akin to fate. Timing isn't entirely within one's control. For instance, when I left Yahoo! in 2006 to return to Kingsoft for the second time, I sacrificed an opportunity for them to assist with my residency registration. At that juncture, staying a month longer could have secured my Beijing hukou, but I changed jobs. All transitions are similar—new employers expect swift onboarding, requiring tough choices. I prioritized my career over the seemingly valuable Beijing hukou.

Selecting the right moment often defies planning. For instance, moving from Kingsoft to Vancl and then to entrepreneurship, despite personal future plans, precise timing eludes prediction. Like the e-commerce wave, next year might determine the tripartite division of the industry or a similar scenario. Delaying action till next year could eliminate opportunities, necessitating immediate action this year. Despite having an eight-month-old child, missing precious time together, only playing with him in the mornings, sacrifices are inevitable. This is my choice.

Host: We hope that next year, when we reconnect with Xiaohui, Chuke continues to thrive and performs even better. Thank you all for following this episode of the e-commerce series interviews, and look forward to our next guest. Thank you!
