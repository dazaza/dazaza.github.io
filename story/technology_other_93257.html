I can't help but tell the stories of my two female senior colleagues in foreign trade.

by yilbi on 2008-09-19 14:27:29

Right now, I'm in an extremely bad mood and can't focus on work. I've even thought about resigning. Here's what happened: the day before yesterday, I bought an HP laptop at Yongle Home Appliances. However, yesterday morning as soon as I got to work, my boss called me into his office and said that I was not allowed to bring my personal laptop into the company. He immediately instructed the IT administrator to issue a so-called computer usage policy notice, one of which stipulated that private computers were not allowed in the company.

Looking back one and a half years ago when I first joined this company, I didn't even have a dedicated computer for myself, while several order-following ladies who entered the company at the same time as me already had their own computers. Every day, I had to run to the computer connected to the scanner to send emails and develop clients. It was very inconvenient. Occasionally, Xiao Fang would need to use the computer to scan some samples, or Xiao Yan would come over to scan some files. The most frustrating thing was when I was chatting with a client enthusiastically, suddenly a colleague would rush over and say that there was an urgent document to be scanned and needed to be sent to the client right away. At that time, I felt like I was the lowest person in the office hierarchy, and perhaps others thought the same way: even an ordinary clerk has a dedicated computer, but I, who is engaged in foreign trade business, didn't have one. What's even more laughable is that a few times my boss actually said that I disappeared somewhere during working hours because sometimes no one answered my extension call. My goodness, if I sat at this desk with only a phone all day, what could I possibly accomplish for him?

I am a competitive person; I want to change my boss's and colleagues' perception of me and show them some achievements. Although the company didn't assign any customers to me for development, my two female predecessors, who have been here two to three years longer than me, one graduated from Guangdong University of Foreign Studies with an English proficiency level eight, and the other graduated from a university in Hunan with an English proficiency level six. As for me, I studied food science and engineering, aimlessly wasted four years in college without mastering my major, never took the English exam during those years, and spent my days chasing girls and drinking alcohol. Back then, I thought that after graduation, my dad would take care of everything, so finding a job wouldn't be a problem since my father was a socially prominent figure in our county with a wide circle of friends. Later... later... I won't go into details, but after graduation, I had to rely on myself to find a job. Back then, my family still had some money, so my parents bought me a Guangzhou household registration, which was a collective registration under the provincial talent exchange center, thinking it would make it easier for me to find a job in Guangzhou. Little did they know that I had no skills whatsoever. At that time, I really didn't even know the basic functions of Word and Excel. Although my father had bought me a computer in college, I barely used it for less than two months before giving it to some girls I liked. Needless to say, I faced countless rejections while job hunting, and if I wasn't fired before the end of the probation period, it was rare. I don't know how many jobs I've changed, leaving footprints of my job search all over Guangdong province. I even obtained a Hong Kong-Macao pass and worked illegally in Hong Kong and Macao for a while. One night, while sleeping on the floor of the basement of the Macao International Center, I was questioned by the Macao Public Security Police along with a group of gamblers who lost their bets. After being detained overnight, we were released but warned not to sleep there at night anymore. With the high accommodation fees in Macao and my boss not providing lodging, the money I earned wasn't enough to cover the accommodation fees, so I returned to the mainland. Oh, I've strayed too far off-topic. If I continue like this, it will never end. Let's talk about our foreign trade life and feelings instead.

Speaking of my two female predecessors with English proficiency levels six and eight, they were much luckier than me. When they joined the company, the boss bought them laptops, and all of his overseas old clients were assigned to them, along with hundreds of customer records. The boss doesn't understand English, I don't know where he got these customers and customer information from, but I heard it was through some English-speaking friends of his. The boss was counting on these two predecessors of mine to secure more clients; they are the first batch of foreign trade talents in our company!

Oh, it's already 7:30, time to sleep and then go to work.

Just got home after work, saw that forum friends were quite interested in my story, so let's skip dinner and continue.

Because I was new to the company and just starting out in the foreign trade industry, I was completely unfamiliar with the products. It made sense that the company didn't assign me any customers to follow up on, but why they didn't give me a dedicated computer, I couldn't quite understand. But at that time, I was still very grateful to the company because I was almost penniless, and if I couldn't find a job, I wouldn't know where to wander on the streets. I remember that the person who interviewed me was our office director, also the boss's fellow townsman. He looked at my resume, asked a few simple questions, and then said when I could start work, without even testing my English. Actually, he didn't understand English either. At that time, the only people in the entire company who understood English were my two female predecessors, but they were busy with something else that day, so I didn't have to go through their tests. Interviews in family businesses are simple; as long as you pass the boss or someone related to the boss, you're good to go.

On the first day of work, my level-eight predecessor, Miss Lisa, asked me what level of English I had passed. I said I had passed level six, so she took out an invitation letter from the Shanghai Underwear Exhibition and asked me to translate it into Chinese, then take it to the boss. She had other things to do. It took me the whole morning to finish translating that invitation letter. During that time, Miss Lisa came over a few times to urge me, asking why I hadn't finished yet. I was sweating profusely, even though the air conditioning was on in the office. I was really afraid that if she found out my actual English level, she might kick me out that day. Finally, I managed to finish it, handed it to her, and she glanced at it briefly, saying, "You take it to the boss and see if he decides to attend that exhibition or not." Since then, for several consecutive days, my excitement of finding a job turned into depression. I was afraid that Miss Lisa would hand me some documents to translate or try to speak English with me...
