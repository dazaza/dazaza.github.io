Businessman A is in the stone mining business in the mountains. He specializes in mining large stones that are sturdy and neat. Mining such stones is very labor-intensive, but they are suitable for use as construction materials. There are many quarries locally, and the competition is fierce. Only high-quality stone materials and relatively low prices can ensure that the stones don't accumulate unsold. Businessman B, on the other hand, doesn't bother with these labor-intensive stones. Instead, he looks for the fragile and oddly-shaped stones discarded by Businessman A, which cannot be used in construction. After simple carving, he sells them as stone carvings at the market. The price of one stone carving can match that of a whole cartload of stone materials. An even smarter businessman, C, buys large quantities of Businessman B's stone carvings, adds beautiful decoration and packaging, and exports them overseas, increasing the price by several dozen times.

The above story reminds us to examine ourselves: Are we selling the price of goods or their value? Businessman A mines stone material, focusing on its most basic value when selling products. To make sales, he sacrifices profit, selling only on price. This not only makes his work exhausting but also earns him less money while facing significant competitive pressure. Businessman B, however, creates more and higher value from the stone material, easily earning more money. Businessman C further digs deeper into the value of the stone material, achieving even richer profits. Today, companies often say that business is getting harder to do, aggressively promoting and slashing prices, leading to thinner profits and even fiercer competition. In fact, by changing perspectives and avoiding price issues, focusing instead on the value of goods, businesses can cleverly escape the red ocean.

Understanding the Essence of Business

Market transactions are like a balance scale. Before consumers decide to purchase and place goods on the actual scale, they mentally weigh the transaction on an invisible scale. On each side of this mental scale are the consumer's purchasing cost and the perceived value of the purchase. When the consumer's purchasing cost side becomes heavier or larger, it's difficult to complete the transaction. However, when the scale tilts toward the consumer's perceived value, the transaction can proceed smoothly. In the market, more transactions occur when the scales are balanced. Thus, increasing the weight on the consumer's perceived value side is the best way to promote transactions. However, adding weight to the consumer's perceived value scale increases the cost of the product. Is there a way to increase the consumer's perceived value without increasing the product's cost? The answer is yes. The value of a product is not entirely determined by its physical attributes but more so by the psychological factors of the consumer. Regardless of the actual value of the product, the key lies in the consumer's perception of the product's value, i.e., how much the consumer is willing to pay.

When the physical attribute value of a product cannot be improved, we can increase the psychological value the customer perceives about the product, tilting the transaction scale toward the consumer's perceived value, thereby improving the closing rate. There are many ways to enhance the psychological value of a product, and different industries need to explore based on industry characteristics and their own situations. Below is a method that is generally applicable in the market.

1. Building Product and Brand Culture

Culture is the soul of a product and a brand. Without cultural essence, a product can only sell based on its physical value. Conversely, culture can create value several times or even dozens of times beyond the product's cost. Take the Ruyun torch as an example; it is said that each Ruyun torch sold for 10,000 to 20,000 yuan after being passed. Is it rare material? No, quite common. The Ruyun torch is made from ordinary metal materials and eco-friendly plastics. Is it scarce in quantity? No, there are as many torches as there are famous torchbearers, and non-limited edition torches exceed 100,000 units. So why is it so valuable? Because the torch carries culture and the spirit of sports. Without these, purely from a cost perspective, it might only be worth a few yuan. Brands like Starbucks, Nike, and Chanel earn several times the premium over other brands through their brand and product culture.

A bottle of mineral water priced at several dozen yuan – would you frown? Because even a famous brand like Wahaha costs no more than 1.5 yuan per bottle. Imported mineral water costs only 3-5 yuan. But Evian mineral water is an exception, mainly due to its rich and appealing brand culture: Evian mineral water comes from the heart of the Alps, where glacier meltwater and mountain rainwater gather year-round. After natural filtration for 15 years and mineralization through glacial sand layers, it forms Evian water... In 1789, a French nobleman suffered from kidney stones. One day, while strolling near a nearby town, he accidentally drank local spring water, finding it sweet and smooth. After some time, he was amazed to find his illness miraculously cured. This news quickly spread, prompting experts to analyze and confirm Evian water's therapeutic effects. Since then, large numbers of people have flocked to Evian Town to experience the wonders of Evian water, and doctors have included it in prescriptions. Napoleon III and his queen were particularly fond of Evian Town's mineral water. In 1864, they officially named the area Evian Town, and Evian mineral water subsequently reached worldwide. How does this sound? After hearing this story, drinking Evian mineral water feels different, doesn't it? And the higher price becomes acceptable. This is the power of culture, and the best way to express the culture of a product or brand is through compelling brand stories that consumers can easily accept and remember.

2. Enhancing Product Class

Many products have additional functions beyond their physical attributes and values, especially luxury items or expensive products like cars, jewelry, watches, etc. These are no longer just modes of transportation, decorations, or timekeeping devices; they also serve to express individuality and signify social status. For such products, emphasizing class is crucial. Rolls-Royce once highlighted class and value through its high price. Not only were the prices steep, but there were strict requirements for buyers' identities. Customers who didn’t meet these criteria wouldn’t be sold a car, even if they offered double the price. This effectively controlled the number of owners, making the product a high-class luxury item accessible to only a select few. This strategy of enhancing product value by raising prices and purchase thresholds not only earned substantial profits but also made consumers eager to buy. It’s evident that value enhancement doesn’t necessarily require lowering prices; elevating the product's class is an effective way to increase value. There are many ways to enhance class, but the key is to combine them with industry and product characteristics. Here are three aspects to consider:

1. Improve product quality – robust product quality is one of the fundamental factors supporting class. Even if a pipe has an elegant design, if it emits an unpleasant odor after just a few uses, it’s hard to associate it with high class.

2. Strengthen appearance – this is a key component in shaping product class. Not only should the design be aesthetically pleasing, but craftsmanship must also be meticulous and refined, something that class-conscious consumers care deeply about.

3. Class can be linked to personality – because everyone wants to avoid being labeled as conformist, personalized products often appeal to those sensitive to class and fashion.

3. Infusing Products with Emotion

When a product has emotional elements, it can break free from the constraints of price, allowing emotion to add value. For instance, giving a product a friendly nickname or imbuing it with emotional significance. Diamonds’ chemical composition is carbon, and diamonds were originally called “stone carbon.” Such names couldn’t possibly connect with their rarity and nobility, nor would any woman willingly wear a piece of "carbon" out in public. Later, when this mineral was commercialized, it was given a somewhat pleasant name – diamond. However, this name was too masculine and rugged, while the wearers were mostly women who wanted elegance and sophistication. Eventually, the gem became known as a diamond, a rational and beautiful name that women liked and accepted. Marketers further capitalized on this rational aspect, creating classic advertising slogans like “A Diamond is Forever.”

"Cold blue, like a woman's purity and self-restraint; mysterious purple, like a woman's subtlety and nobility; passionate red, like a woman's sincerity and hope in emotions. These colors reflect the changeable nature of women."

"A glance upward, a fleeting look back, a flash of unexpected beauty catches your eye, making you the center of attention, a landscape admired by all. This beauty, swaying gracefully, highlights your infinite charm and natural grace..." This excerpt is from promotional soft copy written by the author for a four-color bracelet. By linking the color of the jewelry with the emotional psyche of women, it resonates with female consumers, painting a vivid picture of what wearing the bracelet could feel like, stimulating positive imagination, and guiding these thoughts to freely disperse in the consumer's mind.

Charity sales can endure and still command good prices because they carry an emotional tone. Consumers aren’t simply buying the product; they’re expressing an emotion. A bookmark made from "yearning grass," costing only a few cents, can be sold for tens of yuan under the influence of a touching and beautiful story. The reason behind the purchase isn't the beauty of the bookmark itself but the emotion and life it represents.

4. Clever Terminal Design

After infusing products with culture, class, and emotional content, the next step is to express and convey these elements to consumers. The first step is to shape the terminal environment. The value of a product changes depending on where it’s placed. For example, the same piece of clothing can be sold for 50 yuan at a street stall, but in a boutique, it can fetch 500 yuan. Both places can sell the clothing, but why? The former emphasizes cheapness, while the latter enhances the product's value through ambiance.

The same coffee can taste and feel different, do you believe it? Romantic French seaside coffee, relaxed and trendy Starbucks coffee, serene home afternoon tea coffee... The same ingredients can produce various flavors and feelings entirely due to the emotional impact of the setting. If interested, try a life experiment: On a quiet afternoon, recline in a lounge chair, flipping through a magazine while sipping a cup of coffee. Remember this taste and feeling. Then switch the setting to a bustling nightclub, ordering the same cup of coffee, and see if it feels different from drinking it leisurely at home.

Appropriate terminal arrangements can effectively enhance product value. Therefore, terminals should be designed according to product characteristics, but beware of simply designing them to be luxurious, thinking that will enhance the product's value. Doing so may cause a disconnect between the product and the environment. Only when the product and the environment are seamlessly integrated can the environment enhance the product's value.

5. Selling Value

Effective sales training for sales personnel is very important because, no matter how well advertisements and channels perform, they can only count as excellent passes in a game. The final shot must rely on terminal sales personnel to achieve, requiring them to convey the product's value to consumers. Thus, emphasizing the training of sales personnel is also a critical link in increasing product value.

Avoid arguing with customers about whether the product is worth a certain amount of money. Once caught up in price disputes, it will be hard to sell at a good price, or even close a deal. At this point, guide the customer's thinking, discussing the value of the product together, ignoring the price factor. Especially for non-essential items like jewelry or even luxuries, the price factor is secondary; buyers focus more on the product's value. For example, if a lady complains that a necklace is too expensive, and the salesperson says, "Madam, 6,000 yuan is already very cheap. You won't find a cheaper necklace in any jewelry store..."—it fails. Selling the product solely on price negates its value. At this moment, the consumer's psychology is conflicted: she hopes the necklace is cheap but also valuable. The salesperson denies the necklace's value, telling her it's the cheapest jewelry, which would be devastating for luxury items, significantly reducing the chances of closing the sale. If the salesperson says, "Madam, this necklace suits you perfectly. It looks worth at least 10,000 yuan on you, and it matches your elegant and beautiful气质." The chances of closing the sale will greatly increase because the salesperson is talking about the value created when the necklace complements the lady, which is what she cares about most.

6. Smart Discounts

For most products, discounting can be a source of trouble. If discounted, it violates the value principle, not only losing considerable profit but also damaging the value of the product and the brand. Without discounts, it's hard to make consumers feel they've gained something psychologically. At this point, to protect the product's value and profit, we can adopt a "sacrifice pawn to save king" approach: keep the main product's price intact but offer small gifts. For example, a certain brand of computer maintained its price in the face of an industry-wide discount trend but offered gifts like USB drives, mice, data cables, speakers, DVD recordable discs, and computer covers. Consumers initially perceive that collectively, these gifts make the computer cheaper compared to others, while the firm pricing boosts confidence in the product's quality. In reality, the total value of these gifts is far lower than the increased price of the computer. This way, the brand image is preserved, and profits are maximized.

Suppose someone comes to buy a Mercedes-Benz car, and the salesperson tells the customer it can be bought at 50% off. The customer is likely to walk away because those buying Mercedes-Benz aren't looking for just a mode of transportation but a symbol of status and value. Telling them it's 50% off implies the car can no longer represent status. However, if you tell the customer that buying a Mercedes-Benz gets them a high-end wristwatch, a designer lighter, and 1 million yuan in car insurance, they'll gladly accept it. This satisfies the human tendency to want a bargain in every transaction while preserving the product's value. Therefore, when discounts are necessary, they should be achieved through gift offerings, extended product warranties, home delivery, bundled sales, etc., adding value to the transaction rather than reducing prices.
