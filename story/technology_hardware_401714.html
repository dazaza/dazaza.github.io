Movie review: "The Temptation of a Happy Home"

by shyshy7k on 2011-04-01 19:22:01

The South Korean TV drama "The Wife's Temptation" became extremely popular in Korea, causing office workers to rush home immediately after work to catch up with the show. This resulted in some hotels in Seoul being deserted, and people jokingly referred to it as "The Temptation of Going Home." The Chinese adaptation of "The Wife's Temptation" became "The Temptation of Returning Home," which premiered grandly on Hunan TV, quickly attracting countless viewers and setting new records for viewership.

After all, this is just a popular romance drama that can be summarized in one sentence: the original wife uses the same tactics as the mistress to take revenge on her, staging a drama of vengeance by the original spouse. In the end, the mistress cries in her BMW while the original wife laughs on her bicycle.

"The Temptation of Returning Home" was cloned from the South Korean drama, taking what shouldn't have been taken and leaving out what should have been kept. All the clichéd plot twists that have been perfected in South Korean dramas are present here: extramarital affairs, love triangles, terminal illnesses, shotgun weddings, having children before marriage; furthermore, the music, costumes, and scenes are almost identical to the original. However, while borrowing the surface elements, it lost the essence. The reason why "The Wife's Temptation" was so successful in Korea was because it revealed the intense impact of modern Western thought and culture on traditional ethics. Even wives with traditional virtues were becoming less traditional, so who could still hold onto the last bit of traditional cultural purity? The result of the rampant materialism is the moral disorder and ethical chaos. In an era where mistresses are prevalent, Confucian spirit has vanished. Some South Korean artists struggle in the gap between modernity and tradition, focusing on the pain caused by the gradual loss of traditional ethics.

However, the Chinese knock-off version of "The Temptation of Returning Home" lacks the depth of the original. We only plagiarized the tearful entanglements of romantic love and hysterical love declarations, and focused heavily on the phenomenon of mistresses, exaggerating and promoting it excessively.

Of course, most importantly, we couldn't resist the temptation of South Korean dramas, or the allure of mistresses: Cinderella marrying into wealth, mistresses becoming official, disgraced virtuous wives turning into mistresses, and Ling Xiaosu's real-life and on-screen marital issues—whether true or false. Before you know it, you're connecting the dots, scrutinizing Ling Xiaosu to find traces of injustice for Cui Ping. This drama tells you how to keep a mistress, how to be a mistress, and how to prevent your spouse from having one, so both men and women secretly smile after watching, keeping these lessons in mind. Truth becomes fiction when the fiction is real, and nothingness turns into something when there's nothing. Ai Li became a mistress out of love but ended up trapped by it, while Pin Ru sought revenge as a mistress but ultimately let go of her hatred.

In the face of chaotic marital issues, even the Supreme People's Court stepped in to issue several interpretations. Celebrities also weighed in on marriage issues, talking about things like "If You Are the One" and "Carrying Love to the End," but ultimately no one could clearly define what love really is. Still, the "Marriage Law" put it well: Article 2, implementing a marriage system of freedom, monogamy, and gender equality. Article 4, spouses should be faithful and respectful to each other; family members should respect the elderly and care for the young, helping each other to maintain an equal, harmonious, and civilized marital and family relationship.
