Recently, the new National Space Policy signed by US President Bush has become a focal point of attention. The policy clearly indicates that the US will exclude "hostile forces" from entering space in order to fully control outer space.

Is the new space policy a replica of the "Star Wars" program?

According to American media reports, the new National Space Policy document was secretly signed by President Bush in August this year and was only recently exposed by the media. The UK's New Scientist commented that compared to Clinton's space policy 10 years ago, the new space policy has undergone significant changes, approaching Reagan-era "Star Wars" plans, with distinct unilateral and militaristic characteristics:

Firstly, the new policy shifts focus from development to security. Steve Aftergood, an expert at the Washington-based arms control think tank, the Federation of American Scientists, said that the new space policy clearly contains more content related to national security, indicating that maintaining national security has become the basic starting point for the US's new space policy.

Secondly, the new policy shows that the US aims to dominate outer space alone. The new space policy document strongly states: "If necessary, the US has the right to prevent any country or individual 'hostile to American interests' from entering space."

Thirdly, the new policy hints at the deployment of space weapons. The new National Space Policy requires the Secretary of Defense to provide "space capabilities" to support missile warning systems and develop "multi-layered and integrated missile defense capabilities," which clearly implies granting the military the authority to deploy relevant equipment in space.

Rumsfeld is the instigator of space warfare

American media revealed that during the creation of the new space policy, the Pentagon, led by Rumsfeld, played an important role. US Defense Secretary Donald Rumsfeld is a representative figure of American neoconservatism and also a strong proponent of American space warfare theory and practice. In January 2001, upon taking office, Rumsfeld suggested strengthening America's space capabilities and deploying space weapon systems; in 2002, Bush announced America's withdrawal from the Anti-Ballistic Missile Treaty based on his advice. In March 2005, Rumsfeld signed the new Defense Strategy, pointing out that "space control" is the ability to "ensure freedom of our own space operations while preventing opponents from having such freedom," further clarifying the direction of future space militarization.
