The mobile battle has become the key, Microsoft, you need to wake up!

by anonymous on 2013-09-18 21:35:29

2013 was a year when Microsoft strove to assert its presence. First came the departmental restructuring, then the announcement that CEO Steve Ballmer would soon retire, followed by the rapid acquisition of Nokia's mobile phone business. These moves seemed to be a strong response to the media that had long mocked and belittled the company: criticize me if you dare! We, Microsoft, will rise from where we fell!

As Microsoft entered the internet era, it faced repeated defeats. From Hotmail's expansion to Outlook.com in the email battle, from Live.com to Bing in the search engine competition, and from MSN to Skype in the instant messaging wars, the only thing that brought consumers joy was Ballmer’s flamboyant performances. Let's get serious now; this is a critical moment for Microsoft's survival! The mobile campaign carries the hopes of Microsoft and hundreds of millions of its fans and believers. How should it proceed? Let's start with three major events of the year.

The departmental restructuring seems somewhat redundant.

Departmental restructuring means a change in power dynamics. Microsoft consolidated its eight product departments into four major ones. Although this move simplified the structure and integrated resources, the restructuring increasingly revealed its bureaucratic characteristics. The shift from a business-oriented organization to a function-oriented one concentrated power in the hands of four executives, clearly heralding the post-Ballmer era. In other words, among these new executives, some may become the next all-powerful Ballmer. Microsoft will enter another round of power struggles, which obviously does not help infuse fresh blood into the company and once again dashes the hope for innovation.

Even so, as long as the restructuring achieves what Ballmer hoped for—well implementing the "devices and services" strategy—then such a vertical organization deserves praise. After all, Microsoft lost its way on its once-dominant path, and sweeping reforms are too difficult for the current Microsoft.

Ballmer just stood in the wrong position.

"As long as Ballmer is still at the helm, Microsoft won't improve," Jobs once said. Despite industry media turning their evaluations of Ballmer into ridicule, even Wall Street capital markets mocking his impending departure with a 7% surge in Microsoft's stock price, you underestimate Brother Ballmer too much. He has weathered many storms in the world of fame and fortune and is not someone who gets easily angered.

In fact, Ballmer is highly intelligent, a once-in-a-century talent who simply stood in the wrong position and was overly indulged by his good friend Gates. During Ballmer's 13 years in office, Microsoft's performance did not decline but slowly improved along a smooth curve, tripling the company's revenue and doubling its profits. This sufficiently proves he is an outstanding operator. Meanwhile, Empress Dowager Gates, who reigned behind the scenes during Ballmer's administration, did not make timely judgments based on market changes but continued to focus on her charity work.

Therefore, the blogger believes that even if Gates returns after Ballmer retires, he cannot reverse Microsoft's previous setbacks. Perhaps Gates never imagined that once reaching the peak of his career, courageously stepping back to quietly engage in charity, he would now be pushed back to the forefront by the times.

Microsoft remains the same old Microsoft, but the era is no longer the same. Now, facing the world's cold mockery, Brother Ballmer wants to shout loudly: "It's not my fault, it's the world!" (Gates tearfully told Brother Ballmer: "Dear, your retirement brings relief, but now the whole world is watching me again, I feel immense pressure...")

Acquiring Nokia was necessary.

Acquiring Nokia's mobile phone business was a necessary step in Microsoft's mobile campaign. Why necessary? Nokia has exquisite craftsmanship design, a large number of patents, a good brand effect, and rich experience in mobile device manufacturing—all essential for Microsoft to succeed in the smart mobile campaign. Moreover, Nokia's refusal of Android's invitation two years ago, its insistence on independent strategies, and its collaboration with Microsoft using the WP system have gradually put itself in a difficult position. Now, apart from entrusting itself to Microsoft, there is no other way out. Although there is a Trojan horse element in Microsoft's strategy, it feels almost predestined.

Microsoft's acquisition of Nokia's mobile phone business, to some extent, mirrors Google's acquisition of Motorola, achieving the "software-hardware" integration goal to catch up with the iPhone. However, Microsoft's mobile strategy is bound to be different from Apple's or Google's—it is more "open" than Apple yet more "closed" than Google, adopting an "open but not open-source" strategy, consistent with Microsoft's tradition. Its effectiveness remains to be tested by time and the market.

The battle is imminent; Microsoft needs to wake up.

Now, the industry has formed a tripod, with the mobile war looming ahead. Facing threats from Apple and Google, what strategy suits Microsoft's current layout? Microsoft has been adhering to a strategy centered around the Windows family, intending to unify the handheld, tablet, and PC platforms to dominate the market. This idea is no longer viable and has even disturbed the originally peaceful PC industry, exhausting downstream partners.

Actually, WP is a pretty good system. It draws on the essence of the traditional Windows desktop platform, operates smoothly, offers a good user experience, and has a certain degree of innovation. Compared to iOS and Android, it just entered the market too late, lacks a complete ecosystem, lacks continuous innovation on the platform, and lacks appeal to developers. All these can be reclaimed through Microsoft's advantages (financial resources, human resources, professional foundation).

In fact, Microsoft is capable, lacking only a reasonable and correct strategic direction. Several industry analysts have suggested that Microsoft should treat Windows and WP as two separate strategies, reactivating an independent WP department, fully building the WP ecosystem, and focusing on cultivating WP's core value in the mobile field. The blogger finds this view more reliable than the grand unification concept, after all, WP's current presence is far too low.
