How to Go from Zero to Billions: A Brief History of Twitter's Evolution

by anonymous on 2013-09-24 20:42:37

Twitter's current CEO Dick Costolo

In the Christmas Eve of 2009, many employees at Twitter received an unusual holiday greeting from then-Chief Operating Officer (COO) Dick Costolo.

Anamitra Banerji, who was then in charge of Twitter’s first ad product team, recalled: "We all received an email from Costolo saying 'we must accelerate our development. We don't have time to rest because there are countless opportunities for growth ahead of us.' We were all on vacation at that time, so everyone felt that Costolo saying this was a bit crazy. But Twitter did indeed need this kind of aggressive attitude back then."

Since Facebook went public in May last year, Twitter has become the next anticipated tech company to go public. Now, Twitter has secretly submitted its IPO application to the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission, getting closer to the goal envisioned by Costolo at that time. The valuation of Twitter upon going public is expected to reach up to $15 billion, instantly creating a large number of millionaires including Costolo himself.

Costolo's Efforts

During the process of developing from a disorganized, loss-making startup into a digital media leader, Twitter's rapid growth is deeply imbued with Costolo's personal hard work. Of course, the swift development of various products and decisive and effective personnel decisions made by the management also played a very important role.

Twitter was founded in 2006, and Costolo joined the company three years later in 2009. However, judging from the sudden acceleration of the IPO application process, Costolo's influence on Twitter is becoming increasingly profound: meticulous decision-making, resolute execution, wise public relations, and focused financial results.

Costolo executed resolutely in cutting and reorganizing the management team. At that time, Twitter was ruthlessly stripping away small enterprises that once belonged to the company. Before founding several startups, Costolo had been a comedian, and his experience working at Andersen Consulting was not successful. Therefore, Costolo's relationship with Twitter cannot be compared with Mark Zuckerberg's relationship with Facebook. However, his importance is no less than Zuckerberg's.

However, Bijan Sabet, an investor at Spark Capital, pointed out: "The founders all see Dick as a co-founder, which shows how deep their relationship is. He is not a traditional professional manager; he not only knows how to run a business but also understands products, strategies, and visions." Sabet served as a director of Twitter between 2008 and 2011.

Twitter refused to let Costolo respond, citing the quiet period before the IPO.

Born Promoted Tweet

Twitter founder Evan Williams

In September 2009, when Evan Williams, then CEO of Twitter, recruited his old friend and former Google colleague Costolo, the three-year-old company was already under tremendous pressure.

At that time, Twitter was attracting young users' attention at an unprecedented rate, and its three co-founders—Williams, Biz Stone, and Jack Dorsey—were frequently featured on the covers of famous American business and technology magazines, becoming representatives of San Francisco's cool image. However, doubts about the company within Silicon Valley were increasing. They pointed out that Twitter lacked sufficient technical strength to support its massive scale and had no profit channels.

Alex Payne, one of Twitter's early engineers, said: "As members of the core engineering team in the early days, we indeed lacked the technology to build a world-class service." He also noticed that many team members came from smaller startups or non-profit organizations, rather than large Internet companies like Google.

Williams believed that solving the technical problems that gave Twitter a bad reputation was urgent, but he was also hesitant about the company's business strategy. According to sources, Williams spent several months evaluating various options, from display ads to data licensing, from e-commerce platforms to offering paid "business" accounts to enterprises.

Costolo had previously founded Feedburner, a blog publishing service that generated revenue from advertising, which was sold to Google for $100 million. Due to this background, he seemed less hesitant than Williams. In his second month at Twitter, he convinced Williams to allow the engineering team to establish Twitter's first ad department, which evolved into the Promoted Tweet service. This became the prototype of Twitter's current business model.

Banerji, now a partner at Foundation Capital, said: "Dick's conversation with Evan was crucial. He firmly believed that this was an important path for Twitter's future commercialization. He said, 'You must do it.'"

In the four months leading up to early 2010, Costolo worked with Banerji and Stanford University professor Ashish Goel, who was skilled in auction algorithms, to improve Promoted Tweet. This content is similar to regular Twitter messages, except that when advertisers pay for this information, it will appear at the top of user feeds and search results.

Industry Breakthrough

Costolo established a reputation within Twitter through his advertising strategy. In early 2010, when the advertising team drafted a product called Promoted Trends, Costolo privately told team members that they should only present this product to Williams when he was present, so that it could be smoothly approved.

The core management mechanism of Promoted Tweet proposed by Stanford University professor Goel is so-called "resonance." Since Twitter users can retweet or reply to others' posts, including paid advertisements, the company can understand in real-time which ads are most popular, and these ads will subsequently be promoted more heavily. Since ads have the same format as other Twitter messages, they are well-suited for mobile devices, where traditional display ads cannot play smoothly.

Afterwards, inserting paid ads into status update streams became an industry standard for mobile advertising. Twitter's competitor Facebook adopted this model, and due to its success in mobile business, its stock price has risen by 60% in recent months.

Charlene Li, founder of online research and consulting company Altimeter Group, said: "Previously, the closest thing was Google's text-related ads, but the problem was that they were clearly marked as ads." Promoted Tweets are exactly the same as ordinary Twitter messages. This way of displaying in the feed is a breakthrough."

When Costolo launched Promoted Tweet in April 2010, Twitter announced that it would conduct pilot tests with only five brands, including Starbucks and Virgin America, and users almost never saw ads on this service.

Entering the Television Advertising Market

Adam Bain led Twitter's advertising team into the television market

By the summer of 2010, Costolo had gained enough confidence in this concept, so he began looking for assistants to help Twitter increase its sales efforts. In the following months, he recruited star executive Adam Bain from News Corporation and began attracting marketers on Madison Avenue and at industry conferences.

Under Bain's leadership, Twitter's advertising team even entered the highly profitable television advertising market. Twitter positioned itself as an online chat room for TV viewers, allowing them to discuss the plot while watching TV, thus attracting the attention of TV producers and major brand marketers.

"Hashtags," a feature designed to help users find relevant topics, quickly became popular among TV viewers and even appeared in Super Bowl commercials, NASCAR races, and live broadcasts of the Oscar red carpet.

Ian Schafer, CEO of digital advertising agency Deep Focus, said: "Before selling itself as a TV partner, Twitter struggled to convince people to buy its ad space. Now, you can even see ad networks selling Twitter's ad space. It's amazing."

Twitter has improved its ad targeting capabilities multiple times, and it can now display Promoted Tweets based on geographical location and interests. This month, the company acquired MoPub for $300 million, enabling it to deliver precise mobile ads based on users' browsing history on desktop computers.

With Promoted Tweet becoming a reliable source of income, Twitter's advertising revenue is expected to reach $580 million this year, with a significant portion coming from Promoted Tweet. Twitter has gradually expanded its service scope, no longer limited to 140-character text messages. Embedded photos, videos, and page previews are now included in the content pushed by Twitter, and more interactive functions, even online transactions and shopping, may be added in the future.

Focus and Ruthlessness

Twitter COO Ali Rowghani

Under the leadership of the three founders, Twitter struggled to find a suitable management model. So Costolo became famous for introducing a stable management structure to the company. However, despite this, he did not hesitate to restructure the management team.

A former employee of Twitter said: "He wants to choose excellent talents for himself, but he is always ruthless when replacing executives."

Bain and prestigious Twitter COO Ali Rowghani have become Costolo's two key assistants. Recently, Twitter has carried out a series of high-profile recruitments, including hiring former TicketMaster CEO Nathan Hubbard as head of commerce, former Professional Bowlers Association CEO Geoff Reiss as head of sports partnerships, and former Morgan Stanley executive Cynthia Gaylor as head of corporate development.

Meanwhile, a group of once influential executives have left, including product head Satya Patel, vice president of engineering Mike Abbott, and head of business growth Othman Laraki. These executives revealed after leaving that the employee turnover rate at Twitter is much higher than normal levels.

In the eyes of grassroots employees, this CEO often entertains them during work hours, but making them work overtime is also commonplace. During a meeting last autumn, Costolo told the audience that he had found a new office space in downtown San Francisco because he hoped employees living in the city could go home to have dinner with their families after work and then return to continue working.

Although Costolo is charming in public, some employees believe that this CEO can also be quite cold privately. A former employee said: "He always appears enthusiastic, but if you want to have a deep conversation with him, he will weigh how much time he has to communicate with you because his schedule is packed, with many things to do from morning till night."

Costolo's single-minded focus on performance is not liked by everyone. Many early fans of Twitter liked this platform because they could express their views anytime and anywhere, and they appreciated its potential in politics, social interaction, and technology. But Twitter is now gradually alienating this part of its fan base.

IPO Accelerates Internationalization

Twitter has gradually cut off many third-party services accessing its data, instead using the data for commercial purposes. The company also prohibited some developers from creating new features that interact with the Twitter platform.

Although Twitter has long been seen as an advocate of defending freedom of speech and protecting users from government surveillance, this status seems to be questioned. Previously, Twitter ignored foreign governments' demands that it comply with local laws, but last year it developed a technology to block certain Twitter messages in specific countries, and recently deployed this technology in Germany and Brazil.

Jillian York, international freedom of expression director at the Electronic Frontier Foundation, an American civil rights organization, pointed out: "The most obvious effect of Twitter applying for an IPO is that it will speed up the company's internationalization process. I don't think there is enough evidence to show that Twitter's position on free speech in the United States has weakened, but it has indeed weakened in other countries. I think they definitely faced complex situations when applying to set up offices in other countries, and might have even made commitments that the company couldn't keep."

However, Costolo has kept his biggest promise: to turn Twitter into a large media company. From this perspective, the IPO may just be the beginning.
