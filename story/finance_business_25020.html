The Beauty of a Simple Business Model: Ubiquitous

by huahuaniu on 2007-07-17 20:34:21

Yesterday, Ubiquitous had dinner with a friend and once again experienced the power of a simple business model. This friend returned to China to start a business a few years ago, and in three years, grew his company from 1 million RMB to a scale of 30 million USD, successfully raising tens of millions of dollars in financing. So, what exactly does his company do? As this friend puts it, "Put a printer next to the cash register." Doesn't that sentence immediately spark your curiosity? That's right; this is the power that a simple business model possesses.

It turns out that this friend’s company mainly focuses on analyzing consumers' actual purchasing behaviors in supermarkets and then printing, distributing, and processing personalized discounts, promotions, and advertisements in real-time at the checkout counter to influence and change their next purchase behavior. For example, if a consumer buys diapers, they are given a coupon for formula milk on their next purchase. It's not hard to see that through this targeted marketing approach, a "triple-win" situation can be achieved for brand manufacturers, retailers, and consumers. Now, their marketing network covers 300 large retail hypermarkets nationwide, and they have established partnerships with many well-known brands.

Friends who have lived abroad might quickly associate this with similar checkout coupon printing services in large supermarkets in the U.S. and Europe. In fact, just like the "beer and diapers" case, this is another application of data mining technology in the retail sector. However, the innovation of this friend lies in providing a third-party large-scale targeted marketing medium, reducing communication costs between brands, retail institutions, and consumers. This business model is similar to Focus Media's advertising medium service but can better create value for consumers, making it more enduring. The innovation and first-mover advantage in their business model ensure their current success in the domestic retail market.

A good business model is simple, elegant, and hidden within daily life. Among all the people who have seen such coupon printing services in foreign supermarkets, this friend was the first to bring this model back to China and achieve a breakthrough in value creation. From this perspective, he fully meets the standard of an entrepreneur: "Seeing what everyone sees, thinking what no one else has thought, and making it happen." Another insight this case brings is the beauty of a simple business model.

For those interested in military matters, you may know that in the aerodynamic design of fighter jets, there is a simple principle: the shape of a fighter jet that looks aesthetically pleasing, harmonious, and comfortable is often the optimal design according to aerodynamics. This is because, whether in nature or human society, things that are beautiful and efficient are never complicated or distorted but rather simple and clear. This rule applies equally when deducing down to specific business models in enterprises. As investors often tell entrepreneurs, if you cannot explain your business model clearly in a 30-second elevator pitch, it means there is something wrong with the model because it lacks direct clarity.

From the perspective of an entrepreneur, a simple business model has several advantages. First, during the early stages of entrepreneurship, a simple business model makes it easier for investors to understand and judge its feasibility and market value. After all, investors are not industry experts, and for unclear, hard-to-understand business models, they generally hesitate to invest easily. Second, communicating complex business models to the market (including both consumer and capital markets) comes at a high cost. Baidu's historic success in listing on NASDAQ was partly due to its business model being explainable in just a few words as "China's Google," a classic example of low-cost capital market communication. On the other hand, Ants.com could perhaps serve as an example of high-cost consumer market communication. Third, complex business models are multi-faceted and much harder to operate. Not only might entrepreneurs develop illusions during execution, but even perfect execution without any mistakes cannot guarantee against unexpected events. Murphy's Law states, "Anything that can go wrong will go wrong." Tracy's Law suggests, "The difficulty of any task is proportional to the square of the number of steps involved." Unfortunately, these rules are repeatedly verified in various entrepreneurial activities.

Like any activity in nature or human society, the discovery of simple business models follows certain patterns. For instance, starting from the "selling water" pattern (pattern, different from the model in business model), we can construct countless simple and effective business models. If you have time, you can analyze the commonalities of currently successful simple business models. If someone could compile a set of design patterns for business models (business model, which should actually be translated as "commercial model"), it might reduce the chances of future entrepreneurs taking detours.
