Cook's Solution: Speculations on the Return of the Father of Apple Retail Stores

by geekzhang on 2013-05-03 19:08:06

Ron Johnson, the former senior vice president of Apple's retail business once known as the father of Apple stores, has recently resigned from J.C. Penney where he had worked for less than two years. Given that Apple, under Tim Cook's leadership, is going through its toughest time in the post-Steve Jobs era and that the position of senior vice president of retail is still vacant, it can't help but make one wonder: will this former Apple executive return to Apple to help Cook out of his predicament?

On October 21, 2010, Ron Johnson was at the soon-to-open latest Apple Store in Chicago, USA.

Ron Johnson (Ron Johnson) has left J.C. Penney.

In early April, CNBC reported this news to the public. This former senior vice president of Apple's retail division, who was called the father of Apple stores, left Apple a month after Steve Jobs' death in November 2011 to join J.C. Penney. He attempted to transplant his past successful experiences to this declining department store chain. However, in reality, revitalizing a faltering retail company proved far more difficult than creating an entirely new retail business.

Meanwhile, Apple under Tim Cook's leadership is experiencing its hardest time in the post-Jobs era.

From a high of $702 per share in September 2012, Apple's stock price plummeted straight down to $390 by April 19, 2013, marking a 52-week low.

Since Cook became CEO in October 2011, Apple's market value has decreased by nearly 50%.

In the past two weeks, the most popular rumor on Wall Street has been that Apple is secretly looking for someone to replace Cook.

Ron’s resignation happened right at this sensitive moment, though outsiders don’t think he would become Cook's successor. There are speculations that since the position of senior vice president of retail at Apple is still vacant, Ron might very well return to the role he held for over a decade.

From joining Apple in 2000 to leaving in 2011, Ron expanded Apple's retail business to over 300 cities worldwide, successfully crafting internationally renowned Apple Stores, making them the world's most profitable stores per square foot. His pioneering concept of the Genius Bar has now become the soul and cornerstone of Apple Stores.

In 2007, Jobs commented on Ron: "I can't remember what Apple was like without the stores. Thank you, Ron."

The driving force behind Apple's retail success

Ron's trick was turning Apple from a technology company into America's best retailer.

Around 2000, Jobs realized that Apple needed to enter the retail industry. He began searching for the right person to manage Apple's retail division. The final choice was Ron Johnson, then sales director at Target Corp., whose multiple successful schemes for Target left a deep impression on Jobs.

Ron's solution to Jobs was simple: design stores just like designing products.

The first prototype store was built in a warehouse near Apple's campus. “Ron and I have a brand-new store,” Jobs excitedly said at the time.

BusinessWeek promptly threw cold water on him: "Sorry, Steve, Apple's retail venture is doomed."

But Jobs fully supported Ron. Apple spent another nine months redesigning the retail store based on the prototype.

On May 19, 2001, Apple opened its first global retail store at Tysons Corner in Virginia. Only one-quarter of the space was used for selling products; the rest was divided by interest: the left side for photos, videos, and children's area, the right side for information services, and the back third zone—the Genius Bar—Ron's unique design.

"When we first opened the retail stores, I gathered people with different life experiences and asked them: tell us about the best service experience you've ever had," Ron recalled. Among eighteen people, sixteen mentioned hotels.

This answer was unexpected but reasonable: hotel concierges offer help without selling anything.

"Alright, let's open a store that provides comfort like a Four Seasons Hotel—a hotel inside a store—but instead of serving drinks, we'll provide advice," Ron said.

At that time, an industry insider predicted: "This will be a disastrous mistake. I estimate within two years they'll have to shut down."

However, five years later, one morning at four fifteen, a light turned on.

It was the milky white glow from the Apple logo hanging on a glass cube isolated at the corner of Central Park South and Fifth Avenue in Manhattan. This building stood nearly ten meters tall, completely transparent, dazzling. It was the newly designed appearance of the Apple Store, which was later adopted globally and became the iconic symbol of all Apple Stores.

Jobs said: "No one has ever invested so much time, money, and design into a store before."

Whether consumers know this or not doesn't matter; they can feel it. They will sense that there's something special here.

The specialized store integrating experience and retail became another innovation for Apple beyond its products. And the mastermind behind all this was none other than Ron.

One day in 2007, he walked into the San Francisco store. It was a perfect stainless steel box structure with a large skylight on the roof. Sunlight streamed in through the skylight, and a couple in their thirties were receiving guidance at the Genius Bar.

"Do you see? Look at their eyes, they're learning. This is a focused moment, just like kids studying hard in school," Ron said.

At that moment, a man carrying a damaged MacBook Air walked in.

"The possibility of fixing it today: one-third; tomorrow: two-thirds."

Leaning on the glass bridge spanning the second floor, Ron said, "We want it to be as fast as a dry cleaner."

Not far from the store was Tiffany & Co., whose annual sales per square foot were $2666. Audrey Hepburn liked to linger outside Tiffany's eating breakfast, but Apple was taking everyone's lunch—its annual sales per square foot had reached $4032.

On August 5, 2010, the new Apple Store in London's Covent Garden.

Cook's Answer?

Ten years later, Apple's retail stores not only stood firm but also expanded to over 300 in number, with Ron undoubtedly playing a significant role.

"At that time, I told Steve, since we didn't have enough products to fill the retail stores, why not use user experience to fill the entire store? So we quickly shifted from purchase experience to user experience, which gave birth to the Genius Bar, video rooms, face-to-face guidance, and friendly staff," Ron detailed the birth process of Apple Stores at a retail conference in 2005. Why did Apple Stores succeed? I believe the reason lies not only in our good ideas but also in our passion for paying attention to the smallest details.

One detail was the location selection for Apple Retail Stores. In the first two years after the store opened, Ron and his colleagues found through research that loyal Apple users typically drove 20 minutes to get to the specialty store, but if they weren't happy, they wouldn't spend 20 minutes driving over.

Ron decided to locate subsequent stores in shopping malls or bustling downtown areas to save customers travel time.

"This way, they'd enjoy visiting Apple Stores more often, with no loss anyway. Thus, we decided to set up our specialty stores in high-traffic areas, and it really worked." Jobs praised Ron's decision in an interview with Fortune magazine in March 2008.

And undoubtedly, Jobs at that time must have been secretly pleased with his decision many years ago.

Before joining Apple, Ron had worked at Target, a warehouse-style big-box retailer, for 15 years, serving as Vice President of Merchandising until being poached by Jobs in 2000 to come to Apple to establish the retail business.

Target was also Ron's first formal job. In 1984, shortly after graduating from Harvard Business School, Goldman Sachs Investment Company and American department store chain Mervyns extended offers to him.

Working at an investment bank was Ron's first choice: prestigious, high-paying, and immediate satisfaction. But his father, who worked at General Mills (a Fortune 500 company primarily engaged in food production), told him: "You can choose this, but sooner or later you'll want to switch jobs. If you want to become an expert in a certain field, you must start from the basics. Success has no shortcuts."

Ron decided to learn business operations and started as an apprentice manager in a store in Glendale until he joined Target a year later.

"Whether it's designing operations for Target or launching retail stores for Apple, I always follow the same principle: there are no shortcuts in anything." Ron stated when he left Apple, "I've experienced tough times at Apple too, but I couldn't truly empathize with Steve's pain. When you're in a leadership position, tough times may become even tougher."

Ron's words seem apt to describe Cook's current situation. After Jobs' passing, Apple's luster quietly dimmed; its sales and profit growth, as well as the strong rise in its stock, all reversed course. Under shareholders' close scrutiny, Cook's days aren't easy.

Forbes contributor Marcial believes that the quarterly financial report Apple just released might mean more to Cook than to investors.

On April 2, Apple released its Q2 2013 financial report as scheduled. The report showed that Apple's revenue for the second fiscal quarter was $43.603 billion, an increase of 11% compared to the same period last year; net profit was $9.547 billion, a decline of 18% compared to the same period last year. This is the first time in nearly ten years that Apple has seen a year-over-year decline in profits.

The report attributed this to factors such as the product line becoming increasingly monotonous, intensifying competition in the smartphone and tablet markets, and the sharp contraction of the PC market, causing Apple's market value to evaporate $285 billion in the past six months.

Given that Cook currently enjoys a 93% employee support rate, he may not be in immediate danger of losing his job; however, how to quickly improve Apple's performance and alleviate investor concerns remains Cook's top priority. Is Ron one of the answers?
