2012 Ultimate Guide to the Latest Bali Tourism Indonesia

Bali is an island with an area of about 5,560 square kilometers east of Java in Indonesia. Bali is a well-known tourist destination. Due to its tropical location and marine influence, the climate is mild and rainy, making the soil very fertile. The landscape features lush green mountains and rivers throughout the year, vibrant flowers, and towering trees. The Balinese people are naturally fond of flowers and use them to decorate everywhere, thus earning Bali the nickname "Island of Flowers." It also enjoys the reputation of being a "Paradise in the South Sea" and "The Island of Gods." Historically influenced by Indian culture and religion, most residents follow Hinduism, making Bali the only place in Indonesia that practices Hinduism. However, this form of Hinduism differs from that practiced in India as it combines Hindu doctrines with Balinese customs, known as Balinese Hinduism. There are more than 125,000 temples across Bali, hence the island's additional title of "The Island of a Thousand Temples." Among these temples, the most famous one is the thousand-year-old Besakih Temple, located on the slopes of Mount Agung, referred to as the "Navel of the World," specifically dedicated to the god of this intermittently erupting volcano. The temple's tiered stone carvings resemble those found at Angkor Wat in Cambodia.

Although I have traveled to many countries, my visit to Bali was quite breathtaking. One could say that Bali offers me a certain feeling, a pursuit of tranquility, undisturbed by anything. It's quite simple; Bali resembles a paradise outside the world, peaceful and simple. Or perhaps due to various reasons, I am filled with affection for this small island. For this reason, I have gathered information about Bali's attractions for friends planning to visit Bali as a reference.

1. Built at the beginning of the 11th century, Besakih Temple is the oldest and largest Hindu temple complex in Bali and serves as the headquarters of Balinese Hindu temples, called the "Mother Temple." Comprising over 30 temples, the number of tiers on the temple roofs signifies their importance, ranging from one to eleven tiers. The tallest main temple is dedicated to Shiva, while two other important temples are dedicated to Vishnu and Brahma. The temple's entrance features the unique Balinese "Gate of Good and Evil" (Yin-Yang Gate), a common architectural style seen throughout Bali, with the Mother Temple's gate particularly grand and majestic. Outside the temple gates stand 18 statues representing deities from the Hindu epic Mahabharata.

2. Ubud is a picturesque mountain town in the center of Bali surrounded by lush tropical vegetation and green rice paddies. Ubud is renowned as a painting hub and world-famous art village, as well as a center for handicrafts, art, dance, and cuisine in Bali.

3. Tegallalang Rice Terraces - The famous terraced landscapes of Bali are mainly concentrated around Tegallalang. Here, you will find familiar scenes from many postcards and photographs showcasing Bali's scenery. The coconut trees interspersed among the fields and the majestic volcanic backdrop make it distinct from countless other terraced fields worldwide. Remarkably, Bali harmoniously blends its tropical scenery with pastoral views.

4. Tanah Lot is one of the most important seaside temples in Bali, built in the 16th century. This temple sits atop a large rock by the sea, becoming isolated from the land when the tide rises, standing alone amidst the seawater. Only during low tide is it connected to the mainland. Across from the temple on the mainland lies a small pavilion where visitors can admire the sunset, one of Bali's scenic highlights. Along the road leading to the temple, there are numerous shops selling various souvenirs and food stalls offering grilled seafood, rice cakes, and more.

5. Kuta Beach is near the Bali airport and is the most famous water recreation spot on the island. Its wide, enchanting white sand beaches are accompanied by a dense cluster of stores, bars, restaurants, and shopping centers nearby, making it a paradise for those who don't like solitude. Kuta Beach isn't just a popular spot for sunbathing and water activities but also an excellent location to enjoy the sunset. Here, you can also experience massages provided by Indonesian women. Centered around Kuta Square, known as "Obligi Street," stands a new department store next to the square.

7. Sacred Monkey Forest Sanctuary (Monkey Forest) is located not far northwest of Ubud in central Bali. The park consists of a cardamom forest with lush, dense trees where local Balinese macaques reside. On the park's perimeter stands a temple inhabited by many monkeys. The Balinese people believe that these monkeys are followers of the divine monkey Hanuman and should be revered as deities, hence considered sacred monkeys. Allegedly, these monkeys used to frequently disturb homes, causing distress to local farmers. To resolve this issue, they built temples in Bali to attract tourists, who then feed the monkeys. This way, the local residents are spared from monkey disturbances, and they can profit by selling peanuts, corn, bananas, and other monkey feed to tourists, achieving a win-win situation.

8. Tirta Empul Temple is one of the famous temples on "The Island of a Thousand Temples," Bali. Its history dates back to 962 AD. It is said that the holy spring here remains perpetually clear and has curative properties, with different springs offering different therapeutic effects, attracting devout men and women from all over to worship and bathe. Nearby on a small hill stands a European-style building, which serves as the Indonesian President's retreat and has hosted visits from political leaders worldwide.
