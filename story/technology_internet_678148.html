You work your fingers to the bone but get no praise? You can't get a good review if you don't understand your boss's thoughts.

by qingian007 on 2012-02-27 12:00:29

It is not advisable to give up when you cannot produce any results or receive no positive feedback. Instead, you should take the initiative to communicate with your superior, actively report your progress, and share your ideas with them — this is the first step in overcoming difficulties. Of course, if you have produced no results and need to communicate with your superior, you might fear their criticism, but you must have the courage to overcome this hurdle.

He gradually neglected the simple communication he should have had with me. For both superiors and subordinates, this is the most important thing. In fact, the purpose of work is not merely about communication and reporting, but also about motivating employees to think independently and work with self-discipline.

The reason I became very angry was not because my subordinates failed to produce any work results, but because I expected them to work according to the intentions of their superior — me — yet they went completely against it. During a conversation with one subordinate, even though I repeatedly emphasized, "Do you understand what I mean?" he answered that he did. However, after three months, he still hadn't completed the report, which left me feeling incredulous.

Have you ever thought during your work process, "I am working so hard but not receiving any positive feedback"? If so, my advice to you is just one thing: first confirm whether your approach aligns with your superior's expectations.

If your efforts are contrary to the expectations of the company or your superior, even if you work tirelessly day and night, you will not receive any positive feedback. To gain recognition, you cannot simply act according to your own abilities and preferences; instead, you need to consider the other party's expectations.

In the past, I would occasionally scold those who had no work results. Normally, I am not an easily angered person, so why did I get so upset?

Therefore, if I don't know the content of your work, then I can't provide positive feedback on your work process. It is a big mistake to think that I only expect results from you. Hence, what that subordinate spent months producing and considered as a result was entirely different from what I expected. This is the main reason I became very angry. When I assigned the work to him, I had high expectations for him, and he himself was highly motivated, but in the end, everything turned out to be in vain.

Moreover, people who feel they haven't received positive feedback often fall into this category — they only want to do work they like. Among workers in creative fields, many have achieved great success by being dedicated to their personal preferences. However, regardless of the type of work or career, one should always prioritize the thoughts and preferences of the customer. Simply pursuing one’s own preferences is undoubtedly too selfish.

A person who cannot clearly grasp or satisfy their superior's expectations will find it difficult to consider the thoughts and desires of customers. Such individuals are prone to making the same mistakes, whether facing customers or superiors. Generally speaking, when communicating with customers, we usually engage in multiple discussions — "How about this?" "What do you think of this?" — before reaching an agreement and signing a contract with the customer. However, those who cannot clearly gauge their superior's expectations often blindly prepare a thick proposal overnight without consulting the "customer" first, and then unexpectedly place it in front of the "customer." The result can only be anger or inconvenience caused to the "customer."

When you are doing work you enjoy, you shouldn't just focus on improving the mechanism of work, but rather try to increase the enthusiasm of those evaluating your work results.
