Reading notes

by jjdusr549 on 2010-01-27 17:06:18

It is often felt that expression is a laborious matter. On the one hand, this is because of the influence of factors such as methods of expression and intellectual abilities, making it difficult to meet the requirements of summarization and relevance. On the other hand, due to the inherent limitations in language expression habits and this type of thinking expression method, it often leads to difficulties in communication between the expresser and the receiver. Therefore, before proceeding with an expression, it is necessary to clarify the expression. This clarification includes three dimensions: first, to articulate what specific cultural domain expressers habitually take as implicit consensus without needing to define these after expression; second, to explain one's own habitual way of expressing thoughts, which means clarifying the presuppositions one prefers to use before expressing implicit meanings; third, to strictly define the content being expressed, drawing clear boundaries to avoid merely providing related elaborations rather than essential expressions. Although natural language serves as the medium for expression, its difference from written words lies in the fact that natural language always expresses categories of things rather than individual entities, especially philosophical language. Despite the many varieties of written words, different writing systems can point to the same natural language concepts, striving to construct the same semantic structure for the same thing, differing only in basic units and rhythms. These habits are influenced by philosophical views and language abilities, so after indicating one's own philosophical perspective and level of linguistic cultivation, any expression is actually impossible to fully achieve consensus.

When people read others' discourses and books, they often lack the path to obtain such consensus. In many cases, I have approached these great works with a superficial and worldly gaze, failing to understand their linguistic dimensions and context, feeling that their thoughts are just so-so... realizing that there seems to be no classic, nothing readable, because much of it could be thought of by oneself. Influenced by empiricism and perfectionism, people practically do not feel the existence of classics or masters, or if they admit their existence, they consider them to be far away, not in the same world. Any past classic appears flawed today, surpassing contemporary thought is hardly acknowledged, and truly effective problem-solving narratives often look crude. This is like life; true friends, lovers, and families sometimes appear so ordinary and unattractive, even ugly, leading to neglect and misunderstanding, causing people to fall into chasing dreams instead. Social whitewashing also plays a role, which is the fundamental reason. Our culture always tells us to cross rivers by feeling the stones, but now guides people to break, reference, and reconstruct, without knowing how to continue, rethink, and restructure. Thus, people have no roots or future, we lack naive authority and order, lacking deep reflection, sometimes even spiritual attachment. This creates difficulties in inheritance and reflection, making it hard to reach consensus. If there is any consensus, it might as well say that most of it is commandment, not the result of wise collision and exchange. This buries truly effective problem-solving discourses while superficially brilliant ones are regarded as models. Those regarded as authoritative commentators often lack humanistic spirit and thinker's cultivation, yet their words sound so appealing as if to stir up the whole world.

In political science, there are those classic thinkers who perhaps incorrectly proved a physical question, strongly supported monarchic dictatorship, emphasized absolute state sovereignty even demanding minimal centralization, or stubbornly opposed civil service... which seem terrifying today, but their ideas were profound reflections on the problems of their time, their thoughts were useful and brilliant. Do we need to wait until we can completely copy their thoughts to recognize their classic status? This is especially evident in our attitude towards foreign traditional cultures, as if our past was entirely ugly, shameful, or even unworthy of a glance. Students like me, especially those currently at a low level, look down on those better than us, while those less thoughtful just aim for simple success. People seem to have lost the cultivation of reflection and contemplation, holding onto classics without any sensation, facing reality with fear, dreaming of an unreachable future... Now reading books frequently makes me more aware of my own state.

The expression and reception in reading are so difficult, hence this little sigh, and so on!
