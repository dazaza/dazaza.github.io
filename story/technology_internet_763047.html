How can physical stores make use of big data?

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 12:47:57

July 20, Beijing Time - According to a commentary article published on the American tech blog VentureBeat under the name of Zachary Hyman, as the co-founder of the lifestyle consumption guide service SpotOn, Hyman introduced how physical stores should understand their customers and seize business opportunities in the era of data.

The following is an overview of the article:

Network retailers and physical stores are set to compete for an important resource: data. Part of the immense appeal of e-commerce lies in its ability to provide sellers with better insights into consumers through every transaction. However, this monopoly on information has been broken by new technologies. New POS terminal systems, digitized loyalty software, and other management tools designed for merchants will help physical store owners make more informed decisions. Below are the five key types of data that physical stores need to master to succeed in the data age.

Who are my customers?

Merchants first need to know the critical data about their potential customers. This means gaining a deep understanding of consumer purchasing habits. For example, who are the new customers, who are the regulars, and which lost customers need to be won back. At the same time, merchants also need to understand information beyond purchases, such as which social networks they generally use and which media they prefer most. This data will help merchants tailor promotional strategies suited to their customers, maximizing the benefits of the products they offer.

When do customers shop?

One of the main differences between online sales and physical store sales is that physical stores incur costs as long as they are open for business, from labor to storefronts. Through new POS terminal systems and digitized loyalty programs, merchants can quickly learn the volume of consumer traffic and arrange employees, products, and services according to the changes in traffic over time, maximizing the use of off-peak sales times.

What is the best way to reach customers?

Once contact with the customer is established, the data generated by new technological methods can help confirm the most effective communication method — email, mobile phone, or social media. In this way, merchants can determine which method can stimulate purchasing behavior when launching products.

For example, frequent customers are usually attracted by the main menu, while occasional customers may want to try new products or services. Moreover, each person's consumption habits are different; some consumers dislike using physical coupons, while others don't like using coupons during peak hours.

The data functionality of new technological methods can help merchants capture this consumption information, something that traditional offline marketing channels cannot achieve.

How is the return on investment for loyalty marketing strategies?

Customer loyalty is a very important marketing pillar for many offline merchants, especially those providing customized services based on consumer preferences and shopping behaviors.

In fact, if digital technology is adopted, not all consumers are at an equal position. Different loyalty marketing methods elicit varied feedback across different regions and groups.

With the gradual shift towards digital loyalty management, merchants can now better assess which product services, analyzed effectively through data, have received positive feedback from consumers.

In addition, these data can provide merchants with an overall impact of loyalty management tools, including actual data and subjective experiences. As new marketing channels like group-buying websites emerge, data analysis becomes increasingly important.

What are the advantages of physical commerce?

Most merchants face the issue of how to allocate resources, and efficient data can help businesses understand the differences among customer bases in different regions.

This includes adopting different marketing models for varying foot traffic levels and which products and services attract different customer groups.

Of course, merchants have reasons to stick with offline operations, as the offline shopping experience is fundamentally different from online shopping.

Physical commerce is not just commerce; it is also an integral part of social life. By applying the data brought by next-generation integrated software, these physical businesses will play an important role in social life for the coming decades.
