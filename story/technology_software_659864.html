A citizen who has been sheltering a homeless person for 18 years was accused of using free labor and was claimed compensation

by zhanghui82 on 2012-02-06 23:39:01

The prosecution of Liu's relatives left the Wang family "very confused". They had taken him in and cared for him for so many years, and they hadn't mistreated him. Everything was done out of kindness to help someone in need, but they never expected this result. "Good intentions have turned us into defendants, it feels very unpleasant," said the agent of Mr. Wang. Taking him in was a continuous act of assistance and did not constitute an employment relationship. "There were no written or oral agreements between both parties, no one forced him. Liu Liwang could leave at any time. He chose to stay and live with the Wang family voluntarily, doing some light work he could handle. How can this be considered an employment relationship?"

"For so many years, where have you been? And how have you been living?" This question troubled the entire family. After multiple inquiries, the family learned that Liu Liwang had been living in a place called Baihua Village in Xi'an for 18 years. A villager surnamed Wang took him in, and since then he lived with the Wang family, helping them raise pigs and sometimes delivering goods with a tricycle. "Every year, we raised twenty to thirty pigs."

Liu Liwang is now 56 years old and comes from Yunxi County in Hubei Province. In early 1994, just after the Spring Festival, the then 39-year-old Liu decided to go to Lishan County in Shaanxi Province to visit relatives. Traveling alone by short-distance bus, he arrived in Xi'an, got lost as he couldn't read, and thus lost contact with his family. That year, Liu's family had posted missing person notices near Dong'an train station but received no news.

However, the Liu family did not agree with the Wang family's account.

After being lost for 18 years and suddenly returning to his hometown, this should have been a joyous event, but Liu Liwang's relatives couldn't feel happy. His nephew Liu Deqian said that compared to before he went missing, his uncle had become much thinner, spoke unclearly, and was also sick. "He had 3000 yuan in his bag, saying the money was given by the Wang family."

Recently, the head of the Social Affairs Department of the Shaanxi Provincial Civil Affairs Department stated that in the 2009 Notice from five ministries including the Ministry of Civil Affairs and the Ministry of Public Security on further strengthening the management of urban street vagrants and beggars and the protection of wandering minors, there was indeed a policy encouraging and supporting social organizations or individuals to provide shelter, food, clothing, and blankets to vagrants and beggars, but the goal was still to help them return to their families and society. "The behavior of individuals taking in vagrants is worth affirming and promoting, but rescue work is an inescapable responsibility of the government, with dedicated funds and personnel to carry it out. Long-term individual rescue behaviors have many unstable factors, and it would be best to contact relevant government departments for support to ensure that these vagrants can have a stable life."

After negotiations, disputes between the Liu family and the Wang family continued, leading the Liu family to sue the Wang family in the Weiyang District Court of Xi'an City.

For so many years, why didn't the Wangs send Liu Liwang back to his hometown? Mr. Wang explained that he had asked Liu Liwang multiple times where his home was, but since Liu couldn't read and spoke unclearly, he couldn't understand him, and his ID card was never found, so they couldn't determine his exact address. As such, he stayed at their home for all these years, with full board and lodging provided. "Our family isn't wealthy either. We raised pigs a few years ago, and he helped take care of them, doing some light work. When he fell ill, we sent him to see a doctor."

Lawyer's suggestion: It's best to report cases of personal custody to relevant authorities.

The journalist followed Liu Liwang and his relatives to Baihua Village in Weiyang District, where he had lived for 18 years. Many villagers were familiar with him. One villager said he wasn't sure when Liu Liwang came to the village, but he had been there for a long time, eating and staying at the Wangs' house, which was well-known to everyone. "He usually helps with pig farming and delivery work, getting along harmoniously with the Wang family, like one big family." When the journalist asked if Liu Liwang's relationship with the Wang family constituted an employment relationship, the villagers said it was "unclear" and "hard to say."

The trial lasted more than four hours without an immediate verdict.

How to view Mr. Wang's act of taking in Liu Liwang, who had been wandering for 18 years, was discussed with the parties involved and relevant individuals after the trial.

On October 2nd afternoon, Liu Liwang unexpectedly appeared at the center of his hometown Huangyuntan Village. The village had changed greatly after 18 years. While he was wandering around, an elderly man recognized him and informed his family.

On December 4th, 2011, the case was heard in court, with both sides presenting differing opinions. The focus of contention in this case was whether the Wang family should pay labor compensation for the 18 years Liu Liwang worked for them after they took him in.

Mr. Wang felt deeply wronged. He said that at the beginning of 1994, while driving a tractor through Machashi Crossroad in the northern part of Xi'an city, he saw Liu Liwang wandering alone on the street. Feeling sorry for him, he brought him home and settled him there. Since then, he had lived there for 18 years.

Relatives of the Liu family believed that for 18 years, Liu Liwang had been working for free for the "kind-hearted people" who took him in, and now that he was sick, they sent him back home. "The people who took him in should pay him 18 years of labor compensation."

The Liu family said that after returning home, Liu Liwang was diagnosed with liver cirrhosis, a serious condition, and had already spent over ten thousand yuan on medical treatment. "We're not unable to bear the cost; we just feel it's unfair! When he could work, they used him, and now that his body can't do anything, they send him back home." The Liu family believed that the relationship between the two parties constituted an employment relationship, and labor compensation should be paid, along with medical expenses, transportation fees, etc., totaling more than 300,000 yuan.

A staff member of the Weiyang District Civil Affairs Bureau said that they had never encountered a situation where an adult vagrant was taken in by an individual before, making it difficult to determine whether such actions were appropriate. "Generally, when civil affairs departments discover vagrants and beggars, they are sent to shelters, examined, provided with food and lodging, their identities and addresses are verified, and they are returned home in contact with their families. This individual case is relatively special. Considering the harmonious relationship and years of sentiment between the two parties, it is suggested that they resolve the matter through negotiation."

The journalist first had a conversation with Liu Liwang. Although he spoke haltingly and seemed somewhat slow in reaction, basic communication was not a problem. During the conversation, the journalist found that Liu Liwang did not feel bad about his life in Xi'an over the years. He even led the journalist to the place where he used to raise pigs several years ago. When asked why he hadn't returned home all these years, he said, "I didn't have the money, I couldn't make it back."

The relatives of Liu Liwang said that the Wang family had taken in Liu Liwang and helped him get through tough times, and the whole family sincerely appreciated it. "But for so many years, they didn't notify the Liu family and kept using him as free labor to raise pigs. During this time, he became seriously ill in Xi'an, and now that his illness has become a burden, they give him some money and send him back home, which is clearly inappropriate."

Mr. Wang, a lawyer from the Shaanxi Yongjiaxin Law Firm, said in an interview that ordinary citizens taking in and assisting vagrants and beggars, especially those with mental or intellectual disabilities, is not inappropriate as long as it is done out of compassion and kindness. However, it is best to report such cases to local police stations, civil affairs bureaus, and other relevant departments, which will not only promote socialist moral values but also protect the legitimate rights and interests of the parties involved. If the intention is profit-driven and the vagrants are exploited for labor, it definitely violates labor laws. "From the perspective of practical situations, as long as there is no fraud, coercion, or violence involved, and both parties voluntarily live and work together, this behavior provides convenience for the survival of vagrants and should be affirmed."
