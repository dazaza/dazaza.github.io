Jelly Bean system has covered more than 50% of Android devices

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 19:48:53

According to the latest statistics released by Google, the Android operating system "Jelly Bean" covers more than half of all Android devices.

These data are based on smartphones and tablets that accessed the Google Play store in the seven days ending November 1, 2013. The data show that versions 4.1.x, 4.2.x, and 4.3 of Jelly Bean support 52.1% of Android devices. The latest version, Android 4.4 KitKat, did not appear in the list because it, along with the new Nexus 5 smartphone running it, was only launched at the end of last month.

In the past four months, Jelly Bean's usage rate has grown by over 14 percentage points, making it the most widely used Android system.

According to Google's statistics as of August 2013, Android versions prior to 2.2 accounted for about one percent of all Android devices logging into Google servers (not Google Play).

For developers, this may be good news as they can focus on developing applications for newer operating systems. However, Jelly Bean still faces a strong rival: Android 2.3.x "Gingerbread." This version, first released in February 2011, still accounts for 26.3% of Android devices accessing the Google Play store. However, its significant position is gradually fading, though it might persist for at least another year.

It is worth noting that Jelly Bean suffers from fragmentation issues: Google has released three different versions of Jelly Bean. The version with the largest share of usage is 4.1.x, which was released in July 2012. This means there is considerable fragmentation among devices running Jelly Bean, with most 4.1.x users unable to enjoy the features introduced in versions 4.2.x and 4.3. Now, the latest version 4.4, named KitKat, has also been released.

The fundamental reason users cannot update to the latest version of Android in a timely manner is not due to a lack of interest. On the contrary, the speed at which iOS users update to the latest version shows that users are clearly interested in new operating systems. The problem is that Google's upgrade process is often very long.

No matter when Google releases a new version of Android, original equipment manufacturers must personalize it, adding their own features. Moreover, for smartphones and tablets sold by wireless carriers, the carriers must also add their own branded content to Android. Worse still, neither original equipment manufacturers nor wireless carriers can profit from promoting free Android upgrades, so they prefer to aggressively market new devices.

Starting from April 2013, Google began publishing data collected from Android devices accessing the Google Play store. Previously, these data were only collected from Android devices logging into Google servers. Google believes that the new statistics "can more accurately reflect users who actively use the Android and Google Play ecosystem."
