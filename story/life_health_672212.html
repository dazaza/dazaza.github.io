The symptoms of old rotten leg are chronic skin ulcers on the lower to mid-calf. In the early stages, there may only be inflammatory exudation, followed by ulceration. These ulcers do not heal easily, become larger and deeper over time, and eventually destroy the entire layer of skin. The surrounding skin is affected, becoming atrophied, dark in color, leading to eczema, with occasional shedding and itching.

On the front of the lower to mid-calf, the skin is close to the bone. Once an ulcer occurs, the bone is exposed, and infection follows, forming chronic osteomyelitis. Some patients suffer for years or even decades without healing, enduring immense pain. What are the characteristics of old rotten leg? It is difficult to heal for a long time, or even if healed, it easily recurs due to injury, making it a common and frequent disease in surgery.

Old rotten leg often complicates with varicose veins in the lower limbs. It is caused by prolonged standing work or carrying heavy objects, compounded by damage or insect bites to the skin of the lower limbs, as well as eczema. It commonly occurs on the lower third of the calf, more often on the inner side than the outer side. This condition recurs frequently. During an episode, it first itches and then causes pain, followed by ulceration and wound formation. A few long-term unhealed ulcers, with cauliflower-like wounds, are often signs of cancerous changes. These are also symptoms of old rotten leg.
