Signs of deteriorating weather and protection in hazardous weather conditions

by 422abc48 on 2010-05-25 18:01:49

1. During the day, the wind in the valley blows from the mountaintop to the valley, and at night it blows from the valley to the mountaintop.

2. In the morning, silk-like clouds appear, followed by an increase in black clouds which slowly descend.

3. Clouds move very quickly and show a tendency to increase; this may be a precursor to a storm.

4. A sudden change in wind direction with increasing intensity, accompanied by dark clouds moving in.

5. After a period of dry heat or foggy conditions, visibility suddenly improves.

6. Dense morning fog fills the valley and does not dissipate until evening.

7. During the day, a large halo appears around the sun, and at night a smaller one appears around the moon, indicating strong winds are coming.

8. Before dawn, stars flicker erratically.

9. Evening temperatures rise, nights are warm and humid.

10. Fog rising from halfway up a mountain could be a sign of an impending storm.

11. Additionally, when weather changes, so do natural phenomena and wildlife behavior. If you observe carefully, you can predict future weather conditions.

Sounds and smells: When humidity increases, sound travels further and odors become more distinct—saturated moist air acts like an amplifier, enhancing conductivity. For example, in some places where train sounds cannot usually be heard, they can be clearly heard before rain.

Physical changes: When the weather worsens, those with curly hair may feel their hair tighten and become harder to comb—just like animal fur, if it becomes easily tangled or no longer stands straight and easy to comb, a storm may be approaching. Anyone with rheumatoid arthritis, corns, or related symptoms will feel pain and discomfort when humidity increases.

Observing campfires: If smoke rises steadily, it indicates that the weather will not change much and will remain good. If the smoke flickers or rises and then descends, a storm may be imminent.

When the weather is good, the temperature difference between day and night is large. Water vapor encountering cold air turns into small droplets or frost, so the colder the night, the better the weather the next day. Dew at night also means good weather the next day. If water droplets are seen on spider webs in the morning, the sky will clear up. If frost is observed in the morning, it predicts another fine day.

Protection during dangerous weather - Thunderstorms: Thunderstorms usually start with small cumulus clouds developing and rapidly growing through the towering cumulus stage into mature cumulonimbus clouds. This kind of weather is intense, harsh, and changes abruptly. Outdoor enthusiasts should note the following points:

1) Thunderstorms can occur when cumulus clouds begin to pile up and darken.

2) Thunderstorms usually last for a short time, stay calm, don't panic, and stay in a sheltered place.

Lightning: The danger of lightning lies in its ability to strike objects and people, causing fires, and the thunder it generates can rupture eardrums. Therefore, remember:

1) Cars often provide excellent protection against lightning, so you can hide inside a car.

2) The best protective areas are caves, ditches, canyons, or open spaces under tall forests.

3) If outdoors, crouch at least twice the height of an isolated tree away from it.

4) When you feel an electric charge, such as your hair standing up or your skin tingling, it's likely you're about to be struck by lightning. Immediately fall to the ground to protect yourself.

5) If taking refuge near an isolated protruding object, ensure the top of that object is at least 15-20 meters higher than your head.

6) Avoid vertical walls or cliffs, exposed peaks and ridges, as well as flat open terrains.

7) Avoid cracks in the ground, large patches of lichen, and suspended rocks.

8) If necessary, sit among scattered stones.

9) In hazardous terrain, secure yourself with a rope.

10) If seeking refuge in a cave, stay at least 3 meters away from all vertical rock walls to avoid injury from electrical conduction.

Flash floods: During heavy rain, due to the rapid precipitation, the ground doesn't have time to absorb the rainwater, and frozen soil and rocks cannot prevent the downward flow of rainwater, exacerbating the threat of flash floods. Therefore, when thunderstorm activity is severe, avoid long valleys prone to water accumulation and flat areas below riverbeds. Leave flood-prone areas, and if necessary, abandon equipment and move to higher ground. Also, avoid attempting to wade through streams that reach knee level.

Weather conditions can raise or lower body temperature, affecting health. The three most influential weather factors are temperature, wind speed, and relative humidity. Under cold conditions, the effects of temperature and wind are inseparable. Often, both simultaneously cause the loss of body heat. Clothes soaked through lose up to 90% of their insulation value. Thus, a simple yet effective way to survive in a cold environment is to maintain appropriate movement without sweating. In hot and humid environments, people may experience fatigue, headaches, loss of appetite, insomnia, severe dehydration, and general weakness or heat cramps. It is important to avoid outdoor activities in such climates.
