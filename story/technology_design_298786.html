Professional Criminal Defense: The Relationship between Investigation and Prosecution Highlighted by the Supplementary Investigation System

by lilan2333 on 2009-12-08 11:07:42

During the specific operation process of the supplementary investigation system, there exists a phenomenon of "supplement without investigation." Once the investigation authority transfers the case to the prosecution, they seem to consider their job done. The return for supplementary investigation appears to have little significant relevance to them. They only need to wait until the time for supplementary investigation is up, then send the case back to the prosecution. The resulting phenomenon of over-detention is regarded as normal. I believe that the crux of this issue lies in the fact that our country's investigation authorities and prosecution authorities are on an equal legal footing, and even in some aspects, the investigation authorities have a growing trend; while our prosecution authorities lack sufficient authority to control the investigation authorities. They are combined in a linear structure akin to assembly line operations. Therefore, only by adjusting the relationship between our country's investigation and prosecution, enhancing the status of the prosecution authorities so they have the authority to supervise and control the behavior of the investigation authorities, and to a certain extent, cooperate and coordinate with the investigation authorities to form a good interactive relationship, thereby achieving the goal of partially improving investigative capabilities through institutional improvements, can this problem be truly alleviated.

Whether in common law countries or civil law countries, in terms of the relationship between investigation and prosecution, emphasis is placed on the supervision or leadership of the prosecution authorities over the investigation authorities. Sometimes power may be reserved but unused, but in the configuration of power relationships, the higher status of the prosecution authorities is upheld and emphasized. This is because strengthening the supervision or control of the prosecution authorities (mainly the prosecution agencies) over investigative activities has theoretical justification: it not only enhances the legitimacy of procedures but also makes the entire criminal litigation process more coherent, and will to a certain extent promote the improvement of investigative capabilities.

From a global perspective, prosecution agencies generally bear the responsibility of public prosecution (regardless of whether their enjoyment of prosecutorial powers is monopolistic), and prosecution agencies usually monitor investigative activities. According to foreign practices, the monitoring of investigative activities by prosecution agencies can be divided into three situations: the leadership and control of auxiliary investigative agencies—police agencies—by prosecution agencies as the main investigative power subjects, the general supervision of investigative activities by prosecution agencies as legal supervisory agencies, and the constraints imposed by prosecution agencies as dual subjects of both prosecutorial and investigative powers on the investigative actions of judicial police. Countries may adopt one or several of these approaches. For example, Germany, France, and Italy mainly adopt the first approach, while the former Soviet Union, Russia, Yugoslavia adopt a combination of the first two approaches [6], and Japan mainly adopts the third approach. Currently, countries around the world have generally strengthened the constraints imposed by prosecution agencies on police-conducted investigative activities.

In civil law countries, prosecution agencies are legally the primary investigative power subjects, and police agencies are merely auxiliary agencies assisting in investigations. Prosecution agencies possess comprehensive independent investigative powers, investigative command powers, and investigative supervisory powers. By issuing synchronous orders, commands, and supervision over the investigative activities of judicial police, prosecution agencies effectively constrain the official actions of judicial police. In the former Soviet Union, former Yugoslavia, North Korea, Mongolia, and present-day Russia, prosecution agencies also generally have strong prosecutorial supervisory functions or investigative command functions [6]. In Japan, although the law stipulates that the police are the primary investigative subjects and prosecutors only conduct investigations when necessary, it still stipulates that prosecutors can provide general instructions and specific commands to the police. As a representative of common law countries, American prosecutors mainly focus on prosecution work and generally do not personally conduct investigations, but they have the authority to participate in investigative activities and supervise professional investigators or grand juries [7].

Compared to foreign regulations, the constraints imposed by our country's prosecution authorities (i.e., prosecution agencies) on police investigative activities are significantly weaker, and the means of constraint are clearly insufficient, leading to quite limited effectiveness of prosecutorial supervision: First, constraints on investigative activities are mainly carried out through post-event reviews, without the authority to issue synchronous orders or commands, making it impossible to promptly correct and remedy abuses of investigative powers. Second, the scope of constraints is too narrow. A large number of specialized investigations or compulsory investigative measures involving citizens' personal rights, property, or privacy do not require the approval of prosecution agencies, giving investigative agencies complete discretion. Third, effective sanction measures are lacking. Even if prosecution agencies propose corrective opinions regarding illegal or inappropriate behaviors in police investigative activities, they are difficult to implement due to the absence of statutory sanction measures. For instance, regarding cases where the police should open an investigation but fail to do so, prosecution agencies can request the police to initiate an investigation. However, if the police ignore the suggestions of the prosecution agencies, the law does not specify any provisions for such situations. This lack of supervision and constraints is reflected in the supplementary investigation system as follows: prosecution agencies are powerless against the phenomenon of "supplement without investigation," and due to their equal status with investigative agencies and related interests, they often have no choice but to prosecute cases where substantive supplementary investigations have not been conducted and evidence remains insufficient.

Therefore, in our country, it is necessary to appropriately enhance the status of prosecution agencies in the relationship between investigation and prosecution, strengthen the supervision and constraints of prosecution agencies over investigative activities, and this strengthening should proceed from the following aspects: (1) Regulate synchronous supervision of investigations by prosecution agencies; (2) Expand the scope of supervision beyond just approvals for arrests, including a large number of compulsory investigative measures within the scope of supervision; (3) Specify the results of supervision, making them not just opinions but having mandatory enforcement power and sanction measures. I believe that if we establish and strengthen the supervisory control system of our country's prosecution agencies over investigative activities and enhance the connection between investigative and prosecution departments, the number of cases requiring supplementary investigation will be greatly reduced, reducing the repetitiveness of procedures, and the phenomenon of "supplement without investigation" in supplementary investigations will be fundamentally alleviated. The relative enhancement of the status of prosecution agencies will also make them less hesitant when making decisions to terminate procedures, providing greater confidence to timely conclude cases that remain evidence-deficient after supplementary investigations, thereby reducing the phenomenon of over-detention and laying a solid institutional foundation for ensuring the quality of prosecuted cases.
