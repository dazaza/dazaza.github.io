Deriving the light distribution from the brightness distribution requires a large amount of complex computation, generally including the following main steps:

1) Transform the (i,j) pixel into the C coordinate system with the point on the enclosing spherical surface as the origin;

2) Weight the brightness in the C coordinate system using solid angle surface elements and enclosing spherical surface elements to obtain the flux in each enclosing spherical surface element within the solid angle element;

3) Transform the coordinates to the D coordinate system with the rotation center of the turntable as the origin;

4) Transform the coordinates to the S coordinate system with the measured light source as the center.

3.3 Light Intensity Distribution

Light intensity refers to treating the measured light source as a point source and examining its flux within the solid angle element in a given direction in space. To treat LEDs and LED luminaires as point sources, the ideal measurement or calculation distance for light intensity should be infinitely far. A full-space distribution photometer can measure illuminance using a long-distance photometric probe and approximate the far-field light intensity through the inverse square law of distance, where d is the measurement distance. However, for some LED luminaires with large emitting surfaces, narrow beams, and complex emissions, there will be significant errors in the light intensity value when d is not large enough. As shown in Figure 4, when the measurement distance d is insufficient, the direction of the rays received by the detector will not align with the direction of the light intensity.

3.4 Spatial Illuminance Distribution

Illuminance refers to the flux per unit area, i.e., . In traditional photometry, the spatial light intensity distribution (i.e., the photometric curve) of the luminaire measured by a distribution photometer is generally used, combined with the inverse square law of illuminance, to calculate the illuminance distribution at various distances, planar equal-illuminance curves, and spatial equal-illuminance curves and other spatial illuminance distribution characteristics. As shown in Figure 5, this method is suitable for point sources; however, for LEDs and LED luminaires, there will be significant errors due to at least the following two points:

Firstly, the direction of the rays emitted from LEDs or LED luminaires with large emitting surfaces and complex emission surfaces entering a certain surface element differs greatly from the direction from the center point of the LED or LED luminaire to that surface element, i.e., the direction of light intensity calculation. Therefore, there is a significant difference between the sum of the fluxes of the actual rays entering that surface element and the flux calculated through light intensity;

Secondly, as shown in Figure 5, limited by the beam angle of a single LED, the rays from the left LED cannot reach the surface elements dA and dA. However, the far-field light intensity distribution only represents the overall photometric effect but cannot reflect these details.
