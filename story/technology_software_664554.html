Only when both are well built is it a real culture.

by fbfshtwk0423 on 2012-02-13 16:19:30

So many old buildings have been destroyed and rebuilt in the wave of world cultural heritage applications that it pains one to see antique auctions occur. We must understand that we cannot only focus on large cultural heritages like the Great Wall and the Forbidden City; antique buildings need attention as well. As Wuhan builds its culture, so many old buildings are in this condition. Is this gilding the construction of a cultured Wuhan or is there another construction purpose which requires these old things be removed as an excuse? We do not know. We also learned from reports: in recent years, a few lucky antique houses have received their due treatment. The Industrial and Commercial Bank of China spent 100 million yuan renovating the Citibank building, COFCO Huayue Management Co., Ltd. spent 46 million yuan refurbishing the "Tu Kunshan Mansion", and the Agricultural Bank spent 28 million yuan reinforcing and renovating the original "French Banque de l'Indochine" in the old concession area of Hankou. According to relevant personnel, "if antiques are used and decorated according to relevant regulations, it is not something ordinary enterprises and individuals can bear." Because truly reinforcing and repairing according to the standard of an antique building costs more than rebuilding a house.

According to Changjiang.com: currently, Wuhan has a total of 450 immovable cultural relics and excellent historical buildings at the national, provincial, and district levels. Among them, Jiang'an District has 131, and the historical buildings on Minxin Street in Hankou are especially concentrated. I went to No. 17 Minxin Street, which is an old building next to Jianghan Road. In the early twentieth century, it was a mansion built by a textile factory boss. Judging from its appearance, it still retains the style of a hundred-year-old building, with a grand European style, stone base, gray cement exterior walls, and the entire structure is well-preserved. However, its appearance is already in ruins. The classical Western-style windows on the second floor have been removed and replaced with a smokeless stove. The entire first floor of this historical building has been rented out to a scrap recycling boss. Inside, the smell is extremely unpleasant, and it is filled with all kinds of scrap. The rent for this old mansion is also extremely low, with an area of over 200 square meters and a monthly rent of only more than 2000 yuan. I learned from the community residents' committee that the first floor has been a scrap station for over ten years. A hundred-year-old mansion built by a textile factory boss is now rented out for scrap collection. It sounds like a fairy tale, but it's right in front of our eyes, undeniable. The report came out a few days ago, and it has been stuck in my throat, making me heartbroken for several days without knowing what to do. Nowadays, this report is still prominently displayed, does this hundred-year-old building really have no value and is not favored anymore?

Wuhan's archaeological history can be traced back to the Neolithic period about six thousand years ago. Hankou was formerly known as Xia Rui and Ezhu. The Panlongcheng site in Huangpi District is a palace city of a fang state from about 3500 years ago during the Shang Dynasty, and it is the most complete Shang Dynasty ancient city discovered and preserved so far. Both Hanyang and Wuchang have a history of 1800 years, while Hankou officially opened its port in March 1861. Along the downstream shore of the old Hankou town, concessions of five countries—Britain, Germany, Russia, France, and Japan—were successively established, transforming Hankou from a traditional commercial market town into a modern metropolis. It became one of the earliest three treaty ports (Zhenjiang, Jiujiang, Hankou) along the Yangtze River. Hankou's foreign trade could almost rival Shanghai, hence there are many Sino-Western hybrid old-style buildings, which are also among the few remaining antique buildings.

Since reinforcing and renovating according to the standard of an antique building costs more than rebuilding a house, why wasn't protective work done initially? Why wait until the last minute to hastily prepare, rather than prevent problems beforehand? From this perspective, there are still blind spots in our protection of cultural relic buildings. In 2003, the Wuhan municipal government issued Order No. 138, namely the "Management Measures for the Protection of Old Urban Areas and Excellent Historical Buildings in Wuhan City." This regulation not only makes provisions for the use and renovation of excellent historical buildings but also indicates that legal responsibility can be pursued for acts damaging historical buildings. We need these "Measures," but more importantly, we need supervision and enforcement. Building a cultured Wuhan cannot just stay on slogans; we need to reflect on "Han curses," build spiritual aspects, and protect existing culture as well. We should also protect and develop material aspects, ensuring both spirit and material are passed down. Only when both are well constructed can we achieve true culture.

Buildings like Citibank, Tu Kunshan Mansion, and the French Banque de l'Indochine are all lucky ones that have been reinforced and renovated. Are there other mansions like the "textile factory" that haven't been so fortunate? Only by properly enforcing and implementing the "Measures" can we eliminate the concept of "lucky ones," ensure more equal treatment, and allow more antique buildings to be repaired and protected.

Other buildings receiving similar treatment include the old site of the Hankou "Min Guo Daily" located at No. 2 Taining Street, Jianghan Road, and the Japanese military officer's quarters located at No. 255 Victory Street. The old site of the Hankou "Min Guo Daily" has been listed as a municipal-level cultural relic, but the first floor has been completely transformed by merchants, and the building's facade is covered with various advertisement boards crisscrossing everywhere. Now, the interior is mostly used as a warehouse for shops on the first floor, posing safety hazards for the load-bearing capacity of the floors.
