4 Factors That Will Attract Investors to the Twitter IPO

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 19:51:45

Barron's published an article stating that Twitter is undervalued at a stock price of $20, but if the price exceeds $30, investors should be cautious.

Bright Prospects

Twitter will conduct its IPO this Wednesday with the stock trading symbol TWTR. Judging from various signs, the stock will undoubtedly be popular after listing because it possesses four essential elements of a hot IPO: relatively small scale, strong growth prospects, widely recognized investment opportunities, and reasonable stock prices. The last point may be controversial, but compared to other social media companies, Twitter's valuation is indeed relatively reasonable.

Twitter also has an optimistic CEO — Richard Costolo said in a media interview: "Twitter has only scratched the surface of its potential."

This company, founded in 2007, currently has 232 million users, and its main source of income comes from targeted advertisements based on user interests. More than 75% of users access Twitter via smartphones and tablets, and 70% of the company's advertising revenue comes from mobile platforms. In contrast, the world's largest social network, Facebook, performs slightly worse in the mobile field as less than half of its revenue comes from mobile.

Growth in Twitter Users and Revenue

Twitter's revenue for the first nine months of this year was $422 million, which is only one-tenth of Facebook's, and the company is still operating at a loss. According to U.S. Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP), the company may not turn a profit until 2015 or 2016. However, potential investors firmly believe that Twitter's appeal lies in its growth potential.

"Companies that can achieve triple-digit revenue growth will definitely attract Wall Street's attention," said Rory Maher, analyst at Hillside Partners, an independent research firm in the United States. Twitter's revenue for the third quarter of this year was $168 million, more than doubling from the same period last year, while Facebook's revenue growth during the same period was only 60%.

Reasonable Pricing

Compared to Facebook, Twitter is less mature when going public. Facebook, which conducted its IPO in May last year, has successfully achieved substantial revenue and profit growth and established a position for the entire social network in the capital market. This could enhance the attractiveness of Twitter's stock, and the offering price is likely to be set around $20.

Twitter may have sparked some speculative demand, but the main participants are likely institutional investors. This means retail investors may have to wait until the stock officially lists on Thursday to buy it, by which time the stock price will inevitably be much higher than $20.

If Twitter's stock price is above $30, it will be difficult to sustain — this stock price corresponds to a market value of $21 billion, and an enterprise value (market value minus net cash) of $19 billion. Calculated accordingly, the company’s price-to-sales ratio for 2014 reaches 19 times, while the price-to-sales ratios of other high-priced internet companies like Facebook, LinkedIn, Yelp, and Zillow are between 11 to 13 times. The price-to-sales ratio of the S&P 500 index is less than twice.

Twitter will sell 70 million shares at a price of $17 to $20 per share in its IPO. Assuming the transaction is completed at the upper limit and up to 80.5 million shares are issued through over-allotment, the IPO will raise $1.6 billion — which is only 10% of Facebook's IPO scale.

Twitter hopes to avoid repeating Facebook's mistakes by setting a reasonable price and moderate scale. Facebook suffered a sharp drop in stock price due to selling too many stocks at a high price (with a price-to-sales ratio of about 20 times) and concerns among investors about its mobile business.

Twitter's IPO pricing range is equivalent to the price of a large number of restricted shares granted to employees in 2013. Twitter also emulated LinkedIn's model by selling only a very small portion of its shares. LinkedIn's stock price surged by a factor of two on its first day of listing, and Twitter's IPO scale may only be 10% of its 705 million floating shares.

Optimistic Expectations

Viktor Anthony, analyst at Topeka Capital Markets, wrote in a research report last week that under optimistic expectations, both Twitter's user base and its advertising monetization platform are in their early stages. He set a target price for Twitter at $54 by the end of 2014. Doug Kass, analyst at Seabreeze Partners Management, suggests buying the stock at any price up to $32.50. He believes that Twitter has a first-mover advantage and monopolistic status, and its valuation is relatively reasonable compared to its peers.

Twitter fans noticed that assuming the newly issued stock price is $20, the company's enterprise value after listing will reach $12 billion, which is about one-tenth of Facebook's. Meanwhile, Pinterest, a social photo-sharing site without any revenue, has already been valued at nearly $4 billion in the private market.

Twitter's model is simple; users can post messages of 140 characters and follow others' content. These contents can include links that direct users to other websites. The service has become a major source of news reporting. U.S. President Barack Obama even announced his successful re-election via Twitter in 2012. Most news organizations also use Twitter as a major source of traffic.

Profit Potential

Most of Twitter's advertising revenue comes from Promoted Tweets, which are promoted content appearing in users' personalized feeds. Advertisers only need to pay when users click on these ads. Twitter stated that the company can publish precisely targeted ads based on user preferences, such as displaying messages posted by JPMorgan Chase to people who like financial news and business content.

Twitter began generating revenue in 2010 but is still building its global advertising sales team. The company currently has 2,300 employees, an increase of 100% year-over-year. Although 77% of Twitter users are outside the U.S., only 26% of the revenue in the third quarter came from international markets, highlighting vast opportunities in overseas markets.

Television might become a goldmine because millions of Twitter users often post related messages while watching TV using their smartphones. The television industry is also willing to cooperate with Twitter: actors and writers from American TV dramas like "Scandal" and "The Vampire Diaries" frequently interact with audiences on Twitter. Their goal is to encourage viewers to watch live broadcasts with commercials rather than recorded versions that allow skipping ads. Twitter is developing various TV-related advertising products.

Since it is not yet profitable, revenue will be an important reference for valuing Twitter. The company is expected to generate $600 million in revenue this year and exceed $1 billion in 2014. With a long-term profit margin target calculated by EBITDA (earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization) of 35% to 40%, if the company can maintain 60% growth for several years, its revenue in 2017 will exceed $4 billion, adjusted EBITDA will be $1.5 billion, and earnings per share calculated by conventional accounting standards will be approximately $1.

Barron's has always been cautious about high-valuation stocks but finds Twitter's issuance price of $20 attractive. However, if the price is significantly higher than this level, investors should be cautious.
