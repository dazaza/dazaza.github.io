Alibaba is anxious about mobile Internet, and Jack Ma is strongly promoting Laiwang internally.

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 12:00:45

"If we don't achieve success in wireless, we shouldn't even consider going public!" This was the stance that Jack Ma took on the company's internal network.

An insider revealed that on October 20th, Jack Ma posted an article on the internal network urging Alibaba employees to become promoters of the Laiwang product. In this article, Ma not only emphasized the relationship between wireless and the company's listing but also revealed some of his anxiety. In the mobile internet era, Alibaba still did not have its own flagship product.

And Laiwang was clearly regarded by Jack Ma and Alibaba as the top-tier product representing the future.

In a lighthearted tone, Ma required that by the end of November, every Alibaba employee must have at least 100 users outside the company on their Laiwang app, or else it would be considered as giving up on the red envelope (bonus). "Of course, having over 100 doesn't necessarily mean you'll get a red envelope, but without 100, there's definitely no red envelope," he stated.

"Currently, 80% of my communication tools are Laiwang," Ma also tirelessly showcased his extensive network of contacts in the article, which included not only industry giants like "Charles Li, Yu Zhu, and Guangchang Guo," but also entertainment celebrities such as "Jet Li, Wen Zhang, Xiaoming Huang, Wei Zhao, and Kun Chen."

This clearly demonstrated Ma's emphasis on wireless business. "Whoever does not participate, should not be in this company." He even referred to promoting "Laiwang" as "the effort of Alibabans to fight for survival rights in the wireless era."

Jack Ma started experiencing Laiwang more than a month ago. At that time, Alibaba officially launched a new wireless strategy based on Laiwang.

On September 10th, Alibaba restructured its organizational framework. As the most crucial product of this transformation, Group CEO Jonathan Lu personally supported Laiwang during its 4.0 version launch event.

Since then, Alibaba has not only repositioned products like Alipay Wallet and Taobao client but also introduced innovative products like Etao Shopping Street and Taobao Travel, as well as experimental products like Etao Fire Eye.

"This is Alibaba's instinctive response in the mobile internet era. Even if these products may eventually be integrated into others, the more important thing is to exercise the team." An industry insider told Tencent Technology, "From Alibaba's mobile strategy perspective, it currently lacks systematic layout."

The insider believed that Alibaba's core is transactions, but this has yet to be established in the mobile internet. Therefore, what is most important for Alibaba now is traffic and channels, which is reflected in the decisions made in its investment and acquisition strategies this year.

In fact, Alibaba will face increasing competition from major players in the wireless sector moving forward. Just days ago, Fan Zhiming, president of Alibaba Micro Financial Domestic Business Unit, boldly declared, "Comparing with WeChat Pay is underestimating Alipay!" Moreover, in the case of UC's shareholding, Alibaba had direct competition with Baidu, and even in the process of acquiring 91 Wireless, Alibaba played an important role.

A Battle for Survival in Wireless After 10 Years

From the release and maturity of applications developed based on mobile internet, such as WeChat, the mobile internet entered the true era of mobile terminals. Before this, it was merely a migration from PC-end software environments. Now, this brand-new battlefield is witnessing the division of territories among various players.

In the past year, Baidu's $1.9 billion acquisition of 91 Wireless became the largest merger and acquisition deal in China's internet history, while Alibaba invested $568 million and $294 million in Sina Weibo and AutoNavi respectively. Additionally, news about Alibaba acquiring Umeng, investing in UC, Baidu acquiring Dianxin, etc., kept coming out. Although Tencent didn't have too much rumor, its actions like 'removing' Soso Map when investing in Sogou, strengthening QQ Browser and application distribution channels clearly showed its emphasis on mobile products.

Therefore, if Tencent is using its own products to continuously expand the mobile internet ecosystem, then Alibaba is expanding its strength through R&D, acquisitions, and investments around the ecological chain. Previously, a media person summarized Alibaba's mobile internet strategy as follows: "Transfer wireless shopping (traffic, data) from the PC end, then move from shopping into the lifestyle circle, build application scenarios around the lifestyle circle, and establish traffic entry points to improve application scenarios."

Recently, Zeng Ming, Chief Strategy Officer of Alibaba Group, also revealed in a public interview that Alibaba's business is divided into three parts: Alibaba Micro Financial Company, Cainiao Company that lays out large logistics, and Alibaba Group that includes all remaining businesses. For "Alibaba Group," the two most important businesses moving forward are wireless and e-commerce.

From Taobao's "split into three," to the "Seven Swords Descending from Tian Shan," followed by the establishment of 25 business units, and now further optimization and integration, it can be seen that Chairman Jack Ma is constantly rearranging combinations for Alibaba's future to adapt to the mobile internet era in the best way possible.

Encouragement and motivation were also key words in Ma's post, "Ten years ago, no one would have believed that Taobao could defeat the giant eBay. Ten years later, why can't we give it a try? Many people regret not having the chance to participate in that war, but this time we all can participate."

Where Should We Go? It Lies in Wireless

In this article, Ma recalled, "All the questions colleagues challenge today about Laiwang's future, I've already challenged and questioned. But I really couldn't find any reason for success."

At the IT Leaders Summit held in Shenzhen in March this year, Ma publicly admitted, "I see WeChat, and I'm nervous. But being nervous is normal; it promotes social progress."

Looking at the current mobile instant messaging software on the market, Tencent dominates with its Mobile QQ and WeChat, while NetEase's YiXin and Shanda's Youni have also been launched recently.

The release of Laiwang 4.0 was originally just a strategic product of a giant. Under the strong support of Ma and Lu, the importance of Laiwang continued to rise.

One significant difference between mobile internet products and PC software is that they are easier to showcase and share with others.

Therefore, it’s unsurprising that Ma mobilized his network to support Laiwang. Even before the 2.9 version, when Laiwang was still under the B2B group, Lu had already begun promoting it externally, and he himself was a heavy user of Laiwang.

Currently, most of Alibaba's wireless businesses are directly overseen by Lu. Regarding the Laiwang product, Alibaba's senior management even proposed a goal of covering 100 million users within a year, making it one of the core businesses in Alibaba's social networking domain.

Moreover, Alibaba has some products still in the "stealth" phase in the mobile internet space, which will play an important commercial role in the future, such as Etao Fire Eye. It not only provides technical support for other Alibaba products but also conducts experiments through mini-games like scanning Renminbi and providing shopping guidance. Taobao Secondhand aims to establish a mobile buying and selling platform, supporting users to complete the function of posting goods without using a PC and also supports wireless shopping.

These existing advantages have been strengthened on mobile devices, but from another perspective, Alibaba's advantage in transactions develops relatively slowly in the mobile internet, and the explosive growth period of mobile commerce lags behind, giving other competitors opportunities to develop.

In mobile payment, Alibaba feels a great sense of crisis. The threat and potential of Tencent's WeChat in payment and O2O fields, as well as Baidu's related layouts, all make it possible for Alibaba to be overtaken by these two companies.

The above-mentioned industry insider pointed out, "The anxiety and emphasis of Jack Ma and Alibaba's senior management come from recognizing that they must act quickly now to avoid being disrupted by others, and secondly, to play a defensive role."

Below is the content of Jack Ma's internal forum post:

"Currently, 80% of my communication tools are Laiwang. I used to think Laiwang lacked uniqueness, wasn't distinctive enough, but now I can't live without it. My biggest pleasure lies in helping it grow and perfect itself... A good product comes from use, from perfection. Perhaps its greatest feature is the indomitable spirit of tens of thousands of employees, using the perseverance of Yu Gong to challenge X-something..."

"Haha, ten years ago, no one would have believed that Taobao could beat the giant eBay, and ten years later, why can't we give it a try? Many people regret not having the opportunity to participate in that battle, but this time we all can participate. Whoever doesn't participate, shouldn't be in this company. Because this is something each of us can do, this isn't a strategy, it's the effort of Alibabans to fight for survival rights in the wireless era."

"This time, each of us can help Laiwang. Don't tell me you can't, unless you don't want to. Today's wireless internet, though WeChat looks so powerful, is similar to the internet landscape in 2003, back then Sina, Sohu, and NetEase weren't invincible either. The game of wireless internet has just begun..."

"We aren't just competing in technology and strength; we're competing in the unity, perseverance, speed, and patience of every Alibaban... Besides, in terms of technology and strength, we're not inferior to anyone. If we don't achieve success in wireless, we shouldn't even consider going public!"

"PS: The ridiculous idea of 100+ (each Alibaban must have 100 external users on Laiwang by the end of November) came from me and was insisted upon by me. If an Alibaban doesn't reach 100 users outside the company by the end of November, it will be considered as giving up on the red envelope. Red envelopes shouldn't be given anyway. Of course, having over 100 doesn't guarantee a red envelope, but not having 100 definitely means no red envelope."
