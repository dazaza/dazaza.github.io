Squatter Rush: A New Shortcut to Wealth - Few Celebrities at Home and Abroad Are Spared

by xwkj0983 on 2012-02-23 10:10:21

The practice of preemptive website domain registration has become a new shortcut to wealth. Recently, the suddenly popular American-Chinese player Jeremy Lin has also been "preemptively registered," with 22 related domains in both Chinese and English already snapped up. Major travel websites such as eLong, Ctrip, and Tongcheng have successively reclaimed double-pinyin domains, sparking the dragon year's domain name scramble. With the rapid development of e-commerce, preemptive domain registration has also become a trend, even evolving into a new tool for generating wealth.

Jeremy Lin's Pinyin Domain at 260,000 Yuan

Jeremy Lin, who studied economics at Harvard University, naturally understands the importance of protecting his commercial value. After paying $1,625, Jeremy Lin has already applied for the trademark rights of "Linsanity." The trademark covers the use of various clothing items as well as action figures, beverages, and backpacks. However, the issue surrounding Jeremy Lin's "domain name" is far from over. In China, the competition for Jeremy Lin's domain name has reached a fever pitch. A netizen discovered that the domain name linshuhao.com had already been preemptively registered and was being auctioned online, with the auction price exceeding 260,000 yuan.

Few Celebrities Domestically or Internationally Have Escaped

Chinese domain names, like English domain names, follow the internationally accepted principle of "first come, first served." The rush to register celebrity Chinese domain names is much crazier than imagined, ranging from Confucius, Laozi, Bai Juyi to Qin Shi Huang, Genghis Khan, Qianlong, and then Ba Jin, Louis Cha, even Shakespeare, Li Ka-shing, Warren Buffett, and others have had their Chinese domain names preemptively registered.

Industry insiders analyze that the reason why celebrity domain names are frequently preemptively registered is due to their high economic value. Celebrity names have special appeal; registering a Chinese domain name using a celebrity's name makes it particularly easy to recognize and remember. Moreover, leveraging the celebrity effect can direct traffic to one's own website, increasing visitor flow. On the other hand, virtual hosting, as an investment, also has significant economic potential.

Self-Proclaimed Best Businessman

Xiamen is one of the three most developed internet cities in China alongside Beijing and Shanghai, known as the "capital of domains." Xiamen leads the nation in per capita domain ownership, with more than half of the national market share for domain registration coming from Xiamen. Many ordinary citizens are also enthusiastic about investing in website domains.

"All over China, there are no more than 100 people who have truly made money through buying and selling domains, and I should be the best among them." This is how Cai Wensheng, respected by many webmasters as the father of personal websites and a famous angel investor in China, describes himself.

He has the confidence to say so. When it comes to domain registration, one cannot help but mention Cai Wensheng, not only in China but also globally. At the age of 42, Cai Wensheng was born in Shishi, Quanzhou. He started doing domain business in 1999 and became one of China's most successful domain investors.

Cai Wensheng knows almost all the stories behind the domain names of China's well-known websites, and many of these domains are closely related to him. For example, g.cn was sold by Cai Wensheng to Google China for reportedly millions of dollars. Additionally, he owns the domain names of hundreds of local cities in China, enough to create a local portal alliance.

Domain Investment

Not Everyone Can Afford It

"Ordinary people trying to preemptively register domains now might not be a good time anymore because good domains have almost all been registered. One can only identify domains with future market potential and then buy them from their owners, waiting for their appreciation in the future." Cai Liwen is from Zhangzhou. Although only a member of the '80s generation, he founded his company as early as 1999.

Due to its low investment requirement and lack of high technical content, many common people are enthusiastic about investing in personal website domains, hoping that the domains they hold will soar in value in the future and sell for a good price. In reality, domain investment carries considerable risks and requires a significant amount of capital, making it unaffordable for the average person.

On a certain domain registrar's website, registering a domain ending with .COM, .NET, or .ORG costs 100 yuan annually, while a domain ending with .CN costs 80 yuan annually. For instance, if Cai Wensheng owns over 300,000 domains and each domain costs 100 yuan annually, the total cost would be 30 million yuan, not including the cost of acquiring the domains. Such expenses are beyond the means of the average person.

How to Protect Domains

Common domain disputes mainly involve conflicts between domains and other domains, as well as with trademarks, trade names, name rights, corporate name rights, and other rights. Common methods for resolving domain disputes include arbitration, litigation, and settlement. Different mechanisms for resolving domain disputes are not entirely the same and require attention.

Currently, international domain disputes are mostly resolved through arbitration. Regardless of whether in arbitration or litigation procedures, involving multiple different rights protection scopes, terms, and judgments of malice, fully showcasing factors favorable to oneself has a significant impact on the arbitration panel or court's determination of domain ownership.

Claimants should collect evidence materials beneficial to themselves under the guidance of experienced lawyers and other advisors, form clear and comprehensive written materials, and actively respond. It is equally undesirable for defendants to abandon their defense.
