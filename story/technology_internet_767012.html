Forbes reviews Ballmer's achievements and shortcomings: Microsoft's market value has been reduced by half, but its profit has increased nearly three times.

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 11:08:38

Ballmer delivered a farewell speech in Seattle this Friday

　　According to foreign media reports on September 29, Ballmer delivered a farewell speech in Seattle this Friday. In his speech, he briefly reviewed the reasons for the success of several major Internet technology companies and praised Microsoft's "continuous efforts." The Forbes website recently outlined the legacy left by this Microsoft CEO who served for more than ten years, including a halving of Microsoft's market value but doubling of profits, most products being unremarkable except for the standout Xbox.

　　The following is an abstract of the original text:

　　Market value halved but profits doubled

　　On January 1, 2000, Ballmer succeeded Bill Gates as CEO of Microsoft. At that time, Microsoft's stock price was experiencing a downturn after reaching its peak on December 23, 1999. When Ballmer took over, the stock price was $58.719, with a market capitalization of $616.3 billion. As of this week's U.S. market close, Microsoft's stock price was $33.27, with a market capitalization of $277.14 billion, a decrease of 55% from then.

　　When Ballmer became Microsoft's CEO, the dot-com bubble in the internet industry was still growing larger. Microsoft's quarterly revenue at the time was $6.11 billion, net profit was $2.44 billion, and diluted earnings per share were $0.44. By the end of Ballmer's first year as Microsoft's CEO, the internet bubble began to burst, affecting the entire tech industry. By the end of 2000, Microsoft's stock price had fallen to $21.688.

　　However, in many subsequent years, even after Microsoft's failed $2009 acquisition of Yahoo!, the company's stock price did not reach new lows. Microsoft weathered the global financial crisis of 2008. Strong revenue and diversified products allowed Microsoft to secure enough cash during a period of tight credit. In the previous quarter of this year, Microsoft's revenue was $17.41 billion, net profit was $5.11 billion, and diluted earnings per share were $0.66.

　　From another perspective, during Ballmer's tenure as Microsoft's CEO, the company's annual profit rose from about $25 billion to about $70 billion, with an average annual growth rate of 16.4%. This performance surpassed many famous CEOs, such as Jack Welch of General Electric and Lou Gerstner of IBM, but fell short of Steve Jobs. During Jobs' tenure as Apple's CEO, Apple's annual profit grew from $786 million to $25.922 billion.

　　Lack of focus in seven large acquisitions

　　Compared to his predecessor Gates, during his 13-year tenure as Microsoft's CEO, Ballmer adopted a more experimental attitude towards acquisitions and external investments. These acquisitions and investments yielded different results. Some smaller-scale acquisitions often brought transformative new products, such as Kinect and the famous "Halo" series games on the Xbox platform. However, large-scale acquisitions either failed, like Yahoo!, or eventually turned into disasters, like aQuantive. Meanwhile, Ballmer remained very decisive in some large-scale acquisitions. During his term, Microsoft carried out seven large-scale acquisitions worth over $1 billion.

　　From the data, it appears that Microsoft under Ballmer lacked a clear direction in large-scale acquisitions. Perhaps the lack of focus caused difficulties for Microsoft. Microsoft's attempts to integrate non-software companies, such as aQuantive, were unsuccessful. This suggests that Microsoft may face challenges in integrating Nokia in the future.

　　Major acquisitions during Ballmer's term include:

　　In 2000, acquired Visio for $1.4 billion. Visio is now part of Office.

　　In 2002, acquired Navision for $1.45 billion. Navision later developed into Microsoft Nydamic Nav, one of Microsoft's main enterprise resource planning products.

　　In 2007, acquired aQuantive for $6.3 billion, one of the largest acquisitions in the global advertising industry. However, Microsoft suffered a $6.2 billion asset impairment charge due to this acquisition in 2007.

　　In 2008, acquired Fast Search for $1.2 billion. The core technology of Fast Search became Microsoft's Bing search engine.

　　In 2011, acquired Skype for $8.5 billion. In 2012, Skype held a 34% share of the global internet telephony market.

　　In 2012, acquired Yammer for $1.2 billion. Yammer has become part of Microsoft's Office business.

　　In 2013, acquired Nokia for $7.2 billion.

　　Cautious external investments

　　Before Ballmer, Microsoft was very active in investing in external businesses. However, after Ballmer became CEO, Microsoft rarely made large investments in external companies. Because Ballmer's focus was on Microsoft internally. In the early 21st century, Microsoft sold shares of other companies it held, including its $150 million investment in Apple.

　　However, in recent years, Microsoft has made some noteworthy investments:

　　In 2007, acquired 1.6% of Facebook's shares for $240 million, currently valued at approximately $2 billion.

　　In 2012, acquired 17% of Nook Media's shares for $300 million, entering the e-reader and tablet computer market. To date, Microsoft has not launched any products based on this investment.

　　In 2012, invested $20 million in Klout. Klout scores users based on their social media activity. Klout has been integrated into the Bing search engine.

　　Only the Xbox stands out in the product line

　　When Ballmer became Microsoft's CEO, Windows was at its peak. Windows held over 90% of the PC operating system market share. However, with the development of new products like tablets and smartphones, Windows' share of the PC market dropped significantly, prompting Microsoft to take many measures to reverse this trend.

　　In fact, Microsoft was the first company to develop a tablet computer. This might surprise you, but it's true. In 2002, Microsoft developed an unsuccessful tablet computer called Tablet PC. Eventually, Microsoft only integrated the concept of a tablet into the Windows 8 system and launched the Surface series of tablets. By then, Apple had already become the market leader. Due to poor sales of the Surface tablets, Microsoft incurred a $900 million asset impairment charge.

　　Microsoft's Pocket PC product line was also one of the earliest smartphones, launched in 2000. Because Microsoft attempted to replicate the experience of Windows PCs on mobile phones, it failed to make significant changes to the phone experience like the iPhone. Microsoft's acquisition of Nokia was seen as a strategy to replicate Apple's integration of software and hardware as the way forward.

　　Microsoft's music player Zune and smartwatch SPOT also faced the issue of being introduced early but failing to succeed. The SPOT watch could receive data from the internet and was considered the precursor to smartwatch products from companies like Pebble, Sony, and Samsung. Ultimately, Microsoft abandoned both product lines.

　　Microsoft's Xbox and Xbox 360 achieved tremendous success and will launch the next-generation Xbox One this fall. Initially, when Microsoft launched the Xbox, many industry experts believed it would fail under pressure from Sony, Nintendo, and Sega. However, what happened was that Sega exited the game console business, Nintendo struggled, and only Sony remained a major competitor. Through the Kinect motion controller, Microsoft once again became an innovator.
