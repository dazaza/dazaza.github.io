A entrepreneur's autobiography: How I sell fish online

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 17:29:44

　　In fact, when I first started selling fish online, many of my friends around me thought I was crazy. 　

　　When everyone's eyes were on real estate, technology, mobile phones, and cars, I chose to sell fish. It was indeed a very difficult decision, but it felt solid.

　　How can O2O be played in a novel way? Should we abandon WeChat? Who is more awesome between Lei Jun and J.Wong? Can BlackBerry make a comeback? Can Cook take over from Master Jobs? I don't care. Because these are all far from us. The dream is big, but the reality is small. People should live a bit more mundanely.

　　You Cai failed, and it died with great reluctance. But I still have no doubt about the huge potential of fresh produce in e-commerce.

　　In 2012, I began trying B2C online fish sales. You Cai died, but I survived. Of course, this might be related to the fact that I am just a small-scale start-up company with low operating costs and easy execution. However, some of the experiences I've explored I believe are still somewhat valuable, and I'm willing to share them with you all. Perhaps they can give some inspiration to those friends who are starting businesses online selling fish, vegetables, or fruits.

　　1. About product positioning

　　My product positioning is: never do daily products at low prices, only do mid-range products. In the early stage, don't do vegetables and meat, only do frozen seafood.

　　Let me explain. I believe your biggest competitor in e-commerce for fresh produce is the local farmers' market. Compared to them, I think I am at a disadvantage. First, the traditional group of shoppers is concentrated among middle-aged and elderly people, most of whom will never have the habit of buying fish online. Second, you may say that selling fish online can save you tens of thousands or even hundreds of thousands in stall fees compared to a vendor, but the harsh reality is: compared to vendors who restock almost every morning at midnight with almost zero inventory, your warehousing cost, labor cost, and delivery cost throughout the year will be much higher than theirs.

　　You Cai mainly failed here. Observe its product structure; there are almost no mid-to-high-end products, most are common agricultural by-products found in farmers' markets, which directly faces the impact of local farmers' markets. You want to change the purchasing habits of traditional aunties, just like you want to suddenly make cows stop eating grass and start eating meat—it's not realistic.

　　Compared to supermarkets, I found my advantages are quite obvious: short procurement cycles, fresh products (this refers to the cycle of bulk goods), prices nearly 40% cheaper (don’t think supermarkets are cheap), and a younger customer base (I’ve observed that most supermarket shoppers are aged between 25-40), which fits my requirements well.

　　Seafood has many classifications, mainly divided into live seafood, chilled seafood, and frozen seafood (I won’t explain each one here individually; if interested, you can search yourself). Live seafood has the highest storage requirements; the price difference between a live fish and a dead fish can be around 50%, so it’s not suitable for small-cost operators like me. Frozen seafood after preliminary processing is the easiest to preserve, with minimal loss. Generally speaking, as long as the temperature is maintained, the shelf life can be one and a half to two years (Ding Jingtao said that You Cai’s fresh produce loss rate was 5%; I simply don’t believe it).

　　Therefore, I positioned my competitors as supermarkets. I selected some raw material products used in self-service restaurants, Western restaurants, mid-to-high-end Chinese restaurants, and Japanese cuisine. These products have high profit margins, are not easily found on the market, and are easy to preserve. This way, I avoided the impact of farmers' markets, and the product's own grade also enhances its value.

　　2. About pricing strategy

　　Unlike phones, cars, and houses, this market is extremely sensitive to price.

　　For example, for the same pound of cabbage or salmon, if your price is more than 5 yuan higher than the market price, customers won't be interested in you. You can only attract them by being cheaper than the market price. And the profit margin on a pound of cabbage versus a salmon... well, I don't need to say, you understand.

　　Regarding pricing, Ding Jingtao of You Cai once said: "The price of tomatoes might differ by 2 to 5 times. The quality of vegetables on the market varies greatly, and their nutritional safety is inconsistent. You Cai tries to select the best quality when purchasing. For example, cucumbers come in different prices such as 1.2 yuan and 1.5 yuan per piece; we would choose the 1.5 yuan ones."

　　"So the vegetable prices on You Cai are slightly higher than those in the market. Ding Jingtao emphasized that if the quality is the same, You Cai is definitely cheaper."

　　For the above statements, I would like to say: General Manager Ding must have never sold vegetables himself.

　　90% of aunties absolutely won't buy cabbage that is priced higher than the market price, no matter how much you exaggerate about the cabbage, even if your cabbage is green organic super amazing cabbage. Aunties are practical; whoever offers lower prices with acceptable quality gets their business.

　　Since the product is positioned as agricultural by-products, even the slightest price fluctuation can significantly affect product sales. If Ding General follows this line of thinking, then he can only do agricultural by-products with quality that barely passes inspection but are much cheaper than the market, taking the volume route. This allows him to capture a large market share with low gross margins. However, for a company in the early stages of entrepreneurship, without market experience and market recognition, aiming to capture the market right from the start is a very dangerous thing.

　　Without sufficient gross margins, it's hard to even carry out market promotions. Unfortunately, Ding General's thinking is both wanting to do premium agricultural by-products and wanting aunties to accept prices higher than the market, while not allowing them to inspect the goods on the spot. This undoubtedly pushes him into a dead end.

　　So, I set the price of my products realistically; it must be below the market price but still highly profitable. I don't care about market share; I only care whether the gross profit is high enough.

　　Would you rather earn 1 fen from 1000 people or 1 yuan from 100 people? I know how to calculate this.

　　3. About channel costs

　　Here is a small suggestion from me: any industry you do must have your own special channels. Such channels aren't necessarily monopolizable, but relatively few product channels, making your product somewhat unique and scarce.

　　When I was doing wholesale, I was already thinking about this issue, and solving it was quite simple. First, try to avoid competitive first-tier cities as much as possible; after all, how can you run barefoot against someone driving a car? Second, choose less popular origins. My product origins are concentrated in three places: South America, Africa, and Northeast Asia. The weakness of big players (including other large aquatic companies) lies in their inability to pivot quickly; they are large but not agile. All I need to do is observe the market, choose the right products, and make decisions myself. By the time they get approval level by level, my product channel foundation is already firmly established.

　　You Cai positioned its channels domestically, which had too low a threshold. The principle of wholesale is actually similar to group buying, mainly relying on volume. So today you can negotiate with those farmers, and tomorrow they can just as easily replicate your model. Today you can create a You Cai, and tomorrow they can copy another "Yousaihua" (Oil Vegetable Flower).

　　4. About marketing models

　　This is something we must discuss in detail.

　　You Cai positioned itself as "delivering vegetables like milk," attempting to become a closed ecosystem similar to iOS, where customers can enjoy doorstep vegetable delivery services without leaving home. This involves two issues: 1. How to attract potential customers to your platform? 2. Why should customers stay? (which Ding referred to as increasing customer stickiness)

　　I noticed You Cai earlier due to a report on Sina. Since the product differences were not significant, I curiously clicked to check You Cai. When I opened its UI, I was quite disappointed. An outdated interface with no design sense whatsoever. Apart from descriptions of product types and prices, there was no other relevant information. The variety was insufficient yet included items like disinfectant and toothpaste that had nothing to do with "vegetables"...

　　I really couldn't figure out how You Cai could retain customers. Opening its webpage felt like walking into a roadside shop filled with all sorts of miscellaneous items, making me forget that I originally came to look at "vegetables".

　　Relying solely on an uninnovative milk-style sales model of order-delivery-after-sales to attract customers was undoubtedly You Cai's biggest failure.

　　Therefore, before I started, I had people prepare my products into various dishes. For just one type of crab, I made it into stews, soups, stir-fries, braises, etc. Then I hired professional photographers to artistically process the dishes, making them look as delicious as possible.

　　In this regard, I borrowed from IKEA's concept—tempting customers with finished products but only selling semi-finished products to them. Letting customers fully experience the pleasure of achievement is much stronger than giving them a finished product.

　　Later, when many customers exchanged ideas on QQ, I simply talked to them about how to use my ingredients to cook dishes instead of discussing how delicious the fish or crab was. Taobao actually doesn't sell products but pictures, and the same applies to fresh produce. Eventually, I stopped shouting about selling products and only chatted with food lovers about how to eat them best.

　　Actually, targeting the younger demographic, many people like to eat but don't know how. They really want to eat, and at this point, you need to teach them how to make things taste good. (In this aspect, sometimes I even think Dianping is better suited for fresh produce).

　　Later, more and more customers gave positive feedback. I observed that 80% of them were food enthusiasts, and 20% came because of referrals from friends. For this reason, I specifically created several QQ groups where members freely exchange foodie experiences and compare seafood from other sources under the premise of not violating national laws. Don't fear competition; the more competition, the more it highlights the uniqueness and price advantage of your products.

　　In terms of models, You Cai aimed to create an electronic + commerce model, but personally, I think this was also a mistake. There are two paths to choose from: one is to create a platform-only model like Taobao, establishing relationships with offline wholesalers, and the platform itself does not engage in commerce. The second is to focus only on commerce, quickly building various regional distribution platforms, including SNS, regional BBS, microblog marketing, WeChat marketing, etc., focusing on channel quality and price.

　　I lean towards the latter, after all, it allows direct contact with your customers.

　　My current model is primarily offline wholesaling, mainly supplying hotels, buffets, and sushi restaurants, etc. Online retail is the main focus, with prices slightly higher than offline. But I don't build my own platform; I only affiliate with several popular local BBS communities, attracting customers through their traffic, and if the product gets a good response, it spreads virally.

　　Another benefit of this approach is that it can solve the trust crisis. Doing fresh produce online in China, besides the difficulty of standardization (which I'll discuss later), another issue is the lack of consumer confidence in the hygiene of online fresh produce. To put it bluntly, after drinking Sanlu milk and eating Sudan red eggs, the general public has lost significant trust in merchants. However, if you rely on word-of-mouth promotion, this problem can be resolved.

　　In terms of profitability, I adopt small-batch retailing. My minimum standard is five pounds per package, and I don't retail one or two pounds. Of course, the price is tempting, and the volume goes up.

　　5. About warehousing and logistics

　　I dare say that apart from procurement costs, warehousing and logistics are absolutely another major expense. This includes labor + transportation + warehousing costs + losses.

　　You Cai adopted the idea of using milk boxes, delivering door-to-door at fixed points every day. I think there's a big problem with this: suppose, just before I finish work, I ordered a pound of cabbage, two pounds of pork belly, and half a pound of ginger and garlic on You Cai. But when I got home, I realized that what I saw online was very different from what I see in reality, so I decided not to keep it. And You Cai's customer service was very accommodating, agreeing to unconditional returns after communication.

　　My question is: What am I supposed to eat for dinner tonight?

　　If I have such an experience once, I probably won't buy agricultural by-products on You Cai again just to save a few bucks, because I'd starve...

　　And You Cai not only loses orders and customers, but also faces losses (some products degrade in quality after being left overnight or out of the warehouse for a period), along with the repeated labor and transportation costs for return trips.

　　This is the biggest problem encountered by e-commerce in fresh produce: how to solve product standardization.

　　Frankly speaking, I currently don't have a good solution for this problem. I've heard complaints like "how is it different from the picture online?" All I can do is compensate with service—if unsatisfied, refund it.

　　Because my company is small, I can only save costs. Based on the number of regional orders, I deliver at fixed times and inspect goods face-to-face. This way, I can explain to customers in person to eliminate misunderstandings (some are indeed caused by the customer's insufficient understanding of the product), and avoid the repeated losses of a second trip.
