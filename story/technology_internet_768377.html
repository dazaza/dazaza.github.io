Programmers Abroad: Six Months of Entrepreneurship in Thailand for a Dutch Developer

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 17:45:19

What I learnt from bootstrapping my startup from Thailand in six months

I am an entrepreneur on YouTube who developed Panda Mix Show and a video analytics platform called Tubelytics; I am a web developer who often travels the world with just a backpack. I come from Amsterdam, Netherlands, but I am currently in Asia.

Recollections: Six Months of Entrepreneurship in Thailand

Saturday, October 12, 2013

I have been working in Thailand for six months from Amsterdam. I chose here because of its low cost of living and good security. Since my visa is about to expire, I have to return home. Before leaving, I want to summarize my experiences over these six months. This place might also be a suitable choice for you.

Advantages of Thailand

Low Cost of Living

Life here is really easy. If you live outside Bangkok, you can live comfortably on $1000 per month. With $1000 per month, you can rent an apartment or hotel with a pool, eat out every day, drink some wine at night, and even occasionally indulge in sushi.

Due to the affordability of everything, the quality of food and life is quite good, making it an excellent place to start a business. In the early stages of entrepreneurship, a low cost of living means that your company can operate several times longer without income. On average, I spent about $1400 (equivalent to €1000) per month during my six months. My spending was slightly higher than average mainly because I went to Bangkok once in a while, which is much more expensive compared to other places.

Sustained Focus

Moving to the other side of the world allows you to gain incredible focus, enabling you to truly immerse yourself in what you're doing.

During this time, my already profitable YouTube site "The Panda Mix Show" saw further growth, thanks to automating most of the work and handing over the remaining tasks to two employees. In addition to this, I also built and launched my video analytics project "Tubelytics," which has gradually gained attention with the arrival of the first batch of users.

I could concentrate on work here as I did when I was 12 years old. Back then, I was still a student, and my parents took care of my daily needs, so I had a lot of time to do my own things (like learning Photoshop). Thanks to the low cost of consumption here, I can be as carefree as I was back then.

You can stay in a hotel where someone cleans your room and prepares clean towels for you every day. The Wi-Fi here must be free, and there's no property tax in hotels. They will also iron and wash your clothes for you, even folding them neatly. You can eat all three meals outside, and not having to cook means avoiding a bunch of trivial chores like going to the supermarket or washing dishes. You can always choose a hotel close to the office, so you don't have to spend a lot of time commuting like most people do. I calculated that all these can save me 6 hours a day, which is one-third of the total time I'm awake!

All of this is possible due to the low cost of living and the strong purchasing power of Western currencies. Whether this phenomenon is fair is another topic.

No Need to Worry About Social Life

After being abroad for several months, you may find yourself feeling a bit strange. (I realized this as early as 2009 in Korea.) Being away from home for a long time makes you gradually distant from your homeland's society and customs, and as a foreigner, locals won't accept you into their society either. You become a person who doesn't fit in anywhere.

For those who need a sense of belonging and identity, this can cause harm or even make them lose themselves. But if you are a very independent person, this situation actually gives you unlimited freedom to write your own life story without being tied down by any social structure. You may even become a master who excels in different cultures.

Loneliness is a friend to entrepreneurs; in an environment away from the crowd, you are more likely to come up with novel ideas that others cannot imagine.

Many Office Spaces

There are many office spaces in Thailand. Bangkok has huge Launchpad and comfortable Hubba, both of which fully consider the needs of startups. The second largest city, Chiang Mai, has PunSpace, which is more suitable for entrepreneurs who pursue taste.

Charming Leisure Time

I am accustomed to setting aside a whole period of time to work intensively and achieve goals within a few weeks. Then I take a two-week break to enjoy myself on Paradise Island or in Bangkok.

Thailand has long been known as one of the best vacation destinations in the world. It has the best weather and charming beaches, and sometimes beach resorts cost only $5 per night. Although I still prefer the nightclubs in London, Amsterdam, or Berlin, Bangkok does have some nice rooftop bars and districts suitable for a drink, and drinks here are as cheap as food.

Disadvantages

Distracted Attention

Although there are many conditions here that allow for focused work, remember that this is still a tourist destination. Remember the benefits I mentioned? Beaches, good weather, and cheap drinks can be huge obstacles for people with weak willpower.

Frequent Visa Renewals

Visas in Thailand are a painful matter. Compared to its neighbor Vietnam, visas here are too troublesome. With a multiple-entry visa, I can stay in Thailand for 60 days, then I have to go to the immigration office for renewal, which can only be done once for 30 days. After that, I must leave the country and re-enter to activate another 60 days, then renew for 30 days again, and then leave the country again.

Two rounds of this process allow me to stay here for half a year, but each renewal costs $60, takes a whole day of queuing, and involves running around with the visa (sometimes even going to Myanmar). This is far from ideal, and the Thai government should simplify the procedures since most of us foreigners come here to generate income.

Pornography Tourism and Prostitution Industry

In today's Thailand, the sex industry is still booming. It's not uncommon for an 18-year-old girl to hang out with a 65-year-old foreigner on the street. If you ignore this, it's fine, but I feel uncomfortable. Prostitution is everywhere; if you go to a nightclub for a gathering or dancing, whether it's a themed or non-themed event, there will be prostitution. I don't like the sex industry; these girls could be in more interesting industries rather than selling their bodies to customers for money. Talking to a girl for an hour only to find out she just wants your money is frustrating. Thailand is a Buddhist country, and the sex industry seems very out of place here.

Not being able to identify with pornography or being occasionally harassed isn't the worst part; worse is that the sex industry damages the country's image. Even Rihanna admitted on Twitter that Thailand is perceived as a place for cheap sex and ping pong shows.

"Either I was phuck wasted lastnight, or I saw a Thai woman pull a live bird, 2 turtles, razors, shoot darts and ping pong, all out of her pu$$y" — Rihanna (@rihanna) September 21, 2013

The government should take full responsibility. Prostitution and sex shows are illegal, but Thailand has been ineffective in combating corruption, leading to police often being bribed to turn a blind eye. This industry is deeply entrenched in the country, and unless the president implements strong measures, it will be difficult to see fundamental changes. Unfortunately, the current government is not effective, and people have mostly lost confidence in politics. I don't see significant changes happening anytime soon.

It is well-known that Asia is rising, and a country must position itself: does it want to be a paradise for cheap sex or an economic engine of the future? Thailand has little time to choose, and its neighbors like Vietnam have already taken action and chosen against pornography.

Every Foreigner is a Passing Visitor

When I first arrived in the large northern city of Chiang Mai—a hub for remote-working expats—I met many talented individuals, mostly from the United States, Europe, and Australia, who had profitable online businesses. There was a great circle of friends, and we often worked and had fun together, forming a small overseas community.

But after a few months, people gradually disappeared. At the time, I felt that everyone was like a passing visitor. Entrepreneurs providing online services, due to the ability to work remotely, travel lightly and are highly influenced by personal preferences and emotions. The cost of changing locations is low, so when they lose interest in a place, they can simply move on to the next stop. I am also one of them. Brief interactions make it hard to form deep friendships, and we seem to be constantly on the move.

Heat

Fortunately, Thailand is equipped with air conditioning indoors everywhere, and the temperature in cafes or office buildings is below 75°F (24°C). However, if you go outside, you can't escape the heat. If you walk to work, 10 minutes on the road can make you sweat profusely. On some scorching days, people have to stay indoors all day.

Crazy Inflation

At the time of writing this blog post (end of 2013), inflation had reached crazy levels. Although I don't have official data, everyone here feels it. When I came to Bangkok in April, 40 baht (€1) could buy a chicken rice meal (kow-man-gai), 650 baht (€15) could get you a private hostel for a night, and 40 baht (€1) could cover ten bus stops. When I returned after six months, food prices had risen by 25%, accommodation by 15%, and bus fares by 10%. It is estimated that the annual inflation rate may reach 35%. And 35% does not include the appreciation of the baht against the euro and dollar. This means that the cost of living here will soon no longer be as low.

Surprise

Startup Scene in Thailand

The local startup scene here is still at an elementary school level, as evident from various meetings, hackathons, etc. There is a lot of positive energy around startups, and plenty of enthusiasm, but the prevailing mindset remains rooted in joining big companies and becoming part of the regular workforce. For example, most local programmers I've encountered use Microsoft's proprietary C# language. Why aren't people using open-source languages like C, Java, or Python? I quickly discovered that the reason lies in the fact that most schools teach C#, so students can easily find jobs in large companies. Local companies prefer C#, but this is obviously not the preferred choice for entrepreneurs.

The high poverty rate and significant wealth gap lead most Thais to focus more on short-term benefits. People cannot afford to spend several months or even years developing an uncertain internet or mobile application. This unfortunate reality results in entrepreneurship becoming a privilege for the wealthy, yet the best ideas for entrepreneurship often have nothing to do with money.

Currently, many places where the startup wave is just beginning have similar situations to Thailand: lots of talk, little action. Large Thai telecom companies like TRUE, DTAC, AIS have some incubators that provide training courses, hold competitions, and set up funds, but they have no plans to invest in good projects! There are some industry giants here, all rich, but they have never considered investing in local startups. Big players ignore startups, so who can grow the startup ecosystem? Clearly, what they do for entrepreneurs is just small encouragement, far from true incubation. This is harmful to the development of the local industry. Foreign tech giants like South Korea's NAVER can take advantage of this to enter the Thai market, overshadowing local enterprises. NAVER introduced free inter-device communication service "LINE," which quickly dominated the Thai market, something that should have been done by a local company.

The startup funding I see here ranges from $50,000 to $500,000, which is peanuts compared to Silicon Valley and even far behind Europe. This is so out of sync with the rising Asia we see and the massive Southeast Asian market. Well, if this place is rising, why isn't there active capital investment? The answer is that the money isn't being invested in startups; people are too conservative, and that's the culture here. This must change, and it will change, though it will take time, but the potential is undeniable.

Conclusion

These six months have been wonderful, and I would recommend anyone who wants to start a business outside their home country to come here. Despite all the disadvantages, there are still more advantages here. Life here is comfortable and safe, and the Thai people are the friendliest I have ever seen. Some Americans here always say that the locals' smiles are insincere, but I think it's the Americans who give off that vibe. I have never felt that way about the locals. The Thais are very friendly, and they are always happy about your arrival.
