Did the surge in stock index futures trading result in a dead cat bounce for the market?

by huaxun924 on 2010-04-16 15:29:12

Historic moment! At 9:15 AM today, the CSI 300 stock index futures made its dazzling debut at 3,399 points. By 11:00 AM, the main contract IF1005 closed at 3,434 points, up by 1%; IF1006 closed at 3,473 points, up by 2.17%; IF1009 closed at 3,535.4 points, up by 3.98%; IF1012 closed at 3,576.4 points, with an impressive increase of 5.18%. However, judging from the Shanghai and Shenzhen market conditions, the stock index futures did not immediately bring a positive impact to the market. The two markets opened slightly lower this morning, followed by a significant sell-off dragged down by the real estate sector. By 11:00 AM, the Shanghai Composite Index closed at 3,138.88 points, down by 0.8%; the CSI 300 closed at 3,365 points, down by 0.82%.

In fact, it is reasonable that there was a severe divergence between the spot market and the futures market. First, the listed price of 3,399 points was slightly low, leaving a large space for the rise of stock index futures. Second, like the stock market, investors in the futures market also like to speculate on new varieties. A sharp rise in the opening of stock index futures was expected. However, the number of accounts opened for stock index futures is still relatively small, especially the number of institutional accounts, making it difficult for the surge in futures to get a market response. Therefore, the decline in the CSI 300 index is not surprising. According to statistics, currently, the number of accounts opened for stock index futures is 9,137, including 8,944 individual investors and 193 general legal entities, meaning more than 90% are retail accounts, and less than 200 accounts are institutions.

More importantly, the start of trading in stock index futures means the end of the one-way market in the A-share market. This is a major correction to the past one-way trading method. Investors' bearish expectations can be realized in the futures market. Due to the emergence of short-selling means, the operation methods of dominant funds on weighted individual stocks will change. This requires some time to observe new patterns.

Therefore, some institutions believe that after the launch of stock index futures, due to the transaction transfer effect and short-term speculative profit-taking selling of stocks, it may lead to a decline in the CSI 300 index, which poses a requirement for institutions to hedge their portfolios during the fall.

In addition, international experience shows that the probability of a stock market rising before the launch of stock index futures and falling afterward is relatively high. For example, the launch time of the S&P 500 index futures was in 1982. One month before its launch, the spot market rose by 2.62%; six months before, it fell by 3.65%. One month after the launch, the spot market fell by 0.72%. In 1986, the Singapore Financial Futures Exchange launched the NIKKEI225 index futures. One month before the launch, the spot market rose by 7.2%, and six months before, it rose by 34.51%. Although one month after the launch, the spot market fell by 4.24%, the launch of stock index futures will not change the long-term trend of the market. The A-share market will not therefore change its original price determination mechanism or cause long-term trend changes.

Facts have proven that in the long run, stock index futures can effectively reduce the overall risk of the market. When people no longer make money only by going long on stocks, they will invest more rationally; when the market falls, risks can also be hedged through stock index futures.
