Introduction:

For China's art trading market, 2010 was a record-breaking year. In November, the Bainbridges auction house in the UK sold a Qing Qianlong famille rose porcelain, pierced decoration, "Good Fortune and Surplus" rotating heart vase for a staggering 550 million yuan RMB, breaking the previous record set five years ago by a Yuan dynasty blue-and-white porcelain jar with a Gui Guzi design. This sale price also represents the highest price ever achieved globally for a Chinese art piece. Besides this rotating heart vase, Chinese domestic auction houses also performed exceptionally well - on June 3rd, Poly's 5th anniversary spring auction saw Huang Tingjian's calligraphy work "Dizhu Ming" sell for 430 million yuan RMB; on November 20th, China Guardian International autumn auction saw Wang Xizhi's cursive script "Ping'an Tie" high-ancient copy sell for 308 million yuan RMB; on December 10th, Hanhai autumn auction saw Xu Beihong's painting "Ba Ren Ji Shui Tu" sell for 171 million yuan RMB; Zhang Daqian's silk painting "Aihen Lake" sold for 100.8 million yuan, setting a new record for modern Chinese paintings; a Northern Song dynasty guqin "Songshi Jian Yi" started bidding at 16 million yuan and eventually sold for 136.64 million yuan, breaking the world record for musical instruments... According to incomplete statistics, there were more than a dozen art pieces selling for over 100 million yuan throughout the year.

Behind each sky-high price in the art market lies capital speculation. How much of a bubble exists within?

In the past, it was said that "culture should not speak of profit." However, under the strategic framework where the cultural industry has been elevated to a pillar industry of the national economy, culture and capital have become unprecedentedly "close". On one hand, capital actively seeks out opportunities, injecting large amounts of funds into the cultural domain, propelling the development of the cultural industry onto a fast track. On the other hand, cultural products, chased by capital, have become increasingly valuable: art auctions frequently break the 100 million yuan mark, movie box offices surpass the 1 billion yuan threshold, and the cultural sector has become a new favorite in the stock market.

Not only have individual artworks reached new heights, but the total transaction volume of art pieces has also grown rapidly. The total auction sales of major auction companies such as China Guardian and Beijing Poly reached 11.604 billion yuan, nearly double that of the same period last year. By the end of the year, autumn auctions across various regions were still proceeding vigorously, with Poly alone achieving a total auction sale of 5.28 billion yuan, setting a single-season auction record for Chinese art pieces. Art investment may become the third largest investment hotspot after stocks and real estate.

The driving force behind the great leap forward in art prices [bulk paintings] comes from "a large amount of funds withdrawn from the real estate and stock markets entering the art market." This reason has gained recognition from all sides. Five years ago, people regretted why they didn't invest in stocks ten years ago; today, people regret why they didn't buy more properties ten years ago; we regret why we didn't purchase several antiques today. Art investment may become the third largest investment hotspot after stocks and real estate.

In 2010, the state continuously regulated the real estate market, while the stock market remained in consolidation and volatility. A large amount of real estate and stock trading funds had to seek new ways to make money, and many capital giants shifted their funds to collection accounts, making art the next speculative and investment platform after the real estate and stock markets.

Is the art market full of bubbles and speculators?

As records for art transactions are constantly being broken, various voices are circulating within the market, including investors' cheers and observers' doubts. Some collectors believe that China's rapid economic development has driven up the art market, revealing the true value of Chinese art pieces. Others, represented by Ma Weidu, question whether these sky-high priced artworks actually sell, arguing that the art market is accumulating more and more bubbles, and in time, the inflated prices will inevitably drop. These two viewpoints are sharply opposed, and only the future market will provide the answer.

2010 Auction Data

"Incomprehensible"! The data from China's auction market in 2010 shocked everyone.

This year, in the Chinese cultural relics and art market, people often lamented: "It's incomprehensible!"

Antique merchants and art brokers couldn't understand it either. For many years, they operated in this market, buying low and selling high, moving goods from one side to another, or holding them for a few years before selling, maneuvering skillfully like fish in water. Now, despite their continued efforts to travel around the world searching for items, they often returned empty-handed, feeling powerless and stretched thin because costs are far higher than before.

Even some of the top executives of auction houses that have contributed to the current booming trend of Chinese cultural relics and art say they don't understand it. Some things they considered promising didn't attract much attention, while others they overlooked, which appeared mediocre and occasionally surfaced, suddenly became hot favorites. The shift between cold and hot trends happened too quickly.

The main players in this market have been marginalized, the main thread of market conditions has been adjusted, and the dominant forces in this market have become hesitant... Can we say then, that what people can't comprehend is the market trend, and actually, the basis for this change in trends?

Zhou You, vice general manager of Poly Culture, said that in 2000, the total transaction volume of China's overall art auction market was 1.25 billion yuan, reaching a peak of 22.3 billion yuan in 2007. Due to the impact of the financial crisis in 2008, it declined slightly to below 20 billion yuan. In 2009, it quickly recovered to over 20 billion yuan again. In 2010, Poly's auction transaction volume reached over 9 billion yuan, and combined with China Guardian, it reached over 16 to 17 billion yuan. This year, the art auction market will undoubtedly produce a shocking number. Industry insiders are also optimistic about next year's auction market. China's art market is gradually forming, and its operations are becoming increasingly standardized.

"Warming Up, Recovery, Sky-High Prices" Three-Step Leap

The recovery signs shown in the overseas art market early in 2010 brought a good omen to China's art market. The total transaction amount of Sotheby's and Christie's auctions held in February reached 7.95 billion US dollars, 2.5 times that of the same period last year. Domestically, the recovery trend continued from 2009, with the Spring Auction transaction volume rising from 15.646 billion yuan in the previous season to 20.141 billion yuan, an increase of 28.7%, setting a new record for the total transaction volume of Chinese art auctions. The number of auction sessions reached 628, an increase of 73%.

This season's Spring Auction also repeatedly broke records at the "100 Million Mark," not only producing four lots exceeding 100 million yuan but also setting a new record for Chinese art auctions with Huang Tingjian's "Dizhu Ming" selling for over 400 million yuan including commission, and Zhang Daqian's "Aihen Lake" becoming the first modern painting to exceed 100 million yuan at 100.8 million yuan. Behind the billion-yuan lots lies a market phenomenon dominated by masterpieces, which is closely related to the influx of liquidity funds. Unlike the previous situation where billion-yuan lots were concentrated in Hong Kong and overseas regions, mainly involving porcelain and Buddha statues, since the autumn of 2009, all billion-yuan lots in the two seasons in mainland China have been Chinese paintings, becoming the dominant force in mainland high-end masterpieces.

Two out of Ten Artworks Sell for Eighty Percent of the Price; Billion-Yuan Lots No Longer Novelty

In this Spring Auction, Chinese paintings maintained their mainstream position in the market. According to statistical data, in the 2010 Spring Auction, the total transaction volume of Chinese paintings from seven companies - Beijing Poly, China Guardian, Beijing Hanhai, Beijing Kangshi, Zhongmao Shengjia, Beijing Huachen, and Beijing Rongbao - amounted to over 6.2 billion yuan, accounting for 70.24% of the total transaction volume, an increase of 4.74% compared to 2009. It is not hard to see that all four billion-yuan lots were produced in the field of paintings.

Wang Yannan of China Guardian explained the price formation of the most expensive and important lot in the company's 17-year history using "ample market funds" and "mature collectors who know good deals." This is also widely regarded as the direct cause of the fierce autumn auction. On November 15, 2010, the central bank raised the reserve requirement ratio again, from the original 8.5% to 9%, the third formal increase in the reserve requirement ratio after July 5 and August 15 of this year, each time by 0.5 percentage points. Some financial experts believe that this is the state using financial means to curb mortgage growth while regulating the overheated real estate market.

The Art Auction Market Enters a Frenzy; Hot Trends May Extend to the Spring Auction

When analyzing the market trend and future expectations of China's art auction market in 2010, Zhao Li, director of the AMRC Art Market Analysis Research Center and professor at the Central Academy of Fine Arts, believed that the total transaction volume of China's art auction market in 2010 is expected to exceed 35 billion yuan, setting a new historical record, with an estimated annual growth rate of 75%. The overall development in 2011 is expected to stabilize, with little possibility of breakthrough growth in the market size.

Public data show that since 2004, the annual return on investment in China's art collection market has been 26%, with antiques and collectibles appreciating by 20% annually, surpassing the higher-risk stocks and real estate, attracting a large amount of private capital and even drawing in financial capital. Experts indicate that it is precisely the entry of capital that has led to the current hot market and frequent sky-high prices in the auction market. The challenge now is how to fully utilize the power of capital to put China's art transactions on a sustainable development path, which is both an opportunity and a challenge.

Four, The "Things" in the 2010 Art Collection Market

Investment Welcomes the "Common People Era" - The Chinese Art Market Over the Last Five Years

In 2006, China's art market total transaction volume was less than 20 billion yuan, whereas in 2010, the market transaction total exceeded 50 billion yuan.

To promote and guide the development of the art market, in 2006, the Ministry of Culture established the Art Appraisal Committee of the Cultural Market Development Center, drawing significant attention from domestic and international industries. The introduced Chinese art industry registration certification database standardizes the art market from the source of works, through the entire chain of art operators, sellers, and creators, opening a new path to solve the long-standing issue of unclear product identity in the art circulation field.

In the primary art market, the Ministry of Culture launched the "Chinese Honest Gallery" brand, focusing on supporting and promoting galleries that operate honestly, encouraging more galleries to participate in honest operations and standardize the market.

In the secondary art market, by launching high-quality, high-class, and highly authentic Chinese art investment and collection expos, all works' quality and authenticity are maximally guaranteed. Since 2006, the Ministry of Culture has hosted the China International Art Investment and Collection Expo annually, during which the China Art Industry Forum is also held to study and discuss various issues related to the Chinese art market.

To further regulate the cultural relic art auction market, the "Regulations on Cultural Relic Art Auctions" officially came into effect on July 1, 2010. This is the first industry standard issued in the 20-plus years of development of China's auction industry and also the first industry standard in the cultural art market field in our country. The Regulations define important terms such as auction catalogs and proxy bids in cultural relic art auctions, specify the basic principles that auction activities should follow, and provide detailed normative explanations for the appraisal of cultural relics art, the implementation of auctions, and dispute resolution channels. Industry insiders believe that this regulation, closely related to the auction industry, cultural relic art circles, and collector circles, will not only positively influence the standardization of the cultural relic art auction market but also marks the entry of China's cultural relic art auction market into the "Standard Era."

To promote the development of our country's art, on August 31, 2010, the National Painting Academy separately established seven professional academies: the Chinese Painting Academy, the Oil Painting Academy, the Printmaking Academy, the Sculpture Academy, the Calligraphy and Seal Carving Academy, the Art History and Theory Research Institute, and the Public Art Academy. This is an important sign of the National Painting Academy improving its professional categories and perfecting its organizational system, and will surely drive the development of the national art cause and thereby promote the prosperity of our country's art market.

Behind "Patriotic Porcelain": Is the High-Priced Auction of Porcelain Patriotic or Commercial Operation?

Several Chinese antique objects have successively set sky-high transaction records, stunning domestic and foreign media. Auction professionals stated that prices offered by Chinese buyers are often dozens of times the auction house's estimated price. Foreign media referred to this as "porcelain patriotism," where Chinese people are sparing no expense to buy back lost cultural heritage. However, domestic porcelain collectors frankly admitted that high-priced porcelain auctions are commercial operations aimed at boosting the price of porcelain through publicity for profit.

Establishing Household Registration for Artworks: Auction Companies Should Jointly Ban the Auction of Yuanmingyuan Cultural Relics

At the beginning of December 2010, at a Christie's auction in Hong Kong, three suspected Yuanmingyuan lost cultural relics were auctioned off, with a total price equivalent to approximately 1.94 billion yuan RMB. Among them, a pair of Qing Yongzheng imperial cloisonné enamel crane incense burners sold for over 1 billion yuan RMB, setting a world auction record for cloisonné enamel art objects.

Experts from the China Auctioneers Association released data showing that the total transaction volume of the national auction industry this year is expected to exceed 500 billion yuan, facing the task of building a "market integrity system" in the future. In response, renowned collector Wang Dingqian suggested that a complete "household registration" for modern art should be established as soon as possible.

From the Fake Paintings Incident at Christie's to the "Robber Clauses" in Modern Auction Rules

Christie's made a joke, and the happiest ones were the Chinese auction houses: it turns out that well-known international auction companies also sell fake goods, so selling known fakes can henceforth be done more boldly, because this is also considered an "international practice" with rules to follow. What worries the collectors waiting to buy is: oh my, where else can I find trustworthy milk? What panics the buyers of auctioned items is: no matter when or where they bought it, doubts may arise: is the treasure under the bed also dug up from Cao Cao's tomb?

When faced with doubts, relevant spokespersons from domestic auction houses once said that according to management regulations, art collecting is a risky investment, and risks must be borne individually. Yes, the price of art fluctuates, and there is no guarantee of profit, which is fine. Those East Asian "benchmark kings" who dominate the international auction market, almost without exception, keep their "benchmark kings" trapped in their hands, rotting in the pot, or even dragging down their companies. This serves as a very standard demonstration of the "self-bearing risk" principle in art investment.

Therefore, the "self-bearing risk" principle can be expanded to cover everything, allowing counterfeit and substandard works to parade around under the banner of "self-bearing risk" and commit misdeeds. Auction companies disguise themselves as innocent parties, emboldened to continue selling fake goods due to the protection of risk clauses. Individual buyers, as weaker parties, habitually have their interests ignored because this is an "international practice." Under the protection of "international practices," auction companies, for the sake of their own performance, feign innocence when genuine items are scarce or when rewards are substantial, forming a community of interests in tacit agreement with scammers.

Compared to other markets, the art investment market has always been a niche market with specific main bodies and consumer groups. Even today, after the art market has expanded significantly, due to the professionalism required for art appreciation, the limitations imposed by investment thresholds, and the singularity of consumption value, the market remains distant from mainstream investment groups.

The basic characteristics of the stock market offer insights for the art investment market seeking greater development. Investment is not speculation; it requires public and transparent buying and selling under a series of trading rules. The value of appreciation lies in inheritance, and market value lies in fair circulation. Why not organically combine these two values for art, integrating the cultural and financial industries to promote the better development and inheritance of traditional Chinese culture and art through investment.

How does the art exchange turn the originally unreachable high-end art investment market into a market that mainstream investors can participate in? The person in charge introduced that the share trading model adopted by the exchange is its greatest innovation. The so-called share trading model refers to dividing the share target equally and then publicly listing the shares for trading according to the ownership enjoyed after division. For example, if an art piece is worth 10 million yuan RMB, it can be divided into 10 million shares, subscribed in increments of 1,000 or multiples thereof (each share costing 1 yuan). At this point, investors can participate in the issuance subscription of the art piece before it is listed, holding the original shares of the art piece. This not only lowers the investment threshold and broadens the range of investors but also standardizes art investment, facilitating trading and circulation.
