Those outside the exhibition are called depositors, and in fact, many of them are ruffians::

by william9078 on 2012-02-08 11:33:58

Looking back at the road ahead, when people talk about my experiences, they often delight in recounting the smooth and proud moments of success, rarely mentioning failures to outsiders. As an old saying goes: "Only boasting about crossing five passes and slaying six generals, never mentioning the midnight escape from Mai City." However, those failed experiences are more unforgettable, and perhaps without those failures, there wouldn't have been the subsequent successes. After so many years, thinking about those failed experiences this year, they still seem vividly present.

In 1994, I attended an exhibition with a colleague from our company. Originally, it was just a casual attempt, but we ended up signing two contracts. In the following year, these contracts turned into relatively large accounts for us. In 1995, another exhibition was held by our company. Having tasted success before, this time we brought several colleagues along with all our products, setting up a relatively large booth. The atmosphere at the exhibition was extraordinarily lively, and we easily signed several contracts, succeeding in a way that seemed almost unbelievable. Who would have thought that misfortune was about to strike repeatedly.

First, a customer from Qufu, Shandong kept urging for delivery. The original shipping time was set for ten days, but they requested immediate delivery due to urgent needs. If the goods were not complete, they could send a part first. Since one set of goods required custom production, we only managed to prepare half of the goods and sent them off. Upon arrival in Qufu, we checked their business license, which showed a registered capital of 2.3 million yuan, making them one of the largest customers I had at the time. I originally had an old customer in Qufu, with whom I maintained a very good relationship. And since Qufu is the hometown of sages and a place of etiquette, I felt quite at ease dealing with people there. We agreed that after unloading and inspecting the goods, they would pay immediately. Trusting them, I accompanied their finance staff to the bank for a telegraphic transfer after the goods were unloaded. At the bank, I personally saw them fill out the telegraphic transfer form, the bank stamped it, and I even asked a bank employee who confirmed that the money would definitely arrive in three days. Feeling elated, I thought I had found another big customer who was so straightforward in doing business.

Three days later, upon checking at the bank back home, the payment hadn't arrived. When I called them, no one answered, causing me to panic. I quickly traveled to Qufu, only to find their company had closed down, leaving no trace of them. Rushing to the bank, the bank staff surprisingly claimed that no such transfer had ever been made. I argued that I had seen them stamp the form and was told it would arrive in three days, but they replied that I must have mistaken, as no such thing had happened. Frustrated, I went to the industrial and commercial bureau, where I learned that the other party was not from Qufu but from Taiqian County, Henan Province, and their company had been registered for less than a month. I questioned the bureau staff about how they could register such scammers without thorough verification, but they replied that they strictly followed procedures, and the other party met the registration requirements. Completely deceived, I returned dejectedly to the site of their former company. There, I encountered another person loudly cursing. He was from Heilongjiang Guohua River and had also signed a contract with this company at the exhibition. Now, he had just delivered his goods to Qufu, only to find the company had already shut down and run away. He kept cursing: "Isn't this cheating people? Driving all the way from Northeast China, how much transportation cost would that be?" I said, "Old buddy, you should be satisfied. Although you've also been deceived, you've only lost some transportation costs, at least your goods are still there. But me, I haven't received a single penny of payment, and the goods are gone."

Half a month later, the goods for another customer in Dong'e County, Shandong were ready. They kept urging for shipment. Upon arrival at their store, I told the boss, "Please check the goods first, see if they are satisfactory?" The boss carelessly walked around and said, "Okay, no problem with the goods, unload them." I said, "If the goods are fine, please pay according to the amount on the contract first, then unload the goods, and it must be in cash." Learning from past mistakes, I couldn't be deceived again. Hearing this, the other party got upset, claiming it was distrustful towards him, demanding to unload the goods first and then pay immediately afterward. Thus, we were stuck in a standoff over whether to pay or unload first. Seeing I wouldn't compromise, the other party said, "Wait here, I'll get the money." Unexpectedly, before they returned with the money, law enforcement officers from the Agricultural Research Institute arrived. After checking our relevant documents, they declared our goods as "three-no" products (no production date, no quality certificate, no manufacturer), intending to seal them and impose a fine. Without further ado, they took our driver and drove the truck to the Agricultural Research Institute.

At the Agricultural Research Institute, we were left alone with no one taking care of us. Asking around, we learned that the director was handling it personally, but when asked where the director was, the reply was that the director wasn't there. Half a day passed, and we couldn't find anyone in charge. I called an old customer in Pingyin County, a neighboring county, hoping he might understand the situation here. The Pingyin customer said he knew something about this individual, stating that he intended to deceive us and seeing that didn't work, decided to handle us this way instead. With no other choice, I had to call the person who placed the order, suppressing my anger while politely asking for help. Surprisingly direct, he soon arrived on a motorcycle at the Agricultural Research Institute. After staying in a room for a while, he came out and told me, "The director and I are old acquaintances. Due to my face-saving, the fine will be reduced. Give four hundred yuan to the director, but don't ask for a receipt, understood?" Entering the room, the director didn't even glance up, seeing the four hundred yuan on the table, he simply said, "Go." Outside, I had to thank the person who placed the order and then asked him, "Do you still want these goods? Considering the great favor you've done, I'll give you a discount." He replied, "I can't gather enough money for the full payment, so forget it, I won't take the goods, you return them."

Suddenly, it was the end of the year, and I suddenly received a call from someone in Lincang, Hebei, also met at that exhibition. He urgently needed a batch of goods, promising sincerely over the phone to prepare the payment in advance, paying first after inspecting the goods and then unloading. Scammers are always around, but business must continue. Preparing the goods, I invited a physically strong classmate to accompany me, placing a knife and an electric shock gun in the cab before setting off. Arriving at Lincang, we drove to the location specified by the other party, parking the car on the balcony of their factory. A young man approached me, saying the boss wasn't in the factory and asked me to go with him to find the boss. I instructed my classmate and the driver to watch the goods carefully and followed the young man to find their boss. Upon returning to the factory after finding the boss, I discovered that the goods had already been unloaded halfway. I asked my classmate what happened, and he explained that shortly after I left, about ten people said I had gone with the boss to arrange the payment and let them start unloading the goods. My classmate, being there just to help and unfamiliar with the local customs, believed them. I quickly ordered them to stop unloading and asked the boss to inspect the goods. The boss said, "No issues with the goods, it's getting late, let's have dinner first, and then I'll arrange the payment after dinner." Thinking to myself, after dinner, my goods might be gone. So, I told the boss, "Thank you for your kindness, but we won't eat dinner. Please prepare the payment quickly, as we need to travel soon for a celebration." Seeing that persuasion wouldn't work, the boss gave some instructions to his employees and turned to leave. Just as he stepped out of the gate, his employees slammed the big iron door open. Throughout the afternoon, the boss never showed up, and his employees attempted several times to forcibly unload the goods but were stopped by us.

As dusk approached, seeing they couldn't unload the goods, the employees gradually left, leaving only an elderly gatekeeper. It was cold and hungry in December, and as night fell quietly, we lit a fire beside the car to keep warm. My classmate even brandished the knife dramatically a few times. The old man, feeling scared, pleaded with us, "I'm so old, just a gatekeeper for others, please don't make things difficult for me." He even brought us a pot of boiling water. Seeing this situation, I quickly instructed my classmate and the driver to re-load the unloaded goods onto the car, cover the tarpaulin well, and seal the car. The old man didn't interfere.

This series of events highlights the importance of learning from failure and the necessity of vigilance in business dealings. (Editor: Purchasing Agent Editor)
