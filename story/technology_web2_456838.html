Advice for those who often repair computers

by afahgyerzwgd on 2011-07-04 10:53:02

1. Never deny that you're an expert, because in fact you really aren't, as this title will make you pay a price.

2. After receiving a call, be sure to ask clearly what the caller needs, then inform them of your current location.

3. Never try to solve a problem via phone or QQ. It's very likely that they don't know what the menu is about. This cannot be blamed on them, but only on yourself, because you are always too lazy to explain it to them. Of course, unless you're dealing with someone who isn't a total novice, in which case QQ remote assistance can sometimes be quite useful, although the speed can be unbearable. I once had the successful experience of helping a netizen from Suzhou solve their problem.

4. Don't expect to immediately resolve any issues you encounter, because you'll often come across crashed systems or systems on the verge of crashing.

5. If the other party allows you to reinstall the system, don't attempt any repair work, as reinstallation is the fastest way to solve problems. Unless you want to challenge yourself, have time to waste, or intentionally want to wait until mealtime.

6. Besides asking what materials need to be backed up, don't ask the other party any questions, unless your mental resilience is particularly strong. But it's still necessary to inform them not to place data on the desktop or C drive.

7. The visual inspection of the computer should always come first. Check if the wiring is connected normally, and whether the USB port has other devices connected, etc. Many times, colleagues or friends say their computers are broken, won't turn on, or have no display, but it turns out that the computer cables weren't plugged in, making you feel like dying.

8. Trust the noise resistance of hardware. Whenever possible, don't open the other party's case. Failing to fix it and instead being regarded as the one who caused damage is a very sad thing.

9. Be prepared mentally for criticism from others, because people who lack computer knowledge often understand how to evaluate others.

10. If someone says their memory is 160G, never correct them by saying it's the hard drive, otherwise, your nightmare journey will begin.

11. Inform the other party that you have no antivirus software, otherwise, all the antivirus software you install will be considered the worst and most problematic.

12. Avoid becoming an expert at handling printer malfunctions. Believe me, printers can cause far more trouble than computers.

13. Absolutely don't help others buy computers, especially custom-built ones, unless you want to be accused of taking kickbacks and then spend every day running around to help them deal with malfunctions.

14. Don't be annoyed by the following two sentences, as they will accompany you for a long time:

- "My computer has another big problem."

- "You'd better come over and take a look."

In the office, remember the following principles:

1. Try not to mess with other people's or public computers, otherwise, after several days, your action may lead to their system collapse and bring complaints. I deeply empathize with this. Once, a year group's computer "broke," and when the leader asked, a colleague immediately pointed the finger at me. It was incomprehensible.

2. Don't try to forcefully correct others' mistakes unless you have the ability to convince them.

3. Under any circumstances, avoid exchanging any electronic documents, because your files might be criticized as viruses.

Finally, remember one thing: the joy of fixing computers for friends is always the most important, it's the happiness of giving roses to others, and it's the joy of sharing a small drink.
