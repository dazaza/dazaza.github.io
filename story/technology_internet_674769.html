Causes of Body Odor:

1. Apocrine Bromhidrosis

Apocrine glands are only present in areas such as the armpits, areola, navel, anus, external genitalia, and ear canals. Except for the armpits, the function of these glands in other areas is extremely weak, producing very little sweat. The sweat secreted by apocrine glands is initially sterile but under the influence of microorganisms, organic compounds in the sweat are broken down into short-chain fatty acids and ammonia with a distinct odor, leading to apocrine bromhidrosis.

2. Eccrine Bromhidrosis

This often occurs on the palms, soles, and intertriginous zones. Eccrine sweat is usually colorless and odorless; however, in cases of excessive sweating, the sweat is decomposed by bacteria on the skin surface releasing fatty acids that cause a specific smell. Substances like garlic and arsenic can also be excreted through eccrine glands causing an unpleasant odor.

3. Mental or nervous system damage (such as paranoia, schizophrenia) can also lead to bromhidrosis.

4. Axillary odor may have a genetic link.

Body Odor Test:

Family History

Check if any ancestors including grandparents, parents, siblings, uncles, aunts, and cousins have a history of axillary odor or body odor.

Symptoms

Observe if earwax is wet and sticky (similar to ear oil or otitis media). In some mild cases, it might appear powdery.

Smell

Use a clean handkerchief or paper to rub the armpit area vigorously and identify the smell. It’s best to do this after some physical activity or when you feel warm.

Pigmentation

Check if the armpit area of your underwear has turned yellow or changed color.

Underarm Hair

Check if there are any abnormal greasy substances or sticky liquids around the underarm hair. Check if there is mold growth or secretion stuck to the hair.
