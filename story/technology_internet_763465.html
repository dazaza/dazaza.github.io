Microsoft: A Decade of Hardware Was Just a Dream

by anonymous on 2013-08-02 10:12:30

On July 19th, Microsoft released its Q2 financial report with revenues of $19.9 billion, a 10% increase year-over-year. Net profit reached $4.97 billion, reversing the previous loss. For Microsoft, which is in the sales growth phase of Windows 8 and Office 2013, this performance did not satisfy the market. On the same day, Microsoft's stock fell by 6% after hours, and on the second trading day, it plummeted by 11.4%. Subsequently, more details were disclosed, including a $900 million write-down due to poor sales of Surface RT. The latest news indicates that Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer admitted to overestimating the demand for Surface RT.

Ballmer has always hoped that Microsoft's hardware product strategy would demonstrate his leadership distinct from his predecessor, Bill Gates. However, if we look back to the beginning of this century, we find that apart from the successful Xbox gaming console in the U.S. market, almost all of Microsoft's flagship hardware products have failed.

**Zune Player**

In 2006, Microsoft launched the Zune music player to compete with Apple's iPod. They subsequently introduced a matching music suite, desktop player, and synchronization software. The Zune once achieved nearly 10% market share in the U.S. However, it faced a major setback against the diverse iPod product line, with its market share dropping below 0.5%, leading to its discontinuation in 2011. After launching WP8 last year, the Zune software disappeared from Microsoft's product lineup.

**Kin Phone**

In 2008, Microsoft acquired Danger, the company behind the Sidekick phone. The Sidekick was considered the coolest phone in America before the iPhone and was highly praised by Hollywood stars and young people alike. Its task-switching interface and slide-out keyboard inspired products from HP and Motorola. In May 2010, Microsoft launched the Kin phone with the Sidekick team, focusing on internet and music functions. However, due to various defects and conflicts with Windows Phone, it was abandoned the same year it was launched.

**Surface Tablet**

If people still have reservations about the Surface Pro, the failure of RT is already a consensus. Last month, Surface RT was discounted by $150 globally, and an ultra-low price of $199 was offered for educational bulk customers. Just nine months ago, Microsoft confidently launched this product and mocked the iPad in advertisements for not supporting true multitasking or Office series software.

Microsoft's performance in the hardware sector has left both its supporters and critics confused. Supporters do not understand why excellent products, supported by Microsoft's significant technological and marketing resources, repeatedly fail. Critics wonder, "After making so many foolish decisions and producing ridiculous products, how does Microsoft remain 'too big to fail'?"

**Microsoft doesn't differentiate; it confronts head-on in areas where competitors excel**

As a latecomer in the hardware field, Microsoft ambitiously initiates comprehensive wars against leaders. "What Apple has, Microsoft must have," whether it be hardware or complementary software and cloud services. Often, Microsoft cannot convince consumers to buy another identical or similar product. Amazon and Google are worth learning from in the tablet domain; both bypassed iPad's dominance with low-cost 7-inch tablets to seize the market, eventually forcing Apple to release the iPad mini to recover lost users.

**Severe internal competition**

Microsoft aggressively recruits valuable teams and talents globally, resulting in fierce internal competition. When the Kin phone was just launched, Andy Lee, the head of Microsoft's WP7 project, managed to seize power from the original project leader, Allard. Bing search engine started as an "internal incubation" project without strict supervision. However, after being designated as a strategic direction by upper management, external leaders were parachuted in, causing the original technical personnel to leave. Many products lose their edge and opportunities amidst leadership vacillation.

**Microsoft excels in software, not platforms**

Steve Jobs launched AppStore in the second year after the release of the iPhone, attracting global developers to build the iOS ecosystem. Therefore, even before the iPhone 4, Apple could offer better user experiences with outdated hardware. In contrast, the extremely slow SDK updates and stingy API permissions of RT and WP have led some developers to claim they will no longer write software for Surface. The two major mobile platforms, RT and WP8, are criticized most for the lack of high-quality software.

**Unyielding Windows and Office**

Despite committing numerous errors, Microsoft remains standing thanks to its two "cash cows," Windows and Office. This has led Microsoft to hope to inject the advantages of Windows and Office into its own hardware. Back then, Windows Mobile fully inherited the UI of the desktop version of Windows and a streamlined version of Office software, which were difficult to use on phones. To this day, on the RT system, which emphasizes portability, Microsoft's biggest feature is still full-featured Office, and the market once again dismisses this with disdain.

The stumbling Microsoft has raised concerns among many market observers. Not only has Microsoft failed to expand its territory during the rise of mobile devices, but also its previously unshakable Wintel and OEM alliances have shown cracks. The Surface Pro uses Intel's old Ivy Bridge platform, significantly increasing size and weight while offering only four hours of battery life. Two months later, the Macbook Air adopted the new Haswell platform, achieving a battery life of 12 hours. Acer has been dissuading Microsoft from producing Surface and refused to launch RT tablets. Samsung's CEO stated that Windows products sell poorly. Now, even Nokia is criticizing the slow progress of the WP platform. Borrowing a phrase from the foreign tech blog site The Verge, in the era of mobile internet, Microsoft will not truly be "too big to fail."
