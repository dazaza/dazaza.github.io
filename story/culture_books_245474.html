The enlightened monk and the prostitute Honglian, the scientist Liu Cui and the elementary school teacher Yue Ming - for whom does the peacock cry out? A dam blocks the river! Alas! The unforeseen twists of fate for two generations. Strange! The mystery of the reincarnation of four people.

This is a grand work imbued with ecological concern, as well as an allegory reflecting on modern civilization. Against the backdrop of the historical transformation occurring in the Three Gorges of the Yangtze River, Hong Ying's vision penetrates through the misty barriers between history and reality, reaching directly to the ultimate secrets of humanity and the times. This is an allegory imbued with ecological care. The unforeseen twists of fate for two generations, the mystery of reincarnation identities. Hong Ying says: "After all, who can reach the world before they were born?"

The Peacock's Cry - Part One

It's hard to imagine such a thing happening, and even harder to imagine personally going through it. But when a woman and a man are thrown into a prison cell together, especially handcuffed to a stranger, she must face more than just her own grievances and anger.

After the door slammed shut, the cell was pitch black, and nothing could be seen. The ground and walls were covered in slippery moss, the air was murky, and there was a strange smell - a faint trace of blood mixed with a strong stench of urine.

She propped herself up on her arms, trying to get up, but ended up pulling another person along, causing them both to fall again. This was quite an embarrassing situation. The man tried his best to keep a polite distance from her, but the more they tried to avoid contact, the more likely they were to bump into each other. Each collision made them more awkward - neither of them wanted to confirm the charges that terrified them.

The Peacock's Cry - Part Two

Her pride tormented her; she didn't want to ask what relationship existed between that woman and him. She'd rather not know. Not knowing the details meant less specific harm, which was very different from knowing some concrete details. This marriage probably also gave her a convenient excuse for being too lazy to live a family life.

Suddenly, she felt fear, realizing something was wrong with her. A normal woman should be furious, overturning vinegar jars, throwing pots and pans, at least crying loudly. But she didn't. If he was an unsatisfactory husband, it would have been fine. That Director Kan said this husband was an "important figure" in the world's largest project; her mother heard from the widows of political veterans that "he had limitless prospects." So, what reason did she have not to cherish the name of this happy marriage?

As an outsider passing through, she really had no right to criticize. Criticizing her own country using Western standards was something that shouldn't be done.
