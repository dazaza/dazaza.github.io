Dingmian Wang, a dairy expert from Boye, informed 39 Weakness Network that milk itself does not contain aflatoxin, and the detection of aflatoxin in milk is mainly due to cows consuming feed containing aflatoxin. When cows eat variable or stale feed, aflatoxin can be detected in the milk. Corn, bran, and other such things are prone to mold and produce this toxin if stored for too long. Wang Dingmian said that if cows consume fresh green grass, less of this substance will be detected in the milk. "Aflatoxin M1 is classified as a Class 1 carcinogen. Many countries, including ours, have set maximum limit standards for Aflatoxin M1 in milk and its products, so it is a mandatory inspection item for both raw materials and finished products." According to Wang Dingmian, even with high-temperature pasteurization used in milk processing, it's impossible to eliminate aflatoxin. "Aflatoxin M1 is extremely stable; pasteurization cannot destroy it. Even ultra-high temperatures (138-152 degrees Celsius) cannot kill it; temperatures need to reach 280 degrees Celsius to destroy Aflatoxin M1." Wang Dingmian suggests eating fresh food and recommends drinking more lactic acid beverages with higher total sugar content and fewer additives. "Both milk and powdered milk should be kept fresh, and storage time should not be too long. Ordinary consumers cannot distinguish whether milk contains aflatoxin just by looking at it; testing is required to make that determination."
