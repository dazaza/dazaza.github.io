E-commerce builds logistics and is accused of neglecting their main business

by gtobalmf on 2011-08-12 17:16:52

I don't think an e-commerce company needs hundreds of acres of land to build its own logistics center, which is inevitably suspected of land speculation. Wang Chengzhi, Director of Logistics Infrastructure and Board Member of Jones Lang LaSalle in China, felt quite puzzled by the current situation where e-commerce companies are investing in logistics real estate. It's a public secret that e-commerce companies haven't been able to make money until today. They should be more focused on their e-commerce business with the investors' money, instead of acquiring land and building warehouses. This is moving away from the main business and cannot increase core competitiveness.

The fact, however, is that e-commerce companies' enthusiasm for investing in and constructing logistics centers is rapidly increasing. As JD.com decided to invest its financing funds into logistics construction projects, the momentum of various e-commerce companies entering the logistics real estate sector became unstoppable.

Since 2009, JD.com has acquired nearly 1400 acres of land and plans to simultaneously start construction of seven first-class logistics centers and 25 second-class logistics centers this year. E-commerce companies such as dangdang, Taobao, Joyo, Vancl, Suning E-Commerce, Newegg, and Yihao Dian have also expressed their intention to establish their own logistics centers in various places. Alibaba Group even announced plans to invest hundreds of billions of yuan to establish a nationwide warehousing system.

The initial driving force behind e-commerce companies entering the logistics real estate sector was the insufficient supply of domestic warehouses. According to the data from the "National Warehousing Industry Development Index," the demand for general-purpose warehouse area in 2010 reached 701 million square meters across the country, while the actual available warehouse area was only 550 million square meters. General-purpose warehouses were overall in short supply. A research report from Jones Lang LaSalle also showed that logistics warehouse rents in Shanghai had skyrocketed since last year, with increases exceeding 10%.

However, e-commerce companies' interest in logistics real estate is increasingly surpassing actual demand, which Wang Chengzhi views as dangerous: once land is acquired and warehouses built, these things become assets, and these assets require taxation. If e-commerce companies lease third-party warehouses, the rent can offset taxes. So unless they use it themselves, to make this investment profitable, e-commerce companies would need to act as developers and landlords, renting out their self-built warehouses. But isn't this moving further away from their main business? And do e-commerce companies have the ability to operate logistics centers? Can e-commerce warehouses accommodate furniture, supermarket goods, or industrial products? In fact, e-commerce warehouses have relatively weak versatility.

Wang Chengzhi introduced that sometimes e-commerce companies find it easier to acquire industrial land than logistics real estate developers, which to some extent fuels their desire to acquire land.

Industrial land is divided into three types: factories, warehouses/logistics centers, and R&D centers. For local governments that transfer land, R&D centers and factories can bring continuous tax revenue in the future, whereas the tax revenue generated by warehouses is relatively less. However, e-commerce companies often conduct settlement operations within their warehouses, which can bring tax revenue to the locality, making the government more willing to transfer land to e-commerce companies. Wang Chengzhi said.

Regarding why e-commerce companies are enthusiastic about participating in the development of logistics real estate, Wang Chengzhi stated: First, it's still betting on the appreciation of land prices, because the supply of land is limited, so logistics real estate may even have more appreciation potential than commercial real estate. Of course, on the other hand, this part of the assets can be packaged as a selling point for future listings.

The large influx of e-commerce companies has led to a continuous rise in the price of logistics land. According to Wang Chengzhi, this year in Tongzhou, Beijing, a piece of logistics land was auctioned off at a price close to 2.5 million yuan per mu, showing just how hot logistics real estate has become. In Shanghai, the price of primary logistics land is between 500,000 to 600,000 yuan per mu, relatively cheaper. However, obtaining land is very difficult, as the government strictly controls the qualifications of developers and project requirements. Acquiring a piece of logistics land usually takes one and a half to two years. Therefore, in Shanghai, the situation of insufficient supply of logistics land has persisted for at least five years. The actual transaction volume of logistics land in Shanghai mainly comes from secondary land, with the current average quotation being above 1 million yuan per mu and still rising.

Currently, the return on investment for warehouses in China is considered relatively high globally, with gross yields ranging from 8% to 8.5%, higher than the average yield of ordinary residential properties and commercial real estate. Wang Chengzhi believes that e-commerce companies investing in logistics real estate is not a healthy growth.
