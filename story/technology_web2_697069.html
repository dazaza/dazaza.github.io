Ding Ke: A voice as fragile as Chris Garneau's, but he is one of a kind - "Private Music"

by qweasdzxc4o on 2012-03-08 12:37:52

Ding Ke is an independent musician, currently residing in Beijing. I first came across Ding Ke last April when I was searching for suitable background music for the first issue of my electronic magazine, KINA. By chance, I heard Ding Ke's music, which turned out to be a style I really like but had rarely encountered before. It felt quite unique. At that time, Ding Ke hadn't performed at any music festivals yet, but his follower count on DouBan Musician's homepage had quietly risen to over 3000.

The first issue of KINA naturally used Ding Ke's music. Readers generally commented that the magazine was rather rough, except for the background music, which stood out as a highlight. The song "ORANGE" is also Ding Ke's most satisfying work.

Later, Wang Yue, the former lead singer of Guai Hang Zi, while reading the first issue of KINA, heard Ding Ke's song and shed tears. I think it was due to the direct and subtle resonance of the musician!

Afterwards, Wang introduced Ding Ke to Modern Sky. At last year's Modern Sky Music Festival, Ding Ke performed. However, the performance time seemed to be noon, so there were very few people watching. I think those who attended the music festival back then didn't know who Ding Ke was, nor had they listened to his music. He quietly played and sang by himself. Later, he told me that the performance wasn't very good.

As winter arrived, Ding Ke slowly began performing in bars like MAO. He also performed at this year's May Day Strawberry Music Festival.

I once asked Ding Ke, will you make music for your entire life? He said, probably, yes. Everyone needs a firm ideal, but real life is too unpredictable. Ding Ke is a simple person, and he is accustomed to being sincere with everyone. He will also marry and have children, leading a secular life.

Speaking of marriage, let me mention the piece "Petal". A friend of mine once confided in me online about her abortion and the subsequent misfortunes. At that time, she was only 18 years old. The child was gone, and later, she was unable to bear children again. Such events happen every day. This is a very unfortunate thing. That day, I asked Ding Ke if he could write a song for those children who originally could have come into this world but ultimately disappeared. Ding Ke also had some empathy for such matters and agreed. The initial version of "Petal" was very short, but now it has been extended to two minutes and four seconds. We hope that these children can be happy in heaven.

Ding Ke is a male from Guangxi, born after 1985, a student at the Beijing Modern Music College, yet he possesses a cool female voice. Regardless of whether you feel sadness or happiness, listening to his music gives your heart a bit of satisfaction and stability. It's unclear whether Ding Ke's music leans more towards sadness or happiness, just like sometimes touching ice feels hot. He makes his music, and we listen. Each person finds inner satisfaction within themselves. This is very good. He is an honest musician, truly making music, and we are kind listeners hoping he can be happy.

Q: What style does your music belong to?

Ding Ke: I'm not quite sure how to define it, maybe leaning towards neo-classical.

Q: What kind of life do you prefer?

Ding Ke: Quiet and simple.

Q: In 2009, you performed live, how did you feel?

Ding Ke: It felt pretty good.

Q: Now that you've signed with Modern Sky, what plans do you have for 2010?

Ding Ke: Recently, I'll prepare to record two singles.

Q: Can you recommend some domestic and international musicians you admire? And perhaps briefly discuss them.

Ding Ke: Domestically, I appreciate Dou Wei; internationally, there are many, such as Arvo Pärt, Max Richter, Dustin O'Halloran, Ludovico Einaudi, and Eleni Karaindrou, these are the ones I listen to more often.

Q: Aside from your own music style, what kind of music do you like from others?

Ding Ke: Music that moves me.

Q: Tell us about your favorite movies and brands.

Ding Ke: "Betty Blue", "The Postman", "Nobody Knows", "Central Station"... Quite a few. Brands, I don't know much about.

Q: For you, is there a big gap between ideals and reality? Is there much friction?

Ding Ke: There's always a gap, but the friction isn't too bad.

Q: If there's friction between your ideals and reality, how do you reconcile it? How do you adjust yourself?

Ding Ke: Friction is normal.

Q: Ding Ke, you're from Guangxi, studied in Beijing, and now live in Beijing. Are you accustomed to Beijing? Share your feelings about this city.

Ding Ke: It's too big, too dry, and uncomfortable to live in.

Q: Besides music, what else do you like to do?

Ding Ke: Watch movies, take walks.

Q: Do you have any principles for living?

Ding Ke: Sincerity.

Q: Besides music, do you have any other ideals? Will you continue making music forever? Will you spend your whole life making music?

Ding Ke: Probably.

Q: Will you, like most people, get married, have children, and lead a secular life?

Ding Ke: Of course.

Q: Which of your works do you like the most?

Ding Ke: Orange.
