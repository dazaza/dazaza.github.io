Retired official writes for 10 years and uses royalties to sponsor more than a hundred poor students

by g47fjr6cop on 2012-03-03 09:12:00

Six of the students who received financial assistance were admitted to Tsinghua and Peking Universities, while others went abroad for further studies. According to reports by Xinhua News Agency in Express, paying New Year visits to relatives and friends is an ancient custom during Spring Festival. However, in Yuncheng City of Shanxi Province, an old man named Zhang Shixian has another task at hand during this festive season - selling books while visiting friends and relatives. Over the past decade, he has managed to raise over 1.4 million yuan through writing and selling books, which was used to support 123 underprivileged students.

Born into poverty, Zhang's determination to help students stems from his own hardships. Despite his modest appearance that often leads people to mistake him as a migrant worker, Zhang Shixian is no ordinary person. He is not only a member of the China Reportage Literature Research Association and the Shanxi Writers' Association but also once served as the Secretary of the Political and Legal Affairs Committee of Yuncheng City in Shanxi Province. He retired in March 2001 with full benefits at the level of a principal department head.

Zhang Shixian was born in Xiakou Village of Wanrong County in Shanxi Province and is now 70 years old. During his childhood, his family faced extreme poverty, often lacking food and clothing. In fact, they once gave away one of their children due to their dire financial situation. Zhang recounts, "On June 9, 1951, my mother fell seriously ill, and our family decided to give my four-year-old brother away. I, then nine years old, carried him on my back and walked for several kilometers until we reached the adoptive family’s home. I cried all the way, returning with five shi of wheat, a coffin, and a set of mourning clothes." This heart-wrenching experience deepened Zhang's belief in the power of education to change destinies and strengthened his resolve to help underprivileged students after retirement.

To gain his family's support, Zhang promised not to use his retirement fund but instead find alternative ways to raise money. Initially, he worked as a distributor for five newspapers and magazines, but it did not yield ideal results. Eventually, he turned to writing books, embarking on a challenging journey. From his first book, "Practice and Reflections on Rural Work," to later works like "Footprints" and "Evening Glow Collection," and finally "Me and the Underprivileged Students" published last year, Zhang wrote ten books over a decade, raising over 1.4 million yuan to support 123 impoverished students.

Although Zhang's literary works may not have high artistic value, such intensive writing was still extremely arduous for someone in his seventies. For many years, Zhang suffered from esophageal disease. Due to the removal of his gastric cardia, his digestive function was severely affected, causing him to hiccup constantly while speaking, eat small portions per meal, consume food every two hours, and rely heavily on large amounts of digestive aids daily. Consequently, Zhang always carried a worn-out leather bag filled with essential items whenever he went out: books for promotion, medicine, water, and various foods. Although few people appreciated his works, everyone respected his dedication and love towards helping underprivileged students.

Those unfamiliar with Zhang assumed he became a millionaire through writing, but those who knew him well understood he had accumulated no wealth from it. In Zhang's possession is a thick stack of registration forms detailing each student's information and clearly documenting the use of every educational fund. Zhang emphasized that this money belongs to the underprivileged students, and he must ensure its transparent and proper usage. Through organizing these forms, it was found that these 123 underprivileged students are spread across various regions including Shanxi, Henan, Anhui, Liaoning, etc., covering all age groups from primary school to university. More than 60 have been admitted to universities, among whom six were accepted by Tsinghua and Peking Universities, some of whom are currently studying abroad. The amount of financial assistance Zhang provided varied, ranging from several thousand yuan to over 200,000 yuan.

In Zhang's study, there are more than 300 letters from underprivileged students cherished as treasures. Whenever he has time, he carefully reads them, finding solace and reflecting on his years of effort and achievements. Zhang told reporters, "If I can live for another ten years, I will be able to support 200 underprivileged students. I am determined to continue living, learning, and writing until I meet Marx."

Unconcerned about eating or dressing well, generous with money, untidy in appearance, and ignoring idle gossip, Zhang Shixian might seem like an oddity, yet 123 underprivileged students have benefited from his generosity, some even changing their destinies and lives entirely.

At the age of seventy, Zhang Shixian has just turned 70 this year. Yet, he has not ceased his efforts to support underprivileged students. He continues to ride his old bicycle around selling books and frequently faces indifference. Last year, his new book "Me and the Underprivileged Students" was officially published, marking the past decade of his endeavors. Nevertheless, the next decade has already begun.
