In recent years, with the increase of investment and the increasingly international production standards, Russian films have become more attractive to a wide range of film enthusiasts. Blockbuster films such as "The 9th Company" and "Night Watch" have become evergreens in the audio-visual market, while game-inspired works like "Weapon Human" and "Wolfhound" have also sparked interest. More significantly, Russian filmmakers have connected with Hollywood production agencies through ambitious projects like "Wanted," beginning to create significant global impact and sparking a wave of unconventional Russian cinema. The original translation of "Lost Tomb" is "We Are From The Future," which was a major work from last year in Russia that created a stir and set good records. Its style is very similar to recent Russian films, with distinct fantasy elements. However, the director did not intend to use this commercially viable fantasy element to exaggerate excessively but instead used a time-travel model to retrace history, making the originally absurd plot closer to reality. Four young men living in St. Petersburg earn some money by selling World War II relics but accidentally uncover a decades-old war history after opening an abandoned command post. This experience changes their lives, perspectives, and their decaying spirit and fate. In the baptism of blood and fire, they learn how to take responsibility, cherish friendship, and gain love. Although this process is amusing, it feels like a middle school teacher earnestly teaching students profound truths. Clearly, the director has a clear intention to persuade, using simple logic to warn the current generation of young people who lack ideals and a sense of responsibility to turn back early. At least, they should not let the blood and tears of the pioneers be shed in vain on this vibrant land. The tone of the film is very dynamic, with great joy in the first half and great sorrow in the second half, with obvious transitions. The process of the four ruffians digging for treasures and arguing at the beginning is hilarious until they travel through time and return to the old battlefield of the World War II Patriotic War. They stand naked before the officers, are thrown into the rain to shiver together, and are mocked and teased by soldiers around them, creating a comedic effect and engaging the audience. Afterward, these comedic scenes continue, making the audience find these unfortunate guys endearing. But halfway through the film, it takes a dramatic turn with the death of the old instructor, bringing out a strong sense of tragedy. The sacrifices begin uninterrupted, and blood and passion gradually burst forth amidst the flying bullets. Until the only flower in the film, the female medical officer, sacrifices herself, cutting off the romantic hopes of the group, the climax appears. The ruffians transform from selfish realists into iron-blooded heroes, and the audience shifts from wild laughter to tears. The director is smart, choosing a relatively simple story logic to arrange the images so that the audience can easily accept a spiritual cleansing with a slightly main melody color. No wonder the young people in Russia easily accepted this simple movie and quickly embraced the old talk of their parents' generation with full emotion. At the end of the 1980s to the early 1990s, with the economic downturn and political restructuring, a generation of people who once had the ideal of dedicating themselves to socialism was completely destroyed in the ruins of the times. Since then, the new generation of young people no longer believe in their parents' teachings or grand ideals, collectively falling into a chaotic life without direction, degenerating and becoming confused, leading to decadence. Unable to rely on unreliable political environments, it might be better to revisit the most basic principles and positions of being human, awakening social responsibility. This is the most frequent call from the cultural level in Russia in recent years. Although initially treated as wind in the ears and laughed at by the youth, over time, it has been picked up in the dead-end of the spirit and recognized by more people. The declaration of idealists and the awakening fable can all be used to define this "Lost Tomb." This film does not pursue technique, nor does it force art, nor does it extensively operate scene articles. It just uses laughter and tears to touch the most vulnerable part of your heart, allowing you to shed some restlessness and gradually become clear from confusion. Imagine, a nation that resisted the powerful fascist paper tiger with one person's strength, yet in certain years of a certain era, countless skinheads and neo-Nazis emerged. They would shout those slogans that once terrified the whole world, doing the stupidest things, even tattooing the Nazi swastika on their bodies for fun. The change of times makes people multifaceted and increasingly fearful. Could it be that tens of thousands of people charging on the battlefield, shedding blood and lives, were just so that their descendants could do such foolish things? Fortunately, the brother with the swastika tattoo scraped it off with a knife, reflecting on himself drop by drop in his own blood. The director is named Andrei Malyukov, which translates to Andrey Malikov, a key player in Russian cinema in recent years. He directed the sensational Russian blockbuster "The Gate of Storms" in 2006. When he created "Lost Tomb" in 2009, he received a considerable grant from the government. Although Russian cinema has developed significantly, the economic model in recent years has not been very optimistic. Many film institutions have suffered severe losses, and affected by the international financial crisis, exports dropped by fifty percent in 2009, hurting the core. In response, Prime Minister Putin introduced a grant policy, helping the film industry overcome difficulties through government financing, and announced seven new policies for the development of domestic films at the Moscow State Gerasimov Institute of Cinematography. "Lost Tomb" was born under such a background. Compared to previous large investments and scales, it cut many costs, and the scenes were not as overwhelming as before. However, it is still very entertaining and worth watching, showcasing the creative wisdom and essence of Russian cinema.
