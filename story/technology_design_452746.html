There is a significant misunderstanding regarding the penetration of LED streetlights in foggy conditions. Most people believe that the penetration of LED streetlights is lower than that of traditional sodium lamps, which is a mistaken perception. Many people form this opinion based on information found online or their sensory experiences on the road, without conducting scientific evaluations and analyses, leading to the belief that LED streetlights have poor light penetration. This article will provide a scientific analysis of this situation.

We all know that the range of visible light is between 380nm and 780nm, and any light within this range can be perceived and identified by the human eye. As shown in Figure 1,

This means that all lighting fixtures only have utilitarian value for light within this wavelength range; anything outside of it cannot be identified by the human eye. The analysis of penetration in this article is also confined to this range. Below are the visible spectra of high-pressure sodium lamps and LEDs:

Figure 2: Visible spectrum of a high-pressure sodium lamp (Shanghai Yaming)

Figure 3: Visible spectrum of an LED (Shanxi Guangyu Semiconductor Lighting)

From the spectrum diagrams, we can see that the spectrum of LEDs is much broader than that of sodium lamps. Both have peaks at 580nm. The blue light portion of LEDs accounts for about 6% of the total luminous flux, whereas sodium lamps have almost none. The distribution of red light in LEDs is wider than in high-pressure sodium lamps.

The impact of foggy and rainy weather on road lighting mainly manifests through the scattering effect of suspended particles in the fog and raindrops, which reduces the penetration of light and directly decreases lighting intensity. The theoretical basis for the scattering of light by airborne particles primarily involves Rayleigh scattering and Mie-Debye scattering laws. The applicable range of these scattering laws within the visible light spectrum is as follows: r represents the radius of the particle; when r0.037μm, it follows Mie-Debye scattering law. Rayleigh scattering has a relationship with wavelength, while Mie-Debye scattering does not affect the scattering outcome based on wavelength. The radius size distribution of suspended particles in fog and rain is as follows: fog is 2.8μm~10μm, raindrops are 10μm~5000μm. Since the particles are larger and exceed 0.037μm, the scattering of light during foggy and rainy days is not related to wavelength. In other words, the penetration in road lighting is unrelated to the color of the lamps. The penetration of LEDs is the same as that of sodium lamps.

In traditional experience, it may seem that sodium lamps have stronger penetration, but this is merely a subjective perception. Since the light from sodium lamps is dark yellow and LED light is white, and fog is white, it is much easier for humans to notice sodium lamps against a white background compared to noticing LED lights. As shown in the figure below:

Figure 4: Color distinguishability against a white background

As shown in the figure, it is much easier to identify other colors against a white background compared to identifying white against a white background. This effect is useful in other lighting fields but has no practical use in road lighting where the focus is on the lighting effects along the road direction.

In road lighting, especially during heavy fog, the color rendering index (CRI) cannot be overlooked. The CRI of sodium lamps is around 17, while the CRI of ordinary LEDs is between 65 and 80. Due to the absence of blue spectrum in sodium lamps, their CRI is relatively low. Under sodium lamp illumination, blue objects appear black, meaning there is no reflected light when sodium lamp light hits blue objects. This could lead to situations where pedestrians wearing blue clothing or blue obstacles on the road are not seen due to the lack of reflected light, causing them to blend into the background light in foggy conditions, making them invisible to drivers and potentially leading to traffic accidents. Using LED lighting would prevent such situations.

Additionally, there are distinctions between multi-chip and integrated LED streetlights. Multi-chip LEDs have a larger emitting area and lower surface brightness, while integrated packaged LEDs have a smaller emitting area and higher surface brightness. Therefore, integrated packaged LEDs have a certain advantage in directionality on the illuminated surface, making it easier for people to see the extension of the road in heavy fog, providing better visibility during foggy weather.

In conclusion, the traditional view that sodium lamps have better penetration than LED streetlights in foggy conditions is biased. This article theoretically argues that sodium lamps do not have an advantage over LED streetlights in terms of penetration. On the contrary, considering the color rendering index, LED streetlights perform better in distinguishing physical objects during foggy weather. Furthermore, considering the surface luminous intensity of the light source, integrated packaged LED sources have advantages in lighting during rainy and foggy weather.
