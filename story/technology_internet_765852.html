Microsoft says goodbye to the Ballmer era: succeeded by desktop, departed by mobile

by anonymous on 2013-08-26 14:16:52

After endless controversies, Balmer ultimately succumbed to the pressure and announced he would resign as Microsoft CEO within a year. The news was both shocking and expected.

Balmer's personality and management style have been one of the reasons for criticism. This muscular bald man is an absolute gem in the U.S. tech circle. As the leader of the software giant Microsoft, Balmer never possessed the personal charm of Steve Jobs or former Google CEO Eric Schmidt, nor did he have the appeal within their companies that current Apple CEO Tim Cook and Google CEO Larry Page had. His comedic talent, explosive temperament, and iron-fisted management formed a huge contradiction.

With Balmer present, there would never be a shortage of laughs. He could jump around like a child at a Microsoft conference, shouting excitedly about his love for Microsoft; he could also stick out his tongue wildly during media interviews, loudly mocking competitors' products like the iPhone. These "embarrassing moments" became reasons for people to mock him.

Balmer also had a fierce and cold-blooded side. When senior executives jumped ship to Google, Balmer would throw chairs in the office as a threat; when he saw employees using iPhones, Balmer would snatch the phone and throw it on the ground; when executives potentially threatened his power, he would mercilessly drive away these potential competitors.

Balmer's critics included not only investors and analysts but also many senior executives and employees at Microsoft. Former Microsoft COO Joachim Kempin, who served Microsoft for 20 years, once criticized his former direct superior Balmer in a book after leaving, stating that while Balmer was a good COO, he was absolutely not an outstanding CEO. Kempin also accused Balmer of only considering his own interests and driving away many excellent executives who might threaten his position at Microsoft.

The brilliant executives "driven away" by Balmer included Richard Belluzzo, who brought Microsoft its biggest growth highlight in the past decade with Xbox, and former Windows head Steve Sinofsky. Sinofsky's sudden departure was considered a huge loss for Microsoft and was seen by outsiders as a direct result of his attempt to challenge Balmer for the Microsoft CEO position.

Although Balmer's management style has always been controversial, the fundamental reason forcing him to leave ultimately came down to one thing: the mobile market. Outsiders have always regarded Balmer as an excellent manager, not a forward-thinking leader. Kempin criticized, saying that Balmer had completely lost touch with the market, and Microsoft needed a young commander aged 35-40 who could truly understand the current mobile and social era.

In fact, ever since he took over from Microsoft's legendary founder Bill Gates as CEO in 2000, Balmer has been under immense pressure. In recent years, calls for his resignation have never ceased, viewing the then 57-year-old Balmer as an obstacle to Microsoft's progress. What made Balmer even more embarrassed was that after announcing his resignation, the market reacted with unusual excitement, causing Microsoft's stock price to soar nearly 10% in pre-market trading.

Balmer's controversy wasn't due to Microsoft's weak performance. On the contrary, under Balmer's leadership, Microsoft performed like a cash-generating monster, achieving continuous growth in revenue and profit. Microsoft's sales soared from $25.3 billion to $74.3 billion, operating profits increased from $11.7 billion to $25.3 billion, accumulating up to $63 billion in cash reserves. Microsoft also provided shareholders with over $180 billion in investment returns (dividend payments and share repurchases). However, in stark contrast, the stock price of this software giant stagnated for more than a decade, watching idly as Apple and Google's market values approached and eventually surpassed theirs.

Investors' indifference towards Microsoft mainly stemmed from future growth prospects and missed opportunities in the mobile market, which were also the main reasons for Balmer being criticized. During Balmer's tenure, although Microsoft maintained a significant advantage in desktop operating systems and enterprise markets, it missed the most important development opportunity in the past five years - the mobile market. Meanwhile, under Jobs' management, Apple seized a huge lead in the mobile field with the iPhone and iPad; Google also captured over 70% of the smartphone market share through its free and open Android system.

Microsoft entered the mobile market early but acted slowly as the mobile internet era arrived, almost entirely ceding this future market to Google and Apple. Even though they attempted to catch up in the past two years through an alliance with Nokia, the WP platform just barely surpassed BlackBerry, becoming the bleak third-largest mobile platform with a 5% share. And last year’s launch of Windows 8, Microsoft’s biggest bet in the past decade, didn’t yield the expected results. All these were viewed by outsiders as failures in Balmer's leadership.

It's easy to blame everything on Balmer, but it's also unfair. Microsoft's enormous success in its traditional fields hindered them from quickly entering the mobile market like Google and Apple. But as the company's CEO, Balmer's responsibility cannot be avoided. Comparing Apple and Google's rapid rise in the past five years, Microsoft's decline stands out even more. However, the reason Balmer could remain as Microsoft's CEO for so many years amid controversies was mainly due to the full support of Gates and Microsoft's board.

Balmer and Gates have known each other for 40 years, and he has been at Microsoft for 33 years. There has been little criticism regarding Balmer's business acumen. He was a core contributor to Microsoft's prosperity; it was Balmer who, in 1981, was responsible for reaching an agreement with IBM to launch the PC operating system, laying the foundation for Microsoft's monopolistic advantage in the desktop system. He earned Gates' trust because of this, entering Microsoft's core management team, and ultimately succeeding Gates in leading the then-peak Microsoft.

In Balmer's farewell email to employees, besides discussing how Microsoft is at a critical period of transitioning into a device and service company, much of it expressed his infinite attachment to Microsoft. Balmer used the word "love" seven times in the email, and it's believed that this fully reflected his genuine feelings for Microsoft. During his 33 years at Microsoft, the company's sales grew from $7.5 million to $78 billion, and the number of employees expanded from 30 to nearly 100,000, with a large portion of this growth attributed to Balmer's contributions.

But this time, Balmer seemed unable to withstand the pressure. According to Balmer's fiery and stubborn character, he would never easily give up his position as Microsoft CEO, nor would he announce his resignation right after completing a management restructuring. Although the details of his resignation haven't been uncovered by the media yet, judging from various signs, Gates and Microsoft's board, who have always supported Balmer, finally decided to let him go, hoping to restart Microsoft through a change in leadership.

When Gates gave an interview at the beginning of the year, although he still expressed support for Balmer, he subtly indicated dissatisfaction with Microsoft's current situation. “Are these enough (Microsoft's progress over the past year)? No! Neither he (Balmer) nor I are satisfied.” Perhaps Gates already thought that his old friend might no longer be suitable to lead Microsoft into the new mobile era, thus planting the seeds for Balmer's eventual quiet resignation today.

Transitioning into a device and service company was the future strategy set by Balmer for the software giant Microsoft. However, this strategy also faced consistent criticism from the outside world. Transitioning into a device company might jeopardize Microsoft's long-term cooperative relationship with PC manufacturers, pushing these partners toward the Google Android camp. When Microsoft launched its own brand tablet, Surface, it received much criticism from OEM manufacturers like Acer. Moreover, the RT platform device's sales failure made Balmer feel extremely awkward.

According to Microsoft employees, at the release of the Surface RT tablet, Microsoft internally was overly optimistic about the RT platform's prospects, completely unaware of the difficulty of rebuilding a new platform under the monopoly advantages of Google and Apple. In the previous fiscal quarter, Microsoft was forced to record a $900 million asset impairment related to Surface RT. This massive impairment (along with nearly $1 billion in marketing costs) far exceeded the sales of Surface RT.

In Microsoft's traditional stronghold, the desktop system, the introduction of Win 8 failed to effectively stop the decline in PC sales. At least from the current perspective, Microsoft's big gamble on Win 8 hasn't yielded the expected results. IDC data showed that global PC sales declined by 14% in the first quarter of this year, marking the worst performance in decades. IDC bluntly criticized Microsoft, believing that Win 8, which integrated the desktop and tablet interfaces, not only failed to positively boost the PC market but also slowed market development.

Of course, it's still too early to definitively judge the final effectiveness of Win 8. Microsoft's tablet journey has just begun, and many manufacturers are continuously releasing new Win 8 tablets. However, Microsoft's board and Gates seem to have lost patience. Balmer mentioned in his farewell email that he originally intended to retire halfway through Microsoft's transformation, but now Microsoft needs a new CEO to lead the company along the transformation path for a longer period, and his resignation aligns with the best interests of the beloved Microsoft.

Tech giants who left quietly due to missing out on mobile market opportunities aren't just limited to Balmer. Intel's former CEO Paul Otellini also suddenly announced his resignation at the end of last year. Similar to Microsoft's situation, Microsoft's old ally Intel was also obsessed with the tremendous success in the desktop field, sitting idly by as ARM and Qualcomm took the lead in the mobile chip market, only realizing the missed opportunity after the trend was determined. Although Intel re-entered the mobile field last year, it remains difficult to approach Qualcomm's dominant position in the short term, and they are also facing low-cost impacts from MediaTek (MTK) chips.

Google's market capitalization surpassing Microsoft, and Qualcomm eventually overtaking Intel—these two symbolic changes seem to indicate that the desktop era has officially given way to the mobile market. The tide of technology surges forward, and Balmer ultimately grasped the growth opportunity in the PC domain, leading him to helm the tech giant Microsoft, only to miss the mobile domain's opportunity and leave quietly. Succeeding in the desktop era and parting ways in the mobile era might be the best portrayal of Balmer.

After bidding farewell to the 13-year-long Balmer era, Microsoft needs a forward-thinking new CEO to re-launch an offensive in the mobile field and compete for market share in the smartphone and tablet markets dominated by Google and Apple. This new CEO will face the most severe challenges in the tech sector. Can Microsoft write a new pioneering legend?
