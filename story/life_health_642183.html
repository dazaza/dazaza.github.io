It's getting colder —— Three techniques should be noted when soaking feet

by huiming on 2011-12-23 11:57:19

Water temperature around 40 degrees Celsius, time 30 minutes, water level above the ankles.

Great Cold is the coldest period of the year, and many people start to warm up by soaking their feet. Because the feet are the root of the kidneys, soaking your feet can warm the kidney yang. However, foot soaking also requires a certain timing, just like stewing soup - if it's too short, it won't be flavorful; if it's too long, it will overcook. Only when the timing is just right can the best health benefits be achieved.

First, control the water temperature. Some people have the habit of soaking their feet until they turn red, assuming that the higher the water temperature, the better the effect. In fact, the foot-soaking water should not be too hot, with around 40°C being ideal. This is because excessively high water temperatures can cause excessive expansion of the blood vessels in the feet, leading more blood to flow to the lower limbs, which may result in insufficient blood supply to important organs such as the heart, brain, and kidneys. Especially for those with cardiovascular diseases, this could make things worse. On the other hand, high water temperatures can easily damage the sebum membrane on the surface of the foot skin, causing dryness or even cracking of the stratum corneum.

Second, control the duration. Some people simply put their feet in water for a while and then take them out and wipe them, which is at most just washing the feet. If the time is too short, there will be no health benefits. Generally, the soaking time should be about twenty to thirty minutes. You must feel warm all over and slightly sweating on the forehead for the best timing. The forehead belongs to the stomach meridian, and the stomach meridian is the Yangming Meridian. When the balance of qi and blood is achieved, and sweat appears on the forehead, it indicates that warmth has reached the internal organs and meridians. Some people prefer to soak until they sweat profusely, but this consumes primordial energy and means the soaking has gone too far. If one sweats profusely throughout the winter, they will likely feel weak in the spring. Therefore, controlling the timing is crucial.

Third, control the amount of water. When you start soaking, the water should cover the top of the feet. As the water cools, add more until it covers the Sanyinjiao acupoint, located three inches above the inner ankle bone, for the best effect.

Add some "seasonings" to your foot soak:

Cold feet? Add ginger: For cold feet, use a piece of ginger (about the size of a date), smash it flat with a knife, add safflower, wrap it in cheesecloth, boil it in water, and then add a spoonful of salt.

Sore waist? Add safflower: To promote blood circulation and resolve blood stasis, take 5 grams of safflower, wrap it in cheesecloth, boil it in water, then add a spoonful of salt. First steam your feet, then soak them. This treats sore waist, leg varicose veins, and female dysmenorrhea.

Cough? Add mugwort leaves: Place 10 grams of mugwort leaves wrapped in cheesecloth into a pot, heat the water until boiling, first steam your feet, then soak them when the water temperature reaches between 40-50°C. This is beneficial for coughs caused by wind-cold invasion.

Smelly feet? Add Sichuan pepper: To prevent foot odor, foot sweat, and athlete's foot, grab a handful of Sichuan peppers, wrap them in cheesecloth, boil them in water, first steam your feet, then soak them when the water temperature is suitable for submersion.
