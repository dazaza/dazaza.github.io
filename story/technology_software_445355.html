Buffets are a common dining venue in life.

by hjdsk11879 on 2011-06-13 16:28:01

If we do not know the basic etiquette of a buffet, we will often be laughed at. Don't be like the joke that says "go in holding the wall and come out holding the wall." Holding the wall to go in is because of hunger and instability, and holding the wall to come out is due to eating too much and being unable to walk. Although we pay once and can eat all the food and drink all the beverages as we wish, we still need to eat in moderation; otherwise, it will harm our spleen and stomach, which would not be worthwhile! Hehe, below I will share some buffet etiquette with everyone.

1. The Origin of Buffet

The true origin of the buffet is from the Scandinavian Peninsula of Northern Europe during the 8th to 11th centuries. At that time, when the pirates had a successful hunt, the pirate leader would appear and hold a grand feast for all the pirates to celebrate. But the pirates were unfamiliar with and did not get used to the formalities of eating Western cuisine in Central and Western Europe at that time, so they innovatively came up with this way of eating where they selected and took their own food and drinks from the food station. Later, the Western catering industry standardized and enriched the content of this dining style, which became today's buffet. Many Western professional buffet restaurants are now named "Pirate Restaurants" for this reason.

2. Buffet Etiquette

Buffet etiquette here mainly refers to the specific etiquette norms that need to be followed when participating in a buffet as a diner. Generally speaking, in buffet etiquette, the etiquette of enjoying a buffet is often more important for most people. Usually, it mainly involves the following eight points.

First, you need to queue up to take your food. When enjoying a buffet, even though diners need to take care of themselves, this does not mean that they can do so by any means. In fact, when taking food, because diners often come alone, everyone must consciously maintain public order, follow the first-come-first-served principle, and line up to select food. It is not allowed to push, grab, cut in line, or skip the line altogether. Before taking the food, prepare a plate. When it's your turn to take the food, use the communal utensils to put the food into your plate, then quickly leave. Do not hesitate too long in front of the abundant food. Letting those behind you wait for a long time is also inappropriate. You should not pick and choose excessively, or even directly use your hands or personal utensils to take the food.

Second, you need to take food in sequence. At a buffet, if you want to eat well and be satisfied, you must first understand the reasonable order of taking dishes and proceed step by step. According to common knowledge, the standard order of taking dishes when attending a regular buffet is: cold dishes, soup, hot dishes, pastries, desserts, and fruits. Therefore, when taking food, it's best to first walk around the entire area to understand the situation, and then go to take the food. If you don't understand this reasonable order of taking food and completely act on your own, mixing and eating randomly, it may result in misplaced priorities, conflicting flavors, making yourself feel neither satisfied nor comfortable. For example, at a buffet, desserts and fruits should be eaten as the "finale," last. However, if you don't follow this rule and eat a lot of sweets and fruits first just for novelty, you'll soon be full. When later you see the good food you wanted to eat, you may find yourself unable to help but "look on helplessly."

Third, you need to be realistic. When attending a buffet and encountering food you like, as long as it won't make you overeat, you can fully enjoy it without worrying about quantity, since unlimited supply is one of the reasons why buffets are so popular. Therefore, business personnel attending a buffet can certainly not worry about others laughing at them; just eat what they like. However, it should be noted that when selecting food according to your taste, you must be practical. Do not take excessive amounts of food just to satisfy your craving, resulting in "high expectations but low abilities," leading to food waste. Strictly speaking, while eating a buffet, eating more is allowed, but wasting food is absolutely not allowed. This rule is called the "take less" principle of eating at a buffet. Sometimes, it is also referred to as the "take less each time" principle.

Fourth, you need to take food multiple times. While following the "take less" principle at a buffet, you must also follow the "multiple times" principle. The "multiple times" principle is short for the "take food multiple times" principle. Its specific meaning is: diners at a buffet are allowed to repeatedly take the same type of dish several times. Each time, you should only take a small amount, and after tasting it, if you find it suitable, you can take it again until you feel satisfied. In other words, this principle actually means that when selecting a certain dish at a buffet, taking it multiple times is fine, and adding more each time is allowed. On the contrary, if you take an excessive amount at once to avoid trouble, and end up with too much food that you cannot finish, it will definitely make others stare in astonishment. The "multiple times" principle and the "take less" principle are actually two different aspects of the same issue. "Multiple times" is for practicality, and "take less" is to avoid waste. Therefore, these two principles are often combined and referred to as the "take less multiple times" principle. People who know how to eat buffets understand that when selecting dishes, it's best to take only one type at a time. After finishing, you can then take another type. If you don't know this, and mix various dishes together in your plate, causing mixed flavors and ruining the taste, it will indeed be wasteful.

Fifth, you need to avoid taking food away. All buffets, whether hosted personally by the host as a form of entertainment or operated in formal restaurants open to the public, have an unwritten rule: buffets only allow diners to enjoy the food on-site and absolutely do not allow taking leftovers home after the meal. When attending a buffet, you must firmly remember this point. No matter how much you eat during the meal, it's no problem, but you should never secretly stuff your pockets or bags with things you like, nor ask the waiter to "pack" them for you. Such behavior will undoubtedly make you look foolish.

Sixth, you need to return the tableware. Since the emphasis at a buffet is on self-service, diners must keep this in mind throughout the entire dining process and conscientiously implement it. Emphasizing self-service at a buffet not only requires diners to primarily serve themselves when taking food but also requires them to tidy up and return their tableware to the designated place after the meal. In general situations, most buffets require diners to gather their tableware together after finishing their meal and before leaving the dining area, then return them to the specified location. When having a buffet in a courtyard or garden, this practice is especially necessary. It is not allowed to discard the tableware randomly or even deliberately damage it. When dining seated in a restaurant, sometimes you can leave the tableware on the table when you leave, and let the waiter clean it up. Even so, tidying it up slightly before leaving is better. Do not leave your table in disarray, looking messy and unpleasant. The food you take should ideally be finished. If there are a few leftovers, do not secretly discard, pour out, or hide them, but rather place them in an appropriate spot.

Seventh, you need to take care of others. When attending a buffet, besides strictly controlling your own dining behavior, you must also get along harmoniously with others and take care of them. Special attention should be given to your companions. If they are unfamiliar with the buffet, you might briefly introduce it to them. Under their willingness, you could provide detailed suggestions on choosing dishes. For acquaintances you meet at the buffet, similar consideration should be shown. However, you should not decide for others and directly take food for them, nor should you "dispose" of food you dislike or cannot finish by giving it to them. During the dining process, you should treat other diners politely. When queuing, taking food, finding seats, and moving around, you should actively yield to other diners, avoiding behaving rudely or ignoring others.
