Shipment numbers are deceptive: Samsung's high-end models grow much slower than Apple's

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 20:57:35

At an investor meeting held on Wednesday, Samsung Mobile revealed that its high-end models saw slow sales growth in 2013, far behind its competitor Apple. Although the overall shipment volume was much higher than Apple's, only one-third of the shipments were high-end models competing with the iPhone.

Ignoring sales figures, Samsung is stepping up acquisition efforts. Many investors are concerned about the poor sales of Samsung's high-end models. Samsung convened this investor meeting to instill confidence in these investors, stating that the company is more focused on its previous plans and accelerating its merger and acquisition pace.

A previous Bloomberg report quoted Samsung's Chief Financial Officer, Lee Sang Hoon, as saying: "In the future, we will expand our M&A strategy in some target areas and pursue business opportunities in a wide range of fields."

Samsung stated that since 2010, the company has only carried out 14 acquisitions, with a total amount of just $1 billion. This is a world apart from Google, which acquires a company almost every week, while Apple recently stated that it had completed 15 "strategic acquisitions" last year.

Bloomberg's report indicated: "Samsung beat Apple in smartphone sales by making profits from selling large numbers of low-end phones in emerging markets, but its sales growth in the relatively saturated high-end market was slow."

This also aligns with Samsung's recently released quarterly earnings report, where Samsung stated: "The increase in mass-market model sales drove an overall increase in shipments quarter-over-quarter, while high-end model sales remained flat quarter-over-quarter."

In stark contrast, Apple's iPhone sales for the quarter ending in September this year hit a record high, growing by 26% compared to the same quarter last year. All smartphones sold by Apple are positioned for high-end users, while Samsung continues to sell outdated Galaxy Y models. Neil Mawston, Executive Director of Strategy Analytics, stated that the Galaxy Y is a "mass-market model" that helps Samsung boost its phone sales.

Samsung rarely provides exact sales figures for a single smartphone or breaks down how many high-end phones versus low-end phones running older versions of Android (like 2.0) were sold, which often have thin profit margins. In the third quarter of this year, Samsung's smartphone shipments far exceeded Apple's (about 3.5 times), yet Samsung Mobile's profit was nearly $1 billion less than Apple's.

Sales of Samsung Galaxy S and Note series reached two-thirds of iPhone sales. Samsung Mobile President Shin Jong Kyun once said: "Samsung plans to sell 100 million Galaxy S and Note series phones in 2013."

Referring to a report by Korea's ETNews, Samsung's target smartphone sales for this year are approximately 300 to 310 million units, with an estimated 360 million units for 2014, including 126 million "premium models." The report also mentioned that the flagship model Galaxy S4 "sold slowly this year," and Samsung follows a "business strategy driven by low-end models." Samsung originally predicted that Galaxy S4 sales would exceed 100 million units throughout the year, but after its release, the model's sales were quite sluggish.

Based on Samsung's current sales and future plans, only one-third of the sold phones can barely be considered high-end models that compete with Apple's current models. Interestingly, Samsung's current flagship models are already one generation behind Apple's. The Samsung Galaxy S4 scores slightly lower than the iPhone 5c, and the iPhone 5s, equipped with the A7 chip, features a 64-bit processor and better battery efficiency, surpassing all Samsung smartphones in these aspects.

According to CNET, Apple's iPhone sales reached 150 million units in the last fiscal year, with its high-end model sales growing by 26%, far exceeding the industry average (7%). This is a serious issue for Samsung, as two-thirds of its profits come from the smartphone business. Apple also heavily relies on the iPhone business, but its Mac, iPad, iTunes, and software service businesses are also highly profitable. In comparison, Samsung's Chromebook laptops, Android tablets, and Windows-based personal computers have consistently struggled to gain traction in sales.
