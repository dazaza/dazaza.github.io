Basic settings for a generic gun-type camera

by huiming588 on 2012-02-08 10:38:39

1) AGC ON/OFF (Automatic Gain Control): Inside the bullet camera, there is a video amplifier that amplifies signals from the CCD to usable levels. This amplification, or gain, is equivalent to having higher sensitivity. However, in bright lighting conditions, the amplifier may overload, causing distortion of the video signal. When the switch is set to ON, in low-light conditions, the lens aperture is fully opened, and gain is automatically increased to obtain clear images. When the switch is OFF, natural and low-noise images can be obtained in low light.

2) ATW ON/OFF (Auto Tracking White Balance): When the switch is set to ON, it detects the characteristics/color temperature of the light source through the lens and automatically and continuously sets the white level, controlling the gain of red and blue signals even if the characteristics/color temperature change.

3) ALC/ELC (Automatic Light Control/Electronic Light Control): When ELC is selected, the electronic shutter continuously and automatically changes the exposure time of the CCD image sensor based on the brightness of incoming light (usually adjustable from 1/50 to 1/10000 seconds). Choosing this method allows the use of fixed or manual aperture lenses instead of ALC automatic aperture lenses. Note: In outdoor or bright environments, due to the limited control range of ELC, ALC-type lenses should still be chosen. In certain unique lighting conditions, the following situations may occur:

① Strong trailing or blurring on high-brightness objects such as spotlights or windows.

② Significant flickering and unstable color reproduction.

③ Periodic changes in white balance. If these phenomena occur, an ALC lens should be used. When using a fixed aperture lens with ELC, the depth of field in the image may be less than that obtained with an ALC-type lens. Therefore, when the camera fully opens the fixed aperture lens and uses the ELC mode, the depth of field will be smaller than when using an ALC-type lens, and distant objects in the image may not be in focus. When the lens is an automatic aperture lens, the switch should be set to ALC mode.

4) BLC ON/OFF (Backlight Compensation Switch): When strong and useless background illumination affects the clarity of important mid-range objects, the switch should be set to the ON position. Note:

① When used with a pan-tilt unit or under rapidly changing lighting, it is recommended to set the switch to OFF, as the aperture speed slows down in the ON position.

② Backlight compensation may not function fully if the desired object is not in the middle of the image.

5) LL/INT (Sync Selection Switch): This switch selects the synchronization method for the camera, with INT being internal sync and LL being power sync. Some cameras also have an LL PHASE power sync phase controller. When the camera is in power sync mode, this device adjusts the phase of the video output signal, with an adjustment range of about one frame. (Adjustment requires professional personnel.)

6) VIDEO/DC (Lens Control Signal Selection Switch): ALC automatic aperture lenses have two types of control signals. When installing a DC-controlled automatic aperture lens on a bullet camera, the DC position should be selected. When installing a video-controlled automatic aperture lens, the VIDEO position should be selected. When selecting an ALC automatic aperture video-driven lens, there may be a video level control (VIDEO LEVEL L/H) that needs adjustment. This controller adjusts the control level output to the automatic aperture lens to control the opening and closing of the lens aperture (adjusting light brightness). In the camera accessories, there is a small black plug with four pins that connects to the black socket on the bullet camera. If using a DC-driven automatic aperture lens, the plug is already prepared on the lens; just plug it into the socket and set the selection switch to DC. If using a video-driven automatic aperture lens, users need to solder according to the instructions. Since manufacturers define differently, the soldering methods vary, so please pay attention during installation.

7) SOFT/SHARP (Detail Level Selection Switch): This switch adjusts whether the output image is sharp (SHARP) or smooth (SOFT). It is usually set to SHARP at the factory.

8) FLICKERLESS (No Flicker Mode): In regions where the power frequency is 50Hz, the CCD accumulation time is 1/50 second. If using an NISC format bullet camera, its vertical synchronization frequency is 60Hz, which will cause visual image asynchrony, resulting in flickering on the monitor. Conversely, in regions with 60Hz power using PAL format cameras, the same phenomenon occurs. To overcome this, the electronic shutter has a no-flicker mode setting, providing a fixed shutter speed of 1/100 second for NISC format bullet cameras and 1/120 second for PAL format bullet cameras, preventing image flickering on the monitor. Manual Electronic Shutter: Some users capture relatively fast-moving objects with CCDs. If shot at 1/50 second, trailing occurs, severely affecting image quality. Some bullet cameras provide a manual electronic shutter, fixing the charge coupling speed of the CCD at a specific value, such as 1/500, 1/1000, or 1/2000 seconds. At this point, the CCD's charge coupling speed increases, reducing trailing in the captured image. For observing high-speed motion or electric sparks, this setting must be used. Therefore, some specialized bullet cameras offer manual electronic shutters for special-purpose users.
