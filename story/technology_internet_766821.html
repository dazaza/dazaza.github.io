Microsoft Surface's "dilemma" of not being able to reduce prices

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 21:56:53

While people are flooding with reviews on the new iPhone, both positive and negative, only a few can tell the features of Surface 2. The unremarkable hardware upgrade and the sales strategy of sticking to no price reduction cannot make people see the hope of a big sale. Perhaps the only highlight is why Microsoft doesn't reduce the price.

Like the previous generation of products, the Surface 2 running RT system is priced at 3288 yuan, while the Pro 2 starts at 6588 yuan.

With the two-month-old promotion where Microsoft began offering a $100 discount on the previous generation of products, we can conclude that Microsoft has adopted Apple's previous strategy in its hardware update: one product at a time, software and hardware upgrades, unchanged prices, and price reductions for the previous generation.

But look at the previous generation of products; although the design was praised, it was a complete failure in terms of sales volume. In the first quarter of this year, Surface sold 900,000 units globally, compared to 19.5 million iPads during the same period. By the second quarter, the total sales of Surface amounted to 853 million US dollars, but Microsoft had to write down 900 million US dollars in asset impairment. Including the early 900 million US dollars in marketing costs, Microsoft suffered huge losses.

Under such performance, Microsoft still updates its products following Apple's approach, making it difficult to change the fate of Surface's sales volume. After all, Apple has already started selling the 5c.

High prices are the hard injury preventing Surface from achieving large-scale sales, but Microsoft has its "compulsions" for not reducing the price.

Compulsion one: Incorrect positioning - selling ultra-books' prices for tablet computers' hearts

Although when Surface was launched, Microsoft claimed it aimed to surpass Apple's iPad, comparing prices, this is completely the price of an ultra-book, even comparable to MacBook Air.

Looking back now, Microsoft's plan was likely this: Surface RT competes with iPad, both priced around 450 US dollars, while Surface Pro competes with Air, both around 700 US dollars. Such positioning leads to predictable outcomes.

Not only is the product's positioning inaccurate, but Microsoft's self-positioning is also key. Backed by iOS' vast ecosystem, iPads have countless apps available on the day of shipment, seamlessly transitioning with iPhone usage. However, Microsoft's RT truly lacks usable applications, with the only function being opening a browser.

The biggest misalignment lies in mindset. Microsoft is one of the world's largest software companies, but on the path of tablet computers, Microsoft is a follower. To break into an existing market, Microsoft's stance is too high.

Compulsion two: Pressure from OEM partners

Through Surface, what Microsoft aims to boost isn't itself. Earning over 2 billion US dollars in a year isn't as profitable as selling more software. What Microsoft wants to drive is the PC OEM ecosystem showing signs of decline, but Surface has become the complaint point of former partners.

The most direct complaint came from Wang Zhentang of Acer, who clearly stated early on that Microsoft's move would affect the survival situation of OEM partners after Surface's release. Subsequently, Samsung and Lenovo both released RT-version computers, none of which saw any sales data or significant market promotion.

Due to this pressure, Microsoft cannot drastically lower the price of Surface. OEMs can only rely on price cuts to gain sales. If Microsoft follows this route, former allies will undoubtedly turn against them.

Compulsion three: High manufacturing costs due to low volume

On the road to becoming 'Microhard,' Microsoft does not lack design craftsmanship, but the PC market is a scaled market where quantity determines production costs. Microsoft's quarterly sales of less than one million units also lead to excessively high production costs, preventing Microsoft from freely lowering prices.

Compulsion four: Unable to make money from applications

Low production volumes but profitable examples do exist; Amazon's Kindle Fire is a typical case. Unlike Microsoft, Amazon dares to sell hardware at a loss.

For Microsoft, selling one Surface earns a single profit, but Amazon can continue to make money after selling devices. This is the issue with the entire Windows platform software ecosystem.

Insisting on the RT direction, Microsoft might spend five to ten years copying half of the App Store, but this will result in even worse hardware sales during this period. Switching to Pro may increase sales, but the software ecosystem still won't be profitable because it supports various free software from before.
