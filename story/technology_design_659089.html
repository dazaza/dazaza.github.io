Uh, finally after pausing many times, I've finished this long-delayed article... Actually, I didn't originally plan to write any application reflections, as I personally believe that all aspiring JD applicants have the ability to search, judge, and discern useful information for themselves. However, later on, given that some classmates seemed interested in my application experience, and also as a reference for those applying to other programs (PhD, undergrad, etc.), I will share my thoughts here. These are just my personal opinions.

First, let me introduce my own situation. I applied to six law schools' JD programs. As of the time of writing this article, I've received admissions offers from Columbia, Chicago, NYU, and Georgetown. Among them, Chicago offered a $60,000 merit-based scholarship. The other two schools (Harvard and Stanford) are still under review. Personally, I suspect I might end up on their waitlists, so I've largely decided to attend Columbia.

Before applying to schools, it's crucial to plan your future carefully. After thoroughly understanding each school's characteristics, you should apply to the ones that suit you best. Here, I must cite Adam Smith's point in "The Wealth of Nations" - in the legal profession, the success and high salaries of a few are built on the failure of the majority. Therefore, regarding American law schools, if you can't get into a T14 school, then pursuing a Master's or PhD at an average university might be a better choice. Within the T14, the division of labor is quite clear. For example, Stanford is definitely the top choice for intellectual property law (with UC Berkeley as the second), Columbia or NYU for business and international law, Yale for politics, and Harvard is good at everything but not particularly distinctive in anything. If you plan to work at a large law firm in the future, it's better to study in a big city rather than a rural area, as there are more opportunities for internships, part-time jobs, and even interviews in big cities. (According to surveys, the top law firm placement index for major law schools is Columbia > Chicago > Harvard > NYU > Stanford > Yale, while UC Berkeley unfortunately ranks last among the T14, showing how important location is. Since clerkships are irrelevant to international students, we'll skip that topic. Although finding a job with a JD seems difficult these days, compared to MDs, it's still relatively easier.) In short, what suits you best is the best.

In the application materials, the first thing you need to prepare is undoubtedly various exams and academic records. For law schools, if you can score above 170 on the LSAT (similar to 2200 on SAT, 1500 on GRE?), then applying to any school should be no problem. Of course, if you want to stand out, your score should ideally be above 176. Since I took the LSAT twice with scores of 172 and 171 (the second score was even lower...), I think standardized test scores don't necessarily have to be extremely high; they just need to be sufficient. (And in recent years, the LSAT seems to be getting harder... I could easily score above 176 on practice tests from five years ago.) If you're studying in North America, your GPA will be very important. If your GPA is above 3.7 and you're in the top 5% of your school, this should be sufficient for most schools. (My own GPA is 3.9, but I know friends from the same school who got into NYU with a 3.5, showing how flexible this indicator can be.) Of course, under the same grades, no one would consider Franklin & Marshall College and Princeton to have the same level of prestige, just like being fifth at Tsinghua University and fifth at Guangdong University of Technology are entirely different things. As for TOEFL, most law schools don't require it... For schools that do, although 100 is enough, if you meet the aforementioned conditions, you might as well aim for above 110. In short, scores are hard indicators and naturally, the higher the better.

Next come the recommendation letters. Except for MBA, almost all graduate programs, such as JD and PhD, require at least two university professor recommendation letters. I must emphasize that recommendation letters should be written by people who truly know you well, so they can write meaningfully. The fame of the recommender isn't actually that important, after all, as ordinary students, how many celebrities can we possibly know? Of course, if the recommender knows you well and is also famous, that's ideal. If the recommender has a close relationship with the admission officer or relevant personnel at the target school, that's even better. But for such people, a phone call is usually more effective than a written recommendation...

As for choosing recommenders, you must prepare early and maintain contact through various means, so when the time comes for the recommendation letter, everything feels natural and smooth. I asked a philosophy teacher and a history teacher to write my recommendation letters. I took the philosophy teacher's class for a year and was the VP of the student association he managed, so getting the recommendation letter was smooth, and the teacher even helped proofread my personal statement, which moved me deeply. As for the history teacher, I took his class for a year, audited for half a year, and went to Italy with him for archaeology during the summer, so this recommendation letter should also be meaningful. Another important point is that when asking teachers for recommendation letters, you must provide them with sufficient information, i.e., your resume, personal statement, school transcripts, papers you wrote in their classes, etc. This way, the recommender can have a comprehensive understanding of your application, and the recommendation letter can be closely tied to your other application materials. That is, the materials you provide to the recommender should contain sufficient hints. Moreover, recommenders are very busy, and if they forget to write the recommendation letter, when they see the folder full of materials, they might remember. Finally, don't forget to give the recommender a unique small gift after receiving the recommendation letter and inform them of your application results to express your gratitude. If the recommender feels their recommendation letter really helped you, they will be more enthusiastic about writing recommendation letters for your juniors in the future, thus creating a positive cycle. If you've done all the above and the teacher still asks you to write a "self-recommendation letter," then I can only say, tragedy.

Being outstanding academically alone seems insufficient to secure an admission offer. Therefore, so-called soft skills, such as leadership and similar qualities, are equally important. And these abilities are mainly demonstrated through various extracurricular activities and work experiences. If you're a current student, extracurricular activities will be very important. The overall principle is that the larger the scale and influence of the activities you participate in, the better; the higher your position, the better; the more resources you manage, the better. For law school students, the most conventional path is participating in Model United Nations, winning various awards, and organizing large conferences. This is also the path I followed. Other options include the school newspaper, student publications, student government, debates, etc. In fact, thinking about it, the people involved in these activities are usually the same group, and their relationships are intertwined. For current students, work experience isn't particularly important, but it's best to participate in some internships or other social practices every summer to demonstrate your concern for society and prove that you're not a shut-in who stays home watching anime all day (well, actually I am). For returning students, work experience becomes very important (even more critical than LSAT or GPA), according to law school admission officers. Unless you're a banker at Morgan Stanley, a consultant at McKinsey, a spy at the CIA, or serving in Somalia, other work experiences are optional and won't add points to your application. On the contrary, writing about menial jobs like dishwashing might even deduct points... In comparison, for PhD applicants, the ultimate goal is writing papers, and everything else is trivial.

The last item, and the one most people have questions about, is the Personal Statement. Frankly, I believe that among the application materials, the PS and recommendation letters are the least important. Why do I say that? If an applicant has strong academic abilities and extracurricular activities, a poor PS or recommendation letter won't necessarily lead to rejection; similarly, if their academic abilities and extracurricular activities lack highlights, even the strongest recommendation letters and PS won't secure admission. The true role of the PS is to serve as a judgment criterion when two applicants have similar hardware qualifications, because "personal charm" and "writing style" are indescribable qualities. Meanwhile, if someone has very poor grades or no meaningful extracurricular activities, their application materials will immediately go into the trash bin. In other words, the PS is only significant for borderline cases. Despite this, the more elite the school, the more outstanding the applicants, the greater the possibility of becoming borderline. Therefore, from this perspective, the PS seems to become the most important thing, as it's your rare (or even sole) opportunity to differentiate yourself.

So, what exactly should the PS contain? Many people believe that the PS should comprehensively summarize their abilities, fearing omission. However, in reality, except for specific literary creations, applied articles must have a clear theme and focus. Let's reverse roles. Admission Officers (AOs) read countless PSs every day, spending no more than three minutes on each one on average. If the essay lacks clarity and its main idea isn't immediately apparent, AOs won't spend time carefully analyzing your PS but will simply move on to the next application. Therefore, the PS must be concise and vivid, avoiding making readers lose interest after scanning the first sentence of each paragraph. Besides, word limits for PSs are usually very strict, typically around 500-800 words (two pages, 12-point font, double-spaced, Times New Roman, normal margins), leaving little room for verbosity. The best way to make an article concise is to set a clear central theme and develop the content around it. This way, irrelevant ramblings naturally won't appear in the article. While the article must be concise, it must also be lively, and the liveliest element is genuine emotion. In fact, the original purpose of the PS is to understand the applicant's personal traits, not their achievements or skills (which is the purpose of the resume). In other words, the PS aims to answer "Who are you?" and "What is the significance of your existence among the masses?"—that is, the characteristics that confirm your uniqueness. The reasons why you have these ideals, concepts, and desires must be related to your life experiences. So, talk about these life experiences. Comprehensive and abstract descriptions tend to flatten individuals, making them generic "extras." Any article with substance generally leaves a good impression on AOs. As for how much talent the article reflects, it's not particularly important. In short, the PS must be "interesting" and leave an impression; otherwise, it's as if it wasn't written at all. Our goal is to bring joy to AOs in their busy work. If they're happy, we'll be happy. The topic doesn't matter; what matters is writing it well. Of course, taking the tear-jerker route is also possible, but making a tear-jerking story feel fresh is quite challenging. In short, we're actors aiming to entertain our audience (AOs). In this context, so-called "reader-centric" articles are undoubtedly the easiest to touch AOs. Everyone scored higher than me on college entrance essays, so you likely understand "reader-centric" articles better than I do, and I won't embarrass myself further. After writing the article, it's best to have 1-3 reliable friends review it and offer suggestions, though there's no need to revise it thirteen or fourteen times as some suggest. After all, AOs value the first impression the article makes, and they don't have the time to analyze every detail. One point to note: don't blindly trust the English abilities of Caucasians...

To give everyone a reference, I'll briefly discuss the absurd content of my own PS. Before that, I'd like to share my views on a few common application issues:

"Why Essay." Many people write essays or paragraphs like "Why XXX School?" when applying. My suggestion is, unless it's mandatory or you have a strong emotional connection to a particular school, it's best not to write these types of essays. Reasons easily found by Googling online aren't real reasons, as anyone can write them, and they completely fail to highlight the applicant's uniqueness, wasting valuable space in the PS. As for flattering the school, that's unnecessary; AOs know their school better than anyone. In short, unless it's something only you can write, writing it has no meaning because the ultimate goal of the PS is to differentiate yourself. Some worry that not writing a "why" essay might seem disloyal to the school, but that's unnecessary. If AO doesn't have confidence in their school, you shouldn't go there either.

"Optional Statement/Addendum." Many schools have a final section on the application form that reads:

"Optional Statement: The Law School recruits students from diverse racial, economic, educational, and ethnic backgrounds. The Admission Committee encourages you to attach a brief statement that will enable the Committee to understand the contribution your personal background would make to the student body of XXX Law School."

This is a controversial issue. Many people I know say that if you can write an optional statement (also known as a diversity statement), you should, as it shows effort and submitting extra material never hurts. Personally, I believe that if you can avoid writing an optional statement, you should. First, an Optional Statement should never be longer than the PS; it's generally recommended to be one page single-spaced. In such limited space, I doubt people can write anything particularly diverse. More importantly, if you can read this article, I don't think you're the kind of applicant who fits the American diversity standard. True diversity belongs to underground party members, reincarnated living Buddhas, Somali militiamen, etc. If you have similar experiences, why not use the PS to elaborate instead of putting it in this optional section? Moreover, as I've repeatedly emphasized, brevity is a virtue in law, and verbosity leads to mistakes. If your optional statement is well-written, it will become a distraction because only parts that don't fit into regular application materials go into the optional section, and an overly excellent optional statement will weaken the impression of the formal materials on AO. As for explaining grades, never do it; it's suicidal.

Regarding statistics. I remember when I applied for undergraduate studies, many people meticulously analyzed the admission styles of various schools and summarized experiences like certain schools prefer Chinese students. Personally, I think these patterns may exist, but they aren't absolute indicators. As long as your application materials are good enough, adding an extra spot just for you isn't impossible. For instance, about 12% of Columbia Law School's JD class are international students (about 40 people), while Chicago claims to admit only 2-4 per year. Most other T14 schools admit fewer than 10 international students annually. Personally, I think this phenomenon doesn't indicate that Columbia favors international students in admissions but rather that capable international students who can attend top law schools prefer Columbia. After all, there's no substantial difference in quality between Yale, Harvard, Stanford, Columbia, NYU, and Chicago; their admission standards are equally strict, differing only in academic style and teaching emphasis. Given the special nature of international students, none of us can compete for clerkships (so Yale becomes irrelevant), and our goals are mostly international large law firms, whose headquarters are almost all in New York, making Columbia a natural first choice. For example, my first choices were Columbia and NYU, Harvard was due to alumni recommendations, Georgetown was for insurance, and Chicago and Stanford were just for fun. It's like my good friend in Montreal, whose pre-college CEGEP performance at my alma mater Dazhixin wasn't influential enough, leading her to a relatively average school. Allegedly, historically, at most two students from her school could directly enter our medical school for MD each year, sometimes none. However, in her graduating year, her school produced three MDs, and I strongly suspect that the extra spot was created because of her presence. Thus, this only shows that as long as your capability is high enough, data and statistics are meaningless.

Finally, let's briefly touch on the cost issue. Traditionally, law schools do not offer scholarships, but all schools provide need-based financial aid. Although many people can apply for grants mislabeled as scholarships by locals, the prerequisite for obtaining these grants is to bear hefty loans. For American students, they can apply for federal government-subsidized loans with low interest rates, no repayment required during schooling, and partial loan forgiveness if they later engage in public-interest work, which is worthwhile. However, for international students, the loans we obtain are all private loans, and the compound interest during schooling can easily offset all grants, not to mention numerous additional conditions. Therefore, it's better for international students to cover their tuition fees themselves. Recently, I discovered that some schools actually offer merit-based scholarships without any additional conditions, but this is more luck than strength, something unpredictable. The most reliable way to earn money remains diligent study, maintaining a high GPA, entering the Law Review, and then securing a good job. Scholarships and such are ephemeral...

Thus, having written so much, I realize it's almost all redundant, as the entire five thousand plus words can be summarized in one sentence: Good grades + meaningful academic/social activities + acceptable recommendation letters and application essays = admission. This principle is understood by everyone; the key lies in the degree, i.e., what constitutes "good" grades, "meaningful" activities, and "acceptable" documents. Clearly, these subjective standards vary from person to person, and admission officers are normal human beings. Therefore, the only thing we can do is try our best in areas within our control, and as for others' evaluations, we can only leave it to fate. As the great martial arts master Liu Bangzhu from "Street of Ghost Cries" said, "Supreme power, ultimate martial arts... I wanted to dedicate everything I gained to Rui Li. If she wanted, I could conquer the whole world for her. Don't look at me like this; I once had my moments of glory... But Rui Li wanted nothing. All she ever wanted was you, and everything I gained was as worthless as garbage." Hence, if someone likes you, everything is good; if not, it's all garbage. We are just roses in a garden, products on shelves waiting to be selected, and no one cares about our true hearts and aspirations. Like Saint-Exupery's Turkish astronomer, all we do is dress ourselves in seemingly dazzling attire to gain acceptance from others. Therefore, regardless of the final outcome of everyone's applications, there's no need to be overly elated or despondent. After all, neither admission nor rejection can change a person's intrinsic value. Harvard students aren't necessarily all excellent, and state universities aren't necessarily devoid of talent. My journey from total failure in undergraduate applications to triumph in law school applications illustrates the impact of external factors on this highly subjective selection system. In fact, my failure in undergraduate applications, to some extent, gave me the chance to discover what I truly pursue. Otherwise, I might now be working in investment banking with everyone else, perhaps without even passing the bar exam. Offers and schools are external possessions; internal cultivation is the key to self-realization. Absolute fairness does not exist, and we can never escape the constraints of materialism, which are inevitable facts of life. Who says we're adults and can't win everyone's affection just by behaving well?
