Computer and reading use the computer to teach children how to read

by phonics6168 on 2012-02-18 06:49:39

Over the past 20 to 30 years, computers have become ubiquitous even in the most traditional school classrooms. However, despite districts’ efforts to increase the amount of technological resources by installing computers in classrooms, education often loses out on teaching how to use this new technology. Teachers already have books, audio-visual equipment, specially designed and tactile materials to teach students everything they need with just an understanding of how to use them. So what is this promise of new computer-based technology that will help expand the curriculum to teach children everything they already have and need to learn?

The reality is that computers have opened up an entirely new world for students both at home and in the classroom. When personal computers were just a word-processing tool, there was limited opportunity to teach reading and writing skills, but no exciting new ways to teach subjects like social sciences, science, and above all, real literacy products.

With the explosion into a world of enhanced graphics and increasingly complex programs, advanced personal computers became more than black screens with white words and a blinking cursor. Computers became interactive tools that faithfully inspired students' imaginations, aiding educators. As computer software development moved beyond word processing and financial planning, games turned into age-specific programs, evolving into educational opportunities and age-specific solutions.

A milestone in the adaptation of computer software as video games matured into true educational courses saw many parents expressing that their children spent too much time in front of the computer. After all, few would smile when a child switches from their Nintendo computer pinball or Atari Barbie creativity suite. However, most experts believe that computer software developers truly stepped up their game when designing programs not only with real learning opportunities for children but also appealing to various interests, understanding that the best way to learn is through fun - phonics included. Now, many parents are actually happy when their children want to use the computer.

Computer software, having completed millions in financing for high-end routes gaining recognition, can teach children how to read. In the early stages of learning software games, development work proposed basic computer programs heavily focused on learners, such as "A, B, C" and "1, 2, 3". Early programs introduced simple reading materials based on traditional texts like "Goldilocks and the Three Bears," "Cinderella," and Dr. Seuss books, suitable even for kindergarten students. Subsequent programs may focus on specific reading skills.

Today's computer software programs incorporate various learning media and content across subject areas to support reading. Just as educators traditionally use different subject areas to help teach reading and vice versa, computer software has evolved to propose interdisciplinary reading activities to promote and strengthen learning through a multi-faceted combination for all ages of children. Just as computer games began adapting to users' skill levels several years ago, daily phonetic reading successes now do as well. Complex modern reading plans adjust to individual student needs and abilities. These new "learning" programs are so engaging that students often view them as "games." Now, even good readers think that computer games can be excellent supplementary learning opportunities.

Careful decisions by parents in purchasing software, along with flexible allocation of computer time between teaching and entertainment, can help maintain children's interest and enthusiasm at a higher level in all types of these programs.
