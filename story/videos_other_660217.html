[Reprint] Key Points of Recitation Techniques for Oral Training

by nankan7460 on 2012-02-07 14:56:30

Recitation is the creative activity of converting written works into spoken language. Many people do not know what recitation means, so here at the Oral Talent Training Network, we will briefly explain it to everyone. "Recite" refers to clear and loud sounds, while "chant" refers to reciting from memory. Recitation involves using clear and resonant voices, combined with various linguistic techniques, to perfectly express the thoughts and emotions conveyed in a work. It is a form of linguistic art. Recitation is an important form of oral communication. Not only can it improve reading ability and enhance artistic appreciation, but more importantly, through recitation, one can cultivate temperament, broaden horizons, refine speech and behavior, and enhance understanding. On a smaller scale, it can effectively develop a refined sensitivity to vocabulary and establish self-assessment skills for choosing the best form of oral expression. Therefore, if you want to become an expert in oral expression and communication, you cannot ignore the importance of recitation.

Accurately grasping the content of a work and thoroughly understanding its intrinsic meaning are important prerequisites and foundations for recitation. While the use of various artistic techniques in recitation is crucial, if the premise of accurately and comprehensively grasping the content is ignored, then these artistic techniques become like water without a source or a tree without roots — a pure formalism. This makes it impossible to convey emotion and impossible to move the audience. To accurately and thoroughly grasp the content of a work, the Oral Talent Training Network believes that the following points should be considered:

1. Correct and Deep Understanding

We all know that if we want to read a passage vividly, allowing listeners to clearly understand the meaning, the reciter must accurately express the thoughts and feelings conveyed in the work. This requires looking beyond the literal text to understand the deeper meaning of the work. First, obstacles need to be cleared by clarifying the meanings of unfamiliar words, idioms, references, and sentences within the text, avoiding superficial interpretations or taking things at face value. Second, it's essential to understand the background of the work's creation, its theme, and emotional tone. Only then can the work be understood correctly, preventing fragmented readings and misrepresenting the original ideas.

2. Profound and Detailed Sensitivity

Have you ever had this experience? I certainly have. Listening to speeches that bring even the speaker to tears often moves most of the audience to tears as well. Why? Because the speaker can effectively express their meaning. The article is written by them, they have their own perspective on it, and they can clearly convey it. Such heartfelt emotions can spread through the senses. Therefore, if we want to read a passage well and immerse the audience in such an environment, we must first immerse ourselves in that setting. Often, many recitations sound melodious but fail to touch the audience. If the work itself has no flaws, then it might be due to the reciter having a shallow understanding of the work, failing to truly enter it, and instead "forcing" emotions or "manufacturing" sentimentality. Listeners are perceptive; they won't be moved by false emotions. To evoke the audience's feelings and make them resonate with the reciter, the reciter must carefully savor the work, enter the role, and immerse themselves in the context.

3. Rich and Vivid Imagination

While understanding and feeling the work, rich imagination often accompanies the process. This allows the content of the work to come alive in one’s mind and vision, as if personally witnessed or experienced. Not everyone shares your experiences, so they may not follow your train of thought or feel the same way. You need to use language to stimulate their imagination, drawing them into an empathetic environment. Through deep understanding, sincere feelings, and rich imagination, the reciter can move themselves, thereby moving others. This enables the audience to follow the reciter's train of thought and makes the recitation more persuasive.
