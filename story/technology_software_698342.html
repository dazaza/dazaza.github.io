Dear Net Friend,

I met my male rival in junior high school; he was the object of my secret affection back then. After graduation, we lost touch until certain unexpected circumstances brought us together again. I was so excited that I confessed my feelings to him, but it turned out he liked another girl, a very close classmate of ours. Thus, he didn't give me a definitive response, leaving things somewhat ambiguous.

Our relationship continued like this for two years. One Spring Festival, he confessed his feelings to me, saying that he was going abroad and didn’t want to miss out on me. That's when we officially started dating. Later, due to some reasons, he didn't stay long abroad—only about a month—and returned to my city. He struggled to find a decent job with low pay, and kept complaining about how unprofitable it was for him to work there because of his lack of experience. Soon after, due to conflicts with his manager, he quit within a month. During this period, since he had no money, I gave him quite a bit to cover rent, food, and other expenses. Even before we were together, he had borrowed money from me. At the time, I thought since we were now a couple, these financial transactions weren’t worth worrying about too much.

Later, he moved to another city. In fact, throughout our relationship, we’ve mostly been apart except for the first month we spent together. But even during that time, I didn’t feel we connected deeply enough. Long-distance relationships are exhausting. We would meet once a month, usually with me traveling to see him because he couldn’t afford it. He stayed at hotels when visiting my place (where I live with my parents), always claiming that round-trip travel costs hundreds of dollars, while going to his place wouldn’t cost as much. Sometimes, standing in his shoes, his reasoning seemed logical, so I went along with it. However, it was difficult explaining this to my parents, who scolded me each time I left.

My family has always opposed him, thinking he’s too immature. Moreover, his family isn’t just poor; most importantly, they lack warmth. It’s rare for them all to sit down together at the dinner table, each eating separately, which is something I truly can't stand.

As time passed, I felt our communication became less solid. Additionally, he often says he can’t provide me with much. Previously, when I asked him what age he’d get married, he said 25, then 27. Now, if you ask him, he’ll say he won’t marry early and will wait until 29 or 30. We're the same age, and I feel he gives me too many uncertainties. Sometimes, I wonder, if I disregard my family’s opposition and ignore his background, what happens if, by the time I’m 28 or 29, he suddenly tells me we’re not suitable? By then, I’d have no way out. The distance between us seems to have distanced our hearts as well. We often argue, and I don’t see a future for us.

Xiaolongnü, could you help analyze this situation for me? I look forward to your reply!

Reply:

“Most importantly, it feels like there isn’t enough unity in the family, and it’s rare for everyone to eat together at the dinner table, each eating separately. This point really resonates with me.” I highly appreciate this sentence of yours.

A family gathering around the dinner table every day to enjoy a meal happily is an extremely important matter. When I was young, though my family was also poor, at every mealtime, my parents would cook in the kitchen while my siblings and I helped out by shelling beans or picking vegetables. Then came simple yet delicious meals served on the table, and we all ate happily together. My brother and I would talk about school, and my parents about their jobs, discussing the news. This was the moment when the family shared life and emotions. Many years later, recalling those times still brings sweetness.

Therefore, sitting together at the dinner table, though an ordinary act, is the most crucial form through which Chinese families across generations bond. You noticing and mentioning this indicates that you have a strong need for "home." You desire a responsible, home-oriented man to create a warm and harmonious family together.

I believe your current boyfriend is not such a person.

Firstly, after you confessed your feelings, despite liking another girl, he didn’t immediately reject you but maintained an ambiguous relationship, keeping you hanging. It was only when he was about to leave the country, possibly feeling the fleeting nature of youth or the limited opportunities abroad, that he accepted your feelings. And in the context of a long-distance relationship, he still hesitated to spend money to visit you. All these points suggest one thing: he doesn’t love you enough.

Secondly, his working ability is not strong, nor does he possess perseverance.

Thirdly, his family is also average. As you mentioned, it’s not just about being poor, but more importantly, there is a lack of a pleasant family atmosphere.

Lastly, and most importantly, he doesn’t plan to take responsibility for you. When a man says “I can’t give you much,” it’s a sign that he’s preparing an excuse for his retreat. His reluctance to marry or commit means that one day, when you confront him, he might simply say, “Didn’t I already tell you I couldn’t give you much?”

You see, with a man like this, instead of wasting your youthful years with him, it’s better to cut ties early.
