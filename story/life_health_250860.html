Retinal detachment

by weixing123 on 2009-10-22 16:36:14

Retinal detachment refers to the separation of the neural retina from the retinal pigment epithelium layer at the fundus of the eye, resulting in incomplete or total loss of images transmitted to the brain. Based on its causes, retinal detachment can be categorized into rhegmatogenous, tractional, and exudative types.

1. Primary Retinal Detachment (also known as Rhegmatogenous Retinal Detachment, RRD)

Symptoms:

Flashes of light in front of the eyes, floaters, a curtain or shadow blocking the upper field of vision; metamorphopsia; peripheral or central vision decline, or both.

Main signs:

Elevated retina combined with retinal tears.

Other signs:

Pigment cells visible in the anterior vitreous, vitreous hemorrhage, posterior vitreous detachment, the intraocular pressure of the affected eye is often lower than the opposite eye, subretinal fluid is bright but does not change with body position, sometimes there are fixed retinal folds. The detached retina usually appears wavy. An afferent pupillary defect may occur. There may be areas of retinal degeneration.

2. Exudative Retinal Detachment (ERD)

Symptoms:

Mild to severe visual or field impairment.

Main symptoms:

Serous retinal detachment with mobile subretinal fluid (when the patient changes position, the location of the retinal detachment changes: sitting down accumulates fluid under the lower retina, causing detachment; lying down causes fluid accumulation at the posterior pole, leading to macular detachment), without retinal tears.

Other signs:

The detached retina appears smooth and may be notably spherical. A mild afferent pupillary defect may occur.

Causes:

1. Tumors: such as choroidal melanoma, metastatic tumors, choroidal hemangiomas, multiple myeloma, retinal capillary hemangiomas (FFA and ultrasound examinations help in differential diagnosis).

2. Inflammatory diseases: such as Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada syndrome, posterior scleritis, and other chronic inflammatory processes.

3. Congenital abnormalities: such as optic disc pits, morning glory syndrome, choroidal defects, and Coats' disease.

4. Microphthalmos: small eyeballs with small corneas, shallow anterior chambers, but large lenses and thick sclera.

5. Multiple posterior pole retinal pigment epithelial lesions: spherical retinal detachment due to multiple, extensive retinal pigment epithelial detachments can be observed.

6. Uveal effusion syndrome: bilateral peripheral choroid, ciliary body, and retinal detachment; leopard-pattern retinal pigment epithelial changes, scleral vascular expansion.

3. Tractional Retinal Detachment (TRD)

Symptoms:

Visual impairment and field defects; may be asymptomatic.

Main signs:

Detached retina appears smooth and concave downward; vitreous membrane pulling on the retina. If a retinal tear forms, the detachment becomes a protruding primary (rhegmatogenous) retinal detachment.

Other signs:

Retina is fixed, and detachment rarely extends to the ora serrata. A mild afferent pupillary defect may occur.

Causes:

Fibrous strands in the vitreous, such as those caused by proliferative diabetic retinopathy, sickle cell retinopathy, retinopathy of prematurity, toxocariasis retinopathy, and previous giant retinal tears.

Differential Diagnosis of Three Types of Retinal Detachment

1. Senile Retinoschisis: Usually bilateral, often located temporally inferiorly, no pigmented cells or bleeding in the vitreous, frequently with perivascular sheathing in the inner retina, white "snowflakes" often seen in the inner retina; patients are asymptomatic. Contrary to the relative scotomas seen in primary retinal detachment, absolute scotomas appear during visual field testing. Generally, no demarcation line occurs. When a demarcation line appears, it is necessary to search for retinal tears because long-term detachment looks like schisis, even causing absolute visual field deficits.

2. Juvenile Retinoschisis: Bilateral occurrence, petal-like changes after equatorial schisis, schisis does not extend to the ora serrata. X-linked recessive inheritance.

3. Choroidal Detachment: Brown-yellow color, more substantial than retinal detachment, often visible to the ora serrata without scleral indentation, detachment range can reach 360° around the globe. Generally associated with low intraocular pressure.

Prevention

Long-term visual fatigue, excessive eye strain, high myopia, and heavy blows to the eyes can lead to retinal detachment.

1. Avoid overworking the eyes;

2. People with high myopia should avoid lifting heavy objects and strenuous activities, and preferably avoid donating blood;

3. Prevent ocular trauma;

4. Patients with myopia should regularly visit the hospital for check-ups, especially those with poor fundi;

5. Regular ophthalmic health checks are recommended every six months.
