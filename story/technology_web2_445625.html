Some government agencies and state-owned enterprises worry about food safety and establish their own vegetable supply bases.

Reported by the People's Network on November 12, due to concerns about the current situation of food safety, some organizations and enterprises have rented land to grow vegetables and formed their own food supply bases.

The online virtual vegetable garden has made a large number of Internet users very interested. In order to better and more professionally "grow vegetables", a new online business model has even emerged - hiring people to "grow vegetables" for money: Internet users who do not have time to take care of their "vegetable gardens" or do not want to get up early to "steal vegetables" can purchase services in order to "hire" others to manage their "virtual vegetable gardens" for them.

Little known is that the real-life version of "hiring people to grow vegetables" has quietly emerged in various places, with real land, real "landlords", "farmers" and fresh edible vegetables. A new self-supply, direct-supply, special-supply model has been generated among specific groups and organizations.

According to investigations by reporters from the First Financial Daily, renting a piece of land and hiring some farmers to plant vegetables and raise poultry for one's own organization or enterprise has become a common phenomenon in many areas of China. Due to concerns about the current situation of food safety, some provincial-level government agencies, large state-owned enterprises, private enterprises, listed companies, financial institutions or individuals have spontaneously organized themselves to rent different sizes of land in suburban areas to form self-supply or special-supply food bases.

This organized, planned, and managed self-supply method has nothing to do with entertainment or saving money, but rather expresses deep concerns about food safety in a cold and decisive way.

It seems like we have returned to the self-sufficient small peasant economy era, where scale and industrialization are no longer the value orientation of social division of labor. The distrust between social groups has caused excessively high transaction costs, which ultimately harms far more than just the industry itself.

In Professor He Bing's blog at China University of Political Science and Law, an article titled "Government Agencies Build Their Own Farms" has been widely commented on and reprinted. The article mentions that several provincial-level national agencies visited by Professor He all have their own production bases.

Last year, while eating at a provincial agency canteen in a western province, the accompanying personnel told him: "You can eat our food with confidence, they are all green foods. We rented dozens of acres of land in the countryside and hired farmers to grow vegetables without chemical fertilizers or pesticides."

At this year's two sessions, National People's Congress representative Chi Li, chairman of the Wuhan Federation of Literary and Art Circles, called on the government to manage food safety. She also told the media that she had been growing her own vegetables for three years. Her vegetable garden does not use chemical fertilizers; instead, it only uses soybean cakes and vegetable cakes as fertilizer, and the taste is completely different from the vegetables bought in the market.

Through extensive investigation, we learned that apart from individuals like Chi Li and government agencies mentioned in Professor He's blog, there are also large state-owned enterprises, capable private enterprises, financial institutions, and some spontaneous personal organizations adopting this model. Due to its ease of imitation and word-of-mouth promotion, this model is being widely replicated among different provinces, cities, counties, and different types of institutions and organizations.

A certain provincial company of Sinopec already has such a large production base, supplying side dishes for the company's employees. Meanwhile, the provincial power company in the same city is following suit, and the logistics department is looking for a sufficiently large production base to supply vegetables for the company's employees. According to information leaked from the power company, they are determined to spare no cost to handle this major issue related to employee benefits.

Compared to developed regions, this province in central China is a traditional land of fish and rice with relatively less industrial pollution. However, this model has already appeared in Guangzhou, where industrial pollution is more severe. In the Qifuxin Village in Guangzhou, an entrepreneur rented undeveloped phase three real estate land, employed people to grow vegetables directly supplied to residents who have moved into phases one and two, and thus earned a considerable income.

Leaders and bosses of some units or enterprises arrange dedicated personnel to manage food self-supply affairs from the perspective of improving employee benefits. Yifangda Fund Management Company in Guangzhou is particularly considerate towards its employees. They rented farmland in the suburbs of Guangzhou and used seeds and fertilizers purchased by their own company to ensure non-genetically modified and less industrially polluted products. Besides supplying the employee cafeteria, the surplus vegetables can be purchased by employees to take home.

Guangzhou, a place with particular attention to diet, shows similar phenomena from individuals to enterprises and then to government agencies. A real estate development company in Guangzhou also rented land and employed people to grow vegetables for its employees due to the boss's special focus on resisting fraudsters in Picasso digital oil paintings. During holidays, the extra vegetables were even given to employees as gifts.

An herbicide and insecticide producing listed company in East China also rented a large amount of land in Northeast China and employed people to grow vegetables for their employees.

For units and enterprises with fewer employees, achieving self-sufficiency in terms of quantity under limited variety supply is not too difficult, sometimes even with some surplus. However, for larger institutions, due to limited management resources, "the landlord's house also lacks surplus grain," and can only supply leaders and entertain VIP guests.

Such situations are also quite common in Beijing and Shanghai. A private investment enterprise group mainly focusing on finance and real estate in Shanghai has opened up more than 400 mu of land in Suzhou, accepting orders from some member customers and supplying daily meals for the company's senior management. Under limited resources, this benefit has not yet covered ordinary employees.

In Zhongguancun, Beijing, more than 20 white-collar workers voluntarily organized a vegetable purchasing alliance, with a farmer from the suburbs of Beijing responsible for helping them grow vegetables. An alliance member told the reporter that there are already more than 100 such voluntary organizations nationwide.

If this behavior of enterprises shows concern for employees' health, and the behavior of individuals and voluntary organizations is out of necessity, then it is somewhat glaring when government agencies also engage in sideline food self-supply.

From the后勤Center of the Shaanxi Higher People's Court, we learned that the Shaanxi High Court rented more than 30 mu of land in Gaoling County, 50 kilometers away from Xi'an, and has been employing over ten farmers to grow vegetables for the unit for two years. A staff member of the Shaanxi High Court also told the reporter that the seasonal vegetables supplied by this production base are currently in short supply.

We also learned that even some environmental protection departments of provincial agencies are included. A phenomenon is that whether individuals, voluntary organizations, enterprises, or government agencies, all refused the reporter's interview request, "low profile" is their common attitude towards this behavior.

Real estate companies have keenly seized this business opportunity. A real estate enterprise CEO who travels around also told the reporter that vegetable fields or orchards in some real estate projects have become popular promotional tactics. Some properties with limited ownership are sold under the name of management houses, while simultaneously selling an orchard or several mu of land. Many people use this as a holiday villa and hire people to manage the orchard or vegetable field.

In Beijing's Oriental Sun City real estate project, dozens of mu of land are divided into hundreds of portions, and when owners buy a house, they can also claim a portion of land, either managing it themselves or hiring someone else to manage it.

Hidden concerns about food safety

Just like drinking water when thirsty or sleeping when tired, Chinese people who have just left the agricultural society don't need much thought to associate using this most primitive and simple small-farm production method to solve new industrial age problems. Moreover, the large amount of idle land and cheap labor in rural areas make renting land and hiring people to grow vegetables or raise animals unnecessary cumbersome.

These production bases and farmers provide vegetables only, raise poultry, or even make tofu by themselves, but obviously, limited land and labor cannot cover all varieties of vegetables and poultry eggs available in the market.

Their relationship is loose and primitive. Organizations, individuals, and farmers or production bases are mostly found through friends or relatives, and the lease and employment relationships are determined in a loose and non-contractual form.

There are no clear guarantees regarding the duration of employment, quality of dishes, service, and supervision responsibilities. Of course, some large enterprises and government agencies have more guarantees in obtaining more long-term, stable, and reliable food supplies.

During interviews and undercover investigations, we found that this phenomenon is relatively common, the reason being the exacerbation of mistrust between social groups. This includes society's mistrust of the production process and methods of food producers, producers' and end consumers' mistrust of the distribution channels, and society's mistrust of regulatory authorities.

Dorley Farm Chairman Zhang Tonggui told the reporter that his idea of setting up his own farm originated from seeing many farmers cultivating two plots of land: one plot produces vegetables for sale in the market, while the other supplies vegetables for their own family consumption. Even if the quality difference between the two kinds of vegetables is not significant, it inevitably gives buyers a negative psychological suggestion.

Chickens grow up in 45 days, pigs grow to 200 pounds in five months, vegetables originally take 40 days per cycle, now they take 25 days. Fertilizer and pesticide residues, lack of vegetable aroma... Based on intuitive and sensory understanding plus common sense judgment, the safety of the production process alone is enough to raise serious doubts.

Apart from the production stage, more pollution comes from the distribution stage. Toxic broad beans, toxic rice, sulfur ginger, etc., widely reported by the media, are all illegal acts by unscrupulous vegetable vendors during the distribution stage. Choosing to grow vegetables oneself or hire others to grow them reduces the artificial safety risks and natural quality losses in the distribution stage significantly.

A logistics manager of an enterprise told the reporter that vegetables in the market go through at least five distribution stages from the farmers' fields to the kitchens of ordinary people. The differences in moisture retention and cooling conditions during transportation and transfer inevitably lead to natural quality losses. More frighteningly, some unscrupulous vegetable vendors use various industrial additives to keep food fresh and moist.

Due to uncontrollable quality and safety issues in intermediate stages, profit margins are also severely squeezed. Taking celery in the Shanghai market during the Spring Festival as an example, the price of celery in the hands of farmers was 3 fen per catty, but in the market, it became 3 yuan per catty; when vegetables enter supermarkets, there is a 40% markup.

Who grows vegetables for the masses?

Regulatory inaction or inability to act is also one of the reasons why society votes with its feet.

A manager of an enterprise's self-supply production base told the reporter that he once contacted some organic food farms for supply. These farms could almost supply all kinds of vegetables available in the market, but when he visited the farm, he only saw a limited number of vegetable varieties. "Where do the extra varieties come from? This can't help but make one suspicious."

Dorley Farm General Manager Guo Hai told the reporter that there are two problems with organic food certification: first, organic food standards are all qualitative standards, making it difficult to measure the standard; second, the market operation of organic certification turns certification into a money-for-certification transaction.

Professor He Jiguo of China Agricultural University told the reporter that some companies apply for organic food certification and then sell organic labels externally. This method is relatively common and chaotic, causing society's distrust of organic food.

But can self-supply truly solve food safety issues?

Guo Hai told the reporter that producing organic food is not just about not using pesticides or chemical fertilizers, but also requires good soil and water quality conditions. Generally, soil needs a three to five-year organic conversion period, and professional technical personnel are needed to ensure that the soil and water quality meet the standards. Such self-supply clearly lacks professional and systematic measures.

He Jiguo believes that in addition, self-supply cannot eliminate some chemical contamination. For example, according to traditional farming methods, using animal manure to fertilize vegetables may contain more complex components than chemical fertilizers, such as infectious pathogens, heavy metals, antibiotics, etc. There have been such reports in the United States. Therefore, only professional production bases can remove harmful substances from animal manure through high temperature and separation treatment, which small-scale self-supply farms often cannot achieve.

Professor Zhang Huiming of Fudan University pointed out that this self-supply phenomenon originates from China's special supply system and courtyard economy. Some local governments also want to find stable bases to form their own special supply systems.

From an economic perspective, this phenomenon deviates from the trend of increasingly detailed and professional social division of labor. This indicates that our society has "high institutional costs".

He Jiguo stated that food safety issues originally reflect the problem of social wealth disparity and psychological imbalance, as well as the imbalance of personal value evaluation systems, "the more money, the better, so everyone competes to make money, leading to food safety issues."

Moreover, some institutions implement self-supply because they have the financial means to do so. "If we were like Canada, and didn't have much money left," food safety and self-supply issues would largely disappear automatically.

However, to thoroughly solve food safety and regulatory challenges, He Jiguo believes that the entire society needs systemic transformation. "Looking solely at food safety issues, it's infuriating. But looking at the whole society, industries like construction and clothing are also rampant with counterfeiting."

In a society where capital flows freely, if the quality supervision system in other industries does not improve and only food safety is strictly enforced, it can easily cause imbalanced capital flow, resulting in insufficient food supply, "which would be even more troublesome."
