Surface Phone is Microsoft's "Plan B"

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 20:26:00

After nearly a year of rumors and speculation, the Surface Phone is finally set to become a reality.

In late 2012, The Wall Street Journal reported that Microsoft was testing the Surface Phone. At the time, Steve Ballmer neither confirmed nor denied this, but Stephen Elop (then Nokia's CEO) suggested that if Microsoft were to create a Surface Phone, it would benefit the entire Windows Phone ecosystem.

Personally, I agreed with Elop's statement at the time. Most people likely share my view: the goal of the Surface Phone isn't necessarily about achieving high sales volumes but rather creating a demonstration effect—similar to Google's Nexus phones. When an OS provider makes its own phone, it doesn’t necessarily threaten other manufacturers but instead offers consumers a clearer comparison between "pure" devices and those with deeper optimizations. This can be a good thing.

However, there’s a problem: Android phones dominate as a large group, capturing over 80% of the market in Q3 shipments. Meanwhile, Windows Phones hold only 3.6% of the market.

Moreover, times have changed. Nokia's phone business has been acquired by Microsoft. To put it bluntly, Nokia has transitioned from being a partner to becoming part of Microsoft's family. In this context, if Microsoft introduces its own "Surface Phone," the future for Nokia phones seems predictable.

Still, there are some unresolved questions we should consider:

First, if Microsoft truly wants the Surface Phone to replace Nokia's Windows Phones globally, why acquire Nokia in the first place? Some argue that Microsoft values Nokia's manufacturing capabilities—but this doesn’t hold up. Over the past few years, Nokia has closed all its European production facilities. Even though Nokia recently invested $300 million in building a new factory in Vietnam, its functional phone factories in Dongguan, China, and other locations in China are shrinking rapidly. Furthermore, finding contract manufacturers more cost-effective than Nokia wouldn’t be difficult. Clearly, Microsoft’s acquisition of Nokia wasn’t primarily driven by basic manufacturing needs.

Second, could it be, as some media suggest, that Microsoft will collaborate with Nokia's team to co-create the Surface Phone? This also doesn’t make sense. The Surface Phone project existed long before Microsoft acquired Nokia, and it was kept secret from Nokia—Elop himself admitted he knew nothing about it unless he was lying. The secrecy alone suggests that Microsoft didn’t trust Nokia fully, so collaboration isn’t plausible. If they trusted Nokia, they could have built upon Nokia's existing Windows Phones instead of starting anew. Therefore, it seems the Surface Phone and Nokia may not be closely related—at least not yet.

With these possibilities ruled out, the most probable scenario emerges: Microsoft will transform the Surface Phone into the main brand for what is currently known as "Nokia phones." The "Nokia" brand will eventually disappear entirely from the mobile industry.

This conclusion is inevitable for several reasons. First, the Nokia brand hasn’t disappeared; after selling its phone division, Nokia rebranded its telecom network business as Nokia Networks, which remains the core entity carrying the Nokia name. Second, although Microsoft retains the rights to use the "Nokia" and "Lumia" brands under their agreement, this arrangement isn’t ideal. If the brand value rises, Nokia Networks benefits, but if it falls, Microsoft bears the loss. Given this, it’s highly likely that Microsoft will quickly phase out the use of both "Nokia" and "Lumia" brands. Thus, the introduction of the Surface Phone becomes not just necessary but critical.

This situation parallels the 2013 sci-fi movie *The Host*. The body remains the same, but the "soul" of "Nokia phones" has been replaced by the "soul" of the "Surface Phone." Moreover, Microsoft doesn’t fully trust the stability of this body, so it will implement various controls to ensure that the Surface Phone ultimately takes full control of Nokia's hardware. As a result, European Nokia phones will cease to exist, transforming into American-made Microsoft Surface Phones.

If acquiring Nokia's phone business for $7.17 billion under Elop's assistance constituted Microsoft's "Plan A," then the now-publicized Surface Phone represents "Plan B." During an interview, Vineet Durani, president of Microsoft's Windows Phone business, revealed that Microsoft is awaiting regulatory approval for the Nokia acquisition to integrate its hardware and device operations with Microsoft's software business. He also mentioned that "Microsoft-branded phones are under review."

The phrase "under review" doesn’t mean Microsoft is reviewing the project internally but refers to government-level scrutiny of the Nokia acquisition. Once approved, the process of embedding the Surface Phone "soul" into Nokia's "body" will begin immediately.

Vineet Durani’s somewhat rushed remarks might even indicate Microsoft's eagerness to replace its adopted son (Nokia) with its biological offspring (Surface Phone). This move will completely strip Finns of any remaining pride they might feel toward the word "Nokia."
