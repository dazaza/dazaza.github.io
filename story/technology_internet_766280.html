Alibaba Embraces Mobile Revolution as Monetization Faces Challenges

by anonymous on 2013-09-11 13:24:40

The Wall Street Journal today published an article stating that Alibaba Group, which is preparing for its IPO, is facing the challenge of monetizing mobile platforms. Last year, only 7% of Taobao and Tmall's total transaction volume came from mobile applications. In order to respond to the rise of mobile internet, the company has been actively expanding in multiple key areas including social networks and maps. In the short term, its dominance in e-commerce will not be threatened.

Below are the main points of the article:

As it prepares for one of the largest IPOs in internet history, Alibaba Group faces a major challenge: how to monetize mobile internet.

The combined transaction volume of Alibaba’s Taobao and Tmall exceeds that of any other e-commerce platform, including Amazon and eBay.

However, over 90% of this transaction volume comes from PCs. As Chinese consumers increasingly use mobile devices for various activities, Alibaba is trying to find ways to consolidate its leading position in China’s rapidly growing e-commerce market.

The other two giants, Tencent and Baidu, are also actively entering the e-commerce and mobile services sectors. They all realize that in the battle for the limited time and attention of smartphone users, they cannot be proficient in just one area such as e-commerce, social networking, or search.

"In the future, competition across all areas of the internet will become more intense," said Alibaba CEO Jonathan Lu.

Alibaba does not sell products itself but profits by providing advertising and other services to millions of sellers on its platform.

In order to better respond to the rise of mobile internet, Alibaba has been striving to gain control over various key mobile services and technologies – whether maps, social media, or operating systems – to attract smartphone users to its online markets. In the past few months alone, it has spent about $1 billion acquiring stakes in a series of services.

Mobile Challenges

Alibaba's ability to adapt to mobile trends will be crucial for its growth in the next stage. The company is preparing for its IPO (Initial Public Offering). It may go public as early as this year, with external valuations set at $70 billion or more.

Facebook faced the same mobile challenges as Alibaba after its IPO in 2012 at a valuation of $100 billion. Both companies rely on advertising revenue and are actively competing for the attention of mobile device users. Facebook's late start in mobile advertising caused its stock price to plummet initially. However, as the proportion of mobile advertising rose to 41% last quarter, its stock price rebounded.

In China, consumer behavior changes particularly quickly. A survey released by PwC in May showed that compared to consumers in other countries, Chinese consumers are more likely to use mobile devices for online shopping. According to data from market research company Canalys, China's smartphone shipments are expected to nearly double this year to 352 million units, more than twice the U.S.'s projected shipment of 140 million units. Even in smaller cities and rural areas, the presence of affordable smartphones allows Chinese consumers to complete their first online purchases without using a computer.

Taobao and Tmall both have smartphone apps, but last year, only 7% of their transaction volume came from these apps.

Strategic Layout

To attract more users to its e-commerce sites, Alibaba has successively completed layouts in key battlegrounds of mobile services such as social media and maps, collaborating with other companies.

After Alibaba invested $586 million in April to acquire an 18% stake in Sina Weibo, Taobao began integrating with Sina Weibo last month. Now, products on Taobao can be purchased directly on Sina Weibo. "Sina Weibo brings new significant traffic to Tmall and Taobao, which is exactly what sellers need right now," said Jonathan Lu.

In May, Alibaba agreed to acquire a 28% stake in AutoNavi Holdings. Using AutoNavi's map data, Alibaba can provide location-based advertising and product information to smartphone users.

Alibaba is also promoting its own "Aliyun" operating system, offering subsidies to phone manufacturers who adopt it and providing funding for developers to create applications. However, this strategy has encountered obstacles. A year ago, Google opposed its partner Acer launching a smartphone based on the Aliyun system, stating that Acer could not collaborate with providers of Android-incompatible versions. Alibaba denied that its operating system was a variant of Android, but ultimately, Acer canceled the phone launch.

Tencent and Baidu

Of course, no immediate threat will affect Alibaba's dominance in e-commerce. The company reported a net profit of $669 million in the quarter ending March, more than doubling year-over-year; revenue reached $1.38 billion, up 71%. In contrast, Tencent's e-commerce business is much smaller in scale. Alibaba also has extensive experience in e-commerce operations, including offline logistics and Alipay.

Nevertheless, some analysts believe that Tencent has great potential for rapid growth because WeChat has 236 million active users who spend much of their day communicating with friends and family on the service. If the company can successfully integrate its other services into WeChat, it has the potential to become a large e-commerce company. In August, Tencent introduced electronic payment functionality to WeChat, taking a step towards transforming the messaging tool into an e-commerce platform.

Alibaba began to counterattack last month, starting to ban Taobao and Tmall sellers from using marketing applications associated with WeChat. Previously, its sellers had been using such applications to send WeChat promotional messages to consumers. Alibaba claims that it made this decision because many consumers complained about receiving a large amount of spam.

Meanwhile, Baidu also completed two deals in August, including spending $1.9 billion to acquire 91 Wireless and $160 million to acquire a 59% stake in Nuomi.

Alibaba's Chief Strategy Officer Zeng Ming acknowledged the competitive threats and mobile opportunities coexisting. "Make a basic assumption: the mobile industry's scale can reach more than 10 times that of the PC industry," he said, "You must adopt a completely different strategy to compete."
