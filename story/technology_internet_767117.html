Software Developer Teaches Homeless Man How to Code and Build an App

One day, Patrick McConlogue announced that he would teach a homeless man Java programming language to help him develop his own app. Patrick, 23 years old, is a software engineer who encountered the homeless man on his way to work every day. He decided to put an idea into practice and see if this man could be the right person.

"The idea is simple," Patrick wrote on his website:

1. I'll give you $100 tomorrow when I pass by.

2. I'll give you three JavaScript tutorial books (beginner, advanced, expert) and a cheap laptop when I pass by tomorrow.

When you feel ready, I'll come an hour earlier every day to teach you how to program.

Sure enough, the ambitious homeless man Leo decided to learn how to program. He thought that the $100 would be spent within a week, but knowledge could create wealth in the future. Leo said, "He told me that I could have a laptop to learn something, and I think it can bring more. Anyway, I have plenty of time to start from scratch." Leo had been homeless for two years after losing his job as a life insurance agent, followed by a rent increase due to the construction of luxury apartments nearby. Despite his misfortune, Leo did not feel upset or desperate when Patrick offered him two choices. He hoped to acquire a new skill so that he could stand on his own feet again.

Patrick's idea was harshly criticized by many people, who believed providing Leo with food, clothes, or shelter would be a better choice. Some even thought Patrick's approach was insulting. In response, Patrick used an old saying: giving someone a fish feeds them for a day, while teaching them to fish feeds them for a lifetime. Ignoring the criticism, the young programmer bought Leo a cheap Samsung laptop, three programming books, and shared his own Wi-Fi hotspot, starting to teach him how to program. Every morning, Patrick left home an hour earlier and met Leo in the park to teach him various variables and functions needed to master. They also worked together on developing an app, planning to start its execution in the eighth week. Although they didn't want to reveal too much about the app yet, they promised it would be amazing and closely related to Leo's main interest—global warming and climate change.

Currently, this development experiment is ongoing, and Patrick and Leo are very pleased with their achievements so far. Despite some criticism, their progress has been widely accepted. They even keep updating the project's progress on Facebook and have gathered a large number of fans.
