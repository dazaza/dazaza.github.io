When I met Wang Mei, she was thirteen and I was twenty. At that time, I had just retired from the Navy and was a 37mm gunner on a minesweeper. She was a middle school student spending her summer vacation at her grandmother's house. That winter, we took the teachers of the Naval Academy along the long coastline for a long voyage. We reached the famous southern good harbor and summer resort. Outside the port, we sailed alongside a red passenger ship full of vacationers coming from the north for some time. When entering the port, our ship surpassed the passenger ship, passing by at a great distance. The happy tourists came out of their cabins one after another, crowded the side railings, waved to us and shouted. We also waved back to them. I sat outside the steering room and looked carefully at those men and women who were anxious and happy through the telescope. A girl in a scarlet dress appeared in my view. She was the most enthusiastic, laughing, jumping, waving and attracting my attention for a long time until the main wheel was far behind.

This girl left me such a vivid impression that the next day when she appeared hesitantly at the dock, I recognized her at a glance. I was then standing guard with a pistol in my hand solemnly. She walked along the dock where the warships were moored, stopping occasionally to gaze intently at the seagulls flying around the masts. When she began to scrutinize our warship carefully and happily called out upon seeing the red hull number - she saw me. "Uncle, I saw this warship today," the girl said proudly tilting her head. "I know," I smiled at her. "How do you know? I saw you through the telescope," the girl's eyes shone with joy, her face flushed. She revealed her innermost secret to me:

She dreamed of becoming a real PLA soldier. Why? Because wearing red collar insignia and white cap badges looks so handsome. The girl's simple dream deeply moved me. That winter day was indeed a good day. The girl came to the dock every day to play, and the captain usually allowed her on board. The sailors all liked her and showed her around our proud warship. I let her enter my 37mm gun position, put my heavy steel helmet on her, and told her that although the gun barrel wasn't fine, its continuous firing was quite powerful. Several famous naval battles of ours were mainly fought with 37mm guns, producing many hero gunners. "Uncle, if you encounter enemies, you will also become a combat heroine, right?" "Of course." My logic with the child was simple and reasonable.

One evening, the girl ate dinner on our ship and went home via the dike road. Suddenly, the sea raged violently, huge waves surged over the breakwater and flooded the road, water flowing like streams for hundreds of meters along the dike, bubbling with foam. This was rare in the harbor, but the girl was scared stiff by the fierce waves and dared not wade through the water. We saw her lonely and frightened figure from the ship. The captain said to me, "Hey, go help her." I ran to the dike, rushed into the water while shouting loudly, "Follow me closely!" The girl beamed with joy, mimicking my fearless posture, bravely stepping into the water. We ran quickly on the road with surging water. When we stepped onto dry land, the girl looked at me with reverence as if I were a god. I was indeed somewhat heroic at that moment: my blue-red shoulder cape was fully blown by the wind, highlighting my formidable face, and seagulls flew around me up and down. It might have been an image unforgettable for life... Later, the winter vacation ended, and the girl returned to the north with choked sobs. Soon, she sent a letter filled with childish remembrance. I replied to her, encouraging her to study well, prepare herself, and join our ranks in the future. Our correspondence brought her great joy. She told me that because a sailor uncle wrote to her, she was very admired in her class.

Five years passed, and we never met again. We patrolled, guarded, and escorted endlessly at sea. One year, we even sailed to the city where she lived, hoping to see her. But suddenly, the self-defense counterattack against Vietnam erupted, and we were ordered to change course, joining an urgently formed special mixed fleet at sea, heading to the Beibu Gulf to deter Vietnam's fleet. That was also the last flash of brilliance before my eight-year turbulent sea life ended. I originally expected to establish merit, but we didn't get to fight. Back at the base, our ship entered dry dock. Soon, a group of graduates from the naval academy, having received adequate modern training, replaced the older military personnel who had climbed up from the navy. We veterans were also replaced by younger and more educated new soldiers in batches. I was discharged.

Back in Beijing at home, taking off the loose-waisted military uniform and changing into tight civilian clothes, I almost felt at a loss. Walking on the street, seeing the rapid urban construction and increasingly bustling vehicles and crowds, I felt dizzy as if life was rushing forward. I visited a few classmates; some were preparing for university, others had become the backbone of their workplaces, and a female classmate I once liked had already married someone else. In other words, they all had their own correct life trajectories and were striving forward persistently and optimistically. We used to be the best youths sent to the army, but now we were the early retirees of life, twenty-five years old but looking like seventeen or eighteen-year-old middle school students, struggling to step into society's door. The knowledge and skills learned in the army, the accumulated experience, were temporarily useless. I went to the "Placement Office" to check the jobs provided by the state: factory skilled workers, store clerks, public bus ticket sellers. We sailors, artillerymen, tank soldiers, communication soldiers, and infantrymen from various specialties all felt at a loss facing these new professions. Some people truly couldn't adapt to their sudden change in identity, so they registered at the recruitment office for dignified police officers. A few of my comrades became security guards, they persuaded me to join, but I refused. What if I can't handle it later? Do I have to start all over again? I want to choose a lifelong career and not change it. I find it hard to adapt to new environments, always hard. I was overly devoted to the first career that occupied my mind, and once lost, it was like a bird whose wings were clipped, falling from a high place, from a free realm.

I was very confused, very lost, with no one to consult. Men and women cared about me, but I couldn't confide in them like I did as a child, relying on them for support. They hadn't changed, it was me who didn't want to. Although I seemed unchanged outwardly, eight years of wind and waves had made me a man with a soft heart, determined to be someone who could handle things independently. I couldn't bear the idle days of eating and sleeping, so I used the money given by the army during my discharge to travel around. I climbed mountains and enjoyed the waters, walking northward continuously. By the time I reached the largest northern metropolis, I was exhausted, penniless, and had tasted enough loneliness.

Wang Mei was at the Jin Yun Palace airport in this city. Her first letter told me that she had graduated from high school and became a flight attendant.

Two

I didn't recognize her, and I still didn't recognize her when she walked up to me.

I was calling the cabin crew in the waiting room, her colleague told me that she flew to Beijing and would return at three in the afternoon. She asked whether I was her father or her brother-in-law, I said neither. After hanging up the phone, I picked a seat upstairs with a broad view, smoking while watching the colorful crowd in the waiting room downstairs and the quiet planes taking off and landing on the tarmac outside the glass wall; watching those silver airplanes, like sharp spears, piercing the azure sky. The terminal building was tall and bright, with brilliant sunlight outside the windows. When a graceful air stewardess walked through the bustling crowd toward me, despite my focused gaze, all I saw was the sunlight streaming on her beautiful face; I saw her dazzling sky-blue uniform—almost nothing else. Don't you recognize me? I really don't, but I know it's you. Am I uglier or prettier? Don't force me to compliment you. She sat beside me. I still stared at her, and she also stared at me intently. I failed to achieve what you hoped for, to be in the navy. "It doesn't matter," I said, "I'm not either anymore." Truly, I recognized your face from afar, but I still hesitated. I can't imagine what you look like without the navy uniform? Like this! I also couldn't imagine, so I often look in the mirror. Let's go. Where? I'll arrange a place for you, and then... I'll come find you. Have a good talk? Yes, this place is too noisy and conspicuous. You mean find a secluded, quiet place? Yes. We both stood up, and I kept looking at her. Why are you always staring at me? I'm thinking, did I get it wrong. Truly, it's hard to believe, she has grown up, while I haven't aged.

Wang Mei placed me in the guesthouse, fed me, gave me drinks, and let me take a comfortable hot bath. For dinner, I finished a large plate of braised pork mustard greens, and only stopped after stuffing myself with bananas. I felt like a glutton. Tell you, I really wanted to eat until I was fat. After dinner, we said we'd have a good chat, but in reality, it was meaningless chatter. Wang Mei brought a friend named Chi Xin, who laughed but didn't speak, frequently stealing glances at me. The two of them sat opposite me, shoulders touching, occasionally exchanging meaningful smiles. I couldn't understand Wang Mei's thoughts, was she trying to cover something up or reluctant to leave her good friend alone? Or perhaps... She asked about the situation of others on our ship, which truly disappointed me. I told her, everyone has been discharged. I didn't want to talk about the past; only losers cling to the past. If there's nothing to talk about, then there's nothing to say. They bid farewell, kindly telling me to rest early. I decided on impulse to go home today. Unexpectedly, Wang Mei returned alone afterward, telling me something that wasn't appropriate to say in front of Zhang Xin. Can you guess what my biggest desire was when I came to your ship that year? To become a boy. Still be my girl, but grow up to be the same size as you. That's impossible." I laughed, "If I grow, you shrink. Not true, you can't grow any smaller. I've changed my mind, stay here!

Three

I never got a chance to have a proper conversation with Wang Mei. During the day, she flew all over the country, ferrying foreigners with peculiar accents and serious-looking compatriots back and forth. In the evening, she would bring people somewhere, sometimes one or two, sometimes three or five. I once asked her if the whole journey was boring and needed company? She said it wasn't. Then I didn't understand. She said her colleagues were all adorable children, I wanted to meet them, but didn't she know I desperately wished to have a proper conversation with her? Maybe she was deliberately pretending not to understand. She seemed a bit guilty, always bringing lots of local fruits from different places: pineapple from Hainan, oranges from Chengdu, Hami melons from Xinjiang, apples from Dalian. Eating and eating, I still felt unsatisfied. Was the situation reversed, had I become a child?

In the absence of companionship, I wandered aimlessly in this bustling prosperous city like a lost soul. Once, I mistakenly boarded a bus and almost reached the suburban naval pier, wiping my head, I walked leisurely. I didn't want to see those navy ships painted blue; I would look foolish in civilian clothes, staring at them endlessly, making the newly enlisted young soldiers laugh.

The typhoon unexpectedly made landfall, uprooting trees and houses, and the airport was closed to flights. Wang Mei came, and my spirits lifted — she was alone. Wearing a light green dress, she was clean and fresh. But what exactly happened? She had news. What about Mr. Green and Mrs. Green not speaking? Mr. Green used a note to tell Mrs. Green to wake him up at six in the morning, but he woke up at eight. Mrs. Green wrote "Hey, wake up" on a piece of paper. Mr. Robert had a garden of roses. When a big troublemaker offered to sell him a bunch of roses for a dollar, he refused, saying he had plenty. The little troublemaker said, "No, you don't, all your roses are in my hands."... I protested that I couldn't understand the dialect at all, so Wang Mei said she would repeat it in Chinese, thus extending the time of this jargon twice as long. I had no choice but to tell her a few vulgar jokes circulating among the sailors, but I felt deflated doing so. "Don't be mad at me," Wang Mei said, "I have conflicting feelings inside." She told me then I realized she was "reading" me. She didn't care what her family thought, but she was afraid her friends would gossip. Unfortunately, her friends' opinions were inconsistent, you could say they were divided. There was a conversation after Chi Xin left that day: "I'm satisfied." "You're satisfied?" Wang Mei was greatly surprised. "I mean, I'm satisfied as your friend." But another person, Liu Weiwei, who chatted enthusiastically with me, insisted, "He will leave you in the future." I didn't know what gave her the confidence to make such a bold statement. It seemed she hadn't been told anything special; just when I mentioned that being a security guard was easier, she asked if I would be strict, and I casually said yes. After Wang Mei left, I suddenly felt ashamed of myself. I'm not like the怡Red Prince who would recklessly pursue even his cousin, yet I was playing the role of a lover, mocking relevant clauses of our country's marriage law. From tomorrow, I will revert to my original appearance, becoming a respected, slightly revered big brother (uncle is simply impossible). The next day, intermittent thunderstorms continued. Wang Mei came again, not alone, with raindrops clinging to her temples, her straight calves wet. The dignified demeanor I had maintained collapsed instantly. Time wouldn't stop, and our relationship wouldn't develop. Moreover, heavens! I should have seen that nothing could stop its rapid development. And I told you, don't hint subtly. If you don't clearly express it, write it down in black and white, I won't be moved. At that time, this matter remained unresolved. Whenever I mentioned it, Wang Mei generously said, "Even if I chased you, it wouldn't work." Frankly, it was me chasing her, and I was stubbornly denying it. Often, I could only mutter, "At that moment, I felt as if I had been hit by sugar bullets." In summary, it's hard to explain what happened in that short moment, there's no reason to explain. Do you know what my greatest wish is now? What? Before death, the last thing I see is you. Silly fool, by then I will be old, looking decrepit. By then, maybe you'd want to see our children. No, no, I won't.

Four

What deeply touched me wasn't qualities and actions like burning passion, loyalty, or sacrifice, but her faint infatuation with me, a childlike pure yet profound infatuation. Every time we met, she repeatedly asked me one question: In your imagination, what kind of girl are you looking for? Initially, I joked with her, "At least divorced once. Short, strong, capable of saving the world, poor in one area." Later, realizing this joke wasn't appropriate, I said, "The girl in my imagination is like you, it's you." She insisted I say that I fell for her at first sight. That wasn't true, I couldn't deceive my conscience; she was still a child then, what kind of person would I be? She insisted I say it, so I said, "I fell for you at first sight. When you were born, I wasn't there, but if I had been, I would have fallen for you immediately." Every evening when she returned to the cabin crew quarters, she always lowered her head, dragging her feet, walking slowly, silent and sorrowful. I couldn't bear it and had to tell her, "Don't be like this, it's not good. Don't look so heartbroken, aren't you coming again today? When you came today, the parting was the same expression."

My mouth kept talking nonsense, worried that I might unintentionally hurt her, she might die in front of me. I told myself: the bad thing I did was loving a small friend.

One evening, she didn't come. I kept calling the cabin crew quarters, every five minutes. Finally, Zhang Xin and Liu Weiwei came on bicycles, telling me that the plane malfunctioned, and Mei couldn't return from Guilin tonight.

I was shocked, unable to sleep soundly. Without seeing her for a day, I couldn't even sleep well, and she wasn't even a sedative, how could this happen? I found myself annoying. Knowing there was a direct long-distance call available, I called an old comrade in Nanjing. He was a criminal investigator. I called his office. Old Guan, I'm stuck. Heaven, what crime organization? Change your mindset. It's a web of love. Who set it up? He immediately became interested. Do you remember the girl who came to our ship that year? It's her. She grew up, and I messed around with her.
