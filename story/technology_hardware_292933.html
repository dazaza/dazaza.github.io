How to carry out effective career positioning

by bbeij62s on 2009-12-04 17:07:16

The talent professor of MIT pointed out that career positioning can be divided into five categories:

1. Safety type: Some people are most concerned about the long-term stability and security of their careers. They make efforts for a stable job, acceptable income, superior welfare and pension system. At present, the majority of people in our country belong to this type of career positioning. In many cases, it is determined by the level of social development rather than entirely by personal preference. With the progress of society, people will no longer be forced to choose this type.

2. Management type: This type of talent has a strong desire to become a manager, and experience also tells them that they have the ability to reach high-level leadership positions. Therefore, they set their career goals as management positions with considerable responsibilities. The capabilities required to become a senior manager include three aspects: 1) Analytical ability: The ability to judge, analyze, and solve problems when information is insufficient or circumstances are uncertain; 2) Interpersonal ability: The ability to influence, supervise, lead, deal with, and control people at all levels; 3) Emotional control: The ability to face critical events without becoming discouraged or disheartened, and to shoulder heavy responsibilities without being overwhelmed.

3. Creative type: These people need to establish something that is completely their own, whether it is a product or process named after themselves, their own company, or personal property that reflects personal achievements. They believe that only these tangible things can demonstrate their talents.

4. Independent type: Some people prefer to work alone and do not like to depend on each other as in large companies. Many people with this type of career orientation also have a relatively high technical career orientation. However, they are different from those who simply have a technical orientation. They do not want to develop within an organization but would rather be a consultant, work independently, or start a business with others. Others with independent tendencies may become freelance writers or open a small retail store.

5. Technical type: People with this type of career orientation often do not want to engage in management work due to personal preferences, but instead wish to develop in their professional technical field. In the past, in our country when professional managers were not trained, outstanding technical personnel were often promoted to leadership positions, but they often did not like this work and preferred to continue studying their own specialties.

The above descriptions may give you a sense of familiarity. To better clarify your career positioning, try the following method: Take a piece of paper, carefully consider the following questions, and record the key points on the paper:

1. What areas did you invest the most energy in during middle school and college?

2. What was your first job after graduation, and what did you hope to gain from it?

3. What was your long-term goal when you started working, has it changed, why?

4. Have you changed jobs later, why?

5. In which situations at work do you like most, least?

6. Have you ever tried to transfer or get promoted, why?

Then determine your dominant career positioning based on the explanations of the above five types of career positioning. As with many classifications, there is no distinction between good and bad in the above classification. It is proposed to help everyone better understand themselves and reconsider their careers accordingly, setting realistic and achievable goals.
