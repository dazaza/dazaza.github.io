Review shows that Google Glass has a lot of problems, and the applause is just following the herd.

by geekzhang on 2013-05-05 16:38:29

Introduction: This week, the US tech blog BusinessInsider published an article stating that the tech industry elites generally believe that Google Glass represents the future. However, if the early reviews are accurate, then there is no doubt that Google Glass does not belong to the present or even the near future. Although this product looks cool, it lacks a killer application.

The following is the full text of the article:

After reading numerous reviews and communicating with people who have used Google Glass, I found that this is a product with many issues. Google Glass has attracted a lot of attention, which is merely due to people's desire to try out new digital products and their fear of missing the next important technology trend.

Here are some facts about Google Glass:

- The battery life is terrible. The US tech blog Engadget estimated that the battery life of Google Glass is only 5 hours, while Kevin Smith of BusinessInsider indicated it was only 3 hours. For a product that needs to be worn all day and push various messages, such performance is far from enough.

- Google Glass can cause dizziness or headaches. Alyson Shontell of BusinessInsider stated that Google Glass causes dizziness, making users unable to focus on the people and things around them. Additionally, wearing it can also cause headaches since looking at the screen from such close proximity requires you to squint. Hedge fund manager Eric Jackson also posted on Twitter that some venture capitalists told him that Google Glass is currently not very good and makes wearers feel headaches.

- The screen is difficult to see in bright light conditions. According to Engadget's report, watching the screen in bright sunlight is a problem.

- All settings of Google Glass cannot be adjusted. Engadget reported: You cannot adjust the volume or display brightness, cannot turn off WiFi or Bluetooth, cannot rearrange the app cards or set priorities on the user interface, cannot modify the default screen activation time, and cannot enable silent or do-not-disturb mode.

- The voice control function of Google Glass has problems. There are currently two ways to control Google Glass. One is through the touchpad on the side of the glasses, and the other is through voice. Users can say: OK Glass, followed by issuing commands. Megan Rose Dickey of BusinessInsider said that during her test of the voice command, some problems occurred: While wearing Google Glass, my colleague was talking to someone else nearby. I didn't say OK Glass, but Google Glass started listening to what my colleague was saying and began searching.

- When outdoors, you need a smartphone to use Google Glass. Google Glass does not have built-in support for mobile communication networks, so users need to pair it with a smartphone, meaning they will need to pay extra fees and it will also accelerate the power consumption of the smartphone.

- Removing Google Glass is inconvenient. Unlike foldable regular glasses, Google Glass cannot be folded to reduce its size. Engadget noted: The titanium frame looks great and is very elastic, but this also means that Google Glass cannot be folded like traditional glasses to hang on the front of a T-shirt or fit in a pocket. The exposed refractive display appears fragile, and we were told not to touch this display. Google kindly provides a microfiber case to protect sensitive components, but ultimately after packaging, it becomes bulky, requiring users to carry a large handbag.

- Replying to messages is difficult. Editing messages when replying is troublesome. If the user speaks slowly, clearly, and grammatically correct, then they will be able to reply to a correct email. But if Google Glass incorrectly receives the voice, the user will need to cancel the entire message and start over.

Industry insiders believe that these aforementioned issues will gradually be resolved over time, which just requires some hardware and software adjustments. However, even if these problems are resolved, users still don't know what attractive applications Google Glass has now. According to well-known tech blogger Robert Scoble, the biggest advantage of having Google Glass is that it allows faster photo-taking, but the photo quality is not as good as the iPhone 5 and the latest Android phones.

Scoble also mentioned: Compared to looking at a phone, Google Glass is better in social situations. This is because when using Google Glass to get navigation information or do other things, I don't need to look elsewhere. But in reality, users need to look up to activate Google Glass or check the display, and the person you're conversing with will notice that you're checking emails or doing something else.

Google Glass has also sparked some concerns about social interactions. It's hard to imagine that when you're having dinner with your wife or hanging out with friends at a bar while wearing Google Glass, you can still convince them that you're fully engaged in the conversation. Taking out your phone in front of friends to send messages conveys a direct and honest message: you're not paying attention to them right now. But when wearing Google Glass, you don't convey such signals, instead making others feel suspicious.

Since Google Glass is a brand-new and interesting product, it has garnered a lot of attention. The last product like this was the iPad, but that was three years ago. For ordinary people, three years may not seem long, but it's different for product reviewers and new app developers, who are highly interested in such new products. Therefore, tech media and the investment community cheered the launch of Google Glass.

On the other hand, Google restricted the early release of Google Glass. Initially, only some Google employees got Google Glass, naturally, they were very enthusiastic about its potential. Then, Google held a competition where the prize was Google Glass. Google gave priority to people who paid $1500 to become testers of Google Glass.

The initial price of Google Glass was expensive, but purely from a hardware configuration perspective, the configuration of Google Glass is low, equivalent to the first-generation Kindle e-reader, which costs $159. So, does miniaturization and a metal frame really worth $1341? People who spend $1500 on Google Glass will naturally like the product. Would you tell your friends that you spent $1500 and ended up buying something completely useless?

People like Google Glass for another reason: no one wants to guess wrong about the future. The elites in the tech industry have already decided that Google Glass is the future. If you hold the opposite view, you'll be considered short-sighted. If five years later, men and women of all ages on the streets are wearing such glasses, those holding the opposite view will be a laughing stock. If you don't speak well of it now, people will remember you, mock you, and you'll look like a fool. Saying that Google Glass will change the market is safe, even though you've seen its problems.

The outside world is excited about Google Glass, but the early reviews clearly show that this is a first-generation product filled with numerous issues, possibly unable to revolutionize the industry, at least for now.
