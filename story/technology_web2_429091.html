The American camp housed more than 1,000 American soldiers, divided into 12 teams labeled in order with letters such as A, B, C, D, etc. The division of labor included the USMC (Marine Corps), USN (Navy), etc., and also the MP (Military Police). The highest rank among them was the general, including major generals, etc. More than a hundred Chinese people worked as laborers, with five servants and two cooks serving each small team. Liu Yishou.

At that time, American soldiers were on mission-based service, each for three years, earning a monthly salary of $50, of which 40% was given to the individual and the remaining 60% directly sent to their families in the United States. During regular times, the camp would also organize troops for outdoor training exercises outside the dam. A long line of soldiers, led by a band at the front, always drew large crowds of spectators. Liu Yishou went once a year to Tangshan's Donglei Village for target practice. All the camp's officers and soldiers went in three batches, with servants and cooks accompanying the team to provide services. Once, during target practice, there was an accidental death. When the case reached Beijing, the representative from the American camp said: "We have regulations; accidental deaths during target practice are compensated with $500." The farmer representative from Lei Village responded: "Our regulation states that killing an American soldier results in a compensation of $3,000." Count how many American soldiers you have! After negotiation and mediation, the farmer representative proposed three conditions: all American soldiers coming for target practice must wear mourning clothes to escort the funeral; three volleys of gunfire must be fired at the gravesite as a sign of mourning; and they must immediately dismantle the camp after the incident and not come back. The U.S. side agreed to all these conditions. On the day of the funeral, 300 American soldiers lined up, each with a white cloth tied around their heads and a white cloth hanging on their guns. They fired three volleys at the gravesite, with loud gunshots echoing through the air. From then on, target practice was moved to Nandaihe Nan Dashishi. In 1947, the U.S. military withdrew from Tianjin, effectively ending the history of the American camp. Liu Yishou.

The American camp in Tianjin, also known as the American Camp, was established in 1912 when the U.S. Army's 15th Infantry Regiment from the Philippines was transferred to Tianjin. Initially located in a building at the intersection of Boros Road (today's Yantai Road) and Hai Avenue (today's Dagulu), it moved in 1917 to its current location near the intersection of Macao Road and Guangdong Road. This area, known by the people of Tianjin as the American Camp, had Guangdong Road to its west and Pukou Road to its south. The surrounding red walls were over three meters high, with four gates left on the east, west, southwest, and northeast sides. Inside the compound, buildings were intricately arranged, with almost every corner of each building featuring a round three-story fortress, with shooting holes and observation ports on the top floor.

To the north of the camp, across Hefei Road, lay a large courtyard, now the outpatient department of the Third Affiliated Hospital of the Medical University, which was then the U.S. military club.

In the northeast corner of the American Camp Club courtyard, on Yingpan Road, there was a side gate leading across the street to the Military Attaché Building. In earlier years, American officers and their families generally resided here, hence the name Military Attaché Building. Additionally, outside the southwest corner of the American Camp on Guangdong Road, there was another large courtyard (now Huaxia International Apartments), commonly known as the American Courtyard (Ronghua Li), where some American expatriates also lived.

Liu Yishou - Liu Guanxiong under pressure from debt collectors

This place was originally constructed around 1916 by Liu Guanxiong, China's first naval minister who had stepped down, commissioned by the U.S. military. After completion, it was leased to the Americans for use as a camp. After settling in Tianjin, Liu Guanxiong built the camp and rented it out to foreigners, transitioning from official to businessman. Liu Guanxiong once constructed a group of buildings at the current intersection of Macao Road and Guangdong Road (now the East Campus of Tianjin Medical University), along with nearby stables, supply garages, and power plants, leasing them to the American camp, with rent calculated in U.S. dollars. Later, when the value of the U.S. dollar dropped, Liu Guanxiong's income decreased accordingly. Being a gambling addict, he found himself short on cash and mortgaged the property deeds to wealthy British Indian merchant Taleydi for a loan of 200,000 yuan, with a term of three years. If the loan wasn't repaid upon expiration, the real estate would be used to repay the principal and interest. At the end of the three years, Liu was unable to repay, and despite proposing to pay full interest to extend the deadline, Taleydi refused and forcibly took over the entire real estate. Liu’s sixth son, Liu Xiaoying, took the matter to court, but Taleydi won the lawsuit, acquiring ownership of the building worth 500,000 yuan for just 200,000 yuan. Liu Guanxiong's business ventures failed one after another, so he mortgaged his private residence to his nephew Shi Tinggan. Shi was originally the head of the Naval Department's Finance Division, selected by Liu Yishou. Now as a creditor, he came to collect the debt, forcing Liu to move out. Liu was extremely upset, and his health gradually deteriorated. On June 24, 1927, Liu Guanxiong died of intestinal disease at the age of 67. Liu Yishou.
