Definition of City Tiers

The city tier definition system is a comprehensive consideration of existing levels of economic development and activity levels in the main real estate sectors (supply and demand). First-tier cities include Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, and Shenzhen; second-tier cities correspond to those in the growth stage; third-tier cities are those in the early and budding stages. When the market environment changes, the tier of a city can also change. For example, Ningbo and Wuxi were promoted from third-tier to second-tier cities in 2009, reflecting an improvement in their level of real estate activity.

Overview

● By 2020, China's second- and third-tier cities will play a crucial role in the domestic real estate market.

Despite the economic downturn in 2009, which will inevitably affect the short-term development of China's second- and third-tier cities, their long-term economic fundamentals remain solid. Compared to first-tier cities, second- and third-tier cities are more domestically oriented, making them less impacted by the global economic storm. Given China's deepening participation in the global economy, improving legal systems, and the expectation of economic recovery in 2010-2011, the size of China's real estate market is expected to quadruple by 2020. At that time, the proportion of first-tier cities in the total commercial real estate will be less than 10%, indicating great potential for development in second- and third-tier cities over the next decade.

● These 40 emerging cities will attract the attention of commercial real estate tenants, investors, and developers over the next decade. Two years ago, we selected 30 promising cities, whose growth momentum remains strong. Some have even exceeded expectations. Tianjin has further consolidated its leading position among second-tier cities and has the most potential to become China's fifth first-tier city. Ningbo and Wuxi have successfully been promoted from third-tier to second-tier cities due to their rapid economic growth and active real estate activities. Following these classifications, we have added ten emerging cities such as Foshan, Guiyang, and Shijiazhuang to our observation list.

● Through research, we have identified a series of cities with the greatest potential for real estate growth in the medium to long term. We believe that Chengdu, Shenyang, and Wuhan, as leading second-tier cities, possess the strongest overall potential in office buildings, shopping malls, and logistics real estate due to their healthy economic fundamentals.

● In each major real estate sector, we have listed the three cities with the best long-term development prospects:

Office Buildings: Tianjin and Chongqing have the potential to become economic centers in northern and western China, while Nanjing is gradually rising as a regional headquarters hub. The office building markets of these three cities will continue to strengthen over the next decade.

Logistics: Chengdu, Qingdao, and Zhengzhou all have unique geographical advantages, large populations, and increasingly evident railway and port hub statuses, giving them tremendous potential for logistics real estate development.

Shopping Malls: Changsha, Wuhan, and Wenzhou currently have relatively underdeveloped shopping mall markets but possess significant room for growth.

● Government policies and massive infrastructure investments will undoubtedly favor the concentration of real estate development investment activities in inland and northeastern Chinese cities. Most second- and third-tier cities will benefit from this round of development. However, in the aforementioned cities, proactive government policies, high-quality labor markets, and convenient transportation make them the most promising.

● Western cities like Chongqing, Chengdu, Wuhan, Xi'an, Lanzhou, Urumqi, and Zhengzhou will see greatly enhanced urban competitiveness due to the construction of highways and railways. Coastal cities like Qingdao, Ningbo, Tianjin, Xiamen, and Dalian will benefit from the increasingly strengthened port networks in the Yangtze River Delta, Pearl River Delta, and Bohai Bay regions.

● For a city, having a highly skilled workforce is essential and critical in attracting enterprises. Second-tier cities like Chengdu and Wuhan have large numbers of highly skilled workers, enabling them to attract high-tech companies, financial institutions, and other service industries.

Introduction to 40 Emerging Chinese Cities

Business activities in China's second- and third-tier cities are entering a fast track of development, providing enormous opportunities for domestic and international real estate investors who are not only focused on first-tier cities like Shanghai, Beijing, Guangzhou, and Shenzhen.

In 2006, an in-depth study was conducted on areas outside China's first-tier cities to find new business centers and identify the driving forces shaping the future urban landscape. The findings were reflected in our 2007 report, "Top 30 Emerging Cities in China," which listed 30 second- and third-tier cities with significant development potential and predicted they would attract the attention of tenants, investors, and developers.

Since then, these 30 cities have shown strong economic growth trends. However, it is worth revisiting how these cities and their real estate markets have developed over the past two years.

Additionally, due to increased uncertainty in the economic and trade environment in 2009, we must timely adjust our assessment of the long-term opportunities and risks for the development of China's second- and third-tier cities. Jones Lang LaSalle's latest report, "Top 40 Emerging Cities in China," expands the list of potential second- and third-tier cities to 40. These ten newly added cities—Foshan, Guiyang, Jiaxing, Jinhua, Nantong, Shaoxing, Shijiazhuang, Taiyuan, Xuzhou, and Yantai—all have good prospects, giving us reason to expect their performance over the next decade. This latest report details the evolution of the original 30 cities over the past two years, evaluates the future development momentum of the Top 40 Emerging Cities in China, and identifies the top performers in various major real estate fields.

Mapping Development Opportunities

Jones Lang LaSalle has established a method to determine how and when the real estate markets of the Top 40 Emerging Cities in China will begin to receive attention. Leveraging our professional strengths, combined with data information and analysis, we have created urban development curves and categorized these 40 cities into three development stages: growth stage, start-up stage, and budding stage.

Different Stages of Urban Development

Growth Stage Cities: Represented by cities like Tianjin and Chengdu, these cities generally have influential mayors or efficient local governments, along with clear development strategies. In these cities, the successive entry of domestic and international banks, manufacturers, retailers, and hotel operators creates strong demand for the real estate market. Many multinational real estate consulting companies are preparing or have already set up branches locally.

Start-up Stage Cities: Cities in this stage are beginning to establish their future directions and create conditions to build a reliable economic image. Some of these cities, benefiting from local and international enterprises, have successfully attracted real estate demand. However, the market transparency and availability of market data in these cities are far lower than in growth-stage cities.

Budding Stage Cities: The economic development of these cities still almost entirely relies on the local market. Compared to their more successful neighboring cities, their level of economic development lags significantly behind. The combination of developing economic foundations, improved government efficiency, enhanced workforce quality, and distinctive local business developments gives these cities potential and advantages for future development.

City Ranking Battle

Two years ago, we selected 30 promising cities, whose growth momentum continues unabated, with some now leading the pack. Tianjin has firmly consolidated its leading position among second-tier cities and has the most potential to become China's fifth first-tier city. Over the years, Tianjin has gained significant attention due to the development of the Binhai New Area. Tianjin's foreign direct investment (FDI) reached $5.3 billion in 2007, ranking third nationally after Shanghai and Suzhou.

Second- and third-tier cities, despite having a long way to go in narrowing the gap with cities like Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, and Shenzhen, continue to surpass these mature markets in terms of development speed. Even so, from 2005 to 2007, the average annual GDP growth rate of these 30 cities reached 20%, exceeding the average growth rate of the four first-tier cities by five percentage points. Cities are ranked based on the average growth levels of indicators related to the economy, education, infrastructure, wealth, and investment situations. Under the continuous drive of coastal city economic development, Zhengzhou, Chongqing, Hefei, and Kunming have shown the most significant growth momentum over the past two years.

Hefei, one of the smaller economic capacities among the 30 cities, maintained annual growth of 17%-18% over the past two years. Hefei, Zhengzhou, and Chongqing, due to their central geographical locations, continuously improving transportation networks, and low labor costs, have attracted substantial manufacturing entries. Kunming's development depends on its tourism industry and its proximity to trade partners like India and Southeast Asia. A common feature of these rapidly growing cities is their rapid increase in foreign direct investment (FDI). Kunming's attractive business environment makes it have the highest FDI growth rate among the 30 cities (growing by 263% from 2005 to 2007). Similarly, Zhengzhou, Chongqing, and Hefei have doubled their FDI levels, exceeding $1 billion. Among the Top 30 Emerging Cities in China, the continuous expansion of enterprises has generated sustained growth in demand for the real estate market, enhancing overall real estate market activity. Increased market transparency further attracts domestic and international investors. The fastest-growing real estate markets are in larger second-tier cities like Xi'an, Shenyang, Tianjin, and Hangzhou. These cities, with their rapid transportation and highly skilled labor markets, are very attractive to manufacturing and logistics enterprises. Tianjin has successfully attracted Airbus, while Shenyang has seen the establishment of a new General Motors joint venture production base.

In the office building sector, Tianjin has been favored by many financial institutions, evidenced by the consecutive entry of HSBC and Standard Chartered Bank. The vast consumer markets and growing affluent classes in these Top 30 cities have also attracted numerous retailers. For instance, higher-income cities like Shenyang and Hangzhou have been favored by luxury retailers like Gucci, while less-developed retail markets in Tianjin and Chongqing have attracted large hypermarket retailers like Walmart.

Among third-tier cities, Hefei is one of the most attractive to foreign enterprises. As one of China's four major "science and education bases," Hefei boasts convenient transportation networks and relatively low labor costs, making it an ideal location for manufacturing giants like Unilever and Coca-Cola.

Some cities, through rapid development over the past two years, have successfully advanced to another city tier or reached another stage of urban development. Ningbo, connected to Shanghai via the Hangzhou Bay Bridge, and Wuxi, home to Caterpillar's new R&D center, have both been promoted to second-tier cities. Kunming, Hefei, and Nanchang have all moved from the "budding stage" to the current "start-up stage."

Emerging Cities, Transforming China on the Move

Building on the original Top 30, we have added 10 new cities with development advantages and success potential to our watchlist. These 10 cities have not only elevated their city rankings but have also drawn increasing attention due to their active performances.

Among these 10 emerging cities, Foshan stands out with the strongest economic strength and the most complete real estate foundation, currently in the start-up stage. Adjacent to Guangzhou, Foshan ranks ninth nationwide in per capita disposable income, third in the Pearl River Delta in terms of GDP, retail sales of consumer goods, and industrial output, and has attracted investments from international enterprises such as 3M, Siemens, Bosch, and Toshiba. Now, intercity rail transit connecting Foshan and Guangzhou is under construction, and the Foshan government plans to build a port with an annual cargo throughput capacity of 1.5 billion tons by 2020. Large foreign developers like CapitaLand and Shimao Group have already entered Foshan.

Guiyang, as the capital of Guizhou Province, is poised for high-speed development thanks to China's "Western Development Strategy" and its ongoing large-scale infrastructure projects. For example, the high-speed railway under construction connecting to Guangzhou will reduce travel time between Guiyang and Guangzhou from 18 hours to 3 hours, with completion expected in 2010. Additionally, Guiyang's low cost and convenient highway network will attract more multinational investments. Currently, Guiyang has successfully attracted foreign developer investments, such as New World China's ongoing construction of a large multi-functional project in Guiyang.

Yantai, a third-tier city in Shandong Province, is now accelerating its urban development. Located in the Bohai Bay area, across the sea from Dalian, Yantai ranks 19th nationwide in GDP. With over 20,000 industrial enterprises and a strong automotive industry, Yantai is also a tourist destination. Yantai is one of China's top 15 ports and has an airport connecting to more than 20 cities domestically.

Shijiazhuang, as the capital of Hebei Province, is a large city with nearly 10 million people. Its low cost, central location, and status as a major highway and railway hub connecting other domestic regions will increasingly attract manufacturers and logistics companies. Notably, Shijiazhuang's pharmaceutical industry has developed significantly, hosting China's largest pharmaceutical company, CSPC Pharmaceutical Group, potentially attracting more domestic and international enterprises to invest in Shijiazhuang.
