[Editing] Game Theory

Many scholars have found that gaming not only exists among humans but is also prevalent in many mammals. The history of gaming is very long, but its study as a theory began only in modern times. Research on the essence of gaming is still developing, and there is currently no final conclusion.

The main theories of gaming include:

[Editing] Instinct Theory

German poet and playwright Schiller proposed a game theory. This theory suggests that "humans in life are subject to both spiritual and material constraints, and in these constraints, they lose their ideals and freedom. Thus, people use leftover spirit to create a free world, which is gaming. This creative activity originates from human instinct."

Schiller said: "Only when man is fully human does he play; only when man plays is he fully human."

[Editing] Surplus Energy Theory

Surplus Energy Theory is a game theory proposed by British philosopher Herbert Spencer, further supplementing Schiller's instinct theory.

This theory suggests that "after humans complete the primary tasks of maintaining and continuing life, there remains surplus energy. The release of this surplus energy is gaming. Gaming itself has no utilitarian purpose; the process of gaming is its own goal."

[Editing] Practice Theory

German biologist Karl Groos revised the surplus energy theory of British philosopher Herbert Spencer and Schiller's instinct theory.

This theory suggests that gaming is not an activity without purpose, nor is it unrelated to real life. Gaming is a preparatory activity for future life challenges. For example, kittens playing with yarn balls practice catching mice, little girls feeding dolls practice being mothers, and boys playing war games practice fighting.

[Editing] Catharsis Theory

Freud also proposed a game theory. He believed that gaming is a substitute behavior for repressed desires.

[Editing] Types of Games

There are many types of games, appearing in different formats across various media. Different props or numbers of participants lead to different types of games. These include:

[Editing] Manual Games

Humans entertain themselves using their bodies (such as hands and feet) or toys. Common examples include "Rock, Paper, Scissors." Sometimes, sports activities and even martial arts competitions are considered games. Common toys include stuffed animals, balls, and cold weapons.

[Editing] Tabletop Games

Tabletop games describe any game played on a table or flat surface, including board games, paper-and-pencil games, card games, and dice games. Recently, tabletop game designers have increasingly integrated actions, sounds, rhythms, props, pictures, figurines, story backgrounds, and even elements like animation, film, music, and dance into games, providing players with more sensory stimulation and experiences different from before, thereby enhancing the fun and quality of the games.

[Editing] Board Games

Board games describe games where pictorial symbols are drawn on hardboards as recording tools, requiring chessboards and pieces, including common chess games like Xiangqi. The number of participants can range from one to multiple people.

[Editing] Paper-and-Pencil Games

These are games that only require a pen and blank paper, including Tic-Tac-Toe, Sudoku, Pictionary, and crossword puzzles, among others.

[Editing] Card Games

Also known as card games, broadly speaking, they are games that use cards. Narrowly speaking, they refer to games that use poker cards.

[Editing] Group Games

Also known as collective games, the number of participants must exceed three. These games can be seen as experiential learning activities, helping group members understand themselves, learn interpersonal skills, and how to make teams function. Participants or facilitators may come from groups like churches, teachers, and students. Due to the potentially large number of participants, these games usually require larger spaces, such as auditoriums or school playgrounds.

[Editing] Video Games

Including console games, computer games, and portable gaming devices. Their characteristic is the need to use electronic devices with displays for gaming. See gaming consoles.

[Editing] Role-Playing Games

In games, players assume one or several character roles in a virtual world under specific scenarios. Characters have different abilities based on varying game plots and statistics (such as health, mana, strength, agility, intelligence, etc.), and these attributes change according to game rules. Role-playing games are divided into tabletop role-playing games and computer role-playing games.

[Editing] Puzzle Games

Puzzle games, or brain teaser games, intellectual games, or riddle games, require players to use their wisdom to solve in-game challenges to pass levels. Modern commercial games often add action elements, allowing puzzle games to enhance hand-eye coordination through gameplay, with some benefits for maintaining intelligence and promoting open-minded thinking.

[Editing] Others

Other types of games include outdoor games, puzzle games, and math games, among others.
