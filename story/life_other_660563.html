A touch of melancholy

by lili1 on 2012-02-08 00:10:23

That trace of sorrow

Submission online

It's the first month of the lunar year again. Back in the old hometown, I open doors and visit graves, repeating the same actions that have been done for over a decade. I don't know if it's because I've grown up, or my heart has matured, but this time around, that lingering trace of sorrow in my heart just won't go away.

When I was little, I loved the New Year in my old hometown the most; it was lively and bustling. On the first day of the New Year, I would go from house to house to pay New Year visits, with peanuts, sunflower seeds, and candies filling my pockets. After visiting a few households, I would rush back home, dumping all the snacks from my pockets onto the bed before rushing out again excitedly to continue the New Year greetings. After our family suffered an unexpected event, my mother brought me to a distant place. Every New Year, I always missed the lively atmosphere of the New Year in my old hometown; this made myself, who was wandering far from home, more sensitive, insecure, and adept at pretending. Therefore, every time I returned to my old hometown, I was particularly happy. There were familiar friends, the alleys I liked, and warm-hearted fellow villagers. I went back to live in my old hometown again when I was in the sixth grade, five years later. Five years had passed, creating a distance between me and this small mountain village I once loved. In those five years, the once cheerful and optimistic "Little Swallow" turned into a silent child who didn't like to talk. Too much happened during this period, making my heart tired, and even my gaze weary. Three years later, I left that small mountain village again.

Afterwards, I still went there a few times each year; sometimes with my mother, sometimes alone. But every time I went, it was for some reason, and when there was nothing pressing, I no longer wanted to return. Although the villagers would still say, "Oh, Little Sister is back," whenever they saw me, the former warmth now felt different. Perhaps familiarity bred a sense of oppression and fear within me.

On the ninth day of the first lunar month, just like previous years, I returned home to open the door and visit the graves. However, inexplicably, a deep sorrow welled up in my heart. I parked the car on the playground in the village and walked along the alley to reach the front door. The green pebble stones that used to line the alley were long gone, replaced by cement roads. The old houses on both sides had also become new buildings. What remained unchanged was our own dilapidated adobe house and the old gate. I took out the key, prepared the firecrackers, and carefully lit them after several attempts. After opening the door, I then proceeded to visit the graves.

First, I went to my father's grave on the mountain beside the village. It was already time to go back to work, so there were fewer people in the village, making the tomb-visiting process much quieter. Following the winding muddy path up the mountain, an inexplicable wave of emotion surged in my heart. Look, the two small trees on this family’s grave that used to be tiny have now grown over three meters tall, lush and verdant. On both sides were still tea gardens, and better-off families had paved the small road with cement leading to their ancestors' graves. Walking down this short stretch of cement road laid by one family, stepping onto the still bumpy muddy road, I could see my father's grave ahead. It had been over a decade, yet it still looked the same. As I approached slowly, I noticed that the weeds on my father's grave seemed to have been tidied by uncles and other relatives, and there were remnants of sacrifices on the mound. Perhaps because he was buried close to my grandparents, every year someone would remember him. The characters on the tombstone were blurred, but I could still vaguely make out my name: Daughter Xiao Mei. Seeing these words, I couldn’t help but feel sad, as fragmented childhood memories resurfaced in my mind. Lighting the firecrackers went smoothly, as if receiving care from my father, and I wasn’t startled. Still, I felt heavy-hearted when leaving.

Driving to the foot of the mountain outside the village, there was another grave to visit. Regrettably, I realized I was wearing stockings and boots, but I had no choice but to trudge on regardless. After searching for about half an hour, I finally found my biological father's grave. Due to neglect, the paths here were hard to traverse, covered with weeds and brambles. The abandoned land was overgrown with weeds and licorice plants, with bamboo shoots everywhere. My biological father's grave, due to lack of maintenance, could only be called a pile of earth. Seeing it every year made my heart ache. Though not superstitious, I couldn't help but blame them for not blessing me during the college entrance exam, but now that resentment had faded. Maybe it was their arrangement that allowed me to come back as a teacher. Now, seeing such a desolate grave, tears involuntarily streamed down my face, and I couldn't help but wail uncontrollably in the uninhabited mountainside. All the burdens I've endured over the years, all the things I couldn't bear, were released through tears. My heart was filled with too much suppressed sorrow, and I wanted to confide in my father at his grave. All the grievances flashed through my mind like images. When I saw the grave again, this pile of soil, partly eroded by rainwater, made my heart even sadder.

On the way back from visiting the graves, I felt much lighter.

Having found an outlet for my sorrow, my pain diminished significantly. In 2011, I will continue to live life powerfully, bundling up all my pain!
