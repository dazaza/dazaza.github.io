Who knows why Google wanted to buy Motorola Mobility?

by geekzhang on 2013-05-03 14:24:20

It has been 20 months since Google announced the acquisition of Motorola Mobility, and one full year since the completion of the acquisition. After engaging in several patent wars, it seems that this once shocking acquisition might not have been as great as initially imagined. TheVerge wrote an article suggesting that Google may have overpaid, with the $12.5 billion expenditure failing to deliver the expected results.Limited impact of patentsThis acquisition primarily focused on Motorola Mobility's patents and hardware. In terms of patents, Motorola Mobility failed to demonstrate its expected power. Previously, the U.S. Federal Trade Commission (FTC) ordered Google not to ban competitors from using Motorola Mobility’s essential standard-essential patents (SEPs), and the licensing of these patents must adhere to the Fair, Reasonable, and Non-Discriminatory (FRAND) principle. In other words, Motorola Mobility's patents cannot be used indiscriminately as weapons against competitors.This policy affected the judgment of the patent dispute between Motorola Mobility and Microsoft. Not long ago, Motorola accused Microsoft products such as Xbox, Windows 7, and Windows Phone 7 of infringing on its video and Wi-Fi-related patents. Motorola demanded a patent tax of 2.25% of product retail prices, amounting to several dollars per device, equivalent to an annual licensing fee of $400 million.However, the ruling determined that Microsoft only needed to pay Motorola Mobility an annual patent licensing fee of $1.8 million, far from the requested $400 million. This penalty will influence more of Motorola's FRAND licensing behaviors.In Google's acquisition, patent fees accounted for $5.5 billion. Calculating by Microsoft's annual $1.8 million licensing fee, it would take continuous payments for 3235 years to recover the cost.In other patent lawsuits, Motorola Mobility also did not gain the upper hand. Not long ago, Motorola phones were found to infringe on Microsoft patents and were banned from sale in Germany. The ITC also ruled that in the patent dispute with Apple, Motorola Mobility's patents were invalid, resulting in Apple's victory.Apart from patents, after the acquisition, Google had to bear Motorola Mobility's losses. In the second half of 2012, Motorola Mobility incurred losses of up to $850 million, with its market share in smartphones and tablets continuously shrinking. Google implemented a series of measures to lighten the burden, including selling off the set-top box business, massive layoffs, and selling factories. Google only wanted to control the two core contents: patents and hardware.The uncertainty of hardwareWhat is less known is that Motorola Mobility's current predicament could likely be the reason that prompted Andy Rubin to leave Android. A senior person in the mobile communications industry told TheVerge that Rubin was responsible for this acquisition and actively promoted the integration of both parties, believing that this acquisition was crucial for Google. However, when the new CEO Dennis Woodside reviewed the acquisition details, he discovered that reality was not as beautiful as Rubin described.Moto Mobility's new CFO Patrick Pichette publicly criticized Motorola products (including the RAZR product line) for lacking innovation, stating they couldn't produce a wow effect, and planned to replace them with a completely new innovative product line. As a result, several existing product lines of Motorola, including the classic RAZR series, would be canceled.Moto Mobility's entirely new innovative product has been referred to as the Xphone by the outside world. Google executives have praised the design of the Xphone in various settings. Eric Schmidt even described this product as phenomenal. But currently, Google has yet to prove that it can create hardware comparable to Apple or Samsung. Previously, Google replaced most of the Motorola Mobility managers; the newly appointed employees came from Google's internet services department and were unfamiliar with hardware.The argument about corporate DNA is not baseless. Just like how Apple can't do social networks, how can we trust that Google can make hardware better than Apple?At the same time, Google still depends on OEM manufacturers such as Samsung, LG, and HTC. If Google takes too big a step in hardware, it might provoke retaliation from its partners. These factors combined mean that the value of Motorola Mobility in Google's hands might not be as significant as imagined.The poor performance in patents and the huge uncertainties in hardware cast a shadow over this acquisition by Google. So why did a smart company like Google acquire Motorola Mobility?The answer will be worth $12.5 billion.The featured image comes from gigjets.
