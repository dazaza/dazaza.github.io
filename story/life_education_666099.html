The kitchen is the first starting point for nurturing talent.

by hunanshida on 2012-02-15 13:57:21

The teachers of the self-study program at Hunan Normal University have found that "the kitchen is the first starting line to cultivate geniuses." This concept may seem quite unfamiliar to many parents today.

Perhaps due to the increasing competition in modern society, many parents go to great lengths to teach their children various "competitive" skills. Correspondingly, the market, which has a good grasp of parents' psychology, has also launched a variety of classes aimed at cultivating children's "competitive" skills: piano classes, Olympiad math classes, English classes, dance classes, calligraphy and painting classes, martial arts classes, gymnastics classes... In addition, various certification exams aimed at obtaining certificates are also impacting the childhoods of children, with some children even competing with college students in English proficiency tests.

As a result, children's "competitive" skills may indeed improve, but at the same time, their most basic and essential "survival" skills are gradually deteriorating or even completely lacking. What is the outcome? The teachers from the Hunan Normal University self-study website believe that many parents may have already noticed it—

Some children, although they have been admitted to universities, cannot take care of themselves, either requiring their parents to continue accompanying them to school or having to drop out. Some children even choose suicide under pressure; some children, despite going abroad and seemingly having a bright future, suffer from increased pressure due to a lack of basic survival skills, such as not knowing how to cook, wash clothes, or shop, or even not knowing how to get along with others, resulting in many people having to give up studying abroad. Some children become addicted to money-related "services" and eventually stray further and further down the wrong path in life.

Therefore, the teachers of Hunan Normal University's self-study program want parents to understand that while "competitive" skills are important, "survival" skills are the most fundamental. If a child cannot even survive, how can they talk about competition? On the contrary, if a child has strong survival skills and is fearless in the face of storms, what kind of challenges cannot they handle? Moreover, as Chu Tiwen said, if you can't even cook a simple meal, how can you possibly conduct more complex scientific experiments well?

Thus, parents need to remember: don't do everything for your children; make sure they learn some basic "survival" skills through daily labor, such as washing clothes, cooking, sweeping, etc., so that when they leave home, they can independently face the various challenges of life. After all, you cannot take care of your children for their entire lives; ultimately, they still have to walk their own paths in life.
