Jony Ive, the Man Behind Apple's Magic

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 20:41:03

　　Jony Ive: The man behind Apple's magic curtain

　　When Jony Ive was asked, "If Apple no longer needed his talent, where would he go?" there was a long silence, followed by a deeply meaningful answer: nowhere.

　　"Look at this chair; we understand it because its form and function are the same, which is how manufacturing has operated for hundreds of years," he explained in a soft British accent while playing with a 5S in his hand. "But in recent years, a series of problems have arisen: form is no longer related to function, and they are bound by many complex reasons."

　　If Apple had a magician of design, it would undoubtedly be Ive. Joining Apple in 1992, he has always been known for being prolific and detail-oriented. His products include the candy-colored iMacs (1998), iPod (2001), iPhone (2007), and iPad (2010). Outstanding designs helped him win countless design awards and earned him a new name from Queen Elizabeth II: Sir Jonathan.

　　Ive and his hardware design team worked closely with Craig Federighi, Apple's senior vice president of software engineering, to design the iPhone 5S and 5C equipped with iOS 7. Industry observers and consumers alike were watching this new product to see if Jobs' company could still think differently.

　　Compared to the amiable and public-facing Federighi, Ive remains quite low-profile, only appearing in Apple videos. But due to Apple’s efforts to avoid competition from Samsung and other Android phones, we can only catch glimpses of this mysterious figure from behind the scenes.

　　In a rare interview, we saw Ive and Federighi together in a minimalist meeting room discussing their teams, personal philosophies, and unwavering passion for Apple products.

　　Chemical reactions cannot be forced

　　Apple is famous for its secrecy and orderly progression of plans. Sitting on the same gray sofa, they listened to each other attentively. Both dressed casually—Ive in a blue polo shirt and white loose pants, Federighi in a slightly wrinkled dark shirt paired with jeans—they greeted each other politely with a handshake.

　　Although both could be considered peers in various ways, Ive's presence clearly dominated. He began by mentioning that Apple was launching an entirely new operating system that would disrupt existing logic, accepting the challenge with innate confidence.

　　"Last November, we sat down to discuss iOS 7, knowing people were already accustomed to touchscreens, so they no longer needed physical buttons," Ive said. "So we had tremendous freedom and didn't need to rigidly refer to the physical reality world. We aimed to create a less tangible new environment, requiring breakthrough design."

　　Ive was actually referring to the more minimalistic, two-dimensional design of iOS 7. The skeuomorphic templates established during the Jobs era—when physical materials and objects still bore great resemblance. For example, the Game Center logo resembled a green pool table, but now it's just a cluster of colorful bubbles.

　　Federighi believed that the new look of iOS 7 was inseparable from technological advancements. "This is the first 'post-Retina' UI design, thanks to GPU (graphics processing unit) progress, allowing us to adopt more refined graphics and solve problems encountered when the first iPhone was released seven years ago in different ways. Previously, shadow effects could effectively divert attention, alleviating display limitations. With clearer displays, all details will be exposed, so we wanted a simpler, clearer image."

　　Ive continued, "Indeed, we want to rely on content and take a different path." He picked up his iPhone, swiping to the notification center, immediately showing a frosted blur effect on everything touched, while the rest remained clear. This is the charm of transparency.

　　Production is a philosophy

　　Many manufacturers rarely talk about philosophy, focusing solely on feel, pleasing consumers with the idea that "bigger means better," or adopting low-cost strategies. Apple recently announced that the price of the 5C would be higher than the predicted $99 (with carrier subsidies), sparking speculation among merchants—how would Apple, which already holds a 40% share of the U.S. smartphone market, attract new markets like China without going the low-cost route? After the release of the new iPhone, Apple's stock price plummeted accordingly.

　　Despite Ive and Federighi remaining silent on business topics, one can clearly infer from their answers that Apple continues to prioritize quality over quantity.

　　"Look at the camera part; everyone is chasing megapixels, but images can look terrible because the sensors are too fine," Federighi said. "My family cares more about taking better photos rather than the number of megapixels. With this mindset, we make every phone. We want to convey an experience, not complete a set of numbers."

　　Ive said, "Indeed, using numbers makes it easier to demonstrate product attributes. Focusing on price, screen size, etc., is simple. But it's harder to make better products, and it's hard to estimate their actual value. This has fatal importance; we care about what you can't see inside the product because we believe it's the right choice."

　　Apple co-founder Steve Jobs was obsessed with consumer-unseen details, stemming from his adoptive father Paul. In 1996, Jobs described design this way: "It's an interesting word. Many people think design is about appearance, but if you dig deeper, design is really about how the product works." A year later, he and Ive jointly launched the candy-colored iMac, believing the same joy and change reflected in the new OS X system.

　　From various angles, Ive maintains the same fervor for detail. This might also explain why Jobs, who was very demanding of employees, still regarded him as a "spiritual partner."

　　Robert Brunner, who served as Apple's design director from 1989 to 1997, strongly recommended this Ive from Chingford, England, who attended the same high school as David Beckham. At the time, he graduated from Northumbria University and worked at a design company called Tangerine.

　　"He showed me a radically designed cordless home phone, and all its features were stunning," Brunner signed several Apple design contracts with Tangerine just to recruit Jony.

　　Initially, Ive refused Brunner's request to work at Apple, but subsequent trips to the company's headquarters in Northern California swayed this Londoner. Subsequently, he made the Bay Area his home, moving there with his wife and two sons.

　　"In his mind, Jony is a craftsman," Brunner said. "He's also an incredibly good person, generally celebrities aren't so low-key. He enjoys a high reputation at Apple."

　　Brunner believes Ive fits better at Apple, as it is one of the few companies that fully prioritizes design. They start from the user experience and bring everything into reality through external construction. All this requires a strong voice to guide, so Jony is so important.

　　Max Chafkin recently spent months interviewing former Apple employees. He said, "Apple optimists believe Jony will become the guardian of the company's design. The radical design of iOS 7 has met such expectations."

　　"A voice from former Apple employees suggests that Apple hasn't taken risks for a long time, whether you're talking about the iPod or the iMac, they've always conflated design with features. But iOS 7 is a completely new experience, and they seem to be turning back, shocking us again."

　　Call him Jony

　　Despite the fact that Ive's products over the years have profoundly shocked the world—carrying 1000 songs in your pocket?—he himself isn't as flashy as his products, still maintaining a low profile, elegant, and deep style.

　　"I know many people equate simplicity with the opposite of chaos, but true simplicity is walking and walking until you discover what you truly want, and beyond that, there are no choices that can tempt you."

　　Speaking of the TouchID on the iPhone 5S, he said, "This is precisely why I love Apple; those high-end complex and powerful technologies that you don’t notice constantly surprise me. All seemingly simple technologies cannot function without the collaboration of multiple functions."

　　Ive said that working in Apple's engineering department taught him not just problem-solving skills but also the integration of hardware and software teams for future designs.

　　Now, Tim Cook is Apple's CEO, but is Ive also at the helm? Or do Ive and Federighi's teams jointly steer the giant ship created by Steve?

　　Although Jobs' spirit has never been far away, and the air is filled with admiration for him, currently, people can feel that Apple can continue to move forward outside of this halo.

　　"I've worked here for many years, and our way of working is the same. Everyone is trying to solve a series of unprecedented, reference-less complex problems for future products."

　　Federighi continued, "People gather together for a common value, and this value is reflected in every one of our products. Every decision is made for this value: simpler, more focused. It runs through every discussion we have; you can say it's Jobs' legacy, but now it's Apple's."

　　Ive admitted that he pays attention to competitors' designs, but he remains unmoved. "We lead Apple's future with our fans, and we won't change because of other companies. Making new products is simple, but they won't be new the day after tomorrow, so we need to make better products."
