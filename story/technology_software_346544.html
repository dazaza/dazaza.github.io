by cnet9577 on 2010-04-16 11:26:25

The whole process of chasing "Happy Ears" - In the early hours of the morning, engineers from Rising's customer service team discovered Mr. Li's email while monitoring user correspondence and reported the situation to the Beijing Municipal Public Security Bureau's Network Supervision Department according to relevant regulations. Meanwhile, following standard procedures, this email was quickly transferred from the customer service department to the R&D department and all related departments.

After analysis, it was soon determined that the "Happy Ears" virus came in two forms: "Happy Ears (Trojan.Happyear.a)" and "Happy Ears Variant B (Trojan.Happyear.b)." Both viruses had similar technical features, specifically targeting the theft of banking information from a certain bank’s personal edition users, thereby stealing user funds. Unlike previous similar Trojan viruses, these two were specifically designed for the personal edition of this bank, capable of obtaining digital certificates, passwords, and account numbers for the professional version of the bank's online banking system, allowing them to fully carry out the process of stealing funds online, posing a significant threat to users.

After analyzing the virus, the corresponding antivirus program was promptly released. Rising also announced an anti-virus emergency hotline. Soon after, the Beijing Municipal Public Security Bureau's Network Supervision Department issued a public warning about the "Happy Ears" virus. The timely response and swift implementation of solutions prevented the widespread spread of "Happy Ears."

Although the urgent and effective measures relieved some tension, the severity of the virus left people with lingering fear. How exactly did "Happy Ears" steal bank accounts?

Uncovering the truth of the theft:

"Happy Ears" primarily targeted a bank that was among the first to offer online transfer and consumption services. After continuous upgrades and enhancements to its online system, this bank provided services such as online transfers, consumption, and financial management. The system required users not only to have an account and password but also to hold specific digital certificate files.

When a user infected by the virus logged into their bank account to perform operations, the virus would steal their account, password, and digital certificate. "Happy Ears" would then automatically send the stolen information via email to a designated mailbox. Using this information, attackers could arbitrarily operate the user's online bank account for transfers and online consumption. Online thieves could obtain relevant personal information and use extended functions like transfers and consumption to steal user funds.

After analysis by Rising's R&D department, it was confirmed that the "Happy Ears" virus was a Trojan virus written in Visual Basic that stole user information (specifically login information for a particular bank).

This virus included three programs:

1. exp1orer.exe – This is the main virus program, pretending to be a decoder when running. It detects whether the user is logging into a certain bank's online banking system by searching "IE title bar" and "IE URL." If so, it automatically records keyboard input, stealing information such as the 4.0 main login username, 4.2 main login username, user information, and certificate recovery information.

2. search.exe – Through this program, the virus will automatically search for email addresses within a certain range.

3. sendmail.exe – This is the module used by the virus to send emails. The virus uses this module to send enticing emails to searched email addresses and also sends the stolen information to a specific email address controlled by the virus propagator.

Although the "Happy Ears" virus targeted a specific bank, experts stated that the appearance of this virus does not mean there are vulnerabilities in the bank's online banking system. Instead, it mainly exploits users' curiosity, deceiving them into logging into malicious websites, thereby infiltrating their computers and stealing banking information.

Currently, the "Happy Ears" virus has not caused significant damage. However, experts say that it is currently unclear whether the "Happy Ears" virus may lie dormant in computers, waiting to steal customer data at a later time. Additionally, according to Rising's anti-virus experts, based on the development trends of past Trojan worms, future variants of "Happy Ears" or similar viruses will likely be numerous. As online banking and e-commerce continue to grow, users must remain vigilant.

With new viruses constantly emerging, even if virus vendors respond quickly, there will always be a time difference. So, how can people adopt effective measures to minimize the impact of virus infections? The network supervision department of the Ministry of Public Security reminded the general public that "Happy Ears" might appear in new variants in the near future, disguising itself to continue spreading and threatening internet security. Users are advised to use legitimate antivirus software approved by the police department and immediately upgrade their existing antivirus systems. Additionally, if users discover that they have been infected by a similar virus resulting in stolen funds, they should immediately report it to the Ministry of Public Security. For this purpose, a 24-hour virus reporting hotline (65249006) and mobile text message reporting number (send to 05110 for mobile users and 8110 for Unicom users) have been set up.

Similarly, professional anti-virus engineers suggest that users develop good safety habits: do not easily trust URLs circulating on the web, such as those obtained through spam emails or forums; close or delete unnecessary services in the system; many viruses spread through vulnerabilities, so it is essential to keep the system patched in a timely manner; install professional antivirus software for real-time monitoring and ensure that the real-time monitoring function is enabled during normal web browsing. Especially after downloading files from the internet, they should be scanned. When performing important operations like transfers, consumption, or stock purchases through online banking, activate the antivirus software's real-time monitoring feature and enable the personal firewall to prevent leakage of critical information.

In conclusion, staying vigilant while online and maintaining good hygiene practices is fundamental to ensuring one's own safety.
