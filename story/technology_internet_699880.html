Xiong Yaqin's lecture today: What are the symptoms of hemorrhoids?

1. Bleeding during defecation: Bleeding during defecation is a common symptom of internal hemorrhoids, but not every bowel movement results in bleeding. It is more common when the stool is dry. The amount of bleeding varies, and can appear as blood on the stool, blood on the toilet paper after defecation, or dripping blood. Severe cases may experience spurting blood.

2. Pain: Pain is a common symptom of external hemorrhoids. Since external hemorrhoids are located below the dentate line and innervated by the spinal nerves, they belong to painful areas. Once external hemorrhoids become inflamed and swollen, the pain becomes obvious.

3. Protrusion and prolapse: A protrusion at the anal margin with a foreign body sensation, difficulty wiping clean during defecation, and stained underwear are signs of external hemorrhoids. Prolapse is a common symptom of internal hemorrhoids. Early-stage internal hemorrhoids do not prolapse, and only during an anoscopy can the presence of a hemorrhoidal mass be detected, or during defecation due to increased abdominal pressure and downward force from the stool, causing internal hemorrhoids to prolapse outside the anus. Severe cases may prolapse during walking, coughing, or physical exertion.

4. Anal dampness and itching: Dampness and itching around the anus caused by internal hemorrhoids are often due to increased secretions when internal hemorrhoids become inflamed and swollen, or due to prolapsed internal hemorrhoids stimulating the large intestine glands to secrete large amounts of mucus, which flows out along the lax anal canal. These symptoms will disappear after treating internal hemorrhoids. External hemorrhoids generally do not secrete, but when external hemorrhoids become inflamed and the skin breaks down, inflammatory secretions increase, leading to frequent dampness around the anus, often accompanied by anal pain.

5. Difficulty in defecation: Bowel movements are closely related to the occurrence, development, and changes of hemorrhoids. Because patients fear anal pain caused by defecation, they hold back bowel movements, worsening constipation and disrupting defecation habits, increasing symptoms of defecation difficulty.
