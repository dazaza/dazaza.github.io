Improper washing can damage your car - summer car washing also requires attention to details.

Car owners all hope that their beloved cars can last long and reduce repair costs. To achieve this, one must learn how to scientifically maintain their car. However, during the car maintenance process, many owners, due to a lack of knowledge about car maintenance, often end up damaging their cars inadvertently. Some even damage the paint protection layer, leading to paint peeling and rusting, especially in hot summers. After reading the following incorrect maintenance methods, car owners can check if they have any of these improper behaviors:

◎ Washing the car under intense sunlight – damages the paint

Many car owners like to wash their cars under the sun, thinking it will quickly dry the water on the body. In fact, washing the car under the sun causes the water droplets to form a convex lens effect, creating localized high temperatures on the top layer of the paint. Over time, the paint will lose its luster. If waxing is done at this time, it can also cause uneven color on the body. Therefore, it’s best to wash and wax the car in a shaded area. If that's not possible, choose a cloudy day or the early morning/evening on a sunny day.

◎ Washing the car like taking a shower – damages the air conditioning

In the summer, with high temperatures and lots of dust, the car gets dirty easily, so the frequency of washing increases. Many owners want to thoroughly clean their cars like they would when bathing themselves. It’s important to keep the car air conditioning exterior dry; if it accidentally gets wet, it could affect its lifespan.

◎ Tools for washing the car – need careful selection

The car's paint is constantly exposed to the air and is prone to damage, causing the paint to peel off easily. Therefore, tools such as plastic brushes, regular towels, or coarse cloths should never be used.

When cleaning the body, use soft and clean sponges because there is usually hard dust on the body. If you directly rinse it with high-pressure water, it's equivalent to letting the dust particles perform "friction motion" on the surface of the paint. When wiping the glass windows, use specialized glass cleaners and dry them with clean towels instead of letting them air-dry. For oil stains, use a sponge dipped in kerosene or gasoline to gently wipe. When wiping the steering wheel, lamps, and other plastic and rubber parts, only use ordinary soapy water and avoid organic solvents like gasoline, stain removers, and diluents.

◎ Water temperature for washing – has its considerations

Often, after a day's journey, some owners want to clean their cars before going home. Although the intention is commendable, the method is wrong. After a period of driving, especially in hot summers, the engine, chassis, and various moving systems are in a high-temperature state. At this moment, using cold water to wash the car is akin to letting someone who just finished an intense exercise drink large amounts of cold water, which can be very stimulating and may even cause cracking.

Additionally, even if the car hasn't been driven for a long time, if the body has been exposed to sunlight for a certain duration, immediately washing it with cold water can cause aging of the paint due to thermal expansion and contraction. In winter, more attention needs to be paid to water temperature, and warm water is recommended for washing. This not only ensures the quality of the car wash but also effectively protects the paint.

◎ The technique of water flow – has its tricks

At first glance, washing a car seems like simple manual labor without any techniques, but the water flow during car washing is quite particular. Professional car wash shops generally don’t start by spraying high-pressure water guns indiscriminately. The first round usually involves a gentle stream of water, slowly rinsing from top to bottom to let the water flow carry away the dust particles on the car. Starting with a high-pressure water gun is equivalent to letting the dust particles perform "friction motion" on the surface of the paint.

Sometimes, car owners for convenience often wash their cars at roadside stores. If you observe carefully, you'll find that their buckets contain a lot of sand and mud, making it no different from using sandpaper to scrub the car. Additionally, some car wash shops use towels that hotels have discarded. These towels may look new and white, but they are actually rough and harmful to the car.

◎ Choice of detergent – is crucial

The fundamental purpose of washing a car is to remove dirt, and the choice of detergent is very important. Currently, car wash shops mainly use three types of detergents: laundry powder, dishwashing liquid, and specialized car wash liquids.

Laundry powder is cheap and effective, so some car wash shops use it. Laundry powder is an alkaline detergent that greatly harms the car's paint. If laundry powder is used for a long time, the car's surface will gradually lose its shine. Moreover, the corrosion caused by laundry powder isn't visible in the short term, so many car owners aren't aware of the severity.

Some car wash shops also use dishwashing liquid, usually the large buckets commonly used in the catering industry. Although dishwashing liquid is slightly cheaper than specialized car wash liquids, it's still widely used by car wash shops. Dishwashing liquid is also an alkaline detergent, and long-term use can damage the car's paint, causing it to lose its shine.

An owner of a car beauty shop said, "Specialized car wash liquids are neutral and won't harm the paint. They are slightly more expensive than dishwashing liquids. Nowadays, water fees are getting higher, and the profit margins for car washing are getting lower, so store owners are trying every way to cut costs." This owner also revealed a trick to distinguish between the two: when washing the car, look at the hands of the car wash workers. If their hands are white, it proves they've been using alkaline detergents for a long time.
