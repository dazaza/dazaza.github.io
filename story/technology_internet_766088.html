A Chronology of Major Events in Nokia's Century of Rise and Fall

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 15:02:40

Nokia was undoubtedly the most successful European technology company since the 1990s, holding the title of the world's largest mobile phone manufacturer until 2012. However, due to its failure to quickly make the right strategic adjustments in the smartphone era, it gradually declined, and was eventually acquired by Microsoft Corporation of the United States for $7.17 billion. Below is a summary compiled by Tencent Technology of major events and historical moments since Nokia's establishment in 1871.

In 1871, mining engineer Fredrik Idestam, with the help of Leo Mechelin, reorganized a pulp factory by a river in the town of Tampere, on the southern border of Finland with the Russian Empire, and named it "Nokia." For the next 100 years, Nokia dominated the global mobile phone market, but who would have thought that this tech giant once started as a "pulp factory."

In 1963, Nokia first ventured into the telecommunications industry, providing radio communication equipment for military and economic rescue personnel.

In 1982, Nokia produced its first car-mounted mobile phone, the Mobira Senator, and launched another portable car phone, the Talkman, two years later. This was the most advanced product at the time, making a big splash in the Nordic telecommunications market.

In 1987, Nokia officially released the 1.7-pound hand-held phone, the Mobira Cityman. Because Soviet leader Mikhail Gorbachev was reportedly using this hand-held phone, it unexpectedly earned the nickname "Gorba" (Gorbachev).

In 1992, Nokia began to fully enter the mobile phone market and network infrastructure sector, gradually divesting itself of traditional industries such as cable and electronic consumer goods. The GSM digital mobile phone it launched became the standard for European digital mobile technology in 1987.

In 1998, Nokia took the lead in the global smartphone industry.

In 2001, despite the extremely severe challenges and fierce competition in the global mobile communications market, Nokia approached its goal of a 40% global mobile phone market share, leading competitors by more than double and becoming the undisputed global leader.

In 2002, Nokia first introduced the 3G hand-held phone, the Nokia 6650, offering web browsing services to users, highlighting the trend of the integration of the Internet and mobile phones.

In 2007, Apple entered the smartphone market with the iPhone, posing a fatal threat to Nokia.

In October 2009, Nokia announced its first quarterly loss in over a decade. This was also the first year HTC joined the Android camp and launched the HTC Dream. To date, Android devices occupy nearly 60% of the smartphone market.

In September 2010, to break free from the encirclement of the iPhone and Android smartphones, Nokia formally appointed former Microsoft executive Stephen Elop as CEO. Stephen Elop began working on a revival plan for Nokia's smartphone business and announced the layoff of 1,800 employees in October.

In February 2011, Mr. Stephen Elop announced a strategic partnership with Microsoft, stating that Nokia smartphones would gradually transition from the Symbian operating system to the Windows Phone operating system.

In May 2011, according to the latest research by market research firm Gartner, Nokia sold 107.6 million units in the first quarter of 2011, down 5.5% from the same period last year. The global market share of Nokia mobile devices dropped to 19.9%, the lowest level since 1997.

In September 2011, Nokia again announced the layoff of 3,500 employees worldwide, with a total of 14,000 layoffs for the entire year of 2011.

In October 2011, at the Nokia World Conference, the Finnish company officially launched two Windows Phone smartphones, the Lumia 710 and Lumia 800.

In February 2012, Nokia again announced plans to cut 4,000 jobs at factories in Hungary, Mexico, and Finland.

In March 2012, Samsung Electronics' smartphone shipments surpassed Nokia for the first time, taking over as the world's largest smartphone manufacturer.

In March 2012, Nokia held a Lumia launch event in Beijing, officially launching its first Windows Phone smartphone, the Lumia 800C, in collaboration with China Telecom, which went on sale on March 31.

On June 14, 2012, Nokia issued its third profit warning in nearly a year, stating that by the end of 2013, it would lay off an additional 13,000 employees in the mobile department. Observers also believed that since Nokia's mobile device department employed 53,000 people, significantly more than Motorola Mobility's 20,500, the layoff plan might be further expanded. On the same day, Nokia announced at its flagship store in Beijing's Zhongguancun that its Windows Phone smartphone, the Lumia 900, would officially go on sale in mainland China.

On June 25, 2012, Nokia's stock price in Helsinki fell to a 16-year low, plunging 11% intraday to €1.72, the lowest point since August 1996.

In December 2012, Nokia CFO Timo Ihamuotila said the company would sell its Espoo headquarters building in Finland to software consulting company Exilion for €170 million, then lease it back at a lower price.

On December 19, 2012, market data showed that Samsung's global market share rose from 24% last year to 29%, successfully surpassing Nokia to become the mobile phone brand with the highest global market share. Nokia's market share fell from 30% last year to 24%.

In May 2013, market research company IDC released data showing that Nokia's market share in Finland in the first quarter dropped to 33.5%, the first time it was overtaken by Samsung. A year earlier, Nokia and Samsung's market shares in Finland were 48% and 28%, respectively.

On September 3, 2013, Microsoft and Nokia officially announced that Microsoft would acquire Nokia's Devices & Services business, i.e., the mobile phone business unit, for $7.17 billion.
