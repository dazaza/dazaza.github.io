Three tumors affecting the Weibo ecosystem

by anonymous on 2013-08-20 14:48:08

The Weibo Era

Under the backdrop of "mobile internet anxiety" and large-scale industry mergers and acquisitions, the 2013 China Internet Conference was still bustling with activity. I am particularly interested in what kind of voice Alibaba's recent stake acquisition in Sina Weibo will bring to this conference. However, it seems that aside from a decent financial report, there has been little additional commentary.

The latest Sina Weibo financial report shows a 209% increase in revenue, and the level of user engagement has also stopped its decline, showing some improvement. This financial report did not surprise me much, and could even be considered predictable. Because Sina Weibo now has Alibaba as a powerful partner, an improvement is inevitable. The theme of this year's conference is "Building a Good Ecological Environment, Serving a Better Network Life". From the perspective of the ecosystem, I will discuss the commercialization process of Weibo.

After four years and an investment of no less than 2 billion US dollars, Sina Weibo in 2013 faced dual competition from internal commercialization and external platforms like WeChat and Tencent Weibo. In my view, when discussing Weibo’s commercialization, one must pay attention to the overall status of the Weibo platform system's ecosystem. The health of the ecosystem plays a subtle yet profound role in the development and growth of social commerce.

Currently, the main factors affecting the Weibo ecosystem are information overload, rampant zombie accounts (a decrease in real user activity), and the open concept. These three problems form the three major tumors of the Weibo ecosystem, intentionally or unintentionally influencing users' recognition of Weibo as a form of social commerce, as well as its developmental trajectory.

Tumor One: Information Overload, Dazzling Scenery Confusing the Eyes

Google CEO Eric Schmidt once said that the amount of information produced globally in two days now exceeds the total amount produced throughout 2003.

Weibo has a massive user base that spontaneously generates billions of pieces of information every day. In reality, many users' views and comments are likely to be the same or similar. For users, highly overlapping information only becomes a burden, having little value. However, screening for appropriate, valuable information among the vast sea of data requires a significant amount of time and effort from users.

Information overload is not a problem unique to Weibo; early BBS systems also had this issue. But it is especially severe on Weibo and has clearly become one of the major tumors affecting the healthy development of the Weibo ecosystem.

In fact, in the face of overwhelming information overload, many users do not have effective ways to manage or obtain real and useful information. When users consistently fail to absorb effective information, their product experience declines, and they choose to leave.

Clearly, information overload is not a problem ordinary users can solve; its essence is a filtering mechanism issue within the Weibo platform! As early as 2010, Twitter CEO Evan Williams expressed that Twitter would address the issue of information overload through technical means. Fortunately, both Sina Weibo and Tencent Weibo have begun tackling this problem from a technical perspective, striving to provide users with a healthier Weibo ecosystem environment.

Regarding this point, both Sina Weibo and Tencent Weibo are taking action. Sina Weibo chose to partner with Alibaba. Whether it’s display ads, pages based on accumulated information, or the recently launched heavy-hitting product “Weibo Taobao Edition,” all these initiatives seem somewhat urgent in terms of timing. If Sina Weibo is doing "addition," Tencent Weibo is clearly doing "subtraction." Based on a combat system including QQ, WeChat, and QZone, Tencent Weibo improves users' efficiency in obtaining hot information, aggregates diverse and personalized content, and gives more presentation opportunities to lifestyle and vertical category information for ordinary users.

In fact, what the Weibo platform needs to solve is not just the problem of information overload but more importantly, how to quickly present valuable information to users. When providing e-commerce operation advice for the Canton Fair, I conducted in-depth research and communication on this strategy—targeting different users' browsing times, semantics, word groups, etc., for deep mining and analysis, inferring the most interesting information and content for users, thus achieving systematic platform-based push notifications. I believe this is the core value and purpose behind solving the problem of information overload, which I summarize as "big data marketing."

Sina Weibo's most direct way of monetization is through advertising, but advertising is a "double-edged sword." If done appropriately, it works well; if not, it easily provokes user resentment. Sina Weibo needs to grasp the right degree and key aspects carefully.

Tumor Two: Rampant Zombies, Low Real User Activity

Half a month ago, Hong Kong actor Tony Leung Chiu-Wai posted his last Weibo, stating that he was leaving because "there were too many zombies." Some public intellectuals, celebrities, and marketing big accounts, who often post heartwarming quotes, routinely receive hundreds of thousands of forwards. Recent events such as the "Wang Shuo screenplay" blunder make it obvious that the Weibo verification system is virtually non-existent. The apparent hustle and bustle already cannot hide the underlying falsehoods.

The problem of Weibo zombie followers has existed for a long time. Twitter also encountered this issue back in 2008. Zombie followers refer to fake Weibo fans that can be bought with money, having names without substance. They are usually maliciously registered users automatically generated by the system.

Currently, there is no exact data indicating the proportion of zombie followers among the overall Weibo user base. I once specifically consulted several friends engaged in Weibo marketing, and they mentioned that many Weibo marketing companies use technical means to generate hundreds of thousands of zombie followers daily.

The basic premise of Weibo commercialization is integrity (our dear Mr. Jack Ma often emphasizes this). A Weibo platform with a high proportion of zombie followers is clearly unhealthy. A healthy Weibo ecosystem should be jointly constructed by countless grassroots users, mighty influencers, and reliable merchants. We laugh here, we cry here, we live here. And of course, we SHOPPING here!

In fact, an unhealthy Weibo ecosystem will not only cause the gradual loss of user groups and a decline in Weibo activity but more importantly, it will directly lead to businesses being unable to gain effective returns (or gradually declining returns) on their investments. In the long run, both parties will increasingly lose trust in each other, thereby failing to truly promote the development and rise of social media commercialization!

Tumor Three: Open Concept, Closedness Is Not the Big Future

On July 31, 2013, a piece of news created a stir on the Internet: Alibaba would block data interfaces for WeChat Taobao affiliate marketing applications. On August 14, Taobao issued another "ban order," announcing a comprehensive blockage of external link QR code images within a month. This move was widely seen as a response to WeChat 5.0's powerful payment function.

Both of these bans were justified by Alibaba under the guise of security. In reality, everyone can see that this is a blatant "strategic maneuver," rooted in the competition over mobile e-commerce interests.

After Sina Weibo teamed up with Alibaba, it quickly launched the "Weibo Taobao Edition" and introduced more Taobao advertisements. I think Sina Weibo is playing a dual strategy: rapidly achieving Weibo commercialization while indirectly curbing the momentum of WeChat mobile commerce. As early as June this year, Taobao took action to ban Meilishuo and Mogu Street. In fact, Alibaba might want to turn Sina Weibo into an alternative version of "Meilishuo" and "Mogu Street."

Recently, the topic of openness versus closure among the BAT trio has been very popular. From the perspective of third-party benefits, Tencent is undoubtedly the most open. Back in 2011, when I was involved in group buying sales, I collaborated with Tencent. Initially, Tencent accounted for around 30% of group buying sales. Recently, 100,000 Redmi phones were sold out in QQ Space within 1 minute and 30 seconds. These cases, to some extent, confirm Tencent's openness to third parties and its monetization value. Such cases seem to have never appeared on Sina Weibo.

Closing off the road may yield short-term gains, but strategically speaking, it will only narrow the path forward. Closure is not the grand future. In the future, there will definitely be more challenges and variables. How to maintain and establish Weibo's own presence is a strategic direction that Weibo platform operators must consider.

There is no doubt that openness is the mainstream trend of the global Internet. Any ecosystem can only coexist through mutual prosperity, and shared interests can achieve long-term benefits. Any attempt to drain the pond to catch all the fish is bound to be unsustainable. How to ensure that every small unit constituting the Weibo ecosystem can find its own value within it, ultimately building a stable ecosystem and value system, is a test that Weibo platform operators need to answer for the future. It is certain that only an open platform is truly beneficial to users and merchants alike.
