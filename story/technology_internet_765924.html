6 Reasons Why Ballmer Is Leaving Microsoft

by anonymous on 2013-08-29 16:40:13

Last Friday, Microsoft announced that CEO Steve Ballmer would retire from Microsoft within the next 12 months. This is the official statement. The unofficial word on the street is that Ballmer had no choice but to leave Microsoft. Affected by this news, Microsoft's stock price surged by 7.29% to $34.75 on August 23.

Prior to this announcement, Ballmer had just declared a reorganization of Microsoft, making his retirement seem somewhat abrupt. Ballmer himself also admitted in an interview with The Wall Street Journal that the decision to retire was made just a few days ago.

Some analysts believe that from a strategic business perspective, the key responsibilities of a CEO are products, talent, and culture, and Ballmer has made widely recognized mistakes in all three areas. During Ballmer's 13 years in power, Microsoft's market value shrank by nearly $330 billion, and eventually, even old classmate Bill Gates probably couldn't support him anymore.

Looking back at Ballmer's performance over the past decade managing Microsoft, one can find numerous reasons why Ballmer had to leave Microsoft.

Reason 1: Repeated mistakes in consumer hardware

In the 13 years he held the reins of Microsoft, Ballmer's errors in the consumer product market were one of the direct factors leading to Microsoft's predicament.

First, there were tablet computers. Nearly a decade before Apple released the iPad, Microsoft had already developed a tablet computer called Tablet PC. Gates once claimed that Tablet PCs would become ubiquitous within five years. Unfortunately, Microsoft ended up shelving this product. After the Apple iPad became popular, Microsoft launched the Surface tablet in 2012, but users found the product experience unsatisfactory. Microsoft’s financial report disclosed that the revenue from Surface tablets in the last fiscal year was $853 million, while the expenditure related to inventory adjustments for the Surface RT tablet was as high as $900 million.

Microsoft was also one of the pioneers in the mobile phone market, but after Apple released the iPhone in 2007, Microsoft's share in the smartphone market dropped from nearly 50% at the time to single digits today. Although Microsoft partnered with Nokia, this partnership did not significantly help Windows Phone. Moreover, industry insiders believe that Ballmer missed the potential of two products that could have been shining stars: the Zune player and the Xbox One gaming console.

Reason 2: Failure to keep up with the times

Over these 13 years, Microsoft failed to change direction in a timely manner. Microsoft missed the opportunity to transition to mobile internet when it had the best chance, watching as Google, Facebook, and Amazon seized the mobile internet market.

Aichao, the founder of Tech Love Network, believes that the fundamental reason for Microsoft missing the internet wave lies in its genes, mindset, and mode of operation, and over the years, Ballmer never adapted to the times or adjusted the corporate culture accordingly.

Under the new wave, iOS built a mobile application ecosystem through App Store; Android relied on being open and free, attracting device manufacturers and forming an ARM alliance to counterbalance iOS. Initially, Microsoft seemed unable to accept this trend, insisting on selling software per copy. Now, as Microsoft attempts to transition to "devices + services," it is too late.

Li Yi, Secretary-General of the China Mobile Internet Industry Alliance, stated that Google can now serve as a model for the entire technology industry because it can innovate by adapting to the times. By comparison, Microsoft lacks both spontaneous originality and the willingness and action to imitate others.

Reason 3: Unable to retain talent

Over the past decade, reports about the outflow of Microsoft executives have been frequent. Around 2004 when Google was rising, many Microsoft executives quickly defected to Google. It is said that in that year, nearly a hundred Microsoft employees jumped ship to Google. Upon learning that Microsoft Vice President Kai-Fu Lee also left for Google, Ballmer decided to escalate the verbal battle with Google into a legal one to warn employees against reckless defections.

In 2010, Microsoft's Chief Software Architect, Entertainment Division Director, and Office Division Director Stephen Elop resigned. In 2011, Barney Pell, co-founder of semantic search service Powerset, Dave McLaughlin, Business Development Manager of Microsoft's ZundHD music player, and Ruchit Garg, Project Manager of Windows 7 games, all announced their resignations from Microsoft on the same day. Industry commentators noted that the phenomenon of technical talent leaving Microsoft was worsening.

In 2012, the president of Microsoft's Windows and Windows Live departments resigned, along with core management member Paul Maritz. In June 2013, the head of Microsoft's Interactive Entertainment department resigned.

Reason 4: Windows product failures

Almost all of Microsoft's profits come from two types of products: Windows and Office. However, Microsoft has made several recent mistakes with Windows. For example, Vista was so poor that Microsoft had to reintroduce Windows XP Home Edition in 2009 to counter Linux netbooks' impact on Microsoft in the low-end PC market. After announcing his retirement, Ballmer admitted in an interview with the media that his biggest regret was developing Vista.

Last October, Microsoft launched Windows 8, but this product failed to capture user interest because it changed the traditional way Windows operated and eliminated the "Start" button.

Combined with the decline in traditional PC sales, Windows 8 barely made any progress in the market.

Reason 5: Bing search investment failure

After witnessing Google's rise, Microsoft attempted to build its own web search framework. Microsoft launched the Live Search service in 2006 and renamed it Bing in 2009.

In 2009, Ballmer stated that Microsoft would invest 5%-10% of its operating income into the search engine business over the next five years, amounting to approximately $5.5-$11 billion. At a Microsoft annual employee meeting held in Seattle, Ballmer even declared that he wouldn't retire until Microsoft's search engine surpassed Google.

Microsoft later struck agreements with Yahoo! and Facebook and acquired Yahoo!'s search business. However, Bing's market share has steadily declined, and currently, Google's search traffic is nearly four times that of Bing. According to Microsoft's data, as of May this year, Google held a 66.7% market share in the U.S., while Bing only occupied 17.4%.

Reason 6: The market no longer trusts Ballmer

In January 2000, Ballmer became CEO. On December 31, 1999, the day before Ballmer officially took charge at Microsoft, the company's market value was $600 billion; the day before Ballmer announced his resignation, Microsoft's market value was $277 billion. After Ballmer announced his retirement plan, Microsoft's stock price rose by 7% in pre-market trading on Friday. Investors seemed to be expressing dissatisfaction with Ballmer in this way.

Foreign media commented that Ballmer was never a good CEO candidate in the past; he was a salesman. After so many years, he had to sell more hype than substance, and the market has begun to oppose him.
