Beware, free space may be a trap for horse hanging

by liwenbo100 on 2008-04-27 21:52:12

Most webmasters start with free hosting. Some use foreign free hosting, some use domestic ones. But I recently discovered that there are many so-called free hosting providers in China who are actually fraudulent.

Everyone knows that most foreign free hosting services come with ads. Now, some domestic providers are following suit, like the "free" ones. Of course, these free hosting providers need to make a living too; they can't be entirely selfless. On the basis of being free, users must give up something in return to keep the server running and even make a profit. What I want to talk about today is my personal experience. I also warn webmasters using free hosting to be vigilant. At the same time, this serves as a warning to such lousy free hosting providers: stop playing with fire.

Around the end of March, my girlfriend wanted to create her own independent blog and asked me for hosting space. I sell hosting myself, but I thought it would be a waste of money since it's just a blog with not much traffic. So I helped her look for some free hosting online. After research, I found that most only support static pages. Later, I locked onto a domestic free hosting provider. They claimed to offer permanently free unlimited space, no traffic restrictions, no IIS restrictions, domain binding supported, FTP supported, multiple scripts supported, all-in-one space, and even three types of databases with complete components. At the time, I thought, how could I get such a great deal? Is this what people call good luck? Without thinking too much, I quickly applied for one. After getting the FTP account and password, I uploaded the project, and the FTP speed was incredibly fast. After installation, the blog loaded at an amazing speed. I was grateful to this service provider.

To get my girlfriend's blog indexed faster, I linked it with two of my websites. The blog used a secondary domain from my Chinese Show Community website (www.ecnshow.com). Every night, she tirelessly updated her blog, carefully crafting each article. Later, I found that this hosting space was indeed fast and had many supported functions, so I applied for another free space and moved the homepage of my new site (www.pspbus.com.cn) over as well, just one page. A week later, I noticed that the traffic to my Chinese Show website had dropped sharply by 60%. I started investigating the cause. After two days of searching, I couldn’t find the reason. It was strange; the PR dropped from 3 to the current 1. Originally, Google had indexed 80,000 pages of my site, but within two days, more than 10,000 disappeared, and the number kept decreasing by hundreds every day. On the third morning, while habitually checking my site's index, I discovered the root of the problem. It turned out that under every piece of information indexed by Google on my site, there was a line of text like this:

This site may contain malicious software that could harm your computer.

Even now, if you search on Google, this issue still exists. You can check it out here: http://www.google.cn/search?hl=zh-CN&q=site%3Awww.ecnshow.com

Hey, I was puzzled. I always lead by example and never attach malware. Where did this message come from? Upon checking the website's source code, apart from ads and statistics carrying JS files, there were no other suspicious malicious codes. However, I have multiple sites with these same ads and statistics codes, and none of them had this issue. Suddenly, I wondered if it was due to my girlfriend’s blog. I immediately opened her blog. As soon as it loaded, my Kaspersky antivirus popped up a virus warning. I quickly right-clicked to view the source code and disconnected from the internet. In the first line of the source code, I saw the problem: a line of JS code was added at the very top. I immediately used another computer to search online for the name of the Trojan file warned by Kaspersky. It turned out to be a Trojan stealing online banking credentials, QQ accounts, and game accounts, which would automatically download numerous Trojans and viruses. At that moment, I felt extremely cold inside. I quickly deleted the domain and downloaded the original blog database. Fortunately, the second new site hadn't been indexed yet, so I quickly moved it back to the original space before it got infected. I also reformatted my hard drive and reinstalled the system.

No wonder they provided such good free hosting. Their purpose was this! By offering free hosting to webmasters, they mass-distribute malware to make huge profits. It's utterly disgraceful. Here, I won't name the specific website. Please be cautious, cautious, and extra cautious when applying for free hosting. I also advise those fraudulent providers to stop immediately.
