Daily Dietary Guidelines

When choosing food, the primary consideration should be its nutritional value, while also paying attention to freshness, hygiene, and economy.

With so many types of food available, how can we choose to obtain balanced nutrition? Nutrition experts recommend that we select the necessary portions from the following six basic food groups every day:

Data source: Executive Yuan Department of Health

Grains and Tubers: 3-6 bowls

Rice, noodles, sweet potatoes, and other staple foods mainly provide carbohydrates and some proteins.

Dairy Products: 1-2 cups

Milk, yogurt, cheese, and other dairy products contain abundant calcium and protein.

Eggs, Beans, Fish, Meat: 4 servings

Eggs, fish, meat, tofu, dried tofu, soy milk all contain rich proteins.

Vegetables: 3 servings

Various vegetables mainly supply vitamins, minerals, and fiber. Dark green and deep yellow-red vegetables, such as spinach, cabbage, carrots, pumpkins, etc., contain more vitamins and minerals than light-colored vegetables.

Fruits: 2 servings

Fruits can provide vitamins, minerals, and fiber, such as oranges, tangerines, papayas, guavas, pineapples, bananas, etc.

Fats and Oils: 2-3 tablespoons

Cooking oil and nuts like peanuts and cashews can supply fat.

Category Quantity Unit Description

Grains and Tubers 3-6 bowls Each bowl: one bowl of rice (200 grams); or one medium-sized steamed bun; or four slices of toast.

Dairy Products 1-2 cups Each cup: one cup of milk (240cc); one cup of fermented milk (240cc); one slice of cheese (about 30 grams).

Eggs, Beans, Fish, Meat 4 servings Each serving: one ounce of meat, poultry, or fish (about 30 grams) or one block of tofu (100 grams); or one cup of soy milk (240cc) or one egg.

Vegetables 3 servings Each serving: three ounces of vegetables (about 100 grams).

Fruits 2 servings Each serving: one medium-sized orange (about 100 grams); or one guava.

Fats and Oils 2-3 tablespoons Each tablespoon: one spoonful of oil (15 grams).

This "Dietary Guide" applies to generally healthy adults, but due to individual body types and activity levels, the intake of grains and tubers can be adjusted accordingly.

The selection of each type of food should vary frequently, and it is not advisable to eat the same food at every meal. Cooking oils should preferably be plant-based, and attention should be paid to the amount used.

Among the vegetable servings, at least one should be dark green or deep yellow vegetables.

Teenagers, the elderly, and pregnant/lactating women have more specific physiological conditions and can make slight adjustments based on this dietary guide:

Teenagers

Increase the intake of grains and tubers, dairy products, and eggs, beans, fish, and meat, especially adding one egg or one cup of milk.

Elderly

Can appropriately reduce the intake of fats and oils and grains and tubers.

Pregnant/Lactating Women

All six categories of food should be moderately increased. To avoid osteoporosis, it's best to increase one to two cups of milk daily. If necessary, low-fat milk can be substituted to reduce calorie intake.

Nutrition is the foundation of health, and food is the source of nutrition.

Our bodies need nutrients from food to sustain life. These nutrients are carbohydrates, fats, proteins, vitamins, and minerals.

The physical energy for daily activities comes from the calories produced by carbohydrates and fats.

Protein is a necessary raw material for human growth, development, and metabolism.

Vitamins and minerals can regulate physiological functions.

The Nutritional Value of Food

The nutritional value of food is determined by the types and quantities of nutrients it contains.

Most foods contain more than one nutrient, and the content of nutrients varies in different foods.

Fresh produce contains more nutrients than processed foods.
