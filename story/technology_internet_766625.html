Apple's most senior elder and founder: Resigned after working for only 12 days, even Jobs had to listen to him

by anonymous on 2013-09-24 21:00:37

Last week, Apple Inc. released its new iPhone, and anyone associated with Apple became the focus of media reports. Even Ron Wayne, who lives in Nevada, USA, was stopped by reporters at his doorstep. This 79-year-old man lives in a mobile home, has a large beer belly, and asked the reporters who came to interview him: "Is my life really that interesting?"

Of course, he is one of the co-founders of Apple Inc., and even Steve Jobs once sought his guidance. On April Fool's Day in 1976, he signed the contract to establish Apple Inc. together with Jobs and Wozniak. As the most senior member of the company, he designed the first trademark for Apple Inc., wrote the first user manual for Apple computers, and even drafted the stock agreement for Apple Inc.

Nowadays, this white-haired old man has little to do with the trendy Apple. After working at Apple for only 12 days, he resigned. Now he runs a small stamp shop in Nevada, living on social assistance payments. When he has money, he goes to the nearby casino to play slot machines; when he doesn't have money, he stays at home. There are neither iPhones nor iPads there. He uses an old-fashioned flip phone that can't access the internet, and the most advanced technological product in his room is a Dell computer bought in 1996.

"People are interested in me just because I know famous people, and I am merely a footnote in someone else's story," Wayne said.

In fact, the more well-known founders of Apple Inc. - Jobs and Wozniak - invited Wayne back to Apple to continue working after he resigned. Wayne was over 20 years older than them and could be considered a "mentor." Especially when the company was first established, Jobs and Wozniak were just over 20 years old, and their signatures on the contract looked like those of students, but Wayne had already become a core engineer at a computer gaming company at that time.

"In the early days of Apple Inc., Wayne was a key figure," Wozniak wrote in his memoirs. He and Jobs were very excited when they saw Wayne, "Wow, this person is really great, he seems to know all the things that Jobs and I don't understand."

Wayne recalled that those two young men were like "a rapidly advancing whirlwind" at the time. They were determined to create an "extraordinary personal computer," discussing crazy ideas one after another in their own garage and planning to realize these seemingly impossible ideas at the time. The inexperienced youngsters seemed fearless and planned to spend a large amount of investment.

This attitude of fearlessness made the experienced predecessor Wayne "worried." That year, he was already 43 years old and had experienced a failed investment, although he "had a premonition that what Apple was going to do would succeed," but when facing "roller-coaster-like risks," he hesitated. After all, if the Apple computer project failed, the losses for the two young men would be their wild dreams, at most future debts, while he would have to pay with his house and car.

Moreover, those two founders had somewhat peculiar temperaments. Wozniak was a genius full of strange ideas, and the picky Jobs was known for his bad temper. He abandoned his pregnant girlfriend, turned against his old friends, and often got angry with his staff. A store clerk who worked at the grocery store outside Apple Inc. recalled that Jobs would casually occupy "disabled parking spaces" and shout into the phone from inside the car: "I don't want this kind of blue, it's not blue enough!"

"If you have to choose between hugging Jobs or hugging an ice cube, you'd rather hug the ice cube to keep warm," Wayne said, "Once Jobs decides to achieve his goal, you'd better not get in his way, otherwise he might step on your face and move forward."

After working for 12 days, Wayne resigned and returned to his original company to resume his old job. Before leaving, he also feared that holding shares would be too risky, so he sold the 10% stake he held for $800.

Later, Wayne changed jobs several times and even thought about starting a business again. Because he held multiple patents, he always lacked a bit of money to implement these ideas. People no longer remembered that he was "one of the founders of Apple Inc.", only occasionally when someone visited did he take out the drafts he once drew up at Apple Inc. On the yellowed paper at the corner folds, there were various logo samples of the early Apple Inc.

"My whole life is always a bit off, either one day too early or one dollar short," Wayne said, "But I won't worry about the things that didn't happen, it's just a waste of time. If I had stayed then, maybe today I would be the richest person, but more likely, I would be the richest person in the grave."

Now, he runs a stamp shop on the streets of the small town, selling rare coins and stamps. His house was once robbed, and now Wayne is penniless, waiting for welfare payments every month to live on. In fact, perhaps he could have lived without being so strained, because if he had "boldly" kept the 10% stake in Apple Inc. at that time, it would be worth tens of billions of dollars today.

However, he left Apple Inc. completely for just $800. Nowadays, there are no more obvious traces of Apple in his house, except for the wooden sign hanging at the door, which depicts a man sitting under an apple tree. That was the first logo designed by Wayne as one of the founders during the early days of the company.
