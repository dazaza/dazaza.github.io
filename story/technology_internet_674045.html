State Council: Lift restrictions on household registration in medium and small cities

by king2z9o on 2012-02-24 10:01:11

The General Office of the State Council announced yesterday "the Notice on Actively and Steadily Promoting the Reform of the Household Registration Management System". The notice requires that future policies regarding employment, compulsory education, skills training, etc., should not be linked to household registration status. Efforts should continue to explore the establishment of a unified urban-rural household registration system. Gradually implement a residence permit system for transient populations. Policies that cause inconvenience to transient populations in terms of study, work, and life should be reviewed comprehensively. Those that need revision should be revised carefully, and those that need abolition should be abolished resolutely.

[In recent years, with the reform of the household registration management system, some problems have emerged in various places that cannot be ignored. Some areas, regardless of their socio-economic development realities, blindly pursue the scale and speed of urbanization; some areas, without distinguishing between city categories or considering the comprehensive carrying capacity of cities, indiscriminately relax the conditions for registering a city household; some areas arbitrarily break national policies, harming the legitimate interests of the public.] The notice summarized the current status of the household registration system reform and clearly regulated some policies.

Regarding the household migration policy, the notice made classified regulations. In county-level city districts, county people's government towns, and other established towns, individuals with a legal stable occupation and a legal stable residence (including rental) can apply to register as permanent residents along with their cohabiting spouses, unmarried children, and parents. In districted cities (excluding municipalities, sub-provincial cities, and other large cities), individuals with a legal stable occupation for more than three years and a legal stable residence (including rental), and who have participated in social insurance according to national regulations for a certain period, can apply to register as permanent residents along with their cohabiting spouses, unmarried children, and parents. Efforts should continue to reasonably control the population size of municipalities, sub-provincial cities, and other large cities, further improving and implementing the current urban settlement policies.

For rural populations who have already settled in towns, their rights to enjoy equal benefits with local urban residents must be guaranteed. For migrant workers who do not yet meet the conditions for settling, relevant systems should be improved to address outstanding issues such as labor remuneration, children's schooling, skill training, public health, housing rental and purchase, social security, occupational safety and health.

Expert Suggestions

[Three-year term could be extended to large cities]

Regarding the classification of clear household migration policies, Professor Tao Ran from the School of Economics at Renmin University of China believes that this policy is indeed an improvement over previous ones. For example, in county-level city districts, county people's government towns, and other established towns, as long as one has a stable occupation and stable housing, they can apply for local household registration, unlike many developed regions where an积分制 (point-based system) is used, or where paying social security for a certain period is required, which excludes many self-employed individuals. However, the policy also stipulates exceptions: [places with heavy pressure on urban comprehensive carrying capacity] may impose certain restrictions, which is debatable and should prevent some regions from using this as a reason to restrict settlement.

Moreover, for districted cities, the [having a legal stable occupation for more than three years] requirement is explicitly stated. [I believe this policy can fully cover and extend to sub-provincial cities and other large cities. The policy of restricting the development of large cities itself is worth debating, as these are where employment and economic development opportunities lie.]

Classified Household Migration Policy

1. County-level city districts, county people's government towns, other established towns

With occupation and residence, one can settle.

Individuals with a legal stable occupation and a legal stable residence (including rental) can apply to register as permanent residents along with their cohabiting spouses, unmarried children, and parents.

(Note: Places with heavy pressure on urban comprehensive carrying capacity can make detailed regulations on the scope, duration, and conditions of legal stable occupations and residences (including rentals). At the same time, effective measures should be actively taken to address the settlement issues of temporary workers and businessmen.)

2. Districted cities

After working for three years, one can settle.

Individuals with a legal stable occupation for more than three years and a legal stable residence (including rental), and who have participated in social insurance according to national regulations for a certain period, can apply to register as permanent residents along with their cohabiting spouses, unmarried children, and parents.

(Note: Western and central regions can appropriately relax the requirements for the duration of stable occupations based on local realities. In places with heavy pressure on urban comprehensive carrying capacity, stricter regulations can be made on the scope, duration, and conditions of legal stable occupations and residences (including rentals). At the same time, effective measures should be actively taken to address the settlement issues of temporary workers and businessmen. The specific duration of social insurance participation is determined by the local people's government and approved by the provincial people's government.)

3. Municipalities, sub-provincial cities, and other large cities

Improve and implement settlement policies.

Continue to reasonably control the population size and further improve and implement the current urban settlement policies.

Household Registration Reform Milestones

1954

China promulgated and implemented its first constitution, which stipulated that citizens have the freedom to migrate and reside.

1958

The "Regulations on Household Registration of the People's Republic of China" was promulgated, establishing the household registration management system.

1963

Division into "agricultural household" and "non-agricultural household" based on whether one owns planned supply of commercial grain.

1975

The constitution formally abolished the provisions related to the freedom of migration.

1985

The resident identity card system was announced and implemented. Guangzhou issued "Regulations on the Management of Temporary Population Household Registration."

2001

The State Council approved the nationwide implementation of small town household registration system reform.

2006

"The Several Opinions on Implementing the Guangzhou Work Residence Permit System" was implemented, issuing Guangzhou "green cards."
