The stalemate in property market regulation eases: the government will increase guidance on existing supply.

by g3qfxz18053 on 2012-03-01 14:05:37

Hot topics such as land hoarding, unsold inventory rate, and vacancy rate will prompt relevant departments to increase their efforts in guiding the supply of existing properties. This effect, combined with the increased market supply in the second half of the year, should alleviate the heavy task of curbing a rapid rebound in housing prices. At the same time, we must be vigilant against another round of supply shortages.

Reporters Zhang Jie, guest author Kou Bo, and Li Gaoyang from Caixin report that since the new real estate policies introduced in April, variables have rapidly increased as the regulation enters its middle phase, making the real estate industry enter a sensitive period.

The latest variable is the unexpected exposure of a "blacklist" of land hoarders held by the Ministry of Land and Resources. On August 2, Netease Real Estate was the first to disclose this list of idle lands, detailing information such as provincial regions, project names, land grantees, contract numbers, contract signing dates, and agreed start and completion times. By September 30 last year, there were a total of 1,457 idle plots nationwide covering an area of 9,772 hectares, over 70% of which were residential lands.

Previously, the National Bureau of Statistics had published data on unsold commercial properties by real estate developers since 2005. As of the end of June this year, the total unsold commercial property area across the country was 192 million square meters, up 6.4% year-on-year. Among them, unsold residential properties amounted to 106 million square meters, up 0.2% year-on-year. At the end of 2009, among the unsold residential properties, 55.84 million square meters had been unsold for 1-3 years, while 5.08 million square meters had been unsold for more than 3 years.

It is expected that clearing idle land, reducing the unsold inventory rate and vacancy rate will continue to be hot topics of public concern, and relevant departments may introduce substantive measures in the future to signal the market's determination to crack down on hoarding.

Looking for the market equilibrium point

Another brewing variable is that plots supplied last year are likely to form a supply peak in the second half of the year, coupled with the increasing inventory of houses by developers, putting mounting pressure on price cuts.

On August 10, major real estate companies including China Merchants, Poly, Vanke, and Gemdale successively announced their semi-annual performance reports. A common feature was the decline in sales area in the first half of the year. All four giants judged that the possibility of regulatory easing in the second half of the year is small, and housing prices will continue to fall.

That day, 28 real estate companies released their semi-annual reports. Their combined net cash flow from operating activities amounted to -44.856 billion yuan, compared to 24 billion yuan during the same period last year. Of these 28 companies, 20 had negative indicators, accounting for as high as 71%, indicating that tight cash flow has become a general phenomenon in the industry. Meanwhile, among the 28 companies that have disclosed their financials, inventory growth reached as high as 58%, reaching the highest level in a year.

Qin Hong, deputy director of the Policy Research Center of the Ministry of Housing and Urban-Rural Development, believes that at this time last year, the real estate market had already warmed up, with increased transaction volumes and amounts, but all early indicators of real estate companies were still sluggish. This year, all early indicators of real estate development are high, but both transaction volumes and amounts are declining, showing two completely opposite patterns from last year, which again verifies the real estate development cycle.

In her view, the supply in the second half of the year will be very sufficient, leading to a concentrated sales period, because from January to June, the national real estate development investment growth rate was 38.1%, and the newly started construction area growth rate reached 67.9%, almost at historical highs.

This is precisely the main focus of the evolution of price and volume in the second half of the year's real estate market, and also the main basis for predicting falling housing prices. In its semi-annual report, Gemdale Group stated that the increase in market supply in the second half of the year will deepen adjustments, and further declines in housing prices will be natural.

If so, the heavy task of curbing a rapid rebound in housing prices could shift from policy regulation to market supply and demand factors, possibly marking the successful conclusion of this round of real estate market regulation? Professor Ren Ruo'en from the School of Economics and Management at Beihang University believes that currently, one can only roughly judge that real estate regulatory policies will not easily be tightened, but it won't be easy to loosen either.

Economists have given their respective predictions on the next trend of housing prices. Stephen Green, chief economist at Standard Chartered Bank, believes that a 10%-20% drop in housing prices would bring about a policy "window period." Mingchun Sun, chief economist for China at Nomura Securities, believes that a correction in the real estate market is inevitable, with a 10%-20% drop in the national average housing prices over the next 12-18 months, followed by stabilization and recovery in the second half of 2011.

In an interview with Caixin, Ren Zhiqiang said that the current volume of housing sales has dropped significantly, and the real estate market will show polarization in the future, with larger drops in sales and prices in first-tier cities, while second- and third-tier cities will see some growth. "Real estate companies that only operate in first-tier cities will feel greater financial pressure in the second half of the year."

Ren Zhiqiang analyzed that old real estate projects will not lower prices because the owners will protest, but new developments will reduce their opening prices. "In fact, low-price sales by developers have already become quite common, but due to the lack of a starting point for reference, it is difficult to calculate these decreases in statistics."

Ren Ruo'en believes that the effects of regulation-induced market calmness and falling housing prices will gradually manifest in the second half of the year or early next year. However, he also refused to predict specific decreases, saying, "Trying to find the bottom of the real estate market is probably the hardest thing."

Who will push for regulatory relaxation?

Nationally speaking, except for a few cities where there has been some loosening, housing prices in most cities remain high and volatile.

In July, the average housing prices in 70 large and medium-sized cities rose 10.3% year-on-year and slightly fell 1.2% month-on-month. For the "hundred-day new policy," evaluations of this scorecard vary widely. Optimists believe that finally, after 15 months, housing prices have fallen for the first time, especially in the severely affected areas of Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, and Shenzhen, where three out of the four cities saw monthly declines except for Beijing which remained flat. Pessimists, however, argue that the downward trend in housing prices has yet to begin.

Now, signs of economic slowdown have become increasingly evident, putting the real estate market regulation back into a waiting period.

"The theory of a second economic dip" and "the theory of relaxing real estate policies" have gained traction. On August 12, Zhu Zhongyi, vice president of the China Real Estate Association, revealed at the Hainan Boao Real Estate Forum that due to the shrinking real estate transactions, the association had repeatedly "submitted petitions" to the State Council, suggesting to suspend the introduction of new tightening real estate regulatory policies.

Upon hearing this, discussions about the new real estate policies that had temporarily subsided due to collective statements from the Ministry of Land and Resources, the Ministry of Housing and Urban-Rural Development, and the Banking Regulatory Commission stating "no relaxation in real estate regulation and strict enforcement of all policies" flared up again.

Harteng, former vice chairman of the Financial and Economic Affairs Committee of the National People's Congress, told Caixin in an interview that real estate policies cannot be inconsistent. The 2008 real estate regulation had already come at a great cost: on one hand, the government lost credibility; on the other hand, the rebound formed an even bigger bubble. Fan Gang, professor at the Graduate School of the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences, bluntly stated, "Don't scare people by saying that a decrease in real estate investment will affect GDP."

The macroeconomic data for the first half of the year also supports the confidence of the ministries in maintaining "no relaxation" in regulation. On August 11, the National Bureau of Statistics released the economic operation data from January to July: in the much-watched leading indicators of real estate, real estate development investment from January to July totaled 2.3865 trillion yuan, growing by 37.2%. In the earlier released economic data for the first half of the year, the national GDP grew by 11.1% year-on-year, although the growth rate in the second quarter declined somewhat, but the GDP growth rate still reached 10.3%.

Whether real estate regulation will relax, the attitude of local governments is also a key variable.

Recently, it has become a scene in the industry that major real estate companies are pessimistic about acquiring land. According to monitoring by the Research Center of Centaline Property, from January to June this year, the amount spent by the top ten leading real estate companies on land purchases ranked as follows: 26.2 billion yuan, 21.8 billion yuan, 16.9 billion yuan, 11.2 billion yuan, 8.9 billion yuan, and 6.6 billion yuan, decreasing by about 20% each month on average, and in just six months, the amount spent on land purchases has sharply decreased by about 75%.

The biggest victims of the cooling land market are undoubtedly local governments heavily dependent on land finance.

Yin Zhongli, deputy director of the Financial Markets Research Office of the Institute of Finance and Banking of the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences, believes that the central government's regulation of the real estate market and the cleanup of local government financing platforms simultaneously test the tolerance of local governments, and the next step should closely monitor local governments.

A recent report published by the National Audit Office shows that the loan balance of financing platforms in 13 provinces exceeds the total fiscal revenue of the province, some exceeding twice as much, seven provinces, ten cities, and fourteen counties have debt balances exceeding 100% of their available financial resources for the year, with the highest reaching 364.77%.

This is the soft underbelly of local governments, and developers are well aware of it. "The real estate market is like cancer cells; everyone knows there is a problem, but if you forcibly remove it, the economy itself will suffer significant damage." Su Xin, chairman of Gaohe Investment, told Caixin reporters that recently, several developers privately gathered and judged that by the fourth quarter of this year, real estate regulation will inevitably see varying degrees of policy relaxation.

However, some scholars do not agree with this judgment. Wang Xiaoguang, researcher at the Decision-making Consulting Department of the National Academy of Governance, even proposed that the regulatory policies are not severe enough and should not be relaxed going forward.

Beware of another round of insufficient supply

The real estate regulation in the first half of the year has made developers cautious about the future market. One of their instinctive actions is to reduce land acquisition and lower the bidding price range. Qin Hong pointed out that starting from June, land transaction volumes have significantly decreased, and the average land transaction price in the first half of this year has dropped by 9% compared to the second half of 2009.

Frontline real estate practitioners deeply feel that when housing prices are still relatively strong, the land market has cooled down first. In first-tier cities such as Beijing and Shanghai, individual cases of land bids failing to attract any bidders or being withdrawn have appeared consecutively. Even for plots that have been successfully traded, their total transaction prices, floor prices, and premium rates cannot compare with the heat before the regulation in April.

Ren Zhiqiang has already sensed a dangerous sign from the still optimistic real estate investment data from January to July. He told Caixin reporters that usually, the effects of real estate regulation policies become apparent three to six months after their implementation, first reflected in the stock market, then in the investment levels of developers.

Ren Zhiqiang believes that in the rapid growth of fixed asset investments in real estate in May this year, most of it belonged to land acquisitions, with no net investment growth, reflecting merely early decisions of enterprises.

"Policy makers should pay attention to changes in the direction of real estate companies' investments above the land floor price. Simply buying a piece of land and digging a big hole does not mean anything for subsequent market supply. Based on past experiences, China's real estate regulation has never exceeded one and a half years. Real estate has its own cycles, and if regulation does not take this cycle seriously, it will only lead to another round of supply shortages," Ren Zhiqiang said.

Regarding this, Liu Yuanchun, deputy dean of the School of Economics at Renmin University, believes that to fundamentally break the cycle of chaos in real estate regulation, the goals of real estate regulation should be shifted: 'supply-driven' is better than 'demand control'; 'institutional construction' is better than 'bubble squeezing' and 'price suppression'.
