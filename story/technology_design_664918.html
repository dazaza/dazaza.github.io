Steel enterprises respond to trade frictions: transfer market, adjust structure, and rely on domestic demand

by ldfgskkgfea2 on 2012-02-14 09:27:30

Chinese Steel Enterprises' Response to Trade Frictions: Shifting Markets, Adjusting Structures, and Relying on Domestic Demand – Steel Information - Industry News - Building Materials Network

People's Daily reported that the "cloud" of trade sanctions has successively loomed over China's steel industry. Seamless pipes, oil well pipes, drill rods, steel gratings, steel wire laminates... An increasing number of Chinese steel products have become targets of trade sanctions, making China's steel industry one of the "severely affected areas" in international trade frictions.

Facing a severe situation, how can Chinese steel enterprises navigate through the "perilous shoals" of trade frictions?

Frequent Barriers, Decline in Volume and Price

The anti-dumping and countervailing duty lawsuit initiated by the U.S. against Chinese oil well pipes exported to the U.S. since April last year, with an involved amount of approximately $2.7 billion, became the largest "double anti" case so far in U.S. trade sanctions against China, involving 200 Chinese steel enterprises with export records at U.S. customs from the beginning of 2008 to the first quarter of 2009.

Currently, this largest "double anti" case against China by the U.S. has basically concluded: On November 5 last year, relevant U.S. authorities had made an unfavorable preliminary ruling for China regarding the anti-dumping part. On December 30 last year, the U.S. International Trade Commission made an unfavorable final ruling for China regarding the countervailing duty part. Although the investigation is still ongoing, some Chinese companies have already abandoned their appeals due to slim hopes.

The "double anti" case of oil well pipes is just a typical example of the international trade sanctions faced by China's steel industry in recent years. Since 2008, the EU, the U.S., Russia, India, and other countries have successively launched "anti-dumping" and "countervailing duty" investigations on various steel products exported by China, including seamless pipes, oil well pipes, drill rods, and steel gratings. Relevant experts predict that after the global economic crisis, the world will enter a high-frequency period of international trade frictions lasting three to five years. Affected by the international financial crisis, international market demand has sharply contracted, and the continuous occurrence of trade frictions has further hindered steel exports which were already obstructed. Customs data shows that in December 2009, China's steel exports amounted to 3.34 million tons, with a cumulative total of 24.6 million tons in the first 12 months, a decrease of 58.5% compared to the previous year. Steel prices have generally declined; for instance, the average price of LME nickel spot, with higher added value, dropped by 29.95% in 2009 compared to the previous year.

Steel pipe products are the "severely affected areas." In 2009, China's seamless pipe exports decreased by nearly 50% compared to 2008; in the same year, the lowest price of oil well pipes exported to the U.S. market was only around $1,600 per ton, while the price in 2008 had been as high as $3,600 per ton, a decline of 35%.

Shifting Markets, Adjusting Structures

For Hualian Steel Pipe Co., Ltd., one of China’s main producers of seamless steel pipes, its export dependency rate reached as high as 48%. In 2009, the company's exports to its original major markets in North America and Europe dropped by more than 70% compared to the previous year, but the total export volume only decreased by 10%. The secret weapon for counterattacking in this "desperate situation" was the development of new markets. In 2009, although Hualian Steel Pipe suffered heavy blows in the North American and European markets, its sales in the Asian market increased by 30%, and those in the African market grew by over 100%.

There are many Chinese steel enterprises like Hualian Steel Pipe that shift target markets. Last year, Tianjin Pipe Group, the largest domestic steel pipe producer, began developing Southeast Asia, the Middle East, and the African markets, successfully maintaining export volumes at levels similar to the previous year. As early as April last year, before the U.S. initiated the "double anti" case against Chinese oil well pipes, Baosteel stopped exporting oil well pipes to the U.S. and turned to develop other markets instead.

Emerging markets are not entirely sunny either. Wu Guohua, the chief analyst of Hualian Steel Pipe, believes that there is currently a tendency for trade sanctions to spread to other countries. Due to increasingly closed-off markets in North America and Europe, a large number of companies are crowding into emerging markets, leading to intensifying competition.

Changes in target markets drive adjustments in the structure of export products. According to Liu Gaoxiang, the director of the market department of Tianjin Pipe Group, the company used to mainly export high-value-added oil well pipes to North America and Europe. After shifting to Southeast Asia and Africa, they focused on developing a series of oil and gas transmission pipeline products. "The steel pipe industries in some countries in Southeast Asia and Africa are almost blank, so the risk of encountering trade sanctions there is relatively small. However, on the other hand, due to fierce competition, even though the added value of pipeline products is not low, the profit level of enterprises is not high," said Liu Gaoxiang, the director of the market department of Tianjin Pipe Group.

Segmenting markets and adjusting product positioning to adapt to new changes in existing markets can also avoid impacting "similar products" in other countries. Since last year, Taiyuan Iron & Steel (Group) Co., Ltd. (TISCO) has adjusted its product supply structure following the principle of "what others don't have we do, what others do we specialize in, what others specialize in we excel in." For example, exporting ultra-pure ferritic stainless steel to India when others don't, exporting wide stainless steel plates to countries like the U.S., and exporting high-quality stainless steel products to Russia.

Adjusting product structures based on market changes has been recognized by experts. Qi Zhaodong, deputy secretary-general of the China Iron and Steel Association and chief analyst, said at the Fifth Bohai Bay Steel Market Forum hosted by Lange Steel Network that in 2010, steel exports should implement three major strategies: diversification of export regions, multi-variety of export structures, and fine-tuning of export benefits. More attention should be paid to the cost, quality, and benefits of exported products to improve export standards.

Relying on Domestic Demand, Accelerating Consolidation

In 2009, Taiyuan Iron & Steel Group exported 134,600 tons of stainless steel, a decrease of 56.07% compared to the previous year. However, driven by national macro-control policies, domestic stainless steel sales reached 2.1456 million tons, an increase of 58.75% compared to the previous year. Ultimately, it achieved a profit of 1 billion yuan in 2009, remaining stable compared to the previous year.

Taiyuan Iron & Steel Group's experience is not an isolated case. According to my understanding, many domestic steel mills showed a trend of increased domestic sales and decreased foreign sales in 2009. Therefore, expanding the domestic market and relying on internal demand is also seen as one of their risk-avoidance approaches.

However, the lack of technical standards for high-end steel products domestically and the high entry threshold to some extent restrict the "return" of high-end steel products to the domestic market. Analysts from Hualian Steel Pipe introduced that the promotion of some mature products sold overseas in the domestic market has been very challenging. The main reason is that there are no technical standards for such high-end steel products domestically, and each enterprise must go through certification from scratch for each product promoted. Additionally, domestic users have relatively lower recognition of domestically produced high-end steel products, preferring imported products even if the quality reaches or exceeds imported ones. They suggest that the state quickly perfect the technical certification standards for high-end steel products to promote their application and promotion domestically.

"One of the reasons for trade frictions is the excess capacity and low industrial concentration in domestic steel pipe production," explained Liu Gaoxiang with examples. Currently, the excess capacity of seamless steel pipes domestically is nearly one-third, with low industrial concentration. Numerous enterprises rush overseas to find markets, but the growth in foreign steel pipe market demand cannot keep up with the increase in capacity. Various factors lead to mutual price undercutting among steel export enterprises, causing prices to be controlled by users, leading to a continuous decline. To reduce trade frictions, it requires strengthening industry coordination by the state, accelerating the integration of domestic steel enterprises, improving industrial concentration, and reducing malicious competition among enterprises.

Since November last year, China's steel has resumed net exports. Qi Zhaodong believed that if no special catastrophic events occur, the international steel market situation in 2010 will be better than in 2009. At the same time, he pointed out that the world economic recovery is fragile and the foundation is still unstable. The recovery of international market demand will be a slow process, while the pressure of steel overcapacity is significant, and international trade protectionism is escalating. China's steel exports are still facing many difficulties.
