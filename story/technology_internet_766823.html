BlackBerry Out: Microsoft Secures Third Place in Smartphone Race

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 21:57:14

Introduction: The American IT website CNET today published a commentary article titled "BlackBerry's Failure Benefits Windows Phone" which argues that since BlackBerry has agreed to be sold, it is effectively handing over the third place position in the smartphone market to Microsoft's Windows Phone. However, for Microsoft to truly seize substantial market share, it still needs to overcome many challenges.

The following is the full text of the article:

BlackBerry Out

Employees in Microsoft's Windows Phone division might not publicly admit it, but they are certainly celebrating today.

This is because their most evenly matched competitor, BlackBerry, has raised the white flag. The Canadian company announced on Monday that it has accepted a $4.7 billion privatization offer and will continue to retreat from the consumer smartphone market.

"Today's news will accelerate people's abandonment of BlackBerry, as no one believes they can continue to receive post-sale support," said Carolina Milanesi, an analyst at the American market research company Gartner. "Windows Phone, on the other hand, can fully capitalize on this opportunity."

A spokesperson for Microsoft's Windows Phone department declined to comment.

Given that the current smartphone market is dominated by iPhone and Android, both Windows Phone and BlackBerry have set their sights on the third position in the market. This seemingly ordinary goal is not easy to achieve; every bit of market share these two companies gain requires arduous efforts.

However, in recent months, the balance of fate has tilted towards Windows Phone. According to estimates from the American market research company IDC, Windows Phone's market share increased from 3.1% a year ago to 3.7% in the second quarter of this year, while BlackBerry's dropped from 4.9% to 2.9%.

The Verdict

Although BlackBerry 10 and Windows Phone are two very different operating systems, they face the same predicament: they both offer unique mobile experiences, yet market reality proves that it is difficult for consumers to recognize and appreciate them; both lag behind iOS and Android in terms of app ecosystem scale; and both encounter difficulties in promoting their products.

Moreover, the similarities between these two systems also work in favor of Windows Phone. Both aim to attract both business and consumer users simultaneously. Analyst Jan Dawson of the market research firm Ovum stated that BlackBerry's decline may cause many users who seek more office functionalities beyond Android or iOS to pay attention to Windows Phone. Productivity features on Windows Phone will also appeal to former BlackBerry users.

"Whether contracting into a niche product or completely disappearing from the phone market, BlackBerry's users and developers will switch allegiance," Dawson said.

BlackBerry's withdrawal essentially hands over the third-place throne to Windows Phone. If carriers desire a multi-operating system coexistence scenario, they will inevitably promote Windows Phone with greater effort.

Meanwhile, BlackBerry is essentially abandoning the consumer smartphone market. The company may eventually become a niche brand, but its days as a major smartphone manufacturer are numbered.

In contrast, Windows Phone at least ensures that it has one committed supporter. The company recently acquired Nokia’s phone and services business, the largest member of the Windows Phone camp, thereby ensuring the quality of Windows Phone devices. The company will also continue to invest engineering resources.

Challenges Remain

However, BlackBerry's exit does not guarantee Windows Phone's success; it merely provides an opportunity. While Windows Phone has the potential to successfully increase its market share, its standing is clearly far from that of Android and iOS, whose shares are 79.3% and 13.2%, respectively.

Apple announced on Monday that the iPhone 5s and iPhone 5c sold a total of 9 million units in just three days after launch. No Windows Phone has ever received such enthusiastic consumer reception.

Moreover, many business users are already using iOS or Android. "Microsoft's challenge remains convincing users to consider other options outside of the first and second ranked platforms," said Avi Greengart, an analyst at the American market research company Current Analysis.

If Windows Phone cannot maintain or accelerate its momentum, the smartphone market could develop towards a duopoly. This would be detrimental to everyone. For the benefit of the entire industry, Windows Phone must rise to the challenge and must not waste this rare opportunity.
