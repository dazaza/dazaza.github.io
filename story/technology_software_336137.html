International aspects

by cg4fh5rr2 on 2010-03-29 21:39:02

The biggest problem with ASCII is the first letter of the acronym itself. ASCII is a truly American standard, so it cannot adequately meet the needs of other English-speaking countries. For instance, where is the British pound symbol (£)? English uses the Latin (or Roman) alphabet. In languages that use the Latin alphabet, words in English rarely require accent marks (or diacritical marks). Even those English words that traditionally include diacritical marks, such as "cöoperate" or "résumé," are fully acceptable without them in spelling.

However, in many countries to the south, north, and across the Atlantic from the United States, the use of diacritical marks is common in their languages. These marks were initially introduced to adapt the Latin alphabet to the phonetic needs of these languages. Traveling in the Far East or southern Western Europe, you will encounter languages that do not use the Latin alphabet at all, such as Greek, Hebrew, Arabic, and Russian (which uses the Cyrillic alphabet). If you travel further east, you will find Chinese logograms and the Japanese and Korean adoption of the Chinese character system.

The history of ASCII began in 1967, after which it primarily focused on overcoming its own limitations to better suit non-American varieties of English. For example, in 1967, the International Organization for Standardization (ISO: International Standards Organization) recommended a variant of ASCII where codes 0x40, 0x5B, 0x5C, 0x5D, 0x7B, 0x7C, and 0x7D were "reserved for national use," while codes 0x5E, 0x60, and 0x7E were marked as "available for other graphic symbols when special characters required by domestic needs occupy 8, 9, or 10 space positions." This was clearly not an optimal international solution because it did not guarantee consistency. However, it does show how people tried to devise encodings for different languages.
