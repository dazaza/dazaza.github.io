Apple in talks with TV content providers to cut out carriers

by anonymous on 2013-08-26 13:57:56

It has long been an open secret that Apple intends to venture into the television domain, but negotiations with cable TV operators over the years have seen little progress. According to informed sources, Apple seems to have recently adopted a new negotiation strategy: bypassing TV operators and negotiating directly with TV content providers, hoping to deliver TV content primarily through apps rather than channels. Notably, TV content providers currently in negotiations with Apple include ESPN, HBO, Viacom, Nickelodeon, and Comedy Central.

However, it is unclear how these negotiations are progressing. Analysts believe that considering the current chaos in the TV industry, it is difficult to predict whether Apple can reach agreements with TV content providers, as almost every company is seeking its own possible way forward.

Informed sources revealed that Apple's current strategy includes establishing its own paid TV service, essentially transforming itself into a cable TV company that exclusively releases content via the internet. Moreover, companies such as Intel, Sony, and Google are also pursuing similar plans and may release this service before Apple. Additionally, Apple plans to launch a fully functional TV product to completely control the user experience of watching TV. Previously, Apple released a set-top box product called Apple TV, which allows users to watch TV shows from platforms like Netflix after connecting this device to their TV.

Spokespersons for Apple and Viacom refused to comment on this matter. However, ESPN spokesperson Chris LaPlaca stated: "Discussions among relevant parties are still at a very early stage, and formal negotiations have not even begun."

Meanwhile, HBO spokesperson Jeff Cusson said: "We believe that HBO's current operational model is the best one, and we do not have plans to enter these markets through a new model."

Initiating Change

The biggest obstacle Apple faces in launching a TV product is content sourcing, as its attempts to convince TV operators to broadcast programs on Apple devices have almost failed. More frustratingly, these operators are actively preventing content distributors within the industry from signing similar content-sharing agreements with Apple.

In response, Apple seems to have its own countermeasures. According to informed sources, the company previously indicated that it does not need to establish partnerships with all content suppliers before releasing its TV product; having enough excellent programs to stand out is sufficient while striving to provide users with a smooth connected viewing experience.

Of course, if Apple could reach cooperation agreements with top-tier content providers like ESPN or HBO, it would be more confident in launching its product. However, most TV content providers are unwilling to take risks unless other companies remain inactive, as such collaborations could damage their relationships with cable TV companies, who have always viewed Apple as a "market disruptor."

For these cable TV companies, Apple's future TV product might revolutionize users' habits of watching TV. For instance, replacing traditional channels with content provider apps and substituting channel surfing with search functions. In such a scenario, cable TV companies would merely become one of many options for consumers to watch TV, no longer the core, akin to the relationship between American mobile carrier AT&T and the iPhone now.

There are reports that Apple also hopes to reach agreements with cable TV manufacturers that do not require set-top boxes, but negotiations in this area have seen little progress over the past two years. Notably, there were previous rumors that Apple was about to reach an agreement with Time Warner Cable. According to their plan, cable TV subscribers would be able to watch Time Warner-provided TV content via Apple TV.

Perfect Timing

Currently, Apple TV is widely regarded as an effective carrier for the iTunes store. Apple CEO Tim Cook stated in May: "Half of the 13 million Apple TVs sold were sold last year."

In recent months, Apple has acquired several companies in the industry to advance its entry into the TV market. Its most recent acquisition was Matcha.tv, a streaming media aggregation platform whose services help users find desired video content more conveniently on the internet. Reports suggest that Apple is currently negotiating with Israeli 3D sensor chip company PrimeSense for an acquisition, aiming to acquire the company for $280 million because its sensing technology can be used for TV gesture control systems.

Meanwhile, Apple is facing increasing competition pressure from other rivals. For example, Microsoft is about to launch Xbox One, a gaming console that can both play games and stream videos; Roku, a smart TV set-top box manufacturer, has successfully secured $60 million in financing and partnered with companies including Time Warner, Comcast, and DirecTV; Google has just released Chromecast, a device that can play YouTube and other video content on TVs; Amazon also plans to launch its own set-top box product.

As more people can watch more video content on these devices without subscribing to cable TV services, more will likely turn to streaming services like Netflix, iTunes, and Amazon Instant Video. Simultaneously, the number of subscriptions to U.S. pay-TV services has reached its lowest level in four years.

However, we do not expect the number of cable TV subscribers to drop dramatically, as cable TV companies still hold exclusive broadcasting rights to sports events and some special programs. For instance, Apple recently added ESPN and HBO apps to Apple TV, but only cable TV subscribers can use these features, which is precisely why Apple hopes to reach agreements with cable TV companies before launching its TV product.

Second Choice

If things do not develop as smoothly as imagined, Apple's alternative choice is to create its own cable TV company. At that time, Apple might introduce what is known as a "virtual multichannel video programming distributor," a service capable of capturing all pay-TV and satellite service programs. Currently, companies including Intel, Google, and Sony plan to launch similar services.

For consumers, this service is not much different from set-top box services, but technical differences can further promote negotiations between Apple and content providers. However, for cable TV companies, the introduction of this service by Apple could have a disruptive impact on the TV industry.

According to informed sources, during negotiations, Apple consistently emphasizes its nearly 600 million iTunes users and its ability to effectively convert them into paying customers. In previous negotiations with music and publishing companies, Apple also highlighted this advantage, and subsequently successfully transformed the status quo of these two industries.

It is worth noting that Apple co-founder and CEO Steve Jobs once said: "I hope to create a highly integrated, easy-to-use TV product."

At the time, people widely expected Apple to soon launch a TV product, but two years later, this product has yet to appear. The main reason is that this industry is far more complex than any Apple has ventured into before.
