Nintendo Executive Says 3DS Games Are Not Afraid of Price Competition from Mobile Games

by tunzuosz5 on 2011-03-22 16:54:14

Nintendo President and CEO Satoru Iwata and Reggie Fils-Aime, president of Nintendo of America, have both made similar assessments of the mobile and social gaming market, stating that despite the market being flooded with free or inexpensive games, Nintendo aims to uphold the "value" of its games.

In a recent media interview, Hideki Konno, the head of the Nintendo 3DS development project, expressed that Nintendo will not engage in a race to the bottom with digital games on its portable platforms. This strategy is primarily for the consideration of game developers.

He stated, "In the face of a large number of one-dollar or free games in the market, I just want to say that we do not plan to compete with these kinds of games; we don't want to compare ourselves with them. Nintendo not only aims to protect the quality of our games but also enhance the entertainment experience we provide, making it more extraordinary. Moreover, we are not worried about price competition in games."

He believes that this perspective is shared by more than just Nintendo. "I believe Sony and Microsoft share the same view. Of course, as a user, if someone told me, 'We can get Call of Duty on your portable device for just 100 yen,' I would be very pleased."

"I am really happy to see good, affordable games, but I think it's impossible to invest a large amount of money into developing a super game like Call of Duty and then sell it cheaply; it simply doesn't work. If you want to sell such games at low prices, the only way is to offer simplified versions with level restrictions or limited content, which reduces costs. This allows other content-rich games to remain high-priced."

"If we developed a big-budget game for Nintendo priced at just 100 yen, how many copies would we need to sell to break even? The required sales volume would be astonishing, and this is even less possible."

"As a game developer, I love my career and hope that the products I launch can attract users and entertain them. As a user, I desire the same. If I find a good game that attracts me, I would be willing to try it out and wouldn't mind spending some money on it."

"I'm not saying that I think there's anything wrong with mobile games, nor am I implying that they're cheap or have no value. I'm just saying that they belong to different types of games."
