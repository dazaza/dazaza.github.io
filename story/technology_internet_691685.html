&；nbsp；&；nbsp；&；nbsp； Speaking of where the gathering square is in the capital city of Amsterdam, it has to be the Dam Square. Whether it’s for domestic residents celebrating the New Year or for protests, Dam Square is always the meeting place. The Royal Palace (Koninklijk Paleis) built in 1648 at the edge of the square is the most prominent landmark here, designed by Jacob Van Campen in the Dutch classical style. Since it was constructed on wetlands, it required a total of 13,659 wooden piles and still stands strong today. This palace was originally used as the town hall. In 1808, it became the palace of Louis Napoleon, and now it is mainly used for receiving foreign guests. The carvings on the western and eastern pediments are the work of Artus Quellien, intricate and delicate, showcasing the confidence of the Dutch people after World War II.

&；nbsp；&；nbsp；&；nbsp； Next to the Royal Palace is the Nieuwe Kerk (New Church), built in the 15th century, holding a status in the Netherlands equivalent to London's Westminster Abbey. The coronation ceremonies of several queens, including Queen Wilhelmina (September 6, 1898), Queen Juliana (September 6, 1948), and Queen Beatrix (April 30, 1980), were held here.

There is also an obelisk on Dam Square, standing at 22 meters tall, designed by J.J.P. Oud and carved by John Raedecker, erected in memory of the Dutch compatriots who perished in World War II. The monument was completed in 1956, with two stone lions in front representing the Netherlands. Every year on May 5th, from the queen and prime minister down to common citizens, people come here to lay flowers in remembrance.

On days without gatherings or parades, Dam Square often becomes a venue for street artists' performances and is a must-visit spot for tourists to take commemorative photos. Its joyful and leisurely atmosphere provides a breather for those engaged in intense shopping.
