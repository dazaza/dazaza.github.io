2009.01.23 (Is Google lagging behind?)

by bjseeprof on 2009-12-07 11:28:59

Fortune Magazine Rates the Best US Employers: Google Falls to Fourth http://tech.qq.com/a/20090122/000278.htm

First reaction: Is Google falling behind? I have always admired Google's "Don't Be Evil" philosophy and its completely open working environment. But all of this requires a solid financial foundation. Previously, Google was reported to have chaotic management, with former Microsoft talents and others returning. This makes sense; there needs to be a balance between freedom and control, much like China's Doctrine of the Mean. An entirely open philosophy is good, but it must have limits, which means having control. How can we effectively supervise? Can the high-cost working environment correspond to high-efficiency work outcomes in proportion?

In my impression, the American working environment emphasizes individuality and openness. Therefore, Google's approach aligns with American cultural values, even pushing it to extremes to attract IT talents from other domestic companies. However, when applied to other countries, such as Google China, simply copying their methods may not achieve the original goals. Chinese workers are accustomed to being supervised and following orders. If a completely open way were introduced in China, beyond the initial surprise, it might lead to situations where people take advantage of the openness without assuming responsibility.

In contrast, a more suitable working environment and internet culture for China would be something like Tencent or Baidu, which balance freedom and control effectively. Yesterday, an IT professional wrote an article titled "Steamed Sea Turtles, Braised Local X," analyzing why many expatriates fail as executives in Chinese companies. This roughly explains how local teams have broken through in China's internet wars, causing significant concern among foreign IT giants eager to enter the Chinese market. The influence of expatriate teams has become increasingly weak, indicating that in future internet trends, overseas giants will adjust their entry strategies by directly appointing local executive teams.

The financial tsunami reveals who isn't prepared.
