Chinese couple faces kidnapping charges for trying to take children back to their homeland

by q1abzj28341 on 2012-03-07 16:43:33

A Chinese Canadian couple living in Toronto, Canada, are suspected of secretly taking away their two biological children who were under the supervision of child protective services. On October 25th, the family of four was preparing to fly via Vancouver to Hong Kong when they were intercepted by the Royal Canadian Mounted Police (RCMP) in Richmond. The Chinese Canadian couple faces charges of kidnapping or abducting children, and on October 26th, the RCMP returned all four individuals to Toronto.

Reported by our correspondent Zhang Yaoqing, with contributions from Hong Yu and intern reporter Zhou Jia, reporting from Vancouver.

The older husband and younger wife "kidnapped" their own children.

The arrested Chinese Canadian couple consists of a 75-year-old husband, Hung-Kwan YUEN, and his 34-year-old wife, Ana Cheung.

It is understood that the couple has a 6-year-old son named Timothy and a 5-year-old daughter named Jasmine. According to Toronto police detective Jim Giczi, due to past incidents where the couple was deemed neglectful towards their children, their 5-year-old daughter and 6-year-old son were placed under the care of the Catholic Children's Aid Society of Toronto two years ago. During this time, the couple was allowed weekly supervised and unsupervised visits with their children. The Children's Aid Society was preparing to seek permanent custody of the children this week; if successful, the couple might no longer be permitted to visit their children.

Detective Giczi stated that on October 24th, the couple visited the Children's Aid Society in Toronto for a two-hour unsupervised visit. Around 2 p.m., after taking their children out, the couple disappeared with them. They were last seen leaving the couple's residence at 100 Wingarden Court in Scarborough East, boarding a silver minivan driven by an unknown man. After the children failed to return on time, the Children's Aid Society reported the incident to the police, who immediately notified other law enforcement agencies to assist in locating the family.

Intercepted before flying to Hong Kong

Giczi indicated that prior to the incident on October 24th, the couple's two children had been living in an adoptive family, with adoption procedures set to be finalized within the week. Afterward, the Chinese Canadian couple would no longer be able to visit their children.

Giczi continued, explaining that the court currently allows the couple to visit their children on Mondays, Wednesdays, and Fridays under the supervision of Children's Aid Society staff, as well as a two-hour unsupervised visit every Saturday.

On the morning of October 25th, RCMP officers located the missing Fung family and their two children at a company belonging to Hung-Kwan Yuen and Ana Cheung in Richmond, Greater Vancouver. At the time, the four were preparing to board a flight to Hong Kong.

The police stated that the two children were found to be in good health. The RCMP arranged for the Fung family to be returned to Toronto, where Hung-Kwan Yuen and Ana Cheung will face two counts of kidnapping or abducting children. Their two children will be returned to the care of the Children's Aid Society.

Children to be returned to adoptive family

Giczi pointed out that the two children will be returned to their adoptive family. After the couple is escorted back to Toronto, they may each face two counts of kidnapping charges from the Toronto police. The male driver who transported the couple may not face charges.

Cases where parents are charged with kidnapping their own biological children are uncommon in Toronto but have occurred twice in the past two years. In Canada, there have been several such incidents.

Regarding the case where the Toronto Chinese Canadian couple could face kidnapping charges for taking their biological children, British Columbia’s Minister of Children and Family Development Mary Polak stated that she was unaware of the specifics of the case and thus could not comment specifically. Although the regulations of Ontario’s Children’s Aid Society may differ from those in British Columbia, ensuring the safety of the involved children remains the top priority in all circumstances.

Minister Polak noted that the Children’s Aid Society in British Columbia prefers to see families reunited but sometimes certain children must be permanently removed from their homes. The society first considers placing them with other family members, such as grandparents, uncles, or aunts, rather than having the Children’s Aid Society directly take care of them.

She stated that this case involves many legal issues, but determining who has custody of the children and ensuring their safety will be the primary problems to address.
