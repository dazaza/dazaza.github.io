Hong Kong Company FAQs

Q: I want to establish a limited company in Hong Kong, what are the requirements?

A: You need at least one shareholder who is over 18 years old. Mainland citizens or overseas individuals with a passport or ID card are eligible. You also need a registered address and a statutory secretary for the limited company (usually provided by us).

Q: How do I proceed with setting up a Hong Kong company?

A: You just need to fill out a power of attorney form. The form covers three main aspects:

1. Confirming the Chinese and English names of your company.

2. Determining the amount of registered capital. The minimum registered capital is HKD 10,000 (usually divided into 10,000 shares), which does not need to be fully paid upfront but can be increased as needed.

3. Submitting the shareholder's information and a copy of their ID card. If there are more than two shareholders, not all shareholders need to be directors, but there must be at least one director. After receiving these three pieces of information, we will prepare the complete legal documents for you to sign the next day. The process usually takes about 10 working days after signing the documents.

Q: How long does it take to set up a new company? How long if I buy an existing shell company? Can I change the name of an existing company? How long does that take?

A: Setting up a new Hong Kong company takes about 10 working days from the date of signing all legal documents.

Purchasing an existing company takes 1-2 working days, expedited service can be completed on the same day.

Setting up a branch company takes 3 working days.

After purchasing an existing company, you can change its name, which requires 15 working days.

Q: What is the difference between a shareholder and a director?

A: A shareholder is an investor in a joint-stock company, while a director is elected by shareholders and is responsible for managing the company. The chairman of the board is the head of the board of directors. Often, shareholders are also directors.

Q: What are the differences between naming a Hong Kong company and a mainland company?

A: Naming a Hong Kong company is relatively free. Some companies that are difficult to register on the mainland can be registered in Hong Kong as long as the name is not duplicated. Regardless of the size of the registered capital, the Hong Kong government allows company names to include words such as international, group, holding, industry, investment, college, association, center, foundation, and institute. You can also add your favorite place name before the company name, such as France, USA, Italy, Japan, Hong Kong, Shanghai, etc., or use your own name and hometown name as the company name, fully reflecting personalization.

Q: Are there any restrictions on the business scope of a Hong Kong limited company?

A: Hong Kong is one of the freest trading ports in the world, and in principle, there are no significant restrictions on the business scope. Companies can engage in any type of business, such as finance and investment, healthcare, shipping, import-export trade, real estate, construction, decoration, information networks, clothing textiles, tourism, cultural publishing, etc. Many SME owners do not specify a business scope so as not to limit their operations within a certain range.

Q: Where is the registered capital of a Hong Kong company reflected? Is it necessary to pay the registered capital upfront?

A: The minimum registered capital for a Hong Kong limited liability company is HKD 10,000, and there is no requirement to pay it upfront (no capital verification). The registered capital is only reflected in the company's articles of association. If you need to prove your registered capital, showing the articles of association is sufficient. We can also have our accountant issue a credit certificate (CERTIFIED TRUE COPIES).

Q: What services are included in setting up a Hong Kong limited company?

A: Services included in setting up a Hong Kong limited company are:

1. Government certificate fees (company registration certificate, business registration certificate)

2. Printing costs (18 copies of the articles of association, 1 stock book, 1 shareholder register, 1 set of application documents)

3. Seal costs (1 signature stamp, 1 steel seal, 1 amendment seal)

4. One year of registered address fees

5. One year of statutory secretary service fees for the limited company

6. Miscellaneous fees (document fees, courier fees, travel expenses)

7. Accountant professional service fees

Q: How should we make payments after deciding to set up an offshore company?

A: Payment methods: You can pay in cash, by transfer, telegraphic transfer, check, or draft to our designated Hong Kong or mainland account. Cash payment locations include Hong Kong, LA, KUALA LUMPUR, Shenzhen, Guangzhou, Chengdu, Yiwu, etc. Overseas individuals can remit funds to the Hong Kong account; mainland China can remit funds to the Shenzhen account. Account details are available upon request.

Payment method: After the client fills out the power of attorney, 70% of the fees must be prepaid, and the remaining 30% is paid upon completion of the company's establishment. The full stamp duty on the registered capital (1/1000 of the registered capital) must be prepaid. The payment method for agents and clients is the same.

Q: After establishing a Hong Kong company, what annual fees are required?

A: There are two types of annual fees: annual review and annual report, and accounting and auditing.

1. The annual review fee for the tax department and the annual report fee for the company registration office are mandatory annual fees. Prices may vary according to government adjustments, typically ranging between HKD 3,000 to 5,000. These fees must be paid even if the company has no business activities.

2. Regarding accounting and auditing, if the company begins operations and has business transactions, it must conduct accounting and auditing. If the company has no business transactions, it does not need to conduct accounting and auditing. However, tax reporting is still mandatory.

Q: Who is responsible for the annual accounting and auditing of a Hong Kong company? If you handle the accounting for us, what documents are required? Specific charges? How long does it take?

A: Accounting can be done by the company itself or entrusted to a Hong Kong accounting firm. Auditing must be entrusted to an accountant or auditor. If we handle it for you, you must submit all records of business transactions: ledgers, invoices, receipts, payment vouchers, bank statements, accounts receivable, and accounts payable. Generally speaking, bookkeeping takes about one month, and auditing takes 1-2 months.

Q: What are the differences between the statutory secretary services and business secretary services you provide?

A: According to government regulations, a limited company must have a statutory secretary, who can be a local individual or corporation in Hong Kong. Generally, we can arrange a statutory secretary for you. A statutory secretary is a formal secretary required by the Hong Kong government to facilitate government contact and handle official documents.

A business secretary provides customized business practical services based on customer needs, from answering calls, transmitting information, collecting and sending mail, to letters of credit, logistics, and more. The advantage of a business secretary is that it helps build your corporate image, equivalent to having your own employees, office space, and a suite of company resources in Hong Kong. This makes business handling smooth and efficient without missing any orders.

Q: Our company has no office or staff in Hong Kong. After setting up a Hong Kong company, how should we manage and operate it?

A: Our business secretary service is specifically designed for this purpose. For more details, please refer to the business secretary service details.

Q: How does Hong Kong tax businesses?

A: Hong Kong is a free port with few taxes and low rates. The only tax a business must pay annually is profits tax, which is calculated at 17.5% of the company's net profit. For example, if a company has an annual turnover of HKD 10 million, gross profit of HKD 2 million, and net profit of HKD 1 million, the profits tax owed to the government would be 1 million x 17.5% = HKD 175,000. If the company does not make a profit, it does not need to pay taxes.

Q: Are goods entering and leaving Hong Kong taxed?

A: Except for tobacco and alcohol, general goods entering and leaving Hong Kong are not taxed. However, all imported and exported goods must be declared, and customs fees are charged at 0.5% (for goods valued under HKD 470,000) and 0.3% (for goods valued over HKD 470,000).

Q: Are there any taxes to pay when setting up a company?

A: Yes. When setting up a Hong Kong company, you must pay the stamp duty on registered capital. The stamp duty is calculated at 1/1000 of the registered capital. For example, if the registered capital is HKD 1 million, the stamp duty would be HKD 1,000, which is paid once to the government during registration and does not need to be paid again.

Q: Which department issues invoices and receipts for Hong Kong companies?

A: Invoices and receipts for Hong Kong companies are not uniformly printed by the tax department but are printed and issued by the company's board of directors based on the nature of the business. Invoices, receipts, and other documents become effective after being stamped with the company seal and signed by the responsible person.

Q: Can all shareholders of my Hong Kong company be mainland residents if the purpose is to set up a foreign joint venture or wholly-owned enterprise in mainland China?

A: Generally, a Hong Kong company can consist entirely of mainland shareholders. However, if you want to set up a foreign joint venture or wholly-owned enterprise, the Ministry of Foreign Trade and Economic Cooperation usually invites at least one overseas shareholder to sign the documents. Therefore, it is necessary to have an overseas shareholder in the company.

Q: Can our newly established Hong Kong company directly set up a foreign joint venture or wholly-owned enterprise in mainland China?

A: Generally, foreign investors coming to China must have been established for more than one year. Specific situations depend on local policies.

Q: Can a company account be set up in a domestic foreign bank if registered in Hong Kong, such as a branch of a Hong Kong bank in Shenzhen?

A: Yes. After the company is established, the company directors can open an offshore account at a domestic foreign bank using their mainland ID cards. However, the primary condition is that the directors must personally visit the bank to open the account.

Q: How are various daily tasks such as tax filing for Hong Kong companies handled? Can we entrust you to handle them? What are the annual fees?

A: In Hong Kong taxation, taxes are filed once a year. The specific charges depend on your company's actual situation. If you choose our business secretary service, tax filing is free. However, accounting and auditing require payment, and the fees are determined by the turnover and the size of the invoices. If the company has no business transactions, no such fees are incurred.

Q: How is the tax rate for Hong Kong companies calculated? What is the specific tax filing time?

A: Hong Kong's tax rate is calculated at 17.5% of actual profits. Hong Kong companies usually use March 31st as the fiscal year-end, and you can file taxes up to one year later.

Q: What do the terms "CORP" and "BODY CORPORATE" mean on the business registration certificate?

A: The term "CORP" in the business nature section means there is no limitation on a specific business activity; any business activity allowed under the Companies Ordinance can be conducted. Of course, you can also specify electronic, chemical, or trade as your business scope in this section. As for "BODY CORPORATE," it refers to a corporate entity, representing its legal status.

Q: How do I rent an office if needed?

A: Depending on different client requirements, we offer company incubation, desk rental, office rental, and building suite rental. Just let us know your needs, and we will promptly find suitable office space for you.

Q: Can I check the background information of a Hong Kong limited company online?

A: Currently, it is not possible. Anyone with questions about a company can visit the Hong Kong Companies Registry at Hong Kong's Golden Bell Road for inquiries.

Q: After establishing a publishing house, how can I legally publish books?

A: After setting up a publishing house, you already have the qualifications to handle book publication matters. However, there are two additional steps: applying for an International Standard Book Number (ISBN) or International Standard Serial Number (ISSN). Additionally, you must apply for publishing registration before publishing. Completing these two steps allows you to legally publish books. If you wish to distribute in mainland China, you can apply through the Hong Kong publisher's qualifications.

Q: What do ISBN and ISSN mean? What are the differences between ISBN and ISSN?

A: ISBN stands for International Standard Book Number, and ISSN stands for International Standard Serial Number. Having these two numbers indicates that our publications meet international standards and have been registered with the ISBN and ISSN international institutions. You can query the background information of the publication at any time through this number. The fundamental difference between ISBN and ISSN lies in the objects they are issued to: the former is issued to book publishers, while the latter is issued to magazine publishers.

Q: If business is not good, and I apply for the company to suspend operations, how much must I pay annually?

A: A Hong Kong company can apply for temporary suspension at any time. Besides paying the annual report fee to the Companies Registry and the annual review fee to the Tax Department, no other fees are required. If the market improves, the company can resume operations at any time. This measure can save many costs.

Q: Can my Hong Kong company be dissolved?

A: Yes. If your company is no longer in use and you do not cancel it, the Hong Kong government will assume that your company continues to exist and engages in illegal operations. The government will impose cumulative fines on illegally operating companies according to Hong Kong law, and in severe cases, the board of directors may face criminal penalties. Since dissolving a company also incurs fees, we recommend the best solution is to sell the company.
