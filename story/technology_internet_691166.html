[Qingdao Auto] The key points of consumer rights protection for new and used cars are different

by qssqwe215 on 2012-03-05 14:38:15

For many consumers who are preparing to buy a new car, the car purchase contract provided by the merchant seems very complete when viewed from both sides, but behind these seemingly rigorous contracts, there are many pitfalls that are hard to guard against. Perhaps a difference of one word can make a world of difference in the outcome of rights protection. Consumers buying second-hand cars also reported that buying a second-hand car brought endless troubles, and the money spent almost matched the cost of buying a car.

The stakes are high, and a contract cannot be underestimated.

In August last year, Mr. Wang bought a new car at a 4S store. At that time, the salesperson said they would give away window film, and the words "free window film" were also written on the car purchase contract. However, after using the vehicle for a week, Mr. Wang found that the film was only attached to the two side windows of the new car. After negotiating with the 4S store, the salesperson inside indicated that although the purchase contract indeed wrote "window film", it did not specify whether the front windshield needed to be covered, and insisted that attaching the film does not include the front windshield. This behavior is an example of the dealer switching concepts.

The importance of a car purchase contract goes without saying, and some merchants sometimes manipulate the contract to gain more benefits or avoid responsibility. Consumers must be extremely cautious when signing a car purchase contract with the merchant.

Look at the contract before signing - this is the most basic point. However, many people think that "the contract will definitely be reviewed before signing". In reality, in many car purchasing behaviors, potential car owners directly sign the contract after verbally discussing and agreeing with the dealer, and assume that the details have been included in the contract. Reviewing the contract is merely skimming through it without carefully checking the terms. Moreover, due to the large number of contract clauses, some content is vaguely summarized, making it impossible to notice during a general skim. After the owner signs, they discover they've suffered a silent loss when conflicts arise. Therefore, the contract terms must be thoroughly studied, and the detailed parts must be rigorously written into the contract.

The delivery time must be constrained in the contract. Some dealers often leave the "delivery time" blank when signing the contract, which is very disadvantageous for consumers. If the dealer does not deliver the car for a long time, there is no penalty policy for the dealer. For consumers, waiting for a long time without receiving any compensation is problematic. Besides specifying the delivery time, it is necessary to clearly note the liability for breach of contract to constrain the merchant. For instance, if the car cannot be delivered on time, the merchant should compensate the consumer in the manner of "returning the deposit and compensating one-to-one".

Vehicle information is relatively easy to be "switched". When signing the contract, consumers need to carefully check the vehicle information, including brand, car identification number, engine number, car code (chassis number), etc. They also need to verify the main configurations, color (specific to seats), manual or automatic transmission, and documents delivered with the car, to prevent difficulties in protecting their rights if problems occur with the vehicle components later.

When involving projects handled by the dealer, consumers need to pay special attention when signing the contract, especially clarifying the price. For example, a simple item like "registration fee" may also include sub-items such as fuel fees and tolls, which need to be listed one by one to avoid having no evidence to rely on in the future.

Particular attention needs to be paid to the details of the free items. Salespeople often give extra gifts or discounts to attract customers, a common situation. However, few people know that when signing the contract, these free items should also be included, and the name, quantity, price range, etc., should be specified. It's important to clearly specify the deadline and liability for breach of contract, rather than just verbal agreements.

When the contract involves vehicle quality issues, it often only uses "new cars follow the manufacturer's standards" to pass over. Once consumers find out that the purchased car is a stock car or a refurbished car, it's difficult to protect their rights. Therefore, when signing the contract, it should be noted that "this car shall not be a refurbished car or a stock car," and it should be agreed that if it is found to be a refurbished car, a stock car, or has collision marks in the future, the buyer has the right to return the car. Additionally, if problems are found upon delivery, they should be noted on the contract or rejected on the spot to avoid future trouble.

Rights protection is not difficult, but second-hand cars need comprehensive inspection.

Recently, Mr. Lin, a citizen, bought a used Santana car. During the transfer process, he was informed that the car had multiple speeding and red-light violations records, and fines of more than 4000 yuan had to be paid to handle the related procedures. Since the contract did not involve whether the car had traffic violation records at the time of purchase, and the seller was nowhere to be found, Mr. Lin could only accept his misfortune.

The second-hand car market is inherently complex with a lower insurance coefficient, but this does not mean that the rights of second-hand car buyers are not protected. If infringement occurs, rights protection can still be actively pursued. To avoid post-purchase troubles, it's best to check all items as much as possible before buying. Here, the reporter summarizes several common issues in second-hand car transactions.

Incomplete vehicle procedures. Complete procedures for a second-hand car should include: vehicle registration certificate (or original purchase invoice), driving license, vehicle purchase tax payment certificate, road maintenance fee procedures, and compulsory insurance, etc. If the vehicle registration certificate or driving license is missing and not promptly reissued, the car may be a "black car." If there are outstanding fees for compulsory insurance or road maintenance fees, the buyer will incur significant additional expenses. During second-hand car transactions, if the seller fails to produce all the procedures for any reason, consumers should raise their vigilance at this point.

Many consumers judge the condition of a second-hand car based on the mileage gauge when selecting one. Sellers often take advantage of this psychology of buyers and tamper with the mileage gauge. The mileage gauge of a second-hand car can only serve as a reference. Based on the previous owner's purchase time or the factory release time, one can determine if the mileage data is normal. If the mileage is significantly lower than normal usage.

Many consumers focus too much on the "heart" of the car while neglecting the health of the body when buying a car. Sellers precisely exploit this psychology of consumers, rejuvenating the vehicle's "heart" by replacing engine parts to inflate the price. If the old car has been repainted and there are repair marks around the front face, rear end, and A, B, C pillars, the car has likely been involved in a traffic accident and may have "external injuries."

In the second-hand car market, Fukan, Jetta, and Santana are the most favored by consumers, but care must be taken to avoid "commercial-to-private" vehicles mixing in. So-called "commercial-to-private" vehicles refer to cars previously used for rental operations that enter the second-hand car market through illegal channels, masquerading as non-commercial vehicles. These cars usually look newer, have shorter usage periods, and offer very tempting prices.

There are many unsold second-hand cars in the market with unprocessed traffic violation records. Once such cars are purchased, consumers will have to "take responsibility" for the previous owner's traffic violations. In the above example, Mr. Lin encountered such a situation. Some people think that if the second-hand car is very cheap, spending an extra 4000 yuan can be acceptable. However, if encountering higher amounts for violation records, it could be a significant loss. Therefore, when buying a second-hand car, besides paying attention to the completeness of the contract and procedures, it's best to ask knowledgeable friends to help check.
