Professor Helmut Liang discusses ecological science writing and publication (Fushan Botanical Garden, Taiwan, August 2007):

**Question from student:** Based on your experience, if you receive a manuscript that you immediately reject upon reading, what are the typical mistakes in these manuscripts?

**Professor He's Answer:** It depends on the journal. For example, *Ecology Letters*, with an impact factor of 7.6, has a rejection rate of 60%. Only 40% of the submitted manuscripts are sent out for review, and half of those are still rejected, leaving only about 10-15% of submissions to be accepted. So, the rejection rate is quite high for this journal. If your paper is rejected by *Ecology Letters*, don't feel bad. It's not necessarily because your research is poor; it's simply that the journal can only publish a limited number of articles each year. The rejection rate for *Ecology* is also high, with 40-50% of submissions not even sent out for peer review. As for *Plant Ecology*, it's a more general journal where most papers are sent out for review.

However, some manuscripts are so poorly written that they give reviewers headaches. In such cases, the authors themselves seem unclear about what they're trying to say. These manuscripts will definitely not be sent out for review. While it's hard to predict exactly which papers will be accepted or rejected, there is one basic rule: the fundamental purpose of scientific research is to "advance knowledge." Your contribution might be local knowledge with local impact, or it could have global impact—this doesn't matter as long as the paper tells a "complete story." This story can be big or small, but it must be coherent and self-contained. First and foremost, you need to ask yourself: does your work provide new knowledge, new ideas, or something novel to the readers? This is very important!

Everyone knows that the most critical part of writing a paper is the **Introduction**—it’s the soul of the paper. If the Introduction is poorly written, even if the rest of the work is good, you've already put yourself at a disadvantage. Your paper may not be rejected outright, but it certainly won’t be favored. Therefore, the Introduction must be well-written. Even now, when I write papers, I sometimes spend a whole week just crafting the Introduction, carefully scrutinizing every word and sentence. There must be a reason for every sentence you write, and logical flow is crucial.

From a scientific perspective, when writing a paper, try to **formulate a hypothesis** as clearly as possible. State explicitly that your research aims to test this hypothesis. Doing so places your paper in a favorable position. Even if your actual study is descriptive (e.g., describing a phenomenon), it’s better to reframe it as testing a hypothesis. This approach gives your paper an edge, though it doesn’t guarantee acceptance. Reviewers and editors prefer this kind of framing. While your primary goal might be descriptive, try to incorporate hypotheses and make it interesting. Even if your main goal is description, you should always be able to identify one or two points to test. For instance, if your paper merely describes vegetation composition, distribution, etc., that alone won’t work! Few journals are willing to publish purely descriptive papers.

Additionally, your paper should have a great question, a solid idea, robust data, and, ideally, sophisticated analysis methods. For example, if you perform a simple regression, a traditional regression won’t excite many people. However, adding spatial components or using newly developed analytical methods can significantly enhance the quality and competitiveness of your paper.

Another tip: **Don't write a big paper. Write a small paper.** Focus on one specific problem and explain it thoroughly—make it a complete story. Let me give you an example from my own experience. I co-authored two papers: one published in *Ecology Letters* and another in *American Naturalist* (in press). Originally, these were part of the same paper submitted to *Ecology Letters*. It was reviewed, but one reviewer, who had a background in physics, misunderstood a formula derivation in our paper and insisted it was incorrect (though it was actually correct). Due to this misunderstanding, *Ecology Letters* rejected our paper. Later, we split the paper into two smaller ones: one resubmitted to *Ecology Letters*, which was quickly accepted, and the other to *American Naturalist*. So, instead of tackling a huge question, focus on making a complete story. Even though the paper we sent to *Ecology Letters* was relatively short (five journal pages), it was indeed a complete and interesting story. Therefore, avoid writing overly broad papers with too many ideas or points. A single paper should have just one interesting point and build a complete story around it. Trying to include too much often backfires.
