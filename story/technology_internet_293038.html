If a teacher hits a student, what should the student do?

by yang5230 on 2009-12-04 17:34:31

When the teacher hits the student, what should the student do? (Subtly revealed, Original author: Liu Fu) The original title comes from a metaphor by an old teacher, Maomao. This silver-haired old professor, when talking about learning from the West, used this vivid example. He said that since ancient times, countless Chinese people have firmly believed in learning from the West, which is equivalent to regarding the West as a teacher. However, after the Opium War, this Western teacher repeatedly beat the Chinese student, and each time it was a severe beating. So, as a student, should China still learn from the West?

This elderly professor explained his metaphor in the following way: when a teacher hits a student, it shows that the teacher has some bad mistakes, but this does not hinder learning the good qualities and real skills from the teacher.

From my perspective, I have very strong opposing views on this analogy and its interpretation given by the elementary school teacher. But I must point out one detail to avoid misunderstanding of this metaphor.

It can sometimes be reasonable for a teacher to hit a student while teaching. For example, a math teacher may be temperamental and often beats students, but this does not mean the teacher's knowledge of mathematics is wrong; math should still be learned. However, what if hitting and teaching cannot be separated? For instance, the content of the teacher's lessons is about hitting others, acting like a thug, or committing crimes, with students being used as "experimental" objects. In such cases, whether to learn or not becomes a serious question.

Looking at the history of the modern Western world, there are two types of "teacher-student" relationships between the West and "backward" nations and ethnic groups. Japan’s path to modernization prominently features both types. On one hand, Japan indeed learned much knowledge, systems, and thoughts from the West. On the other hand, Japan also learned the "hitting" style of criminal behavior from Western teachers. Moreover, we see that the rise of every major Western power was accompanied by bloodshed and death, wars and gunpowder smoke. Behind every newly risen major power lay the corpses of others. Therefore, we must seriously ask: Can we learn only knowledge from the Western teacher and not their violent tendencies, and still achieve development similar to the West?

The path of global modernization underwent significant changes after the Second World War. One key aspect of this change was the attitude towards war. After the First World War, all rising powers held the same view on war: the law of the jungle where the strong dominate the weak. Using war to destroy others was considered natural; plundering and looting by the victorious side from the defeated was inevitable, without any sense of shame. This war mentality originated from nomadic culture and matched the "defense" and "welcoming" war mentality of industrial civilization.

This war mentality was most concentratedly displayed after the First World War. The victors imposed extremely harsh reparations on the defeated. When President Wilson of the United States saw the draft of the Treaty of Versailles, he said: If I were the German president, I would absolutely refuse to sign such a treaty. The British Prime Minister privately said after the signing of the Treaty of Versailles: This treaty has only two outcomes: either it will be broken, or it will lead to another war. It is merely a 20-year armistice agreement. In 1939, Germany, one of the defeated countries in the First World War, attacked Poland with a "blitzkrieg," marking the beginning of the Second World War, seeking revenge on the whole world. It had been exactly 20 years and 2 months and 3 days since Germany signed the Treaty of Versailles. We cannot help but admire the prescience of the leaders of the victorious side 20 years ago, but we also wonder: if they knew it would lead to war, why didn't they eliminate the hidden dangers 20 years ago? Instead, they planted a precise timed bomb.

Japan's modernization steps came relatively late among Asian countries. Japan learned and applied the old rules of Western warfare and gained enormous benefits from them. The main source of these benefits was China, the defeated party in the Sino-Japanese War, and Russia, the defeated party in the Russo-Japanese War. If the world continued to follow these old war rules, then after the Second World War, as the victorious side, China would have had ample reason to demand Japan be impoverished and make several generations pay for the war. However, after the end of the Second World War, the war rules completely changed. To a certain extent, Japan became the biggest beneficiary of the subtle transformation of the old war rules: Japan lost wars and still had to pay huge reparations, but Japan won wars and did not need to pay reparations. Such "good fortune" was unparalleled, making Japan the last victim of the brutal jungle rules of war.

For foreign countries, the greatest failure lies in: we cannot participate in setting the rules, yet we must accept the rules set by others. It is an unreasonable world of bandits.

The change in war rules after World War II had many reasons, but one of the main reasons was to prevent the disintegration of the defeated party leading to further vengeance. The process of modernization in Europe, up to this point, began to show a bit of true cultural advancement. However, this culture will not make up for past brutality. What was previously stolen remains stolen, and it will not be returned just because of current "civilization." Those who failed to steal in the past could only accept bad luck, and it will be difficult to steal in the future. Those who were robbed have no place to appeal for justice, and there will be no more opportunities to rob others. In this sense, even if the teacher hits the student, the student cannot learn, let alone choose not to learn.

So, returning to the question at the beginning: Can we face the Western teacher, learn only knowledge, and not violence, and still achieve development similar to the West? Unfortunately, there is no answer. Because there is no Western great power that developed without using violence. Thus, the idealized beautiful question's answer can also be said to be: no precedent, historically unprecedented.

In the Western world, we cannot find examples of development without using violence. Then, outside the West? In Africa and Latin America, we cannot find them either. Some of these countries copied the U.S. Constitution verbatim for their own constitutions, yet they still struggle in poverty with no hope in sight. In Asia? We see Singapore and other "dragons" seemingly developing by learning only knowledge and not violence. However, first, they are very small, Singapore is even smaller than Shanghai. Second, a big crocodile standing outside the mother's room, making a few phone calls, tapping on the keyboard, using financial weapons, caused a financial crisis that quickly wiped out decades of wealth accumulated by the "dragons." To save their own gardens, South Koreans handed over their gold and silver to the state! Without China, it is unknown how long it would take for the "dragons" to recover. Therefore, the "dragons" of Asia are also hard to become models for China.

Therefore, hoping that China learns only knowledge from the West and not violence to develop modernization can only explore China's own methods and seek a Chinese-style development path. Returning to the metaphor of the old teacher, the simple answer is: don't blindly copy the teacher, nor can you entirely reject the teacher. You can only base it on your own reality and be a smart student. Parroting the teacher without understanding, lacking creativity, will surely result in a failing student.
