Why Tencent and Chery are fighting for the QQ trademark: neither can afford to lose

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 14:55:12

Does Tencent produce cars? The answer is negative, but as early as 2005, the company applied to register the "QQ" trademark on goods such as automobiles in Class 12, and it was approved in 2008. However, this move made Chery Automobile Company, which produces cars, dissatisfied. In 2009, Chery Company requested the Trademark Review and Adjudication Board to revoke Tencent's automobile-related trademarks on grounds of identical or similar trademarks for similar goods, and they received support. With its trademark revoked, Tencent sued the Trademark Review and Adjudication Board of the State Administration for Industry and Commerce. Yesterday, the First Intermediate People's Court held a hearing on the case, with Tencent as the plaintiff, the Trademark Review and Adjudication Board as the defendant, and Chery Company as the third party.

As everyone knows, in China, two companies are associated with "QQ": one is Tencent Technology, and the other is Chery Automobile; both belong to different fields. Tencent has secured a place in the Internet sector, while Chery's QQ car sales have reached over a million units cumulatively. Looking at the timeline of using "QQ", Tencent launched the instant messaging software QQ in 1999, while Chery introduced the microcar Chery QQ in 2003. The issue of who should own "QQ" has entangled these two companies for nearly 10 years.

On May 19, 2005, Tencent applied to register the "QQ" trademark for goods such as automobiles in Class 12, and it was officially registered on March 7, 2008. On November 26, 2009, Chery argued that the trademark violated the Trademark Law by replicating and imitating well-known trademarks, having identical or similar trademarks for similar goods, and infringing on others' prior rights, thus requesting the Trademark Review and Adjudication Board to cancel the disputed trademark. After review, the board believed that the registration of the disputed trademark constituted the preemptive registration of another party’s already used and influential trademark under Article 31 of the Trademark Law, and therefore revoked Tencent's registered trademark.

Tencent argued that the decision made by the Trademark Review and Adjudication Board contained obvious errors and omissions. The disputed trademark did not violate legal provisions and should be maintained. Their representative explained that on August 31, 2001, Tencent had already applied to register the "QQ" trademark for services such as "information transmission, computer terminal communication" in Class 38. Through extensive promotion, the number of registered users of QQ communication services grew from 5 million in 2000 to over 200 million in 2003. Therefore, before Chery Automobile used the "QQ" trademark, Tencent's "QQ" trademark had already become a well-known trademark. In 2009, Tencent had obtained a certificate of recognition for a well-known trademark from relevant national departments, and any other company using the "QQ" trademark would constitute an infringement.

Chery Automobile, however, believed that Tencent's registration of the "QQ" trademark in the automobile field was malicious. As the third party in this case, Chery's representative pointed out that the trademark registered by Tencent in 2001 was not the "QQ" letters but a graphic. Meanwhile, Chery Automobile registered the "QQ" letter trademark in the automobile field in 2003. Thus, the two companies were using their respective trademarks in different fields. Chery's "QQ" trademark in the automobile field preceded Tencent's, and Chery QQ has sold over a million units with advertising costs exceeding 200 million yuan, so it should be protected.

Furthermore, Tencent believed that its registration of the "QQ" trademark in multiple fields was a protective measure for "QQ". However, Chery Automobile claimed that from the initial planning of the car name to later promotional activities, everything was based on the already famous "QQ" trademark and penguin mascot to achieve publicity effects. This kind of infringement could not generate legitimate prior rights.

The court will announce the verdict at a later date. For both Tencent and Chery, having operated the "QQ" trademark for nearly 10 years, this lawsuit is one neither can afford to lose.

Is there a third way in the QQ dispute?

Tencent QQ is the first instant messaging software launched by Tencent in 1999, while Chery QQ is a microcar introduced by Chery in July 2003. Both "QQ"s enjoy high recognition in their respective fields.

According to reports, as early as when the "QQ" car was launched in 2003, Tencent had raised objections regarding trademark infringement against Chery. Tencent stated that due to being busy with its listing at the time, it did not file a public lawsuit. Later, Tencent sent a lawyer's letter to Chery, but Chery never provided Tencent with any substantive response.

In late 2005, media reports emerged about both parties going to court over the QQ trademark, but no further developments were seen in this case, with some media even calling it hype.

This time, the trademark dispute between the two parties was presented in court with Tencent as the plaintiff, the Trademark Review and Adjudication Board as the defendant, and Chery as the third party. Trademarks are the banners for enterprises to stand in the market. For Tencent and Chery, who have cultivated their brands for around ten years, neither can afford to lose. Some believe that whoever is determined to infringe in this case will face enormous compensation. Is there only victory or defeat in this trademark dispute, or is there a third way? Tang Guangliang, an intellectual property expert from the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences, told reporters that Tencent and Chery could fully collaborate to achieve a win-win situation.

Tang Guangliang said that the role of a trademark is to attract consumer attention, serving as a link between merchants and consumers, and also as the image of merchants in the minds of consumers. A trademark itself is a means rather than an end, and how to use this means is something each merchant must consider. In recent years, China's trademark protection has become increasingly mature. Whether there is infringement does not solely depend on whether two marks are similar; the key is whether consumers would be confused. If there is no confusion, then the use by two or more merchants can expand the influence of the trademark, benefiting all users.

"For example, if 'consumers buy Chery QQ cars because they think the car is produced by Tencent,' or 'consumers use QQ chat tools because they like Chery QQ cars,' then there is confusion," Tang Guangliang said. If the above situations do not occur, both parties have no need to compete. Because when it comes to trademarks, it is not who says it belongs to them that matters, and even if it does belong to them, they cannot eliminate every single usage. As long as the trademark is not maliciously tarnished or the enterprise's market is not preempted, it might even have a promoting effect, allowing both parties to coexist peacefully and share benefits.

Moreover, Tang Guangliang said that from a rational market perspective, Tencent and Chery have no conflict. Chatting tools mainly target young people, and QQ cars are also popular among young people. Both sides can fully collaborate, operate mutually, share the market, and achieve a win-win situation without the need to fight over trademark ownership in the absence of damage.
