2. By Warren Esty's critical test, "Estimating the Size of a Coinage: A Survey and Comparison of Methods in Numismatic Chronicle". 3. Lee, Jonathan and Ziman, "Pictish Symbols", pp. 11-13.

4. The best discussion, even now, is Watson in WJ Communications, Celtic Place-names of Scotland, the History of Scottish Names, Archaeological Rhind Lectures (expanded) 1916 (Edinburgh 1926), but see now more simply by Katherine Forsyth, The Language of Pictland: Non-Indo-European Picts, Studia Hameliana 2 (Utrecht 1997), online despite this terrible infringement warning, cases pp. 24-25, who Simon Taylor thinks these names are actually post-Gaelic. This may be clearer in Taylor's paper that she cites, but I will take some brain to accommodate while bending. Where does PS come from? Why do they seem to fit secondary stones and long cist burials centered east if they came from the west? And so on.

Explanation, see Forsyth, The Language of Pictland pp. 31-36 and PLL. 1-4.

6. Bede, Ecclesiastical History of the English People, I.1; Adomnán, Vita Columbae, I.26. Regarding the latter, see David N. Dumville, "Primarius Cohortis in Adomnán's Life of Columba" in Scottish Gaelic Studies Vol. 13 (Glasgow 1978), pp. 130-131; it is important to emphasize that Adomnán did not say the nameless man to whom Columba could not speak was a PICT.
