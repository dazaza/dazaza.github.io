History of the Nike Brand

by naidilishoes on 2012-03-07 15:14:14

In 1962, a business school graduate student named Phil Knight inadvertently heard personnel from the *Oregon Journal* debating whether Japanese Nikon cameras could one day replace the expensive German-made Leica cameras. This sparked an idea in him, and he wrote a paper. As a middle-distance runner at the University of Oregon, Phil Knight believed that if Japanese manufacturers, who were skilled at producing low-cost goods, could make quality running shoes, they could leverage this price advantage to open up a new market and compete with European shoemakers such as Adidas and Puma. Even though Knight worked as an accountant for a company after graduation, in the summer of 1963, he still made a trip to Japan and met with managers of Onitsuka Company, which produced Onitsuka Tiger athletic shoes. When he saw these cheap, lightweight sneakers that matched the quality of European brands, Knight was immediately inspired. He took some samples back home and showed them to his classmate and also a sports coach, Bill Bowerman. These samples left a deep impression on the legendary Oregon coach, who then agreed to invest $500 and become a partner. The two later signed a contract with Onitsuka Company to purchase 1,000 pairs of Tiger shoes under the name "Blue Ribbon Sports" (BRS). They transported the shoes in the trunks of their cars and sold them at sports events. In just a year, Knight sold shoes worth $8,000. He subsequently quit his job and imported more shoes. Knight hired several salespeople, all of whom were fellow runners, and BRS gave them this opportunity to earn decent income while enjoying the sport.

In 1966, Bowerman, who had been experimenting with various methods to create lighter shoes, sent a pair of shoes he designed to Onitsuka Company. The shoes featured soft nylon instead of traditional leather uppers. This shoe was called Cortez. Although initially mocked and ridiculed by Adidas sales representatives, it became wildly popular in track and field competitions and was the best-selling model under the Tiger brand. By 1969, BRS had already sold over $1 million worth of shoes. However, due to long-term over-reliance on Onitsuka Company, they began to worry about potential changes in their relationship. This concern eventually came true when the Japanese side started looking for larger wholesalers and threatened to terminate the partnership. Knight was forced to choose: either sell his shares and face legal issues, or find another way to produce shoes. Therefore, with a few employees who had already begun developing a different production line, Knight believed it was time to go solo and accept a greater challenge. Due to breach of contract, the partnership with Onitsuka Company was severed, and he found a factory in Exeter and partnered with the Japanese trading company Nissho Iwai (which provided credit financing) to start selling shoes under a new name and trademark. Inspired by a dream of an employee named Johnson featuring the Greek goddess of victory, they chose the name "Nike" (the ancient Greek goddess of victory is named Nike). A local female student, Caroline Davidson, charged only $35 to design a fat tick symbol, which became Nike's new logo. Initially unpopular because it lacked the arch-supporting function of the stripes on Adidas shoes or the circular support features of the Puma logo, serving purely as decoration, the "Swoosh" (referring to the sound of "flying past") nickname of Nike would eventually become one of the most recognized symbols worldwide and played a pivotal role in the success of Nike.
