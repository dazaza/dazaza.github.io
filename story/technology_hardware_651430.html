Recognizing and Protecting Forests

Forests are crucially important to humans, mainly manifested in the following ways: forests provide oxygen for people and animals to breathe, absorbing carbon dioxide emitted from industry and daily life; forests regulate surface runoff, conserve water sources, and prevent soil erosion; forests reduce wind speed, adsorb dust, and absorb toxic gases like sulfur compounds; urban green belts neutralize noise and reduce noise pollution. Forests are the most active areas of life on Earth and are important regions for protecting biodiversity. However, forests are disappearing rapidly. If we lose forests, the Earth's ecosystem will collapse, and humans will be unable to survive. Our country currently has 134 million hectares of forest, ranking fifth in the world, but the forest coverage rate is only 14%, much lower than the global average of 27%, ranking 104th in the world, making our country one of the nations with scarce forest resources. Additionally, the quality of our country's forests is not high, with a large proportion of young and middle-aged trees, accounting for about 71% of the national forest farm area, and the proportion of young and middle-aged trees in artificial forests reaching as high as 87%; forest resources are unevenly distributed, with more forests in southwest, southeast, and northeast China, and fewer in northwest and north China; forest resources are severely damaged, with illegal logging still occurring despite repeated bans; forest disasters occur frequently, such as insect pests and sandstorms. We cannot live without forests. Let's realize the severity of the situation and start protecting our forests by reusing paper and refusing to use disposable chopsticks. Not using furniture made from precious woods

Currently, there is a prevailing trend of blind competition and pursuit of luxury in consumption. The idea of "rarity determines value" makes people willing to spend high prices to buy and use furniture made from precious woods. However, this distorted consumption concept is causing severe damage to nature. Take rosewood as an example. Rosewood is a valuable wood produced in tropical rainforests, with its price increasing year by year. A pair of rosewood chopsticks costs over a hundred yuan, and a set of rosewood furniture can cost tens of thousands to millions of yuan, yet people still buy them. Our country strictly prohibits the cutting down of rosewood, so all our rosewood furniture comes from foreign markets. However, the Earth's ecosystem is a whole, and the deforestation of tropical rainforests in any region destroys the habitat of animals, leading to overall ecological imbalance. Moreover, precious woods come from rare tree species, which are irreplaceable natural heritages. Ten thousand years ago, about half of the Earth's land area was covered by forests, approximately 6.2 billion hectares, and now only 2.8 billion hectares remain. Globally, tropical rainforests are decreasing at a rate of 17 million hectares per year, and it won't take many years for the world's tropical rainforest resources to be completely destroyed. Rainforests are the "lungs of the Earth," and losing the lungs would be unimaginable for the Earth. Protecting rainforests and rare tree species starts with refusing to consume products made from precious woods.

Saving Paper and Recycling Waste Paper

In newspapers and on TV, we often see reports about paper mills polluting the environment. Large amounts of wastewater turn water bodies black and smelly, eliminating fish and shrimp, and reducing or completely wiping out crops on nearby farmland. Most paper production uses wood as raw material, and the source of wood—forests—are the foundation of our survival, the "lungs of the Earth." Additionally, papermaking causes environmental pollution, so saving paper is equivalent to protecting our living space. In our busy daily study and work, if we pay attention to the waste paper we're about to throw away, perhaps the other side could still be used. Even waste paper without blank spaces shouldn't be carelessly thrown into the trash bin. Recycling one ton of waste paper can save 17 large trees, produce 800 kilograms of good paper, reduce water pollution by 35%, and save more than half of the energy used in papermaking. The recycling rate of waste paper in our country is very low, and we import waste paper every year. In just 1996, we imported 1.37 million tons. We can collect old newspapers, old textbooks, scraps of paper, etc., from schools and homes and send them to recycling stations. When purchasing paper, choose recycled paper and support the recycling of waste paper through practical actions. We can also inform classmates and parents about the benefits of recycling waste paper, encouraging everyone to recycle it.

Rejecting Excessive Packaging

In daily life, the food or items we buy often come with two or three layers of packaging, sometimes even four or five layers. Investigations in 1997 showed that the annual per capita packaging weight in our country was 10 kg, resulting in nearly 10 million tons of packaging waste annually, and this number is sharply increasing with economic growth. Producing packaging consumes a large amount of metal, glass, paper, and plastic, and these packaging materials become garbage after single-use. In the United States, food packaging waste accounts for half of household garbage, and the expenditure on packaging equals the pure income of farmers. Unfortunately, the recycling rate of waste in our country is very low. The recycling rate of paper in the European Community is 26%, while in our country, it is only 15%; the recycling rate of plastics in Japan is 26%, while in our country, it is only 9.6%; the global average recycling rate of aluminum cans is 50%, while in our country, it is only 1%. This not only causes enormous waste but also leads to serious pollution. Completely rejecting packaging is impossible, but we can avoid multiple layers of packaging, excessive and overly luxurious packaging, and not buy foods or items with extravagant packaging. In some countries, excessively packaged goods are considered outdated and face consumer resistance, while in our surroundings, resisting excessive packaging requires the joint efforts of everyone.

Rejecting Single-Use Items

In modern social life, the abandonment and arbitrary disposal of goods are widespread, especially the sharp increase in the use of single-use items. Statistics show that British people discard 2.5 billion diapers annually; Japanese people use 30 million "disposable" cameras each year; Japanese companies give away millions of batteries containing cadmium and mercury for free; besides disposable pens, Americans discard 1.83 billion razors, 2.7 billion batteries, 1.4 billion cubic meters of polystyrene plastic used for packaging, 3.5 billion paint cans annually, along with enough paper and plastic products to supply a monthly picnic for the entire world population. Although single-use items meet certain special needs to some extent, they also waste a large amount of energy and resources, create a lot of garbage, increase the burden on the environment, and even cause environmental pollution. Our country, with a population of over a billion, if we promote the use of single-use items, the consumption volume will be enormous, not only wasting resources but also bringing unforeseeable disasters from the generated waste.

Using both sides of paper

Some so-called "post-industrial society" prophets once predicted that the use of computers would consign paper to historical archives. However, the reality is quite the opposite; the annual consumption of writing and printing paper in the United States increased from 7 million tons in 1956 to 22 million tons in 1986, and the usage continues to grow. Of course, paper is recyclable, but the process of regenerating waste paper also produces a significant amount of harmful waste. Printing paper must go through a deinking process to be recycled, producing two products: fibers without ink that can be used for papermaking and a large amount of sludge. Regenerated paper changes as it goes through various uses, from high-quality adhesive paper to newsprint to corrugated cardboard, and finally, as compost or fuel within the same production facility. During this process, the physical properties of the paper degrade. We know that extending the lifespan of items can slow down the flow of resources. Doubling the lifespan of a product is equivalent to halving the waste. Using both sides of paper reduces paper usage by half, thereby also reducing waste paper output by half.

Not burning wasteland outdoors

When walking on the street, troubled by the polluted sky and suffocating air, you may certainly lament: our atmospheric quality is getting worse. The government has taken great measures to treat the atmosphere, starting from treating car exhaust, residential coal smoke, industrial emissions, among which one measure is to ban outdoor burning. We have entered a civilized society and should no longer stay in the primitive slash-and-burn farming methods. Outdoor burning produces a large amount of dust particles and carbon dioxide, carbon monoxide, reducing atmospheric visibility and harming people's health. Moreover, grass and straw are important biological resources and should return naturally to nature. Burning them is a huge waste. Furthermore, every year, there are reports of fires caused by outdoor burning, destroying forests and grasslands. Some people burn wasteland in forests, killing trees and damaging the habitats of birds, wild animals, and insects, disrupting local ecological balance. We should advocate composting weeds and leaves instead of burning them.

Not dumping garbage into rivers, lakes, and seas

Dumping garbage into rivers, lakes, and seas causes water pollution. The harm of water pollution is multifaceted. First, it directly affects the quality of water used in industry and agriculture. According to surveys, up to 1980, water pollution in our country caused direct economic losses of 37.7 billion yuan annually; secondly, it destroys the business environment, reduces fishery yields, and endangers human health. For instance, the world-famous Minamata disease in Japan was caused by mercury and cadmium pollution of water, a serious public hazard event; thirdly, water pollution further reduces usable water resources. Studies show that every cubic meter of sewage discharged pollutes 14 cubic meters of natural water. It is estimated that by the year 2000, the world's sewage discharge will reach 600 billion cubic meters. Without sewage treatment, a large amount of natural water bodies will be polluted, exacerbating the contradiction of insufficient water resources and possibly leading to global water shortages, etc. The causes of water pollution are multifaceted, but each of us can contribute to reducing this pollution if we pay attention.

Using more soap and less detergent

Soap is made from natural ingredients—fat plus alkali. After use, soap quickly decomposes through microbial action. Therefore, relatively speaking, soap has a minor impact on the environment during production and use. Compared to soap, detergents have a greater impact on the environment. The manufacturing process of synthetic detergents produces large amounts of wastewater and exhaust gas. Their use, especially phosphorus-containing detergents, adds another series of environmental pollution. Phosphates in phosphorus-containing detergents stimulate excessive algae growth. When algae die, their organic matter overloads the aquatic ecosystem, causing eutrophication problems. Water bodies polluted by phosphorus contain rich fertilizers for algae growth. Excessive algae growth causes oxygen depletion, leading to the death of fish and shrimp unable to compete with algae for oxygen. In phosphorus-polluted rivers, lakes, and seas, "dead zones" form. Therefore, to minimize environmental destruction as much as possible, we should all use more soap and less detergent.

Turning off taps promptly

In daily life, we often encounter dripping taps or taps left open after use. Many people think this doesn't waste much water. Let’s do a small experiment: using a measuring cylinder under a dripping tap, 200 milliliters of water can be collected in 15 minutes, which translates to nearly 20 liters per day and over 7,000 liters per year. With countless taps nationwide, the total amount is no small figure. Our freshwater resources are extremely limited. Although 71% of the Earth's surface is water, totaling about 1.385 billion cubic kilometers, 97.2% of it is undrinkable saltwater, 2% is polar and mountain ice and snow or difficult-to-utilize freshwater, leaving only one-thousandth of the global water volume truly available for human use. The global annual freshwater usage is continuously increasing. Eighty countries and regions, home to 40% of the world's population, are experiencing water shortages, and about 1 billion people lack access to clean drinking water. In developing countries, 10 million people die each year due to drinking unclean water. Such scarce freshwater resources are still being wasted and destroyed. Water is the source of life; cherishing water resources means cherishing humanity's future. Let’s start from what’s around us and turn off the tap after use, while washing hands, taking showers, applying soap, and washing dishes intermittently.

Not wearing clothing made from wild animal furs

Someone said, "Every piece of wild animal fur may be a murder case." This sounds terrifying, but upon careful consideration, it is not exaggerated. Because each wild animal only has one skin, how can "plotting with tigers for their skins" leave the tiger alive? Some people pursue so-called fashion (which is actually already outdated) but are cold and indifferent to wild lives. There is such a story: A girl's mother bought a fox fur coat, which triggered sad associations for her daughter because books say that vixens usually give birth to about 5-8 cubs. She drew a picture showing a group of pitiful little foxes opening their mouths and crying to the girl: "Your mother wore a fur coat and killed our mother!" Later, this painting was selected for an international children's environmental painting competition, and the organizing committee specially printed posters for it. On the poster were prominent words: "Your mother wore a fur coat, and more than 100 baby wild animals lost their mother!" It is easy to imagine how tragic the background is behind wearing clothes made from wild animal furs. Currently, many civilized countries worldwide have begun to resist fur clothing, a sign of awakening ecological moral awareness in humanity.

Promptly reporting behaviors that destroy the environment and ecology

Nowadays, many of us admire the beautiful environment of Western European countries, but they also went through periods of environmental destruction and pollution. Environmental awareness, laws, social ethics, and public supervision have formed good social customs, achieving today's environmental protection achievements. Alongside highways in the United States, you often see fallen big trees, occasionally even dead animals hit by cars, but absolutely no one takes them home, otherwise they will be prosecuted and punished. In Singapore, laws and public supervision constrain behaviors that destroy the environment. Our country has also established many laws and regulations regarding environmental protection, such as the "Environmental Protection Law," "Solid Waste Pollution Prevention and Control Law," "Noise Control Prevention and Treatment Law," etc., to punish bad behaviors that destroy the environment.
