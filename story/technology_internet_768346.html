The Genesis: Jobs said, "Let there be iPhone"

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 20:41:15

From Campbell, California to San Francisco is an 88-kilometer journey, which could be considered the most beautiful of all commutes. The majority of the route winds along the Junipero Serra Freeway, a grand and sparsely populated highway hugging the eastern side of the Santa Cruz Mountains. If you want to see tech tycoons testing the speed of their Ferraris, this is one of the best spots in Silicon Valley. However, in terms of mobile phone signals, it's one of the worst places. Therefore, for Andy Grignon on January 8, 2007, this was the perfect place to think deeply alone.

This wasn't Grignon's usual commute. He was once a senior engineer at Apple, located in Cupertino, a small town west of Campbell. His morning drive to work usually covered 11 kilometers and took exactly 15 minutes. But this day was different. He was heading to San Francisco, where at the Macworld trade show, he would witness his boss Steve Jobs make history. For years, Apple fans had begged Jobs to incorporate phone functions into their iPods so they wouldn't have to carry two devices in their pockets. Jobs was about to fulfill that wish. Grignon and some colleagues would stay in a hotel near the venue the night before the event. The next morning at ten o'clock, they would join the rest of the world in watching Jobs demonstrate the first iPhone.

However, as Grignon drove north, he didn't feel excitement. He felt fear. In Silicon Valley, most product demos are pre-recorded. People think: even a great product can fail if there are issues with internet or mobile network connections. But Jobs insisted on live networking. This was one of the reasons his product launches were so attractive. Part of his legend was that his product presentations almost never experienced noticeable glitches. But for those like Grignon working behind the scenes, this was one of their most stressful jobs.

Grignon was the senior manager responsible for all wireless signals on the iPhone, a crucial role. Although today's phones can help us do countless useful things, fundamentally, they are just fancy two-way radios. The components Grignon managed were what made a phone a phone. If the device couldn't make calls, connect to Bluetooth headphones, or access Wi-Fi, Grignon would be held accountable. As one of the earliest engineers on the iPhone project, he had devoted two and a half years of his life to it, often working seven days a week.

Grignon was part of the iPhone rehearsal team, first at Apple and later at the Moscone Center in San Francisco. He watched Jobs rehearse his 90-minute presentation multiple times, and hardly any session went without problems. Jobs had been rehearsing for five days, but until the last day, the iPhone still experienced dropped calls, lost its internet signal, crashed, and even shut down automatically.

Grignon said, "At first, being in the rehearsal room was pretty cool — it was like a sign of trust." Only a few carefully selected people were allowed to attend. "But soon, rehearsals became uncomfortable. I rarely saw Jobs lose control completely, but it did happen; most of the time, he just stared at you and then shouted directly at you with immense severity, 'If you mess up, it's all your fault.' He was very intense. You always felt a bit small in front of him." Like everyone else in the rehearsal room, Grignon knew that if something went wrong during the actual presentation, Jobs definitely wouldn't blame himself. Grignon said, "It was like we rehearsed a hundred times, but every time something went wrong. It wasn't a great feeling."

All these preparations were absolutely confidential. From Thursday until the following weekend, Apple rented out the entire Moscone Center. Behind the scenes, Apple set up an eight-by-eight-foot electronics lab specifically for storing and testing all the iPhones. Next to the lab was a lounge equipped with a sofa for Jobs. Then, Apple hired dozens of security personnel to guard these rooms and all entrances to the building 24 hours a day. Only those who passed electronic identity verification and whose names were on a list personally reviewed by Jobs were allowed inside. The hall where Jobs was rehearsing was off-limits to anyone except a few top executives. Jobs' obsession with preventing leaks was almost obsessive. On the eve of the presentation, he even tried to require all contractors hired by Apple to sleep at the exhibition center, whether they were controlling the stage, managing the presentation, or handling lighting and sound. His assistants eventually convinced him not to do it.

Although Grignon knew that the unveiling of the iPhone wasn't just another product launch, no one could predict how groundbreaking a moment it would become. Over seven years, the iPhone and its offspring, the iPad, became one of the most important innovations in Silicon Valley's history. They transformed the rigid cell phone industry. They created a platform for an entirely new software industry with enormous business opportunities — mobile apps, which since 2008 have generated over $10 billion in revenue. At the same time, the iPhone and iPad also disrupted the multi-billion-dollar personal computer industry. If you include iPad sales in desktop and laptop sales data, Apple has become the world's largest personal computer manufacturer. Last year, Apple sold 200 million iPhones and iPads, more than twice the global car sales.

The impact was not only economic but also cultural. Apple's innovation sparked new thinking about human-computer interaction. This wasn't just about replacing the mouse with our fingers. Especially smartphones, they have become extensions of our brains, fundamentally changing the way people acquire and process information. Consider the impact of the following inventions: books, newspapers, telephones, radios, recorders, cameras, camcorders, compasses, TVs, VCRs, DVDs, personal computers, cell phones, video games, and iPods. Smartphones encompass all of these within a volume so small they fit in your pocket. This technology is changing the way we learn in schools, the way doctors treat patients, and the way we travel and explore. People also have new ways to access and experience entertainment and media.

However, Apple is now surrounded. By the end of 2007, when Google launched Android and its own plan to dominate the global mobile phone and other mobile device markets, Google wasn't just trying to compete with the iPhone; it successfully challenged the iPhone. Since Android gained traction in 2010, its market share has rapidly increased, reaching nearly 80% globally in the smartphone market, while Apple's share has fallen below 20%. The iPad has seen a similar trend: in 2010, the iPad dominated nearly 90% of the tablet market share; now over 60% of tablets sold come with the Android system.

What worries Apple fans most is that they don't know the company's direction. When Jobs passed away in October 2011, the biggest question wasn't whether Tim Cook could replace him, but whether anyone in the world could. When Jobs ran Apple, the company was like an innovation machine, producing revolutionary products every three to five years. Jobs told his biographer Walter Isaacson that he had another breakthrough product coming — one that would revolutionize television. But under Cook's leadership, no substantial new products have emerged, and investors clearly lack confidence. In September, when Cook unveiled the latest smartphones, the iPhone 5c and iPhone 5s, to the world, Apple's stock fell by 10%. A year ago, the company's stock reached $702 per share, making Apple the world's most valuable company. But now, its stock price has fallen by more than 25% from its peak.

Comparing anyone to Steve Jobs is unfair. In the two years since Cook became Apple's CEO, he has repeatedly emphasized that Jobs explicitly told him he didn't want Cook to run Apple the way he thought Jobs would want, but rather the way Cook thought it should be done. This is self-evident. If you look back at how the iPhone was created, it's clear that it was the result of unrealistic demands from an unparalleled individual and his extraordinary power.

When Jobs decided to launch the iPhone in January 2007, he was literally taking a huge risk. He wasn't just introducing a brand-new phone — something Apple had never made before — but he was doing so with a prototype that barely worked. Although the iPhone wouldn't hit the market for another six months, he wanted the world to desire it right then and there. The reality was that there were still many issues to resolve. The production line hadn't been set up yet. At the time, only about a hundred iPhones had been manufactured, and their quality varied greatly. Some screens had obvious gaps between them and the plastic frames, others showed signs of wear. And the operating system software was riddled with bugs.

The iPhone could play a snippet of music or a short video, but couldn't reliably play entire songs or videos without crashing. If you sent an email first and then browsed the web, it worked fine; but if you did it in reverse order, it might not function properly. After hours of trial and error, the iPhone team finally found what engineers called the "golden path" — meaning, if specific tasks were performed in a particular order and manner, the phone appeared to work normally.

Even if Jobs stayed on the golden path, numerous last-minute fixes were still needed to ensure the iPhone would operate correctly. On the release day, the software running the wireless signals Grignon managed still had errors. The software managing the iPhone's memory also had issues. Moreover, no one knew whether the additional electronics Jobs required for the demo units would make matters worse.

Jobs demanded that the screen of the demo phone he used on stage could be mirrored onto the large screen behind him. Most companies would simply point a camera at the device if they wanted to display it on a big screen, but that was unacceptable to Jobs. That way, the audience would see his fingers appearing on the iPhone screen, which would detract from his presentation. So he had Apple engineers spend weeks equipping the few iPhones he planned to use on stage with extra circuit boards and video cables, connecting the cables to projectors. This way, when Jobs tapped the calendar icon on the iPhone, his finger wouldn't appear on the large screen, and the image on the large screen would respond to his finger's commands. The effect was magical. The audience felt as though they were holding an iPhone themselves. However, given the other major issues with the iPhone, making the demonstration flawless seemed nonsensical at the time.

The software controlling the Wi-Fi signals in the iPhone was unstable, so Grignon and his team extended the phone antennas and connected them to wires backstage to shorten the wireless transmission distance. At the same time, they ensured that the audience didn't interfere with their signal frequency. "Even if we hid the base station ID," — meaning when laptops searched for Wi-Fi signals, the name of the demo base station wouldn't appear — "but we had 5,000 geeks in the audience," Grignon said, "they'd definitely find a way to intrude on this frequency." He said the final solution was to modify the AirPort software to make it appear as if it were running in Japan, not the U.S. Japanese Wi-Fi uses some frequencies not allowed in the U.S.

They had far fewer options to ensure that the calls Jobs made on stage would definitely go through. All Grignon and his team could do was ensure a strong signal, and beyond that, they could only pray. They had AT&T, the iPhone's wireless carrier, set up a portable cell tower on site, so they knew the reception would definitely be strong. Afterwards, with Jobs' approval, they used software to preset the phone's displayed signal strength to the highest five bars, regardless of the actual signal strength. During the few minutes Jobs used the iPhone on stage to make calls, the chance of the wireless signal collapsing was low, but the entire demonstration lasted 90 minutes, and the probability of the signal collapsing at some point was high. Grignon said, "If the signal really collapses and restarts as we feared, we don't want the audience to notice. So we hard-coded the fixed number into the program, and the phone would always display five bars of signal strength."

However, none of these makeshift solutions addressed the iPhone's biggest problem: its memory often ran out, requiring a reboot if several applications were run simultaneously. To deal with this issue, Jobs prepared several demo units on stage. If one phone's memory ran out and needed a reboot, he could switch to another phone. But considering the number of demonstrations Jobs intended to give, Grignon worried that there were too many potential points of failure. Even if everything went well with the dozen or so demonstrations he planned to do, there would likely be problems during the climactic final demonstration, as Jobs planned to showcase all the main features of the iPhone on the same unit.

He would start playing a piece of music, make a call, keep the caller on hold, then make another call, find a picture, send it via email to the second caller, search for something online for the first caller, and then return to continue listening to his music. Grignon said, "We and our colleagues were all nervous about this final demonstration. These engineering units only had 128 megabytes of memory," — roughly equivalent to the capacity of twenty high-resolution photos — "and all these applications weren't fully developed yet, consuming a lot of memory and were quite bloated."

Jobs rarely put himself in such precarious situations. His reputation as a demanding boss was well-known, and he seemed to always know how tough he could push his employees to turn the impossible into the possible. But he always had a backup plan, an emergency scheme, in case the first task couldn't be completed on time, he could substitute it with the second.

However, the iPhone was the only extremely cool new thing Apple was doing at that time. The iPhone's mission was so comprehensive that this time, the company had no backup plan. Grignon said, "At the time, we had to choose between Apple TV and iPhone. If Jobs had chosen to only bring Apple TV to Macworld, the whole world would have said, 'Damn, what is this crap?' Apple TV was a new product that could connect iTunes to a TV."

The thought of possibly ruining the most significant moment of his career made Grignon's stomach hurt. By 2007, his entire career could be said to have been dedicated to Apple or its affiliates. In 1993, when he was still a student at the University of Iowa, he and his friend Jeremy Wyld adapted the MessagePad software on the Newton to enable it to wirelessly access the internet. Although the Newton didn't become a successful product, many still regarded it as the first mainstream handheld computer, and their hack of the device was a considerable achievement; this helped both of them get jobs at Apple. Wyld joined the Newton team, while Grignon entered Apple's famous research and development lab — the Advanced Technology Group — to participate in the development of video conferencing technology.
