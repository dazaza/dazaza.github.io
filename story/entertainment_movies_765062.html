Synopsis:

In 1996, in Buyeo, North Jeolla Province, large-scale construction was underway to host the Winter Olympics. However, the International Olympic Committee sent a notice stating that due to the lack of interest from the South Korean public in the Winter Olympics and South Korea's non-participation in one of its official events - ski flying, Buyeo's Olympic bid was not approved. The organizing committee was thrown into chaos and decided to immediately form a ski flying national team, inviting Tae-mo Mountain children's skiing coach Bang Jong-sam and hastily gathering some national representatives to participate. These representatives were completely unfamiliar with ski flying competitions, and the lack of suitable training grounds posed a thorny issue. Therefore, they built an artificial site for arduous training. After much effort, the members could only finish last at the Ski Flying World Cup. Ultimately, Buyeo was eliminated in the Olympic bidding competition, but South Korea had its first ski flying team.

Behind-the-scenes Production:

High-budget Production of a Commoner's Movie

Director Kim Yong-hwa's 2006 comedy "My Girlfriend is an Agent" successfully attracted nearly 7 million viewers to the cinema and made Kim Ya-jung an overnight sensation. "National Team" gives off a strong scent of a sports inspiration film right from its title, telling the story of how five ski jumpers overcame difficulties to bring glory to their country. In 2008, "The Most Brilliant Moment of My Life," starring Kim Jung-eun and Moon So-ri, was based on the Korean women's volleyball team competing at the 2004 Athens Summer Olympics and was hailed as a tribute to the 2008 Beijing Olympics. This film, on the other hand, is a movie to cheer on "Gangwon-do Pyeongchang's bid for the 2018 Winter Olympics."

Kim Yong-hwa's works consistently focus on the theme of "extremely common, simple rescues." Stories like brotherly affection in "Oh! Brother" and the probability contrast between female beauty and happiness in "Ugly Duckling." The commonality in "National Team" is that the protagonists are all ordinary individuals with painful histories, emphasizing how they overcome obstacles and weaknesses to challenge the limits of their dreams, making the film relatable to people of all ages.

This is probably the only film in the world truly based on ski jumping. Ski jumping originated in Northern Europe, initially just a game played by local residents—symbolizing humanity's ancient dream of flying freely in the sky. To showcase skiing movements lasting less than a few seconds from a 90-120 meter high platform, athletes need to put in tremendous effort behind the scenes. Statistics show that one needs to engage in this sport for at least 10 years to have a chance at success, with an extremely harsh elimination rate.

The stories of the five players in the film are adapted from real-life prototypes, pioneers of South Korea's ski jumping movement, navigating towards their dreams amidst adverse realities and skeptical public opinions. Unlike athletes in other sports who can focus solely on training and competition, real-life ski jumpers must work part-time jobs to pay for the expensive training costs while continuing to train. Despite these challenges, they repeatedly achieve good results in competitions. "National Team" discloses for the first time the beautiful and moving postures of these previously unsung athletes striving toward their dreams in the sky.

Not Letting Go of Even 0.1 Seconds: Capturing the Splendid Moments of Flight

To vividly present the magnificent moments of flight in the air, this film has put considerable effort into its visual effects. Before actual filming, special 3D charcoal pencils were used to finely adjust and cut the images, marking the first time Red One camera technology and Flycat CAMCAT cableway camera system (one of the most advanced camera systems in the world, capable of multi-angle shooting) were introduced in South Korean films. The crew also invited Austrian professional technicians responsible for photographing world championships to capture high-speed moments from multiple angles during ski jumping.

The lead actors underwent three months of training with professional ski jumpers before filming began, starting with basic physical training such as running, hurdles, stair climbing... progressing to general equipment adaptability training indoors during summer, then practicing one-on-one with athletes outdoors. By the time filming officially started, the actors' physiques closely resembled those of real competitors, and they shared a deeper empathy with the athletes' courage in challenging their dreams.

Ha Jung-woo: A Box Office Guarantee That Never Fails

Whether in supporting or leading roles in films like "Breath," "The Most Brilliant Moment of My Life," "Beastly Boys," "A Great Day," Ha Jung-woo seems to bring "good fortune"—solid box office returns. Thanks to his explosive performance in "The Chaser," Ha Jung-woo quickly rose to become one of the hottest male stars. His participation significantly boosted the popularity of this film. He plays Cha Hyeong-tae, a Korean-American who was once a young American skier. Searching for his biological mother, he travels back and forth between the US and Korea and accidentally joins the South Korean national team. Before filming commenced, he underwent three months of ski jumping training alongside the other main cast members, including basic physical conditioning exercises and skill coaching by one-on-one national team players. With an already impressive physique, Ha Jung-woo's new set of stills from "National Team" earned praise from fans who noted that the "blue-chip actor" was increasingly leaning towards "idol status."

Kim Yong-hwa openly expressed admiration for Ha Jung-woo, saying that after watching "The Chaser," he became captivated by the unique charm in Ha's acting and believed him to be the perfect candidate for the lead role in "National Team." During negotiations, due to scheduling conflicts, Ha Jung-woo initially couldn't confirm his participation. Kim Yong-hwa adjusted the shooting schedule specifically for him, finally realizing their collaboration. During the promotion of "National Team," Ha Jung-woo was embroiled in a heated rumor about a romance with a famous model. He commented, "What I hope to find most is 'love' from the audience because if that happens, everyone will buy tickets to watch my performances."

A Basket Full of Handsome Men: A Feast for the Eyes

Besides the ruggedly handsome Ha Jung-woo, other male actors in the film are also worth noting. Kim Dong-wook, who performed admirably in "Coffee Prince," plays a popular male bartender in nightclubs in the film, admired by many female customers. With a colorful romantic history, he joins the national ski team to avoid the arduous military service, sparking a series of comical incidents amidst the tedious training while being pursued by the coach's daughter.

Kim Ji-seok gained wider recognition through the highly-rated family drama "Love or Hate," which exceeded 40% in ratings. In this film, he portrays a ski jumper shouldering the responsibility of providing for his grandmother and younger siblings, with a quiet and steady personality, showcasing a complete 180-degree transformation. Child star Lee Jae-eung, known for his impressive acting in "The Host" and "I Love You, Ma Soon," plays a four-dimensional character in "National Team." The strong coach leading the five team members in fierce competitions is played by seasoned actor Sung Dong-il, who excels in comedic performances but also brings out the pure and delicate inner side of his character.

Fun Facts:

- Filmed at the actual Winter Olympics skiing venue at Namsan Park in Seoul, the film went through three overseas shoots, capturing footage at the prestigious German leg of the Ski Flying World Cup to ensure authenticity.

- The film cost 11 billion Korean won, making it the most expensive sports movie in South Korean cinematic history.

- Lee Jae-hyeok, who won the Best Music Award at the Grand Bell Awards for "My Girlfriend is an Agent," collaborated again with the director, adding significant value to the film.

- The film won five major awards, including Best Film, at the Chunsa Film Awards in September 2009.
