How to create an eco-friendly home environment in decoration and renovation?

by longersing on 2009-08-25 17:44:49

Nowadays, eco-friendly homes are an indispensable part of daily life. While people pursue urban trends, they never forget the principle of returning to nature. Today, let's explore how to decorate and furnish our eco-friendly homes and create a first-class living standard. The Shanghai Decoration Network will guide you in understanding the voices of netizens.

Related Link: What is an Eco-Friendly Home?

Many people equate "eco-friendly homes" with planting trees, grass, wooden interiors, artificial mountains and waters, and pastoral charm, believing these to be synonymous with ecological living. In reality, this understanding of ecology often remains at a superficial level and can sometimes lead to misconceptions, even contradicting ecological principles by going against them.

For instance, wooden interiors themed around returning to nature cannot simply be considered an "eco-friendly" way of living because it directly leads to deforestation. A "home ecosystem" is built on the foundation of being human-centered, utilizing natural conditions and artificial means to create a comfortable, healthy living environment that is conducive to living. At the same time, it seeks to control the use of natural resources, promoting the use of artificial, composite, and recyclable materials, benefiting the larger ecological environment and achieving a balance between taking from nature and receiving its rewards.

Invisible pollution should not be underestimated; protecting health is one of the basic issues that the "home ecosystem" needs to address. It has been nearly two years since the ten national mandatory standards for "harmful substance limits in indoor decoration and renovation materials" were issued. Nowadays, people try to avoid toxic substances during renovations and consciously use green building materials. However, who would know that there are still some hidden sources of pollution in the home? A house is a relatively enclosed space, where air circulates within a very limited area. Any corner that harbors dirt eventually becomes an indoor pollution source, thereby endangering the health of family members.

For example, in bathrooms, to save on renovation costs (as detailed in articles such as "Secrets to Saving Money on Renovations Series I-III" and "Mastering Renovation Tricks – Enhancing Quality while Saving Costs"), cheap tiles and sanitary ware are used. These surfaces are full of pores and particles. In environments with poor ventilation, high humidity, and warmth over extended periods, they become breeding grounds for bacteria. These mold spores spread through airflow, polluting the entire room.

Similarly, examining all the textiles in your home—sofa covers, carpets, wallpapers, and even the plants we highly recommend—the soil nurturing these plants also nurtures countless harmful mites, becoming long-term hidden killers lurking in the home. Many modern individuals' sub-health states could be due to prolonged exposure to such environments. Ventilation plays a respiratory role in the "home ecosystem," but improper design layouts can produce effects detrimental to health.

There is a Chinese saying, "a sick bed," which refers to the chronic damage the body experiences during sleep. Improper ventilation caused by poor layout design may be one of the reasons. Taking the bathroom as an example, if it is too close to the bed, excessive humidity or already polluted air may worsen indoor air quality when ventilated, leading to a situation where "ventilation does not purify." Resting in such polluted air undoubtedly affects health.

Additionally, furniture in the home, audio systems, gas stoves, walls, doors, and windows can all contribute to a microclimate indoors. Temperature differences within the home can promote air circulation. This moving air can bring fresh oxygen from outside and disperse any residual harmful gases indoors. Shoe cabinets containing dirty shoes, poorly ventilated wardrobes, and the placement of these pieces of furniture, whether reasonable or not, all affect the health of those in the space.

To ensure good air quality indoors, the arrangement of plants, lighting, and furniture must be scientifically planned. Objects should have sufficient spacing to create a healthy "home ecological" environment conducive to well-being.

In summary, an eco-friendly home goes beyond surface-level aesthetics and involves careful consideration of health and sustainability.
