Confronted with the selling fake goods of online stores, can Taobao step back and watch?

by anonymous on 2011-03-25 14:39:04

Products that sell for several thousand yuan in stores can be obtained for a few hundred yuan on Taobao! Friends who have shopped on Taobao won't be unfamiliar with such exclamations. And the so-called "products that can be obtained for a few hundred yuan" are usually what's known as "Grade A goods," or simply blatant counterfeits. While becoming China's largest e-commerce platform, the rampant sale of counterfeit and pirated products has become a huge obstacle hindering Taobao's further growth. Since its establishment, Taobao has faced multiple lawsuits over the sale of fakes and infringement. The plaintiffs include ordinary consumers who bought fake goods, professional counterfeit fighters like Wang Hai, and brand owners...

Most of the time, Taobao has weathered various litigation storms unscathed with the reasoning that it is "merely a network service provider offering a publishing platform, unaware whether users are counterfeiting or infringing." Until the matter fades from everyone's memory.

However, the verdict of a Taobao counterfeit sales lawsuit that occurred in December last year could possibly change the existing "rules of the game." A financial advisory company took a Taobao seller and Taobao itself to court in Beijing's Haidian District Court. This financial advisory company, named "Zhiqian," primarily engages in investment training. It pursued Taobao because this Taobao seller was selling video footage of a stock training course developed by "Zhiqian" for only a few hundred yuan on their online store, whereas the legitimate price was as high as 12,000 yuan. The court's first-instance judgment ruled that Taobao bears joint liability, ordering a compensation of 10,000 yuan.

Taobao was judged liable for infringement

The infringement lawsuit filed by "Zhiqian" had a relatively straightforward case.

The plaintiff, Zhiqian Company, claimed that in 2009, the company launched the "Zhiqian Club Stock Training Course" (teaching video), which was registered with the copyright registration authority and then sold to members. However, numerous "Zhiqian" members complained that the training courses they had purchased for over ten thousand yuan could be bought on Taobao for just a few hundred yuan.

Upon investigation, "Zhiqian" discovered that on Taobao, there were hundreds of operators illegally copying and selling the involved works, including the defendant Wang Chao's Taobao store. Consequently, "Zhiqian" filed a lawsuit, requesting the court to order the defendant Wang Chao to cease the infringement. Simultaneously, they requested Taobao to remove all links selling the "Zhiqian Club Stock Training Course" on Taobao except those from the plaintiff, and to order the infringer to compensate for losses, with Taobao bearing joint liability.

Taobao argued that the dispute arose due to the information published by its registered member using the information publishing platform provided by Taobao. All the involved information was uploaded and published by the online store operators, including the first defendant. Taobao merely provided an information publishing platform. As a network service provider offering an information publishing platform, Taobao had fulfilled its legal obligations and did not know beforehand whether the involved information infringed, nor could it have known. After receiving notice from Zhiqian Company, Taobao deleted the involved information posted by the online store owner and all the links provided by Zhiqian Company that were reported as infringing, complying with legal requirements. Therefore, Taobao and the first defendant did not constitute joint infringement and should not bear joint liability.

Previously, Taobao's argument that it was "merely a network service provider offering a publishing platform, unaware whether users were counterfeiting or infringing" generally received support from the courts, but this time things changed. The court believed: even if, as Taobao claimed, it does not bear the obligation to review network transaction objects beforehand and can only take deletion measures based on notifications from rights holders after the fact, Taobao should have promptly deleted related infringing information after receiving multiple notifications from rights holders. Taobao's actions had caused the losses of "Zhiqian" to expand, and Taobao should bear corresponding responsibility for this expanded loss. It was ordered to pay 10,000 RMB in compensation.

There's still a chance to "turn things around"

After the first-instance verdict, both "Zhiqian" and Taobao expressed dissatisfaction with the ruling. The second-instance trial is reportedly scheduled for this month.

"Zhiqian's" dissatisfaction with the first-instance ruling stems from the excessively low amount of damages awarded for infringement. According to relevant personnel at "Zhiqian," as of May 7, 2010, Taobao's sales records showed that a total of 75 sets of "Zhiqian" "Series 3" course materials had been sold. At "Zhiqian's" pricing, these teaching videos were valued at 952,500 yuan, and the value of the infringing videos sold by just the first defendant in this case amounted to 114,300 yuan. What troubled "Zhiqian" even more was that due to the existence of a large number of infringing videos online, it was becoming increasingly difficult to attract customers through video teaching, directly impacting "Zhiqian's" business model.

In contrast to "Zhiqian's" "high-profile" approach of inviting experts from all fields in Beijing to comment on the verdict, Taobao's attitude could be described as "outwardly relaxed but inwardly tense" and "not showing emotions." Analysts from the IT consulting firm Analysys International believe that although Taobao's compensation amount was merely 10,000 RMB, if the final ruling determines that Taobao is infringing, the effect would be akin to opening a "Pandora's box" - it sends out a "dangerous signal," meaning that Taobao, China's most popular C2C online trading platform, might face endless infringement lawsuits in the future. Therefore, the best choice at this stage is silence, leaving everything to be resolved in court. The key is, according to past experience, Taobao is not without any chance of "turning things around" in the second instance.

It seems that Taobao may indeed be prepared. Ironically, Taobao draws confidence from previous cases where its former rival eBay successfully avoided liability in numerous infringement lawsuits in the U.S. eBay entered the mainland Chinese market in 1999 and was one of the earliest multinational giants to venture into China's C2C e-commerce sector, but it was eventually forced out by the locally grown Taobao.

Same lawsuits, different rulings

In July 2008, eBay finally achieved a long-awaited victory after prolonged battles with numerous top luxury brands. On July 14, 2008, the U.S. Southern District Court of New York made a ruling in the case where the top jewelry brand Tiffany & Co. sued eBay for selling counterfeit goods - eBay won. Tiffany & Co. filed consecutive lawsuits in 2003 and 2004, claiming that a large number of merchants on eBay were selling counterfeit clothing, bags, and jewelry, causing the company significant losses. They demanded that eBay remove 19,000 pieces of counterfeit jewelry bearing their brand and immediately suspend the trading qualifications of those selling counterfeit goods, while also seeking compensation for infringement from eBay. eBay, on the other hand, argued that it only provides a marketplace connecting buyers and sellers and is not responsible for verifying the authenticity of the billions of items sold on its site. Only when Tiffany notifies eBay about counterfeit goods will eBay take action against counterfeiters.

This lawsuit lasted four years, and on July 14, 2008, the U.S. Southern District Court of New York ruled that users selling counterfeit Tiffany jewelry on eBay infringed upon Tiffany's interests, and Tiffany should bear full responsibility for anti-counterfeiting efforts, while eBay, as an online retailer, does not need to assume liability. The judge stated that the court sympathizes with famous brands like Tiffany that have invested substantial resources in development, but current U.S. law favors online retailers. He suggested that Congress reconsider whether it is fair for brand owners to bear the entire responsibility for maintaining their brands on the internet based on the characteristics of e-commerce.

Regarding the court's ruling, eBay told the media that since 2006, it has invested tens of millions of dollars to combat counterfeit goods. To prevent the proliferation of counterfeit items, eBay launched a special authorized merchant program. Some well-known brands can use this tool to search for counterfeit goods across the website, and once they discover counterfeit goods, they can request immediate removal. eBay promised that within four hours of being notified of counterfeit goods, it could remove 90% of them. It is said that 18,000 companies participated in this plan, including Apple, Coach, and Oakley. Meanwhile, eBay also imposed restrictions on sellers who frequently sell counterfeit goods, including limiting the number of items they can sell and the duration of auctions.

Surprisingly, just half a month before the New York court announced its verdict, eBay suffered a complete defeat in a similar lawsuit in a French court. On June 30, 2008, a Paris court ruled that eBay failed to prevent counterfeit goods from well-known brands under the French Louis Vuitton Moet Hennessy (LVMH) group from appearing on its French website, infringing on LVMH's rights, and ordered eBay to pay LVMH $61 million in compensation. This ruling was the third time European courts had rejected eBay's arguments in recent years. eBay was extremely dissatisfied with the French court's decision, believing it set a "very bad precedent," meaning that eBay would have to invest more resources in monitoring its website. The ruling could also encourage other well-known brands to file similar lawsuits, placing an unbearable burden on eBay. eBay stated that this ruling undermined a core concept, namely that buyers and sellers should be responsible for transactions. The victorious LVMH's argument was straightforward: regardless of whether the goods were genuine or counterfeit, as an online trading platform, eBay profited from every transaction through handling fees or advertising revenue, thus it was "not innocent."

Link

Yu Guofu, a member of the Policy and Resources Committee of the China Internet Association, believes that according to Article 36 of the Tort Liability Law, "If a network user uses network services to commit tortious acts, the aggrieved party has the right to notify the network service provider to take necessary measures such as deletion, blocking, or disconnection of links. If the network service provider fails to take necessary measures in a timely manner after receiving notification, it shall bear joint liability for the expanded part of the damage with the network user."

Notably, the aforementioned clause in the Tort Liability Law specifies that the measures network service providers should take are "to take necessary measures in a timely manner," which is stricter than the responsibility specified in the previously often-cited Regulations on the Protection of the Right to Communicate Works Over Information Networks, which stipulated the responsibility of deleting notification links. Yu Guofu believes that in this case, although Taobao repeatedly deleted some infringing links based on complaints from rights holders, according to relevant evidence, Taobao neglected to delete identifiable infringing goods merely because they were not included in the complaints from rights holders. Its behavior clearly did not meet the standards of "timely" and "necessary" as stipulated in the Tort Liability Law. For the expanded damages caused to rights holders due to Taobao's neglect in deleting such infringing activities, Taobao should bear joint liability for infringement.

As of Thursday afternoon this week, there were still sellers on Taobao offering a product called "Stock Training Course - 2009 Xuzhengwen Zhiqian Club Internal Training Course," with the latest transaction date shown as March 7, 2011, at a price of 10 yuan. (Written by Wu Qiong)

Lawyer's Perspective

Whether it's the lawsuit by "Zhiqian" against online stores and Taobao, or the series of lawsuits between eBay and luxury brands, the fundamental issue involved is: who should bear the responsibility for combating counterfeits?

From a legislative perspective, the legal principles and spirit established by various countries regarding the definition and handling of online infringement are basically consistent. That is, network providers are required to assume joint liability for infringement under two conditions: First, knowing or should have known that network users are committing acts that infringe on others' rights through the network but fail to act; Second, when rights holders present evidence-supported warnings but still fail to take measures such as removing infringing content to eliminate the consequences of infringement. Third, directly benefitting economically from the infringing behavior. Laws worldwide categorize the fault of network providers in terms of "assisting indirect infringement," where the form of fault is non-action.

Why do similar cases result in drastically different rulings in different regions, courts, and under different judges despite similar legal frameworks? This reflects differences in legal systems, thinking, logic, traditions, and value preferences. In the eBay cases, the legal evaluations of the same behavior in Europe and America were completely opposite. There are certainly influences of legal pragmatism serving national interests, and whether the law promotes new network industries or protects traditional brands at all costs—i.e., which side the legal balance tilts towards—and, of course, the judges' understanding of legal concepts. Regarding the concept of tort liability, in the eBay case, American law ruled that the online store infringed on the rights holder's interests, and the rights holder should bear full responsibility for combating counterfeits, while eBay, as an online retailer, does not need to assume liability. French law, however, ruled that eBay failed to prevent counterfeit branded goods from appearing on its French website, thereby infringing on the rights of the well-known brand and requiring it to assume compensation responsibility. The crux of the disagreement between the two rulings is whether eBay has the responsibility to prevent counterfeit branded goods from being sold on its network platform.

Analyzing issues cannot be detached from reality. Taking online transactions as an example, today's e-commerce websites involve tens of millions of merchants and billions of items listed online. These websites themselves do not own or directly sell counterfeit goods. Therefore, expecting websites to distinguish genuine from counterfeit goods solely through technical checks is almost impossible. Thus, network service providers generally do not bear the "general duty of care," meaning courts typically recognize that websites have no obligation to review and filter all the information in their network services.

Looking back at the "Zhiqian" lawsuit case, should Taobao bear tort liability? The key here lies in the facts and evidence. Rights holders must provide evidence proving they have fulfilled their duty to inform, issued notices demanding cessation of infringement, and that the notified party failed to take measures to stop the infringement after receiving the notice, thereby holding the website accountable for tortious non-action. As for how much joint liability this entails, according to the principle of "whoever acts bears the responsibility," network users (online stores) are direct infringers and bear the greatest responsibility; network service providers, upon receiving notice of infringement, should promptly take necessary measures to prevent the further spread of infringing information. If they fail to fulfill this obligation, they must bear corresponding tort liability. The current trouble Taobao faces in the "Zhiqian" case is that it must provide sufficient proof showing that after receiving "Zhiqian's" notice about counterfeit sales, it promptly disconnected all infringing links and closed down the online store. Given the sophistication of the internet today and the "alertness" of domestic counterfeiters, convincing the judge that it has "taken necessary measures in a timely manner" poses quite a challenge for Taobao. (Lawyer Lin Changfu, Wenhui Daily)
