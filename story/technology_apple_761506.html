iPad: Apple's Next iPhone?

by geekzhang on 2013-05-03 14:42:31

Despite revolutionizing the industry, for Apple, the iPad has always taken a backseat to the iPhone. Due to its extremely high profit margin - with every $1 of revenue including $0.5 or $0.6 in profit - it naturally became the company's most important business.A recent article on Slate analyzed that the iPad, which has been launched for three years, has been flying under the radar, but its prospects are limitless. Meanwhile, the dominant advantage of the iPhone is weakening, and the next Apple product to achieve the status of the iPhone will not be the Watch or TV, but the iPad.Apple's latest earnings report shows that the company sold 19 million iPads in the first quarter, far below the 37 million iPhones, but the year-on-year growth rate of 65% far exceeds the 7% of the iPhone. The reason is simple: with the rise of competitors and the breakthrough of low-cost smartphones, the advantages of the iPhone are slowly eroding. In the tablet market, however, the iPad remains the absolute dominant force, even in the low-price tablet market, where Apple can still consolidate its competitive advantage with the iPad mini.This means that the revenue and profit brought by the iPhone to Apple will slow down. Slate analyzes that if this trend continues, the iPad may surpass the iPhone by 2015 to become the company's best-selling product.Slate believes that the iPad is very similar to the iPod strategy: first launch a revolutionary product, then continuously expand its product line, covering all price ranges from high to low. Just like the iPod Classic, iPod touch, iPod nano, iPod Shuffle, although the iPad does not have as many varieties, its price range is equally comprehensive: from the $499 iPad 4, $399 iPad 2 to the $329 iPad mini.Besides diverse pricing, another similarity between the iPad and iPod is their relatively low profit margins. Slate analyzes that compared to profit, Apple places more emphasis on their significance to the Apple ecosystem. Thanks to the halo effect of the iPod, many people eventually became iPhone users. The frightening aspect of the Apple ecosystem lies here: products are not isolated entities, but an overall experience and service that runs through the core iOS system. A single breakthrough leads to horizontal expansion, where hardware, services, and experiences form a mutually extending, resource-sharing powerful ecosystem.The result is that despite the greatly weakened status of the iPod, it still holds a staggering 75% share of the digital music player market after being on the market for so many years.However, the competitive environment for the iPad is obviously more severe than that of the iPod, and it is increasingly being impacted by low-cost Android tablets. But Slate points out that although these competitors have a price advantage, their profit margins are even lower. Neither Amazon nor Google can directly make money from hardware. On the other hand, the iPad, with its exclusive 350,000 apps, far surpasses Android tablets in both quantity and quality of applications. For tablets, the value of applications is even more prominent, making this advantage very critical.Slate concludes by saying that the industry's anticipation for Apple to release new products has lost rationality. What might trigger another explosion from Apple won't be the rumored smartwatch or TV, but rather the iPad that people have been ignoring all along.
