Facebook has no future relying on user content alone

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 15:32:19

July 22nd news: Facebook gets content for free from users, then uses this content to gain over a billion users, many of whom spend countless hours on the site. For all this, Facebook doesn't pay a cent but reaps huge advertising revenue.

Is it really like that? Wrong.

The most creative people in the world work hard to create the best content, such as an ordinary photo of breakfast, but it is precisely this content that brings Facebook huge profits.

Saying that Facebook does not need professionally produced content is purely sophistry. In fact, Facebook not only benefits from third-party content; its very existence depends on third-party content.

Of course, Facebook itself has never promoted this point. Like Google, Twitter (and recently Yahoo), Facebook claims it is not a media company. Last year, Facebook closed the door to Social Reader, causing traffic that had once stimulated publisher growth to plummet suddenly, leading one to agree: media is not Facebook's focus. Why is this? It's easy to understand: Facebook cleverly shifts the focus, placing emphasis on advertising on its own site and moving away from areas that don’t generate revenue, such as collaborations with publishers.

In the long term, Facebook will highly depend on paid content. Because status updates will no longer be fresh, the next generation will become immune, and Facebook must enhance its utility. Posting a picture of a child may get lots of "likes," but people who come to Facebook to build connections don’t want just to give a thumbs up. The only way to establish real connections is through "conversation." What stimulates conversation? Meaningful, more relevant content.

This, LinkedIn understands best. Last fall, it launched its own content platform: content mixed with business news, opinions of important figures (from CEOs, famous individuals), which greatly increased traffic and interaction. YouTube is the same; what attracts large numbers of internet viewers isn’t user-aggregated content. When users realized they could watch "Saturday Night Live" and comment on it there, when they realized they could watch other carefully produced programs, YouTube truly took off.

We share and write about things we feel strongly about: opinions, news, favorite TV shows. If these types of conversations were absent from Facebook’s News Feed, it would inevitably lose its appeal. In fact, according to Murdoch (Chairman of News Corporation), this phenomenon has already begun to show.

On May 17th, 2013, Murdoch said: "Pay attention to Facebook! Time spent by each user on it has dropped significantly. This is a terrible sign, just like MySpace years ago."

Although Facebook hasn’t publicly admitted it, it clearly knows how important content is for long-term survival. In fact, according to the Wall Street Journal, Facebook has quietly been developing its own news reader, starting a year ago.

Can it execute well? It's too early to say. If it acts correctly, Facebook will become a "one-stop shop" for news and paid content, content that we currently can only obtain outside of Facebook. Even better, Facebook sufficiently understands its users and can become the ultimate manager, knowing personal updates, media content, and what products users are truly interested in. The more it knows, the more valuable it becomes, allowing Facebook to position itself as a key content distributor for media companies. This is not unattainable... Not at all. In fact, some recent moves by LinkedIn have already confirmed the opportunities present here, and Facebook can provide consumers with a service that can match competitors, or even complement their shortcomings.

Fundamentally speaking, Facebook needs publishers' content to succeed: content is the "currency" of conversations between users. Without good, thought-provoking, emotionally resonant content, all that remains are profile pictures and sponsored posts.

We've seen something like this before; it was called MySpace.
