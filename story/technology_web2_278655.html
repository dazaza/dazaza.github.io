The King of Pop

by nimomuricc on 2009-11-26 03:46:59

In the 19th century, the king of pop emerged as a significant figure in Europe's music scene from the 1950s. Elvis led the charge into the Beatles' "British invasion" of the 1960s. The major channels for popularizing music were still the most primitive forms of voice transmission, with radio being the most important propaganda tool. Having a radio station across Britain or achieving a single band championship could make one a beloved popular icon. In the United States, the Billboard charts showed that the Beatles had held 20 number-one records. Elvis Presley had 17 first-place singles last year, just behind Maria Carey, who ranked third in history. Meanwhile, with the advent of color television, radio and TV began replacing print publications and became the most influential media. Whether you were in New York City, in the western United States, or busy on a farm, everyone could see synchronized transmissions on their TV screens. This meant that the image of a product could be repackaged through visual imagery to stimulate consumer purchasing desires. Both in the U.S. and the UK, Elvis and the Beatles gained unprecedented fame through televised performances. Compared to them, although Jackson started lower on the rankings after Elvis Presley appeared on the radio, he was sensitive enough to perceive the arrival of modernism in a series of images. Not only did his music videos serve as the best advertisement for his music works, but they even became commodities themselves. Jackson thus transcended all his predecessors and other singers, claiming the throne as the "King of Pop."

When MTV viewers tuned in during 1981, the content was still dominated by rock bands playing instruments. However, Jackson had already begun planning his groundbreaking music video works. A year later, record companies invested in three teams to send MTV video music broadcasts, the reason being quite simple: Phil Jackson’s skin tone. Therefore, the company even prioritized other artists' songs until they finally threatened MTV into compliance, broadcasting the "Billie Jean" and "Beat It" music videos. American audiences were captivated by Jackson's lip-syncing performances on TV, mesmerized by this black man's miraculous dance styles. Another record-breaking 17-minute video in "Thriller" featured Michael wearing a red jacket, dressed as a vampire, dancing and singing with detestable zombies. This MV premiered to a billion people worldwide, selling a total of 9 million copies globally, making it the most popular music video ever. Jackson also became the first black singer to appear on MTV. On July 7, 2009, at the Staples Center in Los Angeles, a public memorial ceremony for Michael Jackson was held. Guests like the younger Bryant and Magic Johnson recalled their acquaintance with Jackson from thirty years ago, saying: "Thanks to Michael for everything he created for all African-Americans, so we can appear on TV; he opened such wide doors for us."
