Synopsis:

The film is based on real events that occurred during the reign of King Louis XV of France.

In the mid-1760s, during the reign of King Louis XV, a monstrous wolf terrorized the Gevaudan region, with most victims being women and children. The entire country was shocked by the gruesome killings, with no one knowing the true identity of the beast, leading some to believe it was the devil incarnate. To calm the public, Louis XV dispatched royal knight Francis (Samuel Le Bihan) to hunt down the monster. While in Gevaudan, Francis fell in love with the local count's daughter, Marianne (Emilie Dequenne), had an affair with the beautiful courtesan Sylvia (Monica Bellucci), and befriended Marianne's brother Jean-François (Vincent Cassel). As the investigation deepened, Francis discovered a steel tooth on one of the victim's bodies, and a witness claimed that someone was controlling the giant wolf. As the investigation progressed, more terrifying conspiracies were uncovered, leading to a series of bloody battles...

Historical Background:

From 1764 to 1767, the monster depicted in the movie was a nightmare for the Lozère region of France, with over a hundred people brutally killed, mostly women and children. Survivors described the beast as large as a cow, with a broad chest, thick neck, short upright ears, a nose similar to a greyhound, fangs protruding from its mouth, and a black stripe running from head to tail, capable of leaping 30 feet at once. Hunters and soldiers tried to encircle and kill what appeared to be a giant wolf, but even when hit at close range, the beast managed to escape quickly and grew increasingly bold. As the situation worsened, the entire country became anxious, prompting the local government to strengthen security measures and deploy the regional army, but all efforts proved futile. In January 1765, after allocating 3 million pounds from the provinces of Languedoc and Mende, the French king added another 600,000 to reward whoever could kill the beast.

In February 1765, the king invited renowned wolf-hunting expert Denneval, but even this legendary hunter who had killed 1200 wolves failed. For a time, the monster incident became a laughing stock for the British ridiculing the French, angering the king who sent his own combat expert Antoine de Beauterne to personally organize the hunt, yet the death toll continued to rise. The church began claiming the beast was a messenger of sin, punishing sinners. On September 21st, 1765, Beauterne led 40 local hunters and 12 hunting dogs to lure the beast, which indeed appeared, moving skillfully using the forest as cover. Amidst a hail of bullets, large lead pellets struck its right shoulder, entering its right eye and head, and despite breaking through the encirclement, the beast finally died from multiple bullet wounds. Eventually, it was preserved as a specimen and displayed in the National History Museum until the early 20th century.

However, the monster incidents were far from over; in the following two months, there were still numerous deaths. But the king had ordered that the truth be kept hidden. Nonetheless, the silent populace knew that another beast was still wreaking havoc. After a brief calm during the winter of 1766, the beast returned in the spring of the next year. Between May and June, local farmers started relying on pilgrimages to pray for deliverance from the beast's ravages. A pilgrim named Jean Chastel received three holy bullets. On June 19th, 1767, another round of hunting began, with as many as 300 participants this time. Chastel set up an ambush where Beauterne had killed the first beast, and the fleeing beast encountered Chastel face-to-face. Calmly finishing his prayer, closing the Bible, removing his glasses, Chastel raised his gun and fired, causing the beast to fall instantly. Later, it was said that grass never grew again where the beast had fallen.

About the Film:

It can be said that the first third of the film stays true to the real events, and all characters except Mani are historically accurate. Director Christophe Gans said: "At the time, France was at war with Britain, and they were always mocking us because we couldn't catch a single wolf. In the film, we portray the wolf as a sacrifice, a scapegoat for the whole story."

As a child, Gans had heard his parents talk about the 'Beast of Gévaudan', and in the 1960s, he watched a TV documentary about the 'Beast of Gévaudan', so he was very familiar with the entire event. "If you carefully watch 'Crying Freeman', you'll find that although it's a gangster film, it has the style of a historical drama," Gans said, "I've always wanted to make a period piece, and the interesting thing about 'Crying Freeman' is that its plots and ideas are equally applicable to the Middle Ages, whereas 'Brotherhood of the Wolf' is the opposite; although the story takes place in the 18th century, the film contains contemporary themes."

Initially, the rough draft of the screenplay was merely a 20-page outline written by young screenwriter Stéphane Cabel, detailing the real events surrounding the 'Beast of Gévaudan'. At the time, Christophe Gans was collaborating with producer Samuel Hadida on another film after 'Crying Freeman'. Upon seeing the script, Gans was immediately captivated, believing all the elements possessed the qualities of a French adventure film, and decided to direct it personally.

Two months after seeing the initial draft, Gans worked alongside the author to rewrite the screenplay and invited Samuel Le Bihan and Mark Dacascos to join the cast. Previously, Dacascos had starred in Gans' 'Crying Freeman', while Le Bihan was seen by Gans as the perfect choice to elegantly yet combatively portray Francis.

Gans has always believed that heroic characters should have flaws, hence under his pen, Francis is both brave and immature, and quite clumsy when dealing with relationships with the opposite sex. Gans said: "Francis is a contradictory character, sometimes doing things that are hard to admire. Audiences like to see heroes make mistakes occasionally because many ordinary people might become heroes one day. In Stéphane Cabel's original draft, Francis was designed to be very perfect, and I've been considering creating a refreshing French hero."

Filming began on February 13, 2000, in Gers, France, with as many as 80 different locations used. There are no two identical weapons in the film because mass production of weapons was impossible at the time, so each weapon had to be custom-made, tailored to specific individuals and methods of use.

Fun Facts:

Universal Pictures acquired the U.S. distribution rights for $2 million, and during its limited release, the film grossed $10.9 million in the U.S., making it the second highest-grossing French-language film in the U.S. in 20 years.

Goofs:

Marquis is injured in his right arm while fighting the giant wolf, but later, his left arm is the one bandaged.

The soldiers in the movie use breech-loading muskets in 1766, a type of firearm that wasn't used until 90 years later during the American Civil War; flintlock muskets should have been used instead.
