A few days ago, I received (Part 2) from a friend and immediately updated it. The humorous and witty style remains unchanged, but the author's expertise in auditing is truly remarkable - thumbs up and admiration~

(Part 1)

Yesterday evening, I originally planned to go to bed early to prepare for the painful training courses this week.

But Rabbit forwarded me a video link, http://www.tudou.com/programs/view/VSS1t0DbspQ/, which made me watch until 11 PM...

The name of this video is "Audit Storm"... A Japanese TV drama claiming to be based on Japan's PwC. I first habitually googled some background information and found out that only one episode had just come out. Due to the handsome male lead, many young girls downloaded this first episode just to enjoy the eye candy. Only Rabbit and I were sobbing and shouting: Finally, we auditors are also on screen!! It's not easy!!

Out of curiosity about the first auditor-themed TV drama and skepticism about the profession... I opened this video on Tudou.com... The next scene left me speechless...

As the first episode, the show briefly introduced the scale of Japan's capital market and the significant public trust responsibility borne by accounting firms in society. Then it exaggeratedly introduced the powerful authority accountants possess (quite impressive, sounds like they can easily delist listed companies -___- I'm sweating... obviously misleading). The next line was: "This drama introduces how young accountants struggle and fight amidst massive financial statements and heavy responsibilities." Holy crap!!! I was clearly shocked and felt a sense of satisfaction after indulging in imagination... Feeling that my profession has somehow become so noble and full of challenges...

But TNMD (Chinese curse), before I could indulge in imagination for a few seconds, the next scene directly stunned me again... A fleet of 4-5 Crown cars drove by like a gangster group, parked, and a group of well-dressed beasts got out neatly. They looked exactly like henchmen and robbers. I thought there would be a street fight. But upon closer inspection, something was off - there were women? And this group of people marched into the building with their big boxes, where another group of neatly dressed businesspeople were waiting in line. When the two groups met, the "gangsters" stood still, and the other side bowed, saying, "Please take care of us." At this point, the chilling part came - the leader of the "gangsters" arrogantly shouted: "Hello, we are XXX Accounting Firm. Please let us start the year-end audit work immediately!" Holy crap, it's our peers!! So arrogant, wanting to start the job right away without even drinking tea, showing professionalism like hell. But isn't the actor's expression too professional... like coming to investigate a death case... Is this Japanese-specific hypocrisy? Don't try to get along with the client?!

Before he finished speaking, he boldly stepped into the meeting room beside the finance department, and the office environment was pretty nice. The beasts quickly opened their luggage cases, what could be so heavy... Files!!! Dozens of boxes of files... What the hell... Isn't this company small enough? Worth it? JV looks like IPO. Truly fake!!! Then the auditor migrant workers opened their handbags with military speed and took out a row of computers... Carefully looking, holy crap, DELL!!! Too local!!! How stingy are these Japanese auditors when it comes to equipment? I can only assume that Dell is the major sponsor of this TV drama...

Alright, the Final Audit begins... Apparently no control testing, straight to vouching, then a group of migrant workers started madly pressing calculators... What era is this... Still manual accounts and vouchers... And drawing pink checkmarks (-___-!!) on the ledger... I've lost words, so local... Ignoring internal controls and just flipping through vouchers... The scene switches to the warehouse... The handsome male lead starts inventory counting, loudly demanding the client open a sealed box to see if there are products inside. Another senior-looking woman (later known to be a CPA) counted a few thousand yen in cash and arrogantly demanded the client provide a cash flow statement to reconcile the book figures... My god, is talent used this way? No concept of materiality? Why not assign a junior staff member to do such silly tasks? Use a senior? The charge rate will definitely leave the client speechless...

The audit fieldwork supervisor gloomily flips through various ledgers in the background... This supervisor seems to be one of the main characters of the drama, Naoto Onodera... Indeed wild enough, often criticized by partners for being overly detailed (-____-!! Is this possible... Nowadays partners are not the least detailed, only more so...) This person represents the so-called "strict auditing faction" in the drama, having deep resentment towards some CPAs of the "fly-by-night" school, especially a very rough old CPA who only plays golf on-site and signs reports randomly...

The messy and unbearable audit site is quickly skipped over by fast-cutting scenes, then the camera directly switches to the audit meeting... Surprising dialogues come again. That Naoto Onodera, whether due to brain freeze or something else, said only two sentences throughout the meeting: "Please allow me to report the results of this audit... The problems we discovered and pointed out have not been properly adjusted by your company. Accordingly, XXX Accounting Firm issues a negative audit opinion on your company's financial statements..." Then the client's chairman jumped up... Five minutes later, the TV shows this chairman crying and jumping off a building... My god!!! The audit killed someone!!! Communication!!! Brother, how did you communicate with the client? Making them commit suicide rather than adjust the accounts?? You can't blame them... These days, I've only seen jobs refused... Never seen accounts not adjusted preferring a negative opinion... Even less seen clients crying and jumping off buildings right after the audit meeting...

At this point, the official TV drama prelude begins... A series of silly numbers scroll across the screen like in "The Matrix". One by one, pairs of eyes suddenly open behind the numbers!! Wow, so bright, who is this?! Damn, it's the auditors' eyes!! So sharp, so awesome!! The music is so stirring!! I couldn't help but indulge in infinite imagination and self-satisfaction... Until the accounting treatment in the following plot shocked me even more, waking me up...

(Part 2)

Today is freezing cold. Because in the afternoon, I discovered that this little article had already been reprinted and mass-mailed to everyone at the Big Four...

Almost everyone in the Training Room received this email containing my little article... Out of curiosity, I asked about the source, and the reply was: "It was forwarded from a friend at EY..." (I -___-!!)

Even the Training Manager mentioned "Audit Storm" today, referring to "gangsters" and such terms... Could it have been forwarded to them too? The speed of junk mail circulation within the firm is astonishing... And I have no choice but to continue writing my review of this Japanese TV drama... However, if any friends continue to forward it, please don't reveal the source. After all, this article is mainly for personal entertainment, full of loopholes, and contains many coarse words, unfit for formal occasions. Keep it low-key, keep it low-key.

In order to transition smoothly, let's review the shocking opening of the last episode. In short, summarized in sixteen words: Handsome debut, on-site verification, negative opinion, CEO jumps off building... The seemingly calm words are filled with silence and bloodshed... The story unfolds under this bizarre beginning...

Peak season always strongly compresses the feeling of time flying by, soon burning away a whole year. The young male lead of the drama has now become a Senior and is taking on his first role as the fieldwork supervisor for an audit, involving a real estate company.

In principle, doing it for the first time, one should be low-key and not too tough, otherwise, you might end up embarrassing yourself if the client gets upset... Everyone loses face.

I guessed this young handsome man originally thought the same... Just follow the procedures strictly, right... Get along well with the client, if there's no major risk, future relationships can further improve, paving the way for future juniors... OT a bit, report expenses, and the client won't have much to say. Occasionally, you can even... (see the picture...)

But if that's the case, why bother making the movie... If you happily skip audits, the audience will fall asleep... (Being a scriptwriter is frustrating, every episode must have drama...)

So here comes the plot... The young handsome man receives a mysterious tip-off call, claiming that the client's sales data is suspicious, half of it is fake, and threatening that if the audit dares to overlook it, they will expose the matter... Rather than letting it get worse, let's just ruin each other's reputations. Now this is serious... Fraud is severe... Such audacious listed companies in our smoggy great country's A-share market are probably rare, but for convenience, the scriptwriter makes this fraud seem as common as rice porridge... Japanese, I feel sorry for you guys...

Now, let's set aside the plot and briefly introduce what this fraud process entails. Actually... Very simple... Simple to the point of making you feel stupid...

First, the client built a batch of houses, unfinished... Then the client forged a batch of sales contracts, finally recognizing the revenue from the sale of these unfinished residences...

The motive for fraud mainly lies in inflating profits, making the operating conditions appear more satisfactory to obtain bank financing.

Thinking about it, real estate developers are indeed struggling... Not to mention the collapsed Japanese market, even in Shanghai where house prices are skyrocketing, developers frequently face broken capital chains, Wang Shi donated money and said some nonsense and was forced to reveal his poverty, and dared to donate projects but not cash. Cash flow is obviously tight, so committing fraud is understandable. If Shanghai developers didn't have funding issues, they could still make lots of money. I don't know the situation in Japan, but poor capital turnover will definitely result in unfinished buildings being abandoned... So the client resorted to this measure... But... This measure is too extreme... Still under construction, they directly recognized sales revenue, bold to the point of ignorance... If they hired a CFO from one of the Big Four, perhaps the level of falsifying accounts could be improved slightly, this kind of simple and brutal fake accounts is unacceptable...

The consequence of simplicity and brutality is panic... Three CPAs arrived on-site, with a very strong lineup, causing the client to be extremely nervous, the boss extremely fawning, smiles extremely ridiculous, eating and drinking extremely lavish, golf extremely pleasant...

After all, it's a relatively local enterprise, once the boss steps in, he knows the conventional means, deploying the four great weapons of eating, drinking, playing, and entertainment, quickly winning over that old CPA who signs reports after drinking a little old wine. (I'm very confused, are there really such dumb people in the world? Signing reports bears risks, even in local firms, it can sell for tens of thousands of yuan... Are you willing to bear the risk just by eating, drinking, and playing? Can't even calculate the costs, still a CPA? Was it that easy to pass the CPA exam back then?)

Unfortunately, all those good dishes went to waste. Once the old one was handled, the young ones wouldn't recognize it. This boss forgot that this TV drama is starring our young handsome man, this time the fieldwork supervisor isn't that old oil-soaked CPA anymore...

The young handsome man checked the sales contracts provided by the client based on the tip-off clue. Holy crap!! Signatures are all copied!! The young handsome man immediately felt the risk was significant, demanding to visit the site (just look, don't buy -___-)...

Seeing they couldn't cover it up, the client called the partner to complain, asking the old oil-soaked CPA to help deal with these two rookies, and sent people overnight to dismantle the scaffolding outside the unfinished building facade (they still knew not to fake it too much... dare to claim delivery and move-in with the scaffolding still up...) Resulting in one person falling to their death during dismantling, immediately declared a company employee to pressure the audit personnel...

After all the chaos, it was still impossible to block our righteous and penetrating gaze of the protagonist... Auditors watching here are truly elated... Refuse to adjust? No report!! (Isn't this the last resistance force for many seniors...?) The boss was naturally pushed to the limit, shouting loudly to cancel the job and switch firms!! Surrounding officials hurriedly advised, switching this firm would be quite bad... Equivalent to confessing without being questioned... The boss had no choice, thinking it was really TMD pathetic, being cornered by two rookies, resulting in him kneeling down to beg... All officials knelt collectively... (Sweating profusely again... First death, then kneeling... Auditors earned enough face...)

The result clearly maintained the impartiality of the audit... The brilliant image and shining eyes of the young handsome man once again made auditors unable to stop indulging in imagination...

But the interlaced appearance of the leading partner's hypocritical demeanor and the naive expressions of the two young officials sent by the National Audit Office to learn "strict auditing" made me burst out laughing uncontrollably...

Since this involves the large background of the story, we'll talk about it in Part 3...

In summary, this episode tells the story of a failed fraud case by a foolish client, leading to the young handsome man's struggles between humanity and morality, creating space for the plot development. But as auditors, we can mainly understand the following principles from it:

1. A company's finances should never be too foolish, making fake accounts at such a low level, if you don't die, who will?

2. Bosses who are overly fawning towards auditors are highly suspicious (of course, bosses who are overly difficult towards auditors aren't good either);

3. Judging the intention of fraud requires combining the company's fundamentals and potential risks and difficulties;

4. Control over the sales cycle is indeed crucial... Problems generally mean big issues (this seems obvious);

5. Eating and drinking at the client's expense, don't hesitate, but basic professional ethics must still be upheld;

6. Forgery of contracts is actually quite easy, don't think copying a contract is safe;

7. The client's competitors or archenemies often provide valuable fraud information... (this doesn't have much practical value);

Finally, please be realistic after entertaining yourselves, don't daydream about clients' CEOs kneeling down to you, dreaming about catching a major fraud every day, then laughing triumphantly while holding the client hostage. Most of the time, auditors are still fools... That's all for today... Will continue talking about this weird story when I have time.
