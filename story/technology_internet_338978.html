Special tips: *When rolling out the pancake, avoid using extra flour. Many people sprinkle some extra flour (dry flour) to prevent the pancake from sticking to the board. In fact, it is best to use oil instead of extra flour when making and rolling out pancakes, especially for spring pancakes. Using oil to roll out the pancake makes the cooked pancake very soft. Actually, not much oil is used because no oil is added during cooking.* 3. When rolling out the pancake, apply some vegetable oil on the board, place the pancake dough, and roll it thin evenly on both sides. 4. Place a flat pan on the stove and heat it over medium heat without adding any oil. Put the rolled-out large pancake in, cover the pot, and cook for about half a minute to one minute. Open the lid and see if the pancake has puffed up. Flip it over, cover the lid again, and cook the other side. The time should not be too long; flip it over as soon as it puffs up in the middle. It takes about two or three minutes to cook one pancake. This will result in two or three layers. (After putting the first pancake in the pot, start rolling out the second pancake. When you have rolled it halfway, flip the pancake that's already in the pot; at this point, it should have started puffing up. By the time you finish rolling out the second pancake, the first one can be taken out of the pot. Continue until all pancakes are cooked.) 5. Place the cooked pancakes in a covered container to keep them warm; they will remain very soft when eaten. When entertaining friends in larger groups, I usually make pancakes first and then cook dishes. To ensure the pancakes stay warm after cooking the dishes, place some hot water in a steamer, lay down a cloth on the rack, and put the cooked pancakes on top. The slight steam and water vapor will keep the pancakes warm and soft when eaten.

Looking at these single pancakes, also known as spring pancakes, they are extremely soft, almost translucently thin. When stacking three pancakes together to cook, the middle one becomes even softer, somewhat like a steamed pancake, perfect for elderly people and children. If most people prefer the thin and soft texture like the middle one, you can make steamed spring pancakes, and I will detail the method later... Two. Accompaniments for spring pancakes: Most accompaniments for spring pancakes are stir-fried dishes. The choices vary slightly across northern China. In Northeastern regions, when eating spring pancakes, four to five dishes are commonly stir-fried: typically including pork stir-fried with sour cabbage and vermicelli, shredded pork stir-fried with bean sprouts, stir-fried shredded potatoes, stir-fried eggs with chives, and sweet bean sauce shredded pork (originally using sliced preserved meat or braised meat, sometimes also made into sweet bean sauce shredded pork), along with green onion shreds and sweet bean sauce. Special recommendation: Use Guanzhen Sauce Garden's seafood sauce instead of sweet bean sauce, which tastes excellent, sweet with a hint of saltiness, delicate and delicious, perfectly matching with spring pancakes. The homemade sour cabbage, the taste is really good. Method: Select pork with a little fat and slice it into strips for reserve; shred the sour cabbage slices, rinse with cool water, and squeeze out most of the water for reserve; soak the vermicelli in cold water for an hour, cut into three-inch lengths for reserve; prepare chopped green onions, minced ginger, Sichuan pepper powder, soy sauce, and salt. Place the wok on the stove, turn on high heat and add two large soup spoons of vegetable oil. After the oil heats up, add the pork strips and stir-fry until they change color, then add chopped green onions, minced ginger, a little Sichuan pepper powder, two soup spoons of soy sauce, and a small amount of salt. Stir-fry a few times until the aroma comes out, then add the sour cabbage shreds and stir-fry for three minutes. Add chicken broth or water (about half a bowl), stir-fry a bit more, then add the soaked vermicelli. Stir-fry a few times and it's ready to serve. Note: Do not marinate the pork strips with cornstarch, cooking wine, and soy sauce before stir-frying the sour cabbage, as it would make the sour cabbage sticky and undercooked. Once the sour cabbage is cooked, add the vermicelli, which will naturally thicken the sauce.
