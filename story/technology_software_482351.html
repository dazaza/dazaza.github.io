Who should the commercial property rights of national athletes belong to?

by qqqoo60xs on 2011-08-27 19:19:47

To whom should the commercial property rights of national athletes belong? Liu Xiaoren

"The above post is purely malicious rumor." A statement from Nike Sports (China) Co., Ltd. directly refuted the so-called "conspiracy theory" that Liu Xiang's frequent commercial activities could only allow him to do some sporadic training in the intervals, and his physical condition could no longer meet the requirements of high-volume competitions; "Nike's US headquarters required Liu Xiang to withdraw from the race due to injury, greatly reducing the sense of non-recognition in consumers' minds if he failed to win a medal." It was claimed that Liu Xiang's withdrawal did not affect the trust of advertisers, and among the more than ten domestic and foreign brands he had signed with, including Nike, many brands quickly stated that their cooperation would not change due to his withdrawal. (August 21, 2008, China Youth Daily)

It was reported that www.xbbys.com currently has Liu Xiang signing contracts with more than ten domestic and foreign brands, including Nike, Coca-Cola, Visa, Yili, China Mobile, Ping An of China, Amway, etc., which is probably just part of his commercial activities. Whether these commercial activities are frequent enough to affect Liu Xiang's training and physical health, the media might as well pursue this further. What I want to discuss this time is: to whom should the commercial property rights of national athletes belong?

With the marketization of sports, famous athletes, especially Olympic champion-level athletes, have become hundreds of times more valuable and naturally became the target of competition for well-known domestic and foreign enterprises, becoming the "best partner" for commercial advertisers and the "seed candidate" for film and television cultural activities. Of course, it also brought substantial economic income to the athletes. We don't know how much Liu Xiang's annual advertising income is. Yao Ming's personal wealth reached 260 million RMB last year (August 7, Caijing magazine). Gymnastics star Li Xiaopeng currently endorses brands such as Li Ning, Pepsi Cola, KFC, Latitude Land Holdings Limited, Budweiser Beer, and his income in 2007 exceeded 2.5 million RMB (August 21, China Economic Network), although relatively small, it is still far beyond the reach of ordinary people.

It can be said that however rich sports stars may be, the Chinese public does not envy them, just like they don't envy Yuan Longping. Some foreign sports stars are incredibly wealthy, but the public will not suffer from "red eye disease". Many Olympic champions train out of love, spending their own money and using their own time, even spare time, neither professional competitors nor the result of heavy investment by the state. Like Luan Juji, who had to pay for everything herself, whether winning gold medals or making huge commercial profits, as long as she pays taxes according to regulations, others really have no right to criticize her. But in China, things are different.

The nationwide system of Chinese sports determines that athletes are trained in a "state way". Selected from an early age, entering various officially-run sports schools, enjoying generous salary and welfare benefits, syzjzx.school.snsyedu.com/news/1.html, participating in domestic and international competitions is fully funded by the state treasury. According to netizens' estimates, in China, the cost of cultivating an Olympic gold medal-level "thousand-gold" athlete is 700 million RMB (August 7, Caijing magazine). Therefore, the commercial income rights of athletes do not belong solely to individuals, but more to the state, which conforms to the game rule of who invests and who benefits. It was claimed that the General Administration of Sport and the former National Sports Commission had relevant regulations, where many business activity development rights of athletes in the national team belonged to the national team, and a significant portion of the athletes' related income must be remitted to the affiliated association. These incomes also include bonuses from some grand prix events. Although these regulations lack the extension of a "contract", they still contain reasonable cores. Although there is still a distance between these regulations and the commercial rights rules under market conditions, it is impossible to judge right and wrong without considering the national conditions of the nationwide system.

What urgently needs clarification is: what kind of contractual relationship exists between athletes and the state? How does the sports administration develop the commercial value of athletes while cultivating them to win gold medals? How to handle the relationship between sports training and commercial activities? How to deal with the income from commercial development to make the property rights clearer? And how to ensure the rights of individual citizens and make such property division have incentive and constraint effects? How can a win-win or multi-win situation be formed between athletes and the state? Including establishing what kind of reward mechanism, www.ttkgq.com, especially the welfare security system for retired athletes, etc.

Currently, regarding the "gold mine" of Chinese sports commerce, functional departments are not unaware, "holding a golden bowl and begging", and there is no phenomenon of indiscriminate exploitation and overfishing. Whether it is the national supervisory body or commercial enterprises, the commercial development of sports is still in the initial stage of feeling the stones to cross the river. In the context of the continuous maturity of China's market economy and the inevitable trend of integration with the international community, it is urgent to clarify the commercial development property rights of athletes to mobilize the enthusiasm of all parties. National sports organizations need to innovate management methods, use contracts, laws, systems, and other sound property models to fully tap the commercial potential of sports, enhance the self-hematopoietic ability of competitive sports, reduce the burden on the state, and at the same time eliminate the worries of retirement for more athletes. Our goal should be that, in the face of money, markets, and commercial forces, enrich more athletes and establish the backbone of Chinese sports.
