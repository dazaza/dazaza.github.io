[1] Don't wait for me to tell you before you act

After Premier Wen's previous statement "It's the people who are supporting you, so you better figure it out yourselves!", another comment during the government work report symposium has once again been warmly embraced by netizens: "Don't wait for me to tell you before you act."

Because the urban registered unemployment rate reported in the government work report was 4.6%, while the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences' Institute of Sociology published the Social Blue Book, which announced an urban surveyed unemployment rate of 9.4%. Why is there a 5.2% difference between the Ministry of Human Resources and Social Security and the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences unemployment statistics? The public has developed a strong curiosity about this. In light of this "squabbling" behavior, Premier Wen expressed his position:

"You in the Ministry of Labor and Social Security should hurry up and go online, or you should quickly hold a press conference, and explain the differences between the 4.6% and the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences' 9.4%. Don't wait for me to tell you before you act."

Why are these two numbers different? Li Peilin, director of the Institute of Sociology at the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences, explained it like this:

"For the past dozen years, there has always been a difference between the urban registered unemployment rate and the urban surveyed unemployment rate, usually the latter being higher than the former. When the job market is relatively stable, the two figures approach each other; when the employment situation is not good, there will be a large gap between the two figures. Because our unemployment insurance does not cover all urban workers, such as migrant workers who have not participated in unemployment insurance, they are difficult to be counted after becoming unemployed."

However, the Ministry of Human Resources and Social Security has another explanation:

"The 9.4% surveyed unemployment rate is the result of a sample survey of seven thousand people. Among these seven thousand people, there are 2288 urban economically active individuals, among whom there are more than 200 unemployed individuals. This number is derived from comparing these two figures."

In fact, for these two sets of data, we ordinary people all have our own opinions:

"Like me, graduates who have worked in the city for only a few years, my hukou (household registration) is still left at school. Where would I go to register as unemployed? After becoming unemployed, I can only quickly look for a new job myself, how dare I tarnish the national unemployment rate."

For this calculation method that does not include people who are neither employed nor registered, it has long been questioned by domestic and international scholars. Premier Wen should make them explain not just the severely underestimated urban registered unemployment rate, but give us a number that can withstand scrutiny.

[2] Is it because of "May Day" that they're squabbling?

Some departments indeed acted without waiting for the premier's instructions. After the Guangdong provincial government officially announced the "May Day" holiday plan, Hangzhou, Suzhou, and Nanjing proposed to jointly restore the May Day long holiday, making whether or not to restore the May Day Golden Week a focus of discussion.

In response to the current heated debate, the General Office of the State Council officially responded: "Do not arbitrarily restore the May Day long holiday."

Should the number of working days in a year be unified across the country? Although some comments refer to Germany's central government only specifying some unified statutory holidays, allowing the rest of the holidays to be determined by each state, suggesting whether the "May Day" holiday could be decided by local governments, have they considered whether this would suit China's national conditions under different political systems?

In fact, I just want to ask, why did various regions shout across the airwaves without reporting to the General Office for approval?

[3] Every province could save

The case of Yunnan police officer shooting someone to death, which has already garnered much attention, has recently caused controversy due to a settlement agreement. Mengzi County Public Security Bureau reached a reconciliation agreement with the deceased's family, paying a huge compensation of 550,000 yuan to the victim's family.

Regarding the case of police officer Ji Zhongchun shooting someone to death, the Mengzi County Public Security Bureau originally classified it as a personal action, completely unrelated to his identity and position as a police officer. Since it was a personal action, why did the Public Security Bureau pay the compensation? Deputy Director Zhao Xiangrong of the Mengzi County Public Security Bureau explained it like this:

"The reason the Public Security Bureau paid this amount is considering Ji Zhongchun's specific circumstances. Ji Zhongchun is an old police officer who has worked at the Public Security Bureau for over 20 years, with poor economic conditions at home. Out of humanitarian considerations, the Public Security Bureau first gave humanitarian compensation to the deceased's family. And this 550,000 yuan is the office expenses saved by the Public Security Bureau."

It is well known that the incident occurred because Ji Zhongchun had a dispute while reversing his car. How can a police officer who owns a private car be defined as having poor economic conditions? This cannot help but raise the question: what exactly constitutes good economic conditions?

According to Article 14 of the "Regulations on the Use of Police Equipment and Weapons by the People's Police," if a police officer illegally uses weapons, causing unnecessary casualties and property damage, the agency to which the police officer belongs shall compensate according to the relevant provisions of the "State Compensation Law." Why doesn't the Mengzi Public Security Bureau admit that this compensation is state compensation? If it indeed isn't, why does the Public Security Bureau use taxpayers' money to "help privately"?

[6] Overblown internet entrepreneurship

Suddenly, as if overnight spring breeze came, millions of online shops sprouted everywhere. In the current economic crisis, the internet seems to have become an important way to solve employment problems. Not only do media outlets repeatedly promote online employment as a great path for students, even during the Two Sessions, representatives suggested that the state should subsidize college students starting businesses on Taobao.

Low cost, low investment, low risk, internet entrepreneurship became an overnight darling. Many domestic institutions have opened online shop training courses to help students start businesses. Yes, if everyone starts following the trend and opens online shops now, the employment problem can be solved immediately, but what about one year later, ten years later? How many people can truly succeed? Let alone issues like medical insurance and social security. And cybersecurity and legal standardization have always been the most overlooked difficulties in the information economy tide. Are our experts and professors considering this point while advocating for college students to choose internet entrepreneurship?
