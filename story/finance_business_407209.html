One, Flexible use of "Food Exchange Portions Method"

"Food Exchange Portions" is a concept in nutrition. Any food that generates 90 kilocalories of heat is considered one "Food Exchange Portion". In other words, each "Food Exchange Portion" contains 90 kilocalories of heat, but their weights can differ. For example, one "Food Exchange Portion" of food is equivalent to 25 grams of rice or flour, 500 grams of leafy vegetables, 200 grams of fruit, 125 milliliters of milk, 50 grams of lean meat, 50 grams of eggs, or 10 grams of oil, etc.

Foods are divided into four major categories and eight subcategories according to their origin and nutritional components. The proteins, fats, and carbohydrates ratios within the same category are roughly equivalent. Different foods within the same category can be exchanged based on "Food Exchange Portions" while maintaining the same calories. For instance, in the grain and potato category, 1 taels of rice can be exchanged with 1 tael of white flour, 1 tael of cornmeal, 1 tael of dry vermicelli, or 6-8 soda crackers. In the meat and egg category, 1 tael of lean pork can be exchanged with 1 egg, 2 taels of fish or shrimp, 2 taels of dried tofu, or 5 taels of tofu.

When the nutrient structure of different categories is similar, they can also be exchanged. For example, 25 grams of oatmeal can be exchanged with 200 grams of oranges, as they have similar heat and carbohydrate content.

Therefore, by using the Food Exchange Portions method, diabetic patients can more freely choose different foods, enjoy various delicacies, and make their diet less monotonous.

Two, Watch the dietary "Traffic Light"

The Food Exchange Portions method is designed to help diabetic patients control total energy intake and maintain and balance energy levels. However, although the provided exchangeable foods in the Food Exchange Portions method have the same heat, their glycemic index may not be the same. Therefore, the Food Exchange Portions method only provides uniformity in heat and does not show the impact on blood glucose generation, which is a drawback of this method. To address this shortcoming, nutritionists have introduced the concept of Glycemic Index (GI).

Different foods have different glycemic indices. Patients consuming low-glycemic-index foods not only gain the same nutrition but also have lower blood glucose compared to consuming high-glycemic-index foods. Its application provides a more scientific way and means for diabetic patients to control blood glucose through diet. Choosing low-glycemic-index foods within the Food Exchange Portions allows patients to easily control energy intake without raising blood glucose, achieving both goals simultaneously.

Here's a simple method we can call the "Traffic Signal Light" for diabetic diets. For example, whole wheat steamed buns made from wheat are green light, meaning you can eat them; if refined into white flour and made into white steamed buns, it becomes yellow light, meaning you shouldn't eat too much; if butter and sugar are added to make cookies, it turns into red light, meaning it's best not to eat them. This signal light theory originates from the glycemic index of diabetic diets. A lower index is beneficial for controlling blood glucose. When the food glycemic index is less than 55, the green light is on.

The key factor affecting the glycemic index of food is the type of food and cooking methods. Elaborate cooking, slow simmering, heavy oil, and strong flavors bring food closer to the red light. So sometimes, shortcuts can be taken in the kitchen; generally, tubers and vegetables should not be cut too small or mashed. Additionally, the hardness, ripeness, consistency, and particle size of food all affect the glycemic index. For example, quick steaming and eating when cooked results in a green light; however, if overcooked, the red light comes on because absorption is faster, leading to higher blood glucose levels.

Food pairing is also very important. The following methods can turn red lights into green:

① Clever pairing of meat and vegetables, such as eating buns instead of steamed buns, eating dumplings instead of noodles. Proteins in meat and dietary fiber in vegetables can slow down absorption, providing nutrition while keeping blood glucose rise slow;

② Adding a bit of vinegar when eating vegetables, vinegar lowers the glycemic index, and adding lemon juice has the same effect.
