Mother and daughter travel to Guangzhou

by takxpkfj on 2010-04-14 11:27:52

Recently on vacation, on the 23rd I suddenly got this idea to escape the cold by going to Guangdong. Since the menfolk all have their big or small businesses, or rather big or small matters, I managed to get two plane tickets for the 24th and flew with my mom to Guangzhou at around seven in the evening. Let me say something about Shandong Airlines' in-flight meals: not eating is wasteful, but eating makes you feel nauseous; next time I'll just skip it altogether. Traffic at Baiyun Airport is very convenient; we took Line 5 and got off at Jinhan International Exhibition Center (16 yuan per person). A few more minutes walking south brought us to the pre-booked Jinjiang Inn Zongshan Memorial Hall branch. The facilities here are decent, and it's also relatively clean. Before ten o'clock that night, both my mom and I were quite satisfied, but things weren't so good when it came to sleeping. Liberation North Road in front of the door is a major thoroughfare, and there's also an overpass on the road. All night long, we heard various vehicles speeding by and braking. We wanted to switch to a room across the hall, but the staff said they were all full, so there was no switching available. We could only move to a higher floor, so we moved from the 12th floor to the 18th floor, but it didn't help much. We didn't sleep well and decided to either change rooms or check out the next day. (Room rate: member price 197 yuan) On the morning of the 25th, the staff still said there was nothing to switch to, so we decided to go play at Baiyun Mountain first and come back at noon to see. Breakfast was solved on the third floor of Yue Xiu Tian An Grand Hotel at the intersection of Liberation North Road and Panfu Road. This place does authentic local cuisine: Teochew style steamed fruit, oyster sauce BBQ pork buns, Zhuangyuan Jidi porridge, etc., all eaten very comfortably. Each person paid a tea fee of 4 yuan, totaling 35 yuan. During the few days in Guangzhou, we ate breakfast here every day, whether coming at 7 or 9, it was always packed with people. There was an auntie around forty years old, her employee number seemed to be 159, who smiled brightly and patiently recommended various local snacks to us. We took bus No. 24 to Baiyun Mountain. Admission fee was 5 yuan, which surprised me a bit. Walking up the mountain, getting off the cable car, Baiyun Mountain was like a public fitness area. Every few steps you'd encounter a group playing shuttlecock together, then another group practicing Tai Chi, then another practicing rackets... The plants on the mountain were great, and the Guangzhou Stele Forest at the top made my mom very happy. Scenic spots like "The First Peak Under Heaven", "Pu River", "Nine Dragon Walls", and "Cloud Ripple" were all pretty good. If I were to rate it out of five stars, I'd give it three. Lunch was had at a fast food restaurant at the foot of the mountain: fragrant ribs rice, braised beef brisket with pig intestines rice. After lunch, we took bus No. 24 back. The Jinjiang Inn staff still said there were no rooms to switch to, making me a little angry, so I said let's check out. The staff then said they'd try to find one for me, and sure enough, they found one. Truly... So we moved my mom to the nearby Gui Zheng Hotel. One side wasn't facing the road, quiet, and the conditions were also pretty good. In the afternoon, we took the subway from Memorial Hall Station to Huangsha, crossed the overpass, and arrived at the famous Shamian Island. The artistic atmosphere was somewhat like Beijing's 798 Art Zone, and the architectural features were somewhat like Qingdao's Badaguan. It was a place full of sentiment where one couldn't hurry. Surprisingly, right in the center of this fresh place was a "Shamian Primary School". It was exactly dismissal time, and BMWs, Volvos, etc., all came to pick up the kids. Studying here truly made one envious. Just in time for Christmas, the Shamian Church was decorated particularly beautifully. Saying goodbye to Shamian, we took the subway from Huangsha Station to Changshou Road, walked to Guangzhou's most famous Shangxiajiu Street, a bustling scene. Here, you must not miss the "Nanxin Milk Dessert Shop", which is incredibly delicious. Ginger milk pudding is our favorite. On the 26th, we visited the South China Botanical Garden, Yuexiu Park, and the Nanyue King Mausoleum. My mom loves plants and also relics, so she was quite happy at the South China Botanical Garden and the Nanyue King Mausoleum, but these places didn't really interest me. The admission fee for the Nanyue King Mausoleum was only 12 yuan, and history enthusiasts should definitely not miss it, as there are many treasures inside. Yuexiu Park has free entry, and it's a landmark scenic spot. The Five Rams sculpture is a symbol of Guangzhou, but other than that, there's not much else interesting. Dinner was at Ruan Fishlet on Panfu Road, a very nice little restaurant. Drum roll... Drum roll!!! Below, I will introduce Zhaoqing grandly. The biggest gain from this trip was Zhaoqing; unexpectedly, Qixing Rock and Dinghu Mountain turned out to be so enchanting, leaving one reluctant to leave. On the morning of the 27th, after breakfast, we took the subway to the provincial long-distance bus station, bought tickets for the bus from Guangzhou to Zhaoqing, 43 yuan each, one every fifteen minutes, very convenient, arriving at Zhaoqing Bus Station in about two hours, not far from the city center. We had lunch at a chain store called Shuihegu, then walked to the Qixing Rock Archway. The archway was large and magnificent. Continuing straight ahead led us to the southern gate of Qixing Rock. That day, it was drizzling slightly, adding a special charm to the lake and mountain scenery. Tianzhu Rock is the tallest peak among the various peaks of Qixing Rock. Considering my mom's physical strength, I was initially a bit worried that we might not reach the top, but Mom always pulls through in crucial moments. Walking and resting intermittently, resting and walking again, we enjoyed the various plants hanging off the cliffs, passed a steep path akin to the eighteen bends of Mount Tai, and finally climbed to Zhaixing Pavilion! Tianzhu Rock isn't actually that tall, but the path up is sufficiently steep, requiring some effort. Also, those with acrophobia shouldn't attempt it. From Zhaixing Pavilion, one can take in the beautiful views of the various peaks of Qixing Rock, see the urban landscape of Zhaoqing, feel so close to the sky that breathing becomes smooth, gaze far into the mist-covered green mountains resembling a fairyland, and look down to see the rippling emerald water beneath, dreamlike and fantastical. With us at Zhaixing Pavilion were three university students, making Mom's perseverance even more astonishing in comparison, haha. Fortunately, the weather cooperated. After climbing to the peak amidst light rain and enjoying the rainy beauty of Star Rock, the sun slowly peeked out, presenting another set of scenes. The distant mountains were ink-green like Chinese paintings, the nearby trees fresh and verdant, the lake water crystal clear to the bottom. Especially the metasequoia in the lake, standing brownish-red in the midst of all that green, looking exceptionally vivid under the sunlight. Fellow travelers must not miss the stunning views from the summit of Tianzhu Rock; if my mom over fifty can climb up, young people have no excuse not to. Following the indicated path down the mountain, we then went to see the Fish Pool and Xianxia Cave, walking along the western route past Star Rock Rain Mist. Each scene did not disappoint, and I deeply regretted not bringing my DSLR... After exiting the west gate, we took a vehicle to Taining North Road in the city center, ate some snacks at a nameless dessert shop, and resolved dinner at a fast-food restaurant. Then we hopped on bus No. 3 heading towards the Dinghu Mountain Scenic Area to meet Aunt He. Aunt He (phone number 13679589599) has some fame on various travel websites. I also saw a guide written by netizens on Ctrip and contacted her. Spending one night at Aunt He's homestay, I immediately decided that if friends come next time, I would recommend them to stay here. Aunt He is over fifty years old, and her small house is located within the Dinghu Mountain Scenic Area. After getting off the bus at the Kangkou Gas Station, Aunt He drove her own car to pick us up, directly leading us into the scenic area, saving us 120 yuan for two people's entrance tickets. Next to the small building was a babbling brook, and the sound of running water could be heard while sleeping. Trees surrounded the brook on both sides, and plucking any branch and placing it in a bottle created a picturesque scene. Stone tables and chairs were placed by the brook bank, and breakfast was eaten sitting there. The bridge, flowing water, and houses gave a refreshing and serene feeling. The rooms were very tidy, and outside the bedroom was a living room. Calligraphy, paintings, fruits, sofas, TVs—it truly felt like home. On the morning of the 28th, Aunt He kindly prepared breakfast for us, including glutinous rice dumplings, steamed buns, and soy milk. Having eaten heartily, we began ascending the mountain. Since we planned to spend the whole day at Dinghu Mountain, we walked leisurely, following the pedestrian path to Feipu Tan, where Sun Yat-sen once swam. Every breath we took was extraordinarily fresh, and upon looking up, we realized it was a negative ion oxygen zone—no wonder, haha. Alongside the pool stood a line of people practicing Tai Chi, truly knowing how to choose their location. Passing through Shuangqian Feihong, we reached the top at Bao Ding Garden, where various sizes and types of caldrons were displayed. Comparatively speaking, I still prefer natural landscapes. From Bao Ding Garden to Butterfly Valley, since it was autumn and winter now, there were few butterflies, so we didn't enter the valley to play, but the Ding Lake where Butterfly Valley is located greatly satisfied my eyes. A sign beside read "Guilin's mountains, Jiuzhai's waters." Although it couldn't compare with Jiuzhai, the water was indeed breathtakingly beautiful, emerald green and crystal clear, containing no impurities whatsoever. We descended the mountain via electric vehicle, passing through Qingyun Temple, where we had vegetarian fast food recommended by Aunt He at the dining hall before returning by vehicle. Not far outside the Dinghu Mountain Scenic Area was the "Zhaoqing East Bus Station," where we took a bus to Yuexiu South Bus Station to return to Guangzhou. We then went to Shangxiajiu Street to feast at Nanxin again, taking some dim sum from Lianxianglou before returning to the hotel. On the morning of the 29th, we returned via Shenzhen Airlines flight.
