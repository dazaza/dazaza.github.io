Summary of Corporate Management Practices

Discussions on corporate management have existed for a long time, but systematic research began in the 1980s. A comprehensive review of domestic and international literature reveals that research on corporate governance has become quite extensive. This article attempts to incorporate these findings into a unified analytical framework.

### I. The Essence and Benefit Orientation of Corporate Governance

#### 1. The Essence of Corporate Governance

Berle and Means (1932) as well as Jensen and Meckling (1976) believed that corporate governance should focus on resolving the relationship between owners and operators, with the core being the alignment of their interests. Fama and Jensen (1983) further proposed that corporate governance addresses agency problems under the separation of ownership and control, with the central issue being how to reduce agency costs. Shleifer and Vishny (1997) believed that corporate governance deals with how capital providers ensure they receive returns on investment, considering the protection of the interests of capital providers (including shareholders and creditors) as the central topic. These scholars focused on the benefits of owners (generally shareholders), advocating the "shareholder governance model."

Cochran and Wartick (1988) believed that corporate governance addresses various specific issues arising from interactions among senior management, shareholders, boards of directors, and other stakeholders. Blair (1995) defined corporate governance as a set of legal, cultural, and institutional arrangements concerning the distribution of corporate control or residual claims. These arrangements determine the objectives of the company, who owns it, how it is controlled, and how risks and rewards are distributed among constituents, including shareholders, creditors, employees, users, suppliers, and the communities in which the company operates. These scholars placed stakeholders on an equal footing with shareholders, thus advocating the "stakeholder governance model."

#### 2. Benefit Orientation

##### (a) Shareholder Governance Model and Maximization of Shareholder Benefits

The shareholder governance model sets the maximization of shareholder benefits as the company's goal. Its underlying assumption is that in a typical corporation, shareholders receive "residual returns" and bear "residual risk," thereby achieving the maximization of shareholder returns also achieves the maximization of social wealth. However, there are two perspectives on how to achieve this goal (Blair, 1995):

- **First Perspective: Financial Model**

The financial model assumes that companies are owned by shareholders and therefore should be governed according to shareholder interests. It seeks to make managers more accountable to shareholders, especially in Berle and Means corporations. Advocates of this model believe that policy incentives and actions aimed at maximizing short-term stock prices best serve shareholder interests because they trust that today's stock price reflects the best market assessment of future profits and growth. Its theoretical basis is the "Efficient Market Hypothesis." Thus, they advocate for an unrestricted market for corporate control and increasing shareholder power.

- **Second Perspective: Market Myopia**

Proponents of market myopia argue that financial market pressures lead corporate managers to focus only on short-term gains. This can result in operational decisions that harm the company's long-term management and reduce the value of its long-term assets. Robert Hayes and William Abernathy (1980) pointed out that American companies suffer from "competitive myopia," including overemphasis on short-term cost measures as criteria for evaluating managerial performance. While financial model advocates want to increase shareholder oversight and influence, market myopia proponents hope that corporate governance protects managers from shareholder pressures, particularly short-term stock price performance, or alternatively, through trading restrictions and encouragement of long-term stock holding, aims to realize shareholder interests. Both sides agree that maximizing shareholder benefits leads to the maximization of overall social welfare.

##### (b) Stakeholder Governance and Maximization of Social Wealth

The stakeholder governance model believes that the maximization of social wealth should be the goal of corporate governance. Blair (1995) argued that in most modern corporations, shareholders bear limited liability. Shareholder risks can be mitigated through diversified investments or by choosing to exit, transferring part of the residual risk to creditors and other stakeholders. When shareholders do not bear all residual risks, the assumptions of the shareholder governance model fail, and the maximization of shareholder benefits cannot guarantee the maximization of social wealth.

Blair believed that despite flaws in the assumption that shareholders receive all residual returns and bear all residual risks, if those who monitor and control the company receive (at least partially) residual returns and bear (partially) residual risks, and if stakeholders sharing residual returns and bearing residual risks are granted monitoring rights, then the joint-stock company can achieve the maximization of social wealth.

Cui Zhiyuan (1996) noted that since the 1980s, 29 U.S. states have revised their corporate laws. The new laws require managers to serve stakeholders, not just shareholders. Stiglitz (1995) believed that companies have multiple stakeholders, and the objective of the company is not to maximize corporate value but to meet the diverse needs of various stakeholders. Company decisions are the result of the combined efforts of multiple stakeholders. He believed that the shareholder-centric theory neglects many stakeholders, including the government.
