I. Introduction to Photoelectric Switches

Photoelectric switches are members of the sensor family. They convert changes in light intensity between the emitter and receiver into changes in current to achieve detection purposes. Since the output circuit and input circuit of photoelectric switches are electrically isolated (i.e., insulated), they can be applied in many scenarios.

II. Introduction to Photoelectric Switches

1. Working Principle of Photoelectric Switches

The photoelectric switch (photoelectric sensor) is a short form of photoelectric proximity switch. It uses the object's obstruction or reflection of a light beam, which is then selected by a synchronous circuit to detect the presence of an object. The object is not limited to metal; any object that can reflect light can be detected. The photoelectric switch converts the input current into a light signal at the emitter, and the receiver detects the target object based on the strength or absence of the received light. 2. Classification and Term Explanation of Photoelectric Switches

(1) Classification of Photoelectric Switches

① Diffuse Reflective Photoelectric Switch: This is a sensor that integrates both the emitter and receiver. When a detection object passes through, it reflects enough light back to the receiver, causing the photoelectric switch to generate a switching signal. When the surface of the detection object is bright or highly reflective, the diffuse reflective photoelectric switch is the preferred detection mode.

② Mirror Reflective Photoelectric Switch: This also integrates the emitter and receiver. The light emitted by the photoelectric switch is reflected back to the receiver via a mirror. When a detection object passes through and completely blocks the light, the photoelectric switch generates a detection signal.

③ Through-beam Photoelectric Switch: It includes an emitter and receiver structurally separated but aligned along the same optical axis. The light from the emitter directly enters the receiver. When a detection object passes between the emitter and receiver and blocks the light, the photoelectric switch generates a switching signal. For detecting opaque objects, the through-beam photoelectric switch is the most reliable detection device.

④ Slot-type Photoelectric Switch: It typically adopts a standard U-shaped structure, with the emitter and receiver located on opposite sides of the U-shaped slot, forming an optical axis. When a detection object passes through the U-shaped slot and blocks the optical axis, the photoelectric switch generates a switching signal. Slot-type photoelectric switches are suitable for detecting high-speed moving objects and can distinguish between transparent and semi-transparent objects, ensuring safe and reliable operation.

⑤ Fiber Optic Photoelectric Switch: It uses plastic or glass fiber optic sensors to guide light and can detect distant objects. Typically, fiber optic sensors are divided into through-beam and diffuse reflective types.

(2) Term Explanation of Photoelectric Switches

Common terms are illustrated in Figure 4.

① Detection Distance: This is the spatial distance measured from the reference position (the sensing surface of the photoelectric switch) to the detection surface when the switch operates as the detection body moves in a certain way. The rated action distance refers to the nominal value of the proximity switch's action distance.

② Hysteresis Distance: The absolute value of the difference between the action distance and the reset distance.

③ Response Frequency: Within the specified 1-second time interval, this is the number of cycles the photoelectric switch is allowed to operate.

④ Output State: Divided into normally open and normally closed. When there is no detection object, the load connected to the normally open type photoelectric switch does not work due to the cutoff of the internal output transistor of the photoelectric switch. When an object is detected, the transistor conducts, and the load works.

⑤ Detection Method: Based on the different paths by which the light emitted by the photoelectric switch is reflected back to the receiver during object detection, methods include diffuse reflective, mirror reflective, and through-beam.

⑥ Output Form: Common output forms include NPN two-wire, NPN three-wire, NPN four-wire, PNP two-wire, PNP three-wire, PNP four-wire, AC two-wire, AC five-wire (with built-in relay), and DC NPN/PNP/normally open/normally closed multifunctional outputs.

⑦ Pointing Angle: As shown in the pointing angle diagram of the photoelectric switch, i.e., the three small diagrams at the bottom of Figure 4.

⑧ Surface Reflectivity: The light emitted by the diffuse reflective photoelectric switch needs to be reflected back to the receiver of the diffuse reflective switch via the surface of the detection object. Therefore, the detection distance and the surface reflectivity of the detection object will determine the intensity of the light received by the receiver. The intensity of the light reflected back from a rough surface will definitely be less than that from a smooth surface. Moreover, the surface of the detection object must be perpendicular to the emitted light of the photoelectric switch. The reflectivity of common materials is shown in Table 1.

⑨ Environmental Characteristics: The environment in which the photoelectric switch is used can also affect its long-term reliability. When the photoelectric switch operates at the maximum detection distance, the optical lens may be contaminated by environmental dirt or even corroded by some strong acids, thereby reducing its usage parameters and reliability. A relatively simple solution is to determine the optimal operating distance by derating based on the maximum detection distance (Sn) of the photoelectric switch.

(3) Precautions for Using Photoelectric Switches

① Infrared sensors belong to the diffuse reflective type, and the standard detection object used is flat white drawing paper.

② Infrared photoelectric switches can stably operate under high environmental illumination conditions, but in principle, they should avoid direct sunlight or other strong light sources shining on the sensor's optical axis.

③ The minimum detectable width for through-beam photoelectric switches is 80% of the width of the lens of such photoelectric switches.

④ When using inductive loads (such as lamps, motors, etc.), their transient impact currents are large, possibly degrading or damaging the AC two-wire photoelectric switch. In such cases, please use an AC relay to convert the load.

⑤ The lens of the infrared photoelectric switch can be wiped with lens paper, and diluted solvents or other chemicals should be avoided to prevent permanent damage to the plastic lens.

⑥ According to the actual requirements of the user's field, in some harsh environments such as dusty areas, the sensitivity of the produced photoelectric switches has been increased by 50% to extend the maintenance cycle of the photoelectric switch during long-term use.

⑦ All products are manufactured using SMD technology and undergo strict testing before leaving the factory. Under normal circumstances, they will not be damaged. To avoid unexpected incidents, please check the wiring before connecting the power supply and verify whether the voltage matches the rated value.

III. Application Examples of Photoelectric Switches

(a) Photoelectric switches are used for material positioning and cutting control;

(b) Photoelectric switches control the upper and lower limit values of liquid levels. When the liquid level is higher or lower than the upper or lower extreme levels, the photoelectric switch control circuit can open or close the valve, keeping the liquid level height within the upper and lower limits;

(c) Utilizing the object's blocking effect on light, the passage count or existence of the object is detected;

(d) Using the straight-line propagation of light, checks if the products are arranged at equal heights;

(e) Photoelectric switches are used on production lines to detect the quantity of products;

(f) Photoelectric switches detect the height of the liquid level.

IV. Conclusion of Photoelectric Switches

In addition to the examples introduced above, photoelectric switches have applications in many other areas, such as travel control, diameter limitation, rotational speed detection, airflow control, etc. We believe that photoelectric switches will become increasingly advanced, and their applications will become increasingly extensive.
