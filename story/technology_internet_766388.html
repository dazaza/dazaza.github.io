Which major e-commerce platforms have been hacked: JD.com, Vancl, Taobao

by anonymous on 2013-09-11 14:39:46

On September 9, CNNIC officially responded to the attack on .CN domain names. Hacker attacks have always been an industry-wide issue and have never ceased since the development of the Internet. Last month, a small e-commerce site named Waga Mall was also attacked by hackers, resulting in the loss of user data. To avoid further losses, the site had to be shut down for several days and still cannot operate normally today.

Event Recap

In early August, before the CNNIC attack, the social software Cihi (Hi Chat) collaborated with a local e-commerce site to provide users with a limited-time flash sale event. To make the event more attractive, Cihi (Hi Chat) proposed a daily earning activity of 1 yuan. However, due to the limited number of products provided during the event and the large number of participants, some users failed to obtain the desired items on the day of the event. One user, expressing dissatisfaction, resorted to hacking methods, leading to this incident of a hacker attack on the e-commerce site.

Hacker attacks on e-commerce sites are common, but Waga Mall is a small e-commerce site with relatively weak overall technical strength. As a result, the hackers not only infiltrated the website's backend but also maliciously altered the website code, forcing Waga Mall to close down. In response, they had no choice but to report the incident to the police. Recently, the latest news indicates that the police have filed a case and entered the investigation phase.

What started as a normal commercial activity resulted in the collaborating e-commerce site being taken down by an "angry" hacker. For Hi Chat, this can be considered collateral damage. Currently, Hi Chat has ceased its cooperation with the mall.

Historical Events of Hacker Attacks on E-Commerce

In fact, hacker attacks on e-commerce have never stopped; most attacks have not been successful. Unfortunately, Waga Waga Mall was breached this time. Looking back at past incidents, we can find many reports of hacker attacks on e-commerce sites.

In April of this year, the second-largest U.S. group-buying site, LivingSocial, suffered a hacker attack, causing the leakage of data from 50 million users. Fortunately, there was no financial loss. However, LivingSocial is currently facing operational difficulties, and the hacker attack causing customer data leakage has added insult to injury.

Looking further back, in March of this year, Jumei Youpin, a domestic cosmetics e-commerce platform, encountered a website crash during its third-anniversary celebration. Subsequently, some speculated that LeFeng.com, a competitor, launched a promotional event called "Peach Blossom Fate" at the same time, which benefited greatly from Jumei's crash. It was even suggested that 360, LeFeng's strategic partner, might have been behind the attack. However, there was no conclusive evidence, and 360 found itself unfairly implicated, turning the incident into a public relations battle. In fact, the hacker defense war between LeFeng and Jumei goes beyond these incidents. Back in June of last year, Li Jing, founder of LeFeng.com, criticized on Weibo that LeFeng had been under sustained hacker attacks, pointing the finger at their competitor Jumei Youpin. Since it's difficult to identify the perpetrators of hacker attacks, such incidents usually end unresolved, and both e-commerce companies seem to use the topic of hacker attacks as a PR campaign.

Speaking of larger e-commerce enterprises, Alibaba Group's small foreign trade platform AliExpress was also hit by a large-scale hacker attack in April 2010. Alibaba stated that the purpose of launching AliExpress was to "wholesale China's high-quality, low-cost goods to the U.S." This event was generally suspected to be initiated by overseas trade protection organizations. Afterward, Alibaba did not disclose the final results, although it could not be ruled out as a PR stunt for the launch of AliExpress.

Going back another decade to 2012, when e-commerce was just beginning to emerge internationally, a British e-commerce site, CloudNine, was attacked by hackers, preventing the site from recovering operations and eventually declaring bankruptcy. Its remaining affairs were taken over by its competitor Zetnet. This CloudNine became the first British e-commerce company to go bankrupt due to a hacker attack.

In the initial stages of the e-commerce industry boom in China from 2009 to 2011, many of today's large e-commerce platforms were breached to varying degrees. However, with the rapid development of the industry, all major e-commerce platforms place great emphasis on backend protection, investing substantial funds to enhance system security. Just a few days ago, Alibaba offered an annual salary of 600,000 RMB to recruit top graduates in technology. Currently, the technical systems of major e-commerce platforms are increasingly sophisticated, and there have been almost no reports of large e-commerce platforms being hacked this year, though it's possible that some breaches went unreported.

Larger e-commerce platforms are more profitable targets for hackers than smaller ones.

Reports of hacker attacks on e-commerce are not common. First, successful attacks causing widespread impact are rare. Second, attacking smaller e-commerce platforms offers little profit, so hackers prefer targeting larger ones. Even if larger e-commerce platforms are compromised, they often choose not to disclose the information unless it serves a PR purpose. Using hacker attacks as a publicity tool is not uncommon.

From a technical structure perspective, smaller e-commerce platforms are generally easier to breach, but for hackers, attacking smaller e-commerce sites is not cost-effective. The example of Waga Mall mentioned earlier occurred because an activity angered a user with hacking skills, leading to the site's shutdown. Therefore, smaller e-commerce platforms are relatively safer. Although they lack robust technical systems, they are usually not the primary targets of hackers. Larger e-commerce platforms, on the other hand, are like silent golden bulls to hackers.

Large e-commerce platforms generally do not respond to hacker attacks. Firstly, ordinary hacker attacks are insufficient to pose a substantial threat to large e-commerce platforms. Secondly, hacker attacks are common and not worth mentioning. Even if compromised without causing significant events, large e-commerce websites typically choose to cover up the incidents. Such events, if magnified, can easily attract public scrutiny, cause user panic, lead to investor doubts, and severely damage brand image. Therefore, they usually opt to minimize or ignore minor issues. For more persistent hackers, paying some protection fees can help avoid unnecessary trouble.

In reality, hacking e-commerce is not the most profitable path. Some private game servers are the main targets of hackers. Since private servers themselves are in the gray area of the internet, hackers demanding protection fees from server operators are safer and yield higher profits. E-commerce can only be considered a side gig for hackers.

Conclusion

Hackers' capabilities vary, and their purposes differ. Some steal user balances, others database information, some seek financial gain, while others aim to vent frustrations. Overall, there have been no official reports this year of large domestic e-commerce platforms being paralyzed by hacker attacks. Hackers generally avoid attacking smaller e-commerce platforms. The previously mentioned Waga Waga Mall can only regretfully learn from its mistake of unintentionally angering a hacker. Other start-up e-commerce platforms can take this as an example, proceeding cautiously when conducting activities to avoid unnecessary trouble and potentially fatal consequences.
