The Relationship between Toxic Fungal Vaginitis, Seasons and Diet (March 31, 2010, 1:36 PM)

How does toxic fungal vaginitis relate to seasons and diet? These seemingly unrelated aspects are connected in ways explained by experts from the Air Force 466 Hospital. Through the following content, readers will gain a clear understanding of the pathogenesis of vaginitis.

Spring: High Reproduction Period for Candida

Candida albicans is a conditionally pathogenic fungus that resides in many parts of the human body, such as the vagina, mouth, and intestines. Normally, it does not cause symptoms unless the local environment changes, such as increased glycogen and acidity in the vagina or decreased immunity of local immune cells, which create conditions favorable for Candida growth and cause inflammation. Warm and humid environments are especially conducive to the growth and reproduction of Candida. Some women prefer tight nylon pants, jeans, synthetic underwear, or tights. These clothes obstruct the discharge of vaginal secretions and, due to poor breathability and water absorption, prevent the absorption of sweat from the groin and perineum, increasing the humidity and temperature of the vulva, providing a good growing environment for Candida. This leads to its massive reproduction and causes vaginitis.

Vaginitis refers to the inflammation of the vaginal mucosa and submucosal connective tissue, a common disease in gynecological outpatient clinics. Normally, healthy women have natural defense functions against pathogens invading the vagina due to anatomical and biochemical characteristics. When this natural defense function is compromised, pathogens easily invade, leading to vaginal inflammation. Young girls and postmenopausal women, due to a lack of estrogen, have thin vaginal epithelium, reduced intracellular glycogen content, and a vaginal pH of around 7, making them more susceptible to infections compared to adolescent and reproductive-age women.

Clinically, vaginitis is characterized primarily by changes in the nature of vaginal discharge and pruritus and burning sensation of the vulva. Dyspareunia is also common, and when the infection involves the urethra, symptoms such as dysuria and urgency may occur. Common types of vaginitis include bacterial vaginosis, trichomonas vaginitis, candidal vaginitis, and atrophic vaginitis. Gardner et al. studied 1,181 cases of vaginitis and found that 41% were bacterial, 27% were fungal, and 24% were trichomonal. Atrophic vaginitis occurs after menopause, oophorectomy, or pelvic radiotherapy, with an incidence rate reportedly as high as 98.5%.

Sugar: Nutrient for Fungal Growth

Recurrent episodes of toxic fungal vaginitis are related not only to a decrease in the body's resistance but also directly to a fondness for sweets. Many patients with recurrent toxic fungal vaginitis share a common trait: a love for sweet food. Some women constantly snack on candies and chocolates, habitually drink sugary beverages like cola, and even add sugar while cooking. Since fungi thrive in sweet environments, frequent sugar consumption promotes their growth and reproduction in high-sugar environments, leading to toxic fungal vaginitis. Due to their habitual sugar intake and preference for sweets, even if the fungi are temporarily suppressed, the continued presence of a sweet environment allows glycogen to accumulate in the vaginal epithelium, enabling fungi to revive, proliferate, and cause recurrent vaginitis.

Experts from the Air Force 466 Hospital emphasize that diabetic patients are prone to toxic fungal vaginitis because of higher blood sugar levels. If the primary disease is well controlled, candidal vaginitis can be improved. Therefore, during outbreaks of toxic fungal vaginitis, it is advisable to reduce sugar or sugary foods. In terms of treatment, drug administration, physical therapy, or surgery can be chosen.
