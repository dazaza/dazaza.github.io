For more than 11 years, Liu Qiang (a pseudonym) from Qijiang County in Chongqing had been raising his only son, believing him to be his own flesh and blood. When he discovered the truth, it was like a thunderclap on a clear day.

Due to his ex-wife's disappearance, when Liu attempted to file a lawsuit to change the custody relationship, the court ruled in the first trial that he must continue to raise the child until the boy's mother reappears. He was troubled: what if he couldn't find his ex-wife? He was confused: does he have to keep playing the role of the victim?

In 1994, Zhao Tingbi, a 22-year-old woman from Tongzi Town in Wulong County, Sichuan, went to Qijiang County in Chongqing to work and met Liu Qiang, who was four years older than her. They got married that same year.

In 1996, Zhao went to Guangdong to work. In May 1998, she gave birth to their son Xiao Ming (a pseudonym) at home. After about eight or nine months, she returned to Guangdong and didn’t come back until August 2001.

On the third day after returning home, during lunch, they started arguing because one dish was too salty.

That night, when Liu came back from running his motorcycle business, he found that his wife and her luggage were gone. His mother thought that his wife had gone to visit relatives out of anger, so she didn’t pay much attention.

A week later, Liu never expected to receive a divorce summons from the Qijiang County Court. However, since Zhao did not appear in court, the case was eventually dismissed as an automatic withdrawal.

Liu claimed that after the "cooking incident", neither he nor his son ever saw Zhao again until 2007 when he finally obtained Zhao’s phone number through his mother-in-law.

In August 2007, Liu sued in the Qijiang County Court for a divorce with Zhao. Since Zhao could not be summoned, the court eventually announced an absent judgment granting the divorce, with the son being placed under Liu's custody.

After the divorce, Liu noticed that his previously obedient and well-behaved son began to become addicted to video games, often skipping school and staying out for days at a time. Therefore, he decided to send the child to his brother-in-law's home in the countryside of Wulong County, where there were no internet cafes or electronic games, hoping that his son would overcome his internet addiction.

Unexpectedly, after studying for one semester and returning to Qijiang, the child became even worse, often arguing with him: "You're not my father, why should you control me? My real father is in Guangdong, together with my mother!"

Liu asked his son why he said that, and the child replied, "My mom said so." This raised Liu's suspicion, especially when some friends joked that the son didn't look like him. Thus, he decided to conduct a paternity test.

On February 26, 2008, the testing center issued its conclusion: Liu Qiang was not Xiao Ming's biological father. "I've been a victim for 11 years, raising someone else's child!" Liu was heartbroken and sued in the Wulong County Court, demanding a change in custody and seeking compensation of 97,500 yuan for the child's upbringing costs and 40,000 yuan for mental distress.

Since Zhao Tingbi did not appear in court, the court could only proceed with the trial in absentia. In late August 2009, the court made its first-instance ruling: Xiao Ming would be raised by his mother Zhao Tingbi. However, this case was very special, as Zhao had disappeared for 11 years, making it difficult to locate her. For the sake of the child's growth, before Zhao starts raising the child, Liu must continue to bear the responsibility of raising him.

Upon receiving the judgment, Liu was left speechless. "The son is not mine, why should I still take care of him?" So, he appealed to the Third Intermediate People's Court of Chongqing. Currently, he is waiting for the final result of the case.

In March, when the reporter met Liu in Qijiang County, he looked anxious. "If the child was obedient and listened, I would still be willing to raise him to adulthood. But he doesn't obey me and doesn't recognize me as his father. What can I do?"

Liu said that among the two brothers, he and his brother only had Xiao Ming as their son. When he was born, the whole family treated him like a star. Never would he have imagined that he wasn't actually his biological child. "However, having raised him for so many years, I still have feelings for him," Liu said. He and his parents were willing to treat the child as part of the Liu family. Moreover, at 42 years old and disabled, it would be difficult for him to remarry, and having this child would provide some support in the future. But now that the child refuses to follow his discipline, he feels completely helpless.

At noon on the day of the interview, Liu took the reporter to the school gate to find Xiao Ming. Xiao Ming was very wary and coldly stared at them. When the reporter expressed a desire to talk with him, he impatiently said, "I'm not going, it's none of your business," and ran away.

"I just hope I can find Zhao Tingbi and let her properly educate the child," Liu said.

During the investigation, the reporter unexpectedly learned that Zhao Tingbi might be involved in bigamy.

To verify this matter, the reporter found Zhao Tingbi's sister-in-law Ms. Hou in Tongzi Town, Wulong County.

Ms. Hou admitted that they all knew about Liu's paternity test but believed that the child was indeed Liu's biological son. "If it wasn't his biological son, why did he invite the whole family to celebrate the child's full moon party in Qijiang? They had a great time then." "If it wasn't his biological son, why did he bring this up after such a long time?"

Ms. Hou mentioned that Zhao Tingbi dropped out of school before finishing elementary school and rarely returned home. After their parents passed away, there was already a rift between the sisters-in-law, leading to a complete severance of contact. Regarding the issue of Xiao Ming's upbringing, Ms. Hou said, "Liu Qiang doesn't have to raise him; he can directly send him to the orphanage, or Xiao Ming can go find his mother."

In late March, when the reporter inquired about this matter at the Tongzi Town Police Station in Wulong County, where Zhao Tingbi's household registration was located, they unexpectedly found out that Zhao Tingbi's household book also listed two daughters. The two girls surnamed Yi were born on April 30, 2003, and July 2, 2004, respectively, and their relationship with the household head was recorded as mother-daughter. Due to the lack of original registration materials, currently, it is impossible to ascertain Zhao Tingbi's marital status or whether the children were adopted or biological.

Liu Qiang stated that after marrying Zhao Tingbi, she indeed did not transfer her household registration to Qijiang. "She initially said she would register Xiao Ming's household registration in her hometown, but after she left, it was never done, and the child remains unregistered to this day."

Moreover, Liu Qiang pointed out that the Qijiang County Court issued the divorce judgment on August 31, 2007, while the two daughters listed on Zhao Tingbi's household book were born in 2003 and 2004, indicating that Zhao had already formed a new romantic relationship at that time.

He Tongyu, a partner lawyer at Chongqing Qianwei Law Firm, believed that "if Zhao Tingbi lived with another man as husband and wife during the marriage and gave birth to daughters, then she may be suspected of bigamy."
