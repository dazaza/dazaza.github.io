ICBC's "Smart Purchase" Suspected of Selling Counterfeit Goods

by dfdfffffffffffffffffffff007 on 2009-06-23 10:05:19

Consumers, attracted by the reputation of the Bank of China, purchased products through the Bank of China's official website but found numerous defects in the products. Recently, a reader of the *21st Century Business Herald* called the hotline to file a complaint against the Bank of China's "Smart Purchase" service and its partner merchant, Beijing Hong Ying'er Internet Technology Co., Ltd. (hereinafter referred to as "Hong Ying'er"). The office of the Bank of China stated that this is a newly launched business. Considering that complaints are concentrated on the partner merchant "Hong Ying'er," the Bank of China will conduct a serious investigation and, with an attitude responsible to customers, terminate all cooperation with Hong Ying'er if necessary.

It is understood that the "Smart Purchase" service is a mail-order business introduced by the Bank of China targeting all cardholders of the Bank of China credit cards. This business is conducted in collaboration between the Bank of China and other merchants, where the other merchants provide the goods, and the Bank of China provides payment and product display platforms. The Bank of China can also offer interest-free installment payments for the products.

**Case 1: Mismatched Products, Counterfeit Goods**

Ms. Wang Xin (a pseudonym) from Jilin purchased a GUCCI classic jacquard large dumpling bag model 131230 on May 26th this year through the Bank of China's "Smart Purchase" webpage for 4580 yuan. The product information displayed dimensions of 44x21x31. On June 5th, Ms. Wang received the package, but the item name on the invoice was "GUCCI classic jacquard large dumpling bag 13121," with dimensions of 44x18x23. The shipping party was Hong Ying'er's Beijing branch.

According to the product ordering agreement, if the delivered product does not match the ordered product, it can be returned. After multiple calls to the customer service hotline and negotiations with Hong Ying'er's shopping website, along with presenting the shipping documents, Hong Ying'er agreed to return the item and promised to refund within 8 to 10 working days.

However, the problem was not just about the mismatched product. Ms. Wang stated that the inner lining of the GUCCI bag she purchased from Hong Ying'er had no GUCCI logo, the pattern on the bag's exterior was blurry, and after pulling the zipper a few times, it began to peel off paint. Upon inspection by the local GUCCI store staff, they indicated that this GUCCI bag was a low-grade counterfeit, with quality even worse than a high-quality imitation sold on a certain website for 258 yuan.

As of the time of reporting, Ms. Wang had still not received the refund from Hong Ying'er.

**Case 2: Quality Defects**

Miss Yang purchased a HP 6531ST4200 laptop on February 6th this year through "Smart Purchase." After receiving the item and using it for three hours, she discovered that the wireless network card slot was not powered. After much effort, the manufacturer confirmed it as a quality issue, and Hong Ying'er eventually agreed to replace the item.

On March 2nd, Miss Yang received the replacement notebook computer sent back by Hong Ying'er, but the model had changed to 6531ST6570, with 1GB less memory. On the same day of use, the computer would blue screen and restart approximately every two hours.

Subsequently, she visited the local HP after-sales service five times, and the conclusion was that there was a problem with the computer motherboard.

She contacted Hong Ying'er again to request a return. After multiple communications, Miss Yang sent the computer back on March 9th, and on March 12th, Hong Ying'er received the computer and stated: "There is a 3cm scratch on the surface of Miss Yang's computer shell, possibly caused by nails or a pencil." Miss Yang expressed that this clearly did not make sense; if nails could scratch the shell, then the quality of the computer was too poor. Out of desperation, she filed a complaint with the 315 Consumer Electronics Complaint Network. With the help of the personnel at the 315 website, Miss Yang finally exchanged for the computer model she originally ordered, and it has not encountered any problems since. By this point, three months had passed since her initial purchase of the computer.

**Question 3: Whom Should Consumers Turn to When Their Rights Are Infringed During Online Purchases?**

The Bank of China's credit card shopping process involves paying first and then shipping the goods. Ms. Wang stated that such a process design also leads to difficulties in returning or exchanging defective products when there are quality issues.

In the promotional flyers attached to the Bank of China credit card bill, the reporter saw the following statement at the bottom of the page: "... The Bank of China credit card is only used as a payment tool for purchasing goods or supplier services in this event; the final interpretation rights belong to the Bank of China."

Lawyer Liu Chunquan stated that this is a "standard form clause" unilaterally established by the bank. According to relevant judicial interpretations, the party providing the standard form clause should use sufficient text, symbols, or other special identifiers during contract formation to draw the attention of the other party. However, according to investigations, the above standard form clause only appeared on the Bank of China's promotional flyer and was displayed in small font at the end of the table at the bottom. Lawyer Liu Chunquan believed that the bank did not fulfill its obligation to truthfully inform about the "standard form clause."

**Response from the Bank of China: Will Conduct Serious Investigation**

A staff member of the Bank of China's office stated that the purpose of developing "Smart Purchase" was to provide convenience to consumers. Its products include computers, communication equipment, household appliances, digital audio-visual devices, watches and jewelry, cosmetics, etc. After the product launch, most customers were satisfied. However, due to being online for a short period, defects are inevitable. Considering that complaints are concentrated on the partner merchant "Hong Ying'er," the Bank of China will conduct a serious investigation and, with an attitude responsible to customers, terminate all cooperation with Hong Ying'er if necessary.

As of the time of reporting, Hong Ying'er had not provided any response to this matter.

Lawyer Liu Chunquan believed that the legal responsibility of the Bank of China in this matter still needs to be confirmed. However, currently, those capable of online shopping are some premium customers. If banks lose the trust of premium customers, causing customer churn, it will be a loss greater than the gain.
