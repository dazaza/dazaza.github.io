6 Simple Ways to Stop Being Catfished Online

by cqe on 2009-07-26 01:51:47

Phishing has become an increasingly rampant threat in recent years, which can lead to the theft of personal information such as credit card numbers or online banking passwords. Phishing attacks are carried out by sending deceptive emails that appear to come from legitimate websites. One of the main reasons users easily fall for these scams is that the so-called seemingly legitimate website holds information related to online transactions, such as a bank, credit card company, or account. These emails may request you to respond with your account details for "security updates."

Phishing emails may also direct you to a fraudulent site or pop up a window that looks like a real site but is actually set up to steal your personal information.

According to research by the U.S. Anti-Phishing Working Group, phishers can convince 5% of recipients to respond.

So, how do you protect yourself from falling victim?

1. Do not respond to emails requesting personal financial information.

Phisher emails often include false but "urgent" messages (such as: "Urgent: Your account may be stolen!") aimed at eliciting an immediate response from you. Reputable companies do not ask their customers for passwords or account details via email. Be cautious when opening attachments and downloading files in emails, regardless of where they come from.

2. Access bank sites by typing the domain name into the browser's address bar.

Phishers often direct victims to fraudulent sites through links in their emails. These sites usually resemble a bank's domain name, such as using mybankonline.com instead of mybank.com, a trick known as spoofing. When clicked, the fake URL displayed in the address bar may seem normal, but there are various methods used to ultimately lead you to a fraudulent website. If you suspect that an email from a bank or online financial institution is fake, do not open any links included in the email.

3. Check your accounts frequently.

You should regularly log in to your online accounts and review their status. If you notice any suspicious matters or transactions, report them immediately to your bank or credit card provider.

4. Check if the sites you visit are secure.

Before submitting your card details or other sensitive information, it is best to perform a series of checks to ensure that the site you are visiting uses encryption technology to protect your personal data:

Check the address in the address bar. If the site is secure, it should start with "https://" rather than the usual "http://". You can also look for a lock icon on the browser's status bar. You can even check its encryption level. However, note that even if a site uses encryption, it does not mean the site is legitimate. It just tells you that the data is being sent in encrypted form.

5. Handle emails and personal data with caution.

Most banks have a security webpage on their site focusing on information about secure transactions and some advice related to personal data: Never let anyone know your PINS or passwords, don't write them down on paper, and don't use the same password for all your online accounts. Remember to avoid opening or responding to spam emails, as this gives the sender the message that this address is active or usable. Be discerning when reading emails. If something seems too good to be true, don't believe it!

6. Secure your computer.

Some phishing emails or other spam may contain information that records user internet activities (spyware) or opens a "backdoor" for hackers to access your computer (Trojan horse). Installing reliable antivirus software and keeping it updated helps detect and deal with malicious software, while using anti-spam software can prevent phishing emails from reaching your computer. For broadband users, installing a firewall is also important. This helps ensure the security of information on your computer and blocks communication with illegal data sources. Ensure that you update and download the latest security patches for your browser in a timely manner. If you haven't installed any patches, visit the browser's site to find security update information.

Additionally, many organizations' websites have a dedicated email address to report suspicious emails. If you receive similar emails, you should report them promptly.
