[Interview] MadFinger Founder Talks About 'Samurai II: Vengeance'

by diglog on 2010-12-19 13:51:26

Last year, I introduced an iPhone game called *Samurai: Way of the Warrior*. At the time, I said it was "undoubtedly the most brilliant and excellent action game you can find on the iPhone today." Not long ago, the sequel to this game was released, named *Samurai II: Vengeance*. My review of this game is very simple: it's the first time I felt that an iPhone could offer a PSP-level action game. In terms of combat feel and excitement, it is definitely a peak in iPhone gaming, even reminding me of playing *God of War* on the PSP and *Ninja Gaiden* on the NDS. Due to the immense joy this game provided, I couldn't help but write to the creator of the game, Marek Rabas, hoping to exchange ideas—surprisingly, the company that makes Japanese-style samurai games (more accurately, "侍") is actually in the Czech Republic. Below is the interview:

Zhang Liang: In your previous game, *Samurai: Way of the Warrior*, players used multi-touch gesture recognition to control the protagonist, but in *Samurai II: Vengeance*, you changed the control method and added a virtual game controller. Why did you make this change?

Marek Rabas: We really liked using gesture recognition to control the game. However, frustratingly, we found that many people didn’t know how to play with gestures, including some journalists. Instead of swiping on the screen, they clicked, and then thought the game was broken. This was the biggest criticism of the last game, which even affected its evaluation. Additionally, we wanted to make the fighting faster and change the perspective more frequently, and for these considerations, gesture recognition wasn’t convenient.

Zhang Liang: Another highlight of this game is that it automatically switches to slow motion during gameplay—especially when you use your ultimate move—which feels like watching a kung fu movie. How did you consider implementing this?

Marek Rabas: Undoubtedly, players love cutting off their opponents' heads, so we wanted to give players more time to enjoy that moment. Of course, this doesn’t mean we like killing; it’s just a game, so don’t do this in real life ;)

Zhang Liang: Honestly speaking, this game really made me feel like I was playing a PSP game on my iPhone, and I might even say it's better than many PSP fighting games (think of the lack of impact in Dynasty Warriors and Prince of Persia). Could you introduce the process of creating this game? Is there anything you regret? Any advice for other developers?

Marek Rabas: Previously, we made realistic games. For example, first-person shooters about WWII and the Vietnam War, and third-person action-adventure games like *Mafia* and *Mafia II*. We wanted to try something different: bringing the console experience to mobile phones. The iPhone 4 is an extremely cool phone, and the Unity 3D engine is also extremely powerful, so why not give it a try? I think the results are pretty good: 60 frames per second, not bad, right?

As for regrets... it’s mainly due to resource and budget limitations. We're still a small company; in our team, there are only two artists, one programmer, and one sound designer. This limits us from creating more content or making it more refined.

As for advice to developers, we’re not arrogant enough to give specific suggestions, but developers should create games they truly love and enjoy the process of developing.

Zhang Liang: What also surprised me is that you're a Czech company, yet you've created such a cool Japanese-style game. How did you manage that? Do you read Japanese comics?

Marek Rabas: We are big fans of hack-and-slash games—games like *God of War*, *Ninja Gaiden*, *Devil May Cry*, etc.—and I myself have studied Tenshin Shoden Katori Shinto-ryu for several years. I also taught some of this to my colleagues. Yes, we’ve watched a lot of old samurai movies and read a lot of samurai comics. It's a very exciting theme.
