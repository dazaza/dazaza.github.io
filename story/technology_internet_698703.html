Both Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) and Western medicine have corresponding symptoms for "internal heat."

Although "internal heat" is just a colloquial term among the public, both TCM and Western medicine have corresponding symptoms. Western medicine views internal heat as an inflammatory response, while TCM believes it relates to the "fiery evil" of different organs within the body. This "fire" often travels along the meridians upward, primarily manifesting in the head, face, mouth, and throat, such as:

- Dry mouth and nose: Symptoms include mouth ulcers, cracked lips, insomnia, vivid dreams, yellow urine, painful urination, dry nose, nosebleeds, coughs with thick yellow phlegm, etc.

- Loss of appetite: Symptoms include swollen gums, tooth root inflammation, lack of interest in food, dry mouth, thirst, etc. In spring, many people experience reduced appetite and dental problems, possibly due to internal heat.

- Constipation: Mainly characterized by hard stools, irritability, dry itchy eyes, tinnitus, dizziness, hoarseness, etc.

- Weakness: Deficient fire manifests as weight loss, weak knees and lower back, forgetfulness, insomnia, red tongue with little moisture, etc. Yin deficiency-induced internal heat symptoms differ slightly, including palpitations, night sweats, etc.

Wild vegetables and fruits can help "cool the fire."

To prevent and treat internal heat in the spring, experts recommend trying these methods:

First, maintain a regular lifestyle. Recently, many people have started experiencing various symptoms of internal heat, which are related to staying up late, irregular sleep patterns, and decreased immune function. Restoring a normal sleep schedule and getting life back on track is very important.

Some foods can help "cool the fire," so try them out. In the spring, many fresh wild vegetables become available, all with heat-clearing, detoxifying, and fire-reducing properties, such as Malan leaves, shepherd's purse, dandelion, etc. You can also drink Goji berries regularly, which nourish the liver and are less likely to cause internal heat.

Additionally, chrysanthemum, honeysuckle, American ginseng, and Dendrobium can clear liver heat and improve vision but correspond to different types of "fire." Chrysanthemum and honeysuckle are effective against more substantial "fires," with symptoms like throat inflammation, yellow phlegm, etc., typical of colds, showing elevated white blood cells in blood tests. If it's "deficient fire," then American ginseng and Dendrobium can help cool the fire. Deficient fire symptoms include no obvious inflammation but feelings of dry skin, dry mouth, dry throat, red tongue, and thin coating on the tongue.

Of course, eating more fresh vegetables and fruits is also beneficial. For example, celery can clear heat and detoxify, benefiting those with excessive liver fire and rough skin. It contains a lot of fiber, stimulating intestinal movement. Pears are well-known for reducing internal heat, and other fruits like bitter melon, tomatoes, bananas, etc., are excellent vegetables and fruits for relieving spring dryness.

From a health preservation perspective, TCM advocates "nourishing yang in spring and summer." It recommends consuming more onions, ginger, garlic, chili peppers, etc., but for those already suffering from internal heat, it's best to avoid these foods. Spicy foods should also be avoided.

Lastly, maintaining a cheerful mood is crucial. Internal heat in the spring is also related to psychological state, so keeping an optimistic mindset is an essential "fire extinguisher."
