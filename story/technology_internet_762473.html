"The Inevitable 'Death of Yougou': Belle's E-commerce Has Many Fatal Flaws"

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 15:38:44

Recently, Yougou.com, which has been suffering from a severe loss of senior executives, received another blow. On July 17th, it was reported that according to insiders at Yougou.com, COO Zhang Xiaojun is leaving and is currently processing his resignation. As one of the founding members of Yougou.com, Zhang Xiaojun's departure is particularly significant, indicating that Yougou.com is indeed facing serious crises.

Let’s first review Belle International’s e-commerce journey:

A few years ago, Belle quietly tested the waters in e-commerce, developing steadily through three channels: Taobao distribution, Tmall, and B2C distribution (B2C distribution was completed via Taoxiu.com). Led by group veteran Xie Yunli, a not-so-luxurious e-commerce team initially sold products under Belle's own brands. Later, they launched exclusive online models and eventually introduced new online brands. This development path was considered before 2011 as a more solid case of traditional brand enterprises successfully entering the e-commerce sector compared to Li-Ning.

With the rise of Letao.com and HaoLeMai receiving over $100 million in financing and rapidly expanding in 2010, Belle Group became restless. On one hand, they were concerned about being constrained by distribution channels again; on the other hand, after evaluation, they realized they had significant advantages in the e-commerce field, including:

1. Numerous brands: In addition to its own several major women's shoe brands, Belle also managed international well-known sports brands like Adidas and Nike, whose online sales grew astonishingly fast.

2. Easier category expansion: For Belle Group, which focuses on women's shoes and agency for famous sports brands, how to achieve absolute market advantage and status through multi-brand and multi-category strategies has always been a key consideration. Expanding offline stores for brands and categories was obviously too slow, while the ongoing e-commerce business could easily expand brands but struggled to quickly add categories.

3. Financial advantage: During cooperation with e-commerce companies, Belle discovered that e-commerce is a capital-intensive business. With its financial strength, Belle could develop without relying on external capital, thus defeating many vertical e-commerce platforms that must depend on capital, becoming the "last man standing."

4. Talent advantage: Through years of e-commerce practice, Belle already had a highly professional and efficient e-commerce team capable of addressing scale development or higher challenges.

5. Supply chain advantage: This excited Belle internally and was also the core reason for attracting executives from brand e-commerce or platform e-commerce. After all, supply chains are always fascinating in e-commerce because they seem to be the part deciding life or death aside from capital. The supply chain includes product research and development, production, warehouse management, delivery, after-sales service, and a series of “business” content. In traditional enterprises that emphasize “commerce” over “e-commerce,” this is considered the most competitive advantage over e-commerce companies.

Based on these advantages, Belle decided to make a big move in e-commerce. First, they purchased the double-pinyin domain name yougou.com,

abandoned the somewhat counterfeit "topshoes.cn" Taoshu website for redirection, stopped supplying all distribution websites, though they still retained direct-operated third-party online stores such as Taobao and Tmall Mall with less investment and attention. They recruited JD.com VP and Vancl VP at high salaries and high profiles as CMO and COO respectively, and dispatched group veterans as CEO, forming a golden triangle. Their goal was to build a full-category vertical B2C mall, implementing multi-brand strategies on one hand, and expanding into clothing and other categories on the other, realizing the dream of a department store empire impossible to achieve offline!

All this sounded very promising, but every dream needs to face the harsh reality. The reality was:

1. Higher technical requirements for independent B2C: Being based in Shenzhen headquarters, Belle couldn't recruit more excellent technical talents, and outsourcing tech services were scarce in Shenzhen. Technology determines user experience, backend management efficiency, and reliable data. Independent B2C involves dynamic technology, different from the relatively static ERP technology, and continues to change, which remains a hurdle for many traditional enterprises.

2. Increased management difficulty with category expansion: Someone once said that excelling in women's shoes doesn’t guarantee success with men's shoes, something Belle probably deeply felt when expanding into men's shoes. Expanding into women's wear and accessories within a year can imagine what difficulties the backend encountered. For traditional brand enterprises accustomed to meticulous calculations, this rapid approach might have worked in special periods for Vancl, understandable within Vancl and JD.com systems, but whether Belle, despite having Vancl's COO and JD.com's CMO, could understand and execute with "exaggerated costs" is questionable!

3. System: Many people cannot comprehend the difference between e-commerce and traditional enterprises. Simply put, department settings (organizational structure), approvals, finance and budgeting, reporting methods, corporate culture, communication styles, execution methods, risk control, process systems, etc., constitute an enterprise's so-called "mechanism." The result of this conflict is that e-commerce tries to persuade the acceptance of its "system," while the traditional team attempts to understand but never truly grasps e-commerce's "system," leading to inefficiency and instability in the execution team. Eventually, the chairman or board might say: "E-commerce and traditional enterprises each have their strengths and weaknesses, the future will definitely involve integration, so in these areas, you must integrate..." If this so-called "integration without harmony" occurs, it leads to so-called management innovation, and even the "CEO rotation system" appeared in a company like Belle!

Initially, it seemed that Yougou.com from Belle's e-commerce division was leading the pack, and the top executives were waiting for spin-off listings to enjoy success and wealth, setting examples of countless traditional enterprises succeeding in the e-commerce era. However, they ended up tangled in these hard issues.

After all, whether it’s the veterans who grew up with Belle or the poached e-commerce VPs, it’s clear they didn't leave due to lack of ability. When the VP from JD.com returned, they continued to perform excellently, and when the VP from Vancl had the courage to start a business. Are these three points the hard injuries? Or are these not just Belle's hard injuries but also those of many traditional enterprises attempting to enter e-commerce?
