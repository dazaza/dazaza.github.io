Synopsis:

Ivan, an antique car dealer in his early forties, is at the age where irritability due to a midlife crisis begins. One day, he catches a thief named Eli red-handed while burglarizing his home. Strangely, Ivan does not resort to violence against Eli. Instead, he feels an inexplicable sense of closeness to Eli and agrees to drive him back to his parents' house in his old Citroën. Through charming country roads, these two peculiar individuals embark on an extraordinary journey...

Behind the Scenes:

[The Director]

"Eldorado" was written, directed, and starred in by the renowned Belgian multi-talented artist Benoît Poelvoorde. This is Poelvoorde's second feature-length drama and his fourth film. Born in 1965, this Belgian director is a jack-of-all-trades. Before becoming famous through the Belgian series "Les Snuls," he was a self-taught painter who worked extensively behind the scenes in films. After gaining fame, he started directing movies and occasionally made cameo appearances in Turkish and French films. His 2005-directed film "Super Nova" won the International Federation of Film Critics Award at that year's Berlin Film Festival, and his 2001-directed short film "Muno" won three awards at the Toronto International Youth Film Festival, including Best International Short Film and a Special Mention from the International Federation of Film Critics. He is one of the most promising young talents in Belgian cinema.

El Dorado means "golden" in Spanish and originally refers to a legend of a South American tribe about a wealthy king and his nation, which later evolved into the myth of the "City of Gold," attracting many Spanish colonizers to search for treasure.

[Story Origin]

The idea for the movie came from a real-life incident experienced by director Benoît Poelvoorde: one night, when he returned home, he caught two burglars inside— one hiding under the bed and the other under the desk. "It's a moment in life that you don't often experience. The three of us spent the night talking, each filled with our own fears." Using this strange event as inspiration, the director conceived the movie's story. "Various trivial matters gathered together, giving this story flesh and blood, making it increasingly rich. Ivan and Eli's adventure is truly like a novel, blending real-life events with purely fictional moments."

The movie's title comes from the Spanish word El Dorado, which originally referred to a South American tribal legend about a wealthy king and his nation, evolving over time into the myth of the "City of Gold." By borrowing this metaphorical title, the director seeks the "City of Gold" of the soul. "Combining infinite sadness with infinitely foolish scenes, Bérénice L'Hermitte and Benoît Poelvoorde wanted to direct a comedy that explored certain depths and humanity."

[Before and Behind the Camera]

While writing the script, the director never considered playing the role of Ivan. It was producer Jacques-Henri Bronckart who came up with the idea and convinced Benoît Poelvoorde. In fact, the character Benoît created undoubtedly reflects another side of his personality. Thus, we can find traces of Benoît both in front of and behind the camera. The director says, "This almost mad experience was only possible thanks to my team. However, for me, seeing myself in shorts, standing in the middle of the river, endlessly appearing in front of the camera, made it difficult for me to judge the quality of the footage."

The filming took place in the Brabant region of Belgium, where Benoît Poelvoorde chose a vibrant and bright color style for the imagery. Through Benoît's wide-angle lens, the usually gray and sorrowful plains appeared to have the hues of the American West or the South American grasslands. The director said, "I wanted to break free from the gray and sorrowful image of Belgium; I wanted to make myself happy."

As for why he chose to turn such a story into a road movie, the director admitted it stemmed from childhood experiences. "When I was a child, every weekend we traveled back and forth in the Calamine-Bastogne area to visit my grandparents. During each trip, I would stick my face to the window, watching the scenery rush by, feeling cocooned within the car—a kind of happiness."

[Movie Soundtrack]

From writing to editing, the director also participated in the creation of the music. Music is an indispensable part of this movie. "Fortunately, I met Renaud Meyer, whose intense and undulating musical crescendos perfectly matched what I had envisioned in my mind while writing the script. Moreover, Ampierre and Coenghis composed a melody so beautiful it left one speechless. My friend Steven Libessart also gave me his musical pieces. To integrate all of these, we bought out the copyright of these three cherished musical segments. All of this contributed to this soundtrack, powerful and enchanting, just as I had always dreamed."

This film was self-written, self-directed, and self-starred by the renowned Belgian multi-talented artist Bérénice L'Hermitte and Benoît Poelvoorde. The story revolves around a car dealer who discovers a burglar, and after some negotiation, instead of turning him in, the car dealer develops compassion for the thief and decides to drive him out of Belgium. Of course, the plan to harbor the criminal and escape is bound to encounter difficulties. This comedy, heavy with black humor, allows actor-turned-director Benoît Poelvoorde to fully display his acting talent, and co-star Fabrice Adde also delivers a commendable performance. "Eldorado" is Poelvoorde's fourth film and second feature-length drama. His 2005-directed "Super Nova" won the C.I.C.A.E. Award at the Berlin International Film Festival, and his 2001-directed short film "Muno" won three awards at the Toronto International Youth Film Festival, including Best International Short Film and a Special Mention from the International Federation of Film Critics, making him one of the most promising young talents in Belgian cinema.
