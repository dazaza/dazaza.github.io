Bill Gates: If I hadn't dropped out of school, Microsoft would still have been the king.

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 10:56:17

Microsoft founder and former world's richest man, Bill Gates, recently participated in a charity fundraising event held at Harvard University and was interviewed by David Rubenstein, co-chair of the fundraising event. Besides sharing his recent charitable experiences, Gates also mentioned some anecdotes from his time studying at Harvard and during Microsoft's development.

Gates stated that if he had not dropped out of Harvard but instead waited until after graduation to establish Microsoft, the company would still have achieved today's success. Microsoft's success, aside from their own efforts, was also related to the mistakes made by various competitors.

What if Gates hadn't dropped out?

As one of the most famous Harvard dropouts in the world, Gates' decision back then became one of the topics that intrigued the audience on-site. David Rubenstein, co-chair of the Harvard fundraising event, asked Gates: "If you had persisted and graduated from Harvard, how would your life have been different?"

Gates first laughed and answered: "Once I gave a speech in this very hall, and there was a student in the audience named Mark Zuckerberg. If I had stayed until graduation, I probably wouldn’t have influenced him to drop out of Harvard either."

Of course, Gates admitted that although he did not receive a diploma from Harvard, he had already studied for three years and had taken almost all the necessary courses (despite being known as a "class-skipping king" during his time at Harvard), and his university life had always been enjoyable.

Back then, when informing his parents of his decision to drop out, because he feared their opposition, Gates cleverly said he was just “leaving” Harvard, and if Microsoft failed, he could return to complete his credits and graduate. Seeing the ignored prospects of the PC software industry by other companies, Gates, in his third year, felt that even if it took only one more year to graduate, he couldn’t wait any longer.

At that time, major technology companies focused their strategies on hardware, believing only hardware could make money, paying insufficient attention to software. However, Gates and his partner Paul Allen saw the opportunity and formed relatively mature ideas and strategies about the future of the software industry. In 1979, Microsoft was established, focusing on computer systems and software businesses. Over the next two to three decades, Gates truly built a vast software empire.

But looking back at the landscape of the PC industry back then, Gates believed that with his and Paul’s foresight, even if he had waited another year before decisively dropping out, Microsoft would still have achieved today's success, making no less of a contribution to global development. Unlike today's rapidly changing tech industry where opportunities can vanish quickly, back then, Silicon Valley was just emerging, and many things that seem natural today were enormous innovations that could influence the course of history.

What might be embarrassing for today's entrepreneurs who often go through multiple rounds of financing and burn hundreds of millions of dollars is that Microsoft has never lacked money from its inception to now. The sole round of financing wasn't due to a lack of funds. The software industry is one of the most scalable economies in the world; once you develop a piece of software, selling thousands or millions of copies involves nothing more than copying and pasting.

In the first three years after Microsoft's establishment, neither Gates nor Paul received salaries, but afterward, as IBM and Apple's Apple II computers began running Microsoft's operating system, the company’s cash reserves grew steadily. As Microsoft's CEO, Gates never had to worry about the company lacking funds.

This single "round of financing" occurred in 1981 when Microsoft sold 5% of its shares to a Silicon Valley venture capital firm at a valuation of $20 million. The real purpose was to bring David Marquardt, a venture capitalist, onto Microsoft's board to provide "professional advice" on various aspects of the young company. Gates considered this a wise decision, as David Marquardt was highly experienced in technology company investment management and strategic development and remains a member of Microsoft's board of directors to this day. The $1 million raised from this round of financing has remained untouched in the company's bank account until today.

Competitors' Mistakes Helped Microsoft Succeed

Those familiar with Microsoft's history know that Microsoft's success was built on defeating various competitors step by step. Many times, it was the competitors' mistakes that provided Microsoft with opportunities in corresponding fields.

Back then, everyone saw the vast potential of personal computers, but all companies were fixated on the hardware sector, treating software merely as an accessory to hardware, with no one believing software could be more profitable than hardware.

Host Rubenstein asked Gates: "Back then, Apple's operating system was very beautifully designed, but Jobs and his team did not choose to license it to other hardware companies. If Apple had done so, would Microsoft have missed the opportunity? After all, Apple's operating system looked better than Microsoft's at the time."

Gates recalled that Apple's situation should be divided into three parts: For the Apple series of personal computers, Apple developed an operating system for the Apple I, but by the time of the Apple II, "Steve Wozniak, one of Apple's three founders, got a bit lazy, or perhaps not focused enough, so we ended up developing the Apple II Basic for the Apple II and licensing it to Apple."

For the Macintosh computer, Apple developed its own operating system, but the rest of the software applications were developed by Microsoft. Interestingly, because the software applications Microsoft developed for Apple received excellent market feedback, Microsoft started lobbying Apple to license the Mac operating system to other hardware manufacturers to help expand the penetration rate of its other software applications. Fortunately, "Apple considered it for a while, but ultimately decided not to do so."

Thus, MS-DOS and later Windows lost one fewer competitor; finally, in the post-Mac era, Apple's strategy gradually took shape — developing its own software and not licensing it to third parties, whereas Microsoft was a staunch supporter of the "licensing use" model from the very beginning.

During the period when Microsoft developed application software for the Apple II, Jobs was in charge of the Apple II project. Later, Jobs was ousted by the board of directors, and Apple began to decline. At that time, Microsoft was Apple's partner. After Apple fell into financial difficulties, it turned to Microsoft for help, but after a full year of negotiations, there were no results.

It wasn't until Jobs returned to Apple that the two parties reached a strategic cooperation agreement within just three days, one clause of which was that Microsoft would invest in purchasing 6% of Apple's stock. Unfortunately, Microsoft's legal staff deemed these stocks worthless and soon sold them off.

IBM's decision back then also somewhat contributed to Microsoft's rise. "At that time, everyone was focused on hardware. IBM approached you to develop software for their personal computers. What would have happened if IBM had chosen to directly purchase your software rather than using a licensing model?" Rubenstein asked Gates.

"That would absolutely have been a mistake they made," Gates smiled in response. "IBM is a great company, but at that time, their strategy was 'fast,' aiming to update products within 18 months. They had quickly developed their own operating system, but the results were average. So they brought Intel in for chips, Oracle for software, and another company to manage computer sales channels. We got the operating system part, and the goal was to quickly launch new products."

"The reason they chose licensing over direct purchase was that they didn't anticipate such high demand later on, assuming licensing would be more cost-effective than outright purchase." However, Microsoft anticipated the future demand for personal computer operating systems, chose the licensing model to cooperate with IBM, and rapidly established an almost monopolistic position in the industry due to the explosive growth of the personal computer industry.

During the interview, Gates was in good spirits, even mentioning that the "Ctrl+Alt+Del" command used to manage the Windows backend system was a mistake — though the error wasn't Microsoft's, it was another mistake made by IBM. Microsoft had the chance to use a single key for such commands, but IBM's keyboard designer didn't want to provide that kind of key to Microsoft. Therefore, Microsoft ultimately decided to use the "Ctrl+Alt+Del" command to log into the system.
