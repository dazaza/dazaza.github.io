Facebook Launches Topic Search Tool to Help News Organizations Track Public Opinion

by anonymous on 2013-09-11 14:05:32

On Monday morning local time, Facebook launched two new search tools, providing news agencies and marketers with more convenient real-time monitoring services for trending topics. These trending topics may be related to TV programs, major news, and sporting events.

With the help of one of the new search tools, news agencies can use keywords such as "Syrian vote" or "Tokyo Olympics" to search for comments made by individuals or companies on Facebook regarding these topics.

The other search tool can also be used to search private content on Facebook. However, the returned results are aggregated and anonymized information, such as geographical location, gender, and age.

In an interview with reporters, Andy Mitchell, Facebook's Director of Strategic Media Platform Partnerships, said: "News agencies can understand the focus of people's discussions in a brand-new way through this. For talented and creative journalists, this means infinite possibilities."

Recently, Facebook has been making continuous moves, constantly competing with Twitter for the dominant position as a public opinion platform. Undoubtedly, the launch of the new search tools gives Facebook more leverage.

Most users register accounts on Twitter specifically to publicly express and disseminate their opinions, making Twitter an unparalleled research platform. Researchers can analyze social sentiments, zeitgeist, and fleeting moments on it. For example, according to the analysis by Twitter's trending topic tool, we can know that American users' favorite topic of discussion on Sunday is football.

Similar to Facebook, Twitter also spares no effort to attract advertisers and TV stations: Twitter helps them target fans of TV series and specific sports activities to make advertising more precise.

Globally, Facebook has more than five times the number of users as Twitter. Therefore, Facebook is also a massive database. For instance, Facebook released a data statistic last week claiming that the opening of the new season of the National Football League (NFL) attracted 8 million users with 20 million "likes," comments, and shares.

However, most posts on Facebook are not public but are limited to sharing with friends or even smaller groups. Therefore, Facebook must carefully weigh what kind of statistical data it can release, especially during this sensitive period—Facebook has recently faced wave after wave of criticism due to modifications in its privacy policy.

Despite this, the company is increasingly actively transforming Facebook into an open urban square. In June this year, Facebook enabled the hashtag function, allowing 1.2 billion users to search related topics via hashtags like "#NFL"—similar to Twitter's hashtag function.

Additionally, Facebook is testing a "trending topics" module. This module is located on the user's feed page, displaying the hottest topics on Facebook. Recently, Facebook also opened up the embedded message feature, allowing users to embed posts and videos from Facebook on their websites or blogs.

The launch of the new tools symbolizes another significant step forward for Facebook in this direction.

Initially, the new search tools will only be provided to a few news agencies for testing and optimization. These agencies include Buzzfeed, CNN, NBC, Sky News, and Slate. Facebook will also provide the tool to Mass Relevance, a company offering social media analysis and user interaction services. Facebook stated that it plans to offer this functionality to more media and marketing agencies in the near term.

The selected agencies participating in the test are already eager to get started.

Ryan Osborn, Vice President of Digital Innovation and Social Media at NBC, said in an interview: "As a news agency, we have always been trying to figure out what the general public is discussing."

He said that before the U.S. Congress votes on President Obama's proposal to take military action against Syria, NBC will use the tool this week for an online survey titled "Decision: Should America Strike Syria?" Moreover, this tool can also be used for TV programs.

KC Estenson, Senior Vice President and General Manager of CNN's digital business, said they hope to mine data from Facebook that differs from Twitter's. He said: "Compared to Twitter, you might get more personal and intrinsic information from Facebook."

He also pointed out that Facebook's new search tool won't replace frontline reporters conducting field reports but will serve as a supplement. "These tools are like conducting field interviews on social networks. In the long run, we can obtain more information from them."

Currently, it is unclear how news agencies will use the aggregated anonymous data extracted from private content. However, Estenson said that the new tool is another method for news agencies to gauge public opinion, similar to traditional polls.

He pointed out: "By studying the topics people discuss, we can gain clues to perceive global trends."
