Processing of Yellow Mealworm Food

The processing of yellow mealworm food, aside from the initial detoxification and impurity removal process, varies depending on the type. Taking yellow mealworms as an example, the main challenge in their food processing is dealing with the exoskeleton. The body wall and tissue structure of yellow mealworms, like other arthropods, is an exoskeleton. The exoskeleton's outer layer is primarily composed of chitin. Chitin has a very stable and solid structure, making it difficult to soften or decompose under normal conditions using strong acids or bases. Consequently, this directly affects the texture of the processed yellow mealworm food, resulting in a rough, hard, and tasteless outer layer that is difficult to digest and absorb. There are three methods for handling the exoskeleton:

1. Using enzymes specific to yellow mealworms to break down the stable bonds between the large chitin molecules, allowing them to be hydrolyzed and filtered out. This method should be combined with the extraction and processing of chitin; otherwise, filtering out the exoskeleton without utilizing it would be wasteful. Roasting yellow mealworm food is relatively simple and can be done using conventional small food processing methods for yellow mealworm ingredients. Using enzymatic methods to soften chitin requires higher standards in equipment and technology, but adopting enzymatic production techniques is an inevitable trend for large-scale insect food production.

2. Removing the yellow mealworm's exoskeleton through filtration and using its body fluids to process food, while using the exoskeleton as raw material for chitin extraction.

3. Processing food by roasting, frying, or baking yellow mealworms, directly destroying the exoskeleton at high temperatures to make it crispy and fragrant, with a pleasant aroma, suitable for direct consumption.

Introduction to Food Processing Methods Using Insects as Raw Materials

When processing food using various types of yellow mealworms as raw materials, different methods must be adopted based on the characteristics of the yellow mealworms. Due to the features of their exoskeleton, the exoskeleton of yellow mealworms is hard and stable. Insect exoskeletons are rich in chitin, lime, and wax, which are difficult to decompose and digest using conventional methods. Only strong acids or bases heated can hydrolyze them, so the human digestive system cannot directly digest insect exoskeletons. Many catering departments and companies now produce insect-based foods, such as cicadas, locusts, silkworm pupae, yellow mealworms, and scorpions, mainly focusing on frying or roasting. Frying or roasting can destroy the insect exoskeleton structure, causing the chitin to denature, resulting in products that are crispy and fragrant, with a unique flavor of insect-based foods. If the exoskeleton is not fried until crispy, the texture will be poor, with tough residue that is difficult to digest and absorb. However, frying or roasting also destroys the protein and vitamin nutrients within the exoskeleton, causing protein denaturation and resulting in some nutrient loss. Therefore, during processing, it is important to protect the nutrition while maintaining the crispy and delicious taste of insect-based foods. The processing techniques should be exquisite and detailed, ensuring color, aroma, and taste are all present, which is not an easy task.

1. Locusts

Locust-based foods have a long history of being consumed in folk culture, with frying and roasting being the primary processing methods. Below is a summary of our locust food processing technique from 1994:

Locust intestines contain 6% to 12% impurities. After collecting live locusts, they are placed in a large mesh cage and starved for 3 to 6 hours to encourage them to defecate. Random samples are taken at regular intervals to check if their digestive tracts are empty by opening the abdomen. Once the digestive tract impurities are completely expelled, the locusts are scalded in boiling water, washed, and boiled in hot water for 2 to 3 minutes. They are then fished out, drained, sprinkled with 3% to 6% salt relative to their weight, and quickly frozen (approximately -15°C) for storage. Since locusts have a strong seasonal occurrence and can only be collected once a year, freezing allows them to be used throughout the year. During processing, they are thawed first, excess salt is removed, dehydrated, and then fried in low-temperature edible oil for 3 to 5 minutes until crispy and fragrant. They are then fished out, drained of oil, seasoned, vacuum-packed, sterilized, and processed similarly to cicada-based foods. The process flow is as follows:

Capture — Starve and defecate — Boil in water — Dehydrate — Fry and roast — Season — Vacuum pack — Sterilize — Outer packaging

This is the convenient bagged version of locust food intended for use as semi-finished ingredients in restaurants and family meals. Fresh locusts can also be frozen in small bags using the same method as cicada frozen food ingredient processing.

2. Cicadas

Primarily using mature cicada nymphs, these are mainly purchased and should undergo preliminary processing locally. During the summer cicada emergence period, newly emerged "cicada monkeys" are collected, cleaned, and boiled in boiling water for 2 to 3 minutes. This kills the digestive enzymes and bacteria inside the cicadas, causing the proteins to coagulate. They are then fished out, dried, and sprinkled with 5% to 10% salt relative to their weight. Once cooled to room temperature, they are packaged in small packets and quickly frozen, typically at -16°C (low-temperature freezing), preserving freshness for over 6 months.

Cicada-based food processing mainly involves roasting and frying. Due to their long life cycle, their bodies, walls, legs, and claws are hard, requiring frying to make them edible. Frozen cicadas are thawed, cleaned to remove surface salt, dried, and then fried in oil at a low temperature to prevent burning. Approximately 5 to 10 minutes of frying results in crispy legs and skin. The cicadas are then fished out, drained of oil, and promptly seasoned (mixed flour, monosodium glutamate, salt, Sichuan pepper, sugar, etc.), making them ready to eat. If product packaging is desired, further packaging and sterilization are necessary before sale. For example, the "Golden Cicada" food process: Harvest cicada nymphs — Clean — Boil in water — Salt cure — Freeze store — Thaw — Clean — Fry — Season — Bake — Vacuum pack — High-temperature sterilize — Outer packaging — Golden Cicada Food.

The flavor can be adjusted according to needs, producing five-spice, spicy, or sweet flavors.

Before vacuum packing, the front legs of the cicadas must be removed, otherwise, the well-developed digging front legs may puncture the vacuum packaging bag, causing air leaks and affecting shelf life.

High-temperature sterilization should reach 110°C and last for 20 minutes. Properly vacuum-sealed packaging can preserve freshness for over 3 months.

Frozen cicadas can also be packaged in small plastic bags weighing 300-500 grams and sold directly in the freezer counter as raw materials for restaurant and home dishes (Note: Products processed by this method have passed the first phase of safety toxicology tests for new food resources). People in the Guanzhong region of Shaanxi province have the habit of eating cicadas. These cicadas are mainly fried. Fried cicadas are highly nutritious, with protein content exceeding 75% in their dry form, which is 2.8 times higher than beef of equal weight. Cicadas also have many medicinal and health functions. It is estimated that over 100 tons of cicadas can be collected annually in the Guanzhong region of Shaanxi, making it a worthwhile species to develop.

3. Salted Insect Foods (Semi-finished)

In addition to frozen bagged insect foods, canned salted insect foods can also be produced if conditions permit. The process flow is as follows: Raw materials — Remove impurities — Boil in water — Clean — Salt cure — Can — Seal — High-temperature sterilize — Seal mouth — Label — Salted insect food

Different methods are used for removing impurities from raw materials depending on the type of insect. Salt curing involves soaking in brine (3% to 6%) for disinfection for over 2 to 8 hours before bottling or canning.

High-temperature sterilization generally lasts 110°C for 20 minutes or 100°C for 2 to 3 hours. Salted insects are not only suitable for processing cicadas, locusts, and yellow mealworms but also for other edible insects.

Salted insect canned food is mainly semi-finished products, primarily supplied to restaurants and families as raw materials for making insect dishes. If properly sealed and thoroughly sterilized, salted insect food can remain fresh for over a year.
