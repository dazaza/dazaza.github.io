Jia's New Residence

by diglog on 2010-10-01 10:50:32

When it comes to the private residences of super-rich individuals, compared to Bill Gates' super-futuristic smart mansion or Larry Ellison's Japanese garden estate, Steve Jobs' house could be considered rather modest. It was a rustic brick cottage located on the roadside at 416 Santa Rita Avenue in Palo Alto. You can take a look at it yourself via Google Maps' Street View mode [here](https://www.google.com/maps/place/416+Santa+Rita+Ave,+Palo+Alto,+CA+94301).

In fact, Jobs himself was considering moving to a new home. You might think that Jobs, being wealthy, would find buying a new house quite easy. However, he once refrained from purchasing any furniture because none of it appealed to him. As a result, his plan was to build his own house.

Last year, we wrote an article titled "Steve Jobs' House Demolition." Now, there is finally an update to this story:

Back in 1983, at the age of 29, Jobs purchased a property in Woodside, a town on the Peninsula of California. The house was built in 1926, half a century before, by Daniel Cowan Jackling, an American copper magnate at the time. From 1983 to 1993, during most of the time Jobs spent away from Apple, he lived here until two years after his marriage when he and Laurene Powell moved to the rustic cottage in Palo Alto.

By around 2000, this old building had deteriorated significantly, with multiple collapses making it uninhabitable (see "Inside Steve Jobs' Old House," where some readers commented that Jobs' old house was perfect for shooting ghost movies :p). Jobs also ceased maintaining it. In 2004, Jobs applied to the Woodside Town Council to demolish the old house, planning to build a new one on the original land. The Town Council had initially approved Jobs' application, but local historical preservationists sued both the Town Council and Jobs, halting the demolition.

The preservation group argued that the building was designed by the late renowned Californian architect George Washington Smith, representing the Spanish Colonial Revival style, which was already rare in the area. Moreover, it was the residence of the legendary mining figure Daniel Cowan Jackling, featuring numerous household utensils and decorations made of copper, reflecting the living environment of early 20th-century California families. Therefore, it should be preserved as cultural heritage.

In 2006, the California Superior Court ruled in favor of the preservation group, ordering Jobs not to demolish the old building. Subsequent appeals by Jobs' lawyers for a retrial were all rejected by the court.

However, after six long years of back-and-forth, the court finally agreed this year that Jobs could demolish the old structure, allowing him to build a house of his liking on the original land. According to Gizmodo's report, Jobs has already submitted detailed design plans for the new residence to the town of Woodside:

According to the plan, the budget for the new Jobs residence is $8.45 million, not exactly a mansion but rather a typical family-friendly home. It occupies only one-third of the original building area, approximately 450 square meters (the red area in the image above): five simple rooms, many windowsills, an open deck platform, a garage that can accommodate three cars, and gravel-paved paths. Everything is simple, compact, practical, and comfortable.

There is also a large amount of idle land surrounding the house, and Jobs plans to plant six acres of flowers and plants around it, along with a small vegetable garden. Note that six acres is equivalent to more than 30 mu, or 24,000 square meters. In spring, this place will be a sea of flowers. This may not just be because of Jobs; his wife Laurene Powell has been maintaining a vegetable garden and flower garden at their doorstep for many years.

Compared to the luxurious homes of many wealthy individuals, Jobs' residence stands out as an anomaly. A building partner remarked while evaluating Jobs' new residential plan:

This plan clearly demonstrates an "abnormal self-restraint" for someone wealthy, something that only happens when the client gives explicit instructions to the architect demanding functionality and simplicity. It is certain that this architectural plan is the result of Jobs' specific requirements. Compared to Larry Ellison spending $70 million to build a Japanese garden estate, Jobs almost imposed the lifestyle of an Eastern ascetic monk on himself.

[Update]: A reader pointed out that the actual area of Jobs' new residence is 450 square meters, not 150 square meters, which has now been corrected. Thanks!
