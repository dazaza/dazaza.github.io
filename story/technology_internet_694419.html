Intellectual property protection has a far-reaching impact.

by keyizhang1 on 2012-03-07 09:38:31

Recently, legal disputes over trademark rights can be said to be "one after another", becoming one of the hottest topics.

First was the little known Shenzhen Proview company's lawsuit against Apple for trademark infringement over the iPad, which was likened to "an ant trying to trip up an elephant"; recently, American basketball "flying man" Michael Jordan accused China's Qiaodan Sports company of infringing on his name; at the same time, the name of NBA rising star Jeremy Lin was registered as early as 2010 by a sports goods enterprise in Wuxi, Jiangsu.

Who is right or wrong in these trademark infringement disputes remains to be seen. From a legal perspective, registering trademarks is not improper. However, how to judge, tests China's Trademark Law and judicial practice. In addition, the frequent occurrence of trademark infringement cases also reflects that small trademarks have big "profits" and "harm".

Why did the famous Michael Jordan get successfully registered by Qiaodan Sports far away in Jinjiang, Quanzhou? Currently, Qiaodan Sports' initial public offering application in the domestic A-share market has been approved and is in the midst of a busy listing process. What commercial secrets lie behind this reputation rights lawsuit?

Regarding this, the Strait Trademark Office of Fujian Province explained that since 2000, Qiaodan Sports has successively registered various Chinese and English trademarks and graphics related to Jordan. This practice complies with the current Trademark Law of our country.

It is understood that according to the Trademark Law, there will be a public announcement period after the trademark application. If no organization or individual raises objections during the public announcement period, then it takes about 10 months to 1 year to complete the entire process from application to registration.

Netizen Min Haixia expressed that Qiaodan Sports was born in 2000 and has existed for more than 10 years, but Michael Jordan only suddenly rose up now to file a transoceanic lawsuit, the "hang time" is slightly too long.

Can "Flying Man" Jordan's lawsuit in China successfully make a "powerful breakthrough" in the Chinese market?

On the evening of February 26, Qiaodan Sports officially responded, stating that the company did not intentionally use the name "Michael Jordan". Qiaodan Sports enjoys the business name right, and has exclusive rights to the registered trademarks of "Qiaodan", etc., and all main trademarks and defensive trademarks have obtained the "Trademark Registration Certificate", protected by law.

Brand manager Huang Tao of Qiaodan Sports stated that "Jordan" as a common foreign surname does not have specificity. The issuer of the company and Michael Jordan currently do not have any commercial cooperation relationship, nor has the company used his image for corporate or product promotion.

Trademarks are the "sharp weapons" of market competition. Although Michael Jordan said: "This lawsuit is not about money, it is about principle, it relates to my personal reputation." The economic value represented by trademark rights is self-evident.

When the "Qiaodan" brand was gaining momentum in the Chinese market, Nike, which had signed Michael Jordan, questioned Qiaodan Sports' trademarks several times, all of which were rejected by the Trademark Review and Adjudication Board of the State Administration for Industry and Commerce.

The Trademark Office and the Trademark Review and Adjudication Board ruled that: "Nike is insufficient to prove that before the application for registration of the disputed trademark, the cited trademark 'Michael Jordan' had already become a well-known trademark widely known and enjoying high prestige on clothing and other goods in China among relevant public. Meanwhile, athlete Michael Jordan only has a certain degree of fame in the field of basketball. But 'Jordan' is a common surname in English-speaking countries, in fields other than basketball, 'Jordan' does not have a unique corresponding relationship with athlete Michael Jordan. The athlete's fame cannot be equated with the fame of the 'Michael Jordan' trademark on clothing, shoes, and hats."

I found that currently there are many similar "Qiaodan" cases in China, such as "Kobe" basketball and "Maradona" football, etc.

Recently, Jeremy Lin, the Chinese-American basketball star of the National Basketball Association, quickly became famous due to his excellent performance, becoming a focal point of admiration both domestically and internationally. Two years ago, a sports goods enterprise in Wuxi, Jiangsu spent only 4,460 yuan to register the Chinese name "Lin Shu Hao", along with his English name. Additionally, a website with the domain name [lingshuhao.com] is seeking transfer, the website indicates that Lin Shu Hao has become the fastest growing brand name in the world in terms of value, worth approximately 14 million US dollars.

"Registering trademarks is a double-edged sword. Relying on famous brands and celebrities may bring 'profit' in the early stages of enterprise development, expanding fame and product sales. But in the long term, it may also bring greater 'harm', planting a time bomb for the enterprise itself." Zhao Zhanling, researcher at the Intellectual Property Center of China University of Political Science and Law, stated that enterprises should not only focus on short-term benefits, but besides having attractive trademarks, they should also have solid products.

Intellectual property protection has far-reaching effects.

Whether it is the outcome of the iPad trademark infringement case, or the final result of the Qiaodan reputation infringement case, the "profit" and "harm" triggered by small trademarks give profound thoughts and far-reaching impacts to Chinese enterprises—

On one hand, more and more Chinese enterprises should regard intellectual property as a top priority, focusing on playing the role of intellectual property protection in investment, research and development, innovation, stimulating economic growth, etc., taking multiple measures to protect intellectual property.

On the other hand, enterprises should also realize that the Chinese market is no longer a "closed-door market", and the international market is no longer a forbidden area for Chinese enterprises. International brands want to occupy the Chinese market, Chinese brands want to go global, the conflicts and competitions generated by the two on their respective development paths have quietly begun.

Ma Guangyuan, a doctoral candidate in economics at the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences, said that the iPad trademark infringement case sounded a warning bell for Chinese enterprises going abroad: to go international, one must not undervalue one's own trademark, and must take intellectual property protection seriously, possibly avoiding the awkward situation Apple is currently facing.

It can be seen that although enterprises registering various names with public resource nature as enterprise brands may facilitate market entry, the disputes thus triggered can easily damage the brand image, hurt consumers' recognition of corporate culture, thereby constraining the enterprise's transformation and development.

Experts say that although registering trademarks often carries the motive of improper profit-making, it is difficult to simply define it as illegal. Our country's Trademark Law solves it through a relatively passive cancellation mechanism, that is, if a registered trademark infringes on one's rights, one can request the Trademark Review and Adjudication Board to rule its cancellation within 5 years. It can be seen that if the infringement behavior is not discovered in time, or if it cannot be canceled in time after discovery, the registered trademark is still legally valid in form.
