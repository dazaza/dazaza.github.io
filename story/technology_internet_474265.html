The fashion separated from tradition is called old-fashioned, and the fashion separated from tradition is called superficial. White wine and red wine, though they take different paths, are both seeking a moderate state of harmony.

As for the current alcohol market, one surprising thing is that white wine has become fashionable while red wine has become traditionalized. This inverted marketing method raises questions about who truly embodies the Chinese spirit. If white wine doesn't talk about tradition but instead focuses on fashion, it detaches itself from five thousand years of civilization and becomes an element of high life. Then consumers may wonder how white wine can represent a noble element. Has the scholarly aura accumulated over thousands of years just disappeared? Perhaps this concept might make sense to the Chinese, as times change and technology advances. For the generation that grew up drinking cola and eating hamburgers, talking about national spirit might be a bit forced. But we shouldn't abandon tradition just because it seems outdated. It's like red wine entering China, which strangely emphasizes the cultivation of traditional culture, making it even more perplexing. It leaves people wondering who is more qualified to discuss cultural connotations.

Currently, the hottest marketing term in the white wine industry is undoubtedly "fashionable." It makes sense, as this traditional Chinese liquor with a long history has never lacked historical anecdotes. Liquors such as Maotai, whose brewing techniques are listed in the first batch of national intangible cultural heritage, Wuliangye that traces its brewing history back to the pre-Qin era, and Guojiao 1573 named after the founding year of "Guojiao," all seem to have a glorious history behind every famous brand, making them descendants of prestigious families. However, in modern times, this seniority-based Chinese thinking has lost its influence. Especially with the aggressive entry of foreign liquors that appear fashionable, appealing to the individualistic thinking of young Chinese generations, being overly conservative finds it hard to attract young consumers. Thus, white wine feels the crisis and pressure, leading to its transformation towards fashion.

Among the white wines pursuing fashion, none does it better than Swellfun (Shuijingfang). Without commenting on its acquisition by Diageo, which marked the first page of foreign capital entering Chinese white wine enterprises, merely looking at the fact that right after the acquisition was settled, Swellfun launched its "Swellfun First Well International Fashion Alcohol" and promoted the slogan "Swellfun - The First Fashion Element," shows how much it desires to embed fashion into its very essence. In the "2006 CCTV China Economic Annual Figure" selection event, Swellfun became the sole unit to fully sponsor the event. Experts commented: "People say that Chinese people drink alcohol but actually drink 'culture,' yet when selling alcohol, the Chinese fall far short of such romance. Compared to many well-known domestic alcohol brands, Swellfun is a latecomer. However, since its inception, Swellfun has infused the charm of 'culture' into every drop of its liquor."

2006 was the year that Chinese consumers re-recognized Swellfun. By seizing multiple opportunities for event marketing, Swellfun established its brand image; simultaneously, it broke away from the operating patterns of many backward domestic alcohol brands, focusing on high-end precise marketing, achieving remarkable results. Swellfun uses real actions to make consumers accept it, and also makes the white wine industry realize that cultural marketing can be done so subtly yet deeply flavorful.

Wuliangye, this giant in the white wine industry, naturally wouldn't miss any opportunity to let white wine revive.
