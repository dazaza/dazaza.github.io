I. The Role of Internal Audit in the Risk Governance Process

1. In the process where a company has not yet established a risk governance process, internal audit should actively propose relevant recommendations to management for establishing a risk governance process. If a company has not yet built a risk governance process, internal audit personnel should bring this situation to the attention of management while simultaneously proposing relevant recommendations for building a risk governance process. Because internal audit personnel are long-term positioned in specific roles within the enterprise, they are relatively familiar with the company's operations and can delve into the entire production and operation process at any time to understand and grasp specific situations. Only by conducting thorough pre-audit investigations and collecting a large amount of first-hand material can auditors identify potential risk issues, perform risk analysis, and based on the principles of materiality and cost-effectiveness, formulate comprehensive and realistic audit work plans. However, if management has established a risk governance process, then information from an integrated risk governance process will help internal audit personnel more quickly formulate audit work plans and improve efficiency. Therefore, internal audit personnel can promote the establishment of a risk governance process or make its establishment possible.

2. Internal audit can actively assist in the establishment of the company’s risk governance process through advisory services. Risk management is a complex system project; within an organization, responsibilities must be clearly divided, with each department performing their respective duties. The board of directors is responsible for setting strategic objectives, senior leadership is responsible for various aspects of risk management tasks, other managers are allocated part of the work by the management layer, operational staff are responsible for daily monitoring, and internal audit personnel are responsible for regular evaluation and assurance work. If management requests the establishment of a risk management system, the internal audit department can assist but must not exceed the normal scope of assurance and advisory services to avoid compromising independence. Internal auditors can promote and assist in the establishment of the risk management process but do not bear responsibility for risk management.

3. Internal audit focuses on assessing risk management as part of its audit work to inspect and evaluate the adequacy and effectiveness of the risk management process. Internal audit mainly evaluates the adequacy and effectiveness of the risk management process from two aspects.

(1) Assessing the achievement of major risk management objectives. This is primarily reflected in evaluating the growth and trends of the company and its industry peers to determine whether there may be risks that could affect corporate growth; reviewing the company's business strategy to understand the level of risk the company can accept; discussing departmental objectives, existing risks, and management's activities to reduce risk and enhance control with relevant management levels, and evaluating their effectiveness; assessing whether the risk monitoring reporting system is appropriate; evaluating the sufficiency and timeliness of risk management outcome reports; assessing whether management's analysis of risks is comprehensive, whether measures taken to prevent risks are complete, and whether recommendations are effective; conducting field observations and direct testing of management's self-assessment, checking the accuracy of the information upon which the self-assessment is based, and other auditing techniques; assessing management weaknesses related to risk management and discussing them with management, the board of directors, and the audit committee. If their accepted risk levels are inconsistent with the company's risk management strategy, it should be reported.

(2) Evaluating the appropriateness of the risk management approach chosen by management. Due to differences in cultural atmosphere, management philosophy, and work objectives among companies, the implementation of risk management also varies greatly. Each company should design a risk management process based on its own activities. Generally speaking, larger companies that raise funds in the market must use formal quantitative risk management methods; smaller companies with less complex operations can set up informal risk management committees to conduct periodic assessment activities. The responsibility of internal audit personnel is to assess whether the company's risk management method is appropriate relative to the nature of the company's activities.

4. Internal audit should actively and continuously support and participate in the risk management process, managing and coordinating the risk management process. Under the modern enterprise system, after a company has comprehensively established a risk management process, internal audit can thus take on the function of risk governance. First, internal audit begins by evaluating the internal control systems of various departments, identifying management gaps in areas such as production, procurement, sales, finance accounting, and human resource management, recognizing and preventing risks, and making relevant evaluations. Second, internal audit can delve into even the finest details of enterprise management to identify problems and analyze their rationality. Internal audit personnel focus more on the likelihood of risks occurring, delving into various stages of operational management to identify and prevent risks.
