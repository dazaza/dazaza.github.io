Zhejiang enterprises掀起a wave of mass entrepreneurship

by rodari on 2008-11-28 18:13:52

As the global financial tsunami hit, how should business owners and investors ride out the difficulties with laborers?

When some private enterprise bosses in Zhejiang diluted their own shares to give to employees, it immediately attracted attention.

Shen Lisong, chairman of Zhejiang Yishi Decoration Engineering Co., Ltd., is such a boss. He aspires to cultivate 100 entrepreneurs within his company, allowing employees who usually work as masons to participate in entrepreneurship.

Shaoxing Phoenix Zhuang Textiles Co., Ltd., on the other hand, gifted shares to its employees, enabling them to participate in dividends without bearing any losses.

At least there was no need to worry whether the boss was transferring liabilities to the employees, because this process had already started two years ago.

All employees except the boss are shareholders with "dry shares"

Shaoxing County Light Textile City Phoenix Zhuang Textiles Co., Ltd. is currently perfecting its 2009 version of the employee shareholding plan. According to the plan that has been passed by the shareholders' meeting, the scope of shareholding employees will expand from the management level in 2006 to all employees.

Compared to the first shareholding reform in 2006, this reform went very smoothly. In the first round of reforms in 2006, Jiang Chaoshu, general manager of the Guangdong branch, received 10% of the shares in the Guangdong branch, earning dividends of two or three hundred thousand yuan that year, which reached 400,000 yuan in 2007.

Thus, employees currently see only the benefits of holding shares. Since these shares do not require capital contribution, they only participate in dividends and do not bear the company's losses. Of course, shares without capital contribution can only receive 50% of the dividend funds. Jiang Chaoshu even introduced his daughter Jiang Linlin, who had just graduated from university, into the headquarters to serve as a specialist in administration and human resources.

The company's boss, He Xihui, is originally from Dongyang Village, Tuzhu Township, Luzhou City, Sichuan Province. In 1993, he opened a fabric shop in Shaoxing Light Textile City, mainly dealing in fabric trading. In 2001, he established the current Phoenix Zhuang Company and began producing related products. Most of the company's employees are from his hometown, mainly operating in women's casual wear fabrics, and have quite a reputation in the textile industry of Shaoxing County.

In the face of the financial tsunami hitting and the textile industry encountering a "cold wave," the shareholding reform of this enterprise even caught the attention of the China Light Textile City Management Committee.

This shareholding reform is similar to the 2006 plan but expands the scope of shareholding. In the previous reform, employees who had worked in the company for more than five years and were primarily responsible for production and operations received shares gifted by the boss. At the time, for convenience, the company's fixed assets were evaluated at one million shares, with each share equivalent to 100 yuan. Members of the management team received shares ranging from 9% to 13%. Of course, since these shares were gifted, they only had the right to dividends and no disposal rights.

The size of the shares is not determined solely by the boss but is evaluated by all shareholders based on individual contributions, deciding the number of shares accordingly. Employees can hold shares in either the headquarters or one of the five branches, but not both simultaneously.

To encourage employees to truly invest in shares, it was stipulated at the time that employees who did not contribute capital would receive dividends at a ratio of 1:0.5, meaning they could only receive half of the profit per share; correspondingly, they would not be responsible for the company's losses.

Employees who contributed 10% of the capital for their shares would receive dividends at a ratio of 1:0.6, with the capital ratio and dividend ratio increasing proportionally. If they contributed 50%, they would receive dividends at a ratio of 1:1 and bear corresponding risks.

"This is equivalent to selling the shares to employees at half price," said Jiang Chaoshu. Even so, no one invested at the time, "Back then, we didn't fully understand it and had doubts." In the end, only the general manager and deputy general manager of the Zhengzhou branch symbolically invested cash.

In the company's decision-making voting, everyone has voting rights, and each shareholder has only one vote, including in matters of distributing shares. "If it were based on proportions, the boss alone could make the decision, as the combined shares of other shareholders would not surpass his," said Jiang Chaoshu.

The biggest change in the 2009 shareholding reform plan is the addition of "department shares," equivalent to employee shares. However, these shares do not directly go to individuals but are distributed to departments first through year-end dividends, which are then further distributed to all employees within the department.

The reason for adding "department shares" is to "make every employee feel like a shareholder and treat doing well in the company as their own career," said boss He Xihui. As the company continues to grow, potential risks in development also increase, and this measure is aimed at responding to the current economic situation and helping the company and employees weather the difficulties together.

In fact, the company had already felt the cruelty of competition two or three years ago, and the current economic situation merely confirmed the earlier judgment. To adapt the company to the new situation and ensure healthy development, improving the company's operational system became inevitable.

Jiang Linlin, being a new employee, does not have any shares and can only receive dividends through the department shares in this reform. "No shares? There's no reason not to want them."

In the distribution of "department shares," employees with outstanding performance, those rated as excellent employees of the year, longer tenure employees, and department leaders may have their dividend ratios appropriately increased. Employees who have worked less than a year, those dismissed or voluntarily leaving the company during the year, do not have the right to dividends.

Like the personal shares of the management, each department also has different shares. For example, the sales department has 8,000 shares, while the logistics, administration, and human resources departments have only 2,000 shares.
