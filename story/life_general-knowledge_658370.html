The falling leaves are tired butterflies.

by lili1 on 2012-02-03 21:22:06

Falling leaves are tired butterflies.

Today is the first anniversary of my grandfather's death. I found an article online that once moved me a lot. Without my grandfather on the road ahead, I still have to walk forward... The article was written by the author about his mother. For my grandfather, I still chose this piece of text and dedicate it to you as well. May heaven be good...

As the sunset fades, the west wind grows stronger.

The leaves have fallen, and autumn arrives with the falling leaves. When autumn comes, people become thinner and more melancholy with the season.

But the golden yellow fallen leaves have no sorrow; they know how to comfort themselves in the autumn wind. They understand that their rest is for a new awakening.

There are advantages to falling leaves - they no longer need to get entangled in the complexities of love. There is beauty in falling leaves - they are like butterflies that have grown weary. I can even feel the faint calls of the falling leaves.

At that moment, my heart trembled slightly, as if I were one of the countless leaves fluttering down.

I saw my hometown, the old tree in front of our house that never ceases to thrive, and the cooking smoke that sways because of the returning wanderer. For those who have traveled far from home, and for the wings that soar in the sky, the cooking smoke is a rope that can never be severed. Like the big tree at the crossroads, its branches point to many roads, but there is only one starting point and one endpoint. Each person who leaves the village takes away a green leaf but leaves behind a root.

I saw the cliffs of my hometown, where stones on the cliffs compete with flowers to bloom; where sheep on the cliffs vie with clouds to drift.

I saw the eaves of my home, covered in icicles in winter and filled with bird songs in summer. A string of red peppers is often regarded as the spark of life in hard times. The sparrows flitting around the eaves harmonize so well with the farming households. The thing that constantly tugs at the heart of the traveler is this very eave.

I saw my mother, who, to keep us warm in the winter, picked up every dry twig, piecing together the broken days, then handed us warmth. The woodpile grew higher and higher, but mother became shorter and shorter. I saw her shriveled breasts, like two incomplete begging bowls, yet they provided us with a lifetime of feasts. The dim red flame ignited by mother in the stove became the only shoulder we could lean on during those nights, the only warm hand we could hold.

Leaves fall back to their roots - am I getting old? We spend so much time pursuing wealth but little time enjoying it; we have increasingly larger houses but spend less and less time living in them; we travel to the moon and return, yet find it difficult to visit the neighbors downstairs; we conquer the outside world but know nothing of our inner world.

Travelers, what sound makes you change your name and hide? What wind blows you to foreign lands? Autumn does this: it shakes the leaves off and hangs people's thoughts on the branches. It's time to go back, to see the great tree that gave birth to me, made me green with growth, and turned me yellow with maturity, along with the mother sleeping among the fallen leaves. Mother, my hurried steps are like your closely stitched seams. Mother, I who carry broken luggage will return, and even I who have found paradise will return.

Layers of fallen leaves cover the path home, and I will tread on this warm carpet to visit my mother. My mother is like these fallen leaves, slowly descending from the brilliant branches, only she hasn't awakened again.

This world, what keeps people here isn't houses, and what takes them away isn't roads. Time cannot stretch out a hand to grasp the passing clouds for you. If everything could be retrieved, mother, I would retrieve your smiles, your footsteps, and the breeze, using your love as lamp oil and your kindness as the wick. I would light it and place it in my heart, never forgetting the way home.

The cold weather has come, and the tree's leaves have fallen, bringing the tree closer to me. I seem to hear them slowly solidifying.

With the cold, they stand row by row, guarding secrets in their hearts that cause pain now and then. But the leaves have fallen, covering everything.

Mother has gone, leaving my soul without support, suddenly making me feel exposed to the winds from all directions. Yet the strong wind continues to blow, clearing the dust around my small hometown. My tiny hometown is being wrapped in autumn.

On mother's grave stands a tree, a poem I wrote for her. Every autumn, the leaves fall profusely, completely covering mother's grave. Those leaves sighing softly in the wind, seen from afar, resemble a group of weary butterflies, quietly gathering their beautiful moments in life: a red blush, a vow, or perhaps simply a sigh.
