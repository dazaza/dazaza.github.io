How do you deal with the common career confusion faced by technical personnel?

In my impression, technical personnel who have worked for two or three years are the most likely to encounter career confusion. At this point, they have some foundation in their specialty and some experience in business operations. Dealing with daily work is a piece of cake for them, so their lives are relatively comfortable. However, a person's most comfortable time is often also the time when they grow the slowest. For a technically minded person with ambition, what they may feel is not comfort but frustration. This might be the first bottleneck in the career development of technical personnel. At this moment, people become very restless, lose enthusiasm for their work, and there is an increasingly strong voice inside: I really don't want to continue like this anymore. Then comes the desire to switch jobs. The job is switched, but unexpectedly, the same situation appears again after half a year. I am not against switching jobs, but I am against blindly switching jobs. If the problem lies within oneself, changing companies will not solve the problem. Switching jobs should be done under the guidance of a career plan.

Also, a career plan needs to be very clear and cannot be a simple assumption. I often catch people below me, talking about life and ideals while casually discussing career plans. The situation is often as follows: when I ask him if he has a career plan, he says yes. When I ask him what it is, he says it is definitely moving to a higher position. So I keep asking, specifically what position, why choose this position, what are the requirements for this position, where is the gap between you and the target position, how long do you plan to use, and in what way will you improve your abilities...? Very few can answer all these questions.

Besides, I always think that helping subordinates clarify their career plans is the responsibility of the superior. An excellent manager can not only help subordinates formulate career plans but also actively strive for resources to meet their career growth requirements.

Why is a career plan important?

Because for a ship without direction, any wind from any direction is a headwind. Similarly, for a person without direction in their career, they become like a headless fly. The feeling of being a headless fly is very bad, and a career plan helps solve this problem. A person with a clear career plan will know exactly what they want and can focus on how to grow themselves on the existing platform. They will no longer get angry about some trivial matters in the workplace because that is really a waste of emotion. Instead, they should relax and gain more growth, which is equivalent to gaining a natural immunity. This should be what is known as emotional dullness.

For technical personnel, how should they choose a suitable direction?

For technical personnel, typical growth directions are two: technical route and management route. The former is from engineer to scientist, growing in expertise; the latter is from engineer to CTO, growing in management. Specific growth paths may vary depending on the business characteristics of each company, but they are generally similar. Whether growing along the technical line or the management line, the common requirement is to first become an excellent engineer. Why? The process of becoming a good engineer is the process of finding a suitable reference point. We need two or three years to understand the target position, more importantly, to understand ourselves, gradually realizing our advantages, disadvantages, and personal preferences, and then making a plan accordingly, which is more realistic. Secondly, if one cannot perform well as an engineer, even if you want to climb higher, you probably won't get the resources.

The next question is how we choose? The most direct advice is that those who are more interested in people, enjoy interacting with others, and have a high sensitivity to interpersonal relationships can prioritize choosing the management path. On the contrary, they can choose the technical path for growth.

Note that no matter what position, it is impossible to avoid dealing with people. In this sense, whether you like it or not, you should actively expand your interpersonal skills. A good architect is often very skilled at communication, just that he prefers technology a bit more. This is similar to a good technical manager who must have a certain technical foundation, otherwise it will lead to amateurs leading experts.

A large portion of engineers who tend to take the technical route worry that they will lose competitiveness after thirty. I think this has nothing to do with age. I know many people over thirty who are doing very well on the technical path. The key is, after five years as an engineer, if you have only repeated the same year five times, then who else should be eliminated but you?

When technical personnel face confusion, which paths are more conducive to getting out of trouble?

First, the most likely person to help should be your immediate superior. Your immediate superior is the person who understands your situation best. He has a relatively comprehensive understanding of your career growth, so he usually can give the most practical advice. Therefore, it is not a bad idea to communicate more with your superior, tell him about your confusion, and ask your superior for some advice. Of course, there is a psychological barrier to overcome here, which is the fear of facing your superior, thinking that telling your superior about your confusion is a pretty bad thing, and it might even affect your image in his eyes. My view is that helping subordinates grow is the duty of every superior, and it is part of management work, belonging to the scope of responsibilities. If a superior looks down on you because you told him about your confusion, then such a superior is unqualified, and I suggest applying for internal rotation or directly switching jobs.

Secondly, seek advice from senior peers. After all, there is pressure when dealing with immediate superiors. So our second choice is to exchange with peers who are at a similar level to our own superiors. This person should ideally have been working three to five years earlier than you (if it's only one or two years earlier, he himself may still be confused), and because there is no hierarchical correlation, communication may be smoother.

Thirdly, everything depends on yourself. Being in a state of career confusion is painful, and eliminating confusion can be even more painful. This stronger pain must be borne by yourself, which requires courage and determination. I have seen many people who just talk about it. I clearly gave them several specific methods, but they did nothing. Such people lack self-discipline and are unwilling to take responsibility for their own growth, so even gods can't save them.

During the process of career growth, besides confusion, there is another special condition called being out of sync, which is close to what is known as aesthetic fatigue. What should one do when they are out of sync? My suggestion is to do some beneficial physical labor. For example, if your main work during normal times is management, you can set your physical labor as studying open-source frameworks, reading code and writing comments. Although you may not read much each time, after accumulating for a year or half a year, you may also have studied seven or eight out of ten. Studying such a framework in detail takes quite a lot of time, and it's hard to find whole blocks of time to study it during normal times. The benefits of doing such physical labor are obvious? First, as a technical manager, continuously smelling the taste of ammunition helps remind yourself not to stay too far from the battlefield. Secondly, it's hard to say that one day a new career opportunity won't require you to make more contributions in architecture, especially in domestic situations. Of course, everyone can define their own beneficial physical labor according to their own circumstances.
