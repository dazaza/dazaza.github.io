What opportunities do the three major weaknesses of Amazon leave for e-commerce entrepreneurs?

by anonymous on 2013-09-18 21:59:10

What is Amazon's market share in the online retail industry? A piece of data provided by The Wall Street Journal once showed that last year, the combined total retail sales of the 12 largest online retailers outside of Amazon could not surpass the amount achieved by this e-commerce giant alone.

Thus, people often say, if you want to run an e-commerce website in the age of Amazon, aren't you afraid of being wiped out by this giant? The founder of a U.S.-based subscription e-commerce platform targeting the maternal and infant sector answered: If you focus on what Amazon does worst and innovate sufficiently in that area, then you still have a chance.

Recently, the site's founder, Mauria Finley, introduced her views on the American tech blog Pandodaily and pointed out three weaknesses of Amazon in the e-commerce field.

The primary reason for the existence of entrepreneurial opportunities in the e-commerce industry lies in the fact that this market is indeed large enough. E-commerce retail in the U.S. has been growing rapidly, with eMarketer estimating that the growth rate will reach 14.8% in 2013. Between 2012 and 2017, the average annual growth rate will also stabilize at 14%.

In this vast market, Amazon will continue to grow larger, but as its size increases, its various weaknesses will become increasingly apparent.

Finley, speaking as a mother, recounted the shortcomings of shopping on Amazon: "As a new mother, I am too tired and busy to have time to spend hours looking for quality products like I used to. And websites like eBay don't offer traditional e-commerce formats either."

Amazon's convenience lies in knowing exactly what product you want to buy. However, when your purchase intent isn't clear, it can be very confusing. As a newcomer in any specific shopping category, finding truly trustworthy, high-quality products with relevant user references isn’t easy, especially when faced with multiple pages of product listings.

What Amazon lacks expertise in are these three areas: product curation and display, product discovery, and social recommendations.

Firstly, product curation and display means offering a special selection and shopping experience. It’s akin to browsing through a brand store with its own style and ambiance, where you're drawn to the charm conveyed by the products inside. But Amazon emphasizes practicality; you can quickly select the diapers or computer you want, but styling the presentation of products with flair is Amazon's weak point.

Secondly, product discovery is another innovative field. The advantage of offline shopping lies in the serendipitous discovery of surprising items. Online, however, achieving this is not easy. Take Amazon's website as an example; it offers such a vast array of products that after just a few pages, you might feel overwhelmed by the sheer volume. How to reduce users' stress during shopping while maintaining a sense of novelty is something entrepreneurs should seriously consider.

Finally, social recommendations. Shopping requires opinions and decision-making based on others’ information. Although there is a massive amount of review information on Amazon's site, this information is more useful for purchasing items like digital products or non-personalized goods. However, for fashion-related or personally preference-driven purchases, opinions from similar groups become much more important. This is why communities centered around shopping often emerge in these fields.

Online shopping will become an increasingly common online lifestyle for more and more people. After experiencing the traditional shopping experience driven by Amazon, people will become increasingly interested in products that offer new shopping experiences. Like the essence of technology, its allure lies in providing different choices to different groups of people.

Although there have been countless failed cases in the exploration of building new types of e-commerce websites, it doesn't mean that this direction is wrong. Instead, it depends on whether you truly understand the nature of others' weaknesses. Of course, the premise is that innovation should not violate the fundamental principles of an industry.
