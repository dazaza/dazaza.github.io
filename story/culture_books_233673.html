Father and Son Special Forces: Vietnam Jungle Warfare - The Buried Sword Author: Yu Jingxu, Shui Nengchen

by jqrxass on 2009-09-05 14:52:08

In 1995, retired Chinese General Zhang Guodong and retired American General Carl Anderson arrived in Vietnam at the same time. They did not know each other but came for the same purpose: to find the remains of their sons who had gone missing 26 years ago during the Vietnam War. Zhang Guodong's son was named Zhang Jianhua and had been a special forces instructor; Carl Anderson's son was named Carl Tom and was also part of the special forces. Zhang Guodong was accompanied by his English-speaking grandson, Zhang Chongwu, while Anderson was accompanied by his granddaughter, Alice. Based on the clues they possessed, with the cooperation of relevant Vietnamese authorities, the two elderly generals began their investigation. Unexpectedly meeting each other, they both had a strong feeling that their sons might have been opponents back then. Therefore, they decided to share information and intelligence, and conduct a joint investigation.

The pain of burying one’s own child is the most tragic and sorrowful thing in the world. Holding a pocket watch, Zhang Guodong told Carl Anderson that their sons had died at the same moment. There is an ancient Chinese saying: "We do not wish to be born on the same day, but we wish to die on the same day." This saying originally referred to sworn brothers of different surnames, but it ended up being true for these two adversaries.

"The Buried Sword" is a long novel describing the jungle warfare between Chinese, Vietnamese, and American special forces during the Vietnam War era. It showcases the love and hatred of two generations of soldiers and recreates heroic images through artistic grandeur. A war legend sealed away for forty years, a journey to find lost family after the smoke of battle has cleared; a vast and magnificent war canvas, stirring emotions of camaraderie amidst the battlefield! A literary masterpiece delving into the cultural foundations of China and America, restoring the authenticity of history, and showcasing the love and hatred of two generations of soldiers while recreating heroic images through art.

Part One of The Buried Sword

Zhang Guodong and Carl Anderson faced immense difficulties in their search. Every time they reached a place, the leads would always break off, yet each time there were unexpected twists. During their investigation, scenes of brutal battles that had taken place in the jungle were repeatedly replayed. Eventually, they found the remains of their sons. Zhang Jianhua and Carl Tom had perished together. Carl Anderson found an identity tag on his son's remains; Zhang Guodong found a rusted pocket watch under his son's remains, which was a war trophy he had captured during the Korean War.
