Breast ptosis is a physiological phenomenon, commonly seen in middle-aged and elderly women who have been pregnant and lactated. It occurs because the mammary glands and connective tissues proliferate, causing the breasts to enlarge, followed by atrophy. With the enlargement of the breasts, the skin and suspending support structures lose elasticity due to stretching, and under the effect of gravity, they cannot return to their original state, resulting in loose, drooping breasts that resemble bags.

Breast ptosis can be divided into three degrees based on its severity:

Degree I: The breast sags, and the nipple is parallel to the inframammary fold.

Degree II: The nipple position is below the inframammary fold but higher than the lowest point of the breast.

Degree III: The nipple is located at the lowest point of the breast. In some cases of severe ptosis, especially in those with hypertrophy of the distal part of the breast, even though the ptosis is severe, the nipple position is not at the lowest point of the breast. Such cases should still be considered as Degree III ptosis.

The external appearance of breast ptosis generally comes in three forms: spindle-shaped breast ptosis, triangular scarf-shaped breast ptosis, and bull-horn shaped breast ptosis.

Spindle-shaped breast ptosis is characterized by shortening of the transverse diameter of the base of the breast. The circular coronal plane at the base of the breast is smaller than the coronal plane at the distal end of the breast, resembling a spindle, hence the name spindle-shaped breast ptosis. This type of breast ptosis deformity is caused by the herniation of breast tissue into the subcutaneous area of the areola region, often associated with large areola syndrome. It is common in middle-aged and young women after breastfeeding.

Triangular scarf-shaped breast ptosis presents the entire breast as flat, like a triangular scarf hanging on the chest wall, hence the name. The coronal plane at the base of the breast is long elliptical, with the longitudinal diameter of the breast greater than the transverse diameter, without obvious herniation of breast tissue into the subcutaneous area of the areola region. It is common in middle-aged and elderly women, especially postmenopausal women.

Bull-horn shaped breast ptosis has the coronal plane at the base of the breast and the distal coronal plane almost equal, or slightly smaller at the distal end, with a longer longitudinal diameter of the breast. The nipple is located at the bottom line of the breast, resembling a bull's horn. There is a relative increase in fibrous and fatty tissues within the breast, with fewer mammary tissues. The breast feels denser, and the skin elasticity is good. This type of breast ptosis is common in young women.

Methods for correcting breast ptosis: There are many surgical techniques for correcting breast ptosis, such as dermal fixation, breast lifting and fixation, double-ring fixation, etc. The basic principle of all these techniques is to lift and fix the sagging and loose breast tissues and remove the corresponding "skin-areola" to achieve a normal breast appearance. However, since removing skin can lead to scarring, which affects aesthetics, modern practices more frequently use the double-ring fixation technique to correct breast ptosis. After this surgery, there will only be a scar at the junction of the areola and the skin, which becomes almost invisible over time.

Correcting breast ptosis solely through breast augmentation is not advisable, as breast augmentation cannot lift and fix breast tissues and skin. It merely increases the volume of the breast temporarily, increasing skin tension. As edema subsides, the continued relaxation of the skin and tissues and the downward displacement of breast tissues can result in an unpleasant appearance known as "double-peak breast," with different textures.
