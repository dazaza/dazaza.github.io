by hdudkjsk on 2011-05-19 13:21:49

Red blood threads are a facial phenomenon caused by poor dilation performance of capillaries, damage to the stratum corneum, or the shallowness of some capillaries. They appear as fine, crisscrossing lines resembling a spider web, distributed sporadically. In severe cases, they can merge into patches, turning the face red. This type of skin is thin and sensitive, and becomes even redder when exposed to extreme cold, heat, emotional excitement, or sudden temperature changes. People with red blood threads on their faces appear redder than those with normal skin tones; in some cases, only the cheeks are red, with clear, round boundaries. This kind of skin is thin and sensitive, and in serious cases, it may lead to the formation of deposited pigmentation spots. Treatment for such spots is difficult, not only affecting physical beauty but also causing psychological shadows and bringing great inconvenience to daily life.

Traditional treatment methods for red blood threads and their drawbacks: Angiectasis has long been a treatment challenge in dermatology. Before the invention of new lasers, common treatments included cryotherapy, high-frequency electroknife therapy, and isotope radiation therapy. Although some of these treatments could achieve certain effects, they were prone to cause serious complications such as ulcers, scars, and radiation necrosis, failing to achieve ideal cosmetic results and being unacceptable to beauty-conscious individuals.
