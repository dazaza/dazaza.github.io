Google Chairman Schmidt: The Importance of University Education Is Irreplaceable

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 20:43:35

Although the question of "whether college is still worth attending" has become one of the most fiercely debated topics among the American public today, Google's executive chairman Eric Schmidt firmly believes that the foundational role of college education can never be replaced, and thus attending college is still "very much worthwhile."

Due to the rising cost of higher education in the U.S., improvements in the quality of online education, and the persistently sluggish American job market, the utility of attending college has sparked heated discussions among the American public. However, Schmidt, who attended Princeton University and the University of California, Berkeley, believes that there should be no doubt about the importance of obtaining a college education.

At a recent panel discussion hosted by the New America Foundation, Schmidt explained why college education remains important. Other notable attendees at this event included Ann-Marie Slaughter, president of the New America Foundation, and Jon Steinberg, president of social news website BuzzFeed.

During the discussion, Schmidt stated that college education not only helps improve the quality of life for those educated but also increases their potential earnings. Schmidt said: "A significant part of college life isn't just about studying hard but learning how to live away from home, collaborate with others, and experience various life lessons. I think these things take a considerable amount of time."

Schmidt continued: "I believe parents should have expectations regarding their children attending college. From an economic standpoint, people with a college education typically earn more than those without. When the education sector thrives (which we hope is the case for the U.S.), it drives the real estate market, which in turn boosts the construction industry — jobs primarily held by people without degrees. I think parents would certainly not want their children to miss out on the next wave of educational prosperity."

Schmidt added: "Over the past 50 to 100 years, higher education has shown a trend toward increasing stratification, which has well suited our needs. I have two daughters, and I want my children to be part of this system, as they already are. I believe college education makes adults more mature. In my view, claims by Peter Thiel and others that college education and its associated costs are essentially a waste of time simply do not hold up."

Furthermore, Schmidt discussed the importance of liberal arts education in colleges: "It is obvious that we need not only more well-educated individuals but also more people educated in the liberal arts. At Google, we hire these young, talented people in their twenties. We provide them with professional training. After receiving such training, they all become excellent employees."

Regarding employee training, Schmidt believes companies should invest more effort into this area. Whether for newly hired employees or experienced staff being promoted to management roles, Google offers corresponding professional development programs applicable to a wide range of employees. Google has established an educational training project called "GoogleEDU." Besides excelling in data management and investment matters, Google continuously strives for perfection in its recruitment and employee management practices.

Schmidt indicated that none of this implies that the current American education system is already perfect. On the contrary, due to historical, cultural, and vested interest factors, the American education system is far from reaching its optimal state and has yet to achieve its full efficiency.

Schmidt said: "How do we improve these deficiencies? By quantitatively evaluating them. Over the next few years, we will likely see 100 different educational approaches, and we must evaluate these methods since they are now based on cloud computing technology. For example, I am a board member of the non-profit educational organization Khan Academy. The academy is currently testing a new teaching method to determine if the questions posed to students are appropriate and whether they are closely related to what the students are learning."

Finally, Schmidt emphasized that replacing college education with online education is not the ultimate solution. He believes that while we can certainly use network technology to make college education more complete, efficient, and reduce costs, nothing can compare to the irreplaceable value of the on-campus college education experience.
