[Planning] "Fear-to-Return Tribe" and "Year-Dodging Tribe's" Financial New Year's Eve Dinner

[Case] The Spring Festival is approaching, and for Xiao Hua, the so-called year-end really feels like a significant "test." Xiao Hua will be 23 years old after the New Year. Since graduating from technical school at the age of 18 in Jiangxi, he has been working alone in Shanghai as an operator in an automobile manufacturing company. Over the years, his monthly post-tax salary is around 4000 yuan, with bonuses, overtime pay, and other benefits amounting to an annual income of about 58,000 yuan. However, after deducting rent of 2000 yuan per month, utility bills of 500 yuan, and various daily living expenses, Xiao Hua has barely saved anything.

Last year, Xiao Hua started dating a young woman named Xiao Juan from Shanghai. Xiao Juan works as an assistant beautician at a beauty salon, earning around 3500 yuan post-tax each month. They plan to save more money before considering marriage in a few years.

Xiao Hua hasn't returned home for the New Year for three years. The cost of travel, gifts, red envelopes, socializing, and facing family inquiries has become overwhelming both financially and emotionally. He always finds excuses such as being busy with work or not being able to buy train tickets during the Spring Festival rush.

However, this year, if Xiao Hua stays in Shanghai, as a "quasi-future son-in-law," he would have to attend a family gathering at Xiao Juan's house. Facing her large family, he would need to prepare gifts for elders and red envelopes for children, and his generosity couldn't appear too meager. Feeling financially strained, Xiao Hua decided to apply for an out-of-town car repair assignment during the Spring Festival period to avoid it altogether.

Xiao Hua has become a typical member of the "Fear-to-Return Tribe" and "Year-Dodging Tribe." Deep down, however, he still hopes for a day when he can truly celebrate a reunion with his family.

The "Fear-to-Return Tribe" and "Year-Dodging Tribe's" Financial New Year's Eve Dinner

"The heart that moves restlessly" — an old song expresses the loneliness and helplessness of many wanderers. Home is a warm harbor, an irreplaceable place. In traditional Chinese culture, returning home for the New Year is essential. However, in recent years, more and more young people have joined the "Fear-to-Return Tribe." Some fear the hassle of buying tickets during the Spring Festival travel rush, some cannot afford the costs of returning home, some dread parental pressure to marry or arranged blind dates, and others fear questions from relatives about their jobs and salaries. Even those already at home sometimes find the financial burden of giving red envelopes and visiting relatives unbearable, leading them to join the "Year-Dodging Tribe."

For Xiao Hua, who finds it hard to return home or stay home, his decision to go on an out-of-town assignment during the Spring Festival is a reluctant choice. However, avoidance is not a permanent solution. It's crucial to adapt strategies to individual circumstances, manage finances wisely, and prepare a "financial New Year's Eve dinner."

Cold Appetizers - Cool-Faced Cost Cutting

The first course of the "financial New Year's Eve dinner" is the cold appetizer. Just as financial management begins with "cool-faced" frugality, discipline is essential; otherwise, financial planning remains an illusion.

Whether part of the "Fear-to-Return Tribe" or "Year-Dodging Tribe," most people aim to avoid financial expenditures, especially among low-income "moonlighters" like Xiao Hua. For example, if Xiao Hua were to return home for the Spring Festival, round-trip train tickets would cost him at least 500 to 600 yuan, gifts for his parents another 800 to 1000 yuan, plus New Year goods, presents for friends and relatives, and red envelopes for younger generations, totaling nearly 5000 yuan, exceeding his monthly salary.

Xiao Hua earns 4000 yuan per month, but half goes to rent (2000 yuan), followed by utility bills (500 yuan), leaving only 1500 yuan for daily transportation, meals, clothing, etc., which quickly depletes his funds. Analyzing his expenditure structure reveals that rent consumes too much of his total income. Reducing this expense could significantly improve his financial situation.

Two methods can reduce rent: one, Xiao Hua could rent a smaller, older apartment in a less convenient area with cheaper rent, reducing it to within 1500 yuan, while also considering increased commuting costs; two, Xiao Hua could share his current apartment with a colleague or seek a roommate through housing websites, cutting rent and utility costs in half. While sharing may sacrifice some privacy, establishing clear agreements on shared expenses can prevent misunderstandings.

If Xiao Hua finds a suitable roommate, he could immediately save around 1200 yuan per month. Other minor expenses can also be trimmed. For instance, buying a bicycle for commuting saves on transportation costs, eating at work during overtime helps economize, choosing the right mobile phone package, and wearing work uniforms regularly can all contribute to savings.

For those who ultimately decide to return home for the New Year, small tips can help reduce travel expenses. For example, purchasing New Year goods and gifts online via group-buying can save 20-30% compared to shopping in department stores. If train tickets are hard to come by, consider long-distance bus tickets or carpooling with fellow countrymen. Credit card points can also be exchanged for gifts, and applying for interest-free installment payments can ease financial pressure.

Hot Stir-Fried Dishes - Timely Investment

The second course of the "financial New Year's Eve dinner" is hot stir-fried dishes. Financial management involves both saving and earning, with cool-faced saving contrasting with the heat of earning.

At any time, we must remember that "time has value," a key principle in investment. Suppose two people, A and B, are both 10 years old. A starts managing finances at 10, investing 100 yuan per month with a 12% annual return, while B does nothing. After six years, A's account grows to 10,470.99 yuan, while B's remains empty. At this point, A stops adding to the investment, letting the money grow, while B starts investing 100 yuan per month with the same 12% return. After 30 years, A's account balance reaches 376,428.45 yuan, while B's is 349,496.41 yuan. Yet over these 36 years, A invested only 7,200 yuan, while B invested 36,000 yuan. Despite similar investments over 36 years, timing makes a huge difference. B's total investment exceeds A's, yet his final balance is less due to the magic of "time." Starting just six years earlier gave A the edge over 36 years.

Therefore, financial investment requires "hot stir-frying," starting early is best. No matter when you start, it's never too late. Delaying like the cry-bird, enjoying today and waiting for tomorrow, leads to tragic outcomes. With insufficient pension and medical security systems, building a solid financial safety net early is the best strategy against future hardships.

Many mistakenly believe finance is for the wealthy, but everyone needs financial management, with different asset allocation priorities. Xiao Hua, with no property or assets, belongs to the lower-middle-income group. The key to his financial management lies in "mandatory savings." Following the cost-saving plan, Xiao Hua can save around 1500 yuan per month. It's recommended that half be transferred into three-month or six-month fixed deposits, and the other half invested in secondary bond funds. Low-income families should avoid high-risk products like stocks or equity funds, prioritizing safety. As Xiao Hua is only 23, he can moderately allocate to controlled-risk products like secondary bond funds, which primarily invest in low-risk bonds but allow up to 20% in stocks. This offers growth potential in good markets and loss control in bad ones.

Investments often suffer from inertia. By emphasizing saving immediately upon receiving salary, Xiao Hua avoids spending unconsciously. Setting up automatic transfers from his salary card on payday and initiating regular investments in secondary bond funds ensures mandatory savings and investment. Over time, Xiao Hua will be pleasantly surprised by the growing numbers in his account.

Fine Wine - Enhancing Education and Professional Qualifications

For the "financial New Year's Eve dinner," cold appetizers and hot stir-fried dishes are essential, complemented by a fine wine, enhancing the meal's flavor. Fine wine symbolizes education and professional qualifications.

China is entering an economic transformation period focusing on "sustained growth and structural adjustment," requiring specialized talent proficient in advanced technology. Observing the development of capitalist societies, the industrial revolution freed numerous manual laborers, pushing simple operators towards skilled professionals—a lesson relevant to contemporary China.

Xiao Hua's technical secondary school diploma is relatively low. Although currently stable in a large automobile manufacturing company, without further education, his career may stagnate as a basic operator. At 23, imagining 30 years stuck at the bottom, repeating monotonous tasks, earning a fixed salary, or even facing layoffs, reflects a complete career failure. To change this, Xiao Hua must enhance his education and professional qualifications.

Xiao Hua can develop three three-year educational plans: the first focusing on adult continuing education to obtain an associate degree, the second upgrading to a bachelor's degree, aiming to complete undergraduate education before 30, and the third pursuing a part-time master's degree. Additionally, during the second three-year plan, Xiao Hua can attend professional skill training courses to obtain relevant national certification.

Holding multiple degrees and professional qualifications may not fully reflect actual abilities but opens more opportunities—promotions, job changes, or career shifts. Seizing one opportunity can drastically alter life trajectories.

Continuing education is a significant investment but highly cost-effective. Xiao Hua should maintain mandatory savings and consider credit card installment payments to reduce tuition burdens. Scholarships or employer incentives, though limited, reward hard work.

Dessert - Adding Life's Joys

The final course of the "financial New Year's Eve dinner" is dessert—delicious mousse cakes, rich red bean paste, or beautiful fruit platters—all enjoyed after the feast. Financial management's goal isn't to live like an ascetic monk; we should occasionally add color and joy to life.

Life's joys often involve spending. Wealthy people enjoy lavish pleasures, while simple pleasures suffice for the less affluent. Who says dining at a five-star hotel surpasses drinking beer with friends at a roadside stall? Dating can easily inflate expenses. Greek philosopher Epictetus said, "Happiness depends not on external conditions but on habit." Xiao Hua and Xiao Juan, with modest incomes, should consider "low-carbon" dating options, like watching movies at home, cooking together, or playing badminton in parks instead of frequenting cinemas or malls.

True emotions are built on shared ideals. Creating a family together requires controlling unnecessary spending. Like Xiao Bei in "Snail House," despite financial constraints, he and Hai Zao found ways to enjoy life happily despite their poverty.

Chinese people hold special sentiments for the New Year's Eve dinner, symbolizing family reunions and fulfillment. Both the "Fear-to-Return Tribe" and "Year-Dodging Tribe" secretly yearn for such moments. Adjusting financial habits and planning wisely can make this "financial New Year's Eve dinner" less burdensome, eventually savoring the warmth of family.
