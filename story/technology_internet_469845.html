Story 1

One day, a tiger encountered a gadfly. "Don't disturb me while I'm hunting for food. Otherwise, I will eat you," the tiger angrily growled.

"Hehe, if you can reach me, come and eat me," the gadfly mocked the tiger and then landed on its nose to suck blood. The tiger tried to scratch it with its claws, but the gadfly flew to the tiger's back and hid in the fur to suck blood again. The tiger furiously swung its tail like a steel whip to drive the gadfly away. The gadfly burrowed deeper, and the tiger rolled on the ground trying to crush it. The gadfly then attracted a large group of companions who attacked the tiger together. It wasn't long before the tiger was gasping for breath.

Never look down upon small things. Compared to the tiger, the gadfly is tiny, yet it could lead to the tiger's death. In fact, our biggest enemy in management is often the small faults we disregard.

Many times, businesses fail because managers have the mindset of prioritizing big issues over small ones, thus allowing these small problems and defects to fester. These small problems and defects are precisely what we should be most cautious about.

Story 2

An old pine tree was cut down, which had been the home of an owl. This owl had always lived in a dark and gloomy hollow in this tree. Due to its age, the trunk was already rotten. Among the many residents of the hollow were numerous plump rats, all of which had been rendered limbless by the owl using its sharp beak. The owl kept them in a pile of wheat as a way of feeding them. There was logic behind the owl’s actions.

In the past, the owl had caught rats, but they all escaped from it. To prevent this from happening again, the owl made sure that any rat it caught would be disabled. Without legs, it was convenient for the owl to eat them at will, managing its consumption one per day. This solved the problem of not being able to finish all the rats at once and also aligned with principles of health preservation. Thus, the owl planned for the future just like humans do: if the rats were to escape after being caught, there would be no choice but to eat them immediately; but since it couldn’t finish all of them at once and needed provisions for urgent situations later, some food must be stored. Therefore, the owl had to both find a way to raise the rats and ensure they didn’t escape. After much thought, the only solution was to disable the rats so they couldn’t run away. To keep the rats from starving, it even brought them grains.

A successful business has many collaborators, and the best strategy is to make them dependent on your existence for survival. This principle can also be applied to company management. A successful manager should cultivate a substantial talent reserve for the company.

Story 3

There was a wealthy Arab. However, his house was infested with many mice.

To deal with the rat problem, he specifically got a cat known for catching mice. This cat was especially good at catching mice but also liked eating chickens. As a result, all the mice in the Arab's house were caught, but there were hardly any chickens left.

His son wanted to get rid of the chicken-eating cat, but the father said, "The pests in our house are the mice, not the chickens. The mice steal our food, destroy our clothes, dig through our walls, and damage our furniture. If we don't get rid of them, we will surely starve and freeze. So we must eliminate them! Not having chickens means we won't eat them, but we are far from starving or freezing!"

Benefits and drawbacks are often two sides of the same coin, hard to separate. To deal with something, introducing another might sometimes cause more harm than the original issue. Company operators introduce various systems to prevent employee misconduct, but these systems themselves are also a considerable cost. Therefore, the balance between benefits and drawbacks depends on the intelligence of the operator.

Story 4

The frogs had no king. Seeing that other animals all had kings, they thought they should also have a king to rule them. So, they asked the gods to appoint a king for them.

The gods found their behavior very amusing and threw a round and thick piece of wood into the pond. The frogs were terrified by the sound of the wood hitting the water and all dived deep into the water.

This piece of wood thrown by the gods was regarded by the frogs as a king given by the gods. In reality, it was just an ordinary piece of wood with nothing special about it. But when it appeared on the water surface, the entire pond became much quieter than usual. Perhaps out of respect for this silent king, they spoke less to each other and behaved more peacefully.
