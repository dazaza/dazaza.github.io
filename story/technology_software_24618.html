Trend Master Wei Lun Ye Exposes Insider Information: Antivirus Vendors Are Deliberately Mis-killing

by huahuaniu on 2007-07-13 02:31:39

This year, Norton's "mis-killing gate" caused a large number of computers to crash, and the subsequent antivirus software "mis-killing" incidents continued to occur. On one side, viruses were rampant, while on the other side, "healthy" programs faced the risk of being mistakenly killed, leaving users at a loss.

The industry's inside story gradually came to light. Yip Wai Lun, President of Trend Micro China, told reporters yesterday that in fact, some antivirus vendors intentionally mis-killed. Some did it to gain fame, while others took the risk of mis-killing to expand their antivirus result lists. Trend Micro has just announced the establishment of a "Cybersecurity Monitoring Laboratory" in Shanghai. Yip Wai Lun helplessly stated that foreign brands are striving to adapt to the fierce competition in China, "but there are certain principles that must be upheld."

Catering to customer psychology by indiscriminately killing "innocents"

Reporter: How much impact did the Norton mis-killing incident have on "foreign brand" antivirus software?

Yip Wai Lun: Sales were not affected much, but whether customers understand or not, they will inquire about the product's mis-killing rate when purchasing. This can be considered an educational role of the incident. Of course, some users may delay purchasing Norton, but it does not have much impact on other manufacturers.

Reporter: In the antivirus software industry, is mis-killing a common phenomenon?

Yip Wai Lun: It should be said that no software can completely avoid mis-killing, but the key is what you mis-killed and whether you damaged system software and user databases, which would be serious mistakes.

Reporter: Is it true that some companies intentionally mis-kill to make their virus list appear longer to show off their technical strength?

Yip Wai Lun: Indeed, some manufacturers use mis-killing to create selling points because users usually think the more viruses detected, the more effective the antivirus software is. Of course, this is a misunderstanding, but manufacturers take advantage of this point, such as putting some dubious programs into the "blacklist."

Reporter: According to your understanding, how many manufacturers in China are doing this?

Yip Wai Lun: The previous war against rogue software was a typical example. In fact, whether those programs called "rogue" should be put into the "blacklist" is debatable, but some manufacturers are still doing it. However, Trend Micro will stick to its principles on this issue, even if customers complain that the listed viruses are not enough.

China produces the largest number of malicious software annually

Reporter: Some say the concept of "antivirus software" is outdated because the threat from trojans has surpassed malicious viruses?

Yip Wai Lun: Among the threats encountered in cybersecurity, the proportion of trojans and other malicious software has exceeded traditional viruses, and the creators often target specific objectives, such as attacking online banking for theft rather than just for fun.

Reporter: In China, is the "virus creation" chain more special than abroad?

Yip Wai Lun: According to our monitoring, the number of computer viruses and malicious software produced annually in China ranks first globally. Therefore, antivirus software vendors domestically must emphasize detection rates, response times, etc. Undoubtedly, Chinese peers excel in this area and have won many individual consumers.

Reporter: Because of this, do international brands' antivirus software entering China have natural weaknesses?

Yip Wai Lun: Weaknesses can be overcome. After our monitoring center moves to China, the collection and analysis of virus samples and response speed will significantly improve, and we have also published virus codes specifically targeting the Chinese market.

Reporter: By establishing a security monitoring laboratory, does Trend Micro want to lean towards personal customers and directly compete with domestic brands?

Yip Wai Lun: Trend Micro's business focus has always been on corporate clients. Now, foreign antivirus software has obvious advantages in the enterprise client market, but due to the monitoring laboratory being overseas, services for individual users are somewhat lacking. The purpose of establishing a security monitoring laboratory in China is to reverse this situation, but it does not mean that we will adjust our business focus.
