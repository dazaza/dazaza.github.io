Coronary heart disease has two critical periods.

by 60888888 on 2010-02-01 10:52:47

Today, I will introduce two critical periods for coronary heart disease that are prone to misdiagnosis. Below, I will explain them one by one.

After the age of 40, during the amplification period, it is easy to be misdiagnosed.

The occurrence and development of cardiovascular diseases are indeed closely related to age. In people around the age of 45, there is often an "amplifier" effect, meaning many middle-aged individuals exhibit various symptoms similar to cardiovascular diseases. At this time, if the doctor lacks experience, patients are easily misdiagnosed with coronary heart disease. Therefore, individuals in this age group who want to confirm whether their discomfort is caused by cardiovascular disease should go to the cardiology department of a large hospital to avoid "miscarriages of justice." Otherwise, it not only increases psychological burden but may also lead to unnecessary treatments.

Women entering menopause may inexplicably experience palpitations, chest discomfort, tightness in the chest, and feel particularly severe. Professor Zhou Yujie introduced that these symptoms may cause panic, assuming it is heart disease. In fact, this is often caused by "menopausal cardiac syndrome." When seeking medical attention, due to patients excessively exaggerating their cardiac discomfort or due to insufficient doctor experience, it is often misdiagnosed as organic heart disease.

In fact, "menopausal cardiac syndrome" is a special type of menopausal syndrome, a functional disease caused by endocrine disorders in the body. Therefore, it must not be mistaken for coronary heart disease or angina pectoris, leading to self-medication. Women in menopause experiencing such issues should first visit the gynecological endocrinology department for examination.

After the age of 60, during the silent period, it is easy to be overlooked.

An elderly person experienced toothache when they were anxious or active, and even painkillers did not improve the condition. After examination, it was found to be angina pectoris. Professor Zhou Yujie explained: "The common location of angina pectoris is behind the sternum or in the precordial area. Typical angina pectoris presents as dull pain or constricting pain, radiating to the left shoulder and left arm. It generally appears after fatigue, emotional excitement, or a heavy meal, and the pain may last for a few minutes, relieved by rest or sublingual nitroglycerin. This is called 'angina pectoris.'" Elderly people, especially those of advanced age, often have "silent" cardiovascular diseases. Even when angina or myocardial infarction occurs, the pain diminishes. Many elderly people with heart disease only show symptoms when the condition is severe, as if processed by a "silencer," making it very easy to be overlooked. By the time diagnosis is confirmed, it might be too late.

How can we prevent the "silencer" phenomenon from happening to the elderly? First, use other examination methods to detect early. People with high-risk factors for cardiovascular disease, if categorized as "high risk" in risk stratification, should undergo further examinations even if their electrocardiogram is normal. These include 24-hour Holter monitoring, echocardiography, cardiac stress tests, and coronary artery CT scans, detecting potential heart abnormalities before a heart attack occurs. Second, identify through symptoms other than pain. When angina or myocardial infarction occurs, even without pain, myocardial ischemia or necrosis already exists, possibly affecting the heart's pumping function, reducing blood supply to important organs, causing symptoms such as sudden unexplained fatigue, dizziness, shortness of breath during activity, difficulty breathing, nausea, vomiting, and upper abdominal discomfort. All these should be checked to exclude the possibility of myocardial ischemia or even myocardial infarction.

Why does the location of angina vary from person to person?

During an angina attack, both the nature and location of the pain differ from person to person. This is because the heart itself has no pain nerves. When the myocardium experiences ischemia and hypoxia, many metabolic products accumulate locally. These products stimulate receptors, triggering neural impulses that travel through the sympathetic nerve ganglia in the chest to the corresponding spinal segments, then further upload to the cerebral cortex, producing the sensation of pain. Most pain transmissions occur in lower segments, causing localized pain in the left chest or left arm. However, if the transmission segment is higher, it may manifest as toothache, jaw pain, or throat pain, which explains why the pain location of angina differs.

Therefore, some manifestations of angina seem less related to the heart, but we should not overlook them. If pain or discomfort occurs in the aforementioned areas under conditions such as physical exertion, full meals, cold air stimulation, or emotional changes, and these symptoms disappear within a few minutes after the trigger disappears, it is likely a sign of angina.
