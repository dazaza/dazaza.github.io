First: The issue of promotion and planning. Everything requires a goal, knowing it before taking action. This is especially true in the commercial market. How big a company wants to be, what percentage of the market share it aims to occupy, and what sales targets it wishes to achieve (100 million yuan annually or over 300 million yuan within two years) must be considered while thinking about how to promote. These questions are essential because having clear goals makes promotional actions more effective. Promotion is not merely about organizing one or two events, conducting sales promotions, or placing advertisements. Promotion must be viewed from a broad perspective; it must have planning, strategy, and steps. It should aim to achieve the most effective results with optimal resources.

Second: The issue of promotion and positioning. After making a loud noise initially, new brands and products inevitably require promotion. Many companies often make the mistake of having a "small impact" during the early stages of promotion due to inaccurate positioning. A brand needs clear positioning to target specific customers (especially in today’s diversified market demand where customer needs are increasingly varied). Therefore, it is necessary to identify where the target customers are. Only by identifying the customers can we understand their needs, consumption characteristics, and what our competitors are doing. How do we discover our unique advantages? This is the work that market research needs to accomplish. Only after clarifying these points can an effective promotion plan be formulated to progressively achieve victory.

What is the positioning of the **** brand? If its positioning is described as mid-to-high-end, this is too vague! This is not positioning! Because positioning should address the future development direction of the enterprise and brand. Positioning should be clear.

Third: The issue of promotion and costs. Carrying out promotion involves costs. Different budget allocations result in different promotion plans. The worst scenario is "a clever housewife cannot cook without rice." Similarly, "logistics first" reflects this principle. As the ancient saying goes, "the army has not moved yet, but the provisions have already been prepared."

Fourth: The issue of promotion and product channels. There are many forms of promotion, depending on the product and customer. The key lies in what choices the enterprise will make. Promotion must have planned priorities for channel promotion and terminal promotion, which differ in methods. The **** brand plans to conduct franchise recruitment, agent channel expansion, and local specialty store terminal formats in the future. Additionally, for new brands, the initial promotion focuses more on the enterprise and the brand itself. These are matters I need to carefully consider because I cannot afford wasteful promotion.

Fifth: The issue of promotion and long-term industry development. Market demand requires collective efforts from everyone to drive progress. Therefore, a company's development cannot be separated from the overall industry environment. Promotion must also align with the industry environment. The most feared situation is being at the forefront of the charge but ultimately failing to reap the benefits of victory. Collaborating with major enterprises and relevant industry associations and institutions to formulate effective promotions and build one's reputation is crucial. Only by starting high can one see further. "Aim for the highest, and you may reach the middle," so it is even more necessary for me to view certain matters from a high industry standpoint.
