Mom, I once hated you.

by gfrw0738 on 2010-04-13 23:20:29

My mother and I are incompatible, or so I have repeatedly thought. It's because we're too alike. I've inherited almost everything from my mother: her youthful appearance, her abundant emotions, her slowness to act, the romanticism of her youth, and also her stubbornness, her obsession with face-saving, her indecision when faced with anything that has buttons, her lack of direction and fear of horses.

After I started elementary school, I became a kid leader, with two or three dozen children following me around. I was domineering, highly commanding. Every day, I led a group of children from the school residential area climbing mountains, crossing rivers, climbing trees, and catching fish. I organized their colorful childhoods. At the age of six, I even convinced a group of children older than me by three or four years to climb a several dozen meters high oil tank at a military enterprise under my leadership. We climbed up along a narrow and open iron ladder, lay disordered on the curved surface of the oil tank, gazing at the bright moon. I remember one of the older kids said something profound to me: "Facing the ground, how small we are." On the way back, I was extremely proud of this feat. When I got home, however, I was met with my mother's belt. She made me take off my pants and lie on the bed. I still remember the sensation and sound of the belt striking my flesh; I cried my heart out. Later, my mother told me that after beating me, she cried endlessly herself. She didn't know what to do with such a mischievous child. Because she worried about what other mischief I might get into, I was sent to school at the age of six, bidding farewell to the happiest six years of my life. This beating was very effective; I suddenly changed into a compliant child, obedient and well-behaved.

I began to study hard, consistently ranking first in exams, becoming the obedient child in my mother's eyes, making her happy. In middle school, there was a period when I grew tired of writing essays. I hated reading comprehension, always answering questions incorrectly, and I detested the three-part essay structure because I never had any opinions. During a school-wide essay competition, I didn't even qualify to enter. That afternoon, the dim light shone through the small window onto my half-eaten bowl of rice. My mother wouldn’t let me eat dinner; she stood on the bed and scolded me furiously. As a Chinese teacher, she couldn’t accept such an outcome. She threw down her chopsticks, hitting the bowls, causing grains of rice to jump around, making me tremble with fear. "Starting today," my mother declared, "you must write an essay every night for me to review. I don’t believe your essays can't improve!" That night, I began writing my first nightly essay titled "Desk Lamp" — "When I want to study, your eyes light up, watching over me silently; when I don't study or work hard, you remain gloomily dark here." From that moment on, I kept writing nightly essays until now, already dozens of volumes, though no longer needing my mother's review.

After I turned seventeen, my life was entirely arranged by my mother. She was authoritative at home, deciding and arranging everything. The obedient ones were my honest father and me. My reckless brother often fell outside of her control, and it took her a long time to accept this reality.

Every evening, my mother was always the last one to sleep. She would go out for a walk, personally sensing the temperature of the place, then come back and prepare our clothes for the next day. When I refused to wear cotton pants in summer because it made me lose all style, one night my mother shouted loudly and argued fiercely with me. She judged others' feelings based on her own, believing that it was already cold enough to wear cotton pants. That argument was terrible, audible throughout the entire building. Despite my determination to resist, I was ultimately overpowered. The matter ended with me wearing a bulky pair of cotton pants to class.

In addition to choosing physical items, my mother also arranged my spiritual world. She carefully selected magazines and newspapers for me, subscribing to many each year despite her modest salary.

My mother married late and gave birth to me when she was already thirty-one. My most rebellious early youth coincided exactly with my mother's early menopause. At that time, I was very disobedient, often resisting my mother's arrangements, leading to constant conflicts. My mother would shout loudly, and I would shout louder. Then my mother would cry and bring my father to lecture me. But my father often remained silent about my mother's irrationality and fussing. Finally, my mother would pull out a green suitcase from the cabinet, crying and muttering as she packed things, threatening that either she or I could stay in the house, and she would return to her parents' home in Hebei. But this suitcase was always packed and unpacked, never fully prepared. The conflicts between my mother and me were always so intense, causing me great pain. I often stood on the fifth-floor balcony imagining jumping down, picturing the various scenarios of my stiff body surrounded by my mother's tears. In my imagination, her sorrow satisfied me superficially. After each failed argument with my mother, I indulged in such imaginings. I had jumped off buildings countless times, and each time, my mother’s reaction was different, growing more painful each time.

In 1989, I applied to Fudan University, also chosen by my mother. Those days, she often heard a Shanghai teacher at her school talk about how excellent Fudan was, and how her children must attend top-tier schools. Although my scores were sufficient, unexpectedly due to special circumstances that year, Fudan did not recruit students from our province, and I missed the chance to reapply, ending up being admitted to a less prestigious foreign language institute. After six years of university, my performance was so poor that everyone believed I should have gone to Nanjing University or Fudan University, including my mother who was so confident. But fate cruelly dashed my mother's hopes. For years, my mother lamented the loss of my Fudan dream, even thinking about it daily until today, years later. Only then did I understand the extent of the devastation this event caused my mother.

It was said that my mother excelled academically during her school days, and the university she dreamed of attending was the one I eventually attended. Unexpectedly, because of my grandfather's history of serving as a military doctor in the Nationalist army, this historical stain caused my otherwise qualified mother to be denied admission due to her "bad background." Sometimes, history repeats itself in astonishing ways. My mother was too strong-willed, too obsessed with face-saving. I've thought about it many times: was it because of this incident that shaped my mother's subsequent personality—irritable, easily angered, anxious, lacking security, distrustful of the future and people around her? I don't know the answer.

My mother's life turned out that way, so she imagined me as another version of herself, meticulously polishing and designing me, placing her unfulfilled dreams on my shoulders. I was her entire career. Her expectation for me was to finish university and continue studying until obtaining a Ph.D. But she didn't expect that I started dating in my sophomore year. Perhaps to defy her early arrangements, I lived a life in college that was beyond her control—free and unrestrained. In accordance with my mother's expectations, I passed the postgraduate entrance exam, but stopped pursuing her "Ph.D. dream" after achieving just a little success.

After graduation, I wanted to be as far away from my mother as possible, going somewhere she couldn't control me. I ran all the way to Hainan, then Shenzhen. Being far from my mother, only when I needed to live independently did I realize how tenderly my mother had nurtured my life by never letting me do housework. At the same time, it made me incapable of doing anything besides studying—unable to do housework, unable to interact with people. Facing society, I felt utterly lost, like a clumsy fool.

Being far from my mother, without opportunities for intense conflict, I began to think about my mother's various good qualities. I called her every week, chatting at length. My mother aged, losing the dominance she once had. Faced with the irregular pension payments, she felt powerless against society. She often fantasized about her old-age life—whether to live with my brother or with me—and repeatedly discussed this issue with my father until he couldn't bear it anymore.

Last Spring Festival, I brought my parents to Shenzhen to stay. Riding the elevator downstairs together, only the two of us in the elevator, I noticed that I was almost a head taller than my mother. Suddenly, I saw the nearly bald spot on her head, her face full of wrinkles, her murky eyes. She seemed helpless in this unfamiliar place, unsure where to put her limbs, acting unnaturally. She was like a child, nodding in agreement with whatever I said, even flattering me when she cried. I stared at this frail old lady with a strange gaze for a long time, wanting to burst into tears: it was this old lady before me who decided my past life. Back then, she was invincible, controlling everything, unbeatable. Now, she is small, facing a society she increasingly doesn't understand, becoming like a big child, hoping for my care. Crossing the street, she stretched her neck nervously like a newborn bird, anxiously looking left and right, her body rigid, always wanting me to hold her hand.

My mother has grown old, truly small. Along with the passage of time, she carries not only her age but also the strength and power of her prime years. Now, she is merely an old lady who needs my comfort, someone who repeats the same sentence countless times, desperately seeking others' attention.

I want to hug my mother and cry, telling her: forgive me, mom. All those years, I loved you. But now, I only wish to take care of you, gently accompany you, hoping you feel comfortable, safe, and secure with me, finally free from worry and anxiety.
