Amoebas discovered in the Pacific Ocean's Mariana Trench, the deepest sea trench on Earth

by wujizhui on 2011-11-21 05:10:50

According to a report by the American Physicists Organization Network on October 23, during an expedition this summer, Scripps Institution of Oceanography at the University of California, San Diego discovered a type of giant amoeba in the deepest part of the ocean. Researchers stated that this new discovery opens up a new continent for future studies on biodiversity, biotechnological possibilities, and adaptability to extreme environments.

In July, Scripps researchers and engineers from National Geographic sailed to the Mariana Trench in the Pacific Ocean, reaching what is currently known as the deepest part of Earth's waters. They detected these amoebas, named Xenophyophores, at a depth of 10,641 meters underwater in the Sirena Deep. These single-celled organisms are Foraminifera, generally exceeding 10 centimeters in size, with an extremely large population, clearly dominating among various life forms. Researchers said that these amoebas are the largest existing single-celled animals, a life form well-suited to dark, cold, and high-pressure deep-sea environments. They can accumulate high concentrations of lead, uranium, and mercury by capturing particles in the water and can withstand high doses of heavy metals.

Previously, records only showed these amoebas had been found at depths of about 7,500 meters in the New Hebrides Trench. This expedition carried free-falling landers equipped with digital cameras and light sources to explore previously unvisited waters. They used instruments called "Dropcams" to locate marine animals. "Dropcams have versatile uses, carrying underwater automatic cameras, HD cameras, and internal light sources, capable of capturing high-quality videos at any depth in the ocean," said Eric Berkenpas, remote-sensing camera engineer at the National Geographic Society. "It adopts a thick-walled glass sphere design, able to support pressure of eight tons per square inch. Additionally, it can be equipped with bait to attract marine life for filming."

"This type of amoeba is a very rare kind found in the deep sea; their massive size makes them highly suitable for extreme deep-sea environments, playing a dominant role among various multicellular organisms there," said Lisa Levin, professor at Scripps Institution and director of the Center for Marine Biodiversity and Conservation. However, people know very little about this animal, which is structurally important like many other deep-sea creatures. As human activities expand into deeper ocean areas, we need to conduct in-depth research on them.
