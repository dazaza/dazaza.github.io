Improvement methods for inferior jujube tree varieties: It is difficult to distinguish between varieties during the dormancy period or the budding period of jujube trees. You need to determine the objects that require improvement when last year's jujube fruits are nearly mature. Decide on high-quality varieties through observation and evaluation, avoiding inaccurate identification of improvement objects during the grafting period, blind grafting, and mixing good with bad. If all the trees in the orchard need to be grafted, this step can be skipped.

Scion preparation: First, determine the variety and mother plant that needs to be grafted. According to the above method, collect scions from better-performing mother plants. Scions collected from one-year-old jujube trees are optimal. The scions should have buds that are plump, free from jujube madness disease and quarantine targets, with a thickness of 0.4-0.8 cm, leaving 1 cm above the bud and 4-5 cm below. After collecting the scions, perform wax sealing treatment. The material for sealing the scions is industrial-grade paraffin wax. The temperature of the wax should not be too high, ideally between 70°C-80°C. Too high temperatures can easily damage the scion buds, phloem, and cambium layer, making them difficult to store. After sealing the scions with wax, if they are not used immediately, they should be stored in a cold storage facility under humid conditions.

Grafting methods: There are two most suitable methods for improving the quality of inferior jujube tree varieties: cleft grafting and bark grafting. After the jujube tree bark separates, the survival rate of bark grafting is higher than cleft grafting. Cleft grafting requires three points: the cut surface should be flat, the cambium layers should align, and the binding should be tight. Bark grafting, also known as sub-bark grafting, is simple to learn and easy to operate. It is a method with a very high survival rate, with skilled grafters achieving a 99% success rate. However, using bark grafting for variety improvement must wait until after the jujube tree bark separates.

Post-grafting management: After grafting, there is a process for the cambium layers of the scion and stock to heal, and the dormant buds of the stock may quickly sprout after stimulation. This creates competition for nutrients between the scion and the stock, so regular and timely bud removal is necessary to concentrate nutrients on the scion, promoting the healing of the scion and stock. Generally, remove buds every 3-5 days. Fertilizer and water management should generally be done before grafting to enhance the vitality of the stock and activate the formation layer of the stock, enhancing the healing ability of the formation layer's meristematic tissue, thereby increasing the survival rate.
