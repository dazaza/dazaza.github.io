Case: Recently, there was a patient named Ms. Liu in the newspaper who consulted about her sleep issues. She had been taking sleeping pills for quite some time but would always wake up in the middle of the night and could not fall back asleep. She asked the doctor whether she should take another sleeping pill.

The Doctor's Response: In ancient times, due to agrarian lifestyles, people had more relaxed lives, and with various physical and mental factors influencing them, insomnia became a common issue. For treating insomnia, tranquilizers are one of the doctor's go-to solutions. Many insomnia patients also seek out and consume "sleeping pills." Additionally, the notion that "taking sleeping pills can lead to dependency" is deeply ingrained in many people, and many patients are terrified at the thought, refusing medication, which leads to their condition worsening. In reality, treating insomnia is a scientific and rigorous matter, and insomnia patients should take medication under the guidance of sleep specialists and follow standardized treatments. Finding the root cause of insomnia is key.

In recent years, the incidence of insomnia has continued to rise. It is estimated that about 20% of Americans (40-70 million) suffer from chronic or intermittent insomnia, while in China, approximately 30%-35% of the general population experiences insomnia. A 2006 survey on insomnia in some rural areas of the People's Republic of China showed that about 57% of elderly individuals suffered from insomnia. In Guangzhou, the rate of insomnia among the red-haired population reached as high as 60%.

Insomnia can be both a primary disease and a clinical symptom of other diseases. The critical first step in treating insomnia is identifying its cause. There are multiple reasons for insomnia:

1. Various emotional or mental disorders (depression, anxiety, mania, schizophrenia), which are relatively common, accounting for about 50%;

2. Various physical illnesses (pain, restless leg syndrome, heart failure, brain injury), accounting for about 20%;

3. Sleep apnea syndrome (commonly known as snoring), accounting for about 15%;

4. Primary and complex insomnia, accounting for about 15%.

Insomnia is not a simple problem. When experiencing insomnia symptoms, one should not assume it is primary insomnia without further investigation, nor should medication be taken arbitrarily. Professional diagnosis and testing are necessary to determine the cause, type of insomnia, and assess the severity before receiving standardized treatment.

How should sleeping pills be taken for insomnia?

As previously mentioned, there are many causes of insomnia, such as physical illness or pain, psychiatric conditions like depression, side effects of medications, circadian rhythm disorders, or obstructive sleep apnea syndrome. Therefore, the best method for treating insomnia is to find and treat the underlying cause, i.e., causal treatment. For example, if the cause of sleep disturbance or insomnia is depression, then antidepressant medication is the best choice. Similarly, if the reason for inability to sleep is joint pain caused by arthritis, the best treatment drug would be a painkiller.

Currently, sedative-hypnotic drugs are an important clinical tool for treating insomnia. Clinically, these drugs should be chosen based on the different types of insomnia:

1. Difficulty falling asleep: Choose drugs that induce sleep quickly, such as short half-life sedatives like zolpidem and zaleplon.

2. Waking up during the night or shallow sleep: Select hypnotics that extend sleep duration, such as ramelteon.

3. Early awakening: Use medium or long-acting sedatives. Early awakenings are often associated with depression-induced insomnia; thus, while treating insomnia, attention should be given to the primary disease.

4. Insomnia due to sleep apnea: Focus on treating sleep apnea, using methods such as CPAP or surgery.

5. If insomnia accompanies anxiety or depression, use anti-anxiety or antidepressant medications.

6. If insomnia is caused by mental abnormalities, use antipsychotic drugs, possibly combined with sedatives.

If a patient wakes up in the middle of the night, can they take an additional sleeping pill? Different sedative-hypnotic drugs have varying effects on insomnia symptoms. Choosing the right medication is crucial. Some patients initially experience good results with a certain sedative-hypnotic drug, but after a period of time, the effectiveness may diminish or even disappear, as seen in Mr. Liu’s case. In such situations, clinicians should reassess the patient’s condition and adjust the treatment plan accordingly.

Mr. Liu recently experienced waking up in the middle of the night and being unable to fall back asleep. Possible reasons include:

1. Coexisting other diseases:

- Most commonly, depressive or anxiety disorders. Acute insomnia is one of the causes of depression and anxiety disorders. If primary insomnia is not treated early and appropriately, it may lead to mood disorders such as depression and anxiety. Conversely, depressive and anxiety disorders can cause secondary insomnia symptoms like daytime alertness, early awakening, and difficulty falling asleep.

- Concurrent sleep apnea hypopnea syndrome (SAHS). When SAHS develops or worsens, it may cause nighttime breathlessness, difficulty breathing, trouble falling back asleep after waking, and fatigue during the day. If an insomnia patient has a family history of snoring, childhood snoring, obesity, thick necks, or recent weight gain, combined with insomnia symptoms, SAHS should be highly suspected.

- Various physical illnesses. Chronic insomnia can weaken the body, leading to hormonal imbalances and impaired organ function, making the patient susceptible to various physical ailments. Disrupted sleep can exacerbate existing physical illnesses, creating a vicious cycle.

2. Drug tolerance. Long-term use of a specific sedative-hypnotic drug, especially benzodiazepines, especially when used improperly, may result in drug tolerance.

Ms. Liu should seek help from a sleep specialist as soon as possible for reevaluation and examination to clarify her condition. If other diseases are found, comprehensive treatment targeting those specific diseases should be administered (e.g., adding anti-anxiety treatment for anxiety disorders and adjusting the dosage and type of the original sedative-hypnotic drug accordingly). If drug tolerance has developed, adjustments can be made as follows:

1. Change medication. Gradually reduce the original drug while switching to another suitable drug for the patient.

2. Add a sedative-hypnotic drug. This applies only when taking less effective drugs, such as zaleplon or zolpidem. Since different drugs have different half-lives (the time required for metabolism and excretion in the body), caution is needed. For instance, the half-life of zolpidem is 1.5-4.5 hours, and slower metabolizers may need 4.5 hours for the drug to wear off. If a patient needs to get up for work at 7 PM the next evening, they should avoid taking additional zolpidem after 2:30 AM to prevent drowsiness affecting their daily life. Patients must consult with their doctor to understand the half-life of the medication and whether it is appropriate to add more after a certain time.

3. Increase pre-sleep medication dosage. If the patient's original dosage is low, consider increasing it before bedtime. However, if the original dosage has already reached the maximum safe limit, no further increase is recommended.

4. Add another drug. Before combining two or more drugs, doctors should scientifically evaluate the patient's condition and strictly adhere to indications.

Are you afraid of side effects or addiction from sleeping pills?

After reading about Ms. Liu's medication adjustment, many people might wonder: how much harm do all these drugs cause? What if I become addicted? To address these concerns and misconceptions, sleep medicine experts strongly advocate: correct diagnosis, standardized treatment—please take your sleeping pills with peace of mind!

"All medicines have side effects," which is true. Any drug (even vitamin tablets) has some side effects. However, side effects aren't necessarily scary as long as they are minor and within the range that our bodies can tolerate. Newer sedative-hypnotic drugs, primarily benzodiazepines, if misused, may lead to drug tolerance or dependence. Abrupt cessation may make insomnia rebound worse. But these drugs' toxic side effects on the human body are generally minimal. With advancements in geriatric medicine, many newer sedative-hypnotic drugs have been introduced and applied clinically. These drugs differ from traditional benzodiazepines, with fewer side effects and generally no risk of dependency, ensuring safety for a wide range of insomnia patients.

Here, there is an important point for insomnia patients to note: not treating insomnia and avoiding medication can be far more harmful to physical health than taking medication! Sleep is a fundamental physiological need for humans. Lack of sufficient rest due to insomnia can lead to endocrine imbalance, impaired bodily functions, weight loss, fatigue during the day, and anxiety or depressive disorders affecting work and life. Please manage your sleep scientifically.

Scientific sleep management is very important. Good sleep habits can effectively prevent various sleep disorders. For insomnia patients, forming good sleep habits will help with quicker recovery. Here are a few tips for scientific sleep management:

1. Maintain regular sleep schedules. Avoid erratic sleep patterns, staying up late one night and sleeping excessively the next.

2. Exercise reasonably. Engage in moderate exercise in the afternoon or after dinner. Exercising before bed can affect sleep, while vigorous morning exercise can cause daytime fatigue.

3. Follow a healthy diet. Ensure balanced nutrition. Avoid drinks containing caffeine or theobromine, such as coffee or tea, especially in the evening or before bed.

4. Adopt the right attitude towards sleep. Do not overly focus on or worry about sleep. Occasional sleepless nights or poor sleep quality can be ignored. Avoid excessive concern or anxiety, which might turn occasional insomnia into chronic insomnia. Consult professional doctors to learn more about sleep knowledge.

5. Seek medical help promptly if problems arise. Don't delay treatment! Many sleep problems can be quickly resolved in their early stages.
