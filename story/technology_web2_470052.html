Yesterday, staff members from Ningxia Entry-Exit Inspection and Quarantine Bureau were conducting nuclear radiation monitoring on imported Japanese products in a special warehouse at Yinchuan Hedong Airport. Picture taken by Xinhua News Agency reporter Peng Zhaozhi.

Xinhua News Agency report - In response to the possible impact of the Fukushima Daiichi nuclear power plant accident in Japan on China, the National Nuclear Accident Emergency Coordination Committee issued an authoritative announcement on March 28:

According to reports from the International Atomic Energy Agency, the situation at the Fukushima Daiichi nuclear power plant has stabilized, with surrounding environmental radioactivity levels continuing to decrease. Following the detection of artificial radionuclide iodine-131 in the air in northeastern Heilongjiang Province, minute amounts of radioactive substances have also been detected in parts of the southeastern coastal areas.

Experts believe this is the result of radioactive substances released from the Japanese nuclear accident diffusing through the atmosphere. The dose that the public may be exposed to is less than one hundred thousandth of the natural background radiation dose, which will not affect the environment or public health, and no protective measures are necessary.

Based on comprehensive analysis by the World Meteorological Organization, the Beijing Regional Environmental Emergency Response Center of the International Atomic Energy Agency, the State Oceanic Administration, and the Ministry of Environmental Protection (National Nuclear Safety Administration), it has been determined that the Fukushima nuclear power plant accident in Japan has not affected China's environment or the health of residents within its borders.

China may detect increased radioactive levels

Based on meteorological data, the general direction of atmospheric circulation is currently from west to east, but there is a small circulation in northeastern Japan that has brought extremely minute amounts of radioactive pollutants to China. This is a preliminary judgment.

As radioactive substances continue to disperse, China may detect increased radioactive levels, but this does not mean it will affect the environment or health. Even if some regions detect data that is slightly abnormal compared to local baseline levels, the radioactive substances have been greatly diluted and will not reach levels that could affect public health. Current detection technology and instruments are very advanced, so there is no need for concern - Expert Chen Zhuozhou from the National Nuclear Emergency Coordination Committee.
