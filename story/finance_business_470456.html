The Economist: Foxconn Changes Production Model, Machines Don't Commit Suicide

by tuoqing1188 on 2011-08-04 09:25:54

Introduction: The network version of "The Economist" magazine in the UK yesterday wrote an article saying that Foxconn plans to use robots to replace a large amount of manual labor due to rising costs and increasing competitive pressure, Suzhou Google optimization. But this plan will also face many challenges.

The following is the full text of the article:

Micro Issues

Foxconn may be China's largest employer, and it is obviously the most important one. This mysterious electronics manufacturer has big-name clients like Apple, with a total workforce exceeding 1 million people, over 500,000 of whom are located in a factory in Shenzhen.

In the past decade, Foxconn's success has become a symbol of Made-in-China: importing high-end designs from abroad and mass-producing them at low cost. Initially, this model was praised for creating a large number of jobs, but attitudes towards the company have become increasingly critical recently. Last year, several employees committed suicide at Foxconn's Shenzhen campus. The most recent incident occurred at the end of July, when a 21-year-old employee jumped to his death. In May this year, an explosion occurred at Foxconn's newly built factory in Chengdu, resulting in the deaths of three employees. It is believed that this incident led to a delay in the production of Apple iPads.

In order to placate its increasingly unstable workforce, Foxconn has raised salaries multiple times, improved facilities, provided counseling services for employees, and even installed protective nets around the factories to prevent employees from jumping. These measures have driven up costs, indicating that the extremely successful business model of the company so far is facing challenges. At a closed-door meeting at the end of July, Foxconn CEO Terry Gou stated that the company plans to replace manual labor with a large number of robots by 2013.

Foxconn publicly stated that this move will push more than 1 million employees up the value chain, freeing them from basic manufacturing work. The company also talked about moving employees away from repetitive and boring tasks to higher-value manufacturing positions, such as R&D, innovation, and other areas equally important to the company's development. However, large-scale automation also means that some of these employees are bound to lose their jobs.

Macro Challenges

From a macro perspective, China's economic development model in recent years has been contrary to the trend of automation: using human labor instead of capital in the manufacturing industry. However, due to the increased demand for skilled labor and the rise in the statutory minimum wage, current labor costs are rapidly increasing, leading companies to adjust the input ratio of labor and capital.

Many smaller Chinese manufacturing enterprises have already started similar attempts, reducing the growing need for costly labor while increasing investment in capital-intensive equipment, Hangzhou Google optimization. Foxconn's plan represents a huge leap forward, but it also faces significant challenges: its advantage lies in quietly organizing manpower to produce goods for star companies rather than creating highly automated production lines. This transformation will also lead the company to directly compete with those highly automated enterprises.

However, for Terry Gou, this is a necessary move. Foxconn's customers care about their image and are therefore very concerned about the employee suicides at the company. If the production model does not change, these customers may seek other suppliers.

Manufacturing experts and economists have discussed whether the rising labor costs and lack of skills in China mean the end of cheap Chinese products. This trend could lead global consumer product companies to shift their production bases to other cost-effective Asian countries or even back to their home countries. Of course, some of the most powerful and highly automated Western manufacturing companies have proven that they have surprising adaptability to cheap Chinese labor.

But there are skeptics: they believe that Chinese companies have improved production efficiency and can produce more products with fewer workers, so they will still be formidable competitors. Nevertheless, the Chinese government will closely monitor this trend. Like the West, the Chinese government is also concerned that national industrial progress may lead to large numbers of relatively well-educated and articulate workers becoming unemployed. (Ding Hong)
