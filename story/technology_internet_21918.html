Borrowing Channel From Banks to Speculate in Hong Kong Stocks -- the First Product of Direct Investment in Hong Kong Stocks in the Mainland Comes Into Being

by kemi on 2007-06-08 10:19:44

Title: First Focus: Investing in Hong Kong Stocks Through Banks

Body: In the first instance, our primary focus today is on investing in Hong Kong stocks. Nowadays, more people are interested in stock trading and, in addition to paying attention to the Shanghai and Shenzhen stock markets, they also hope to buy and sell stocks in places like Hong Kong and the United States. Now, this opportunity has arrived; recently, the first wealth management product from mainland China that directly invests in Hong Kong stocks has been introduced.

This product differs from previously launched offshore wealth management products by some banks that invested in foreign bonds or stock indices. The product offered by Bank of Communications (BoComm) is very similar to a mutual fund, as it can directly invest in Hong Kong stocks on behalf of clients. Stocks such as PetroChina, Construction Bank, and China Telecom H-shares and red chips are among its investment targets. The minimum investment threshold is 300,000 RMB as stipulated by the CBRC. Investors can use Renminbi (RMB), Hong Kong Dollar (HKD), US Dollar (USD), Euro (EUR), Japanese Yen (JPY), Australian Dollar (AUD) for investment. Liu Lizhi, Deputy General Manager of BoComm's Personal Financial Products Department: "The opening paragraph + yield... sold 50 million RMB worth on the first day."

Unlike personally buying and selling stocks, with this type of wealth management product, investors themselves cannot choose specific stocks; the bank selects and invests on their behalf. BoComm stated that to avoid investment risks associated with Renminbi appreciation, this product is linked to the Australian dollar. BoComm explained that although Renminbi against the US dollar has been appreciating, the Australian dollar exchange rate against Renminbi has remained strong, effectively hedging currency risks. We also noticed that while this product only guarantees 100% principal protection according to its statement, based on the contract terms, it will distribute an 8% dividend after six months of investment. This means that even if BoComm's investment incurs losses at the six-month mark, investors will still be assured of an 8% return. It is understood that the new QDII products from banks such as China Merchants Bank, Everbright Bank, and Minsheng Bank are nearing completion and will be launched soon.

Title: Editor's Note for First Focus

Body: QDII stands for "Qualified Domestic Institutional Investor," which refers to an investment method where government-approved domestic financial institutions raise funds domestically to invest overseas and then distribute dividends to all participating investors.

Title: First Focus: Investment in Hong Kong Stocks - Banks Compete with Various Overseas Investment Products, Each Having Pros and Cons

Body: In fact, Bank of Communications is not the first bank to launch a wealth management product linked to the Hong Kong stock market. More than three months ago, Bank of China issued a similar product, but instead of investing in stocks, it invested in equity funds. All domestic and foreign banks have developed their own QDII products, so how do the returns on these investment products compare? Let's take a look at the reporter's investigation.

Currently, there are three Chinese banks offering QDII products for overseas stocks. Among them, Bank of China's product mainly indirectly enters the Hong Kong stock market by investing in Hong Kong equity funds, while the products issued by Bank of Communications and Industrial and Commercial Bank of China mainly enter the stock market directly by purchasing a basket of Hong Kong blue-chip stocks. The biggest feature of these products is their relatively short term, generally around one year or six months, with moderate expected returns of about 10%. On the other hand, foreign banks offer higher expected returns to attract investors, but with longer terms. For example, Citibank launched a QDII product linked to two real estate stock indices in Japan and the United States, with a maximum return of 60% over five years. HSBC and Bank of East Asia even claim "no upper limit," but investors may need to hold for up to five years, with the shortest period being one and a half years. Although most of these products allow early redemption, investors would face high fees or risk of principal loss.

Title: Editor-in-Chief's Note for First Focus

Body: We recommend that friends interested in QDII products should not just focus on the highest yield when making investments, but rather inquire more about whether the product guarantees principal safety. If the bank states that the principal is not guaranteed, then you should consider your own risk tolerance carefully before proceeding.
