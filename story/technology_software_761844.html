Windows system slow? This is not a technical reason

by geekzhang on 2013-05-13 10:13:19

Developer Marc Bevand posted a short article on Hacker News, briefly explaining why Windows runs slower than other systems. Subsequently, a Microsoft employee who claimed to have been involved in the development of the Windows NT kernel responded, offering explanations from two aspects: internal company management and talent loss. However, for some reason, this response was deleted.Marc Bevand believed this reply was valuable, and after obtaining permission from the employee, he published it on his personal blog. The employee stated that in many ways, Windows is slower than other systems, and the gap is widening.We occasionally see some naive individuals trying to improve the situation. These people almost always fail. We only improve system performance when there are special circumstances, and those who can allocate resources believe it will affect business goals. But this work is Sisyphean. There are no formal or informal projects focused on system-level performance improvements. We started caring about security because Windows XP prior to SP3 posed an existential threat to our own business. Our low performance does not pose such a threat to our business.Whether project managers, testers, or product managers, they all openly oppose external patches. They lack motivation to accept changes from outside, and you can always find reasons to say no, while rarely having incentives to say yes. Moreover, employees rarely have the motivation to seek change. Only major improvements can gain leadership attention or promotion possibilities. Incremental improvements merely bore people and are detrimental to one's position. As a result, employees eventually learn to work by the book and stop thinking about innovation.Besides management issues, another reason is the company's difficulty in retaining talent. Google and other large companies continuously poach our best and most experienced developers, and we hire young graduates to replace them. These people are usually smart, but they don't understand the system's specifics. More importantly, they don't want to make any changes to things that are already running. When they want to improve the system, they tend to add new features rather than improving existing ones. As a result, many old functions cannot be improved.In summary, the employee believes that Microsoft does not lack truly top-tier developers, and the NT kernel is better than Linux in many ways. However, these people keep retiring or moving to other companies, and few can replace them. To fill the ranks, we employ nine-to-five workers with families, H-1B visa holders eager to please, and those rejected by Google. Occasionally, we manage to hire capable individuals, which feels like a mistake, but it’s not enough. Still wondering why we're falling behind? Corruption has set in.The employee's response garnered so much attention that he became somewhat concerned. Thus, he contacted Marc Bevand and issued a statement detailing various inappropriate aspects of his article. He said, "All these matters have gotten out of control. I was too harsh, and I didn’t intend for this to be some kind of massive exposé; it was just some complaints. I don’t agree with the appetite for political studies among those outside Microsoft. I should have thought more deeply about my post. I apologize for this misleading so-called internal impression."Perhaps the employee's complaints were overly subjective, but the issues raised have been reported before. For instance, *Vanity Fair* once had an article suggesting that Microsoft's management system stifles innovation. As competition in the tech industry intensifies, perhaps Microsoft has reached a point where it needs to confront its problems and make radical changes.The image is from Mashable.
