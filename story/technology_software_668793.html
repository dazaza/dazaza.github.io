Open registration for .cn domain names: form outweighs meaning

by yyy852456 on 2012-02-18 14:58:55

Previously, IP Mirror, a domain name registration company located in Singapore, released a message stating that starting from 9:00 on February 12, 2012, individuals or organizations from the UK, Hong Kong, Singapore, Malaysia, and Australia would be able to directly register Chinese domains. Would this inject a shot of adrenaline into China's consistently underperforming domains? In my view, this small step towards internationalizing .cn domains is more about making a declarative statement in terms of attitude, which falls under formalism.

It is understood that the domains opened for partial overseas regions include ".cn, .中国 (China), .公司 (Company), and .网络 (Network)." According to CNNIC's approval process, the Server Hold status of these domains will be lifted, allowing direct resolution to websites and enabling email services. For .cn domains whose total registered quantity has been stagnant, can this clear up the gloom? Some industry insiders estimate that this internationalization move might trigger the largest .cn registration wave in over two years.

Do international audiences need .cn?

Is it really like that? Actually, no. For a country's top-level domain, its main audience is the Internet users of that country. Of course, in some international exchange environments, the .cn domain can also serve as a national image. Experts from China Nuowang nuo.cn, a well-known domestic domain management expert, analyzed that examples include the Beijing Olympics (beijing2008.cn), Shanghai Expo (expo2010.cn), and Guangzhou Asian Games (gz2010.cn). These all belong to international events, and the official websites adopting their own country's top-level domain reflect national honor and play a better role in promotion and publicity. It is reported that during the Beijing Olympics, the website received 200 million visits per day. However, in daily exchanges, stepping outside the national boundaries, borrowing another country's top-level domain seems like a rootless water lily, losing direction.

For a local enterprise, there is almost no need to use another country's top-level domain on its official website. Whether from cultural integration, commercial promotion, or customer acceptance, there is no appropriate entry point; for multinational enterprises, if the business reach has extended to other countries or regions, then it is feasible to apply for a corresponding country-specific domain as an official sub-site, but this does not require applying outside the branch office, otherwise it would be superfluous.

Why push .cn outwards?

In 2008, the number of registrations for .cn domains among Chinese domains once surpassed Germany’s .de domain at 12.36 million, jumping to the top globally in terms of national top-level domain totals. The reason for such a rocket-like rise in numbers mainly stems from two points. First, between 2006-2007, it was the period when the Internet rebounded from a trough, and with the recovery of the domestic economy, the development of numerous small and medium-sized enterprises drove the informatization process, increasing the demand for website construction; Second, facing the gradually improving economic environment, in order to further promote the development of China's Internet industry, in March 2007, CNNIC launched a "1 yuan/year for .cn domain" promotional discount, which became a huge inducement, leading to a surge in .cn domain registrations.
