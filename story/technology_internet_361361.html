Solar energy policy in the United States

by ditan360 on 2010-07-30 14:34:14

Yitan News exclusive report: On July 3, President Obama announced in the weekly radio and online address that the Department of Energy would provide $1.85 billion in loan guarantees to two solar companies to accelerate the transition of the United States to a clean energy economy and create tens of thousands of jobs, ensuring that future jobs and industries take root in the United States. This is the latest move by the US government to promote the transition to a clean energy economy.

This funding will come from the US government's economic stimulus plan. One of the two companies is a Spanish company that will receive a $1.45 billion loan guarantee to build the world's largest solar power plant in Arizona. The plant will provide clean energy to 70,000 households in the United States and can store energy even at night. Obama said, "For many years we have seen American companies building facilities and creating jobs overseas, now we are attracting a company to our shores to build a factory in the United States and create jobs. That is great news." Another U.S. company located in Colorado will receive a $400 million loan guarantee to expand production of the world's most advanced solar panels.

Popularizing solar power generation is a major national policy

Since October 2009, the White House will offer $17.6 million in reward subsidies for six photovoltaic module incubation plans dedicated to photovoltaic manufacturing processes. In these six plans, participating companies will advance the photovoltaic process to accelerate its transition from the experimental stage to the production stage, while reducing costs, improving product quality, and further increasing the output of photovoltaic modules. These reward subsidies will help fully commercialize solar technology, providing a broad market for suppliers of photovoltaic products.

John Mizrocha, Acting Assistant Secretary for Energy Efficiency and Renewable Energy in the United States, said, "These plans will promote the multi-faceted development of photovoltaic processes, making the United States a global leader in next-generation solar technology. These reward subsidies aim to accelerate the comprehensive promotion of high-tech companies into the market."

Previously, the U.S. Energy Bill was passed on July 25, 2007. According to the bill, families installing solar facilities can receive tax incentives, and federal support for commercial solar projects has reached levels not seen in decades. Local governments such as New York, New Jersey, and Connecticut have also introduced incentive policies.

On August 20, 2008, California Governor Schwarzenegger signed the SB1 bill proposed by Senator Murray, marking the realization of the Million Solar Roofs Program. Schwarzenegger said that when he started his campaign for governor, he vowed to make environmental protection the center of his administration and curb environmental pollution. The "Million Solar Roofs" program will provide 3 gigawatts of clean energy and reduce greenhouse gas emissions by 3 million tons, equivalent to removing 1 million cars from the road.

The Million Solar Roofs Program will greatly increase the utilization of rooftop solar power in California, with the energy provided equivalent to that of five modern power plants. The 3-gigawatt target, along with the ambition to obtain 20% renewable energy, will make California a leader in global solar energy utilization.

Since last year 2008, Schwarzenegger required the California Public Utilities Commission to start implementing the Million Solar Roofs Program, under which one million solar installations will be installed on roofs in California by 2018. Media commentary says that the signing of the SB1 bill makes the implementation of the Million Solar Roofs Program more legally binding. To accelerate commercialization, the SB1 bill also standardizes the use of solar facilities in new houses.

American electricity consumption reduced by 50% within the next 20 years

In October 2008, Stirling Energy Systems based in Phoenix completed the plan to set up 20,000 solar collectors in the Mohave Desert in Victorville, California. These collectors facing the sky can cover four square miles of land, collecting solar energy and converting it into electricity, supplying the severely power-deficient Los Angeles area, which is 80 miles south of the desert. These solar collection devices generate 500 megawatts of power, enough to meet the daytime electricity needs of 300,000 households, doubling California's solar power generation capacity.

Since the 1990s, the United States has successively formulated and implemented numerous photovoltaic development plans, including the National Photovoltaic Development Plan, the Million Solar Roofs Plan, and the Photovoltaic Pioneer Plan, among others, to promote the development of the photovoltaic industry. One of the more famous ones is the "Million Solar Roofs Plan" proposed by former President Clinton in 1997. It planned to install solar roofs for one million households in the United States by 2010, with each photovoltaic roof having a 3 kilowatt to 5 kilowatt grid-connected power generation system. When there is sunlight, the solar roof supplies power; when there is no sunlight, the grid supplies power to households. In recent years, with the development of U.S. government energy projects, the design of solar systems has become increasingly advanced, lighter in weight, stronger in function, and more novel in design form, with some solar panels resembling window awnings. Meanwhile, standardized installation techniques and reduced equipment production costs have significantly reduced users' expenses for purchasing solar systems, lowering the price of home solar systems to $3.5 per watt.

According to the plan, the United States will achieve at least 3000 megawatts of solar power supply by 2010. The U.S. Department of Energy announced that the cumulative installed capacity of solar power generation equipment by 2010 would exceed 4600 megawatts, which would greatly stimulate the development of the U.S. solar cell industry. Experts believe that the cost of power generation from collectors could potentially be reduced to less than 10 cents per kilowatt-hour, equivalent to the cost of natural gas-fired power generation.

Solar system business is booming

The solar system business is gradually emerging in the United States. The U.S. Solar Industries Association estimates that the domestic solar market in the United States could grow by 35% or more over the next five years. Recently, the U.S. Department of Energy joined forces with builders to launch a plan to reduce electricity consumption by Americans by 50% over the next 20 years. According to this plan, new buildings will replace traditional heating or cooling equipment with more efficient and less maintenance-intensive alternative energy facilities.

The U.S. Department of Commerce believes that the next ten years will be the most active period for solar construction commercial transactions, forming a new profit growth point for manufacturers. For a long time, the solar industry has received strong support from the federal government, which has consistently regarded sustainable energy as one of the key areas determining America's future international competitiveness. Besides government-funded research and development, a significant number of technology commercialization demonstration projects have been established, with many renowned laboratories and universities involved. The White House hopes to restore its international competitiveness through industrial restructuring. Currently, the solar industry has created 120,000 job opportunities in American society, with the photovoltaic industry alone employing 18,500 people.

With the increasing shortage of oil resources, the global solar industry is developing rapidly. The market sales volume of civilian and commercial solar products in the United States, Europe, and Japan exceeds 1850 megawatts. It is expected to break through 2200 megawatts this year. Over the past few years, innovations in technology and manufacturing processes have expanded the industrial market, improved production efficiency, extended the product life cycle and stability, and made installation easier. This has led to a reduction in average costs and product prices, enhancing the competitiveness of the solar industry relative to ordinary energy industries. Currently, the industry is becoming increasingly mature, with continuous and rapid growth in the global market.

Due to intensified international competition, the United States has lost its leading position. In 1997, U.S. solar products accounted for 100% of the domestic market share, but by 2009, the domestic market share had dropped to 65.2%; In 1997, U.S. solar products held over 40% of the global market share, but by 2009, it had decreased to only 12.2%. During the same period, Europe grew by 41%, and Japan by 45%. Ten years ago, half of all solar panels produced globally came from the United States, but now this proportion is only 8%, with the biggest competitors being Japan and Germany.

Green economy strategy promotes economic transformation

Since taking office, the Obama administration has been committed to promoting the transition of the United States to a clean energy economy, combining this strategy with short-term policies to stimulate economic growth and create jobs. The $800 billion economic stimulus package introduced by the U.S. government to save the American economy is called the "Green Economic Revival Plan," with direct investments in clean energy and tax incentives for clean energy development amounting to $100 billion. According to the plan, by 2012, 10% of the total electricity in the United States will come from renewable energy sources such as wind and solar power, and by 2025, this ratio will reach 25%.

As one of the measures to promote green economic development, the U.S. government has also increased its transformation and support of the automobile industry and nuclear energy development. The automobile industry is a major oil consumer in the United States. While promoting the restructuring of the automobile industry, the Obama administration introduced a nationwide unified automotive fuel consumption standard for the first time, accelerating the upgrading of the U.S. automobile industry. At the same time, it pledged to provide $2.4 billion in funding for the development of hybrid vehicles and new fuel cells, and to reduce taxes for consumers purchasing energy-saving vehicles. In February this year, the U.S. government also announced that it would provide approximately $8 billion in government loan guarantees to Southern Company headquartered in Atlanta, Georgia, to support the construction of two nuclear reactors in Georgia, which will be the first nuclear power plant built in the United States in nearly 30 years.

Although Obama's green economy strategy has opened up a new direction for reshaping U.S. economic competitiveness, it still faces many challenges. The Republican Party claims that some of Obama's policies are tantamount to imposing heavy taxes on U.S. businesses. Some Republican lawmakers criticized Democrats for "uncontrolled spending," continuing to spend lavishly when the United States is facing a $13 trillion debt, which will increase the burden on future generations of Americans. The Obama administration, however, believes that increasing government debt in the short term is necessary to restore the U.S. economy.
