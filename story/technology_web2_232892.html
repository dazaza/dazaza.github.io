The Iron-blooded General Patton

by d93233926 on 2009-09-03 23:48:57

In 1943, the Anglo-American Allied Forces in North Africa were counterattacked by the German army led by Field Marshal Rommel, nicknamed the "Desert Fox," resulting in a large-scale battle. The US forces suffered a惨defeat and fell into difficulties. To reverse the battlefield situation and revitalize the strength of the US military, the American authorities dispatched General George Patton to serve as the commander of the Second Special Forces, with the talented Brigadier General Bradley as his assistant. Patton, over sixty years old but ambitious, immediately reformed military discipline upon assuming his post. He ordered the mess hall to serve meals on time, required soldiers to dress neatly, prohibited nude paintings of women from entering the camp, and formulated an extremely strict training plan. After thorough reforms, the morale of Patton's troops was lifted, transforming them into a disciplined, spirited, and brave force.

Soon, Patton led the Second Special Forces in fierce combat against Rommel's German corps, leading to a significant defeat of the German forces. Patton's troops pursued their victory and eventually drove the remnants out of the African battlefield. Patton was then dispatched to command the Seventh Army, where he planned to land in southern Sicily and attack Palermo, while allowing General Montgomery of the British Army to capture Siracusa as a means to distract enemy forces. However, this plan was opposed by Montgomery. In order to maintain the face of both parties, including Montgomery's emotions, the Supreme Commander of the Anglo-American Allied Forces, Eisenhower, adopted the British operational plan, much to Patton's anger. Patton was unwilling to play second fiddle to the British forces but had no choice but to obey orders.

In July, the Anglo-American Allied Forces landed separately on the southern and eastern coasts of Sicily. Due to the stubborn resistance of the German defense forces, Montgomery's troops were unable to break through enemy lines. The high command then ordered Patton's Second Special Forces to let the road pass to the British forces. However, under heavy artillery fire from the German special forces, casualties were heavy when giving way, and the command ordered them to stop advancing. Despite this, Patton continued full-speed ahead, capturing enemy lines before Montgomery's forces. Later at a press conference, Patton was elated. Patton's troops continued to capture Messina, but due to intense German artillery retaliation and Patton's unilateral combat plan, the advance slowed down and casualties were heavy. Patton had to personally investigate the front line. During his inspection of the field hospital, he struck a soldier who was already exhausted by war, causing shock. Nevertheless, the soldiers followed orders and captured the last German stronghold, Messina, at great cost. Patton earned the title of "Blood and Guts" general, but his unfriendly attitude towards Montgomery and his brutal beating of soldiers caused widespread dissatisfaction. Under Eisenhower's strict orders, Patton reluctantly apologized publicly, but his repentance still failed to gain others' forgiveness, and he was relieved of his military duties.

During the Normandy landing campaign, Patton was reinstated by Eisenhower, but in this campaign, Patton only played a supporting role in creating diversions. He took out his frustrations on the Russians during meetings. Soon after, Patton was appointed as the commander of the Third Army. On June 6, 1944, the Battle of Normandy began, and Patton lifted the siege of Bastogne during the Battle of the Ardennes. Just as he was about to achieve complete personal and military victory, his actions were constrained by Eisenhower. Originally, due to political pressure, Eisenhower had decided to prioritize Montgomery's northern front and let the Russians occupy Prague. Although Patton had advanced deep into Czechoslovakia, near the end of the war, Patton was decorated with numerous medals for his meritorious service and was appointed as the military governor of Bavaria. After Germany's surrender, at a celebration banquet between American and Soviet forces, Patton showed cold hostility towards the Russians and declared his hope to fight the Russians because he believed Americans must not show weakness to the Russians. This greatly angered Eisenhower, and Patton was dismissed from his military duties again, thus ending his wartime career.

When Patton bid farewell to his subordinates, he was indignant about this unjust decision.
