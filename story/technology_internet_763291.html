Chromecast Has Many Defects, Google Should Not Be Overconfident

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 18:06:13

Beijing time, July 27th - According to foreign media reports, Google's $35 Chromecast TV stick has been an initial runaway success, selling out shortly after its launch. However, the American tech website CNET conducted a trial review of the Chromecast and found that the product still has many shortcomings and defects. Google must not let this early victory go to their head.

CNET pointed out that considering the low price, the Google TV stick is a pretty good product, but it could be better with improvements in several areas. CNET suggests the following areas where Chromecast can improve:

More video application support

After the release of the TV stick, one of the first things outsiders noticed was the limited support for third-party applications. Currently, Android and iOS users can stream videos from Netflix and YouTube. Additionally, Android users can stream music, movies, and TV shows provided by Google. However, this means that many well-known audiovisual services are only accessible through competitors' devices, including Amazon Video, HBO Go, MLB.TV, PBS, Spotify, and Rdio.

Some may try to access these contents via "screen mirroring," but when it comes to playback quality and other aspects, dedicated app clients provide a better experience.

In comparison, the Roku internet streaming box offers more video streaming content.

It is widely known that YouTube and Netflix, two major mainstream video websites, have already made their way onto numerous set-top boxes. Therefore, providing more video app support will greatly enhance the usability of Chromecast. However, given the initial popularity among consumers, it is believed that video websites will soon release compatible apps. Reports indicate that HBO Go is preparing to take action. Considering that app support also requires changes on the Chromecast side, frequent updates to Chromecast are expected in the future.

Genuine "phone-to-TV" streaming

The Chromecast can stream video content from the cloud via an app, but strangely, it currently cannot play music, photos, or videos stored on mobile devices. Nowadays, many consumers use their smartphones as their primary camera or camcorder, so logically, Chromecast should allow users to stream content from their mobile devices to their home's largest display device – the TV. For example, Roku's app can display photos and videos from a phone on the TV.

For Google, this should be "a piece of cake." Apple's AirPlay allows iPhone and iPad users to stream photos, music, and videos to the TV via the AppleTV set-top box. Although Google's service doesn't need to be as seamless as AirPlay, if it could play content from the phone, this feature would undoubtedly add value to Chromecast.

Better screen mirroring

If you want to see "all the videos on the internet" on your TV, screen mirroring seems to be the best solution. However, in practice with Chromecast, this function is not smooth. Users must have a very strong Wi-Fi network; otherwise, they will encounter problems. Additionally, even with good Wi-Fi conditions, the transcoding process affects image quality.

In comparison, the screen mirroring function of Apple's AppleTV set-top box is more stable than Chromecast.

Screen mirroring is a feature that many users eagerly anticipate in Chromecast, so Google should address the issues that arose during the initial phase. Additionally, Google stated that screen mirroring is still a test feature, so it is understandable that it is not yet smooth.

Improved support for iOS devices

One of the attractions of Chromecast is its simultaneous support for Android and iOS devices. However, support for iOS can be further enhanced. Currently, iOS devices do not support the Google Music app when using Chromecast. If Google Music Service releases the iOS client that users have been expecting, this issue will likely be resolved. Additionally, Google needs to provide functionality for setting up Chromecast on iOS devices (Google has already indicated that this will be available in the future).

Support for MHL interface

When launching the Chromecast, Google released many "naked" pictures of the Chromecast (without any cables connected). Of course, this TV stick still requires power, which can be supplied via the USB port of the TV or through a power adapter. While users can hide the cables behind the TV, Google could release a Chromecast version supporting the MHL interface, a type of HDMI-like interface that also provides sufficient power to the device.

However, it should be noted that for Google, releasing a Chromecast with only an MHL interface may not be wise, as similar products from Roku have faced market resistance. Overall, many new TVs or video playback devices now include an MHL interface, so a truly "wireless" Chromecast would be something users look forward to.
