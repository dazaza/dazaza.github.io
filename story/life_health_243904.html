The touching article says: A child doesn't think mother is ugly! A dog doesn't think home is poor!

by lwf225 on 2009-09-29 08:54:05

On the day of the wedding, my mom asked me: Who are those two people sitting in the corner that look like beggars? When I looked over, an old man was staring at me, and there was an old woman next to him who quickly lowered her head when she noticed me looking. I didn't know them, but they didn't seem like beggars either; their clothes were new, with creases still visible. My mom said they looked like beggars because they were hunched over, and the old woman had a cane beside her.

My mom said Tianchi was an orphan and no relatives came from his side. If we didn't know them, we should drive them away. Nowadays, beggars can be bad; they like to wait outside hotels, pretending to be relatives at weddings just to eat for free.

I said no, let's ask Tianchi first? Tianchi, flustered, dropped the bouquet I handed him and finally mumbled that they were his paternal uncle and aunt. I gave my mom a glare: we almost drove away our relatives.

My mom said, "Tianchi, you're an orphan, right? Where did these relatives come from?" Tianchi, fearing my mom, lowered his head and said they were distant relatives he hadn't been in touch with for a long time. But since marriage is such an important event, it would have been a regret if no relatives from his family showed up, so...

I leaned on Tianchi's shoulder and complained that he didn't tell me earlier about their arrival. We should have seated them at another table; as relatives, they shouldn't sit at the extra table. Tianchi stopped me, saying let them stay where they are; sitting at another table wouldn't make them feel comfortable.

By the time the banquet started, only the paternal uncle and aunt sat at that table. As we went around to thank guests at each table, Tianchi hesitated for a moment before pulling me past them. Looking back, I saw their heads bowed low. Thinking about it, I pulled Tianchi back: "Paternal Uncle, Paternal Aunt, we'd like to toast you!"

The two looked up, somewhat disbelievingly. Their hair was silver-white, and they looked quite elderly, probably in their seventies or eighties. The aunt's eyes were hollow, her face turned towards me but her gaze was unfocused. I uncertainly waved my hand in front of her eyes, getting no response. It turned out the aunt was blind. "Paternal Uncle, Paternal Aunt, this is my wife Xiao Jie, we're toasting you now!" Tianchi reminded them in his local dialect.

"Ah, ah," the paternal uncle wobbled to his feet, his left hand on the aunt's shoulder, the right tremblingly holding the wine glass. His fingers were full of yellow calluses, and thick cracks in his fingertips were black with dirt. Years of hard labor facing the earth and sky had bent their backs prematurely. To my surprise, I discovered the paternal uncle's right leg was missing. The aunt was blind, the uncle was lame—what kind of couple were they? Don't stand, please sit down. I went forward to support them. The uncle wobbled back into his seat, and for no apparent reason, the aunt suddenly began to cry, tears dripping down as the uncle silently patted her back. I wanted to console them, but Tianchi pulled me away.

I told Tianchi, let's give them some money when they go home; they're too pitiful. Both disabled, it's unimaginable how they get by. Tianchi nodded without speaking, tightly embracing me.

On the first New Year's Eve, Tianchi said he had a stomachache and didn't eat dinner, going to bed instead. I had my mom cook some rice porridge and followed him into the room. Tianchi lay in bed, tears welling up in his eyes.

I said, "Tianchi, you're not supposed to do this, not eating dinner with us on the first New Year's Eve and hiding in the room like this. It's like we've mistreated you. You say you have a stomachache every time there's a holiday? Actually, I know you're not having a stomachache, so what's wrong?"

After a long pause, Tianchi said he was sorry, but he was just thinking about his paternal uncle and aunt, and his deceased parents. He was afraid he wouldn't be able to hold back his emotions at the dinner table, which might upset my parents, so he pretended to have a stomachache.

I hugged him and said, "You're such a silly child. If you miss them, we'll visit them after the New Year. Besides, I also want to know how they live."

Tianchi said forget it, that mountain path was very difficult to walk. I would get tired, and it would be better to wait until the road improved and we had children before taking me there to see them.

I thought to myself: by the time we have children, they might not even be around anymore! But I didn't dare say it aloud. Instead, I suggested sending them more money and goods.

During the second year's Mid-Autumn Festival, I happened to be on a business trip and couldn't return home for the festival. Missing Tianchi and my parents, I called him.

I asked Tianchi, "What should I do if I miss you so much I can't sleep?" Tianchi said he would just surf the internet or watch TV, or if that didn't work, lie there wide awake thinking hard about me.

That night, we chatted until our phones got hot and ran out of power. Lying in the hotel bed, looking at the round moon outside the window, I couldn't fall asleep. I lay there crying, missing Tianchi, my dad, and my mom. Thinking Tianchi was probably also awake, maybe wandering online, I flipped open my computer and registered a new QQ account named "Read You," intending to play a prank on Tianchi. Checking, I found Tianchi was indeed online, so I added him, and he accepted.

I asked him, "On such a wonderful day of reunion, why are you still idling online?" He replied, "Because my wife is on a business trip, I miss her and can't sleep, so I'm surfing the web."

I was satisfied with that answer and typed, "With your wife not at home, you could find a lover to replace her, like online, just for some temporary comfort."

After half a day, he typed out a line: "If you're looking for a lover, I'm sorry, I'm not the person you're looking for. Goodbye."

"Sorry, that's not what I meant, don't be angry." I quickly sent that message.

After a while, he asked me, "Why are you idling online?" I said, "I'm working away from home, now I miss my dad and mom. After finishing a call with my boyfriend, I still can't sleep, so I'm online."

"I miss my dad and mom too, but... parents far away, children wanting to take care of them but unable to." What does that mean? I repeated the sentence, confused as to why Tianchi would say such a thing. "Since you're called 'Read You,' today I'll let you read once. Keeping things bottled up inside for too long can make you sick, airing them out feels better. Anyway, since we don't know each other, consider it hearing a story!" Thus, unexpectedly, I learned the hidden matters of Tianchi's heart.

30 years ago, my father was nearly fifty and still unmarried, because he was lame and poor, no girl would marry him. Later, a begging old man arrived in the village, supporting a blind woman. The old man was very ill, and my father, seeing their plight, let them rest at our place. Unexpectedly, once they settled in, the old man never got up again. Later, his daughter, the blind woman, married my father.

The following year, I was born. Our life was very impoverished, but I never went hungry. My father and mother couldn't farm, so they earned income by helping others peel corn kernels. A day of peeling left their ten fingers full of blood blisters, and the next day they would wrap them in cloth and continue. For my schooling, our family kept three chickens: two laid eggs to sell for money, and one laid eggs for me to eat. My mother said she heard in the city that kids ate eggs to study, so our kid should eat them too, to be smarter than city kids in the future. But they never ate any themselves. Once, I saw my mother licking the egg white remaining on the shell after she cracked an egg into the pot, and I cried, hugging her, refusing to eat eggs anymore. My father, knowing the reason, was so angry he wanted to beat my mother with a stick. In the end, I compromised, on the condition that we all ate together. Although they agreed, each time they would just symbolically touch the eggs with their teeth.

Villagers never called me by my name, only referring to me as "the son of the lame and blind." Whenever someone called me that, my parents would fight them fiercely. My mother, being blind, would grab bricks and throw them randomly, yelling, "You evil ones, we may be lame and blind, but our son is fine! You're not allowed to call him that! One day, you'll all be worse than him!" That year, during the high school entrance exam, the news that "the son of the lame and blind" ranked first county-wide brought great pride to my parents. The town covered all my tuition fees, and the day they sent me off to school was the first time my father left the mountains. Getting on the bus, I burst into tears, and my father, leaning on his crutch, wiped my tears with his other hand: "Study hard in the city, then find a job and a wife there. If people ask about your parents, tell them you're an orphan, with no parents. Otherwise, people will look down on you. Especially when it comes to finding a wife, they'll disdain you. Ruining your chances at marriage would make me ashamed to face our ancestors."

"Dad! Let Dad stop talking, what nonsense, why deny our parents before anything happens?" My mother also said it was the truth, and I should listen. "Don't you remember in school? Whenever they found out you were the son of the lame and blind, they would look down on you. Even teachers didn't like you at first. In the future, when you bring your city wife home, just tell her they're your paternal uncle and aunt." My mother finished and wiped her tears. My father said, "Don't bring your wife home. If she comes, your mother won't be able to keep the secret." Then he stuffed ten boiled eggs into my arms and dragged my mother away.

My tears also streamed down uncontrollably. Disability wasn't their fault, it was heaven's injustice to them. But they gave birth to a perfect Tianchi for me. This foolish Tianchi, such parents, couldn't be more perfect. I was angry; how could he think so little of me?

Later, did you tell your wife they were your paternal uncle and aunt? I typed that question.

At first, I didn't believe it. My wife chose me, not my parents, so why couldn't I acknowledge my parents? However, in ten years away, my parents never visited my school. In my first job, I wanted to take them to the city to play, but they refused, saying that letting people know my parents were disabled would tarnish my reputation and affect my chances of finding a wife. They lived in the mountains all their lives and didn't want to leave. My mother even said she came from the city, but it wasn't interesting anyway. Later, I met my first girlfriend, and when I thought the time was right, I took her home. But after arriving, she didn't even stay for dinner before leaving. Chasing after her, she said she couldn't possibly live with such people. She also said our family had genetic issues, and our future children wouldn't be healthy. I was furious and told her to get lost. Back home, my mother cried, and my father scolded me, saying I didn't listen to them and insisted on ruining our family lineage.

Later, I met my second girlfriend, who is now my wife. I loved her deeply, fearing to lose her even in dreams. Her family was wealthy, and all her relatives were elite. With previous experiences, I was afraid and could only be unfilial. But during festivals, I always missed them, feeling blocked and uncomfortable in my chest.

Have you never told your wife? Maybe she doesn't care about these things?

I haven't told her, nor dared to. If she agreed, I doubt my mother-in-law would. I live with them, and my father-in-law has face in society. If my parents came, wouldn't it tarnish their image? So, I can only secretly return home during business trips or studies. Thank you for listening to all this; now my heart feels much better.

After logging off, I still couldn't sleep. Everyone says children don't despise ugly mothers, and dogs don't despise poor homes. Look at what we've done? I understand Tianchi's helplessness, and I understand his parents' concerns. But they unknowingly placed me in a situation of heartlessness and ingratitude.

As dawn approached, I knocked on the department manager's door, telling him to handle everything under his authority. I had something extremely important to attend to immediately, and I entrusted everything to him. Then, I quickly packed my luggage and headed straight for the train station. Luckily, I made the first train.

That mountain road was indeed difficult to traverse. Initially, I still had some strength in my legs, but later, blisters formed on my feet, and I couldn't move anymore. It was noon, the sun blazing, and I could only gasp for breath. The water I brought was almost gone, and I didn't know how much further I had to go. Taking off my shoes, I squeezed the blisters, the pain making me cry out loud. I wanted to call Tianchi to pick me up and take me home, but I resisted. I tore some reed flowers from the roadside and padded them under my feet, feeling much better. Thinking about Tianchi's parents working at home, my legs suddenly gained strength, and I stood up and continued walking.

When the old village chief led me to Tianchi's house, the red evening glow was shining on the old jujube tree in front of their door. Under the jujube tree sat the paternal uncle—no, it was Tianchi's father. Father looked much older than when I last saw him at the wedding. He was peeling corn, his cane quietly leaning against his amputated leg. Mother was kneeling on the ground, preparing to gather the dried corn, scooping handfuls into the basket. This scene was like a painting, and in the painting were the most perfect parents in the world.

Step by step, I approached them. Father saw me, the corn falling from his hands, his mouth wide open in astonishment: "You, how did you come?"

Mother, nearby, fumbled and asked, "Who's here, dear?"

"Tianchi's family."

"Ah! Where, where?" Mother frantically searched for my direction.

I bent down to put down my luggage, then grabbed her hand and knelt before them, deeply painful and solemnly: "Father! Mother! I've come to take you home!"

Father coughed dryly twice, tears silently streaming down his wrinkled face.

"We always said, we didn't raise our child in vain!" Mother rubbed her hands repeatedly on her own body, then embraced me, row after row of hot tears flowing from her empty eyes into my neck.

When we took my parents away, the village set off fireworks. I brought glory to my parents once more.

When Tianchi opened the door and saw my parents standing on either side of me, he was greatly surprised, stunned speechless.

I said, "Tianchi, I am the one who reads you. I've brought our parents back. With such perfect parents, how could you bear to leave them in the mountains?"

"Thank you!"

Tianchi sobbed uncontrollably, tightly embracing me, just like his mother, letting tears flow into my neck.

This touching story shows how a materialistic society distorts hearts. Those gradually aging, stooped figures, our parents. (Let all of us love and respect our parents.)
