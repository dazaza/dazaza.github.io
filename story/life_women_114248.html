Cup ear correction

by 865512 on 2008-12-23 09:52:40

Cupped ear is a congenital malformation between protruding ear and microtia, accounting for about 10% of all kinds of congenital ear deformities. Bilaterality is more common, but the left and right sides are not necessarily symmetrical, with a certain degree of heredity.

The cupped ear has four main characteristics: The helix curls, in mild cases only the helix itself folds, in severe cases the entire upper part of the auricle hangs down, covering the ear canal opening. The auricle tilts forward, also known as the protruding ear, but it is different from the simple protruding ear deformity. The auricle becomes smaller, mainly due to the shortening of the auricle length. The position of the auricle is low, which is more obvious in severe cases, and often accompanied by maxillofacial deformities.

The cupped ear is also commonly referred to as curled ear or drooping ear because its shape looks like a string was tied around the edge of the helix and tightened. Therefore, some people also call it the ring-constricted ear.

What is Cupped Ear Plastic Surgery?

Most auricle deformities are caused by congenital reasons such as genetics, syphilis, viral infections (especially rubella virus) during the mother's pregnancy; certain drugs taken by pregnant women or metabolic and endocrine disorders, or exposure to certain chemicals and radiation, etc., can all lead to fetal ear developmental deformities. Congenital reasons often do not simply cause auricle deformities but are accompanied by external auditory canal, middle ear, and other structural abnormalities. Postnatal factors such as auricle trauma and infection can also cause serious auricle deformities, some of which can be complicated by external auditory canal stenosis or atresia, but generally do not accompany middle ear deformities.

Cupped ear deformity has a significant impact on appearance and life, so surgery for plastic correction is usually recommended. A common surgical method involves making an incision parallel to the upper edge of the helix at least 1 cm from the inner side of the posterior surface of the auricle to expose the curled deformed cartilage; then arcuately lifting it and appropriately placing it on the posterior inner side of the scaphoid cartilage, fixing it with several interrupted fine silk sutures. If the formed helical curling is not obvious, the scratch method can be used to make it curl.

Who needs Cupped Ear Plastic Surgery?

1. Cupped ear deformity significantly affects appearance and interferes with wearing glasses. Therefore, surgery for plastic correction is generally recommended.

2. For those whose auricle droops and covers the external auditory canal opening, early surgery is advisable to avoid affecting hearing.

3. Surgery can generally be performed after the age of six, and bilateral surgeries can be completed in one operation.

4. For those with severe maxillofacial deformities, a comprehensive treatment plan should be considered from an overall perspective.

Methods of Cupped Ear Plastic Surgery:

(I) "V-Y" Method

This method is suitable for mild Type I cupped ears with only tightness along the edge of the helix. The surgical method involves "V"-shaped incisions in the helical foot and anterior superior part of the helix skin and cartilage, followed by "Y"-shaped suturing, repositioning the helical foot upward and backward, and correcting the antihelix and the normal angle between the auricle and the lateral wall of the cranium according to the condition of the cupped ear deformity.

(II) Cartilage Flap Method

This method is suitable for moderate to severe Type I cupped ears with slight tightness along the edge of the helix accompanied by self-folding of the upper auricular cartilage. The surgical method involves making an incision parallel to the upper edge of the helix at least 1 cm from the inner side of the posterior surface of the auricle to expose the curled deformed cartilage; then arcuately cutting the curled cartilage along the edge of the helix, forming a flag-shaped flap that is lifted and appropriately placed on the posterior inner side of the scaphoid cartilage, fixed with several interrupted fine silk sutures. If the formed curling along the edge of the helix is not obvious, the scratch method can be applied to make it curl.

This method can extend the length of the auricle and restore the normal shape of the helix. For those with obvious protruding ear deformities, the method for protruding ear correction can be used to form the antihelix and reduce the width of the concha.

(III) Mixed Flap Method

This method is suitable for Type II cupped ears with varying degrees of absence or deformity of both the helix and the scaphoid. The surgical method involves making a "Z"-shaped incision in the upper part of the auricle, cutting a piece of cartilage along with the skin incision to form a cartilage-skin flap, and making radial incisions in the upper part of the auricle cartilage. The cartilage-skin flap is transferred to the upper edge of the auricle, and the cartilage is fixed with sutures. The cartilage-skin flap and the posterior auricle incision are sutured after transfer.

(IV) Conchal Composite Tissue Flap Rotation Method

This method is suitable for cupped ears with severe deficiencies in the upper half of the auricle. The surgical method is:

1. Form a tongue-shaped flap on the posterior side of the auricle and mastoid process with the pedicle at the top, with a length-to-width ratio of up to 4-5:1.

2. Extend the anterior incision of the flap forward to the helical foot through the helical foot and tragus into the concha, reaching the tragal notch from the posterior edge of the external auditory canal, then bend toward the inner side of the antihelix upward and fold back. Cut through the cartilage and anterior and posterior cartilage membranes, and deeply strip under the posterior cartilage membrane to gradually lift the cartilage flap.

3. Fix 1-2 stitches between the rotated conchal cartilage and the deep tissue to constitute the upper half of the auricle.

4. Suture the posterior incision of the auricle to reduce and normalize the excessively large cranioauricular angle.

5. Cover the wound surface resulting from the rotation of the conchal cartilage with the posterior auricle flap. The segment of the flap can also be sewn into a tube to help form the helix.

6. Repair the wound surface of the conchal donor area after rotation with full-thickness skin grafts, and fix it with suture compression.

Characteristics after Cupped Ear Plastic Surgery:

Generally, antibiotics are routinely applied for 3-5 days, and the dressing is opened and sutures removed about 10 days after the operation. Avoid stimulating foods such as ginger, green onions, garlic, chili peppers, seafood, etc. In the near postoperative period, pressure and impact on the ear should be avoided to prevent suture rupture.

Contraindications for Cupped Ear Plastic Surgery:

1. No infectious diseases or other body inflammations.

2. Patients with hypertension and diabetes should inform the doctor in detail about their conditions during the initial consultation so that the attending doctor can confirm the surgical plan.

3. Individuals with keloid constitutions should not undergo surgery.
