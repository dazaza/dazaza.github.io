This is adjacent to the President's Office

by zyto389wi7 on 2010-04-20 14:01:27

Zanzibar is one of the least economically developed regions in the world, a fact that becomes clear as soon as you see the airport. Though it claims to be an international airport and does indeed have flights to Kenya, South Africa, and other African countries, it's very small—only one floor—and the waiting area can accommodate only dozens of passengers. There are no jet bridges or shuttle buses. According to my husband, the local government hopes China will help expand the airport, and the project has been placed on the agenda.

On the evening we were supposed to arrive, my husband's colleagues planned a welcome dinner for me, but due to stomach pain, the event had to be postponed. By 8 p.m., I was already half-asleep, occasionally feeling pain in my stomach during my slumber. The next morning, my stomach was still spasming from time to time. Seeing my distressed appearance, sweating cold sweat, my husband felt helpless. I remembered that he had mentioned multiple times during our conversations about the Chinese medical team aiding Zanzibar. I suggested he seek their help. Despite being on Spring Festival holiday, the captain of the medical team, Mr. Zhu, quickly arrived at our residence with anesthesiologist Dr. Qian and some medication to give me an IV drip. Our medical team has a long history. Due to the prevalence of infectious diseases such as cholera, malaria, and AIDS on Zanzibar, since the establishment of diplomatic relations between China and Tanzania in 1964, China has continuously dispatched medical teams to assist in improving the medical standards of Zanzibar's main hospitals. The medical team is composed of personnel selected from Jiangsu Province, working for two years each term. The current team is the 23rd group, consisting of 22 medical backbone staff from the First and Third Hospitals of Yancheng City, Jiangsu Province, and other city hospitals. They are divided into two sub-teams, working at hospitals in Unguja and Pemba respectively.

Zanzibar was ruled by Amman from the Middle East for quite some time, and more than 90% of its residents are Muslim. Pork is not allowed to be sold here. Fortunately, surrounded by sea, seafood is abundant, becoming my husband's main source of protein. He took me to a seaside village to bid on fish in his second-hand Japanese car purchased on Zanzibar. The so-called auction market is merely a dilapidated shed made of a few wooden sticks and iron sheets, with a square cement platform in the middle where various seafood is placed for auction. A group of bidders surround the platform, placing their hands on the wooden frame above it. They are mainly fishmongers who buy the fish and resell them in the market for profit. Among them, two black men mutter while hanging strings of fish on the iron structure above the cement platform. They are the auctioneers calling out numbers in the local Swahili language, which sounds to me like a prayer from a primitive tribe. As the auctioneer calls out numbers, they pull one or two fish from the bidding string and throw them onto the platform or into nearby bags, which puzzles me. My husband explained that these one or two fish serve as the auctioneer's commission. The auction proceeds quickly, and within less than a minute, the fish hanging on the hook are sold. More black people keep coming from the fishing boats outside the shed carrying seafood to join the auction. That day, many golden threadfin breams were up for auction. This pink fish with golden lines on its scales has delicious meat and costs about 32 yuan per kilogram in domestic supermarkets. My husband bought it a couple of times when he first arrived in Zanzibar, but stopped because the small size made it too troublesome to clean. Around 4 p.m., another black man came from the sea with a large string of fish. Upon seeing it, my husband immediately told me that this string of blue-green fish was almost entirely parrotfish, which has tender meat and is very popular in local restaurants. He decided to bid. After several rounds of bidding, my husband won the auction for 23,000 shillings (about 115 yuan RMB). We carefully counted the heavy fish, totaling 18 of various sizes, weighing around 10 kilograms. These deep-sea fish cost at least 200 yuan per kilogram domestically. On one side of the auction cement platform, many locals—mainly women—are sitting on the ground with buckets of water beside them, muttering while cleaning fish. I originally thought they were helping customers clean fish, but later learned that they are small-scale vendors in the catering industry, participating in the auction while simultaneously cleaning the fish. After dark, they would fry or grill the fish and sell them. My husband directly found a relatively neat-looking young man to help clean the fish. My husband said that he often let this young man clean fish over the past year. For 18 fish, the cleaning fee was 1500 shillings (about 7.5 yuan RMB).

While waiting for the black man to clean the fish, another black woman arrived with a small basin on her head. When she put it down, there were three crabs and one conch shell. The price was called at 2000 shillings, my husband offered 1500 shillings, but eventually paid only 1000 shillings (about 5 yuan RMB) because there was no change. My husband said that the crab shells are very thin and easily breakable, making them convenient to eat. Without professional tools, we used the conch shell to scrape off fish scales, which took some time. During this time, I left my husband and wandered through the fishing village behind the shed. The village was small, built along the coast, with dozens of households. Although called a village, almost all the houses were huts made of branches and grass, the most decent ones being two or three square meter tin-roofed houses operating as daily necessities stores. It was already evening, and some women began cooking dinner, using brick stoves with blackened aluminum pots. Several black people sat idly in the huts, whistling in unison as I approached, mixing English or Swahili words.

Back inside the shed, the fish were almost cleaned, and the scales and innards attracted many crows. Locally, crows are considered sacred birds, and harming them is forbidden. In the blink of an eye, the pile of fish scales and innards was swept away by the crows, leaving the beach clean again.

During my days on Zanzibar, fish became my main and almost sole source of protein. Parrotfish, Changgu fish, grouper, golden threadfin bream, and many other unnamed sea fish... steamed, fried, boiled... I ate until I was tired of it. There were also crabs, which were plentiful on Zanzibar. Once, my husband spent 35,000 shillings (about 175 yuan RMB) buying eight crabs weighing 10 kilograms, with the largest one being 2.5 kilograms! My husband hurriedly took out the camera to take a commemorative photo with it. That night, my husband steamed two crabs, probably weighing 3-4 kilograms. The two big claws alone filled a large plate. We ate crab and drank wine, truly indulging in a feast!

Not only seafood, but tropical fruits like mangoes and papayas were also abundant on the island, and these fruits were also the crows' favorite. There were two tall mango trees in the courtyard of my husband's workplace, producing hundreds of kilograms of fruit every year, most of which were pecked by crows. Because of the abundance of food, the crows here were all robust.

During my stay on Zanzibar, at the invitation of General Manager Xie of China Railway Construction Corporation's (CRCC) Zanzibar branch, we attended their New Year barbecue party. In the 1960s, CRCC participated in the construction of the famous Tazara Railway, and they were one of the earlier Chinese enterprises to enter Zanzibar. They purchased a shoe factory building (now abandoned) that was previously aided by China as their base and hired more than 1000 local employees. The Kempinski Hotel in Zanzibar and a teaching building of the Zanzibar Medical College were constructed by them. That evening, a cool breeze blew, the night sky was crescent-shaped with sparkling stars, and eight or nine square tables were set up on the basketball court with guests seated among them. The dishes were very tasty, except for the shrimp, crabs, and squid bought from the market, the lamb, pork, cucumber, and vegetables were all grown and raised by the CRCC employees teaching their employed black workers. Especially the sliced cucumber and stir-fried Shanghai greens tasted better than those in China—because pig manure was used as fertilizer instead of chemical fertilizers and pesticides, making them completely organic food. During the banquet, I met a fellow townsman—Mr. Huang, an engineer from Fujian Construction Group. Miles away, hearing the familiar dialect, I felt particularly close, and couldn't help but marvel that wherever there is seawater, there are Fujian people. The spirit of hard work, perseverance, and courage to win of Fujian people is embodied in Fujian Construction Group. Compared to CRCC, Fujian Construction Group may be a latecomer, but they do not fall behind, contracting the largest project in Zanzibar—the renovation and construction of the Ammani Stadium. This stadium was built free of charge by China for Zanzibar in the early 1970s and is the largest stadium in Zanzibar. Due to long-term disrepair, the stadium and its facilities were severely aged. To implement the eight measures proposed at the 2006 Beijing Summit of the Forum on China-Africa Cooperation, the Chinese government invested 48 million yuan RMB to upgrade the Ammani Stadium in Zanzibar, which is also the largest aid project to Zanzibar in the past decade. This project is scheduled to be delivered for use in April this year. A few days later, my husband took me to visit the stadium. This stadium, capable of accommodating tens of thousands of people, features eight standard plastic tracks and a lush football field, with towering light poles shining brightly under the sunlight. Such a modern venue requires substantial annual maintenance funds, according to Mr. Huang from Fujian Construction Group, just turning on the eight high-rise lights consumes half of Zanzibar's electricity.

My husband also took me to visit the Darajani Market, which, built in 1802, remains the largest market in Zanzibar today. Inside and outside the white arches of Arabic classical style, stalls are full, with swarms of flies flying in the smell of mutton and the stench of sewers. Although there are many stalls, the variety is not rich. Vegetables are limited to cucumbers, tomatoes, eggplants, and local amaranth, and fruits are mainly mangoes and bananas, all wilted under the scorching tropical sun. The back part of the market sells spices, small wood carvings, and other tourist products, where we bought many clove sachets, costing about 1 dollar each. The sachets made of local reeds filled with clove powder are quite distinctive. Cloves from Zanzibar are world-renowned, and the cultivation of cloves is a history filled with suffering. In 1832, Sultan Said bin Sultan of Zanzibar colluded with European colonizers, selling and driving African slaves to cut down dense primeval forests on Pemba Island and Zanzibar Island, planting millions of clove and coconut trees. At the time, it was stipulated that for every coconut tree planted, three clove trees must be planted, otherwise severe punishment would follow. This law might have been somewhat coercive, but objectively expanded the planting area of clove trees. The 4.5 million clove trees gave Zanzibar the reputation of the "world's most fragrant island" and made it one of the main producers and exporters of cloves worldwide. Many tourists visiting Darajani Market, including myself, pinch our noses—not just for sightseeing, but more for experiencing Africa's backwardness.

Directly opposite Darajani Market is the noisy public bus station, with people coming and going every day, and the cries of "dala dala" never ceasing. "Dala dala" means 5 shillings in Swahili. Many years ago, when Zanzibar started developing public transportation, the fare for a single ride was 5 shillings, and thus the buses were called "dala dala." Now the fare has increased significantly, costing about 250 shillings (about 1.25 yuan RMB) per ride, but the name "dala dala" has persisted. These buses are very different from others; apart from a few minibuses, almost all are modified Toyota trucks with a load capacity of 2-3 tons. Benches are installed on both sides of the cargo box, providing about ten seats, and luggage can be stacked on the roof. On many roads in Zanzibar, you can see "dala dala" happily running with the roofs piled high with goods and loaded with passengers, many of whom are farmers carrying agricultural products to the city center for market.

Next to Darajani Market is the Slave Market Memorial, documenting the history of blacks being captured from the African continent to Zanzibar and then sold to America in the 19th century. The former slave auction market, the dark and low-ceilinged basement where slaves were detained, and the Anglican church built by British colonizers to repent for their human trafficking next to the slave market silently narrate the blood and tears of blacks. Today, it has become a must-visit spot for tourists. When my husband took me to visit, we encountered a European tour group seriously listening to the black guide explaining the sins of their ancestors. Time passes, over a century has gone by, the history of blacks being sold as slaves has become forever history, but the life of Africans remains tough.

During my days in Zanzibar, my husband often took me to stroll through Stone Town in the evening. On the way to Stone Town, we always passed the largest hospital on Zanzibar—the One Coconut Tree Hospital, where our medical team provided services, treating patients every morning. From afar, this hospital, neighboring the presidential palace, with decades of history, white exterior walls, and distinct Arab-style architecture, appears grand, but upon entering, it's greatly disappointing. Everywhere, the walls are cracked, and in some places, the wire mesh is completely exposed. In the corridors, some patients sit on the ground expressionlessly waiting for treatment. Although there are five floors with dozens of rooms, whether outpatient clinics, treatment rooms, or wards, there is hardly any medical equipment. Due to power outages, the rooms are dim, contrasting with the moldy walls, making it appear even more dilapidated. The pharmacy in the annex building is at most twenty to thirty square meters, cluttered with cardboard boxes, and only a row of shelves against the wall holds some medicine. Most of these medicines are donated by foreign governments and international organizations, with the Chinese government donating medicines worth 2 million yuan RMB annually.

One reason Zanzibar is recommended by the American "Travel" magazine as one of the three must-visit destinations in East Africa is the Stone Town designated as a World Heritage Site by UNESCO in 2000. Called Stone Town because the building materials are all local coral stones. Although its name is simple, covering only 96 hectares, in the 19th century, it was one of the most important commercial trade centers in the Indian Ocean region. Between 1503 and 1508, the Portuguese occupied Zanzibar; in 1698, the Arabs overthrew the Portuguese rule and occupied Zanzibar in 1710; in the mid-19th century, British colonizers invaded here, declaring Zanzibar as "the connection point of Britain's important strategic plan in East Africa," making it the largest slave transshipment port in East Africa. Due to its unique history, the architecture in Stone Town combines African, Arab, Indian, and European cultures. Most of the buildings we see today were constructed in the 19th century, approximately 150 years ago. The Portuguese Gate, Arab Castle, Sultan's Palace, and the Miracle Palace built by the British stand silently, while the minarets of Islamic mosques harmonize with the Gothic spires of Christian churches. Coral stone is the easiest material to obtain here, but it is also prone to weathering and corrosion, forcing many buildings to undergo large-scale renovations. Wandering through this small city surrounded by numerous narrow streets and alleys takes several days to fully appreciate its charm. In the long and narrow alleys, almost all the ground-floor rooms have been converted into shops selling wood carvings, spices, black jewelry, and T-shirts printed with elephants, giraffes, or the word "ZANZIBAR." Blacks, Arabs, and Indians enthusiastically solicit business. The lavishly decorated Arab-style houses are mostly restaurants, with few guest rooms but quaint and charming. Indian-style houses have long, narrow balconies upstairs, with handrails and railings carved with patterns. The most eye-catching feature is the door of every household, regardless of architectural style, which is exceptionally exquisite. Tall wooden doors are covered with copper nails, intricately carved with complex patterns, and it's said that there are hundreds of styles of these purely handmade doors. However, what captivated me the most was sitting on the beach in Stone Town, watching the sunset gradually sink in the Zanzibar Strait dotted with white single-mast sailboats, while local children performed somersaults using an old tire on the sand; or on the seawall of the Flodani Park with a strong Islamic flavor, watching children jump into the emerald-like sea from a height of three to four meters. Some children have strong performance desires, making various funny or daring gestures when they see tourists raising cameras. These children, the oldest not exceeding sixteen or seventeen years old, the youngest around eight or nine, all wear T-shirts, some even wearing Arabian robes. I imagine their parents may not have much money to buy swimming trunks, but this does not prevent them from enjoying happiness repeatedly diving, a pure joy that enviable.

About 5-6 kilometers opposite Zanzibar's Unguja Island lies a scenic spot called Turtle Island, measuring only 0.2 square kilometers. On a Saturday morning, my husband and I boarded a motorized wooden boat to visit the island. Also aboard were a British couple in their fifties. Since my husband had worked in the UK, they chatted non-stop along the way. The British couple visited Shanghai last year and praised China's rapid development and cuisine. The small boat quickly moved through the clear turquoise water, reaching the shore in less than half an hour. A wooden boardwalk led us to a lush island, while the British couple asked the boat to take them swimming nearby, for which they brought flippers. Indeed, swimming in such gentle waters must be very enjoyable, and both my husband and I regretted not bringing swimsuits. The English name of Turtle Island is Changuu Private Island (or Prison Island). Changuu means Changgu fish in Swahili, likely named after the abundance of this fish around the island—on Zanzibar, my husband once bought Changgu fish, which I found to have rather hard meat and a strong fishy smell; In 1893, Sir Mathews purchased the island from the Arab owner on behalf of the Zanzibar government and built a prison in 1894 to house prisoners from Zanzibar, hence the island was also called Prison Island, but this prison never housed any prisoners; In 1923, the British colonial authorities used the island as a quarantine isolation area for Zanzibar. The island was finally called Turtle Island because in 1919, the British governor presented the island with four giant turtles from Aldabra Island as gifts, and these turtles multiplied rapidly on the island, reaching around 200 by 1955. Later, some people began secretly selling these sea turtles, reducing the number to only seven by 1990. After years of protection, the turtle family has grown to over sixty currently, with the oldest one being 175 years old and weighing 200 kilograms. These large turtles lie in groups in the woods, leisurely eating fallen leaves. Seeing the vegetable leaves used by tourists for feeding, they crawl slowly to the tourists, supporting themselves with their front legs, stretching their necks to eat the leaves, appearing clumsy and adorable. Turtles generally do not attack people, allowing tourists to touch their heads and bodies. Of course, I was cautious when feeding them vegetable leaves and taking photos with them, given the potential danger of their mouths. After interacting closely with the sea turtles, you can stroll along the trails, visit old buildings, some of whose walls are covered with air roots of banyan trees, exuding artistry and nostalgia. The island also hosts a resort hotel, offering a peaceful retreat for those seeking tranquility.

The earthly paradise on Zanzibar is not only in Stone Town and Turtle Island. On a weekend, my husband took me to a resort called KIWENGWA VILLAG (BRAVO CIUB) on the east coast, which was my favorite place during my stay in Zanzibar. Built by the sea, the African-style thatched roof serves as the lobby and restaurant, with over twenty white two-story guesthouses hidden among hibiscus flowers, bougainvillea, and flame trees, extending all the way to the beach. Each guesthouse has eight rooms, accommodating nearly 300 guests in total. Hibiscus flowers produced by the hotel decorate the large beds and bathrooms, creating a romantic and cozy atmosphere. Following the steps downward leads to the swimming pool, and further down to the vast turquoise Indian Ocean. Once here, the fee is all-inclusive, with a per-person cost of 150 US dollars, and no additional charges for food or drinks. Daily activities are simple: breakfast and lunch followed by soaking in the pool or lounging on the beach chairs to tan, aiming to achieve the coveted bronze skin admired by Europeans and Americans. During these activities, staff members frequently engage in various interactive games with you. Since most guests are Italian, the management and main staff are also Italian, making Italian the common language. Although I couldn’t understand, it didn’t hinder my relaxation. When hungry, I could enjoy slices of pizza and drinks at the café by the wading pool, all complimentary. Dinner starts at 8 PM, featuring Italian pasta and various types of bread, along with seafood like lobster, red king crab, and fish, with unlimited wine. My husband has a particular fondness for lobster, indulging himself extravagantly that night. After dinner, like most guests, my husband and I didn’t return to our room. There were plenty of sofas and beds in the lounge, where we could randomly choose one, curl up, drink Italian coffee, listen to live music, and watch performances staged by the staff; or stroll to the beach, gaze at the starry sky, letting the night breeze of the Indian Ocean tousle our hair. Here, there is only ease and freedom.
