Smoking Affects Fetal Development

Smoking during pregnancy is very detrimental to the fetus's development and health. Tobacco contains a large amount of toxic substances, apart from the well-known nicotine, there are also hydrogen cyanide, ammonia, carbon monoxide, carbon dioxide, pyridine, aromatic compounds, and tar. These toxic substances can be absorbed into the mother's bloodstream through smoke, reducing oxygen content in the mother's blood, which in turn reduces oxygen content in the placenta. Due to oxygen deficiency, the fetus may experience delayed growth and development. Therefore, low birth weight babies (weighing less than 2500 grams) born to smoking mothers are twice as likely compared to non-smoking women. These infants are not only weaker but also at higher risk of severe diseases within their first year of life, with higher mortality rates. Many recent studies have also shown that smoking pregnant women are more prone to miscarriages, premature births, and stillbirths. A survey of 14,893 pregnant women in the UK showed that smoking women find it harder to conceive and are more likely to miscarry. The survey also found that smoking during pregnancy damages the future fertility of their daughters when they reach adulthood.

High-Fiber Foods Can Lead to Female Infertility

American scientists have been tracking 250 women of childbearing age for nearly two years and concluded: the intake of high-fiber foods such as whole wheat bread and macaroni can disrupt hormonal balance in women, decreasing the chances of conception as more of these foods are consumed.

Researchers found that the intake of high-fiber foods not only lowers hormone levels but can also stop ovulation. Previous studies have shown that factors like high-intensity exercise, anxiety, and stress can lead to cessation of ovulation, and this study is the first to link healthy eating habits with such effects.

Excessive carotene can affect lutein synthesis in the ovaries, reduce secretion, and in some cases, even cause amenorrhea, anovulation, or menstrual irregularities, increasing the possibility of female infertility.

This condition was first discovered in patients with anorexia nervosa; even though they didn't eat and had no menstruation, blood tests revealed abnormally high levels of carotene. Later, in some non-anorexic female patients, it was found that excessive consumption of carrots could lead to elevated blood carotene levels, resulting in female infertility, amenorrhea, anovulation, and other abnormalities. Researchers explained that this might be due to carotene interfering with steroid synthesis.

Additionally, caffeine, alcohol, and others can also increase the likelihood of female infertility, so women should avoid excessive intake. At the same time, women who wish to conceive can appropriately consume more foods that help with conception.
