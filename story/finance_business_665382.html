Watched this movie "Shorty in Love" at home on the afternoon of New Year's Eve.

by kuailaira2 on 2012-02-14 16:11:55

Playboy Men, Not for the Squeamish

On New Year's Eve afternoon, I watched this movie "Short Man in Love" at home and it felt great. Although my mood was average that day, during the viewing process, I took a phone call and my dad interrupted me near the end, but it still allowed me to maintain a pleasant mood while watching.

A Long-Awaited Hong Kong Comedy

To put it simply, "Short Man in Love" tells the strange tales of a rich young man chasing women. This kind of story can be found in many Hong Kong films from the late 80s to early 90s. The ones that stand out in my memory are Chow Yun-fat's "Lucky Stars" and Andy Lau's "Dancing with the Dragon". All three depict rich young men disguising themselves as other identities to chase women, but this film has a different tone: this rich young man is quite a playboy, not nearly as gentlemanly as Chow Yun-fat or Andy Lau. Think about it, the main character is short! How tall and handsome could he possibly be? If we emphasize the value of money over love in this materialistic era, it would be too idealistic. Undeniably, there are still quite a few movie themes nowadays that prefer this overly idealistic direction, completely disregarding the audience. How much resonance can viewers have when watching? For me personally, a recent example is "Poker King", which made me cringe. Therefore, this film stays quite realistic, reminiscent of comedies like "The Bachelor". As a man, I certainly felt a strong sense of immersion while watching.

Very Vulgar, Very Smooth, No Pretense

A movie that can capture one's full attention without any distractions must at least be tight and not draggy. Happily, "Short Man in Love" has achieved this. In terms of the storyline, you might say it's very vulgar, but overall, it has shown an unusual smoothness in recent Hong Kong films. Whether it's scene transitions, the information contained in the characters' dialogues and monologues, or the handling of the rhythm, they all tightly grasp the plot. There's an example: in many films, you often see MTV-style scenes where the protagonist runs or acts heartbroken for at least half a song, then the screen goes black, and the protagonist starts living again. In "Short Man in Love", the protagonist's "song" suddenly stops at a certain point and takes a turn, like feeling a knot while following a rope, preventing you from continuing to "immerse" yourself. How considerate! In terms of acting, Wong Cho-lam continues his usual mischievous short man image, with exaggerated movements and lewd speech. As the saying goes, bad boys attract girls, and his antics would be sharpest on a more common-looking playboy. But it's precisely this short man who often has a lewd look and bursts out with dirty words when facing women, almost drooling like Stephen Chow. This image determines his uniqueness; if Gu Tianle were to play this role, wouldn't you get goosebumps? It was wise to position Wong Cho-lam's character as a clown. Male audiences won't feel that heaven is too unfair because he's rich but sorry in appearance, thus making it easier for them to relate — yes, you may not be as rich as him, but your appearance isn't worse than his, right? So this setup is quite successful, at least he doesn't act pretentious. I understand that many male audiences have various nasty thoughts in their hearts, now the short man expresses them for you, aren't you all satisfied?

Twists and Turns, Nature Hard to Change

In terms of the plot, "Short Man in Love" revolves around Wang Zulan's structure of playing multiple roles due to having too much money but not getting love, interspersed with the line of wooing Angela Baby's character, showing the "romantic journey" of a passionate young man. At the beginning of the film, he says himself that he has forgotten how many women he has had, let alone what the woman three years ago or even three days ago was called. This time, the story of these three women plus the loving woman arises because of his weariness of love tied to money, leading to a different feeling. However, that's all. A man's nature will never disappear. The scene at the end where the short man meets Xiong Dai Lin perfectly explains this. "When a woman turns her back, a man will..." Fellow men, you know what I mean, the requirement for women is always "fresh." So, by the end of the entire film, it still returns to the original point of the short man. Except that he should be more discerning in the future, he will continue the story of pursuing women one after another. But no matter how women come and go, for oneself, the ultimate feeling is still loneliness, truly helpless. So the short man also said: "No matter when, we must cherish the people in front of us," which somewhat mends the flaw of being a playboy. As a pure Hong Kong production, "Short Man in Love" seems to target only Guangdong, mainly the Cantonese-speaking regions besides Hong Kong itself. The free style of Hong Kong films is given full play, with no restrictions on topics and perspectives. Ugly, cute, humorous, and hypocritical characters are fully displayed, so the audience won't be troubled.

A Few Thoughts on the Actors

In terms of actors, this film features TVB actor Wong Cho-lam as the lead, accompanied by many beautiful women. Wong Cho-lam gives me the impression of hosting several ceremonies and playing Deng Li Jun in "Pedder Street Blues" (the later parts might have been cut due to shooting "Short Man"). He is an eloquent young actor, and this time he can be said to be playing his true self. The most direct benefit is that he appears approachable, really familiar. Another point to mention is that this film, or rather recent Hong Kong films, feature beautiful models. Zhou Xiu Na and Angela Baby have higher visibility, appearing in "Trouble Maker", "Death Note", "Seven Things About Us", and this year's "Flower Field Happy Event 2010". Beautiful models, as a much-criticized group and phenomenon, carry a derogatory connotation in Hong Kong, but Hong Kong society and people's inclusiveness give this group hope of being incorporated into normal development tracks. Often, the roles played by beautiful models don't have much screen time, though they don't flaunt their bodies excessively, they can't avoid focusing on physical attributes in front of the camera. For "Short Man in Love", which woman inside isn't beautiful enough? Hiring beautiful models largely fits this need. Take "Seven Things About Us", if Zhou Xiu Na's character didn't have large breasts, how could she be molested by her boss? It can be said that beautiful models provide a new source of actors for Hong Kong films. Although currently limited to some relatively superficial roles, from an economic perspective, these actors' salaries are not high. Whether for money, fame, or genuine performance, most of these grass-roots actors can meet the requirements of production costs and performances (as mentioned earlier, mostly superficial roles). Under the backdrop of Hong Kong films' weakness, although beautiful models cannot integrate into mainstream creations due to social prejudices, they provide new options for directors and producers who are enthusiastic about Hong Kong original productions. As the saying goes, when things are broken, it's best to break them further. Finding ways out under difficult circumstances is exactly the pioneering spirit that Hong Kong films need. Therefore, moving forward, if "beautiful models" can develop towards professionalism, they should be able to inject new blood into Hong Kong films.

Finally, to the friends who read this piece: Regardless of what mindset you have when watching current Hong Kong films, when watching "Short Man in Love", the most important thing is to be happy. Relax!!
