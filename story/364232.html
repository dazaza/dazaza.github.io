What is acrylic?

by tuxiange on 2010-09-10 20:38:15

1. The term "acrylic" may sound unfamiliar, as it is a relatively new term that has only appeared on the mainland in the past two years. It wasn't until 2002 that it gradually began to be understood by a few people in the advertising industry, furniture industry, and crafts industry. "Acrylic" is a transliteration of a foreign word, with its English name being acrylic. It is a chemical material, whose chemical name is "PMMA" (polymethyl methacrylate), belonging to the acrylate class. It is commonly known as "special treated organic glass." In the application industry, its raw materials generally appear in forms such as granules, sheets, and tubes.

2. Acrylic sheet and organic glass "organic glass" originates from the English term "organic glass." In recent years, in some regions, all transparent plastic-made sheets have been collectively referred to as organic glass, which is actually incorrect. Acrylic specifically refers to pure polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA) material, and PMMA sheets are called acrylic sheets.

3. Characteristics and advantages of acrylic sheets

Acrylic has high transparency, with a light transmittance of 92%, earning it the reputation of "plastic crystal." Moreover, it has excellent weather resistance, especially when used outdoors, surpassing other plastics. It also features good surface hardness and luster. It is highly processable and can be made into various required shapes and products. Additionally, acrylic sheets come in a wide variety of colors (including semi-transparent color sheets). Another feature is that even thick sheets can maintain high transparency.

4. History of acrylic Acrylic (acrylic), commonly known as specially treated organic glass. Research and development of acrylic dates back over a hundred years. In 1872, the polymerization of acrylic acid was first discovered; in 1880, the polymerization of methyl methacrylate became known; in 1901, the synthesis method of polyacrylic esters was completed; in 1927, attempts were made to industrialize production using the aforementioned synthesis method; in 1937, the industrial manufacturing of methyl methacrylate ester was successfully developed, thus entering large-scale production.

During World War II, due to acrylic's superior toughness and transparency, it was initially used for airplane windshields and tank drivers' vision mirrors. In 1948, the world's first acrylic bathtub was born, marking a new milestone in the application of acrylic.

5. Uses of acrylic

PMMA has the advantages of being lightweight, cost-effective, and easy to form. Its forming methods include casting, injection molding, mechanical processing, and heat forming. Especially injection molding allows for mass production, with simple processes and low costs. Therefore, its applications are becoming increasingly widespread. Currently, it is widely used in instrument components, car headlights, optical lenses, transparent pipes, etc.
