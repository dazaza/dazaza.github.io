Users Accuse LinkedIn of Email Info Theft, Plan Class-Action Suit

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 20:42:34

LinkedIn has recently been accused by users. In the accusation, users claimed that LinkedIn misused their email accounts and downloaded contact email addresses to use this personal identity information for marketing purposes.

As a result, these accusing users are preparing to launch a class-action lawsuit against LinkedIn. Currently, they have requested a federal judge in San Jose, California, USA, to issue an order prohibiting LinkedIn from continuing to infringe on user privacy information, and they also asked the court to order LinkedIn to return all revenue obtained by promoting its website using users' personal identity information.

These users initiated charges against LinkedIn as early as September 17th and demanded appropriate compensation from LinkedIn. They accused: "LinkedIn's own website has received hundreds of complaints from users regarding this behavior."

LinkedIn claims to have established the world's largest professional social network, with over 238 million members, including executives of Fortune 500 companies. During Friday's regular trading session on the U.S. stock market, LinkedIn's stock price fell by $5.16, closing at $243.90, a drop of 2.07%.

Doug Madey, a spokesperson for LinkedIn, stated that the accusations from these users were invalid and that LinkedIn would counterattack. The spokesperson said via email on Friday: "LinkedIn adheres to a user-first philosophy, and our measures regarding protecting and utilizing user data are very transparent."

According to the users' accusations, LinkedIn requires users to provide external emails when logging into the site, to be used as usernames for login. However, when users leave, LinkedIn uses this information to access their external email accounts.

Users accused LinkedIn of pretending to be the email owner and downloading contact addresses from users' email boxes. "LinkedIn can download these email addresses without entering the email password or obtaining user consent," users further accused. Users also alleged that the role of LinkedIn software engineer Brian Guan was "to design hacking programs to exploit user information."

Viral Growth:

Users criticized LinkedIn's growth strategy, which involves using contacts to seek solutions to increase the number of website members. They believe this is a viral growth method. A LinkedIn user, Deborah Lagutaris, said that LinkedIn had contacted more than 3,000 users under her name, including the contacts mentioned in her email information.

Deborah stated: "This means that LinkedIn not only directly misused the contacts in my email but also included some external personnel. I contacted LinkedIn, and they argued 'you can manually delete all the invitations appearing in your account content, we don't know how it happened.'" However, Deborah has already stated on LinkedIn that she never sent such invitations.

Another user, Jeffrey Barr, also complained via email that LinkedIn misused over 200 contacts and email addresses from his contact list and even sent out invitations to these contacts inviting them to log onto the LinkedIn website. Barr said: "I haven't been in touch with most of these people for 5 to 10 years, including several ex-girlfriends, just because I forgot to delete these contacts."

These users claim that they currently cannot stop LinkedIn's actions. Currently, Larry Russ from Russ August & Kabat law firm in Los Angeles is the lead attorney assisting these users in making their accusations.
