Experts Claim That High Housing Prices Are Not Conducive to Expanding Domestic Demand and Affecting the Economic Structure Adjustment

by h2q4e4o7r7 on 2012-03-07 20:43:32

● This year, the extent of the housing price increase is worth paying attention to ● Residents' investment demands have pushed up housing price increases ● If this continues, it will suppress consumption-type home purchases ● It will also affect the current economic structural adjustment At present, the continuous rise in China's housing prices is eroding the implementation effects of a series of economic stimulus policies introduced by the government to expand domestic demand. To some extent, high housing prices have already produced a serious crowding-out effect on China's efforts to expand domestic demand. Although this is an old problem, under the current context of "maintaining growth, expanding domestic demand, and adjusting structure," this issue deserves our high attention. Indeed, according to the latest data released by the National Bureau of Statistics, in September, the sales prices of houses in 70 large and medium-sized cities across the country increased by 2.8% year-on-year and 0.7% month-on-month; the sales prices of new residential properties increased by 2.7% year-on-year and 0.8% month-on-month; the sales prices of new residential properties with an area of 90 square meters or less increased by 4.9% year-on-year and 1.0% month-on-month. On the surface, the extent of the housing price increase is not very high, but in reality, on the basis of already high housing prices, the housing prices in cities such as Beijing, Shenzhen, and Shanghai have set new historical records since the beginning of this year, and the extent of the housing price increase is "quite astonishing." The reason why housing prices in some domestic cities have surged so significantly since the beginning of this year is mainly due to the push from housing investment demands. Because housing is a dual-purpose product for both investment and consumption. When housing serves as a consumer good, housing demand is limited and can be determined by market supply and demand relationships. However, if housing is treated as an investment good, factors such as the convenience of financing, high leverage ratios, and investor expectations will all drive up housing price increases, at which point the investment demand for housing becomes unlimited. Further analysis reveals that the current loose credit environment has stimulated investors' infinite investment demand for housing and continuously driven up housing price increases. I have said before that when housing becomes an investment good, factors such as convenient and preferential financing conditions and the profit-making effect of housing investments in previous years will all lead to the conclusion that merely increasing housing supply cannot meet this type of housing investment demand. This is one of the fundamental reasons why China's housing prices have surged so rapidly and remain so high today. Of course, the long-term existence of high housing prices will inevitably have serious negative impacts or effects on residents' consumption. This is because high housing prices "occupy" residents' immediate and potential consumption capabilities. Let us first use internationally recognized methods such as the "housing-income ratio," "price-rent ratio," and "absolute price method" to measure the overall level of China's housing prices. Taking Beijing as an example, the housing-income ratio in the core areas of Beijing has reached 22:1, which is significantly higher than other international megacities such as Tokyo, London, and Vancouver. This means that if residents want to invest in housing through rental income, their investment return rate will definitely be lower than the current interest rate level, indicating that domestic housing investment has actually become "speculation" rather than investment at this point. In addition, from the perspective of absolute housing price levels in places like Beijing and Shanghai, they have already surpassed international megacities in the United States and Japan. However, the per capita income in the U.S. and Japan is several times or even dozens of times higher than that of China. If we also consider exchange rate factors, then the housing prices in some Chinese cities have become excessively high. Therefore, the robust investment demand for housing is the most fundamental reason for the excessively high housing prices in China today. However, such high housing prices are undoubtedly bad news for ordinary consumers of housing. Because high housing prices have basically squeezed out housing consumers from the real estate market in today's China. If they want to afford housing, they would have to rely on the savings accumulated over several generations and future family incomes. Such housing purchases not only lead to limited subsequent housing renovation and furniture consumption capabilities but also cause a comprehensive decline in the immediate consumption capabilities of several generations. It is worth noting that high housing prices will also produce a huge crowding-out effect on the future consumption of these families. For example, most of their income will be used for mortgage payments, which will not only lead to a comprehensive shrinkage of daily consumption for ordinary housing consumers but also affect their human capital improvement, ultimately leading to the固化 (stagnation) of income levels and weakening of consumption capabilities for these families. We can say that the focus of China's current economic stimulus policies is to greatly expand domestic demand. In statistical terms, residents' housing purchases are not consumption but investment. However, in reality, residents' housing purchases are their largest single consumption. This deviant phenomenon can easily mislead our policy decisions. High housing prices will not only have significant negative impacts on residents' current and future consumption but also affect the economic structural adjustments currently underway in China today. In response, on one hand, we need to adopt appropriate real estate credit policies to ensure residents' housing consumption, and on the other hand, we must avoid stimulating GDP growth again through real estate investment, especially high housing prices, thereby promoting the stable and sustained development of China's economy.
