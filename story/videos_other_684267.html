Use of acidimeters

by jd1718 on 2012-02-29 21:01:35

The acidimeter, also known as the pH meter, consists of two parts: an electrode and a main unit. During use, if the electrode is properly maintained, standard buffer solutions are prepared according to requirements, and the pH meter is operated correctly, it can significantly reduce the error in pH readings, thereby improving the reliability of data in chemical experiments and medical tests.

### 1. Correct Use and Maintenance of Electrodes

Currently, electrodes used in laboratories are all composite electrodes. Their advantages include ease of use, no influence from oxidizing or reducing substances, and faster balancing speed. When using them, remove the rubber cap on the electrode’s liquid inlet and the rubber cover at its bottom to maintain the hydraulic pressure difference of the potassium chloride solution inside the electrode. Below is a brief introduction to the use and maintenance of electrodes:

1. When not in use, composite electrodes can be fully immersed in a 3M potassium chloride solution. Avoid washing with detergent or other water-absorbing reagents.

2. Before use, check the glass bulb at the front end of the electrode. Normally, the electrode should be transparent without cracks; the bulb should be filled with solution, with no air bubbles present.

3. When measuring solutions with high concentrations, try to shorten the measurement time and clean carefully afterward to prevent the test solution from adhering to the electrode and causing contamination.

4. After cleaning the electrode, do not wipe the glass membrane with filter paper but instead use filter paper to absorb moisture, avoiding damage to the glass film and preventing cross-contamination that could affect measurement accuracy.

5. During measurement, ensure that the silver chloride reference electrode inside the electrode is immersed in the chloride buffer solution within the bulb to avoid erratic digital readings on the display. When using, gently shake the electrode a few times.

6. Electrodes cannot be used for strong acids, strong bases, or other corrosive solutions.

7. It is strictly forbidden to use dehydrating media such as anhydrous ethanol or potassium dichromate.

### 2. Preparation and Storage of Standard Buffer Solutions

1. Hlter (Hart) pH standard materials should be stored in a dry place. For example, mixed phosphate pH standard materials will deliquesce when exposed to high humidity in the air. Once deliquescence occurs, the pH standard material becomes unusable.

2. When preparing pH standard solutions, use distilled water twice or deionized water. If using a 0.1 grade pH meter, regular distilled water can be used.

3. When diluting to prepare pH standard solutions, use smaller beakers to minimize the amount of pH standard solution adhering to the walls of the beaker. The plastic bag or other container storing the pH standard material should be emptied thoroughly and rinsed multiple times with distilled water before being poured into the prepared pH standard solution to ensure accuracy.

4. Prepared standard buffer solutions generally last 2-3 months. If any turbidity, mold, or precipitation occurs, they should no longer be used.

5. Alkaline standard solutions should be stored in tightly sealed polyethylene bottles to prevent carbon dioxide from entering the standard solution and forming carbonic acid, which would lower its pH value.

### 3. Correct Calibration of Hlter (Hart) pH Meter

Due to differences in design, there are many types of pH meters, each with different operating steps. Therefore, pH meter operations should strictly follow the instructions in the user manual. In specific operations, calibration is an important step in using a pH meter. Despite the variety of pH meters, their calibration methods all use the two-point calibration method, i.e., selecting two standard buffer solutions: one is the pH 7 standard buffer solution, and the second is either the pH 9 standard buffer solution or the pH 4 standard buffer solution. First, use the pH 7 standard buffer solution to calibrate the main unit, then select the second standard buffer solution based on the acidity or alkalinity of the sample solution. If the sample solution is acidic, choose the pH 4 standard buffer solution; if the sample solution is basic, choose the pH 9 standard buffer solution. For manually adjustable pH meters, repeat the operation between the two standard buffer solutions several times until no further adjustment of the zero point and positioning (slope) knobs is needed, ensuring the pH meter accurately displays the pH values of the two standard buffer solutions. At this point, the calibration process is complete. Thereafter, during measurement, the zero point and positioning knobs should not be moved again. For intelligent pH meters, repeated adjustments are unnecessary because their internal memory stores the pH values of several standard buffer solutions for selection and automatic recognition and calibration. However, attention must be paid to the accuracy of standard buffer solution selection and preparation. Intelligent 0.01-level pH meters generally have three to five standard buffer solution pH values stored in their internal memory.

Secondly, before calibration, special attention should be given to the temperature of the sample solution to correctly select the standard buffer solution and adjust the temperature compensation knob on the panel so that it matches the temperature of the sample solution. After the calibration work is completed, frequently used pH meters generally do not need recalibration within 48 hours. However, if any of the following situations occur, the instrument needs to be recalibrated:

1. When there is a significant difference between the solution temperature and the calibration temperature.

2. When the electrode has been exposed to air for too long, such as more than half an hour.

3. When the positioning or slope regulator has been accidentally moved.

4. After measuring very acidic (pH 12) solutions.

5. After replacing the electrode.

6. When the pH value of the measured solution is not within the range selected during two-point calibration and is far from pH 7.
