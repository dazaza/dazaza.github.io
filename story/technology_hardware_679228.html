Case: Recently, there was a patient Ms. Liu who inquired through the newspaper about her condition. She has been taking sleeping pills for quite some time and often wakes up in the middle of the night, unable to fall back asleep. She consulted the doctor whether she should take another sleeping pill. The doctor's response: Due to modern lifestyles and various physical and mental factors, insomnia is becoming increasingly common. For treating insomnia, sedative-hypnotic drugs are not the only solution that doctors rely on. Many patients with insomnia also seek and consume "sleeping pills". Additionally, the idea that "taking sleeping pills can lead to addiction" is deeply ingrained in many people, and many patients become frightened and refuse medication, causing their conditions to worsen. In reality, treating insomnia is a scientific and meticulous issue, and insomnia patients should take medication correctly under the guidance of sleep specialists and follow standard treatments. Finding the root cause is key.

In recent years, the incidence of insomnia has been increasing. It is estimated that about 20% of Americans (40-70 million) suffer from chronic or intermittent insomnia, while in China, approximately 30-35% of the general population experience insomnia. A 2006 survey on insomnia in some rural areas of China showed that about 57% of elderly people suffer from insomnia. In Guangzhou, the rate of insomnia among the red-haired population reaches as high as 60%. Insomnia can be both a primary disease and a clinical symptom of other diseases. The key first step in treating insomnia is finding the root cause. There are multiple causes that can lead to insomnia:

1. Various emotional or mental disorders (depression, anxiety, mania, schizophrenia), which are relatively common, accounting for about 50%;

2. Various physical illnesses (pain, restless leg syndrome, heart failure, brain damage), accounting for about 20%;

3. Sleep apnea syndrome (commonly known as snoring), accounting for about 15%;

4. Primary and complex insomnia, accounting for about 15%.

Insomnia is not a simple issue; when symptoms appear, one should not assume it’s primary insomnia without professional diagnosis and testing. Causes need to be identified, types of insomnia evaluated, and severity assessed before receiving standardized treatment.

How to take sleeping pills for insomnia? As mentioned earlier, there are many reasons for insomnia, such as physical illness or pain, psychiatric disorders like depression, side effects of medications, circadian rhythm disorders, or obstructive sleep apnea syndrome. Therefore, the best way to treat insomnia is to find and treat the underlying cause, i.e., causal treatment. For example, if the cause of sleep disturbance or insomnia is depression, then using antidepressant medication would be the best choice. Similarly, if the reason for difficulty falling or staying asleep is arthritis-induced pain, the best treatment drug would be a pain reliever.

Currently, sedative-hypnotic drugs are an important clinical tool for treating insomnia. Clinically, different types of insomnia should be treated with appropriate sedative-hypnotic drugs: ① Difficulty falling asleep. Choose drugs that induce sleep quickly, such as short half-life sedative-hypnotics like zaleplon and zolpidem. ② Nighttime awakenings, light sleep. Choose hypnotic drugs that can extend sleep duration, such as ramelteon. ③ Early morning awakenings. Consider medium or long-acting sedative-hypnotic drugs. Early awakenings are often seen in depression-related insomnia, so attention should be paid to treating the underlying disease while addressing insomnia. ④ Insomnia due to sleep apnea. Focus on treating sleep apnea, which can be managed with CPAP or surgical interventions. ⑤ If the patient's sleep disorder is accompanied by anxiety or depression, use anti-anxiety or antidepressant medications. ⑥ If the patient's sleep disorder is caused by mental abnormalities, antipsychotic medications should be used, possibly combined with sedative-hypnotics if necessary.

Can you add more sleeping pills if you wake up in the middle of the night? All sedative-hypnotic drugs have equal efficacy in treating insomnia symptoms, but choosing the right drug clinically is crucial. Some patients initially benefit from a particular sedative-hypnotic drug, but after some time, its effectiveness may decrease or even disappear, as seen in Mr. Liu's case. When this happens, the clinician should reassess the patient's condition and adjust the treatment plan accordingly.

Mr. Liu recently wakes up in the middle of the night and cannot fall back asleep. Possible reasons include:

1. Concurrent other diseases:

- Most commonly, depressive or anxiety disorders. Acute insomnia can be a trigger for these conditions, and if primary insomnia is not treated early and properly, it could lead to mood disorders like depression or anxiety. These conditions can cause secondary insomnia symptoms like daytime wakefulness, early awakening, and difficulty falling asleep.

- Concurrent sleep apnea-hypopnea syndrome (SAHS). New-onset SAHS or worsening existing SAHS can cause nighttime awakenings, breathing difficulties, inability to fall back asleep, and daytime fatigue. If the patient has a family history of snoring, childhood snoring, obesity, a thick neck, or recent weight gain along with insomnia symptoms, SAHS should be highly suspected.

- Various physical illnesses. Chronic insomnia can weaken the body, leading to hormonal imbalances and impaired bodily functions, making the patient more susceptible to various physical illnesses. Poor sleep can exacerbate pre-existing physical conditions, creating a vicious cycle.

2. Drug tolerance. Long-term use of certain sedative-hypnotic drugs, especially benzodiazepines, particularly if used improperly, can lead to drug tolerance.

If Ms. Liu experiences this situation, she should seek treatment at a sleep specialty clinic as soon as possible to undergo relevant evaluations and tests to clarify her condition. If other diseases are found during diagnosis, comprehensive treatment targeting those specific diseases should be administered (e.g., if diagnosed with anxiety disorder, add anti-anxiety treatment and adjust the dose or type of original sedative-hypnotic drugs accordingly). If drug tolerance occurs, adjustments can be made in the following ways: ① Change the medication. Gradually taper off the original drug and switch to another suitable alternative. ② Add sedative-hypnotic drugs. This applies only when taking less effective drugs like zaleplon or zolpidem. Since different drugs have varying half-lives (the time required for metabolism and elimination from the body), caution must be exercised. For instance, the half-life of zolpidem is 1.5-4.5 hours. Metabolism may take 4.5 hours for slower metabolizers, so if the patient needs to get up for work at 7 p.m., they should not take additional zolpidem after 2:30 a.m. Otherwise, they might feel drowsy the next day due to incomplete drug metabolism. Patients considering adding medication must clearly understand the half-life of the drug and consult the doctor about the appropriate timing for addition. ③ Increase dosage before bedtime. If the patient originally took a low dose, consider increasing the dose before bed. However, if the dose already reached the maximum safe level, further increases are not recommended. ④ Add another drug. Under scientific evaluation, clinicians may strictly adhere to indications and combine two or more drugs for treatment.

Are there concerns about side effects or addiction when taking sleeping pills? After reading about Mr. Liu's medication adjustment methods, many people might wonder: How much side effects are we talking about with all these drugs? What if I become addicted?

To address these misconceptions and worries, sleep medicine experts strongly advocate: Correct diagnosis, standardized treatment, and don't hesitate to take sleeping pills as prescribed!

"All medicines have side effects," which is true. Any medication (even vitamin tablets) has potential side effects. However, having side effects isn't scary as long as they are mild and within the tolerable range of our bodies. Newer sedative-hypnotic drugs, primarily benzodiazepines, can potentially lead to drug tolerance or dependence if misused. Abrupt discontinuation can make insomnia rebound worse. But these drugs do not pose significant toxicity to the human body. With advancements in medical science, many newer sedative-hypnotic drugs have emerged and are now used clinically. These drugs differ from traditional benzodiazepines, with fewer side effects, generally no dependency issues, and higher safety levels, offering great benefits to insomniacs.

Here, it is important for insomniacs to realize: Refusing medication for insomnia harms physical health more than taking medication does! Sleep is a fundamental physiological need. Without adequate rest, insomnia can lead to endocrine imbalance, physical dysfunction, weight loss, daytime fatigue, anxiety, depression, and other issues affecting work and life. Please manage your sleep scientifically!

Scientific management of sleep is very important. Good sleep habits can effectively prevent many sleep disorders. Insomniacs forming good sleep habits will aid in quicker recovery. Here are a few points to keep in mind for managing sleep scientifically:

① Maintain regular schedules. Avoid irregular sleep patterns, staying up late, or sleeping haphazardly.

② Exercise appropriately. Engage in moderate exercise in the afternoon or evening. Exercise before bed may affect sleep, whereas vigorous morning exercise can make you feel tired during the day.

③ Follow a healthy diet. Balance your meals and avoid caffeine and tea alkaloids. Preferably, do not drink coffee or tea in the evening or before bed.

④ Treat sleep correctly and avoid excessive focus or anxiety about sleep. Occasional insomnia or poor sleep doesn't need undue attention. Consult professional doctors to understand sleep knowledge.

⑤ Seek medical help promptly if issues arise. Don’t delay treatment! Many sleep problems can be resolved quickly if addressed in the early stages.
