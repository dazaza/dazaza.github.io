Synopsis:

The film is based on the story of Michael Peterson, one of the most notorious prisoners in British history. In 1974, a teenager named Charles Bronson befriended some street hooligans and picked up bad habits. At that restless and fervent age, he wanted to become famous quickly, so he made a handgun at home and carried out a post office robbery. The spoils were meager — just £26, but it cost him seven years in prison.

Afterwards, this young man kept getting into trouble in jail and kept receiving additional sentences. He spent a total of 34 years in prison, with 30 of those years spent alone in solitary confinement. The film focuses on his 34-year-long prison life, where fighting became the entirety of his existence.

Behind-the-scenes Production:

The Most Violent Prisoner in Britain

The protagonist of this film, Michael Peterson, is known as "the most violent prisoner in Britain," and the contradictions, confusions, and controversies surrounding him have sparked the curiosity of the British public. He has transcended the typical criminal cases published in the social pages and become a special kind of "celebrity."

Peterson was born in 1954 into an upper-class family in the UK. As a teenager, he befriended some delinquent friends and worked as a circus performer and a boxer. At 19, he was imprisoned for the first time after robbing £26 with a weapon. Contrary to popular belief, Peterson has never committed or been involved in any murder case — he has never killed anyone. Due to his terrifying behavior in prison, including malicious injury, illegal detention, and intimidation, his sentence was repeatedly extended. Starting with a 7-year imprisonment at the age of 19, he served a total of 34 years in prison, with 30 of those years spent strictly in solitary confinement. Having lived in 120 different cells, Peterson was also nicknamed the "King of British Prisons" by the public.

In 1998, the prison authorities established a special action team for him and two other inmates. In 2000, Peterson was sentenced to life imprisonment due to malicious injuries. Over these years, Peterson was released twice, but during the 120 days outside of prison, he mostly spent his time in bars and was eventually re-arrested for robbery and attempted robbery.

During his time in prison, Peterson reportedly did 2,500 push-ups every day and even published a book on how to stay fit in the simplest and most isolated conditions. Later, he gradually began studying poetry and art, publishing 11 books and winning the Koestler Award. He is also a cartoonist. Additionally, Peterson participated in public welfare activities aimed at preventing juvenile crime and protecting animals. It's said that he once wrote to a museum saying, "I was born to be caged, but please spare the birds and small animals. Otherwise, I'll rip the head off your shoulders." Peterson's supporters attribute his actions to uncontrollable temper and confusion about normal life, claiming that his nature is as kind and gentle as any ordinary person’s, "He is not a demon, but a patient."

Peterson married once at the age of 17. Later, a Muslim woman started corresponding with him after seeing his photo in the newspaper. They got married after meeting only three times in the prison. After their divorce, Peterson claimed she merely sought fame through him. In the last eight years, Peterson has had no record of using violence, and he claims that violence is no longer part of his life. He also provided many suggestions for the filming of this movie, engaging in several conversations with the director and lead actor. He was also satisfied with the script.

Box Office Comparison Only With "A Clockwork Orange"

Sometimes, when a director creates a character, the prototype doesn't play a decisive role. As Bronson himself said, "When I watched the script and the movie, I always thought of Stanley Kubrick's 'A Clockwork Orange.' Oh, God, I really want to drag him out of the grave and beat him up."

Because "A Clockwork Orange" is so deeply ingrained in people's minds and almost universally praised, this film inevitably invites comparisons to "A Clockwork Orange" after its production. Director Refn said, "In my heart, 'A Clockwork Orange' is the ultimate classic, impossible to surpass, and I am merely imitating—or rather learning from it." Indeed, Refn isn't wrong; from the film's unique visual style and editing characteristics to the extensive use of classical music, it's all imbued with the style of "A Clockwork Orange." However, Refn hasn't been entirely successful because no one has said, "‘Bronson’ is the most Kubrickian film since Kubrick's death." Clearly, Refn didn't succeed in that regard. Nevertheless, he successfully created a complete and full image of a "prison demon," a man who idolizes violence. "Certainly," Refn confidently stated, "this movie will definitely achieve good commercial success, better than the box office of 'A Clockwork Orange.' Earlier, at the Sundance Film Festival, someone compared this movie to 'Steven Spielberg-style' rather than 'Kubrick-style.'"

Just like the mixed reviews "A Clockwork Orange" received back then, many viewers expressed dissatisfaction with the film. A critic remarked, "This kind of movie always makes me feel incomprehensible, violence, blood, and guts—there's no beauty in it." Knowing it would receive criticism, Refn still bravely directed this film, precisely because of the stylish "A Clockwork Orange" that came before it, giving Refn the courage to direct this movie. Although the film doesn't shy away from directly and sharply depicting violence, under the stylistic visual effects, props, set design, and art direction, it remains captivating. "Yes," Refn said, "If it weren't for Kubrick's 'A Clockwork Orange,' I absolutely wouldn't dare to portray violence poetically. He is truly an unparalleled pioneer. I am just a hardworking student, and I still have a long way to go. I am still young, and I need to keep learning."

The Violent Man on Screen

To depict Michael Peterson's "violent career" on screen, director Refn went through quite a bit of effort to find a suitable narrative approach. Accompanied by chaotic and restless violence, the film does not follow the traditional storytelling method of biographical films. The movie completely disrupts the timeline, organizing the story with an internal logic of "escalating violence." The director said, "On screen, I am portraying the psychological state of a person born for prison. Therefore, I abandoned external temporal relationships and instead used a more direct method to face the soul. Bronson is a man who craves violence immensely, and I tried to express this craving on screen. Through this narrative, I portrayed a person's character and traits. Regarding viewpoints, I want to express that violence is not scary; there is violence in life all the time. What is truly terrifying is indulging in violence and tolerating it passively. This not only fosters violence but also makes it more rampant."

The film's leading actor, Tom Hardy, was very interested in performing such a complex and difficult-to-describe character. He said, "For me, or for most actors, it's a rare opportunity to perform a living person. Moreover, Peterson is such a contradictory and paradoxical composite. On one hand, he is a man who idolizes violence, while on the other hand, he understands life, novels, and poetry well. You can say he is brutal, but he loves animals; you can say he is cold-blooded, yet he shows great kindness towards children and the poor. It's hard to describe. So, playing this role is a huge challenge. To perform him well, one needs a deep understanding of the character and an even deeper understanding of violence."

Fun Facts:

- To portray the muscular Bronson, the lead actor Tom Hardy underwent rigorous muscle training.

- The lead actor Tom Hardy believes Bronson is the most violent character to appear on the big screen in recent years, far exceeding the violence of Daniel Day-Lewis's portrayal of Bill the Butcher in Martin Scorsese's "Gangs of New York."

- To authentically portray prison life, Tom Hardy stayed in a small prison for several months. His comment on the experience was, "Hmm, prison life is indeed disgusting. If I stayed here too long, I might turn violent."

- To celebrate the completion of the film, director Refn specially built a small power station. During the inauguration ceremony, he stated that characters like Bronson are certainly not demons, nor are they martyrs of modern industrial civilization.

- During the filming of the fight scenes, many actors lost their mouthguards.

- The director designed a falling motion for the actors who were knocked down, resembling a form of modern ballet dance.
