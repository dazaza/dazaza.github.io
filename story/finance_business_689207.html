The video industry has already seen bloodshed, and web series are once again popular in Hong Kong, Taiwan, and TV dramas.

by hoouk260 on 2012-03-03 20:17:04

The blood-stained video industry: Liu Hong of Letv.com recently remarked that "the video industry is already bloody." His observation is not without basis. Recently, major domestic video companies have been acquiring film and television companies, spending large sums on film copyrights, with prices reaching staggering levels. Sohu's reputation for burning money is well-known. It is rumored that Sohu spent 30 million yuan to purchase the rights to "New Princess Huanzhu" and even invested 70 million yuan in promotion. Altogether, Sohu has spent 100 million yuan on a single TV series. The price of film copyrights continues to rise, with prices changing daily, yet producers are becoming increasingly picky. Popular TV dramas do not discuss prices during pre-production but only start selling copyrights just before release to secure the highest price. For video websites, more terrifying is the fact that while expenses increase, revenue does not. Because most video websites have not gained the attention of mainstream audiences, their advertising value is greatly underestimated. Compared to TV advertisements, online video ads can only be considered "cheap goods." Moreover, due to insufficient advertising inventory, the high copyright fees cannot be absorbed, turning Taiwanese and Hong Kong dramas into "financial black holes" for video websites. Purchasing copyrights has become the biggest headache for video executives—spending huge amounts is finding death, while not purchasing is waiting to die. The video industry begins to seek warmth together; Letv and Tudou established a joint venture, Sohu and MSN formed horizontal alliances—all examples of industry cooperation. Behind Sina Video is Sina Weibo, behind iQIYI is Baidu, behind Ku6 is Shanda, and 56.com has ties with Renren. The popularity of micro-movies sees people reminiscing about short videos from the video-sharing era. A type of micro-movie, similar in size to short videos, has begun to gain popularity as the video industry returns to short videos. Recently, Ling Si Communication's Film Center produced the micro-movie "There's a Ghost on Weibo," releasing three works: "Witness," "Private Message," and "@Someone." After their premiere on Weibo, they were immediately launched at the first screening on 56.com. As of now, "Witness" alone has garnered over 600,000 views. This year, more than 30 micro-movies have premiered on 56.com, including works from professional film institutions and studios, as well as personal directorial small-budget masterpieces. Notably, people prefer to watch micro-movies on 56.com, which upholds grassroots spirit. Comparing single movie and cumulative plays and comment numbers of three movies, user engagement on 56.com is significantly higher than on Sohu Video and Ku6. Video sharing websites, characterized by grassroots gathering, love for originality, and sharing, are regaining their unique advantages through micro-movies. Returning to short videos has become a trend. Ku6 withdrew from the copyright war, increasing efforts on short videos and UGC; NetEase and Sina parted ways with Sohu, sticking to their own short video paths. Renren's acquisition of 56.com for $80 million was precisely due to its advantage in the short video field. The video industry sees shifting fortunes, and in the era of copyright burnout, short videos and micro-movies have once again become favorites. Why are micro-movies so popular? Actually, it's the same reason why Weibo became popular. Micro-movies require less investment, so big productions like martial arts, police-thriller, or military themes are unsuitable for micro-movies. Depicting trivialities of daily life and recording the minutiae of people's lives instead easily resonates with netizens. "Playing Big" depicts the process of three post-80s growing up playing games, with very intricate plots comparable to mainstream films. Such topics are hard to find in mainstream films, making them easily relatable to the post-80s generation. Micro-movies, like Weibo, gain attention by documenting everyday life. Both micro-movies and Weibo are short, with more compact plots that avoid the verbosity and redundancy of "big films." Movies have become fast-food culture, not requiring trips to the cinema but allowing online premieres and screenings anytime one desires. The several minutes to tens of minutes long plots make micro-movies akin to fast-food culture, much like Weibo. Micro-movies also have extensive uses, serving as hobbies for directors and photography enthusiasts, as well as tools for marketing and promotion. In my software circle, many companies like to promote products using the "micro-movie" format. For instance, when Kingsoft launched WPS2012, they created a parody time-travel movie; Storm Codec also utilized micro-movies to promote their "Left Eye Key." Recently, Nokia collaborated with Fan Bingbing to shoot a micro-movie for N9. Some worry that micro-movies lack a model, but actually, behind micro-movies is "micro-marketing"—embedded advertising. Embedding clips introducing corporate products and functions, or company logos and images, as long as it's natural, users do not resent it, instead unconsciously remembering the product and company. Its effect far surpasses those hard advertisements that viewers switch channels or close upon seeing. Micro-movies allow companies to invest minimal costs yet produce content more interesting than advertisements, achieving much more effective promotional results.
