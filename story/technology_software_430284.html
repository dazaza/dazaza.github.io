The Girl Is 28 Years Old This Year

After the Spring Festival, you will be 28. On the second day of the New Year, while I was washing dishes in the kitchen, my mother mentioned that she might say: "Liping's child is already three years old, and Liu Min's child is already in kindergarten..." In a panic, I wiped my hands and fled the kitchen.

My father was watching TV in the living room, and both of my younger sisters had been called away by their respective boyfriends. The house was cold and quiet, not at all like the festive atmosphere of the New Year. It was just me facing my parents alone again. At times like this, they either beat around the bush to ask about my recent news or earnestly advise me not to be too picky anymore and settle down with someone. They've worried a lot about my marriage, wanting me to get married as soon as possible, but unfortunately, I am already 28 and have not found a partner yet.

A few years ago, my parents never would have imagined that their clever and likable eldest daughter would end up being an unmarried woman. Since childhood, I have dreamed of a day when I would sit on a high throne like a queen, with a handsome prince kneeling before me, holding roses and pulling at my skirt, saying, "Oh, dear maiden, you are my princess, my queen. Please marry me." Who would have thought that I am almost 28 now, and not only has no one proposed to me, but no one has even held my hand. I am eager to get married but can't find anyone. All I do every day is go on blind dates, so much so that I have almost become a professional at it. Finding a partner has even become my second career.

From the initial excitement and nervousness to the current numbness, I have met people of all kinds - tall, short, fat, thin, handsome, ugly, poor, rich, outgoing, introverted, capable, and honest. But none of them seemed suitable for marriage. Among these people, some liked me, but I didn't like them; others I liked, but they didn't like me; and there were those whom neither of us liked.

I clearly remember my first blind date. After spending three hours in front of the mirror, I followed my mother to the matchmaker's house, my heart pounding and my legs trembling. I walked back and forth on the street in front of the matchmaker's house three times before finally calming down enough to enter. Although I didn't think much of his height of less than 175 cm, I was still extremely nervous. When the matchmaker offered him a cigarette, he said no, and when the matchmaker offered me sunflower seeds, I also said no. This incident happened ten years ago, and I am still going on blind dates. Now, for me, blind dating has become a routine. I can meet someone without combing my hair or washing my face after receiving a call from the matchmaker, then return to work, and only remember in the evening when the matchmaker calls to ask how the meeting went.

People who introduce me always say I am too picky, but I feel wronged. I don't seek wealth or high positions; I just want someone who shares my interests and feels mutual affection. Yet, finding such a person is so difficult. A few years ago, my friends who were also having trouble finding partners eventually found boyfriends through various means and methods. After inviting me to their weddings, they disappeared one by one. Now, I am truly the last single person, making me a real hard-core bachelor. Even if I don't have a boyfriend, I don't even have girlfriends anymore. I used to visit their homes, but seeing their sweet embraces or happy holding of their children's hands made me envious. I yearn for and dream of my own destiny coming quickly, wanting to have my own family and person. However, I wait day after day and year after year, and now I am 28 and still haven't found my destiny, leading me to often doubt whether fate actually exists.

A few days ago, I read an article that deeply moved me. From an economic perspective, feelings and relationships are also issues of interest balancing. Love and marriage, like other human behaviors, seek tangible benefits, undergo rational choices, and conform to the rational analysis of maximizing economic utility. Moreover, life is a wonderful product, and God gives us bodies, beauty, wisdom, and time, all of which are resources. How these resources are allocated should be instinctively considered by women, who should economically and carefully calculate their lives.

I feel that according to this view, I have wasted the resources God gave me and haven't calculated my life well. Why? Because when girls are in their early twenties, they are youthful and beautiful, and it's the perfect time to find a partner. Back then, there were plenty of options. If I had realized earlier that fate isn't practical and had chosen carefully, I could have definitely found a great partner. Now that I understand these beautiful ideals, the best options have already been taken, and time has passed. Seasons have changed, and I am no longer young. Even if I encounter a great option now, would they choose me? Regardless, a 28-year-old girl cannot compare with an 18-year-old girl. Younger generations keep emerging, showcasing their beauty. I may not look old compared to those in their thirties and forties, but within the age group for finding a partner, I am definitely part of the older generation and am not advantageous.

No matter how excellent my inner qualities are, compared to the tender 18-year-olds, I possess more restrained beauty, rhythm beauty, and internal beauty. However, old bulls prefer young grass, and young bulls don't prefer old grass. Those who know me and once admired me are mostly my peers, and most of them are already taken. As for those left for me to meet, how many can immediately recognize my inner greatness and kneel before me holding my skirt, saying, "Ah! Dear princess...?" And if there really is someone like that, would I even consider them?

Thus, I often feel regretful and sorry for myself, thinking that I am like an expensive fashion piece—well-made and high-quality, but unsold due to unreasonable pricing. Now that the style is outdated, I can only sell it at a discount. Sometimes when I go on blind dates, I even feel like a high-end commodity being sold at a low price. Whenever I see peers in couples, I feel left behind and imagine giving up and settling for anyone, but love is not something that can be forced or compromised.

In this way, I waited year after year and hoped day after day, but my love bird never arrived.

A few years ago, in my prime youth, I had some charm, and there were people around me every day. But who would have known that youth and beauty are fleeting, changing in the blink of an eye? My skin is no longer rosy but replaced by spots and wrinkles. Plus, I don't invest much in grooming, so now, though I'm not ugly, I am certainly someone who would be hard to find in a crowd. Though my appearance is no longer attractive, love still feels beautiful to me. Chasing modern love with this unattractive appearance seems inappropriate. Last Spring Festival, my mother warned me: "If you don't find a partner soon, you'll be too old, and it will be harder for girls to marry as they get older." I reassured her then, saying, "Don't worry, next year I won't celebrate the New Year with you." But heaven knows, two plans later, it's now another year, and I am still a single woman, still living with my parents. Last Spring Festival, I secretly vowed that no matter what, I would marry this year, but the year ended, and I achieved nothing—not even finding someone to deeply connect with.

Among my colleagues, there is a girl one year younger than me who is also unmarried. Her conditions are similar to mine, but the difference is that she has had many boyfriends, changing every ten days or half a month. Thus, her colleagues privately nicknamed her "Half-Month Talk." Initially, I disapproved of her behavior, but now I no longer feel that way. Why? Because after my own experiences, I realized that being "Half-Month Talk" is not easy. Not only does it require charm, but also courage and broad-mindedness. If someone called me "Half-Month Talk," I would be furious, but when she heard it, she simply said, "Not Half-Month Talk anymore, now it's Weekly Edition." Hearing her words and tone, one can tell how open-minded and broad-hearted she is. Therefore, despite being of the same age, her mindset is better, and she doesn't feel as anxious or pitiful and miserable as I do. While listening to her jokingly call herself "Weekly Edition," I laughed beside her. However, a recent tongue-twister I heard made me realize that while "Half-Month Talk" might be advanced or not, I am definitely lagging behind. The tongue-twister goes: "In the 2000s, love accelerates. From love to breakup, it takes a week. Monday flirts, Tuesday loves, Wednesday sleeps together, Friday gets bored, Saturday breaks up, Sunday looks for new love." Anything that turns into a tongue-twister must have become widespread.

Given that I couldn't even find someone worth investigating last year and realizing how far behind I am in today's era, I have decided to completely change my mindset and direction, appropriately absorbing a bit of the "Half-Month Talk" spirit—not becoming a "Weekly Edition," but at least having one or two people to talk to. For this, I patiently sat down and carefully analyzed why I am left over. Apart from believing in fate, I discovered the following reasons:

1. Being overly influenced by negative examples from my grandmother and mother, I corrected their mistakes too much. Their approaches to finding partners were too fashionable. My grandmother relied on fortune-tellers to choose signs and destinies, ultimately picking a short-lived man. My mother, following the trend, insisted on finding someone with good social standing, eventually marrying a representative of the poorest farmers, with whom she argued for most of her life. My aunt, being a farmer but pretty, insisted on marrying a worker, enjoying a better life than farmers for a while, but now her family of four is unemployed, struggling to survive. Therefore, my grandmother often laments her bad luck, but I think she resembles the story of the mouse marrying its daughter. When it came to my turn to find a partner, I didn't want to be shortsighted like them. I aspired to establish my worldview and partner-seeking view with historical, developmental, dialectical, and materialistic perspectives. So I set two rules for myself: 1. Avoid overly fashionable people, 2. Avoid overly trendy people. Therefore, during the diploma craze a few years ago, I avoided diploma holders, and during the money worship craze recently, I avoided wealthy people, losing many opportunities in the process.

2. Always looking for someone with whom I instantly connect or feel chemistry with at first sight—or someone who seems familiar, as if seen in a dream or known from a past life—but that's not easy. A face you've never seen, a complete stranger with no mystery, feeling familiar right away is indeed quite challenging.

3. Always wanting someone who treats me like a treasure, someone who loves and spoils me like my parents. If I were seventeen or eighteen, maybe someone would spoil me, but now, nearing thirty, neither my grandma nor uncle would care, let alone others. Clearly, this idea is unrealistic.

I believe that because of these reasons, I have become autumn wheat or winter beans.

Having identified the causes and clarified my thoughts, I am determined to change my mindset, shift my perspective, and abandon my previous irrational, impractical, and aimless methods of finding a partner. Instead, I will use scientific, rational, and economic methods to choose a partner.

According to normal people's views, this might seem a bit too extreme, but I believe it will definitely be more efficient and increase the hit rate. After all, a 28-year-old girl talking about romance and feelings is somewhat impractical.

With goals set and direction determined, this year is crucial. My first plan is: move out, it will definitely be better to leave my parents. First, I will have more freedom to go out and meet people without my parents' eyes following me, and I won't have to worry about the time of my return. Second, I can dress more freely, wearing tank tops or sleeveless shirts without worrying about what my parents might say. Third, I won't have to watch my two younger sisters flaunting their dates daily, which makes me anxious. Fourth, without nagging and urging, I can stop aging mentally, and my parents won't argue about my situation anymore. There were a few times when my mother got upset and blamed my inability to find a partner on my father's indulgence, leading to arguments with the representative of the poor peasants. But whether I find a partner or not has nothing to do with the representative indulging me or not; it's because she can't arrange my marriage or force me to find a partner to marry that she gets angry.

The Spring Festival passed quickly, spring arrived, birds started courting, ants started cohabiting, flies became pregnant, butterflies remarried, and frogs had babies. I found a one-room apartment, ignoring my parents' advice, and decisively moved out. Initially, I did feel peaceful, but soon I felt another kind of sadness—a sense of emptiness and loneliness after work, facing four walls alone, and a desire to talk to someone but having no one to confide in, especially after dusk. Seeing people of all ages either walking proudly hand-in-hand on the street or sneaking off into bushes, I felt even lonelier. Especially seeing teenagers with backpacks sneaking into bushes made me tear up. Others start dating at seventeen or eighteen, while I am twenty-seven or twenty-eight and have never dated. At such moments, I feel that fate is particularly unfair and that I am particularly useless, like the old spinster I know. That woman is over forty now, unable to find a partner because she read too many romantic novels when she was young. Later, her temperament became very peculiar. Men who approached her closely, she accused of ulterior motives and trying to take advantage of her. If she encountered difficulties and a man tried to help her, she scolded him. Consequently, she is almost fifty and still unmarried, avoiding interaction with others. She has become one of the well-known figures in our area. I feel that I am now like her, abnormal and strange. Chopsticks come in pairs, gloves and shoes come in pairs, bottles don't need to pair but must fit with their caps, bowls don't need to pair but must match with spoons. God never lets you exist alone in the world, but I am violating natural laws. It's not that I want to violate them, but God is not taking care of me. Do I willingly let anxiety and loneliness gnaw at me every day, accompanied by solitude and helplessness every night?

Recently, I have been feeling unbearable sorrow, as if the whole world is celebrating except for me, a single person, because Linping also got married. Linping is my colleague and friend, three years older than me, already 31. Due to past emotional trauma, she swore that she wouldn't regret remaining single for life. Linping has always been my comfort and bottom line. Whenever my parents urged or relatives asked, I thought, "Linping is over 30 and not in a rush, why should I be?" But who would have thought that even Linping would get married, and so suddenly. From meeting to understanding took only over a month, and she abandoned me, her ally. At her wedding, dressed in white lace, covered in thick powder and laugh lines, she smiled at me and said, "Come visit me often." I nodded, but inside, I was so choked up that my nose was running. Once a woman marries, she ascends like a crane into the clouds, busy building nests or laying eggs, no longer caring about female colleagues. I've experienced this before. From now on, Sundays will have no destination. I lament inwardly, wishing I could find someone today and marry tomorrow.

Linping's marriage left me deeply saddened, making me feel truly abandoned and left behind by society, even eliminated by people. I, who usually prided myself on being superior, developed inferiority complexes. Father said, "Why are you so depressed and old-looking lately?" Mother said, "Where has your former cleverness gone?" Actually, if it weren't for family and friends constantly reminding me—"You're so old, hurry up and find someone"—or "You're almost 30, if you don't find someone soon, you'll be too old"—I wouldn't feel this way. Personally, I don't think I'm old. I'm still in my twenties, not thirties. Just because I'm not married and I'm 28 with no partner, I'm automatically labeled as an old maid and even held up as an example. My neighbor once scolded her eight-year-old daughter, saying, "If you grow up like Aluxiang and can't find a partner, you'll be an old maid." The child innocently repeated this to my mom, deeply hurting her. During that period, I wanted to stomp my foot and go south. Newspapers report that there are many lifelong bachelors and spinsters in Shanghai and Shenzhen. How do they live? Zhengzhou isn't too remote, so why are people's ideas so backward? From then on, I believed that my pressure was all due to societal expectations. But later, when homosexuality appeared around me and people could accept it calmly, and when I increasingly felt lonely and isolated, I realized that marriage is not only a social phenomenon but also a natural need. I had violated natural laws, making me somewhat peculiar and unconventional. Thinking of the ancient saying, "When a man grows up, he should marry, and when a woman grows up, she should wed," I refocused all my energy on finding a partner.

The TV station hosted a program similar to "Rose Appointment," and I hastily signed up. Unfortunately, the phone I used was broken the days they notified me for the interview. By the time I found out, it was too late. They said, "Next time," but there was never any news afterward. Seeing full-page advertisements for matchmaking services in the newspaper, I became interested. With difficulty, I found someone equally eager to marry, and we boldly ventured incognito to a nearby matchmaking agency called "A Thousand-Year Wait." It was better than expected. Checking the records, I was surprised to find many undergraduates, even doctoral students and returned overseas students. These conditions made me feel inferior, but upon further reflection, I dismissed it. Although marriage has never been a purely personal matter but has always been linked to power, status, lineage, and wealth, openly advertising oneself as having a car, house, company, and substantial income to find a beautiful companion was something I had never encountered before. Looking at it, I couldn't help but feel a bit repelled. What a vast world, full of wonders! This was clearly using cars and houses, rather than oneself, to lure beauties. Isn't this method of finding a partner just like fishing with bait? How boring! Complaining like this, I remembered what others often said about scammers and fake matchmakers, doubting the authenticity of these claims. Despite my doubts, I still paid the fee and registered, clinging to a glimmer of hope. When you're sick, you try anything. They say there are many Li Gui here, but even if there are many ghosts, you still have to enter the mountain!

The young matchmaker gave me a code: 4321, telling me that in the future, I don't need to use my name; this code will be my name. This suited me well. It shows that they understand us, knowing that we are undercover workers, fearing acquaintances and leaks, willing to sacrifice our dignity and money for the sake of this great, timeless cause of love.

Dates came quickly, and I basically attended every one. But soon, I realized that among the people they recommended to me, there were all kinds—good and bad mixed together. After a while, still without results, I became greatly troubled and told them that in the future, I would only initiate meetings, not attend those initiated by others. Thus, life became quiet again.
