Deciphering the genetic code

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 14:17:58

THE HUMAN GENOME PROJECT

For thousands of years, humanity's understanding of cancer has been shrouded in the fog of ignorance. However, the spark of hope has never been extinguished. Since entering modern times, keen observers quickly discovered that several factors could lead to cancer, such as people who frequently smoke having a high risk of lung cancer, those who clean chimneys naked being prone to testicular cancer, and radiation also causing cancer.

In addition, there was another special situation that caught the attention of doctors. In some families, female members had an unusually high incidence of breast cancer, and this persisted for several generations. This situation was at odds with the environmental carcinogenesis theory observed earlier by people, leaving doctors helpless, who could only explain these rare cases as "the curse of the family."

The advancement of known knowledge progressed faster than imagined. After Mendel laid the foundation for genetics, Morgan established the chromosome theory, and Watson and Crick jointly unraveled the mystery of the DNA double helix structure, the spell soon revealed its flaws. People gradually realized that genes were closely related to the occurrence and development of tumors, and tumors could be fully regarded as a genetic disease, i.e., diseases caused by structural and functional abnormalities of related genes.

In Angelina Jolie's case, the culprit gene responsible for her choice to undergo a mastectomy to reduce the risk of cancer was named BRCA1 (an abbreviation for Breast Cancer). As the name suggests, this gene is closely linked to breast cancer. According to statistics, BRCA mutations increase the risk of breast cancer from 12% to 60%, and raise the risk of ovarian cancer from 1.4% to 15-40%.

In the 1980s, American epidemiologist Mary-Claire King analyzed familial breast cancer cases and believed that a gene on chromosome 17 might be the main culprit. Subsequently, in 1994, the American company MYRIAD successfully identified BRCA1 based on this finding. The same year, the company also found the BRCA2 gene on chromosome 13 using samples provided by two Icelandic families with a high incidence of breast cancer.

Modern medical research provided a solid basis for Jolie’s medical decision. Researchers from the Department of Biostatistics and Epidemiology at the University of Pennsylvania School of Medicine led a prospective study across multiple medical centers involving over 2,000 women carrying BRCA1 or BRCA2 mutated genes to assess the impact of breast and ovarian removal surgeries on survival rates of breast and ovarian cancer patients. It was found that during the follow-up period, none of the participants who underwent mastectomies developed breast cancer, while 7% of those who did not undergo surgery developed the disease. Similarly, significant differences were also observed in the analysis of ovarian cancer.

In fact, if the BRCA gene does not have harmful mutations, its true role is to repair damaged DNA and inhibit tumor growth. There are many physical or chemical factors in the environment surrounding human cells that can induce DNA damage. However, evolution has endowed the body with corresponding mechanisms. Under normal circumstances, if the degree of DNA damage is mild, the body will initiate a repair mechanism; if the damage is severe and difficult to repair, it will trigger a self-destruction mechanism. BRCA plays the role of a repairman in this process. Additionally, BRCA can regulate the expression of another important tumor suppressor gene, p53. It is easy to imagine that if such an important gene undergoes inactivation mutations or carries this version congenitally, on one hand, damaged cells cannot be handled appropriately and promptly, and on the other hand, affected p53 expression leads to genomic instability. All these are crucial factors in inducing cancer.

A Treasure Map Like a Celestial Book

From the research journey of BRCA, it is clear that if a gene's function is known and it is closely related to disease, it is no different from a treasure trove. By screening for mutations in the gene and its expression levels, one can determine the carrier's risk of developing the disease, and possibly design targeted drugs or therapies based on this gene to treat symptoms accordingly.

However, piecemeal advances in single-gene research cannot unlock the full potential of genetic treasures. In 1986, Nobel laureate Renato Dulbecco published an article titled "A Turning Point in Cancer Research: Sequencing the Human Genome," urging: "If we want to know more about cancer, we must now pay attention to the genome of the cell and analyze and study the genome as a whole." This statement sounded like a call to arms for the advance into genomics, directly leading to the birth of the Human Genome Project (HGP).

[Renato Dulbecco passed away on February 19, 2012. Picture source: www.style.it]

Driven by the dual engines of unraveling the mysteries of diseases and gaining economic benefits, the scientific community and the business world joined hands. Like the American Westward Movement, they began to cultivate virgin lands of unknown genes, which directly contributed to the early completion of the Human Genome Project in 1999. However, HGP merely transcribed an immense celestial book composed of 3 billion base pairs from human cells, and the meaning and flow of its chapters still need to be explored in the post-genomic era.

Just as BRCA is related to breast cancer, many genes are directly associated with disease risks. Based on this, many people hope to judge carriers' prognoses by testing the status of disease-related genes. This field has rapidly given rise to many innovative companies. Among them, the most famous is "23andMe," founded by Anne Wojcicki, a Yale graduate. Her husband is the renowned co-founder of Google, Sergey Brin. Initially, 23andMe received venture capital from Google. It is rumored that the Brin family carries a high-risk gene for Alzheimer's disease (Brin's mother suffers from this disease), so Brin is particularly interested in the development of genetic testing.

Typically, scientists identify disease-causing genes by comparing the genomes of patients and healthy individuals, a method called Genome-Wide Association Studies (GWAS). If differences between the two can be found, this technical approach is obviously effective. Hundreds of GWAS studies have been completed so far, involving various diseases, with tens of thousands of participants from multiple countries.

Unfortunately, the human genome is extremely complex, and its polymorphism exceeds imagination. Gene variants identified using GWAS methods have only weak associations with corresponding diseases, and their numbers are numerous. This creates a maze-like scenario, making it difficult to find an exit. Although increasing the number of participants can help pinpoint targets more accurately, the exorbitant costs make many scientists question whether continuing down this path is worthwhile, casting a shadow over the bright prospects of genetic disease detection.

Gene Reveals Answers

Using genes to predict diseases has reached a bottleneck, but gene-based targeted therapy has gained momentum in areas like oncology, benefiting patients tangibly. Taking breast cancer as an example, according to its molecular characteristics, it can be divided into three basic subtypes: estrogen receptor positive (ER+), human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 positive (HER2+), and triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC, which refers to ER, HER2, and progesterone receptor all being negative). Once the subtype is identified, treatment can be tailored accordingly.

In the past, HER2+ type breast cancer had a very aggressive progression, and patients often had poor prognosis. However, once scientists understood that the key to this type of breast cancer lies in the excessively high expression level of the HER2 gene, which results in super strong growth signals driving rapid cancer cell division, corresponding symptomatic drugs were quickly developed. Researchers designed a specific monoclonal antibody targeting HER2, which specifically binds to HER2, occupying the original "seat" of the epidermal growth factor. This way, the growth signal decreases rapidly, and the growth rate of cancer cells slows down accordingly.

TNBC type was also quite challenging. For nearly twenty years, the diagnostic and therapeutic methods for this type of breast cancer have not seen significant progress, mainly because researchers have yet to discover the engine driving its growth, thus lacking effective drugs. Generally, only the chemotherapy method of "killing a thousand enemies at the cost of eight hundred soldiers" is used to combat it, and the efficacy lasts for a short time. However, a recent study published in Nature brings hope for overcoming TNBC type breast cancer.

This study is part of the Cancer Genome Atlas project, aiming to gain a deeper understanding of the mutation locations in cancer cells. After analyzing the gene sequences of cancer cells from 825 breast cancer patients, researchers found that this subtype differs significantly from other breast cancers but resembles ovarian cancer more closely. If this result is confirmed to be true, then drugs specifically targeting ovarian cancer can be used to treat TNBC type breast cancer. Besides benefiting breast cancer patients, this discovery also raises new thoughts. Previously, tumors were named according to their primary lesion sites. Some scientists propose that in the future, we may name and classify cancers based on their genetic characteristics, and drugs targeting a certain mutated gene may be effective against all tumors with this mutation.

Gene, a term over a hundred years old, continues to bring fresh insights. And when we have a deeper understanding of its connotations and extensions, the "curse" attached to genes may instead become the key to solving puzzles.
