Should Apple Buy Tesla?

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 17:49:14

Elon Musk co-founded PayPal and then sold the company. So, what about Tesla? A disruptor in the automotive industry whose headquarters is almost becoming a pilgrimage site for tech workers. Of course not, at least not yet. Elon Musk has made this clear in several interviews.

But investment analysts seem to be relentless until they see the day Tesla gets acquired. Last week, Adnaan Ahmad, an analyst from Germany's Blomberg Bank, published an open letter to Apple CEO Tim Cook and board chairman Arthur Levinson, urging Apple to abandon its competition in the smart TV and smartwatch markets and instead focus on the automotive industry.

This analyst frequently writes letters with advice to CEOs in Silicon Valley. However, this suggestion was indeed surprising, even he admitted "this might seem absurd to many" — Apple should acquire Tesla. I found this open letter and summarized his reasons as follows:

1) For Apple to achieve long-term growth, the key is not how many iPhones it can sell now, but whether it can continue to launch innovative products in the future. Adnaan Ahmad believes that Apple should not focus on developing smart TVs and watches. When Apple launched the iPod, the music industry was in a slump. Now, the TV market is thriving and dominated by Samsung; the watch market is currently worth $40 billion, with brands like Longines and Swatch achieving over 60% net profit.

So, does the industry need Apple to disrupt it? Or, the cost of participating and becoming the leader is still relatively high. This shouldn't be the direction Apple should pursue in the future.

2) Therefore, attention should be focused on industries truly awaiting innovation. A promising field is the automotive market. Currently, the automotive industry is in the early stages of transitioning from traditional cars to intelligent and electric vehicles. Considering the $16 trillion market size of the automotive industry, Apple's entry could accelerate the transformation of the automotive industry just as it did with smartphones.

The idea is that Apple's iPhone reinvented the smartphone and led the subsequent smartphone trend. The timing of the iPhone's appearance was during the early transition from feature phones and quasi-smartphones to true smartphones. Apple led and accelerated this process. The automotive industry is currently in this transitional phase, and Apple should buy an innovative car company to once again lead the development of smart cars.

3) Entering the automotive industry could bring significant profits to Apple. Although the iPhone is selling well now, it cannot guarantee that it will always be Apple's most profitable product. Therefore, the next high-profit point needs to be found, which is the automotive industry. Adnaan Ahmad provided data: 1% of the global share of the automotive market could bring Apple at least $16 billion in annual revenue, equivalent to 16% of the TV industry's revenue and 40% of the watch industry's revenue.

4) From the perspective of national interest in the U.S., Apple could revitalize the American automotive industry, adding more powerful leverage in the competition against Asian and European manufacturers. For example, Americans lagged behind Europeans in mobile phones and telecommunications for over 20 years, but Apple led the U.S. to become the leader in this field.

5) If an agreement is reached with Tesla, then Apple would find a new soul figure - Elon Musk. Undoubtedly, Elon Musk's product philosophy, and even his way of speaking, are extremely similar to Steve Jobs. Adnaan Ahmad even told Tim Cook that Elon Musk's experience in the online payment industry could help Apple develop its iBeacon and Passbook businesses.

These are the reasons Adnaan Ahmad presented. Personally, I don't fully agree. I wholeheartedly support Apple entering the automotive industry, but achieving this goal by acquiring Tesla is impractical. My view is: Tesla won't sell, and if it does, it would likely be to a major automobile manufacturer, not Apple, or Google as previously suggested.

Here are my reasons:

1) Starting with Elon Musk himself. He has already stated that Tesla will not be easily sold. As a CEO with a style similar to Steve Jobs, with the company performing well and favored by the federal government, he has no reason to easily hand over control of the company. I believe Musk recognizes Apple's corporate culture and product philosophy, but he may not accept these elements left over from the Steve Jobs era being applied to Tesla electric vehicles.

2) Currently, Daimler and Toyota both have stakes in Tesla. Especially Daimler, whose stake in Tesla could increase in the future. Compared to Apple, Daimler, which has a good cooperative relationship with Tesla (both in capital and technology), is most likely to acquire Tesla. Most importantly, the Mercedes-Benz brand under Daimler shares the same "The Best or Nothing" spirit pursued by Tesla in terms of product philosophy. In terms of car intelligence and electrification, Mercedes-Benz is also leading among all car brands.

3) Apple is a consumer electronics company. The best way for it to marry the automotive industry is not through full vehicle manufacturing but by becoming a solution provider. Products like iPod Out, Siri Eyes Free, and iOS in the Car. Future smart cars will be like today's smartphones, having different operating systems, platforms, and app stores. Therefore, Apple should play to its strengths and focus on automotive software engineering rather than full vehicle manufacturing.

Full vehicle manufacturing is something Apple lacks experience in. Becoming a car manufacturer means Apple has zero brand accumulation in this field. Even if it acquires Tesla, who can guarantee that the company won't be overtaken by awakened traditional manufacturers or another emerging electric vehicle brand in the future?
