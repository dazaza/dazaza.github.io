Health Books: Holding in Anger and Heart Disease

Topic: Prevention and Treatment of Cardiovascular Diseases

Category: Health Encyclopedia Series

Release: 08-24

Besides smoking, high-cholesterol diets, and hypertension that are related to heart disease, a poor personality can also increase the risk of people suffering from heart disease. Recently, Dr. Disel, a psychiatrist at Massachusetts General Hospital in the United States, conducted a study on the correlation between personality and the onset of heart disease among 202 patients. The results showed that individuals with Type A personalities (ambitious, irritable, competitive, tense, and easily angered) do not have a higher incidence of heart disease compared to those with Type B personalities (calm and reserved). On the contrary, those who tend to hold in their anger are more prone to developing heart disease. In a certain sense, it is better to express one's dissatisfaction and anger freely rather than holding it in. However, venting anger often leads to adverse social consequences. The correct and proactive approach is to verbalize your "anger" and calmly discuss it with the person who made you angry or a close friend. By doing so, you can alleviate your frustration and restore psychological balance. (Wu Guolong)

Anger Reduces Heart Pumping Activity

According to the "Medical Weekly," Dr. Segall, a professor of radiology and internal medicine at Stanford University, reported at a meeting of the Society of Nuclear Medicine in Cincinnati, Ohio, that their research results have proven that anger can weaken the heart's pumping activity. Eighteen men with mild heart disease and nine healthy men were subjected to a six-minute cycling exercise test. Then, during individual mental stress tests, they were asked to recount an event that made them very angry. During the experiment, researchers completed cardiac radionuclide scans and electrocardiograms. Among the 18 patients with heart disease, the ejection fraction significantly decreased after anger, while the exercise test slightly increased the heart's pumping activity. Conversely, in the healthy group, no cases of significant decline in heart function occurred during either the anger or exercise stress tests. Additionally, during the anger test, none of the heart disease patients reported chest pain or any abnormal electrophysiological activity in the heart. However, during the exercise stress test, several did indeed exhibit typical symptoms of heart disease, including ST-segment depression. Dr. Segall said that heart disease patients are unaware of the adverse effects of anger on the heart. Therefore, some people experience severe heart attacks without any physical warning signs after being stimulated, and half of the patients even die from a heart attack. (Dake)
