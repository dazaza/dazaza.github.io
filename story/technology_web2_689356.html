An American Dream Shattered by a CBA Scoring Champion

by au63fvecy11 on 2012-03-04 01:28:46

The New York Times published a long-term tracking report on Andrew Emmett, a short-term player of the NBA's Nets. The details of his experience reflect another side of the glamorous NBA.

Editorial Note

420 players, every year 1 to 3 MVPs are born, 15 All-NBA team members, and 24 All-Stars. In front of the world, the NBA presents more of its luxurious side. For years, it has encouraged countless young people from the grassroots who want to change their luck through sports with vivid examples. In fact, stories of turning penalties into gold are not common; "cruelty" is the theme of this league. Apart from the stars, about 60% of the people in the NBA are just on the edge, with short-term contracts, non-guaranteed contracts, semi-guaranteed contracts, and ten-day short-term contracts. "These players' paths are cruel because everyone thinks they are basically worthless," Brad Davis (whose jersey number was retired by the Mavericks) commented after signing a ten-day short-term contract and eventually achieving success. Similar to the "Beijing drifters" who block the doors of film studios every day to snatch extras roles, those on this side of the ocean who are waiting for an NBA opportunity often end up with shattered dreams. Perhaps precisely because of such a brutal background, Americans have invested so much passion in Jeremy Lin's "Cinderella story."

◎ Analysis of NBA Ten-Day Contracts

NBA ten-day contract signing rules: NBA teams can sign ten-day contracts with players after January 10 each year (or three games, whichever is greater). After expiration, they can terminate the contract or sign a second ten-day contract. After that, any renewal must be until the end of the season. For this reason, the NBA pays $700,000 annually to the CBA (NBA Development League) to obtain the right to sign any player at any time. Additionally, during the playoffs, NBA teams must pay the original CBA team $2,000 per person.

Ten-day short-term contract salary: The salary is calculated based on the league's minimum wage, divided by the total number of regular-season days, and then multiplied by ten.

Two weeks ago, on February 15, when Andrew Emmett received a call from the NBA, he was quietly practicing jump shots in an empty gymnasium in Nevada. The training session for the Reno team of the NBA Development League, where Emmett played, had already ended, but the 29-year-old Emmett continued to shoot baskets, still looking for his touch in his favorite spot. This call from the NBA was both a timely blessing and a new challenge for him. The New Jersey Nets extended an olive branch to Emmett, who had been away from the NBA stage for seven years - a ten-day short-term contract.

Not enough time to pack

Ten days, leave a deep impression on the team; ten days, welcome a new life. NBA teams use these short-term contracts to fill their benches, but for the players, it is a psychological test. They only have ten days, and they could be cut after the first day. Even if they endure ten days, what awaits them is just another ten-day contract. After a player completes two ten-day contracts, they are either abandoned or offered a contract until the end of the season. How many players can smoothly play until the end of the season? From last season's NBA data, perhaps only one-third.

For the players involved, this is a dangerous situation. The dazzling All-Star world of Orlando is too far away for them; all they face is cruelty. For him, the good news is that the Nets will play six games in ten days, giving him many potential opportunities to play, and he can earn a salary of $50,000 to $60,000. He didn't have more time to think about whether to accept the contract because, 45 minutes after receiving the call, his flight was about to take off. "Hey! This is a contract from the NBA! I must get on this plane!" Emmett said. He even didn't have time to pack his luggage and had to entrust the Reno team coach to courier his suitcase full of Reno team practice jerseys to the destination.

Emmett is a typical example of someone signed on a short-term contract. He began his basketball career at Texas Tech University. The 6-foot-5-inch (1.95-meter) guard was a star in the NCAA at the time. In 2004, he was selected in the second round of the draft by the Seattle SuperSonics and immediately traded to the Memphis Grizzlies. He only played in eight games before being traded to the Miami Heat, where he had no chance to play. During the summer league, he played for the Philadelphia 76ers, who ultimately released him. Unsuccessful in the NBA, he chose to venture abroad, leaving his footprints in Lithuania, Belgium, France, and China. While playing in the CBA, he set a record of scoring 71 points in a single game.

He could have stayed in China, but he decisively chose to return. Giving up his decent treatment in China, he accepted the meager monthly salary of $3,000 in the Development League, knowing that he had an NBA dream that he would never give up. Finally, in February this year, he received the call from the Nets.

First Enjoyment

On the first day, Emmett disembarked from the "red-eye flight" (a flight that departs at night and arrives in the morning) and went straight to the Nets' practice facility in New Jersey for a joint practice session.

On the second day, he wore a home uniform with the number 14 and appeared in the starting lineup an hour before the Nets' game against the Grizzlies. He happily said, "I'm enjoying my moment." Greeting players and coaches, he seemed to have returned to his high school days. Coach Avery Johnson let him play for 15 minutes, during which he scored four points and blocked Marc Gasol, one of the few highlights of the game for the Nets.

Coach Johnson understands Emmett's situation well, as he himself signed two ten-day contracts with the Houston Rockets in 1992, which led to his championship ring with the Spurs. Johnson frankly admitted, "This will put a lot of pressure on you. You're tense, you can't sleep. When you're on the court, you feel like there are countless eyes watching you, and everything you do is magnified." The NBA commentator for the Grizzlies, Elliot Perry, understood Emmett better since he himself signed four ten-day short-term contracts. After the game, he evaluated Emmett thus: "I always cheer for those who have dreams because I know how it feels to achieve an NBA dream. He performed well in this game, but each game is different for him."

On the third day, Emmett's experience validated what commentator Perry said. He played 11 minutes in the second game and scored three points, only getting 50 seconds of playtime in the crucial fourth quarter. The team lost to the Indiana Pacers, but he wasn't defeated. He self-reflected: "Coaches need players who can make an impact immediately, but obviously, I'm not the type who can perform throughout the entire game."

Breaking Mom's Heart

On the fourth day, the team arrived in Chicago. On this relaxing evening without a game, Emmett didn't go out with his teammates. He rested in the hotel, spending the whole night with two room-service meals. Actually, Emmett was very accustomed to this basketball-only life, with the only difference being that the competition in overseas leagues was far less intense than in the NBA.

After relaxation came intense competition. The game against the Chicago Bulls was particularly important for Emmett. This time, his mom, who lived in Dallas, could see her son's performance on TV. "In my career, I've never felt as hopeful about proving myself as I do today," Emmett hoped before the game, but the process broke his heart and his mom's.

The Nets beat the Bulls without Rose and stopped their losing streak, but none of this had anything to do with Emmett. He had several chances to play, but the time was counted in seconds. After the game, his mom received a text message from Emmett: "Only played for one minute, haha! I know I must be patient, and those who support me must also be patient." His mom firmly believed in Emmett, "He knows where he gets his strength from, and he will definitely succeed. I believe in him!"

The subsequent game against the Milwaukee Bucks was no different for Emmett, with only 4 minutes and 37 seconds of playtime and blank game statistics. Emmett couldn't help but worry, "I feel anxious," Emmett sat in the locker room, looking at his hands, as if still waiting for the next pass, "I desperately hope to play, hope to assist or break into the key (three-second zone), and hope to grab rebounds and start fast breaks. I'm too eager! It drives me crazy."

Hugging Jeremy Lin

Now, across the entire NBA, when mentioning the New York Knicks, only one name stands out—Jeremy Lin. Emmett knew Lin, as they once played together in the Development League. Now things have changed, Lin shines on the court while Emmett cherishes every shot during pre-game warm-ups, foreseeing that his opportunities in this game would be few. Unfortunately, he guessed correctly.

With 1 minute and 15 seconds left in the game, Coach Johnson prepared Emmett to enter, but Emmett took off his sweatshirt ready to play, only for Johnson to change his mind at the last moment. Emmett looked at his sweatshirt, standing on the sidelines in confusion. He finally got playing time—with 12 seconds left in the game...

At the end of the game, he felt hurt, refusing everyone's greetings, walking directly to the locker room. But he gave Lin a hug, explaining, "His situation was once the same as mine, I just wanted to congratulate him." After cleaning up, he found his sister who specially came to watch his game. In front of family, Emmett finally couldn't hold back, "Do you know how I feel now? I'm happy for my teammates, they brought a victory with their tough performances. But I didn't come here to watch basketball; I came to play!"

Under the brutal night sky of New York, he boarded the team bus, sitting alone in the shadows.

The next morning, the Nets had no practice schedule, and he exercised alone in the weight room. Those familiar exercise machines made him revert to the original Emmett. "I know I can't control everything; the pressures have now disappeared. I've gained a lot from these hardships. I keep wanting to prove myself, and now I'm just a little frustrated. I must remind myself to control what I can, and that's my work attitude."

Saying Goodbye Before the All-Star Game

On the ninth day, the Nets faced the Magic at home. All attention was focused on Howard of the Magic, with rumors of trades swirling around involving the Nets. When Emmett stepped onto the court to shoot free throws in the optional fourth quarter, bored Nets fans shouted, "We want Howard!"

As the game against the Magic neared its end, fans began to leave. It seemed like only Emmett was left on the court. He slammed the rim with both hands, as if venting his emotions. Emmett walked to the Magic bench, where Howard embraced him. They were rookies together in 2004, and now Howard casually joked with Emmett.

In the locker room, Emmett thanked his teammates one by one. His practice shirt was taken by the laundry staff, and no one knew where it would end up. A team assistant coach, Matt Rick, thought Emmett would get another ten-day contract, "He absolutely can, you just don't know yet."

Disappointingly, on the evening of February 26, an hour before the All-Star main event started, Emmett learned that he wouldn't receive a new contract. The Nets told him they needed a big man.

Now he had to pack again, this time carrying his hopes back to the Reno team in the Development League. Continue playing basketball, wait for opportunities, for his unfinished dream.
