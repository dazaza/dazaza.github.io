US Media: Alibaba's Double 11 is Comparable to America's Black Friday

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 18:54:39

In the United States, the Thanksgiving and year-end holiday season will trigger the largest weekend shopping spree of the year. In China, a promotional event by Alibaba can achieve this effect. On November 11th every year, millions of Chinese consumers flock to Alibaba's e-commerce websites. The amount they spend on these sites on that day exceeds the total online spending by Americans on Black Friday and Cyber Monday across all U.S. retailers.

According to a report by the Wall Street Journal on November 10th, Alibaba is planning for what could be the largest technology company IPO since Facebook. The company is widely recognized as the leader in China’s rapidly growing e-commerce industry. However, it is difficult to categorize this enterprise, which was founded fourteen years ago by a former English teacher in Hangzhou. Although Alibaba is often referred to as China's Amazon, it is actually more like a combination of eBay's PayPal and Google.

By one measure, Alibaba is the world's largest e-commerce platform: last year, the total transaction volume on its two major shopping sites, Taobao and Tmall, exceeded RMB 1 trillion, surpassing the combined total of Amazon and eBay last year. Alibaba stated that the number of packages from Taobao and Tmall accounts for more than half of the total package volume in China's entire express delivery industry.

Atsushi Watanabe, a consultant at T.U. Business Consulting Co. in Shanghai, said that it is hard to find an internet user in China who has never used Taobao or Tmall; his company helps Japanese merchants use Chinese e-commerce websites.

Taobao is a huge marketplace with 7 million merchants offering over 800 million items online. It resembles a bustling Chinese market where numerous small suppliers sell everything from tiger-patterned leather jackets to origami decorations. Tmall, on the other hand, is more like a traditional shopping mall, gathering stores from various brands. Watanabe noted that buyers visit Taobao to purchase low-cost goods and seek surprises, while they shop on Tmall out of trust in the brands.

The report stated that shopping on Taobao has become part of China's social fabric. Zoe Zhou, 27, who works for a home goods company in Huangjiang, a small town in southern China, said that the Taobao packages delivered daily to her tiny office often spark impromptu fashion shows. A few years ago, her company organized a trip to Thailand, and the five women in the office ordered swimsuits on Taobao.

When the packages arrived, the three men in the office were out, so the women tried on their new swimsuits right there in the office. She added that when she encounters poorly-rated shops online, she tells her friends not to buy anything from those stores.

The shopping festival on November 11th began in 2009 when 27 merchants on Tmall launched discount promotions to boost sales during the slow season between China's autumn holidays and Christmas.

Currently, this event has been called the "11.11 Shopping Carnival," and this date also coincides with China's Singles' Day, a day when young people lament or celebrate being single. Alibaba said they chose this date because it is easy to remember.

According to data disclosed by Alibaba, the total sales on Tmall and Taobao on November 11th last year reached $3.1 billion. In contrast, according to comScore, the total online spending on Cyber Monday (the first Monday after Thanksgiving, one of the strongest days for U.S. online sales) last year was $1.46 billion, setting a record for the highest single-day online spending in the U.S. Online sales on Black Friday totaled $1.04 billion.

Since its establishment in 2003, Taobao has quickly grown into Alibaba's largest subsidiary. Just as app developers depend on Apple or Google's Android operating system, millions of Chinese merchants rely on Taobao.

Taobao does not charge sellers commissions, but the site attracts a massive consumer base, forcing sellers to spend large sums on services such as advertising provided by Taobao to stand out among the many sellers. Their ads appear in search results, making Taobao feel somewhat like Google. Tmall is another version of Taobao with fewer merchants. This site requires sellers to pay a deposit and annual service fees, and charges a commission on each transaction.
