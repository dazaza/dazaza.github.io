How to practice Qigong: Practice according to the time. From Zi hour to Wu hour (Zi, Chou, Yin, Mao, Chen, Si, Wu) is Yang; from Wu hour to Zi hour (Wu, Wei, Shen, You, Xu, Hai) is Yin. Among them:

Yin hour is the Yang within Yang, during this time it's easiest for true energy to naturally accumulate in the Dantian, generating real fire in the Dantian. Shen hour is the Yin within Yin, which is the best time for the most Yin force. If there are no conditions, you can also practice at any time, using intention to distinguish between Yin and Yang.

Additionally, the Yin and Yang hands of this skill are divided into left Yin and right Yang. The energy flow follows the Ren and Du meridians first. I hope those practicing this skill do not fail to distinguish...

To understand how Qigong is practiced, watch the genuine performance in videos. The most impressive is standing on two fingers. This kind of practice requires more observation, while other performances aren't too difficult. It could be learned within a few months and mastered within two or three years...

Note: Wudang Songxi Sect internal skills form a complete system. Neidan Gongfa (Internal Alchemy Skill) is an important part of it. Each martial art (referring to the skill itself) has relative independence, and their exercise purposes and effects differ, but they have a certain dependency on each other and complement each other. According to traditional requirements (considering the effect of exercise), before practicing Neidan Gongfa, one must first go through bone and muscle exercises (including various basic techniques). The purpose is to stretch bones, tendons, joints, and lengthen ligaments, from outside to inside. Otherwise, when practicing static skills, one might experience back pain, leg pain, soft bones, stiff muscles, inability to sit cross-legged, and physical weakness. Then, after going through meridian exercises (Qi collection and return technique), the purpose is to smooth the meridians, gather scattered Qi, and adjust the balance of Qi and blood throughout the body. After that, undergo meridian exercises (such as falling hands, containing potential stakes, etc.)

1. Basic sitting posture (seal formation): ① Loosen clothing, face south, and sit upright on a flat and comfortable cushion, allowing the testicles to hang freely without touching anything. Cross your legs with the soles facing up, placing the heel against the Huiyin acupoint in front. Place the right leg crossed over the left leg (half-lotus position). Ensure that the Kunlun acupoint (on the outer ankle) of the right foot presses on the Sanyinjiao acupoint (three cun above the inner ankle) of the left foot. Women should do the opposite, pressing the Kunlun acupoint of the left foot on the Sanyinjiao acupoint of the right foot, with the left foot on top and the right foot below. Both soles should be flipped upwards as much as possible, and the thighs, calves, and knees should be placed flat, not raised. Note: The Kunlun acupoint is an important point on the Foot Taiyang Bladder Meridian, while the Sanyinjiao acupoint is an important point on the Foot Taiyin Spleen Meridian and also the intersection of the Three Yin Meridians. Therefore, pressing the Kunlun acupoint on the Sanyinjiao acupoint conforms to the principle of Yang acupoints locking Yin acupoints, Yang meridians rushing to Yin meridians, and the connection of Yin and Yang. If sitting in full lotus, first flip the left foot upward and place it on the right thigh, then place the right foot pressed on top of the left foot, with the sole facing up, still ensuring the Kunlun acupoint of the right foot presses on the Sanyinjiao acupoint of the left foot. Women should reverse the left and right feet. ② Lean forward, bow down to the ground, then slightly lean back, repeat three times (the purpose is to loosen the hip bones and straighten the spine), then sit upright with the spine straight, the body slightly leaning forward. ③ Loosen shoulders up and down (move shoulder joints up and down, left and right), making the shoulder bones loose and correct. ④ Place the palm of the right hand (Yin side) on the back of the left hand (Yang side), with the Zhongchong acupoint of the right middle finger pressing on the Laogong acupoint of the left palm. The four fingers (index, middle, ring, little finger) of the right hand are close together and pressed on the left palm. The thumb of the right hand presses on the Hegu acupoint at the base of the left thumb (the left tiger's mouth should be round), then flip the left palm outward. Next, extend the left hand outwards horizontally with the right hand gripping the left hand, palm facing outward, both hands parallel to the shoulders. Without changing the hand shape, retract the hands back. Repeat this extension and retraction three times, the purpose being to loosen the wrist, elbow, and shoulder joints of both hands. Afterwards, keep the hand shape unchanged (right hand Yin side grips left hand Yang side, acupoint positions remain unchanged), place both hands under the abdomen, with the back of the right hand lightly touching the navel, trying to flip the palm outward as much as possible. Women should reverse the grip, with the left hand Yin side gripping the right hand Yang side, acupoint positions remaining the same. Note: The thumb is the end of the Hand Taiyin Lung Meridian, and the Hegu acupoint is an important point on the Hand Yangming Large Intestine Meridian. Forming the seal with the hands also connects Yin and Yang. ⑤ Keep the neck loose and straight, head slightly tilted upward, lift the ear roots, tuck the chin, and connect with pulling the back (straightening the Du Meridian). ⑥ Build the magpie bridge: curl the tongue upward to touch the upper palate, called building the magpie bridge (connecting the Ren and Du Meridians, losing the sense of taste). Plug the ears: mouth slightly closed, not thinking about speech; ears not hearing external sounds but listening internally without music. Lower the eyelids: eyes slightly closed leaving a small gap, gaze inward, heart not following the scenery. Beginners may easily fall asleep if they close their eyes. Adjust the posture: limbs motionless, body like a diamond. Nasal breathing: natural breathing, Qi Gong practice, fine and long breaths, but do not force it or intentionally prolong the breath. The sitting posture (seal formation) for static skills varies among different schools. The sitting posture introduced above is secretly transmitted by our school and has two basic functions: First, through promoting circulation, it channels and forms a loop for essence and energy. Second, it ignites the fire, initiating the Qi mechanism (see the discussion on practice timing below). Therefore, the sitting posture should be kept comfortable, with loose bones, relaxed muscles, and tight meridians.

2. Body Adjustment Method: If one can sit cross-legged as described above and remain still throughout the body, there is naturally no need for adjustment. If the sitting posture feels uncomfortable, the body and limbs can be moved forward, backward, left, right, up, and down to check if the requirements of the described sitting posture have been met. Strive for comfort, then the whole body will be free of distraction.

3. Mind Adjustment Method: The key to initially practicing static skills lies in utmost stillness. Before practicing this skill, arrange all matters of the heart (daily affairs, etc.) and focus solely on practicing. Also, gradually adapt to the surrounding environment so that surrounding noise or sudden sounds do not cause irritation. Warn family members or companions not to disturb during practice and mentally prepare for any unexpected shouts, tastes, smells, flashes, etc., so as not to panic or struggle to cope.
