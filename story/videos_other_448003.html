We all know that increasing customer loyalty and reducing customer churn can greatly enhance business performance. Loyal customers will continuously purchase a company's products and services, are willing to pay higher prices, and promote the company's offerings (creating word-of-mouth marketing). Furthermore, loyal customers also help reduce a company's promotional expenses... According to research by renowned American scholar Reichheld, if a company can decrease its "customer churn rate" by 1%, its profits could double. A well-known international insurance company found through analysis and calculation that a 1% increase in repeat customer rates could result in a 20% rise in annual sales revenue for their staff.

However, due to various reasons, customer loss (referred to as "customer churn" below) is still difficult to completely avoid, and almost all companies face the issue of "customer churn." In response to customer churn, different companies have different approaches, but excellent companies often place great importance on churned customers and are adept at reflecting on their own marketing management from customer churn.

### 1. Valuing "Churned Customers"

Marketing managers (including marketing managers, sales managers, and senior corporate executives) should value the customer churn rate. If the customer churn rate increases, the company's profits will inevitably decline. Even if the company attracts enough new customers to compensate, it still has to expend significant resources to attract these new customers, leading to a decline in overall business efficiency. Therefore, it is essential to understand the real reasons behind customer churn. Only by deeply understanding the true reasons for customer churn can marketing managers identify issues in marketing management and take corrective actions (to prevent other customers from churning), and may even win back already churned customers, establishing a stronger relationship with them.

Marketing managers can obtain a large amount of information from churned customers to improve marketing work. However, due to cultural and psychological factors among others, many marketing managers tend to focus only on positive news and are unwilling to delve into the true reasons for customer churn. As a result, they cannot accurately pinpoint mistakes in their marketing work (in some companies, summarizing lessons learned might even affect a marketing manager’s career prospects).

Through proper analysis, marketing managers can effectively improve the company's marketing management. For example, airlines, aircraft manufacturers, and aviation authorities spare no expense in searching for black boxes after an airplane accident, aiming to find the true cause of the disaster and implement improvements to ensure the safety of subsequent flights. This is one of the main reasons why airlines rarely experience major incidents in complex and risky operating environments.

### 2. Analyze Churn Reasons, Improve Marketing Work

If marketing managers can adopt appropriate methods to analyze the true reasons behind customer churn, they can promptly identify errors and deficiencies in their marketing work and take improvement measures to enhance the value of the company's products and services.

- **What is "Customer Churn"?**

Clearly, customers who no longer buy a company's products/services are churners ("complete churn customers"). But what about those who buy the company's products/services while also purchasing competitors' offerings? What about customers whose relative purchasing volume (or amount) from the company is increasing, but whose share of spending with the company is decreasing (a lower wallet share)? The renowned American company MicroScan considers these to be "partial churn customers."

MicroScan is a company that provides automated microbiology testing equipment for hospital laboratories. In the early 1990s, to further enhance the company's competitiveness and economic benefits, the marketing managers asked sales personnel to understand the real reasons behind customer churn. Since "complete churn customers" are extremely rare in the medical equipment industry (customers usually continue purchasing after-sales services and consumables for a long time after buying medical equipment), the sales personnel immediately reported back to the marketing managers that they couldn't find any churned customers. Clearly, these salespeople had overlooked "partial churn customers." Moreover, the marketing managers discovered that some hospitals' "small labs" were "complete churn customers." Consequently, the marketing managers required sales personnel to conduct in-depth interviews with each "complete churn customer" and a group of "partial churn customers" to understand their true reasons for churning. The survey results came as a shock: customers doubted the reliability of the company's medical equipment and were highly dissatisfied with its after-sales service.
