Nokia Chairman: What can the company do in the next 150 years?

by anonymous on 2013-09-12 12:12:23

Risto Siilasmaa, the current chairman of Nokia and interim CEO

Although Microsoft has recently spent $7.2 billion to acquire Nokia's mobile phone business and a series of patent portfolios, this once-great but now-fading mobile phone company will continue to focus on its mapping and network technologies. Risto Siilasmaa, Nokia's current chairman and interim CEO, is very optimistic about these recent changes in the company and stated: "This is the starting point of Nokia's next 150-year story."

150 years ago, this Finnish company made its fortune through businesses such as papermaking, shoemaking, and tire production, eventually venturing into the mobile phone field that would later make it truly famous. In a recent interview with the media, Siilasmaa specifically expressed his views on the acquisition of Nokia's Devices & Services division by Microsoft and the potential impact this could have on its 32,000 employees.

"After leaving the mobile devices and services sector, Nokia will be completely different, but it will still be a powerful company with solid financial performance and three key pillar businesses: Nokia Solutions and Networks (NSN), Here maps, and Advanced Technologies. Moreover, each of them is a leader in their respective technological innovation fields," said Siilasmaa.

In short, Nokia, after divesting its mobile phone business, will continue to focus on network technology, Here maps, and advanced development technology. Among these, Nokia Solutions and Networks mainly handles the sales of communication equipment and mobile broadband research; Here maps is one of Nokia's long-term plans, which has become the default map provider for Microsoft's Windows Phone, and Nokia hopes this service can further penetrate the in-vehicle navigation market; Advanced Technologies essentially acts as Nokia's future product R&D department.

Over a long period, Nokia has built an extremely strong portfolio of mobile and data patents, hoping to benefit from them in the future.

"Previously, we successfully established a set of patent and technology licensing processes, and the company plans to use this advantage to increase revenue and profits in the future," said Siilasmaa.

In fact, in the current deal between Microsoft and Nokia, approximately $2.17 billion out of the $7.2 billion paid by Microsoft was used to acquire Nokia's patent portfolio. There are concerns that after selling its mobile phone business, Nokia, which owns 30,000 utility patents and 8,500 design patents, might turn into a "patent troll." The market value of these patents is roughly around $6 billion.

Currently, Siilasmaa's main task is to reshape Nokia, which no longer has its mobile phone business, and find a new CEO for the company. After losing its smartphone business, Nokia's Here map technology may be its only remaining link to consumers. Meanwhile, its proud Lumia and Asha brands now belong to Microsoft, so the company cannot guarantee that Microsoft will continue to preserve Nokia's traces within these two brands.

It is certain that Nokia employees may feel sad about the deal struck with Microsoft, but Siilasmaa firmly believes that "the remaining employees of Nokia will once again remind people of Nokia's true value."
