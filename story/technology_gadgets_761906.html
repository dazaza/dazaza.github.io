Windows Phone doomed Nokia

by geekzhang on 2013-05-15 09:55:20

On Tuesday, the American technology news website GigaOm published a commentary article authored by David Meyer. The article highlights Nokia's new flagship WP8 platform device, the Lumia 925, which features a metal casing—a strong response to criticisms that high-end smartphones from Nokia only use plastic casings. In reality, however, there isn't much difference between the Lumia 925 and other high-end Nokia smartphones. Nokia's truly impressive capabilities are still reflected in its low-end phones.Below is the full text of the article:Nokia's new flagship phone has finally been unveiled, just as predicted, with Nokia emphasizing the outstanding camera functionality of the Lumia 925. Compared to the Lumia 928, this phone sees an improvement in the camera. However, the real distinction lies in the aluminum casing of the Lumia 925.In Europe, people don’t like thick phones or plastic casings, hence the continuous criticism of Nokia’s previous flagship smartphone, the Lumia 920. The newly launched Lumia 925 is slim, with an aluminum casing, weighing 139 grams—much lighter than the 185-gram Lumia 920 and the 163-gram Lumia 928.The Nokia Lumia 925 is equipped with an 8.7-megapixel camera, supports wireless charging, features a 4.5-inch AMOLED touch screen with WXGA (1280x768) resolution, powered by a 1.5GHz Qualcomm dual-core processor, and comes with a memory combination of 1GB RAM + 16GB ROM.IHS Screen Digest analyst Ian Fogg evaluates the Lumia 925 thus: “The hardware of the Lumia 925 is super similar to the Lumia 920 from seven months ago, which shows that the limited support for hardware in Windows Phone has hindered Nokia's development.”The reason why the Lumia 925 doesn't have many standout hardware features is because the main feature of this phone is its camera application, pre-installing the photo-sharing app Oggl and Nokia's own Smart Camera software. Although these functions are excellent, they aren't enough to surprise.And look downThe limitations of Windows Phone do not allow Nokia to fully showcase its innovative capabilities, but Nokia is not idle either. In the past few weeks, Nokia has launched several low-end phones, completely breaking free from Microsoft's constraints.A few weeks ago, Nokia launched the Asha 210 with a physical keyboard, priced at $72. Last Thursday, Nokia also launched the Asha 501, which runs an operating system evolved from Symbian S40 and features a touch keyboard, priced under $100.Asha was developed by Nokia after acquiring the Norwegian company Smarterphone in 2012. The new version of the Asha phone has a Fastlane interface. This interface resembles a timeline function, arranging applications used by the user in recent days vertically by date. It is a hybrid of usage records and a task manager.Nokia's practice of calling touch-screen Asha phones "smartphones" has drawn some criticism, with some believing it is a tactic by Nokia to pad its smartphone shipment numbers. There may be some truth to this, but I believe Nokia has the right to call them smartphones. When the Asha 501 was released, Nokia had already released a new software development kit and a new in-app payment tool, encouraging developers to focus on the renewed Asha platform. Currently, Facebook, Foursquare, and Twitter applications for this platform have appeared or are under development, and game manufacturers such as EA and Gameloft have expressed interest in participating.Of course, heavyweight applications are still missing. But, to be honest, the apps we discuss seem to operate better on tablets than on smartphones. Combined with Asha's excellent battery life, Asha plus a tablet seems like an enticing combination.Nokia's Innovation TargetThe future of Windows Phone may lie in its price becoming increasingly lower, possibly competing directly with mid-to-low-end Android smartphones at some stage. Currently, Nokia can only keep the price of Windows Phone smartphones around $200. However, the Asha series of phones has begun to challenge Android phones, even having a certain price advantage.Nokia pays great attention to improving its industrial design for Windows Phone and Asha products to enhance user experience. However, modifications on its own system seem more flexible. Additionally, pricing is an issue; the Lumia 925 is six times the price of the Asha 501. If consumers respond well to the new Asha platform, the Asha 501 might have more market potential than the Lumia 925. However, this can only be said hypothetically, as Nokia's latest financial report shows that Lumia product sales are on an upward trend, while Asha shows signs of decline.The Lumia 925 is indeed excellent—if I were choosing a high-end smartphone, I would certainly consider it seriously. However, what truly stands out are Nokia's low-end phones, where its innovation is more prominently displayed compared to the Lumia series.Asha is the way forward
