Yelp Goes Public: A Capital Market Template Emerges for Business Review Model

by bingo123 on 2012-03-08 17:21:57

The Yelp IPO provides a capital market template for the business review model.

Over the weekend, this American version of Dianping went public on the NYSE, opening at $22.01 per share, raising $157 million in total.

It seems that the capital market is quite receptive to the concept based on reviews. The opening price soared by 46.7% compared to the offering price, closing at $24.58, up $9.58 from the offering price, representing an increase of 63.87%. The stock reached as high as $26 during intraday trading.

According to the prospectus, Yelp currently generates most of its revenue through advertising sales on its website. In 2011, its revenue reached $83.3 million, an increase of 74% year-over-year, with 70% coming from local advertising and the remainder from national brand advertising.

In the past year, the number of first-time users of Yelp increased by 67%, reaching 65.8 million people. The number of user-published reviews increased by 64%, reaching 24.8 million, covering everything from dentists to park restaurants, encompassing all aspects of life.

However, Yelp has been in a loss-making state for three years. Its losses expanded to $16.9 million in 2011, higher than the previous year's $9.7 million. Over 90% of Yelp’s income comes from advertising. In 2011, its local advertising revenue was $40.33 million, and brand advertising revenue was $12.65 million.

Co-founder and current CEO Jeremy Stoppelman talked about the company's "grand vision": making Yelp the "Amazon" of local advertising and information.

But it also faces many challenges. Facebook is eating into Yelp's market share. Additionally, Google acquired a restaurant online review site last year, entering this field.

With Yelp going public, attention naturally turns to Dianping, which also provides consumers with dining, drinking, entertainment, and leisure information.

Dianping was actually founded a year earlier than Yelp and currently holds an undisputed leading position in the industry nationwide. However, the company has also been evaluated by many internet insiders as being very conservative. Although it started early and began making profits three or four years ago, it has not yet gone public.

Dianping CEO Zhang Tao commented on the Yelp IPO, saying that Yelp's advantage is only in the online information part, missing out on the group-buying opportunity and not seizing the LBS elements. Yelp's future growth space will be squeezed by Groupon and Foursquare.

Dianping has not gone public because it is expanding into group-buying and LBS services. In April last year, Dianping announced the completion of its third round of financing, receiving a joint investment of $100 million from Trustbridge Partners, Sequoia Capital, Qiming Venture Partners, and Lightspeed Venture Partners. At this point, Dianping's valuation had already reached $1 billion.

Zhang Tao said that before this round of financing, there was indeed a plan to go public that year. "If we hadn't done group-buying and mobile new platforms, we might have already gone public."

This decision paid off. By the end of last year, Dianping's group-buying service ranked among the top four in the industry (excluding Taobao Juhuasuan); currently, the download rate of Dianping's coupons is twice that of PC terminals, and by the end of this year, mobile traffic from Dianping is expected to exceed that of PC terminals. (Xu Jieyun)
