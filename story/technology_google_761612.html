Why Android Notebooks Are a Great Idea?

by geekzhang on 2013-05-03 21:06:02

Although no manufacturer currently plans to develop an Android-based notebook, Mike Elgan from CultofAndroid has still outlined several reasons why Android notebooks might be worth exploring.

Intel Plans to Support Android Notebooks

Recent reports suggest that Intel plans to support Android-powered laptops in the future, though this may actually be a misinterpretation of the original source. Nonetheless, it raises an interesting question: Is an Android notebook a good idea?

Before answering that question, let's first take a look at the rumors surrounding Intel’s plans to develop Android notebooks. These rumors initially surfaced on CNET, which interviewed Dadi Perimutter, Intel's executive vice president and product director. It seems likely that CNET misinterpreted some of Perimutter's statements during the interview. Perimutter mentioned that there would be a series of sub-$200 (approximately RMB 1240) budget laptops coming to market, and he hoped these devices would run on the Android operating system rather than Microsoft's Windows 8.

He also emphasized that Intel is not a laptop manufacturer or retailer but a chip company, which defines its market position.

CNET may have mistakenly categorized these rumored laptops as similar to Windows 8 devices with detachable keyboard bases, but in reality, such products should not be classified as true notebooks. A real notebook typically features a non-detachable keyboard and battery base. To date, neither Intel nor any other manufacturer has released a true Android-based notebook. However, the concept of an Android notebook could still be a promising one.

Skepticism About Android Notebooks Often Stems From Fixed Mindsets

The current skepticism toward Android notebooks stems mainly from two reasons. First, some believe that Google already has ChromeOS, making an Android notebook unnecessary. However, it's important to clarify that ChromeOS was not designed for traditional laptops but to demonstrate the concept of cloud computing. As a result, ChromeOS suits only a small segment of consumers with specific needs. In contrast, Android offers better compatibility with smartphones, tablets, and other devices, along with a rich selection of apps available through the Play Store.

Second, some argue that the form factor of traditional laptops is not suited for touch operations because users must inevitably hover their hands while touching the screen. Elgan believes this is simply a concession to traditional operational habits. Looking back at the history of digital product development, it's clear that groundbreaking new products often face significant skepticism. For example, when the iPad was introduced, some doubted whether touchscreen operations could replace traditional mice and keyboards. However, the iPad proved to revolutionize user habits. So, why shouldn't we expect that incorporating touch operations into traditional notebook designs could once again change how people use their devices?

Real-World Experience Shows Why Android Notebooks Are Feasible

Elgan owns an iPad, a Nexus 7, and a MacBook Pro. Based on his usage frequency, the MacBook Pro accounts for 40%, the Nexus 7 for 10%, and the iPad for 50%. Moreover, when using the iPad and Nexus 7, Elgan prefers to pair them with a keyboard. He uses an Apple Bluetooth keyboard with his iPad, placing it at an angle similar to a traditional laptop screen and completing tasks via keyboard shortcuts and touch gestures. Elgan notes that he uses the iPad more frequently due to its larger screen compared to the Nexus 7. If he had a Nexus 10 and an iPad mini, he predicts he'd use the Nexus 10 even more.

Elgan believes that if given the choice of an Android tablet, he would definitely opt for one with a keyboard dock. Additionally, if the keyboard dock included extra batteries, it would be even more practical. Based on his personal experience, he feels that an Android keyboard dock could prove very useful.

For most adults, traditional laptops are the most commonly used devices, whereas for younger users, their first computer is increasingly likely to be an Android smartphone. Android smartphones have widespread adoption, and many business professionals handle their daily tasks on Android phones. The addition of a companion keyboard could significantly enhance the productivity of Android devices, suggesting that there is indeed a market for Android notebooks.

Elgan argues that since Android is currently the most widely recognized and popular mobile operating system, Android devices in a notebook form factor will undoubtedly gain popularity. Additionally, Android hardware tends to be relatively affordable, allowing users to purchase multiple types of Android devices to meet different needs in their lives.

In reality, many people use laptops primarily for text editing, such as novelists and online writers, who have little interest in the endless array of applications available on the Play Store. From this perspective, an Android notebook can still handle text-editing functions effectively. Furthermore, for users with other needs, Android apps can currently meet most requirements traditionally fulfilled by PCs.

Elgan hopes that Android will evolve into a multi-platform operating system in the future, not limited to just smartphones and tablets. Android should expand to more platforms, such as smartwatches, smart glasses, ATMs, car dashboards, and even home appliances. Of course, notebook computers would also make a great choice. Therefore, although Intel does not plan to develop Android notebooks, someone should try to explore this possibility.
