by stonepx918 on 2012-03-06 09:46:33

Rare consumption motives include value, norms, habits, identity, emotions, etc. According to Jack Trout's positioning theory, human behavior is always driven by certain motives. Some people refer to consumers' consumption motives as consumer psychological positioning. Correspondingly, consumer psychological positioning includes value psychology, normative psychology, habitual psychology, identity psychology, emotional psychology, etc. Below is a brief introduction.

1. Consumer Value Psychology

According to research by the Shanghai Management Consulting Company: Consumers like a certain product because they believe it will bring them greater value than similar products, meaning it has greater potential value. Potential value depends on the product's potential quality. Potential quality refers not to the quality detected by quality supervision departments but to the quality perceived in the consumer's mind, which is the consumer's subjective evaluation of a brand. The reason Coca-Cola has led Pepsi for over a century is because it claims to be "authentic," "original," and "unique," making consumers believe it has irreplaceable value, which is this potential value. In fact, the success of a brand often does not depend on its real value but rather on its potential value. Potential value has uniqueness, independence, credibility, and importance. Potential value is the brand effect, just like the celebrity effect, which is a concept deeply rooted in the minds of consumers.

2. Consumer Norm Psychology

Norms play a huge role. Norms refer to the sum total of all moral and behavioral rules commonly followed by people. In real life, these norms influence my thoughts, constrain my words and deeds, and affect various aspects of my life. Norms can take many forms, including principles, rationality, obligations, politeness, friendship, loyalty, understanding, and more. In many cases, norms can induce the motive for consumption behavior. According to temporary surveys and research by marketing experts, consumers like a certain brand often to prevent or eliminate an inner conflict that contradicts their norms and price. When consumers decide whether to buy or not buy a certain brand's product, norms are an important influencing factor. In the early 1980s, a global environmental protection craze arose. The German brand "Frog," being the first mass brand to emphasize environmental protection, did not only use environmental protection as a supplement to its value advertising strategy but also proudly placed it at the center of its advertising campaigns. Within three short years, its product sales tripled. This victory was due to its alignment with global environmental awareness, giving consumers a good feeling that aligns with the norms they believe in.

3. Consumer Habit Psychology

Habits are behaviors temporarily formed that are difficult to change within a short period. Different people and different ethnic groups have different habits. For example, southerners in China mainly consume noodles as their staple food; northerners prefer rice; northern Europeans like drinking beer, while southern Europeans prefer red wine; some people love smoking, others love dressing up, etc. Habits are often irresistible and may even have a greater decision-making impact than value psychology. Consumers generally have specific consumption habits formed through long-term consumption behaviors in daily life. For instance, when consumers initially use a certain brand and feel satisfied, they form an understanding of the quality and efficacy of that product and gradually develop a preference for the brand, establishing trust and confidence in using that brand. In most cases, they won't switch to other brands and become loyal customers. For example, some consumers prefer to buy clothes and appliances at large malls and purchase daily necessities and food at supermarkets. Once consumption habits are formed, they generally don't change easily. Brand positioning expresses a philosophical emotional appeal that can stimulate consumer purchasing desires, cultivate consumer consumption habits, and enhance brand loyalty. Due to the subtle influence of habits, people gradually form fixed lifestyles. These lifestyles, accumulated over history, become cultural customs and eventually a cultural foundation. Marketing experts have developed a set of business theories after years of exploration and discussion, fully utilizing this latent cultural foundation to achieve sales goals by leveraging consumer habit psychology. In the early 1990s, Wrigley's gum entered the German market. In the minds of consumers, chewing gum’s anti-cavity function is a unique secondary feature. At the same time, mixed mouthwash gum was also launched. In the minds of consumers, the main function of mixed mouthwash gum is oral hygiene and tooth care, while chewing gum is its secondary function. After a period of market competition, mixed mouthwash gum eventually failed, while Wrigley's gum led with a 90% market share. The reason is simple: consumer habits were at play—most consumers were accustomed to chewing gum primarily for its flavor, then secondarily for its cavity-preventing function.

4. Consumer Identity Psychology

Everyone has a certain identity, and people unconsciously reveal their identity. Especially those who have gained a certain reputation, power, and status, are constantly paying attention to their identity and showing their identity, trying to make their speech and social activities consistent with their identity. The most revealing of a person's identity is clothing, food, housing, transportation, and usage, such as someone wearing high-end branded clothing, riding in a Rolls-Royce car, staying in a five-star luxury hotel. Once this information is conveyed to the outside world, the person's identity naturally becomes apparent. Therefore, marketing experts have summarized a corresponding marketing theory based on this psychological aspect of human nature called identity principle, making the brand an effective weapon for consumers to express their own identity. For companies, developing products that outperform competitors in revealing consumer identity has become an important topic because it directly affects consumer purchasing decisions and thereby impacts product sales.

5. Consumer Emotional Psychology

Emotions are psychological responses to external stimuli, such as liking, admiration, sadness, fear, anger, disgust, etc. Consumers' likes or dislikes for certain products are all natural expressions of consumer emotions. Experienced brand operators have already recognized this and often spare no effort to dig into the potential growth of the brand through advertisements, public relations, and other means, shaking consumer emotions and fully utilizing consumer emotional psychology to enhance the brand.
