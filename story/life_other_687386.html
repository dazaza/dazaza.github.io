The sentence that has not been spoken for twenty-five years

by huiming on 2012-03-02 15:30:02

The Unspoken Words Over 25 Years

Dedication: I dedicate this article to my beloved father!

One,

He is old!

As people of his generation would say: How did he turn from a child to an old man all of a sudden?

He is really old!

Two,

I can't remember when my father stopped beating me. What always lingers in my mind is Mom's old saying, among the three children in our family, I was the one who got beaten the most. When I was little, I was naughty and didn't like studying. Instead, I liked to herd sheep, climb trees, and catch fish in the stone river of our village. Father would always break off a willow branch and chase me around to beat me. Back then, I could run so fast! Until father couldn't catch up with me and threw a stone at me. As I dodged it, I turned around and couldn't see him anymore. Then I collapsed on a straw stack, panting heavily while counting the spots on the birds flying overhead.

"Will you study or not?"

"No, I won't."

Father, holding a broom, shouted loudly, "See what happens when you don't study!" All I saw was the handle of the broom being swung wildly in the air. I didn't cry out loud; I just mechanically followed the strong arm movements of my father as the bound bamboo strips hit me repeatedly.

Thus, I repeated preschool for two years. The first grade started again in the second year.

"It's not that we don't want your kid to go to school, but if the sum of Chinese and Math scores doesn't reach 100 points, we'll have to hold him back," said the old principal with a long beard, pushing father.

"Ah! No, let the kid move on since he's already too old," father replied.

"He can't keep up with the studies, what's the point of moving on? Go back and repeat the grade," the fat education director kept pushing father's hunched back until they were pushed out of the red wooden door that creaked as its paint peeled off.

Three,

There's nothing in the mountains except stones, countless stones.

That year, a bearded man came to the mountain with a stone crusher machine. Father joined a group of villagers and went up the mountain. There were endless stones to carry and crush. Father said carrying stones earned more money than growing corn at home.

In spring, Mom said the soil moisture was good, so we should plant corn. She asked me to take care of the children while she planted the corn. That vast cornfield belonged to us. The dew on the grass hadn't dried yet when Mom started digging the land. As the sky gradually brightened, the figure in the distance became clearer.

"Who's going to dig the land for us, Mom?" I was surprised, throwing the corn seeds behind me.

Mom wiped her sweat and said, "Who else but Dad? He must have finished carrying stones and come back to work the field at night."

"Does he still need to carry stones?"

"Yes, otherwise, what will we eat and drink?"

When dawn broke, father stubbornly handed me the stubborn hoe. "Go, start digging from that end. If you don't study well, you'll end up digging fields." He wiped his sweat, chewed a steamed bun with pickles, and went back up the mountain.

Four,

That year, I was admitted to the county high school. Father unfolded the yellow handkerchief folded several times and generously weighed three pounds of candy. He told everyone that my child had been accepted into Yaoling High School, even into the key class. The sweetness of those three pounds of candy lingered from my mouth to my heart, sweetening the big broadcast hanging high on the old locust tree outside the village.

Winter in the city was much colder than in the village. The snow accumulated thickly on the asphalt road, like a cotton blanket. I wobbled on my bicycle, trying to get home quickly, but stumbled and the chain fell off. My frozen hands couldn't fix it, so I had no choice but to push the bike step by step, thinking about the inevitable beating waiting for me at home. The sharp wind cut through my face like knives. In the pitch-black sky, I could only rely on the headlights of passing cars to see the way ahead.

"Na Wa? Why did the chain fall?" Father immediately took the bike from my hands. His cracked hands roughly brushed against my wool gloves. The raised calluses proudly stood out in the wind.

"I'll push slowly behind you, hurry back and warm up."

"You wear the gloves, Dad," I timidly shoved the gloves toward him.

"Just put them on and go back, don't argue!"

No one could argue with the stubborn father. The snow was really heavy, swirling wildly, and even half of the blind Grandma San's mud wall collapsed, leaving a pile of dirt along the way. That was the first time father picked me up from school. Mother said that when it got dark and I didn't return, father couldn't sit still and went to wait at the village entrance. After waiting for a while and not seeing me, he guessed I might be on the way and went alone to look for me along the road to the school. When we got home, his nearly ten-year-old boots were soaked. Mother threw them into the stove ashes and roasted them for two nights before they dried.

Five,

When Dongwa went to school that year, father was working as a fruit orchard guard on the plateau. The pink peach blossoms there were truly beautiful, and the purple-black grapes hung in bunches. Mother made spicy buns, which I was supposed to bring to father during my school break. It was his favorite food. The road to the plateau was very long, winding over two mountain peaks before I could see the red wooden gate. When I arrived, father and a few young men were weighing grapes for the traders. I secretly brought the buns hidden in the cloth bag to the pit where father lived.

"Na Wa, give a bun to Jun Wa and tell him to eat it," father called out loudly.

I took one out, but soon the group of young men were grabbing them one by one. I got angry and shouted, "They're all gone, there's none left for my dad!" I snatched the cloth bag and hid back in the pit amidst their laughter. During dinner, I gave the remaining two buns to father and insisted he finish them in the pit before going out to eat noodles.

When the fruits were fully ripe, father would sneak home late at night to bring us a bag full of apples, pears, and grapes. Those apples were so fragrant and sweet, and even now, when I think about them, my mouth fills with sweetness. At dawn, father, hunched over, took a steamed bun and went back to the orchard.

It was the largest orchard in our village, and occasionally, someone would steal fruit from the plateau. That night, father was on guard duty. Just before dawn, the apple field behind him rustled. The sound in the quiet night was like the wind rising. Father jumped up instinctively and shouted, "Who's doing what over there?" A flurry of footsteps rushed eastward. Father instinctively grabbed a stick and chased after them. Just as he was about to catch up, he stepped into a hole dug a few days ago to bury fallen fruit and got stuck. The next day, father was sent home, and even after rubbing his feet with red flower oil all night, they were still swollen and unable to touch the ground. Supporting father, we limped together to Blind Grandma Ba's house to have her massage his feet.

Grandma Ba had been massaging people's arms and legs for decades in the village. Now that she was older, she found it hard to do so. I shouted loudly in her ear for a while before she realized it was my father's sprained foot. While muttering my father's name, she massaged his foot. "Kid, endure it," she probably noticed me standing dumbfounded nearby, "Na Wa, go hug your father." I stubbornly clung to father's dark, tanned arm, unwilling to embrace him. With a thud, father almost fell off the chair he was sitting on. I suddenly hugged him tightly. At that moment, I was so afraid, fearing he would disappear like Sun Wukong in an instant. Father's pale face showed pain, and his tightly closed eyes slowly opened. "Alright, alright! Go home and apply some red flower oil, it'll be fine soon."

Six,

Not until that year did I realize how old father had become.

He was really old! Just like the grapevine in the yard, I didn't know when it had grown as thick as a fist, whereas it used to be such a thin branch.

That year, I was in my sophomore year of college, rushing home for the New Year's holiday. On New Year's Eve, the whole family sat around the warm brick bed as usual, cracking sunflower seeds and watching the Spring Festival Gala. Father sat right across from me, very close. My younger brother tossed a tangerine to father. Saying his teeth were sensitive, father passed it to me. In that instant, I noticed the white hair on both sides of his temples. That ash-gray whiteness stung my eyes, making my throat tighten so much that I couldn't swallow the tangerine in my mouth.

He was really old. Every time it rained or was cloudy, his lower back and shoulders ached. His teeth had fallen out, and sometimes he couldn't even eat meat. He often said he liked fatty pork because lean meat got stuck in his teeth.

Seven,

Father was one of the rare good cooks in the village. Regardless of weddings or funerals, villagers always came to our house asking father to cook. Initially, others charged five yuan per table, but father said, "These are all neighbors, we may need each other's help in the future," so he charged only three yuan per table. Later, others raised the price to ten yuan per table, but father insisted on five. Tiger Uncle, the chef from the front yard, came to our house and smoked cigarette after cigarette, persuading father, "You have three kids in school, can't you ask for more money? If you don't, I can't continue doing this either." Father thought deeply for a long time and said, "Then let's raise it to eight yuan. That's enough, it's fine. These are all neighbors."

In recent years, working away from home, I've gotten used to fast food but still crave the dishes cooked by father. The sour and spicy cabbage, fragrant and spicy, with a tangy kick, makes my mouth water just looking at it. The "ants climbing a tree" dish has finely chopped meat sprinkled over the vermicelli, perfectly matching its name. Green scallions and red radish flowers, paired with the aroma of soy sauce, stimulate the appetite. Even after eating through all the famous Muslim streets in Xi'an on weekends, I couldn't find the taste of father's cooking. That was father's flavor, the taste of love.

Eight,

Recently, when I returned to my hometown, I organized some old things at home. Among a pile of discarded old baskets, I found toys from my childhood. It was a wooden wheelbarrow, with two thick wooden sticks on each side, a single wheel underneath, and three bars on top. Back then, my younger brother cried for a toy, and father stayed up all night to make this cart for us. Now, the wheel is covered in dust and dirt, the rods are covered in spider webs, and it cannot be pushed anymore. As children, my siblings and I used that cart to play games, move sand, and push each other around, accompanied by laughter throughout our youthful years.

At the bottom of the basket, there was also a wooden gun. The trigger had a red ribbon tied to it, but the dust and dirt had obscured the red color. I remembered fighting countless times with my younger brother over this gun, but now it lay quietly there, buried in a pile of abandoned dirt, unable to function anymore.

Father said I was the one who got beaten the most because I was the eldest, and the eldest should set a good example and study hard.

Father said he had no education in his life, and his greatest wish was to see all three of us siblings go to university.

Father said, if we have time, we should burn more paper money at grandfather's grave and plant two cypress trees there.

Father said, life away from home is different from being at home. Neither mom nor I can follow you, so you must eat well and drink well.

Father said, be careful when you're out and don't work overtime frequently. Don't call home often since phone calls are expensive.

Father said...

Nine,

He is old!

As people of his generation would say: How did he turn from a child to an old man all of a sudden?

For 25 years, the words I never dared to speak, I have kept inside for 25 years.

I love you, father!

Here, I want to shout these words loudly to my father, a simple and honest farmer.

Without you, there wouldn't be the three of us today.

Your hunched back has worked tirelessly for us three sisters, your painful waist has borne the burden for us three, your white hair has aged prematurely for us three, and you...

I love you, father!
