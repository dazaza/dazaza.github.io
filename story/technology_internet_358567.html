Section One: Basic Requirements for the Design and Implementation of Kindergarten Health Education Activities

I. Basic Ideas for Designing Kindergarten Health Education Activities

Currently, integrated educational activities are popular in kindergartens. To better integrate education across different domains, it is essential to first grasp the characteristics and rules of each domain. In the field of health education, we can explore how to create educational activities centered around children's healthy growth.

When designing health education activities for children, the following aspects are generally considered:

(1) Regarding Activity Goals

Specific activity goals refine the overall health education goals and age-specific goals. The descriptions should be concise, clear, accurate, specific, and operable. The number of goal items should not be excessive, usually two to three.

(2) Regarding Activity Preparation

A successful health education activity requires teachers to prepare in multiple aspects, including material conditions and environment setup, as well as children’s knowledge, experience, and psychological preparation.

(3) Regarding the Activity Process

The process typically includes introducing the activity, guiding children to participate in thinking, and leading them to summarize. Teachers should clarify what aspects they hope children will develop, what problems need solving, how to inspire children's thoughts, how to engage them in the activity, when to ask what questions, how to use the best educational methods, and how to let children summarize the activity so that their existing experiences are enhanced.

During the activity, various literary and artistic carriers can be used to enhance the fun and improve efficiency. Common carriers include nursery rhymes, children's poetry, poetic dramas, performance singing, fairy tales, stories, etc.

(4) Regarding Activity Extension

Good educational activities do not stop at a specific one-time event but are a long-term, continuous process, especially for cultivating abilities and habits. Activity extension is indispensable. Methods for extending activities can include cooperation between home and school, integration across domains, environmental creation, and corner activities. For example, if a teacher spends 15 minutes teaching children the correct way to brush their teeth, after the activity, the teacher should contact parents and request them to supervise their children to brush correctly at home, helping them consolidate the right method and develop good brushing habits.

III. Issues to Note During Activities

(1) All activities organized by teachers should involve as much hands-on participation from children as possible.

Research shows that if activities only stay on the level of "teachers talking, children listening; teachers demonstrating, children watching," they have little impact on children's development. Only when children participate actively can they understand the educational content and achieve optimal personal development.

(2) Children may not perform as well as adults when participating, but understanding and patience should be given.

Children have lower levels of physical and mental development, so mistakes are inevitable when they participate. We should provide understanding, give opportunities, exercise children, and help them develop better.

(3) The teacher's questioning plays a crucial role in guiding.

If the teacher's questions are unclear, too difficult, or too easy, they will affect children's enthusiasm for participating in activities. Teachers need to understand children's developmental levels and ask appropriate questions. Teachers' questions should encourage divergent thinking. Diverse answers allow more children to have the opportunity to respond, exercising their abilities.

(4) Avoid using adult language.

Adult language prevents effective communication between teachers and children, affecting educational outcomes. For example, a teacher asked, "There is a snail feeling bored, and there is only itself at home. Can you think of ways to make the snail happy?" A child replied, "It is too hot." In this case, the child did not understand the word "bored," leading to an irrelevant answer and affecting learning.

Section Two: Specific Design and Implementation of Kindergarten Health Education Activities

I. Design and Implementation of Physical Care and Self-Care Education Activities

Preschool children's physical care and self-care education mainly focuses on cultivating the ability to scientifically recognize, use, maintain, and exercise eyes, teeth, ears, nose, and skin. It also promotes hygiene habits, basic life skills such as eating, dressing, sleeping, washing, and social behavioral norms and interaction abilities, promoting the development of sensory organs and enhancing their functions. It is an important component of comprehensive quality education and directly relates to preschool children's quality of life and physical and mental development.

(1) Content of Physical Care and Self-Care Education

1. Eye Care Education Content

(1) Eye knowledge: shape, structure, function, etc.

(2) Good eye-use and maintenance habits, such as doing eye exercises, eating foods beneficial to eyesight, etc.

(3) Letting children understand vision testing methods and their own vision status.

2. Oral Care Education Content

(1) Oral knowledge: shapes, numbers, structures, functions of oral organs, tooth replacement knowledge for older classes.

(2) Oral care knowledge: mainly regarding tooth care, such as good brushing habits, correct brushing knowledge, no thumb sucking, no putting random things in the mouth, etc.

(3) Knowledge about cavities: identifying, preventing, and treating cavities.

(4) Nutrition knowledge: for example, soy products, peanuts, etc., can promote better tooth development.

3. Ear Care Education Content

(1) Ear knowledge: shape, structure, function, etc.

(2) Ear hygiene habits: frequently cleaning the ear shell, not digging the ear, etc.

(3) Hearing test methods: through games, maintaining and exercising hearing methods, etc.

(4) An ear care game example provided by the teacher.

4. Skin Care Education Content

(1) Skin knowledge: construction, function, etc.

(2) Cleaning and maintaining skin methods.

(3) Nutritional knowledge beneficial to skin health.

5. Nose Care Education Content

(1) Nose knowledge: shape, structure, function, etc.

(2) Maintaining, cleaning, and nose care methods: not picking the nose, blowing the nose one nostril at a time, etc.

(3) Nutritional knowledge beneficial to nasal health.

6. Eating

(1) Nutritional knowledge of common foods, nutritional awareness.

(2) Eating skills: using and storing utensils, reasonable chewing methods.

(3) Eating hygiene habits: chewing slowly, eating quietly and attentively, not being picky eaters, not overeating, not leaving food, keeping the dining table clean, cherishing food, not engaging in vigorous activities before and after meals, washing hands before meals, wiping the mouth after meals, etc.

(4) Behavioral norms when eating with others.

(5) Aesthetic awareness of eating.

7. Dressing

(1) Dressing knowledge and motives such as protecting against cold and sun, covering the body, and aesthetics.

(2) Skills such as wearing, removing, folding, and placing clothes and shoes, independent dressing ability and habits.

(3) Keeping clothing clean and cherishing clothing habits.

(4) Dressing aesthetic awareness.

8. Sleeping

(1) Reasonable sleeping posture.

(2) Bed-making and bed-folding skills, independent bed-making and bed-folding, keeping the bed tidy abilities and habits.

(3) Good sleep hygiene habits: washing face and feet before bed, independently and quietly going to sleep, not playing with toys, not sleeping with head covered; placing clothes and shoes in a fixed place before bed.

(2) Relevant Games for Physical Care and Self-Care Education

1. Games to Develop Sensory and Finger Functions. These games mainly use one sensory function to identify objects to develop that sensory function.

(1) Hearing Games: Using closed-eye listening to identify the nature, direction, distance, and people to develop hearing and memory. Such as "Closed-Eye Find Friend" and "Who Came to My House."

(2) Eye Care Games: Including developing visual acuity, adjusting focus, recognizing colors, shapes, volume, area, etc. Such as "Kitten Practicing Eyes" and "Ophthalmology Department."

(3) Skin Care Games: Such as patting and touching the skin, taking objects from a bag, closed-eye ball playing, etc.

(4) Finger Function Exercises: Such as rope flipping, paper folding, throwing and catching packages, etc.

2. Self-Care Games. Currently, two commonly used types of games exist:

(1) Performance and Role-Playing Games. Such as "Washing the Cat's Face," "Teaching the Doll to Brush Teeth," "Dressing the Doll," "Little Rabbit's Nursery," etc. These games use anthropomorphic techniques to reinforce life skills and enhance self-care awareness.

(2) Competitive Games. These games mainly reinforce life skills through competitions and cultivate independence and competitive spirit. Common ones include dressing, shoe-wearing, bed-making, chopstick usage, handkerchief washing contests, etc.

(3) Examples of Physical Care and Self-Care Education Activities

Activity One: Learning to Use the Toilet (Small Class)

[Activity Background] The children in this class are unfamiliar with toilet facilities, showing fear and timidity when using the toilet. Incidents of holding urine, wetting pants, and dirtying the toilet occur frequently, with some children unwilling to defecate in kindergarten. This has affected the children's physical health and adaptation to the kindergarten environment. To help the children overcome these psychological barriers and improve their toilet capabilities, this activity is organized.

[Overall Goals]

1. Understand the importance of timely bowel movements, knowing and learning (male/female) urination methods and post-defecation wiping methods.

2. Respect adult labor, keep the toilet floor clean, and avoid dirtying clothes.

3. Cultivate confidence, bravery, mutual assistance, and cooperative qualities.

4. Cultivate observation, imitation, and evaluation abilities.

Activity Design One: I Know When to Go to the Toilet

[Activity Goals]

1. Understand the importance of regular bowel movements. Know to go to the bathroom voluntarily when prompted by adults or when needing to urinate or defecate, avoiding soiling clothes.

2. Cultivate brave and confident qualities.

Preparation: Video tapes; audio-visual equipment; numerous reward symbols; prior observations and statistics by the teacher on the children's bowel movement situations in the kindergarten, and communication with parents to understand relevant situations.

[Activity Process]

1. Game: "Brave Baby Finds Friends"

Show the puppet "Brave Baby" and ask the children to help it find friends. Share what they consider brave actions, allowing "Brave Baby" to make judgments and wear the symbols.

2. Play video clips, finding "Brave Baby's" friends in the scenes (protecting the recorded children's portraits). TV screens sequentially show the following:

(1) Teacher prompts the children to use the toilet, but they don't hear;

(2) Child playing and wetting their pants;

(3) Afraid to say they need to urinate and crying;

(4) Informing the teacher when needing to urinate or defecate;

(5) Actively going to the toilet when needing to urinate or defecate;

(6) Afraid to defecate in kindergarten.

3. Discuss which children in the scene are doing the right thing and who is timid.

4. Teacher proposes improvement requirements based on the class situation.

[Activity Extension]

Daily Life: Strengthen toilet capability training in daily life, paying special attention to individual differences.

[Home-School Cooperation] Ask parents to cooperate with the teacher's work, providing timely feedback on the child's behavior at home, and helping those with poor abilities.

Activity Design Two: I've Learned to Urinate

[Activity Goals]

1. Know your gender and urination methods, avoid urinating outside the toilet, and keep the toilet clean and hygienic.

2. Understand the importance of timely and regular urination, avoiding soiling clothes.

3. Cultivate observation and mutual help, confident and brave qualities.

[Activity Preparation] Video tapes, audio-visual equipment; several dolls; the toilet cleaned beforehand by the teacher.

[Activity Process]

1. Lead the children to visit the toilet, understand its purpose. Know where boys and girls should urinate and how to use the flush system. Guide the children to observe the toilet floor and fixtures, smell the air, and remind them to respect adult labor.

2. Return to the activity room, separate boys and girls, boys play games led by another teacher.

(1) Teacher asks girls: "What is the toilet used for?" "How do girls urinate?" Invite collective discussion, summarizing the urination procedure. Watch the video, teacher highlights key points, children discuss points to note.

(2) Boys' activity process is the same as above.

3. Game: Teaching the doll to urinate

Children pair up, observing and evaluating each other's teaching of the doll's urination actions for correctness.

4. Select "Confident Dolls." Teacher proposes selection criteria, asking children to observe others' toilet actions carefully, ensuring they do theirs properly.

[Activity Extension]

Daily Life: Consolidate toilet routines in all parts of the day, strengthening guidance for individual children.

[Home-School Cooperation] Same as Activity Design One.
