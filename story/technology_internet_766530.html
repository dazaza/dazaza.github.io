What threatens Android and iOS is the cloud phone

by anonymous on 2013-09-18 21:44:38

On September 15, it was reported that in 2005 Nokia dominated the mobile phone industry with no apparent signs of decline. Today, Nokia has sold its mobile phone business, and it seems unlikely to reclaim its former dominance.

However, Professor Ronald Klingebiel from Warwick Business School in the U.S. believes that neither Apple nor Google is safe. Cloud-based smartphones are under development and they will have several advantages:

1. A vast number of apps. Apps will be developed for cloud computing, not just for mobile phones. Just as PC cloud computing apps can run on any PC, including Windows PCs, Macs, or Linux devices.

2. Inexpensive phones. When apps run in the cloud, phones no longer need fast processors or large amounts of memory, which significantly reduces their manufacturing costs.

Below is an excerpt from an interview with Klingebiel:

Q: There are many alternative operating systems. Which one do you think has the best chance of success? Why?

A: All four major operating systems have their own methods and can enable app interoperability. In a sense, all four systems have the potential to succeed. However, unlike Apple, which generates huge profits through vertical integration of phone-OS-app store, these companies may not make as much money.

Each new entrant needs to offer something different. Tizen could benefit from Samsung's full commitment, while Firefox targets the low-end market. Ubuntu leverages the Linux PC community to unify mobile phones with computing devices. Sailfish targets enthusiasts who appreciate high-quality solutions from Nokia and MeeGo.

These operating systems will likely gain significant market share in the near future.

Q: Which segment of the existing smartphone market poses a threat to both high-end and low-end feature phones? Why?

A: Smartphone operating systems. Soon, smartphones will be connected to smart cloud computing. For example, a phone’s apps will only connect to programs in the cloud, eliminating the need for powerful processors. If this becomes the standard for connecting phone apps, it will impact every aspect of the current smartphone market.

Q: When will cloud-based phones start to show their influence?

A: Development is still in its early stages, and success is not guaranteed. However, it could take off quickly. If a large company invests in a new operating system, it could stimulate the entire industry. Mobile carriers also want to change the status quo. Large app developers (such as Facebook) might develop only one program, a standard and interoperable program, which would force Apple and Google to open up their app stores with no choice.
