National Daily: Slow down the establishment of multi-level capital market to resolve the dilemma of small and medium-sized enterprises

by abcfofas on 2012-02-27 15:29:05

Capital markets play a fundamental role in the economic transformation of developed countries and in the creation of strategic emerging industries.

The global financial crisis that occurred in 2008 and the domestic market turmoil that has continued until now has profoundly affected the global industrial structure that was gradually formed after World War II. Countries around the world are increasing their efforts to develop strategic emerging industries. In a sense, for the first time, we are on the same starting line as developed countries. But what do we rely on to compete? In the past 30 years, the four most important strategic emerging industries globally—mobile scaffolding [http://www.itsen.net], personal computers, telecommunications, internet, and biopharmaceuticals—all were created and promoted through capital markets. That is to say, to a large extent, the key to competition in the future among strategic emerging industries lies in which country's capital market can operate healthily and effectively play its role in creating and promoting the growth of new emerging industries.

Europe dominated globally for a long time after the Industrial Revolution, with its core competitiveness coming from its top-tier scientific research and development and its developed capital markets. The subsequent industrial rise of the United States was accompanied by the prosperity of modern enterprises, excellent universities, and Wall Street. After 30 years of rapid economic development post-war, both the U.S. and Europe fell into [economic stagflation] in the 1970s. However, subsequently, the U.S., through the effective combination of capital markets and high technology, led it out of the doldrums in the 1980s, completing another 30 years of growth. Today, this mechanism of combination continues to function. On the other hand, the European economy has never been able to escape from the low-growth predicament [Guangzhou Car Rental Network http://www.syzc020.com]. With the recent impact of globalization, the lack of a market-oriented mechanism for the creation of emerging industries has caused Europe's competitiveness in the global economy to gradually wane. Similarly, Japan's economy, which experienced 30 years of high-speed growth post-war, encountered a crisis in 1989 and has not been able to emerge from the trough for over 20 years. In 2004, when Japanese Finance Minister Yoji Yamamoto visited China, he said that one major defect of Japan's economy was that its capital market was relatively underdeveloped. The success or failure of the economic transformations of the three major economies—the U.S., Europe, and Japan—demonstrates that an effective capital market and a market-oriented resource allocation system have a decisive influence on economic transformation.

Capital markets can provide significant support to small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) and technological innovation.

SMEs, especially technologically innovative enterprises, have characteristics such as being at an early stage of development, being small in scale, weak in strength, and high in risk. However, historical experience shows that SMEs also incubate new business models or emerging industries and will become a leading force in guiding economic transformation.

Since SMEs are often in a phase of rapid growth, they find it difficult to meet their funding needs through self-accumulation alone, making external financing an inevitable choice. Due to their high uncertainty and asset-light nature, SMEs find it hard to obtain bank credit funds. Capital markets (including stocks, bonds, venture capital, private equity funds, etc.) provide a mechanism where financing parties and investors share risks and benefits, becoming a crucial platform for promoting the growth of SMEs and technologically innovative enterprises. The success of Silicon Valley in the U.S. is precisely a typical example of the combination of the technology industry and capital markets, demonstrating strong institutional advantages.

Firstly, capital markets provide relay-style financial support to SMEs and technologically innovative enterprises, allowing truly competitive companies to ultimately succeed. Secondly, the institutional arrangements of capital markets help improve the management structure of enterprises, motivate enterprise management and employees, especially by optimizing the enterprise incentive mechanism, promoting steady and rapid enterprise growth. Thirdly, capital markets also provide SMEs with various social resources, including merger and acquisition platforms, investment banking professional services, market signal guidance, etc., enabling them to quickly form and expand their competitive advantages.

It is precisely these series of institutional advantages of capital markets that allow numerous SMEs and technologically innovative enterprises that combine with them to achieve rapid growth. In 1971, when the NASDAQ (Weibo) market was initially established, most of the companies listed on this market were little-known small enterprises. 24 years later, in 1995, among the top five largest market capitalization listed companies in the U.S., four were on the NASDAQ market, including Microsoft (Weibo) and Apple. A topic of heated public discussion recently is, "Why doesn't China have a Steve Jobs?" Jobs was undoubtedly the promoter and guide of Apple's technological innovation. Apple was founded in 1976 and went public in 1980, and throughout its process of acquiring other companies and growing stronger, it could not have done so without the capital markets platform.

In fact, in recent years, with the launch of the SME board and the Growth Enterprise Market (GEM), as well as the rapid development of the venture capital and private equity fund industry, capital markets have played an increasingly important role in promoting the development of our country’s SMEs and technologically innovative enterprises. We have every reason to believe that the combination of innovative enterprises spread across the country and capital markets will nurture the future Apples and Jobs of China.

Accelerating the construction of multi-level capital markets to resolve the development dilemmas of SMEs and technology-based companies

In recent years, the role of SMEs in our country's socio-economic development has become increasingly important, accounting for 80% of the total employment population, but the financing situation is not optimistic. First, the threshold for bank loans is high, with large costs, making it difficult for SMEs, which are already at a competitive disadvantage and operate on thin margins, to develop. Second, due to the unstable and non-standard nature of technology-based innovative enterprises, their risk characteristics determine that banks are not suitable to provide loans. Third, SMEs relying on official financing platforms mainly based on credit will bear even higher costs. Fourth, in the indirect financing system, the listing threshold for SMEs remains high, with intense competition; for example, only in Zhongguancun (5.83, -0.05, -0.85%), there are more than 1,000 companies in the science and technology park that meet the GEM listing conditions. Meanwhile, investments by our country's venture capital institutions are generally concentrated in the later stages of enterprises, making it even harder for early-stage innovative enterprises to move forward.

In April 2011, some Wenzhou SMEs went bankrupt due to broken capital chains. On the one hand, a large number of SMEs lack financial support, while on the other hand, a large amount of private funds flow into real estate, coal mines, precious metals, green beans, cotton, and other markets, even becoming domestic hot money and speculating in foreign real estate markets. Spontaneous speculation by social idle funds brings instability factors to economic development. The "Wenzhou phenomenon" indicates that if the development of financial markets cannot keep up, abundant funds not only fail to assist the development of the real economy but can also bring about social problems.

From the structure of multi-level capital markets, in the U.S. stock market, the New York Stock Exchange has 2,311 listed companies, NASDAQ 2,717, the Over-the-Counter Bulletin Board (OTCBB) 2,386, the Pink Sheets market 6,199, and the Grey Market over 60,000, roughly forming a pyramid shape, with a relatively stable and reasonable structure. However, the structure of our country's market resembles an inverted pyramid: as of August 2011, there were 1,405 main board companies, 610 SME board companies, 258 GEM companies, and 115 Zhongguancun proxy transfer system companies.

Therefore, we should accelerate the construction of capital markets to support the development of SMEs and technology-based innovative enterprises, and promote the construction of an innovative country.

First, actively develop the over-the-counter trading market. Drawing on the experience of mature markets, fully considering our country's special national conditions, research and explore the establishment of an over-the-counter trading market under unified regulation, expanding the service scope of capital markets for SMEs, and broadening the financing channels for SMEs.

Second, strengthen and develop the SME board and GEM markets. Continuously improve the mechanisms for new share issuance, refinancing, delisting, and mergers and acquisitions according to the characteristics of SMEs, promoting the growth and strengthening of listed companies; improve governance mechanisms and enhance market transparency.

Third, standardize and develop venture capital and private equity funds. Improve relevant policies, laws, and regulations, and build a social credit system, formulate normative guidelines, protect investors' rights, establish and improve regulatory systems, and promote the standardized development of the industry.

Fourth, accelerate the development of the corporate bond market. Speed up the development of fixed-income products, providing bond financing channels for eligible SMEs; draw lessons from international experiences in private bonds and high-yield bond markets, eliminate disadvantages, and study bond products applicable to technologically innovative enterprises in their early stages of development.

The development of capital markets involves all aspects of the economy and society and is closely related to the interests of many enterprises and investors, making it a systemic project that requires top-level design and overall promotion. We must adopt a series of complementary reform measures. We need to strive to improve the level and efficiency of supervision, making the regulatory system adapt to the requirements of building a multi-level market system; strengthen investor protection mechanisms, enhance investor education, allowing investors to share in the results of SME development and China's economic transformation; at the same time, establish and improve mechanisms to attract long-term funds into the market, develop institutional investors, and promote the benign interaction between social security and pension systems and capital markets.
