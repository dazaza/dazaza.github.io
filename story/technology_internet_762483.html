Developers: Android is the most popular, iOS is the most profitable, and WP is the next

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 15:40:25

Counting the articles with the most comments, most of them simultaneously involve Android, iOS, and Windows Phone (arranged in alphabetical order, please don't make too many associations). However, most of these comments are made from the user's perspective. Now, let's take a look at how developers view each platform.

Developers, engineers, and programmers seem to be trending towards becoming an independent species separate from humans, but they still need to earn money to buy diapers for their children, clothes, jewelry, cosmetics, food, and phones for their wives (this part omits 500 words). Therefore, which platform is more profitable to participate in is a question worth serious consideration.

After surveying 6,000 developers from 115 countries, the statistical result shows that the average monthly salary for iOS developers is $5,200, while Android developers' average monthly salary is $4,700. If there are Chinese people in these 6,000 samples, then they probably lower the average salary.

However, Windows Phone doesn’t have to sit in the corner poking fingers, because 35% of developers are prepared to start development work on the Windows Phone platform in the third quarter of this year, whereas only 26% of developers are ready to enter the iOS field. This means more people will be willing to embrace Windows Phone in the future. This is good news for the relatively application-scarce Windows Phone platform.

Currently, the most favored platform belongs to Android, where 72% of developers are working on Android, 56% of developers are developing applications on iOS, and 21% are on Windows Phone. Developers have lost interest in the BlackBerry platform.

If developers could only stick to one platform and choose just one platform for development, then iOS would lead over Android. 59% of developers are willing to grow old with iOS, while 49% chose Android. However, there is still a question here: isn't this a single-choice question? Why do the numbers not add up properly? It turns out that developers can also be so fickle!

Although Windows Phone has attracted a large number of developers' interests and firmly holds the third position, it is still very difficult for Microsoft and Nokia to break through. VisionMobile pointed out in its report:

"The dual monopoly of Android and iOS has already been formed, and there are currently no signs indicating that Windows Phone will pose a threat to the top two in the foreseeable future. Once network cluster effects and sales foundations are established, the chances for Windows Phone to replace the top two are minimal. In addition, factors such as user loyalty, developer preferences, and the time and effort they invest, as well as operator support, favor Microsoft."

In other words, the road to reversal for Windows Phone is still long. However, a favorable piece of news is that its market share is steadily increasing. Taking the U.S. as an example, it rose from 3.8% last year to 5.6% this year. Another trend is that Windows Phone users are concentrated among young consumers aged 25 to 34.

With these foundations, plus Microsoft's strong financial resources and Nokia's diligent loyalty, the future of Windows Phone does not look so bad.
