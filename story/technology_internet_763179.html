I am a programmer, and I am proud of it.

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 16:31:49

Original in English: Do call yourself a programmer, and other career advice Link

In 2011, Patrick McKenzie wrote an article titled "Don't Call Yourself a Programmer," and this piece can be considered a (rather late) response to that. I think some of his points still hold true, and if someone were to follow his advice early in their career development, it might indeed be helpful. However, had I been twenty years old at the time, I doubt I could have improved myself using the methods he suggested.

Therefore, below, I will mainly focus on the parts I cannot agree with. I suggest you read Patrick's article first before continuing, so you don't miss anything. (Translator's note: The translation of "Don't Call Yourself a Programmer" is an excerpted translation, not a full one.)

It’s not exactly disagreement with his views but rather: "Our experiences differ." We stand on different career trajectories, naturally gaining different insights: Patrick McKenzie is the boss of "bingo card creator" (a small business) and also a successful consultant, while I am an architect for a company valued in the hundreds of millions. Our commonality is that we’ve both moved away from "pure programming," just in different directions.

What kind of company do you want to work for?

Patrick McKenzie says that 90% of jobs are as boring as implementing an internal travel report form, with no opportunity to write code that users actually use. And you must accept this reality. But is it really 90%? Spolsky says it might be 80%, which is terrifying enough. Patrick also explains why being an internal programmer (in-house programmers) is terrible:

There is no business reason to improve "barely functional" internal software. "Please forget about taking pride in craftsmanship, or you'll end up producing embarrassing garbage." In a company, the closer your work is tied to profitability, the more respect programmers tend to receive. Except in a few purely technical companies, programmers rarely rise to become the CEO. No matter how important network stability is to VIACOM (a multinational media group), when it comes down to dividing office space, internal programmers still end up sharing desks three to a table.

Both McKensize and Spolsky agree with the above statements, but Spolsky believes that programmers should work for a software company where the culture fosters their growth. On the other hand, McKenzie disagrees, believing that programmers should "go out into the world."

So the current issue boils down to personal preference for programmers: do you want to be a white-collar worker at a software company or take freelance gigs? But hold on, only 80-90% of people even get a chance to work at a software company. It’s not about whether you want to work there—it's about whether you can.

However, here I disagree. Even if only 10-20% of programmers can find work at software companies (if the data is correct), and assuming this group consists of the best among them, McKenzie also states:

"You often predict the strength of potential competitors based on the people around you, which frequently leads to overestimating them. Many senior engineers can't even write a good fizzbuzz program."

So, if most competitors aren't very strong, you're likely to enter the top 10-20% tier, just like most graduates did when I finished school. Therefore, I prefer to believe that working for a company or doing project-based software development is a matter of personal preference. Now let's talk about McKenzie's advice for those working at software companies.

How to position yourself

One of McKenzie's suggestions is:

"Do not position yourself as a programmer. Here, 'programmer' has roughly the same meaning as a highly-paid poet who writes incomprehensible verses. Instead, position yourself as an engineer who can increase your employer's revenue or reduce costs."

Sure, sometimes internal programmers spend a lot of time writing things that are incomprehensible to others, at least in the eyes of non-technical MBAs.

However, for me, I like programmers who can write things that are hard to understand but meaningful to others’ work.

McKenzie believes that hiring programmers by software companies is no different from hiring by regular companies. For example, at Google, a programmer who wrote the backend accounting system—reportedly used for 97% of Google's accounts—is now a venture capitalist. This means he became wealthy by aligning closely with the "profit center," not through any general technical skill.

I believe this person is Dr. Ron Garret from NSNA Aerodynamics Lab. Can you believe Google hired him because he increased revenue or reduced expenses while at the lab? (By the way, it’s said that Ron Garret didn’t enjoy writing the accounting system much and resigned after buying some Google stock options.)

Did the accounting system involve any special technology? Ron Garret said:

"I ended up writing the credit card expense and account management system, which was not simple. Fortunately, I learned computer security and cryptography before coming to Google, so I happened to be quite skilled for this job. In the system I designed, even if a malicious user obtained root access, they couldn't cause much harm. Assuming they are still using my system, I can confidently say that the credit card information I stored there is impossible to lose."

To me, his technical knowledge and programming ability allowed him to successfully create such a "non-stealable" system, and this "non-stealable" system neither reduced Google's costs nor increased its output.

So, if I were applying for a chip engineer position, I would emphasize my technical role in converting fixed-function switches and programmable processors, not my previous managerial positions. (I believe managerial roles come from prior technical efforts; managing something is abstract, but the actual work done is concrete).

Changing Jobs

"Your colleagues and bosses are not friends. You will spend a lot of time with your colleagues, and you may become good friends with some of them. But generally, you will quickly forget them after leaving.

Your boss will do things your friends would never do, like discussing salary cuts or overtime. Your colleagues will enthusiastically introduce you to things you dislike."

There are certainly some unwritten rules about colleagues not being friends. But I think this argument and its basis are somewhat circular. Colleagues can't become friends because you will leave. But why will you leave?

Therefore, I disagree with this unwritten rule. I worked at one company for over 12 years, largely because I got along well with my colleagues. Although I eventually changed jobs, it was only when the salary increase far exceeded 10% that I considered it. If you frequently change jobs due to minor promotions or salary increases, you won't get to know your colleagues or the work well. You may become very knowledgeable about job hunting and sensitive to market needs, but you won't know your employer deeply. You'll quickly adapt to your new role, but you'll also quickly exhaust your value and leave.

On the contrary, if you stick with a position for a long time (as long as the environment remains interesting and offers occasional challenges), your relationships with others will grow stronger, and you will gain more foresight, learning things you wouldn't otherwise.

Taking myself as an example: most of my knowledge comes from the ASIC hacker colleagues I worked with. They were willing to collaborate with me because of the long-term rapport we built. Achieving a harmonious working relationship is rare and requires significant time to develop.

Incidentally, if you stay at one place for a long time, your value in the eyes of your employer will naturally increase: you can achieve corresponding promotions (in position or salary) without changing jobs. Your salary will be higher than that of new employees with similar experience, making it unnecessary to switch jobs. This method is similar to becoming a consultant by frequently changing jobs.

Regardless of which method, you can succeed. I don't have statistics on which work style is more effective. But I think staying in one place for a period is more interesting. Otherwise, I wouldn't have made so many close friends.

Most things in life fail, and success is rare. You can't expect a single career plan to land your dream job or make you rich. You need to put in a lot of effort, and there are many unknowns involved. All you can be sure of is how your plan changes your lifestyle. And your chosen lifestyle determines the kinds of people you meet and the types of relationships you build—this is more predictable than whether you achieve your ultimate goal.

I completely agree with McKenzie's view on bosses. Some bosses are extremely exploitative and pretend to be your friend. At that point, you should properly defend your rights.

How to evaluate equity

McKenzie says not to overvalue equity but doesn't elaborate much. Here, I want to discuss stock options, which are worse than actual equity and were the only thing I ever received.

As mentioned earlier, my perspective is that everything should be viewed from a long-term perspective. Over 12 years, my company's value increased tenfold. But it is still a private company—my options are worthless.

In the long term, stock options are either better or worse.

They are worse because when you leave, you often can't afford to buy the shares or bear the investment risk. When a company survives and enters a stable phase, you might start getting bored with your responsibilities and want to cash out and leave. At this point, stock options can become a burden. Especially during promotion negotiations, your options become leverage for the company—they know you can't quit.

In other words, stock options become a form of debt.

Conversely, when a company matures, employees tend to underestimate their stock—already so expensive, can it go higher? Especially after it has tripled or quadrupled, this thought becomes natural. But remember, what you own isn't percentages but real dollars. A stock priced at $100 per share rising 20% to $120 means you earn $20 per share, whereas a stock priced at $5 per share doubling to $15 means you only earn $10 per share. So, mature company stocks with steady growth are more valuable investments than cheap stocks from young companies—you're more likely to become rich overnight.

The result is that people overestimate stock options early in a company's life and then underestimate them later. Note that if you don't plan to stay at a company for more than three years, stock options will become your "debt" because they will make it harder for you to quit.

Working at a startup

McKenzie lists many reasons not to work at startups. For instance, job satisfaction, where larger companies offer more interesting opportunities. However, I would argue that there is at least one aspect where large companies can't match startups. Large companies generally don't have many open positions, and in fact, they may not even notice or address these gaps. In simpler terms, large companies assign one person to one role, and once you join, you fill one specific role. New roles aren't created. In contrast, startups are almost always understaffed, so you can create your own roles and even take on multiple ones. This means you can assume more responsibility. In some companies, you must compete with multiple people to accomplish tasks. In a startup, you can simply do what you want because everyone is too busy to compete with you.

As companies grow larger, it becomes increasingly difficult to do what you want. In startups, programmers are nomads, going wherever the grass is greener. In large companies, nomads evolve into settled city-states, and programmers even need approval to look at another department's code.

I'm not suggesting you work at a startup. On the contrary, if you're an older programmer who prefers mature processes, working at a large company might suit you better.

The impact of career choices on life happiness

"At the end of each day, whether you were happy today does not entirely depend on your career."

On one hand, I fully agree with this statement. Regardless of the content of your work, it's just a job. Moreover, I've noticed that when I overly emphasize the importance of work, my productivity decreases. The reasonable approach is: when you get home, don't push yourself as hard as during the day.

On the other hand, we do spend a significant amount of time at work. The question here is: how should work and life be separated (balanced)? Here, I disagree with separating colleagues from ordinary friends in the process of distinguishing between work and life. Cutting ties with colleagues once you leave the company is unnecessary.

Summary

When I introduce myself, I often call myself a programmer (even though my previous roles focused on chip architecture or management), because I make money by writing programs, not just because I feel proud when saying the word "programmer." I believe programming is a great career and suits people like me.

Conversely, if you see programming merely as a stepping stone to transition into another type of career (such as management), then you shouldn't really call yourself a programmer. This approach is incomprehensible to me: you might as well pursue an MBA. Of course, this path may suit some people, as some MBA programs require industry work experience before admitting candidates.

Therefore, the key lies in choosing a path that suits you and sticking with it. Eventually, you will succeed. For a company like mine, if you want a programming position but don't identify as a programmer, it probably won't leave a good impression on the interviewer.

Finally, I strongly emphasize one point from McKenzie's article that I fully agree with: communication skills are crucial. I recommend you read it.
