Dianping: The Bone That Giants Can't Crack, Living in the Discomfort Zone

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 14:29:16

Tao Zhang, CEO of Dianping.com

Written by Yuan Yin, edited by Jing Yang, photographed by Nan Xiao

Tao Zhang, CEO of Dianping.com, has once again postponed the company's IPO schedule.

In 2011, Dianping received a $100 million Series C financing round from Sequoia Capital and others, valuing the company at $1 billion. By convention, it should have gone public within the next one to two years, but it did not. Instead, in 2012, it raised another $60 million in a Series D round.

Given Dianping’s scale and development trajectory, going public would not be difficult. However, Tao Zhang feels that an IPO now would be too costly. He deconstructs the big internet (PC + mobile) into four elements: people, information, goods, and services. Each connection point offers the opportunity for a giant to emerge. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, and Alibaba connects people with goods. The space between people and services is the seat for the fourth giant. Since 2003, Dianping has been diligently accumulating data, finally waiting for the explosion of mobile internet, where smartphones merge "location" with "services." According to Zhang's logic, no company seems more fitting than Dianping for the title of the fourth giant.

However, outsiders do not think so. In recent months, with BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent) frequently making acquisitions, Dianping has become a presumed acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition target; whichever of BAT acquires it could help them achieve greatness sooner. On the other hand, Dianping's size pales in comparison to BAT, and it is still too early to declare it a future giant. Moreover, whether it's Tencent WeChat's public platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after investing in AutoNavi and Meituan, all are directly targeting Dianping's territory - local life services centered on dining, drinking, entertainment, etc.

Before proving the following two points, a Dianping IPO would be equivalent to a "discounted sale." First, Zhang needs time to prove that what Dianping is doing now, BAT cannot. Second, Dianping is about to enter the ultimate battle of group buying, and if it wins, its valuation could increase several times over.

2013 is Dianping's "year of decisive battles." For all the speculation and rumors, Zhang pays them no mind. He is clear: "The internet has indeed entered an era of great integration, but Dianping will not be acquired."

Zhang categorizes companies that might be acquired into three types. The first type has platform attributes but is better suited to develop within a larger system, such as maps and email. These products can easily gain large numbers of users but struggle to find independent business models. Others, like browsers, while profitable, earn far less through navigation services than their strategic value to large companies. "Big players are willing to invest in maintaining an email product because it helps with branding and user stickiness, but there hasn't been a successful standalone email company," he says. The second type involves industry consolidation, like in the video industry, where acquisitions expand market share. The third type consists of companies facing financial difficulties who sell out for survival.

Now, Meituan has already been brought under Alibaba's wing. Theoretically, Dianping should quickly seek a "backer" to avoid being defeated by a capital-boosted rival. However, Zhang argues, "The giants offer us limited help. While we do need users, execution is even more critical. Acquisitions would reduce our execution efficiency and risk marginalization by a large company." Furthermore, he insists on maintaining the most precise "distance" from the giants. Getting too close to any one of them would amount to "taking sides," making Dianping an enemy to the others.

Thus, logically, acquiring Dianping becomes a dead end. Zhang counters, "Why should we take their money? We're not short on cash; what we need are resources. If a giant were willing to make a small equity investment and provide us with many resources, I might consider it. But would they? Would they give us resources if they only held a small stake?"

Weapon One: Dirty and Hard Work

In mobile internet, opportunities for companies solely focused on pure internet products are becoming fewer. New opportunities come from those that intersect with traditional industries. Zhang believes that lifestyle services primarily revolve around dining, drinking, and entertainment. Other niche markets, such as ride-hailing, can form decent business models, but few among them have the potential to grow into giants.

For dining, drinking, and entertainment, user demand is simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is the core of lifestyle services, which is precisely where Dianping started. Whether in content, membership accumulation, or brand, newcomers find it hard to surpass Dianping.

The most representative product form of discounts is group buying. Group buying has now entered the championship battle phase, and Dianping aims to compete with Meituan in 2013. This is a winner-takes-all market; as long as there are two strong competitors, the industry's gross margin will be suppressed, making stable profitability impossible.

Another foreseeable future lies in reservations. Reservations can create a true online-to-offline closed loop, but this must be built on the foundation of Dianping having absolute advantages in both information and discounts. Otherwise, building a closed loop would be very difficult. In 2012, Dianping began experimenting with reservations. Zhang says, "Currently, feedback from users and merchants is good, but if we were to focus solely on reservations, it would be hard to solve the chicken-or-egg problem."

Of course, lifestyle services present some heavier opportunities. For example, food delivery is a rigid user demand and helps merchants increase sales. In the past couple of years, food delivery companies like "To Home Gourmet Society" have been thriving. However, the core of food delivery is delivery - it must be delivered within a very short time frame, and issues like soup spillage must be resolved. The logistics requirements are even higher than those of fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang says, "Food delivery has potential if executed well, but it's harder dirty work. What Dianping is already doing is dirty and hard enough."

If judged by age, Dianping is already an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has painstakingly accumulated merchant profiles and user reviews, which Zhang refers to as "dirty and hard work." This primitive growth model once limited its speed, but past pain has become Dianping's core barrier today. It can be said that, except for Meituan, Dianping has no real rivals in the lifestyle services sector.

Nevertheless, he must remain vigilant against BAT's "clutches." Baidu's involvement is the most direct. Baidu Maps already has over 100 million users, allowing users to search for nearby dining and other lifestyle services. Tencent, deemed to have secured a ticket to mobile internet, has launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest physical goods trading platform, and expanding goods to services is logical. Moreover, it has incorporated Meituan and AutoNavi into its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there hasn't been an explosive growth in usage or data," Zhang says. Brand positioning is crucial; the key is which product users turn to first when they want to dine, drink, or entertain themselves.

Moreover, in the vast field of lifestyle services, competition among giants is inevitably cross-sectoral, and no one can completely monopolize the field. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained on Weibo and WeChat. Zhang feels that this makes Dianping safer instead. "In niche areas like car rental and business travel, the massive user base directed by the giants could easily overwhelm existing companies."

As for Alibaba, which has invested in AutoNavi, wanting to encroach on Dianping's territory isn't that simple either. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, which can always be categorized regardless of how peculiar. A user's experience of a meal depends on factors like the day's environment, waitstaff, and chef. Managing merchants cannot rely on systems and tools like Alibaba does; it must establish a 'ground force' managed by humans.

"All three giants want to go down this path, and there is room for imagination, but this is not a highway; it's a rugged mountain road," Zhang believes. First, providing lifestyle services requires ground teams deployed at various levels, which BAT lacks the genes and capability to manage. Second, they lack the willingness; lifestyle services require focused effort, not something a small team can accomplish, and profits come slowly. "It's hard for a company to excel at two important things simultaneously, let alone needing to consolidate its advantage in its original field."

Weapon Two: Agile Maneuverability

In 2011, when Dianping secured $100 million in funding, Zhang told the world that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, trends change quickly, and before Dianping went public, his analogous companies fell from grace.

He wasn't particularly troubled by this. "They failed to become big companies because they lacked offline teams. Now they all want to be America's Dianping."

The current path is one Zhang has explored step by step. When he felt that selling keyword ads and discount coupons was too outdated, the emergence of group buying caught his eye. Initially, like Americans, he viewed group buying as a marketing platform, offering one deal per day. But as orders increased, he realized that group buying had transactional attributes. Thus, he envisioned Dianping's future as a lifestyle services trading platform. The two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were precisely used for Dianping's transformation from an information platform to a trading platform.

Without this "genetic modification," Zhang might not have had the confidence to vie for the fourth seat among giants today. But genetic modification sounds simple, yet in reality, it is akin to Dianping's second entrepreneurship, an extremely painful process.

First, compared to selling ads alone, the gross margin of a trading platform decreases, requiring stricter cost control and higher efficiency. Second, a trading platform demands higher cross-departmental communication capabilities.

Previously, to maintain Dianping's impartiality and independence as an information platform, the content and sales teams were completely separate. Zhang even deliberately erected a "firewall" between departments - even important clients couldn't alter platform information for sales purposes, leading to some mutual hostility between content and sales teams. Now, as a trading platform, editors and sales teams need deep communication to craft good marketing plans. Beyond planning, editorial cooperation is essential. He says, "Maintaining information quality remains crucial, but there's no need to reinforce animosity. We need to dismantle the firewall."

Though editors may accept this logically, individuals' behaviors and values have inertia, and collectively across the entire company, that inertia is greater. To address this, Zhang specially convened several meetings to encourage more communication between departments. Months later, an editor supervisor casually remarked to a sales supervisor, "I used to see you as an enemy, but now I see you as a colleague."

Sales departments also need genetic modification. To promote conversion, Zhang cleverly gave the offline team dual identities. First, for merchants, salespeople should transform into marketing advisors, not just selling products but helping merchants devise marketing strategies. Second, for the company, marketing advisors act as procurement officers. Dianping is like a supermarket; not only must product quality be good, but the variety of goods must also be rich. Marketing advisors should aim to help users procure group-buying products. Of course, dual roles will inevitably lead to a series of complex issues. For frontline personnel, this is quite challenging, requiring them to balance the scales themselves.

Substantially, Dianping's "dual identity" resembles Alibaba's division into 25 business units. Decentralizing decision-making to the frontlines allows the company to respond faster, but it increases management difficulty. Similarly, compared to frontline employees, Dianping's post-genetic modification management team suffers more.

Each year, Zhang breaks down Dianping's corporate goals into various departments, which are then further broken down by executives layer by layer. However, breaking down goals isn't simple arithmetic. Li Jing, co-founder of Dianping responsible for the sales team, sometimes finds unresolved merchant demands stemming from sales issues affecting sales performance. Therefore, Li Jing introduced a new position, setting up communication specialists who collect daily information from marketing advisors, submit it to other departments, and track feedback. He often tells his team, "Being adept at embracing change doesn't mean liking change. No one likes change; everyone feels uncomfortable when faced with it. All employees must navigate outside their comfort zones, including myself."

The figures reflect the weight of Dianping's execution team. Currently, among more than 3,000 employees, over 2,000 work offline. The ground team directly communicates with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group buying, and bundled marketing solutions.

For this core team resisting BAT's clutches, Dianping has a unique management evaluation system.

They aren't called "salespeople"; from day one, their business cards bear the title "Marketing Planning Consultant." Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the era of watching news and chatting on QQ, lagging behind Taobao merchants by at least three to five years in terms of internet understanding. Marketing planning consultants need to inform merchants about the demands of online users and how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing says, "Even for a high-end restaurant, marketing advisors wouldn't suggest grouping expensive seafood like abalone or shark fin, possibly replacing it with other dishes, as online and offline user demands differ."

An excellent marketing advisor must be a versatile player. They need to explain clearly to restaurant owners or hair salon owners the fundamental differences between discounts and group buying and determine which discount aligns better with their positioning. If dealing with a hot pot restaurant owner, the marketing advisor must know whether it's peak or off-season, which products see rising costs during certain seasons, and which see declining costs, proposing a group-buying menu that pleases both merchants and users.

This places high demands on marketing advisors, requiring them not only to be experts in internet marketing but also in the industry. Li Jing admits, "It's impossible for every advisor to be a double expert, so we've set star ratings for advisors." Similar to hotels, marketing advisors range from one-star to five-star, with the best ones labeled as Super Stars.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline employees aren't tech-savvy graduates from prestigious universities, making it challenging for them to understand internet products. Dianping has established a knowledge base for marketing advisors, containing knowledge points and relevant cases accessible to both new and old employees. If encountering a new issue, employees can ask questions within the knowledge base, where thousands of consultants nationwide can assist.

Most of the knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees based on practical experience. Dianping has established a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, rewarding employees who help others the most each quarter. Every quarter, Dianping conducts skill assessments for employees, linking the results directly to promotions.

Industry knowledge is harder to acquire than internet knowledge. Some experiences are insights gained by traditional merchants after years of practice in the industry. A Super Star marketing advisor told Li Jing that the hairdressing industry should avoid group-buying packages for washing, cutting, and blow-drying, as men lack motivation to buy them, and women prefer blow-drying without washing and cutting. Li Jing was astonished, "We men would never think of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing advisors are not only divided by cities but also by industries such as catering, hairdressing, and leisure. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is further subdivided into hot pot, Western cuisine, and buffets, with one marketing advisor responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing advisors can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, ensuring merchants don't reject selling products," he says.

Each marketing group holds its own case-sharing sessions: what difficulties a particular type of merchant encounters, what marketing needs they have, what product combinations the company offers, and how effective they are. Outstanding cases are reported upwards, and Dianping officially shares one case weekly.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition called "Winning Entrepreneurs." Within the first three months of employment, new hires must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products but listening to merchants' experiences. If responsible for a hot pot restaurant, after three months, they must submit a business plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hot Pot Restaurant Owner." Like graduation, employees present their defense live, with industry supervisors evaluating the winners, and the company offering rewards.

Strategically speaking, whether it's Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, the tasks ahead are similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O are familiar to anyone with basic industry knowledge, and the words spoken sound alike. "The era when success depended on storytelling and concept creation has passed. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model lies in management and execution, not strategy," Zhang concludes.
