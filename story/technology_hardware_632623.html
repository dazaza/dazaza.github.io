White-collar workers cannot be "white-collar", they have to play "planned economy"

by juyaqufa003 on 2011-12-19 09:07:34

As the year-end approaches, the human resources service provider "51job" recently conducted a survey on the financial management status of white-collar workers nationwide. The survey shows that 31.81% of respondents said they could save 3 to 6 months' salary within a year (including 6 months), and only 7.91% of the respondents were still "moonlighters" (those who spend all their income each month). The biggest motivation for white-collar workers to save money is buying a house and preparing for retirement. So, how do white-collar workers in Kunming manage their finances?

**Representative Case: Sun Wenjie**

Sun Wenjie works at a large telecommunications company in Kunming with a monthly income of about 3000 yuan. She has been working for seven years, but the balance in her bank account has never exceeded 2000 yuan. More often than not, it's zero. Her philosophy about money is "Money is like an idiot; you spend it and then earn more."

After paying over 3000 yuan in rent and carrying a debt of 1500 yuan, Sun Wenjie from a major telecommunications company in Kunming began planning how to spend more money. Her philosophy is "Money is like trash; you spend it and then earn more."

Having worked for seven years, the number in Sun Wenjie’s bank account has never exceeded 2000 yuan, and most of the time it is zero. She openly admits she is a "moonlighter" and doesn't think being one is a big deal. "While I can still play and eat, I will enjoy life more. When I am old, I won't be able to eat or play, so what's the point of saving so much money?" Sun Wenjie says many friends around her agree with her view, just that everyone has different spending limits. After deducting social security contributions, she earns about 3000 yuan per month. After reserving 500 yuan for rent, the rest is her disposable income. Food and clothing usually take up three-quarters of her disposable income. She meets up with friends two to three times a month, each costing over 100 yuan. If she goes singing or bar hopping, she can easily spend 300 yuan in one evening. Sun Wenjie believes as a salaried worker, her pace of buying new clothes is relatively slow, and her self-control is relatively strong, at most buying one piece of new clothing per month. But the temptation of new clothes in the mall is something she cannot resist. Sometimes, even if she didn’t plan to buy anything, trying on beautiful clothes leads to hundreds of yuan spent. Sun Wenjie says the only reason to save money is usually when the holidays are approaching, but the purpose of saving is to have fun during the holidays. After the holidays, she is back to being broke.

Nowadays, there are fewer white-collar workers like Sun Wenjie who have decent salaries but can't save money. According to the survey by 51job, 26.79% of respondents actually take home about 2500 yuan per month, while 39.44% earn between 2500-3500 yuan. The proportion of "moonlighters" who live paycheck-to-paycheck is 7.91%, those who spend recklessly account for 30.6%, and those who not only don't save but also become "card slaves" account for 4.14%.

**Representative Case: Yang Yang**

Yang Yang, 27 years old, works as an administrator at a real estate company in Kunming with a monthly salary of over 2500 yuan. She regularly saves 700 yuan per month and currently has savings of about 20,000 yuan. Her philosophy about money is "Adopt a planned economy approach, where saving money does not hinder enjoyment."

With the rise in prices and changes in young people's consumption patterns, more and more white-collar workers dare not live paycheck-to-paycheck. The survey shows that 31.81% of respondents said they could save 3-6 months' salary within a year (including 6 months), 27.49% could save 1-3 months' salary (including 3 months), and 7.72% could save less than one month's salary (including one month). The proportions of respondents who could save 6-9 months' salary (including 9 months) and more than 9 months' salary are 15.2% and 6.6%, respectively. Yang Yang, admired by her colleagues for her beautiful appearance, good eating habits, and ability to save 700 yuan per month, believes that "with a planned economy approach, saving money does not hinder enjoyment." Yang Yang feels that many people have misconceptions about saving money, believing that one must cut down on expenses drastically, not daring to eat, wear, or play. "But in fact, as long as these things are allocated into monthly expenses, both goals can be achieved."

Yang Yang is 27 years old. After graduating from university, she worked as an administrator at a local real estate company for four years with a monthly salary of over 2500 yuan. To keep up with trends, Yang Yang once lived paycheck-to-paycheck. "The essence of fashion is the flow of money," Yang Yang said that in recent years she finally understood the true nature of fashion - where money flows fastest and most abundantly defines the trend. In the past, she would use up her monthly salary in half a month, dazzled by clothes, shoes, accessories, and various consumption venues. The most extravagant time was when she bought two pairs of sandals and a skirt within a week, living off the remaining 500 yuan for the whole month. "Fortunately, my home is in Kunming, so when I run out of money, I can go home for free meals or borrow some money from my parents."

Living this way for over a year, "One day, seeing a friend change to a new phone, I liked it, but I had no money," Yang Yang said that moment made her suddenly realize that perhaps she should start saving money. At first, Yang Yang meticulously calculated every expense like many housewives, spending 1 yuan on breakfast for a steamed bun, keeping lunch under 10 yuan, and limiting her monthly clothing budget to no more than 300 yuan. This gave her a considerable surplus after several months. "But saving money this way is too painful," Yang Yang, being young, couldn't resist seeing her friends constantly changing their styles. So she adjusted her saving plan, depositing 700 yuan immediately after receiving her salary each month, and freely arranging the rest. If she didn't buy clothes that month, she could hang out with friends more often. If she went out and saw something she wanted to buy, she would include it in the next month's consumption plan, balancing her expenses back and forth. Eventually, Yang Yang managed to achieve both goals. Now, Yang Yang already has about 20,000 yuan in savings. She happily plans to increase her monthly savings by 200 yuan next year after a raise, and after a few more years, buy herself a small apartment through a mortgage.

**Representative Case: Liang Haitao**

Liang Haitao returned to Kunming after drifting in Beijing for three years. He now works in sales at a large state-owned enterprise in Kunming with an uncertain monthly income. "I just want to go home, save some money, buy a house, get married, and it's time to consider retirement issues."

Like Yang Yang, many white-collar workers primarily think of using their savings to buy a house. Fan Jiang, an interior designer at a decoration company near Nanscreen Street, said he has been saving for three years. Once he saves up 150,000 yuan, he will buy a house and get married to his girlfriend, planning the wedding on January 4, 2013.

According to the survey by 51job, 34.37% of respondents said they could save 100,000 yuan in 3-5 years (including 5 years), 24.47% need 5-10 years (including 10 years), 18.79% need 1-3 years (including 3 years), and only 1.3% said they could save this amount in less than one year (including one year). Regarding the purpose and motivation for saving money, 20.56% of respondents said the biggest motivation is to buy and renovate a house, 17.67% for future life security (retirement, supporting parents), 11.72% for investment management, and 11.25% for marriage.

Liang Haitao, who recently returned from Beijing to Kunming, said he came back home to reduce living costs and save money for buying a house. After graduating from university, Liang Haitao worked in sales in Kunming for a year, then moved to Beijing in 2008 to continue working in international sales at a joint venture. During his three years in Beijing, his only feeling was that although his monthly salary was over 6000 yuan, his expenses were also high, barely allowing him to save about 1000 yuan each month. Relying on such meager savings to buy a house in Beijing was akin to a pipe dream. "You can drift and explore when you're young, but you can't drift forever," Liang Haitao thought deeply and decided to return home this year, "just to save some money, buy a house, get married, and it's time to consider retirement issues." Now, Liang Haitao works in sales at a large state-owned enterprise in the city, negotiating a contract with companies in Thailand and other neighboring small countries. "If this deal succeeds, I can save at least 10,000 to 20,000 yuan," maintaining this pace, in two or three years, he can achieve his goal of buying a house.

Li Jie, who works at an insurance company, has been married for a year. Although she doesn't have the pressure of buying a house, she and her husband regularly deposit one person's salary into the bank each month. "On the one hand, it's preparation for having a baby in the future, and on the other hand, it's saving some retirement money for ourselves." Li Jie said that combined with her husband's monthly income of about 6000 yuan, although their company has purchased old-age insurance for them, considering the current rapid rise in prices, relying solely on pensions in the future might not lead to a comfortable life. Therefore, they still insist on saving some money for emergencies. For someone who is 28 years old, many people think it's too early to consider retirement issues, but Li Jie believes it's not too early at all, "Money is saved bit by bit, the earlier you start, the more you can save."
