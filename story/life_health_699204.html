Medical Knowledge: Gestational Diabetes

Gestational diabetes refers to the occurrence of hyperglycemia during pregnancy when the patient did not have diabetes before becoming pregnant. The incidence rate is approximately 1% to 3%. Screening methods involve drinking 50 grams of sugary water between the 24th and 28th weeks of pregnancy, followed by a blood glucose test one hour later. If the blood glucose level exceeds the standard, a 100-gram glucose tolerance test must be conducted. If diagnosed as having gestational diabetes by a doctor, the patient should first consult a nutritionist for proper dietary advice and engage in appropriate exercise. A few patients who cannot control their blood sugar well through the aforementioned methods will need insulin injections. This can prevent macrosomia and allow for a smooth delivery.

Gestational diabetes (GDM) is a special type of diabetes. It refers to the discovery of varying degrees of glucose intolerance or obvious diabetes after confirming pregnancy, regardless of whether insulin or only dietary therapy is needed, and regardless of whether this condition persists after delivery, it can be considered gestational diabetes. What is the difference between gestational diabetes and diabetes during pregnancy?

Gestational diabetes: Refers to a temporary diabetic state during pregnancy that disappears after pregnancy. Six weeks after the end of pregnancy, an oral glucose tolerance test should be reviewed, and reclassification should be done according to diabetes diagnostic standards.

Diabetes during pregnancy: Refers to women who already have diabetes before pregnancy, and diabetes continues to exist after pregnancy.

In recent years, numerous studies have shown that if diabetic patients can be promptly diagnosed and treated during pregnancy, the outcomes for most diabetic pregnancies are relatively satisfactory, allowing them to conceive and safely deliver a healthy baby under the supervision of internal medicine and obstetricians. Complications for both mother and child are rarely seen. Pregnant women with severe diabetes, diabetes-related retinal damage, or combined heart and kidney dysfunction should avoid pregnancy.
