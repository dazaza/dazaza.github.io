Teenager addicted to the Internet robs and runs away, father helplessly seeks police assistance

by mkxalfmej on 2012-03-05 21:06:54

Haikou, February 29th - (Reported by Chen Min with photographic documentation) A 13-year-old first-year junior high school student not only neglected his studies due to an obsession with the internet but also repeatedly stole money from his family and robbed elementary school students of their belongings. On the morning of February 29th, Mr. Wu, the boy's father, sought help from our newspaper, stating that his son had been missing for over a month. Faced with his son's constant evasion, Mr. Wu felt both heartbroken and powerless. "Who can help us save our child psychologically?"

**The Son Goes Astray**

**The Father Decides to Act Righteously**

Mr. Wu told reporters that he, born in a rural area, currently runs a small business on Lan Tian Road in Haikou. He has two sons, and although his business is not large, it fully supports the family's basic needs. Mr. Wu mentioned that for over a decade, he worked tirelessly day and night to earn money, hoping to provide a good educational environment for his sons so they could become accomplished individuals in the future. However, he was perplexed as to why his younger son chose a path entirely contrary to his expectations.

According to Mr. Wu, his younger son, Xiao Chuan (a pseudonym), will be turning 14 in two months and is currently in the first year of junior high school at Haikou No. 10 Middle School. Over the past two years, Mr. Wu noticed that Xiao Chuan had become increasingly rebellious, not only being addicted to online games but also refusing to attend school. Even more infuriatingly, Xiao Chuan would often steal money from home when no one was looking, ranging from dozens to hundreds of yuan. Just before this year's Spring Festival, after stealing 2,000 yuan from home, Xiao Chuan ran away and hasn't returned since. Despite multiple inquiries at school among Xiao Chuan's classmates, Mr. Wu found no leads. Out of desperation, Mr. Wu had to rely on friends who understood computers to track his son's online activity. From the login addresses of Xiao Chuan's QQ account, it was evident that during this month, he frequently visited various internet cafes within Haikou city, recently hiding around Liberation West Street.

Mr. Wu also informed reporters that since starting junior high school, Xiao Chuan had regularly associated with a group of dropouts and participated in robbing elementary school students of their belongings. After repeated ideological work, Mr. Wu resolutely reported his son's actions to the police station officers and requested that his son be arrested. However, the police officers believed that since Xiao Chuan was not yet 14 years old and none of the victimized elementary school students had filed complaints, there was insufficient basis for the police to initiate a case.

**Parents Reflect: Poor Choice of Friends is the Main Reason**

In the eyes of Mr. Wu and his wife, Xiao Chuan had always been a very smart and diligent child who quickly grasped new things faster than other children of the same age. So what caused Xiao Chuan to change like this? Mr. Wu recalled that Xiao Chuan began frequenting internet cafes starting from the fifth grade of elementary school, which was also when he started stealing money from home. Mr. Wu had repeatedly told Xiao Chuan, "If you need money, just tell us. We don't forbid you from going online, but you must control the time." However, Xiao Chuan paid no heed to these words.

Mr. Wu showed reporters Xiao Chuan's student handbook. The reporter noticed that since elementary school, his teachers' evaluations were mostly positive, describing him as "smart, hardworking, but does not follow classroom discipline, and is somewhat introverted." In the self-evaluation section, Xiao Chuan also wrote heartfelt messages such as "Thank you for having a good teacher" and "I need to correct my shortcomings." "Actually, this child has a good heart; he's just young and doesn't understand right from wrong, and he doesn't listen to his family," Mr. Wu said helplessly. Since the sixth grade, after befriending a few dropout peers, Xiao Chuan considered them "brothers," constantly evading and hiding with them, refusing to listen to his family, and participating in the robbery of elementary school students under their influence.

**Psychological Expert: Suggests Parents Empathize**

From the perspective of Haikou No. 10 Middle School, although Xiao Chuan's performance last semester was unsatisfactory, the school did not abandon him. His homeroom teacher, Mr. Mo, expressed hope that Xiao Chuan would complete his winter vacation homework according to school requirements, return to school soon, and abide by school rules. "Xiao Chuan is a very smart child with great potential for improvement. We hope he returns to the class soon."

That afternoon, the reporter interviewed Teacher Yao Qing, secretary-general of the Hainan Psychological Counseling Association and a national second-level psychological counselor, regarding Xiao Chuan's issues. He stated that parents should not simply attribute Xiao Chuan's running away to an "internet addiction" problem. Xiao Chuan's involvement in robbing elementary school students has already posed a threat to public security, indicating a severe psychological issue. To this, Teacher Yao believed that parents should not solely blame the child for becoming "bad" but should first reflect on their own actions. Many problems arise from the family or school environment. In response to Mr. Wu's confusion, Teacher Yao suggested that parents should empathize and consider what thoughts might be going through the child's mind. They should reflect on whether family relationships are harmonious, if they have hurt the child in any way, and where they may have placed undue pressure on the child. After identifying issues within themselves, parents should then embrace the child with an open heart, forgive all the child's past mistakes, allow the child to return home and feel warmth, and then gradually communicate to help the child rebuild his worldview. "We hope that parents do not impose their will on the child or resort to violence to solve problems. Communication and finding common ground is the best choice," Teacher Yao emphasized.
