Designed specifically for Africa! The mobile phone dream of a 20-year-old Congolese youth.

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 19:51:25

As the fastest-growing market for smartphone sales globally, in the past five years, the number of mobile phone users in Africa has grown at a rate of 20% annually. Despite this, Africa still remains the place with the lowest ownership of smartphones in the world. With the growing African middle class, the demand for smartphones will continue to rise.

A Congolese (Republic) mobile phone company, VMK, saw business opportunities in this trend. This mobile phone company, established in 2006, previously developed affordable smartphones like Elikia and Way-C tablets targeting the African market, claiming them as "the first designed by Africans themselves." However, production and assembly in China led to doubts from outsiders about this slogan.

"Now, we are building our own factory in Brazzaville!" said Verone Mankou, the 26-year-old founder of VMK from the Republic of Congo. "In the past, we designed in Congo, assembled in China, and sold in Congo – now it's different. I hope for design in Congo, assembly in Congo, and sales in Congo."

According to Ars Technica, Verone Mankou founded VMK at just 20 years old. At that time, buying a laptop in Congo cost over $1000, which was very expensive for economically underdeveloped African people.

Mankou believed that technology would influence more and more citizens. Boldly, he started designing a laptop six months later. In the end, he failed. "But I never stopped seeking ways to connect more people to the internet."

In 2007, Mankou learned from a friend that Steve Jobs had released the first-generation iPhone. "For the world, this was a revolution, but for me, it was an inspiration."

This inspiration was to create a "bigger iPhone" — "it had no keyboard," "now we call it a tablet."

Mankou immediately began looking for resources to design a tablet. Six months later, financial reality hit Mankou hard. You see, getting investment in French-speaking African countries is extremely difficult. "When you're young, you can have the best projects of your life, but without the support of bankers, you'll find it hard to move forward," Mankou recalled. A banker once advised Mankou: "Kid, I admire you. But developing a tablet computer is a complete waste of time – stop!"

Mankou was determined to prove the banker wrong. With the help of Time Magazine, Mankou's project became public. Fortunately, on a 15-hour flight from Singapore to Brazzaville, a Congolese minister read the story. The minister was surprised to learn that such an unknown project existed in his country. Upon returning home, the minister began searching for Mankou and helped him secure financing.

In 2011, VMK launched its $200 tablet, the Way-C, meaning "light of the stars" in a northern Congolese dialect. By the end of 2012, the company added a phone product line – Elikia, meaning "hope." Recently, the company also introduced a feature phone. Their next project will be a $50 tablet specifically designed for education, healthcare, and agriculture.

Currently, the company's team has expanded to 150 people, 85% of whom are salespeople. The company focuses on the low-end market, which has millions of potential users in Africa. This aligns perfectly with Mankou's original intention – to allow more ordinary people to experience the charm of the internet.

The road to independent design is not easy. Some people who hold negative views about Africa's development believe that local African technology is immature, and the quality and performance of products cannot compete with established brands. For example, although BlackBerry's sales have been declining in Western markets, it still holds a high market share in Africa. Huawei and ZTE smartphones have also performed well in the African market.

"Only Africans truly understand the needs of Africans," Verone Mankou said at the third annual African Technology Conference held earlier in South Africa. He expressed that VMK aims to become Africa's largest tech company in the future, much like Apple and Samsung have achieved success today.

"Samsung is the world's largest smartphone manufacturer, so is Apple, but I want to be the largest in Africa… It's just a matter of time, I'm young, I have time."
