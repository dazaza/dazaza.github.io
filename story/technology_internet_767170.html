The new round of reshuffling in the domestic mobile phone industry is approaching, and the inventory crisis is emerging.

by anonymous on 2013-10-09 17:22:53

"It is the best of times, it is the worst of times." This might be the most apt description of the current situation for mobile phone manufacturers. In the face of traditional giants like Nokia, Motorola, and BlackBerry stepping down or exiting, the global mobile phone market landscape is being redrawn; and domestically, with the decline of Apple and Samsung becoming apparent, domestic phone manufacturers are fiercely competing. However, behind the surface prosperity, issues such as price competition and overcapacity are causing turmoil in the domestic phone industry. With the imminent issuance of 4G licenses, a new round of consolidation is not far off.

Industry Status

"The Best of Times"

China's mobile phone manufacturers are experiencing a prosperous and lively "best of times." Aside from Samsung and Apple firmly occupying the top tier, Chinese phone manufacturers have taken up the majority of the remaining market. The "Zhonghua Kulián" group composed of ZTE, Huawei, Coolpad, and Lenovo leads the domestic mobile phone market, followed by other brands such as Meizu, Gionee, OPPO, Xiaomi, and VIVO. According to the global smartphone shipment data for the second quarter released by research firm Canalys, the shipment volume of the five major domestic brands ("Zhonghua Kulián" plus Xiaomi) has already accounted for 20% of the global market. After Samsung and Apple who occupy the first two spots on the list, Chinese manufacturers Lenovo and Coolpad have ranked third and fourth respectively, and after LG in fifth place, Huawei and ZTE occupy sixth and seventh places respectively.

Having completed the encirclement of Samsung and Apple, Chinese manufacturers are no longer satisfied with merely gaining market share through low-end, low-profit products. They are now beginning to plan for the high-profit, high-end product market dominated by foreign brands.

In September alone, Coolpad, OPPO, Meizu, and other Chinese mobile phone brands launched their new products one after another. Unlike before when domestic brands concentrated their products in the lower price range, these new domestic products are priced at 2000 yuan and even 3000 yuan or more. Coolpad, which has never been short of high-priced products, set the price of its flagship product Grand View 4 at nearly 4000 yuan.

In terms of hardware configuration, these products all concentrate the most advanced hardware available today. In marketing and promotion, Chinese manufacturers represented by Coolpad have finally placed their products on par with Apple's iPhone5s and Samsung's Note3, showing a clear intention to catch up in the high-end product segment.

Intense Price Competition

"The intensity of competition in China's mobile phone market is rare globally," said He Shiyou, the highest-ranking official responsible for the mobile phone business at ZTE Communications and Executive Vice President. It is rare worldwide for so many competitors to be concentrated in one market, and for the vast majority of them to use price wars as weapons.

"Every now and then, a new domestic mobile phone brand emerges, leaving people overwhelmed," remarked a veteran in the mobile phone industry.

As the number of new entrants in the domestic mobile phone market continues to increase, more and more manufacturers are joining the battle for the low-end market. Even Xiaomi, once known as the "price killer," has become a target for other brands. For example, some brands claim they will launch products with the same configuration as Xiaomi's Redmi but at a price one yuan lower. More dramatically, Hasee, which played the role of a price killer in the PC market, has now publicly announced its entry into the smartphone market, with prices "half of Xiaomi's."

Expert Analysis

Erosion of Leading Brands' Market Share

Independent telecom analyst Fu Liang believes that with the expansion of the domestic smartphone market share and the comprehensive improvement from chips to R&D, design, testing, manufacturing, branding, and after-sales service, conditions have been provided for domestic brands to enter the high-end market. The recent密集launches of new products targeting higher price segments by local manufacturers may lead to breakthroughs, breaking the dominance of Apple and Samsung.

However, while domestic leading brands are striving to catch up with Samsung and Apple, their home-grown mid-to-low-end markets also face challenges from others. Reflecting back, it was precisely the close cooperation with operators on the special category of thousand-yuan smartphones that allowed the "Zhonghua Kulián" group to quickly gain scale and rise to the forefront of domestic brands, establishing their current advantageous position. But now, many domestic brands following closely behind, especially some emerging brands claiming to have an "internet gene" like Xiaomi, Dakele, and Hundred Percent, are also focusing on the thousand-yuan smartphone market, offering lower prices and higher configurations. Leveraging their cost-performance advantage, they have secured their footing and started eroding the territories of the leading domestic brands.

Prelude to a New Round of Consolidation

The seemingly prosperous yet crisis-laden scene bears a striking resemblance to the state of China's mobile phone market at the beginning of this century. At that time, it was also springtime for domestic mobile phone brands, with brands like Bird, TCL, Amoi, Kejian, and Panda brimming with confidence. In terms of market share, they had surpassed foreign brands like Nokia and Motorola, which were still dominant at the time. However, good times did not last long. Just a few years later, due to technical and product shortcomings, they succumbed to the counterattacks of foreign brands, and most either faltered or exited the historical stage during that round of consolidation.

Independent telecom analyst Fu Liang believes that the current competition in China's mobile phone market is not only extraordinarily fierce but also life-and-death struggle. In this competition, the "Zhonghua Kulián" quartet has gradually addressed many shortcomings, enhancing their overall competitiveness. Frequent releases of new phone models have provided more possibilities for them to enter more niche markets. However, this breakthrough will have a significant impact on smaller domestic brands. "By the end of next year, a large number of mobile phone manufacturers will gradually exit this chicken rib industry for them," Fu Liang stated, indicating that a major reshuffling of the mobile phone industry is about to begin.

Future Outlook

4G Licensing as a Key Factor

"Three years will be a window of opportunity. Three years later, some manufacturers will ascend to heaven, while others will descend to hell," He Shiyou predicted. The reason he gave three years is mainly because from the issuance of China's 4G license to the large-scale release of 4G phones, it will take about two to three years. And 4G will largely become the core factor in the next phase of competition among domestic mobile phone manufacturers.

At last month's communication exhibition, Minister of Industry and Information Technology Miao Wei publicly stated that the 4G license would definitely be issued within the year. Although the exact date is still unknown to outsiders, there is less than three months left in the year, so the countdown has begun for the new round of consolidation triggered by 4G competition.

Compared to 3G-era smartphones, 4G phones have much higher requirements in terms of antenna design, supported frequency bands, and chip solutions, raising the technical threshold accordingly. This presents an unavoidable challenge for some domestic brands.

In countries and regions where 4G services have already been commercially deployed on a large scale, international brands like Samsung, Apple, and LG have already introduced multiple mature 4G phones. Their technological advantages allow them to introduce 4G products faster in the Chinese market. For domestic leading brands like "Zhonghua Kulián," 4G phones are not a problem either, as they have already released or are about to release their 4G phones. Therefore, when China's 4G era truly arrives, these manufacturers with accumulated technology and reserves will have a clear leading advantage. On the contrary, smaller manufacturers with poorer technical accumulation will find it difficult to introduce competitive 4G products in a short period of time, and the elimination match in the market may start then.

Inventory Crisis Becomes Apparent

However, no matter how big the market, it cannot absorb continuously expanding production capacity. On one hand, domestic manufacturers continue to join the already crowded red ocean of mobile phones; on the other hand, some existing manufacturers are still expanding their production capacity, hoping to benefit more from this wave of prosperity. This inevitably causes overheating in the domestic mobile phone market. Additionally, the quality of domestic mobile phone brands is often criticized.

In He Shiyou's view, China's mobile phone market now resembles the country's photovoltaic industry in the past, with the hidden danger of serious overcapacity already planted. In fact, according to some insiders in the mobile phone industry, some mobile phone manufacturers have already encountered relatively serious inventory problems. For an industry like mobile phones, which is highly competitive and requires rapid turnover, an inventory crisis can be fatal.

Autonomous Chip Technology Seizes Opportunities

The most critical technical core for 4G phones is the chip solution, and China's 4G network will undoubtedly be dominated by TD-LTE. Currently, Qualcomm is the main provider of mainstream and mature TD-LTE chip solutions. From the few 4G phones that have passed the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology's network access certification, the vast majority use Qualcomm chips. Meanwhile, MediaTek and Spreadtrum, which mainly compete with Qualcomm on low prices and have attracted a large number of domestic manufacturers with price advantages, have not yet been able to mass-produce their 4G chips. This means that domestic mobile phone brands will have to rely more heavily on Qualcomm, and given the cost of Qualcomm chips, it will be difficult to see products at the thousand-yuan level or below in the early stages of the domestic 4G phone market.

However, the only exception among domestic manufacturers is Huawei. Among the 4G phones that have obtained domestic network access permits, Huawei's D2LTE version uses its own HiSilicon chip, giving Huawei more initiative in 4G products. Previously, Huawei's HiSilicon chips were mainly used in its P6, D1, and D2 series of mid-to-high-end phones, and in the 4G era, Huawei intends to expand its advantages using this. Wang Weijun, president of Huawei Terminal China region, told reporters that in the 4G era, Huawei will increase the usage rate of its self-developed chips, especially in the previously uninvolved thousand-yuan smartphone series, allowing Huawei to take the lead by about half a year in this market before its competitors can launch thousand-yuan level 4G phones.

In fact, the idea of gaining advantages from the chip end is not unique to Huawei. He Shiyou also revealed externally that ZTE Communications has been secretly developing its own 4G chip solution, which will be officially launched in the first half of next year, ending ZTE's history of relying on other vendors for phone chips. "Achieving autonomy in chips will be crucial for future market competition," He Shiyou said.
