15 Rules for Short-term Trading

by tuoqing1188 on 2011-08-03 13:16:34

1. Stop-loss is the most important part of short-term trading, and stop-loss should be controlled within 5%.

2. When the MDI index of the overall market or individual stocks falls below 20, you should promptly go empty.

3. Resolutely avoid long-term institutional stocks that have been heavily manipulated in the past, as well as those undergoing suicidal unloading or experiencing a broken capital chain.

4. After a sharp rise in stock price followed by heavy volume but stagnant growth, especially when the single-day turnover rate exceeds 10-20%, resolutely avoid such stocks.

5. Short-term trading experts never predict stock prices; respect market fluctuations and do not favor any particular stock.

6. If an individual stock pulls off 1-3 consecutive limit-up days and then opens high the next day but quickly turns to a sharp decline, with huge sell orders hanging above, and after these sell orders are filled, more huge sell orders appear consecutively, this is usually a method used by the company due to sudden bad news where the major player is trying to unload their shares through deception; don't buy into it.

7. Short-term trading experts must never attempt to buy at the lowest price and sell at the highest price, as this will result in losing more money-making opportunities.

8. Medium-term trading requires a clear understanding of national macroeconomic conditions and policy directions, and the company's annual return on investment should reach over 50%.

9. Market behavior encompasses all information, and prices evolve according to trends.

10. Understanding the concept that consistent profits are more important than large profits is crucial; no matter how much profit you make, if it’s accompanied by significant ups and downs, you're essentially staying in the same place.

11. If an individual stock opens lower by more than 1% at the start of the day, generally there won’t be much action that day; if it opens lower by over 3%, exit immediately after a slight rebound.

12. When the market plunges, patiently stay out of the market and wait for the right moment.

13. Guessing the bottom of a stock in a declining channel is the most dangerous action; even if it seems too cheap, don’t touch it.

14. When the market is weak, take profits from individual stocks early; when the market is strong, continue holding.

15. Grasping the right timing for entering the market is key to earning profits.

16. Successful securities investment is not about taking risks, but learning how to avoid them; many investors fail not because of incorrect analysis, but due to lack of a trading plan or failure to strictly follow a trading plan throughout: therefore, investors need strict risk control and psychological control.
