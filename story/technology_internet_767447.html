American billionaire still uses a flip phone

by anonymous on 2013-10-15 21:30:15

According to CNN, a screenshot of a video of American billionaire Jerry Jones using a flip phone was uploaded online recently and has sparked heated discussions among netizens. People are surprised that in the era dominated by smartphones, billionaire Jones still uses an outdated flip phone. In fact, there are more celebrities who use flip phones than just Jones.

When Jerry Jones appeared at AT&T Stadium in Arlington, Texas last week to watch an American football game, he somewhat "stole the show" as a member of the audience, and his "attention-grabbing tool" turned out to be a flip phone. In fact, the live broadcast of the game even switched the TV screen to Jones, showing him making a call with a flip phone.

The audience was clearly impressed by this scene, and the screenshot of Jones subsequently sparked heated discussions among netizens. To most people, flip phones have almost become "items in museums," so seeing a billionaire still using a flip phone is enough to surprise many.

Jones is definitely not someone who can't afford a smartphone; what surprises people is that he doesn't use one, despite usually appearing before the public as a "high-tech fan." For example, his AT&T Stadium has the world's largest and most advanced high-definition large screen, which is about 48.8 meters wide and 21.9 meters high. The stadium, also known as Cowboys Stadium, was built by Jones, the owner of the Dallas Cowboys, at a cost of $1.2 billion. Completed in 2009, the stadium has 10 entrances, equipped with the latest sports facilities, and can accommodate 100,000 spectators.

Due to the heated discussion caused by the photo of him using a flip phone to make a call, Jones was later invited by a radio station to discuss his thoughts on the matter. Jones said in the radio program: "The flip phone works well for me. It doesn't have a touchscreen, so there won't be any accidental dialing when it touches something."

Jones' explanation seems quite reasonable because when a smartphone is placed in a pocket or bag, the touchscreen may be accidentally touched by other objects. In certain circumstances, this touch could lead to the phone making a call, and if the call is answered, it could easily lead to the disclosure of the owner's privacy. For businesspeople like Jones, the risk of accidental dialing is too great, and even for ordinary people, this is a concern worth worrying about.

Of course, the accidental dialing of smartphones is a low-probability event, and the billionaire Jones, worth $3 billion, might just be accustomed to using flip phones. In today's era dominated by smartphones, many people have probably already forgotten what it feels like to use a flip phone, which is why they find the event of Jones using a flip phone surprising.

CNN journalists also listed some advantages of flip phones: for instance, smartphones are generally expensive, while flip phones are more "affordable"; flip phones are more durable because closing the cover when not in use protects the screen and buttons, whereas smartphones with a straight design are much more fragile.

In addition, since they don't have various mobile applications installed, flip phones obviously consume less power than smartphones; the existence of smartphones has made many people "smartphone addicts," constantly looking down to play with their phones, not only distracting attention but also reducing work efficiency.

Perhaps successful individuals like Jones choose flip phones because they won't distract them, allowing them to focus on work and careers. Smartphones are not just phones; they are high-tech "toys" with multiple functions combined into one, while flip phones are merely mobile phones used to contact others.
