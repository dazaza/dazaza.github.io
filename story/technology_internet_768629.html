Microsoft, watch out! Apple has its eyes on business users.

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 20:15:50

Microsoft and Dell, watch out - Apple is eyeing the meal on your plate with great interest.

For a long time, giants Microsoft and Dell, one in software and the other in hardware, have basically split most of the profits in the enterprise sector. However, Apple, the king of personal consumption, has set its sights on this lucrative market.

Apple aims to take over Microsoft and Dell's territory

Chief Analyst Ted Schadler of Forrester Research said: "Although Apple has not yet dominated the computer industry, it is growing stronger every day. Today's Apple is no longer content with ruling the personal consumption sector; it wants to seize the territory of Microsoft and Dells, aiming to snatch profits from the jaws of giants dominating the enterprise sector."

In order to please enterprise users, Apple is planning a series of stimulating actions, including reducing hardware costs, offering free software, and enhancing software security, among others.

In fact, in recent years, Apple has started to venture into the enterprise sector and has achieved some success in certain areas. Recently, research data from Forrester Research showed that in North America, 18% of corporate employees said they are using Apple products for work, whereas just a few years ago, this figure was only 3%.

What makes Apple's products enter the office?

Actually, it’s quite simple – employees increasingly prefer to use Apple products at work.

Trip Chowdhry, an analyst at Global Equities Research, believes: "The biggest driving force behind Apple's mobile devices and laptops becoming office tools is the love consumers or employees have for Apple products."

"Apple's products are casual and can reduce user stress to a certain extent. Its user interface is centered around the user, where Microsoft falls short. Employees feel more efficient when working with Apple products, and IT managers in enterprises should be aware of this."

Apple is actively preparing to enter the enterprise market

Of course, customer approval alone isn't enough; Apple needs to be more proactive and positive. It is understood that in order to achieve something significant in the enterprise sector, Apple has already begun recruiting talent. On Monday, a job posting for an enterprise sales representative appeared on LinkedIn, a professional social networking site. Moreover, as early as early October, Apple had placed recruitment advertisements near BlackBerry's headquarters, clearly aiming to poach BlackBerry's enterprise sales elites.

According to industry analysts, Apple's sales team has traditionally been strong in segmented markets such as education, but in the enterprise market, Apple's salespeople have always seemed underpowered.

Regarding this, Chowdhry said: "Apple has never had a dedicated sales team for Mac desktops, but even so, Mac has achieved great success worldwide. So, what effect would arise if Apple were to assign specialized sales personnel for Mac? I believe that upon hearing this news, companies like Microsoft, Dell, and HP will surely feel uneasy."

Consumer News and Business Channel CNBC reached out to insiders within Apple regarding whether Apple will increase its efforts in the enterprise sector, but Apple remained tight-lipped.

Apple will focus heavily on mobile development

Undeniably, in the enterprise sector, the price of Apple's hardware products does not match up well against Dell and HP, and its office software lacks a clear advantage compared to Microsoft's Office. Therefore, Apple will shift its focus to mobile terminal devices, seeking to open up new revenue streams in the enterprise sector through iPads, iPhones, and other mobile devices.

Research data from Forrester Research shows that by 2017, tablet sales in the enterprise market are expected to capture an 18% share, which is undoubtedly good news for Apple, with its absolute advantage in the tablet field.

However, Gartner analyst Carolina Milanesi stated: "Apple's move is somewhat too aggressive because enterprise employees need to figure out one thing – what exactly can iPads do during the work process and how to use them effectively?"

It seems Peter Oppenheimer, Apple's CFO, also shares similar concerns. During Apple's quarterly earnings call at the end of last month, Peter particularly emphasized making Apple's mobile devices more suitable for office use.

Peter said: "In fact, globally, 35,000 companies and millions of developers are creating customized apps specifically for iPad and iPhone."

Data published in August by mobile app seller Good Technology showed that in the second and third quarters of this year, Apple's iOS platform accounted for 98% and 95%, respectively, of all enterprise mobile apps. However, just a few years ago, Apple's mobile devices struggled to gain favor from enterprise IT departments due to their high cost and lack of control features.

But now, thanks to the explosive growth and targeted optimization of iPhone and iPad mobile devices, Apple is not far from becoming a major supplier in the enterprise sector.
