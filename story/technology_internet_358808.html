English Business Meeting Guide

by tlwll4w1 on 2010-06-23 11:22:45

Nowadays, many professionals are asked to chair and run meetings with very little notice. This can be stressful and time consuming, especially if you are required to do so in English. In order to help you succeed, you should consider how best to run an efficient and effective meeting, as well as which language to use. Here are some tips to help both sides:

1. Inform and remind everyone of the reason for attending the meeting.

By notifying participants beforehand, they will not only be less likely to forget, but they will also have any supporting materials or topics that need discussion at hand.

English Communication Skills:

Email:

"Dear John Shanghai Spoken English Class

I just wanted to remind you that the meeting is scheduled for tomorrow at 14:30 in the boardroom to discuss the progress of the Paris project. We look forward to hearing how the sales team has performed over the past three months.

I am still waiting to receive your written report prior to the meeting. Could you please send it to me as soon as possible and copy the relevant people?

Thanks.

Best regards,

Simon"

2. Minimize the number of participants.

Only invite those who are truly necessary. As the number of attendees tends to rise, so does the tendency to get lost. You will find that even those who are not required to attend often show up hoping to learn something useful.

English Communication Skills:

Speaking:

Ben: "Do I need to be at the meeting tomorrow, Simon?"

Simon: "No, thank you Ben. Actually, we don't need you this time. John will let you know our decision."

Ben: "Alright, no problem. I'm sure he'll fill me in afterward."

3. Establish and implement an agenda.

As a rule, every meeting should have an agenda with at least three points; the topic, allocated time for each topic, and the 'lead', who is responsible for leading a particular topic.

English Communication Skills:

Writing:

"Agenda for the Paris Project Progress Meeting at 14:00 on February 17, 2010 in the Boardroom

1 Chairman's introduction by Simon - 5 minutes

2 Review of action points from last meeting by Simon - 10 minutes

3 Marketing update by Jane - 20 minutes

4 Logistics overview by David - 20 minutes

5 Recruitment update by Stephen - 15 minutes

6 Introduction of promotional strategy by Simon - 20 minutes

Set time and date for next meeting

Closing"

4. State the purpose and expected outcomes.

The leader of the meeting should not assume that everyone automatically understands the purpose of the meeting and the expected outcomes. By stating them clearly at the beginning, the meeting will start promptly and continue to stay on track.

English Communication Skills:

Spoken:

"Good afternoon everyone. Thank you for coming this afternoon and thank you for your reports. I've asked you here today to check that we're all making enough progress on the Paris project. I look forward to hearing brief updates from each of you on how you're doing. Once we've completed this stage of the meeting, I'll move on to your promotional strategies. If we manage time properly, there should be time for questions. David, are you taking the minutes today?"

5. Try to involve each team member.

Meetings can sometimes become unbalanced when participants do all the talking and dominate the discussion. The leader of the meeting should strive to involve each group member to ensure synergy occurs.

English Communication Skills:

Spoken:

"Thank you, John. Sorry to interrupt, but I'd like to hear what Jane thinks about this point as well."

6. Use charts, pictures, and graphics.

"Show, don't tell" applies absolutely to real meetings. This can reduce the duration of meetings by up to 30%. Whenever practical, encourage participants to use charts, photos, and graphics instead of pages full of text.

7. Park important, but off-topic subjects.

Sometimes, an important topic may arise that isn't on the agenda. Rather than shifting the focus of the meeting, it might be better to list (or 'park') it as a topic for another meeting.

English Communication Skills:

Spoken:

"That sounds interesting, Jane, but could we add it to the agenda for the next meeting? I would like to move on."

8. Record action items, the person responsible, and deadlines.

Most of the time spent in meetings is highly interactive, but if the results aren't converted into action items, they may not yield tangible results. Almost every meeting should end with) the actions to be taken, b) who is responsible for the actions, and c) when the actions are due.

English Communication Skills:

Writing:

"Action to be taken: Complete by person: Deadline:

David to compile supplier list - Monday, March 1st"

9. Issue a short written meeting summary.

Taking a few minutes to record the meeting can go a long way. By summarizing 1) who attended, 2) the main topics discussed, 3) the conclusions reached, and 4) the actions to be taken, information will be easily recorded and disseminated. The meeting summary should be kept to one page containing 'bullet-point' statements.

10. Regularly measure the effectiveness of meetings.

Like any area of your work, it is beneficial to think about how effectively meetings are actually run. Then you can make changes to improve things and avoid the cultural 'way we do things here.'
