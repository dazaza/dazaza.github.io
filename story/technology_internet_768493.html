Twitter's User Growth Strategy: Integration with Media and Developer Innovation

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 18:55:39

With a market value of $25 billion, Twitter's management feels as if sitting on pins and needles—Twitter has too few users, only one fifth of Facebook's, which will become the decisive factor in determining Twitter’s advertising revenue and the trend of its market value. According to foreign media reports, Twitter has already implemented multiple strategies to increase its user base, among which the most important is to launch peripheral tools through third-party developers around Twitter, as well as to integrate Twitter microblogging information into more external media websites.

With tens of millions of daily posts by users, this is an inexhaustible resource. Twitter is encouraging external developers to launch innovative tools, attracting new users for Twitter while gaining new sources of advertising revenue.

Gozaik is such a peripheral tool developer. The company's system scans nearly 500 million posts on Twitter every day, identifying job postings released by corporate employers. They then organize and index these job postings, making it convenient for job seekers to search.

The company also automatically scans the profiles of Twitter's 200 million users, recommending ideal recruitment candidates to corporate employers.

According to statistics, 15 job postings are published per minute on Twitter, reaching 150,000 per week. Recruitment and job hunting have become a perennial hot topic on Twitter.

In addition to passive information searches, Gozaik has also developed an advertising tool that can send job postings to specific Twitter users who meet certain criteria. This system is still in the testing phase, using Twitter's real-time ad bidding system. If successful, Twitter will be able to generate more revenue from corporate recruitment and internet users' job hunting, much like LinkedIn.

It is reported that the above-mentioned efforts to mine Twitter's big data have received support from Twitter, allowing Gozaik free access to scan all user microblog posts.

Other third-party companies, such as Tame and Nuzzel, are also launching innovative products leveraging Twitter's own shortcomings.

Currently, Twitter's main interface displays microblog posts from newest to oldest without considering the importance of the posts. A startup company called Tame, supported by German government funds, has launched an innovative user interface.

Tame scans and indexes user posts, displaying them in three columns: links, topics, and people. The content in these three columns is refined based on how frequently they are mentioned by users. However, Tame's service is not free, charging users €5 per month.

In addition to expanding user appeal through third-party tools, another "going out" approach by Twitter is to integrate microblogging information into more external websites. Currently, this business is overseen by an executive named Chloe Sladden (Vice President of Media Affairs), whose task is to integrate microblogging information into news websites, TV stations, and the entertainment industry.

As we all know, whenever major news events occur, they always trigger passionate discussions among netizens on Twitter. If partnerships with mass media can broadcast these heated discussions on Twitter in real time, it will undoubtedly attract new users.

Twitter confirmed to the outside world that there are no fees for collaborations integrating microblogging information with news media. Clearly, Twitter hopes to increase its exposure to the general public. Previously, a survey by the Pew Research Center showed that Twitter's penetration rate among American adults is only 16%, making it a typical niche product, unlike Facebook, which has become a universally popular service suitable for all ages and well-known households.

However, it should be noted that for third-party software developers who "rely on Twitter for survival," there is still a risk: Twitter may one day rewrite the rules of the game. Last year, Twitter suddenly modified its development interface, cutting off the lifeline for a large number of applications surrounding Twitter, including third-party microblog interfaces that once helped Twitter gain users.
