Microsoft Launches Latest Search Engine Bing

On May 28, 2009, reports emerged that Microsoft would roll out a new search engine service this week. The project had been developed under the code name Kumo and was officially named Bing. Yahoo Search also adjusted the content returned in its search results. Moreover, the new search engine service WolframAlpha went live the previous week. In WolframAlpha, when users enter their query into the search box, the search engine directly returns answers instead of a large number of web links.

Previously released statistics by the U.S. internet traffic monitoring agency comScore showed that during April of this year, Google's share of the U.S. search market was 64.2%, while Yahoo! and Microsoft held 20.4% and 8.2%, respectively. Clearly, Google maintained its dominant position in the U.S. search market. To unseat Google, both new and old competitors like Microsoft, Yahoo!, and WolframAlpha must adopt other indirect strategies.

Carl Howe, technology analyst at Yankee Group, commented: "If you want to compete with Google, direct confrontation is not feasible."

Since February last year, Microsoft has engaged in multiple rounds of negotiations with Yahoo! hoping to merge their search businesses to jointly challenge Google. At the D7 Technology Conference hosted on Wednesday by the Wall Street Journal blog site D: All Things Digital, Yahoo!'s CEO Carol Bartz stated that current negotiations between Yahoo! and Microsoft have made some progress, but no substantial agreements have yet been reached.

Sandeep Aggarwal, an analyst at the British investment bank Collins Stewart, predicted that Microsoft and Yahoo! are likely to reach a deal on the merger of their search businesses by the end of July this year.

Aggarwal believed that after Google established its leading position in the U.S. search market, if it continues on its current trajectory, its market share will continue to grow. Most of Google's search traffic comes from its search homepage, whereas Microsoft's and Yahoo!'s search traffic mainly originates from their respective portals and associated content service pages. As a result, the difficulty for Microsoft and Yahoo! to challenge Google significantly increases. Under these circumstances, Google has become synonymous with online searches.

Susan Feldman, an analyst at the well-known U.S. market research company IDC, said: "Everyone has habitual inertia. The most crucial aspect in increasing search market share is not about advancing technology, but convincing users to switch to your search engine."

Performance of the New Search Engine Bing

Regarding Microsoft’s upcoming new version of the search engine Bing, Aggarwal, who has already tried Bing, remarked that based on preliminary usage results, the relevance of Bing's search results has greatly improved. Its returned results differ not only from those of Google and Yahoo! but even show significant differences compared to Microsoft's current Live Search.

Bing's Intelligence in Search Results

Aggarwal explained that after users input keywords, Bing categorizes the search results into different groups, with each group containing highly relevant results. For example, if a user types in "flights to New York City," the corresponding return group might include information about New York hotels and tourist attractions. Since the relevant search results have been roughly classified, it becomes more convenient for users.

Aggarwal believes that Bing's search technology itself has indeed made significant improvements. If handled properly, Bing could potentially become a destination for internet users to conduct searches, much like Google's search homepage. Aggarwal said: "The main reason Microsoft's Live Search failed was due to immature technology. However, Microsoft currently believes that Bing's technology is now very robust."

A Microsoft spokesperson declined to comment on Aggarwal's statements. Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer was also invited to attend the D7 conference and is scheduled to speak there on Thursday (local time). He is expected to reveal more details about Bing at that time.
