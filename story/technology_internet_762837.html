"The Inevitable 'Death of Yougou': Belle's E-commerce Has Many Fatal Flaws"

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 17:36:43

Recently, Yougou.com, which has been suffering from a severe loss of executives, was hit again. On July 17th, news came that according to insiders at Yougou.com, the company's COO Zhang Xiaojun will be leaving and is currently processing his resignation. As one of the founding members of Yougou.com, Zhang Xiaojun’s departure is indicative of a serious crisis facing Yougou.com.

Let's first review the e-commerce journey of Belle International, to which Yougou.com belongs:

Belle quietly experimented with e-commerce several years ago, developing steadily through Taobao distribution, Tmall, and B2C distribution (B2C distribution was completed via orders placed on Taoshow.com). Led by veteran group member Xie Yunli, a not-so-lavish e-commerce team initially sold products under Belle's own brands, then subsequently launched exclusive online models targeted at the internet market, followed by new internet-based brands. This development path was considered a successful case of a traditional brand entering the e-commerce space more solidly than Li-Ning before 2011.

However, with the over $100 million financing and rapid growth of companies like Letao.com and HaoLeMai in 2010, Belle Group felt compelled to act. On one hand, there was concern about being controlled by channels again; on the other hand, an evaluation revealed significant development advantages in the e-commerce sector, including:

One, numerous brands: In addition to its several major women's shoe brands, Belle also saw astonishing growth in the online sales of internationally renowned sports brands such as Adidas and Nike that it represented;

Two, easier category expansion: For Belle Group, which owns its own women's shoe brands and represents well-known sports brands, how to gain absolute market advantage and status through multiple brands and categories became the most thought-provoking issue over the years. Expanding brands and categories through physical stores was obviously too slow, while the existing e-commerce business could easily expand brands but not quickly enough in terms of categories;

Three, financial advantage: In cooperation with e-commerce enterprises, Belle realized that e-commerce is a capital-intensive business. However, Belle Group's financial strength allowed it to develop without relying on external capital, thereby enabling it to defeat many vertical e-commerce companies that must depend on capital, becoming the "survivor" in the industry;

Four, talent advantage: After several years of e-commerce practice, Belle had cultivated a highly professional and strongly execution-oriented e-commerce team. The cultivation of talent could meet the scale development needs of the e-commerce business or even higher;

Five, supply chain advantage: This excited Belle internally and was the core reason for attracting high-level executives from brand e-commerce or platform e-commerce. After all, supply chains are always fascinating to e-commerce businesses because they seem to be the part that decides life and death aside from funding. Supply chains include a series of "business" contents such as product research and development, production, warehouse management, delivery, and after-sales service. In traditional enterprises that prioritize "commerce" over "electronics," this is considered the most competitive advantage over e-commerce companies.

Based on these advantages, Belle E-commerce decided to make a big move, first purchasing the double-pinyin domain name yougou.com,

abandoning the somewhat copycat “topshoes.cn” Taoshow website for redirection, stopping supplies to all distribution websites. Although it retained direct third-party online stores such as Taobao and Tmall, the investment and focus decreased. It recruited JD.com VP and Vancl VP with high salaries and fanfare to serve as CMO and COO respectively, while appointing a senior group member as CEO, forming a golden triangle. They aimed to build a full-category vertical B2C mall, implementing multi-brand strategies on one hand, expanding into clothing and other categories on the other, realizing the dream of a department store empire impossible to achieve offline!

All of this sounded wonderful, but every dream needs to face the harsh reality of its backbone. The reality is:

One, higher technical requirements for independent B2C: Located in Shenzhen headquarters, Belle couldn't recruit more top technical talents, and technology outsourcing services were scarce in Shenzhen. However, technology determines user experience, backend management efficiency, and reliable data. Independent B2C falls under dynamic technology, different from the relatively static technologies like ERP, continuous changes remain a hurdle that many traditional enterprises cannot overcome;

Two, increased management difficulty due to category expansion: Someone once said that doing well in women's shoes does not necessarily mean success in men's shoes, something Belle probably deeply understood when expanding its men's shoe category. It's easy to imagine the difficulties encountered at the backend when expanding to women's clothing and accessories within just a year. For traditional brand enterprises accustomed to meticulous calculations, such a rapid approach worked for Vancl during special times, understandable within the systems of Vancl and JD.com. However, whether Belle, having acquired Vancl's COO and JD.com's CMO, can be understood and executed with "exaggerated costs" remains a big question;

Three, system: This is something hard to understand unless one has crossed both e-commerce and traditional enterprises. Simply put, it involves department settings (organizational structure), approvals, finance and budgeting, reporting methods, corporate culture, communication styles, execution methods, risk control, processes and systems that constitute an enterprise's so-called "mechanism." The result of this conflict is that e-commerce tries to convince and accept the "system" of e-commerce, while the traditional team attempts to understand but can never fully grasp the "system" of e-commerce, leading to low efficiency and instability in the execution team. Ultimately, the chairman or board may say: "E-commerce and traditional enterprises each have their strengths and weaknesses, and the future is certainly fusion. So, in these areas, you must also integrate..." If this so-called "fusion without harmony" occurs, it leads to what is called management innovation, and thus, the "CEO rotation system" actually appeared in a company like Belle!

It was originally believed that Yougou.com of Belle's e-commerce would take the lead, with top executives awaiting the glory of spin-off listing, enjoying success and wealth, countless examples of traditional enterprises succeeding in the e-commerce era. Instead, they are now entangled and distressed by these issues.

After all, whether it's the veterans who grew up with Belle or the e-commerce VPs hired from elsewhere, it is clear that they did not leave due to lack of ability. When the VP from JD.com returned to JD.com, he continued to perform excellently; when the VP from Vancl had the courage to start his own business. Are these three points the hard injuries? Or are these not only Belle's hard injuries but also those of many traditional enterprises attempting to enter e-commerce?
