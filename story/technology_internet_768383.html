Winners and Losers in the 3-D Printing Wave

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 17:46:20

An Affordable 3D Printer

Nokia sold its terminal and services division to Microsoft, and BlackBerry is also up for sale; it's hard to recall their once dominant positions in the mobile phone industry. Just a few short years ago, more than 50% of smartphone sales were Nokia’s, and BlackBerry had a market value as high as $47 billion.

It goes without saying that Apple's iPhone disrupted everything. One habit of disruptive innovation is to kill off old things and transform entire industries, creating many winners and losers along the way. What other innovations might have disruptive effects? 3D printing is currently still in its infancy, but it has undoubtedly already demonstrated astonishing potential to change the world.

What is 3D printing?

At the Clinton Global Initiative conference held in New York in September this year, Bre Pettis, CEO of MakerBot Industries, gave a speech while holding a 3D-printed prosthetic hand. If you're unfamiliar with 3D printing, just think about the fictional replicator from "Star Trek." You give the computer instructions to make whatever you want, and then it miraculously appears. In today's tech jargon, this means uploading computerized blueprints from your computer to a 3D printer, after which this microwave or washing machine-sized, box-shaped printer melts plastic filament or other materials bit by bit, layer by layer, like stacking Lego bricks. It eventually solidifies into the product you desire.

What have people successfully printed? From simple figurines to pipe parts, there are now countless items being printed every day. The real limitation lies only in imagination—there are statues made from sand, organs printed from tissue, cars, guns, and even NASA rocket engines.

Why does 3D printing have such great disruptive potential? It democratizes manufacturing capabilities. Historically, new manufacturing capabilities that achieve democratization remove entry barriers or high entry costs, erasing the wide moats or competitive advantages owned by established companies, thus repeatedly disrupting related industries.

For example, home computers and blogs enabled ordinary people to write content themselves, causing traditional printing industries to suffer widespread casualties. (Traditional printing machines can cost several million dollars.) Software development kits available everywhere, such as free Android programming kits and $99 iOS kits, have completely disrupted traditional software development industries like video games.

Who wins, who loses?

But how will 3D printing impact various industries today? While the answer isn't yet clear, there are likely many correct answers. Current common 3D printing technology is limited to relatively simple small components like figurines and machine parts. These industries have low profit margins, exhibit significant economies of scale, and are primarily produced by extremely cheap foreign labor. Producing small construction materials for Home Depot or souvenirs for roadside stalls may be big business for some, but they won’t spark a revolution in manufacturing—China could be an exception since China's specialized small manufacturers account for a large proportion.

However, with advancements in technology making complex parts easier to produce, the future possibilities of 3D printing indeed exist. As supply chain costs and infrastructure change, companies that heavily use parts and raw materials, such as car manufacturers, are likely to benefit. From automobiles and aerospace to education and even healthcare, 3D printing has already launched major assaults in many fields.

Although the current 3D printing industry is small and fragmented, the number of related listed companies is astonishing. The two largest are Stratasys and 3D Systems, the latter of which now has a market value approaching $6 billion. In comparison, HP, the leader in the 2D printing field, currently has a market value of $45 billion.

There are two relatively smaller companies: ExOne and Voxeljet, which just went public. There are other stocks related to 3D printing, but these mentioned companies are more pure-play and well-known.

Through several acquisitions over the past few years, 3D Systems has become one of the largest and most comprehensive 3D printing companies in the world, with annual revenue exceeding $400 million. Stratasys is no small player either, with a market capitalization of $4 billion and annual revenue of over $300 million, though its recent performance has been mixed.

Momentum?

So far, these companies have seen impressive stock performances. 3D Systems has risen 60% since the beginning of the year, Stratasys has risen 38%, ExOne has surged 105% since its IPO at the beginning of the year, and Voxeljet has skyrocketed 121% since its IPO on October 18. However, their price-to-earnings ratios seem too high, and their fundamentals are debatable. Stratasys and ExOne have recently been incurring losses, while 3D Systems stands out so far with positive profits and earnings per share. Nevertheless, revenue growth is a highlight across all these companies.

Beyond individual companies, the entire 3D printing industry also appears poised for strong growth. Although the market was only $1.7 billion in size in 2011, it is expected to triple in the coming years. Research firm Gartner recently forecasted a 75% explosive growth in low-cost printer shipments over the next year. The anticipated growth rate for higher-priced commercial printers is similarly high, with estimated corporate spending rising from $325 million in 2013 to $536 million in 2014.

One thing to keep in mind when investing in these pioneer companies is something familiar to many early movers: being first out of the gate doesn't necessarily mean winning the race. Where are Lycos or Altavista today compared to Google?

That said, given the explosive growth of the entire industry and their status as pioneers, once larger companies belatedly realize they've missed opportunities and rush to jump on the 3D printing bandwagon, these companies could become priority acquisition targets. These hypothetical scenarios paint a relatively bright future for investing in the 3D printing industry.
