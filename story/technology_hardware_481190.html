Tragically, it is often at the moment of loss that people remember to focus on strategy. I think the real challenge is how to make people pay more attention to strategy, even in times when there is no crisis." — Michael Porter

Today, in China's business community, especially for small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs), their misfortune has been predicted by this man named Porter, and the new challenge for SMEs lies precisely here!

As Porter said, many people do not care about strategy. Strategy, like corporate culture, is usually written on the walls of their own companies in slogan form or appears in promotional brochures shown to clients. Conversely, few within the company's management and employees understand it; during the execution process, very few consider the strategy or how their work contributes to the achievement of the strategy.

The reason for this lies in the two most common misconceptions about strategy prevalent among SMEs:

One misconception is that strategy is impractical and written for others to see. Due to this misconception, whenever the word "strategy" is mentioned, it feels like a waste of time, something that high-level managers come up with as a "deceptive gimmick."

The second misconception revolves around the question: "Is strategy more important or are details more important?" Most people believe that large enterprises need strategy while small enterprises need details.

Regarding the first misconception, is "strategy really impractical" or a "deceptive gimmick"? In fact, in businesses, especially in the marketing field, there is no action more practical than strategy. If a company lacks a strategy, it's like having a disunited heart, unable to effectively organize itself, unable to form core competitiveness, and even less likely to develop healthily.

The practicality of strategy manifests in at least the following five aspects:

Without strategy, there won't be clear objectives.

Without strategy, there won't be any true advantages.

Without strategy, there won't be efficient execution.

Without strategy, there won't be accurate innovation.

Without strategy, there won't be sustainable development.

For the second misconception, is it correct? No, it's incorrect! In fact, SMEs need strategy even more than large enterprises. For SMEs, strategy will determine survival or death. Large enterprises have sufficient strength, so even if they make strategic mistakes, they still have the ability to adjust or compensate. Welch once said, GE has enough ammunition, so even if some shots miss, it doesn't affect the overall situation. Therefore, giant corporations like Coca-Cola and IBM have experienced strategic confusion, but it hasn't had life-or-death impacts on their development. However, small enterprises aren't so lucky. A single mistake due to lack of proper strategic guidance can deplete all their resources and lead to catastrophic failure.

Kenichi Ohmae, known as the Father of Japanese Strategy, wrote in his book "The Mind of the Strategist": What use is strategy without competition? Indeed, competition is now the norm and becoming increasingly fierce, posing the greatest threat to SMEs. Therefore, SMEs need strategy even more!

In the fierce competition, industries are constantly reshuffling. If you don't become the first or second best, you will be eliminated by those who are. There's no room for failure—only success! Moving from survival to success is a realistic issue, a matter of life or death! According to research interviews conducted by "Scientific Investment," the average lifespan of Chinese SMEs is roughly 3-4 years. Nearly one million SMEs in China go bankrupt every year, making the dream of building a century-old enterprise merely wishful thinking. One of the key reasons for the short lifespan of SMEs is the problem of strategy.

For large enterprises, strategy is about living better and growing faster; for SMEs, strategy is about whether they can survive at all.

There is a harsh saying in the industry: A company without a strategic plan is planning for its own demise.

Despite this, why don't Chinese SMEs take strategy seriously?

Moreover, why are many Chinese companies currently able to survive without a strategy?

The lack of strategy among Chinese SMEs is widespread, which is related to the opportunities available in China's past...
