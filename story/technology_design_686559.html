The TV series "Blood Red Western Hunan" by Bai Jing is set against the backdrop of the magnificent history of the Hunan Anti-Japanese War. It tells a tragic story from 1939-1945, during which period, all the people in Maxi town of Western Hunan, regardless of gender and age, vowed to defend their homes under the leadership of Bai Jing's character. The whole drama is full of twists and turns, on a grand scale, showcasing the unique personality and fierce local customs of the people on this primitive and simple land of Western Hunan, who are full of blood and courage, and dare to love and hate. While we are moved by the plot, we are also fascinated by the unique scenery of Western Hunan. Although Bai Jing's "Blood Red Western Hunan" seems to have faded from people's view after so long, the recent news of Bai Jing's death reminds us of that beautiful girl and the beautiful land of Western Hunan.

Shooting location one of Bai Jing's TV series "Blood Red Western Hunan": Hongshilin

According to geological experts, the rocks of Hongshilin were formed about 450 million years ago. The area of Hongshilin, like Zuolongxi Canyon, was once known as Yangzi Ancient Sea in geological history. The seabed deposited a large amount of carbonate material mixed with sand, and after tectonic movements and erosion and dissolution, this beautiful geological wonder was formed. Famous stone scenes include Shu Dogs Barking at the Sun, Swallows Waiting for Feeding, Snail Moving House, Loulan Ancient City, Seven Color Maze, etc., and there are also underground caves, sheer cliffs, natural pits, millennium ancient trees, etc. The entire scenic area integrates red, beauty, steepness, strangeness, uniqueness, and antiquity into one body, known as the "First Wonder of Wuling".

Shooting location two of Bai Jing's TV series "Blood Red Western Hunan": Old Courtyard of Western Hunan

The Old Courtyard of Western Hunan, a key cultural relic protection unit in Hunan Province, is located on the east side of Lusibay Bridge in Zhangjiajie city, and is the best-preserved ancient Tujia residence in the whole of Western Hunan and even nationwide, known as the first Tujia residence. The old courtyard, also known as Zijing Hall, was built in the early years of the Yongzheng era of the Qing Dynasty. Its architectural style is a Tujia building with an enclosed sky well and fireproof wall, integrating Tujia gardens and hanging foot buildings, typical of Tujia residential houses. The old courtyard is very spacious, with an old house made of blue bricks and black tiles lying stably in the south, while the pair of yellow hemp stone lions in front of the door look mischievous. All the chests, wardrobes, dressing tables, carved beds, screens, chairs, calligraphy and paintings displayed in the old courtyard are original precious items.

Shooting location three of Bai Jing's TV series "Blood Red Western Hunan": Furong Town

Furong Town was originally named Wang Village, located in Yongshun County, Xiangxi Tujia and Miao Autonomous Prefecture, Hunan Province. It sits in the canyon plain at the junction of Hunan, Guangdong, and Guangxi provinces, and is an ancient town where Tujia people gather. The town still preserves intact bluestone roads and ancient residences (mainly stilt houses), with two waterways winding around the town, flowing out of the town mouth about a mile away before converging, thus being surrounded by water on three sides, forming a narrow and long peninsula-like terrain; situated in the canyon plain, it has historically been a place for merchants to rest overnight, heroes to gather, and strategists to compete for military advantages.

Shooting location four of Bai Jing's TV series "Blood Red Western Hunan": Phoenix Ancient City

The scenery of Phoenix blends the natural and cultural characteristics organically, and the heavy feeling after insight may just be the essence of its charm that attracts tourists from all over. This "Phoenix Ancient City," once called one of the most beautiful small cities in China by New Zealand writer Rewi Alley, was built in the Kangxi era of the Qing Dynasty. This "Pearl of Western Hunan" is truly "small," so small that there is only one decent east-west street in the city, but it is a green corridor.
