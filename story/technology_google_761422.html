Sci-fi writer Gibson tries out Google Glass

by geekzhang on 2013-05-03 13:34:09

Recently, William Gibson tried out Google Glass and then shared his thoughts on Twitter.I also got to try Google Glass, if only for a few seconds. I was faintly annoyed at just how interesting I found the experience.The phrase "faintly annoyed" is quite thought-provoking. Attitudes towards Google Glass are often polarized: either fervent support or cold opposition. However, what Gibson expresses seems to be a subtle concern.Gibson is a renowned science fiction writer whose classic work, *Neuromancer*, pioneered a new subgenre of science fiction: cyberpunk. In this novel, he coined the widely recognized term, Cyberspace.The Co.Design website argues that compared to the World Wide Web or the information superhighway, Cyberspace more accurately describes what the Internet has truly become: an entirely new domain, detached from the world we live in. In other words, an independent space that exists freely and autonomously.In a recent interview, Gibson revealed the origin of the word.Gibson wanted to find a new realm for his science fiction novels. Most of what he encountered in his childhood revolved around space travel, but it didn't resonate with him, and he didn't want to write post-apocalyptic wasteland stories either.He hoped to find another sense of a country, a different sense of journey for the protagonist, but he didn't know exactly how to write it. So, he began seeking inspiration from reality. He saw new worlds where people could immerse themselves.Some fragments that came to my mind were kids playing the earliest large arcade games. Their body language was one of intense longing and focus. When I saw arcades that I might have been afraid to walk into, I felt they wanted to go through the mirror; they wanted to be with Pong or something else. You could see they needed it, and I think I saw that soon they would get more complex games than Pong. They did.He also observed the development of personal computers and the budding era of networking.I started hearing about people remotely connecting computers via telephone lines. Fortunately, I knew nothing about computers, which allowed me to mix all these things together and form a vague image of the field I wanted to create.Data space didn't fit, information space didn't fit. Cyberspace—it sounded like it meant something, but after seeing the word written in red marker on a yellow notepad, my entire excitement was that I knew it meant absolute nothingness.Nowadays, Cyberspace is vast enough and dangerous enough. How much time do we spend each day in virtual spaces, feeling unable to survive without the internet? Google Glass aims to reduce our immersion time and focus more on the real world. But is it the right antidote, or a disguised drug? As we obtain information in real-time, will our information addiction worsen or ease? We may not lower our heads and isolate ourselves from the world, but will we look elsewhere and be mentally absent from the world?For this uncertain future, do you feel a subtle unease as well?
