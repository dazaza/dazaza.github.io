Xian Ni full text browse Grandma kneels down and begs on the street to save her granddaughter suffering from kidney failure

by nbthwjjj220 on 2012-02-09 16:46:58

Yesterday, at Southwest Hospital, Wen Lifei had her blood drawn accompanied by her mother. This photo was taken by our reporter Gan Xiangyi. Yesterday morning, under the bright sunshine, 18-year-old Zunyi girl Wen Lifei basked in the golden sunlight, a rare smile appearing on her pale face. With 3700 yuan in cash, Wen Lifei's mother brought her from Zunyi to Southwest Hospital, hoping to open another door of life for her daughter. Among these 3700 yuan, some came from the income of selling pastries by Wen Lifei's parents, some were donations from kind-hearted citizens in Zunyi, and others were obtained by Wen Lifei's grandmother begging on the street...

Suddenly afflicted with a chronic illness, an 18-year-old girl from Zunyi is critically ill. If she hadn't been diagnosed with chronic renal failure, Wen Lifei would now be sitting in the classroom of her dream medical university, studying with classmates how to save lives. But unfortunately, after being diagnosed last year, Wen Lifei helplessly became someone who needed to be saved.

Wen Lifei was a high school student at the Miao Autonomous County National Middle School in Zunyi, known as a very sensible child by teachers, classmates, and family members due to her excellent grades, earning her the title of "model student" every year. For Wen Lifei, the biggest dream was to get into a medical university in the upcoming college entrance exam.

However, an unexpected disease closed the door to Wen Lifei's dreams. Three years ago, Wen Lifei was diagnosed with nephritis at the Wuchuan Autonomous County Hospital. Although she took medicine for treatment, there was no improvement. By the end of last year, her condition began to worsen, with repeated swelling all over her body. After examination at the hospital, she was diagnosed with chronic renal failure.

Wen Lifei was forced to drop out of school, and her young life could only be sustained by dialysis twice a week, resulting in enormous medical expenses that became a heavy burden on her family.

Like all left-behind children in the countryside, Wen Lifei's parents worked in distant big cities when she was only a few years old. For the young Wen Lifei, her grandmother, Zou Shuwei, became her surrogate "mother," taking care of all aspects of her life from childhood to adulthood.

In Zou Shuwei's eyes, her granddaughter had long become an irreplaceable part of her life. When her granddaughter was diagnosed with chronic renal failure, this physically strong 73-year-old woman cried every day, watching her granddaughter grow thinner day by day, her smiles gradually disappearing, which caused immense pain for the elder.

After Wen Lifei fell ill, her parents working far away immediately quit their jobs and returned to their hometown of Wuchuan, selling pastries in the county to support the huge expenses of their daughter's dialysis twice a week. Her grandmother lived with her granddaughter in a rented room of just a few square meters in Zunyi for convenience of going to the hospital for dialysis.

Every week, the parents in Wuchuan would send about a thousand yuan to the grandmother and granddaughter, almost enough for Wen Lifei's two dialysis sessions, but if medication was needed, the money was far from enough.

"On a day in early November, my grandmother told me she would go out begging, asking me not to tell my mom and dad," said Wen Lifei.

To save her granddaughter, her grandmother knelt down on the street to beg.

Wen Lifei still remembers the first day her grandmother went out to beg, wearing clean clothes, black cotton shoes, and a light yellow hat. Afraid that her granddaughter might have an accident alone at home, her grandmother helped her onto a wheelchair and pushed her out of the door.

Their destination was near the Zhongbai Mall at Zunyi's Dingzi intersection, a bustling street.

Accompanying Wen Lifei and her mother to Chongqing yesterday, Zunyi Evening News reporter Leng Zexue told our reporter that although Zou Shuwei was poor, she was highly respected in their village. "She (Zou Shuwei) always dressed neatly whenever she went out. Sometimes, even when the family couldn't afford meals, she wouldn't ask neighbors for food."

But to save her granddaughter, Zou Shuwei knelt down in public on the street.

Wen Lifei also clearly understood what this meant to her grandmother, who was making her first trip from the countryside to the city. That day, her grandmother knelt close to her wheelchair, kneeling on just a few thin pieces of paper, looking at the hurried steps of passersby, while she looked at her grandmother's bewildered eyes and shed tears.

Seeking medical help in Chongqing, if there are no complications, the mother will donate her kidney.

"If a kidney transplant can save her, I'll give mine." This is what the already white-haired elder believed was the last thing she could do for her granddaughter.

But the idea of donating her kidney to her granddaughter was unanimously rejected by the family.

Yesterday morning, accompanied by reporters from the Zunyi Evening News, Wen Lifei and her mother came to the Southwest Hospital in Chongqing to do blood type matching. If successful, Wen Lifei's mother will donate her kidney to her daughter. The nurses at Southwest Hospital specially opened a green channel for the mother and daughter to facilitate them completing the tests as soon as possible.

Elder Zou Shuwei did not accompany her granddaughter to Chongqing. Originally, the elder had packed her bags and followed her granddaughter to the train station, but ultimately, she could not bring herself to spend 24 yuan on a train ticket, instead watching her granddaughter depart far away by train, knowing that the 24 yuan saved would be a life-saving sum for her granddaughter.

Yesterday, at the outpatient registration desk of Southwest Hospital, Wen Lifei took out a stack of slightly crumpled but neatly folded money from her jacket pocket. "Some are from the income of mom and dad selling pastries, some are donations from kind-hearted citizens in Zunyi, and some are from grandma begging on the street, totaling 3700 yuan."

"Two days ago, on the train, Wen Lifei received several calls from her grandmother asking when she would return. The elder was afraid that her granddaughter might never come back after leaving this time," Leng Zexue told the reporter.

Reported by our reporter Xu Xing.
