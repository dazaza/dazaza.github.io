Section Two: This section has 7 questions, totaling 35 points.

Read the following classical Chinese text and answer questions 5-9.

Zhou Fang, styled Shida, was originally from Ancheng, Runan. At the end of the Han dynasty, he fled to Jiangnan. By the time of Zhou Fang, four generations had passed. After Wu was pacified, his family settled in Xunyang, Lujiang. Zhou Fang was young but resolute, humble yet yielding, decisive in judgment, generous to those in need, leaving no surplus wealth at home. As a county official, Tao Kan was a scattered official, and Zhou Fang recommended him as chief clerk, befriending him and marrying his daughter to Tao Kan's son Zhan. Zhou Fang was selected as a filial and incorruptible official, appointed as a junior official and magistrate of Shangjia, but did not take these positions.

When Emperor Yuan crossed the Yangtze River, he ordered Zhou Fang to participate in military affairs in the east. At the time, there was someone with the same name and surname as Zhou Fang who was sentenced to death. Officials mistakenly arrested Zhou Fang, but he fought fiercely against those arresting him, causing dozens of them to scatter and flee. He then surrendered himself to the emperor, who did not punish him. Soon after, he was made a General of Yanglie, tasked with subduing Hua Yi. His subordinate, General Ding Gan of Liwu, communicated with Feng Yi, governor of Wuchang under Hua Yi's command. Zhou Fang captured and executed him. Feng Yi attacked Zhou Fang, but Zhou Fang led his troops to defeat him. Hua Yi's general, Zhou Guang, burned the city to respond to Zhou Fang, leading to the pacification of Jiangzhou. The emperor appointed Zhou Fang as a General of Zhenwu and ordered him to join other armies in suppressing Du Tao. Du Tao used a lever to attack official ships, but Zhou Fang created long forks to counteract them, preventing the levers from causing harm. Zhou Fang again used his navy to reach Xiangcheng. When the army reached Fukan, Du Tao sent Du Hong out to Haihun. Zhou Fang marched over land to Chaisang, secretly crossing the river, engaging in battle with the enemy, and cutting off hundreds of heads. The enemy retreated to defend Luling, and Zhou Fang pursued Du Hong in Luling. Du Hong threw treasures outside the city walls, and the soldiers competed to pick them up. Taking advantage of the chaos, Du Hong broke through the encirclement and fled. Zhou Fang led his troops in pursuit, acquiring countless saddles, horses, armor, and weapons. Du Hong entered Nankang, where the governor led troops to intercept him, breaking his forces and forcing him to flee to Linhe. The emperor promoted Zhou Fang to General Longxiang.

Since Zhou Fang was stationed in Xiangyang, he focused on agriculture and training troops, diligently gathering supplies. Wang Dun feared him, but dared not oppose him because of his strength. With his prestige established, people near and far admired and submitted to him. He was intelligent and brave, surpassing others as a famous general of the revival. He was modest and never discussed his achievements. Someone asked Zhou Fang, "People often boast about small good deeds. With such great accomplishments and not a word, why?" Zhou Fang replied, "It is the soldiers who follow orders; what merit do I have?" The soldiers respected him for this.

Zhou Fang trained and simplified his troops, desiring to exert force in Central Plains, and with determination, had the ambition to pacify the Yellow River and Luo River regions. He excelled in nurturing and accepting soldiers, all willing to die for him. Hearing that Wang Dun harbored disloyal intentions, Zhou Fang constantly gritted his teeth. Although Wang Dun harbored rebellious plans, he never dared to act improperly during Zhou Fang's lifetime.

(Selected from "Biography of Zhou Fang" in the "Book of Jin", with edits)

Note: ① Scattered official: A leisurely official. ② Haihun: Place name.
