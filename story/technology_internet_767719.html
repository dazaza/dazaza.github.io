Is Android's support for 4K excessive or necessary?

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 13:08:53

As the number of 4K products increases, fewer people are saying that it is useless. As the successor to 1080P, 4K video promises to change our visual experience, bringing us unprecedented detail in television, movies, and content. Even the Note 3 supports 4K shooting now. But is Android's support for 4K truly useful or just a gimmick? Without a doubt, 4K video is the future, and its prominence can be attributed to the promotion of market development.

The Inevitability of 4K

Companies believe that unique technological features will win them potential buyers, and 4K is included in this belief. Previously, the GS4 incorporated countless sensors and apps, the Nokia 1020 featured a 42M camera, and the latest Note 3 directly supports recording 4K video. So far, resolution has garnered significant attention; displays have become brighter year by year, and HD output remains a highlight. However, HD isn't what it used to be—anything less than 4K might not even attract user attention.

Pioneers

If you remember your first HD TV, you surely recall the sudden sense of thinness, the prominent screen resolution labels, and how close the images felt, which left a significant impact on you. This prompted you to decisively abandon your old CRT TV.

This happened back in 2003. Today, 1080P is everywhere; 75% of Americans own an HD TV, and the most popular computer displays are full HD. Therefore, both TV manufacturers and smartphone manufacturers are striving to produce unprecedented resolutions, making 4K the target with a resolution reaching 3840*2160. Among smartphone manufacturers, LG's 5.5-inch screen approaches 4K, as does Ortus Technology's 9.6-inch screen.

Why 4K?

4K resolution is twice that of 1080P, and large-screen TVs make 4K increasingly mainstream. 4K cameras also provide more sources for 4K films, and now smartphones are starting to offer clear 4K content.

Advantages and Disadvantages of 4K

Advantage: The benefit of 4K is that you can capture stunning 4K video, significantly improving video quality. After 4K, video resolution rises to 3840x2160, compared to 1080P's 1920x1080. 4K is nearly 8 million pixels, while 1080P is only 2 million, a substantial difference.

For those skilled in video editing, 4K means less cropping loss, and any loss still results in an HD video. For example, the Note 3's 13MP BSI camera can shoot 4K video at 26-35fps, with video length up to 5 minutes and file size around 1.5GB. Although it’s not yet essential, capturing such high-quality video from your phone is still exciting.

4K content is amazing, maintaining picture quality even when magnified significantly, surpassing today's 1080P. Thus, more pixels are its most obvious advantage.

Disadvantage: The cost of 4K video is not low, whether it's tools supporting 4K shooting or PC software needed to play 4K, consuming a lot of battery life and producing large file sizes. Qualcomm, as the first manufacturer to produce a processor supporting 4K (Snapdragon 800), hasn’t yet clarified ideas about upcoming compression and bit rate usage. Just like how smartphones can't quickly replace DSLRs, the Note 3 can't achieve Hollywood-level film production. Studies show that the 4K footage captured by the Note 3 can be played at a frequency of 50mbps, which is beyond the capabilities of ordinary smartphones.

Without a doubt, 4K is the future, and ultra-high-definition HD video and content have the ability to redefine our media experiences. Although it may take a few years to enter the mass market, as more 4K displays enter the market, prices may drop earlier due to consumer demand. In the coming quarters, we will see more reasonably priced 4K cameras and smartphones entering the market.
