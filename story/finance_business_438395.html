L'Oreal (欧莱雅)

The founder of L'Oreal was a young chemist named Eugene Schueller. In 1907, he developed the first French hair dye in his Paris laboratory. Although Schueller was dedicated to research in the lab, he was not an isolated scientist who ignored worldly affairs. On the contrary, he had an entrepreneurial mind and was very forward-thinking. Realizing that the hair dye he created was a necessary beauty product for hairdressers, he started a business in 1908, establishing La Societe des Teintures Inoffensives pour Cheveux, which was the predecessor of the L'Oreal Group.

From the start, Schueller hired salespeople to visit salons and promote the products to hairdressers. By 1919, over forty thousand professional hairdressers across France had become loyal customers of the company. After World War I, the company steadily grew and expanded overseas the following year.

On April 4, 1939, the company changed its name to L'Oreal, marking a new era. The new name didn't have any special meaning; Schueller just liked how it sounded. Later, Schueller introduced shampoo and body wash, expanding the product range. After World War II, L'Oreal continued its research and development, introducing new hair dyes and hair care products, solidifying its position as a hair care expert.

In 1957, Schueller passed away, and his successor took over, eventually leading to the company's stock market listing in 1963. A series of acquisitions followed, including the French cosmetic brand Lancome. In 1974, a share exchange agreement was reached with Nestle, but Schueller's daughter Liliane Bettencourt still retained the largest control, making her the richest woman in France. From then on, L'Oreal focused on scientific research while acquiring well-known beauty and fragrance companies, even venturing into publishing, making the group even stronger.

Today, L'Oreal not only holds a leading position in the hair care industry but is also the world's largest cosmetics company. It owns multiple beauty and fragrance brands, with a total revenue of $11.2 billion in 2000, over 400 subsidiaries, and a presence in 150 countries and regions worldwide, truly deserving the title of the world's largest beauty group.

Estee Lauder

Born and raised in New York in 1910, Estee was naturally destined for beauty, envied by many. She possessed the elegant and radiant appearance that women dream of, with classical delicate features, and also had a highly creative business mind. Her outstanding leadership skills and decisive approach made many men look up to her.

At a young age, she was influenced by her uncle, who was a dermatologist, hoping to become an excellent dermatologist herself when she grew up. This dream made Estee pay special attention to skin health issues and significantly impacted her future career in beauty.

The first laboratory of Estee Lauder was converted from a small storage room in the suburbs of New York. Her first office was the dining room at home. After numerous experiments and considerations, her dermatologist uncle formulated four products: cleansing oil, moisturizing lotion, mud mask, and the famous All-Purpose Creme. Although there were few products, their excellent quality and functionality impressed beauty columnists, dealers, and consumers alike. With this, Estee took her first step toward success. Next, Estee began tirelessly promoting the products for sale. To open the first counter at Saks on Fifth Avenue in New York, she visited the store daily to request a meeting with the general manager until one day, the general manager said, "You're here again, come in." She emphasized that she only needed ten minutes to introduce the products and used them on the general manager for a trial. After ten minutes, the general manager was impressed by the product's performance and her persistence, ultimately opening the first Estee Lauder counter at Saks on Fifth Avenue in New York.

Because of her confidence in the quality and efficacy of her products, Estee often personally promoted them everywhere. Whether at hair salons or department store counters, she enthusiastically pulled customers to try her products, believing that once the products touched the customers, half the battle was already won. Moreover, Estee pioneered the practice of distributing free samples to customers, resulting in surprisingly ideal sales and a marketing strategy that other companies quickly emulated.

With her keen market sense and creative business mind, Estee Lauder's revenue reached $800,000 annually by the 1950s, a very astonishing figure at the time. The company's performance was excellent and steadily rising, but Estee never relaxed her requirements for product and service quality. She placed great importance on employees' service attitudes, believing that a warm and enthusiastic attitude was key to successful sales. Therefore, she often personally flew to cosmetics counters or specialty stores around the world to inspect operations and personally trained employees in sales skills. She would kindly say to her employees, "Let me teach you how to introduce products to customers!" Her serious and pragmatic work style perfectly embodied her motto, "Dare to imagine, dare to implement."

P&G (Procter & Gamble)

Founded in 1837, P&G is one of the world's largest consumer goods companies. In the 2003-2004 fiscal year, the company's annual sales reached $51.4 billion. In the latest Fortune magazine ranking of the world's 500 largest industrial/service enterprises, it ranked 86th. P&G has nearly 100,000 employees globally, with factories and branches in more than 80 countries, and its over 300 brands are sold in more than 160 countries and regions, including fabric and home care, hair and beauty, baby and family care, health care, food and beverages, etc.

LVMH Moet Hennessy - Louis Vuitton

LVMH Moet Hennessy - Louis Vuitton is a pioneer in global luxury goods, owning more than 50 distinctive renowned brands. The group's main businesses include five areas: wine and spirits, fashion and leather goods, perfumes and cosmetics, watches and jewelry, and premium retail.

Thanks to brand development strategies and the expansion of international retail networks (with over 1,700 stores worldwide), the group has rapidly grown since its establishment in 1987.

Currently, the group employs over 59,000 people, 68% of whom are located outside France. They all adhere to the same values in their work. While developing their business, LVMH also contributes to society through its welfare institutions such as LVMH House, LVMH-ESSEC Chair, and LVMH Asia Research Fellowship Scholarships. Additionally, LVMH is committed to environmental protection. The group adheres to its public welfare mission, dedicated to preserving cultural traditions, advocating humanism, promoting education, and nurturing young artistic and design talents.

Shiseido

Shiseido was established in 1872, originally as a Western pharmacy in Tokyo's Ginza district. Its name originates from the phrase "Great indeed is the origin of Kun, and all things are born" in the Book of Changes, symbolizing the creation of new life and value. The founder, Yushin Fukuhara, studied pharmacy abroad and served as the head of the Japanese Navy's pharmaceutical department. Besides selling medicine, Yushin Fukuhara also participated in drug production, absorbing the essence of Western medicine to produce medical supplies with Eastern characteristics. Early products were mostly medicines and hygiene cleaning products. In 1888, he successfully developed Japan's first tube of toothpaste, quickly replacing the popular tooth powder at the time, making Shiseido a familiar name in Japan. The concept of toothpaste originated from the West, but the toothpaste bottle was specially printed with Chinese characters and English letters, foreshadowing a story combining Eastern and Western cultures.

Until 1897, Shiseido created Eudermine (Greek for "good" and "skin"). This was a groundbreaking facial toner, colored wine red, marking Shiseido's first step into the cosmetics industry.

When Yushin Fukuhara's son Shinzo Fukuhara took over, Shiseido's business situation began to change. Shinzo had traveled extensively in Europe and America, gaining deep insights into local aesthetics and culture. In 1916, he established Shiseido's design department, responsible for product packaging and promotion. From then on, they defined the appearance of the name "Shiseido" with unique Art Deco-style letters and used camellias as the company logo, along with Arabic-style floral patterns to decorate product bottles. Shiseido's brand image can thus be considered to have taken shape. Although today, camellias and Arabic patterns no longer exist, Shiseido's font has been revised twice, but the current "Shiseido" wording is quite similar to the original.

In 1923, focusing on whitening skin, Shiseido developed a chain operation model, strengthened nationwide promotions, and became more prominent. By 1948, Shinzo passed away, and his nephew Yoshinobu Fukuhara took over, representing the third generation of family successors and opening a new chapter in the business. In 1957, Shiseido began developing overseas markets, starting with the Zen perfume launched in 1965 and the Moisture Mist cosmetics series launched in 1978, whose packaging design was inspired by Japanese lacquerware. During this period, various cosmetics, fragrances, and skincare series launched by Shiseido achieved full internationalization, adopting Western-style packaging.

Kose

Despite the severe shortages of food and materials that disrupted life in Tokyo in 1946, Kose's founder, Mr. Kohei Kobayashi, still saw beauty and hope, and it was this hope and entrepreneurial passion, along with his firm belief in the business, that created Kose's legendary beauty myth.

Just two years later, the small company founded in 1946 grew into a large enterprise renamed Kose Corporation, with its name derived from the Greek word "KOSMETICOS," symbolizing "universe," "order," and "beauty." Since then, Kose has been dedicated to the production of cosmetics and the establishment of these two major industries, taking solid steps in the ever-changing cosmetics industry. Over the years, Kose has upheld the brand's existence philosophy of "integrating wisdom and rationality to create unique values and cultures of beauty" and developed with the beautiful ideal of "pure wisdom, for humanity, for the earth." While continuing the concept of "professional cosmetics," it strives to develop itself into an enterprise centered on beauty, creating a beautiful vision of life as its responsibility and committing to a refined lifestyle culture and attitude.

Initially, it wasn't a high-end skincare brand but started mainly by distributing other beauty and cosmetic brands (at the time, L'Oreal). Two years later, it began developing its own brand and within ten years aimed for more self-owned brands.

After launching its first powder cake in 1986, it established a major cosmetics group. Kose's early R&D products were mainly various powder cakes, earning it the reputation of "the powder foundation Kose."
