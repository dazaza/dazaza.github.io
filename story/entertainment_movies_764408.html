Introduction: As the planet Krypton is about to be destroyed, scientist Jor-El sends his infant son Kal via a spaceship to Earth in order to preserve the continuation of their race. Kal is raised by the Kent couple on Earth and possesses superhuman abilities, becoming the guardian of justice and truth on Earth. The grown-up Kal moves to Metropolis and becomes a reporter for the Daily Planet. Normally, he is a mild-mannered ordinary journalist, but in times of crisis, he transforms into the guardian superhero Superman, flying freely in the sky wearing a tight suit and cape. When the evil Luthor attempts to rule the world, Superman is the only one who can stop him.

This film is based on the famous comic series. With a budget of $55 million, it grossed $134 million domestically in the U.S. and $166 million overseas, officially launching the era of cinematic comic heroes. It also made Christopher Reeve one of the most beloved idol actors. A few years later, this movie even reached China, cultivating an entire generation of "Superman" fans. Superman (1978) is considered a classic in the history of science fiction films.

In the second installment in 1980, Superman faces three villains from Krypton, and his romance also progresses; in the third part in 1983, which takes a comedic route, a computer operator accidentally helps bad guys defeat Superman; in the fourth part in 1987, Superman eliminates nuclear weapons from the world, though with poor special effects and unoriginal plot, the lead actor's performance remains meticulous. The actor portraying Superman, Reeve, suffered a fall from horseback in the 90s, resulting in full-body paralysis.

The 1984 Supergirl tells the story of Superman's cousin, featuring several big names, but was considered a major flop.

Fun Facts:

- Steven Spielberg was once invited to direct this film, but the producers struggled to agree with him on salary terms. They waited until they saw the success of "Jaws" (1975), after which Spielberg moved on to other projects.

- To convincingly portray Superman, Christopher Reeve underwent rigorous physical training to build muscle. His trainer was David Prowse, who played Darth Vader in one of the Star Wars trilogy.

- Due to excessive physical exertion during filming, Reeve had trouble matching earlier flying scenes with later ones, necessitating re-shoots.

- The best way to showcase Superman's flight was through a long process of trial and error, including using a slingshot to launch a dummy into the air, or a remote-controlled model plane. The production team also designed a movable camera lens fixed with a launcher to create the illusion of movement while filming Reeve.

- Clark Kent and Superman's hair parts are on opposite sides.

- Marlon Brando appeared on screen for 10 minutes and earned four million dollars.

- To portray Clark Kent, Christopher Reeve referenced Cary Grant's performance in the movie "Bringing Up Baby" (1938).

- The scene at the end where Superman flies rapidly towards the sky was reused in the following three Superman movies.

- Marlon Brando refused to memorize most of his lines beforehand, so when he placed baby Kal-El into the escape pod, he was actually reading his lines off a child's diaper.

- Due to Christopher Reeve's lack of fame at the time, almost every movie promotion listed Marlon Brando and Gene Hackman's names before the main character's.

Superman II (1980)

Plot Summary: A group of terrorists seizes the Eiffel Tower, threatening to detonate a hydrogen bomb unless their demands are met. Superman immediately rushes to Paris and carries the crane with the hydrogen bomb into outer space. Unexpectedly, the explosion of the hydrogen bomb damages the "Phantom Zone" where Jor-El imprisoned three Kryptonian criminals. Led by the ruthless General Zod, these three criminals head to Earth, intending to enslave the humans Superman protects.

At this moment, our Superman is deeply entangled in a romantic relationship with his girlfriend Lois Lane, unaware of the impending threat, and has lost all his superpowers. Upon discovering Zod and his followers, Superman immediately returns to the "Fortress of Solitude" to restore his powers to fight against the vicious criminals, leading to an inevitable fierce battle...

Fun Facts:

- Initially, Richard Donner included scenes in Superman II where Superman converses with his father Jor-El. However, after Marlon Brando, who played Jor-El, sued for a percentage of the profits from the first Superman movie and won, the producers cut his scenes and replaced them with Superman's mother. Although Brando does not appear in this movie, his lawsuit secured him dividends, and his scenes were planned to appear in the 2006 Superman Returns.

- Tom Mankiewicz was hired to adapt Mario Puzo's initial Indian version of the Superman script into the Superman II screenplay. Mankiewicz removed much of Puzo's vulgarity and added religious implications as requested by the producers, drawing parallels between Superman's legend and "Christ's Second Coming," such as Jor-El (God) banishing Zod (Satan) from Krypton (Heaven), Kal-El being brought to Earth by a star-shaped spaceship (Star of Bethlehem), and allowing an infertile couple to have a child. If Richard Donner had been able to continue directing Superman II, there would have been more religious content in the plot, but unfortunately, he was fired and replaced by Richard Lester, who showed no respect for Superman and mythology.

- According to Richard Lester, he had never heard of Superman before taking over this project because his family did not allow him to read comic books as a child. Some believe that Lester's lack of understanding of the Superman character led to disrespect, especially evident in his independently directed Superman III.

- Richard Lester strongly opposed the epic film image style established by Richard Donner in the first Superman movie and decided to act according to his own wishes. At the time, shortly after Donner's Oscar-winning cinematographer Geoffrey Unsworth passed away, Lester planned to discard most of Unsworth's footage and hire the less talented Robert Paynter to set the film's visual style to resemble comic book styles, even deliberately creating a flat feeling. Lester often used three cameras simultaneously for different shots, two for close-ups and one for long shots, causing dissatisfaction among actors who were unsure when they were being filmed closely. The actress playing Lois Lane, Margot Kidder, particularly disliked Lester's methods. Additionally, the film's color tones were designed to resemble comic books. Naturally, Lester's contemptuous actions were criticized, and the box office results were disastrous.

- In 2004, Margot Kidder claimed that the existing deleted Richard Donner version footage was sufficient for theatrical release. In 1984, ABC aired a 30-minute segment of deleted scenes from Superman II, most of which were directed by Richard Donner.

- In the original script version, it was the nuclear missile from the first Superman movie that allowed Zod and his cohorts to escape the "Phantom Zone."

- Gene Hackman did not join this movie, and his scenes were pre-filmed by Richard Donner.

- In the original script version, there were four escaped Kryptonian criminals, one more than shown in the final film, named Jack-El, who specialized in comedy.

- Despite the numerous difficulties in filming, Warner Bros. premiered the movie in Europe by the end of 1980, delaying the U.S. premiere until the summer of 1981.

Superman III (1983)

Plot Summary: Superman returns to his hometown to attend a high school reunion, where he meets a former female classmate and falls in love instantly. Meanwhile, a disaster occurs, and Superman, distracted by romance, delays his rescue efforts, leaving him feeling very guilty. On the other hand, an ambitious billionaire manipulates weather around the world using meteorological satellites to control the market and later employs a computer genius to design a supercomputer to fight Superman. Despite the danger, Superman defeats the supercomputer and captures the greedy billionaire.

Fun Facts:

- The opening scenes in Metropolis were shot in Calgary, Canada.

- The video game played by Ross Webster was specifically designed for this movie and initially looked quite realistic, but the producers asked for a more computer-styled appearance so that viewers could easily recognize it.

- The program Gus designs at the beginning of the movie is impossible to complete.

- Margot Kidder appears in this movie for less than 5 minutes and speaks only 12 lines due to her protest over Richard Donner being fired midway through the filming of Superman II.

- Actress Enid Saunders plays a character named Minnie Benestein, a joke secretly planted by director Richard Lester, referencing a character from his long-running BBC show "The Goon Show."

- The film's original title was "Superman vs. Superman."

- Gus says in the movie: "I don't believe a man can fly!" Ironically, this was the exact tagline for the first Superman movie: "You'll believe a man can fly."

- The screenwriters originally wanted Alan Alda to play Ross Webster because they wanted an actor who was cruel yet charming.

- After the producers revealed Lana as a single mother in the comics, subsequent comic issues quickly explained her situation.

- The young boy waiting next to the photo booth when Clark turns into Superman is the same child actor who played young Kal-El in the 1978 Superman movie.

- The scene where Superman straightens the Leaning Tower of Pisa was initially planned for Superman II, while the ending space scene comes from the 1978 Superman movie and was reused in Superman II and Superman IV.

- The technique Gus uses to steal money from the company in the movie is called the "salami technique," referring to a computer crime method where small amounts are siphoned from authorized transactions and redirected to a hacker's account.

- Although this movie was completed two years after Superman II, Christopher Reeve had been away from the Superman role for four years because his scenes for the sequel were filmed in 1979.

- This is the first Superman movie where Christopher Reeve became the top-billed actor. In the first movie, he was listed after Marlon Brando and Gene Hackman, and in the sequel, he was listed just below Gene Hackman.

Superman IV: The Quest for Peace (1987)

Plot Summary: Superman's old nemesis, the genius villain Lex Luthor with an IQ of 200, is back. This time, he engages in nuclear weapons smuggling, and naturally, Superman will stop him. Luthor creates a Nuclear Man to fight Superman, leading to a battle for universal peace.

Fun Facts:

- The initial budget for the movie was $36 million, but before filming began, Cannon Group faced financial crises, reducing the budget to $17 million, forcing the crew to reuse older special effects.

- Richard Donner, who was fired during the filming of Superman II, was approached to direct this movie but declined.

- Several special effects technicians who worked on the previous three Superman movies and Supergirl left before filming began due to salary disputes.

- According to Richard Lester's biography, Lester also declined to direct this movie, though it is unclear who was approached first.

- When the movie was trimmed from its original 134 minutes to 90 minutes, the producers intended to use the cut scenes for "Superman V."

- The fifth Superman movie was titled "The New Superman." Christopher Reeve was supposed to direct and write instead of starring, but plans were abandoned due to the failure of this movie.

- Many exterior scenes were shot in Milton Keynes, UK, due to budget constraints preventing filming in New York.

- The American release omitted a scene where Superman saves a group of Soviet generals in Moscow.

- The movie's box office failure caused Cannon Group to abandon its Spider-Man movie plans.

- Originally, the movie featured two Nuclear Men, but the first's scenes were cut and planned for use in "Superman V."

- Christopher Reeve agreed to return as Superman for the fourth time, but only if the producers funded another movie he was involved in.

The Death of Superman (Animated Version)

Director: Duane Capizzi

Cast: Adam Baldwin, Anne Heche, James Marsters

Genre: Action, Animation, Drama

Year: 2007-09-18

Region: USA

Language: English

Runtime: 75 minutes

Color: Colorful

Movie Introduction:

LexCorp inadvertently releases the murderous creature "Doomsday," presenting Superman, the undefeated champion, with his greatest challenge—adapted from the award-winning "Death of Superman" trilogy.

The focus of this movie is, of course, Superman dying after a fierce battle.

The animated movie differs somewhat from the comic original, lacking the tragic tone of the original but retaining the biggest highlight: Superman's resurrection and continued presence among people. The story goes like this:

Superman's arch-nemesis Lex Luthor (Lex Luthor) accidentally opens a container placed on Earth long ago by aliens during a secret excavation of underground mineral sources. Out of this container emerges Doomsday, a failed product of alien technology. It was created to defend against external enemies but was found incapable of distinguishing friend from foe. Unable to kill it, the aliens confined it and sent it deep into the Earth.

Luthor's engineering team mistakenly destroys the spaceship imprisoning Doomsday, releasing it onto the surface, and chaos begins!

At this time, Superman is enjoying a holiday in his secret base in Antarctica with Lois. Lois already believes Superman is Clark himself, pressuring Superman to admit it verbally, leading to an argument. Just then, news arrives, and Superman rushes to the scene to duel with Doomsday. Doomsday's strength is astonishing, leaving Superman powerless. Gathering his last strength, Superman takes Doomsday into space, then falls from the sky, crashing into the ground. Doomsday dies, and Superman also succumbs to exhaustion and dies.

As Metropolis mourns Superman's death, Lex Luthor extracts DNA from the blood Superman left at the battle site and begins his plan to clone a Superman army. Without Superman, the crime rate in Metropolis rises significantly. An evil toy maker develops a mechanical spider, lifting a busload of elementary students to the top of a building. Lois rescues the children but is thrown off the building with the car by the spider. At this moment, Superman reappears, saving Lois and capturing the toy maker, prompting cheers from everyone.

The reborn Superman is actually a clone produced by Luthor. He possesses Superman's strength and even moral values but lacks Superman's thoughts. Though temporarily obedient to Luthor, he is not willing. Meanwhile, the robots in Superman's secret Antarctic base steal Superman's body from Luthor's lab and begin reviving Superman using Kryptonian technology.

The cloned Superman starts forming his own ideas about controlling Metropolis. Hearing that the escaped toy maker killed a child, the clone snatches the toy maker from the police and kills him, angering Luthor. The clone removes the Kryptonite fragment implanted in his brain by Luthor and begins managing Metropolis in his own way.

Lois, who harbors doubts about the resurrected Superman, sneaks into Luthor's secret lab and discovers a large number of nearly completed Superman clones. At this moment, the clone arrives, destroying the lab and throwing Luthor into a locked room before tossing him out of the building.

A major battle between the military and the clone ensues. In the Antarctic, Superman has restored 67% of his abilities and rushes back to Metropolis to resolve the crisis.

During the battle with the clone, Superman initially struggles but eventually uses Luthor's kryptonite laser gun to defeat the clone, who dies. Superman returns to the people of Metropolis.

In the story's conclusion, Superman reveals to Lois that he is indeed Clark.
