A Close Encounter with the Mysterious Google Self-Driving Car Team

by anonymous on 2013-11-21 14:01:41

In the Google X Lab, engineers are brewing plans to change the world, one of which is the self-driving car. Compared to projects like Google Glass and Internet via balloons, this is a longer-term project with broader implications and greater audacity. Convincing people that machines can be intelligent isn't easy, and self-driving cars also face various legal issues. However, from the perspective of Google's engineers, self-driving cars can bring about a better future. At that time, traffic accidents could be minimized, roads would be safer. People might not even need to own cars; self-driving cars could be summoned at any time to take people wherever they want to go.

Google’s self-driving car project is no longer a secret, but the workplace of the development team enforces strict confidentiality measures. Outsiders find it difficult to enter. Recently, Burkhard Bilger, an editor for The New Yorker, was finally granted permission to visit the project. He met with Google co-founder Sergey Brin and conducted a key interview with Anthony Levandowski, an engineer on the self-driving car team.

Anthony Levandowski always wants to do cool things. In his first year at Berkeley, he provided an intranet service in his apartment, earning $50,000 a year. In his second year, he won the national robotics competition with a machine made out of Legos. After joining Google, he served as the architect for Street View and the map database. Now, his main work is on self-driving cars.

If you're familiar with the Google self-driving car project, you might know the key role played by Sebastian Thrun. His outstanding performance in the DAPRA autonomous vehicle driving competition caught Google's attention. Subsequently, he became the head of the Google self-driving car project and helped establish the Google X Lab. Actually, the DAPRA autonomous vehicle driving competition held two events, and Sebastian Thrun participated in the second one.

Anthony Levandowski participated in both competitions, and his autonomous vehicle wasn't a car, but a motorcycle. The DAPRA competition plan was announced in 2002, with the event scheduled for 2004. It offered a prize of $1 million. Competitors had to accomplish what seemed like a simple task: drive 142 miles without human intervention. "Nowadays, we can complete it within hours," Sebastian Thrun said, "but back then, it was like trying to land on the moon in sneakers."

Since DAPRA didn't specify the type of vehicle, Levandowski decided to build the world's first autonomous motorcycle. He thought motorcycles were more agile and not slow. The problem was maintaining balance on the motorcycle. When he told his mentor Ken Goldberg about his idea, Goldberg frankly told him there was no chance of winning. "Anthony might be the most creative student I've seen in nearly 20 years," he said, "but this idea is too far-fetched."

Levandowski did not back down. Over the next two years, he made over two hundred calls to potential sponsors, eventually securing $30,000 in investment. Meanwhile, he began gathering graduates from the school until one day, Goldberg realized half of his graduate students were involved in Levandowski's project.

After a year of working on the project, Russian engineer Alex Krasnov solved the issue of how to keep the bike balanced. They called the bike the Ghost Rider.

In the first competition, all vehicles performed poorly. Of the final 15 vehicles, none lasted beyond 10 miles, and seven fell within the first mile. Levandowski's Ghost Rider also failed. It had defeated 90 cars in the qualifying round, but during the finals, Levandowski forgot to turn on the balancing system, causing it to fall after only three feet.

Three months later, DAPRA announced a second competition in October, increasing the prize money to $2 million. In this competition, Sebastian Thrun led the Stanford team to victory. Their vehicle Stanley was later collected by the Smithsonian. Levandowski's Ghost Rider was eliminated in the semi-finals. Nevertheless, the Ghost Rider remained an engineering marvel, having defeated 78 cars. Two years later, the Smithsonian Museum added the motorcycle to its collection.

In 2007, both Thrun and Levandowski joined Google. Their first task was to help the Street View team. Levandowski quickly realized that the data obtained from Street View could make digital maps more accurate. For example, the names of streets and exits could be directly extracted from images rather than relying on error-prone government records. Over the following two years, Levandowski traveled between Hyderabad, India. His 2000 data processors continuously corrected the maps. Therefore, when Apple's map software had problems, Levandowski knew why. By then, his team had spent five years correcting maps, making millions of corrections daily.

The self-driving car project was originally not part of the plan. Sebastian Thrun did not believe that autonomous vehicles could drive on regular streets due to too many variables. However, in February 2008, Levandowski received a call from the producer of Discovery Channel's "Prototype This!" asking if he could create a self-driving pizza delivery car.

Over the next five weeks, Levandowski and his colleagues modified a Prius and convinced the California Highway Patrol to allow the vehicle to cross the Bay Bridge. This was the first time a self-driving car legally drove on public streets. It was a successful debut. Soon after, Page and Brin asked Thrun to start the self-driving car project.

"They didn't even mention the budget," Thrun said, "they just asked how many people I needed and how to find them. I said, 'I know who they are.'"

Thrun selected technical experts who had participated in the self-driving car competition: Chris Urmson for software, Levandowski for hardware, Mike Montemerlo for electronic maps. He also chose others: lawyers, interface designers, etc. Every week, dozens of Google employees tested the self-driving cars and filled out user surveys.

At the start of the plan, they used Levandowski's pizza delivery car and Stanford's open-source software. But they soon found that everything had to start from scratch. Page and Brin gave the team a challenge: to complete ten 100-mile routes. It took the team a year and a half to meet this challenge.

Now, Google's self-driving cars have driven a million kilometers without any accidents. However, driver intervention is still needed in difficult road conditions. Thrun said that if fully autonomous, it could ensure no major accidents within 50,000 miles.

For Levandowski, the self-driving car project has special significance. Three years ago, his fiancée Stefanie Olsen encountered an accident while driving across the Golden Gate Bridge. At the time, a car in front suddenly stopped, Olsen slammed on the brakes and came to a stop, but was hit by a car from behind. Fortunately, she was not injured, and the fetus in her abdomen was unharmed.

"That accident should never have happened," Levandowski said. If the car behind Olsen was self-driving, it should have been able to quickly assess the situation and brake urgently. Google's cars drive more cautiously and don't follow too closely behind other vehicles.

In Levandowski's view, our fear of self-driving cars is unfounded. "When the vehicle you make is better than the driver, letting the driver drive is irresponsible," he said, "the more we delay, the more people will die."
