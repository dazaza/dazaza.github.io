                                       Server Traffic Monitoring, Network Management, and Security Logs

 

Commonly used Local Area Network (LAN) traffic monitoring software includes "Bailuo NetGuard" LAN management software, among others. Nowadays, many hardware devices also have traffic monitoring functions.

So, how do data center server rooms monitor the incoming and outgoing traffic of our servers? The LAN traffic monitoring software listed above can monitor every port on a switch. You might say that this LAN speed-limiting software monitors the switch, not my server's traffic. In fact, traffic monitoring software uses SNMP (Simple Network Management Protocol) to monitor the incoming and outgoing traffic of switches.

Since the moment your server is officially racked in the data center and connected to the Internet, your server's traffic has already been monitored and recorded by them. If you have any issues with your server's traffic, they can send you the traffic graph at any time. Below is an example of a switch traffic graph monitored by the company I work for:

The vertical axis represents the highest network bandwidth reached by the switch. The numbers on the horizontal axis represent the current and historical times monitored by MRTG (Multi Router Traffic Grapher). The red arrow on the horizontal axis indicates the current monitoring time. There is a vertical red line at 0, which represents the intersection point of two days, i.e., midnight.

SolarWinds can reflect the traffic status of ports on a switch using different colors and comes with many small tools useful for network management.

If the staff in the data center server room notice abnormal traffic on your server, they will take corresponding measures, which involves limiting the speed of the port connected to your server on the switch (data centers use intelligent manageable switches, which have many commands to control the switch and the data packets passing through it). Then, they will notify you that your server's traffic is abnormal, and you can log into the server to investigate the cause.

Of course, if the reason for your server's abnormal traffic is due to being subjected to a DoS/DDoS attack, the data center staff will contact you immediately. If conditions allow, they will help connect your server to a system that resists DoS/DDoS attacks. (DoS/DDoS stands for Denial of Service/Distributed Denial of Service, which is an attack method that uses an overwhelming amount of data packets exceeding the processing capability of the target, consuming available system and bandwidth resources, leading to network service paralysis.)

The specific implementation technology of traffic monitoring will not be detailed here. If you are interested, you can look it up online. There are plenty of tutorials waiting for you to discover, mostly focusing on MRTG and SolarWinds.

Don't complain that I am teasing you; technology needs to be stored in your own brain. Even if I explain it here and you understand it today, you might forget it in a few days. As always: computers are highly practical. Doing it yourself will leave a much deeper impression than me telling you.
