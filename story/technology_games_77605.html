Online game marriage, poppy or rose

by lingtao98 on 2008-07-14 08:30:12

A Marriage Craze in Online Games, Players Flock In

Currently, the majority of online games in China have introduced a marriage system. Getting married within these games has become a common phenomenon. Take for instance the online game "Fantasy Westward Journey" which boasts 1.5 million concurrent online players. Male players must first own a house before they are eligible to go with their beloved to the "Matchmaker Temple" and apply for marriage through the matchmaker. If both parties agree to get married, they can then pay homage to heaven and earth. Of course, getting married is not free; a fee of 500,000 game coins is required for each marriage. After paying homage, the system will announce their good news to everyone online and automatically send messages to their friends. Everyone will then rush over to ask for wedding candy and celebrate their wedding. Afterward, the two can "wander through the world as hero and beauty together."

In the game "Fantasy Westward Journey," the reporter conducted an online random survey of 20 players, out of which 11 were married, accounting for 55%. The players gave varied responses to the reporter's question of "Why get married in the game?" Three players' answers were particularly representative.

A player named "Love Miao" in the game said: "My 'wife' in the game is my real-life girlfriend. We haven't truly gotten married yet, so we decided to experience it first in the game—it feels fresh and exciting."

Another player named "Alone on the Western Tower" said: "It's boring to wander alone in the game world. Don't you see in Jin Yong's novels, heroes always have beauties by their side, and talented scholars are paired with beautiful women? Finding my soulmate and exploring the world together sounds romantic."

A player with the ID "Season of Troubles" expressed: "In real life, I am rather introverted and even if I like someone, I don't dare to confess my feelings boldly. Virtual marriage can make up for this regret of mine, because I don't have to face the other person directly, so I feel more at ease. Even if I say or do something wrong, I don't have to worry, because when I leave the game, I'm still me, and she's still her, living our own lives."

It is evident that the reason why online game marriages attract so many players is multifaceted. Some do it to continue their love with their real-life husband (wife) or boyfriend (girlfriend) in the game; others find the game's marriage system so well-developed that many unmarried players in real life get to experience what marriage feels like. Proposing, registering, holding a wedding, buying a house, having children... all these elements make them feel fresh and exciting. Some people believe that wandering alone in the game world is not as carefree and independent as finding a "soulmate" to travel through scenic spots. Finding a picturesque place to express affection can add a touch of romance to the otherwise monotonous battles in the game world. Walking hand in hand through the bustling city in the game can also satisfy one's vanity. Some others hope to make up for their emotional deficiencies in real life through virtual game marriages where there is no responsibility to bear.

Problems Caused by Online Game Marriages

Although online game marriages only exist in the virtual game world, games have their rules, and life has its principles. When the boundaries between the two become blurred, various problems undoubtedly arise. As online game marriages become increasingly popular, the negative impacts they bring are gradually becoming apparent. The most prominent issues are how online game marriages begin to threaten real-life marriages and the influence they have on teenagers.

Virtual marriages are not bound by real-world laws, but examples of real-life marriages or relationships being jeopardized due to "losing control" in the virtual world are not uncommon.

Mr. Xiao Zhang from a bank in Yinchuan encountered a relationship crisis for this reason. Xiao Zhang and his real-life girlfriend had always had a good relationship. After graduating from university, Xiao Zhang became addicted to online games and got married to someone else in the game. Upon learning about it, his girlfriend felt betrayed and proposed a breakup. "If I had known this would happen, I definitely wouldn't have 'gotten married'," Xiao Zhang regretfully told the reporter. Another victim of "online game marriage," Mr. Deng, said that he and his wife were university classmates who fell in love for six years before getting married, establishing a deep emotional foundation. His wife liked to surf the internet and play games. After meeting a netizen named "Poetic Charm," she quickly became his online lover. Not long ago, the two underwent a "marriage registration" in the game and held a "wedding." They had their own home and started an alternative family life. Due to this incident, Mr. Deng's marital relationship deteriorated, and they decided to divorce.

Although many players emphasize the gaming nature of "online marriages," numerous instances of turning fake into real have brought crises to many families. Virtual marriages are harming real-life marriages, leading to increasing doubts about the ability of "online marriage" participants to distinguish between virtuality and reality. In the virtual world of online games, since there is no need to bear the obligations and responsibilities of real-life marriages, many people take marriage lightly, breaking up and reuniting repeatedly without any remorse, making a mockery of feelings and offending morality. Most people hold a negative attitude towards such "online game marriages." The reporter once posted online to gather opinions on online game marriages. Out of 32 respondents, 26 clearly stated that they could not accept it. One netizen commented, "In a certain sense, infidelity in spirit is harder for me to accept than infidelity in body."

What is even more alarming is that among those participating in "online game marriages," teenagers make up a significant proportion. Data shows that currently, there are over 15 million young internet users in China, with approximately 2 million being addicted to the internet. Around 30% of these internet-addicted youths are obsessed with online marriages. During the summer vacation, the reporter's friend Ms. Wang discovered that her son, who was still in junior high school, had already gotten married three times in a certain online game. "When I found out, I was completely stunned. Although it's just a game, it will surely affect his attitude towards marriage in the future. What should I do!?" Ms. Wang worriedly said.

Deputy Director of the Ningxia Academy of Social Sciences, Chen Tongming, believes that online game marriages are detrimental to the growth of minors in every way. "Online game marriages are too casual, which will inevitably affect children's understanding of marriage and family. For minors, their understanding of marriage is still in the启蒙 stage. Before they understand what marriage really is, they receive their first interpretation of 'marriage' from online games, leading them to imitate. Those who treat marriage and divorce casually from a young age may not take them seriously in real life later on. After all, in their eyes, the essence and seriousness of love and marriage have been diluted."
