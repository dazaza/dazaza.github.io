The book is about the origin and evolution of the meaning of characters, but I feel that it is more about the communication between two generations of father and son. The author is a writer, so naturally he uses knowledge of characters to educate his children. If it were a physicist, he might write a book called "Physics Around Us", and if it were a mathematician, he might write a book called "The Beauty of Mathematics". In any case, they all want to pass on their knowledge to their children and exchange emotions while transmitting knowledge.

Returning to the theme of the text in this book. Based on my personal experience, understanding the original meaning of some characters is not necessarily a good thing, because there is often a contradiction between the original meaning and the distorted meaning. Once, a colleague told me about a young female colleague who died of cancer, leaving behind a child who was only half a year old. As she spoke, she sighed deeply. I said, "Are you feeling sorry for the rabbit's death?" She immediately became angry. Later, I explained to her that "feeling sorry for the rabbit's death" actually means feeling sad due to the misfortune of a similar or same kind, not what she thought as fake sympathy or crocodile tears. She still held a grudge, thinking that I was showing off. I give this example to illustrate that when I want to express a word whose meaning has been widely distorted, I often have to choose between its original meaning and its distorted meaning. Choosing the original meaning may lead to misunderstanding by the other party who has accepted the distorted meaning, while choosing the distorted meaning makes me feel uncomfortable. For my state of dilemma, do you feel sorry for the rabbit's death?

The book frequently contrasts the scenes of the author being educated by his father back then with the scenes of him educating his own children now. The adult author can already understand his father's intentions, but his own efforts are often laughed at by his children as pedantic and verbose. This is actually an inevitable cycle. There is always a contradiction between the earnest advice of parents and the stubbornness of their children. Communication requires crossing an invisible chasm. It is rare for the author to always recall the scenes of facing his father in the past. How many fathers are like Ma Linsheng in Wang Shuo's "I Am Your Father," who after beating his son, recalls how he hid under the covers and resolved never to lay a finger on his own child after being beaten by his father. The author's attitude of kindness and patience towards his children is truly admirable.
