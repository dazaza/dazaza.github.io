Introduction:

A French girl named Mathilde fell from a ladder at the age of three, leaving her disabled and becoming a cripple. She grew up with childhood friend Manech, and as they grew older, their bond deepened. As World War I spread, Manech was conscripted into the army, and before joining, he and Mathilde had already agreed on an engagement.

On the Somme front in France, amidst the chaos of war, Manech and four other young men who feared war collectively shot themselves in the hands, hoping to escape the bloody nightmare. Their actions were later discovered and brought before a military tribunal. These five soldiers, who sought to evade the war, were sent to a barren land between the German and French armies for punishment, where the dense fire from both sides left them with little chance of survival.

Mathilde received a letter from the army informing her of Manech's fate and placing him on the death list. Unwilling to accept this tragic destiny while waiting for her fiancé to return home, Mathilde's intuition told her she would feel it if Manech truly left her. Although the smoke of war had dissipated, Mathilde embarked on a difficult emotional journey.

In the following two years, Mathilde read over 70 letters Manech had sent her from the battlefield daily, expecting him to suddenly appear one day. With each step closer to the truth, her heart broke further as she imagined how Manech endured his final moments, like knives piercing her heart. Nevertheless, Mathilde remained resolute.

Her firm belief, urgent hope, and tenacious investigation gradually revealed the truth. She pieced together useful information, uncovering the lesser-known stories behind these five unfortunate soldiers and their cruel punishment. Mathilde deeply felt the horrors of war, which left indelible marks on all who participated.

Highlights:

Mention Jean-Pierre Jeunet and Audrey Tautou, and you're reminded of the well-received "Amélie" in 2001. This French film gained significant popularity back then. The new production "A Very Long Engagement" garnered much praise even before its release. The film features over 200 characters and includes Jodie Foster, whose limited role is still a highlight of the film's promotion.

"A Very Long Engagement" is hailed as the "best French film in three years." Although it missed the submission deadline for the Best Foreign Language Film at the Oscars on September 30, it might still be recommended by Warner Bros. for next year's Best Picture category. The film cost approximately $50 million, featuring grand scenes and a majestic atmosphere. Director Jeunet deviates from his usual surreal romantic style, focusing instead on portraying the cruelty and heaviness of war, melancholic human nature, while maintaining stunning originality and impact visually.

The Talented Director and Angelic Actress:

Jean-Pierre Jeunet initially worked in television advertising and video clips. In 1991, Jeunet and partner Marc Caro completed their first feature film, "Delicatessen," which won four César Awards, including Best New Director and Best Set Design. The success of "Delicatessen" surprised even Jeunet and Caro. Buoyed by their success, they went on to create another film, "The City of Lost Children," a dream of theirs for over a decade. In 1997, Jeunet directed the fourth installment of the Alien series, "Alien Resurrection," in the United States. In 2000, he returned to France to shoot the surreal romantic film "Amélie," which achieved unprecedented success in French cinema history, becoming the highest-grossing French film at the time in both France and the United States with box office earnings of $33 million.

When casting for "Amélie" in 2001, Jeunet's first impression of Audrey Tautou wasn't Amélie but Mathilde. Later, during the Oscar ceremony when "Amélie" was nominated, Jeunet asked Tautou, "Would you like to work with me again?" Despite not having read the novel "A Very Long Engagement," Tautou replied affirmatively, saying, "Of course! As long as it's the same crew from 'Amélie.'" In fact, the main creative team, from screenwriters, cinematographers, sound engineers, makeup artists, to minor supporting actors, are all the original cast from "Amélie."

Tautou herself also loves the character Mathilde: "Mathilde's optimism and perseverance amazed me. I've always been someone who enjoys laughing and gets discouraged easily, but while filming this movie, I felt myself becoming more serious and stronger. It was the first time I experienced how a character could genuinely influence my behavior."

Perception:

"A Very Long Engagement" is adapted from Sebastian Japrisot's best-selling novel of the same name. In theory, only fools compare two entirely different systems, yet in reality, people worldwide enjoy comparing adaptations to their original works. The result is often that the film doesn't match up to the book. So if you're smart, watch the movie first and then read the book for a better experience. As one netizen put it, "Film is okay, Book is great."

The most striking aspect of the film is undoubtedly the depiction of World War I battlefields. The camera presents the pain of war vividly before the audience's eyes. Jeunet uses extremely realistic details to portray the brutality and madness of combat, with visual effects surpassing those of "Saving Private Ryan," giving viewers a sense of being right there on the battlefield. The battlefield is hell for the living; for Jeunet, it symbolizes the decline of humanity.

The romantic elements in the film differ from Jeunet's usual style. Audrey Tautou becomes the sole source of warmth in this cold film, even her short hairstyle being carefully considered. A limping woman, determined to find her love shattered by war, Tautou successfully portrays a wise, loving, and war-weary female image.

Another notable aspect of the film is its strong visual contrast: peaceful rural life versus bloody battlefields, the picturesque Paris of 1920 versus the Somme front devouring lives, and the stark difference in themes between the first and second halves of the film, all emphasizing the cruelty of war.
