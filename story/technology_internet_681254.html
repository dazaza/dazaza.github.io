A Summary of What Pregnant Women Should Eat and What They Should Avoid

by childrenstory126 on 2012-02-28 12:10:48

Snacks are the favorite food of children, but now many snacks have added a large amount of artificial ingredients, which will harm the brain development of children and affect their intelligence development.

Culprit 1: Trans Fat

Ms. Zhu has been fond of fried food since she was young, and this habit seems to have been passed on to her son.

Ms. Zhu's son is in the third grade at a primary school. Recently, during the winter vacation, Ms. Zhu often fried lotus root patties, lamb skewers, and five-spice sticks for her son at home, and both mother and son enjoyed them heartily. However, yesterday morning when they got up, Ms. Zhu found that her son had developed blisters all over his mouth, so she quickly took him to see a doctor at the oral department of the hospital.

When Ms. Zhu received the test report, she was greatly shocked: her son's blood viscosity was twice as high as normal. After the doctor inquired about his living habits, he said it was because he had consumed too much trans fatty acid. The doctor also reminded her that if he continued eating like this for a long time, it might increase his chances of developing Alzheimer's disease in the future.

Trans fat, also known as trans fatty acid, is generally produced after plant oil is processed with [hydrogenation] technology. Compared to regular plant oil, artificial trans fat has advantages such as heat resistance, not easily going bad, and being able to be stored longer. Currently, the use of artificial trans fat in the American fast-food industry is quite common. It is usually used to make crispy bread, fried French fries, and donuts. Some fats that make pastries crispy, margarine, and frying oils may all contain artificial trans fat.

After pregnant women consume excessive amounts of trans fatty acids, they can pass them through the placenta to the fetus, and lactating mothers can also feed trans fatty acids to infants through breast milk, thereby affecting their growth and development. This can also cause lipid deficiency in the brains of infants, affecting their intellectual development.

Therefore, parents should pay special attention to the following foods: First is artificial oil, such as margarine (vegetable cream); second is fried food, such as instant noodles, potato chips, and fries. Some processed foods containing oil, such as instant soups, fast food, frozen food (such as glutinous rice balls), baked goods (such as cookies, biscuits, and bread), various instant powder-like foods (such as powdered oatmeal, coconut powder, sesame paste powder, etc.), various cream candies, peanut butter, chocolate sauce, etc., may all contain trans fatty acids.

Culprit 2: Puffed Food

In recent years, puffed snacks have become popular among children due to their crisp, crunchy, fragrant, and sweet flavors. However, medical experts from both domestic and international fields believe that puffed food is not truly a safe or healthy food. If children consume these types of food for a long time, it will be harmful rather than beneficial to their brain and physical development.

Nutrition experts in the United States warned years ago: there are more harmful metal elements such as lead or aluminum in puffed food, and parents should not let their children eat these foods frequently. The reasons why puffed food contains higher levels of lead and aluminum are: one, additives such as leavening agents are often added during the processing of these foods, some leavening agents (such as alum and sodium bicarbonate) contain more lead or aluminum and other heavy metals; two, during the processing of food, it goes through metal pipes, which inside usually have lead-tin alloys. Under high temperatures, this lead easily vaporizes, and after vaporizing, the lead pollutes these puffed foods. Regular consumption of puffed food can cause significant harm to children (especially those under three years old).

When "lead enters through the mouth" for a long time, it gradually becomes latent in the blood and bones, making it difficult to naturally metabolize. Children are in the stage of growth and development, absorbing several times more lead than adults, and their ability to excrete lead is weak, making it easier to accumulate in the body. When harmful heavy metals accumulate to a certain amount, they will significantly damage the nervous, digestive, hematopoietic, and other systems, especially leading to cognitive impairments or decreased thinking abilities, and may even affect the lifelong health of children.

Excessive intake of aluminum mainly damages brain function. For example, it interferes with human thought, consciousness, and memory functions, causing nervous system diseases, manifested as memory decline, visual and motor coordination failure, brain injury, intelligence decline, and severe cases may lead to dementia.

Culprit 3: Artificial Coloring

Nowadays, children's food comes in a wide variety, with exquisite packaging and vibrant colors, but some "colorful foods" containing coloring pose hidden dangers to human health. Overconsumption or long-term consumption is detrimental rather than beneficial to children's health.

During the stage of children's intellectual development, they need a large amount of high-quality protein and lipids and other nutritional elements, which artificial synthetic colorings cannot provide. At the same time, artificial synthetic colorings themselves or their metabolites are toxic, and during storage, they may mix with arsenic, lead, or other toxic intermediate products, all of which will affect children's intellectual development and neurological behavior.

Moreover, excessive intake of synthetic colorings can cause allergies, such as asthma, laryngeal edema, rhinitis, urticaria, skin itching, and neural headaches; certain artificial synthetic colorings acting on the human nervous system can affect the transmission of nerve impulses, thus causing a series of symptoms.

Parents are advised to strictly control children's diet, allowing them to eat more natural foods, and when buying processed foods, choose those without any artificial additives as much as possible. To avoid the influence of artificial colorings, attention should be paid to daily diet. When purchasing brightly colored foods or beverages, caution is needed, avoiding overly bright processed foods, and developing the habit of reading food ingredient lists, paying attention to whether ingredients such as cochineal red, tartrazine, and Sudan red synthetic colorings are added.
