To Zisheng Education Network [http://www.zsjykj.com/]: The finance major is a popular choice for the self-study examination, favored by candidates for its practicality and ease of employment. As a bachelor's degree graduate in the self-study examination finance major, I would like to share with candidates the techniques I have learned over the years for studying finance, as well as how to handle the relationship between general courses and specialized courses.

First, study the general courses, then the specialized courses.

My associate and bachelor’s degrees are both in finance. Here, I first remind candidates whether they have already decided to study finance. Since finance courses are highly specialized, whether at the associate or bachelor level, there are at least five courses that are not compatible with other majors. If you pass these courses and then decide to change your major, it would be a waste.

After selecting your major, I suggest candidates start with the general courses, mainly to prevent changing majors during the learning process because most general courses are universal, and there is basically no situation of "wasted effort." After passing the general courses, you can choose courses from the unified exams for economics-related majors, laying the foundation for specialized courses. For example, the "Introduction to Economic Law" in the associate segment and "Marketing" in the bachelor’s segment of the finance major. Then, you can focus on preparing for the specialized courses.

First, thoroughly read the textbook, then understand the content.

Whether it's a general course or a specialized course, when studying for the self-study examination, the textbook is the most important learning tool, and all the exam content is in the textbook. After receiving the textbook, candidates should familiarize themselves with the course through looking at the table of contents, syllabus, etc. As mentioned earlier, since finance courses are highly specialized and most candidates do not work in financial institutions, it is difficult to digest the textbook content by linking theory to practice. Therefore, candidates need to have a thorough understanding of the textbook to comprehend the content of each knowledge point. Hence, being familiar with the textbook is crucial.

In fact, candidates can use media, the internet, and other means to understand and reinforce what they have learned. For instance, in textbooks such as "Monetary Banking," there are two important concepts: M1 and M2, which refer to narrow and broad money supply. During exams, test makers usually assess candidates' understanding and differentiation of these two concepts through multiple-choice questions. Most candidates choose to memorize the book, but I remember them by browsing the People's Bank of China website and reading news. The People's Bank regularly releases relevant statistical reports on its website, which include M1 and M2. This way, turning dead knowledge into active knowledge, and having data to support it, makes understanding and memorization easier.

First, outline the structure, then identify the key points.

Through thoroughly reading the textbook, we can discover that there is a pattern in the textbook's compilation, meaning it modularly introduces the course. For example, "Financial Market Studies" covers several aspects such as an overview, market, risk, regulation, etc., throughout the entire textbook. After fully familiarizing yourself with the textbook, you can try outlining its structure and then enrich it, which is commonly referred to as "reading the textbook thinly." The next step is to find the key points. Candidates can refer to past exam papers, exam outlines, and teaching aids to summarize the key content and review it targetedly.

Most specialized courses in the finance major exam plan only arrange one exam per year, so candidates need to prepare seriously, attach great importance to it, and spend most of their study time on the textbook to build a solid foundation.
