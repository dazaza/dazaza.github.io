The internet, though virtual, is fundamentally human-centered. Thus, the open internet represents a real society. At one time, the highly efficient and valuable industry of the internet gave rise to a group of people with alternative motives—like knights in novels or assassins in movies... They move freely through the vast and boundless world of the internet, leaving no trace behind them. These highly skilled network technology experts, seemingly harmless, are known by an elegant name: "hackers." They pose an extremely high challenge to the normal operation of the internet. Their confrontations with security personnel have never ceased since the birth of the internet, but back then there were hardly any cybersecurity professionals.

In 1983, Kevin Mitnick was sentenced to six months of juvenile detention in California after being found using a university computer to illegally access ARPANET, the precursor to today's internet, and through it, the computers of the Pentagon.

In 1988, Kevin Mitnick was arrested by law enforcement authorities for allegedly stealing $1 million worth of software from DEC and causing $4 million in losses.

In 1993, an organization calling itself "Master of Deception" targeted the American telephone system. This organization successfully infiltrated the U.S. National Security Agency and Bank of America, creating a system that could bypass long-distance call systems and access dedicated lines.

In 1995, Russian hacker Vladimir Levin performed a remarkable heist on the internet. He was the first hacker in history to profit by infiltrating a bank's computer system. In 1995, he infiltrated Citibank USA and stole $10 million. He was arrested by Interpol in the UK in 1995, and transferred funds to accounts in the United States, Finland, Netherlands, Germany, and Ireland.

In 1999, the Melissa virus caused the collapse of computer systems in over 300 companies worldwide, resulting in nearly $400 million in damages. It was the first virus with global destructive power. The creator of the virus, David Smith, was only 30 years old at the time. David Smith was sentenced to five years in prison.

In 2000, a 15-year-old hacker nicknamed "Mafia Boy" successfully infiltrated the servers of major websites including Yahoo, eBay, and Amazon during the Valentine's Day period from February 6 to February 14, 2000. He successfully prevented the servers from providing services to users and was arrested in 2000.

In 2008, a global hacking organization used ATM fraud programs to steal $9 million from banks in 49 cities around the world in a single night. The hackers breached a banking system called RBS WorldPay, obtaining credit card information within the database using various techniques. On the night of November 8, they withdrew $9 million from more than 130 ATMs in 49 cities worldwide. Crucially, the FBI has yet to solve the case and reportedly hasn't even identified a suspect.

In China, the proportion of information network security incidents has been increasing year by year.

In August 2006, a 16-year-old dropout (online name Cai Ba, Xiumu) exploited a system vulnerability he discovered in Tencent's system. He illegally infiltrated the company’s computer information system and gradually obtained domain passwords and other important data through data stream monitoring, thereby gaining control permissions for multiple servers and PCs. He subsequently modified the passwords and password protection data for multiple user numbers, stealing valuable virtual assets. Subsequently, Tencent reported that approximately eighty internal systems, including OA management systems, customer service systems, virtual currency card management systems, and game backend management systems, had been illegally infiltrated. Additionally, malware was installed on 13 internal servers, severely damaging the company's computer information system.

At the end of 2007, Zhang Bo, only 23 years old, attacked a well-known portal site, causing the servers hosted in Beijing, Tianjin, Jinan, and Guangzhou data centers to completely fail, resulting in economic losses of 190,000 RMB, affecting millions of internet users browsing the website.

This year’s 5/19 internet outage incident (Storm Door) and the 6/25 QQ national disconnection event of unknown cause once again sounded the alarm for us, constantly reminding us of the importance of network security.

In the face of hackers' reckless attacks, what can we do? Perhaps so-called online security experts will tell you to install legitimate antivirus software and frequently update patches. Is this all we can do?

Famous hacker Kevin Mitnick spent five years in federal prison after two years of rampant hacking activities, and was banned from using the internet for eight years. At the Citrix iForum, he proposed that the best weapon against social engineering attacks targeting sensitive information is trained people, not technology. He said, "People usually rely on technology to solve problems, but social engineering bypasses all technology, including firewalls. Technology is crucial, but we should place more emphasis on people and methods. Social engineering is an attack method that uses enticement strategies. It is not merely a technical issue, but rather a human one."

Kevin Mitnick wasn't saying that technology isn't important. Network security, like anything else, is fundamentally human-centered. Therefore, the most critical issue remains human behavior.

JarryJarry once published a blog post titled "A Brief Discussion on Cybersecurity Awareness," mainly emphasizing the dominant role of security awareness in cybersecurity. Interested friends can click here to read it.

The current state of domestic security is quite alarming. Jarry once infiltrated a server. It was a virtual host running 200 websites. It was evident that this server lacked necessary security measures. A simple injection could expose all the data of these 200 websites to the public. Moreover, the permission settings were overly open. The entire hard drive partition had read and write permissions enabled, meaning you could format the partition at will, destroying all 200 websites.

Security awareness is indeed crucial, but technology is the weapon in our hands. With both, we can counteract hackers, using awareness as motivation and technology as a means to continuously strengthen our defenses.

Finally, I want to criticize our media. Over the years, various media outlets have painted the term "hacker" in increasingly darker tones in the public's mind, making their image ever more mysterious and demonized. Hackers are not mysterious; they are highly skilled network technicians, the elite in this field. There is another type of person in our internet world called "red hackers." Red hackers embody a spirit of patriotism, justice, and progress. Anyone who possesses this spirit and loves computer technology can be called a red hacker. Red hackers typically use their technical skills to maintain the security of domestic networks and counter foreign attacks.
