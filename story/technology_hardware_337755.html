How Much Do You Know About the Songs and Dances of the Tang Dynasty?

by micro365 on 2010-03-31 16:48:11

Through previous research, the author has confirmed the price of alcohol during the Tang Dynasty and verified the drinking capacity and marital status of the poet Li Bai. However, what comes to mind is the scene of wine accompanied by songs and dances, with flowing sleeves. So how much do we know about the songs and dances of the Tang Dynasty? Let's turn to history. As a type of court music known as "Yan Music," it was already established in the era of "The Rites of Zhou", "The Book of Rites" and "The Record of Rites" to serve banquets. In one of China's earliest classics - "The Book of Documents: Yi Xun," there are records of "constant dancing in the palace, revelry in the room, which is called witchcraft" as early forms of Yan Music. However, wine banquets and dances did not become a widespread social phenomenon until the Sui and Tang Dynasties. Compared to the past, the feasting and entertainment of this period was more lively and vivid.

According to historical records, Emperor Taizong Li Shimin not only innovated in the art of dance but also valued the direct absorption of the original essence of Western Region music and dance, a tradition inherited by all Tang emperors. Empress Wu Zetian, the only female emperor in Chinese history, continued the "Zhen Guan Prosperity" and initiated the "Kai Yuan Golden Age," laying the foundation for the full prosperity of the Tang Dynasty. The peak of music and dance was reached during the reign of Emperor Xuanzong Li Longji, who was well-known for his expertise in musical laws and his love for music and dance. It was he who created the "Qin Wang Po Zhen Qu" (Array Breaking Tune of King Qin) and the "Ni Chang Yu Yi Qu" (Feather Dress and Rainbow Skirt Dance), with the "Qin Wang Po Zhen Qu" being hailed as the national anthem of the Tang Dynasty.

There were two types of wine banquet dances in the Tang Dynasty: one was artistic appreciation-oriented wine banquet dances, where dancers and spectators had different roles. In these dances, the programs were predetermined, and the content mainly consisted of song singing and dance performances. The other type was game-oriented wine banquet dances. In these dances, drinkers were also performers, the programs were temporarily decided, and the lyrics were mostly improvised works. At that time, poets described the emphasis on dance art at wine banquets with phrases like "the color of wine in the cup should always be full, the songs in the tunes should never be tired" and "Qi songs send clear cups, rising dances disrupt the order." By the mid-Tang Dynasty, wine banquet dances spread throughout urban and rural areas, presenting an unprecedentedly grand spectacle. Phrases like "everywhere you hear strings, there is no lack of sending songs" and "songs and wine in every household, flowers everywhere" depicted the scene of drunken singing and dancing. This situation led to a change in the artistic components of wine banquets: dances that were once auxiliary content for feasting now became the main program. During the early and prosperous periods of the Tang Dynasty, common wine banquet dances were self-entertaining solo songs and dances. Li Bai made many descriptions of such dances, such as in "Let Us Drink": "Master Cen, Master Danqiu, let us drink, don't stop the cup, I will sing a song for you, please listen carefully." And in "Solo Drinking": "I drink alone, urging my lonely shadow, idly singing facing the fragrant forest." These dances were spontaneously inspired, did not need to follow game rules, thus they were not categorized as wine-order dances; but these dances had the function of encouraging drinking, representing a more simple form of sending-wine dances. From Li Bai's "I urge you a cup of wine, wipe the dust off your fur, you dance Chu for me, I sing Chu for you," it can be seen that these dances clearly imitated ancient self-entertainment dances.

Based on the above textual explanations, Tang Dynasty dances can be divided into two major categories: sending-wine dances and writing-lyric dances.

1. Characteristics of Sending-Wine Dances:

1. Most sending-wine dances were mutual responses, with the response song and the command song needing to be in the same tune. The rule for sending-wine singing was to write lyrics according to the tune.

2. Sending-wine adopted the form of one person holding a cup and inviting another person to sing. When the wine reached a certain person, that person could hold the cup and invite another person to sing and send wine. Therefore, it belonged to the category of singing within wine orders.

3. The banquet included both singing and dancing, with the dance using the form of command-dance followed by response-dance.

4. Various types of dances were interspersed throughout the banquet. For example, the sequence of entertainment recorded in the story of Jiang Chen was: first, women's voices sent wine. Dozens of female musicians, some sang and some danced. One of the songs ("Gong Wu Du He") used a poem by the poet Wang Rui from the Da Zhong period of Emperor Xuangong. Then various river gods took turns singing and dancing to send wine, also with singing and dancing. Finally, each guest contributed their own skills, with poetry recitation and singing. It can be seen that purely performance-oriented wine banquet dances coexisted with game-oriented wine banquet dances on the banquet.

2. Characteristics of Writing-Lyric Dances:

1. From the perspective of the relationship between singing and accompaniment, the opposite concept of "writing-lyrics" is "Xiang He Ge" (Mutual Harmony Songs), referred to as "string songs" in qin songs. "Xiang He Ge" includes vocal harmony, percussion instrument harmony, and wind-string instrument harmony.

2. All are matched with six-character verses; most use the "Hui Bo Le" tune, adopting the performance method of self-singing and self-dancing. During the performance, "dances are performed in rotation" according to unified dance rules.

3. They were produced under the background of wine banquet customs, all featuring fast-paced rhythms and using the fast three-beat structure. They were all dance tunes, so when folk tunes were introduced into the teaching halls, they were remade into large suites (large suites are musical works combining music, song, and dance, without dance tunes, there would be no large suites).

If the origin of sending-wine dances can be traced back to ancient self-entertainment wine banquet dances and ancient affiliated dances, then writing-lyric dances are specific products of medieval music culture. Their origins can be found through the three songs "Hui Bo Le," "Qing Bei Le," and "San Tai Ling," which are several music pieces with the same cultural characteristics. They all originated from Northern Dynasties popular music, being the first examples of Hu-Yi fusion music widely circulated in the Tang Dynasty, influencing all wine order tunes. This proves that Tang Dynasty writing-lyric dances are products of the historical event of Hu music entering China.

The Tang Dynasty was an unprecedentedly open era in China, although it is already very distant from us. However, places where overseas Chinese gather around the world are still called Chinatown, traditional Chinese clothing is called Tang costume, faintly reflecting the twilight of this brilliant era. The phenomenon of wine order dances transforming into rich Tang Dynasty dances welcomed the multi-cultural challenges of that era with a broad and inclusive heart, fully absorbing its essence. Not only did it enhance the vitality of our country's culture, but it also created the magnificent Tang culture through integration, smelting, and recasting, forming a Chinese cultural circle covering East Asia. We can also see that for a country to be strong, it must have a stable social environment, govern people with virtue, and adapt policies to meet the needs of the times and public opinion! Currently, China is entering a new Tang Dynasty...
