A promise in return for a lifetime

by adfhb3fk on 2011-09-08 19:52:36

My little baby is born!!

There was a girl who, when she was young, had weak legs and could only sit at the door all year round watching other children play, feeling very lonely. One summer, a relative from the city of a neighboring family came to visit, bringing their child, a boy five years older than the girl. Because they were both young, the boy quickly became friends with the nearby children, climbing mountains and crossing rivers with them, getting just as tan, and laughing happily. The difference was that he didn't swear, and he noticed a little girl who couldn't walk. The boy was the first to put the dragonflies he caught in the girl's palm, the first to carry her to the river, the first to tell her stories, the first to tell her that her legs could be cured. The first, come to think of it, was also the last. The girl rarely smiled. When summer was about to end, the boy's family was leaving. The girl tearfully came to see them off and quietly said in his ear: "If I cure my legs, will you marry me?" The boy nodded. In the blink of an eye, twenty years have passed. The boy grew from a naive child into a mature man. He opened a coffee shop, had a fiancee, and led a very ordinary and peaceful life. One day, he received a call. A woman's delicate voice told him that her legs were healed, and she had come to this city. For a time, he couldn't even remember who she was. He had long forgotten the story of a certain summer in his childhood, forgotten the pale-faced little girl, and forgotten a child's kind promise. However, he still took her in and let her help out in the store. He found that she was almost always silent. But he had no time to care about her, his fiancee was pregnant with someone else's child. He was filled with shame and anger, threw away all the things prepared for marriage, drank daily, became violent and irritable, alienated by his family, and neglected his business. Soon, he fell seriously ill. During this time, she stayed by his side, taking care of him, tolerating his drunken scolding, and independently propping up the teetering store. She learned a lot, and became emaciated from exhaustion, but there was always a sparkle in her eyes. Six months later, he finally recovered. Faced with everything she had done, he only felt gratitude. He gave the store to her, but she insisted on not accepting it, so he announced that she was half-owner of the store. With her help, he slowly regained his spirit. He regarded her as a close friend, pouring out his heart to her. She remained silently listening. He didn't understand what she was thinking, he just needed a patient listener. Several more years passed, and he dated a few girlfriends, none of which lasted long. He couldn't find the right feeling anymore. Neither could she; she remained unmarried. He realized that she was actually very elegant, naturally charming, and not lacking in suitors. He teased her for being too picky, and she just smiled. One day, he tired of his tranquil state and decided to go out and travel. Before getting his passport, he formally handed over everything in the store to her. This time, on a rainy day, she didn't oppose it, only saying that she would keep it for him until he returned. Life wandering in a foreign land was hard, yet in this hardship, he found a broadened perspective and mind. All past sorrows seemed distant and light, and he suddenly realized that through sickness and health, poverty or wealth, success or failure, the one truly by his side was her. His whereabouts were uncertain, psychological and drug therapy were temporarily unsuitable for infants, but her letters followed him closely, brief words, lightly written, yet always warm. He thought it was time to return home. When he returned home, he was moved by her painstaking efforts. Whether at home or in the store, his things and his place were well preserved, as if waiting for him to return at any moment. He loudly called her name, but no one answered. The store had a new manager, who told him that she had died of exhaustion half a year ago. According to her instructions, he had specially assigned people to track his movements, sending out hundreds of letters she left behind one by one, managing the store for him, tidying up his house, and waiting for him to return. He handed over her belongings to him, a dragonfly specimen, and a cassette tape, which was her last words. There was only her faint whisper like a young girl regaining consciousness: "I... will marry you... okay?..." Throwing away 27 years of time, he cried like a child. No one knows, sometimes, a woman has to use her whole life to say such a simple sentence...
