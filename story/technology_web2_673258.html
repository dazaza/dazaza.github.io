Sale of "Lin Shuhao" domain name for less than 260,000 is "impossible"

by xwkj0983 on 2012-02-23 14:59:01

The outstanding performance of NBA rising star Jeremy Lin on the court has recently attracted global attention. A netizen revealed that they had accidentally discovered that the pinyin domain name for Lin Shuhao (Jeremy Lin) already existed, and the holder expressed willingness to transfer the domain. Yesterday, a reporter contacted the holder of the www.linshuhao.com domain.

**High Demand for Domain Transfer**

Yesterday, the reporter clicked on the aforementioned domain and found that the page was publishing information about the domain being available for transfer. According to a report by Forbes, "Lin Shuhao" has become one of the fastest-growing valuable brand names in the world today, with an estimated value of approximately $14 million. Forbes stated that Lin Shuhao's brand value is equal to Kobe Bryant’s from the Los Angeles Lakers, ranking sixth among athletes globally. The seller indicated in the advertisement: "The domain you are visiting can be transferred!"

In addition, the page provided contact details for potential buyers, allowing the reporter to connect with the seller. The seller stated: "There are too many inquiries for quotes now. Please provide your budget price, otherwise we will not respond." Media reports suggested that someone had offered 260,000 yuan for the domain. Currently, it seems unlikely to purchase the "Lin Shuhao" domain for less than 260,000 yuan.

**Low Cost of Domain Registration**

Selling a domain could bring in hundreds of thousands of yuan, but what is the holding cost? It was reported that the seller of the Chinese domain name for Lin Shuhao said their annual fee was only over 60 yuan. Yesterday, the reporter logged into a well-known domain service provider's website, where the information showed that if registering an English domain, the annual charge would be 139 yuan; if paying three years in advance, the annual fee would be 116 yuan; if paying five years in advance, the annual fee would be just 99.6 yuan.

**Compliance Depends on the Situation**

Yesterday, the reporter interviewed Ma Hui from the Tianjin branch of Zhonglun Wende Law Firm regarding domain registration. Ma Hui stated that whether cybersquatting constitutes infringement depends on the specific situation. "Although personal names are legally protected, if the cybersquatting involves Chinese pinyin, the same pinyin may have multiple Chinese character interpretations, making it difficult to determine infringement based solely on this point. Even if it involves a Chinese domain name, due to the possibility of homonyms, one must specifically examine the purpose of the domain after cybersquatting." Ma Hui added that apart from cybersquatting on celebrity names, there are also cases of cybersquatting on company or product names. In such cases, one must check whether the cybersquatted pinyin or Chinese domain name is already a registered trademark. If it wasn’t a registered trademark at the time of cybersquatting, it is difficult to judge whether it constituted a violation.

**High-Value Domains Sold**

In fact, registering famous people's domain names at low cost and then reselling them at high prices is no longer a new phenomenon. After Zhao Benshan's skit "Not Short of Money" became popular, the holder of the "Not Short of Money" domain successfully transferred the domain, earning tens of thousands of yuan within a few months with an initial investment of just a few dozen yuan. In January this year, according to media reports, influenced by the official railway department website domain 12306.cn, the 12306.com domain was sold for a high price of 3.8 million yuan.
