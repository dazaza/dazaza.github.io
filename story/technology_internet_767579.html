I am an Indian programmer, and I want to stand up for Indian programmers.

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 11:18:02

English Title: Bad Indian Programmers

I recently came across a post on Reddit. It was about a Reddit user "chinan" who was forced to maintain a project that was initially outsourced to an Indian PHP programmer.

He was devastated.

This amusing post was filled with all sorts of funny remarks by active Reddit browsers, but most of them had never dealt with Indian programmers.

As an Indian programmer, I want to defend.

Living in India

I just left India. In India, I used to spend 3 hours every day commuting to work (driving or taking the bus), which adds up to 15 hours a week.

Public transportation is usually extremely crowded. The summer temperature can reach 45 degrees Celsius. Everything requires queuing, long queues that are incredibly frustrating.

To put it briefly, the living environment is terrible, beyond words.

If you live there, you'll understand what I mean.

If I told this to someone working in India and they could still be happy living like that, I would really admire them.

Perhaps, just perhaps, quality of life and quality of work are closely related?

Do I deny that Indian programmers are bad?

Maybe. Having worked with Indian colleagues for 10 years, I've seen some truly horrifying Indian programmers that might scare you more than you know.

But I've also seen many Indian programmers who are extraordinarily talented, beyond your imagination.

Looking at India's economic situation, since the mid-1990s, IT/software has been the hottest industry for young Indians, and they flock into it — regardless of whether they're actually interested in the field.

This is why many Indian programmers you encounter lack passion, motivation, and produce low-quality software.

They're not stupid; they lack passion.

I wouldn't blame this on India's education system. That's an excuse. If they have passion, they can completely overcome such unfavorable limitations.

It's precisely because they lack passion that they choose to outsource themselves.

Since they lack passion (as I said), why would you choose to outsource your projects to them?

If you choose to do so, isn't it your responsibility to inspire their passion?

Failed Software Projects

Even with the most brilliant programmers, a large number of various projects ultimately fail to see the light of day.

This is the nature of software development. This is why various gurus keep selling us their methodologies (Agile, Scrum, Kanban, the-latest-greatest-process-of-today), as if these theories are silver bullets that solve all problems.

Can you blame the failure of the project entirely on these "Indian guys"?

Other People's Code is Always Awful

Maintaining legacy code, no matter how well-written it is, will always be a challenge.

Even if you understand its design logic and the purpose behind each product change, the challenge remains.

Legacy code will always become a problem — no matter where or when it was written.

The Trade-off Between Cost and Quality

You want to hire a programmer for less than $20/hour.

You expect to get the quality of work from a highly skilled programmer charging $200/hour.

Don't have such unrealistic expectations.

Code Quality is a Shared Responsibility

If X recruited Y, and Y is terrible, then X is also terrible.

If X manages Y, and Y makes mistakes, then X isn't doing well either.

If X works with Y, Y is terrible, and X fails to positively influence Y, allowing Y to continue being terrible, then X is also terrible.

The real-life situation of outsourcing companies may be even worse.

Many of the Xs I've worked with:

Hope to save costs

Hope to finish immediately

Don't know what they want (requirements issue)

Change their minds every two weeks

Sit around doing nothing, hoping for a miracle to happen on "milestone day"

Now our funds are depleted. Isn't it easiest to blame the bad Indian programmers?

Tsk, it's not my fault. It's not my responsibility.

Look at these absolutely terrible Indian programmers!
