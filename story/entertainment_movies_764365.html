Synopsis:

South Korean director Kim Ki-duk's films have always been bold and radical in their subject matter. In February 2004, he won the Best Director award at the 54th Berlin International Film Festival, becoming the first South Korean director to win an award at that festival. His new work, "Spring, Summer, Fall, Winter...and Spring," changes his previous style and uses the story of a monk's growth to explain the principle that life is like the endless cycle of the four seasons.

In spring, the playful young novice ties stones to fish, frogs, and snakes, teasing the small creatures. The old monk (played by Wu Yong-soo) discovers this and punishes him with the same method, warning him not to misbehave.

In summer, the seventeen-year-old young monk (played by Kim Young-min) finds the days of seclusion in the deep mountains tedious. One day, a sick girl (played by He Li-zhen) comes to the temple with her mother to pray for peace and stays there to recuperate. The young man begins to be unable to resist the temptation brought by the attraction of the opposite sex...

In autumn, the young man returns to the temple after killing someone outside, seeking forgiveness. The old monk orders him to carve the Heart Sutra, to remove his sins, while at the same time, the police chasing the fugitive also arrive at the temple...

In winter, the middle-aged man (played by Kim Ki-duk) who has served his sentence returns to the mountain temple and begins to practice again. A woman suddenly visits the temple and leaves a male baby, hoping it can be taken in.

Afterward, another spring arrives.

Behind-the-scenes Production:

A person's life is like the recurring four seasons. The ending of the film returns to the beginning, and such narrative may no longer be fresh in today's cinema, but South Korean director Kim Ki-duk has a way of making people feel an indescribable subtle sadness. First, the scenery in the movie is like an ink painting, the temple on the water, the flowers of spring and the moon of autumn, all are meticulously depicted, making the audience feel as if they were there; secondly, the film is full of Zen opportunities, filled with various metaphors about life. Viewers with different insights will certainly have different feelings; finally, it is still the combination of things that the director excels at, blending seamlessly, making Easterners feel familiar when watching such scenes, and Westerners satisfying their curiosity without being too rigid and obscure. Therefore, this movie can be considered another successful example of Kim Ki-duk's work.

The South Korean movie "Spring, Summer, Autumn, Winter and Spring" —— A Buddhist Verse

The 24th Blue Dragon Awards for Best Film, directed by the famous director Kim Ki-duk. Let's start with the name. The English title of the movie is "Spring, Summer, Fall, Winter...and Spring." I prefer its direct translation "Spring, Summer, Autumn, Winter and Spring Again," or according to my own understanding, I call it "Reincarnation." Why? It will be explained in detail below.

The movie is divided into five parts, each part's title combined forms the name of the film. In the "Spring" section, it tells the story of the protagonist's childhood. In a small temple, an elderly monk and a young novice live together. One day, the child monk plays around and ties stones to three animals: a fish, a frog, and a snake, ultimately leading to the death of two of them. The child monk breaks the commandment against killing, which could be called the origin of sin. In the "Summer" section, the child monk grows into a teenager. At this time, a young girl comes to the temple to recuperate from an illness. The young monk tempts her to engage in sexual relations, breaking the commandment against lust, deepening his sins further. After the incident, the girl is persuaded by the old monk to return home, and the young monk leaves without saying goodbye. In the "Autumn" section, more than ten years later, the protagonist in his thirties cannot bear his wife's infidelity, kills her, and flees back to the small temple. Under the enlightenment of the old monk, he confesses and serves his sentence. This segment deepens the sins even further. In the "Winter" section, after serving his sentence, the protagonist returns to the small temple again, only to find the old monk has passed away. He inherits the old monk's legacy and devotes himself to studying Buddhism. Unexpectedly one day, his disfigured but still alive wife brings a baby, intending to leave the baby and sneak away when she accidentally falls into an icy pond and dies. (This speculation is actually uncertain because I have never seen the woman's face.) The monk feels even more burdened by his sins, carrying stones and hugging Buddha statues to climb high mountains in order to seek liberation. "And Spring Again," the old monk and the child monk start a new life. One day, the child monk plays around and puts stones in the mouths of a fish, a frog, and a snake...

The Cycle of Life

From the entire form of the movie, it can be known that it speaks exactly about one of the important ideas of Buddhism - reincarnation. Starting with the sins of a young monk and ending with the sins of the next generation of young monks, it is precisely a cycle. I believe that the already deceased old monk must have committed the same sins in his youth. All the processes of sin and redemption are constantly cycling and repeating. The director merely selects one person's four seasons of life to show us.

Besides the form, in terms of content, the director is also ingenious everywhere, weaving the teachings of Buddhism throughout the entire movie. Buddhism believes that life is suffering, and living in this world means enduring hardships and torment. The desire to play is suffering, the desire for sex is suffering, and the emotional setbacks between men and women are also suffering. If a person cannot overcome these various sufferings, they will inevitably commit sins and be sent to the eighteenth level of hell after death. Sins can be redeemed through cultivation, like the hard training in the movie, carrying heavy stones to climb mountains. More interestingly, the protagonist obtains an ancient martial arts book from the old monk's relics and starts practicing martial arts, indicating that the director is quite influenced by our martial arts films. Moreover, the director somewhat deifies the cultivated old monk, as with a wave of his hand, the small boat moves freely, the mountain gate opens and closes automatically, and the isolated temple-like small temple can move on the water surface. It seems that the director cannot simply talk about Buddhist principles but also wants us to see "Buddha's boundless power" with our own eyes. Actually, the highest realm of thought and cultivation is "extinction," meaning the liberation from烦恼. The old monk finally sets fire to himself on the small boat. Has he been liberated? I don't know, but the director allows the protagonist to extract something similar to relics from his ashes, perhaps believing that the old monk has "extinguished."

Violence and Religion

Director Kim Ki-duk's works have always been bold and unique, and this film is no exception. Just the content is already very peculiar, a story about a monk talking about Buddhist principles, yet it mixes naked sex and violence. Compared to the director's other works like "Address Unknown," the depiction of sex has already been restrained a lot, mostly using long shots to express, and the scene in the prayer hall is conducted under the blanket, despite this, many people should already feel it as a great sacrilege. From torturing animals to self-mutilation and suicide, it continues Kim Ki-duk's previous style, while also showing some restraint. Originally, this was a Buddhist teaching film guiding people towards goodness, is the use of violence and bloodshed appropriate? This question is worth discussing. Evil and good are opposites, talking about good actually cannot avoid talking about evil, and evil itself will not disappear just because you avoid it. Bloodshed and violence have always existed in the content of Buddhist teachings, such as the Buddha himself once "sacrificed his body to feed the tiger" and "cut his flesh to feed the eagle," if presented in movie images, it would definitely be very bloody scenes. I once saw a Dunhuang mural depicting the hell in Buddhist legends, with decapitations, nose-cutting, eye-gouging, and various cruel punishments, making people feel terrified. I think religion uses such extreme bloodshed and violence to face the most ordinary lay believers. Only such extreme and strong methods can make the common people respect and believe more, thereby achieving the purpose of spreading the teachings. Similarly, the Bible of Christianity says that Jesus suffered so much hardship and extremely cruel violence before his resurrection for all humanity, aiming to make us always cherish gratitude. As for whether Mel Gibson completely transferred this bloodshed and violence to the big screen is correct, that is really difficult to distinguish. In addition, Du Qifeng's "Big Gangster" also borrows violence and murder to talk about Buddhist principles, which I once did not agree with, but now looking back, it seems to have its own logic.

The entire movie has only two supporting characters who are named, while others remain unnamed, further showing that this is a parable, a Buddhist verse. Nameless and surname-less, it is like an X, any of our names can be substituted. The dialogue in the movie is also scarce, only used when the plot cannot be conveyed through the visuals alone. Moreover, whether it's the dialogue, plot, or setting of the film, they are all full of Zen meaning and sharpness. For instance, the old monk enlightens the young novice with his own methods after he commits wrongdoings, the seemingly non-existent mountain gate and temple doors, and the small temple surrounded by water yet entangled with the outside world... Everything depends on whether you can understand it. The movie uses the four seasons to represent different stages of life, so the surrounding scenery is clearly defined by the four seasons. It seems that the director spent a whole year to complete the movie, because the spring flowers, summer greens, autumn leaves, and winter snow of the same location cannot be faked, showing the director's sincerity in making this film.

The movie is like a Buddhist verse that enlightens life. The last shot is quite profound, the Buddha statue located on the mountaintop quietly gazing, as if observing all the joys and sorrows of the mortal world...
