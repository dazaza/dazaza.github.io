So every time they ask us to be there at three in the afternoon.

by loveyan5 on 2012-02-09 10:17:16

The path from being a junior college student in a school that was about to close down, to becoming a postgraduate of a key university, and finally signing with the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China (ICBC) as a permanent staff member, was very arduous.

As a child, I was an average student, an average playful kid, and had average intelligence. There was nothing special about me. My parents spent a lot of money to get me into the best high school in our city, but my grades were always mediocre. I also had a bit of a playfulness streak. I remember failing my first college entrance exam miserably, not even reaching the second-tier undergraduate line. Unwilling to attend a junior college, I decided to retake my exams. During this time, I felt like I had already mastered everything the teachers taught, so I didn't study hard. As a result, my second attempt at the college entrance exam only slightly improved upon my first try, just barely passing the second-tier undergraduate line by 5 points.

After much deliberation, I thought attending a bachelor's program at a certain agricultural university would be fine, but unexpectedly, that year their admission line was exceptionally high, surpassing previous years by a large margin. Thus, I failed again to enter a second-tier undergraduate program, leaving me no choice but to attend a junior college. I accepted this fate because I really didn't want to retake my exams again.

I applied to a junior college in Beijing without ever having been there before. Back then, information wasn't as accessible as it is now, so I simply chose based on the name of the school. The name "China Defense Science and Technology Institute" sounded good, so I applied and was surprisingly admitted. Thus, I went to Beijing.

My hometown is in the southern part of the country, and neither my parents nor I had ever been to Beijing. However, I went alone, thinking that as a man, I shouldn't be afraid. Luckily, there was a direct train from my city to Beijing, so my parents helped me board the train and left me to my own devices since they couldn't accompany me further. They believed boys should be independent and venture out into the world.

When the train arrived in Beijing at dusk, there was a school bus waiting for us at the station. We drove past Tiananmen Square that night, but I didn't feel much since it was my first time seeing it. After driving for nearly three hours, we finally reached our destination. Getting off the bus, I wondered if this was really Beijing. It seemed desolate, with sparse human habitation and darkness all around. This place was far worse than the small towns in my area. Later, I found out this wasn't Beijing but Yanjiao Town in Sanhe City, Hebei Province, adjacent to Beijing. The China Defense Science and Technology Institute was located here. The school was smaller and more run-down than my high school.

Gradually, I learned more about the school. We were the first batch of students formally recruited through the college entrance exam after the school was newly established. Many people discovered how terrible the school was and dropped out halfway, but I persevered. I believed that as long as one has talent, they will shine regardless of where they are. Most of the students who came to this junior college were academically poor and came here to pass the time, but I was different. While others played cards and games, I studied amidst their distractions, reminiscent of Mao Zedong reading in a bustling market.

Although my academic performance was above average, it wasn't outstanding. When the three-year junior college life was nearing its end, the school announced that some students could directly advance to a bachelor's degree program. The top 14 students based on comprehensive scores could continue studying for another year and directly obtain a bachelor's degree, equivalent to those who entered through the national college entrance exam, without needing a separate junior-to-bachelor exam. Tragically, I ranked 16th, seemingly missing out on the opportunity. However, the school later added two more spots, making a total of 16 eligible students, with me being the last one. I felt like I had gone from hell to heaven.

In my senior year, many wanted to take the postgraduate entrance exam and leave. Although many registered, most took it just for fun or luck, but I persisted like before. While others played games or poker, I continued studying. In the end, only two of us in the entire grade were admitted to postgraduate programs, including me. The whole school had few successful candidates, perhaps around ten.

I was admitted to a key university in Beijing for my postgraduate studies. The major I chose wasn't very popular in the school, but I aimed for it because the chances of getting in were higher. For me, success meant getting admitted, whether to a popular or unpopular major. Eventually, I succeeded.

Here, I must mention my alma mater, the China Defense Science and Technology Institute. As part of the first batch of formally recruited students, the management was still relatively decent, though not exceptional. However, starting from the next batch, the school began admitting students irregularly, accepting those who failed to even reach the junior college line by paying tens of thousands or even hundreds of thousands of yuan, promising them official diplomas. Unfortunately, these promises couldn't be fulfilled, leading to numerous complaints and protests which eventually reached the Ministry of Education. The outcome was a one-year suspension of admissions, repeated each subsequent year due to continued violations. After three consecutive years of suspensions, the school was effectively doomed due to lack of students.

Reflecting now, the school's irregular admissions were partly due to financial constraints. Since the Ministry of Education did not provide any funding, the school had to resort to such measures to sustain operations. However, this was akin to drinking poison to quench thirst, leading to its eventual demise. This issue reflects a deeper problem in China's education system. The victims were the students who paid exorbitant fees, wasted several prime years of their youth, gained no knowledge, lost their money, and ended up without any formal qualifications, a devastating blow to young individuals.

Moving on to my postgraduate life, when I arrived at this 211 key university, I realized how truly wonderful university life could be. Compared to my previous school, it was a world apart. My previous institution was merely a place to pass the time, lacking proper teaching facilities and employing graduate students from other schools as instructors just to make ends meet. At this new university, I actively participated in various activities unavailable at my previous school. Despite my academic performance still being average, I faced competition from more talented peers. Additionally, my weak foundation became apparent as many essential courses, like calculus, linear algebra, and probability theory, were never offered during my undergraduate studies. I self-studied mathematics to pass the postgraduate entrance exam.

During these three years of postgraduate studies, I learned much more than expected, filling in gaps from my undergraduate education. Besides academics, I developed significantly in various aspects, including life philosophy, interpersonal relationships, and social affairs handling. These improvements laid a solid foundation for my future life.

Upon nearing graduation, job hunting began. Initially, I randomly sent out resumes without much strategy, resulting in negligible outcomes. Among the offers received, I considered three companies: Zhongyou Ruifei Information Technology Co., Ltd., Postal Savings Bank of China Beijing Branch, and Century Oriental National Rail Co., Ltd. The first two companies were relatively professional in their recruitment processes, while the third company, Century Oriental National Rail Co., Ltd., was extremely inefficient. Each round of interviews required traveling from North Fourth Ring Road (where our university was located) to South Fourth Ring Road (where the company was located), taking over two hours each way, totaling nearly five hours per trip. To make matters worse, each interview involved waiting for hours until a leader became available. I was required to arrive by 1 PM, so I would leave early in the morning to ensure punctuality. Each time, I waited from 1 PM until 5 PM for the interview, finishing just as it got dark, missing dinner back at school. This process occurred five times, wasting five full days and costing me dozens of yuan in transportation fees. After waiting for two months without any updates, I confronted their HR representative at a Tsinghua University job fair. They explained they hadn't yet filled all positions and planned to contact me soon. Upon receiving a call from their Human Resources Director, I declined their offer, advising them to consider applicants' time and effort more seriously. Their surprise was evident, leaving them speechless.

Among the best options for job hunting, I believe banks are ideal, especially given their large-scale recruitment drives towards the end of each year. I applied to several banks but was rejected during the interviews, gaining valuable experience along the way. Finally, I made it to the final round of interviews at ICBC, competing against top talents from prestigious universities like Tsinghua and Peking University. Despite my school being a 211 key university, it was still inferior compared to Tsinghua and Peking. Remaining calm, I performed exceptionally well during the interview, successfully passing psychological tests. ICBC contacted me the following evening, offering me the position. Even surpassing candidates from Tsinghua and Peking University, I smoothly passed physical examinations and signed the contract. ICBC's HR team was highly professional, treating us with respect and thoroughly answering all our questions regarding benefits and development opportunities.

Among the other offers I received, I ultimately chose ICBC due to its superior benefits, status as the largest bank in the world by market value, and its humane treatment of employees, valuing us as talents.

Additionally, I applied for a civil servant position at the Hunan Provincial Highway Bureau, which reimbursed travel expenses. Competing against graduates from top local universities like Hunan University and Central South University, I ranked first in the interview, significantly outperforming the second-place candidate. The bureau's leadership approached me after the interview, expressing interest in hiring me. However, after careful consideration, I declined the offer to give others a chance.

I attribute my success in interviews to the numerous experiences gained in Beijing, contrasting sharply with the lack of exposure among local university graduates. This highlights the importance of sending children to big cities for education, broadening their horizons.

To summarize, perseverance is crucial regardless of circumstances. Networking and maintaining strong interpersonal relationships are vital in society. Lastly, staying calm and confident during challenging situations, especially job hunting, is key.

The only regret during my postgraduate studies was not having a girlfriend, a lesson everyone should learn from.

Thank you for reading my lengthy experience.
