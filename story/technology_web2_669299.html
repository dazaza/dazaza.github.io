On July 24, 2007, Blizzard Entertainment in California, U.S. announced that the number of global players of the Massively Multiplayer Online Role-Playing Game (MMORPG) "World of Warcraft" had exceeded 9 million, reaching a new milestone. For Blizzard, it was both good and bad news as they would face immense pressure to update; constantly introducing new instances; balancing the Player vs Player (PvP) system accordingly; Blizzard lacked sufficient time for development and testing; and the game world was gradually becoming unbalanced. The complexity of "World of Warcraft" has led to many irreconcilable contradictions regarding class balance. For instance: World of Warcraft has nine classes, each with three talent trees, and there are significant differences between different talents. For example: Shadow Priest, Holy Priest, Protection Warrior, Fury Warrior - they can practically be considered entirely different classes. In other words, we can consider that "World of Warcraft" has at least 27 classes! To achieve balance in a game with at least 27 or even more classes is fundamentally an impossible task! Moreover, the overall game framework of "World of Warcraft" is too simple - TANK, HEAL, and DPS. To ensure that at least 27 or even more classes find sufficiently unique roles in these three areas, it would require a decade of refinement. Is it possible?

Blizzard's Class Balance

Blizzard once posted the principles of class balance design on the WoW class forum:

1. All classes should be able to solo up to the maximum level;

2. All classes should find suitable roles in a 5-man group;

3. Each class should have an important status in Raids;

4. Each class should be competitive in PvP and have significant differences from other classes. In 5v5 duels, each class should have its own special moves;

5. Each class should be fun.

Indeed, Blizzard does not have a principle of class balance like: if a Rogue and a Warrior with equal equipment, level, and operational skills fight, they should each have a 50% chance of winning. Because that is too difficult, and it is also what players often nitpick about. In fact, this is all that Blizzard can and possibly achieve. "World of Warcraft" will never reach the class balance that players expect.

Case Analysis: Hunter vs Rogue

Blizzard has said that the Rogue is the standard for DPS.

This means that the strength of any class's DPS should be measured against the Rogue.

The DPS classes in "World of Warcraft" can be simply divided into ranged DPS and melee DPS.

Basic Elements

1. The Hunter is a ranged DPS, which gives it a distance output advantage over melee DPS.

(1) It takes time to approach a target;

(2) Melee DPS close to the target is prone to being attacked (taking damage), while ranged DPS is safer.

Therefore, the Hunter's DPS cannot be higher than the Rogue's.

2. Hunters have pets, Rogues do not. The pet's DPS cannot be too low, otherwise, it would become just decoration.

Thus, the combined DPS of the Hunter and the pet is lower than the Rogue's, making the Hunter's DPS even lower compared to the Rogue's.

Problems

1. If the Hunter's DPS is much lower than the Rogue's, why bring a Hunter to a Raid?

(1) Give the Hunter a special skill that must be used for certain Raid bosses;

(2) Let some bosses have intermittent AoE effects, reducing the Rogue's DPS time.

However, the team can raid certain bosses without Hunters, and some bosses without Rogues.

Relative to other classes, this creates imbalance again.

(3) Adjust the Hunter's talents so they cannot use their pet or increase the Rogue's dodge talent?

Pets are the Hunter's feature. Balance is important, but fun is also important; without pets, Hunters will lose their farming ability, and if the above hypothetical Rogue talent adjustment occurs, PvP will lose balance.

(4) If a Hunter is not afraid of getting close?

Getting close and breaking free in PK are important components of showcasing a Hunter's operational feel. Reducing the game's operability will lose Blizzard's characteristic!

Online games maintain player loyalty and game playability through constant updates, while balance is a static state; constant changes, how to balance?

Experiencing WoW

1. Graphics

The graphics of the game are the first direct impression given to players. Players who pursue "beauty" in reality, let alone in games? WoW uses the DDO game engine, which leans towards the antiquity of textures, allowing WoW's beautiful cartoon style to display the fantastical world of Azeroth. With a moderate amount of polygons, it greatly reduces the resource consumption of gaming machines. Combined with Blizzard's top-notch artistry, it creates a smooth yet splendid visual impact.

2. Sound

In terms of game music, relative to the vast number of maps, the effect is still somewhat thin. Multiple scenes use the same music, always giving a sense of déjà vu. Every piece of music is very exquisite and expresses its own thoughts.

3. Story Background

No one denies the grandeur and beauty of WoW's story background, just as no one denies Mr. J.R. Tolkien's stories. Without his "Lord of the Rings," perhaps we wouldn't have the fantastical world of Azeroth either—"one book, one world," Mr. Tolkien said. He was actually telling the world that every existence has its own characteristics. Just like game design, the most important factor in WoW's success should be its rigorous world structure and reasonable game rules. Speaking of rules, the term AD&D immediately comes to mind. Since "The Secret History of the King" and "Dungeons & Dragons," RPGs have had a set of rules: leveling up, items, etc. These rules have already been familiar to players, so most RPG players also go for the game's plot and story background. Players can find the battles and chaos they experienced in the "Warcraft" series within WoW. Domestically, many game designers, to a greater or lesser extent, are learning from World of Warcraft, introducing some of its game systems and settings. But all domestic game designers look down on World of Warcraft because they only imitate its system rather than its true "essence." Therefore, the games they design are suppressed by World of Warcraft.

4. PvP (Factions, Battlefields, Arenas)

The two factions form the confrontation setting in WoW. They are like the two trays of a scale, balancing the entire game world. Although balance is crucial, it cannot be regarded as part of the gameplay. We can see the factions in WoW, but immediately notice the presence of conflict. There must be another attribute comparable to the confrontational elements in WoW, and that is layers. Here, layers refer to the difficulty for players to understand the game rules and the inherent meaning of the rules themselves. On the other hand, if the rule hierarchy in WoW is weak, then it must first be set as hard to understand, thereby increasing its sustainability to retain players. Secondly, it must have another aspect of game rules: balance and confrontation. The setup of factions, battlefields, and arenas initially aims to enhance player interaction through online game interactivity, forming a team foundation, creating默契through teamwork, and helping players find enjoyment in the game, relaxing themselves. However, as competition in the game increases, players develop a competitive mindset and forget the original purpose of entering the game. PFU (Play for Uber, honor above all) and PFF (Play for Fun, fun first) thus become standards by which WoW players constantly evaluate themselves! PFU players are derivatives caused by competitiveness, one could say PFU is honor and life. They take strategic honor goals as the highest direction, prioritizing everything for the ultimate goal, endlessly pursuing it. This spiritual pursuit is beyond the reach of PFF players. In fact, players understand whether their designed gameplay is successful through continuous faction, battlefield, and arena confrontations—when successful, they feel happy, which is also the joy of factional confrontation and other PvP aspects in WoW. Of course, if it is proven that others' strategies are better than theirs, learning is necessary, so WoW videos on youtube.com often have high click rates.

5. Quest System

WoW's quest system mainly exists in the traditional A-B model quest format, and on this basis introduces the concept of "instances." The birth of instances is a milestone. However, Blizzard's design for later Raid instances, attempting to improve player experience through quantitative change, failed. MC and BWL were basically acceptable, but when it came to Naxxramas, all requirements suddenly became extreme, even appearing BOSSes like Electrolyte Man that extremely demand network and server quality and machine specifications. The long duration of 40-player instances and the drawbacks of reduced distribution due to large numbers led to many players AFK or leaving directly. In fact, regarding WoW instances, necessary innovations in instance concepts are now particularly important. We can try to solve this problem using PSP games like "Monster Hunter" or adopt new instance concepts based on it, such as "Guild Wars."

Blizzard's "Learning"

Games like WoW, which are deep and highly engaging large-scale graphical online games, have complex gameplay from the perspective of game characteristics, but they are based on traditional wisdom dialogues, so user growth is relatively stable. Deep online games have a problem: they are quickly replaced! What happened to those few online games that started operations in 2000? And WoW continues to keep players loyal to the game thanks to Blizzard's "sustainability" principle.

Here, "sustainability" should be included in the game's playability. Actually, it's not just about whether players want to play this game; it represents whether a player looks forward to the next installment of this game. Blizzard once said they don't plan to release a sequel to "StarCraft" because they think "StarCraft" is already good enough. However, the craze for "Warcraft III" shows that if "StarCraft II" were really released, it would cause a huge sensation.

Regarding this sensation, we can reflect and identify problems. In "Diablo II," players don't aim to complete the game early, and even PKing on Battle.net isn't the main purpose - playing this game is to find better equipment. Thus, "Diablo II: Expansion Set" naturally appeared, adding a lot of equipment, satisfying everyone. We can think this way: the reason we look forward to its expansion pack isn't because it offers more gameplay (although it indeed provides more gameplay), but for the equipment. Blizzard clearly realized this point, which is why the expansion pack sold so well.

And this "sustainability" can also become the biggest selling point of a game, like WoW. While playing the game, people also focus on its new expansion packs. After waiting, when players finally get the sequel they've been looking forward to, besides comparing it with the previous version, they unconsciously start finding advantages of the new version due to their long wait.

Conclusion

Actually, before playing a game, whether it's WoW or other games, we always have an idea in our minds about what the game should (possibly, maybe, roughly) be like. Then, after truly playing it, we compare the actual situation with our expectations. A good game must have outstanding playability in at least some aspects, and if its playability happens to align with your expectations, then the game is already on your hard drive.

That's how we fall into the abyss of gaming.
