Top Ten Misunderstandings about Underfloor Heating Systems

by longersing on 2009-11-17 15:31:49

The floor industry has many irregularities, and how many of the top ten misconceptions about underfloor heating floors do you know? As it is currently the preparation stage for winter heating, and also the peak sales season for flooring, the consultation hotline for flooring is constantly ringing, with more inquiries than can be handled, among which the most numerous are inquiries about underfloor heating floors! "Which type of floor is good for my home's underfloor heating?"

"Is thicker flooring better for warmth?" "Is an aluminum foil foam pad specifically for underfloor heating?"... To clear up the confusion and help consumers make informed choices when selecting and using underfloor heating floors, our association's expert group has summarized the top ten misleading points in current underfloor heating floor consumption as a warning:

1. "Are all types of flooring suitable for underfloor heating?"

"Can your flooring be used for underfloor heating?" The salesperson replies: "Absolutely no problem, specifically designed for underfloor heating." Almost every store selling flooring gives such a definitive answer, even providing user examples and some detection reports. Thus, consumers may feel that "all flooring is suitable for underfloor heating." However, they may not realize that flooring used for underfloor heating must meet four key criteria: thermal conductivity, dimensional stability, anti-pollution environmental protection, and durability. The flooring industry is currently formulating standards to quantify these four factors and establish quality inspection norms for installation and warranty periods, making technical indicators for laying and maintenance quantifiable.

2. "Is the name 'specialized underfloor heating floor' correct?"

In today’s flooring market, there are various types of flooring for underfloor heating systems, with even more confusing names: "heat-resistant flooring," "anti-underfloor heating flooring," "underfloor heat-resistant flooring," etc. The most commonly used names include "heating flooring," "underfloor heating flooring," "heat-resistant flooring," "underfloor heating flooring," "specifically designed underfloor heating flooring," etc. We believe some of these names are unscientific, while others seem inappropriate. Currently, especially during the early development stage of ground radiation heating systems, naming conventions must be standardized. It should be called "underfloor heating flooring," defined as wooden flooring suitable for installation in ground radiation heating systems, through which heat is transferred into the living space via the board surface.

3. "Is thicker flooring better for insulation?"

The thickness of wooden flooring determines the comfort of the foot sensation, so many consumers prefer flooring with a thickness of 15-18mm when choosing solid wood, multi-layered solid wood flooring, or bamboo flooring. Therefore, when selecting underfloor heating flooring, they still use this method to pick their choice. However, wood is a poor conductor, with a thermal conductivity coefficient between 0.17-0.34, compared to water at 0.5. If the flooring is too thick, it becomes even less effective for transferring heat from the floor to the surface, instead dissipating it during the process. Regarding insulation, the large temperature difference between the top and bottom of the floor can lead to significant deformation, causing issues like swelling, shrinking, twisting, bending, and cracking, thus failing to ensure dimensional stability.

4. "Is wider underfloor heating flooring better?"

Consumers purchasing solid wood flooring often seek comfort and status. Therefore, they tend to choose larger-sized flooring, especially those with widths reaching up to 180mm. Experts do not recommend selecting such wide flooring due to potential issues with moisture expansion and contraction, leading to gaps. This issue is exacerbated by heating, where faster moisture evaporation increases the likelihood of gaps forming. Hence, experts advise consumers to avoid overly large-sized flooring when selecting underfloor heating flooring.

5. "Is flooring with an aluminum membrane specifically for underfloor heating?"

This is one of the most typical misleads. Practice has proven that in ground radiation heating systems, heat transfer primarily occurs through radiation, followed by convection, and lastly conduction. If aluminum foil is placed over the heated floor, it forms a reflective barrier. In maintaining the same surface temperature of the underfloor heating system, foam pads with aluminum membranes have higher thermal resistance compared to ordinary foam pads, which have lower thermal resistance. Clearly, ordinary foam pads provide better heat conduction and facilitate heat transfer from the floor to the living space. Therefore, users should opt for ordinary foam pads or materials with low thermal resistance, good moisture resistance, and corrosion resistance when installing underfloor heating flooring.

6. "Can plywood or fine woodwork boards be added under the flooring?"

Firstly, laying plywood or fine woodwork boards under underfloor heating flooring hinders heat conduction, increasing heat loss during the transfer process and raising the heating cost of the underfloor heating system. Secondly, if the selected boards fail quality control, they can lead to excessive indoor free formaldehyde content or cause problems like warping and arching due to non-standard moisture content after being absorbed by the flooring. Given the disadvantages outweigh the benefits, adding plywood or fine woodwork boards underneath the flooring is counterproductive. Moreover, padding materials under the flooring are not recommended.

7. "Only floating installation allowed?"

Due to the special structure of underfloor heating systems, nails cannot be used on cement floors during floor installation. For a long time, underfloor heating floors were mostly installed using the floating method. However, with technological advancements, two other methods have emerged: adhesive bonding and joist installation. Recently, a quick-install joist has appeared on the market. This joist can be installed without nails, fixed directly onto the cement floor with glue, and then the floor is secured onto the joist using clips. This method enhances the foot sensation, allows air circulation beneath the floor for better heat distribution, ensures even heating, reduces deformation, and improves room heating efficiency. This is the currently recommended installation method.

8. No floor warming test after installation

After installing underfloor heating floors, apart from routine acceptance checks, it is crucial to conduct a temperature rise acceptance test. After the floor installation is completed or before winter heating begins, the first heating of the underfloor heating system should be done gradually. When the base floor temperature rises to 25°C, it should remain constant for 12-24 hours. Afterwards, slowly increase the temperature by 1°C per hour until the desired indoor temperature (14-22°C) is reached. The temperature should not be too high; the floor surface temperature should not exceed 28°C. The maximum temperature should not exceed 32°C within a short period. Higher temperatures increase heat loss, shorten the lifespan of the heating system, and risk hidden dangers, as well as causing floor deformation such as twisting, bending, and cracking.

9. No environmental acceptance after floor installation

Most flooring used in underfloor heating systems is composite flooring (solid wood composite, laminate, and bamboo flooring), which inherently has formaldehyde emission issues. Heating exacerbates formaldehyde release. Therefore, after installing underfloor heating floors, environmental acceptance must be conducted to ensure that the free formaldehyde release meets the standard (the national standard is 1.5mg/L). To ensure normal use of the floor, two points must be observed when purchasing: ① Conduct environmental testing before installing underfloor heating floors, generally free of charge for testing but charged for treatment. Re-test after installation for environmental acceptance. ② Ensure that the environmental protection indicators of the floor and auxiliary materials meet the standard. Do not prioritize price and buy environmentally unfriendly cheap products, as it could lead to endless future problems!

10. Placing legless modular furniture or carpets on the floor, disrupting the floor's heat dissipation surface

With the continuous improvement of modern people's living standards, people increasingly pursue personalized home decoration. Many personalized modular furniture options have emerged, allowing owners to assemble them according to their preferences. However, these pieces are often legless and directly contact the ground. If such furniture or large carpets cover underfloor heating floors, it prevents heat dissipation, causing excessive concentration of heat, leading to uneven heating of the floor, resulting in deformation, wasting heat energy, and lowering the indoor temperature.
