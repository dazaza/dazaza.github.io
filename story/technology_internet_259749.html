Must-read for women: 7 misconceptions about dental care for Chinese babies

Written by Xu Shuguang, Chairman of the Board of GreenTree Inn and www.519.com Wine Direct Sales Network

Xiaohong: Xiaoming, why didn't you come to class yesterday?

Xiaoming: I had a toothache and went to the hospital to have a tooth pulled.

Xiaohong: Does your tooth still hurt now?

Xiaoming: Who knows, that tooth is in the hands of the dentist.

In 2009, during a press conference held by the China Young Pioneers Committee, it was revealed that the incidence of cavities among Chinese children reached 90%; while in the United States, two nationwide oral health surveys conducted in 1980 and 1987 showed that the cavity-free rate among children and teenagers aged 5-17 had increased from 36.6% to 49.9%, with 95% of children aged 5-6 having no cavities.

Oral hygiene care in the United States began in the 1940s, and now there is a good habit of taking care of teeth from an early age across the country. American children get their teeth cleaned or wear braces at a very young age, and each child has a complete dental health record. In the process of caring for their children, Chinese parents often neglect this important aspect of dental care, and there are still many misconceptions in the care of baby teeth. In fact, if children start taking care of their teeth from an early age, they will have fewer opportunities to go to the doctor to have their teeth pulled.

One, No need to take care of baby teeth, as they will be replaced by new teeth anyway

Parents often think that a child's baby teeth will be replaced by new ones, so they don't need to pay too much attention to their early teeth. However, when a new tooth grows out prematurely and other teeth haven't fallen out yet, if not treated in time, the child's teeth will grow crooked.

Two, The mother's oral hygiene has nothing to do with the child's teeth

Research conducted by Professor Cofield from the Department of Dentistry at the University of Alabama on 46 children aged 19-31 months shows that children's cavities originate from the mother's mouth, and bacteria can be transmitted to the child through kissing, feeding, coughing, sneezing, playing, etc. Therefore, American experts suggest that mothers should not feed their children food that they have chewed themselves, brush their teeth with medicated toothpaste before getting close to their children, and try to avoid coughing and sneezing near their children.

Three, Sleeping with a bottle containing milk is likely to cause cavities

If a child's teeth are constantly exposed to sugary liquids day and night, such as milk, breast milk, powdered milk, juice, soda water, etc., the sugar will accumulate around the teeth and gums, mixing with bacteria in the mouth and causing cavities. Since saliva flow slows down when a child sleeps, sugary liquid accumulates around the teeth for a longer period of time, increasing the risk of cavities. Therefore, parents and nursery staff in kindergartens should pay special attention to not letting children fall asleep sucking sugary drinks or porridge, and not allowing children to carry bottles all day to drink sugary beverages. The more a child likes to suck juices and eat snacks, the greater the chance of developing cavities.

Four, Children without teeth don't need to brush

In fact, even when an infant hasn't sprouted a single tooth, parents should clean the child's gums with a soft cloth after each feeding because good "roots" are needed to grow healthy teeth; when baby teeth are about to emerge, chewing toys should be provided to help develop the child's gums. This way, there is no need to go to the hospital for surgery to cut the gums when the teeth don't come out. Such traumatic practices can create psychological shadows for children.

Five, Children don't need to spend long brushing their teeth

Maintaining oral hygiene requires brushing teeth for 10 minutes every day. It's important to teach children the correct method of brushing teeth, ensuring not only the duration but also the appropriate pressure.

Six, Dental problems don't require medical records

A dentist needs to work with your teeth for more than 5 years to judge whether your crown is healthy, so a comprehensive dental health record should be established from the age of 2, which is just as important as a physical health record.

Seven, Pulling a child's tooth is a trivial matter

In the United States, dentists focus on helping children overcome their fear of visiting the dentist, so it's important to make children feel that taking care of their teeth and maintaining dental health is a happy thing, and not something to fear or reject. Take your child to a professional pediatric dentist clinic where the doctors will make them realize that seeing the dentist can also be fun.
