"The Inevitable 'Death of Yougou': Belle's E-commerce Has Many Fatal Flaws"

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 17:07:13

Recently, Yougou.com, which has been suffering from a severe loss of executives, was hit again. On July 17th, it was reported that according to an insider from Yougou.com, the COO Zhang Xiaojun will be leaving and is currently processing his resignation. As one of the founding members of Yougou.com, Zhang Xiaojun's departure holds significant implications, indicating that Yougou.com is indeed facing serious crises.

Let’s first review the e-commerce journey of Belle International, the parent company of Yougou.com:

Belle started experimenting with e-commerce several years ago in a low-key manner, developing steadily through three channels: Taobao distribution, Tmall, and B2C distribution (B2C distribution was completed through Taoxiu.com). Led by veteran group member Xie Yunli, a not-so-luxurious e-commerce team initially sold products under Belle's own brands, subsequently launching exclusive online versions and eventually new network brands. This development path became a successful case study before 2011, regarded as more solid than Li-Ning's foray into e-commerce.

With the rapid rise of Letao.com and Haolemai after securing over $100 million in financing in 2010, Belle Group felt pressured. On one hand, they were worried about being constrained by distribution channels; on the other hand, they realized their significant potential for growth in the e-commerce sector, including:

One, a wide range of brands: Besides its own major women's shoe brands, Belle also saw astonishing growth in the online sales of internationally renowned sports brands like Adidas and Nike;

Two, easier category expansion: For Belle Group, known for its women's shoes brands and agency of well-known sports brands, how to gain absolute market advantage and status through multiple brands and categories has been a key consideration. Expanding offline stores proved too slow, while existing e-commerce operations could easily expand brands but not quickly enough in terms of categories;

Three, financial advantage: In cooperation with e-commerce enterprises, Belle discovered that e-commerce is capital-intensive. With financial strength, Belle did not need to rely on external capital to develop, thereby defeating more vertical e-commerce platforms dependent on capital, becoming the "last man standing";

Four, talent advantage: Through years of e-commerce practice, Belle had built a highly professional and efficient e-commerce team capable of handling business scale or even higher;

Five, supply chain advantage: This was particularly exciting for Belle, attracting top executives from brand e-commerce or platform e-commerce companies. After all, for e-commerce, supply chain management is always fascinating, as it seems to be the decisive factor apart from capital. The supply chain includes product development, production, warehouse management, delivery, after-sales service, and a series of “business” content. In traditional enterprises focusing heavily on “business” rather than “e-commerce,” this was considered the most competitive advantage over e-commerce companies.

With these advantages, Belle's e-commerce decided to make a big push. They first acquired the double-character domain name yougou.com,

abandoned the slightly copycat “topshoes.cn” Taoxiu website and redirected traffic, stopped supplying all distribution websites, reduced focus on third-party direct-operated online shops like Taobao and Tmall, hired JD VP and Vancl VP at high salaries to serve as CMO and COO respectively, and dispatched senior group members to act as CEO, forming a golden triangle. They aimed to build a full-category vertical B2C mall, implementing multi-brand strategies on one hand, expanding into clothing and other categories on the other, realizing the dream of a department store empire impossible to achieve offline!

Everything sounded perfect, but every dream needs to confront harsh realities. The situation was as follows:

One, higher technical requirements for independent B2C: Located in Shenzhen headquarters, Belle couldn’t recruit more excellent technical talents, and outsourcing technical services in Shenzhen were scarce. However, technology determines user experience, backend management efficiency, and reliable data. Independent B2C belongs to dynamic technology, different from relatively static technologies like ERP, continuous changes remain a hurdle for many traditional enterprises;

Two, increased management difficulty due to category expansion: Someone once said that excelling in women's shoes doesn't necessarily translate to men's shoes, which Belle must have deeply understood when expanding into men's shoes. Expanding into women's clothing and accessories within a short year would undoubtedly pose challenges on the backend. For traditional brand enterprises accustomed to meticulous planning, such rapid strategies worked for Vancl during special periods, understandable within Vancl and JD systems. Although Belle possessed Vancl’s COO and JD’s CMO, whether they could be understood and executed at an "exaggerated cost" remains questionable!

Three, organizational structure: Many people without experience crossing between e-commerce and traditional enterprises struggle to understand this. Simply put, department settings (organizational structure), approvals, finance and budget, reporting methods, corporate culture, communication styles, execution methods, risk control, processes, and systems form what is called a company’s "mechanism." The conflict results in e-commerce attempting to convince traditional teams to accept e-commerce's "structure," while traditional teams try but fail to fully understand e-commerce's "structure," leading to inefficiencies and instability in the execution team. Eventually, the chairman or board might say, "E-commerce and traditional enterprises each have their strengths and weaknesses, the future is integration, so you must integrate in these aspects..." If this kind of "fusion without harmony" appears, it leads to so-called management innovation, thus the "CEO rotation system" emerged even in a company like Belle!

It was originally thought that Yougou.com, part of Belle's e-commerce division, was far ahead, and the senior executives were waiting for spin-off listings to enjoy success, fame, and fortune, setting countless examples of traditional enterprises succeeding in the e-commerce era. Instead, they are now entangled in these critical issues.

After all, whether it's the veterans who grew with Belle or the poached e-commerce VPs, it can be seen that they aren’t leaving because of lack of ability. When the VPs from JD return to JD, they continue to perform excellently; when the VPs from Vancl have the courage to start businesses. Are these three points the hard injuries? Or are these not only Belle's hard injuries but also those of many traditional enterprises trying to enter the e-commerce field?
