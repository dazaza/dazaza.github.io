Digg Website Faces Make-Or-Break Test

by service on 2007-05-02 22:12:46

Digg has just shut down its site to respond on its official blog: "You would rather see Digg fight on instead of succumbing to bigger companies. We hear your voices, and no matter what the consequences, we will not delete the articles and comments that contain the code."

What exactly happened? Is it the anarchy of netizens? An electronic rebellion? Or just a mob trial? No matter how you look at this social news site, Digg is facing the most critical moment of its young life.

Some people recently posted links to articles about the HD-DVD crack and included the crucial processing key for cracking HD-DVDs in the titles and article descriptions. The Digg staff removed those articles because they were concerned that they would be sued by the MPAA.

It was the action of the staff that caused this turmoil; now, most of the articles on the Digg homepage are either related to the key articles or variations of the originally deleted articles.

"Can a user base so loyal that it openly rebels and even vandalizes really be loyal to Digg?" A famous blogger wrote.

Now, the situation is more serious as internet users are very dissatisfied with Digg's previous actions.

Seeing the drastic change in the situation, the Digg authorities had no choice but to yield to public opinion and stopped deleting articles and comments. The founder of Digg said, "If we fail, then it's fate, at least we have fought hard."
