Android's openness is criticized: only benefiting the operators

by geekzhang on 2013-05-06 15:08:46

Introduction: Recently, the US tech blog TheVerge wrote an article pointing out that the openness boasted by Android phone manufacturers like HTC and Samsung is still a blank check. Their so-called openness is only aimed at carriers, not ordinary consumers. In 2013, the BootLoader of flagship Android models is still in a locked state, and the keys are held by major carriers.The following is the full text of the article:A Blank CheckAbout two years ago, HTC's CEO publicly made such a commitment in response to external pressure: we will no longer lock the BootLoader on our own devices. But today, HTC has only partially fulfilled this promise, and other manufacturers' performances in maintaining the openness of the Android ecosystem are even less commendable. The BootLoader is still locked, and the key is in the hands of the carriers.The so-called BootLoader refers to a small program that runs before the operating system kernel starts. Through this small program, hardware devices can be initialized, memory space mapping diagrams can be established, thereby bringing the system's software and hardware environment to a suitable state, in order to prepare the correct environment for ultimately calling the operating system kernel.If users want complete control over the hardware and software functions of their phones, then the BootLoader will play a crucial role. If the BootLoader is locked, users will not be able to run their own operating systems on their phones; if the BootLoader is unlocked, users will not be constrained by the carrier and can customize the ROM at will.In view of the fact that the hottest Android flagship phones of 2013 have all been launched, we will now examine the status of their BootLoaders. As you might guess, the results are not as ideal as we imagined. As far as we know, major US carriers are still forcing Android manufacturers to lock the BootLoader on their phones.Developer EditionsHowever, the actual situation seems not as terrible as it sounds. Occasionally, Samsung will release developer versions of its flagship models, with the BootLoader not locked, although these versions are released later. HTC and Sony also follow this practice. Although developer editions are not an ideal solution—they are all full-price, unsubsidized models, and there’s always no stock at carriers—they at least offer users a choice.Moreover, despite the barriers set up by carriers, hackers can still find ways to unlock the BootLoader. Even Motorola's well-known locked edition phones have recently been cracked, and there are signs that the BootLoader of the Samsung Galaxy S4 has also been hacked.HTC has also created a system where users can register their unique device identification codes themselves and then receive unlocking keys. This system has been applied to the HTC One, and most users can visit the HTC website to request relevant codes to unlock the BootLoader.When we asked Samsung which of their models were locked, the company was unwilling to give a direct answer, instead suggesting we ask each carrier individually. HTC told us in an interview that among the 185 operators selling the HTC One, only two prevent users from unlocking the BootLoader. However, HTC did not disclose who these two operators are.Coming to LightAfter some investigation, we found that these two operators are very likely AT&T and Verizon. The BootLoader of the Samsung Galaxy S4 and HTC One they operate are both locked. Verizon has not yet officially announced that it will sell the HTC One, but various signs indicate that it will certainly do so. We asked Verizon why they continue to lock the BootLoader of phones, and received the same answer as last November: to ensure an excellent user experience.Verizon stated in a statement that our position on BootLoader openness has not changed due to the HTC One. Consumers expect a certain level of service from us. Once a device changes due to an open BootLoader, we cannot guarantee the performance, security, and excellent user experience that consumers have always expected. Moreover, Verizon values the security of devices highly, and opening the BootLoader would jeopardize the security of the device. A secure BootLoader does not limit or bind the consumer's ability to access or download relevant applications for the device.As usual, among the four major carriers in the US, the slightly weaker carriers are more willing to tout openness. Both Sprint and T-Mobile have a more positive attitude towards HTC's BootLoader unlocking project. Sprint said that HTC One users can unlock the BootLoader, and the Galaxy S4 will not be locked when it is launched. T-Mobile claimed that both the HTC One and Galaxy S4 are locked, but the company will not prevent users from unlocking them.Complicated SituationT-Mobile also issued a statement saying that T-Mobile will not prevent users from unlocking the BootLoader, but we are committed to providing users with the best experience, and installing unauthorized firmware will have adverse effects on this experience. In addition, this helps avoid fraud caused by lost devices, as well as prevents security threats from third-party firmware not tested by T-Mobile and its OEM partners.Therefore, the current situation of openness on the Android platform is very complex, at least for well-known Android manufacturers like Samsung and HTC. The largest carriers in the US still try to keep Android devices locked as much as possible, while smaller carriers are slightly more willing to leave room for users to unlock the BootLoader.What is most surprising is that neither HTC nor Samsung is willing to publicly disclose the specific methods they use to control the software on their own devices. It is often complained by outsiders that the so-called openness of Android is only aimed at carriers, not consumers. Finally, we came to such a conclusion: the BootLoader of flagship Android models in 2013 is still in a locked state, and the keys are in the hands of flagship carriers.
