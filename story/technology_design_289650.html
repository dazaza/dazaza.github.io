China News Service, December 2 -- According to Hong Kong's Wen Hui Bao, the annual report for 2008 submitted by Hor Kwok-hung, the Commissioner for Interception of Communications and Surveillance in Hong Kong, to the Chief Executive, revealed 11 cases of law enforcement officers conducting unauthorized wiretaps. Two of these cases originated from the Independent Commission Against Corruption (ICAC), while the remaining nine involved other law enforcement agencies. Seven of the cases involved officers continuing surveillance even after the authorization period had expired, with instances of excessive interception ranging from three minutes to one hour. In a more outrageous case, an officer from the ICAC knowingly copied down the wrong telephone number but failed to correct it, leading to the illegal interception of an innocent citizen's phone calls for four days. Hor Kwok-hung criticized some law enforcement officers as arrogant and nearly insubordinate. Tang Hing-ming, the Commissioner of the ICAC, stated that they valued the content of the report and would learn from the experience to improve.

Hor Kwok-hung will submit the report to the Legislative Council on December 2. According to reports, the report involves 11 cases of unauthorized wiretapping. One case involved a senior investigation officer at the ICAC copying down the wrong telephone number. Another senior officer discovered the mistake and reported it to their direct superior, the chief investigator, but this chief investigator did not clarify the issue and proceeded to apply to the court for authorization to intercept communications, resulting in the ICAC illegally intercepting the communications of an innocent citizen. Later, when the investigation target was found to be female but the intercepted party was male, the ICAC re-examined the case and discovered the error in the telephone number, thus stopping the operation.

After the incident, the ICAC gave warnings to both the senior investigation officer who initially discovered the number error and reported it to their superior, as well as the chief investigator who knowingly made the mistake without clarification. Hor Kwok-hung believed that the punishment given by the ICAC was unfair, as the chief investigator's offense was the most severe. They failed to verify the telephone number at multiple stages, causing the safeguard procedures to fail, which was considered a serious mistake. Hor Kwok-hung also explicitly warned the ICAC not to use this chief investigator for monitoring functions again, but the ICAC responded in the report stating they were satisfied with the past performance of this chief investigator.

Hor Kwok-hung disclosed that during the investigation of a case where the ICAC mistakenly intercepted communications involving legal confidentiality rights, Tang Hing-ming sent him two letters indicating that the remaining interception time length records would be properly preserved. However, ten months later, Tang Hing-ming sent another letter to Hor stating that the previous letters and investigation materials were not entirely accurate. Hor Kwok-hung felt extremely frustrated that ICAC personnel did not respect their superior’s instructions and failed to retain relevant materials, which significantly wasted his time in fulfilling his review responsibilities.

Additionally, the report mentioned a case where system failure prevented the termination of interception. In this incident, the law enforcement agency continued interception for 6 to 18 minutes even after the authorized interception period had expired. Hor Kwok-hung requested data from a director of a law enforcement agency, but the director claimed that the data Hor sought was irrelevant to the investigation and therefore did not retain it. Although the director apologized for the event, Hor Kwok-hung believed that the attitude of the law enforcement officers was disappointing, showing arrogance and almost insubordination towards his role as a commissioner responsible for supervision and review functions.

A spokesperson for the Security Bureau responded by saying that although there were two relatively serious violation cases last year, no malicious or deliberate non-compliance by law enforcement agencies was found. Tang Hing-ming did not respond to the criticism in the report on December 1 but emphasized the importance of the report's contents, stating that they would learn from the experience to improve.

Lau Wai-hwa, Chairman of the Legislative Council's Security Affairs Committee, believed that the incident reflected that ICAC personnel had not learned their lessons, failing to protect citizens' rights. A special meeting would be held later to invite Secretary for Security Ambrose Lee, Police Commissioner Raymond Tong, and Tang Hing-ming to attend and explain the report.
