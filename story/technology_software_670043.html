MFA's "Taobao-style" recruitment Weibo does not cover shipping

by hdghu19056 on 2012-02-20 14:47:43

"Dear, have you graduated from an undergraduate program? Are you proficient in using office software? Can you communicate fluently in English? Do you have a driver's license? Come quickly, the Secretariat of the Trilateral Cooperation among China, Japan and Korea is recruiting! This is an international organization that will soon be established in South Korea, the hometown of Bae Yong-joon, Lee Young-ae, Song Hye-kyo, Joo Jin-mo, Kim Hyun-jung, and Rain! Six positions are available for research and planning, as well as public relations and external publicity. For more information, please contact xx. Shipping not included."

On the morning of the 1st day, a Weibo post by the Ministry of Foreign Affairs about recruitment became popular online, retweeted over 4800 times within three hours, sparking heated discussions among netizens. The recruitment Weibo was posted by the official Weibo platform "Diplomatic Little Messenger" of the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, adopting the currently popular "Taobao style."

Following the "Taobao style" arrest warrant and "Taobao style" admission notices, this is another "Taobao style" voice issued officially.

Recently, various "Taobao style" posts appearing on the Internet have caught the attention of netizens. "The Ministry of Foreign Affairs is so cute", "The police uncles are so adorable!", "University admissions offices are so trendy, dear!" The forwards and comments on Weibo were lively, and for a time, whether it was the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, Anhui urban management, local police Weibos, or some university official Weibos, they were all labeled with the "adorable" tag.

"Dear, say goodbye to the pain of daily escapes, constant panic, and second-by-second surprises. Hurry up and place your order!" "Love roaring, love being cute, love justice even more. I'm not Ultraman, I'm the Wuhou District Public Security Bureau of Chengdu City." "Dear, congratulations! You've been admitted to Nanjing University of Science and Technology! It's great! A 211 institution! ... Scenic beauty, a holy land for reading!" ...

Network text refers to new styles of writing that originate or become popular on the Internet. They are usually caused by a sudden ingenious post, a collective parody, or a hot event. They are free in form and distinct in characteristics, attracting significant attention during a certain period. Before this, common network texts included "Zhizhi style", "Xiaonei style", "Approaching Science style", "Qionghai style", "Dream of Red Mansions style", etc.

Nowadays, network texts are not only popular on the Internet but also active in real life. They are used not only on the Weibo platforms of the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, the police, and university admissions offices but also cited in graduation ceremonies at various universities.

"At any moment, you should maintain sufficient tolerance and rationality. Only then will you not feel empty because 'everything is fleeting' and not become obsessed because 'dreams really hurt too much.'" The speech by the teacher representative at the 2011 graduation ceremony of the China Youth University of Political Studies was very popular among students. The video of the speech has been viewed more than 350,000 times online. Additionally, Peking University president's "roar style" and Tsinghua University president's "Fanke style" have also sparked widespread interest among young people, making the graduation ceremonies of various universities full of joy.

Not only "Taobao style," but currently popular are also "roar style" and "poetic style." They are applied to the promotion of laws and regulations, recruitment notices, and even arrest warrants in serious "occasions." "Using light-hearted and humorous network texts to express originally serious topics with a 'serious face,' this emerging network text reflects the awakening of people-oriented consciousness from the side," said Xia Xuelian, professor of sociology at Peking University.

"Very approachable, very touching," said Zhang Gesource, a 2011 graduate of the China Youth University of Political Studies. Interweaving the use of familiar network texts in graduation speeches expresses the earnest expectations of teachers and elders in a way that resonates with young people, not just heard but felt deeply.

"Differences should be respected, and diversity should be embraced," pointed out Zhou Xiaozheng, professor of sociology at Renmin University of China. Emerging network texts should be treated with tolerance as long as they are within the legal boundaries. Citizens should have the freedom to adopt any network text.

"Language is alive, a product of the development of the times. The rise of network language not only presents the development of the Internet but also reflects the multi-dimensional integration of social culture," said Xia Xuelian.

"The use of network texts should still pay attention to the occasion of use," Xia Xuelian believes. Law enforcement agencies and educational institutions should pay attention to the degree and the standardization and dissemination effect of language, avoiding vulgarity.
