A hero appreciates another hero: Gates should strongly recommend Balmer to take on the重任 of Microsoft CEO again.

by anonymous on 2013-11-21 14:21:48

The much-anticipated Microsoft shareholders' meeting was recently held, but Microsoft did not announce Ballmer's successor as CEO during this meeting as the industry had predicted. Not even a list of candidates was revealed. However, Microsoft founder and chairman Bill Gates became choked up when talking about his feelings towards Microsoft and thanking Ballmer for his long-term contributions to the company, which left a deep impression on us.

Recalling Ballmer's passionate and tearful farewell speech at his last employee conference at Microsoft, both Gates and Ballmer are emotional individuals (with a love and pride for Microsoft). Furthermore, Gates' remarks at the shareholders' meeting that "the next CEO will be a very complex position" and his consensus with Ballmer on finding the right CEO candidate indicate their comprehensive and objective understanding of Microsoft's current and future state. Given this, it would seem logical for Gates, who has significant influence as Microsoft's chairman, to recommend that shareholders allow Ballmer to continue serving as Microsoft's CEO. Why?

Previously, we briefly analyzed why Microsoft's board of directors should keep Ballmer as CEO. With the convening of this shareholders' meeting, especially Ballmer's final attendance to answer shareholder-related questions, we believe Microsoft needs to reassess the issue of its future new CEO.

Coincidentally, just as Microsoft held its shareholders' meeting, Nokia also convened one and approved the sale of its mobile phone business to Microsoft with 99.7% approval. Microsoft shareholders at their own meeting asked about Ballmer's acquisition of Nokia (whether it was worthwhile). Shareholders hope the company will exercise caution when deploying tens of billions of dollars in large-scale acquisitions. Ballmer acknowledged this advice, but stated that bold actions are sometimes necessary. We cannot deny that some of Ballmer's acquisitions during his tenure have not had the expected substantial impact on Microsoft's overall strategy. However, in the heavily criticized mobile internet sector, Ballmer's acquisition strategy is commendable, most notably the acquisition of Nokia.

The industry knows that when Nokia first faced the arrival of touch-screen smartphones represented by the iPhone and began its decline, Intel, a major chip player, once cooperated with Nokia to develop the MeeGo operating system. Later, Nokia suddenly announced its exit from MeeGo development. Although officially claiming no future prospects for MeeGo, it appears that Microsoft's lobbying was the real reason behind Nokia's decision to abandon its collaboration with Intel on MeeGo. Of course, Microsoft's move was not aimed at Intel, but rather under the circumstances at the time, Nokia was Microsoft's only chance to remain in the smartphone industry and await future opportunities.

If convincing Nokia to abandon the development of its own MeeGo system reduced a potential future competitor in the smartphone market, then acquiring Nokia can be said to have saved Microsoft at a critical moment. According to subsequent revelations, due to dissatisfaction with progress in the smartphone market through cooperation with Microsoft, Nokia internally had already begun developing smartphones based on Google's Android system. This is frightening to think about. Everyone knows that Microsoft currently holds the third-largest ecosystem in smartphones, with over 90% of Windows Phone shipments relying on Nokia. If Nokia defected to Google's Android, Microsoft would instantly be squeezed out of the smartphone industry, and Nokia's remaining vast functional phone user market would become Android's domain. Now, with Microsoft's acquisition of Nokia, all these threats have been minimized. Microsoft remains in the mobile ecosystem and gains millions of potential future Windows Phone ecosystem users each quarter from Nokia's functional phone users.

If these facts cannot convince Microsoft shareholders of the strategic value and foresight of Ballmer's $7.2 billion acquisition of Nokia, then compare this acquisition with Google's $12.5 billion acquisition of Motorola Mobility two years ago. Despite paying twice the price, Google's Android did not rapidly develop to today's level due to the acquisition of Motorola Mobility (Samsung is Android's mainstay), nor did it achieve the goal of protecting Android from patent litigation through Motorola Mobility's patents (Microsoft currently earns nearly $2 billion annually from Android's licensing fees). In contrast, Microsoft's acquisition of Nokia strategically secured its place in the smartphone industry, representing the future trend of mobile devices, and surpassed Motorola to enter the top four in the fiercely competitive U.S. smartphone market last quarter.

In addition to acquisitions, Ballmer did not directly respond to calls for the spin-off of Xbox and Bing businesses at the shareholders' meeting, but indicated that these two businesses are important components of Microsoft's overall vision. For example, when discussing the Xbox One, Ballmer mentioned integration with Bing and SkyDrive, believing it demonstrates Microsoft's unified strategy of integrating its devices and services. To this end, Microsoft recently released the latest details of Bing and Xbox integration, facilitating natural language searches using the new gaming console. Bing is also key to improving and testing Windows Azure cloud computing services. This seems to be a response to Stephen Elop's campaign declaration, where he suggested selling Microsoft's unprofitable search business Bing and Xbox. Elop, Nokia's former CEO and a popular candidate for Microsoft's future CEO.

As Ballmer delivered a speech at the shareholders' meeting, soon-to-depart Nokia CEO Stephen Elop became the target of criticism. Several Nokia shareholders severely criticized Elop, even calling his leadership of Nokia a "3A failure." They attributed the decline of Nokia and its mobile phone brand to Elop. The criticisms against Elop are not without merit, as he failed to balance Windows Phone, Symbian, and feature phone businesses across Nokia's entire operations. The "Elop Effect," focusing on one area at the expense of others, accelerated Nokia's decline. This reflects Elop's lack of ability to control the overall situation and strategic vision in enterprise management. The fact that such a person could enter the pool of candidates for Microsoft's future CEO highlights the difficulty Microsoft faces in finding a suitable CEO and the caliber of candidates, further proving Ballmer's irreplaceability as Microsoft's CEO.

Regarding Microsoft's stock price, which is of utmost concern to shareholders, why does Microsoft's stock not rise? Ballmer believes it is due to the disconnect between profits and stock prices. He noted that Microsoft's current stock price is 60% of what it was when he first became CEO, yet Microsoft's profits are three times higher (in reality, during Ballmer's thirteen-year tenure as CEO, Microsoft's employee count tripled, annual revenue grew fourfold, annual profit increased tenfold, but Microsoft's market capitalization shrank by half). Regarding Ballmer's explanation, opinions may vary, and we will not evaluate it here. However, it is common for stock prices to poorly reflect a company's actual performance in the market, and this can change rapidly. Apple, Google, Amazon, Facebook, and Yahoo have all experienced significant discrepancies between their stock performances and actual results. Therefore, Microsoft's shareholders should rationally assess Ballmer's merits and abilities based on Microsoft's actual performance, truly caring about Microsoft's future and their own interests.

Undoubtedly, whether in the past or present, Ballmer has made great contributions to Microsoft's status as a globally significant technology company, a fact well known to Gates as Microsoft's board chairman. Regarding Microsoft's future, from the strategies Ballmer has formulated and executed, whoever takes over as Microsoft's CEO will likely not surpass him. The only remaining criterion—and one that must not be overlooked—is passion and dedication to Microsoft itself. In this regard, only Ballmer can match Gates.

Speaking of Ballmer's passion and dedication to Microsoft, Flipboard CEO Mike McCue recently told the media that Ballmer personally led a Microsoft team to Flipboard's office to promote the creation of an application for Windows 8. Ballmer's passion and inspiring product promotion ultimately convinced Flipboard to develop an app for Microsoft. In fact, Ballmer also visited many other companies in Silicon Valley and strongly promoted the Windows 8 system.

Over the years, Ballmer has received much attention and criticism from the tech industry, some fair, some unfair. Regardless, one undeniable fact about Ballmer is his deep love and unwavering belief in Microsoft.

Perhaps the industry feels that the era of Ballmer (and Gates) has passed, that they are too old. This reminds us of a passage from Tennyson's "Ulysses" recited by M in the movie "Skyfall": "Now we are old, no longer possessing the vigor of our youth, memories fading like smoke, time passing like frost, fate often cruel, making heroes feel disheartened. But my enthusiasm remains undiminished, I shall strive tirelessly, seek endlessly, fight forever." Such is Microsoft, Gates, and Ballmer.
