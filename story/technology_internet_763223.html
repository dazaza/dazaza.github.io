Become a more satisfied programmer today.

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 17:14:54

Become a More Satisfied Programmer. Today.

In the first article of this series, I have already discussed the indicators of job satisfaction and how to score your job based on each one. In this article, I will outline some simple steps to help you increase your satisfaction in the following areas: project quality, work/life balance, bureaucracy & office politics, recognition & respect, compensation, and teamwork.

Before we begin, it is worth noting that the most direct way to increase job satisfaction is to change jobs. When you are unhappy with your current job, switching jobs is something you will likely consider. However, according to Justin James' article "10 Signs You're Not Cut Out to Be a Developer," if you hope to get a raise by frequently changing jobs, it may indicate that you are not naturally suited to be a programmer. Of course, job-hopping is part of the culture and in many cases, it is the only choice for career development as a programmer. Changing jobs might be the best option, but it usually takes a lot of time.

Regardless of whether you are looking for a new job, increasing your satisfaction with your current job is a natural thing to do. Right now. Why? Work takes up a large part of life. You work every day, and you want to do anything to enjoy your work. If you are unhappy, you will bring those emotions into your work. This can lead to lack of motivation, poor performance, and leave a bad record for the future.

Project Quality

● During your free time, participate in projects you like, learn new languages, contribute to open-source projects, read a book, attend a conference, or write a blog. These activities will keep your skills up-to-date and make you more competitive.

● Start fun side projects. Write tool code to automate development. Improve version control systems, add email notifications for check-ins.

● Request a transfer to another project. A rational management team would rather reassign a star programmer to a different project than lose them.

Work/Life Balance

● Many programmers enjoy long hours of work, relishing the focus on a single task. If this is your style, short breaks after long hours of work are also important for you. Take a day off, don’t save up your vacation days.

● Relax and simplify your life.

● Plan to set aside time for your family, friends, personal projects, and others. Stick to this plan.

● Reduce your working hours. Don't be afraid to let management know your limits. They will respect you more. Really.

Bureaucracy & Office Politics

● Accept the possibility that things could get worse.

● Avoid rumors. Focus on your work and stay dedicated.

● Stay as far away from additional bureaucracy and office politics as possible.

● If your manager can protect you from these issues, thank him sincerely. Let him know how much you appreciate what he does for you.

Recognition & Respect

● Become a mentor. You can open your door to newcomers, offering them more basic information. Or more formally, join a mentoring program.

● Become a project leader. Don’t wait for the company to promote you to a project leader. You can start an open-source project or participate in existing ones.

● Choose a small circle and become an authority within it. Speak up in meetings.

Compensation

● Request a raise. A feasible method is to ask for a performance review. Be prepared to highlight your achievements and contributions to the company's success. Research the current salaries in your field and inflation rates, and be ready to cite these data.

● Ask for better health insurance, tools that improve productivity, longer vacations, more remote work opportunities, free M&Ms (pick the green ones), or anything else you care about.

● Obtain certifications, return to school, or take online courses. As education levels increase, employers tend to offer higher compensation.

Teamwork

● Participate in the interview process so you can personally build a strong team. Hire five star players and keep them happy.

● Enjoy not having to work with programmers like Paula.

● Expand your network beyond your current job. Join LUGs (Linux User Groups), programming associations, alumni networks. Use LinkedIn.

Everyone goes through periods when they feel dissatisfied with their job. It’s normal. I’m not an expert in boosting employee motivation, but the measures listed above have worked well for me. Hopefully, some of them will work for you too.
