To offset the erosion of Android, it is an important reason for Apple to launch a low-cost iPhone.

by anonymous on 2013-08-19 15:18:33

English Title: Defending iOS with cheap iPhones

iOS stands out among mobile operating systems due to the quality of its applications. Benedict Evans analyzes this condition in an unmissable blog post:

If Android's engagement surpasses that of iOS, then the discussion about whether iOS can maintain its leading position will become irrelevant. iOS will eventually find its place as either first or second in line. Based on current sales trends, this scenario could begin in 2014. When you (Android) have 5 to 6 times more users but only one-fourth of the engagement, you still hold enough appeal for the market.

This poses a significant strategic threat to Apple. The best apps are often exclusive to or appear first on the iPhone, which is a key selling point (though not the only one). A change in this dynamic could directly disrupt the virtuous cycle of "quality apps — quality users — quality apps." Consequently, Android phones, with their advantage in product lines and pricing, would start eroding the customer base of the iPhone. Quality apps are the primary reason users choose a $600 iPhone over a $300 Android phone—this is a point that cannot be compromised.

Evans carefully notes that apps are not the only selling point of the iPhone. He clearly understands that the more elegant design and refined details of iOS attract a specific consumer group, even when there are some standout apps on the Android platform.

However, this type of user represents only a small fraction of the iPhone-buying audience. In response to Evans, Rene Ritchie offers an insightful comment on the differences between iOS and Android developers:

The Mac has always maintained a relatively low market share, especially compared to PCs, which hold around 90% of the market. However, the Mac has consistently attracted a group of extraordinarily talented and loyal developers who highly value design and user experience. OS X enjoys support from both the traditional Mac OS and NeXT communities. The ratio of talent to market share has never been proportional. These developers, along with those they influence, not only buy iPhones and iPads but also design apps for them…

Buyers, especially developers, aren't defined by mere numbers. They have their own tastes and preferences. Otherwise, five years ago, the most popular iPhone apps would have already appeared on Symbian, Palm OS, and J2ME, and Windows would have had Mac-level software for over a decade.

If all three conditions are met, we'll gladly beg to develop products for you.

Now is the time to introduce the interesting topic of app categories. I believe all apps fall into one of the following three major categories:

Consumer apps, including entertainment and social apps. These are the front-ends of web services. Since they can absorb large amounts of funding, many companies choose to launch apps on as many platforms as possible to increase their user base.

Games. Games emphasize immersive experiences, making them well-suited for mobile devices in terms of user experience. However, the cost of cross-platform support for games is relatively high.

Productivity apps. As I've said before, "If games focus on maximizing user output while minimizing application output, and consumer apps prioritize application output while reducing user input, then productivity apps aim to balance both. Users input information, and the app provides better feedback."

Productivity apps are closely tied to the underlying platform, especially in terms of UI, resulting in the highest incremental costs for cross-platform support.

I placed these three elements on one chart because they are highly interrelated.

Consumers who value user experience are likely to choose a Mac. These individuals are also more inclined to use or develop productivity apps and are willing to pay for their favorite apps.

On the other hand, people who buy iPhones simply because they're cool or trendy rarely view their phones as productivity tools. They seldom download apps beyond basic entertainment and social ones.

However, for the market, the two tails of the bell curve play a much smaller role than the prominent central hump. While I share similar views with Ritchie regarding how quality and detail impact iOS developers, the majority of people won't choose iOS because of apps like OmniFocus.

More often, it's popular games like Clash of Clans, the hottest apps in the App Store, or apps like Instagram (before it launched on Android) that distinguish the iPhone from other smartphones. All these apps fall into the slightly right-leaning region of the bell curve: their developers are primarily motivated by earning money or recovering their investments. They are fully aware of this fact:

The average value of Android users is far lower than that of iPhone users, but Android has a vastly superior number of users.

Clearly, financial returns are an important criterion for most developers when choosing a platform; and users frequently select based on the availability of apps (especially games), sometimes even prioritizing this over user experience.

Evans is right: Apple should introduce affordable iPhones as soon as possible.
