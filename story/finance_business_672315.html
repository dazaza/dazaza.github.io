Breast Reduction Surgery and Macromastia Reduction

I. Macromastia · Gigantomastia: Excessive development of the breasts leads to an over-enlarged breast volume, forming macromastia syndrome, commonly known as gigantomastia. Macromastia brings both mental and physical pain to women. To varying degrees, it is accompanied by breast ptosis (sagging). Severe cases of macromastia and breast ptosis can cause the lower edge of the breast to extend below the navel or even reach the pubic area, leading to a bulky figure, inconvenience in movement, shoulder and back pain, discomfort in the chest when lying down. In hot weather, the area between the two breasts and under the breast folds often remains moist, easily leading to skin diseases such as heat rashes and eczema. Enormous or severely sagging breasts cause women to lose the balanced and slim curves that define beauty, resulting in a stocky figure. This makes patients feel embarrassed, suffering from unspoken physical and psychological pressures, losing confidence and the courage to participate in social life.

Breast reduction plastic surgery involves the removal of part of the breast skin and mammary tissue to reduce the size and improve the position of the breast, along with reshaping the nipple and areola. Breast reduction surgery is used for overly developed and sagging breasts.

II. Types of Macromastia:

Macromastia can be divided into three categories: excessive hyperplasia of the mammary gland, obese-type macromastia, and adolescent-type macromastia. Due to differences in pathology and clinical symptoms, treatment methods also vary slightly.

1. Excessive Hyperplasia of the Mammary Gland:

Characterized by excessive hyperplasia of mammary tissue, firm enlarged breasts, obvious lobular hyperplasia, often with tenderness. During the menstrual cycle, there is frequently spontaneous pain, accompanied by breast ptosis, more common in married women who have given birth.

2. Obese-Type Macromastia:

Characterized by uniformly enlarged breasts. Structurally, it is mainly due to uniform hyperplasia and enlargement of fat cells in the breast. During surgery, fat hyperplasia can be found under the breast skin and between mammary tissues. Patients with this type of macromastia often have systemic obesity, although the breast ptosis may be less severe than in cases of excessive hyperplasia of the mammary gland.

3. Adolescent-Type Macromastia:

This type involves progressive breast enlargement during puberty, characterized by excessive development and hyperplasia of mammary tissue. The breasts show uniform enlargement with no significant breast ptosis. These patients sometimes have a family history.

III. Gigantomastia Reduction Surgery:

Suitable candidates can be categorized into eight points:

1. Breasts are too large, disproportionate to body shape.

2. Breasts are excessively heavy and droop, with nipples pointing downward.

3. One breast is significantly larger than the other.

4. Back, neck, and shoulder pain caused by the weight of the breasts.

5. Skin inflammation under the breasts due to irritation.

6. Bra straps cutting into shoulders in a saw-tooth pattern.

7. Daily activities restricted by the size and weight of the breasts.

8. Loss of confidence due to dissatisfaction with overly large breasts.

IV. General Information on Gigantomastia Reduction Surgery:

Currently, the commonly used double-ring incision involves only one incision around the areola, making scars less noticeable after recovery.

V. Post-Surgery Breast Recovery:

Stitches are gradually removed within two weeks starting from the first week post-surgery. In some patients, decreased sensation in the areola and nipple may be noticed, usually temporarily. Sensory recovery may take weeks, months, or even up to a year. Time is needed for the breasts to regain their natural shape. Initially, surgical incisions will appear red and may remain so for several months.

VI. Pre-Surgery Considerations:

1. Avoid taking aspirin-containing medications two weeks before the surgery, as aspirin reduces platelet coagulation function.

2. Hypertensive and diabetic patients should fully inform the doctor about their conditions during the initial consultation to confirm the surgical plan.

3. Ensure good health before surgery, free from infectious diseases, other inflammatory conditions, or a family history of breast cancer.

4. Do not undergo surgery during menstruation, pregnancy, or lactation within six months.

VII. Post-Surgery Considerations:

1. Avoid raising your arms after breast reduction surgery; if necessary, use bandages on the upper chest.

2. Sleep on your back to avoid pressing on the breasts.

3. Wear supportive bras for several weeks until swelling and bruising subside.
