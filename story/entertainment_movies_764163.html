Introduction:

The protagonist in "City of God" is not a person but a place. The City of God was a slum in Brazil in the 1960s and became one of the most dangerous places in Rio de Janeiro from the 1980s.

The movie tells many stories, but all are seen through the eyes of a child: Buscapé, a black boy born into a poor family. Though small and weak, he fears becoming a criminal and is content with a low-paying job.

His environment is full of violence, offering him no escape. However, his way of observing and exploring society differs from others. He has unique insights into reality because he possesses an artist's eye for discovering life. Eventually, he becomes a professional photographer. This might be his redemption...

Buscapé is not the true protagonist of this film, nor does the plot revolve around him. However, his life is intertwined with the story, and through his observations, we see the film's condemnation of moral decay and endless violence.

The film is divided into three phases:

Late 1960s

Buscapé is an 11-year-old boy in the City of God, a poor town on the outskirts of Rio de Janeiro. One day, he sees a working photographer and decides to make it his career.

Dadinho moves to the City of God, also an 11-year-old boy whose dream is to become the most dangerous criminal in Rio de Janeiro. His evil nature shows from an early age. Soon, Dadinho becomes part of the local crime scene. He admires Cabra e Lera and their gang, who intercept gas trucks and take over other second-rate armed gangs. It is Cabra e Lera who gives Dadinho his first chance to kill, and as he grows up, murder becomes a daily occurrence.

1970s

The stories of both characters continue. Buscapé goes to school and occasionally works odd jobs. At this time, he seems like a marginal youth, wavering between falling into depravity and leading a "normal" life.

Dadinho’s hands are already stained with blood, responsible for many deaths. Now, he leads a small gang. Of course, he hasn't forgotten his childhood dream. Soon, he discovers a shortcut: selling cocaine and marijuana is far better than robbery and essential for becoming a major crime boss. Thus, he begins building his "business."

Early 1980s

After several failed armed robberies, Buscapé finally gets a camera and becomes a photographer, fulfilling his childhood dream.

Dadinho achieves his dream too: at 18, he changes his name to Pequeno and becomes the most feared and respected drug dealer in Rio de Janeiro. In the City of God, every word he says is law. Childhood friends become members of his gang. To expand his power, boys aged 9-14 join his ranks. No one dares challenge his position until Mané Galinha appears. A bus conductor, after witnessing his girlfriend being raped, swears to kill Pequeno at any cost to avenge her. This declaration forms, almost overnight, a group of armed children with the same belief - to kill Pequeno. The City of God is about to face a war.

Behind the scenes:

The film delivers sharp visual impact, with rapid editing akin to a repeatedly sharpened dagger gleaming with a chilling light. It presents everything worth showing without dragging its feet. The documentary-style techniques used in the film, such as MTV-like surreal colors, high-speed flashes, and sudden freezes, are rarely seen. More shocking than the visual imagery are the direct depictions of crime scenes. Killing in this film lacks exaggerated criticism or implicit subtlety; it makes you believe that this city was originally a paradise for criminals and executioners. Endless plundering and slaughter are not only their pleasures but also constitute the entire meaning of their lives.

The film is not just about Buscapé and Dadinho's psychological journeys. The true protagonist is the City of God itself. In this impoverished place, dozens of tangled stories occur, presenting us with a completely new and unexplored field. Each story is real, filled with love, laughter, and conflict. "City of God" is a true reflection of Rio de Janeiro, which is why it has become a breathtaking film.

Some novels attempting to describe the current state of Brazil have not captured its real turmoil. Accelerating social division has made violent crimes seem trivial.

Few authors have detailed descriptions of Brazil's current political situation (Brazil's apartheid policies and violent crimes in the slums) in their books. Author Paulo Lins, born in the Brazilian slums, reveals Brazil's political issues from a new perspective in "City of God." The slums are not only plagued by rampant drug trafficking but also by power struggles. Most young actors in the film come from slums surrounding Rio de Janeiro. Directors Meirelles and Katira spent six months with these children.

"City of God" revitalized Brazil's film industry and gave audiences some understanding of the social roots of Brazil's chaotic situation.

About the original work:

"For 20 years, whenever my son asked me: Dad, what are Rio de Janeiro or other big cities in Brazil like? I told him: Why don't you read 'City of God,' where there are descriptions of them. Do you get my meaning?"

— Maraisa Sales (documentary actor)

"City of God" is adapted from the novel "God's Town" by Paulo Lins, who spent eight years interviewing and collecting materials related to drug trafficking. After being published by Cia Das Letras, the novel received high praise from critics and quickly became one of Brazil's best-selling novels. It will be translated into six languages and released in 12 countries, including France (Gallimard), the United States (Strauss & Giroux), Italy (Einaudi), and England (Bloomsbury).

Paulo Lins and "City of God"

In 1982, anthropologist Elba de Barros organized a discussion about crime in the City of God called "Crime Among the Underclass." During this discussion, Paulo directly or indirectly contacted and interviewed some people. Elba de Barros asked him to write an article on anthropology, but Paulo told her he was more interested in literature. So, Paulo wrote a poem, which was later published by Roberto Schwartz in the Novos Estudos Cebrap magazine. Roberto Schwartz had always hoped he would write a novel. In 1986, Paulo began writing "City of God" and completed this work reflecting the violence in Brazil's underclass ten years later. The book was published in 1997.

Paulo said, "When I was writing this novel, I often thought that maybe one day it would be adapted into a movie. I like movies; from 1980 to 1987, I was a member of the City of God Film Club. This club often screened political films, such as 'Crossed Arms,' 'Maquinas Paradas,' and 'They Don't Used Tuxedos.' I often thought that if the City of God were put on screen, many people would participate."

When Fernando Meirelles enthusiastically told Paulo that he wanted to shoot this movie, Paulo was very excited. In 1997, Paulo sold the original copyright to Luis Schwartz. Fernando and Bráulio Mantovani began adapting the screenplay. "My first contact with Bráulio was discussing the background era and language of the film when he was writing the initial three drafts. Two years later, I acted as a consultant again, this time for Kátia Lund. She joined the script production and wanted to delve deeper into the characters' inner worlds. By then, the film had already started shooting in Rio de Janeiro. Early on, when making documentaries 'News from a Private War' and the short film 'A Minha Alma' (My Soul) with the band 'O Rappa,' Kátia and I collaborated, so I could be sure that the cast selection would follow a realistic style."

Shooting began in June 2001, and Paulo was invited to assist in discussing some issues in the film, such as historical context, costumes, and music. Paulo also tried to participate in the filming. On set, Fernando and Kátia often adopted some of his suggestions. Paulo was very satisfied with the film and was amazed at the actors' natural performances.

Paulo said, "I am glad that 'City of God' has been translated into Italian, French, Catalan, Swedish, and Danish, and will soon be translated into English. What pleases me more is that more and more people can understand 'City of God' through this movie, gaining a deeper understanding of violence, abandonment, education, and poverty in Brazil."

Director Fernando Meirelles talks about "City of God":

Reading "City of God" is like reading a revelation, a revelation about another side of my homeland. Before reading this book, I thought I had a deep understanding of the racial segregation existing in Brazilian society. After finishing this book, I realized that we, people from the middle class, sometimes cannot see things happening around us. The concepts of nation, law, citizenship, education, future, etc., are all abstract.

I decided to make a movie faithful to the original work, a film reflecting life in the slums without any acting skills. There is no fixed plot in the movie, and no professional actors participate. Only real-life children appear in it. I am fortunate that each actor shares the same enthusiasm for the film as I do.

The problems reflected in "City of God" exist not only in Brazil but throughout the world. Violence breeds in our civilized world. The wealth of the first world cannot be replicated in the third and fourth worlds. Or rather, they struggle on the edge of suffering.

Cinematographer César Charlone:

"...Pequeno mercilessly beat nine-year-old Bigolinha until he passed out. Pequeno thought he was faking death, so he didn't stop. Afterwards, Pequeno laughed wildly as he shot bullets into Bigolinha's motionless body..."

"...He quickened his pace, turned a corner, and held his breath while looking at his wound. He leaned his cheek against the wall, trying to confirm if anyone was following him. But suddenly, a bullet hit his forehead, and Pequeno appeared..."

Cinematographer César Charlone copied the above paragraphs from the original text and posted them on the door so that every time I saw them, I formed a clear character image in my mind. César and Fernando discussed the positioning and overall direction of the movie. Fernando hoped to faithfully shoot this film according to the original work. The stories in Paulo's book were entirely unfamiliar to us, but his observation angle was unique. We still tried to make the film as authentic as possible.

When César began designing the shooting process, Fernando had already decided to use non-professional actors living in the slums instead of professional actors for the shoot. This was indeed a good choice, making the film look more "real," but it also posed certain difficulties for the production team. The biggest challenge was avoiding turning the piece into a documentary. The actors had no concept of basic elements like lighting, and some (especially the children) couldn't distinguish between fiction and reality.

At the start of the shoot, César thought it was just an action movie. They had various violent film materials available and could easily find countless "dreamlike" materials from the "archive" to create a dazzling action film. But after César and his assistants carefully read "City of God," all the material seemed meaningless to the crew. The novel brought them back to reality, and all flashy plots were impossible in this film. Everything was a great challenge for César. Despite this, we see a successful movie.

The film is adapted from Brazilian writer Paolo Lins' 600-page novel of the same name, vividly presenting the criminal records of black street gangs in the slums of the satellite town of Rio (Rio) from 1960 to 1980. Paolo Lins grew up on the streets of the City of God, and every lively character in the book was what he saw with his eyes and heard with his ears.

To make the entire movie more objective and closer to realism, director Fernando extensively employed non-professional actors for real performances. They recruited more than 400 non-professional actors from various streets in Rio without informing them of the purpose of the film, allowing them to audition and undergo physical training first. The entire process was recorded and documented, and finally, 200 suitable candidates were selected. According to age and personality differences, they were divided into 8 groups and underwent professional actor courses twice a week for 11 hours each session, lasting two months. Finally, 110 outstanding performers aged 12 to 19 were selected to undergo intensive actor training for 8 months. During the training process, through close interaction with them, the most genuine and lifelike black street language and battle experience were refined and incorporated into the script. As for the shooting process, the director emphasized the spontaneous performances of non-professional actors, giving the film a more realistic style close to the urban landscape.

During filming, to fully present the dark side of the City of God, the film production team took risks regardless of personal safety and insisted on shooting on location. This work not only required a producer familiar with the local situation who could communicate with the underworld and handle internal and external affairs but also needed to draft production agreements detailing various filming regulations (such as the timing of vehicles coming and going every day, the list of temporary actors provided by the underworld and the costs involved). On the first day of filming, there was even a threat from the underworld boss who believed the script was too violent and overly emphasized drug transactions, potentially setting a bad example for teenagers.

Ultimately, the production team managed to complete the entire film on schedule without casualties through comprehensive police protection and random changes in shooting locations. In total, the two-month-long (June to August 2001) filming process, costing $3.3 million, felt like a nightmare, requiring countless negotiations and communications or facing threats with guns and knives at any moment.
