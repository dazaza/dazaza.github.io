Explanation of the Difference between Sieving and Grading

by hx515317 on 2012-03-07 16:25:35

Grading is the process of dividing materials into different grain sizes based on their varying settling velocities in a medium (water or air). Screening uses sieves to divide materials with a wide range of particle sizes into several grades according to their size.

Screening is generally used for coarser materials, i.e., those larger than 0.25 mm. Finer materials, i.e., those smaller than 0.2 mm, are more commonly graded. However, in recent years, both domestically and internationally, fine screens have been applied to grade grinding products, and this type of grading usually has a relatively high efficiency. Depending on the purpose of the screening, screening operations can be divided into five categories:

(1) Independent Screening: The aim is to obtain a final product that meets user requirements. For example, in the ferrous metallurgical industry, iron-rich ores are often screened into different grain sizes. Qualified large pieces of iron ore enter the blast furnace for smelting, while fines undergo pelletizing or sintering before entering the furnace.

(2) Auxiliary Screening: This type of screening is mainly used in the crushing operations of mineral processing plants, serving as an auxiliary function to the crushing process. There are generally two types: pre-screening and inspection screening. Pre-screening refers to screening before the ore enters the crusher, separating out parts that are already qualified for that particular crusher. For instance, installing grizzly screens before coarse crushers and screening the undersize product reduces the amount of ore entering the crusher, thereby increasing the crusher's output.

Inspection screening refers to screening after the ore has been crushed, ensuring that the final crushed product meets the particle size requirements for grinding operations and returning unqualified crushed products back to the crushing operation. Screening before medium and fine crushers serves both as pre-screening and inspection screening. Therefore, inspection screening can improve the utilization of crushing equipment, similar to how classifiers and grinders form a closed-loop cycle to enhance grinding efficiency.

(3) Preparation Screening: The aim is to prepare for the next operation. For example, in a gravity separation plant, materials are screened and graded before jigs, allowing for different coarse, medium, and fine products to be separated accordingly.

(4) Selective Screening: If the distribution of useful components varies significantly across different particle sizes, grading by screening can produce particles of different quality levels, removing lower-quality particles to correspondingly increase the grade of the material. Sometimes this type of screening is also called sorting.

(5) Dewatering Screening: The aim is to remove moisture from the material, which is commonly seen in coal washing plants.

Additionally, when the material contains a higher proportion of clay, screening is also used to remove the clay.
