Mu Yu, the author of the ticket-grabbing plugin, talks about why he developed the Leopard Mobile Ticket Grabbing Edition.

by anonymous on 2013-09-11 13:26:11

It took two months to develop the Cheetah Mobile version of the ticket-grabbing app.

Back in April last year, there were rumors that a mobile version of 12306 (the official Chinese train ticket booking platform) would be launched. After one cycle of seasons, the rumor turned into hearsay. Promises made were forgotten as time passed by without anyone knowing who initially committed to it.

Cheetah Mobile Browser approached me wanting support at the core functionality level, asking me to create a ticket-booking software suitable for mobile use. With special privileges from the Cheetah Mobile Browser core (like cross-domain permissions), I spent two months tucked away in a corner of the vast office developing a purely front-end technology (HTML+JavaScript+CSS) based ticket-booking software designed for mobile use.

Of course, when the front-end code was released, it was compressed and obfuscated – something I had always been reluctant to do, but later realized that not doing so would lead to numerous small people causing endless frustration.

National Day train tickets will soon be available for purchase. Can Spring Festival tickets be far behind?

I wonder if tickets for the Spring Festival will still be hard to come by. If they are, will the situation improve even just a little compared to last year?

What I can confirm is that some people will definitely make a fortune during this time, filling their pockets; others will search endlessly without finding tickets, standing in crowded ticket halls gazing at the remaining tickets display screens with despair; some will joyfully celebrate after purchasing their ideal tickets, feeling like they’ve just won over their dream girl; and many companies will start offering salvation, such as Cheetah Browser, and another number I've always disliked.

As for me, I can only use this opportunity to assert my existence.

Father's experience buying tickets in the snow inspired the ticketing assistant

Many people ask me why I made the ticketing assistant, was it because I saw a market opportunity, or was it purely to help others?

I always smile and say no, it wasn’t for those reasons. I don't consider myself that noble.

I did it for myself, because I needed it. I needed it to save me from the agony of having to queue for ten hours outside the ticket window just to buy a single ticket. I’ll never forget the look of guilt on my father’s face after he queued in the snow for fifteen hours and bought me a standing-only ticket, feeling responsible for making me stand for ten hours.

Within my capabilities, because it’s free and public, allowing more friends to avoid such pain is a very joyful thing.

It seems like I’m always disconnected from money. Too many people ask me why I don’t take advantage of this opportunity to make a fortune, pointing out how others have bought houses and cars through similar ventures.

But I’m not in such a rush for money. A friend hearing me say this immediately assumed I must be a rich second-generation.

I can't afford a house, but so what? Life goes on. I can't afford a car either, but so what? Riding a bicycle makes me happier.

Even though many browser manufacturers use assistants for promotion, like Cheetah Browser which almost everyone knows about when it comes to buying tickets, I have no objections. As a business, promoting to gain market share allows more people to benefit from the assistant’s advantages. Why shouldn’t they, despite their motives, contribute to helping more people get tickets?

Though I seem to have become an outsider in this game.

Resolutely refusing to implement CAPTCHA recognition

Many people ask me, since you made it to help people buy tickets, why not make it more powerful, like adding CAPTCHA recognition as a selling point. With this feature, wouldn’t it be stronger and more automated?

Initially, when asked, I would simply and lightly reply, "No, it won’t be added."

After being asked too many times, I started to wonder why so many people were asking this. Sometimes I would counter-question, "Don't you think that adding CAPTCHA recognition, which breaks the rules of the game, would only make tickets harder to buy?"

But it seems they don’t see it that way.

Tickets are already insufficient, automatic CAPTCHA recognition would only exacerbate the problem, which isn’t the result I want to see.

Some say, "If you add CAPTCHA recognition, I'll pay for it." I say, "No amount of money will change my mind." Some persist, saying things like, "Look, others have it, why can't you?" When they irritate me, I lose my temper and tell them, "If someone else has that feature, go use theirs. Don't waste your breath here."

Why should there be CAPTCHA recognition? It would be truly unfair, and I can't guarantee that such a function won't be abused, leading to tickets being monopolized under numerous accounts for speculative purposes.

Many also say, "But your tool is open-source and free for anyone to use. If everyone uses it, won’t the result be the same? Many people will still not be able to buy tickets because the total number of tickets remains unchanged. And isn't it unfair that some people who couldn't buy tickets before can now do so using the tool?"

When I first discussed the assistant with a classmate who reportedly works for the Ministry of Railways, he also asked me this question.

Yes, tickets are insufficient, I can't magically produce more tickets, nor can I guarantee that you will definitely get one. All I can do is ensure that before you succeed or fail in getting a ticket, you don't need to invest a lot of time and effort waiting for tickets. That's the sole significance.

Many also say that using the tool speeds up the process and increases competitiveness, making it unfair for those who don't use tools.

I wrestled with these questions for a long time before finally coming to a conclusion: anything done within the boundaries of the game's rules, improving efficiency and competitiveness, should not be obstructed by considerations of fairness.

Sometimes I console myself that there are so many scalpers using tools that break the rules, like automatic CAPTCHA recognition, to recklessly profit. If we could empower more people who genuinely need tickets to fight back against them, perhaps it would be a positive development.

The Rules of the Game

In this matter, I have consistently adhered to this principle. What are the rules of the game? They are the things you could originally do, which I help you do better and more efficiently, while still following the rules. Things you couldn’t originally do, or technically could but society's rules or systemic regulations forbid you from doing, violate the rules.

So CAPTCHA recognition is on my blacklist; it violates the rules.

Please Mourn Those Who Get Tickets

Sometimes I feel I'm unsuited to be a businessman because I consider too many human aspects, losing opportunities for development. Sometimes I feel I'm just a scholar, always prone to self-righteousness.

Whether it's naivety or immaturity, I've written many letters to the Ministry of Railways suggesting improvements to CAPTCHA systems to prevent greedy individuals from using CAPTCHA recognition to dominate others' tickets, but received no response. The CAPTCHA system remained the same. They all said not to worry, assuming the Ministry's priorities differ from mine, and what concerns me isn't what they care about.

Once, chatting with that classmate who supposedly works for the Ministry, I asked him if they find it easy to buy tickets. He replied, "Don't mention it, we often can't buy them either." It was heart-wrenching to hear.

The author of the "Mom, I'm Home" software wrote in the documentation, "When you book a ticket, it means someone else can't. So please mourn a moment."

I agree, and maybe next time I'll write the same. Maybe I'll also write, "Please remember to despise those who use CAPTCHA recognition and relationships to resell tickets."

Sincerely hoping there are no difficult-to-buy tickets in the world.

Many say, why bother? This lets everyone know you. Isn't that a good thing for you? But I don't really need such a good thing. Everyone benefits, that's the real good thing.

I probably only fit being an ordinary person, doing things that aren't closely related to money.

Last time someone commented on me, saying, "With so many ideas, it's fitting that others buy houses and cars while you sit there eating instant noodles."

Alright, I deserve it, but so what?

Someone once told me, "You remind me of Ruff's love."

The author of OpenGG, who later abandoned it.
