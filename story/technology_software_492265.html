Legal Application and Risk Control in Layoffs

1. Layoff Conditions and Risk Control

According to Article 41 of the Labor Contract Law, employers must meet statutory conditions for layoffs. The burden of proof for meeting layoff conditions lies with the employer, who must have sufficient evidence to demonstrate that the legal conditions for layoffs are met. Failure to provide such evidence will result in the layoffs being deemed illegal. The following are the conditions under which an employer may conduct layoffs:

When the employer reorganizes according to the provisions of the Enterprise Bankruptcy Law. In practice, providing proof of reorganization (such as a court ruling) makes it relatively easier for the employer to justify layoffs based on this condition. Without a court-issued reorganization ruling, layoffs cannot be justified on these grounds.

When the employer's production and operations encounter severe difficulties. This is a significant reason for layoffs by employers. Employers must provide evidence proving that their operations have faced severe difficulties. This often requires financial documentation. Many employers use the financial crisis as a pretext for layoffs when their operations are not actually affected, which can lead to the risk of illegal dismissals.

When the enterprise transitions to another product line, undergoes major technological reforms, or adjusts its business model, and after changing labor contracts, still needs to reduce personnel. Employers must note that upon encountering such changes, they cannot immediately initiate layoffs but must first negotiate changes to labor contracts with employees. Only after these changes, if further layoffs are still necessary, can layoffs proceed. Failing to change the labor contract before initiating layoffs constitutes illegal termination of the contract, and the employer must bear legal responsibility for such actions.

Other situations where there are significant changes in the objective economic circumstances upon which the labor contract was based, making the contract unenforceable. Note that this refers to significant changes in objective economic circumstances, not the third item of Article 40 of the Labor Contract Law, which involves changes in the objective circumstances at the time of signing the labor contract that make the contract unenforceable, even after negotiations between the employer and the employee fail to reach an agreement on modifying the contract content. Objective economic circumstances generally refer to force majeure events or other situations that render all or part of the labor contract terms unenforceable, such as relocation, mergers, spin-offs, joint ventures, etc.

2. Layoff Procedures and Risk Control

Article 41 of the Labor Contract Law states that if any of the following situations occur, and it is necessary to lay off more than twenty people or less than twenty people but more than ten percent of the total number of employees, the employer must notify the union or all employees thirty days in advance, listen to the opinions of the union or employees, and report the layoff plan to the labor administrative department before proceeding with layoffs. This article specifies the requirements for the number of layoffs and procedural requirements:

(1) Number Requirement: Layoffs must involve more than twenty people or less than twenty people but more than ten percent of the total number of employees to initiate the layoff process. In practice, the risk for employers lies in initiating the layoff process without reaching twenty people or without reaching ten percent of the total number of employees in smaller companies. If the number of layoffs does not meet the legal standard, the company cannot initiate mass layoffs and must instead terminate individual contracts according to Articles 36, 39, and 40 of the Labor Contract Law. Otherwise, the layoff action will be deemed illegal, and the company will bear the legal risks associated with wrongful dismissal. It is recommended that employers opt for negotiated terminations to better avoid risks.

(2) Advance Notification: Employers must notify the union or all employees thirty days in advance, listening to their opinions. Note that notification can be made either to the union or all employees, and the employer can choose. During the operation, employers must ensure that all employees are notified directly, not through representatives, and should retain written evidence of the advance notice to the union or all employees. Failing to notify in advance or failing to provide evidence will lead to the risk of illegal layoffs. Additionally, the employer must propose a layoff plan, including details such as the list of laid-off personnel, the timing and steps of the layoff, and measures for severance pay in accordance with laws, regulations, and collective agreements, while also listening to the opinions of the labor administrative department.

(3) Reporting Procedure: The layoff plan must be reported to the labor administrative department. Note that the law does not require the approval of the labor administrative department but only the fulfillment of the reporting procedure. Employers should retain evidence of the labor administrative department's receipt.

(4) Layoff Execution: The employer formally announces the layoff plan, processes the termination of labor contracts with the laid-off personnel, pays severance compensation in accordance with relevant regulations, and issues layoff certificates to the laid-off personnel.

3. Priority Retention of Personnel During Layoffs

According to the provisions of the Labor Contract Law, during layoffs, priority should be given to retaining the following personnel: (1) Those who have signed fixed-term labor contracts with longer durations with the unit; (2) Those who have signed indefinite-term labor contracts with the unit; (3) Those who have no other employed family members and need to support elderly individuals or minors. Additionally, within six months after layoffs, if the employer rehires personnel, they should notify the laid-off personnel and prioritize rehiring them under equal conditions. Note: According to Article 19 of the "Notice on Several Issues Regarding the Implementation of the Labor Contract System," employees who were laid off due to economic reasons and are rehired by the original unit within six months should have their work years before and after the layoff continuously calculated as part of their tenure with the unit.

If an employer fails to consider the legal requirement to prioritize retaining certain personnel, such as dismissing employees with long-term or indefinite-term contracts while retaining those with short-term contracts, it could also be considered an illegal layoff.
