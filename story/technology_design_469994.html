Smart calculation of the bookmaker's position and cost

by tuoqing1188 on 2011-08-03 13:14:43

How can we cleverly calculate the major player's position size and cost from the K-line chart and trading volume? Using the turnover rate should be accurate.

The formula for calculating the turnover rate is trading volume divided by the total number of floating shares multiplied by 100%. Since we are calculating the total turnover rate during the period from when the major player starts to build a position to when they start to lift the price, based on my experience, the weekly line chart has the greatest reference significance.

Referencing the individual stock's weekly K-line chart, the weekly moving average parameters can be set as 5, 10, and 20. When the moving average system in the weekly K-line chart shows a bull market arrangement, it proves that there is a major player involved in this stock. Only when the major player invests a large amount of funds does the individual stock's trading volume continue to expand at a low level, which is a characteristic of the major player building a position. Due to the insufficient supply of chips, the stock price gradually rises, making the weekly K-line's moving average system show a bull market arrangement, allowing us to preliminarily confirm that we have found the major player.

Whether it is a short-term, medium-term, or long-term major player, their control degree should be at least 20% or more. Only stocks with a control degree reaching 20% can be operated. If less than 20%, unless the overall market is extremely good, it is impossible to act as a major player. If the control degree is between 20%-40%, the stock nature is most active, but there are more floating chips, the rising space is smaller, and the lifting difficulty is higher; if the control volume is between 40%-60%, the activity level of this stock is better, and the space is larger, achieving relative control; if the control volume exceeds 60%, the activity level is poorer, but the space is huge, which is absolute control, and most big dark horses are produced in this control area. Why is there such a rule? Because generally 20% of a stock's circulation volume is locked and immovable, these are absolute long-term investors. Among the remaining 80% of circulating chips, only 20% are the most active floating chips. If you collect all these 20% chips, there will be very few floating chips in the market, so you can roughly control the position. The last 60% are relatively stable chips. If the major player absorbs half of them, i.e., 30%, then the major player holds 50% control of the chips, plus the 20% held by long-term investors, equaling the major player controlling 70% of the chips. This way, the major player can control the movement direction of the stock price. This is also the control degree adopted by most major players. If the major player controls more than 60%, there are only 20% floating chips outside. The major player can then control the stock price at will. Investors should follow the major player who holds more than 50% of the position. Of course, the more holdings, the better, because the rise is roughly proportional to the position size.

Generally speaking, when the stock price rises, the major player accounts for about 30% of the trading volume, while during a decline, it is 20%. During an increase, the volume expands, and during a decrease, it contracts. We can preliminarily assume the expansion: contraction = 2:1. Therefore, within a certain period, the major player's position volume is (2×30%) - (1×20%) = 60% - 20% = 40%, which is the initial calculation result of the major player's control degree when the turnover rate is 100% during an increase and also 100% during a decrease. But this calculation is too complex. We can roughly calculate that during this period with a total turnover rate of 200%, the major player may hold 40% of the chips. Then, when the total turnover rate is 100%, the absorbable chips should be (40%)/(200%) × 100% = 20%.

We start calculating from the time when the weekly KD indicator crosses at a low position and the weekly trading volume moderately increases at a low position until the week you are calculating. Add up the trading volumes of each week to get the total weekly trading volume, then divide by the floating shares and multiply by 100%, which gives the total turnover rate. If the total turnover rate is 100%, the main force's position volume is 20%; if the total turnover rate is 200%, the major player's position volume is 40%; if the total turnover rate is 300%, the major player's position volume is 60%; if the total turnover rate is 400%, the major player's position volume is 80%. This is calculated based on the assumption that for every 100% turnover, the major player can absorb 20% of the chips, and so on.

Generally speaking, when the total turnover rate reaches 200%, the major player accelerates chip absorption and builds positions by raising prices because there are no more low-price chips available. This is a good opportunity for short-term involvement. When the total turnover rate reaches 300%, the major player has basically absorbed enough chips. Next, the major player either quickly lifts the price or forcibly washes out the chips. You should judge the main force's intention and direction from the market conditions, avoiding blindly advancing and passively turning short-term into medium-term. At this point, we need to refer to the short-term indicator KDJ, especially the 60-minute KDJ indicator. If it is at a high position, you can wait for the indicator to adjust before entering. This can both improve the utilization rate of funds and avoid being trapped in the short term. By calculating the total turnover rate, we can roughly judge the main force's position building and locking degree, maximizing our profits and minimizing time. This method is particularly accurate for analyzing newly listed stocks. In daily stock watching, we can track and analyze those individual stocks with a turnover rate exceeding 300% at a low position, then comprehensively analyze their daily K-line, trading volume, and combine with some technical indicators to grasp the best timing for entry, ensuring substantial returns.

Next, we also need to calculate the major player's cost. We will use the following two methods to estimate:

1. If you use Honghui analysis software, it is the simplest. Use Shift + → two keys to estimate the turnover rate and cost price, which is the most accurate algorithm;

2. Generally speaking, the medium-term major player's building time is approximately 40-60 days, i.e., 8-12 weeks. Taking the average value as 10 weeks, from the weekly K-line chart, we can objectively consider the 10-week average line as the main force's cost area. This algorithm has a certain error, but it won't deviate by more than 10%. As a major player, the rise of the individual stock they operate is at least 50% or more, mostly 100%. Generally speaking, if the rise from the lowest point to the highest point in a wave of trend is 100%, then the normal profit of the major player is 40%. After we calculate the main force's cost, multiplying this price by 150% gives the major player's minimum target position. Regardless of how tortuous the road is, the stock price will eventually reach this position because if not forced, the major player will never leave with a loss. Major players have large funds, making it difficult to enter and exit. Sometimes, you cannot sell even if you want to. This is the major player's eternal pain.

This is what the major player's details should be calculated. Seeing through the major player's bottom card allows us to aim precisely in operations, no longer fearing the major player's soft and hard tactics. Seeing through the rise and fall of the stock price, it is merely a psychological game played by the major player with us.
