Microsoft wants to surpass Google and Apple by acquiring Nokia? Difficult.

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 15:51:40

Two years ago, what should have been done is finally completed: Microsoft acquires Nokia's main department and takes control of its own smartphone destiny. Despite Microsoft still claiming that Windows Phone has many partners, today's acquisition will obviously terminate the WP licensing business. It will make Microsoft officially become the third in the smartphone battle.

By integrating software and hardware into one body, Microsoft's smartphone department looks increasingly like the standalone Windows RT department and Apple. But unlike Apple, the phones sold by Nokia are not high-priced, high-profit models. They also do not occupy Microsoft's enterprise customer market. Microsoft claims it has become a "devices and services" company, but this is not necessarily a good thing. If Microsoft uses its advantages in the service field to help the sales of WP, it may ultimately hinder its greater goals.

When Ballmer announced his resignation, the Wall Street Journal asked a group of tech CEOs who would be the next CEO of Microsoft. Bret Taylor, Facebook's former CTO, pointed out the core of the problem:

Microsoft's recent strategy is to shift focus to "services and devices" - eventually becoming a combination of Google and Apple. I doubt whether this strategy will succeed, as the service model and hardware model are very different. The hardware model relies on the close integration of software and hardware, with the primary source of profit being hardware. In contrast, the key to the service model is popularity - making your services available on different hardware and systems and seeking to maximize users. I would advise Microsoft's new CEO to carefully examine their strategy and choose a single, simple business model.

How Google does it

Taylor's point is that Google and Apple are very different. Google is happy to see the success of iOS as long as its own services such as Google Maps, Gmail, and search can also be used on Apple devices. In fact, almost everything Google does is centered around acquiring more user data and continuously improving ad targeting based on this data. Although Google acquired Motorola, it currently does not seem to intend to make a big splash in the smartphone market. Moto X will increase Google's market share in smartphones, but this remains limited. If the nine-figure advertising budget Google invested in Moto X fails to bring expected returns, it is likely that it will gradually abandon the Motorola brand.

How Apple does it

Apple's iPhone, despite having a low market share, accounts for half to three-quarters of the revenue in the smartphone market. This phone's accessories cost about $200, but it sells for as high as $600, with each iPhone generating nearly $300 in profit. The iPhone 5C is expected to be released on September 10th, but this will not fundamentally change Apple's business model. Apple seeks higher market share, but only on the condition that it can still generate huge profits from hardware sales. Although the iOS platform also brings significant software and service businesses, these are all developed based on the iPhone and iPad.

What Microsoft wants to do

Nokia's Lumia smartphones generate only $30 in profit per unit, and each unit also incurs a $10 software licensing fee, so together Microsoft can gain $40 in profit per unit. Considering the enormous personnel and marketing expenses, there is almost no profit left. In reality, despite Nokia's record-breaking WP sales last quarter, it incurred huge losses. Although Nokia has high-end phones like the Lumia 1020, their sales are not good, and the best-selling model is the low-end 520. Nokia relies on discounts and emerging economies to develop its smartphone business. Objectively speaking, this is not much of an issue, but considering Microsoft's strategy, it cannot reach its intended goals.

WP's ability to win Android and iPhone users is not strong. If Microsoft wants to expand the currently third-place platform, it cannot solely rely on the low-end market. Microsoft promoted Surface RT by first applying its own Office software on this platform, but this strategy did not succeed, and on WP, Microsoft did not adopt this strategy, and even if it did, it would not succeed. Nokia still has a huge feature phone department, so what does Microsoft plan to do with them?

Microsoft needs to make a choice

What Microsoft should do is strive to promote Office and its cloud services across various platforms. Microsoft is present in almost all Fortune 500 companies, and most of these companies are equipped with iPhones, iPads, and Androids. Selling low-end smartphones and promoting Office 365 are not in conflict, but the former does not significantly benefit the latter.

This again comes back to the issue of strategy. Naturally, Microsoft must work hard to make WP and RT tablets successful after acquiring Nokia, but the reality is that these two platforms cannot compete with high-end competitors and can only hold a place in the low-profit market, selling more means losing more. Meanwhile, Microsoft's attention to its own advantageous services has decreased. If Elop becomes the next CEO of Microsoft, this tendency will only worsen.

The final result is that Microsoft may become a low-profit version of Apple plus a low-presence version of Google. There is currently no visible profit generated by this business, but it is clearly going to distract Microsoft's focus, just like Bing - competing for the sake of competition rather than seeking the least loss in competition. This distraction led Microsoft to spend countless advertising fees on Surface RT but failed to attract iPad users. In the era when PC sales are continuously declining, can Microsoft maintain its competitiveness by doing this?
