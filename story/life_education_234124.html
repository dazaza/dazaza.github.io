Why doesn't Chinese education have the courage to face poetry?

by laobi on 2009-09-06 23:42:59

By Shi Xin

In the vast and profound Chinese culture, poetry is undoubtedly the most brilliant pearl on the crown of Chinese culture. From "The Book of Songs" to "Li Sao", from Tao Yuanming to Li Bai, Du Fu, Bai Juyi, from Su Dongpo to Li Qingzhao and then to Xin Qiji,..., until the New Culture Movement, until vernacular poetry, until Xu Zhimo, until Yan'an literature, I would like to ask, where is there no trace of poetry? Where is there no sound of poetry? Where is there no place for poetry? Talking about literature in China, you absolutely cannot bypass poetry.

When Chinese children receive enlightenment education, one of the textbooks they choose is "Three Hundred Tang Poems". There is a saying that goes, "If you memorize Three Hundred Tang Poems, even if you can't write poetry, you will still be fluent." The extent to which poetry education has spread in China is self-evident, as is the importance of poetry in our lives. The positive value that poetry has in cultivating our moral character, elevating our morality, educating our lives, and poeticizing our existence is something that no era could possibly erase.

Today, I went online to check out the college entrance exam essays and was fortunate enough to come across two pieces of news. One was an interview with Wang Xuming, the former spokesperson for the Ministry of Education, expressing regret over the exclusion of poetry in college entrance exam essays. The other was poet Zhao Lihua's "jest" regarding the absence of poetry in college entrance exam essays. I think their opinions are very accurate. I would like to ask, why does Chinese education, especially the college entrance exam, not have the courage to face poetry? I thought about it for a long time, but I couldn't find the reason why poetry is despised by the college entrance exam. If I were to say that after much thought I still couldn't figure it out, I think the decline of poetry in our education is one of the reasons.

What is the strongest pillar supporting the brilliance of Chinese culture? Without a doubt, it is poetry. What enriches our language? Without a doubt, it is poetry. Is it true that with our development today, poetry has lost its significance and value? Is it true that we no longer need poetry today? It is probably not. If we were to review the shortcomings of Chinese education, I believe that the almost moribund poetry education is one of the more prominent aspects.

Poetry is the essence of a nation's spirit; poetry is the blossoming of a nation's temperament; poetry is the flower of a nation's thoughts; poetry is an indispensable spiritual nourishment for a country. A country without poetry, if it were to talk about culture, would likely be laughed at. Our poetry has fallen from its peak into a low point, and now has reached a state of mediocrity, which is truly lamentable. The Ministry of Education has not explained why poetry should be excluded? The Ministry of Culture has not explained why poetry should be excluded? How has poetry offended Chinese education? Why is the rejection of poetry so absolute?

If it is inconvenient to evaluate solely written poetry in the college entrance exam essay, then why not allocate 5 or even more points out of several dozen points in the essay section, allowing candidates to write a poem? Isn't poetry better able to measure a person's depth? I believe that an exam without poetry evaluation is definitely not comprehensive. I don't want to say much more. Still, I share the hope of Wang Xuming and Zhao Lihua, hoping that poetry can soon appear on the college entrance exam papers, and that poetry can get a share in the college entrance exam papers and occupy a place. At the same time, this is also a suggestion and an appeal.
