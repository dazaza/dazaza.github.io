Butterfly Effect, Frog Phenomenon, Crocodile Rule, Catfish Effect, Herd Effect, Hedgehog Rule, Watch Law, Broken Windows Theory, 80/20 Rule, Barrel Theory, Matthew Effect, do you understand all these?

1. Butterfly Effect: In the 1970s, an American meteorologist named Lorenz explained the air system theory and said that an occasional flutter of a butterfly's wings in the Amazon rainforest might cause a tornado in Texas, USA in two weeks.

The Butterfly Effect means that very small changes in initial conditions can be amplified to create extremely significant differences in future states. Some small things can be unclear, but some small things, if amplified by the system, such as men and women, who are more lustful, are important for an organization or a country and cannot be vague.

2. Frog Phenomenon: If you put a frog directly into a pot of hot water, since it is highly sensitive to adverse environments, it will quickly jump out of the pot. If you place a frog in a pot of cold water and gradually heat it up, the frog won't immediately jump out. The final outcome as the water temperature rises is that the frog gets boiled alive because by the time the water becomes too hot for the frog to endure, it is either too late or incapable of jumping out.

The Frog Phenomenon tells us that sudden events often easily attract people's attention, while what leads to death is when people feel good about themselves and fail to clearly notice the gradual worsening of the actual situation.

3. Crocodile Rule: Its original meaning is to assume that a crocodile has bitten your foot; if you try to free your foot with your hand, the crocodile will also bite your hand and foot. The more you struggle, the more parts of you it will bite. Therefore, if a crocodile bites your foot, your only option is to sacrifice one foot.

For example, in the stock market, the Crocodile Rule means that when you find your trade going against the market direction, you must immediately cut your losses without any delay or luck.

4. Catfish Effect: Previously, during transportation, sardines had a very low survival rate. Later, someone discovered that if a catfish was placed among the sardines, the situation improved significantly, and the survival rate greatly increased. Why?

It turns out that when a catfish enters a new environment, it becomes "irritable" and swims around restlessly, which undoubtedly stirs up the mostly calm sardines. When sardines notice this "outsider," they naturally become tense and swim faster. This solves the problem of oxygen deficiency, and the sardines don't die.

5. Herd Effect: Wherever the lead sheep goes, the rest follow.

The Herd Effect was originally a term in stock investment, mainly referring to investors mimicking each other during transactions, "following suit," blindly copying others, which leads them to buy and sell the same stocks during certain periods.

6. Hedgehog Rule: Two sleepy hedgehogs huddle together for warmth due to the cold. However, since both have spines, they move apart after getting pricked. But then they get too cold and huddle together again. After several attempts, the two hedgehogs finally find a suitable distance: they can keep each other warm without being pricked.

The Hedgehog Rule mainly refers to the "psychological distance effect" in interpersonal interactions.

7. Watch Law: The Watch Law states that when a person has one watch, they know what time it is. However, when they have two watches, they cannot determine the time. Two watches do not provide a more accurate time; instead, they make the person lose confidence in knowing the correct time.

In terms of enterprise management, the Watch Law gives us a very intuitive insight: the same person or organization cannot be managed using two different methods at the same time, nor can two different goals be set simultaneously. Furthermore, one person should not be directed by two people at the same time; otherwise, the enterprise or individual will be confused.

8. Broken Windows Theory: If a house has a broken window that no one repairs, soon other windows will mysteriously be broken as well. A wall, if there are graffiti that are not cleaned up, will soon be covered with unsightly things. In a clean place, people hesitate to litter, but once there is trash on the ground, people will throw garbage without hesitation, feeling no shame.

9. 80/20 Rule (Pareto Principle): At the end of the 19th century and the beginning of the 20th century, Italian economist Pareto believed that in any group of things, the most important ones only account for a small part, approximately 20%, while the remaining 80% majority, although secondary, is still crucial.

Society concentrates approximately 80% of its wealth in the hands of 20% of the population, while 80% of the population only owns 20% of the social wealth. This statistical imbalance exists everywhere in society, economics, and life, which is the 80/20 rule.

The 80/20 rule tells us not to analyze, handle, or view problems equally. In business operations and management, we need to focus on the critical few; identify those key customers who bring 80% of the profits but only account for 20% of the total volume, enhance services, and achieve twice the result with half the effort. Business leaders need to carefully categorize and analyze work, focusing their main energy on solving major issues and capturing major projects.

10. Barrel Theory: If the planks composing a barrel are of uneven lengths, the amount of water the barrel can hold does not depend on the longest plank but rather on the shortest one.

11. Matthew Effect: There is a famous saying in the Bible’s Gospel of Matthew: "To everyone who has, more will be given, and he will have abundance. But from the one who has nothing, even what little he has will be taken away."

Social scientists derived the concept of the "Matthew Effect" from this, describing the widespread polarization phenomenon in various aspects of social life.

1. Birdcage Logic

Hang a beautiful birdcage in the most visible place in the room, and within a few days, the owner will surely make one of the following two choices: throw the birdcage away or buy a bird to put inside it. This is called the Birdcage Logic. The process is simple. Imagine you are the owner of the room. Whenever someone walks in, sees the birdcage, they cannot help but ask you: "Where's the bird? Is it dead?" When you answer: "I've never kept a bird." People will ask: "Then why do you want a birdcage?" Eventually, you will have to choose one of the two options because it is easier than endless explanations. The reason behind the Birdcage Logic is simple: people generally think in a habitual way most of the time. Thus, it is evident how important it is to cultivate logical thinking in life and work.

2. Broken Window Effect

In psychological research, there is a phenomenon called the "Broken Window Effect," which means that if a house has a broken window and no one fixes it, soon other windows will mysteriously be broken; a wall, if there are graffiti that are not cleaned up, will soon be covered with messy and unsightly things. In a clean place, people hesitate to litter, but once there is trash on the ground, people will throw garbage without hesitation, feeling no shame. This is a strange phenomenon.

Psychologists study the "tipping point," how dirty the ground needs to be before people think, "Since it's already dirty, a bit more dirt doesn't matter." How bad does the situation need to get before people give up and let it deteriorate completely?

Any bad behavior, if not stopped at the beginning, forms a trend that is hard to change, like a levee where a small gap not repaired in time can lead to a dam burst, causing millions of times the loss.

Crime is actually the result of disorder. In the 1980s, New York City was rampant with robbery and murder everywhere and every day. Walking on the streets in broad daylight was frightening. Subways were even worse, with dirty carriages covered in obscene graffiti. Sitting in the subway, everyone felt endangered. Although I wasn't robbed, a professor was hit on the head in broad daylight, lost his eyesight, and ended his research career, making me fear talking about it for years and unwilling to attend conferences alone in New York. Recently, New York's appearance and reputation have improved significantly, surprising me. A city that had declined could revive and improve.

So, when I attended a conference and met a criminologist, I immediately asked him for advice. It turned out that New York used the Broken Window Effect theory from textbooks, first improving the crime environment so people were less likely to commit crimes, then gradually catching criminals and restoring order.

Although this approach was criticized as slow and ineffective ("The ship is sinking, yet they're cleaning the deck"), New York started by keeping subway carriages clean and publicly handcuffed ticket evaders, lining them up on the platform to show the public the government's determination to reform. The results were found to be very effective.

Police discovered that people indeed committed fewer crimes in clean environments, and catching fare evaders proved rewarding because one out of seven evaders was a wanted criminal, and one out of twenty carried weapons. Therefore, police were willing to seriously catch fare evaders, making criminals afraid to evade fares, carry weapons, or risk losing more than they gain. Thus, New York broke the crime chain by starting with the smallest and easiest measures, preventing the vicious cycle from continuing.

3. Diffusion of Responsibility Effect

On March 13, 1964, at 3:20 AM, outside an apartment in the suburbs of New York, USA, a young woman named Kitty Genovese was stabbed on her way home from a bar. As she hopelessly shouted: "Someone's trying to kill me! Help! Help!" nearby residents turned on their lights and opened their windows, scaring the murderer away. After everything calmed down, the murderer returned and attacked again. When she shouted again, the nearby residents turned on their lights again, and the murderer fled. Thinking she was safe, as she went upstairs to her apartment, the murderer appeared again and killed her on the stairs. During this process, despite her loud cries for help, at least 38 neighbors watched from their windows, but none came to her rescue or even called the police. This incident caused a stir in New York society and drew attention from social psychologists. People refer to this phenomenon of many bystanders failing to help as the Diffusion of Responsibility Effect.

Regarding the reasons for the formation of the Diffusion of Responsibility Effect, psychologists conducted extensive experiments and surveys, finding that this phenomenon cannot simply be attributed to people's cruelty or declining morality. In different situations, people's helping behaviors indeed vary. When a person encounters an emergency, if only they can provide help, they will clearly recognize their responsibility and assist the victim. If they don't help, they would feel guilty, which comes at a high psychological cost. However, if there are many people present, the responsibility to help the person in distress is shared among everyone, leading to the diffusion of responsibility. Each person bears a small portion of the responsibility, and bystanders may not even be aware of their own share, resulting in a mindset of "I won't help, let others help," creating a situation of "collective indifference." How to break this situation is an important topic that psychologists are studying.

4. Parkinson's Law

British historian C. Northcote Parkinson conducted long-term research and wrote a book called "Parkinson's Law." In the book, he explained the reasons and consequences of organizational personnel expansion: An incompetent official has three possible ways out: First, apply for resignation and let capable people take over; second, let a capable person assist in their work; third, hire two subordinates who are less competent than themselves. The first path is absolutely unacceptable because it would mean losing many rights; the second path is also unacceptable because that capable person would become a rival; therefore, the third path seems most appropriate. Thus, two mediocre assistants share the work, allowing the official to remain superior, issuing orders from above, without posing a threat to their authority. Since these two assistants are incompetent, they imitate their superior and hire even less competent assistants. This continues, forming a bloated, inefficient leadership system filled with bureaucracy and inefficiency.

5. Halo Effect

Russian literary giant Alexander Pushkin suffered greatly due to the halo effect. He passionately fell in love with Natalia, known as the "first beauty of Moscow," and married her. Natalia was stunningly beautiful but incompatible with Pushkin. Whenever Pushkin read his poems to her, she always covered her ears and said, "Don't listen! Don't listen!" Instead, she always demanded that Pushkin accompany her to entertainment venues, attend lavish parties, and balls. Pushkin abandoned his writing, accumulated debts, and eventually died in a duel for her sake, prematurely extinguishing a literary star. In Pushkin's view, a beautiful woman must also possess extraordinary intelligence and noble character, but reality did not match this perception, demonstrating the halo effect.

The halo effect refers to a cognitive obstacle in interpersonal interactions where one characteristic overshadows others. In daily life, the "halo effect" quietly influences our understanding and evaluation of others. For example, some elderly people see individual flaws in the young, or disapprove of their clothing or habits, concluding they lack potential. Conversely, some young people admire a friend's charm and perceive them as entirely charming, truly embodying the phrase "one strength conceals a hundred flaws." The halo effect is a subjective psychological assumption based on partial information. Its errors lie in: First, it easily focuses on individual traits and generalizes them to the whole, akin to blind men touching an elephant and mistaking the part for the whole. Second, it connects unrelated personal characteristics or appearances, assuming one feature implies another. Third, it fully affirms or denies everything based on a single impression, reflecting a tendency towards absolutism driven by subjective bias. In summary, the halo effect is a significant cognitive obstacle in interpersonal interactions, and we should strive to avoid and overcome its negative effects in our interactions.

6. Hawthorne Effect (Hawthorne effect)

An experimenter effect in psychology. In the 1920s-30s, American researchers conducted experiments at the Hawthorne plant of Western Electric Company in Chicago to explore the relationship between working conditions, social factors, and production efficiency, discovering the experimenter effect known as the Hawthorne effect.

The first phase of the experiment began in November 1924, examining the relationship between working conditions and production efficiency, setting up experimental and control groups. Regardless of whether lighting was increased or controlled, the output of the experimental group increased, and even the control group with unchanged lighting saw an increase. Additionally, experiments on wage incentives, rest breaks, daily work duration, and weekly workdays showed no direct impact of these conditions on production efficiency. The second phase of the experiment was led by Harvard University professor Mayo, focusing on the relationship between social factors and production efficiency. It was found that the improvement in production efficiency was mainly due to significant changes in the mental state of the participants. Participants were placed in a dedicated laboratory under researcher supervision, changing their social circumstances and receiving attention from all sides, forming a sense of participation, feeling they were an important part of the company, motivating workers socially and increasing output.

This effect tells us that when students or ourselves receive public attention or observation, learning and interaction efficiency significantly increases. Therefore, in daily life, we should learn to get along well with others, understand what behaviors classmates and teachers accept and appreciate, and continuously improve our good behaviors in life and study to gain more attention and appreciation, thereby promoting our studies and building self-confidence!

7. Learned Helplessness Experiment

Learned helplessness was initially discovered by Seligman and Maier and later extensively explored in animal and human studies. Simply put, many experiments show that dogs can cross barriers or perform other behaviors to escape shocks administered by experimenters. However, if the dog previously experienced unpredictable (not knowing when they would occur) and uncontrollable shocks (such as shocks stopping independently of the dog's behavior), when the dog later has the opportunity to escape the shock, they become unable to escape. Moreover, the dog exhibits other deficiencies, such as depression and reduced initiative.

The reason dogs exhibit this condition is due to learning helplessness early in the experiment. That is, they realize that no matter what they do, they cannot control the termination of the shock. In each experiment, the termination of the shock is always under the experimenter's control, and the dog learns that it cannot change this external control, thus acquiring a sense of helplessness.

If humans experience learned helplessness, it becomes a deep despair and sadness. Therefore, in learning and life, we should broaden our perspectives, see the true determining factors behind events, and avoid falling into despair.

8. Witness Memory

Witnesses, in our understanding, typically provide objective evidence—people who truthfully recount what they personally see and hear. However, psychological research shows that much of the testimony provided by witnesses is inaccurate or biased, carrying personal views and consciousness.

The confidence a witness has in their testimony does not determine its accuracy—a surprising research result. Psychologists Perfect and Hollins decided to conduct further research on this conclusion. To examine whether there is anything special about witness testimony, they compared witness memory with general knowledge memory.

They had participants watch a short video about a kidnapping case. The next day, they answered questions about the content of the video and rated their confidence in their answers, followed by a recognition memory test. Then, using the same method, they selected general knowledge questions from encyclopedias and popular literature.

Like previous findings, Perfect and Hollins also discovered that those confident in their answers were not necessarily more accurate in recalling witness memories than those lacking confidence. However, for general knowledge, the opposite was true—those with high confidence recalled better than those with low confidence.
