Smart Education - A Detailed Explanation of the SSAT Score Percentage

If you want to enter an excellent American private high school, there's no doubt that the SSAT is the most powerful "stepping stone." The SSAT (Secondary School Admission Test) is a very important entrance exam for private middle schools in the U.S., Canada, and other countries. It is also known as the American Middle School Entrance Exam. Private schools in the U.S., Canada, and other countries choose to use it on their own. The SSAT includes four major parts: verbal, mathematics, reading, and essay writing. The essay section is not scored (some schools may refer to this score during admissions), while the rest are multiple-choice questions. For each correct answer, you get 1 point; for each incorrect answer, you lose 1/4 of a point; and for unanswered questions, you receive 0 points. Since your score only correlates with the number of correct answers and incorrect answers, knowing when to appropriately skip questions is also a form of wisdom. The advanced SSAT is aimed at students in grades 8-11, with each section's scaled score ranging from 500 to 800 points. The lowest total score is 1500 (i.e., 1500 is the base score), and the highest score is 2400.

So, does a higher SSAT score mean better results? The answer is: Not necessarily. In fact, there is one item on the SSAT score report called the "SSAT percentile," which is the key factor that American high schools consider when admitting students. So, what exactly is the SSAT percentile? What does it signify? Let’s take a look at a student's score report:

What Does My SSAT Percentile Mean?

Your SSAT percentile is a score between 1 and 99. Your score compares your performance on the test to the performances of other students of the same grade and gender who have taken the SSAT within the past three years. For example, if seventh-grader Sue Smith received a 65 SSAT percentile, she performed as well as or better than 65% of the other 7th-grade girls who took the SSAT in the past three years.

The SSAT percentile actually represents where the student stands compared to all other candidates of the same age and gender globally over the past three years. The higher the percentage, the more outstanding the student (ranging from 1%-99%). As shown in this score report, some might think that scoring over 2000 is a good result, but in reality, her SSAT percentile is only 48%, meaning that compared to candidates of the same age and gender over the past three years, her score only ranks ahead of 48% of them. Similarly, a score of 2000 by an 8th-grade (male/female) student and a 10th-grade (male/female) student could result in vastly different percentiles. For top-notch American private high schools, the SSAT percentile is generally required to be no less than 60%, and some even require it to be no less than 85%. Conversely, sometimes a student might have a total score of only 1900, yet their SSAT percentile can be as high as over 70%. Therefore, don't let the total score make you either overly proud or disheartened. Instead, check your SSAT percentile before making decisions about school selection.

SSAT Exam Precautions:

1. Standard Exam Fee: $215 (Mastercard / VISA), no refunds.

2. Exam Time: 9:00 AM - 12:15 PM (must sign in half an hour earlier and be assigned a seat by the exam center teacher).

3. Score Report: Free delivery to the candidate's home (requires waiting approximately 10 business days after the scores are released). Alternatively, pay an additional $20 to have the exam center email the scores to the registered email address, or pay an additional $36 to have the scores delivered via FedEx to your home. Note: Unlike TOEFL, SSAT scores are not published online, so anxious candidates might choose one of these two options.

Additionally, the SSAT exam center can send your scores to ten schools for free. You can add or cancel schools either before or after the exam, but you must complete modifications by 5:00 PM on the first Tuesday after the exam (based on Eastern Standard Time (EST)).

4. Items to Bring: Original passport, one copy of the passport (the page with the photo), two identification photos, two #2 pencils, an eraser, and drinking water.
