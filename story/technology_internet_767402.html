Evan Williams, co-founder of Twitter, gives new signposts to entrepreneurs

by anonymous on 2013-10-15 20:59:55

Another important figure has spoken about the essence of the Internet:

The Internet is not an endless magical world; it is merely another engine to improve the quality of life.

The speaker is Evan Williams, co-founder of Twitter. He has recently garnered attention because his 56 million shares in Twitter will soon be worth over a billion dollars.

Evan Williams is the founder or co-founder of several renowned Silicon Valley companies. In 1999, he co-founded Blogger (originally Pyra Labs, sold to Google in 2003), created the podcast platform Odeo in 2004, co-founded Twitter in 2006, and founded the Medium blogging network in 2012. Now, Williams has left Twitter and is still operating Odeo (though under significant pressure from iTunes), and has just established the Medium company.

In understanding the "essence of the Internet," he delivered the aforementioned speech at XOXO 2013 held in Poland at the end of September. He also made more profound statements: The Internet is a massive machine designed to meet people's needs. It is not utopia, nor magic; it is simply a simple engine that provides convenience. Those who can fully adapt to this engine—solving basic human problems faster and more conveniently than predecessors—can immediately create profits. Those who ignore fundamental human needs and pursue fanciful ideas may encounter difficulties.

Since we have yet to find the video of Williams' speech at the XOXO event, we can only reconstruct it from media reports. According to GetGuidance, Williams' speech was divided into five main parts:

The Internet can do two things: do new things, and do existing things better.

People want to solve the problems they frequently encounter.

The Internet can help people solve existing problems in completely different ways and more conveniently.

The principle of convenience has two key elements: helping us solve problems faster and with less effort.

The added "convenience" in solving problems is valuable.

According to Wired, Williams elaborated further on convenience: If you want to build a billion-dollar internet company, you should fulfill people's desires, preferably a desire that has existed for a long time... After identifying the desire, use modern technology to achieve it.

Williams also pointed out sharply that by utilizing convenience well, allowing people not to wait or think, value can be created, as we often believe that the Internet allows us to engage in new activities. But people actually just want to pursue what they have always been pursuing.

Williams' conclusion might seem dull, seemingly undermining the value of "innovation." However, in reality, looking at Apple, Facebook, Amazon, Microsoft (Windows), or domestic companies like Tencent, Alibaba, Baidu, 360, Xiaomi, etc., most have achieved their current success by simplifying originally complex processes.

No wonder Wired magazine commented on Williams' speech: This speech could serve as a signpost, guiding the eager young generation of Silicon Valley. (It’s a speech that should serve as a signpost, a bit of much-needed direction for the Valley’s younger generation.)

In Wired's report on Williams' speech, I noticed his elaboration on the "connection between humans and machines": Hardware is interconnected, and these interactions all involve data and software. A quick study of the giants in the internet industry reveals that they are involved in many connections. "Following" is a connection, and "liking" is also a connection.

Last year, when we reported on the Meitu team, we coined the term "micro-link" (Welink) to explore the significance of WeChat in the Internet of Things: Perhaps, in the mobile internet field everyone is exploring, WeChat could redefine the oft-discussed concepts of the Internet of Things, LBS, and O2O. Its presented interconnected form does not replicate hyperlinks but instead creates micro-links that connect real relationships, connect all objects, and become a human-machine interaction interface.

Zhang Xiaolong proposed at the WeChat Forum during Tencent's Partner Conference in July this year, "to solve communication issues between people and enterprises, and between people and devices." In fact, after an exclusive interview with the WeChat team not long ago, I learned that Zhang Xiaolong had responded to the "micro-link" perspective: Eventually, the Internet of Things will arrive, and WeChat doesn't just connect people; it can also connect internet-capable machines. Each machine has a QR code as its device ID, and through WeChat, you can converse with the device to control it.

Like Williams' remarks, those at the forefront of the internet or mobile internet are all thinking about the "convenience of connections." And this, is the essence of the internet. Mobile internet strengthens connections, making them more diverse, but the essence remains unchanged.
