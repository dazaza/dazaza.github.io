The Hard-to-Do Taobao Business: Small Sellers Can't Survive, Medium-sized Sellers Can't Make Money

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 14:03:45

Author: Yang Yang

On July 17th, dozens of Taobao sellers held a two-day closed-door meeting at a hotel on Wener Road in Hangzhou. These sellers do not want to rely solely on discounts to increase their income.

They split into several groups to discuss the question: "If in the future we don't depend on discount promotions, will there be new selling points that can maintain sufficient traffic?" They attempted to attract buyers with new scenarios and packages, such as offering all the equipment needed for a trip to Hawaii — straw hats, beach skirts, sunglasses... even condoms, or providing a full year of custom services, sending sanitary napkins monthly to female customers who purchased the package.

Zhan Lu, General Manager of the Seller Operations Division of Taobao, aims to guide these sellers who no longer wish to increase their income through discounts towards finding a new approach.

Zhan Lu stated that they are attempting to establish an increasingly autonomous set of rules without reliance on 'Taobao Xiaoer' (Taobao's customer service representatives). Taobao is responsible for establishing systems and the 'rules for setting rules', providing tools and opening data, while everything else is left to the sellers to handle.

In the words of Yu Yan, the person in charge of Taobao, the project that Taobao plans to undertake in 2013 is described as 'losing control' — they have decided not to hold onto all the traffic, rules, and judgments within Taobao itself but instead let the market make its own determinations.

From the surface, it seems like Taobao is doing something akin to 'suicide' — the new policy will allow merchants to no longer depend on heavy advertising placements, promotions, or spending large sums on SEO optimization. The first two items are important sources of Taobao's revenue.

But Taobao still needs to change. The managers of the Taobao marketplace are planning another major adjustment to the game rules, which will impact the entire seller ecosystem, redefine 'Taobao Xiaoer', and recast the information channel between sellers and buyers. This plan may very well be implemented in September this year.

Hard Business

An increasing number of people believe that doing business on Taobao is becoming difficult: small sellers complain about struggling to survive, while medium-sized sellers lament not making money.

The Economic Observer randomly surveyed over 100 small and medium-sized sellers on Taobao. From the results of 75 valid questionnaires, it was found that more than 90% of the sellers had been operating their online stores for less than three years, with 60% having been in operation for less than two years and nearly 30% for less than one year. This indicates that most Taobao sellers are new operators who need time to build credibility and performance. Additionally, in the survey, about half of the small sellers had teams of fewer than three people, over 30% of the medium and small sellers had teams of four to six people, and the remaining 20% had more than eight members.

Zhang Ruchen's online store is a family workshop, with only her father and mother as 'employees', earning an average monthly income of less than 3,000 yuan. Her biggest challenge is the relentless price competition: wave after wave of university graduates come to Taobao to start businesses, using their parents' hard-earned money to buy 'good reviews'. After about a year, when their money runs out, they exit Taobao, but by then, a new batch of entrepreneurs arrives, starting again by burning money to buy 'good reviews'.

Manager Lu's company currently has fewer than 30 employees, with total revenue in 2012 amounting to less than 20 million yuan, and still no profit. The markup rate for a piece of clothing above cost is 2-3 times, with industry average promotion costs being 10% to 15%, plus rent, utilities, employee salaries, and various other expenses... If inventory cannot be controlled below 10%, it absolutely means losses.

"We just endure; a grassroots self-owned brand company cannot achieve large-scale profitability unless its annual turnover exceeds 30 million yuan," Manager Lu said. His company paid several million yuan in tuition fees over the past few years, learning that mastering the supply chain isn't something that can be learned overnight. 90% of clothing companies fail due to inventory issues — of course, this may not be Taobao's fault.

However, this brings up a paradox in e-commerce: merchants can only reduce average costs by placing large orders and maintaining large inventories, but this comes with significant inventory risk: "Even gods find it hard to predict sales volumes." Therefore, startups can only produce in small batches and multiple times. However, this leads to high costs and lack of competitiveness.

Despite not having the pressure of storefront rents, without promotion, sellers cannot expect good income. Fierce price competition, endless discounts, and necessary promotional expenses result in extremely low profitability for online stores.

In the Economic Observer's random survey, only 18.67% of respondents indicated that their profits increased within six months, 38.67% reported stable profits, and 42.67% of the shops claimed their profits were declining. This makes 57.33% of sellers believe that the competitive environment is already "quite intense," while 29.33% openly state it is "too intense."

Of course, it is impossible to ignore that the number of sellers on Taobao has reached millions.

Taobao provided data to show that the situation is not as bad: sellers with annual transaction amounts below 100,000 yuan, between 100,000 and 1,000,000 yuan, and above 1,000,000 yuan have all seen varying degrees of growth. By June this year, sellers with annual transaction amounts below 100,000 yuan increased by 60%, those between 100,000 and 1,000,000 yuan increased by 30%, and those above 1,000,000 yuan increased by 33%.

In May 2012, the number of online stores on Taobao was 5,964,460. After the redesign, the number of stores disappeared permanently. This newspaper learned that the current number is around 8 million.

Ma Yun emphasized two points at the NetBusiness Conference in 2012: first, Taobao should reduce intermediary links and allow sellers to communicate directly with buyers; second, cultivate one million sellers with annual turnovers of 1,000,000 yuan.

Is Taobao still a paradise for entrepreneurs? In the Economic Observer's random survey, 29.33% of sellers think "yes," 26.67% think "no," and 44% think "it's hard to say."

So, what is Taobao now?

Change

China's internet has long followed a certain logic: where there is traffic, there is business; without traffic, you're forgotten by the world.

The opportunity for products to be displayed on Taobao comes from purchasing page-right side advertisements, optimizing so-called SEO to rank higher in search results, and buying ad spaces to gain traffic — the rules of online business have become fixed as a competition for resource acquisition and traffic generation.

In the Economic Observer's survey, 40% of online stores spent between 10%-30% of their total costs on promotions, 22.67% spent between 30% to 50%, and even 5.33% spent between 50% to 75%. Only 33% of stores spent less than 10% of their costs on promotions.

Manager Lu's experience is that money-burning activities must be avoided, such as advertising on the Taobao homepage.

Although the ROI (conversion rate) for advertising on Taobao might be the highest in the entire e-commerce sector, some merchants feel that the price of 'Direct Express' (a keyword bidding system) is continuously rising while the effect is getting worse. Spending money on 'hot keywords' doesn't necessarily bring better business. And Yu Yan also saw complaints on Weibo about how 'business is tough, Taobao is now a platform of fierce competition,' but Taobao's data made her think 'it's not like that': those shouting about not making money outside are the ones who haven't earned, while those who have earned keep quiet and make big money silently. In this manager's eyes, what needs solving is the merchants' mindset: originally, with fewer shops, some merchants could earn money effortlessly, but now with more merchants, traffic has become a scarce resource, and buyer behavior has changed. If merchants cannot adapt accordingly, doing business will become increasingly difficult.

How to detect changes? Search is the barometer of buyer behavior changes.

According to Yu Yan, buyer behavior has significantly changed from 2011 to 2013. For example, in May 2013, the contribution of long-tail keyword searches to PV (page views) increased by 9.4% year-on-year, while the search volume for high-frequency keywords and hot keywords decreased by 6.4%. Additionally, the proportion of low-frequency keywords contributing to the overall guiding PV rose by 3.5% — this means buyers clearly know what they need. At this point, sellers need to make changes: search volume is moving towards long-tail development and won't concentrate on just a few head terms. If sellers don't change their keyword ads, their business will naturally not be as good as before.

In fact, Taobao's managers have long been reflecting on whether past mechanisms of buying search traffic and ad space benefit Taobao's future. Their answer is obviously negative — traffic based on money competition only belongs to a small portion of merchants, and some products will never get display opportunities.

Thus, Taobao's managers have been trying to establish a new order to give Taobao merchants a fair chance to showcase their products. This opportunity does not come from throwing money, but rather from defining product "tags" themselves — allowing buyers to directly find the seller's product through searching tags, without needing to compete for capital to acquire traffic.

For instance, a shop selling bras labels small-size bras with the tag "small chest also gathers," and girls with the "airport" body type can search for "small chest" or "small chest also gathers" to see the product appear in front of them without needing to buy ads.

Step Back, Then Disrupt

When Taobao was established, it followed eBay's auction mechanism, sorting according to the listing and delisting times of goods. Later, around 2006, the emergence of search triggered a major transformation in Taobao's entire ecosystem, disrupting the previous order and presenting goods according to search content. Meanwhile, another traditional way of presentation was category-based, which has continued until today. Subcategories under categories, and subcategories under subcategories... Currently, Taobao has approximately 20,000 categories.

In the second period, Taobao sellers basically relied on traffic as the foundation of commerce — referred to internally as "hunter-type sellers," who would obtain traffic through various means and sell goods.

This time, Taobao is preparing for its third major transformation, whose core is to create SNS (social networking services), establishing capillaries diffusing outward from each merchant. At this point, if a merchant acquires traffic, they no longer simply sell goods but focus on identifying buyers most suitable for the store and retaining them. Taobao attempts to enable these merchants' businesses to grow not by acquiring new users but by relying on old customers. In this period, traffic is no longer absolutely crucial; the efficiency of using traffic is what matters.

Yu Yan says "the game has changed in 2013." Zhan Lu uses "unleashing" and "enable" (note: enable means "empower, make able") to describe the new policies Taobao is about to launch: allowing sellers to directly find new and old buyers, freeing them from concerns about "store location" (i.e., traffic). Sellers themselves can attract new users, retain old users, and buyers can smoothly find products, improving shopping efficiency.

Taobao's managers aim to achieve this intention through personalized search, recommendation engines, and big data: they try to let buyers see their favorite stores ranked higher in search results, and the "My Taobao" page heavily promotes new items from stores users have favorited, regardless of whether they are crown stores. Taobao also plans to guide sellers to promote via Weibo, WeChat, forums, etc. For example, Ji Bingjian of Hongwei Fang and Diaoye Niu Rou never use Taobao Direct Express ads, yet their businesses remain prosperous. There are also countless characteristic small shops passed down by word of mouth.

More big data tools and a "better environment" may be opened to Taobao sellers — this stems from some observations by managers like Zhan Lu: some familiar shops suddenly changed their names to something like "Tribute to Xiaomi Bugs." This made them reflect: why would merchants abandon their names for business? Managers believe it's because they didn't provide a way for buyers to remember sellers through their shop names.

Therefore, Taobao will launch "Taobao Brand Names," giving each seller one chance to rename themselves, ensuring no name duplication. Taobao will establish channels to enter shops through shop names and tools for connecting buyers and sellers based on Taobao Brand Names. Yu Yan calls this "possibly the most fundamental and basic thing for sellers to settle down and thrive."

Additionally, there will be intellectual property protection against image theft, more data-driven real-time marketing for old customers, personalized search, classification of goods by tag systems, understanding consumer interests and turning consumers into fans, thoroughly connecting sellers to influence and reach buyers through platforms like Micro Taobao, Weibo, shopping carts, etc.

And the next "model room" concept mentioned by Yu Yan becomes even more "mysterious" — previously, sellers often agonized over which category to place a product in to gain more traffic. Yu Yan is attempting to free sellers from such traffic dilemmas. She hopes that the future Taobao will be like IKEA, where all products are stored in fixed places in the warehouse, but consumers choose products by visiting model rooms, and finally retrieve their purchases from the warehouse based on item numbers.

Thus, the seller's task simplifies to: figuring out how to build their own "model room," whether by building it themselves, collaborating with others, hiring someone to build it, or following Taobao's guidance. Ultimately, consumers recognize the product through the model room, freeing sellers from the problem of competing for traffic resources across categories.

How much advertising revenue will Taobao lose as a result? And how much new revenue will emerge? All of this remains unknown.

The current Alibaba Group is the most profitable company — according to figures disclosed by Yahoo during an analyst call, Alibaba Group's net profit in the first quarter of 2013 was $669 million, compared to $220 million in the same period last year, representing a threefold increase.

According to insiders at Taobao, if Taobao wants to move forward, it must retain both buyers and sellers, and this must be done. Once successful, Taobao will enter a new phase of rapid development with no bottlenecks.

Of course, the premise is that Taobao merchants quickly learn to use the new rules, including the new tools.
