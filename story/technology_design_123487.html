● Plot Introduction

The movie "Chi Bi" begins with the battle of Changbanpo. After defeating Liu Bei, Cao Cao considered Eastern Wu as a threat to his ambition of ruling the whole world. Moreover, because his beloved Xiaoqiao refused to yield and chose death instead, he became furious and decided to attack Eastern Wu.

Sun Quan sent Lu Su to meet with Liu Bei under the pretense of mourning Liu Biao, discussing the possibility of uniting against Cao Cao. After Liu Bei agreed to cooperate with Eastern Wu, he sent Zhuge Liang to Eastern Wu.

Sun Quan's sister Sun Shangxiang and Lu Su used psychological tactics to strengthen Sun Quan's determination to resist Cao Cao, recalling Zhou Yu to lead the resistance.

Ultimately, both armies met at Chi Bi. Under Huang Gai's fake surrender strategy, Cao Cao suffered a great defeat and retreated along the Huarong Road back to Jiangling.

====================================================

The Battle of Red Cliffs was one of the famous battles in Chinese history where the weaker side achieved victory over the stronger side. In AD 208 (the thirteenth year of Jian'an during Emperor Xian of Han), Cao Cao led a large naval and land army, claiming a force of a million men, initiating the Jingzhou campaign, then attacking Sun Quan.

In July, Cao Cao personally led more than ten thousand troops southward to Jingzhou, described by Wang Fuzhi in "Reading Comprehensive Mirror for Aid in Government" as "taking advantage of the momentum of defeating Yuan Shao to subdue Jingzhou and Wu". He aimed to eliminate Liu Biao and Sun Quan of Jiangdong in one fell swoop, unifying the world. Just as Cao Cao was advancing rapidly toward Wan and Ye, Liu Biao passed away in August. In September, Liu Biao's second son Liu Cong surrendered without a fight in Xinye (now part of Henan Province). The thousands of ships from Jingzhou's navy, including many warships, all fell into Cao Cao's hands, greatly increasing his naval capabilities. Liu Bei, who had been stationed in Fancheng (now Xiangfan in Hubei Province) under Liu Biao's protection, heard the news and began retreating southward.

At that time, Jiangling (now Jiangling in Hubei Province) stored a large amount of grain and weapons belonging to Liu Biao. Therefore, Liu Bei retreated from Fancheng towards Jiangling on one hand, and ordered Guan Yu to lead the navy down the Han River to rendezvous at Jiangling on the other hand. In September, Cao Cao personally led five thousand elite cavalry, including his personal tiger and leopard guards, pursuing Liu Bei. After defeating Liu Bei's forces at Changbanpo in Dangyang (northeast of today's Dangyang in Hubei), they occupied Jiangling.

After arriving at Jiangling, Cao Cao immediately took measures to settle the local officials and civilians, issuing an order stating "to give the people of Jingzhou a new start."

Jia Xu advised Cao Cao to first utilize the resources of Jingzhou, rest and recuperate his troops, and stabilize the newly acquired territory, but Cao Cao did not heed his advice. Cao Cao's military deployment was roughly as follows: appointing Zhao Yan as the governor of Zhangling (county seat located southeast of present-day ZaoYang in Hubei) and commander-in-chief of the seven armies, unified command of their actions between the Han and Huai rivers. Regarding Jingzhou, Cao Cao stationed Cao Ren and Xiahou Yuan in Jiangling, Cao Hong in Xiangyang, and dispatched a combined naval and land force from Xiangyang southward towards Xiakou. Cao Cao personally led a contingent along with the newly attached Jingzhou forces downstream, attacking Eastern Wu.

When Cao Cao moved southward, Sun Quan sent Lu Su as an envoy to Jingzhou, mourning Liu Biao's death and persuading Liu Bei to join forces against Cao Cao. Lu Su met with Liu Bei at Dangyang. Later, Liu Bei followed his plan and retreated southeast towards the Han River direction, meeting up with Guan Yu's navy descending from the Han River. They joined forces with Liu Qi, the eldest son of Liu Biao and governor of Jiangxia with more than ten thousand troops, retreating to Xiakou (now Hankou in Wuhan, Hubei), planning to unite with Sun Quan to resist Cao Cao. Liu Bei sent Zhuge Liang to accompany Lu Su to Eastern Wu to discuss strategies against Cao Cao.

Zhuge Liang arrived at Chaisang (southwest of Jiujiang, Jiangxi), where Sun Quan had already received a threatening letter from Cao Cao, saying: "Now commanding a water army of eight hundred thousand men, I am about to hunt with you in Wu." Sun Quan did not want to subject "all the lands of Wu and ten thousand troops" to Cao Cao, yet he was also concerned that the combined forces of Sun and Liu might not be able to match Cao Cao. Among Sun Quan's subordinates, there were intense debates between the pro-war faction represented by Lu Su and the pro-peace faction led by Zhang Zhao. Zhuge Liang analyzed the pros and cons for Sun Quan: "Although the forces of Yuzhou (Liu Bei) were defeated at Changban, now the warriors returning and Guan Yu's navy have ten thousand elite soldiers, and Liu Qi's combined forces in Jiangxia are no less than ten thousand." He pointed out (1) Cao Cao's army was exhausted from long campaigns, the soldiers were fatigued; (2) northerners "are not accustomed to naval warfare"; (3) the people of Jingzhou have not yet fully submitted to Cao Cao. Zhuge Liang concluded that if Sun and Liu united, they would surely win.

Lu Su then employed psychological tactics to persuade Sun Quan, while simultaneously urging Sun Quan to immediately recall Zhou Yu from Poyang (northeast of present-day Boyang in Jiangxi). Zhou Yu supported the opinions of Zhuge Liang and Lu Su, analyzing the weaknesses of Cao Cao's army, pointing out: first, Cao Cao's army was extremely fatigued; second, "now it is very cold, and the horses lack grass"; third, Ma Chao and Han Sui still remained in Guanzhong, posing a threat to Cao Cao's rear.

Sun Quan gained confidence in the success of uniting with Liu Bei to resist Cao Cao, appointing Zhou Yu and Cheng Pu as left and right grand commanders, and Lu Su as assistant grand commander. Zhou Yu led three ten-thousand elite troops upstream to Xiakou, joining Liu Bei's two ten-thousand plus troops, jointly resisting Cao Cao.

Defeat at Wulin: Zhou Yu's forces met with Liu Bei's forces at Fan Kou. Then they advanced upstream, reaching Chi Bi, where they encountered Cao Cao's forces. Cao Cao's newly reorganized and newly attached Jingzhou naval forces lacked combat strength, and were also affected by an epidemic, resulting in initial disadvantages. Cao Cao had no choice but to "withdraw his troops to the north bank of the river", anchoring his warships on the northern shore near Wulin. Zhou Yu anchored his warships on the southern shore near Chi Bi, facing each other.

Northern troops were not accustomed to life on boats, so Cao Cao ordered the warships to be chained together with iron chains to reduce the rocking caused by waves. Zhou Yu's general Huang Gai suggested using fire attack tactics to defeat Cao Cao's army: "Now the enemy is numerous and we are few, making it difficult to sustain a prolonged battle. However, observing Cao Cao's fleet lined up head to tail, we can burn them and drive them away." Zhou Yu adopted Huang Gai's suggestion and decided to let Huang Gai pretend to surrender to approach Cao Cao's fleet. Huang Gai prepared ten warships filled with dry grass and oil, disguised with curtains outside, flying flags agreed upon with Cao Cao.

It was late autumn, usually windy from the north, but according to meteorological patterns, after several days of severe cold, warmer weather often appears, and the wind direction may change to east or south. It is said that on November 12th, Ji Zi day (equivalent to December 7th, AD 208), the sky was clear and warm, and the south wind rose in the evening, becoming strong by midnight. Huang Gai set sail with the prepared ships, sailing towards Cao Cao's ships with the wind. When they reached the middle of the river, Huang Gai lit torches and ordered the soldiers to shout loudly: "We surrender!" The Cao Cao soldiers were completely unprepared, "all craning their necks to watch, pointing and saying Huang Gai has surrendered." When they were about two li away from Cao Cao's army, Huang Gai ordered the straw to be ignited, setting fires simultaneously. With fierce winds and flames, the ships sped forward like arrows, burning all the northern ships and spreading to the camps on the shore. Soon, "smoke and flames covered the sky, and a large number of men and horses were burned or drowned."

On the south bank, the main fleet of the Sun army advanced with drums beating; meanwhile, Liu Bei also moved from Shu Mountain towards Wulin. The allied forces crossed the Yangtze River, dealing a heavy blow to Cao Cao's army. Seeing the situation was beyond saving, Cao Cao immediately burned the remaining ships and retreated. Zhou Yu and Liu Bei's armies pursued by land and water. Cao Cao retreated through the Huarong small path (north of present-day Jianli, Hubei) towards Jiangling, suffering great losses due to plague and hunger. Thanks to reinforcements from Zhang Liao and Xu Chu, Cao Cao managed to escape. Upon reaching the city of Jiangling, fearing instability in the rear, Cao Cao returned to the north, leaving Cao Ren and Xu Huang to defend Jiangling, and Man Chong stationed in Dangyang.

Consequences: Before the Battle of Red Cliffs, Cao Cao had significant advantages: first, Cao Cao "held the emperor hostage to issue orders to the vassals," making it difficult for the vassals to compete morally; second, Cao Cao's victorious army moving southward was naturally full of spirit; third, Cao Cao's forces were several times larger than those of Sun and Liu. Given Cao Cao's such overwhelming advantages, the achievements of the alliance between Sun and Liu were even more brilliant. This battle showcased the strengths of Sun and Liu's naval warfare, skillfully employing fire attacks, which can be considered a decisive and famous example of winning with fewer numbers in Chinese history. After the Battle of Red Cliffs, Cao Cao retreated to the north, never again having the opportunity to invade Jingzhou on such a large scale. The Cao Cao group also lost the possibility of unifying the country in a short period of time.

Zhou Yu and Cao Ren faced each other across the river. Gan Ning captured Yiling (now Yichang, Hubei), and Cao Ren surrounded him. Zhou Yu led reinforcements, severely defeating Cao Ren's army. Liu Bei returned to Xiakou, intending to advance up the Han River to flank Cao Ren's rear. The following year, Cao Ren was forced to retreat. Sun Quan continued to ally with Liu Bei to resist Cao Cao. Liu Bei took the opportunity to gain control of most of Jingzhou, including Wuling (county seat now in Changde, Hunan), Changsha, Guiyang (county seat now in Chenzhou, Hunan), Lingling (both in present-day Hunan), and later seized Yizhou from Liu Zhang. Sun Quan controlled the Jiangdong region, forming the tripartite division of the Three Kingdoms between Wei, Shu, and Wu.
