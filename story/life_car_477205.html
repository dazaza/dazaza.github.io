2030 Vehicle Networking is Not a Mirage but a Symbol of Future Smart Cities

Cars can drive, park and charge themselves.

Last week, we introduced a "car-sharing" service that is emerging in Germany. This service can reduce car ownership by 5/6, solving many energy and traffic resource issues. In fact, in 20 years from now, our car life will no longer be what you understand it to be today.

Can you imagine cars that do not require manual driving, even for the blind? Just input a command, and the car can drive directly to your destination or even from a distant parking lot to your doorstep to pick you up. Your car can help you book flights, hotels, and even find charging stations to complete charging.

The outbreak of the third technological revolution has given the automotive industry an unprecedented opportunity for reflection: if cars can embrace the ever-evolving electronic information technology, perhaps they could open up new possibilities. Starting now, in 20 years, you will enter the era of vehicle networking.

Vehicle Networking: Driving with Eyes Closed

After the Internet and the Internet of Things, "vehicle networking" has become another symbol of future smart cities.

At the 2010 Shanghai Expo, the concept of future car life based on "vehicle networking" designed in the "SAIC-GM Pavilion" attracted audiences from around the world. In May this year, at the first theme forum of the Expo, "Information Technology and Urban Development," "vehicle networking" also became a focal topic of interest for global top IT experts.

In the "SAIC-GM Pavilion," audiences can watch the sci-fi blockbuster "2030, Go!" in the dome theater, which depicts the urban life scene of Shanghai in 2030. At that time, roads, rail transit lines, and air routes will crisscross in space from the ground to the cloud layer, infinitely extending. City dwellers will drive adorable two-wheeled vehicles, with a body volume only one-third of today's ordinary private cars. These small vehicles will be highly intelligent, powered by solar, wind, and electricity, capable of fully autonomous driving and overall networking. They can also be loaded onto larger transportation modes like subways and trains at any time. After reaching the destination, all cars can automatically park in the elevating balconies of every household. Even a visually impaired little girl can drive, with smooth traffic flow due to the "vehicle networking" era where cars can drive autonomously without traffic lights, congestion, or accidents... Such a perfect driving life is entirely controlled by an invisible network called "vehicle networking." Some people leisurely drink coffee and listen to music inside the car, while others handle their company business on the computer display screen that the front windshield turns into. Driving becomes something you don't need to "focus" on, yet remains absolutely safe.

If explained theoretically, "vehicle networking" means that cars have "highly intelligent" onboard information systems and can connect with urban traffic information networks, smart grids, and community information networks, thus obtaining instant information anytime and anywhere, making wise decisions related to travel.

GM's electric concept car EN-V plays the leading role in this sci-fi movie.

Car-to-Car Communication: Vehicle Networking Will Redefine the DNA of Cars

From the above text, do you feel that this is no longer the four-wheeled car you know well? The CEO of GM China, Kelvin Tan, once said, "Vehicle networking technology will redefine the DNA of cars." Because, with vehicle networking technology, through wireless communication, cars within the city, buildings, and urban infrastructure can achieve interconnectivity. "It's like a bat positioning system, which, after receiving local information, quickly transmits it to a broader network, helping the traffic system allocate traffic flows to different areas."

Highly intelligent vehicle driving systems enable countless vehicles to move quickly like fish schools in the deep sea without ever colliding. This is thanks to the 3D intelligent navigation system that future cars will possess. It acts like a smart robot, capable of exchanging information with traffic facilities and other vehicles, automatically guiding the car without human intervention. This is the smart car of 20 years from now. Just as the Internet enables people to achieve "point-to-point" information exchange, "vehicle networking" allows cars to "talk" to each other. Experts point out that future cars with "vehicle networking DNA" will not only be efficient, environmentally friendly, and intelligent but more importantly, they can provide unprecedented traffic safety guarantees, potentially reducing the probability of drivers having accidents to zero. Currently, some major global car brands have already begun exploring this area. GM, Volkswagen, Mercedes-Benz, Toyota, and other famous car manufacturers realize that the next significant driving force to improve traffic safety is the "communication" between cars. If cars can communicate information with each other, even dangers around the next bend or further away can be identified and prevented in advance. Therefore, future cars will have pedestrian detection functions, allowing automatic braking and emergency stops without the driver stepping on the brake pedal. This is the basic mode of vehicle networking.

Four Major Changes in the Era of Vehicle Networking

1. Achieve High Efficiency in Traffic

Since all cars are intelligently connected to the urban traffic information network, your car can choose the perfect route for you. You don’t need to decide whether taking a certain road will be congested or smoother.

2. Achieve Zero Accident Rate

Since cars can drive autonomously, minor collisions and major accidents can be avoided, greatly ensuring human safety.

3. Direct Car Charging

In 20 years, most cars will primarily rely on charging. Your car can determine the optimal location and time for charging and navigate itself to the charging station.

4. Parking No Longer a Problem

In megacities like Guangzhou, 30% of petroleum is wasted searching for parking spaces. Traditional cars require parking spots over 10 square meters. In the era of vehicle networking, cars can find parking places themselves, being only one-third the size of current cars.

Vehicle Networking in China is Just Starting

On October 28, at the China International Internet of Things (Sensor Network) Conference held in Wuxi, the Automotive Mobile Internet of Things (Vehicle Networking) project was listed as an important project in the third major special project of our country. Relevant content has been submitted to the State Council, and the first phase funding is expected to reach the billion-level. It is anticipated that by 2020, the scale of controllable vehicles will reach 200 million. This means that the research and development and promotion of vehicle networking in China are quietly progressing.

The government will develop vehicle networking through policy guidance, financial subsidies, and initial investment. However, China lags significantly behind the United States and Japan and is currently in its infancy. To realize vehicle networking, an information highway must be built, which requires RFID technology. The digital standard information source technology for automobiles is based on RFID-developed vehicle-related information resource application technology. This project was organized by the Ministry of Public Security, certified by the Ministry of Science and Technology, and has long been listed as a key special application demonstration project under the "National Science and Technology Support Program" in 2007. Currently, GM has already collaborated with China Telecom to provide users with onboard information services via its 3G network and gradually build vehicle networking.

Vehicle networking in the U.S. and Japan has developed into relatively mature applications. Japan's VICS system has formed advertising and value-added business models — America's IVHS and Japan's VICS have achieved intelligent traffic management and information services by establishing effective information communication between vehicles and roads.
