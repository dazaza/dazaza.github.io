Second, the reproduction of centipedes

Centipedes are oviparous animals. They usually mate on sunny days after rain from March to June. Every spring and early summer, the eggs gradually develop and mature. From mid-June, they begin laying eggs, with the peak egg-laying period being in the early and mid part of June and July, continuing until early August. Before laying eggs, the mother's body becomes swollen, with her abdomen close to the ground, moving slowly. For mothers showing signs of egg-laying, about 3-4 days before, they should be individually covered with a bottomless canister about 10 cm in diameter. The opening of the canister should be covered with fiberboard or glass, effectively utilizing the breeding area, avoiding mutual interference and predation, which helps centipedes lay eggs smoothly in a quiet environment and facilitates the separate rearing (or separate pooling) of young and mother centipedes.

Generally, each mother lays 20-60 eggs, and the smooth egg-laying process takes about 2-3 hours. The eggs stick together, about the size of a peanut, oval in shape. The mother centipede guards the eggs for about 43 days. During this period, if disturbed or stimulated by external factors, the mother may become agitated and aggressive, potentially eating all the hatching eggs and causing the hatching to fail. Therefore, the environment of the hatching pool must be kept absolutely quiet. It is especially important to note that feeding should be stopped a few days before the centipede lays eggs, especially during the hatching process within 40-50 days, feeding should be strictly prohibited. Otherwise, when the centipede eats, food may stick to the egg mass or embryo, causing the centipede to eat the egg mass or embryo together. The hatching process of the egg mass is relatively slow. After 15-16 days, the eggs form a kidney shape and start to crack open, marking the first molting; after 20 days, the second molting occurs into a crescent shape, initially resembling a larva; after 35-40 days, the young enter the third molting stage and can crawl up and down in the mother's embrace; after another 5-7 days, the young's exoskeleton thickens, turning yellow-brown, living under the protection of the mother. After about a month, they can live independently and start searching for food on their own.

Preventing centipedes from disease: Due to poultry, rats, and geckos preying on centipedes, especially ants being the main natural enemy of centipedes, it is necessary to regularly observe whether there are any pests inside and outside the centipede field, preventing these natural enemies from entering the pool and attacking centipedes. To prevent ants from entering the pool, several lines can be drawn around the pool using cockroach ant powder pens, or one part camphor balls, one part vegetable oil, and five parts sawdust can be mixed evenly and frequently sprinkled around the outer wall of the breeding pool as a line of medicine. Especially during molting, egg-laying, or hatching periods, centipedes' resistance is very weak, making them easy targets for ants. Once ants enter the pool, the centipedes should be quickly moved and the ants scalded with boiling water.
