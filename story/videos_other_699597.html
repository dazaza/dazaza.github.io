Outside, at most, only exists in the early stages of marriage. As the length of marriage increases, romantic feelings will inevitably decrease. However, if the quality of this union is indeed good, another kind of emotion will gradually grow. This new emotion evolves from the original romance and seems less passionate and infatuated than romance, but it has many factors that romance does not possess. The most important ones are mutual trust, tacit understanding in behavior patterns, deep longing, and a sense of shared destiny formed through long-term cohabitation. We might as well regard this emotion as a form of familial affection, though it differs from blood-related familial affection and is instead a familial affection based on sexual love. I believe it fully deserves to be recognized as a form of love, and more importantly, a mature form. To distinguish it from romantic love, I call it familial love. The love in marriage exists in such a form. According to this line of thought, marriage is not only not the grave of love, but also the soil for the growth of love - familial love.

Let me ask married men a question: In this world, who is the woman that stirs your heart the most? You say it's not your wife? That's normal, you don't have to feel ashamed, it doesn't mean you don't love your wife. Ask another question: In this world, who do you think is your closest family member? You say it's your wife? Very good, that's enough, it shows that you still love your wife very much.

Love is a process of continuous pursuit and attraction between two souls, and this process should not end because of marriage. Viewing marriage as the completion of love is a harmful concept. Under this concept, those who get married mistakenly believe they have achieved great success, already obtained the other person, and no longer need to continue pursuing them. However, courtship, courtship, love lies in the pursuit. Once the pursuit stops, love will inevitably disappear. A good marriage should keep love in an uncompleted state, which means that both parties always maintain necessary distance and tension, each party views the other as an independent individual, thus making them an object that always needs to be pursued again, and cannot be possessed once and for all. In this state, both parties can constantly rediscover and appreciate each other rather than bind and bore each other, and love can gain space for continued growth.

There are various kinds of marriages in the world, and truly love-based unions are not many, hence they are particularly precious. However, paradoxically, the more love-based the union is, compared to marriages based on traditional ethics and practical interests, the more fragile it tends to be. What people often refer to as "good couples find it hard to last", and what people perceive as heaven-made pairs often fail to live happily ever after, this is almost an old yet ever-new story. Investigating the reasons, perhaps it is because human inner emotions are harder to fathom and grasp than external norms and interests, and love is more changeable than secular marital ties. Taking love as the sole basis for marriage logically implies that love is higher than marriage. Therefore, on one hand, if the existing love has flaws, the marriage becomes problematic. On the other hand, once new love arises, the marriage must give way. Truly love-based marriages will never achieve ultimate success or be done once and for all; even the best marriage cannot obtain lifelong insurance.

Sexual love, in essence, is a quite uncertain feeling. On one hand, it has a romantic tendency, as the saying goes, "human nature values difficulty and despises ease, prefers novelty and dislikes the old." This psychology is especially prominent in sexual love. People often beautify and idealize unknown and difficult-to-obtain things. Thus, the freshness of encounters and the freedom of breaking taboos become the main sources of sexual love pleasure. For this reason, the most unforgettable love experiences are either premarital loves when first entering love or extramarital affairs. Such situations cannot simply be attributed to moral defects but have psychological reasons. On the other hand, sexual love is a purely personal experience without any objective standard to speak of. Whether one has fallen in love, whether two hearts truly resonate, and where the boundary between liking and love lies, these are not only difficult for others to judge, sometimes even the parties involved cannot grasp them. If such an unstable and unclear feeling is taken as the sole bond of marriage, the precariousness of any marriage can be imagined.

To seek a way out in the paradox of marriage, we should change our thinking and distinguish the love as the foundation of marriage from romantic love. Romantic love may lead to the establishment of marriage, but it cannot serve as the lasting foundation of marriage. The foundation should be a kind of familial affection developed from love, distinct from romantic love, which I call familial love. In this kind of love, romantic elements may still exist, but they have been reduced to a secondary position. The basic components are the mutual trust and appreciation formed through long-term cohabitation. This sense of trust is not merely based on good intentions but is a capacity cultivated over time to respect and approve each other's important behaviors. It increases with the length of marriage, giving people a sense of security and imbuing the marriage with a solemn and peaceful atmosphere.

In fact, the reason why many families have not broken up is not because they have never encountered the temptation of romantic love, but precisely because the parties value this hard-won trust-based familial love, thus consciously avoiding such temptations, or even after falling into temptation, still make rational choices, and the aggrieved party is also willing to forgive. In my view, marriages built on this familial love are not only stable but also of high quality.

I do not deny the possibility of a new romantic love bringing about a better marriage, but first, this is ultimately unknown, so it is a risk, second, even if it is really so, after marriage, the new romantic love will sooner or later turn into familial love. I believe that recognizing the inevitability and necessity of taking familial love as the basis of marriage, people will evaluate their current marital status more objectively, and once faced with the choice of staying or leaving, they will be more cautious.

One of the important reasons why people see marriage and love as conflicting is the overly narrow understanding of love, limited only to the romantic feelings between men and women. This kind of romantic feeling depends on some kind of encounter and freshness, its manifestation is at first sight, soul-stirring, infatuated, and inseparable. Such a kind of feeling is indeed beautiful, but it definitely cannot last, and this has nothing to do with marriage, even if one does not get married, it cannot last either. Because once it lasts, any encounter will return to the ordinary, any strangeness will become familiarity. Trying to use the form of marriage to continue this romantic feeling will inevitably fail, but the fault is not in marriage.
