Intel Lowers 4Q Revenue Forecast on Hard Drive Shortage

by zhanglin859 on 2012-02-19 20:31:06

On December 1st, Western Digital stated that its Bang Pa-in (BPI) factory in Thailand had partially resumed production, but it was expected to take until March of the following year for full recovery. Western Digital primarily produces hard disk heads in this region.

The direct consequence of the tight supply of hard drives has been a rise in prices for consumers. However, multiple distributors have openly admitted they cannot clearly identify the reasons behind the price hikes—whether it is due to flooding causing supply shortages or intentional stockpiling and speculation by upstream suppliers.

Tang Kuirui's forecast aligns with those of other manufacturers.

An earlier report by Is Dongdong

Not only hard drive manufacturers, but all parties within the PC supply chain have been affected by the floods in Thailand, including Intel, the world's largest chip manufacturer.

However, a central China-based regional distributor of hard drives revealed to the reporter that while the floods in Thailand have had an impact, the effect on retail sales and supply of hard drives is not significant. The person mentioned that as the year-end approaches, it is customary for inventory buildup during this period. Due to inconvenient transportation, shipping costs are high, and most importantly, domestically retailed hard drives originate from local factories. Southeast Asian hard drive capacities mainly supply computer manufacturers like Lenovo and Asus.

IHS iSuppli believes that the impact of the floods in Thailand on hard drive manufacturers differs somewhat from the effects of the earthquake in Japan on memory manufacturers. At the time, the Japanese earthquake mainly affected Toshiba, whose global market share was not particularly large. This time, Thailand accounts for one-quarter of the global hard drive shipments, and there is little room for other regions' hard drive manufacturers to increase production speed to compensate for the shortfall caused by the shutdown in Thailand. "Another difference is that Japanese companies quickly resumed production, whereas in Thailand, they must wait for the floodwaters to recede before resuming."

IHS iSuppli analyst Dee Nguyen said that hard drive suppliers might have learned some lessons from the Japanese earthquake, especially regarding flexibility in production. Dee Nguyen suggests that manufacturers should diversify their layouts and pay more attention to inventory management. (Editor’s note: On November 29th, Bank of China Hong Kong noted that comparing HDD shipments to branded PC shipments showed that HDD shipments were still higher, indicating no signs of insufficient HDD inventory, but rather revealing issues with supply chain shifts: HDD producers and suppliers are restoring production and shifting production to other countries. Additionally, given that Q1 is typically a slow season, weaker shipment volumes help alleviate the HDD supply gap.)

Since the floods began in late July in Thailand, they have caused billions of dollars in losses, resulting in temporary shutdowns at several hard drive manufacturers' Thai factories. Thailand is the world's second-largest hard drive producer after China. Market research firm IHS iSuppli reported that Thailand supplies one-quarter of the world's hard drives, and three of the five major global hard drive suppliers have operations in Thailand. Reuters reported that the floods in Thailand will reduce global hard drive production by about one-third this quarter.

"Channel Merchants Speculating on Floods"

A quick look at comparison shopping websites reveals that many well-known hard drive brands, including Seagate and Western Digital, have seen price increases of varying degrees since October, with some increases exceeding 100%, showing no sign of decline.

It is worth noting that HIS iSuppli last week predicted that next year's PC shipment growth rate would be only 6.8%, compared to the previous estimate of 9.5%.

It is noteworthy that Intel's CFO mentioned in a telephone conference with investors that he expects the shortage situation to accelerate the adoption of solid-state drives (SSD) as the primary storage device in PCs.

The dealer stated that the water disaster at Western Digital's factory in Thailand is one reason, but it should not be the sole cause of the supply shortage.

PC Manufacturers Issue Warnings

Without a doubt, Western Digital has been the hardest hit by the floods in Thailand. This global leader in hard drives currently has about 40,000 employees in Thailand who are on paid leave, and they expect the supply situation to remain tight until next year, with a potential shortage of 60 million hard drives this season.

Compared to Western Digital, Seagate has been much luckier. Seagate's factory is located on higher ground in northeastern Thailand and has not been affected by the floods. On the 9th, Seagate announced an investment of $30 million in Thailand for the construction of a new head factory, which is expected to be operational by February of next year. Seagate estimates that industry-wide hard drive shipments for the fourth quarter of this year will be between 110 million and 120 million units.

A manager surnamed Lao from a Shanghai-based hard drive distributor told the reporter that hard drive prices have remained high since the floods in Thailand, but the reasons for the price increases are unclear. Sales figures at the end of last year were good, but not this year. "One speculation is that if one party sets a high price, others cannot sell at lower prices, or it could be channel merchants speculating upstream. You can say there isn't a real shortage; goods can still be obtained, just at higher prices."

Lenovo, Acer, HP, and Dell have also issued varying levels of warnings since October, stating that current hard drive inventory may lead to reduced PC production. Lenovo previously stated that the hard drive supply shortage would likely persist until the first quarter of next year. ASUS CEO Shen Zhenglai mentioned in early December that ASUS's hard drive inventory could support operations until the end of this year.

Solid-state drives made from NAND chips are far faster than hard drives but are much more expensive, currently used only in premium PC products like Apple's MacBook Air.

Due to the reduction in hard drive production, PC manufacturers are forced to decrease output, meaning a corresponding decrease in required CPUs. Last week, other chip giants, including Texas Instruments and Altera Corp., also lowered their expectations for the fourth fiscal quarter.

Intel Vice President Tom Kilroy stated in a telephone conference that supply issues have become increasingly apparent over the past two weeks. "We've noticed significant fluctuations in orders, with most customers concerned that the shortage issue will persist, especially into the start of the first quarter."

This Thai flood is another test for the global supply chain after the Japanese earthquake earlier this year.

On December 12th, U.S. time, Intel announced a downward revision of its fourth-quarter revenue due to the shortage of hard drives caused by the floods in Thailand affecting the company's performance. Intel expects fourth-quarter revenue to decrease to $13.7 billion and gross margin to be adjusted down to 64.5%. Intel's previous forecasts for these figures were $14.7 billion and 65%, respectively.
