10 Best Practices for Top-Notch Programmers

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 18:36:51

10 best practices from top coders at Google, Pinterest & more

1. Choose your first language carefully

Programming languages vary, but not by much. However, the people using the language can differ greatly. By choosing a language, you're also choosing a community.

– Sam Kaufman, Freelancer, iOS Developer, 10x Management

If you want to quickly build prototypes (especially for founders hoping to iterate and upgrade their product), use Ruby or JavaScript.

– Erin Parker, Founder and Chief Developer at Spitfire Athlete

2. You are not just a coder!

Great developers can build and develop applications. Exceptional developers can do this while keeping business needs in mind. People on the business side generally don't know how to code, but they certainly understand the motivation behind specific features.

It's too common that developers do what others say without understanding why, resulting in missed opportunities for both sides.

– John Coggeshall, Freelancer, Web Developer, 10x Management, PHP Core Contributor

Becoming proficient in programming is a noble career goal. Once you achieve it, don't forget about yourself. Don't become a slave to any company or waste your time on worthless things.

– Greg Sadetsky, Python and JavaScript Expert, 10x Management; Founder of Co-working Space Abri.co

To meet deadlines, communicate about the project before starting technical work (even if it's not a prerequisite), because other people's response times can vary widely.

– Andrew Wilcox, Web Application Developer, Meteor Core Contributor, 10x Management

3. Stay agile, keep delivering

Release early, release often, release while rapping.

– Max Nanis, Freelancer, Web Developer, Bioinformatics Specialist, 10x Management

Test constantly. Good test packages are like a combination of insurance policies and canaries in coal mines. They help you find errors earlier in the production cycle, and the earlier you catch mistakes, the easier they are to fix.

– Jeremy Green, Freelancer, Web Developer, Ruby on Rails Expert, 10x Management

Fail fast. When coding (and in life), I prefer to know as soon as possible where something won't work instead of letting it grow unchecked. Let everything go, fail fast, patch the issues, and keep moving forward.

– Stephanie Volftsun, Co-founder and CTO at Knotch

Write automatic tests for all code! Strive to practice test-driven development as much as possible.

– Zoran Kacic-Alesic, R&D Director at Industrial Light & Magic

4. Keep control over the testing process

Many projects suffer from multiple testing cycles. This slows down the project and causes high-level organizational problems.

Programmers should focus on unit testing and semi-regression testing of their own code. They know the codebase better than anyone else and understand which changes will affect them. Sometimes these changes may be missing due to limited QA test scope, leading to significant issues in production.

– Sanjib Sahoo, CTO at tradeMONSTER

To develop defect-free code as soon as possible within your capabilities, never ever put off writing tests. We know this well. Check the test coverage to ensure there are no blind spots.

– Seth Purcell, VP of Engineering at Signpost

5. If you're a freelancer, learn to say no, even when money is involved

Make a reasonable estimate of time and cost, then double it. If everyone says, "This should be easy," then proceed with caution.

– Ryan Waggoner, Freelancer, Web and Mobile Application Developer, 10x Management

6. Glory belongs to the past—Theory is one thing, but practice matters more

The best way to improve software development quality is to develop software. Many ambitious beginner engineers spend a lot of business hours reading books about the latest tools, open processes, and so on.

Many people spend their free time this way, but it's easy to get sidetracked. Don't do that. Strengthen the part of your brain responsible for developing software by using it as much as possible.

– James Cropcho, Ruby on Rails Expert and Instructor at General Assembly

Explore continuously. Many coders I've met have several ongoing side projects. Working on side projects forces you to explore new technologies and learn all aspects of creating applications. You might need to do front-end HTML/CSS, back-end API integration, database optimization, mobile app development, and set up your own servers.

– Andrew Waage, CTO and Co-founder at Retention Science

7. Pair reviews are your secret weapon

Pair programming is essential. Two programmers working together on the same module can review each other's code. Development teams should also hold weekly code review meetings where every developer provides feedback on others' code and explains how to improve it better. This fosters a collaborative culture and leaves programmer egos aside!

– Sanjib Sahoo

8. Avoid premature optimization like the plague

Only refactor when both the problem and solution are right in front of you—premature refactoring is a massive waste of time. Don't invest in perfecting anything that could be thrown away in six months. Premature optimization is the root of all evil.

– Seth Purcell

Don't optimize prematurely! I constantly see engineers worrying about scaling to a million users when they don’t even have a thousand yet.

– Mariya Yao, Founder and Creative Director at Xanadu Mobile, Mobile Developer and Designer

9. Your code is written once, but read a thousand times

The code you write is parsed and executed by machines, but others need to read your code, understand it, and manipulate it. You must realize that your code has future readers. Code is also a form of written communication.

– Tracy Chou, Software Engineer at Pinterest

It sounds strange, but you always need to think about your future self. Ask yourself: If you had amnesia, would you still understand the code you wrote?

– Wai Ching Jessica Lam, Co-founder and CTO at Sugarbox

Read through your documentation. Design changes frequently, and sometimes comments may not be updated when code is. Keeping your documentation up-to-date makes it easier for future people (including yourself) to understand. I can't count how many times I've looked back at my own code and thought, "What was I doing?" Good comments save headaches in the future.

– Kitt Vanderwater, Software Engineer at Google

10. This is a noble profession. Use your skills for good.

Helping others is a deep human need. Find ways to use your work to improve humanity, and you'll have a good chance of success.

– Greg Sadetsky
