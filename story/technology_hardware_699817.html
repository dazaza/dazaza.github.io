"On 'Abandoning Self and Following Others' in Tai Chi Chuan Combat"

On "Abandoning Self and Following the Other" in Tai Chi Chuan Combat Techniques - Zhang Zhijun

The combat strategy of Tai Chi Chuan in pushing hands and free fighting can be summarized in four words: "abandoning self and following the other."

"Abandoning self and following the other," this is the general method and guideline. If you want to hit me, the common response would be to block or dodge your attack, then look for an opportunity to strike back. However, there's a more sophisticated approach: when you try to hit me, I guide your hand towards my body. By following the force you exert, combined with circular and spiral movements, I can take control of your force. To put it into a formula: your force plus my force, through circular and spiral movements, all under my command. This is what Tai Chi Chuan refers to as...

Some might say, "I don't use the principle of 'abandoning self and following the other,' yet I can still knock people down. Isn't that possible?" We argue that although you may still be able to knock people down, that is not the essence of Tai Chi Chuan, nor does it come close to the subtlety of Tai Chi combat techniques. Tai Chi requires us to comply with the opponent no matter how stringent the conditions are, and to be able to "abandon self and follow the other" under various circumstances, using sticking, adhering, connecting, and following to solve problems. At the same time, "abandoning self and following the other" aims to achieve the goals of "self-compliance and others' resistance" and "leading the opponent to fall short." It lets the opponent miss rather than hit their mark. When you come, I let you miss; if you want to issue power, go ahead, if you want to strike, do so, stretching and opening up freely. This is the elegant state that everyone practicing Tai Chi push hands and free fighting aspires to.

The core of "abandoning self and following the other" is transforming passivity into initiative, protecting oneself with the optimal method. One must take the lead, skillfully dismantling the opponent's offense while simultaneously organizing and implementing one's own offensive strategy. "Abandoning" should not be done blindly, otherwise it becomes wasteful; "following" should not be done aimlessly, otherwise it leads to being controlled. To achieve "abandoning self and following the other," one should master the following points:

1. Adapt and change the opponent's force.

2. Avoid losing contact or resisting too hard.

3. Develop a sense of cyclic defense and continuous strikes.

4. Distinguish between guiding and striking hands.

5. Achieve "storing and releasing transformation" and "integration of neutralization and attack."

6. Utilize the subtleties of folding.

7. Incorporate appropriate body and footwork methods.

In conclusion, "abandoning self and following the other" is the overarching principle of Tai Chi combat techniques. On this basis, Tai Chi also features superior combat skills such as "using softness to overcome hardness," "using stillness to control movement," "avoiding strength and attacking weakness," and "responding after but striking first." These skills represent the most brilliant part of the profound Tai Chi system, which is easy to learn but difficult to master, easy to understand but challenging to practice. Learners can only progress step by step and gradually reach higher levels through diligent training, frequent summarization, and courageous exploration.
