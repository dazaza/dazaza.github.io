Dianping.com: A Bone Too Big for the Giants to Chew, Living Outside the Comfort Zone

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 17:26:13

Tao Zhang, CEO of Dianping

By Yuan Yin, edited by Jing Yang, photographed by Nan Xiao

Tao Zhang, the CEO of Dianping, has postponed the company's IPO once again.

In 2011, when Dianping secured a $100 million Series C financing round from Sequoia Capital and others, its valuation had already reached $1 billion. Following convention, it should have chosen to go public within the next one to two years, but it didn't. Instead, in 2012, it raised another $60 million in a Series D funding round.

Given Dianping's scale and development trajectory, going public would not be difficult. However, Tao Zhang feels that an IPO at this stage would be too costly. He deconstructs the large internet (PC + mobile) into four elements: people, information, goods, and services. Each connection point presents an opportunity for a giant to emerge. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, and Alibaba connects people with goods. The space between people and services represents the seat of the fourth giant. Since 2003, Dianping has been diligently accumulating resources for this position, finally waiting for the explosion of mobile internet, where phones merge "location" and "services." According to Zhang's logic, no company seems more suited to fulfill the imagination of the fourth giant than Dianping.

But the outside world doesn't see it that way. In recent months, with frequent acquisitions by BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent), Dianping has become a presumed acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition candidate; regardless of which family name it takes on from BAT, it could help them achieve greatness sooner. On the other hand, Dianping's size pales in comparison to BAT, and it is premature to conclude that it can become a giant. Moreover, whether it's Tencent WeChat's public platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after acquiring Gaode and Meituan, they all aim directly at Dianping's territory—local life services centered around eating, drinking, and entertainment.

Before proving the following two things, Dianping's IPO would be equivalent to a "discounted sale." First, Zhang needs time to prove that what Dianping does now, BAT cannot do. Second, Dianping is about to engage in the ultimate battle of group buying. If it secures the top spot, its valuation could increase several times over.

2013 is Dianping's "decisive year." Regarding speculation and rumors, Zhang remains deaf to them. He is very clear: "The internet has indeed entered an era of great convergence, but Dianping will not be acquired."

Zhang categorizes companies that might be acquired into three types. The first type possesses platform attributes but is better suited to develop within a larger system, such as maps and email. These products can easily attract large numbers of users but struggle to find independent business models. Others, like browsers, may make money, but the revenue from navigation services is far less than their strategic value to large companies. "Big companies are willing to invest in maintaining an email product because it helps with branding and user stickiness, but there aren’t many successful standalone email companies." The second type involves industry consolidation, such as in the video industry, where acquisitions expand market share. The third type involves companies facing financial difficulties, selling out to survive.

Now Meituan has been brought under Alibaba's wing. In theory, Dianping should quickly seek a "backer" to avoid being defeated by a capital-injected rival expanding rapidly. However, Zhang says, "The giants' help to us is limited. We do need users, but execution is even more important. Acquisitions would reduce our execution capability and we might get marginalized by a large company." Not only that, he must ensure Dianping maintains the most precise "distance" from the giants. Getting too close to any one of them would be akin to "choosing sides," making Dianping an enemy to the others.

Thus, logically, the acquisition of Dianping becomes a dead end. Zhang counters, "Why should we take their money? We don't lack funds; we need resources. If a giant is willing to make a small investment and give us many resources, I might consider it. But would they? If the giant holds only a small stake, would they provide resources?"

Weapon One: Dirty and Tiring Work

In the mobile internet sector, opportunities for companies doing pure internet products are becoming fewer. New opportunities lie in those combining with traditional industries. Zhang believes that life services mainly revolve around eating, drinking, and entertainment. Other niche markets, such as ride-hailing, can form decent business models, but it’s unlikely any will grow into giants.

For eating, drinking, and entertainment, user demands are simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is the core of life services, which is exactly where Dianping started. Whether in content, member accumulation, or brand, newcomers will find it hard to surpass.

The most representative product form of discounts is group buying. Group buying has now entered the championship contest phase, and Dianping plans to face off against Meituan in 2013. This is a winner-takes-all market. As long as two strong players coexist, the industry's gross margin will be reduced, making stable profitability impossible.

Another foreseeable future is reservations. Reservations can create a true online-to-offline closed loop, but they must be built on the foundation of Dianping's absolute advantage in information and discounts. Otherwise, creating a closed loop would be difficult. In 2012, Dianping began experimenting with reservations. Zhang said, "Currently, both users and merchants provide positive feedback, but if we were to focus solely on reservations, it would be hard to solve the chicken-or-egg problem."

Of course, life services also present some heavier opportunities. For example, food delivery is a rigid demand for users and can increase sales for merchants. In the past couple of years, food delivery companies like "To Home Gourmet" have gained momentum. However, the core of food delivery is delivery itself—it must be delivered in a short period of time, and issues like soup spilling must be addressed. Its logistics requirements are higher than those of fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang said, "If food delivery is executed well, there is still an opportunity, but it's dirtier and harder work. What Dianping is doing now is already dirty and tiring enough."

If judged by age, Dianping is already an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has painstakingly accumulated merchant profiles and user reviews, which Zhang refers to as "dirty and tiring work." This primitive growth model once restricted its speed, but the pain of the past has become Dianping's core barrier today. It can be said that, apart from Meituan, Dianping has no real competitors in the life services field.

However, he still needs to beware of BAT's "clutches." Baidu's intervention is the most direct. Baidu Maps already has over 100 million users, who can use the map to search for nearby dining and other life services. Tencent, believed to have secured a ticket to the mobile internet era, launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest goods trading platform, and extending goods to services is a natural progression, especially since it has incorporated Meituan and Gaode into its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there hasn't been a noticeable explosive growth in reality and data," Zhang said. Brand positioning is crucial; the key is which product users think of first when they want to eat, drink, and entertain.

Moreover, in the vast domain of life services, competition among giants will inevitably overlap, and no one can completely monopolize the field. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained on Weibo and WeChat. Zhang thinks this makes Dianping safer instead. "In niche areas like car rentals and business travel, the massive user base directed by the giants could easily overwhelm existing companies."

As for Alibaba, which has invested in Gaode, invading Dianping's territory won't be so simple either. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, which can be categorized no matter how peculiar. A user's experience of a meal depends on factors like the day's environment, waitstaff, and chefs. Managing merchants cannot rely solely on systems and tools like Alibaba does; a 'ground force' managed by people is necessary.

"All three giants want to take this path, and there is room for imagination, but this is not a highway; it's a rugged mountain road," Zhang believes. First, providing life services requires ground teams deployed at various levels, which BAT lacks the genes and ability to manage. Second, they lack the willingness; life services require focused effort and cannot be accomplished by dispatching a small team. Making money is slow. "A company finds it hard to excel at two important things simultaneously, let alone needing to consolidate its advantages in the original field."

Weapon Two: Agile Small Boats

When Dianping secured $100 million in financing in 2011, Zhang told the media that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, times change fast, and before Dianping went public, his analogies fell from grace.

He wasn't overly worried about it. "They failed to become big companies because they lacked offline teams; now they're trying to become America's Dianping."

The current path is one Zhang has gradually figured out himself. When he felt that selling keyword ads and coupons was too outdated, the emergence of group buying caught his attention. Initially, he understood group buying as Americans did—as a marketing platform, one deal per day—but as orders increased, he realized group buying had the characteristics of a trading platform. Thus, he envisioned Dianping's future as a life service trading platform. The two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were precisely used to transition Dianping from an information platform to a trading platform.

Without this transformation, Zhang might not have had the confidence to compete for the fourth giant's position today. But the transformation sounds simple; in reality, it was equivalent to Dianping starting over, a process filled with extreme pain.

First, compared to selling ads alone, the gross margin of a trading platform decreases, requiring stricter cost control and higher efficiency. Second, a trading platform demands stronger cross-departmental communication skills.

Previously, to maintain Dianping's impartiality and independence as an information platform, the content and sales teams were completely separated. Zhang deliberately erected a "firewall" between departments—even for important clients, information on the platform couldn't be altered for sales purposes, which led to some mutual hostility between content and sales teams. Now, as a trading platform, editors and the sales team need deep communication to craft good marketing plans. Besides planning, editorial cooperation is essential. He said, "Maintaining information quality is still important, but there's no need to reinforce hostility. We need to dismantle the firewall."

Though editors logically accept this, individual behaviors and values have inertia, and collectively, the entire company's inertia is greater. To address this, Zhang specifically held several meetings to encourage more communication between departments. After a few months, an editor manager casually mentioned to a sales manager, "I used to think you were my enemy, but now I realize you're my colleague."

The sales department also needed transformation. To promote conversion, Zhang cleverly gave the offline team dual roles. First, for merchants, salespeople should become marketing consultants, not just selling products but helping merchants formulate marketing strategies. Second, for the company, marketing consultants are the company's buyers. Dianping is like a supermarket; not only must the product quality be good, but the variety must be rich. Marketing consultants should aim to help users buy group-buying products. Of course, dual roles will inevitably lead to a series of complex issues. For frontline personnel, this is quite challenging, as they must balance the scales themselves.

Substantially, Dianping's "dual role" is very similar to Alibaba's division into 25 business units. By decentralizing decision-making power to the front lines, the company gains faster response speeds but increases management difficulty. Similarly, compared to frontline employees, Dianping's management team suffered more during the transformation.

Each year, Zhang breaks down Dianping's company goals into various departments, which are then further broken down layer by layer by senior executives. However, breaking down goals isn't simple arithmetic. Li Jing, co-founder of Dianping responsible for the sales team, sometimes finds that unresolved merchant demands stem from sales issues but affect sales. Thus, Li created a new position, setting up communication specialists who collect information daily from marketing consultants, submit it to other departments, and track feedback. He often tells his team, "Being good at embracing change doesn't mean liking change; no one likes change. When faced with change, everyone feels uncomfortable. All employees must go through discomfort zones, including myself."

The numbers reflect the weight of Dianping's execution team. Out of more than 3,000 employees, over 2,000 work offline. The ground force is responsible for directly communicating with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group buying, and combined marketing solutions.

Dianping has a unique management and evaluation system for this core team that resists BAT's reach.

They are not called "sales"; their business cards have always read "Marketing Planning Consultant" from day one. Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the phase of watching news and chatting on QQ, lagging behind Taobao merchants by at least three to five years in understanding the internet. Marketing planning consultants must tell merchants what online users need and how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing said, "Even for a high-end restaurant, marketing consultants wouldn't suggest a grouponsale of abalone or shark fin, perhaps opting for other dishes, because online and offline users have different demands."

An excellent marketing consultant must be a versatile player. They need to explain to restaurant owners and hair salon owners the fundamental differences between discounts and group buying and which kind of discount fits their positioning better. If dealing with a hot pot shop owner, the marketing consultant must know whether it's the off-season or peak season, which products' costs rise in a certain season, and which decrease, proposing a group-buying menu that both merchants and users like.

This imposes high requirements on marketing consultants—they must be not only internet marketing experts but also industry experts. Li Jing admitted, "It's impossible for every consultant to be a double expert, so we set star ratings." Like hotels, marketing consultants range from one-star to five-star, with the best ones called Super Stars.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline employees are not tech-savvy graduates from prestigious universities; understanding internet products is difficult for them. Dianping established a knowledge base for marketing consultants, containing knowledge points and relevant cases that both new and old employees can browse. If encountering a new issue, employees can ask questions in the knowledge base, where thousands of consulting advisors nationwide will help answer.

A large number of knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees based on practical experience. Dianping set up a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, rewarding employees who help others the most each quarter. Every quarter, Dianping conducts skill assessments for employees, linking the results directly to promotions.

Industry knowledge is harder to obtain than internet knowledge. Some experiences are summarized by traditional merchants after decades of practice in the industry. A Super Star marketing consultant told Li Jing that the beauty industry should avoid wash-cut-blow package deals because men lack motivation to buy them, and women prefer blow-drying without washing and cutting. Li Jing was astonished, "We men would never think of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing consultants are not only divided by cities but also by industries such as catering, beauty salons, and leisure. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is further subdivided into hot pot, Western cuisine, and buffets, with a marketing consultant responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing consultants can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, so merchants won't resist when selling products," he said.

Each marketing group must conduct case-sharing sessions: what difficulties a particular type of merchant encounters, what marketing needs they have, what product combinations the company provides, and the effects. Outstanding cases are reported upward, and Dianping officially shares one case per week.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition called "Entrepreneurial Winner." In the first three months of employment, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products, but listening to merchants share experiences. If responsible for hot pot shops, after three months, they must submit a business plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hot Pot Shop Owner." Similar to graduation, employees defend their plans on-site, with industry supervisors evaluating the winners, and the company giving certain rewards.

From a strategic perspective, whether it's Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, the tasks to be done are quite similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O are familiar to anyone with a basic understanding of the industry, and their statements sound alike. "The era where success could be achieved by telling stories or creating concepts has passed. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model lies in management and execution, not strategy," Zhang said.
