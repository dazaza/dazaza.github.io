NY Times: Intel and Sony's network TV plans questioned

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 17:33:39

Internet TV services being planned by companies like Intel and Sony may one day change how Americans watch television. However, the priority of these services is to launch as soon as possible, but their future has been questioned. The New York Times wrote an article analyzing this.

Content is King

Similar to Netflix, Intel will also offer paid streaming TV programs to users. However, in order to meet the requirements of channel owners, the OnCue TV service originally planned for release at the end of the year has been postponed to 2014.

Sony's plans are more mysterious. Like Intel, the company has not yet announced any licensing agreements with channels. But without such agreements, it would be equivalent to having nothing.

"This time, content is absolutely king," said Mike Vorhaus, president of MagidAdvisors, a division of a media consulting company.

Google also showed interest in OTT TV services earlier this year, but the related negotiations clearly encountered obstacles. OTT refers to providing services using existing broadband networks, so future Intel or Sony users must first have wired or wireless broadband.

The Right Time

In theory, the timing for reforming the American pay-TV industry is indeed ripe. Most Americans can only choose between DirecTV, Comcast, or Time Warner Cable, but satisfaction with the entire industry is low. An increasing number of households are supplementing cable or satellite TV services with streaming video services from Netflix or Amazon. A small portion of people have even abandoned cable TV altogether. However, most people still stick to traditional services because they know that changing service providers is not easy, and the industry as a whole is similar across providers.

Intel's OnCue aims to solve these issues. It will provide users with services similar to existing cable TV packages but accessible through the internet and various screen sizes, including smartphones and large TVs. Its interface will be smoother than most cable TV set-top boxes, and it will offer personalized customization. For example, the program recommendations seen by a daughter will differ from those seen by her father.

The most important feature is that OneCue can automatically record all programs from all channels and save them for three days, offering great flexibility to users. Informed sources mentioned that during demonstrations of this technology to channel owners, the automatic recording function often amazed those present. However, Intel still needs to overcome some obstacles, even encountering resistance within the company.

Seeking Partnerships

It was reported last week that Intel hopes to find powerful partners for OnCue to convince its CEO, Brian Krzanich, that OnCue will grow rapidly after its launch.

Informed sources revealed that Intel expects to attract 5 million users in the three years before OnCue's launch to realize its value. Ensuring scale is partly due to the need to commit to minimum fees when signing contracts with channel owners. Intel hopes to collaborate with companies like Samsung or Roku that already have millions of users to quickly expand its scale.

According to informed sources, talks with Amazon are at an early stage. Being able to integrate Amazon's Instant Video on-demand service into OnCue could combine live and on-demand TV services. However, both Intel and Amazon refused to comment.

Sony seems to be further along than Intel. It was reported this summer that Sony had reached a preliminary cooperation agreement with media giant Viacom. However, neither company has confirmed this, and no other deal news has emerged since then.

Sony's streaming TV service will initially be sold via PlayStation gaming consoles and may later extend to other products such as mobile phones and TVs. However, the company does not seem ready for an official launch either. A Sony spokesperson declined to comment.

The main selling point of this internet cable TV service is convenience rather than low price: because smaller new enterprises typically pay higher fees to channel owners. For Intel and Sony, such services are peripheral to their core businesses, at least initially. Nevertheless, it can help them get closer to users. For instance, Sony could promise three months of free internet TV services when selling a $3,000 flat-screen TV.

Future Outlook

The six major media companies, who own most of America's TV channels, are the ones openly discussing Sony and Intel's TV plans. These media companies say they are happy to attract more users for their channels.

"We're happy to sell them our product," Disney CEO Robert Iger said last week. He believes that some new platforms have advanced technology that can help Disney and its competitors provide more attractive services, benefiting from it.

These services can help media companies package content in new ways: through OnCue or PlayStation, Disney could create a dedicated "Phineas and Ferb" channel playing this animated series around the clock. NBC could also create a special channel for "The Voice."

However, Iger must tread carefully, as cable and satellite TV providers remain their main customers. Some older companies have recently set obstacles for Intel and other OTT service providers.

Media companies like Disney also want to ensure that these services do not disrupt their core businesses, such as advertising sales. Intel initially planned to record all content for 30 days, but after opposition from some channel owners, they shortened the time span to 3 days, mainly because Nielsen only counts TV on-demand statistics for three days.

Although these services have faced some doubts, and some question whether OnCue will ever be released, analysts expect that similar services will eventually become popular, possibly offered proactively by veteran cable TV companies like Comcast or Dish Network.

Magid's Vorhaus expects that internet TV services like Netflix and OneCue will gradually erode the traditional pay-TV sector.
