E-commerce New Force Interview: JD.com's Richard Liu's Overseas Pilgrimage for Experience

JD.com CEO Richard Liu

Sina Tech has launched a series of interviews titled "New Forces in E-Commerce," which will focus on the diversified development environment of e-commerce, new entrepreneurial opportunities, new business models, and the psychological journey of these entrepreneurs. Please stay tuned.

Fifth Guest: JD.com CEO Richard Liu

After investing $500 million in JD.com, Russian investment group DST specially arranged an American trip for Liu: to visit and meet with companies like Facebook, Groupon, and Zynga, all of which are also invested in by DST. Liu defined the purpose of this American trip as: examining how emerging social media can influence future e-commerce marketing.

Two things that left a deep impression on Liu: first, these American social media platforms have a strong desire to enter China but cannot do so due to various review reasons, which they regret. Of course, Liu also tactfully expressed his view that not entering China soon might result in missing great opportunities, but they don't seem too concerned.

For example, when Facebook discussed this topic with Liu, they didn't emphasize influence but rather technology and innovation. In their view, influence is a result of innovation and technology, so as long as the product is good, it can conquer users with the product itself, rather than through timing.

The second thing that left a deep impression on Liu was that social media marketing in the U.S. has been developing for several years, with very rich marketing tools specifically for e-commerce, allowing for extensive analysis and distribution.

Still using Facebook as an example, for instance, inputting a lifestyle information advertising value would yield a large and continuously adjusting set of computational data, knowing the main satisfaction of lifestyle information advertising values. Such a simple query task in China requires dozens of online marketing personnel + numerous third-party marketing companies.

Looking into the reasons for the differences, Liu believes that Chinese social media still mainly focuses on traffic in its early stages of development. Moreover, Chinese internet media remains concentrated on CPM (Cost Per One Thousand Impressions), CPA (Cost Per Action), etc., settlement models, with relatively low involvement of CPS (Cost Per Sale).

Furthermore, since marketing companies are unwilling to accept the CPS method, no one studies related marketing tools. However, this seems to be just a process of acceptance. Liu firmly believes that marketing tools based on CPS will have significant development in the future, with a potential explosion point in the second half of 2012.

Returning to the B2C e-commerce topic, Liu believes there is still a significant gap between China and the U.S.: American suppliers can guarantee inventory, and third-party logistics companies almost ensure 100% customer satisfaction. In the U.S., "it's hard to provide poor user experience even if you try." However, for example, at JD.com, third-party courier services account for 30% of deliveries but bring over 12 times more complaints than self-operated couriers.

"In the U.S., doing an e-commerce company means focusing on what you need to do well. But in China, simply doing your job well isn't enough; you also need to work with suppliers, handle delivery, manage manufacturers' after-sales service, and deal with payment issues. These tasks aren't actually part of our e-commerce responsibilities, but in China, you must do them all."

Previously, Liu's most famous statement was that one should not start an e-commerce business without at least 1 billion RMB. The recent 1.5 billion USD financing completed by JD.com will primarily be invested in logistics, warehousing, and research and development. (Host: Cui Xi)

Below is the interview transcript:

Host Cui Xi: Hello everyone, welcome to Sina Tech's New Forces in E-commerce interview. Today, we're delighted to have JD.com's CEO, Mr. Richard Liu, to share with us his experiences from his recent overseas exchange with some internet tech companies.

Mr. Liu, it's been a while since we've seen you. You were probably engaged in overseas inspection activities recently. Was this your first formal, large-scale visit to these internet companies?

Liu Qiangdong: Hello everyone, I'm Liu Qiangdong from JD.com. This is my second time visiting the U.S.; I’ve been before, but mostly in group meeting formats, so there wasn’t much one-on-one private exchange. It was mainly meetings.

Host: So, this time you visited many companies. Why did you decide on such a plan? What were your expectations before departure?

Liu Qiangdong: This trip was arranged by one of our shareholders, DST, because quite a few companies are their investments. Our primary goal for this visit was to examine how emerging social media could influence future e-commerce marketing. Because in China, including platforms like Sina Weibo, traffic is high, but there is no mature marketing model yet, whereas the U.S. has done very well in this aspect. Therefore, the main purpose of this visit was to learn about this.

Host: Could you share which specific companies you visited? From your Weibo posts, I saw Zynga and Facebook, both DST investments, and Twitter. Were there any others?

Liu Qiangdong: Yes, we also visited Groupon and Google.

Host: I also saw that you visited an e-commerce warehouse.

Liu Qiangdong: The warehouse we visited belongs to GSI, which was recently acquired by eBay as an e-commerce service provider.

Host: You mentioned that this trip was mainly to study the marketing situation of new social media. Are there any specific interesting viewpoints you'd like to share with us?

Liu Qiangdong: First, I felt a strong desire among these social media platforms to enter the Chinese market. However, due to the country’s political environment and various review processes, they can't enter, resulting in the loss of access to billions of internet users, which they deeply regret. That's my first feeling.

Secondly, social media marketing in the U.S. has been going on for several years, and their marketing tools, especially those targeting e-commerce, are very rich. They include third-party tools and their own marketing platforms, with extensive analysis and distribution capabilities, leaving a deep impression on us.

Host: China's internet should be said to be synchronized with the world. However, in specific aspects of marketing and profitability, the U.S. is still ahead. What do you think are the biggest differences between our social media and those in the U.S.?

Liu Qiangdong: Currently, China's social media mainly focuses on traffic in the earliest stages of development, while in the U.S., their traffic is already very substantial. For instance, Facebook's total online duration has surpassed Google. Its page views and traffic also exceed Google. Therefore, the display opportunities far outweigh Google. Consequently, countless third-party marketers supply a rich variety of services, which currently doesn't exist in China. The marketing supply services we use, such as those introduced by iResearch, are not yet fully functional for social media.

Host: Are there any specific tools that caught your interest?

Liu Qiangdong: For example, you can directly input an LI value in the backend. If JD.com requires LI to reach 15, then you only need to input the number 15, and after that, all displays on Facebook, when and to whom they appear, do not require manual intervention. There will be a very large and constantly adjusting computation until your LI target is met. This saves marketing companies a lot of costs. Currently, JD.com employs more than ten online marketing personnel and also hires a large number of third-party marketing companies to provide dedicated marketing services. In the U.S., with such tools, you only need to input a number. You can sit at home all day and not worry about anything, ensuring that every penny you spend brings returns, and the return exceeds 15.

Host: What are the reasons for the gap in between? Is it because China's marketing capabilities in this area are not strong enough, or possibly because Chinese internet companies were not open enough before?

Liu Qiangdong: I believe one reason is that Chinese internet media has not yet widely adopted the CPS network model. Many companies are still focused on past settlement methods like CPA, CPM, CPC, etc. In contrast, the U.S. marketing companies are relatively mature, with many companies already adopting CPS as the settlement model for marketing, especially representative e-commerce companies. This is the key difference.

Secondly, media companies, including display media companies, have not reached this level of understanding. Consequently, third-party marketing tools based on CPS as a settlement method are significantly behind. Since marketing companies are unwilling to accept this method, no one develops such tools. In the U.S., they have accepted it, naturally leading to a large number of third-party developments of such tools, making this type of advertising much more prosperous in the U.S. than in China.

Host: But this kind of situation isn't something JD.com alone can change, right?
