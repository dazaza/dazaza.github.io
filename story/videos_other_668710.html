Phobia (phobia, phobic neurosis) is a type of neurosis characterized primarily by symptoms of fear. Patients experience intense and unnecessary fear towards certain specific objects, accompanied by avoidance behavior. The object of fear may be single or multiple, such as animals, squares, closed rooms, heights, or social activities. Patients know that their reactions are unreasonable but cannot control them and they recur frequently. So, what kind of phobia do you have? Below, we will introduce some basic types of phobia to see which one applies to you.

Social Phobia: Mainly the fear of appearing in front of a crowd, especially being sensitive to being noticed. They dare not go to public places, showing a lack of confidence. Some patients feel uncoordinated with their environment, fearing that they will tremble, blush, sweat excessively, or behave clumsily, drawing attention to themselves. Therefore, they are always unwilling to walk out quietly from a meeting, dare not eat opposite others in a restaurant, never sit face-to-face with others, and especially avoid talking to others. Blushing phobia is a relatively common type where patients feel shy, uneasy, awkward, clumsy, and slow in public places, fearing becoming the subject of ridicule. Some patients are afraid to look into others' eyes, fearing encountering others' gazes, known as gaze phobia.

Simple Phobia: Simple phobia is a common type, often occurring during childhood. For example, fear of spiders, snakes, heights, darkness, thunderstorms, etc. Those who fear thunderstorms not only dread the storms but also feel strong uneasiness when the weather is cloudy or humid, possibly leading to thunderstorms. To relieve anxiety, some actively leave these places to avoid thunderstorms.

Agoraphobia: Not only fear of public places but also worry about being unable to quickly leave crowded areas or seek help, causing anxiety. These public places include train stations, supermarkets, barbershops, and movie theaters. Therefore, patients of this type prefer staying at home and rarely go out to avoid restlessness and irritability.

Additionally, those with claustrophobia fear small enclosed spaces, like elevators, subways, trains, and ships.
