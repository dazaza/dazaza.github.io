The causes of computer failures are complex and difficult to predict. Due to the absolute complexity of Windows operating system components, when a computer fails, it is almost impossible for general users to correctly identify the cause of the failure. Does this mean that we are completely at a loss when encountering computer failures? In fact, it is not so. Although there are many reasons for computer failures, as long as we carefully observe and summarize, we can still grasp some rules and handling methods for computer failures. In this booklet, we will present the diagnosis and maintenance methods for the most common and typical computer failures. Through it, you will find - the method to solve computer failures is right by your side, simple but effective!

One: Motherboard

The motherboard is a key component of the entire computer and plays a crucial role in its operation. If the motherboard fails, it will affect the entire PC system. Below, let's take a look at the most common faults encountered during the use of the motherboard.

Common Fault One: No display on startup

When the computer starts up with no display, the first thing we need to check is the BIOS. The motherboard's BIOS stores important hardware data, and the BIOS is also a relatively fragile part of the motherboard, easily susceptible to damage. Once damaged, it can lead to the system being unable to run, and such faults are generally caused by the motherboard BIOS being corrupted by the CIH virus (of course, it cannot be ruled out that a motherboard fault causes the system to fail to run). Generally, after the BIOS is corrupted by a virus, all the data on the hard drive will be lost. Therefore, we can determine whether the BIOS has been corrupted by checking if the hard drive data is intact. If the hard drive data is intact, then there are three other reasons that can cause the no display phenomenon:

1. Because of problems with the motherboard expansion slot or expansion card, causing no display after plugging in an expansion card like a sound card.

2. Incorrect CPU frequency settings in the CMOS for jumper-free motherboards may also cause a no display fault. This can be resolved by clearing the CMOS. The CMOS jumper is usually near the motherboard's lithium battery, with its default position generally shorting 1 and 2. Changing it to short 2 and 3 for a few seconds will resolve the issue. For older motherboards, if the user cannot find the jumper, simply removing the battery, entering the CMOS settings upon startup, and then shutting down and reinstalling the battery will achieve the goal of discharging the CMOS.

3. The motherboard cannot recognize the memory, the memory is damaged, or the memory is incompatible, which can also cause the no display fault upon startup. Some older motherboards are quite picky about memory, and once they plug in memory that the motherboard cannot recognize, the motherboard will fail to boot, and sometimes certain motherboards won't even give you any fault prompts (beeps). Of course, sometimes in order to reduce memory to improve system performance, inserting different brands or types of memory can also lead to this kind of fault. Therefore, attention should be paid during inspection.

For BIOS corruption faults, we can insert an ISA graphics card to see if there is a display (if there is a prompt, follow the steps indicated). If there is no startup screen, you can create an automatic BIOS update floppy disk to refresh the BIOS. However, sometimes after the motherboard BIOS is damaged, the floppy drive doesn't work at all. In this case, you can try the hot-swap method to resolve it (I have tried it before; as long as the BIOS is the same, it can be successfully burned on motherboards of the same level.). But using the hot-swap method not only requires identical BIOS but may also damage some components on the motherboard. Therefore, a reliable method is to use a coder to write the BIOS update file into the BIOS (you can ask a computer merchant who provides this service to solve it safely).

Common Fault Two: CMOS settings cannot be saved

This type of fault is generally caused by insufficient voltage from the motherboard battery. Replacing it should fix the issue, but sometimes replacing the battery does not solve the problem. In such cases, there are two possibilities:

1. A motherboard circuit issue, which requires professional repair;

2. A CMOS jumper issue on the motherboard. Sometimes due to incorrect settings of the CMOS jumper to the erase option or external battery setting, the CMOS data cannot be retained.

Common Fault Three: After installing the motherboard driver program under Windows, system crashes or the optical drive reading speed slows down

This phenomenon sometimes occurs on some off-brand motherboards. After installing the motherboard driver program, the computer cannot enter the normal mode of Windows 98 desktop upon reboot, and the driver program cannot be uninstalled under Windows 98. If this happens, it is recommended to find the latest driver and reinstall it. Generally, the problem can be solved. If it really cannot be fixed, the only option is to reinstall the system.

Common Fault Four: Mouse is unusable when installing or starting Windows

The software cause of this type of fault is generally due to incorrect CMOS settings. In the power management section of the CMOS settings, there is an item called modem use IRQ. Its options are 3, 4, 5... NA. Generally, the default option is 3. Setting it to an interrupt other than 3 will solve the problem.

Common Fault Five: Frequent system crashes, even during CMOS settings

System crashes during CMOS operations are generally due to problems with the motherboard or CPU. If the following methods do not solve the fault, then the motherboard or CPU needs to be replaced.

Such faults are generally caused by issues with the motherboard cache or poor motherboard design leading to inadequate heat dissipation. I have encountered this fault on an 815EP motherboard due to insufficient cooling. After touching the motherboard components around the CPU after a crash, I found them extremely hot. After replacing with a high-power fan, the crash fault was resolved. For cache-related faults, we can enter the CMOS settings and disable the cache to smoothly solve the problem. Of course, disabling the cache will definitely affect speed.

Common Fault Six: COM port, parallel port, IDE port on the motherboard malfunction

Such faults are generally caused by users connecting or disconnecting related hardware while powered on. At this point, users can use a multifunction card as a replacement, but must first disable the COM port and parallel port built into the motherboard (on some motherboards, even the IDE port needs to be disabled to function properly).

Graphics Card

Common Fault One: No display on startup

This fault is generally caused by poor contact between the graphics card and the motherboard or issues with the motherboard slot. For some integrated graphics motherboards, if the video memory shares the main memory, attention should be paid to the position of the memory module. Generally, there should be a memory module in the first memory slot. Due to graphics card reasons causing no display on startup, a long beep followed by two short beeps is usually heard after startup (for AWARD BIOS graphics cards).

Common Fault Two: Screen distortion, unclear handwriting

This fault is generally caused by the monitor or graphics card not supporting high resolution. When distortion occurs, you can switch the startup mode to Safe Mode, then enter Display Settings in Windows 98, select "Apply" and "OK" in the 16-color state. Restart the computer, then delete the graphics card driver in Normal Mode under Windows 98 and restart the computer. Alternatively, without entering Safe Mode, edit the SYSTEM.INI file in pure DOS environment, change display.drv=pnpdrver to display.drv=vga.drv, save and exit, then update the driver in Windows.

Common Fault Three: Abnormal color display, possible reasons include:

1. Poor contact between the graphics card and the monitor signal cable

2. Monitor itself fault

3. Color appears abnormal when running certain software, commonly seen in old machines. There is an option in BIOS to calibrate colors, enabling it can help.

4. Graphics card damage

5. Monitor magnetization, generally caused by being too close to magnetic objects, which may also cause screen distortion.

Common Fault Four: System crash

Such faults are often due to incompatibility between the motherboard and graphics card or poor contact between them. Incompatibility between the graphics card and other expansion cards can also cause crashes.

Common Fault Five: Strange spots or patterns appear on the screen

This fault is generally caused by issues with the graphics card's video memory or poor contact between the graphics card and the motherboard. Cleaning the gold fingers of the graphics card or replacing the card can solve the problem.

Common Fault Six: Loss of graphics card driver

After loading the graphics card driver and running for a period of time, the driver automatically disappears. This fault is generally due to poor quality of the graphics card or incompatibility with the motherboard, causing the graphics card temperature to be too high, leading to unstable system operation or crashes. In this case, the graphics card needs to be replaced.

Additionally, there is a special situation where the graphics card driver could be loaded before, but after loading the driver, the system crashes when entering Windows. This can be solved by replacing with another model of graphics card, loading its driver, and then inserting the old card. If this still cannot solve the fault, it indicates a registry issue, which can be resolved by restoring the registry or reinstalling the operating system.

Sound Card

Common Fault One: No sound from the sound card. Common reasons for this fault include:

1. Default output set to "mute" in the driver. Click on the small speaker icon in the lower right corner of the screen, a volume adjustment slider will appear, with a "mute" option below. Click the checkbox in front to remove the tick mark, and normal sound will be restored.

2. Conflict between the sound card and other inserted cards. The solution is to adjust the system resources used by the PnP card to ensure each card does not interfere with each other. Sometimes, opening "Device Manager", although no yellow exclamation marks (conflict indicators) are seen, the sound card still does not produce sound, indicating a conflict exists but the system did not detect it.

3. No sound after installing DirectX. This indicates poor compatibility between the sound card and DirectX, requiring an updated driver.

4. One channel is silent. Check if the audio line from the sound card to the speaker is broken.

Common Fault Two: Excessive noise from the sound card. Common reasons for this fault include:

1. Improper insertion of the card. Due to insufficient precision in the case manufacturing, poor manufacturing or installation of the sound card's outer panel leads to poor contact between the sound card and the motherboard expansion slot. This is visible on both ISA and PCI cards, and is a common fault. It can generally be corrected with pliers.

2. Active speakers connected to the sound card's Speaker output. For active speakers, they should be connected to the Line out end of the sound card, which outputs signals that have not passed through the amplifier on the sound card, resulting in less noise. Some sound cards have only one output, determined by the jumpers on the card, with the default setting often being Speaker, so the sound card needs to be removed to adjust the jumpers.

3. Poor built-in Windows driver. When installing the sound card driver, choose "driver provided by the manufacturer" instead of "default Windows driver". If installing via "Add New Hardware", choose "Install from Disk" instead of selecting from the list. If the built-in Windows driver has already been installed, go to "Control Panel → System → Device Manager → Sound, Video and Game Controllers", select each sub-device, click "Properties → Driver → Change Driver → Install from Disk". Insert the disk or CD-ROM provided with the sound card to install the manufacturer's driver.

Common Fault Three: Sound card cannot be plug-and-play

1. Use the latest drivers or alternative programs. I once had a sound card that wouldn't install the original driver disk in Windows 98, so I had to use Creative SB16 drivers, which worked fine. Later, when upgrading to Windows Me, it didn't work again, so I had to use the built-in sound card driver from Windows 2000 (full version) to make it work.

2. The biggest headache is when Windows 9X detects a plug-and-play device but insists on installing its own driver, which doesn't work. Each time you delete and reinstall, the same problem recurs, and it cannot be solved using "Add New Hardware". Here's a secret trick: enter the Win9xinfother directory, delete all *.inf files related to the sound card, and then restart and manually install. This works 100% of the time and has saved countless sound cards... Of course, editing the registry can also achieve the same result.

3. Installation of non-PnP sound cards (also applies to PnP sound cards that cannot be installed via the above PnP method): Go to "Control Panel" / "Add New Hardware" / "Next", when prompted "Do you want Windows to search for new hardware?", choose "No", then select "Sound, Video and Game Controllers" and use the driver disk or directly select the sound card type to install.

Common Fault Four: No sound when playing CDs

1. Completely silent. Using Windows 98's "CD Player" to play CDs results in silence, but the "CD Player" works normally, indicating that the audio line of the optical drive is not properly connected. Use a 4-core audio line to connect the analog audio output of the CD-ROM to the CD-in on the sound card, which is usually included when purchasing a CD-ROM.

2. Only one channel is audible. The output of the optical drive generally has left and right lines, with the middle two lines being ground lines. Since the four lines of the audio signal line usually have different colors, you can find the corresponding interface based on the color of the lines. If the sound card has only one interface or none of the interfaces match the audio line, you need to modify the wiring sequence of the audio line. Usually, just swapping two lines will suffice.

Common Fault Five: PCI sound card produces popping sounds

This is generally caused by the Bus Master technology used in PCI graphics cards, amplifying background noise during hard drive read/write and mouse movements on the PCI bus. Solution: Turn off the Bus Master function of the PCI graphics card, replace it with an AGP graphics card, and change the PCI sound card slot.

Common Fault Six: Unable to record normally

First, check if the microphone is incorrectly plugged into another socket. Next, double-click the small speaker, select "Properties → Recording" from the menu, and check if all settings are correct. Then, in "Control Panel → Multimedia → Devices", adjust the "Mixer Device" and "Line Input Device" to set them to "Use" status. If "Multimedia → Audio" has the "Recording" option grayed out, it's problematic. You can try adding "ISA Plug and Play bus" in "Add New Hardware → System Devices", or simply install the tool software that comes with the sound card and restart.

Common Fault Seven: Unable to play Wav music, Midi music

Unable to play Wav music is relatively common, often due to multiple "Audio Devices" listed under "Multimedia" → "Devices"; disabling one should resolve the issue. Unable to play MIDI files could be due to the following three possibilities:

1. Early ISA sound cards might be incompatible between 16-bit and 32-bit modes, causing abnormal MIDI playback. Installing software wavetables should solve the problem.

2. Modern PCI sound cards mostly use wavetable synthesis technology. If the MIDI section cannot produce sound, it might be because you haven't loaded the appropriate wavetable sound library.

3. The MIDI channel in the Windows Volume Control is set to mute mode.

Common Fault Eight: PCI sound card malfunctions in WIN98

Some users report that during the sound card driver installation process, everything seems normal, with no device conflicts appearing. However, in WIN98, the sound card either doesn't produce sound or exhibits other issues. This phenomenon typically occurs with PCI sound cards. Check which PCI slot the PCI sound card was installed in during the installation process. Some users, considering heat dissipation, prefer to install the sound card in the PCI slots farthest from the AGP slot and closest to the ISA slots. The problem often arises here because Windows98 has a bug: sometimes it can only correctly identify sound cards in the PCI-1 and PCI-2 slots. On ATX motherboards, the two PCI slots closest to the AGP slot are PCI-1 and PCI-2 (on some ATX motherboards, the opposite is true, with the slots closest to the ISA being PCI-1), so if the PCI sound card is not installed in the correct slot, problems will occur.

Memory

Memory is one of the most important components in a computer, and its role is beyond doubt. So what are the most common memory faults?

Common Fault One: No display on startup

This fault is generally caused by poor contact between the memory stick and the motherboard's memory slot. Simply wiping the golden fingers with an eraser can solve the problem (do not use alcohol for cleaning). Another possibility is that the memory is damaged or there is an issue with the motherboard's memory slot, which can also cause this fault.

Since memory stick issues causing no display on startup, the host speaker generally emits a continuous beep (specifically for Award BIOS).

Common Fault Two: Windows registry frequently gets damaged unexpectedly, prompting the user to restore it

This fault is generally caused by poor-quality memory sticks, making it difficult to repair. The only solution is to replace the memory stick.

Common Fault Three: Windows frequently enters safe mode automatically

This fault is generally caused by incompatibility between the motherboard and memory stick or poor quality of the memory stick. This is common when high-frequency memory is used on motherboards that do not support such frequencies. You can try lowering the memory read speed in the CMOS settings to see if it solves the problem. If not, you need to replace the memory stick.

Common Fault Four: Random system crashes

This fault is generally caused by using memory sticks with different chips, resulting in a time difference due to varying memory speeds, leading to system crashes. This can be resolved by lowering the memory speed in the CMOS settings. Otherwise, you should use memory sticks of the same model. Another possibility is incompatibility between the memory stick and the motherboard, which is relatively rare. Additionally, poor contact between the memory stick and the motherboard can also cause random system crashes.

Common Fault Five: System resources decrease after increasing memory

This phenomenon is generally caused by incompatibility between the motherboard and memory. It is common when high-frequency memory is used on motherboards that do not support such frequencies. You can try lowering the memory speed in the CMOS settings.

Common Fault Six: Frequently receive prompts indicating insufficient memory when running certain software

This phenomenon is generally caused by insufficient free space on the system drive. Deleting some unnecessary files and leaving more space can resolve the issue. Generally, maintaining around 300MB of free space is advisable.

Common Fault Seven: Prompt indicating insufficient memory when installing Windows from the hard drive and detecting disk space

This fault is generally caused by including the emm386.exe file in the config.sys file. Simply disabling it will resolve the issue.

Optical Drive

The optical drive is one of the components in computer hardware with the shortest lifespan. In fact, many discarded optical drives still have significant value and can be repaired with a little effort. This often does not require advanced radio electronics knowledge or the use of complex repair tools and materials. As long as you carefully observe the fault symptoms and follow the elimination methods below, your old optical drive can likely regain its former functionality.

Common Fault One: Hard drive light stays on continuously while the optical drive is working

This is a false impression. The hard drive light flickers because the optical drive and hard drive share the same IDE interface. When the optical disc operates, it also controls the hard drive light. To solve this, connect the optical drive to its own IDE interface.

Common Fault Two: Displays "32-bit disk access failed" and crashes when operating the CD-ROM in Windows

Clearly, Windows' 32-bit disk access affects the CD-ROM. Most CD-ROMs are connected to the hard drive's IDE interface and do not support Windows' 32-bit disk access, causing internal errors and crashes in Windows. After entering Windows, double-click "Control Panel" in the "Main Group," enter the "386 Enhanced Mode" settings, click the "Virtual Memory" button, then click "Change," and turn off the "32-bit disk access" verification box in the lower-left corner. Confirm and restart Windows
