People have a very common mentality and behavior called: following the crowd. Simply put, it's going where there are more people.

When you seriously look at the goods, you will find that the goods themselves are not particularly "unique". Goods seek sameness but need to be new.

In the case of middle-to-low-end commonly known as "volume running," remember an old saying: "Goods sell in piles."

It's best to pile goods like mountains, with many styles and complete categories.

But categories do not mean having everything from men's to women's, from shirts to socks, but rather making the "high acceptance" dresses, skirts, pants for the customer group defined by your store positioning in multiple styles and small quantities.

Why choose high acceptance instead of unique styles?

So-called best-sellers are styles that most people think are good-looking and sellable, conforming to the public aesthetic view.

Everyone thinks they sell well, so they buy them, and the more they sell, the more people buy, thus becoming a trend.

But then you might ask: if everyone sells the same style, we can only compete on price, how to make more money?

So I insist on the principle of "many styles, small quantities." Every time I purchase goods, I remind myself not to rush, try them at home first, replenish if they sell well.

Is it worth the extra effort for novices to purchase goods?

Where to buy goods? This is one of the most frequently asked questions by people who want to open or are currently opening a clothing store.

Without considering all external factors, everyone would say "Guangzhou," and "Shenzhen" is also a good choice.

For us small shops with a street penetration rate of about 60%, under what circumstances is it worth going to Guangzhou, and how is it economical?

Here are a few conditions I think justify going to a primary sourcing center to purchase goods:

1. Store area greater than 15 square meters

2. Rent higher than 2000 yuan

3. Distance from the primary market (Guangzhou) within 10 hours one way

4. Besides the store owner, there is another salesperson

5. Operating time longer than three months

If three or more of these conditions are met, you can consider going to Guangzhou or Shenzhen for a visit.

Why set these criteria? These can be divided into two parts: cost calculation and market understanding.

If operating costs are low, then the overall market environment of the store may not be good, limiting sales.

If the distance is too far, the journey is arduous, unfamiliar with the place, eating, accommodation, and transportation all cost money. Two trips a month to purchase goods could cost 1,000 to 2,000 yuan, making the cost too high.

If the store is newly opened, inexperienced, unfamiliar with the sources in one aspect, unclear about customer needs in another, and inaccurate estimation of the overall market in the third, this easily leads to inventory accumulation.

Small stores themselves have thin capital and cannot withstand too much折腾or take too many detours.

I suggest new store owners adopt this approach when purchasing goods:

1. Purchase nearby

2. Frequent visits

3. Follow the right upstream supplier

4. Multiple styles, small quantities

In nearby wholesale markets, find several sources that meet the store requirements, try to concentrate purchases, visit every two to three days, figure out the timing of new arrivals, and build good relationships with the bosses. If you reach a certain amount of purchases from a particular stall, returns and exchanges can be more convenient.

Of course, it's tiring, but doing business and making money without hard work is a lie. This is better than traveling far to Guangzhou, thinking it's hard to come here, wanting to buy more. Carrying ten or eight thousand yuan, vowing not to spend it all before returning home, picking styles even if they're not good, blindly purchasing goods. Purchasing nearby actually offers more convenience and benefits than these.

After operating for three to five months, when you have a basic understanding of the store's sales volume, some knowledge of the goods, and accumulate some regular customers, pick a season-changing time when a large number of new styles arrive, prepare money, arrange the store's business, go to Guangzhou, stay for three to five days, run around, compare extensively, and then purchase goods. Believe me, this will bring your store's business to a whole new level~

Season and styles, how to accurately determine the quantity when purchasing goods?

Purchasing goods is a road that never reaches its end in your business process.

This road, whether it's flat or steep, curved or straight, easy or difficult, can only be smoothed by paying attention to the information along the way, analyzing, and being mindful.

Common big seasonal changes occur in early March and mid-September. At these times, when we purchase goods, we need to focus on the big trends first.

In March, prefer thinner over thicker, and the opposite in September. Move towards goods that can be sold for a longer period next, to maximize the sales cycle of the goods, avoiding inventory buildup caused by missing the consumption time.

Actually, controlling the quantity of purchases should be data-driven.

The formula I roughly use for calculation is:

Single purchase quantity = Monthly total sales / Number of purchases - Current seasonal inventory

This basically defines a base purchase quantity, and then combining it with sales style data, clarify which type of style to purchase and what proportion.

When I operate, combining the actual weather change patterns of southern cities and local market consumption habits, the ratio of tops to bottoms is generally 3:1. When styling, I usually skip spring jackets directly; my store is already heavily stocked with dresses, and we've been selling summer clothes for nearly a month.

I give this example to illustrate that each person faces a different market from a fine perspective, so it requires self-analysis, especially for stores where a young lady watches the store. Data accuracy becomes even more critical.

Actually, I suggest that the best scenario is to personally guard the new store for at least two months, to have a basic understanding of sales volume, customer demographics, and a clear concept of sales prices before becoming a hands-off manager.

Let me briefly explain my purchasing strategy:

Early March: Long-sleeve shirts, medium-length T-shirts, slightly thicker fabric, bright colors mainly.

Early April: Short-sleeve shirts, short-medium length T-shirts, sleeve dresses, rich patterns and colors mainly.

Early May: Large quantities of dresses, tank tops, lightweight chiffon, light and tender colors mainly.

Early June: More cotton, sweat-absorbing and breathable, light colors, small decorative color blocks mainly.

By July, it's the off-season, but summer is still long. Generally, things don't sell for good prices at this time. Basically, choose popular styles of T-shirts, various shorts, tops, skirts, etc.

By late August, clear out a large amount of inventory, and by mid-September, introduce new models to boost sales during the National Day Golden Week.
