Alibaba's listing location will still be Hong Kong only.

by anonymous on 2013-10-11 19:21:30

Gejia / Article

Alibaba CEO Jonathan Lu formally announced yesterday at a media conference the abandonment of listing in Hong Kong. He believed that Alibaba had not submitted a listing application, did not propose a dual-class share structure, and did not challenge Hong Kong's principle of one share, one vote. However, discussions and conclusions were drawn based on non-existent facts and rumors without seriously listening to Alibaba's real needs. Therefore, the group decided to abandon listing in Hong Kong.

Please note, the above statement in the press release has no subject and does not directly mention the Hong Kong Stock Exchange or the Hong Kong government. This means that Alibaba does not want to have a direct conflict with the Hong Kong government and regulatory agencies. Their reason for giving up was that they did not make unreasonable requests to modify the system but were instead falsely accused, making them feel wronged and thus forcing them to withdraw. The talk about partners and AB shares must have come out of nowhere.

I am unclear how many people understood the press release, but my interpretation is that Alibaba said to Hong Kong: "Ah Hong, I didn't ask you for those things, yet you discussed me based on what others said. Your side’s public opinion backlash is so high that I have to leave you." If you were a regulatory agency in Hong Kong, how would you understand this? Since Alibaba did not make these demands, it still met the listing conditions as long as it followed the Hong Kong system. Why refuse such a big money god?

The statement made by Alibaba today feels strange, like a scorned woman leaving home because she feels neglected by her husband but doesn't want to leave completely, leaving room for her husband to bring her back. Alibaba's usual dominance disappeared, and it began to soften its stance. Although the decision to abandon the Hong Kong IPO seems quite firm, it is as if Alibaba is saying to Hong Kong: "I originally wanted to give my heart to the bright moon, but alas, the bright moon shines upon the ditch." Since you don't appreciate me, I have to leave. I'm really leaving. Don't hold me back; I've left.

If we compare New York, Hong Kong, and Shanghai to three potential homes for Alibaba, then rejecting the home in Hong Kong makes Alibaba a homeless dog because neither New York nor Shanghai are suitable for Alibaba's listing. These two places are merely virtual listing locations used by Alibaba to exploit Hong Kong's willpower, aiming to gain a higher status within the Hong Kong home, living in better rooms, and eating separately every day. Therefore, Alibaba tirelessly promotes how good it would be to go to America and Shanghai to put pressure on the owners of the Hong Kong home.

Do you remember what Jack Ma shouted at Wall Street in 2009? Publicly insisting that shareholders come third; do you remember how Alibaba refused to disclose detailed business documents for dignity and rejected going public in the US? Do you remember the Alipay that was taken from Yahoo?

Facing Wall Street's shouting is irrelevant; it's just a matter of speaking freely. Wall Street, which prioritizes interests, doesn't mind being cursed as long as it brings profits. But there is one thing Alibaba cannot avoid, and that is the Alipay divestiture issue. After this incident erupted in 2011, domestic public opinion criticized it fiercely. Wall Street seemed to have no reaction, but you should know that investors are good at voting with their feet. They may not say anything, but they will definitely take action.

Since Jack Ma revealed the VIE cap, NASDAQ-listed Chinese stocks collectively plummeted, with a single-day drop of nearly 20%. Since then, Chinese stocks have started a long bear market, and short-selling forces have continued relentlessly. In other words, Wall Street has lost trust in Chinese stocks, remaining constantly vigilant and alert. The image of Chinese stocks and even the entire Chinese capital market has deteriorated rapidly on the international stage.

Yahoo is an old-school American tech stock with a high status but low market value. Jack Ma quietly took Alipay away, though he received tacit approval from major shareholder Yahoo, but Yahoo's small shareholders were all unaware. This matter is not over yet. Countless small shareholders, fund managers, and lawyers are waiting for Alibaba to list on NASDAQ so they can sue, earn commissions, and claim high damages. Otherwise, who would feed so many people?

This matter is beyond NASDAQ's control. Even if NASDAQ rolls out the red carpet and kowtows to bring Alibaba in, it cannot manage litigation matters. Alibaba knows this, which is why it dared to shout loudly across the sea at Wall Street before. Oh, right, any U.S. company that claims its intellectual property and product rights are infringed upon on Taobao can file a lawsuit regardless of the state or county. However, before U.S. prosecutors file a lawsuit, they usually contact the involved companies to allow negotiation resolutions. Being willing to spend money can solve the problem. As the saying goes, problems that can be solved with money are not problems, and this is especially true for Alibaba.

As for Shanghai, forget about it. As of today, the SSE Composite Index is at 2200 points, a stock market that has been running for 20 years. PetroChina fell from 48 yuan to 7 yuan, and everyone saw it. While the Shanghai Stock Exchange might help resolve some of Alibaba's legal issues, it cannot offer a high price. Moreover, even if the market capitalization were three to five hundred billion RMB, investors who have been oppressed by state-owned enterprises for 20 years may not afford it. In this era, even monopolistic enterprises like PetroChina can't fetch a good price, let alone Alibaba.

Oh, right, Alibaba is still a VIE structure and foreign-controlled, and many conditions do not comply with current securities regulatory laws. Adjustments need to be made to meet the requirements for listing on the Shanghai Stock Exchange. Agreement control inherently carries certain moral risks. Once credit is broken, who will still trust you?

In the end, neither New York nor Shanghai are suitable for Alibaba. Alibaba's true preferred listing destination is still Hong Kong. They are currently playing psychological warfare with the Hong Kong government. First comes cuteness, then coquetry, then threats, and finally some actions, managed by professional manager Jonathan Lu. By the way, isn't the IPO handler supposed to be Jack Ma? Yes, Jonathan Lu can go out and play the bad cop to threaten, while Jack Ma finds an opportunity to further communicate with Hong Kong.

Hong Kong has also gone through great storms. George Soros once boasted in Hong Kong, with trillions of dollars in his Quantum Fund, ready to flood Hong Kong. It was indeed tough at the time, but in the end, Soros didn't gain much in Hong Kong and left disappointed. Jack Ma wants to play psychological warfare with Hong Kong, mocking, criticizing, threatening, and complaining, grossly underestimating the intelligence of the people of Hong Kong.

Therefore, Jonathan Lu's current statement of abandoning the Hong Kong IPO can basically be ignored. It is a PR tactic used to deceive the Hong Kong government and the media. Alibaba's real intention is still to list in Hong Kong because only Hong Kong can give Alibaba a high market value, fewer legal lawsuits, and no need for Alibaba to adjust its equity structure and governance system.

As long as the Hong Kong government sticks to its principles and ignores such childish psychological warfare, Jack Ma's resolve will collapse in the coming months. At that point, he will lower his pride, actively show goodwill towards the Hong Kong government, and show goodwill towards the people of Hong Kong.
