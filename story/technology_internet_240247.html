Recently, the term "Internet of Things" has started to appear in various media reports, including CCTV-2's Economic Half Hour.

What exactly is this so-called "Internet of Things"? The "Internet of Things" (IoT) refers to a massive network formed by integrating various information sensing devices—such as RFID devices, infrared sensors, global positioning systems, laser scanners, etc.—with the internet. Its purpose is to connect all objects to the network for easier identification and management. The IoT is established using ubiquitous networking technology. [For more details, see the explanation on Baidu Baike.]

When I was a child, I was very fascinated by an animated series: *Transformers*, which frequently mentioned the term "sensor." Transformers are "living humanoid machines" that can change their shapes. Since they are machines, how do they perceive information from the external world? Through sensors. So, is a Transformer itself an example of the "Internet of Things"?

Furthermore, nowadays, video surveillance systems are installed in various places such as residential areas, entertainment venues, parking lots, and highways, all connected through multiple lines to a monitoring center. Isn't this also a typical example of the "Internet of Things"?

Therefore, the concept of the "Internet of Things" is absolutely not a new innovation. We know that the human body has five senses: sight, hearing, smell, taste, and touch. Our relationship with the "Internet of Things" is akin to the relationship between the brain and the rest of the body. Different sensory organs on the body receive information and transmit it to the brain through the nervous system. After receiving signals from different senses, the brain responds and directs the body to perform the correct actions. Conceptually speaking, the idea of the "Internet of Things" has been around for a long time. This is a bronze mask unearthed from the ancient Shu site of Sanxingdui, located north of Chengdu in Sichuan province, dating back about four thousand years. The mask's eyes are prominently protruding and elongated, symbolizing the ancients' hope to see further, while its large ears represent their wish for "千里眼，顺风耳" (a far-seeing eye and a wind-assisted ear). Later, we developed telescopes, radios, televisions, and more...

If the "Internet of Things" expands sufficiently to connect with all objects in daily life, then the capabilities of the human body will also be infinitely expanded. As seen in many movies, intelligent machines may eventually rebel against humans.

So, what is the value of the "Internet of Things"? Is it merely a buzzword? Perhaps we can leave this topic for netizens to discuss.
