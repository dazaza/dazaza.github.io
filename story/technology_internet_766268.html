Nokia Sells Phone Business: The Finns' Sense of Loss and Relief

by anonymous on 2013-09-11 13:14:42

In the Finnish epic Kalevala, there is a magical machine called Sampo which can produce money, bread, and salt. However, this machine was stolen. In the battle to retrieve the treasure, it was shattered and lost in the sea. Starting from a small paper mill, Nokia's 150-year history can be considered a modern epic. It was once the largest mobile phone producer in the world, controlling 40% of the global market, but within a few short years, it missed the opportunity to develop smartphones and was unable to prevent the domino effect of its downfall. It had been regarded as the Sampo of the tech world, ultimately unable to escape the fate foretold in myth.

For Finns, Nokia’s decline is not just an economic issue, but also an emotional one. The Guardian interviewed former Nokia employees and other Finns to understand their views on the sale of Nokia’s mobile phone business.

Päivi Tuohimaa worked at Nokia for 14 years as a senior manager. She said, “For me, this is a very sad day. For many years, I have been responsible for Nokia brand marketing. I am deeply saddened to see that all of this will be destroyed. But life must go on.”

Alf Rehn from Åbo Akademi University believes that the emotional impact of this event is immense, “This marks the end of an era, and because both Finland's internal and external image are tied to Nokia, this will change the nation's spirit.”

Politically, Finland's opposition party, the "True Finns," has already voiced concerns. Its leader Timo Soini questions the low price at which Nokia’s mobile phone business was sold. He calls it a "strategic failure." As the mayor of Espoo, Soini also worries about the impact on the local economy, “Losing Nokia’s mobile phone business is a blow to Espoo and the entire country.”

Some Finns blame the company's CEO for the sale of Nokia's mobile phone business. Because he had previously served as a Microsoft executive, he is viewed as a "Trojan horse" whose purpose was to destroy Nokia. Alf Rehn from Åbo Akademi University does not agree with this view. He says he does not believe in conspiracy theories, “Doing such things would be extremely risky, highly unethical, and even illegal. Don't forget, without the board's support, the CEO could not have accomplished all these things.”

However, he is certain that any CEO would have various plans, and what has happened should be one of them, “Elop made some strategic decisions that clearly favored this deal and made it more attractive to Microsoft. But I don’t think his sole purpose at Nokia was to carry out a trick.”

During Nokia’s prosperous period, Esko Aho served as Prime Minister of Finland and later became Senior Vice President of the company. He believes that this matter will have positive impacts on Nokia, Finland, and Microsoft. He says that in the short term, the impact won’t be significant because the transaction won’t be completed until early 2014. In the long term, it depends on both parties’ strategies. Microsoft has stronger resources and marketing capabilities, so it has a greater chance of success. Additionally, in Microsoft's collaborations with European companies, they often retain local resources, so there is no need to worry about employment issues in the short term.

Among the employees at Nokia’s Espoo headquarters, there is a mix of grief and relief. Pekka Siltala-Li, a consultant who once collaborated with Nokia, says that many people were waiting for something to happen, “One option was bankruptcy. Now, people at least know what is going to happen, rather than being completely uncertain about the future.” Apurva Jaiswal, a software engineer who worked at Nokia for two years, says that people have gradually accepted the reality.

“Some people are crying over Nokia now,” says Liisa Rysa, who worked in Nokia’s IT department for ten years, “Why didn’t they cry five or seven years ago when something could still be done to save it? Many times, we buried our heads in the sand. Our tradition is not to discuss Nokia’s problems with outsiders.”

Without Nokia’s mobile phones, what else does Finland have that captures global attention? Perhaps Rovio, the company behind Angry Birds. It has indeed become a global phenomenon, but can it become a major player in the entertainment industry, especially in such a competitive environment?

In Liisa Rysa’s view, the sale of the mobile phone business might prompt Nokia’s Finnish employees to venture out and create new businesses. For these individuals, excessive identification with Nokia was a constraint, and now they can break free. “The attitude towards Nokia is emotional rather than rational. Finland doesn’t have royalty or crown jewels, and Nokia became a substitute.”
