Synopsis:

The main cast includes Kim Hye-ja, Won Bin, Jin Goo, and Yoon Jae-won. A mother (played by Kim Hye-ja) who has been widowed for many years lives with her only son, Tae-woo (played by Won Bin). Despite being 28 years old, Tae-woo's intellectual disability leaves him with the innocence of a child. One day, a shocking murder case occurs, and unfortunately, Tae-woo becomes the prime suspect without any reason. Ignorant of everything, Tae-woo is taken away by the police and unknowingly confesses to the crime! To save her son, the mother, knowing it’s like hitting a rock with an egg, comes from the countryside to the city, uncovering one dark secret after another...

Good or not? Neither side feels compelled to express either way. "Mother" is a competent film, not more, not less.

—Totalfilm

Bong Joon-ho successfully creates an extreme yet fluid character to study the raw essence of motherhood. Meanwhile, "Mother" is a brilliant murder mystery filled with sudden twists that always occur at just the right moment.

—Hollywood Reporter

Without the subtlety of "Memories of Murder," Bong Joon-ho still demonstrates the same skillful technique in unraveling this rural murder case, progressing naturally throughout the story. The actors are outstanding; Kim Hye-ja delivers a restrained performance despite her madness, showcasing surprising physical strength when necessary. Won Bin seamlessly integrates into the complex layers of his intellectually delayed role, while Jin Goo injects the necessary intensity into his bad-boy character.

—Variety

In Bong Joon-ho's scene direction, through the reversal of light and shadow in the imagery, he subtly influences the psychological state of the audience, naturally immersing them in the "illusion" he creates. Unlike Park Chan-wook's extreme portrayal of family relationships in "Thirst," the protective sentiment towards a family member who has made mistakes in "Mother" resonates more with ordinary people.

—Daum

Kim Hye-ja's mad acting shines brilliantly, somewhat overshadowing Won Bin's quiet and introspective performance due to the excellent script. Beyond carrying the label of a "flower boy," Won Bin's performance in "Mother" proves that he is an actor worthy of high expectations in the future.

—Daum

With multiple plot twists, the protective shield of a mother willing to sacrifice herself for her son, the psychological defense structure of the "idiot," the destruction and revival of memory, and the cycle of victim and perpetrator... As familiar as Bong Joon-ho's style, the themes are numerous and controversial. The nickname of the murdered high school girl is also unsettling.

Stacked with cruel imagery and the premise that "a mother's love can turn her into a beast losing sanity," "Mother" lacks the profound themes and dignified presence of Bong Joon-ho's previous works like "Memories of Murder" and "The Host." Behind the Scenes:

"Wanted to make the most tragic crime movie in the world"

After gaining fame with "Memories of Murder," Bong Joon-ho amazed audiences again with "The Host" in 2006, setting box office records that remain unbeaten. Industry comments suggest, "Perhaps only Bong Joon-ho himself can break the miracles he has created"—highlighting the high expectations for "Mother." The success of "The Host" was praised for its comprehensive Hollywood-style storytelling combined with a strong national identity theme. Balancing entertainment with meaningful reflection, this approach has been key to his films' critical and commercial success.

In both early works like "Memories of Murder" and "Kidnapping Policeman," as well as recent ones like "The Host," government and police forces often fail to be saviors for civilians, instead unintentionally pressuring or even persecuting vulnerable groups. "I want to create the most tragic crime movie in the world"—as Bong Joon-ho stated, the hero in "Mother" is not the typical shrewd detective or a righteous passerby but a frail elderly mother. Upholding his anti-genre spirit, much of the film focuses on the intimate, almost symbiotic relationship between mother and son. After the division of the Korean Peninsula, South Korea underwent tremendous changes under the influence of Western material technology and cultural contexts. Growing up in such a historical background, Bong Joon-ho places the unspoken dual cultural personality into his films: while inevitably relying on Western power to spur local development, the fervent nationalism quietly deforms under the state's will, leading to the fading and decline of people-centered ideologies, causing distress among intellectuals. Under this contradictory psychology, Bong Joon-ho subtly integrates his critique and even resistance towards modern Korean society and Western trends into engaging storylines, a sophisticated narrative style unmatched by others.

"What exactly is a mother?"

"Mother" kept its themes under wraps, sparking speculation. In a recent interview with Cine21, Bong Joon-ho clarified that unlike "Memories of Murder" and "The Host," "Mother" does not comment on societal or era changes but purely focuses on the relationship between mother and son. "Mother" returns to the fundamental question: "Parent-child is the closest and most complex relationship; what exactly is a mother?" The film presents his understanding of these questions.

Under the financial tsunami, the trend of the Korean box office took on a peculiar shape. Expensive productions like "Cell Phone," "Operation," and "Sea Boy" failed to impress the market, whereas small-scale productions focusing on family and kinship like "Scandal Makers" and "Sound of the Bell" surprisingly gained favor with audiences. Much of the appeal of "The Host" drawing families of all ages into cinemas stemmed from its return to family-based themes. "Mother" may appear as a crime film but centers around the immense energy released by a long-suffering mother after her son is unjustly accused, transitioning from silent obedience to powerful emotion. It could be said that "Mother" launched at a time when everyone craved warmth and courage amid fragile psychological defenses, making its timing perfect.

The concept for "Mother" came to Bong Joon-ho while serving as a jury member at the Jeonju International Film Festival. He suddenly had inspiration upon returning to his hotel and began writing. Kim, the representative who had previously produced "Memories of Murder" and collaborated with the director for many years, revealed that receiving the final draft of "Memories of Murder" from Bong Joon-ho was fraught with delays—initially promised for March 1st (Korean Independence Movement Day), it was postponed until Arbor Day, then beyond Mother's Day and Constitution Day, finally arriving on Liberation Day. Bong Joon-ho's pursuit of perfection ensured no hasty completion. This time, the "error range" for completing the "Mother" script let Kim breathe a sigh of relief: "It was expected not to be completed by the agreed-upon date of March 15th, but fortunately, the wait was only four days."

Fun Facts:

Kim Hye-ja: Convinced by Bong Joon-ho's Sincere Efforts

Chinese audiences might recognize Kim Hye-ja from her role as the elegant and slightly mischievous Empress in the popular 2006 drama "My Princess." In South Korea, she is a renowned veteran actress, having starred in the longest-running TV series in Korean history, "Diary of a Country Girl," and recently winning her third Best Actress award at the Korean Baeksang Arts Awards for "Mommy Has Horns." "Mother" marks Kim Hye-ja's long-awaited return to the big screen since "Late Autumn" in 1981 and "Mayonnaise" in 1999.

Unlike Won Bin, who immediately accepted the invitation after reading the script, Bong Joon-ho had to visit Kim Hye-ja multiple times to persuade her to join the project. Bong Joon-ho admitted that although the script wasn't specifically written for Kim Hye-ja, "the idea of casting Kim Hye-ja as the mother grew stronger as I wrote." Contrary to public perception of her as gentle and virtuous, Bong Joon-ho described Kim Hye-ja as a "four-dimensional mom." He first met her idol in 1992 during a university activity at Hongik University, where he saw her taking advantage of living nearby to sneak home to use the bathroom while shooting a TV drama, "wearing slippers, plotting something mysterious—a memory I still vividly recall." Bong Joon-ho highlighted Kim Hye-ja's unexpected quirky, sharp, and intelligent side, aiming to portray a mother different from the stereotypical submissive, overly understanding, and sometimes weak "Korean mom."

After the success of "Memories of Murder," Bong Joon-ho was often asked in interviews, "As a now influential director in Chungmuro, who would you most like to collaborate with next?" While answering with popular actresses like Jun Ji-hyun would have resulted in headlines like 'Bong Joon-ho Sends LOVECALL to Jun Ji-hyun,' he chose honesty: "The actor I most want to work with is Kim Hye-ja." Many journalists dismissed this heartfelt statement as a joke at the time.

In 2004, Bong Joon-ho formally expressed his desire to collaborate with Kim Hye-ja. Due to delays in the "Mother" script and other commitments, the collaboration was finalized about a month before filming began. Kim Hye-ja, whose "Late Autumn" won her Best Actress at the Manila International Film Festival and whose critically acclaimed "Mayonnaise" didn't perform well at the box office, bluntly asked Bong Joon-ho upon their first meeting, "Do you think 'Mother' can achieve box office success like 'The Host'?" Interestingly, during filming, Bong Joon-ho discovered Kim Hye-ja's lesser-known youthful side: "She can sit gazing out the window daydreaming for hours, like a 20-year-old girl."

Won Bin: Exceeding Expectations

Even Kim Hye-ja couldn't help but be captivated by Won Bin's flawless appearance. After enlisting in 2005 and retiring prematurely in 2006 due to a torn knee ligament, Won Bin planned to return in 2006 but followed medical advice to rest. During nearly three years, apart from occasional public service activities and fashion endorsements, the once-celebrated "handsome talent" was rarely seen. Among numerous script offers, he ultimately chose the role of Tae-woo, an intellectually disabled young man, a decision that surprised many, "after years of absence, expecting him to choose a heroic or at least flamboyant role."

"Anyone seeing Won Bin for the first time would surely be drawn to his natural and refreshing charm," emphasized Bong Joon-ho, noting that while Won Bin was perceived as a "gentleman," his upbringing in Gangwon-do gave him a rustic side: "He told me about catching snakes in the woods on his way to school in middle school." Bong Joon-ho assured that with Won Bin's experience living in rural areas, there was no need to worry about his ability to portray the rural flavor.

Initially, Won Bin left a quiet and reserved impression on the staff, gradually warming up over time: "But you can see his focused demeanor when acting." Crew members noted that direct dialogues between Won Bin and the director were rare, reflecting Bong Joon-ho's trust in his acting. At the end of 2008, "Mother" was sold to Japan's renowned Bitters End company at the highest price. Although CJ Entertainment attributed more credit to Bong Joon-ho's fame from "The Host" and "Tokyo!" in Japan, discerning observers realized that "Won Bin's return after a five-year hiatus" was the primary reason Japanese distributors were willing to pay a hefty sum for "Mother's" rights amidst the financial crisis.
