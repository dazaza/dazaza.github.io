Synopsis:

A new brain revolution, advertising saves the nation. After years of military dictatorship, Chile held a referendum in the 1980s where the people had to decide with 'Yes' or 'No' whether to continue being ruled by the dictator for another eight years. Facing an oppressive regime and a numb, indifferent populace, opposition party leaders hired a young advertiser to use a series of ads and propaganda to convey their resistance beliefs, shouting 'No' for freedom. This time, their battlefield was right under the people's noses, pitting visuals against the regime, with vibrant rainbow patterns confronting rigid political slogans, using imagination to liberate hearts that had stopped beating under the iron curtain.

The film indirectly describes Chile's military dictator Augusto Pinochet and shows how he eventually stepped down from his dictatorial position after an advertising campaign orchestrated by a Coca-Cola company executive.

Behind-the-scenes production:

This film, along with Pablo Larraín's previous two works "Tony Manero" and "Post Mortem," is part of the "Pinochet Trilogy," telling stories set during the rule of Chile's military dictator Pinochet (1973-1990). Pinochet's life has been controversial; in 1973, he led a coup that overthrew the democratically elected president Salvador Allende, subsequently imposing a terrifying dictatorship in Chile, accused of murdering thousands of dissidents. During his tenure, he vigorously promoted neoliberal economic policies, which temporarily improved Chile's economy. After stepping down, he faced numerous lawsuits from countries around the world and was placed under house arrest multiple times. After his death, hundreds of supporters cried outside the hospital, chanting "Long live Pinochet" and singing the Chilean national anthem to mourn his passing. In the film "No," the director focuses on how Pinochet lost public support due to a successful advertising campaign by the opposition during the 1988 Chilean national plebiscite, ultimately stepping down from his dictatorial position. The mastermind behind this ad campaign was René Saavendra, who was not a politician but the advertising director for Coca-Cola.

When that election took place, Larraín was only 12 years old. "My memory isn't clear, but I remember the atmosphere at the time—it was terrifying. I remember seeing the election campaigns on TV—they were as thrilling as the World Cup. Pinochet and his group didn’t take these seriously enough. They underestimated the power of television. As a result, TV changed everything. The reason we're interested in this story is that it shows how some very simple ideas spread through television could change the fate of a country. And the person who accomplished all this wasn't some genius reformer. He wasn't Che Guevara. He was just an advertising director who believed in Coca-Cola more than freedom or politics. What surprises us is that Pinochet was defeated by the capitalist economy and social system he himself established. In a sense, you could say he dug his own grave."

With a fanatical attention to detail akin to "Mad Men," Larraín insisted on using outdated U-Matic video tape recorders to shoot the film to blend seamlessly with the real archival footage used in the movie. Additionally, Larraín attempted to show what the real Pinochet was like.

"Pinochet was very stupid," he said. "He wasn't someone with the intelligence of Margaret Thatcher. I don't like Thatcher, but I wouldn't call her stupid. Pinochet reached his position mainly through luck and brutality. Some of his subordinates were smart, but Pinochet himself couldn't even speak clearly. I had to reveal his true side. If one day someone wants to make a biographical film about him, it would end up being a comedy similar to Sacha Baron Cohen's 'The Dictator.'"

Shooting in an outdated video format was quite challenging, starting with the difficulty of finding the equipment. "We found 20 old machines in Utah, then sent them to Hollywood, assembling them into four units. One was damaged during air transport, leaving only one that lasted until the end of filming. To convert the video into digital format, we needed a whole new system."

Larraín grew up as a cinephile from a relatively powerful wealthy family in Chile. His father, Hernán Larraín, is one of Chile's most famous right-wing politicians and currently serves as a member of parliament. His mother was the former Minister of Housing and Urban Development in Chile. "In fact, my parents both voted in favor of Pinochet back then. I can still remember holding a sign that said 'yes' on the last day of the campaign, just like in the movie. I was one of the millions of people on the streets supporting Pinochet. It wasn't until later when I grew up that I understood everything."

So, how do his parents view this film?

"My father hates Pinochet, and Pinochet hated him too. My father is quite famous; he entered politics during Chile's transition to democracy and was once the principal of one of Chile's main universities. But before being a politician, he was first a father. He is proud of me and won't miss any news about me. I have two children, and he focuses most of his attention on his grandchildren."

Trivia:

- Gael García Bernal's participation brought about a significant change in the film's destiny, bringing in funds and helping it enter Cannes, winning the Grand Prize of the Directors' Fortnight, and ultimately receiving an Oscar nomination for Best Foreign Language Film in 2013.

- To create a retro period effect, the film was shot using the long-obsolete U-Matic video tape format.

- The film is the final installment of the "Pinochet Trilogy" (also known as the Dictatorship Trilogy), following "Tony Manero" and "Post Mortem," directed by Larraín.

One-line review:

"No" is the film I saw at Cannes that comes closest to a masterpiece classic.

Thoughtful, excellently performed, filled with personal touches from the director and various elements of interest. Due to the success of this film, all of the director's previous films are worth revisiting. The director deliberately created a videotape-like feel throughout the film to recreate the feeling of the past era, including grainy image quality and shaky camera work.
