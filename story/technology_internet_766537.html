Twitter Founder on Future Media Strategy: Key is Growth of Knowledge

by anonymous on 2013-09-18 21:51:44

One of Twitter's co-founders: Evan Williams

As one of the co-founders of Twitter, Evan Williams has a brand-new grand plan: to shift people’s daily news consumption from passively absorbing an ever-growing stream of news to actively engaging in a heuristic thinking process powered by intelligent algorithms. To most people, such a goal might sound impractical if it weren’t for the fact that Williams had previously founded several internet companies that had already dramatically transformed the media landscape.

Even before Twitter changed the way news is disseminated, Williams' first groundbreaking platform, Blogger, had already become the progenitor of today's blogs. More importantly, Williams later developed Medium, a new platform for publishing long-form content. For the often chaotic internet world, Medium serves as a beacon. Medium lacks "follow" or "friend" functions. For readers, there are only two buttons: "comment" and "recommend." Although there isn't much interaction on Medium, my personal experience using it far surpasses other platforms. The website's design is very reasonable, catering to human curiosity and the desire to explore. It connects you with numerous thought-provoking and insightful writers, allowing you to gain inspiration from them.

Now, more than a year has passed since Williams unveiled the beta version of Medium at the TechCrunch Disrupt conference last year, and he has something to say.

The Internet is flooded with junk content

Williams pointed out bluntly the most embarrassing flaw in the news industry: the need to produce a great deal of mundane reporting so that readers can develop the habit of reading news every day, just like addicts needing their daily fix.

Williams said in an exclusive interview: "In general, the news itself is not that important in most cases. If we spent less time reading the news and more time reading ideas with a deeper impact, most people would lead better lives with improved attitudes. Even though this may sound a bit ethereal, it is likely to work in most situations."

This is indeed the case. Nowadays, news often plays no role in enhancing American democratic awareness. In a recently published research report titled "Does the Media Really Matter?" economists found that a group of randomly selected American citizens who read The Washington Post regularly did not significantly improve their understanding of "political knowledge and expression of opinions."

Clearly, news alone is insufficient to make our society more informed.

On the contrary, Williams believes that American citizens should direct their urgent need for information towards more meaningful news content. He pointed out: "Any idea or story published has the potential to change someone's life. The internet has completely unleashed people's demand for information, especially breaking free from the limitation that knowledge could only be obtained through books. The world has thus become increasingly fascinating."

However, apart from providing better content, news should ideally come from professional hands. Williams once pointed out that the current state of tech blogs is very poor, often filled with junk content. When explaining this view, he said: "Many tech blogs are terrible partly because the people writing them don't really know what they're writing about. Therefore, I want to refine our definition of professional writing. At least make this definition more detailed."

It is clear that Williams has taken a stand between the long-standing debates among journalists and insiders of the news industry. He pointed out that the weekend columns written by business professionals hired by TechCrunch are "obviously more worth reading" compared to the everyday news written by journalists lacking business experience.

However, Williams clarified one point: "Don't assume that Evan himself thinks all tech blogs are garbage, and thus uses this to promote Medium. People will also publish junk content on Medium. There is junk content on Twitter, on other blogs, and everywhere on the entire internet. If we tried to completely eliminate all the junk content on the internet, perhaps the importance of the internet wouldn't be as high as it is now."

So, how does Williams emphasize the importance of good content?

Medium: Attracting good ideas through simplicity

Medium publishes a large number of articles with distinct viewpoints and profound insights.

Evan Hansen, former editor-in-chief of Wired.com and current senior editor of Medium, said: "Everyone has stories or insights worth mentioning repeatedly, and they just can't find the right channel to share these stories and insights." Medium positions itself as a fusion of professional media (such as The New York Times) and blog-type media where individuals freely express personal opinions (like The Huffington Post). Medium truly wants to become a platform for everyone to publish their best ideas.

A more important point is: Articles published on Medium are written from the heart. Users do not need to register on the Medium website, nor do they have to reorganize their blogging habits; they just need to write down their real thoughts.

The simplicity of Medium's writing platform has received consistent praise from well-known writers and designers. Julie Zhuo, design director of the Facebook Home project, wrote in an article on Medium: "Medium really provides me with the best writing experience on the internet." Nick Bilton, technology columnist for The New York Times, also gave Medium high praise. He wrote in an email: "I really like Medium a lot. I would be happy to replace my WordPress blog on my personal site with Medium." However, good news for WordPress is that Medium does not intend to become a separate blog platform.

Hansen emphasized that Medium is still in the exploratory testing phase. Regardless of where it ultimately develops, Medium hopes to become a home for bold and widely influential ideas, and the company will invest manpower and resources to overcome any obstacles in writing.

Gaining knowledge is key

Traditional editors build their reputations by increasing their sensitivity to events that attract readers' attention, but they don't care whether readers accumulate more knowledge while reading.

Williams believes that Medium has found a perfect solution: a smart algorithm that mainly recommends articles based on the length of time readers spend reading a particular type of article. This is the first time he has discussed Medium's smart algorithm in a public setting. Just like Pandora's exploration in the music field, Medium's new algorithm aims to improve the manual browsing of the internet followed by passive recommendations for readers.

In this algorithm, Medium emphasizes the amount of time readers spend reading an article rather than simply the number of page views. Williams explained: "Reading duration itself doesn't have much value. But in a world where people have infinite choices, reading duration is an important measure of whether people are gaining value."

For editors, we indeed know how much time readers spend reading a particular article. We know that fewer than 60% of readers will read more than half of an article's content, and a significant portion of readers will just open the article page without reading any content. Technology columnist Farhad Manjoo wrote in an article titled "You Won't Finish Reading This Article: Why People Don't Read Online Articles" published in the well-known IT network magazine Slate: "To summarize briefly, it's because people are unwilling to spend too much time reading an article."

However, advertisers pay based on page views, so attracting clicks is king. Our efforts to promote other types of high-quality content are completely sacrificed.

I believe that if readers only cared about articles with substantial depth, many practitioners in the news industry would be very happy. But in reality, high-quality content with few clicks cannot bring real money. So, how does Medium make a profit without relying on advertisements?

Two Profit Channels

In short, no one currently knows Medium's exact profit channels. Hansen also hinted that Medium hasn't focused too much on profitability issues.

However, one profit channel is selling e-books. Some readers might buy the Kindle version of "Mercenary" for convenience. Another profit channel is technology licensing and sharing revenue with well-known media brands willing to forward Medium articles.

Although Medium doesn't yet have a clear business plan, the concept of "product first, profit second" has taken shape. Williams explained: "We love the web. We value the possibility of everything. We enjoy innovation and being part of it. We didn't start this business solely to make money."

Williams is completing the final piece of his product trilogy. The first product, Blogger, opened up a door for bloggers to compete with media giants; years later, the second product, Twitter, gave people the ability to spread information with just 140 words; and Medium aims to make infectious information even more powerful.

Williams pointed out: "If more and more people share their ideas, it will improve the lives of more people. Very optimistically, we hope that Medium can draw readers' attention to good things. Besides satisfying people's basic curiosity, substantive creativity and stories are what attract readers' attention more effectively."
