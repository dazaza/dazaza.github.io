What are the differences between Zhengwei, Xiaqing, and Tuosuan Tieguanyin?

by ipqess on 2012-03-01 21:22:42

What are the differences between Zhengwei, Xiaqing, and Tuosuan Tieguanyin? [http://www.teaguest.com/article-360.html]. Nowadays, tea tasting is in vogue, and Tieguanyin tea stands out among teas. The traditional manufacturing process has evolved into three categories: Zhengchao (normal stir-fried), Xiaqing (reduced green), and Tuosuan (delayed acid). These teas differ in appearance, aroma, and taste. Today, we will discuss the differences between Zhengwei, Xiaqing, and Tuosuan.

**Aroma Differences**

1. **Zhengchao**: The aroma of Zhengchao tea is usually not as pronounced on the lid, even with high-quality tea. The first brew's lid aroma tends to be weaker, becoming clearer by the second brew, and most intense by the third brew — a style that builds gradually. In terms of aroma, Zhengchao teas often have a subtle fragrance and lack the intensity found in Xiaqing products. The tea liquor's aroma is also more delicate and understated.

2. **Xiaqing**: The lid aroma of Xiaqing tea is clear and distinct. High-quality Xiaqing teas can either have a bold or subtle aroma but are generally easily perceptible — their aroma is elegant. However, in recent years, extremely sharp and dominant aromas have become rare. Some top-grade Xiaqing teas have an extremely delicate aroma that is highly penetrating. While these are rare, they're not recommended for pursuit. Overall, Xiaqing teas tend to have a clear and high-pitched aroma that many tea enthusiasts find appealing. For Zheng-style Xiaqing, the aroma leans closer to Zhengchao; for Suan-style Xiaqing, after rinsing the leaves, there is a noticeable pleasant acidic aroma which disappears after the first brew, leaving no sourness in the tea liquor.

3. **Tuosuan**: After rinsing the leaves, a strong acidic aroma can be detected at the bottom of the leaves. Tuosuan teas typically have a lingering sour taste, which represents a fatal defect in the process, making it devoid of any value in preservation.

4. **Summary**: In terms of aroma intensity, Tuosuan ranks first, Xiaqing second, and Zhengchao third. Regarding elegance of aroma, Zhengchao ranks first, Xiaqing second (high-end varieties can also rank first), and Tuosuan last. Note: An important concept in Tieguanyin's aroma is "clear." This means absolutely no mixed flavors — it may not have a strong aroma, but it must not have any impurities. High-quality teas must achieve a clear and fragrant aroma. Therefore, some very high-grade Tieguanyins may appear less aromatic but are actually fresh and elegant upon closer inspection. If the lid aroma contains mixed flavors, even if it is very intense, the grade cannot be high. A "clear" Tieguanyin indicates extremely pure raw materials, all from the same area and picked according to standards. Conversely, mixing tea leaves from different areas or picking them inconsistently can easily introduce mixed flavors.

**Taste Differences**

1. **Zhengchao**: The tea liquor of Zhengchao is mild due to a slightly higher fermentation level, resulting in less stimulation and a comfortable drinking experience. However, during tasting, one might feel the tea liquor itself is not very aromatic and the flavor is relatively thin. After drinking, however, a strong aftertaste emerges, often accompanied by a refreshing sensation. Breathing between sips reveals a noticeable aroma in the mouth. Simply put, the post-tasting experience is better than the tasting experience. The term "Guanyin韵" (Guanyin charm) refers more specifically to Zhengchao and traditionally processed teas. The highest-grade Zhengchao Tieguanyin transforms into sweetness immediately upon entering the mouth, filling the mouth with fragrance before one can fully savor it. However, smelling the tea liquor itself reveals that its aroma is often not very pronounced. Additionally, after the fourth brew, Zhengchao teas typically become sweet. Appearance-wise, Zhengchao teas are generally yellowish, though occasionally they may be greenish-yellow. Unfortunately, since Zhengchao or semi-Zhengchao style Tieguanyins do not have a high-pitched aroma or freshness, many tea lovers do not prefer this style. However, currently, the high-priced top-tier teas in Anxi are almost exclusively Zhengchao Guanyin. Some tea makers believe that if a tea has a high-pitched aroma, it becomes vulgar and should instead be elegant and long-lasting, reflecting a new trend in the industry.

2. **Xiaqing**: The soup aroma of Xiaqing tea is often rich, with a greenish-yellow hue, making it visually appealing. The taste is also richer, with excellent varieties characterized by smoothness and thickness, suitable for slow savoring in the mouth. After drinking, the aftertaste is sweet and refreshing but does not match Zhengchao tea. Occasionally, some Xiaqing teas may also exhibit sweetness, but this is rare. Xiaqing processing is easier to achieve a high-pitched aroma and fine texture, making it popular in the market.

3. **Tuosuan**: Drinking Tuosuan tea can easily cause nausea, and as storage time increases, this discomfort worsens. Beginners might not easily notice this, but once experienced, most people avoid Tuosuan tea. Even well-made Tuosuan teas do not age well. Ultimately, the choice of tea should depend on personal preference rather than others' recommendations, as taste buds are the most honest judge.

**Differences in Leaf Base**

1. **Zhengchao**: Generally a soil-yellow green tone.

2. **Xiaqing**: Generally ranges from dark green to soil-yellow green.

3. **Tuosuan**: Generally dark green with bright colors.

**Production Process Differences**

1. **Zhengchao**: Stir-fried on the morning of the second day after picking — to quickly eliminate bitterness, heavy drying or prolonged shaking is often used, tending towards medium fermentation, hence the mild tea liquor. Zhengchao tea is already close to traditional Guanyin, except traditional Guanyin has a higher fermentation degree. The industry consensus is to remove red edges, so even traditionally processed Tieguanyin today lacks red edges. However, traditionally charcoal-baked Tieguanyin must retain red edges to achieve a glossy black color. Once red edges are removed, precise baking becomes much harder.

2. **Xiaqing**: Stir-fried from the afternoon to evening of the second day after picking. Those stir-fried closer to noon are called Xiazheng, while those closer to the afternoon are called Xiasuan.

3. **Tuosuan**: Stir-fried early morning to morning of the third day after picking, with the lightest shaking of green.
