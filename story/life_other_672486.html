Diary Written Deep in Autumn

The sun is playing hide-and-seek with people again, hiding deep within the thick clouds of autumn. Despite repeated calls, it has no intention of coming out. The sunlight that was still on the treetops yesterday is nowhere to be found today. The abrupt mountains and withered grass seem still intoxicated by last night's starlight and moon glow, not yet awakened.

The colors of heaven and earth are all dim and dark. There is no wind, the sky seems lower, the clouds and trees are motionless. Occasionally, a leaf falls from a branch, hitting other branches and making an extremely faint sound before landing like a broken-winged butterfly on the roadside soil or in the grass.

In the distance, the fields still echo with the usual sounds of threshing rice. There is no rain, only the biting cold that moves freely through the air.

On days like these, it’s not just outside but even inside, with doors and windows closed, one still feels the cold as if winter has already arrived. That cold surges like tidal waves, one after another, rushing through my body from all directions, both wantonly and arrogantly.

By this time in previous years, a stove would have been lit in the house, but this year, my home hasn't finished renovations. Staying in someone else's house, without the iron stove for warmth, my limbs suffer daily from the cold invasion. Sometimes, I wish I could stay under the covers forever, never getting up. However, though warm, under the covers, I cannot read books or go online. Reading books and surfing the internet are indispensable parts of my life. Without reading, I feel my brain empty, life meaningless. Without the internet, it means being cut off from the world, losing touch with the outside. Although the online world isn’t as beautiful as imagined, it provides us with a platform to exchange ideas and pursue dreams. Leaving the internet would mean leaving my dreams and friends, most of whom I've met online. They come from all over, separated by thousands of miles, perhaps never meeting, but as long as I don't leave the network, I feel they are close by, so real and comfortable.

Moreover, the cold season is so long; it's impossible to stay under the covers waiting for spring to come.

Although my current life remains fraught with difficulties, because of having a computer, I can chat, write, and open a window to view the outside world, making it more meaningful than two years ago.

Two years ago, especially during my earlier teenage years, when I had just dropped out of school at home, I either wandered around aimlessly or did nothing, thus becoming addicted to reading martial arts novels. But due to a lack of fixed book sources, I read mostly fragmented editions passed around by others. Often, there weren't even fragments, so my biggest wish back then was to have a thick book that I could read endlessly without ever finishing. However, there is no such thing as a book you can never finish, so my wish was never fulfilled. Nevertheless, martial arts novels brought me much joy.

Thinking about many years ago, that summer, and that girl in her dress with her radiant, shy smile.

I remember her not because she was pretty, nor because anything unforgettable happened between us, but because of the martial arts novel "Ho Yuanjia" she held. It was the first martial arts novel I'd ever read, leaving a particularly deep impression. Therefore, whether it was her lending me the book or the fascinating plot within, even after so many years, everything is still vivid in my mind.

Those days, those bygone years, were as barren as desert land. Looking back now, it's hard to revisit, but regardless of scorching heat or severe cold, with a martial arts novel in hand, I would forget the world outside the window, my increasingly frail body, and arguments with my mother or grandmother.

Later, I gradually learned to read romance novels and various literary magazines or novels. After reading a few literary books, I naively conceived a dream of becoming a writer. Thus, every day, I read and wrote, using a pen on manuscript paper, writing stroke by stroke, carefully forming each character. Since I was just starting, I often wrote, tore it up, and rewrote it, continuing this way for several years without producing any decent work, so I never submitted to any magazine.

As the sun rose and set, flowers bloomed and withered, while I was bent over my desk writing furiously, several more years slipped by. The girl who once accompanied me through many storms has left.

With more books read, I came to know Helen Keller, Shi Tiesheng, and Zhang Haidi. I learned that they, like me, were disabled, yet their achievements were no less remarkable than those of any healthy person. Under the brilliance of their accomplishments and writings, I couldn't help but think: originally, a flawed life can also be so wonderful, so beautiful. Thus, they became the people I admire most, towering idols in my heart. When I encounter obstacles in writing or fall into trouble in life, it is they—especially Helen Keller's extraordinary will—that light a beacon of hope for me, encouraging me time and again to raise my sails and brave the waves of life.

Desperately reading books, writing poems and essays, then sending them off through the post office to magazines near and far, followed by one long wait after another. It was tough, but ultimately, not even a single encouraging message arrived.

Now, as summer and spring fade, autumn ends and winter comes, the days grow colder and harder. Due to various physical limitations, my dreams have to be temporarily put on hold until I can face the sea, where spring warms and flowers bloom.

Thus, I finally understand that beneath the dazzling exteriors of my idols lies endless hardship. And I do not know how many more trials I must endure before I see the light I have longed for.
