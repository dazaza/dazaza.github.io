Exclusive Interview with Google's Engineering Director: How to Create True Artificial Intelligence

by geekzhang on 2013-05-03 19:00:59

Google Engineering Chief Ray KurzweilMay 1, Beijing Time - According to foreign media reports, Google engineering chief Ray Kurzweil recently gave an interview where he introduced the purpose of his joining Google. As a famous entrepreneur, inventor, and futurist, his joining of Google caused a sensation. However, Kurzweil explained that his lifelong goal was to create true artificial intelligence, and Google's vast data and computing resources provided advantages that no other company could offer, which were also essential factors for him in developing artificial intelligence.The following is the main content of the article:In January this year, when Google announced that Ray Kurzweil would be joining, many people wondered why this accomplished excellent entrepreneur and futurist would join a large company not founded by him.Kurzweil's answer is: outside of Google, no company can provide the computational and engineering resources needed for him to achieve his lifelong work. Kurzweil is 65 years old, with numerous inventions including music synthesizers and voice recognition systems. At the age of 14, he wanted to create real artificial intelligence and even predicted that machines would reach human intelligence by 2029.Now, as the engineering director at Google, he is particularly focused on making computers truly understand, and even produce natural language, and ultimately hopes to create an electronic friend (cybernetic friend) that knows what you want to do before you do it.In a recent interview, Kurzweil revealed details of the work he plans to accomplish at Google. Without a doubt, as Kurzweil delves deeper into his work at Google, this work will also evolve, but so far, this is the deepest disclosure of his plans.At least initially, this work will not be directly related to advertising, but marketers need to know that the work of Kurzweil and other Google employees not only can change increasingly intelligent machine searches, but also can change our interaction with information and each other. All of this means significant changes in the essence of advertising and marketing.Q: In your book "How to Create a Mind", you propose a theory about how the brain works. Can you briefly explain?A: The world is multi-layered, and only mammals have cerebral cortexes, which evolved to help better understand the structure of the world, allowing for better adjustments based on needs, and solving problems in a multi-layered world. We should think in layers. Our first invention was language, and language is layered too.Deep learning, or what I call hierarchical learning, is based on the idea that you should have a pattern that reflects the non-hierarchical nature of the natural phenomena you want to learn. Without such a pattern, your learning ability weakens, and you get confused by surface phenomena.Q: How do you apply this theory at Google?A: Here, I will develop hierarchical methods, especially for understanding natural languages and extracting semantics. In fact, it's about developing a method to describe and mimic the semantic content of documents, thereby improving search and answering questions better.More and more searches sent to Google are in the form of questions, but currently these questions lack the complexity of semantic ambiguity. But if we can truly mimic language hierarchically, mimicking what billions of web pages are trying to say, we can answer these questions better and improve overall search.Q: Hasn't Google or any other company done similar work before?A: There have been some academic projects in this area, but no one has really come up with solutions. I must say, IBM's Watson computer performed very well in understanding semantics during the Jeopardy competition, demonstrating the feasibility of this work. Watson's knowledge isn't encoded in computer language; actually, recording all common sense in computer language is very fragile because it can't reflect the ambiguity of language and common sense.Watson doesn't work that way; it gains knowledge by reading Wikipedia and other encyclopedias, then participates in a not-so-simple quiz show. This is essentially answering questions, with potentially very broad question coverage. In the Jeopardy competition, Watson scored more than the combined total of the two best human contestants.Q: Why did you choose to work at Google?A: Of course, I've thought deeply about it. This is my first time working at a company I didn't found, and I believe it's the best choice. Over the past few years, I've had the opportunity to collaborate with Larry Page on some projects, and he and I have discussed artificial intelligence extensively. Some of the algorithmic techniques used here aren't profound, but Google's massive scale of data makes its algorithms powerful. We both agreed that Google's vast amount of data and computing architecture are necessary elements for creating stronger artificial intelligence.In July last year, I met with Larry Page regarding my book, as I had given him an unpublished draft. I said I was interested in initiating a project, possibly creating a company to implement certain ideas. He suggested that I consider doing this work at Google, because as Larry Page and I discussed, Google's vast amount of data and computing architecture are essential elements. He said: I can give you some access points, it would be difficult to do this work in an independent company. This touched me deeply, so I am convinced that I cannot accomplish this work anywhere else except at Google.Q: This still surprises many people, because after being an entrepreneur for so many years, you became a corporate employee.A: This is an opportunity to make an impact, which is my goal as an inventor. A blind reader might have achieved some scientific breakthroughs, but the real satisfaction comes from hundreds of millions of blind people saying that it helps them get jobs or receive education. Now a billion people use Google. If I can make similar contributions, it would greatly help people. This is the full utilization of human knowledge.This isn't a small project; it's the accumulation of over 50 years of experience in artificial intelligence. I've always considered how to face the ultimate challenge, which is truly imitating and understanding natural language and using it to perform practical tasks.Q: Why is understanding natural language the ultimate challenge?A: Alan Turing's Turing test is entirely based on written language. Fundamentally, it's an instant messaging game. To truly master natural language, or even just written forms, to the point where it completely convinces humans, requires comprehensive artificial intelligence. You need more than just simple language processing skills; many chatbots can do such things, misleading some people. In fact, they can't effectively pass the Turing test.Therefore, natural language is a very profound field of artificial intelligence, and I can't do such projects anywhere else like I can at Google. Now, I've worked here for a few months, and I can see the wisdom in Larry Page's team.Q: Why do you say that? What can Google do to help you achieve your goals?A: Take Google's Knowledge Graph as an example; it's a directory with 700 million topics, places, people, and other concepts. To understand natural language, you must understand these concepts and things in the world, whether abstract or concrete. Now the Knowledge Graph has 700 million entries and billions of links, and it's still growing rapidly. This is something I couldn't create. Clearly, we must fully utilize the Knowledge Graph because if you want to mimic what language says, you must link to the knowledge base of these concepts, which already have established connections. There are other technologies like syntactic analysis that you won't find outside of Google.Q: Does the chapter in your book outline the steps to cultivate true thinking in chips and software? Is that the purpose of your work at Google, at least the ultimate goal?A: I've disclosed the general direction, but I also have some private views that obviously aren't shared in the book, especially how to build hierarchies. I haven't talked about these contents in the book. The key is that these hierarchies aren't innate; what is innate are unrelated modules without forming any patterns between them. Then we start learning, even before we're born, because we open our eyes at 26 weeks and start learning sounds.It's important that the cerebral cortex creates these hierarchies, i.e., how these modules connect to higher-level modules. It takes many years, even decades, to reach a certain level. Therefore, even though the cerebral cortex is extraordinary, it's useless without a learning process. Thus, building artificial intelligence is actually creating a learning process.Q: How do you plan to proceed?A: I know how to use data hierarchies formed by simulating the brain. That's exactly what I need to do here. Larry Page was very excited about this book and took artificial intelligence very seriously. So I have great independence; I'm not restricted by any specifications or limited to what needs to be done in the next few years.Although Watson once defeated two top human players, it won because of its scale, as it can handle 200 million pages of content, while you and I can't read a million pages. Therefore, the concept is to have enough semantics, even if we can't uncover all the semantics or reach the scale of the human world, we can step by step improve in the search field through every webpage, every page of a book. This is the overall direction of search and knowledge navigation.Q: Where do you start? Are there any challenges you need to overcome to get on track?A: The challenge in my mind is how to mimic semantics. Regarding speech recognition, I can simply describe the meaning of the output of speech signals: it's actually a restatement of what others have said. Then, I can strive to create a database with a million pronunciations and correct translations. This is a huge project, but it's feasible. Subsequently, you can perform some learning algorithms. We use a hierarchical learning method. Then, you can start learning from your own experience.This is the first step, which is correctly translating language pronunciation, which is as difficult as understanding natural language. How do you reflect the correct meaning of language? Even without considering the ambiguous meanings in people's language, how do you describe its meaning? I have an idea in my mind to express semantics graphically. Perhaps it can't capture every detail, but current computer programs can't grasp any semantic details.Then, I want to build a speech recognition database. You can easily obtain a lot of text examples, just go to Wikipedia and get millions of pages. Then you can establish a database of correct translations to express semantics. Subsequently, we can develop a deep learning algorithm for semantic translation. This way, when encountering a new sentence, whether it's a new sentence used by users in their questions or a new sentence appearing on a new webpage every minute, it can translate correctly.Q: Will you and your team cooperate with other AI-related teams within Google?A: I've already formed a team, some internally transferred, some externally recruited. I'll make full use of other resources here. For example, we need to enrich the Knowledge Graph, making it have a broader relationship network. The current Knowledge Graph is not sufficient to cover all the relationships expressed by language. Therefore, the first thing we need to do in natural language understanding is to expand the Knowledge Graph and add more relationships.Q: Is your ultimate goal to create an AI that can pass the Turing test, or to enhance the human brain using these technologies? Or will you separate these two tasks?A: If you're talking about my career, I'm first and foremost an inventor. I entered the future academic field to serve my invention career. Timing is crucial; famous inventors can all seize opportunities well. Larry Page and Sergey Brin formed outstanding ideas in organizing internet links and building better search engines. If they were a few years earlier or later, you might not know their names. It's like clay pigeon shooting. Looking back three to four years ago, most people weren't using social networks, Wikipedia, and blogs. Recalling ten years ago, most people hadn't used search engines.My personal motivation isn't to build a computer that can pass the Turing test, but to make short-term contributions. Natural language understanding is a goal without an end, and so is search. I think this project will never end.Q: How will deep learning and neural networks change the nature of computers?A: The structure of von Neumann computers (the basis of all traditional computers today) is very different from the structure of the brain. In the brain, 100 trillion neural connections perform calculations simultaneously. This is truly large-scale parallel processing. But these calculations are very slow, for example, they can only perform 100 calculations per second. And these calculations themselves aren't important; they just form a probabilistic whole. Computers aren't like that.Supercomputers' speed has surpassed the computational power required to imitate the human brain, not only imitating at the molecular level but also functionally. This is an important distinction. In the past, there was the Blue Brain Project initiated by Henry Markram, which received 1 billion euros in funding, and now in the U.S., there's the Brain Activity Map, aiming to replicate the brain at the molecular level.Q: Do you think this isn't a feasible path?A: It's a great project, but it's not the right way to create artificial intelligence; it's a method to test our understanding of how the human brain works. It's an effective way to understand the brain, how neurons work, and how ion channels operate. It's also a good way to create a large brain simulator and see if it runs correctly. This is a method to study the brain, and then we can draw lessons from it to use biological algorithms to create artificial intelligence.Q: What other factors will determine when we see true artificial intelligence?A: We need to consider the law of accelerating returns. Based on the progress of information technology, we always use today's technology to create the next technology. The same applies to the software field. As long as a system is running, we can improve it, and improve it exponentially rather than incrementally. The same technology allows us to see inside the brain, with the resolution of brain scans continuously improving, and the data modules we obtain through neuroscience increasing. Then, we can use this information to study how to use the biological algorithms of the brain to improve artificial intelligence.
