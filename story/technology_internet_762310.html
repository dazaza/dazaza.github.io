Dianping: A Bone the Giants Can't Chew, Living in the Discomfort Zone

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 14:48:55

Zhang Tao, CEO of Dianping.com

Written by Yuan Yin, edited by Yang Jing, photographed by Xiao Nan

Zhang Tao, CEO of Dianping.com, has once again postponed the company's IPO plans.

In 2011, Dianping raised $100 million in its Series C round from Sequoia Capital and others, reaching a valuation of $1 billion. According to convention, it should have gone public within one to two years after that, but it did not. Instead, in 2012, it raised another $60 million in its Series D round.

Given Dianping's scale and growth trajectory, going public would not be difficult. However, Zhang Tao feels that an IPO at this point would be too costly. He breaks down the large internet (PC + mobile) into four elements—people, information, goods, and services—with each connection point presenting opportunities for giants to emerge. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, and Alibaba connects people with goods. The space between people and services represents the fourth giant's position. Since 2003, Dianping has been diligently accumulating resources, finally waiting for the explosion of mobile internet, where smartphones bridge "location" and "services." Following Zhang Tao's logic, no company seems more fitting than Dianping for the role of the fourth giant.

However, the outside world doesn't see it this way. In recent months, with frequent acquisitions by BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent), Dianping has become a speculative target for acquisition. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition target; under any of the BAT brands, it could help them achieve great success sooner. On the other hand, Dianping's size pales in comparison to BAT, and it is still premature to declare it a future giant. Moreover, whether it’s Tencent WeChat's public platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba post-acquisition of AutoNavi and Meituan, all are directly targeting Dianping's territory—local life services primarily focused on food, drink, entertainment, and leisure.

Before proving the following two points, a Dianping IPO would equate to selling at a discount. First, Zhang Tao needs time to demonstrate that what Dianping is doing now is something BAT cannot replicate. Second, Dianping is about to enter the final battle of group buying. If it secures the top spot, its valuation could increase several times over.

2013 is Dianping's "year of decisive battles." Regarding speculations and rumors, Zhang Tao remains deaf to them. He is very clear: "The internet era has indeed entered a period of great convergence, but Dianping will not be acquired."

Zhang Tao categorizes companies that might be acquired into three types. The first type has platform attributes but is better suited to develop within the system of a giant, such as maps and email. These products can easily attract a large number of users but struggle to find independent business models. Others, like browsers, can make money, but the revenue from navigation services is far less than their strategic value to large companies. "Giants are willing to spend money to maintain an email product because it helps with branding and user stickiness, but I've never heard of any standalone email company succeeding." The second type involves industry consolidation, like in the video industry, using acquisitions to expand market share. The third type is when a company encounters financial difficulties and sells itself to survive.

Now, Meituan has already been brought under Alibaba's wing. Theoretically, Dianping should quickly seek a "backer" to avoid being defeated due to rapid expansion funded by competitors. However, Zhang Tao says, "The help from giants is limited. We do need users, but execution is more important. Acquisitions would reduce our execution efficiency, and there is also the risk of being marginalized by a large company." Furthermore, he aims to keep Dianping at the most precise "distance" from the giants. Getting too close to any one of them would be akin to "taking sides," making them enemies with the others.

Thus, logically, acquiring Dianping has become a dead end. Zhang Tao counters, "Why would we take their money? We don’t lack funds; what we need are resources. If a giant is willing to take a small stake and give us many resources, I could consider it. But would they? Would a giant give you resources if they only hold a small stake?"

Weapon One: Dirty and Hard Work

In the mobile internet era, opportunities for companies solely focused on pure internet products are decreasing. New opportunities come from those combining with traditional industries. Zhang Tao believes that lifestyle services mainly revolve around food, drink, entertainment, and leisure. Other segmented markets, such as ride-hailing, may form good business models, but it is unlikely for any of them to grow into giants.

For food, drink, entertainment, and leisure, user demand is simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is at the core of lifestyle services, which is exactly where Dianping started. Whether in content, member accumulation, or brand, newcomers find it hard to surpass Dianping.

The most representative product form of discounts is group buying. Group buying has now entered the championship contention phase, and Dianping is set to face off against Meituan in 2013. This is a winner-takes-all market. As long as there are two strong players coexisting, the industry's gross margin will be pulled down, preventing stable profitability.

A visible future lies in reservations. Reservations can create a true online-to-offline closed loop, but they must be built on the foundation of Dianping having absolute advantages in both information and discounts. Otherwise, creating a closed loop would be difficult. In 2012, Dianping began experimenting with reservations. Zhang Tao said, "Currently, both users and merchants provide positive feedback, but if reservations were done separately, it would be hard to solve the chicken-or-egg problem."

Of course, lifestyle services present some even "heavier" opportunities. For instance, food delivery satisfies a rigid user demand while helping merchants increase sales. Over the past few years, companies like Daojia Cuisine have thrived. However, the core of food delivery is distribution—it must be delivered within a short timeframe, and issues like soup spillage must be resolved. The logistics requirements are even higher than those of fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang Tao said, "Food delivery offers opportunities if executed well, but it's harder dirty work. What Dianping is doing now is already dirty and tiring enough."

If judged by age, Dianping is already an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has painstakingly accumulated merchant data and user reviews, which Zhang Tao refers to as "dirty and hard work." This primitive growth model once restricted its speed, but the pain of the past has become Dianping's core barrier today. It can be said that currently, apart from Meituan, Dianping has no real rivals in the lifestyle services sector.

Nevertheless, he still needs to be wary of BAT's "claws." Baidu's involvement is the most direct. Baidu Maps already has over 100 million users who can use the map to search for nearby dining and other lifestyle services. Tencent, considered to have secured a ticket to mobile internet, has launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest physical goods trading platform, and expanding its offerings to services is natural. Moreover, it has incorporated Meituan and AutoNavi into its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there are no signs of explosive growth in reality or data," Zhang Tao said. Brand positioning is crucial—the key is which product users turn to first when they want to eat, drink, play, or relax.

Besides, in the vast field of lifestyle services, competition among giants is bound to be cross-sectoral, and no one can completely monopolize the domain. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained on Weibo and WeChat. Zhang Tao feels that in this way, Dianping is safer. "In niche areas like car rentals and business travel, the massive user base directed by giants could easily overwhelm existing companies."

As for Alibaba, which has invested in AutoNavi, invading Dianping's territory isn't so simple. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, which can be categorized regardless of how peculiar. A user's dining experience depends on various factors such as the day's environment, waitstaff, and chefs. Managing merchants cannot rely on systems and tools like Alibaba does; a "ground force" managed by humans is necessary.

"The three giants all want to go down this path, and there is room for imagination, but this is not a highway; it's a rugged mountain road," Zhang Tao believes. First, providing lifestyle services requires ground teams deployed at various levels, which BAT lacks the genes and ability to manage offline. Second, they lack the willingness. Lifestyle services require dedicated effort, not something accomplished by sending a small team, and profits come slowly. "A company finds it hard to excel in two major tasks simultaneously, let alone needing to consolidate its advantage in its original field."

Weapon Two: Small Boats Turn Easily

When Dianping raised $100 million in financing in 2011, Zhang Tao told the public that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, times change fast, and before Dianping went public, his analogies fell from grace.

He didn't seem anxious about it. "They failed to become big companies because they lacked offline teams; now they all want to become America's Dianping."

The current path is one Zhang Tao has gradually explored step by step. When he felt that selling keyword ads and coupons was too outdated, the emergence of group buying caught his eye. Initially, like the Americans, he understood group buying as a marketing platform—one deal per day—but as orders increased, he realized group buying had transaction platform characteristics. Thus, he envisioned Dianping's future as a lifestyle services transaction platform. The two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were precisely used for Dianping's transformation from an information platform to a transaction platform.

Without this "genetic modification," Zhang Tao might not have had the confidence to compete for the fourth giant's seat today. But genetic modification sounds simple, yet in practice, it is equivalent to Dianping's second entrepreneurship, a process extremely painful.

First, compared to selling ads alone, the gross margin of a transaction platform decreases, requiring stricter cost control and efficiency. Second, a transaction platform demands higher cross-departmental communication capabilities.

Previously, to maintain Dianping's impartiality and independence as an information platform, the content team and the sales team were completely separate. Zhang Tao even deliberately established a "firewall" between departments—no matter how important the client, the platform's information could not be modified for sales purposes, leading to some mutual hostility between the content and sales teams. Now, as a transaction platform, the editorial and sales teams need deep communication to achieve good marketing schemes, which require planning and editorial cooperation. He said, "Maintaining information quality is still important, but there's no need to reinforce enmity anymore; we need to dismantle the firewall."

Though editors can logically accept this, individual behaviors and values have inertia, and when combined across the entire company, the inertia becomes even greater. To address this, Zhang Tao specially convened several meetings to encourage more communication between departments. Months later, an editor manager casually chatted with a sales manager, saying, "I used to think you were my enemy, but now I realize you're my colleague."

The sales department also needed genetic modification. To promote conversion, Zhang Tao cleverly gave the offline team a dual identity. First, for merchants, salespeople must transform into marketing consultants, not just selling products but helping merchants devise marketing strategies. Second, for the company, marketing consultants act as the company's procurement agents. Dianping is like a supermarket; not only must the product quality be good, but the variety of goods must also be rich. Marketing consultants should aim to help users procure group-buying products. Of course, dual identities inevitably lead to a series of complex issues. For frontline personnel, this is quite headache-inducing, as they must balance the scales themselves.

In essence, Dianping's "dual identity" resembles Alibaba's division into 25 business units. Decentralizing decision-making to the front lines allows the company to respond faster, but increases management difficulty. Similarly, compared to frontline employees, Dianping's management team suffers more after the genetic modification.

Each year, Zhang Tao breaks down Dianping's corporate goals into various departments, then executives further break them down layer by layer. However, decomposition is not simple addition or subtraction. Li Jing, co-founder of Dianping responsible for the sales team, sometimes finds that unresolved merchant demands stem from sales issues, but affect sales performance, prompting Li Jing to create a new position: communication specialist, who collects information daily from marketing consultants, submits it to other departments, and tracks feedback. He often tells his team, "Being good at embracing change does not mean liking change. No one likes change; it feels uncomfortable when faced with change. All employees must go through discomfort zones, including me."

Digital evidence reflects the weight of Dianping's execution team. Among the current 3000+ employees, over 2000 work offline. The ground force communicates directly with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group buying, and combination marketing solutions.

For this core team resisting BAT's claws, Dianping has a unique management and evaluation system.

They are not called "sales"; from the first day, their business cards read "marketing planning consultant." Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the era of watching news and chatting on QQ, lagging behind Taobao merchants by at least three to five years in understanding the internet. Marketing planning consultants must inform merchants about online users' needs and how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing said, "Even for a high-end restaurant, the marketing consultant wouldn't recommend shark fin or abalone group buying; perhaps other dishes instead, because online users and offline users have different needs."

An excellent marketing consultant must be a versatile player. They need to explain to restaurant owners and hair salon owners the essential differences between discounts and group buying and which discount aligns better with their positioning. If facing a hotpot restaurant owner, the marketing consultant must know whether it's the off-season or peak season, which products' costs rise during certain seasons, and which fall, proposing a group-buying menu that both merchants and users love.

This imposes high requirements on marketing consultants, who must not only be internet marketing experts but also industry experts. Li Jing said, "I must admit, not every consultant can be a double expert, so we have set up star ratings for consultants." Similar to hotels, marketing consultants range from one-star to five-star, with the best called Super Star.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline employees are not tech-savvy graduates from prestigious universities, making it challenging for them to understand internet products. Dianping has established a knowledge base for marketing consultants, containing knowledge points and relevant cases accessible to both new and old employees. If encountering new problems, employees can ask questions in the knowledge base, and thousands of consultants nationwide will help answer them.

Most knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees based on practical experience. Dianping has established a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, rewarding employees who help others the most each quarter. Each quarter, Dianping conducts skill assessments for employees, directly linking the results to promotions.

Industry knowledge is harder to acquire than internet knowledge. Some experiences are insights gained from decades of working in traditional industries. A Super Star marketing consultant told Li Jing that hair salons should avoid offering shampoo-cut-blow dry package deals, as men lack motivation to buy them, and women prefer blow-drying without the need for shampoo-cut combos. Li Jing was astonished, "We men would never think of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing consultants are divided not only by city but also by industry—catering, beauty salons, leisure, etc. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is further subdivided into hotpot, Western cuisine, and buffets, with a marketing consultant responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing consultants can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, reducing resistance when selling products," he said.

Each marketing group holds its own case-sharing sessions: what challenges a particular type of merchant faces, what marketing needs they have, what products were used to devise solutions, and the outcomes. Outstanding cases are reported upward, and Dianping officially shares one case per week.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition—"Startup Winner." In the first three months of employment, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products but listening to merchants sharing experiences. If responsible for hotpot restaurants, after three months, they must submit a business plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hotpot Restaurant Owner." Like graduation, employees defend their presentations on-site, with industry supervisors evaluating winners, who receive rewards from the company.

Strategically speaking, whether it's Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, the things they need to do are quite similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O are common knowledge for anyone familiar with the industry, and the words spoken sound alike. "The era where success depended on storytelling and concept creation is over. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model lies in management and execution, not strategy," Zhang Tao said.
