The CEO who achieved Nokia's glory

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 19:25:10

Former Nokia CEO Jorma Ollila

Nokia's golden age began in the 1990s and gradually declined after the advent of the iPhone in the early 2000s. During this period, one CEO held the reins of Nokia for over a decade, making him indispensable to its success: Jorma Ollila.

Ollila initially worked in investment banking and joined Nokia in 1985, rising through the ranks to become the company’s CFO before eventually taking the helm. His tenure as CEO spanned over a decade, witnessing Nokia's rise to become the world's largest smartphone manufacturer and also the early signs of decline before Apple launched the iPhone.

Considered Selling the Mobile Phone Business

In his biography "Impossible Success," Ollila describes the process of selling off Nokia assets during his leadership. The most challenging asset divestiture was the sale of Nokia's paper mill in 1989. The paper industry was one of Nokia's earliest ventures.

This sale took an entire year to complete, “It was the holiest part of Nokia, and people were unwilling to openly discuss its sale.”

The second asset Nokia sold was its television production department. Nokia even considered selling its mobile phone division at one point. However, in 1991, Boston Consulting Group assessed that Nokia's mobile phone business could not compete with Motorola and Japanese manufacturers.

At the time, Nokia was still constrained by Finnish banks. In 1991, they attempted to sell the entire company to Ericsson, but the Swedish company deemed Nokia's TV business too risky and thus did not agree to the acquisition. A year later, Siemens proposed acquiring Nokia's mobile phone division, but Ollila rejected it.

Eventually, Nokia decided to persist with its mobile phone business and dominated the mobile phone industry from the late 1990s to the early 2000s. It wasn't until the launch of Apple's iPhone in 2007 that Nokia started to decline.

Too Focused on Mobile Phones, Neglected New Frontiers

So, how did Apple surpass Nokia? According to Ollila, Apple had superior products, better features, and more profitable pricing.

Although acknowledging the failure, Ollila insisted that Nokia was only a step away from success. Nokia's leadership understood that the company needed to transform from a mobile phone company into a software company, and Ollila repeatedly discussed such a strategic shift both inside and outside the company.

Ollila claimed that as early as the 1990s, the success of the Nokia 101 phone and the failure of Ericsson in the mobile phone market reminded Nokia not to grow too fast.

In 1998, Nokia held a strategy meeting where executives gathered to plan the company's future. Ollila said that Nokia "had the right thinking," but did not delve deep enough and failed to invest the profits from the mobile phone business into new areas.

Grasping Timing Requires Intuition

Ollila also pointed out that Nokia faced a severe shortage of software engineers while expanding in Europe. Moreover, the company developed a sense of complacency, being reluctant to emulate others in software development and preferring to innovate behind closed doors.

American carriers often complained to Nokia that users didn’t want smartphones priced over $300. As a result, when Apple's $600 iPhone hit the market, it became a hot commodity.

"Ultimately, timing requires intuition; it is not the result of democratic decision-making. This is the CEO's responsibility, and grasping timing is the CEO's most important mission," he said.

In 2006, Ollila stepped down as CEO, succeeded by then-COO Olli-Pekka Kallasvuo. Ollila said that Nokia's management tends to continue executing the previous CEO's plans for about a year and a half after a new CEO takes over, meaning rapid transformation for the company is almost impossible.

After Kallasvuo, Stephen Elop became Nokia's CEO, but Ollila publicly admitted that Elop was not his first choice. His preferred candidate was the number two person at a well-known American tech company, although he did not name this individual.

In 2012, Ollila resigned as Chairman of Nokia; now, he serves as a non-executive director at the oil giant Shell. However, there are rumors that he is eyeing a seat on Microsoft's board of directors.
