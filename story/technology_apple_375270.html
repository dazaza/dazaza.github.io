Instapaper Creator Talks About the Dilemma of Default.png Launch Images

by diglog on 2010-12-19 13:51:26

Apple recommends in the iPhone Human Interface Guidelines that developers avoid using splash screens and instead create a launch image that resembles the app's main interface, giving users the psychological impression that the app has already loaded. Marco Arment, the creator of Instapaper, tried to adhere to this principle while designing the app's launch screen but encountered many subtle user experience issues. It is precisely through weighing and prioritizing countless such minor details that the seemingly simple Instapaper was achieved. The original article can be found here. — Editor

When iOS apps launch, they display a pre-drawn static image called Default.png. This image can be arbitrarily specified by the developer. Instapaper uses the following image:

This complies with iOS regulations, which state that Default.png should represent a blank version of the app's main interface. This makes the app appear as if it has already loaded (even though it hasn't), and after one or two seconds, the data "pops out." This small trick, originating from the first-generation iPhone, makes the app's startup speed and responsiveness seem much faster. The interface shown in the image above is just a facade; it is merely a static picture and may not necessarily resemble the actual interface once the app has finished loading.

Apple’s own iOS apps have an advantage when applying this technique: they can dynamically change the launch image at runtime. Before exiting, they can save a snapshot of the current interface, making it appear seamless when restoring the previous state upon re-entering the app.

Third-party apps, however, cannot dynamically change their launch images. This creates a visual continuity issue: we can only display the same launch image every time, regardless of whether the app's restored state matches the image.

For Instapaper, this mainly involves two states:

- Full-text reading mode. This interface looks very different from the initial article list interface.

- Dark background mode.

The dark background mode presents a bigger problem. A bright white launch screen flashing before a dark background appears can make users feel uncomfortable or even uneasy. I spent countless hours trying to avoid white screens or flashes of white in dark background mode, but there was nothing I could do about it, which was incredibly frustrating.

One solution would be to use a dark-colored launch screen. However, I couldn’t simply use the dark background interface because doing so would result in a strange experience for users who prefer the white background mode (which is more common): they would first see a dark launch screen followed by a bright white background.

Many developers use splash screens to solve this issue. When working on Instapaper 2.3, I considered it but ultimately couldn’t bring myself to implement it. Below is a rough sketch of what I had in mind:

This approach also has its problems:

- It confuses users because Apple’s own apps don’t use splash screens.

- Splash screens don’t stay long enough on most new, high-end iPhones and iPads, causing users to feel like they’ve missed something since they didn’t have time to fully process the content.

- For reasons unknown to me, I always felt that splash screens looked cheap and outdated.

Thus, both types of launch screens have significant drawbacks. The situation is somewhat similar to putting all settings options in the "Settings" app. However, I believe that issue has been largely resolved (most apps no longer put settings inside the "Settings" app).

Neither of the two launch screen solutions is ideal. Currently, I still follow the approach used by Apple’s apps [Note 1], but I will continue to wrestle with this until I come up with a better solution.

— [Note 1] The first rule for non-game iPhone apps: when you don’t know what to do, follow Apple’s lead.
