The Economic Significance of Raising Earthworms

With the progress of society and the development of science and technology, people's food structure has already or is currently undergoing significant changes, shifting from starch-based to protein-based diets. In particular, in some economically developed Western countries, meat almost accounts for about half of people's diet. To meet the demand for protein-rich foods, the livestock breeding and aquaculture industries worldwide have developed rapidly. However, feed production, especially protein feed, has become a serious issue. Due to environmental pollution and the insufficiency of traditional fishmeal and other animal protein feeds, it has become an urgent need for countries around the world to explore new sources of protein feed.

Eliminating Environmental Pollution

Earthworms' burrowing, soil loosening, breaking down organic matter, and promoting microbial proliferation play crucial roles in maintaining ecological balance. Earthworms' digestive tracts provide environments for certain soil microbes to continue their activities. Earthworms' decomposition function plays an important role in material cycles, participating in carbon and nitrogen cycles, converting organic carbon into inorganic carbon, and digesting and assimilating various forms of nitrogen. If earthworms were absent from the natural decomposition process, corpses would pile up everywhere, creating unimaginable scenarios. The earth would become a lifeless planet. Because earthworms actively participate in material cycles, they help maintain ecological balance. In modern society, if people violate natural laws by only taking from nature without protecting it, ecological imbalances will lead to punishments from nature. For instance, excessive reliance on chemical fertilizers in agriculture might temporarily increase crop yields but leads to soil compaction, acidification, deterioration of microbial habitats, reduced biodiversity, and decreased soil fertility. Overuse of chemical fertilizers or pesticides can also cause rapid spread of pests and diseases, reducing agricultural productivity.

Earthworms are widely distributed, numerous, and have a large biomass, making them a vast biological resource. They tirelessly contribute to material cycles and ecological balance. Whether in farmland, gardens, mountains, grasslands, or forest soils, earthworms can be found. Their decomposition and transformation capabilities are immense. While soil microbes play vital roles in decomposing dead animals and plant residues, items like fallen leaves, straws, animal shells, and keratin must first be broken down by earthworms and other soil animals before microbes can further decompose them. Reports indicate that in apple orchards, about 80 kilograms of dry leaves per mu (Chinese acre) are mostly dragged into soil burrows by earthworms before winter, gradually being consumed. In temperate forests, each mu produces 166.7 to 244.0 kilograms of fallen leaves, while tropical forests produce 366.7 to 1,000 kilograms per mu. These plant residues are primarily broken down and decomposed by earthworms before aerobic and anaerobic microbes take over the decomposition and digestion process. On grasslands, many withered grasses and livestock manure are mainly decomposed by earthworms. Thus, the presence of earthworms significantly accelerates the humification process of many organic materials, which has been confirmed by countless experiments and natural phenomena.

Nowadays, industrial waste such as organic substances produced by food processing, brewing, papermaking, wood processing, and textiles—such as pulp, residue, and sludge—can all be treated by earthworms. In Japan, some paper mills use earthworms to handle pulp sludge, not only improving the environment and eliminating pollution but also generating substantial economic benefits. For example, a paper mill producing 100,000 tons annually generates approximately 45,000 tons of waste pulp. If handled by earthworms, it could produce about 20,000 tons of earthworms and 15,000 tons of worm castings, representing considerable wealth.

Earthworms secrete many special enzymes with astonishing digestive abilities. In many countries around the world, using this unique function of earthworms to process household garbage and various organic waste has become a reality. Reports indicate that in Los Angeles, a worm farm with over one million worms can handle 7.5 tons of garbage monthly. A company in California raises five billion worms, processing 2,000 tons of waste daily. In Ontario, Canada, Cluck uses worms to handle 20 tons of garbage weekly, simultaneously obtaining several tons of worm castings and fresh worms for nurseries, farms, and other needs.
