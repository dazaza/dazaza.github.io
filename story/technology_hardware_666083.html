The correct curing method in the production process of autoclaved aerated concrete blocks by aerated concrete equipment.

by ie9sk3sjd on 2012-02-15 13:42:33

In the production process of autoclaved aerated concrete (AAC) blocks, selecting an appropriate curing method is crucial. Choosing a good AAC equipment manufacturer is essential as well. Autoclaved curing during production is a necessary condition to achieve desired strength and performance characteristics. It not only affects the quality of the finished product but also influences production efficiency and energy consumption. The best curing regime not only ensures adequate and reasonable curing of the green body, allowing the product to reach its designed strength in a short time, but also maximizes the prevention of potential damage to the green body and finished product.

Saturated steam at a specific pressure is an indispensable condition for autoclaved curing. Experiments show that AAC block equipment typically uses saturated steam with a gauge pressure greater than 0.8MPa. This pressure allows silica from fly ash to quickly react with active calcium oxide from lime within a certain period, forming the required calcium silicate hydrate gel to bind the fly ash particles, thus achieving the desired strength in the AAC blocks. The autoclaving curing regime should be determined based on the quality of raw materials, steam pressure, and the required strength of the product. Both excessively long or short autoclaving times are detrimental to the production of fly ash autoclaved bricks.

During autoclaved curing, a vacuum pump is first used to evacuate air from the autoclave for 30 minutes, ensuring the internal pressure is pure steam or nearly pure steam pressure to maintain the temperature required for the chemical reactions of the mixed material. To prevent cracks caused by thermal stress differences, the temperature should be increased slowly and uniformly over 1.5 hours until the pressure inside the autoclave reaches a gauge pressure of 0.8MPa, corresponding to a temperature of approximately 170℃～174.5℃. The temperature should then be maintained for 7 hours to allow sufficient time for the active calcium oxide in the lime to chemically react with the silica in the fly ash, forming the required calcium silicate hydrate gel to bind the fly ash particles and achieve the desired strength. The steam can then be released gradually, first slowly and then more quickly, over 1.5 hours to cool down the autoclave, preventing thermal stress differences that could cause cracks in the blocks. The entire autoclaving cycle lasts 10.5 hours. When the steam pressure inside the autoclave drops to zero and the temperature is below 70℃, the door can be opened to remove the bricks. If a steam pressure of 1.0MPa～1.2MPa gauge is used for curing, the constant-temperature time can be significantly shortened to 4.5h～6h to achieve the required strength, greatly improving the utilization rate of the autoclave.

The essence of steam-cured brick equipment lies in ecological economics, with the core being the improvement of ecological environment utilization efficiency. Viewing circular economy as a new production method, it is considered a new technical-economic paradigm in the stage where ecological conditions become constraints on economic growth and excellent ecological environments become public goods. Its essence involves adjusting human production relationships, aiming for sustainable development.

Autoclaved curing of bricks is an indispensable necessity. Therefore, we urge investors to pay attention to this aspect to avoid unnecessary losses due to minor oversights.
