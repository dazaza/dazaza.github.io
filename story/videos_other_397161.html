Chemical Structure: Molecular Formula: C4H3FN2O2 Molecular Weight: 130.08

[Appearance] This product is a clear liquid that is colorless or almost colorless.

[Pharmacology and Toxicology] This drug is first converted into 5-fluoro-2-deoxyuridine monophosphate in the body. The latter inhibits thymidylate synthase, blocking the conversion of deoxyuridine monophosphate to deoxythymidine monophosphate, thereby inhibiting DNA biosynthesis. In addition, by preventing uracil and orotic acid from being incorporated into RNA, it achieves the effect of inhibiting RNA synthesis. This drug is cell cycle-specific and mainly inhibits S-phase cells.

[Pharmacokinetics] This drug is mainly metabolized in the liver and decomposed into carbon dioxide exhaled through the respiratory tract. About 15% of fluorouracil is excreted unchanged via the kidneys within one hour after administration. High doses can cross the blood-brain barrier, reaching cerebrospinal fluid half an hour after intravenous infusion and maintaining for 3 hours. T1/2 is 10-20 minutes, T1/2 is 20 hours.

[Indications] This drug has a broad spectrum of antitumor activity and is mainly used to treat gastrointestinal tumors or large doses of fluorouracil for choriocarcinoma. It is also often used to treat breast cancer, ovarian cancer, lung cancer, cervical cancer, bladder cancer, and skin cancer.

[Usage and Dosage] There is a great difference in dosage between intravenous injection and intravenous infusion of fluorouracil. The general single dose for intravenous injection is 10-20 mg/kg per day based on body weight, used continuously for 5-10 days, with each course being 5-7g (even up to 10g). If administered by intravenous infusion, it is usually 300-500mg/m² per day based on body surface area, used continuously for 3-5 days, with each infusion lasting no less than 6-8 hours; continuous infusion can be maintained for 24 hours using an infusion pump during intravenous infusion. For primary or metastatic liver cancer, intra-arterial catheterization is often used. Intraperitoneal injection is given at a dose of 500-600mg/m² once per week, with 2-4 administrations constituting one course.

[Adverse Reactions] 1. Nausea, loss of appetite, or vomiting. Usually not serious with normal doses. Occasionally oral mucositis or ulcers, abdominal discomfort, or diarrhea may occur. A common side effect is reduced white blood cell count in peripheral blood (mostly reaching the lowest point 2-3 weeks after the start of treatment, and usually returning to normal after 3-4 weeks), platelet reduction is rare. Extremely rare cases include coughing, shortness of breath, or cerebellar ataxia. 2. Long-term use may lead to neurological toxicity. 3. Rarely, ischemia of the heart muscle may occur after medication, which may present as angina pectoris and changes in the electrocardiogram. Discontinue if cardiovascular adverse reactions are confirmed (arrhythmia, angina, ST segment changes).

[Contraindications] There have been extremely rare cases where congenital malformations occurred due to the use of this drug during the first three months of pregnancy, and it may have long-term effects on the fetus. Therefore, this drug is contraindicated during the first three months of pregnancy in women. Due to the potential mutagenic, teratogenic, carcinogenic properties of this drug and possible toxic side effects in infants, breastfeeding is not allowed during the use of this drug. When accompanied by chickenpox or herpes zoster, this drug should not be used. Fluorouracil is contraindicated in debilitated patients.
