Forbes: 9 Things To Watch For At The Apple Event

by anonymous on 2013-09-11 13:45:11

Forbes website published a commentary article titled "9 Things to Watch for at Apple's Event", which is now fully excerpted as follows: Apple has invited global media to attend the company's new product launch scheduled for this Tuesday, September 10. Almost everyone has their own opinion about the upcoming products. Clearly, this event will drive Apple to continue advancing in the mobile market over the next 12 months. Below are the nine key points I believe need attention at this event.

First, the iPhone to be launched this Tuesday will use a combination of the number 5 and the letters S and C for its naming.

The market generally expects that two new iPhones will be introduced at this event, namely the 5S and 5C. It is expected that the 5S will be an iterative version of the current iPhone 5. Given that the two-year contracts of most iPhone 4S users are about to expire, the timing of the 5S launch can encourage these users to renew their contracts and remain within the Apple ecosystem. Additionally, it is worth noting what features the 5S will have to maintain maximum freshness relative to handheld devices like the Samsung Galaxy S4.

Second, there will be a difference between the price announced at the event for the 5C and the actual retail price.

I expect that the pricing of the iPhone 5C may be relatively high compared to what it represents. A low-cost version of the iPhone could open up new markets for Apple but would also deviate from the company's previous strategy. I anticipate that the price without contract bundling will be quite close to the prices of other iPhones in this range, especially the older iPhone 5. The key is the subsidy prices from various networks, but it is unlikely that they will be announced at the Apple event.

Third, vibrant shell colors prepared for the youth market.

Rumors suggest that the low-cost iPhone (possibly the Apple 5C) will come in a variety of shell colors. The market speculates that they could be black (or blue-gray), white, red (or pink), yellow, green, blue, which are the same colors Nokia uses for its stylish phones. Therefore, if you want to stand out, purple might be a good choice, just like how the purple iPod Nano stood out among Apple's MP3 players.

Fourth, C does not necessarily mean cheap.

Apple is negotiating with China Mobile, one of the world's largest wireless operators. It seems that the iPhone 5C will eventually be available on China Mobile, but negotiations are ongoing and the contract may not be signed by the time of the Tuesday event. Thus, the letter C could represent China (China), rather than cheap (cheap).

Fifth, not all products require cellular radio.

Do not overlook the iPod series. From a consumer electronics perspective, these products may not be very attractive, but they are very suitable for attracting new users into the Apple ecosystem. The iPod Touch can update its hard drive to keep the device associated with the iPhone 5S. However, more likely, due to the launch of iOS 7, the iPod can be resold as a "new product" for another year, possibly with a price reduction below the estimated price of the iPhone 5C.

Sixth, a new star returns to the Apple stage.

Previously, Connie Guglielmo also mentioned this point in Forbes. Craig Federighi, Apple's senior vice president of software engineering, appeared more times at this year's Apple Worldwide Developers Conference (WWDC) than any other Apple executive, making this senior vice president quite prominent. Federighi returned to Apple in April 2009, and it was Tim Cook's promotion that made him a focal figure.

Seventh, responding to Google's Chromecast TV stick.

The multimedia TV stick has been a "little monster" in Apple's product lineup. Since its debut in September 2006, it has driven Apple into the television and set-top box market. To date, Apple has iterated and improved the software multiple times and revised and perfected the hardware three times. Of course, Google's Chromecast is also available, but Apple's seven years of accumulated consumer data is very valuable. Apple may release an "Apple TV" software update, which will also be good news for consumers.

Eighth, no response to Samsung's smartwatch Galaxy Gear.

Regardless of whether Apple announces the iWatch at the event, the product itself will be a news point. Unlike the numerous exposures of the iPhone, there has been very little information about the iWatch. Previously, Samsung's Galaxy Gear did not make any breakthroughs in hardware or software, merely piecing together the current components to the best of its ability. Unless Apple has achieved a significant technological breakthrough, there will be no difference in functionality between the iWatch and the Galaxy Gear. Moreover, based on past historical trends, Apple will not announce entering a new market unless it is certain of achieving significant improvements, so I do not expect to see an Apple watch device at this event.

Ninth, is anything missing?

The products not mentioned above are equally important. It is expected that Apple will launch a series of products before the end of the year, including the already announced MacPro, as well as updates to the much-anticipated iPad and iPad mini lineups. I expect that at this Tuesday's event, Apple will focus only on the iPhone and iPod families, dominating the news cycle for the rest of the week, with product launch dates possibly near the end of this month. Then, repeat the process at the October iPad launch, just as they did last year, Apple consistently dominates the headlines, and it is clear that the company's PR model will not change this year either.
