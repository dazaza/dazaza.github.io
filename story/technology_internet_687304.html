Childbirth is more crowded than the "Spring Festival travel rush."

by lmxbbwudi23 on 2012-03-02 15:00:10

[Adding a child in the Year of the Dragon] is an event worth celebrating for every family. But when everyone rushes to have children, the process becomes less smooth. According to experts' predictions, this year will be another big birth year following the year 2000 millennium, the 2007 "Golden Pig Year", and the 2008 Olympic Games year. The number of newborns is expected to increase by 5% compared to previous years.

This "baby boom" has overwhelmed hospitals that are already crowded. It has also caused headaches for expectant mothers and women planning to conceive: many have failed multiple times to register at hospitals, and even after finally registering, monthly prenatal check-ups and eventual childbirth become extremely difficult, almost like living through a series of shocks. During interviews, one expectant mother sighed, "Having a child is more congested and difficult than the Spring Festival travel rush. The Spring Festival travel rush only makes buying tickets painful, but having a child smoothly requires enduring months of pain."

It's hard to register at top-tier hospitals.

In early January, upon learning she was pregnant, Li Li (a pseudonym) from Chaoyang District, Beijing, was so excited she couldn't sleep all night. The next day, she and her husband took leave specifically and arrived early at the Beijing Obstetrics and Gynecology Hospital. As soon as she entered the outpatient hall, she was stunned - the hall was packed with hundreds of people, leaving barely any room to move. "It's just 8 o'clock in the morning, and all the daily appointments are already gone." After inquiring with the registration staff, Li Li decided to have her husband come back the next morning at 4 AM to queue up. The next day, the doctor confirmed Li Li's pregnancy and informed her that she needed an ultrasound, but the earliest appointment for an ultrasound was over a week away. A week later, after waiting half a day, Li Li finally finished her ultrasound, but the doctor's words left her confused: "There are too many people giving birth, and all the beds are already booked. We can't create a file for you now." Li Li felt wronged and wondered, "Isn't it supposed to be easy to create a file after becoming pregnant? Why is it so difficult now?"

Zhang Quan (a pseudonym) from Haidian District, Beijing, had a similar experience to Li Li. After learning she was pregnant, she visited Peking University Third Hospital, Sino-Japan Friendship Hospital, and Beijing Union Medical College Hospital, only to be told each time that the beds were already fully booked. At many hospitals' obstetric triage desks, signs reading "Full registration for August" were displayed, clearly informing expectant mothers with due dates in August that they could not register. Zhang Quan eventually chose a nearby second-tier hospital out of necessity. "This is just a backup plan; I'll still have my family check if there are any available beds at the top-tier hospitals," said Zhang Quan.

According to the reporter's investigation, according to tradition, pregnant women usually go to the hospital to register after being pregnant for more than 12 weeks. However, because of the large number of women rushing to give birth this year, the registration reservation times at many top-tier hospitals in Beijing have been significantly advanced. Some hospitals even require reservations four weeks in advance, meaning that women should register immediately after finding out they're pregnant.

Regarding the difficulty of registration, a doctor from the obstetrics department of Beijing Obstetrics and Gynecology Hospital explained: "There are a particularly high number of pregnant women coming to register this year. Due to the limited number of beds each month, we have no choice but to reject some." During the interview, the reporter encountered a pregnant woman who was already 22 weeks along, crying and begging the doctor for help because she couldn't register. The doctor expressed helplessness, saying all the beds were full. This pregnant woman stated that both she and her husband were migrant workers in Beijing. If they couldn't register at a Beijing hospital, it would mean going back to their hometown to give birth or choosing a private hospital with much higher fees. "Going back to give birth means losing our jobs and being separated from my husband. And we simply can't afford the private hospital fees." In the end, she followed the doctor's advice and checked if there were any available registrations at a second-tier hospital.

Not just in Beijing, during visits to several top-tier hospitals and maternity hospitals in Shanghai, the reporter discovered that many obstetric departments were already overwhelmed. Among them, well-known top-tier hospitals' obstetric departments and specialized maternity hospitals faced severe bed shortages. On the morning of February 13th, the reporter spent over an hour trying to get through to the booking hotline of Shanghai First Maternity and Infant Hospital. The customer service representative stated that the workload had at least doubled recently. On the first working day of the lunar New Year, the number of obstetric patients exceeded 2000.

Every step is arduous.

For pregnant women, registering is just the first step in a long journey. Subsequent prenatal check-ups and childbirth present new rounds of challenges. After interviewing multiple pregnant women, the reporter deeply appreciated their hardships during the childbirth process.

Waking up in the middle of the night to queue for registration, scheduling prenatal check-ups is even harder. Mr. Wang's wife is six months pregnant, and every time he accompanies her to prenatal check-ups at Beijing Obstetrics and Gynecology Hospital, they need to wake up between four and five in the morning to queue for registration. Upon arriving at the hospital entrance, one of them looks for parking while the other rushes to queue up. "I thought arriving at four would definitely secure us a spot, but when I entered the registration hall, I saw it filled with small stools, and many pregnant women sitting on them holding milk and bread while queuing," Mr. Wang said. Even so, it's still difficult to get registered. Most numbers are taken through phone reservations, leaving very few available for on-site queuing. "Many people know there might be no numbers left but still refuse to leave until they personally ask the staff." Mr. Wang added that he even saw several pregnant women experiencing dizziness and nausea due to prolonged queuing. Eventually, they stopped going to Beijing Obstetrics and Gynecology Hospital for check-ups and switched to another hospital.

Long queues for examinations and using the restroom. At Beijing Obstetrics and Gynecology Hospital, the reporter observed that by 9:30 in the morning, the corridors on the second and third floors were already packed with pregnant women, most accompanied by family members. The few available seats in the corridor were highly sought after. If a pregnant woman wanted to stand up and walk around after sitting for a long time, she had to ask her family member to hold her seat, otherwise, it would immediately be taken by someone else. The line outside the women's restroom stretched long. Ms. Wu, who was undergoing a prenatal check-up, mentioned that every time she had an ultrasound, she had to wait from 8 AM until noon. She dared not step out for fresh air, fearing she might miss her name being called. The waiting process was excruciating - sitting for too long made her legs swell, while standing for too long made her abdomen uncomfortable.

Short prenatal check-up times and insufficient doctor explanations. "After waiting three hours in line, the check-up only lasts three minutes," Ms. Wu expressed her discomfort during each prenatal check-up. Thinking about the pain of registration and waiting, she felt that visiting the hospital was a torment. "At 30 weeks pregnant, I haven't even had a proper look at my baby." Ms. Wu said that during each ultrasound, she wanted the doctor to explain things in detail, but due to the large number of people waiting, the doctors could only give cursory answers.

Insufficient delivery beds force some mothers to recuperate in the hallway after childbirth. Ms. Yang gave birth to her "dragon baby" at the Shanghai Pudong Women and Children's Health Care Hospital, but due to a lack of beds, she had to temporarily recover in the hospital corridor before finally getting a bed near discharge time. Another patient, Ms. Lin, admitted through emergency services, spent her initial postpartum days in a five-bed room at Beijing Obstetrics and Gynecology Hospital. "The beds in the room are extra beds, the toilet is at the end of the corridor, and the drafty hallway causes excessive sweating after childbirth, making it easy to catch a cold when using the restroom," she recounted.

Plan ahead for giving birth in the Year of the Dragon

The hardships of pregnancy are evident. If you want to have a "dragon baby," it's wise to heed the experts' advice:

Consult hospitals in advance to understand the reservation and registration situation. Senior health science popularization expert Yang Chao reminds expectant parents to consult multiple hospitals during pre-pregnancy and pregnancy stages to understand their reservation and registration situations. Preferably choose hospitals closer to home. Pregnant women preparing to give birth in Beijing should prepare necessary materials for registration, such as maternal and child health manuals and birth permits.

Understand your physical condition and seek medical attention promptly. Yang Chao suggests that women planning to conceive should learn essential prenatal knowledge through professional books and consulting doctors, consciously observe changes in their bodies, and seek professional support at the earliest opportunity.

Prepare well for prenatal check-ups. Yang Chao advises expectant mothers to clarify the precautions given by doctors beforehand to avoid improper preparation that may delay or affect the examination results.

No need to blindly pursue top-tier hospitals. Yang Chao emphasizes, "If you are not an older woman, do not have a history of pregnancy obstacles or gynecological diseases, and are in good health, there is no need to overly worry or blindly chase top-tier hospitals." For routine prenatal check-ups, you can choose qualified hospitals closer to home instead of crowding into major hospitals.
