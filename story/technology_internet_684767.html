Is it true that Catholics believe they're the only ones going to heaven? It's not what the Roman Catholic Church officially teaches, as defined by Vatican II. I don't get it, where does this thought come from?

1) Not every Catholic knows or has fully absorbed what their own church officially teaches.

2) Some people (I'm not saying your mother and extended family or your friend’s mother) have a comparatively strong desire to feel superior or "more right" than others, but this is true of people in many different Christian groups, not just in Catholicism.

3) Non-Catholics spread misinformation about what the Roman Catholic Church officially teaches.

4) There are a number of groups - some "traditionalists," Sedevacantists, the Society of St. Pius X, Feeneyists, etc. - who consider themselves true Catholics (but are condemned by the official Catholic church) that claim that Vatican II contradicts what the Catholic Church has previously taught. For example, if you type in "no salvation outside the church" or a variant into Google, one of the top results is a site maintained by the group "Slaves of the Immaculate Heart of Mary" who argue for a very strict interpretation of the phrase "outside the Church there is no salvation."

5) Historically, the Catholic Church has not only officially said "outside the Church there is no salvation" but popes have written official documents that, at face value, more strongly claim that submission to papal authority and/or being united to the Roman Catholic Church is necessary for salvation. For example, that Feeneyist web page provides the following quotes in support of their strict interpretation of "outside the Church there is no salvation."

"We declare, say, define, and pronounce that it is absolutely necessary for the salvation of every human creature to be subject to the Roman Pontiff." (Pope Boniface VIII, the Bull Unam Sanctam, 1302.)

"The most Holy Roman Church firmly believes, professes and preaches that none of those existing outside the Catholic Church, not only pagans, but also Jews and heretics and schismatics, can have a share in life eternal; but that they will go into the eternal fire which was prepared for the devil and his angels, unless before death they are joined with Her; and that so important is the unity of this ecclesiastical body that only those remaining within this unity can profit by the sacraments of the Church unto salvation, and they alone can receive an eternal recompense for their fasts, their almsgivings, their other works of Christian piety and the duties of a Christian soldier. No one, let his almsgiving be as great as it may, no one, even if he pour out his blood for the Name of Christ, can be saved, unless he remain within the bosom and the unity of the Catholic Church." (Pope Eugene IV, the Bull Cantate Domino, 1441.)

I've heard Catholics today say that such words apply only to those who have heard the message of what the Roman Catholic Church teaches concerning itself and yet knowingly and willingly reject it, and not to those who have not heard this message. Concerning those who apparently have heard what the Roman Catholic Church teaches concerning itself and yet rejected it until their death, I've heard Catholics say that they might still be saved because:

1) they didn't really hear what the Catholic Church teaches and hence could not knowingly and willingly reject it,

2) they might not have been able to submit to the teaching, through no fault of their own,

or 3) God might have given them an opportunity to become a Catholic after death but before the soul leaves the body.
