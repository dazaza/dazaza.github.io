Samsung's smartwatch snubbed, already working on second generation product

by anonymous on 2013-09-24 21:09:15

At the beginning of this month's new product launch, Samsung took the lead as expected, releasing its first wearable computing device - the Galaxy Gear smartwatch.

The Galaxy Gear features a brushed metal surface and comes with six different colored rubber straps. In terms of specific configuration, the Galaxy Gear has a 1.63-inch Super AMOLED screen with a resolution of 320x320, equipped with an 800MHz single-core processor, a 1.9 million pixel camera, 512MB of memory, 4GB of storage space, a non-removable 315mAh battery, built-in speakers, Bluetooth 4.0, accelerometer, gyroscope, and two microphones.

However, according to reports from South Korea's Digital Times, this product may become one of the shortest-lived wearable computing devices in history because Samsung has already started developing its next generation product.

In fact, since the Galaxy Gear made its debut on September 4 in Berlin, consumer response to this product has been rather calm. Many critics complained that the Galaxy Gear's battery life of 25 hours is too short, and the number of smart devices that can be paired with it is extremely limited.

Currently, only the Samsung Galaxy Note 3 smartphone and the Galaxy Note 10.1 tablet can connect with it. However, Samsung stated that more devices will support the Galaxy Gear in the future.

The general view outside regarding Samsung's release of the Galaxy Gear is that the company hastily released its own wearable computing device to seize market opportunities ahead of Apple's rumored "iWatch smartwatch." Moreover, after Apple registered the "iWatch" trademark in Japan and included a dedicated "M7" coprocessor chip in the new iPhone 5s to manage various sensors and motions within the device, expectations for the iWatch have only grown.

Meanwhile, Ian Fogg, Director of Mobile and Communications at the well-known market research firm IHS, commented:

"Samsung's Galaxy Gear is just a prototype disguised as a consumer electronic product, and for this reason, it will be very difficult for the product to succeed in the market. Currently, there are many shortcomings with the device, such as high pricing, poor battery life, and the limitation of needing to be used in conjunction with a smartphone. Overall, this product is likely to struggle to succeed, and Samsung will need to launch a better-designed device to make another attempt at this market."

Meanwhile, other testers indicated that the Galaxy Gear's operations experience delays, the system menu design is confusing, and its $299 price tag seems somewhat excessive.

According to Daum, a Korean portal website located in Samsung's headquarters, the first Galaxy Gear is merely a "trial product" for Samsung. The company hopes to gather market feedback through this product and then use it as a basis to develop the second-generation Galaxy Gear. Daum predicts that Samsung may release the next-generation Galaxy Gear 2 smartwatch at the Consumer Electronics Show (CES) in January next year or at the Mobile World Congress (MWC) in March next year. Moreover, the second-generation Galaxy Gear might introduce location-based services based on its built-in GPS positioning chip.

Samsung declined to comment on this matter.

There is no doubt that the field of smartwatches and the underlying wearable computer devices has become a large industry that no tech company can ignore in recent years. However, most analysts believe that Samsung's first Galaxy Gear cannot adequately stimulate consumer purchasing interest.

IHS estimates that smartwatch shipments will reach 268,000 units in 2013, with a significant portion contributed by California-based Pebble Company. The company's smartwatches have shipped over 100,000 units and are sold through a series of U.S. retailers.

In addition, Sony's second-generation smartwatch also has some market appeal. IHS stated that smartwatch shipments will see a significant increase in 2014 and are expected to reach around 39 million units by 2018.
