Advice for Job Seekers in the Cold Winter

by hophi on 2008-12-29 08:02:50

Under the financial crisis, enterprises are "getting through the winter", and the voices of layoffs and salary cuts are endless. Naturally, under such a situation, many employers' recruitment plans have been significantly "reduced", either reducing or completely eliminating new recruitment needs - they don't even have time to lay off employees! In contrast, the tide of job seekers looking for work remains strong.

That is to say, at this moment, facing the severity of the employment situation, it's harder to find a job, and even harder to find a satisfactory one! From this perspective, isn't it also like "getting through the winter" for job seekers?

Therefore, many job seekers have to pragmatically view the world they face. For example, when job hunting, in order to secure a position first, some people voluntarily propose that they can go without pay for the first few months. A few days ago, I wrote a blog post specifically about this topic called "What Does 'Zero-Wage Employment' for College Students Mean?"

And just a few days ago, when our firm was recruiting legal assistants, one applicant also voluntarily proposed, "I can go without pay for the first three months." The feelings of the job seeker are certainly understandable, but for rational employers, whether or not to hire someone, I think the key issue is not whether they can save a few months of salary, nor would they take legal risks for a few months of salary, but rather whether the applicant truly meets their recruitment expectations.

Speaking of our recent recruitment at the firm, since I participated in part of the interviews, from the perspective of the employer, I could indeed feel the urgency of the job seekers. However, I must also say that among the current job seekers, many problems lie with themselves.

Below, I will briefly outline some issues found among job seekers during the recruitment process.

—In these issues, even a small detail may lead to the loss of a job opportunity, thus extending one's job search journey.

1. What kind of image should be presented during an interview?

We have seen a large number of books or suggestions guiding interview preparation. If you value the potential job enough, you will definitely care about how you present yourself at the interview site. Conversely, if you do not take it seriously, the interview results will naturally not "take you seriously." For example, in my industry, for someone who will be working at a law firm, what kind of attire is appropriate when working professionally in a relatively high-end office building? This should be common knowledge, right? However, among many of the interviewees, very few actually wore formal attire (suits, ties, leather shoes)! Whether they were newly graduated college students or those with similar work experience, they seemed completely unaware of the importance of professional appearance. I am truly baffled by this! I even began to wonder: who exactly is looking for jobs among the workplace population, needing to continuously look for jobs without success? Who needs to constantly send out resumes, constantly attend interviews, and then endlessly wait without any response?

2. Punctuality! Punctuality! — Unacceptable lies about being late

This topic involves two issues: the job seeker's sense of time and honesty.

For a professional, especially for someone who needs a job, having a strict sense of time is crucial during job interviews. When you receive an interview notice, if you agree to attend, you should naturally arrive at the scheduled time as agreed. Whether arriving too early or too late, neither is acceptable.

There have been instances where applicants remembered the wrong interview date and showed up a day early. For many recruiting units, many things are carried out according to plan. Although the applicant wasted a trip, it doesn't cost the recruiter anything, but what impression does it leave on the recruiter?

Regarding being late, generally speaking, there is no excuse for being late during an interview unless it's due to unforeseen circumstances. Things like not knowing the way or traffic jams cannot exempt you from responsibility for being late. If you unfortunately arrive late for an interview, the most sincere apology should be the most understandable answer, without further explanation.

If being late makes people uncomfortable, lying about being late is even worse! The former is merely a matter of time management, while the latter is an issue of integrity—serious consequences.

Once, an applicant arrived late, and I asked him—after he entered without showing any remorse—"Why are you late?"

The applicant replied, "I live far away, coming from a suburban county."

I asked again, "Distance alone shouldn't be a reason for being late, right?"

The applicant answered, "I live far away, haven't been here before, didn't know how long it would take to get here, so I didn't manage my time well."

I stopped asking.

Later, during the interview conversation, the applicant casually mentioned another company located in the same office building as our firm. I took the opportunity to ask, "Oh, have you been to our office building before?"

Answer: "Yes, I have a classmate who works at that company."

I stopped asking. The interview ended.

—Naturally, this job opportunity for the applicant came to nothing.

3. Reflecting on oneself, how is your resume printed?

Often, how well a job seeker's resume is done largely indicates their attitude towards this "job" of job hunting.

Some resumes are very well-designed, although a good job is not determined solely by a good-looking resume, I still don't want to criticize overly. Here, I'm referring to poorly done resumes.

Some resumes have random layouts, messy tables, inconsistent fonts, incomplete sentences, and punctuation errors;

Some resumes were printed years ago when the applicant was still in school and never updated;

Some resumes are carelessly folded and stuffed into pockets, only to be pulled out during the interview;

Some resumes exaggerate excessively, claiming "vast knowledge" and "definitely competent";

Some applicants avoid the trouble of designing their own resumes and directly use templates found online, downloading and printing them;

Among these, the most intolerable issue is typos in the resume (some are ones the applicant has noticed but were too lazy to correct, thinking the recruiter might not notice; others are simply unnoticed).

Among the typos I've encountered, the most frequent one is mistyping "为人踏实认真" (diligent and serious) as "为人塌实认真" (careless typo).

...

All of the above are not fabricated or imagined by me. All these issues were discovered in resumes placed on my desk.

4. Think carefully before stating your "expected monthly salary"

Under the current employment situation, while some job seekers actively promote the idea of "zero-wage employment," others overestimate themselves, lacking understanding of the industry standards and unrealistically setting their "expectations" too high. For instance, in today's China after thirty years of reform and opening-up, the "sea turtles" (returnees from overseas study) no longer carry much prestige. If one lacks proper market insight and self-awareness, it’s no surprise that "sea turtles" become "sea waits" (those waiting for jobs).

In this regard, there is a perfect case in point. My friend, senior mental improvement expert Huang Hua, had a consultation example on his BoWen website about the confusion of a returned female student, which might offer some enlightenment to job seekers.

Netizen asks: I am a so-called returnee from the UK, but I find it really difficult to find a job after returning home. Is it because there are too many talents domestically or have I lost touch with society?? Several employers approached me, offering only a 4000 RMB monthly salary. Isn’t that insulting? Is this all the worth of a returnee talent? I really can't understand. Have the times changed, or is my level really only worth this much money? Please give me some advice.

Huang Hua answers: There's a saying in the workplace: in the first five years after graduation, people often ask you which school you graduated from? Because back then, a good school means high ability and wide resources. But after fifteen years of work, it's rare for anyone to ask which school you graduated from. Instead, they'll ask where you've worked and what achievements you've made?

Salary does not equal ability, and a high degree does not equal ability. Enterprises are economic entities; as long as it's profitable, they will do it. As long as there is a clear expectation of returns on talent, enterprises can accept any price.

So, my personal suggestion is to start from the work you're good at. Don't worry too much about the exact amount of salary. Especially in this economically tough environment, finding your core value is much more meaningful than worrying about your current salary.

Now, in the midst of the cold winter, job seekers waiting for hope in the cold, I hope this article can give you some food for thought and reflection.
