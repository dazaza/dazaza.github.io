There's No Such Thing As Software Productivity

Through repeated conversations, Bill Caputo eventually convinced me of some incredible things. These things changed the way I look at problems entirely and made me rethink how to work better.

There is no such thing as "productivity" in software development.

Almost exactly as Martin Fowler discovered 10 years ago, measuring software development work in terms of productivity makes no sense. The reason is that they do not belong to the same category. In other words, productivity lacks applicability as a measure of software development work. "How much code/software was created today?" is a meaningless question. Even if it could be measured this way, the productivity of software development work cannot estimate its business value in any meaningful way.

This is because software development does not necessarily have to produce anything. Let me give you an example: Suppose there are two programmers working on two identical projects separately, and they were assigned the same task on the same day. The first person, Frank, went back to his computer and wrote a 1000-line framework that perfectly solved the problem. The code was written to standard, fully tested, and came with detailed documentation describing deployment and operation processes. The second programmer, Pete, turned around and went to the park, where he fed pigeons while thinking about the problem. Around 4:45 PM, Pete strolled back to the office, deleted 200 lines of code, and deployed his changes... and thus the problem was solved.

Which programmer had higher "productivity" today? The answer is: it doesn't matter. What matters is that Pete solved the problem while reducing the team’s long-term maintenance costs. Frank also solved the problem, but by producing code, he increased the maintenance cost, so (all else being equal) his solution is worse. And calling Pete more "productive" completely distorts the metaphor from a practical standpoint.

I believe that what excellent programmers should do is eliminate problems rather than produce something. Conversely, producing something is secondary. Therefore, technical outputs, such as code, documentation, and data, are necessary but harmful for achieving the goal of "eliminating problems." This is why sometimes the most effective solution is a 5-minute conversation.

The strongest support for this way of thinking: when you view software development through this lens, many thorny issues that can be seen but not measured suddenly become much easier to understand. For example, why does isolating programmers from their clients make them inefficient? Wouldn’t avoiding interruptions improve work efficiency? The answer is no; logically, it would make them more efficient... but it would also make them less effective. Because their job is to solve the client’s problems, isolation from the client prevents them from identifying or confirming the problems. On the contrary, maintaining communication with those who have the problem allows them to solve it more effectively, and sometimes you don’t even need to touch the keyboard for 8 hours in a day.

This leads us to another question: why does the cost of maintenance in software development seem so hard to accept compared to other costs? Why can we never get it "right" the first time? One explanation is that software is a fixed solution to a potentially changing problem. When the problem changes (or our understanding of it changes), a gap arises between the problem and the solution. The continuous patching required to bridge these gaps caused by evolving problems is costly. This also explains why video games typically have lower maintenance costs compared to other software projects. This is because the problems they need to solve (getting people to buy and play the game) are largely based on human psychology, which doesn’t change often.

Where does the talk of a 10x difference in "productivity" between good and bad programmers come from? Everyone says it’s true, but in fact, no one can directly measure it. Our theory can also explain this. Compared to work efficiency, "solving problems" is something that is easier to "manipulate" (a financial term), making it easy to achieve effects that differ by an order of magnitude. Solving problems requires information and insight. You either have it or you don’t. There’s no need for raw materials, no production capacity limit. It’s not that bad programmers type slowly. It’s not that they could do better if they tried harder. They lack the vision and necessary information to efficiently solve problems. Perhaps the reason we can't measure the difference in productivity between good and bad programmers is that there is nothing to measure.

Many other phenomena can be explained by this theory. If you look for them, you'll find some. Recently, I've been collecting examples of this kind... try it out and see if this theory shows up in your own work. Whenever you find yourself talking about improving "productivity/work efficiency," ask yourself if you're solving the problem in the right way. Keep in mind: if you can solve the problem without producing anything, then producing anything is a waste.
