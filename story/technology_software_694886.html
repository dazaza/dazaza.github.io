Mistakes Easily Made on a Treadmill

by anonymous on 2012-03-07 12:26:39

Because it is not affected by weather and venue, the treadmill has taken up a place in many families. However, although running at home is convenient, improper methods can make the exercise effect less satisfactory. For this reason, our reporter interviewed Li Liang, a fitness coach at Beijing Felix Fitness Club, who pointed out several common misconceptions when using a treadmill.

1. Sprinting as soon as you get on the treadmill. Before getting on the treadmill, you should do warm-up exercises such as pressing your legs, squatting, stretching muscles, and flexing joints to increase muscle temperature, making them more flexible and less prone to injury. After getting on the treadmill, start with "dynamic" warm-ups like slow walking or jogging, gradually increasing the intensity. This process usually takes about 10-15 minutes. In addition, when getting off the treadmill, you should also gradually slow down to avoid dizziness.

2. Running for too long. The time, intensity, and method of exercising on a treadmill should be determined based on your exercise goals. The supply of energy substances in the body during treadmill exercise goes from sugar to fat, and then to protein. Only after jogging for more than half an hour will fat be consumed, but after more than an hour, protein will be consumed. Therefore, if your goal is weight loss, the duration of exercise should neither be too short nor too long.

3. Holding onto the handles while running. Running is not just about moving your legs; the coordinated swinging of both arms helps maintain overall body balance and allows the upper body to participate in energy consumption. Some people run holding onto the handles with their chest caved in and back arched, causing the center of gravity to lean forward, which increases pressure on the lumbar vertebrae and may lead to lumbar muscle strain over time. Moreover, during the running process, the impact force felt by the foot upon landing is almost five times the person's weight, and leaning forward increases the impact force on the leg and foot joints. Therefore, when exercising on a treadmill, it is essential to keep your abdomen tucked in, stand upright, and tighten the muscles of your waist and back.

4. Higher incline is better. Increasing the incline and speed of the treadmill increases the intensity of the exercise, but the choice of intensity should vary from person to person. For example, elderly people running at too high an incline can cause damage to the knee joints, so it is best to exercise under level conditions. Also, speed is not necessarily the faster the better; under different exercise modes such as fast running, slow running, fast walking, or climbing, the proportion of energy substances consumed by the body differs. For instance, fast running consumes more sugar but less fat, and solely adopting this method is not beneficial for those exercising with the goal of weight loss.

5. Not wearing shoes or wearing the wrong shoes. Some people use the treadmill at home barefoot or only wear socks. In fact, barefoot running on a treadmill can cause unnecessary damage to the knee, ankle, and other joints due to the vibration, and sweaty feet can easily cause slipping. Wearing thick socks can provide some shock absorption, but since socks lack the elasticity of athletic shoes, they cannot replace them. Therefore, it is best to wear running shoes when exercising on a treadmill. Running shoes are lighter than regular shoes, have flexible soles, and offer good cushioning.

6. Watching TV while running. Running can be a monotonous activity, and interesting TV programs can make your workout more enjoyable. However, watching TV while running may distract you, and any misstep could result in injury, especially for those unfamiliar with operating the treadmill or engaging in high-intensity exercise. On the other hand, experienced individuals doing slow jogging can choose upbeat or relaxing music.

7. Using the treadmill only for running. The multifunctionality of treadmills means they are no longer just a running conveyor belt. Additional features such as rowing machines, pushers, and pedal bikes allow users to exercise cardiovascular function, upper and lower limb muscles, and abdominal muscles in multiple areas on the same machine. Additionally, adding dumbbells or similar exercises on the treadmill can work more muscle groups. However, it is important to note that these types of exercises should ideally be conducted under the guidance of professional fitness coaches.
