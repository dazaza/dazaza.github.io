12 Key Points for Solving SAT Math Fill-in Questions

by thatseed123 on 2011-04-28 21:45:30

For the SAT Math grid-in questions, it's crucial to carefully read and understand the problem. After calculating your answer, take a moment to consider its reasonableness. The grid-in section includes spaces for writing your answer, but these are not read during scoring. Only the bubbles below are scored. Therefore, even if you write the correct answer in the space but bubble in the wrong one, you won't receive credit. Don't assume that the computer can interpret your handwriting or expect leniency for incorrectly bubbled answers.

1) The symbols available on the grid include only the digits 0-9, a decimal point, and the fraction slash "/". Therefore, the answers for grid-in questions will always fall within the range [0, 9999]. If your calculation results in an answer outside this closed interval, stop immediately, reread the question, check the data you substituted, ensure the result is what the question asks for, and confirm that the units are consistent.

2) A number can be written in any position on the grid and still be scored correctly. For example, the number 35 could be written in the first two boxes, the second two boxes, or the last two boxes. However, as a matter of habit, it’s recommended to use right-alignment when filling in numbers. Additionally, a number cannot be split across non-consecutive boxes. For instance, the number 35 cannot have the digit 3 in the first box and the digit 5 in the third box.

3) Do not round your answers unless absolutely necessary. For example, if you obtain an infinite repeating decimal like .3535353535, both (.353) and (.354) would be scored as correct. However, rounding introduces the risk of errors. Whatever number appears on your calculator, simply fill in as many digits as fit without spending extra time on rounding operations. Never reduce a three-digit answer to one or two digits. For example, if your answer is .3535353535, don’t round it down to .4 or .35. If possible, fill all available boxes with digits.

4) Do not include a leading zero before a decimal point. For instance, if the answer is .35353, simply write (.353). Writing (0.35) is less precise than (.353).

5) Ensure that you clearly see where the decimal point is located on your calculator.

6) For complex fractions, there’s no need to simplify them. You can directly enter something like (38/6) and still get credit. For fractions like (26/35), which don’t fit directly into the four-grid format, use your calculator to divide the numerator by the denominator and then fill in the answer as a decimal.

7) When answering grid-in questions, never directly enter mixed numbers; convert them to improper fractions first before filling in the answer.

8) Whether fractions or decimals, as long as the answer is correct, it will be scored accordingly. For example, for (6/9), entering (2/3), (4/6), (8/12), (.666), or (.667) will all earn full credit.

9) Occasionally, you may encounter questions with multiple possible answers. Filling in any one of them will be considered correct. Based on typical experience, such questions usually yield fewer than five answers, often just 2-3. If you find more than ten solutions, reread the question carefully.

10) When filling in fractions, make sure to distinguish between the fraction slash "/" and the number 1. It's helpful to write the slash slightly slanted and the number 1 straighter. After completing each grid-in question, always double-check your calculated answer against what you’ve filled in, verifying each digit individually. Also, avoid exaggerating the size of the digits you write so they don’t obscure the bubbles underneath.

11) Although the written portion isn’t scored, don’t skip this step, as writing out the answer before bubbling increases accuracy significantly.

12) Each grid-in question is worth one point, so if you can't solve it, don't dwell on it excessively. The advantage is that there’s no penalty for incorrect answers, so feel free to guess if necessary. In the SAT Math section, while the problems are straightforward, there are often traps involving unit conversions, place value names, and the use of negation words. These points require special attention, which will be noted after reviewing relevant knowledge points.
