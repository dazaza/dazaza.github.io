A Blue Ocean Strategy for Appliance Repairmen

by qozrjzo1 on 2012-03-06 20:49:34

On the weekend, I was listening to the radio and they mentioned a person named Liu Xianbao who owns an electrical appliance repair shop. This man came to Shanghai from his hometown in Anhui province in 1996 to work. Now he has a car, a house, a wife, a son, and even a Shanghai hukou (residency permit). In 2008, he was also awarded as an "Excellent Migrant Worker Nationwide."

Liu Xianbao's entrepreneurial journey doesn't sound particularly extraordinary. Initially, he worked as an air conditioner installer for Chunlan Company. Later, he attended night school and obtained intermediate-level certification in refrigeration technology and a basic-level certificate in electronics, which helped him get promoted to an air conditioner repairman. Eventually, not wanting to be an employee anymore, he gathered over 20,000 yuan and started his own electrical appliance repair business.

He doesn’t trade stocks, invest in real estate, engage in pyramid schemes, raise funds, or scam people. For over a decade, he has been doing just one thing—repairing electrical appliances. Moreover, his repair shop isn't even on a main street; it's reportedly tucked away in a corner of a certain market, requiring some twists and turns to find.

Yet, apparently, 85% of the electrical appliance repair business in the Longbai and Hanghua areas now belongs to him.

In casual conversations with friends, I often joke that there are two kinds of entrepreneurs: those who have read the Harvard Business Review and those who haven't. The ones who read the Harvard Business Review generally succeed. Among those who don't, some can still succeed, but only if everything they do aligns with the principles advocated by the Harvard Business Review, because these principles are derived from best practices.

After hearing Liu Xianbao's story, I am certain he falls into the latter category.

Initially, his friends advised Liu Xianbao to open his shop in Xujiahui because of the larger foot traffic. However, he insisted on staying in Longbai and Hanghua. While Xujiahui does attract crowds, most are busy white-collar workers rushing about, few of whom need their electrical appliances repaired. Besides, there are already many repair shops in Xujiahui, and the rent is expensive.

Longbai area is different—it’s full of residential neighborhoods, mainly inhabited by elderly residents. Elderly people tend to cherish their belongings and when something breaks, they usually look for someone to fix it first.

Therefore, instead of joining the bustling crowd in Xujiahui, Liu Xianbao chose to stay in the small market in Longbai, mixing daily with old uncles and aunts. Look at how clever he is—he hasn’t read the Blue Ocean Strategy, yet he knows how to avoid competition.

Even in the blue ocean, there are red waves. Although Longbai is a large area with other repair shops that are more visible than Liu Xianbao's, when you ask the locals where to repair electrical appliances, nine out of ten times they will recommend Liu Xianbao. It seems everyone knows his inconspicuous little shop.

Why do people recommend him?

His skills are good, and his prices are fair, that's for sure. But I believe that these aren't the only reasons other repair shops differ much either. After all, setting up shop near residential areas isn't the same as wandering around sharpening scissors or mending knives. If you stay in one place and fail to repair something properly, customers will come back to complain.

In my view, the biggest difference between Liu Xianbao and other shop owners is his kindness or sense of corporate social responsibility. Every morning, he sends two apprentices to provide free services in the residential communities, rotating through 31 neighborhoods year after year.

So, who receives this free service every morning? The old uncles and aunts, of course. In Shanghai, retired old uncles and aunts are known for being frugal, enthusiastic, and energetic. Whenever they encounter something good, new, or cost-saving (or less costly), they spread the word quickly, telling ten people who then tell another hundred. Just look at the trial runs of the Expo or visit Jing'an Bread House in Huaihai Road at three or four o'clock in the afternoon.

Thus, wouldn't Liu Xianbao lose money by offering free services? No.

Old uncles and aunts like Liu Xianbao because of his kindness, and they support his business. They may bring small appliances like kettles and hair dryers for free repairs, but they still need technicians to come to their homes for air conditioners and refrigerators, which is exactly the part of the business Liu Xianbao wants to make money from.

The old uncles and aunts themselves aren't enough; during their grocery shopping, morning exercises, or mahjong games, they inevitably exchange information and introduce him to their relatives and friends. This way, even someone like me living in Pudong heard about him. And after I spread the word, even more people know about him.

What is this called? People who have read articles such as "How Much Is Word-of-Mouth Worth?" and "Predict Growth by Watching One Number" — both published in the Harvard Business Review in April 2008 and February 2004 respectively — might remember a concept: Customer Referral Value (CRV). This concept reminds us that the value of a customer comes not only from the products they buy but also from their experiences with your business and their evaluations of you in front of others. Your best promoter might be more valuable than your most loyal customer, so companies should focus on developing high CRV customers.

For Liu Xianbao's repair shop, the old uncles and aunts in the Longbai area are clearly such customers. Not only do they have high CRV, but their lifetime value (CLV) is also significant.

Liu Xianbao probably doesn't know about theories like the Blue Ocean Strategy, Corporate Social Responsibility, or Customer Referral Value, but he is lucky because everything he does unconsciously aligns with these principles.

And so, he succeeded.
