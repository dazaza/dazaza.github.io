Analysis: Chromecast is the most important smart TV technology in history

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 18:06:54

Beijing Time, July 28 - According to foreign media reports, Google has recently launched a streaming device called Chromecast. As more details about this product have been revealed, its importance and value have become increasingly apparent. Although the device is priced at only $35, it's not as simple as it appears. In fact, analysts believe it might be the most significant smart TV technology in history.

Famous electronics teardown website iFixit concluded after dismantling the Chromecast that it is essentially a luxurious device with limited purposes. From recent reviews, we know that Chromecast can currently stream audio and video content from Google Play, Netflix, and YouTube. Simply put, its functionality doesn't surpass existing devices by much.

But this is just for now. What about tomorrow or the day after?

A deeper understanding of Chromecast's internal workings reveals that it is a technology with unlimited potential. In fact, if Google can successfully build an ecosystem around Chromecast, it will undoubtedly become the most important smart TV technology to date.

It should be noted that when you purchase a Chromecast, you're getting more than just a device capable of streaming YouTube content. This is precisely what makes Chromecast unique. Nowadays, many smart TVs, set-top boxes, Blu-ray players, and other devices can already stream YouTube content. These devices all come pre-installed with a YouTube app in their firmware.

However, unlike a Blu-ray player with a pre-installed YouTube app, Chromecast itself does not actually stream YouTube content. It is essentially just a receiver. When transmitting content externally, you need a "sender" app running on an Android device, iOS device, or Chrome browser. The combination of receiving and sending functions constitutes the complete Chromecast system.

How important is this sender app? The Google Cast SDK documentation explains: "The nature of interactive mode means that tags, windows, or pop-up windows cannot be created, and the screen of the receiver device should require no input. All interactions with the application must be completed through a sender app."

When using Chromecast to stream content, you won’t see any buttons, input fields, or menus on your TV screen. All user interactions occur on the sender device. Thus, the user interface displayed on the TV is exactly the same as what the user sees on their Android or iOS device or in Chrome browser. The only difference is that when you press the "send" button, the content appears on your TV screen.

However, Chromecast doesn’t merely mirror what you see on your sender device’s screen onto the TV. Even while content plays on the TV, you are free to browse the web, send emails, play games, or even turn off the screen without interrupting playback on the TV.

This is possible because you aren't transmitting content directly from your tablet to the TV. The sender app sends only an instruction telling Chromecast where to fetch the content stream, which it then renders via a custom receiver app loaded and run on the device. Netflix content is transmitted via a Netflix receiver app running on the device, and YouTube content is transmitted via the corresponding YouTube receiver app.

The Chromecast device itself runs an embedded version of Chrome OS, and all Chromecast receiver apps are web applications. Therefore, any content that can be rendered in a browser using HTML5, Google-supported media formats, and DRM technologies should be supported by Chromecast receiver apps. (Despite being built on Chrome, Chromecast currently does not support Google's Native Client technology.)

For various reasons, all of this is very important. First, it means Chromecast is flexible. While we like to call it a platform now, Chromecast is definitely more of a media platform than most dedicated media players available today.

Unlike a Blu-ray player, Chromecast’s streaming capabilities are not limited to the apps pre-installed on it when you buy the device. Instead, it can support almost limitless streaming sources. Adding new sources doesn’t require a firmware update; you just need to download the app to your Android or iOS device. When you press the "send" button, Chromecast can automatically load and start the corresponding receiver app.

Equally important is that independent developers don’t need to wait for Google to support their services. They can add Chromecast support to their own apps and media content. In fact, the number of streaming sources Chromecast can support is limited only by developers' willingness.

Designed for both consumers and developers

Chromecast still has a lot of untapped potential. It currently doesn't support streaming from local media sources, but it likely will in the future. All it needs is a streaming server, such as adding Chromecast support to the existing Android and iOS apps from Plex. In fact, employees at Plex have already tweeted that they’ve ordered several of these devices.

Smart TVs and set-top boxes tend to place all control functions on the TV screen, but users have consistently been disappointed with these interfaces. Enabling users to send content to the TV using interfaces they already know and understand is a clever move that could spark mainstream interest in internet TV.

Finally, don’t assume that this $35 device is the final form of Chromecast. This technology is likely to be integrated into Google’s future smart TV products, and other device manufacturers may eventually incorporate it into their own devices. Given the current low price and relatively low power consumption of the device, the barrier to entry seems quite low.

However, some issues remain to be resolved. Browsing and searching for content on mobile devices may be better suited for a TV-based user interface, but for playing, pausing, rewinding, and fast-forwarding content, using a traditional remote control seems more convenient. I hope Chromecast can support more home control devices, reducing the reliance on computers (or human brains) for the TV-watching experience.

The Google Cast SDK also raises some concerns. Google stated upon releasing the 1.0 SDK: "You may not publicly release or distribute your own Cast app without written permission from Google." It’s unclear how much leeway developers have in creating Cast apps. I sent a letter to Google asking for clarification on this policy, but have yet to receive a response.

At the same time, whether media companies are willing to cooperate on streaming remains an issue. Of course, many media companies are eager to work with Google. At the Chromecast launch event, Android Vice President Sundar Pichai announced that Pandora would be the next service to support Chromecast. A Hulu representative mentioned in an email that Hulu is actively working with Google to enable Hulu Plus support for this technology.

But what about other media companies? For example, Amazon, Apple, and Walmart's Vudu - are they willing to play in Google's backyard?

Other companies are likely to develop and launch a technology that competes with Chromecast. Other large streaming companies have the ability to do this, as do device manufacturers like Roku or Sony. Once competing technology emerges, how much impact will it have on Chromecast's ability to capture market attention? Although it's difficult to assess now, there will certainly be an impact.

In order to succeed in this field, Google must possess two qualities. Other companies looking to develop similar technologies must also have them: substantial financial resources and a willingness to learn from failure. Google has already experienced failure with the Nexus Q project, having to reduce the pre-order price to zero less than two months after announcing its launch.

Chromecast appears to hold more promise. It's said that pre-orders were snapped up instantly, indicating that this affordable device has caught the attention and expectations of many consumers.

Whether Chromecast can fully realize its potential depends on many factors, but if you think its lack of novel features is a weakness, I suggest reconsidering. Through this affordable hardware device, you'll see a mature and highly potential streaming technology.

Smart TV manufacturers have made many mistakes, but this may make Chromecast even more worth watching.
