Fang Zhouzi and Cui Yongyuan Battle Over GMO Weibo Posts: Mutual Questions About Each Other's Chinese Proficiency

by anonymous on 2013-09-11 14:04:41

Recently, science writer Fang Zhouzi initiated an event encouraging netizens to taste genetically modified (GM) corn, claiming that conditions should be created for the Chinese people to eat GM food every day. He stated that the safety of GM food has long been proven, while online rumors such as "rats developing tumors after eating GM corn" are all false information. He and TV host Cui Yongyuan debated the issue of GM food on Weibo. This debate started with a Weibo post:

Screenshot of Fang Zhouzi's Tencent Weibo

Subsequently, both parties attacked each other regarding issues of linguistic logic and qualifications for popularizing science.

@Cui Yongyuan - Speaking Frankly: #GM Food, Would You Eat It?# You can choose to eat it, and I can choose not to. You can claim you understand 'science,' but I have reasons and rights to question whether the 'science' you understand is really scientific. You can call me an idiot, and I can say you're wasting your time.

@Fang Zhouzi: Doing science popularization requires language and logic, just like being a host does. For example, you just said, "You can call me an idiot," then when people actually consider you an idiot regarding GM issues, you immediately change your tune and say, "How do you know I don't understand?" What kind of 'language and logic' is this? Can others really call you an idiot?

@Fang Zhouzi: Of course, you can choose not to eat it, but don't spread rumors and hinder the development of China's agricultural technology. I am promoting science recognized by international authoritative scientific institutions, which you fundamentally do not understand. On what grounds do you question it?

@Cui Yongyuan - Speaking Frankly: Which part is 'spreading rumors'? How do you know I don't understand? Why don't you brush up on your Chinese and basic logic before doing 'science popularization'? If you can't even understand these simple Chinese sentences, what qualifies you to do science popularization?

@Fang Zhouzi: Is it a rumor to say that GM corn causes tumors in rats or that Golden Rice has problems? After how many biology classes did you take to think you know more about GM than biologists? I am a Ph.D. in biochemistry and have written over twenty popular science books. Am I still unqualified to do science popularization? Should my qualification for science popularization be determined by a TV host?

@Cui Yongyuan - Speaking Frankly: Popular Science 1: Joseph Needham was born in 1900 and became a member of the Royal Society at the age of 37. His famous works in biochemistry and embryology, "Chemical Embryology" and "Biochemistry and Morphogenesis," were published before he turned 40. I believe he has the 'qualification' for popular science. The first part of the seventh volume of his seven-volume masterpiece ("Science and Civilization in China") is titled: Language and Logic. See, popular science indeed requires these fundamentals.

@Fang Zhouzi: Of course, popular science requires language and logic, but your evidence for proving this point is that Joseph Needham wrote a book called "Language and Logic." What kind of reasoning is this? Needham's book is a work of the history of science, so how does it relate to how to do popular science?

@Cui Yongyuan - Speaking Frankly: Popular Science 2: In discussing the diagnostic methods and regulation of yin-yang imbalance and the disruption of the five elements in traditional Chinese medicine, Needham said, "Although we are in modern times, we remain puzzled by how medieval doctors penetrated the secrets of the interaction between these two forces. However, many of these regulatory treatments indeed help restore the balance of the human nervous and endocrine systems, and we have no objections to this." It seems that there can be different views on whether traditional Chinese medicine is pseudoscience.

@Fang Zhouzi: Of course, one can hold different views on any issue, but not all different views are valuable. To prove that a discipline is scientific, it is not done by quoting a certain celebrity's opinion, nor by playing word games to force connections with modern medical discoveries. This is what is called 'language and logic.' Moreover, Needham did not think traditional Chinese medicine was scientific; hence the 'Needham Question' (why did modern science not emerge in ancient China).

@Cui Yongyuan - Speaking Frankly: Popular Science 3: Interestingly, doubts about many of Joseph Needham's viewpoints have never ceased worldwide, despite his excellent academic background. However, he never referred to these doubts as 'rumors,' nor did he say these doubts 'hindered' the development of Chinese science and technology, let alone ask 'what qualifications do you have to question?' So, 'famous' popular science experts come in both pro-GM and non-pro-GM varieties.

@Fang Zhouzi: At most, Needham could be considered a great historian of science, where does 'popular science expert' come from? Whether his research conclusions stand holds no relation to the development of Chinese science and technology. However, genetic modification technology is the direction of modern agriculture. Using one's influence to demonize GM technology with rumors and lies will certainly hinder the development of Chinese science and technology. To question scientific and academic issues, one must have qualifications to prove they understand the field. Otherwise, why list how great Needham is?

@Fang Zhouzi: The thresholds for different disciplines are not the same. For instance, the threshold for the history of ancient Chinese science is not high. Even if you are not in that field, if you can read classical Chinese and master historical materials, you can completely question some of Needham's statements (in fact, Needham's research has been found to contain numerous errors, some due to his misinterpretation of classical Chinese). But biotechnology is different. Modern biology is a highly specialized discipline, and those who are not trained in this field cannot identify its problems. That's why almost all the people demonizing GM technology are amateurs like TV hosts, and very few are biologists — it comes down to Jiang Gaoming, Wang Yuandan, and a couple of oddities like them.

The Controversial Genetically Modified Organisms

Genetically modified food has been debated for many years. Generally speaking, the scientific community tends to support the promotion of GM technology, while the public has a natural resistance to this new thing because they worry that there aren't enough samples to prove that GM food is 100% safe. Controversy is reasonable and necessary. In the process of debate, on one hand, it allows scientists to be more cautious in conducting stricter assessments of the safety of GM food; on the other hand, it can better educate the public about GM knowledge.

In fact, this is also the norm for the development of science and technology. Science always advances through controversy, and GM simply manifests this more prominently.
