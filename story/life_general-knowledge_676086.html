What to do when a baby has a cold?

A cold usually refers to an upper respiratory tract infection, including rhinitis, pharyngitis, laryngitis, otitis media, sinusitis and other infections above the throat. For older children, there will be 4-6 such infections every year. As long as the child's temperature does not exceed 38.5℃, no special treatment is needed. Pay attention to rest, give more water, and give some pediatric cough syrup for symptomatic relief if the child coughs. However, for infants, even a cold can be dangerous. At first, it may just be a stuffy nose and slightly coarse breathing. If not paid attention to, it can quickly lead to breathing difficulties, bronchitis, pneumonia, or even pose a threat to life.

Colds in older children are mostly caused by viral infections, while in infants there are two situations:

(1) Caused by infection. In this case, the child appears lethargic, irritable, with elevated body temperature, and unwilling to breastfeed.

(2) The child’s physiological structure and allergies cause nasal congestion and breathing difficulties.

In this situation, the child only shows symptoms of a stuffy nose and sneezing, but normal breastfeeding, good spirits, and no fever. When cold air stimulates the nasal mucosa, due to allergy, it can cause swelling of the nasal cavity mucosa, which is mostly manifested as a blocked nose, increased secretion in the nasal cavity, leading to runny nose or nasal congestion. This will inevitably cause breathing difficulties, making it hard for the child to eat well, taking a few bites and then pausing to catch their breath; some children become irritable, crying and screaming because they cannot feed comfortably.

Do not easily use nasal drops for children when they have a blocked nose, especially adult nasal drops are not suitable for children. A better method is:

(1) Use a warm towel to apply heat to the root of the nose.

(2) Increase indoor humidity, such as placing a damp towel on the radiator or using a humidifier.

(3) When bathing the child, drop a little water into the nasal cavity, moisten the nasal crust and then gently remove it with a cloth strip.

(4) Use a manual aspirator to suck out the nasal crust, etc.

When a baby has a cold, sucking milk becomes difficult. You can also express breast milk and feed it with a dropper or spoon to avoid affecting food intake due to breathing difficulties.

When family members have a cold, try to minimize contact with the infant to avoid infecting them. If the mother has a cold, she can continue breastfeeding, but should wear a mask while feeding and wash her hands before touching the child.
