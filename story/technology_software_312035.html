Barcelona's Alleged Championship Handout Sparks Controversy: Rivals Claim "Give Them 70 Points, We'll Compete for Second"

Sina Sports - Last weekend’s Barcelona derby left Espanyol feeling hard done by and Barcelona unimpressed with their own performance, all thanks to referee Iturralde. He "mysteriously" awarded Barcelona a penalty, helping the home side secure a 1-0 victory over their fierce local rivals, and then sent off Espanyol's Baena in the players' tunnel after the game for questioning his penalty decision.

In all fairness, this penalty seemed somewhat intentionally detrimental to Espanyol, and intriguingly, two different versions of the truth emerged from the Barcelona and Espanyol camps regarding this single penalty.

If Brualla is considered part of the "Real Madrid faction" of referees, then Iturralde, one of Spain's most famous referees for this match, belongs to the "Barcelona faction." Previously, Barcelona hadn't lost any games at the Camp Nou under his jurisdiction, and this season he had only officiated one game involving Barcelona—a 2-0 away win against Getafe. Another telling fact is that in the last six matches Iturralde refereed involving Barcelona, five penalties were awarded—all benefiting Barcelona. According to *Marca*, four of these penalties were questionable, suggesting they might have been "given" to Barcelona.

The contentious moment occurred in the 39th minute of the match against Espanyol when Ibrahimović crossed from the left edge of the box, and as Xavi contested for the ball, he suddenly fell. Iturralde ruled that Baena had pulled Xavi's shirt during the challenge and awarded a penalty, a decision that sparked strong dissatisfaction from the Espanyol camp. After the game, Espanyol coach Mauricio Pochettino said cryptically: “I didn’t see clearly what happened in the box, maybe the referee did. Even after watching the replay, I can't be sure it was a penalty. My players told me at halftime that nothing happened, so in my view, there was no penalty.”

Espanyol player Luis García expressed frustration, complaining that Iturralde's unfair call hurt Espanyol: “If we apply this standard, we could get penalties in Barcelona's box too. Yes, Barcelona is very strong, but they shouldn’t score like this.”

The reaction within Barcelona's camp was quite different. The key player involved, Xavi, testified: “This was an obvious penalty, didn’t you see? In the box, my jersey was pulled, Baena grabbed my shirt and prevented me from jumping. There's no doubt, it was a penalty—he pulled me, and since I couldn’t use my legs, I fell.” After Xavi went down, Guardiola helplessly threw up his hands on the sidelines, indicating support for his player's appeal for the penalty. However, post-game, Guardiola admitted he wasn't entirely sure if it was a penalty, saying only, “Xavi told me it was Baena, and I trust Xavi.”

Clearly, Espanyol's manager Planes didn’t believe Xavi: “How can Xavi say that? According to Iturralde’s standards, we could’ve had 12 penalties in this Barcelona derby! I insist that there was no physical contact between Baena and Xavi, and even if there was, it wasn’t enough to cause him to fall.” CEO Collet added, “Why are we always the ones hurt in every derby? Last time it was a 93rd-minute penalty, and Messi’s handball. This time again... If it were Real Madrid, someone would've surely called for Franco's overthrow already. The federation should consider this issue—if this keeps happening, why not just give Barcelona 70 points and let the rest of us compete for second place?”

No matter what others said, it made no difference. Regarding the penalty controversy, Baena, the central figure, remained tight-lipped, refusing to speak to anyone until two days later, when the Barcelona-trained Espanyol player appeared on Catalan Radio to recount the incident. “Yes, I did pull Xavi,” he admitted, confirming the possibility of the penalty, though either Xavi’s fall was exaggerated or Iturralde made a wrong call. Baena continued, “But it definitely wasn’t a penalty. The force of my pull wasn’t enough to make Xavi lose balance and fall. Xavi was positioned very far forward, and I reached back to feel where he was. That’s all. I slightly blocked Xavi, but the strength wasn’t enough to knock him down—check the replay. Honestly, I can’t believe it was called a penalty because I was thinking about whether Xavi would shoot before turning around.”

Reports also suggested that Baena confronted Iturralde in the players’ tunnel after the match and was shown a red card. What exactly did Baena say to irritate Iturralde? “I simply told him, ‘Go check the replay!’ But I certainly didn’t use an aggressive tone—just that.”

Baena was also upset with Guardiola, who after the game claimed, “Xavi told me that Espanyol players admitted it was a penalty.” “I really don’t understand how they can say that—I never told Xavi it was a penalty. If there was any conversation between us, I would have definitely told him it wasn’t a penalty.”

This controversy gave Madrid media a chance to stir things up. Both *Marca* and *As* criticized Iturralde, believing his calls protected Barcelona and cost them three crucial points; otherwise, Real Madrid, having played fewer games, would have trailed Barcelona by only two points. Meanwhile, Catalan media, despite Espanyol being a Barcelona-based team, wouldn’t advocate for a club bearing the Spanish royal crest. To counter capital city media, *Sport* carefully analyzed match footage and provided screenshots showing Baena indeed pulling Xavi's jersey. Deputy editor Carazo stated, “It’s clear—it was a penalty.” More provocatively, the paper showed a screenshot of Real Madrid's controversial first goal against Valencia, implying Benzema was offside when receiving the pass, titling it: “Real Madrid’s first goal was offside—what’s there to argue about?”

Fortunately, Barcelona, Xavi, and Guardiola, embroiled in the penalty scandal, have now moved beyond the storm center of Spain and begun their FIFA Club World Cup journey in Abu Dhabi. For them, securing this championship is far more meaningful than debating whether that penalty was justified. (Liu Hui)
