"Hainan watermelons are top quality." At the Xinfadi fruit wholesale market, there are watermelons from Vietnam, Myanmar, and Hainan, but Hainan watermelons are on average 0.6 yuan more expensive per pound. In response to this phenomenon, watermelon vendors unanimously said: "Vietnam and Myanmar watermelons use less organic fertilizer, their sweetness and appearance cannot compare with those from Hainan." Pan Xiaohai is the largest watermelon vendor in Beijing, selling 20,000 tons of Hainan watermelons annually. He said that almost all watermelons sold in Beijing supermarkets come from Hainan, all carefully packaged by distribution companies before being sent in. Jingkelong Company owns 230 chain supermarkets in Beijing, with fresh produce sales exceeding 300 million yuan. Li Sheng, deputy manager of the company's fresh produce procurement department, introduced that last year, the sales of Hainan fruits and vegetables at Jingkelong broke through 3000 tons, and this year it is expected to reach the 4000-ton mark. Why such a large increase? The main reason, according to analysis, is the shift in consumption patterns, with more and more people buying fruits and vegetables at supermarkets because supermarket products have labels and origin information, giving consumers peace of mind; additionally, Jingkelong itself is expanding relatively quickly, adding 30 chain stores annually. It is understood that selling Hainan fruits and vegetables has become a competitive strategy among Beijing supermarkets, with Jingkelong Company and Beijing Wumart Group establishing production bases in Hainan or teaming up with large Hainan growers to achieve direct entry of Hainan fruits and vegetables into supermarkets. From early January to mid-April each year, most of the bananas, mangoes, papayas, green beans, and peppers sold in Beijing's major supermarkets are produced in Hainan. Huang Dong from the Hainan Provincial Department of Agriculture's Beijing Agricultural Products Circulation Office said that consumers are increasingly concerned about food safety issues, but traditional farmers' markets cannot trace the origins of produce, which does not give people peace of mind; additionally, large supermarkets directly purchasing from Hainan can save more than 15% of costs, making retail prices increasingly close to those of farmers' markets. In recent years, the pace of Hainan fruits and vegetables entering supermarkets has continuously accelerated, and currently, about 30% of Hainan fruits and vegetables sold in Beijing have entered supermarkets.
