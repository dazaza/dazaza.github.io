According to Xinhua News Agency's Henan Channel on February 12, from February 10 to 12, in a village in Henan Province, which has the largest rural population in China, a farmers' basketball game akin to an "NBA" finals attracted people from neighboring villages to watch. The "Ten Thousand Villages and Thousands of Townships" farmers' basketball league that spans nearly a year is referred to by Chinese farmers as "our own NBA league."

On February 10, the final of Henan Province’s "Ten Thousand Villages and Thousands of Townships" farmers' basketball league officially kicked off. Teams from the top three places in the southern and northern zones gathered at the Dàyíng Village Farmers' Gymnasium in Shǎnxian County in western Henan. This village with over ten thousand residents was bustling like during the Spring Festival. Bicycles, motorcycles, and tricycles were parked outside the gymnasium gates. Stalls selling fried dough sticks, húlàtāng (spicy soup), and snacks were set up again on the streets, filling the air with delicious aromas.

The players of these six teams participating in the finals looked nothing like professional athletes. At the opening ceremony, one player walked onto the court eating a big pretzel. They are all farmers from across the province; some farm at home, while others work in local township enterprises. Basketball is their shared passion.

Li Hongwei, head coach of the Sanmenxia City representative team who works at a local enterprise, began participating in farmers' basketball games in 2003. “Back then, we played matches on dirt courts in the village, and better ones had cement floors. After a match, our bodies and mouths would be full of mud. For lunch, we just ate a big mixed stew,” Li Hongwei said, “But playing ball in the village was lively, with many spectators. People would stand on trees and rooftops to watch.”

The 33-year-old Li Hongwei lives in Dàzìyíng Village in Xīyán Township, Língbǎo City. He said that the format of the village competitions is quite formal, complete with commentators and sponsors, such as the local noodle shop sponsoring free bowls of noodles for the players after the match.

“Village games have their own rural charm and aren't about how well you play,” Li Hongwei said.

If following the NBA format, the village games would be considered "pre-season" games, mainly centered around basketball-themed competitive activities within the village, either as matches or as entertainment activities like shooting hoops or dribbling contests.

The "regular season" involves county-level competitions determining city champions, while the "playoffs" divide the 18 provincial cities and 6 expanded rights counties into southern and northern zones, each consisting of 12 teams. Teams within each zone compete to determine the top three finalists for the championship, also known as the North-South Basketball Rivalry.

Deputy Director Shi Zhanshang of Henan Province's Mass Sports Division said that farmers' basketball games originally started in western Henan because the atmosphere there for basketball was good, with spontaneous organization of village-level matches. Starting in 2003, the Provincial Sports Bureau began organizing matches systematically and gradually expanded the farmers' basketball league throughout the province.

Inside the Dàyíng Village gymnasium, the stands were packed with spectators, including elderly people with white hair, women holding children, uncles smoking, and children eating lollipops, creating a very lively scene. The audience's enthusiasm was no less than that of large-scale competitions.

"The number eight in red plays pretty well; this guy has a brain when it comes to basketball," said 63-year-old Nán Shìyǒng, commenting on the game while discussing with someone beside him. This retired soldier used to be one of the "tough guys" playing basketball in the army and frequently participated in village matches after retirement. Now, whenever there is a major match here, he rides his bicycle for several miles to watch.

During halftime, energetic music played as the "basketball beauties" took the stage. They were the village dance team, who usually danced and exercised at the cultural square in the village. These women, ranging from their twenties to forties and fifties, immediately drew cheers from the audience as they danced, heating up the atmosphere on the court.

Above the stands in the Dàyíng Village gymnasium hung a huge photo of He Zhenliang, honorary chairman of the Chinese Olympic Committee, inspecting Dàyíng Village in 2003, along with his inscription: "Only when farmers' sports develop will there be true nationwide sports."

Shi Zhanshang said that basketball is still very popular in rural areas. We aim to create a brand and increase the visibility of this competition. Then, we'll organize these award-winning teams to conduct exhibition matches in rural areas, encouraging more farmers to participate in basketball and promoting nationwide fitness in rural areas.

Shi Zhanshang added that currently, approximately 300,000 farmers participate in this activity annually, but compared to the tens of millions of rural residents in Henan, this is still a small number. Moreover, our goal isn't just to make farmers love basketball but to make them fall in love with sports and engage in physical activities to enhance their health.
